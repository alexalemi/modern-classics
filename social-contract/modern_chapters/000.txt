Foreword

The Social Contract, or Principles of Political Right

"Fœderis æquas / Dicamus leges" — "Let us set equal terms for the truce." (Virgil, Aeneid XI)

This little book is part of a much longer work that I began years ago, before I understood my own limits, and abandoned long since. Of all the fragments that could have been rescued from what I wrote, this is the most substantial, and, I think, the least unworthy of being offered to the public. The rest no longer exists.

Here is what I want to find out: can there be, in civil society, any rule of government that is both reliable and legitimate — taking men as they are, and laws as they might be? Throughout this inquiry I will try to keep two things joined together: what right permits and what interest demands, so that justice and usefulness never find themselves pulled apart.

I begin without pausing to prove that the subject matters. Am I a prince or a legislator, that I should write about politics? I will be asked the question, and my answer is no — I am neither, and that is exactly why I write. If I were a prince or a legislator, I would not waste my time saying what ought to be done. I would do it, or keep silent.

But I was born a citizen of a free state and a member of its sovereign — a member, that is, of the body of people who together hold the lawmaking power. However faint my voice may be in public affairs, the right to vote on them makes it my duty to study them. And I am happy to find, whenever I reflect on governments, that my inquiries keep handing me new reasons to love the government of my own country.


Book I

Chapter 1: The Subject of This First Book

Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains.

One man thinks himself the master of others — and remains a greater slave than any of them. How did this change come about? I do not know. What can make it legitimate? That question I believe I can answer.

If I considered nothing but force and the results that flow from it, I would say this: as long as a people is compelled to obey, and does obey, it does well; the moment it can shake off the yoke, and does shake it off, it does even better. For if a people regains its freedom by the same right that was used to take that freedom away, then either it is justified in taking it back, or no one was justified in taking it in the first place. But the social order is a sacred right, and it is the foundation of every other right. Yet this right does not come from nature. It must therefore rest on agreements. Before we come to those agreements, I have to prove what I have just claimed.

Chapter 2: The First Societies

The oldest of all societies, and the only natural one, is the family. And even there, the children stay bound to the father only as long as they need him in order to survive. The moment that need ends, the natural bond dissolves. The children, released from the obedience they owed their father, and the father, released from the care he owed his children, both return to independence. If they remain together, they do so no longer by nature but by choice; and from then on the family itself holds together only by agreement.

This shared freedom is a consequence of human nature. Man's first law is to look after his own preservation; his first cares are the ones he owes to himself. As soon as he reaches the age of reason, he alone can judge the best means of preserving himself — and at that moment he becomes his own master.

The family, then, may be called the first model of political societies. The ruler is the image of the father; the people are the image of the children; and since all are born free and equal, none of them gives up his freedom except for his own advantage. The whole difference is this: in the family, the father's love for his children repays him for the care he gives them, while in the State, the pleasure of commanding takes the place of a love the chief cannot feel for the peoples beneath him.

Grotius denies that all human power is established for the benefit of the governed, and he offers slavery as his proof. His usual method of reasoning is to establish right by appealing to fact. ("Learned inquiries into public right," wrote the Marquis d'Argenson, "are often only the history of past abuses; and we waste our effort when we insist on studying them too closely." That is exactly what Grotius does.) One could choose a more logical method, but none friendlier to tyrants.

So according to Grotius, it is an open question whether the human race belongs to a hundred men, or whether those hundred men belong to the human race; and throughout his book he seems to lean toward the first answer. That is Hobbes's view as well. On this account, the human species is divided up into so many herds of cattle, each with its own ruler, who guards the herd in order to devour it.

Just as a shepherd is a being of a higher nature than his flock, so the shepherds of men — their rulers — are beings of a higher nature than their peoples. That, Philo tells us, is how the Emperor Caligula reasoned, concluding with equal logic either that kings were gods or that men were beasts.

Caligula's reasoning matches that of Hobbes and Grotius. Aristotle, before any of them, had said that men are not naturally equal at all: some are born for slavery, others for mastery.

Aristotle was right — but he mistook the effect for the cause. Nothing is more certain than that every man born in slavery is born for slavery. Slaves lose everything in their chains, even the desire to escape from them. They come to love their servitude, just as the companions of Ulysses came to love their brutish condition. (Plutarch wrote a short treatise on this, called "That Animals Reason.") If there are slaves by nature, it is because there were first slaves against nature. Force made the first slaves; their cowardice kept them slaves.

I have said nothing about King Adam, or about Emperor Noah, father of the three great monarchs who divided up the universe among themselves like the children of Saturn — indeed, some scholars have claimed they were the children of Saturn. I hope to be thanked for my restraint. For as a direct descendant of one of these princes — possibly of the eldest branch — how do I know that a careful check of the records would not show me to be the legitimate king of the human race? In any case, there is no denying that Adam was sovereign of the world, just as Robinson Crusoe was sovereign of his island, for as long as he was its only inhabitant. And that empire had one great convenience: the monarch, secure on his throne, had no rebellions, wars, or conspirators to fear.

Chapter 3: The Right of the Strongest

The strongest is never strong enough to be always the master, unless he transforms strength into right, and obedience into duty. Hence "the right of the strongest" — a phrase that sounds like irony, yet is laid down in practice as a genuine principle. But will no one ever explain it to us? Force is physical power; I do not see what morality could ever come from it. Giving in to force is something you do out of necessity, not choice — at most, out of prudence. In what sense could it ever be a duty?

Suppose for a moment that this so-called right exists. I say the only thing it produces is a heap of unexplainable nonsense. For if force creates right, then the effect changes whenever the cause does: any force greater than the first inherits its right. The moment you can disobey without being punished, your disobedience is legitimate; and since the strongest is always in the right, the only question that matters is how to become the strongest. But what kind of right is it that dies the moment force fails? If you must obey because you are forced to, there is no need to obey from duty; and if you are no longer forced to obey, you are no longer obliged to. Clearly, the word "right" adds nothing to force here. It means nothing at all.

Obey the powers that be. If that means "yield to force," the advice is sound but unnecessary — I can promise it will never be violated. All power comes from God, I grant; but so does all sickness. Does that mean we are forbidden to call a doctor? A robber surprises me at the edge of a wood. I must hand over my purse — I am forced to. But suppose I could manage to keep it from him: am I bound in conscience to give it up anyway? After all, the pistol in his hand is a power too.

Let us agree, then, that force does not create right, and that we are obliged to obey only legitimate powers. And with that, my original question returns.

Chapter 4: Slavery

Since no man has natural authority over his fellow man, and since force creates no right, we are left with agreements as the basis of all legitimate authority among men.

If an individual, says Grotius, can alienate his liberty — give it away or sell it — and make himself the slave of a master, why couldn't a whole people do the same and make itself the subject of a king? There are several slippery words in that sentence that would need explaining, but let us stay with the word alienate. To alienate is to give or to sell. Now, a man who becomes another man's slave does not give himself; he sells himself, at the very least in exchange for his keep. But what would a whole people sell itself for? A king is so far from supplying his subjects with their living that he draws his own entirely from them — and as Rabelais says, kings do not live on nothing. Do subjects, then, hand over their persons on condition that the king takes their goods as well? I fail to see what they would have left to protect.

It will be said that a despot guarantees his subjects civil peace. Granted — but what do they gain, if the wars his ambition drags them into, his bottomless greed, and the harassment they suffer from his ministers grind them down harder than their own quarrels ever would? What do they gain, if this very peace is one of their miseries? There is peace in dungeons too; is that enough to make dungeons desirable places to live? The Greeks shut up in the cave of the Cyclops lived there very peacefully — while waiting for their turn to be devoured.

To say that a man gives himself away for nothing is to say something absurd and unthinkable. Such an act is null and illegitimate for the simple reason that anyone who does it is out of his mind. To say the same of a whole people is to imagine a nation of madmen — and madness creates no right.

Even if a man could alienate himself, he could not alienate his children. They are born human and born free; their liberty belongs to them, and no one but they has the right to dispose of it. Before they reach the age of reason, their father may, in their name, set conditions for their preservation and well-being — but he cannot give them away, irrevocably and unconditionally. Such a gift runs contrary to the purposes of nature and exceeds the rights of fatherhood. So for an arbitrary government to be legitimate, every new generation would have to be free to accept it or reject it — and in that case the government would no longer be arbitrary.

To renounce your liberty is to renounce being human — to surrender the rights of humanity, and even its duties. There is no possible compensation for someone who gives up everything. Such a renunciation is incompatible with man's very nature; to strip all liberty from his will is to strip all morality from his actions. Finally, an agreement that sets absolute authority on one side and unlimited obedience on the other is empty and self-contradicting. Is it not obvious that we owe nothing to a person from whom we have the right to demand everything? And does not that single condition — no equivalence, no exchange — make the act null and void all by itself? For what claim could my slave have against me, when everything he has belongs to me? His right is my right; and a right of mine against myself is a phrase with no meaning at all.

Grotius and the others find another source for this so-called right of slavery: war. The victor, they hold, has the right to kill the vanquished; so the vanquished can buy back his life at the price of his liberty — an agreement all the more legitimate, supposedly, because it benefits both sides.

But it is clear that this supposed right to kill the conquered cannot be derived from the state of war. Men living in their primitive independence have no relations with one another stable enough to constitute either a state of peace or a state of war; therefore they cannot naturally be enemies. War arises from a relation between things, not between persons. And since the state of war cannot be born of simple personal relations, but only of property relations, private war — war of man against man — can exist neither in the state of nature, where there is no settled property, nor in the social state, where everything is under the authority of the laws.

Individual fights, duels, and skirmishes are acts; they do not make a state of affairs. As for the private wars authorized by the Establishments of King Louis IX of France and suspended by the Peace of God, they were abuses of feudalism — a system as absurd as any that ever existed, contrary to the principles of natural right and to all sound politics.

War, then, is a relation not between man and man, but between State and State. In war, individuals are enemies only by accident — not as men, not even as citizens, but as soldiers; not as members of their country, but as its defenders. Finally, a State can have only other States for enemies, never men; for between things of completely different natures, no genuine relation is possible.

— On this point the Romans deserve a note, for no nation on earth understood and respected the right of war better. They carried their scruples so far that no citizen was allowed to volunteer without swearing himself in expressly against the enemy — against this particular enemy, by name. When the legion in which the younger Cato was serving his first campaign under Popilius was reorganized, the elder Cato wrote to Popilius that if he wanted the young man to go on serving under him, he must administer a new military oath; for the first oath had been annulled, and without a new one his son could no longer bear arms against the enemy. Cato also wrote to his son, warning him to take great care not to set foot in battle until he had sworn that new oath. I know the siege of Clusium and other isolated incidents can be quoted against me; but I am citing laws and customs. The Romans broke their own laws less often than any other people — and no other people has had laws so good.

This principle also agrees with the established rules of every age and the constant practice of every civilized people. Declarations of war are warnings less to governments than to their subjects. The foreigner — whether king, private person, or whole people — who robs, kills, or imprisons subjects without declaring war on their prince is not an enemy but a brigand. Even in the middle of a real war, a just prince may seize everything in enemy territory that belongs to the public, but he respects the persons and property of individuals: he respects the rights on which his own rights are founded. The object of war is the destruction of the enemy State, so the other side has the right to kill its defenders as long as they bear arms; but the moment they lay down their weapons and surrender, they stop being enemies, or instruments of the enemy, and become simply men again — and over their lives no one has any right. Sometimes a State can be killed without a single one of its members dying; and war confers no right that is not necessary to its purpose. These are not Grotius's principles, and they do not rest on the authority of poets. They flow from the nature of things, and they are founded on reason.

As for the right of conquest, it has no foundation but the right of the strongest. If war does not give the victor the right to massacre conquered peoples, then a right that does not exist cannot be the basis of a right to enslave them. A man has the right to kill an enemy only when he cannot make him a slave; so the right to enslave him cannot be derived from the right to kill him. It is therefore an unfair exchange to make him buy, at the price of his liberty, a life over which the victor holds no right at all. Founding the right of life and death on the right of slavery, and the right of slavery on the right of life and death — is the vicious circle not plain to see?

Even if we grant this terrible right to kill anyone and everyone, I maintain that a slave made in war, or a conquered people, owes a master nothing except obedience for exactly as long as it is compelled. By taking an equivalent for his victim's life, the victor has done him no favor: instead of killing him without profit, he has killed him profitably. Far from acquiring any authority over him beyond force, the victor remains with him in a state of war just as before. Their relation is itself the effect of that war, and exercising the rights of war implies no treaty of peace. An agreement has been made, yes — but that agreement, far from ending the state of war, presupposes that it continues.

So from whatever angle we examine the question, the right of slavery is null and void — not only because it is illegitimate, but because it is absurd and meaningless. The words slave and right contradict each other; they cancel each other out. Whether spoken by one man to another man, or by one man to an entire people, this sentence will always be equal nonsense: "I make with you an agreement entirely at your expense and entirely to my advantage; I shall keep it as long as I please, and you shall keep it as long as I please."

Chapter 5: Why We Must Always Go Back to a First Agreement

Even if I granted everything I have so far refuted, the friends of despotism would be no better off. There will always be a vast difference between subduing a crowd and ruling a society. Suppose scattered individuals, however many, were enslaved one after another by a single man. I still see nothing there but a master and his slaves — certainly not a people and its ruler. It is, if you like, an aggregation, but it is not an association. There is no public good there, and no body politic. That man, even if he enslaved half the world, is still only an individual; his interest, cut off from everyone else's, is still nothing but a private interest. When he dies, his empire is left scattered and without unity behind him, as an oak crumbles into a heap of ashes once the fire has consumed it.

A people, says Grotius, can give itself to a king. Then according to Grotius himself, a people is already a people before it gives itself. The gift itself is a civil act; it presupposes a public deliberation. So before examining the act by which a people gives itself to a king, we would do well to examine the act by which a people becomes a people. That act, coming necessarily before the other, is the true foundation of society.

Indeed, if there were no earlier agreement, then — unless the vote were unanimous — where would the minority's obligation to accept the majority's choice come from? By what right do the hundred men who want a master vote on behalf of the ten who do not? The rule of majority vote is itself something established by agreement, and it presupposes unanimity on at least one occasion.

Chapter 6: The Social Compact

I assume men have reached the point where the obstacles to their survival in the state of nature have grown stronger than the resources each individual can muster to maintain himself in it. At that point the primitive condition can last no longer, and the human race would perish if it did not change its way of living.

Now, men cannot create new forces; they can only unite and direct the forces that exist. So they have no other means of preserving themselves than to form, by combination, a sum of forces great enough to overcome the resistance — forces they must set in motion by a single impulse and make act together.

Such a sum of forces can arise only where many people come together. But each man's force and liberty are his chief instruments of self-preservation. How can he pledge them to the group without harming his own interests, without neglecting the care he owes himself? Brought back to my subject, the difficulty can be stated in these terms:

"The problem is to find a form of association which will defend and protect with the whole common force the person and goods of each associate, and in which each, while uniting himself with all, may still obey himself alone, and remain as free as before." This is the fundamental problem to which the social contract gives the solution.

The clauses of this contract are so completely determined by the nature of the act that the slightest modification would make them empty and useless. Though they have perhaps never been formally stated, they are everywhere the same — everywhere tacitly accepted and recognized — until the social contract is violated, at which point each person regains his original rights and takes back his natural liberty, losing the contractual liberty for which he gave it up.

Properly understood, all the clauses come down to a single one: the total alienation of each associate, with all his rights, to the whole community. For, in the first place, since each gives himself entirely, the condition is the same for everyone; and since the condition is the same for everyone, no one has any interest in making it burdensome to the rest.

What is more, since the alienation is made without reservation, the union is as perfect as it can be, and no associate has anything further to claim. If individuals kept certain rights for themselves, then — there being no common superior to judge between them and the public — each man, being his own judge on one point, would soon demand to be his own judge on all. The state of nature would live on, and the association would inevitably become either useless or tyrannical.

Finally, each man, in giving himself to all, gives himself to no one; and since there is no associate over whom he does not gain the same right that he grants over himself, he gains the equivalent of everything he loses — plus greater force to preserve what he has.

If, then, we strip the social contract of everything that is not essential to it, we will find that it reduces to these terms:

"Each of us puts his person and all his power in common under the supreme direction of the general will, and, in our corporate capacity, we receive each member as an indivisible part of the whole."

In that instant, in place of the individual person of each contracting party, this act of association creates a moral and collective body, composed of as many members as the assembly has votes, and receiving from this same act its unity, its common self, its life, and its will. This public person, formed by the union of all the others, once took the name of city, and now takes that of Republic or body politic. Its members call it the State when it is passive, the Sovereign when it is active, and a Power when comparing it to others like itself. (Mark the word Sovereign well, for everything in this book depends on it: here it never means a king. The sovereign is the people themselves, acting together as one body to make the law.) Those who are associated in it take collectively the name of people; individually, each is called a citizen, as a sharer in the sovereign authority, and a subject, as one bound by the laws of the State. These terms often get mixed up and mistaken for one another; it is enough to know how to tell them apart when they are being used with precision.

— A word about that word city, whose true meaning has been almost entirely lost in modern times. Most people take a town for a city and a townsman for a citizen. They do not know that houses make a town, but citizens make a city. This same mistake once cost the Carthaginians dearly. I have never read of the title of citizen being given to the subjects of any prince — not even to the ancient Macedonians, or to the English of today, though they are nearer to liberty than anyone else. Only the French adopt the name citizen with easy familiarity — because, as their dictionaries show, they have no idea what it means; otherwise, in usurping the title they would be committing lèse-majesté, treason against the sovereign's majesty. Among them the name expresses a virtue, not a right. When Bodin tried to speak of our citizens and townsmen, he blundered badly, taking the one class for the other. M. d'Alembert avoided the error: in his article on Geneva he correctly distinguished the four orders of men (five, if you count simple foreigners) who live in our town, of which only two make up the Republic. No other French writer, as far as I know, has understood the real meaning of the word citizen.

Chapter 7: The Sovereign

This formula shows that the act of association contains a mutual commitment between the public and the individuals, and that each individual — contracting, so to speak, with himself — is committed in two capacities at once: as a member of the Sovereign toward the individuals, and as a member of the State toward the Sovereign. But the maxim of civil law that no one is bound by commitments made to himself does not apply here, for there is a great difference between an obligation to yourself and an obligation to a whole of which you are a part.

Notice, further, that public deliberation can bind all the subjects to the Sovereign, because each of them can be regarded in those two different capacities; but, for the opposite reason, it cannot bind the Sovereign to itself. It is therefore against the nature of the body politic for the Sovereign to impose on itself a law it cannot break. Since the Sovereign can regard itself in one capacity only, it is in the position of an individual making a contract with himself — which shows that there is not, and cannot be, any kind of fundamental law binding on the body of the people, not even the social contract itself. This does not mean the body politic cannot make commitments to others, so long as those commitments do not violate the contract; for in relation to the outside world, it is a simple being, an individual.

But the body politic, or Sovereign, draws its very being from the sanctity of the contract, and so it can never bind itself — even toward an outsider — to anything that violates that original act. It cannot, for instance, alienate any part of itself, or submit to another sovereign. To violate the act by which it exists would be to annihilate itself; and what is itself nothing can create nothing.

The moment this multitude is united in one body, no one can injure one of the members without attacking the body — still less injure the body without the members feeling it. So duty and interest alike oblige the two contracting parties to help each other; and the same men should seek to combine, in their double capacity, all the advantages that come with it.

Now the Sovereign, being formed entirely of the individuals who compose it, has no interest contrary to theirs, and can have none. Therefore the sovereign power has no need to give any guarantee to its subjects, because it is impossible for the body to want to harm all its members; and, as we shall see later, it cannot harm any one of them in particular either. The Sovereign, simply by being what it is, is always what it ought to be.

The same is not true of the subjects in relation to the Sovereign. Despite the common interest, nothing would guarantee that the subjects keep their commitments unless the Sovereign found means to assure itself of their fidelity.

In fact, each individual, as a man, may have a particular will that runs contrary to the general will he has as a citizen — or simply differs from it. His private interest may speak to him very differently from the common interest. His absolute and naturally independent existence may lead him to see what he owes the common cause as a donation freely given, whose loss will hurt others less than its payment burdens him. And since the moral person of the State is a fiction — an abstraction, not a flesh-and-blood man — he may want to enjoy the rights of a citizen without being willing to fulfill the duties of a subject. Left to spread, that injustice would be the ruin of the body politic.

So in order that the social contract may not be an empty formula, it tacitly includes one commitment, which alone gives force to all the others: whoever refuses to obey the general will shall be compelled to do so by the whole body. This means nothing less than that he will be forced to be free. For this is the condition which, by giving each citizen to his homeland, secures him against all personal dependence. In this condition lies the key to the working of the political machine; this alone makes civil commitments legitimate. Without it, they would be absurd, tyrannical, and open to the most monstrous abuses.

Chapter 8: The Civil State

The passage from the state of nature to the civil state produces a truly remarkable change in man. It substitutes justice for instinct in his conduct, and gives his actions the morality they had always lacked. Only then, when the voice of duty replaces physical impulse, and right replaces appetite, does man — who until now had thought only of himself — find himself forced to act on different principles, and to consult his reason before listening to his inclinations. In this new state he loses several advantages that nature gave him, but he gains advantages so much greater in return — his faculties exercised and developed, his ideas enlarged, his feelings ennobled, his whole soul lifted so high — that if the abuses of this new condition did not so often drag him below the one he left, he would have to bless without ceasing the happy moment that tore him from it forever, and that turned a stupid, shortsighted animal into an intelligent being and a man.

Let us reduce this whole balance sheet to terms that are easy to compare. What man loses by the social contract is his natural liberty, and an unlimited right to anything that tempts him and that he can get. What he gains is civil liberty, and ownership of everything he possesses. To avoid miscalculating in this exchange, we must clearly distinguish natural liberty, which is bounded only by the individual's own strength, from civil liberty, which is limited by the general will; and possession, which is merely the effect of force or the right of the first occupant, from property, which can rest only on a positive legal title.

To all the gains of the civil state we could add one more: moral liberty, which alone makes man truly master of himself. For the impulse of mere appetite is slavery, and obedience to a law we prescribe to ourselves is liberty. But I have already said too much on this point, and the philosophical meaning of the word liberty is not my subject here.

Chapter 9: Property

Each member of the community gives himself to it at the moment of its founding, just as he is — himself and all his forces, including the goods he possesses. This act does not make possession change its nature as it changes hands; it does not turn possession into property in the hands of the Sovereign. But since the forces of the city are incomparably greater than those of any individual, public possession is in fact stronger and more irrevocable, without being any more legitimate — at least in the eyes of foreigners. For the State, in relation to its own members, is master of all their goods through the social contract, which inside the State is the basis of all rights; but in relation to other powers, it is master only by the right of the first occupant, a right it holds from its members.

The right of the first occupant, though more real than the right of the strongest, becomes a true right only after the right of property has been established. Every man naturally has a right to everything he needs; but the positive act that makes him owner of one thing excludes him from everything else. Once his share is assigned, he must keep to it, and he has no further claim against the community. This is why the right of the first occupant, so weak in the state of nature, commands the respect of every man in civil society. What we respect in this right is not so much what belongs to another as what does not belong to ourselves.

In general, to establish the right of the first occupant over any plot of ground, these conditions are necessary: first, the land must not yet be inhabited by anyone; second, a man must occupy only as much as he needs to live on; and third, he must take possession of it not by an empty ceremony, but by labor and cultivation — the only sign of ownership that others are bound to respect when no legal title exists.

In granting the right of first occupancy to need and to labor, haven't we already stretched it as far as it can go? Can this right be left without limits? Is setting foot on a piece of common ground enough to make you instantly its master? Is having the strength to drive other men off for a moment enough to strip them of the right ever to return? How can a man or a people seize a vast territory and keep the whole human race out of it, except by a usurpation that deserves punishment — since it robs the rest of mankind of the dwelling place and the food that nature gave to all in common? When Núñez de Balboa stood on the shore and took possession of the South Seas and the whole of South America in the name of the crown of Castile, was that enough to dispossess every inhabitant and shut out every prince on earth? On that basis, such ceremonies could be multiplied without end, and the Catholic King need only take possession of the entire universe at one stroke, from the comfort of his private apartments — subtracting afterward, of course, whatever already belonged to other princes.

We can understand how the lands of individuals, lying side by side and joined together, become public territory, and how the right of sovereignty, extending from the subjects to the very ground they occupy, comes to cover both things and persons. This places the possessors in greater dependence and turns their own forces into guarantees of their fidelity — an advantage the ancient monarchs do not seem to have appreciated. Calling themselves King of the Persians, of the Scythians, of the Macedonians, they appear to have regarded themselves as rulers of men rather than masters of a country. Today's monarchs are cleverer: they call themselves King of France, of Spain, of England, and so on. Holding the land, they are quite sure of holding its inhabitants.

What is remarkable about this alienation is that the community, in accepting the goods of individuals, is so far from robbing them that it does nothing but assure them legitimate possession — changing usurpation into a genuine right, and mere use into ownership. From then on, the possessors are regarded as trustees of the public good; their rights are respected by every member of the State and defended with all its force against foreigners. By a surrender that benefits the public, and themselves still more, they have — so to speak — acquired everything they gave up. This paradox is easily explained by the distinction between the rights the Sovereign and the owner hold over the same piece of ground, as we shall see later on.

It can also happen that men begin to unite before they possess anything, and that, afterward taking over land enough for everyone, they enjoy it in common or divide it among themselves — either equally, or in shares fixed by the Sovereign. However the acquisition is made, each individual's right over his own plot is always subordinate to the community's right over the whole. Without that, there would be no strength in the social bond, and no real force in the exercise of sovereignty.

I will end this chapter, and this book, with a remark that should serve as the foundation of the whole social system. The fundamental contract, instead of destroying natural inequality, does the opposite: for whatever physical inequality nature may have set between men, it substitutes an equality that is moral and legitimate. Men may be unequal in strength or in genius; by agreement and by right, they all become equal.

— Add one caution to that: under bad governments, this equality is only apparent and illusory. It serves merely to keep the poor man in his poverty and the rich man in everything he has usurped. In reality, laws are always useful to those who possess and harmful to those who have nothing. From this it follows that the social state is advantageous to men only when all have something and none has too much.
