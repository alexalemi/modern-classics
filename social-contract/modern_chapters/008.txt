Book IV

(Part 3 of 3)

From the simple fact that a god was set over every political society, it followed that there were as many gods as there were peoples. Two peoples foreign to each other, and almost always enemies, could not recognize the same master for long; two armies locked in battle could not obey the same leader. So national divisions produced polytheism, and polytheism in turn produced theological and civil intolerance — which, as we will see in a moment, are by nature the same thing.

The Greeks had a fancy for rediscovering their own gods among the barbarians, and it came from their habit of regarding themselves as the natural sovereigns of those peoples. But nothing is more absurd than the scholarship of our own day, which identifies and confuses the gods of different nations. As if Moloch, Saturn, and Chronos could be the same god! As if the Phoenician Baal, the Greek Zeus, and the Latin Jupiter could be the same! As if imaginary beings with different names could have anything in common at all!

Someone may ask why, in pagan times, when every State had its own cult and its own gods, there were no wars of religion. My answer: for precisely that reason. Each State, having its own worship as well as its own government, drew no line between its gods and its laws. Political war was theological war; the provinces of the gods were marked out, so to speak, by the borders of nations. One people's god had no rights over other peoples. The gods of the pagans were not jealous gods; they divided up the empire of the world among themselves. Even Moses and the Hebrews sometimes lent themselves to this way of thinking by speaking of the God of Israel. True, they considered the gods of the Canaanites powerless — those being a proscribed people, marked for destruction, whose land they were to occupy. But notice how they spoke of the gods of the neighboring peoples they were forbidden to attack. "Is not what your god Chemosh possesses lawfully yours?" Jephthah said to the Ammonites. "We hold by the very same title the lands our conquering God has made his own." Here, I think, is a clear admission that the rights of Chemosh and the rights of the God of Israel were rights of the same kind.

— That is the text in the Vulgate (Judges 11:24): Nonne ea quæ possidet Chamos deus tuus, tibi jure debentur? Father de Carrières translates it: "Do you not regard yourselves as having a right to what your god possesses?" I cannot judge the force of the Hebrew; but I notice that in the Vulgate, Jephthah positively acknowledges the right of the god Chemosh, and that the French translator watered down that admission by slipping in an "according to you" which is not in the Latin.

But when the Jews, subject first to the kings of Babylon and afterward to the kings of Syria, still stubbornly refused to recognize any god but their own, that refusal was treated as rebellion against their conqueror. It brought down on them the persecutions we read of in their history — persecutions without parallel until the coming of Christianity.

— It is quite clear, by the way, that the Phocian war, the so-called "Sacred War," was not a war of religion. Its purpose was to punish acts of sacrilege, not to conquer unbelievers.

Since every religion was attached solely to the laws of the State that prescribed it, there was no way to convert a people except by enslaving it, and no missionaries except conquerors. The obligation to change cults was simply the law imposed on the defeated, so you had to be the victor before you could even raise the subject. Far from men fighting for the gods, the gods — as in Homer — fought for men. Each side asked its god for victory and paid for it with new altars. Before taking a city, the Romans formally summoned its gods to leave it; and when they let the people of Tarentum keep their outraged gods, it was because by then they regarded those gods as subject to their own and compelled to pay them homage. They left the conquered their gods just as they left them their laws. A wreath for the Jupiter of the Capitol was often the only tribute they demanded.

Finally, as the Romans spread their cult and their gods along with their empire, and often adopted the gods of the conquered as well, granting the rights of the city to both alike, the peoples of that vast empire gradually found themselves with multitudes of gods and cults that were nearly the same everywhere. And that is how paganism, throughout the known world, finally became one and the same religion.

These were the circumstances in which Jesus came to establish a spiritual kingdom on earth. By separating the theological system from the political system, this destroyed the unity of the State, and it brought on the internal divisions that have never stopped troubling Christian peoples. Since the new idea of a kingdom of another world could never have entered the heads of the pagans, they always looked on the Christians as actual rebels — people who, behind a mask of submission, were only waiting for the moment to make themselves independent and supreme, and to seize by cunning the very authority they pretended, in their weakness, to respect. That was the cause of the persecutions.

What the pagans feared came to pass. Then everything changed its face: the humble Christians changed their language, and soon this so-called kingdom of the other world became, under a visible leader, the most violent despotism in this one.

Yet since there has always been a civil ruler and there have always been civil laws, this double power has created an endless conflict of jurisdiction that has made all good polity impossible in Christian States. People have never managed to settle whether they are obliged to obey the master or the priest.

Several peoples, even in Europe and on its borders, have tried to preserve or restore the old system, but without success: the spirit of Christianity has won everywhere. The sacred cult has always remained, or become once again, independent of the Sovereign, with no necessary link between it and the body of the State. Muhammad had very sound views; he tied his political system firmly together, and as long as the form of his government lasted under the caliphs who succeeded him, that government was exactly one, and to that extent it was good. But when the Arabs grew prosperous, lettered, polished, soft, and cowardly, they were conquered by barbarians, and the division between the two powers began again. Though it is less visible among Muslims than among Christians, the division exists all the same, above all in the sect of Ali — the Shia — and there are States, such as Persia, where it makes itself felt continually.

Among us, the kings of England have made themselves heads of the Church, and the tsars have done the same. But with this title they have made themselves less the Church's masters than its ministers; they have acquired not so much the right to change it as the power to maintain it. They are not its legislators; they are merely its princes. Wherever the clergy forms a corporate body, it is master and legislator in its own house. So there are two powers, two Sovereigns, in England and in Russia, just as everywhere else.

— Note carefully that what binds the clergy into one body is not so much their formal assemblies as the communion of Churches. Communion and excommunication are the social contract of the clergy — a contract that will always make them the masters of peoples and of kings. All priests who share communion are fellow citizens, even if they come from opposite ends of the earth. This invention is a masterpiece of statecraft: there is nothing like it among pagan priests, which is why pagan priests never formed a clerical body.

Of all Christian writers, the philosopher Hobbes is the only one who saw both the disease and the remedy — the only one who dared to propose reuniting the two heads of the eagle and restoring political unity entire, without which no State and no government will ever be rightly constituted. But he should have seen that the domineering spirit of Christianity was incompatible with his system, and that the interest of the priest would always be stronger than the interest of the State. It is not what is false and terrible in his political theory that has drawn hatred down on it, but what is just and true.

— See, for instance, what that learned man Grotius found to praise and to blame in the De Cive, in a letter to his brother of April 11, 1643. It is true that, inclined to indulgence, he seems to forgive the author the good for the sake of the bad; but not everyone is so forgiving.

I believe that if the study of history were developed from this point of view, it would be easy to refute the opposite errors of Bayle and Warburton — the one holding that religion is of no use whatever to the body politic, the other maintaining, on the contrary, that Christianity is its strongest support. To the first we could prove that no State was ever founded without religion at its base; to the second, that the law of Christianity does the constitution of the State more harm by weakening it than good by strengthening it. To make myself fully understood, I only need to give a little more precision to the overly vague ideas about religion that bear on my subject.

Religion, considered in relation to society — whether society in general or some particular society — can be divided into two kinds: the religion of man and the religion of the citizen. The first, with no temples, no altars, no rites, confined to the purely inward worship of the supreme God and to the eternal duties of morality, is the pure and simple religion of the Gospel, the true theism — what may be called natural divine law. The second, established in a single country, gives that country its gods, its own guardian patrons. It has its dogmas, its rites, its external worship prescribed by law; outside the one nation that follows it, everything is, in its eyes, infidel, foreign, barbarous; it extends the duties and rights of man no farther than its own altars. All the religions of the early peoples were of this kind, and we may call it civil or positive divine law.

There is a third, stranger sort of religion which, by giving men two sets of laws, two rulers, two homelands, subjects them to contradictory duties and makes it impossible for them to be faithful to religion and to citizenship at the same time. Such is the religion of the Lamas; such is that of the Japanese; such is Roman Christianity. One may call it the religion of the priest. It produces a kind of mixed, anti-social code of law that has no name.

Considered politically, all three kinds of religion have their defects. The third is so obviously bad that it would be a waste of time to stop and prove it. Whatever destroys social unity is worthless. Every institution that sets a man in contradiction with himself is worthless.

The second is good in this respect: it joins the worship of God to the love of the laws, and by making the homeland the object of the citizens' adoration, it teaches them that to serve the State is to serve its guardian god. It is a kind of theocracy, in which there should be no pontiff but the prince and no priests but the magistrates. Then to die for one's country is martyrdom; to break its laws is impiety; and to subject a guilty man to public execration is to deliver him to the anger of the gods: Sacer estod — let him be accursed.

But it is bad in this respect: being founded on error and lies, it deceives men, makes them credulous and superstitious, and drowns the true worship of the Divinity in empty ceremony. It is bad again when it turns exclusive and tyrannical and makes a people bloodthirsty and intolerant — when it breathes nothing but slaughter and massacre, and counts it a holy act to kill anyone who does not accept its gods. That puts such a people into a natural state of war with everyone else, which does its own security no good at all.

There remains, then, the religion of man, or Christianity — not the Christianity of today, but the Christianity of the Gospel, which is altogether different. Under this holy, sublime, and true religion, all men, children of the same God, recognize one another as brothers, and the society that unites them is not dissolved even by death.

But this religion, having no particular relation to the body politic, leaves the laws only the force they draw from themselves, adding nothing to it; and so one of the great bonds that hold a particular society together is left without effect. Worse: far from attaching the citizens' hearts to the State, it detaches them from it as from all earthly things. I know of nothing more contrary to the social spirit.

We are told that a people of true Christians would form the most perfect society imaginable. I see only one great difficulty in this supposition: a society of true Christians would no longer be a society of men.

I say more: this society, with all its perfection, would be neither the strongest nor the most lasting. Its very perfection would rob it of its bond of union; the flaw that destroyed it would lie in its perfection itself.

Everyone would do his duty; the people would obey the laws; the leaders would be just and moderate; the magistrates would be upright and incorruptible; the soldiers would despise death; there would be neither vanity nor luxury. So far, so good. But listen to what comes next.

Christianity is an entirely spiritual religion, concerned only with the things of heaven. The Christian's homeland is not of this world. He does his duty, yes — but he does it with deep indifference to whether his efforts succeed or fail. As long as he has nothing to reproach himself with, it matters little to him whether things go well or badly down here. If the State prospers, he hardly dares enjoy the public happiness, for fear of growing proud of his country's glory. If the State declines, he blesses the hand of God lying heavy on His people.

For the society to be peaceful and harmony preserved, every single citizen without exception would have to be an equally good Christian. But if, by bad luck, there is one ambitious man among them, one hypocrite — a Catiline, say, or a Cromwell — he will most certainly get the better of his pious compatriots. Christian charity does not easily allow a man to think ill of his neighbors. The moment such a man discovers, by some trick, the art of fooling them and grabbing a share of the public authority, there he stands, a man established in dignity: it is God's will that he be respected. Soon there stands a power: it is God's will that it be obeyed. Does the man who holds that power abuse it? It is the rod with which God punishes His children. Conscience would balk at driving out the usurper: public peace would have to be disturbed, violence used, blood spilled — all of which sits badly with Christian meekness. And after all, in this vale of tears, what does it matter whether we are free men or serfs? The essential thing is to get to heaven, and resignation is just one more means of getting there.

If war breaks out with another State, the citizens march readily to battle; not one of them thinks of fleeing. They do their duty, but without any passion for victory; they know better how to die than how to win. What does it matter whether they conquer or are conquered? Doesn't Providence know better than they do what they need? Just imagine what a proud, fiery, passionate enemy could make of their stoicism! Set against them those generous peoples who were consumed by a burning love of glory and of homeland; imagine your Christian republic face to face with Sparta or with Rome. The pious Christians will be beaten, crushed, and destroyed before they know where they are — or they will owe their safety to nothing but the contempt their enemy comes to feel for them. To my mind, the oath taken by the soldiers of Fabius was a fine one: they did not swear to conquer or die — they swore to come back victorious, and they kept their oath. Christians would never have taken such an oath; they would have thought it was tempting God.

But I am wrong to speak of a Christian republic; the two words exclude each other. Christianity preaches nothing but servitude and dependence. Its spirit is so favorable to tyranny that tyranny always profits by it. True Christians are made to be slaves; they know it, and it hardly troubles them: this short life counts for too little in their eyes.

I will be told that Christian troops are excellent. I deny it. Show me one example. For my part, I know of no Christian troops. The Crusades will be cited. Without disputing the valor of the Crusaders, I will point out that, far from being Christians, they were the priests' soldiers — citizens of the Church. They fought for their spiritual country, which the Church, somehow or other, had made temporal. Properly understood, this is a relapse into paganism: since the Gospel establishes no national religion, a holy war is impossible among Christians.

Under the pagan emperors, the Christian soldiers were brave; every Christian writer affirms it, and I believe it. It was a competition of honor against the pagan troops. Once the emperors became Christian, that rivalry disappeared; and when the Cross had driven out the eagle, all Roman valor vanished with it.

But let us set political considerations aside and come back to right; let us fix our principles on this important point. The right which the social contract gives the Sovereign over the subjects does not, as we have seen, go beyond the limits of public utility.

— "In the republic," says the Marquis d'Argenson, "each man is perfectly free in whatever does not harm others." That is the unchanging boundary; it cannot be stated more exactly. I have not been able to deny myself the pleasure of quoting now and then from this manuscript, though the public has never seen it, to honor the memory of a good and illustrious man who kept, even as a Minister, the heart of a true citizen, along with sane and right views on the government of his country.

The subjects, then, owe the Sovereign an account of their opinions only so far as those opinions matter to the community. Now it matters very much to the community that each citizen have a religion that makes him love his duties. But the dogmas of that religion concern the State and its members only so far as they bear on morality and on the duties that whoever professes them is bound to fulfill toward others. Beyond that, everyone may hold whatever opinions he pleases, and it is no business of the Sovereign's to know what they are. The Sovereign has no authority in the other world; whatever the fate of its subjects in the life to come, that is not its affair — provided they are good citizens in this one.

There is therefore a purely civil profession of faith, and it is the Sovereign's place to fix its articles — not precisely as religious dogmas, but as sentiments of sociability, without which it is impossible to be a good citizen or a faithful subject. The Sovereign cannot compel anyone to believe these articles, but it can banish from the State anyone who does not believe them. It can banish him not for impiety, but as an anti-social being — as someone incapable of sincerely loving the laws and justice, and of sacrificing, if need be, his life to his duty. And if anyone, after publicly acknowledging these same dogmas, behaves as though he does not believe them, let him be punished with death: he has committed the greatest of all crimes — he has lied before the laws.

— When Caesar pleaded for Catiline, he tried to establish the dogma that the soul is mortal. Cato and Cicero, to refute him, did not waste time philosophizing. They were content to show that Caesar was speaking like a bad citizen and advancing a doctrine harmful to the State. That, and not a question of theology, was what the Roman senate had to judge.

The dogmas of the civil religion ought to be simple, few in number, and precisely worded, without explanations or commentary. The existence of a powerful, intelligent, benevolent Divinity, possessed of foresight and providence; the life to come; the happiness of the just; the punishment of the wicked; the sanctity of the social contract and of the laws: these are the positive dogmas. As for the negative dogmas, I limit them to a single one: intolerance. It belongs to the cults we have ruled out.

Those who distinguish civil intolerance from theological intolerance are, in my opinion, mistaken. The two are inseparable. It is impossible to live at peace with people one believes are damned; to love them would be to hate the God who punishes them; one absolutely must either reclaim them or torment them. Wherever theological intolerance is admitted, it cannot fail to have some civil effect; and the moment it has one, the Sovereign is no longer Sovereign, even in temporal matters. From that point on, the priests are the real masters, and kings are merely their officers.

— Marriage, for example, being a civil contract, has civil effects without which society cannot even subsist. Suppose, then, a clergy that claims the exclusive right to perform this act — a right it must necessarily claim in every intolerant religion. Is it not clear that in establishing the Church's authority on this point, it destroys the authority of the prince, who from then on will have only as many subjects as the clergy chooses to grant him? Free to marry or not to marry people according to whether they hold this or that doctrine, accept or reject this or that formula, and show more or less devotion, the Church alone — acting with firmness and holding its ground — will dispose of all inheritances, all offices, all citizens, and even the State itself, which cannot survive once it is composed of nothing but bastards. But, I will be told, there will be appeals against abuses; there will be summonses, decrees, seizures of Church revenues. What a pity! The clergy, if it has even a little — I will not say courage — sense, will let it all happen and quietly go its way. It will calmly permit the appeals, the summonses, the decrees, and the seizures, and in the end it will remain the master. Giving up a part, I think, is no great sacrifice when one is sure of securing the whole.

Now that there no longer is, and no longer can be, an exclusive national religion, tolerance should be extended to all religions that tolerate others, as long as their dogmas contain nothing contrary to the duties of the citizen. But whoever dares to say "Outside the Church there is no salvation" should be driven from the State — unless the State is the Church and the prince is the pontiff. Such a dogma is good only in a theocratic government; in any other, it is fatal. The reason Henry IV is said to have had for embracing the Roman religion ought to make every honest man leave it — and above all, every prince who knows how to reason.

Chapter 9: Conclusion

Now that I have laid down the true principles of political right and tried to set the State on a foundation of its own, what remains is to support it through its external relations. That would take in the law of nations, commerce, the right of war and conquest, public right, leagues, negotiations, treaties, and so on. But all of this forms a new subject far too vast for my narrow scope. I should always have kept my sights closer to home.
