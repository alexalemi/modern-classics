Book II

(Part 1 of 2)


Chapter 1: Sovereignty Cannot Be Given Away

The first and most important conclusion from the principles we've laid down so far is this: only the general will can steer the State toward the purpose it was founded for — the common good. If the clash of private interests made it necessary to establish societies, it was the agreement among those same interests that made it possible. What these different interests have in common is what forms the social bond; if there were no point on which they all agreed, no society could exist at all. And it is solely on the basis of this common interest that every society must be governed.

So I hold that Sovereignty, which is nothing other than the exercise of the general will, can never be alienated, and that the Sovereign — a collective being and nothing less — can be represented only by itself. Power can be handed over. Will cannot.

In practice, it isn't impossible for a private will to line up with the general will on some particular point. But it is impossible for that agreement to be steady and lasting, because the private will leans, by its very nature, toward playing favorites, while the general will leans toward equality. And it's even more impossible to have any guarantee of such agreement; even if it held forever, that would be luck, not design. The Sovereign can certainly say, "Right now I will what this man wills — or at least what he says he wills." But it cannot say, "Whatever he wills tomorrow, I will too," because it is absurd for a will to tie its own hands for the future, and no will can be obligated to consent to anything that harms the being who does the willing. So if a people simply promises to obey, by that very act it dissolves itself and stops being a people. The moment there is a master, there is no longer a Sovereign — and from that moment the body politic has ceased to exist.

This doesn't mean the commands of rulers can't pass for expressions of the general will — so long as the Sovereign is free to oppose them and chooses not to. In such a case, universal silence is taken to mean the people consents. I'll explain this more fully later on.


Chapter 2: Sovereignty Cannot Be Divided

For the same reason that sovereignty cannot be given away, it cannot be divided. A will either is general or it is not — and let me note that for a will to be general, it need not always be unanimous; but every vote must be counted, for any exclusion breaks the generality. The will belongs either to the body of the people as a whole, or only to a part of it. In the first case, the declared will is an act of Sovereignty and makes law. In the second, it is merely a private will, or an act of administration — at most, a decree.

But our political theorists, unable to divide Sovereignty in principle, divide it by its objects instead: into force and will; into legislative power and executive power; into the rights of taxation, justice, and war; into domestic administration and the power to make foreign treaties. Sometimes they mash all these pieces together, sometimes they pull them apart. They turn the Sovereign into a fantastical creature built of connected parts — as if they were assembling a man out of several bodies, one with eyes, one with arms, another with feet, and nothing else. They say that the jugglers of Japan cut a child to pieces before the audience's eyes, throw the pieces into the air one by one, and the child comes down alive and whole. The conjuring tricks of our political theorists are much the same: first they dismember the body politic with an illusion worthy of a carnival, and then they put it back together — nobody knows how.

This mistake comes from not having exact ideas about Sovereign authority, and from mistaking mere emanations of that authority for parts of it. For example, declaring war and making peace have been treated as acts of Sovereignty. But they are not. Neither act makes a law; each merely applies a law — a particular act deciding how the law falls in a given case. This will become perfectly clear once we've defined what the word law actually means.

If we worked through the other supposed divisions the same way, we would find that whenever Sovereignty appears to be divided, we're looking at an illusion. The rights taken to be pieces of Sovereignty are in fact all subordinate to it; every one of them presupposes a supreme will, which those rights merely carry out.

It is hard to overstate how much confusion this lack of precision has poured over the conclusions of writers on political right whenever they used their own principles to judge the respective rights of kings and peoples. Anyone can see, in chapters three and four of the first book of Grotius, how that learned man and his translator, Barbeyrac, tangle and trip themselves up in their own clever arguments — terrified of saying too little or too much of what they really think, and of offending the interests they need to flatter. Grotius, a refugee in France, unhappy with his homeland and eager to please Louis XIII, to whom his book is dedicated, spares no effort to strip peoples of all their rights and dress kings in them by every trick he can devise. That approach would have suited Barbeyrac just fine too — he dedicated his translation to George I of England. But unluckily for him, the expulsion of James II — which he tactfully called an "abdication" — forced him to hedge, dodge, and talk out of both sides of his mouth to avoid making William look like a usurper. If these two writers had adopted the true principles, every difficulty would have vanished and they would have been consistent throughout. But they would have been telling a sad truth, and the only court it would have paid was to the people. Truth is no road to fortune, and the people hands out no ambassadorships, no professorships, and no pensions.


Chapter 3: Can the General Will Be Wrong?

It follows from everything above that the general will is always right and always tends toward the public good. But it does not follow that the people's deliberations are always equally sound. We always will our own good — but we do not always see what it is. The people is never corrupted, but it is often deceived; and only then does it seem to will what is bad.

There is often a great deal of difference between the will of all and the general will. The general will looks only to the common interest. The will of all looks to private interest, and is nothing more than a sum of particular wills. But take those same wills and cancel out the pluses and minuses that offset one another, and what remains — the sum of the differences — is the general will.

— "Every interest," says the Marquis d'Argenson, "has different principles. The agreement of two particular interests is formed by opposition to a third." He might have added that the agreement of all interests is formed by opposition to the interest of each. If there were no differing interests, the common interest would scarcely be felt, because it would meet no resistance; everything would run on its own, and politics would stop being an art.

If, when an adequately informed people deliberates, the citizens had no communication with one another, then the grand total of all the small differences would always yield the general will, and the decision would always be good. But when factions arise — when partial associations form at the expense of the great association — the will of each of these groups becomes general with respect to its own members while remaining particular with respect to the State. Then we can no longer say there are as many votes as there are people; there are only as many votes as there are associations. The differences become fewer, and the result less general. Finally, when one of these associations grows large enough to outweigh all the rest, the result is no longer a sum of small differences but a single difference. At that point there is no longer a general will at all, and the opinion that wins is purely a private one.

So if the general will is to express itself, it is essential that there be no partial society inside the State, and that each citizen think his own thoughts — which was precisely the sublime and singular system established by the great Lycurgus. And if partial societies must exist, then it is best to have as many of them as possible and to keep them from being unequal, as Solon, Numa, and Servius did. These precautions are the only ones that can guarantee that the general will is always enlightened and that the people never deceives itself.

— "There are in fact," says Machiavelli, "some divisions that harm a Republic and some that benefit it. The harmful ones are those that come with sects and parties; the beneficial ones are those that come with neither. Since the founder of a Republic cannot prevent enmities from arising, he ought at least to prevent them from hardening into sects" (History of Florence, Book VII).


Chapter 4: The Limits of the Sovereign Power

If the State is a moral person whose life consists in the union of its members, and if the most important of its concerns is its own preservation, then it must have a universal and compelling force to move and arrange each part in whatever way best serves the whole. Just as nature gives every man absolute power over all his limbs, the social contract gives the body politic absolute power over all its members; and it is this power, directed by the general will, that bears, as I have said, the name of Sovereignty.

But besides the public person, we have to consider the private persons who compose it, whose life and liberty are naturally independent of it. So we must distinguish clearly between the respective rights of the citizens and the Sovereign, and between the duties the citizens must fulfill as subjects and the natural rights they should enjoy as men. — Attentive readers, please don't rush to accuse me of contradicting myself here. Given the poverty of the language, the terminology made it unavoidable. Wait and see.

I grant that under the social contract each man alienates only that portion of his powers, goods, and liberty which it matters to the community to control. But it must also be granted that the Sovereign alone is the judge of what matters.

Every service a citizen can render the State, he owes the moment the Sovereign asks for it. But the Sovereign, for its part, cannot load its subjects with any chain that is useless to the community — it cannot even wish to, for under the law of reason, just as under the law of nature, nothing happens without a cause.

The commitments that bind us to the social body are binding only because they are mutual, and their nature is such that in fulfilling them we cannot work for others without also working for ourselves. Why is the general will always right? Why does everyone constantly will the happiness of each member? Because there is no one who doesn't take the word "each" to mean himself, and who isn't thinking of himself when he votes for all. This proves that equality of rights, and the idea of justice that such equality produces, spring from the preference each man gives to himself — and therefore from the very nature of man. It proves that the general will, to be truly general, must be general in its object as well as in its essence; that it must come from all in order to apply to all; and that it loses its natural rightness the moment it is aimed at some particular, identified target — because then we are judging something foreign to us, with no true principle of fairness to guide us.

Indeed, as soon as a dispute arises over some particular fact or right that no prior general agreement has settled, the matter becomes contentious. It is a case where the individuals concerned are one party and the public is the other — but I can see neither what law should be followed nor what judge should decide. It would be absurd to propose referring such a question to an express decision of the general will, because that decision could only be the conclusion of one of the parties, and so, for the other party, it would be merely an external and particular will — inclined, on this occasion, toward injustice and open to error. So just as a particular will cannot stand in for the general will, the general will in turn changes its nature when it takes a particular object: as general, it cannot pass judgment on an individual man or a single fact. When the people of Athens, for instance, appointed or removed its leaders, voted honors to one man and penalties to another, and through heaps of particular decrees carried out all the functions of government at once, the people in those moments no longer had a general will in the strict sense. It was acting no longer as Sovereign but as magistrate. This will sound contrary to accepted ideas — but give me time to lay out my own.

From all this we should see that what makes a will general is less the number of voters than the common interest that unites them. Under this system, each person necessarily submits to the same conditions he imposes on others — an admirable harmony between interest and justice that stamps the community's deliberations with a fairness which evaporates the instant any particular question comes up, because then there is no common interest to unite and align the standard of the judge with the standard of the party.

From whatever angle we approach our principle, we reach the same conclusion: the social contract establishes among the citizens an equality of such a kind that they all commit themselves under the same conditions and must all enjoy the same rights. So by the very nature of the contract, every act of Sovereignty — that is, every authentic act of the general will — binds or benefits all citizens equally. The Sovereign recognizes only the body of the nation and draws no distinctions among the individuals who make it up. What, then, is an act of Sovereignty, strictly speaking? It is not an agreement between a superior and an inferior, but an agreement of the body with each of its members. It is legitimate, because it rests on the social contract; fair, because it is common to all; useful, because it can have no object except the general good; and solid, because it is backed by the public force and the supreme power. As long as subjects submit only to agreements of this kind, they obey no one but their own will. To ask how far the respective rights of the Sovereign and the citizens extend is simply to ask how far the citizens can commit themselves to one another — each to all, and all to each.

From this we can see that the sovereign power, absolute, sacred, and inviolable as it is, does not and cannot overstep the limits of general agreements, and that every man may fully dispose of whatever goods and liberty those agreements leave him. The Sovereign never has the right to burden one subject more than another, because then the matter becomes particular, and falls outside the Sovereign's competence.

Once these distinctions are admitted, the claim that individuals make any real renunciation in the social contract collapses. So far from giving things up, they find their position after the contract genuinely preferable to what it was before. Instead of a renunciation, they have made a profitable exchange: instead of an uncertain, precarious way of living, one that is better and more secure; instead of natural independence, liberty; instead of the power to harm others, safety for themselves; and instead of their own strength, which others might overpower, a right which the social union makes invincible. Their very lives, which they have devoted to the State, are constantly protected by it; and when they risk those lives in the State's defense, what are they doing but giving back what they received from it? What are they doing that they would not have to do more often, and at greater peril, in the state of nature — where they would inevitably fight battles at the risk of their lives to defend the very means of staying alive? Everyone must fight for the homeland when it needs them, true; but then, no one ever has to fight for himself. To preserve what keeps us safe, we run only a part of the risks we would have to run for ourselves the moment we lost it — do we not come out ahead?


Chapter 5: The Right of Life and Death

The question is often asked: since individuals have no right to dispose of their own lives, how can they transfer to the Sovereign a right they do not possess? The question seems hard to answer only because it is badly put. Every man has the right to risk his own life in order to preserve it. Has anyone ever said that a man who jumps out a window to escape a fire is guilty of suicide? Has that crime ever been charged to someone who dies in a storm at sea, even though he knew the danger when he boarded the ship?

The purpose of the social treaty is the preservation of the contracting parties. Whoever wills the end wills the means, and the means here involve some risks — even some losses. Whoever wants his life preserved at the expense of others must also be ready, when necessary, to give it up for their sake. Furthermore, the citizen is no longer the judge of the dangers the law requires him to face; and when the government says to him, "It is expedient for the State that you should die," he ought to die — because it is only on that condition that he has lived in security up to now, and because his life is no longer simply a gift of nature, but a conditional gift of the State.

The death penalty inflicted on criminals can be viewed in much the same light: it is so that we will not become the victims of a murderer that we consent to die if we become murderers ourselves. Under this treaty, far from disposing of our own lives, we are thinking only of protecting them — and it is hardly to be supposed that any of the parties, at the time, is planning to get himself hanged.

Moreover, every wrongdoer who attacks the rights of society becomes, by his crimes, a rebel and a traitor to his homeland. By violating its laws he ceases to be a member of it; he even makes war upon it. Then the State's preservation and his own are incompatible — one of the two must perish — and when the guilty man is put to death, it is less as a citizen than as an enemy. The trial and the verdict are the proof and the declaration that he has broken the social treaty, and consequently is no longer a member of the State. And since he had acknowledged himself a member, at the very least by living there, he must be cut off from it — by exile, as a violator of the contract, or by death, as a public enemy. For such an enemy is not a moral person but simply a man, and in that case the right of war is to kill the defeated.

But, it will be objected, the condemnation of a criminal is a particular act. Granted — and for that reason it is not a function of the Sovereign. It is a right the Sovereign can confer without being able to exercise it itself. All my ideas hold together, but I cannot lay them all out at once.

Let me add that frequent punishments are always a sign of weakness or laziness in the government. There is no man so bad that he cannot be made good for something. The State has no right to put anyone to death, even as an example, if that person can be left alive without danger.

As for the right of pardon — of exempting a guilty man from the penalty set by the law and pronounced by the judge — it belongs only to an authority above both judge and law: that is, the Sovereign. And even the Sovereign's right here is far from clear, and the occasions for using it are extremely rare. In a well-governed State there are few punishments — not because pardons are many, but because criminals are few. When a State is in decay, the sheer multitude of crimes guarantees they will go unpunished. Under the Roman Republic, neither the Senate nor the Consuls ever attempted to grant pardon; even the people never did, though it sometimes revoked its own judgment. Frequent pardons announce that crime will soon need no pardon at all — and anyone can see where that road leads. But I feel my heart protesting and holding back my pen. Let us leave these questions to the just man who has never offended, and who has never needed a pardon himself.


Chapter 6: Law

By the social contract we have given the body politic existence and life. Now, by legislation, we must give it movement and will. For the original act that forms and unites the body still determines nothing about what it must do to preserve itself.

Whatever is good and consistent with order is so by the nature of things, independent of any human agreement. All justice comes from God; he alone is its source. But if we knew how to receive inspiration from so high, we would need neither government nor laws. There is, no doubt, a universal justice that flows from reason alone — but for that justice to be accepted among us, it must be mutual. Speaking in human terms, the laws of justice, lacking any natural enforcement, are empty among men. They serve only to profit the wicked and ruin the just, when the just man keeps them toward everyone and no one keeps them toward him. So agreements and laws are needed to join rights to duties and bring justice back to its object. In the state of nature, where everything is common, I owe nothing to a man to whom I have promised nothing; I recognize as belonging to others only what is of no use to me. In the civil state, where all rights are fixed by law, the case is different.

But what, in the end, is a law? As long as we settle for attaching purely metaphysical ideas to the word, we will keep arguing without ever understanding each other — and even once we have defined a law of nature, we will be no closer to defining a law of the State.

I have already said that there can be no general will directed at a particular object. That particular object is either inside the State or outside it. If it's outside, then a will that is foreign to it is not general with respect to it. If it's inside, then it is a part of the State — and a relation springs up between the whole and the part that makes them two separate beings: the part is one, and the whole minus the part is the other. But the whole minus a part is not the whole; so as long as this relation holds, there is no whole at all, only two unequal parts. It follows that the will of one is in no way general with respect to the other.

But when the whole people decrees for the whole people, it considers only itself. If a relation is formed, it is between the entire object seen from one angle and the entire object seen from another, with no division of the whole at all. Then the matter decreed about is general, just like the will that decrees it. This is the act I call a law.

When I say that the object of law is always general, I mean that law considers subjects in a body and actions in the abstract — never an individual person or a particular action. So the law can perfectly well decree that there shall be privileges, but it cannot grant them to anyone by name. The law can create several classes of citizens, and even specify the qualifications for belonging to each class, but it cannot name this or that person as a member. It can establish a monarchical government and hereditary succession, but it cannot elect a king or appoint a royal family. In a word, no function that bears on a particular object belongs to the legislative power.

Seen this way, several questions evaporate at once. We need no longer ask whose business it is to make laws, since laws are acts of the general will. We need not ask whether the government is above the law, since the government's members belong to the State. We need not ask whether the law can be unjust, since no one is unjust to himself. And we need not ask how we can be both free and subject to the laws, since the laws are merely the registers of our own wills.

We can also see that since the law unites universality of will with universality of object, whatever any man commands on his own authority — no matter who he is — is not a law. Even what the Sovereign commands about a particular matter is no law either; it is a decree — an act not of sovereignty but of magistracy.

I therefore give the name Republic to every State that is governed by laws, whatever its form of administration may be; for only then does the public interest govern, and only then is the public thing — the res publica — a reality. Every legitimate government is republican. — By "republican" I do not mean only an aristocracy or a democracy, but in general any government guided by the general will, which is the law. To be legitimate, the government must not be confused with the Sovereign, but must serve as its minister. In that case even a monarchy is a Republic. All this will become clearer in the next book. — What government itself is, I will explain later on.

Laws, properly speaking, are nothing but the conditions of civil association. The people, being subject to the laws, ought to be their author; only those who form the association have the right to set its terms. But how are they to set them? By spontaneous common agreement? By sudden inspiration? Does the body politic have an organ for declaring its will? Who will give it the foresight to frame its acts and publish them in advance — or announce them at the moment of need? How can a blind multitude, which often does not know what it wills because it rarely knows what is good for it, carry out by itself an enterprise as vast and difficult as a system of legislation? Of itself, the people always wills the good; of itself, it does not always see it. The general will is always right, but the judgment that guides it is not always enlightened. The people must be made to see things as they are — sometimes as they ought to appear; it must be shown the good road it is looking for, shielded from the seductions of private wills, taught to take in times and places in sequence, and brought to weigh the pull of present, visible advantages against the danger of distant, hidden evils. Individuals see the good they reject; the public wills the good it does not see. Both stand equally in need of guides. Individuals must be compelled to bring their wills into line with their reason; the public must be taught to know what it wills. When that happens, public enlightenment unites understanding and will in the social body; the parts mesh exactly, and the whole rises to its greatest power. This is why a Lawgiver is necessary.


Chapter 7: The Lawgiver

By the Lawgiver I mean not a legislature, but the extraordinary founder-figure who frames a people's laws at its beginning. To discover the rules of society best suited to nations would require a superior intelligence: one that could see all of men's passions without feeling any of them; one with no connection to our nature, yet knowing it through and through; one whose happiness was independent of us, yet who was willing to concern itself with ours; and finally, one that could look ahead, across the march of time, to a distant glory — working in one century to reap the reward in the next. It would take gods to give laws to men.

What Caligula argued from the facts, Plato argued from right, in the dialogue called the Statesman, when he set out to define the civil or kingly man. But if great princes are rare, how much rarer are great Lawgivers? Princes have only to follow the pattern; the Lawgiver has to invent it. The Lawgiver is the engineer who designs the machine; the prince is merely the mechanic who assembles it and keeps it running. "At the birth of societies," says Montesquieu, "it is the leaders of Republics who shape the institutions — and afterward, it is the institutions that shape the leaders."
