From all this it is easy to see why only five classes are almost always mentioned, though there were really six. The sixth supplied no soldiers to the army and no votes in the Campus Martius, and since it had almost no function in the State, it was rarely counted as anything at all.

— I say "in the Campus Martius" because that is where the comitia met when they voted by centuries. In their two other forms the people gathered in the Forum or elsewhere, and there the capite censi — the citizens counted only by head — carried as much weight and authority as the foremost men of the city.

These, then, were the various ways the Roman people was divided. Now let us see the effect on the assemblies. When lawfully summoned, these assemblies were called comitia. They usually met in the public square at Rome or in the Campus Martius, and they were known as the Comitia Curiata, the Comitia Centuriata, or the Comitia Tributa, depending on the form under which they were convened. The Comitia Curiata were founded by Romulus, the Centuriata by Servius, and the Tributa by the tribunes of the people. No law was ratified and no magistrate was elected anywhere but in the comitia; and since every citizen was enrolled in a curia, a century, or a tribe, it follows that no citizen was shut out from voting, and that the Roman people was truly sovereign both in law and in fact.

For the comitia to assemble lawfully, and for their acts to carry the force of law, three conditions had to be met. First, the body or magistrate convening them had to hold the proper authority. Second, the assembly had to be held on a day the law allowed. Third, the auguries — the official reading of the omens — had to be favorable.

The first rule needs no explanation. The second is a matter of practical policy: the comitia could not be held on festival days or market days, when the country folk, in Rome on business, had no time to spend the whole day in the public square. And by means of the third, the senate kept a rein on a proud and restless people, and usefully cooled the ardor of troublemaking tribunes — though the tribunes found more than one way around that obstacle.

Laws and the election of rulers were not the only questions put to the judgment of the comitia. Since the Roman people had taken the most important functions of government into its own hands, you could say that the fate of Europe was settled in its assemblies. This variety of business explains the various forms the assemblies took, according to the matters they had to decide.

To judge these various forms, it is enough to compare them. When Romulus set up the curiae, he meant the people to check the senate and the senate to check the people, while he kept his own ascendancy over both. Through this assembly he gave the people all the authority of numbers, to balance the authority of power and wealth that he left to the patricians. But, in the true spirit of monarchy, he still left the patricians the greater advantage through the influence their clients had over the majority of votes. This excellent institution of patron and client was a masterpiece of statesmanship and humanity; without it the patriciate, which flatly contradicted the spirit of a republic, could not have survived. Rome alone has the honor of giving the world this great example — one that never led to any abuse, and yet has never been imitated.

Since the assemblies by curiae lasted under the kings down to the time of Servius, and the reign of the last Tarquin was not regarded as legitimate, royal laws came to be known generally as leges curiatae.

Under the Republic, the curiae — still confined to the four urban tribes, and containing only the city populace of Rome — suited neither the senate, which led the patricians, nor the tribunes, who, plebeians though they were, stood at the head of the well-off citizens. So the curiae fell into disrepute, and sank so low that thirty lictors — the magistrates' ceremonial attendants — would simply gather and do what the Comitia Curiata should have done.

The division by centuries was so favorable to the aristocracy that at first it is hard to see why the senate did not always carry the day in the comitia that bore their name — the assemblies that elected the consuls, the censors, and the other curule magistrates, the high officers of state. Of the hundred and ninety-three centuries into which the six classes of the whole Roman people were divided, the first class held ninety-eight; and since the vote was counted by centuries alone, that one class outvoted all the rest combined. When all its centuries agreed, the other votes were not even collected; what the smallest number had decided passed for the decision of the multitude. In the Comitia Centuriata, you may fairly say, matters were settled far more by depth of purse than by count of votes.

But this extreme power was softened in two ways. First, the tribunes as a rule, and always a large number of plebeians, belonged to the class of the rich, and so counterbalanced the influence of the patricians within that first class.

The second way was this. Instead of having the centuries vote straight through in order — which would have meant always starting with the first class — the Romans drew one century by lot, and that century alone proceeded to the election. After that, all the centuries were summoned on another day according to their rank, the same election was repeated, and as a rule it was confirmed. In this way the authority of setting an example was taken away from rank and handed to the luck of the draw, on a democratic principle.

This custom had a further advantage. Between the two elections, the citizens from the countryside had time to learn something about the merits of the provisionally nominated candidate, so they did not have to vote in ignorance of the case. But under the pretext of speeding things up, this custom was eventually abolished, and both elections were held on the same day.

The Comitia Tributa were, properly speaking, the council of the Roman people. Only the tribunes could convene them; in them the tribunes were elected and passed their plebiscita — their popular decrees. The senate had no standing in them, and senators had not even the right to attend; forced to obey laws they could not vote on, the senators were in this respect less free than the lowest citizens. This injustice was altogether ill-conceived, and it was by itself enough to invalidate the decrees of a body to which not all its members were admitted. Had every patrician attended these comitia by the right he held as a citizen, they would have been mere private individuals there, with hardly any influence in a vote counted by heads, where the poorest proletarian weighed as much as the princeps senatus — the first man of the senate.

We can see, then, that besides the good order achieved by these various ways of distributing so great a people and collecting its votes, the different methods were not just interchangeable formalities. Each produced results tied to the purpose for which it was preferred.

Without going into further detail here, we can gather from what has been said that the Comitia Tributa were the most favorable to popular government, and the Comitia Centuriata to aristocracy. As for the Comitia Curiata, where the city populace formed the majority, they were fit only to further tyranny and evil designs, and so they naturally fell into discredit; even troublemakers avoided a method that exposed their schemes too plainly. It is beyond dispute that the whole majesty of the Roman people resided solely in the Comitia Centuriata, for they alone included everyone — the Curiata excluded the rural tribes, and the Tributa excluded the senate and the patricians.

As for the manner of taking the vote, among the early Romans it was as simple as their morals, though not so simple as at Sparta. Each man declared his vote aloud, and a clerk duly wrote it down. The majority in each tribe determined the vote of the tribe, the majority of the tribes the vote of the people, and the same with the curiae and the centuries. This custom worked well as long as honesty reigned among the citizens, and each man was ashamed to cast a public vote for an unjust proposal or an unworthy candidate. But when the people grew corrupt and votes were bought, it was fitting that the vote be cast in secret, so that distrust might rein in the buyers, and scoundrels be given a way of not being traitors.

I know that Cicero attacked this change and blamed it in part for the ruin of the Republic. And though I feel the weight Cicero's authority must carry on such a point, I cannot agree with him. I hold, on the contrary, that for want of enough such changes, the destruction of the State was hastened. Just as the diet of the healthy does not suit the sick, we should not try to govern a corrupted people by the laws that a good people requires. There is no better proof of this rule than the long life of the Republic of Venice — its shadow still exists today — and it has lasted solely because its laws suit none but wicked men.

So the citizens were given tablets, by which each man could vote without anyone knowing how he voted. New procedures were also introduced for collecting the tablets, counting the voices, comparing the totals, and so on; but none of these precautions kept the good faith of the officers charged with these duties — the custodes, diribitores, and rogatores suffragiorum, the guards, sorters, and collectors of the ballots — from being frequently suspected. Finally, to prevent intrigue and the trafficking of votes, edicts were issued; and their sheer number proves how useless they were.

Toward the close of the Republic, it was often necessary to resort to extraordinary expedients to patch the inadequacy of the laws. Sometimes miracles were invented; but that trick, while it might fool the people, could not fool the men who governed them. Sometimes an assembly was called together in haste, before the candidates had time to organize their factions. Sometimes a whole sitting was eaten up with talk, once it was clear the people had been won over and was about to take the wrong side. But in the end ambition slipped past every attempt to check it. And the most incredible fact of all is this: in the midst of all these abuses, that vast people, thanks to its ancient regulations, never stopped electing magistrates, passing laws, judging cases, and carrying through business both public and private, almost as smoothly as the senate itself could have done.

Chapter 5: The Tribunate

When no exact proportion can be established among the constituent parts of the State, or when causes that cannot be removed keep altering the relation of one part to another, the remedy is to set up a special magistracy that forms no corporate body with the rest. This office restores each term to its right relation with the others, and supplies a link or middle term either between the government and the people, or between the government and the Sovereign, or, if necessary, both at once.

This body, which I shall call the tribunate, is the preserver of the laws and of the legislative power. It serves sometimes to protect the Sovereign against the government, as the tribunes of the people did at Rome; sometimes to uphold the government against the people, as the Council of Ten does now at Venice; and sometimes to keep the balance between the two, as the Ephors — Sparta's board of overseers — did.

The tribunate is not a constituent part of the city, and it should have no share in either legislative or executive power. But this very fact makes its own power the greater: for though it can do nothing, it can prevent anything from being done. As defender of the laws, it is more sacred and more revered than the government that executes them, or than the Sovereign that ordains them. This was seen very clearly at Rome, when the proud patricians, for all their scorn of the people, were forced to bow before one of the people's plain officers — a man who had neither auspices nor jurisdiction of his own.

Wisely tempered, the tribunate is the strongest support a good constitution can have. But let its strength run even slightly past the mark, and it overturns the whole State. Weakness, on the other hand, is not in its nature: provided it is anything at all, it is never less than it ought to be.

It degenerates into tyranny when it usurps the executive power it should only restrain, and when it tries to set aside the laws it should only protect. The immense power of the Ephors, harmless as long as Sparta kept its morals, hastened the corruption once corruption had begun. The blood of Agis, slaughtered by these tyrants, was avenged by his successor; the crime of the Ephors and their punishment alike sped the republic's destruction, and after Cleomenes, Sparta counted for nothing. Rome perished the same way: the excessive power of the tribunes, usurped degree by degree, ended by serving — with the help of laws made to secure liberty — as a shield for the emperors who destroyed that liberty. As for the Venetian Council of Ten, it is a tribunal of blood, an object of horror to patricians and people alike; far from giving the laws lofty protection, now that the laws have been degraded it does nothing but strike in the darkness blows that no one dares to notice.

The tribunate, like the government, grows weak as its members multiply. When the tribunes of the Roman people, who at first numbered only two, and then five, wished to double that number, the senate let them do it — confident that it could use some to check the others, as indeed it freely did afterwards.

The best way to prevent usurpation by so formidable a body — though no government has yet tried it — would be not to make it permanent, but to fix by rule the periods during which it stands suspended. These intervals should not be long enough for abuses to take root, and they can be set by law so that extraordinary commissions may easily shorten them in case of need.

This method seems to me to have no drawbacks, because, as I have said, the tribunate forms no part of the constitution and can be removed without the constitution suffering. And it seems effective too, because a newly restored magistrate starts out not with the power his predecessor wielded, but with the power the law allows him.

Chapter 6: The Dictatorship

The inflexibility of the laws, which keeps them from bending to events, can in certain cases make them disastrous — can make them, in a moment of crisis, the very ruin of the State. The order and the slowness of legal forms demand a stretch of time that circumstances sometimes refuse to give. A thousand situations can arise that the lawgiver never provided for; and to recognize that not everything can be foreseen is itself a very necessary kind of foresight.

It is wrong, therefore, to want political institutions made so rigid that their operation can never be suspended. Even Sparta let its laws sleep.

But only the greatest dangers can outweigh the danger of tampering with the public order, and the sacred power of the laws should never be halted except when the survival of the country is at stake. In these rare and obvious cases, public security is provided for by a special act that entrusts it to the worthiest person. This commission can be carried out in either of two ways, depending on the nature of the danger.

If quickening the activity of the government is remedy enough, power is concentrated in the hands of one or two of its members. In that case it is not the authority of the laws that changes, only the form of administering them. But if the peril is such that the whole machinery of the laws is an obstacle to saving them, then a supreme ruler is named — one who silences all the laws and suspends the sovereign authority for a moment. In such a case there is no doubt about the general will: clearly the people's first intention is that the State shall not perish. The suspension of the legislative authority is in no sense its abolition. The magistrate who silences it cannot make it speak; he dominates it, but he cannot represent it. He can do anything, except make laws.

The first method was used by the Roman senate when, in a consecrated formula, it charged the consuls to provide for the safety of the Republic. The second was used when one of the two consuls named a dictator — a single man given supreme emergency power for a fixed term, a custom Rome borrowed from Alba.

— The nomination was made secretly, by night, as if there were something shameful in setting a man above the laws.

During the first period of the Republic, the dictatorship was used very often, because the State did not yet rest on a base firm enough to stand by the strength of its constitution alone. Public morals in those days made many precautions unnecessary that would have been needed at other times, so there was no fear that a dictator would abuse his authority or try to keep it past his term of office. On the contrary, so much power seemed a burden to the man clothed in it, and he hurried to lay it down, as if standing in the place of the laws were a post too troublesome and too dangerous to hold.

So it is the danger not of its abuse, but of its cheapening, that makes me criticize the careless use of this supreme magistracy in the earliest times. While it was being spent freely on elections, dedications, and purely formal functions, there was reason to fear it would grow less formidable in time of real need, and that men would come to treat as empty a title used only for empty ceremony.

Toward the end of the Republic, the Romans, grown more cautious, became as unreasonably stingy with the dictatorship as they had once been lavish. It is easy to see that their fears were groundless; that the very weakness of the capital was its protection against the magistrates inside it; that a dictator might, in certain cases, defend the public liberty but could never endanger it; and that the chains of Rome would be forged, not in Rome itself, but in her armies. The feeble resistance Marius offered Sulla, and Pompey offered Caesar, showed clearly what could be expected from authority at home against force from abroad.

This misjudgment led the Romans into great mistakes — such as, for example, failing to name a dictator during the conspiracy of Catiline. For since only the city itself was at stake, with at most some province in Italy, a dictator, armed with the unlimited authority the laws gave him, would have made short work of the conspiracy — which was in fact smothered only by a lucky cluster of chances that human prudence had no right to count on.

Instead, the senate settled for handing its whole power to the consuls. The result was that Cicero, in order to act effectively, was driven to exceed his powers on a capital point; and though in the first transports of joy his conduct was applauded, he was later, and justly, called to account for the blood of citizens spilled in violation of the laws — a reproach that could never have been leveled at a dictator. But the consul's eloquence carried all before it; and he himself, Roman though he was, loved his own glory better than his country, and looked less for the most lawful and secure way of saving the State than for the way that gave him the whole honor of having done it. — That honor was just what he could not be sure of if he proposed a dictator; for he dared not name himself, and could not be certain his colleague would name him. — So he was justly honored as the liberator of Rome, and also justly punished as a breaker of the laws. However brilliant his recall from exile may have been, it was undoubtedly an act of pardon.

However this important trust is conferred, it matters greatly that its duration be fixed at a very short term that can never be extended. In the crises that lead to its adoption, the State is either soon lost or soon saved; and once the pressing need has passed, the dictatorship becomes either tyrannical or useless. At Rome, where dictators held office for six months only, most of them resigned before their time was up. Had the term been longer, they might well have been tempted to stretch it further, as the decemvirs — the board of ten lawmakers — did with their term of a year. The dictator had only time enough to meet the need that had caused him to be chosen; he had none to dream of other projects.

Chapter 7: The Censorship

Just as the law is the declaration of the general will, the censorship — the office charged with watching over public morals — is the declaration of the public judgment. Public opinion is the kind of law the censor administers; and, like the government, he only applies it to particular cases.

Far from being the arbiter of the people's opinion, the censorial tribunal only declares it; and the moment the two part company, its decisions are null and void.

It is useless to distinguish a nation's morals from the objects of its esteem: both rest on the same principle and necessarily run together. Among all the peoples of the world, it is opinion, not nature, that decides the choice of their pleasures. Set men's opinions right, and their morals will purify themselves. Men always love what is good, or what they take to be good; it is in judging what is good that they go wrong. So it is this judgment that has to be regulated. Whoever judges morals judges honor; and whoever judges honor takes his law from opinion.

A people's opinions are born of its constitution. Although the law does not regulate morals, it is legislation that gives them birth; and when legislation weakens, morals degenerate. But at that point the judgment of the censors will not accomplish what the force of the laws has failed to do.

From this it follows that the censorship may be useful for preserving morals, but it can never be useful for restoring them. Set up censors while the laws are vigorous; once the laws have lost their vigor, all hope is gone. No legitimate power keeps its force when the laws have lost theirs.

The censorship upholds morals by keeping opinion from growing corrupt, by preserving its rightness through wise applications, and sometimes even by settling it while it is still uncertain. The use of seconds in duels, which had been carried to wild extremes in the kingdom of France, was abolished by nothing more than these words in a royal edict: "As for those who are cowards enough to call upon seconds." This judgment anticipated the public's own, and so decided it at a stroke. But when edicts from the same source tried to pronounce that dueling itself was an act of cowardice — which it certainly is — then, since common opinion did not see it that way, the public simply ignored a ruling on a question its mind was already made up about.

I have said elsewhere — and dealt with the subject at greater length in my Letter to M. d'Alembert — that since public opinion is not subject to constraint, there must be no trace of constraint in the tribunal set up to represent it. One cannot admire too much the art with which this device, wholly lost to us moderns, was put to work by the Romans, and even better by the Spartans.

A man of bad morals once made a good proposal in the Spartan Council. The Ephors ignored it, and arranged for the same proposal to be made by a virtuous citizen. What an honor for the one, and what a disgrace for the other — without a word of praise or blame for either! Certain drunkards from Samos defiled the tribunal of the Ephors; the next day, a public edict gave the people of Samos permission to be filthy. A real punishment would have stung less than impunity like that. When Sparta pronounced on what was or was not decent, Greece did not appeal her judgments.

Chapter 8: Civil Religion

At first men had no kings but the gods, and no government but theocracy. They reasoned as Caligula did — and at that stage, they reasoned rightly. It takes a long time for feelings to change enough that men can bring themselves to accept their own equals as masters, and to persuade themselves they will profit by doing so.
