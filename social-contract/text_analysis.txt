TEXT ANALYSIS AND TRANSLATION STRATEGY
The Social Contract, or Principles of Political Right
Jean-Jacques Rousseau, 1762 (G.D.H. Cole translation, 1913)

================================================================================
1. WHAT THIS BOOK IS
================================================================================

The third leg of the social-contract trilogy (after Hobbes' Leviathan and
Locke's Two Treatises, both already in this collection — consistency with
their vocabulary matters where the concepts overlap). Rousseau asks: what
could make political authority legitimate? His answer: only an agreement in
which each person gives himself to the whole community, creating a collective
sovereign whose GENERAL WILL aims at the common good.

Four books:
  I.   Foundations — against natural authority and slavery; the social
       compact itself (the famous formula); civil vs. natural liberty;
       property.
  II.  Sovereignty — inalienable, indivisible; the GENERAL WILL vs. the
       will of all; the limits of sovereign power; law; the Legislator
       (the extraordinary founder-figure); the people fit to receive laws.
  III. Government — government as agent of the sovereign, never its master;
       democracy, aristocracy, monarchy compared; climate arguments; how
       governments degenerate and states die; the English are free only
       during elections; deputies vs. direct sovereignty.
  IV.  Institutions — voting and elections; the Roman comitia, tribunate,
       dictatorship, censorship (a long Roman-institutions digression —
       translate completely); CIVIL RELIGION; brief conclusion.

This is a canonical work of political philosophy. Its discussions of
revolution, tyranny, civil religion, and the death penalty are 18th-century
political theory and must be rendered faithfully and completely in a
measured scholarly storyteller's voice.

================================================================================
2. ROUSSEAU'S STYLE
================================================================================

- APHORISTIC SNAP. Rousseau writes in short chapters that each land one
  thesis, often opened or closed with a quotable paradox. Where Hobbes
  builds Euclidean chains, Rousseau detonates epigrams. Preserve the snap:
  short sentences at the punch moments, longer flowing ones in the
  argument.
- PARADOX AS METHOD. "Forced to be free," born free yet in chains, the
  sovereign that cannot bind itself. Never smooth a paradox into something
  reasonable-sounding — the jolt is the point.
- PASSIONATE LOGIC. He argues like a geometer who is also angry. First
  person throughout ("I mean to inquire..."); direct address; rhetorical
  questions. Keep the heat AND the rigor.
- HONEST HEDGING. Rousseau frequently concedes difficulties ("I am aware
  of the objection..."). Keep his concede-then-answer rhythm.

================================================================================
3. FORMAT CONVENTIONS IN THE SOURCE FILES
================================================================================

- Files are BOOK-level (with "(PART n OF k)" markers for split books).
  File 000 opens with the title block and Rousseau's short FOREWORD —
  render the foreword under its own subheading "Foreword".
- Chapters appear as "CHAPTER I" on one line, blank line, then the title
  in caps ("SUBJECT OF THE FIRST BOOK"). Render each as ONE subheading
  line: "Chapter 1: The Subject of This First Book" — number and
  modernized title combined. These are the book's navigation; keep every
  one, on its own line.
- The Latin epigraph (Fœderis æquas / Dicamus leges) stays, with an
  em-dash gloss ("Let us set equal terms for the truce" — Virgil,
  Aeneid XI).
- Footnotes appear inline in the source. Rousseau's own substantive notes:
  integrate gracefully in place (a short bracketed or dashed aside). Pure
  cross-references: drop.
- Scripture-style citations none; classical references (Aristotle, Grotius,
  Montesquieu, Roman institutions) stay by name.

================================================================================
4. VOCABULARY MAP (use consistently)
================================================================================

  GENERAL WILL (volonté générale)  -> general will — THE term of art; never
                                      paraphrase as "popular opinion" or
                                      "majority view." Defined Book II.
  will of all                      -> will of all (the mere sum of private
                                      wills — keep the distinction from the
                                      general will razor sharp, II.3)
  particular will                  -> particular/private will
  Sovereign / Sovereignty          -> sovereign — gloss EARLY and clearly:
                                      for Rousseau the sovereign is THE
                                      PEOPLE acting collectively as
                                      lawmaker, never a king. (Readers
                                      coming from Leviathan need this.)
  Prince                           -> the government / the magistracy as a
                                      body — gloss at first use: NOT a
                                      monarch. Use "the government" freely.
  Magistrate                       -> magistrate / government officer
  social compact / contract        -> the social contract (Cole alternates;
                                      standardize, keep "compact" only in
                                      the chapter title "The Social Compact"
                                      if rendered, else contract)
  body politic                     -> body politic
  state of nature / civil state    -> state of nature / civil state
  natural liberty / civil liberty / moral liberty -> keep the triple
  the Legislator (législateur)     -> the Lawgiver — gloss: the
                                      extraordinary founder-figure who
                                      frames a people's laws (Lycurgus,
                                      Moses, Numa), not a legislature
  fatherland (patrie)              -> homeland; "citizen" vs "subject"
                                      distinction kept (citizens as sharers
                                      in sovereign authority, subjects as
                                      under the laws)
  civil religion                   -> civil religion (Book IV term of art)
  aristocracy: natural/elective/hereditary -> keep the triple
  Democracy/Monarchy/Aristocracy   -> as in Leviathan: plain modern terms
  comitia, tribunate, censorship   -> keep Roman terms with one-time
                                      glosses

================================================================================
5. FAMOUS PASSAGES — PRESERVE NEARLY VERBATIM
================================================================================

- "Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains." (I.1 opening — the
  most famous sentence in political philosophy; let it stand alone as its
  own paragraph.)
- The contract formula (I.6): "Each of us puts his person and all his
  power in common under the supreme direction of the general will..."
- "...he will be forced to be free" (I.7 — keep verbatim, with Rousseau's
  own surrounding explanation intact).
- "Were there a people of gods, their government would be democratic. So
  perfect a government is not for men." (III.4)
- The English people "is free only during the election of members of
  parliament; as soon as they are elected, slavery overtakes it" (III.15).
- "The strongest is never strong enough to be always the master, unless
  he transforms strength into right, and obedience into duty." (I.3)

================================================================================
6. CONTENT NEEDING CAREFUL, SCHOLARLY HANDLING
================================================================================

- SLAVERY (I.2, I.4): Rousseau's demolition of slavery arguments
  (Grotius, Aristotle) — render with its full moral force; it is an
  abolitionist landmark. "The words slave and right contradict each
  other."
- CIVIL RELIGION (IV.8): includes the notorious line that one who
  publicly accepts the civil dogmas and then acts as if he does not
  believe them "should be punished by death." Render Rousseau's argument
  precisely as HIS argument, in context (his dogmas are minimal and
  tolerance itself is among them); never soften, never sensationalize.
  His attack on Christianity's political effects is landmark
  church-state theory — same treatment as Hobbes Part III/IV received.
- REVOLUTION/TYRANNY (III.10-18): analytical, not incendiary — measured
  register.
- CLIMATE/NATIONAL-CHARACTER claims (III.8) reflect 18th-century
  geography; translate precisely without editorial intrusion.
- Brief dated references to "savages" → render per established project
  convention ("indigenous peoples", a light in-his-view signal where
  needed).

================================================================================
7. VOICE TARGET — SAMPLE TRANSFORMATION
================================================================================

Original (I.1):
  "Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains. One thinks himself
  the master of others, and still remains a greater slave than they."

Modern target:
  "Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains. One man thinks
  himself the master of others — and remains a greater slave than any of
  them."

Original (I.3):
  "Force is a physical power, and I fail to see what moral effect it can
  have. To yield to force is an act of necessity, not of will."

Modern target:
  "Force is physical power; I do not see what morality could ever come
  from it. Giving in to force is something you do out of necessity, not
  choice."

Register: 8th grade–high school. Contractions in conversational flow, none
at aphoristic punch moments (an epigram with a contraction loses its
marble). Complete: every argument, example, footnote-aside, and Roman
detail survives.
