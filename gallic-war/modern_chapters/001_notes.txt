TRANSLATION NOTES: Book I
==========================

1. MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-------------------------------

A. Opening orientation paragraph
Added a brief scene-setting paragraph at the very start (as specified in the text
analysis) to orient modern readers: the year, Caesar's position, and the two crises
that drive the book.

B. Sentence structure overhaul
The McDevitte/Bohn translation is dense with nested subordinate clauses, sometimes
running 100+ words. These have been systematically broken into shorter, punchier
sentences to restore Caesar's original directness. Example: the description of
Dumnorix's machinations (Section 18 in the original) was a single enormous paragraph
of indirect speech -- broken into clear, separate statements with active verbs.

C. Speeches converted to direct quotes
Key speeches made direct per the text analysis strategy:
  - Divico's challenge to Caesar (Section 13)
  - Caesar's reply to Divico (Section 14)
  - Liscus's revelation about the saboteurs (Section 17)
  - Divitiacus's entire appeal about the Germanic threat (Section 31)
  - Divitiacus's plea for his brother Dumnorix (Section 19-20)
  - Caesar's speech to his panicking army (Section 40)
  - Ariovistus's replies in the diplomatic exchanges (Sections 36, 44)
  - Ariovistus's speech at the conference (Section 44)
Routine diplomatic messages kept in indirect speech or summary form.

D. Time references modernized
  - "the third watch" -> "around midnight"
  - "the fourth watch" -> "before dawn"
  - "the seventh hour" -> "early afternoon"
  - "the fifth before the kalends of April" -> "March 28th"
  - "the day before the ides of April" -> "April 13th"
  - Consulship dates given with modern year equivalents in parentheses.

E. Archaic vocabulary replaced throughout
Per the vocabulary mapping in the text analysis:
  - "inasmuch as" -> "since" / "because"
  - "betook themselves" -> "retreated" / "fled"
  - "corn" -> "grain"
  - "ensigns" -> "standards"
  - "succour" -> "help" / "reinforcements"
  - "baggage-train" -> "baggage" / "supply train"
  - "provisions" -> "supplies" / "food"
  - "put to death" -> "killed" / "executed"

F. Geographic/cultural context added on first mention
  - Helvetii: "a powerful tribe occupying what is now Switzerland"
  - Vesontio: "(modern Besancon)"
  - Boii: "who had previously crossed the Rhine from Germany and settled in
    Norican territory (modern Austria)"
  - Vergobret explained as "chief magistrate, elected annually, with the power
    of life and death"
  - Suebi: "a massive Germanic federation"

G. Passive constructions made active
"It was reported to Caesar" -> "Caesar received word" / "his scouts reported"
"It was discovered" -> "Caesar learned" / "the full picture emerged"
"He was informed" -> "word came" / "news arrived"

H. Paragraph breaks increased
Long blocks about troop movements, diplomacy, and logistics broken into shorter,
more digestible paragraphs. Section breaks (---) added at major narrative transitions.

I. Closing line
Added a very brief wry editorial comment at the very end ("and, no doubt, to write
the dispatches...") -- a tiny authorial touch acknowledging Caesar's self-promotional
purpose, as suggested by the text analysis ("occasionally wry about Caesar's
self-promotion"). This is the only editorializing in the entire translation.

J. Census passage
Added a single-line editorial summary after the census figures: "The migration had
cost a quarter million people." This drives home the human cost that Caesar reports
clinically. The numbers are all as given in the original.

2. KEY DECISIONS
-----------------

A. Third person maintained throughout. Caesar is always "Caesar," never "he" in
   ambiguous contexts. This is faithful to the original and distinctive.

B. Military terminology: Kept "legion," "centurion," "cohort," "cavalry,"
   "auxiliary." Translated "praetorian cohort" as "personal guard." Explained
   the pilum's bending mechanism during the Battle of Bibracte rather than
   using the Latin term. "Phalanx" kept as-is (recognizable to most readers).

C. Numbers: All troop counts and distances preserved exactly as in the original.
   Roman miles kept as "miles" (close enough to modern miles per text analysis).

D. The Battle of Bibracte is the centerpiece of the first half. Structured for
   maximum clarity: the setup (hill, deployment), the initial charge, the javelin
   effect, the Boii/Tulingi flanking move, the two-front response, the long
   grinding fight into the night. The wagon barricade scene is vivid and important.

E. The army panic scene before facing Ariovistus is the major dramatic set piece
   of the second half. Translated to maximize the emotional progression: the
   spreading fear, the will-writing, the tears, the open talk of mutiny -- then
   Caesar's speech breaking the spell. The tenth legion joke about being "turned
   into knights" preserved for comic relief.

F. Ariovistus's threat to assassinate Caesar (passed along by Roman nobles) is
   one of the most remarkable passages -- translated directly and without
   editorializing. Let the reader absorb it.

3. NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (Book II)
-------------------------------------

Character names established:
  - Caesar (always "Caesar")
  - Labienus (Titus Labienus, Caesar's chief lieutenant)
  - Divitiacus (loyal Aeduan leader, Caesar's friend and trusted advisor)
  - Dumnorix (Divitiacus's treacherous brother, under surveillance)
  - Publius Crassus (young cavalry commander, son of the famous Crassus)
  - Ariovistus (Germanic king, defeated and fled across the Rhine)
  - Procillus (Gaius Valerius Procillus, Caesar's trusted friend and envoy)

Tribal names used:
  - Aedui (Rome's chief Gallic allies, "Brothers and Kinsmen")
  - Sequani (hosting Caesar's winter quarters at end of Book I)
  - Belgae (northern Gaul, the "toughest fighters" -- Book II's main subject)
  - Helvetii (defeated and sent home)
  - Suebi (Germanic federation, retreated back across Rhine)

Vocabulary choices to maintain:
  - "the Province" (capitalized) for Roman Transalpine Gaul
  - "grain" not "corn"
  - "standards" not "ensigns"
  - "supply train" or "baggage" not "baggage-train"
  - Legion numbers spelled out (the tenth legion)
  - Tribal names in Latin form (Helvetii, not "Swiss")

Ongoing storylines:
  - Dumnorix is under surveillance but not punished -- this will come back
  - Caesar is wintering among the Sequani with Labienus in command
  - The Belgae have been established as the fiercest Gallic fighters --
    they are the subject of Book II
  - Rome's military reputation has been enhanced by two victories in one season
  - The German threat has been checked but not eliminated

Tone established:
  - Confident, matter-of-fact military narrative
  - Direct, active voice
  - Short paragraphs, clear cause-and-effect
  - Important speeches in direct quotes
  - Light editorial touches extremely rare (one at the very end)
  - Violence reported clinically, without sensationalizing
  - Strategic reasoning always made clear
