TRANSLATION NOTES: Book VI
===========================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: The McDevitte/Bohn translation renders Book VI in
   characteristically Victorian style -- enormous compound-complex sentences
   with multiple subordinate clauses. Nearly every paragraph in the original
   translation is a single sentence. These have been broken into short,
   direct sentences following the text analysis strategy of restoring Caesar's
   original clarity. The ethnographic sections especially benefited from this
   treatment.

2. LABIENUS'S SPEECH: Converted from indirect to direct speech for dramatic
   effect. The original "he openly says in a council that since the Germans
   are said to be approaching, he would not bring into..." becomes a direct
   quote. Similarly, his battlefield speech to the soldiers is rendered as
   direct address.

3. THE PRISONER'S SPEECH TO THE SIGAMBRI: Converted to direct quote. This
   is one of the most dramatic moments in the book -- a prisoner essentially
   handing the Sigambri a roadmap to attack an undermanned Roman camp. The
   direct speech makes the treachery vivid.

4. UBII AMBASSADORS: Kept as a blend of direct and indirect speech. Their
   groveling tone is preserved but streamlined.

5. ETHNOGRAPHIC DIGRESSION: This is the heart of Book VI and the most famous
   section. Key decisions:
   - Broke the long block paragraphs into shorter, thematic segments with
     natural breaks between topics (Druids, knights, religion, customs, etc.)
   - Made the Druid section vivid and specific -- the ban from sacrifices as
     "effectively a death sentence" captures what the original laboriously
     explains about social consequences
   - The human sacrifice passages (wicker man) translated faithfully without
     editorializing, per the text analysis guidance
   - The animal descriptions (elk, aurochs, "unicorn" deer) given an
     entertaining but faithful treatment. The elk that "can't lie down" is
     one of ancient literature's most charming zoological errors
   - Germanic customs section emphasizes the contrasts with Gaul that Caesar
     is drawing, keeping his anthropological framing

6. GEOGRAPHIC/CULTURAL CONTEXT: Added brief identifications per text analysis:
   - Treviri: "near the Rhine in what is now Luxembourg"
   - Menapii: "vast stretch of marshes and dense forests in what is now the
     Low Countries"
   - Lutetia: "modern-day Paris"
   - Durocortorum: "modern-day Reims"
   - Hercynian forest given extended contextual treatment since Caesar himself
     provides lengthy description

7. VOCABULARY MAPPINGS APPLIED:
   - "inasmuch as" -> "since" / "because"
   - "succour" -> "reinforcements"
   - "levy" -> "draft" / "mobilization" / "levy" (kept where natural)
   - "corn" -> "grain"
   - "despatch" -> "speed"
   - "baggage-train" -> "baggage train" / "supply train"
   - "ensigns/standards" -> "standards"
   - "put to death" -> "killed" / "executed"
   - "betook themselves" -> "retreated" / "fled"
   - "it was reported" -> attributed to specific actors where possible

8. TIME REFERENCES: No specific Roman time references (watches, kalends)
   appear prominently in Book VI, but "before winter ended" and "beginning
   of spring" are retained as natural seasonal markers.

9. AMBIORIX CHASE SEQUENCE: The near-miss at his house is one of Book VI's
   best action sequences. Translated to maximize the dramatic irony -- fortune
   delivers him and then snatches him away. Caesar's philosophical aside about
   fortune is preserved.

10. SEXTIUS BACULUS: His heroic stand at the gate is given vivid, economical
    treatment. The detail that he hadn't eaten in five days is highlighted --
    it makes his courage more remarkable.

11. ACCO'S EXECUTION: "After the custom of our ancestors" is glossed as
    "flogged to death while bound to a stake" -- this is the Roman punishment
    known as "more maiorum," and modern readers need the specifics to
    understand the severity.

12. CATIVOLCUS'S SUICIDE: The yew-tree poisoning is a striking detail that
    reads almost like a tragic aside. Preserved faithfully with his curse
    against Ambiorix.

KEY DECISIONS
--------------

- "Caesar" maintained in third person throughout, never replaced with "he"
  when ambiguous
- Tribal names kept in Latin forms: Treviri, Menapii, Eburones, Senones,
  Carnutes, Nervii, Aduatuci, Ubii, Suebi, Sigambri, etc.
- "Gaul" for the country, "Gauls" for the people, "Gallic" for adjective
- "the Province" not mentioned in Book VI
- Pompey identified but not over-explained -- readers of earlier books know him
- Commius the Atrebatian identified by tribe name as in original
- Cingetorix distinguished from other characters by context
- The "unicorn" deer: translated faithfully as described. This is likely a
  confused account of reindeer. Did not editorialize about the identification.
- The elk's "jointless legs": translated faithfully. This is ancient folklore,
  not zoology. Caesar is reporting what he was told.
- The aurochs: identified as nearly elephant-sized per original. These were
  real animals (Bos primigenius, extinct since 1627) and Caesar's description
  is broadly accurate except for the size comparison.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER TRANSLATOR
-----------------------------------

Book VII is the masterpiece -- Vercingetorix's great revolt. Key consistency
points:

1. CHARACTERS ESTABLISHED: Labienus is Caesar's most trusted and capable
   officer. Cicero (Quintus, not Marcus) is competent but makes one costly
   error of judgment in Book VI. Ambiorix remains at large -- he is never
   caught. Commius the Atrebatian is currently loyal to Caesar but will turn
   against him in Book VII.

2. TONE: Book VI ends on a note of frustrated near-success (Ambiorix escapes)
   and ominous punishment (Acco's execution). Book VII opens with the
   consequences -- Acco's execution galvanizes Gallic resistance. The tone
   should shift from Book VI's episodic, almost anthropological quality to
   Book VII's sustained dramatic narrative.

3. MILITARY VOCABULARY: Maintained "legions" (not numbered beyond "fourteenth"
   in this book), "cohorts," "cavalry," "standards," "fortifications,"
   "rampart." Continue this vocabulary.

4. AEDUI: Currently Rome's closest Gallic ally. They will betray Caesar in
   Book VII -- one of the war's most dramatic reversals. Don't foreshadow.

5. NAMES TO MAINTAIN:
   - Vercingetorix (new in Book VII -- needs strong introduction)
   - Labienus, Trebonius, Fabius (officers)
   - Commius (will change sides)
   - The Aedui, Arverni, Senones, Carnutes (key tribal players)

6. The execution of Acco "in the traditional Roman manner" -- this detail
   directly motivates the opening of Book VII. The severity of the punishment
   terrifies other Gallic leaders into thinking they might be next, which
   helps trigger the revolt.

7. STYLE: Book VII demands more sustained narrative tension than Book VI.
   The siege of Alesia especially needs to build and maintain pressure across
   many paragraphs. The ethnographic/episodic rhythm of Book VI should give
   way to continuous dramatic momentum.
