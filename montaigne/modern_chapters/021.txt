Of Books

I have no doubt that I often end up talking about things that are handled better, and more truly, by the people who actually master them. What you have here is purely a test of my natural abilities, not my acquired ones. Catch me in some ignorance and you've won nothing off me — I'd hardly answer to someone else for writings I don't answer for to myself, or even feel satisfied with. Anyone hunting for knowledge should go fish for it where it lives; there is nothing I claim less. These are fancies of my own, and I'm not trying to explain the world with them — I'm trying to lay myself open. The things themselves may be known to me someday, or may have been once, back when fortune happened to set me down wherever they were being explained. But I've completely forgotten. I may be a man of some reading, but I am a man of no retention.

So I can promise nothing certain, beyond showing you exactly how far the knowledge I currently have happens to reach. Don't pay attention to the matter I write about — pay attention to the shape I give it as I write. Watch, in what I borrow, whether I knew how to choose the right thing to lift or support the idea, which is always my own. Because I make others say for me — not before me, but after me — what I can't say so well myself, whether for want of language or want of sense. I don't count my borrowings; I weigh them. If I'd wanted to make them impressive by quantity, I'd have packed in twice as many. They are almost all from names so famous and so ancient that they seem to introduce themselves, without my help.

In the reasonings, comparisons, and arguments that I transplant into my own soil and mix in with my own, I deliberately hide the author. I do it to put a check on those rash critics who pounce on every kind of writing — especially recent writing, by men still living, and in the common tongue, which lets every man fancy himself qualified to judge, and makes the whole conception and design seem common too. I want them to flick Plutarch's nose thinking it's mine, and burn themselves insulting Seneca in me. I have to hide my weakness under these great reputations. And I will love anyone who can pluck my feathers off me — by sheer clearness of understanding and judgment, by nothing but the force and beauty of the writing itself. Because I, whose memory fails me at every turn when I try to sort the borrowed plumes out by their nation, am still wise enough to know, by the measure of my own abilities, that my soil could never produce the rich flowers I find growing there, and that all the fruit of my own raising isn't worth a single one of them.

For this much, though, I do hold myself accountable: if I trip over myself; if there's vanity or weakness in my writing that I don't sense myself, or can't see even when someone points it out. Plenty of faults escape our eye — but the real infirmity of judgment is failing to see them when somebody else lays them open to us. Knowledge and truth can lodge in us without judgment, and judgment can live in us without them; in fact, confessing your ignorance is one of the finest and surest proofs of judgment that I know.

I have no drill sergeant to put my writings in rank and file except chance. As things come into my head, I pile them up; sometimes they crowd in whole battalions, sometimes single file. I want people to see my natural, ordinary pace, lopsided as it is. I let myself jog along however I happen to go. And anyway, these are not subjects a man is forbidden to be ignorant about, or to discuss casually, at a venture. I'd love to understand things more perfectly, but I won't pay what perfect understanding costs. My plan is to pass what's left of my life gently, not laboriously. There is nothing I am willing to break my head over — not even knowledge, whatever it's worth.

In books, all I look for is the pleasure of an honest pastime; or if I study, the only learning I'm after is the kind that teaches me to know myself, and to die well and live well. As Propertius says, "this is the post my horse must sweat toward."

When I hit difficulties in my reading, I don't sit there chewing my nails over them; after a charge or two, I let them go. If I dug in, I'd only lose myself, and my time with it. My mind is impatient — it has to be satisfied on the first pass. What I don't see at once, staring makes more obscure, not less. I do nothing without gladness; grinding away too stubbornly darkens, dulls, and tires my judgment. My sight blurs and scatters with poring; I have to pull it away and send it back in fresh attempts — exactly the way they teach us to judge the sheen of scarlet cloth: pass the eye lightly across it, then sweep over it again in several quick, repeated glances.

If one book bores me, I take up another — and I only touch them at all in the hours when doing nothing has started to weary me. I don't bother much with new books, because the old ones seem fuller and stronger; nor much with the Greeks, because my judgment can't do its work on a half-understood text.

Among the books that are simply pleasant, of the moderns I find Boccaccio's Decameron, Rabelais, and the Kisses of Johannes Secundus — if that one may be filed under the heading at all — worth the time, for amusement. As for Amadis and that sort of stuff, it couldn't hold even my childhood. And I'll say further — boldly or rashly, take your pick — that this old, heavy soul of mine is no longer tickled by Ariosto, nor even by the worthy Ovid. His easy flow and his inventions, which used to ravish me, hardly interest me now; I can barely sit still for them.

I say my opinion freely of all things — even of things that may well exceed my capacity, and that I don't pretend for a moment fall under my jurisdiction. What my judgment delivers is the measure of my own eyesight, not the measure of the things I'm bold enough to judge. When I find myself put off by Plato's Axiochus — finding it a feeble work, with all due respect to such an author — my judgment doesn't trust itself. It isn't arrogant enough to set itself against the authority of so many famous judgments of antiquity, which it regards as its tutors and masters, and with whom it would rather be wrong. In a case like that, it convicts itself: either it stopped at the outer bark, unable to reach the heart, or it was looking at the thing in some false light. It settles for keeping itself clear of confusion and disorder; as for its own weakness, it owns up to it freely. It believes it's giving a fair reading of what its perceptions show it — but those perceptions are weak and imperfect. Most of Aesop's fables carry several layers of meaning. The interpreters pick out one that happens to fit the story, but for the most part that's only the first face it shows, the surface; there are other layers underneath — more living, more essential, more profound — that they never managed to reach. And that's just how it is with me.

But to get on with the business of this essay: I have always thought that in poetry, Virgil, Lucretius, Catullus, and Horace stand many degrees above the rest — and Virgil above all in his Georgics, which I consider the most accomplished work in all of poetry. Set the Georgics beside the Aeneid, and you can easily see passages in the Aeneid the author would have given one more polish if he'd had the time. The fifth book of the Aeneid strikes me as its most perfect. I also love Lucan, and read him gladly — not so much for his style as for his own worth, and the truth and solidity of his opinions and judgments. As for good Terence, the very refinement and grace of the Latin tongue, I find him wonderful at catching, alive, our manners and the movements of the soul. What we do keeps throwing me back on him at every turn; and I can't read him often enough to stop discovering some new grace and beauty.

People who lived near Virgil's own time complained when anyone compared Lucretius to him. I think the comparison is, in truth, badly unequal — though it's a belief I have a hard time holding steady when I'm caught up in one of Lucretius's superb passages. But if that comparison made them angry, what would they say to the brutish, barbarous stupidity of the people who nowadays compare Virgil to Ariosto? What would Ariosto himself say? "O what a stupid and tasteless age," as Catullus put it.

I think the ancients had even more reason to complain about those who set Plautus beside Terence — a much closer match — than about Lucretius beside Virgil. It counts heavily in Terence's favor that the father of Roman eloquence has him, and him alone of his class, so constantly on his lips, and that the best judge of Roman poets passed the verdict he did on Terence's rival.

It has often struck me that our modern comedy writers — imitating the Italians, who are happy enough at this game — take three or four plots from Plautus or Terence to make one of their own, and cram five or six of Boccaccio's tales into a single comedy. What makes them load up on material like this is distrust of their ability to stand on their own merits. They need something to lean on; lacking stuff of their own to hold our attention, they bring in story to cover the weakness of the writing. With my author it's the opposite: the elegance and perfection of his way of speaking makes us lose our appetite for his plot. His grace and refinement of language hold us everywhere; he runs so clear throughout — "liquid, like the purest stream," as Horace says of him — and so fills the soul with his own beauties that we forget the beauties of his story.

The same observation takes me a step further. I notice that the best of the ancient poets avoided straining for effect — avoided not only the fantastic flights of the Spanish and Petrarchan styles, but even those softer, gentler touches that have decorated all the poetry written since. Yet no sound judgment will hold that against the ancients, or fail to admire, beyond all comparison, the smooth polish, the perpetual sweetness, the flowering beauty of Catullus's epigrams over all the stingers Martial fastens to the tails of his. The reason is the one I just gave — the one Martial gave about himself: "my subject did the work, and spared my wit the labor." The first sort make themselves felt without working themselves up, without getting angry; they have material enough to laugh with everywhere — they don't need to tickle themselves. The second sort need outside help: the less wit, the more body. They get on horseback because they can't stand on their own legs.

It's the same at our balls. The hired men who teach dancing, unable to carry off the bearing and dignity of our nobility, try to recommend themselves with dangerous leaps and other strange tumblers' tricks. And the ladies have an easier time of it in dances full of fancy steps, changes, and quick flurries of motion than in certain stately dances where they have only to walk at a natural pace and carry their ordinary grace and presence. I've likewise seen first-rate clowns, in their everyday clothes and wearing their everyday faces, give us every pleasure their art can give — while their apprentices, not yet up to that pitch of perfection, have to flour their faces, climb into ridiculous costumes, and pull a hundred grotesque expressions to scrape up a laugh.

This idea of mine is nowhere easier to see than in setting the Aeneid against Orlando Furioso. The first you see up on a strong wing, in a high, brave line of flight, always tracking its point. The second flutters and hops from tale to tale as from branch to branch, never trusting its wings except for the shortest hauls, perching at every turn for fear its breath and strength will give out — making, as Virgil says, "only short excursions."

Those, then, in that line, are the authors that please me best.

As for my other reading — the kind that mixes a little more profit with the pleasure, and teaches me how to order my opinions and my conduct — the books that serve me here are Plutarch (now that he's been translated into French) and Seneca. Both have this notable convenience, perfectly suited to my temperament: the knowledge I'm after comes in loose pieces that don't demand long stretches of reading, of which I am incapable. That's how it is with Plutarch's minor works and Seneca's letters, which are the best and most profitable parts of everything they wrote. It costs no great effort to pick one up, and I quit whenever I please, because they have no sequence, no dependence on one another.

These two authors agree in most of their useful and true opinions, and their parallel runs further: fortune sent them into the world in roughly the same century; both were tutors to Roman emperors; both came from foreign lands; both were rich, and both were great men. Their teaching is the cream of philosophy, served plainly and to the point. Plutarch is more uniform and steady; Seneca more various, more in motion. Seneca toils and strains every muscle to arm virtue against weakness, fear, and vicious appetite; Plutarch seems to rate their power lower — he won't alter his pace or even bother to put up his guard. Plutarch's opinions are Platonic, gentle, fitted to civil society; Seneca's are Stoic and Epicurean, further from common use but, to my mind, more useful to a single man and more solid. Seneca seems to bend a little toward the tyranny of the emperors of his day — and only seems, I'm sure, for I take it as certain he is speaking against his own judgment when he condemns the noble killers of Caesar. Plutarch is frank from end to end. Seneca sparkles with sharp touches and sallies; Plutarch with things that warm you and move you more. Plutarch contents you and pays you better. He leads us; the other drives us.

As for Cicero, the works of his most useful to my purpose are the ones that treat of character and the rules of living. But to confess the truth boldly — since once you've jumped the fence of impudence there's no reining in — his way of writing strikes me as tedious, and so does every writing of the same kind. His prefaces, definitions, subdivisions, and etymologies eat up the greater part of his work; whatever life and marrow there is gets smothered under the long windup. If I spend an hour reading him — which is a lot for me — and then try to recall what juice and substance I've extracted, most of the time I find nothing but wind, because he hasn't yet gotten to the arguments that serve his purpose, or the reasons that actually tie the knot I'm looking for.

For me, who only want to become wiser — not more learned, or more eloquent — these Aristotelian arrangements of parts are beside the point. I want a writer to begin with the main proposition. I know perfectly well what death and pleasure are; let no man waste his time dissecting them for me. What I want, from the first stroke, are good solid reasons that teach me how to stand up to their shock — and for that, neither grammatical subtleties nor ingenious weavings of words and syllogisms are the slightest use. I'm for arguments that charge straight into the heart of the strongpoint. Cicero's loiter around the edges of the subject. They're fine for the schools, the bar, and the pulpit, where we have leisure to doze and can wake up a quarter of an hour later in plenty of time to pick up the thread.

That manner of speaking is necessary with judges you mean to win over, right or wrong; with children; with ordinary people, to whom you have to say everything and see what sticks. I don't want an author laboring to get my attention, shouting "Hear ye!" fifty times like a herald. The Romans opened their religious rites with Hoc age — "attend to this" — the way we open ours with Sursum corda, "lift up your hearts." For me those are so many words wasted: I arrive already fully prepared from my own room. I need no appetizer, no invitation, no sauce; I eat the meat raw. All those preparatives, instead of whetting my appetite, tire it out and spoil it.

Will the license of the times excuse a sacrilege? — because I find Plato's dialogues themselves draggy, smothering the matter; and I mourn all the time a man with so many better things to say spent on those long, useless warm-up conversations. My ignorance excuses me here in better style, since I don't know Greek well enough to see the beauty of his language.

In general I choose books that use learning, not books that merely lead up to it. The first two, and Pliny, and others of their kind, have no Hoc age about them: they want readers who come already instructed — or if they do say "attend to this," it's a substantial attending, with a body of its own.

I also delight in reading the Letters to Atticus — not only because they hold so much of the history and affairs of Cicero's times, but far more because in them I discover his own private moods. For I have a singular curiosity, as I've said elsewhere, to pry into the souls of my authors, their natural and genuine opinions. From the works they put on the world's stage you may judge their talent — but not their character, and not the men themselves. I have lamented a thousand times the loss of the treatise Brutus wrote on virtue: it is a fine thing to learn the theory from those who best know the practice. But since the sermon is one thing and the preacher another, I'd as gladly see Brutus in Plutarch as in a book of his own. I would rather know for certain what he said in his tent to a few particular friends the night before a battle than the speech he made to his army the next morning; rather know what he did in his study and his chamber than what he did in the public square and the senate.

As for Cicero the man, I share the common opinion: learning aside, there was no great excellence in him. He was a good citizen, of an affable nature — as fat, easy men like him usually are — but soft-living, in truth, and with a mighty share of vanity and ambition. And I don't know how to excuse him for thinking his poetry fit to publish. Writing bad verses is no great flaw; failing to see how unworthy they were of the glory of his name — that is a flaw. As for his eloquence, it stands beyond all comparison; I believe no one will ever equal it.

The younger Cicero, who resembled his father in nothing but his name, was commanding in Asia when one day he found several strangers at his table — among them Cestius, seated at the lower end, the way men slip into the open tables of the great. Cicero asked one of his servants who the man was, and was told his name. But like a man with his mind elsewhere, who keeps forgetting the answer, he asked again — three or four times. The servant, tired of repeating himself, and wanting to fix the man in his memory by some particular detail, said: "It's that Cestius — the one you were told makes no great account of your father's eloquence next to his own." Cicero, suddenly stung, ordered poor Cestius seized on the spot and had him soundly whipped, in his own presence. A most discourteous host.

Even among those who, all things weighed, judged Cicero's eloquence incomparable, there were some who didn't hesitate to note its faults: his great friend Brutus, for one, who called it a broken and feeble eloquence. The orators nearest his own age also faulted his obsessive care for a certain long cadence at the close of his sentences, and singled out the words esse videatur — "it would seem to be" — which he leans on so often there. For my own part, I prefer a style that comes off shorter and rounder. He does sometimes snap his clauses together briskly enough, but very seldom. One passage I marked myself: "I had rather be old for a shorter time than be old before I am old," as he says in his book on old age.

The historians are the ball that comes to my strong side — pleasant and easy, and at the same time the place where man in general, the creature I hunt the knowledge of, appears more vividly and more entire than anywhere else: the variety and truth of his inward qualities, in bulk and in detail, the diversity of materials he's assembled from, and the accidents that threaten him. Now the writers of lives — since they dwell more on counsels than on events, more on what issues from within than on what happens without — are the historians fittest for me; and that is why, in every way, Plutarch is my man. I am very sorry we don't have a dozen Diogenes Laertiuses, or that his lives of the philosophers aren't longer and better known, for I am every bit as curious about the lives and fortunes of these great teachers of the world as about the variety of their doctrines and opinions.

In this branch of study you have to tumble through all sorts of authors without distinction, old and new, French and foreign, to learn the things they variously treat of. But Caesar, in my opinion, deserves study in a class by himself — not for the history alone, but for the man, he has so much excellence and perfection above all the rest, Sallust included. Truly, I read this author with rather more reverence and respect than we usually grant human writings: now considering the man himself, through his actions and his miraculous greatness; now the purity and inimitable polish of his language, in which he surpassed not only every historian, as Cicero admits, but perhaps Cicero himself. He speaks of his enemies with such fairness that — apart from the false colors he uses to dress up his bad cause, and the filth of his pestilent ambition — I think the only fault you can charge him with is speaking too sparingly of himself. So many great things could not have been done under his command unless his own personal share in them was far larger than he lets on.

I love historians either very simple or excellent. The simple ones, who have nothing of their own to mix in, and whose only business is to gather everything that comes to their notice and record it all faithfully, without choosing or sorting, leave our judgment whole for discerning the truth. Such, among others, is honest Froissart, who marches through his enterprise with so frank a plainness that when he's made a mistake, he isn't ashamed to confess it and correct it on the spot where it's pointed out — and who gives us even the variety of rumors then in circulation, and the different reports that were made to him. His is the naked, unshaped stuff of history, and every reader can profit from it according to his own understanding.

The truly excellent ones have the judgment to pick out what is worth knowing; of two reports, to weigh which is the likelier true; from the condition and temperament of princes, to infer their counsels and put fitting words in their mouths. They have the right to assume the authority of bending our belief to theirs — but certainly that privilege belongs to very few.

The middle sort — and most of them are the middle sort — spoil everything. They insist on chewing our meat for us. They take it on themselves to judge, and so, inevitably, to bend history to their own fancy; for once the judgment tilts to one side, the narrative can't help twisting and writhing to that bias. They undertake to select what's worth knowing, and meanwhile hide from us exactly the word, the private act, that would have taught us most; they leave out as unbelievable the things they don't understand — and some things, perhaps, because they can't render them in good French or Latin. Let them flaunt their eloquence and their cleverness all they like; let them judge to their hearts' content; but let them also leave us something to judge after them. Let them not alter or dilute, by their trimmings and their selections, anything of the substance of the matter, but hand it to us pure and entire, in all its dimensions.

For the most part — and especially in these latter ages — the people picked for this work are picked out of the crowd for the single qualification of speaking well, as if we came to history to learn grammar. And having been hired for that alone, and bringing nothing to market but chatter, they're right not to trouble themselves over anything else. So with a fine jingle of words they cook us up a tidy stew of the reports they gather on street corners. The only good histories are those written by the men who themselves held command in the affairs they describe, or who had a share in conducting them, or who at least have conducted others of the same kind. That's nearly all the Greek and Roman histories: with several eyewitnesses writing on the same subject — in an age when greatness and learning commonly met in the same person — any error that slips in must be a slight one, on a deeply doubtful point. What can you expect from a physician writing about war, or a schoolman writing about the designs of princes?

If you want to see how scrupulous the Romans were on this point, one example will do. Asinius Pollio found, even in Caesar's own histories, things misreported — because Caesar couldn't have his eye on every part of his army at once and had trusted individuals who brought him less than accurate accounts, or because his lieutenants hadn't fully informed him of what they'd done in his absence. Which shows how delicate a thing the hunt for truth is, when you can't trust the report of a battle to the knowledge of the man who commanded it, nor to the soldiers who fought in it, unless — as in a judicial inquiry — the witnesses are confronted and the objections heard on the proof of every smallest incident. Honestly, the knowledge we have of our own affairs is far murkier still. But that has been handled well enough by Bodin, and in line with my own opinion.

To prop up the weakness of my memory a little — so extreme that more than once I've picked up a book as new and unread that I'd carefully gone through a few years before, scribbling it full of my notes — I've adopted a custom of late: at the end of every book I don't intend to read again, I write down the date I finished it and the verdict I'd reached, so it can at least show me again the general air and idea I'd formed of the author while reading him. And I'll copy out some of those annotations here.

Here's what I wrote, some ten years ago, in my Guicciardini (whatever language my books speak to me in, I speak to them in my own): "He is a diligent historiographer, from whom, in my opinion, a man may learn the truth of the affairs of his time as exactly as from any other — and in most of them he was himself an actor, in honorable rank. There's no sign that he ever disguised anything out of hatred, favor, or vanity; the free judgments he passes on the great — particularly on those who advanced him and employed him in posts of trust, like Pope Clement VII — are ample testimony to that. As for the part he thinks himself best at, his digressions and reflections, some are genuinely good, enriched with fine touches; but he loves them too much. Determined to leave nothing unsaid, with a subject so full, so vast, so nearly infinite, he turns flabby, and smacks a little of scholastic prattle. I've also noticed this about him: of all the souls and deeds he judges, all the motives and counsels, he never credits a single one to virtue, religion, or conscience — as if those things had died out of the world entirely. Of every action, however fine it looks on the outside, he refers the cause to some vicious motive or some hope of profit. It is impossible to imagine that among the countless actions he passes judgment on, not one was produced by way of honest reason. No corruption could have infected men so universally that nobody escaped the contagion. Which makes me suspect the bad taste was his own, and that he judged other men by himself."

In my Philippe de Commines there is this: "You will find the language here sweet and agreeable, of a natural simplicity; the narration pure, with the author's good faith showing plainly through it — free of vanity when he speaks of himself, of partiality and envy when he speaks of others; his reflections and exhortations carried more by zeal and truth than by any exquisite skill; and throughout, an authority and gravity that mark him as a man of good stock, brought up in great affairs."

And on the Memoirs of Monsieur du Bellay, this: "It is always a pleasure to read things written by men who have experienced how such things ought to be done; but it can't be denied that in these two lords there is a plain falling-off from the freedom and liberty of writing that shines in the older men of their kind — the Sire de Joinville, companion of Saint Louis; Eginhard, chancellor to Charlemagne; and, nearer our own day, Philippe de Commines. What we have here is less a history than an apology for King Francis against the Emperor Charles V. I won't believe they falsified anything as to the bare facts; but they make a regular practice of twisting the judgment of events, often against all reason, to our advantage, and of leaving out whatever is awkward in their master's life. Witness the affairs of Messieurs de Montmorency and de Biron, which are simply missing — and the very name of Madame d'Estampes is nowhere to be found. Secret doings a historian may conceal; but to pass over in silence what the whole world knows, and things that dragged such great public consequences after them, is an inexcusable defect. In short: whoever wants a complete knowledge of King Francis and the events of his reign should look for it elsewhere, if my advice carries any weight. The only profit to be had from these Memoirs is in the particular accounts of the battles and campaigns these gentlemen took part in personally; in certain words and private acts of the princes of their time; and in the treaties and negotiations handled by the Seigneur de Langey, where there are things worth knowing on every page, and reflections above the common run."
