Of the Education of Children
(Part 3 of 4)

But the tutor I have in mind — one who knows his real duty is to fill his pupil with affection for virtue, not just reverence toward it — will be able to explain that the poets have always played to the popular mood, and to make the boy see that the gods placed far more toil and sweat on the road to Venus's chamber than on the road to Minerva's. And when the lad's own feelings begin to stir, the tutor will set before him, as a mistress worth wanting, a Bradamante or an Angelica — a natural, active, generous beauty, vigorous rather than soft, the kind dressed like a heroic youth in a glittering helmet, instead of one tricked out in curls and ribbons like a flirt. He'll then count his own taste truly brave and manly when he chooses exactly the opposite of what that soft shepherd of Phrygia chose.

Such a tutor will teach him this new lesson: that the height and value of true virtue lie in the ease, usefulness, and pleasure of practicing it. Virtue is so far from being difficult that boys can master it as well as men, the simple as well as the clever. Her instrument is order, not force. Socrates, her first favorite, deliberately set aside all strain so as to slip into the natural ease of her path. She is the nursing mother of all human pleasures. By making them just, she makes them pure and lasting. By moderating them, she keeps them fresh and keeps our appetite alive. By cutting off the ones she refuses, she sharpens our taste for the ones she allows — and like a kind and generous mother, she allows in abundance everything nature asks for, right up to satisfaction if not exhaustion. Unless we want to claim that the rule which stops the drinker before he's drunk, the glutton before he's sick, and the lecher before he's caught the pox is somehow pleasure's enemy.

If ordinary good fortune fails her, virtue does without it and builds another fortune of her own — one that doesn't wobble and slip like the first. She can be rich, powerful, and learned, and she knows how to lie on soft, perfumed beds. She loves life; she loves beauty, glory, and health. But her own special office is to know how to use these good things with judgment, and how to lose them without despair — an office far more noble than burdensome, and without it the whole course of a life runs unnatural, turbulent, and deformed. That is where the monsters on the rocks and cliffs really belong: in the picture of a life without her.

If this pupil turns out to have so contrary a nature that he'd rather hear some idle yarn than the true account of a noble expedition or a wise conversation; if, when the drumbeat fires up his companions' young blood, he drifts off after another drummer calling him to a street dance or a bear-baiting; if he wouldn't honestly find it sweeter and finer to come back dusty, sweaty, and victorious from a battle than to come back from tennis or a dance with the prize — then I see no remedy except for his tutor to apprentice him early to a pastry cook in some good town, even if he's the son of a duke. This follows Plato's rule: that children should be placed in life not according to their father's wealth or rank, but according to the capacities of their own souls.

Since philosophy is the thing that teaches us how to live, and since childhood needs that lesson as much as every other age, why don't we give it to children early? The clay is moist and soft, as Persius says — now, right now, is the time to hurry and shape it on the fast-turning wheel. Instead, we begin teaching people how to live when life is nearly over. A hundred students have caught the pox before they ever get to Aristotle's lecture on temperance. Cicero said that even if he lived two men's lifetimes, he would never find leisure to study the lyric poets — and I find our quibbling academics more deplorably useless still. The boy we're raising has even less time to spare: he owes only the first fifteen or sixteen years of his life to education; the rest belongs to action. So let's spend that short time on what's necessary. Away with the thorny subtleties of logic-chopping — they're abuses, things that will never improve a single life. Take the plain discourses of philosophy instead. Learn first how to choose them rightly, then how to apply them. They're easier to understand than one of Boccaccio's stories. A child fresh from his nurse can take them in more readily than he can learn to read and write. Philosophy has lessons fit for childhood just as it has lessons fit for feeble old age.

I'm with Plutarch on this: Aristotle did not spend his time troubling his great pupil with the knack of building syllogisms or the elements of geometry, but with planting in him good principles about courage, boldness, greatness of spirit, temperance, and contempt for fear. With that ammunition, Aristotle sent him out — still a boy, with no more than thirty thousand foot soldiers, four thousand horsemen, and forty-two thousand crowns — to conquer the empire of the whole earth. As for the other arts and sciences, Plutarch says Alexander praised their excellence and charm, and held them in great honor, but was never so swept away as to want to practice them himself. Young men and old, as Persius says, draw from philosophy a settled aim for the mind, and provisions for miserable gray hairs.

Epicurus, at the start of his letter to Meniceus, says that the youngest should not refuse to philosophize, and the oldest should not grow weary of it. Whoever says otherwise seems to imply that the season for living happily either hasn't arrived yet or is already past. For all that, I would not have this pupil of ours imprisoned and made a slave to his book, nor handed over to the gloom and bad temper of some sour-natured pedant.

I would not have his spirit cowed and broken by putting him to the rack, as some do — tormenting him fourteen or fifteen hours a day and turning him into a pack-horse. Nor, if he turns out to have a solitary, melancholy temperament and an excessive appetite for his books, would I think it wise to feed that mood in him; it makes a boy unfit for ordinary human company and pulls him away from better occupations. How many men have I seen in my time made stupid by an immoderate thirst for knowledge! Carneades was so drunk on it that he couldn't find time to comb his hair or trim his nails. Nor would I have the boy's generous manners spoiled by the rudeness and barbarism of someone else's. There used to be a proverb about French intelligence: early to bloom, quick to fade. And it's true — nothing is more charming and promising than the children of France, but they routinely cheat the hopes people form of them, and grown to manhood they show nothing remarkable at all. I have heard sensible people say that it's these colleges of ours, the ones we send our young people to — and of which we have far too many — that turn them into such animals.

But for our young gentleman, a study, a garden, the table, his bed, solitude, company, morning and evening — every hour will be the same to him, and every place a classroom. For philosophy, the shaper of judgment and character, will be his principal lesson, and she has the privilege of having a hand in everything. The orator Isocrates, asked at a banquet to speak about his art, gave an answer the whole company rightly applauded: "This is no time for what I can do; and what it is time for, I cannot do." Delivering orations and staging debates in a company gathered to laugh and eat well would have been badly out of tune — and the same could be said of all the other sciences. But philosophy, at least the part of her that deals with man and his offices and duties, is different: all the wise have agreed that her conversation is so sweet she should be welcome at every game and every dinner party. When Plato invited her to his banquet, we see how gently and gracefully she entertained the company, fitting herself to the time and place even while speaking of the loftiest things. As Horace says, she profits poor and rich alike — and neglected, she hurts young and old alike.

By this method of teaching, my young pupil will be far better occupied than his schoolmates at the college. But just as the steps we take pacing in a gallery, though three times as many, tire us less than the same steps on a fixed journey, so our lesson — happening as if by accident, with no set time or place, woven into everything we do — will sink in without his even noticing. Even his games and exercises will become a solid part of his study: running, wrestling, music, dancing, hunting, riding, fencing. I want his outward bearing, his manners, and the carriage of his body trained at the same time as his mind. It is not a soul we are educating; it is not a body. It is a man, and we must not split him in two. As Plato says, we shouldn't fashion one without the other, but drive them together like two horses harnessed to the same coach. And listening to him, doesn't he actually seem to give more time and care to exercising the body, trusting that the mind gets her workout at the same time?

For the rest, this education should be conducted with a severe gentleness — the very opposite of our pedants' practice. Instead of coaxing children toward learning by apt and kindly ways, our teachers present them with nothing but rods and canes, horror and cruelty. Away with this violence! Away with this compulsion! Nothing, I'm convinced, so dulls and degrades a well-born nature. If you want your boy to dread shame and punishment, don't harden him to them. Harden him instead to heat and cold, to wind and sun, to the dangers he ought to hold cheap. Wean him from all softness and fussiness in his clothes and his bed, his food and his drink. Get him used to everything, so he doesn't grow into a pretty Sir Paris, a carpet-knight, but a sinewy, hardy, vigorous young man. I have been of this opinion from childhood to the age I've reached now, and I hold it still.

But among other things, the discipline of most of our colleges has always disgusted me. They might have done less damage by erring on the indulgent side. A college is a genuine jail for captive youth. The boys are made debauched by being punished for debauchery before they've committed any. Just drop in while lessons are in session: you'll hear nothing but the shrieks of boys under torture and the thundering of schoolmasters drunk with rage. What a charming way to give tender, timid souls a love of their book — a furious face and a rod in the hand! A cursed and ruinous way of proceeding. Besides, as Quintilian rightly observed, this tyrannical authority carries dangerous consequences with it — especially our style of whipping. How much more fitting it would be to see their classrooms strewn with green leaves and fresh flowers than with the bloody stumps of birch and willow! If it were left to me, I would paint the schoolhouse with pictures of Joy and Gladness, with Flora and the Graces, as the philosopher Speusippus did his. Where children's profit is, let their pleasure be there too. Food that's good for a child should be sweetened with sugar; food that's bad for him, soured with gall.

It is marvelous to see how much care Plato takes, in his Laws, over the gaiety and amusements of the young people of his city — how long and often he dwells on their races, sports, songs, leaps, and dances, whose ordering and patronage, he says, antiquity entrusted to the gods themselves: to Apollo, Minerva, and the Muses. He goes on at length with endless rules for physical exercise. About book-learning he says very little, and seems to recommend poetry in particular only for the music's sake.

Every oddity in our manners and habits should be avoided as unfit for living among other people. Who wouldn't be astonished by a constitution like that of Demophoon, steward to Alexander the Great, who sweated in the shade and shivered in the sun? I've seen men flee the smell of a ripe apple faster than they'd flee gunfire; others terrified of a mouse; others who vomit at the sight of cream, or nearly faint when a feather bed is being shaken out. Germanicus could endure neither the sight nor the crowing of a rooster. There may, I grant, be some hidden cause and natural aversion in such cases; but in my opinion a man could conquer it if he took it in hand early. Training has worked so well on me — though not without some effort, I admit — that except for beer, my appetite adapts itself to any food you like.

Young bodies are supple, so that is the age to bend and shape them to every fashion and custom. Provided a young man can keep his appetite and his will within proper bounds, then in God's name make him fit for all nations and all companies — even, if need be, for excess and disorder, where the customs of the place call for it. Let him be able to do everything, and love to do nothing but what is good. The philosophers themselves don't excuse Callisthenes for losing the favor of his master Alexander the Great by refusing to match him cup for cup. Let the lad laugh, play, and carouse with his prince. Even in revelry, I'd want him to outdo the rest of the company in stamina and skill — so that if he refrains from doing wrong, it's never for lack of strength or knowledge, but purely for lack of will. As Seneca says, there is a vast difference between not wanting to sin and not knowing how.

I once thought I was paying a compliment to a certain lord — a man as free of these excesses as any in France — by asking him, in front of very good company, how many times in his life he had gotten drunk in Germany while there on the King's business. He took it exactly as intended, answered "Three times," and told us the whole story of his sprees. I know men who, lacking this talent, have been at a serious disadvantage negotiating with that nation. And I have often marveled at the wonderful constitution of Alcibiades, who could shift himself into such different styles without any damage to his health — now outdoing the Persians in pomp and luxury, now the Spartans in austerity and thrift; as reformed in Sparta as he was self-indulgent in Ionia. As Horace says, every shade of life, every station and circumstance, suited Aristippus. That's the kind of man I would make of my pupil — one, as Horace also says, who wears his patched cloak with patience, and would wear a changed fortune just as well, playing either part with equal grace.

These are my lessons. The one who puts them into practice will profit more than the one who merely has them read to him and only knows them. If you see him, you hear him; if you hear him, you see him. God forbid, says someone in Plato, that philosophizing should mean nothing more than reading a great many books and learning the arts. As Cicero says, the masters of that fullest of all arts — the discipline of living well — pursued it with their lives rather than their reading.

Leo, prince of the Phliasians, asked Heraclides Ponticus what art or science he professed. "I know neither art nor science," he answered, "but I am a philosopher." Someone sneered at Diogenes that an ignorant man had no business dabbling in philosophy. "Then I dabble in it," he replied, "with all the more reason." And when Hegesias begged him to read a certain book aloud, Diogenes said: "You amuse me. You choose real, natural figs over painted ones — so why not choose exercises that are real and natural over written ones?"

Our boy will not so much recite his lesson as practice it. He will repeat it in his actions. We shall see whether there is prudence in his undertakings, whether there is sincerity and justice in his conduct, grace and judgment in his speech; whether there is constancy in his sickness, modesty in his fun, temperance in his pleasures, order in his household, and indifference at his table — whether what he eats and drinks is meat or fish, wine or water. He should be a man, as Cicero says, who treats his learning not as a display of knowledge but as the law of his life — who obeys himself and abides by his own rules.

The conduct of our lives is the true mirror of our doctrine. Zeuxidamus, asked why the Spartans did not write down their rules of valor and give them to their young men to read, answered that it was because they wanted to accustom them to deeds, not entertain them with words. Compare such a man, after fifteen or sixteen years of that schooling, with one of our college Latinists, who has thrown the same years away learning nothing but how to talk. The world is nothing but babble, and I have hardly ever met a man who didn't say too much rather than too little. Yet half our lives are embezzled this way: we're kept four or five years just learning words and stitching them into clauses; as many again shaping them into a long discourse, neatly divided into four or five parts; and another five years, at least, learning how to blend and interweave them in some subtle, intricate fashion. Let's leave all that to the people who make a profession of it.

Going to Orleans one day, I met, on the plain this side of Cléry, two pedants traveling toward Bordeaux, walking about fifty paces apart; and a good way behind them I spotted a troop of horsemen with a gentleman at their head — the late Comte de la Rochefoucauld. One of my servants asked the first of these masters of arts who the gentleman was who was coming along behind him. The pedant, who hadn't seen the riders and assumed his companion was meant, answered comically: "He is not a gentleman; he is a grammarian, and I am a logician." Now we, who are trying to breed exactly the opposite — not a grammarian or a logician but a gentleman — can leave these two to squander their leisure. Our business lies elsewhere.

Let our pupil only be well stocked with matter, and the words will follow all too readily; if they won't come on their own, he'll drag them after him. I've heard people excuse themselves on the grounds that they can't express what they mean — claiming their heads are full of fine things they simply can't deliver for lack of eloquence. That is a dodge, nothing more. Want to know what I think? Those fine things are shadows of half-formed notions they can't make sense of inside themselves, and so can't bring out. They don't yet understand what they're trying to say. Watch them hem and stammer at the moment of delivery and you'll see their struggle isn't with the birth at all — it's still back at the conception. They are only licking their shapeless embryo into form. For my part I hold — and Socrates insists on it — that whoever has a clear, living idea in his mind will get it out in one language or another, and if he's mute, in signs. As Horace says, once the thing is grasped, the words follow willingly. Another writer put it just as poetically in prose: when things take possession of the mind, says Seneca, the words come courting. And Cicero again: the things themselves seize the words to express them.

Our pupil may know nothing of ablatives, conjunctives, substantives, or grammar — but then, neither does his footman, or a fishwife on the Petit Pont, and they'll give you a bellyful of talk if you'll listen, and probably trip over their language as little as the finest masters of arts in France. He knows no rhetoric, nor how to butter up "the courteous reader" in a preface — and he doesn't care to. The truth is, all that pretty paintwork is wiped out in a moment by the shine of one plain, blunt truth. Those flourishes serve only to amuse the crowd, which can't digest more solid and nourishing food — as Aper demonstrates plainly in Tacitus.

The ambassadors of Samos came to Cleomenes, king of Sparta, armed with a long, elegant oration meant to stir him to war against the tyrant Polycrates. He heard their whole performance with great gravity and patience, and then gave them this answer: "As for your opening, I don't remember it — nor, therefore, the middle; and as for your conclusion, I will not do it." A very pretty answer, it seems to me, and a pack of learned orators left beached on the sand. And what about that other man? The Athenians were choosing between two architects for a great public building. The first, a slick, self-satisfied fellow, presented himself with a long rehearsed speech on the project and swayed the crowd in his favor. The other settled it in a few words: "Athenians, what this man says, I will do." When Cicero was at the full blaze of an eloquent harangue, many of the listeners were struck with admiration — but Cato only laughed and said, "We have an entertaining consul."

Coming before or coming after, a good maxim or a thing well said is always in season. If it doesn't quite fit what went before or what follows, it is still good in itself. I am not one of those who think good rhyme makes a good poem. Let the poet stretch a short syllable or clip a long one if he likes — what does it matter? If the invention is there, if wit and judgment have done their work well, I'll say: here is a good poet, but a bad rhymer.
