Of Cripples

It is now two or three years since they shortened the year by ten days in France. How many changes were supposed to follow that reform! It was moving heaven and earth at once. And yet nothing has budged from its place. My neighbors find their seasons for sowing and reaping, the right moments for their business, the harmful days and the lucky ones, exactly where they had always set them, time out of mind. There was no more error felt in our old usage than there is improvement felt in the new. That is how deep the uncertainty runs everywhere — how coarse, dim, and dull our perception is.

They say the correction could have been managed with less fuss by following the example of Augustus: skip the leap day for a few years — it is a day of nuisance and trouble anyway — until the debt was exactly paid off. (Which this reform did not even accomplish; we are still running a few days in arrears.) And by the same means we could provide for the future, ordering that after every so many years the extra day would be thrown out again, so that from then on our reckoning could never wander more than twenty-four hours off.

Years are the only accounting of time we have. The world has used them for ages, and yet it is a measure we still have not agreed on — we still wonder what shapes other nations gave it and what their true usage was. And what about the claim some make, that the heavens are growing old and sagging down closer toward us, throwing even our hours and days into uncertainty? Or what Plutarch says of the months — that in his time astrology still had not pinned down the motion of the moon? A fine condition we are in, to keep records of things past.

I was just now musing, as I often do, on what a free and roving thing human reason is. I see ordinarily that when something is set before men, they would rather hunt for reasons than find out the truth. They slide past the presuppositions but examine the consequences minutely. They leave the things themselves and run off after the causes. Comical reasoners! The knowledge of causes only concerns whoever runs the world, not us, who merely undergo it — and who get the full and perfect use of things, according to our need, without ever penetrating to their origin and essence. Wine is no more pleasant to the man who knows its first properties. On the contrary, both body and soul interrupt and weaken their right to the use of the world, and of themselves, by mixing in the pretension of learning. Effects concern us; the means, not at all. Determining and distributing belong to mastery and command; accepting belongs to subjection — and to us.

Let me scold a custom of ours. People commonly begin, "How does such a thing happen?" — when they ought to ask, "Does it happen at all?" Our reason is equipped to build a hundred other worlds and discover their beginnings and their architecture. It needs neither material nor foundation. Just let it run: it builds as readily on air as on earth, with emptiness as with substance — able, as the poet says, to give weight to smoke.

I find that in almost every case we ought to say, "There is no such thing." I would use that answer often myself, but I dare not: people cry that it is a dodge born of ignorance and a weak understanding. So most of the time I have to play along for company's sake, and chatter about frivolous subjects and tales I do not believe a word of. Besides, in truth, it is a little rude and quarrelsome to flatly deny a stated fact; and few people — especially with things hard to believe — will fail to insist they saw it themselves, or to name witnesses whose authority stops our mouths. By this method we come to know the foundations and causes of a thousand things that never happened, and the world scuffles over a thousand questions where both the pro and the con are false. The false stands so close to the true that a wise man should not trust himself near the edge.

Truth and lies wear the same face — the same bearing, the same taste, the same manners — and we look on both with the same eye. I find we are not merely slack in defending ourselves against deceit; we seek it out, we volunteer to be fooled. We love to tangle ourselves in vanity, since vanity suits the stuff we are made of.

I have watched the birth of many miracles in my time. Even when they died in the cradle, we can foresee well enough what they would have grown into, had they lived to full age. It is only a matter of finding the end of the thread; then a man can wind off as much as he pleases — and there is a greater distance between nothing and the smallest thing in the world than between the smallest thing and the greatest. Now the first people steeped in a budding marvel, when they go out to spread their tale, can tell from the resistance they meet exactly where the hard-to-believe part lies — and they caulk that seam with some false patch. Besides, men have a natural appetite for feeding rumors on purpose, so we naturally make it a point of conscience never to return what was lent us without some interest and addition of our own. First the private error creates the public error; then, in its turn, the public error creates the private one. And so this whole vast fabric goes on building and piling itself up from hand to hand, until the remotest witness knows more about the thing than the nearest one did, and the last man told is more thoroughly convinced than the first.

It is a natural progression. Whoever believes anything thinks it a work of charity to persuade somebody else of it; and to do that better, he never hesitates to add as much of his own invention as his story seems to need to overcome the resistance, or the dullness, he meets in others. I myself, who make a particular scruple of lying, and who hardly care whether what I say is believed or carries authority — even I notice that when I am heated, whether by someone's opposition or by the warmth of my own telling, I swell and puff up my subject with voice, gesture, vigor, and force of words, and with stretching and amplifying besides, not without some damage to the naked truth. But I do it on this condition: that to the first person who pulls me up short and asks for the plain bare fact, I instantly surrender my passion and hand the matter over without exaggeration, without emphasis, without any coloring of my own. A quick, emphatic way of talking, like mine, is prone to run into hyperbole.

There is nothing men are commonly more eager about than clearing a path for their own opinions; where the ordinary means fail us, we add command, force, fire, and sword. It is a misfortune to have come to the point where the best test of truth is the number of believers — in a crowd where the fools so vastly outnumber the wise. As if anything were so common as ignorance. As if a mob of madmen could vouch for sanity.

It is hard to stiffen your judgment against the common opinions. The first persuasion, drawn from the subject itself, seizes the simple; from them it spreads to the wise, under the authority of the number and the antiquity of the witnesses. For my part, what I would not believe from one man, I would not believe from a hundred and one. And I do not judge opinions by their age.

Not long ago, one of our princes — a man whose gout had spoiled a fine nature and a cheerful temper — let himself be so persuaded by reports of a certain priest who cured every kind of disease with words and gestures, that he made a long journey to find him; and by the sheer force of his imagination he talked his legs, for a few hours, into a service they had long forgotten. If fortune had piled five or six such accidents on top of one another, it would have been enough to install this miracle in nature. Afterward the author of these performances was found to be so simple, and so without art, that he was judged too contemptible to be worth punishing — as would happen with most such things, if they were examined at the source. We admire things that deceive us from a distance. In the same way our sight often shows us strange shapes far off that vanish as we approach. Rumor never survives being brought into focus.

It is astonishing from what idle beginnings and frivolous causes such famous impressions usually spring. That is exactly what blocks the inquiry: while we hunt for causes solid and weighty, worthy of so great a name, we lose the true ones. They escape our sight by being so small. And in truth, these investigations call for a very prudent, patient, and subtle examiner — impartial, with no theory already in his pocket. To this very hour, all these miracles and strange events have kept themselves hidden from me. I have seen no more evident monstrosity and miracle in the world than myself. Time and habit make us familiar with anything strange; but the more I frequent my own company and the better I know myself, the more my own deformity astonishes me, and the less I understand myself.

The chief right of producing and promoting such accidents is reserved to fortune. The day before yesterday, passing through a village two leagues from my house, I found the place still warm from a miracle that had just failed there. The neighborhood had been entertained by it for months; the neighboring provinces were beginning to catch the fever and run there in great troops of every sort of people. A young fellow of the place had one night, for fun, counterfeited the voice of a spirit in his own house — meaning nothing by it but the joke. It succeeded a little better than he expected; so, to extend the farce with more actors, he took in a dull, simple country girl, and in the end there were three of them, all of an age and an understanding. From household performances they advanced to public preaching, hiding under the altar of the church, speaking only at night, and forbidding anyone to bring a light. From words aimed at the conversion of the world and threats of the day of judgment (for these are subjects whose authority and reverence give imposture its safest cover), they moved on to visions and gesticulations so simple and ridiculous that hardly anything in the games of small children is so crude. Yet if fortune had favored the scheme ever so little, who knows how far the act might have gone? The poor devils are in prison now, and will likely pay shortly for the common folly — and I do not know whether some judge won't make them smart for his folly, too. We see clearly into this case, because it has been uncovered; but in many things of the same kind that pass our knowledge, my opinion is that we should suspend judgment — neither rejecting nor accepting.

Great abuses in the world — or, to put it more boldly, all the abuses in the world — are born of our being taught to be afraid of professing our ignorance, and being told we are bound to accept whatever we cannot refute. We talk about everything by precept and pronouncement. The style at Rome was that even what a witness swore he had seen with his own eyes, and even what a judge determined out of his most certain knowledge, was couched in this formula: "It seems to me." They make me hate likely things when people shove them at me as infallible. I love the words that soften and moderate the rashness of our claims: perhaps; in some sort; some; it is said; I think — and the like. And if I had been set to raise children, I would have put this way of answering into their mouths — inquiring, not settling: "What does this mean? I do not understand it. It may be. Is it true?" — so that they would rather have kept the manner of pupils at sixty than gone out as doctors, the way they do, at ten. Whoever wants to be cured of ignorance must confess it.

Iris is the daughter of Thaumas — the rainbow is Wonder's child. Wonder is the foundation of all philosophy; inquiry its progress; ignorance its end. But there is a kind of ignorance, strong and generous, that yields nothing in honor and courage to knowledge — an ignorance that takes no less knowledge to achieve than knowledge itself.

In my younger years I read a trial that Coras, a judge at Toulouse, put into print — the strange case of two men who each presented himself as the other. I remember (and I hardly remember anything else) that Coras made the imposture of the man he judged guilty seem so wonderful, so far beyond our knowledge and beyond his own — and he was the judge — that I thought it a verdict so bold it hanged a man on a guess. Let us accept some form of decree that says, "The court understands nothing of this matter" — more frankly and openly than the judges of the Areopagus, who, finding themselves cornered by a case they could not untangle, ordered the parties to come back in a hundred years.

The witches of my neighborhood are running for their lives every time some new author comes along to give body to their dreams. To fit the examples that Holy Writ offers us of such things — most certain and irrefutable examples — onto our modern events, when we can see neither their causes nor their means, takes a different sort of mind than ours. Perhaps it belongs to that single all-powerful testimony alone to tell us, "This one is, and that one is, and not that other." God ought to be believed in this — that is plainly reasonable. But not, for all that, one of us: a man astonished at his own story (and he must necessarily be astonished, unless he is out of his mind), whether he tells it about somebody else or against himself.

I am plain and heavy, and I hold to the solid and the probable, steering clear of the old reproaches: men give the most faith to what they least understand, and by a craving of the human mind, obscure things are the easiest to believe. I see well enough that people get angry, and that I am forbidden to doubt on pain of unspeakable insults. A new way of persuading! Thank God, I am not to be punched into belief. Let them be angry with those who accuse their opinion of being false; I only accuse it of being difficult and bold, and I condemn the opposite affirmation just as they do — only less imperiously. Whoever establishes his proposition by swagger and command shows that his reasoning is weak. In a verbal, scholastic wrangle, let them appear as plausible as their opponents — let them state probabilities, but not affirm. But in the real consequence drawn from the argument, the doubters have much the better of it. To kill men, you need a light that is clear and strong; and our life is too real and too essential to be staked on these supernatural and fantastic accidents.

As for drugs and poisons, I throw those out of my reckoning: that is homicide of the worst sort. Yet even there, they say, you cannot always rely on the personal confessions of these people, for they have sometimes been known to accuse themselves of murdering persons who later turned up alive and well. In these other extravagant accusations, I am inclined to say this: any man, whatever his reputation, deserves to be believed about human things; but about what is beyond his conception and of supernatural effect, he should be believed only when some supernatural approval has vouched for him. The privilege God has been pleased to grant to certain of our testimonies must not be cheapened and handed around lightly. My ears are battered with a thousand stories like this: "Three people saw him on such a day in the east; three more saw him the next day in the west; at such an hour, in such a place, dressed in such a way." Truly, I would not believe my own self on that evidence. How much more natural and likely do I find it that two men should lie than that one man, in twelve hours, should fly with the wind from east to west! How much more natural that our understanding should be blown out of its place by the whirl of our own unhinged minds than that one of us, flesh and bone as we are, should be carried up a chimney flue on a broomstick by a strange spirit! Let us not go hunting for illusions outside and unknown, we who are perpetually shaken by illusions homegrown and our own. It seems to me a man is pardonable for disbelieving a miracle wherever its proof can be sidestepped by some path that is not miraculous. And I am of St. Augustine's opinion, that it is better to lean toward doubt than toward assurance in things hard to prove and dangerous to believe.

Some years ago I traveled through the territory of a sovereign prince who, as a favor to me, and to beat down my skepticism, did me the honor of letting me see, in his own presence and in a private place, ten or twelve prisoners of this kind — among them an old woman, a genuine witch in ugliness and deformity, long famous in the profession. I saw evidence and free confessions, and some insensible mark or other on the miserable creature. I examined her and talked with her and the rest as much and as long as I pleased, and brought the best and soundest attention I could — and I am not a man who lets prepossession take his judgment captive. In the end, and in all conscience, I would have prescribed them hellebore rather than hemlock — medicine for madness rather than poison. The whole business looked more like minds possessed than minds guilty. Justice has its own corrections for such maladies.

As for the objections and arguments that worthy men have made to me, there and often elsewhere, I have met none that convinced me — none that did not admit a more likely solution than their conclusion. It is true that proofs and reasons founded on experience and fact I do not try to untie; they have no loose end to take hold of. I often cut them, as Alexander cut his knot. After all, it is putting a very high price on our conjectures to roast a man alive on their account.

We are told by various examples — as Praestantius tells of his father — that, sunk in a sleep far deeper than men usually sleep, he dreamed he was a mare serving the soldiers as a pack animal; and what he dreamed himself to be, he actually proved to be. Even if sorcerers dream this materially — even if dreams can sometimes take flesh in fact — still I cannot believe that our will should answer for it to justice. I say this as one who is neither judge nor royal councillor, who thinks himself many degrees unworthy to be either — an ordinary man, born and sworn to the obedience of public reason in both word and deed. Anyone who took my idle talk as an injury to the pettiest law, opinion, or custom of his parish would do himself a great wrong, and me a greater one. In what I say, I warrant no certainty except that it is what I had in my thought at the time — a tumultuous and wavering thought. Everything I say is by way of conversation, nothing by way of advice. And I am not ashamed, as those others are, to confess I do not know what I do not know.

I would not speak so boldly if it were my due to be believed — and that is what I told a great man who complained of the sharpness and contentiousness of my exhortations. Seeing you set and ready on one side, I lay out the other for you with all the diligence and care I can — to clear your judgment, not to compel it. God holds your hearts in His hands and will furnish you the means of choosing. I am not so presumptuous as to wish that my opinions should tip you one way or the other in a matter of such importance; my fortune has not raised them to conclusions so potent and lofty. Truly, I have not only a great many moods but a great many opinions that I would try to teach my son to dislike, if I had a son. What if the truest opinions are not always the most convenient for man, wild as his composition is?

Whether this is to the purpose or not, no great matter: it is a common proverb in Italy that a man does not know Venus in her perfect sweetness who has never slept with a lame mistress. Fortune, or some particular incident, put that saying into people's mouths long ago; and it is said of men as well as of women — for the queen of the Amazons answered the Scythian who courted her, "The lame man does it best." In that republic of women, to escape the dominion of the males, they crippled them in infancy — arms, legs, and whatever other parts gave men an advantage over them — and used them only for the one purpose for which we, in our part of the world, use women.

I would have guessed it was the lame woman's rolling gait that added some new pleasure to the business and some extra spice for those who tried it. But I have lately learned that ancient philosophy itself has ruled on the question. It says that since the legs and thighs of lame women, because of their imperfection, do not receive their proper nourishment, the genital parts above come out fuller, better fed, and more vigorous. Or else that, since the defect prevents exercise, those who have it spend less of their strength and come more entire to the games of Venus — which is also why the Greeks called the weaving-women hotter than other women: a sedentary trade, carried on without much exercise of the body. At this rate, what can we not reason about? Of these weavers I might just as well say that the jiggling of their work as they sit stirs and provokes their desire, the way the swaying and jolting of coaches does for our ladies.

Do not these examples prove what I said at the start — that our reasons often run ahead of the facts, and hold so boundless a jurisdiction that they judge and exercise themselves on emptiness itself, on things that do not exist? Besides the suppleness of our invention in forging reasons for every sort of dream, our imagination is just as ready to take false impressions from the most frivolous appearances. On the sole authority of the ancient and common use of that proverb, I once made myself believe that I had gotten more pleasure from a woman because she was not straight, and counted that crookedness among her charms.

Torquato Tasso, in the comparison he draws between France and Italy, says he has observed that our legs are generally thinner than those of the Italian gentlemen, and credits it to our being constantly on horseback — the very same cause from which Suetonius draws the exactly opposite conclusion, for he says Germanicus thickened his legs by that same continual exercise.

Nothing is so supple and erratic as our understanding. It is the shoe of Theramenes — it fits every foot. It is double and diverse, and the matters it works on are double and diverse too. "Give me a drachma of silver," a Cynic philosopher said to Antigonus. "That is not a present fit for a king," he replied. "Then give me a talent." "That is not a present fit for a Cynic." Heat works on the soil, the poet says, either by opening hidden passages so the sap can rise into the new shoots, or else by hardening and binding the gaping veins against thin rains, the fierce sun, and the piercing cold of the north wind — one cause, argued both ways. Every medal has its reverse, as the Italians say.

This is why Clitomachus said, long ago, that Carneades had outdone the labors of Hercules: he had torn assent out of men — that is, opinion, and the courage of judging. That vigorous fancy of Carneades sprang, I believe, from the impudence of those who professed knowledge, and from their immeasurable self-conceit. Aesop was put up for sale with two other slaves. The buyer asked the first what he could do, and he, to raise his own price, promised mountains and marvels: he could do this and that and I don't know what all. The second said as much of himself, or more. When it came to Aesop's turn and he was asked what he could do — "Nothing," he said. "These two have gotten there ahead of me. They know everything." So it has happened in the school of philosophy. The pride of those who credited the human mind with a capacity for all things bred in others, out of spite and rivalry, the opinion that it is capable of nothing at all. The one party holds the same extreme in ignorance that the other holds in knowledge — making it undeniably plain that man is immoderate in everything, and never stops anywhere except from necessity, and from sheer inability to go further.
