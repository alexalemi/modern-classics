A Custom of the Island of Cea

If to philosophize is, as they define it, to doubt, then surely to write at random and play the fool, as I do, must count as doubting too. It is for novices and freshmen to inquire and dispute, and for the chairman to moderate and decide. My chairman is the authority of the divine will, which governs us without contradiction and sits high above these vain human quarrels.

When Philip forced his way into the Peloponnese, someone told Damidas that the Spartans would suffer badly if they did not hurry to make peace with him. "You poor fellow," Damidas replied, "what can people suffer who do not fear death?" Agis was asked how a man might live free. "By despising death," he said. These sayings, and a thousand others like them, plainly mean something more than just waiting patiently for death to arrive on its own. There are things in life far worse to endure than death itself.

Take the Spartan boy captured by Antigonus and sold as a slave. When his master ordered him to some degrading task, the boy said, "You shall see what you have bought. It would be shameful for me to serve when freedom is so close at hand" — and threw himself from the top of the house. When Antipater threatened the Spartans harshly to force them to accept one of his demands, they answered, "If you threaten us with worse than death, we shall die all the more willingly." And when Philip wrote that he would block all their undertakings: "What — will you also keep us from dying?"

This is the meaning of the old saying: the wise man lives as long as he ought, not as long as he can. The kindest gift nature has given us — the one that strips away every excuse for complaining about our condition — is that she has left the keys of life in our own keeping. She has arranged only one door into life, but a hundred thousand ways out. We may run short of land to live on, but land enough to die on can never be lacking, as Boiocalus answered the Romans. Why do you complain of this world? It does not hold you. If you live in pain, your own cowardice is the cause. To die, nothing is needed but the will to die. As Seneca says, "Death is everywhere — heaven has provided well for that. Anyone can take a man's life; no one can take his death. A thousand roads lead to it."

Nor is death a remedy for one disease only; it is the infallible cure of them all. It is a sure harbor, never to be feared and often to be sought. It comes to the same thing whether a man gives himself his end or waits to receive it, whether he pays before his day or waits until payment falls due. Wherever it comes from, it is still his own. Wherever the thread breaks, there is the end of the spool. The most voluntary death is the finest. Life depends on the will of others; death depends on our own. In nothing should we suit our own inclination so much as in this. Reputation has no stake in such a venture, and it is folly to be swayed by any worry about it. Living is slavery if the liberty of dying is missing.

The ordinary course of medicine is carried out at the expense of life. They torment us with burnings, incisions, and amputations of limbs; they forbid us food and drain our blood. One step further and we are cured indeed, and for good. Why is the jugular vein not as much at our disposal as the median vein? For a desperate disease, a desperate cure. Servius the grammarian, tortured by gout, could think of no better remedy than to apply poison to his legs and kill their feeling. Let them be as gouty as they pleased, so long as they could feel no pain.

God gives us leave enough to go when He reduces us to a state where living is far worse than dying. It is weakness to buckle under infirmities, but it is madness to nurse them. The Stoics say it is living according to nature for a wise man to take his leave of life even at the height of prosperity, if he does it at the right moment; and for a fool to cling to life even in misery, provided he still has most of the things they count as natural goods. Just as I do not break the law against thieves when I pocket my own money and cut my own purse, nor the law against arsonists when I burn my own wood, so I am not subject to the laws against murderers for having taken my own life.

Hegesias said that as the conditions of life depend on our choice, so should the conditions of death. And when Diogenes met the philosopher Speusippus, so swollen with chronic dropsy that he had to be carried in a litter, and Speusippus called out, "I wish you good health" — "No health to you," Diogenes answered, "who are content to live in such a condition." And in fact, not long after, Speusippus, weary of so lingering a state, found a means to die.

But this does not pass without dispute. Many hold that we cannot quit this garrison of the world without the express command of Him who posted us in it; that it belongs to God — who placed us here not for ourselves alone, but for His glory and the service of others — to dismiss us when it pleases Him, and not for us to leave without His permission. We are born not for ourselves only but for our country too, and the laws have a claim on us in their own interest, with a charge of manslaughter ready against us. And if those courts fail to take up the case, we are punished in the next world as deserters of our duty. As Virgil writes, the next sad region of the underworld holds those who, though guiltless, made their own deaths with their own hands, and hating the light, threw their lives away.

There is more constancy in wearing the chain we are bound to than in breaking it, and stronger proof of firmness in Regulus than in Cato. It is rashness and impatience that drive us over these cliffs. No misfortune can make true virtue turn her back; she seeks out hardship, pain, and grief as the very food that nourishes her. The threats of tyrants, the racks and the tortures, only stir her up and give her life — like the oak on Mount Algidus in Horace's lines, which draws fresh vigor from the very axe that lops it. And as Seneca says, "Father, it is no virtue to fear life; virtue is to stand against great misfortunes and neither flinch nor retreat." Or as Martial puts it, "In adversity it is easy to despise death; the braver man is the one who can endure to live wretched."

It is cowardice, not virtue, to crouch in a furrow under a tombstone to dodge the blows of fortune. Virtue never halts or leaves her road, however hard the storm blows. As Horace says, "If the world cracked and fell, the ruins would strike a fearless head." Most of the time, fleeing other troubles drives us straight into this one; indeed, in trying to escape death we often run right into its mouth. "Tell me," asks Martial, "is it not madness to die for fear of dying?" — like those who, terrified of a precipice, throw themselves over it. As Lucan says, the very fear of coming evil has pushed many into extreme danger; the bravest man is the one who can face what he dreads when it confronts him, and can also wait. And Lucretius observes that the dread of death so seizes some men that it makes them hate both life and daylight, and they kill themselves in their misery — forgetting that this very fear is the fountain of all their cares.

Plato, in his Laws, assigns a disgraceful burial to the man who has robbed his nearest and dearest friend — himself — of life and of his appointed course, when not compelled by public judgment, by some grievous and inescapable stroke of fortune, or by unbearable disgrace, but driven merely by cowardice and the weakness of a timid soul. And the opinion that holds life so cheap is absurd, for life is our being; it is all we have. Beings of a nobler and higher nature may look down on ours, but it is against nature for us to despise ourselves and hold ourselves of no account. It is a disease peculiar to man, found in no other creature, to hate and scorn his own existence. And it is a vanity of the same stamp to wish to be something other than what we are. Such a desire can never touch us, since it contradicts and cancels itself. The man who wishes to be turned from a man into an angel does nothing for himself; he would gain nothing by it — for once he no longer exists, who is left to feel the improvement and rejoice in it for him? As Lucretius says, he for whom misery and pain lie in the future must himself exist at the time the ills befall him.

The security, the rest, the freedom from feeling, the escape from the evils of this life that we think we are buying at the price of death — none of it profits us at all. The man who cannot enjoy peace gains nothing by escaping war, and the man who cannot taste rest gains just as little by avoiding trouble.

Among those who hold the first of these two opinions, there has been a great debate about what occasions are sufficient to justify the contemplation of self-slaughter — what they call "a reasonable exit." For though they say men must often die for slight causes, since the things that keep us in life are themselves of no great weight, still there has to be some limit. There are wild and senseless moods that have driven not only single men but whole nations to destroy themselves; I have given examples elsewhere. We read of the virgins of Miletus, who by a frantic compact hanged themselves one after another until the magistrate put a stop to it, decreeing that any woman found hanged should be dragged by the same rope, stark naked, through the city.

When Therykion urged Cleomenes to kill himself because of the wretched state of his affairs — having missed the more honorable death in the battle he had just lost, he should accept this one, second in honor, and not give the conquerors the chance to inflict on him either a shameful death or a shameful life — Cleomenes, with a courage truly Spartan and Stoic, rejected the advice as weak and unmanly. "That remedy," he said, "can never run out, but a man must never use it while an inch of hope remains." It was sometimes constancy and valor to live, he said; he wanted even his death to be of service to his country, and meant to make it an act of honor and virtue. Therykion still thought himself right, and did his own business; Cleomenes did the same afterward, but not until he had first tried fortune's utmost malice.

All the troubles in the world are not weighty enough that a man should die to escape them. Besides, there are so many sudden and unexpected turns in human affairs that it is hard to judge rightly when we have truly reached the end of our hope. As the poet says, even the gladiator beaten in the cruel arena keeps hoping, though the menacing crowd turns its thumb. All things, says the old adage, may be hoped for by a man while he lives. Yes, replies Seneca — but why should that thought always run in a man's head, that fortune can do anything for the living, rather than this one: that fortune has no power over the man who knows how to die?

Josephus found himself in danger so close and so plain — a whole people violently set against him, with no visible means of escape — that even when a friend counseled him in that extremity to kill himself, it served him well to hold on to hope; for fortune turned the event beyond all human expectation, and he came out of it without harm. Whereas Brutus and Cassius, by the haste and rashness with which they killed themselves before the proper time and a just occasion, threw away the last remains of Roman liberty, of which they were the sole protectors. Monsieur d'Enghien, at the battle of Cerisoles, twice tried to run himself through, despairing of the day's fortune — which was going very badly on his side of the field — and by that haste came very near cheating himself of a magnificent victory. I have seen a hundred hares escape from the very teeth of the greyhounds. As Seneca says, some men have outlived their executioners. And as Virgil writes, length of days and the shifting labor of changeful time bring things to a better state; fortune, turning, shows another face, and sets men back on solid ground.

Pliny says there are only three kinds of disease that give a man good title to destroy himself, and the worst of them is a stone in the bladder when the urine is blocked. Seneca says only those diseases that unsettle the workings of the soul over a long time.

And some, to avoid a worse death, have chosen one to their own liking. Democritus, general of the Aetolians, was brought a prisoner to Rome and found means to escape by night; but with his keepers close behind him, rather than be retaken he fell on his own sword and died. Antinous and Theodotus, when the Romans had reduced their city in Epirus to the last extremity, counseled the people to kill themselves together; but the people preferred to surrender, so the two leaders went to find the death they wanted, charging furiously into the enemy, intent on striking and not on parrying.

When the island of Gozo was taken by the Turks some years ago, a Sicilian there, who had two beautiful daughters of marriageable age, killed them both with his own hand, and their mother too, who came running in to save them. That done, he went out into the street with a crossbow and a harquebus, killed with two shots the two Turks nearest his door, then drew his sword and charged in among the rest, where he was instantly surrounded and cut to pieces — delivering his family and himself from slavery and dishonor.

The Jewish women, after circumcising their children, threw them and themselves off a precipice to escape the cruelty of Antigonus. I have been told of a man of rank in one of our prisons whose friends, learning that he would surely be condemned, bribed a priest to tell him — to spare him the disgrace of such a death — that his only means of deliverance was to commend himself to a certain saint, under certain vows, and to fast eight days together without taking any nourishment, whatever weakness or faintness he might feel. He followed the advice, and so destroyed himself without knowing it, never dreaming of death or of any danger in the experiment. Scribonia, advising her nephew Libo to kill himself rather than wait for the stroke of justice, told him that preserving his life only to hand it over within three or four days to the men who would come fetch him to execution was doing other people's business, and that keeping his blood for his enemies to spill was simply serving their malice.

We read in the Bible that when Nicanor, the persecutor of God's law, sent his soldiers to seize the good old man Razis — surnamed, in honor of his virtue, the father of the Jews — the old man, seeing no other remedy, his gates burned down and the enemy ready to lay hands on him, chose to die nobly rather than fall into the power of his wicked adversaries and be butchered by them against the honor of his rank. He stabbed himself with his own sword; but the blow, struck in haste, did not go home, so he ran and threw himself headlong from the top of a wall into the crowd below, which parted and made room, so that he landed squarely on his head. Feeling some life still in him even then, he summoned his courage again, got to his feet all bloody and broken, forced his way through the crowd to a steep rock, and there, through one of his wounds, drew out his own bowels and, tearing them apart with both hands, flung them among his pursuers — calling down divine vengeance on them all the while for their cruelty and injustice.

Of all violences done to the conscience, the one done against a woman's chastity is, in my opinion, the most to be avoided, since a certain pleasure is naturally mixed in it, and for that reason the refusal can never be entirely perfect and whole; the violence seems mingled with some small consent of the party forced. Church history holds several examples of devout women who embraced death to protect themselves from the outrages tyrants prepared against their religion and their honor. Pelagia and Sophronia were both canonized: the first threw herself into a river with her mother and sisters to escape being forced by soldiers, and the second killed herself to avoid being violated by the emperor Maxentius.

It may perhaps stand to our honor in future ages that a learned author of our own day — a Parisian — takes great pains to persuade the ladies of our time to take any course at all rather than enter into the horrible contemplation of such a despair. I am sorry he never heard, to add to his other stories, the saying of a woman at Toulouse who had passed through the hands of some soldiers: "God be praised," she said, "that for once in my life I have had my fill without sin." In truth, these cruelties are quite unworthy of the good French temper, and, God be thanked, our air has been well purged of them since that good advice. It is enough that they say "no" while doing it, following the rule of good Marot — "a sweet no, with a sweet smile, is so very gracious."

History is full everywhere of those who, by a thousand different roads, have traded a painful and weary life for death. Lucius Aruntius killed himself, he said, to flee both the future and the past. Granius Silvanus and Statius Proximus killed themselves after being pardoned by Nero — either scorning to live by the favor of so wicked a man, or unwilling to be put to the trouble of obtaining a second pardon someday, knowing how prone he was to suspect worthy men and believe accusations against them. Spargapises, son of Queen Tomyris and a prisoner of war to Cyrus, used the first kindness Cyrus showed him — ordering him unbound — to kill himself, having wanted no other benefit from liberty than to take revenge on himself for the disgrace of his capture.

Boges, governor of Eion for King Xerxes, besieged by the Athenian army under Cimon, refused the terms offered him — safe return to Asia with all his wealth — unable to bear outliving the loss of a place his master had given him to keep. He defended the city to the last extremity; then, when nothing was left to eat, he threw all the gold and everything else the enemy might take as plunder into the river Strymon, ordered a great pyre lit and the throats of all the women, children, concubines, and servants cut, threw their bodies into the fire, and at last leaped into it himself.

Ninachetuen, a lord of the Indies, on hearing the first whisper of the Portuguese viceroy's plan to strip him, for no stated cause, of his command at Malacca and transfer it to the king of Campar, made this resolution by himself. He had a platform built, longer than it was wide, raised on columns, royally hung with tapestries and strewn with flowers and quantities of perfume. Then, in a robe of cloth of gold thick with jewels of great value, he came out into the street and mounted the steps; at one corner burned a pyre of aromatic wood. Everyone ran to see what these strange preparations meant. Ninachetuen, with a bold but indignant face, declared how much the Portuguese nation owed him, and with what spotless fidelity he had carried out his charge; that having so often shown, sword in hand, on behalf of others, that honor was dearer to him than life, he was not going to abandon that concern in his own case; that since fortune denied him every means of opposing the injury planned against him, his courage at least commanded him to free himself from the feeling of it, and not to serve as a fable for the people or a triumph for men less deserving than himself. Having said this, he leaped into the fire.

Sextilia, wife of Scaurus, and Paxaea, wife of Labeo, to hearten their husbands to escape the dangers closing in on them — dangers in which they had no share but their married love — voluntarily gave up their own lives, to serve them in that final necessity as companion and example. What they did for their husbands, Cocceius Nerva did for his country, with less use though equal devotion: this great jurist, flourishing in health, wealth, reputation, and the emperor's favor, had no other reason to kill himself than pure grief at the miserable state of the Roman republic.

Nothing can be added to the beauty of the death of the wife of Fulvius, a close favorite of Augustus. Augustus, having discovered that Fulvius had leaked an important secret entrusted to him, received him coldly one morning when he came to pay his respects, and frowned on him. Fulvius went home in despair and sorrowfully told his wife that, having fallen into this misfortune, he was resolved to kill himself. "It is only right that you should," she answered flatly, "since you have had so much experience of my loose tongue and never took warning from it. But let me kill myself first." And without another word she ran herself through with a sword.

Vibius Virrius, despairing of the safety of his besieged city and of Roman mercy, told the senate of Capua in its last deliberation, after many arguments to that end, that the noblest way to escape fortune was by their own hands. The enemy would hold them in honor, he said, and Hannibal would feel how many faithful friends he had abandoned. He invited those who approved his advice to a good supper waiting at his house, where after they had eaten well they would drink together of what he had prepared — "a drink," he said, "that will deliver our bodies from torture, our souls from insult, and our eyes and ears from the sight and sound of all the hateful evils the conquered suffer at the hands of cruel and implacable conquerors." He had arranged, he said, for fit persons to throw their bodies onto a funeral pyre before his door as soon as they were dead. Many approved this high resolution; few imitated it. Twenty-seven senators followed him, and after trying to drown the thought of that fatal decision in wine, they ended the feast with the deadly dish. Embracing one another, after they had jointly mourned the misfortune of their country, some went home to their own houses, others stayed to be burned with Vibius on his pyre. And they were all so long in dying — the wine filling their veins and delaying the poison's work — that some of them came within an hour of seeing the enemy inside the walls of Capua, which was taken the next morning, and of suffering the very miseries they had paid so dearly to avoid.

Jubellius Taurea, another citizen of the same country, when the consul Fulvius was returning from the shameful slaughter he had made of two hundred and twenty-five senators, called him back fiercely by name, and when he had made him stop, said, "Give the order for someone to dispatch me too, after the massacre of so many others, so you can boast of having killed a far braver man than yourself." Fulvius dismissed him as a madman — and besides, letters had just arrived from Rome condemning the inhumanity of his executions, which tied his hands. So Jubellius went on: "Since my country is taken, my friends are dead, and I have slain my wife and children with my own hands to save them from the desolation of this ruin — since I am denied the death of my fellow citizens — let me borrow from virtue a vengeance on this hateful life." And drawing a short sword he had hidden on him, he ran it through his own breast and fell backward, dying at the consul's feet.

When Alexander was besieging a city in the Indies, the people inside, finding themselves hard pressed, resolved stoutly to deprive him of the pleasure of his victory, and burned themselves all together along with their city, in spite of his humanity. A new kind of war: the enemy fighting to save them, they fighting to destroy themselves, doing everything to make sure of death that men ordinarily do to make sure of life.

Astapa, a city in Spain, finding its walls and defenses too weak to withstand the Romans, the inhabitants piled all their riches and furniture in the public square, placed all the women and children on top of the heap, stacked wood and kindling around it to catch fire quickly, and left fifty of their young men to carry out what they had resolved. Then they made a desperate sally, and lacking the power to win, got themselves killed to the last man. The fifty, after slaughtering every living soul left in the city and setting fire to the pile, threw themselves into it last of all — ending their noble liberty in a manner without feeling rather than in sorrow and disgrace, and showing the enemy that if fortune had allowed it, they would have had the same courage to snatch victory away as they had to make it dreadful, even fatal: for many of the enemy, lured by the gold melting in those flames, pressed close and were suffocated and burned, unable to retreat through the crowd pushing behind them.

The people of Abydos, pressed by King Philip, took the same resolution; but lacking time, they could not carry it out. The king, horrified by their reckless haste — the treasure and goods they had condemned to the flames having first been seized — drew off his soldiers and granted them three days to kill themselves in, so that they might do it with more order and at greater ease. They filled those three days with blood and slaughter beyond the worst excess of any enemy's cruelty, so that not a single soul was left alive that had the power to destroy itself. There are countless examples of such mass resolutions; they seem the more fierce and cruel as their effect is more universal, yet they are really less so than when carried out singly. What argument and persuasion cannot do with men one by one, they can do with all together — the fever of the group sweeping away individual judgment.

In the reign of Tiberius, the condemned who waited to be executed forfeited their goods and were denied burial rites; those who anticipated the sentence by killing themselves were buried and had liberty to dispose of their estates by will.

But men sometimes covet death in hope of a greater good. "I desire to be with Christ," says St. Paul; and, "who shall deliver me from these bonds?" Cleombrotus of Ambracia, having read Plato's Phaedo, conceived so great a longing for the life to come that, with no other occasion at all, he threw himself into the sea. Which shows how wrongly we call this voluntary dissolution "despair," when it is often the eagerness of hope that inclines us to it — and often a calm and temperate desire, proceeding from a mature and deliberate judgment. Jacques du Chastel, bishop of Soissons, on St. Louis's expedition overseas, seeing the king and the whole army about to return to France leaving the affairs of religion unfinished, resolved to go to paradise instead. Having taken solemn leave of his friends, he charged alone, in full view of everyone, into the enemy's army, where he was promptly cut to pieces.

In a certain kingdom of the newly discovered world, on a day of solemn procession when the idol they worship is drawn through public streets on a chariot of marvelous size, many are seen cutting off pieces of their own flesh to offer it; and a number of others prostrate themselves in its path, letting themselves be crushed and broken under the heavy wheels, to earn a veneration for sanctity after death — which is duly paid them. The bishop's death, sword in hand, has more magnanimity in it and less feeling, the heat of combat absorbing part of the pain.

Some governments have taken it on themselves to regulate the justice and the proper occasions of voluntary death. In former times our city of Marseilles kept a poison prepared from hemlock, at public expense, for anyone who wished to hasten his end — provided he first gave the Six Hundred, their senate, an account of the reasons and motives for his design. It was not lawful to do violence to oneself otherwise than by leave of the magistrates and upon just occasion. The same law was in use elsewhere.

Sextus Pompeius, on his expedition into Asia, stopped at the island of Cea, near Negropont. While he was there — we have it from someone who was with him — a woman of great rank, having explained to her fellow citizens why she was resolved to end her life, invited Pompeius to her death, to make it the more honorable. He accepted; and having long tried in vain, with all the power of his eloquence, which was very great, to turn her from her design, he yielded at last to her will.

She had passed her ninetieth year in a very happy state of both body and mind. Lying now on her bed, better dressed than usual and propped on her elbow, she said: "May the gods, O Sextus Pompeius — and rather those I leave than those I go to seek — reward you for not disdaining to be both the counselor of my life and the witness of my death. For my part, having always felt the kindly face of fortune, I am going, for fear that the desire to live too long may show me the other one, to dismiss by a happy end the remains of my soul, leaving behind two daughters of my body and a legion of grandchildren." Then, after urging her family to live in peace, she divided her property among them, commended her household gods to her eldest daughter, took up the bowl containing the poison with a steady hand, and having made her vows and prayers to Mercury to conduct her to some happy seat in the other world, briskly swallowed the fatal draught. After that, she kept the company informed of its progress — how the cold took hold of the parts of her body one after another — until at last, telling them it had reached her heart and bowels, she called her daughters to do the final office and close her eyes.

Pliny tells of a certain Hyperborean nation where, thanks to the sweet mildness of the air, lives rarely ended except by the voluntary surrender of the inhabitants: weary of living and sated with it, they had the custom, at a very great age, of making good cheer and then throwing themselves into the sea from the top of a certain rock set aside for that service.

Pain, and the fear of a worse death, seem to me the most excusable incitements.
