Of Cannibals

When King Pyrrhus invaded Italy and got his first good look at the army the Romans had sent against him, he said: "I don't know what kind of barbarians these are" — the Greeks called all foreigners barbarians — "but there is nothing barbarous about the way this army is arranged." The Greeks said much the same about the army Flaminius brought into their country, and Philip, looking down from a hill at the order and layout of the Roman camp in his kingdom, said it too. Which shows how careful we ought to be about swallowing common opinion. We should judge things by the eye of reason, not by popular report.

For a long time I had a man living in my house who had spent ten or twelve years in the New World — the one discovered in our own century, in the region where Villegaignon landed and which he named Antarctic France. The discovery of so vast a country seems to me worth thinking about seriously. And I cannot promise that another one won't turn up tomorrow, since so many men wiser than we are have been wrong about this already. I am afraid our eyes are bigger than our stomachs — that we have more curiosity than capacity. We grab at everything and catch nothing but wind.

Plato has Solon tell a story he heard from the priests of Saïs in Egypt: that long ago, before the Flood, there was a great island called Atlantis, sitting right at the mouth of the Straits of Gibraltar, larger than Africa and Asia put together. Its kings owned not just the island but a stretch of the mainland too — across Africa as far as Egypt, and into Europe as far as Tuscany — and they set out to swallow Asia as well, and to subdue every nation bordering the Mediterranean all the way to the Black Sea. They overran Spain, Gaul, and Italy, and pushed into Greece, where the Athenians stopped them. Some time after, the Athenians, the invaders, and the island itself were all swallowed by the Flood.

It is quite likely that this tremendous rush of water made strange changes in the dwelling places of the earth. They say it was the sea that split Sicily from Italy — "these lands, once a single country, were torn apart by violence and vast ruin," as Virgil has it — and Cyprus from Syria, and the island of Negropont from the mainland of Boeotia; and that elsewhere it joined lands that had been separate, filling the channels between them with sand and mud. "What was long a sterile marsh, where oars dipped, now feeds neighboring cities and feels the weight of the plough," says Horace.

But there is no real chance that this island was the New World we have just discovered. Atlantis practically touched Spain, and it would take an unbelievable flood to shove it back more than twelve hundred leagues. Besides, our modern navigators have nearly established that the New World is no island at all but solid mainland — joined to the East Indies on one side and to the lands under the two poles on the other, or, if it is cut off from them, cut off by a strait so narrow that it hardly earns the name of island on that account.

It seems that in this great body of the world, as in our own, there are two kinds of motion: one natural, the other feverish. When I consider what my own river, the Dordogne, has done in my lifetime to the right bank as it flows down — how much ground it has gained in twenty years, undermining the foundations of house after house — I can see this is no ordinary disturbance. If it had always moved at this pace, or were to keep it up, the face of the world would be turned upside down. But rivers change their moods: sometimes they wear at one bank, sometimes the other, and sometimes they keep quietly to their channel. I am not talking about sudden floods, whose causes everyone understands. In Médoc, along the seashore, my brother the Sieur d'Arsac watches an estate of his lie buried under the sand the sea vomits up ahead of itself. The rooftops of some buildings can still be seen; his rents and farmland have turned into miserably thin pasture. The locals say the sea has been driving at them so hard in recent years that they have lost four leagues of land. These sands are her advance scouts — and we can see great dunes of moving sand marching half a league ahead of her, conquering ground.

The other ancient testimony that people try to attach to this discovery is in Aristotle — at least if that little book of Unheard-of Wonders is really his. He tells us there that certain Carthaginians, having struck out across the Atlantic beyond the Straits of Gibraltar and sailed a very long time, finally discovered a great fertile island, all covered with woods and watered by broad, deep rivers, far from any mainland. They — and others after them, drawn by the goodness and richness of the soil — went there with their wives and children and started a colony. But the senate of Carthage, seeing its population draining away little by little, issued an express ban, on pain of death, against anyone else going, and drove out the new settlers — fearing, it is said, that in time they would multiply enough to supplant Carthage herself and ruin the state. But Aristotle's tale fits our new lands no better than Plato's.

This man of mine was a plain, simple fellow — which makes him the more likely to tell the truth. Your refined sort of man is far more curious, true, and notices much more; but then he glosses it. To give weight to what he says and win you over, he cannot resist improving the story a little. Such men never show you things as they are; they show you things as they appeared to them, or as they would like them to appear to you. And to earn a reputation for judgment and make their account more convincing, they happily pad out the facts with inventions of their own. What we need is either a man of scrupulous honesty, or one so simple that he lacks the raw material to build false stories and make them look plausible — a man with nothing to gain from a lie. Mine was that man. And besides, he brought to my house, at various times, several sailors and merchants who had made the same voyage. So I am content with his information, without troubling to ask what the cosmographers say about it.

What we need are topographers who would give us exact accounts of the particular places they have actually been. But because a man has had the advantage over us of seeing the Holy Land, he claims the privilege of telling us news about all the rest of the world too. I wish everyone would write what he knows, and only as much as he knows — and not just on this subject but on every subject. A man may have precise knowledge and experience of the nature of one river or one spring, and on everything else know no more than the next fellow; yet to put his little scrap of learning into circulation, he will undertake to write the whole science of nature. From this vice great troubles grow.

Now, to return to my subject: I find that there is nothing barbarous or savage about this nation, from anything I have been told — except that everyone calls barbarism whatever is not his own practice. And indeed we have no other measure of truth and reason than the example and pattern of the opinions and customs of the place we live in. There — always there — is the perfect religion, the perfect government, the perfectly polished way of doing everything.

These people are wild in the same sense that we call fruits wild — wild, the way we call fruits wild that nature produces by herself, in her own ordinary course. When in truth, the ones we ought to call wild are those we have altered by our tricks and twisted out of the common order. In the first, the genuine virtues and properties — the most useful and natural ones — are alive and vigorous; in the second, we have bastardized them, adapting them to the pleasure of our own corrupted taste. And even so, our taste has to admit that several of the fruits of those countries, grown without any cultivation at all, have a flavor and delicacy excellent enough to rival the best of ours.

It is not reasonable that art should win the place of honor over our great and powerful mother, nature. We have so overloaded the beauty and richness of her works with our own inventions and ornaments that we have very nearly smothered her. Yet wherever she shines in her own purity, she puts our vain, frivolous efforts wonderfully to shame. "The ivy grows best where it grows by itself; the arbutus is loveliest in the lonely cave; and birds sing the sweeter for having no training," as Propertius says. With all our straining we cannot even copy the nest of the smallest bird — its structure, its beauty, its convenience — or so much as the web of a poor spider. All things, says Plato, are produced by nature, by fortune, or by art: the greatest and most beautiful by one of the first two, the least and most imperfect by the last.

These nations, then, seem to me barbarous only in this sense: that they have received very little shaping from the human mind, and are still very close to their original simplicity. The laws of nature still govern them, hardly corrupted at all by ours — and in such purity that I am sometimes sorry we did not learn of them earlier, in the days when there were men better able to judge them than we are. I am sorry Lycurgus and Plato never knew them. For it seems to me that what we actually see in these nations surpasses not only all the pictures the poets have painted of the golden age, and all their inventions for imagining a happy condition of man, but the very conception and desire of philosophy itself. The philosophers could never imagine a simplicity as native and pure as the one we see with our own eyes; they could never believe that human society could be held together with so little artifice and human solder.

This is a nation, I would tell Plato, in which there is no kind of commerce; no knowledge of letters; no science of numbers; no word for magistrate or political superiority; no servants; no riches and no poverty; no contracts, no inheritances, no divisions of property; no occupations but easy ones; no respect for any kinship except the common kind; no clothing; no agriculture; no metal; no use of wheat or wine. The very words for lying, treachery, deceit, greed, envy, slander, and forgiveness — unheard of. How far short of this perfection would he find his imaginary Republic? These are "men fresh from the gods," as Seneca says; "these are the ways nature first taught," says Virgil.

For the rest: they live in a country very pleasant and temperate, so that, my witnesses tell me, a sick person is a rare sight. They assure me they never saw a single native palsied, bleary-eyed, toothless, or bent with age. They are settled along the seacoast, walled in on the landward side by great high mountains, with a strip about a hundred leagues wide between. They have a great abundance of fish and meat that bear no resemblance to ours, and they eat them with no preparation beyond plain cooking. The first man who rode a horse there — though he had made friends among them on several earlier voyages — so terrified them with his centaur shape that they shot him dead with arrows before they could recognize him.

Their buildings are very long, big enough to hold two or three hundred people, made of the bark of tall trees set with one end in the ground and leaning together at the top, supporting one another like some of our barns, whose roofs slope all the way down and double as walls. They have wood so hard they cut with it, making their swords of it, and grills for cooking their meat. Their beds are of woven cotton, slung from the roof like our sailors' hammocks, each man with his own — for the wives sleep apart from their husbands. They rise with the sun and eat, immediately, enough for the whole day; that is their only meal. They drink nothing with it — like certain Eastern peoples Suidas mentions, who never drank at meals — but they drink many times through the rest of the day, and sometimes heroically. Their drink is made from a certain root and is the color of our claret; they only drink it lukewarm. It keeps no more than two or three days; it has a slightly sharp, lively taste, goes nothing to the head, is good for the stomach — laxative for newcomers, but a very pleasant drink for anyone used to it. In place of bread they use a certain white substance, like preserved coriander. I have tasted it: sweet, and a little flat.

The whole day is spent in dancing. The young men hunt wild animals with bows and arrows, while some of the women busy themselves warming the drink — that is their chief duty. In the morning, before they begin to eat, one of the old men preaches to the whole household, walking from one end of the building to the other and repeating the same sentence many times until he has finished the circuit — for the houses are a good hundred yards long. He commends only two things to them: courage against their enemies and love for their wives. And he never fails to note, in closing, that it is the wives who keep their drink warm and well seasoned. You can see, in several places — my own house among them — the style of their beds, their ropes, their swords, the wooden bracelets they tie on their wrists for battle, and the long canes, open at one end, whose sound keeps the rhythm of their dances. They shave their whole bodies, and more cleanly than we do, with no razor but one of wood or stone. They believe the soul is immortal, and that those who have deserved well of the gods are lodged in the part of the sky where the sun rises, and the damned in the west.

They have priests and prophets of some sort, who very rarely show themselves to the people, living as they do in the mountains. When one arrives there is a great festival and a solemn gathering of many villages — each longhouse, as I have described it, makes a village, and they stand about a French league apart. The prophet speaks to them in public, exhorting them to virtue and to their duty; but their whole moral science contains just the two articles: resolution in war and affection for their wives. He also prophesies things to come, and the outcomes they should expect from their undertakings, and steers them toward war or away from it. But he had better get it right: if he fails in his prediction, and things fall out otherwise than he foretold, he is cut into a thousand pieces — if they catch him — and condemned as a false prophet. For that reason, a prophet who has once guessed wrong is never seen again.

Divination is a gift of God; that is why abusing it ought to be punished as fraud. Among the Scythians, when the diviners missed the mark, they were laid, bound hand and foot, on carts full of brushwood drawn by oxen, and burned there. Those who handle matters within the reach of ordinary human ability can be excused for doing their best. But these others, who come to us boasting of an extraordinary faculty beyond our understanding — shouldn't they be punished for failing to deliver on their promise, and for the recklessness of their fraud?

They have constant wars with the nations beyond their mountains, further inland — and they go to them naked, with no weapons but bows and wooden swords pointed like our boar-spears. Their steadiness in battle is astonishing, and the fighting never ends without great slaughter, for as to running away — they do not know what it is. Every man brings home as his trophy the head of an enemy he has killed and mounts it over his door. They treat their prisoners well for a long time, giving them every comfort they can think of; then the man who owns a prisoner calls a great assembly of his friends. He ties a rope to one of the prisoner's arms and holds the end of it, standing back out of reach, and gives the other arm to his dearest friend to hold the same way; and the two of them, before the whole assembly, kill him with their swords. That done, they roast him, eat him together, and send portions to absent friends.

They do not do this for food, as the ancient Scythians did, but as the extreme form of revenge. The proof: when they saw that the Portuguese, who were allied with their enemies, used a different sort of execution on those they captured — burying them to the waist, shooting the exposed half full of arrows, and then hanging them — these people of the other world (men who had sown the knowledge of plenty of vices among their neighbors, and were far greater masters of every kind of wickedness than they were) decided that such a revenge must mean something, and must hurt more than their own. So they began to abandon their old method and follow this one.

I am not sorry that we notice the barbarous horror of such an act. I am very sorry that, judging their faults so correctly, we should be so blind to our own. I think there is more barbarity in eating a man alive than in eating him dead; more barbarity in tearing apart, by rack and torture, a body still full of feeling, in roasting it by degrees, in having it bitten and mangled by dogs and pigs — as we have not only read but seen, within living memory, not between old enemies but between neighbors and fellow citizens, and, what is worse, under the banner of piety and religion — than in roasting and eating a man after he is dead.

Chrysippus and Zeno, the two heads of the Stoic school, thought there was no harm in using our dead bodies any way we need to, including for food — as our own ancestors did when, besieged by Caesar in the town of Alesia, they resolved to outlast the famine on the bodies of the old men, the women, and the others who were useless for fighting. "The Gascons, it is said, prolonged their lives on such food," says Juvenal. And our physicians think nothing of using corpses in every sort of way for our health, applying them outside or prescribing them within. But no opinion was ever so unruly as to excuse treachery, disloyalty, tyranny, and cruelty — which are our everyday vices.

So we may well call these people barbarians by the rules of reason, but not by comparison with ourselves, who outdo them in every kind of barbarity. Their warfare is wholly noble and generous, and has as much excuse and beauty as that human disease can have. Among them it rests on one foundation only: rivalry in courage. They are not fighting to conquer new land; what they have is so naturally fruitful that it supplies, without labor or worry, everything they need, in such abundance that they have no reason to enlarge their borders. And they are happy in this too — that they desire only what their natural needs demand; everything beyond that is simply useless to them. Men of the same age call one another brothers; the younger ones are children; the old men are fathers to everyone. The old leave the full possession of their goods to all heirs in common, undivided, with no other title than the one nature gives every creature by bringing it into the world.

If their neighbors cross the mountains to attack them, and win, the victor's prize is glory — the advantage of having proved the better in valor and virtue. They have no other use for the goods of the conquered; they turn around and go home, where they lack nothing they need, and lack nothing of that greatest good of all: knowing how to enjoy their condition happily, and to be content with it. And the others, in their turn, do the same. They ask of their prisoners no ransom but this — an admission of being beaten. But in a whole century you will not find one who would not rather die than make that confession, or yield, by a single word or look, one inch of an unbreakable courage. There is not a man among them who would not rather be killed and eaten than so much as open his mouth to ask to be spared. They treat their prisoners with complete freedom and generosity, so that life will be the dearer to them — and meanwhile entertain them with threats of their coming death, of the torments they will suffer, of the preparations under way, of the chopping of their limbs, and of the feast at which they will be the only dish. All of it for one purpose: to wring some soft or submissive word from them, or to make them want to run — to gain the advantage of having frightened them and bent their constancy. Because, rightly understood, that is the only point in which true victory consists. "There is no victory unless the enemy admits it in his own mind," as Claudian says.

The Hungarians, a very warlike people, never used to press their advantage further than bringing the enemy to surrender. Once they had forced that confession, they let him go unharmed and unransomed — at most making him give his word never to bear arms against them again. Plenty of the advantages we gain over our enemies are borrowed, not truly ours. To have stronger arms and legs is the quality of a porter, not a virtue. Agility is a dead, physical quality. It is a stroke of luck when our enemy stumbles, or has the sun in his eyes. To be a good fencer is a trick of training and skill, which may land on a coward or a nobody. The worth and value of a man lie in his heart and his will; there his true honor lives. Valor is strength not of legs and arms but of courage and soul; it does not lie in the worth of our horse or our weapons, but in our own. The man who falls still fierce in his courage — who "fights on his knees if his legs fail him," as Seneca says — the man who, facing certain death, gives up not an ounce of his assurance, who as he dies still throws his enemy a hard and scornful look: that man is beaten not by us but by fortune. He is killed, not conquered. The bravest men are sometimes the unluckiest.

So there are defeats more triumphant than victories. Those four sister victories, the fairest the sun ever looked on — Salamis, Plataea, Mycale, and Sicily — would never dare set all their combined glory against the glory of the annihilation of King Leonidas and his men at the pass of Thermopylae. Who ever ran more gloriously, more ambitiously, toward winning a battle than Captain Ischolas ran toward losing one? Who ever worked more ingeniously to save himself than he did to destroy himself? He was assigned to hold a pass in the Peloponnese against the Arcadians. Considering the terrain and the odds, he saw it was utterly impossible — every man who faced that enemy would stay on that ground forever. On the other hand he judged it unworthy of his own courage, his greatness of spirit, and the Spartan name to fail in his duty. Between these two extremes he took a middle road: the youngest and fittest of his men he saved for the defense and service of their country, and sent home; with the rest, whose loss would matter less, he resolved to hold the pass and make the enemy buy their entry as dearly as he possibly could. So it fell out. Surrounded on every side by the Arcadians, he and his men made a great slaughter of them and were cut to pieces, every one. Is there a trophy raised to victors that was not better earned by these losers? True conquest plays its part in the fight, not in the outcome; the honor of valor is in fighting, not in winning.

But to return to my story. These prisoners, far from showing the smallest weakness under all the terrors heaped on them, do the opposite: through the two or three months they are kept, they wear a cheerful face. They urge their masters to hurry up and put them to the test. They defy them, insult them, taunt them with their cowardice and with the count of battles they have lost to the prisoners' own people. I have a song composed by one of these prisoners, in which he dares them all to come and dine on him, and welcome — for they will be eating, at the same time, their own fathers and grandfathers, whose flesh has fed and nourished his body. "These muscles," he says, "this flesh, these veins — they are yours, poor fools that you are. You do not see that the substance of your ancestors' limbs is still in them. Taste carefully, and you will find the flavor of your own flesh." There is nothing of the barbarian in that invention. Those who paint these people dying show the prisoner spitting in his executioners' faces and making faces at them. And it is certain that to the last breath they never stop defying and taunting them, in word and gesture. Truly, here are real savages by our standards — for either they must be, or we must be. There is an astonishing distance between their ways and ours.

The men there have several wives, and the higher their reputation for courage, the more wives they have. One genuinely remarkable thing about their marriages: the same jealousy our wives use to keep us from the friendship and intimacy of other women, their wives use to win it for them. Caring more for their husbands' honor than for anything else, they take pains to seek out and bring in as many companions as they can — since that is a testimony to the husband's worth. Our wives will cry miracle; it is not. It is a properly matrimonial virtue, and of the highest order. In the Bible, Sarah, and Leah and Rachel, the wives of Jacob, gave their beautiful handmaids to their husbands. Livia furthered the appetites of Augustus, against her own interest. And Stratonice, the wife of King Deiotarus, not only gave a lovely young chambermaid to her husband's bed but carefully raised the children he had by her, and helped them to their father's throne.

And so that no one thinks all this is done out of simple, slavish bondage to custom, under the pressure of ancient habit, without thought or judgment — because their souls are too dull to do anything else — I should show you a few samples of what they are capable of. Besides the war song I quoted, I have another of their songs, a love song, which begins like this: "Stay, adder, stay — so that my sister may copy the pattern of your markings, and make from it a rich ribbon to give my beloved; so shall your beauty and the elegant order of your scales be honored forever above all other serpents." That first phrase, "Stay, adder," is the refrain of the song. Now I have spent enough of my life with poetry to say this: not only is there nothing barbarous in this invention, it is downright Anacreontic. Their language, by the way, is a soft language, pleasant to the ear, with endings rather like Greek.

Three of these people — not knowing how dearly their peace and happiness will one day pay for acquaintance with the corruptions of our side of the world, and that this contact will be their ruin, which I suppose is already well under way; poor men, to let themselves be cheated by the appetite for new things, and to have left the sweetness of their own sky to come and look at ours! — three of them were at Rouen when the late King Charles the Ninth was there. The king talked with them a long while; they were shown our manners, our pomp, the shape of a fine city. Afterward someone asked their opinion — wanted to know what they had found most remarkable in all they had seen. They named three things. I have forgotten the third, and I am sorry for it; but two I still remember.

They said, first, that they found it very strange that so many tall, bearded men, strong and well armed, who stood around the king — they probably meant the Swiss of his guard — should consent to obey a child, instead of choosing one of themselves to command. Second — they have a way of speaking in which they call men halves of one another — they said they had noticed among us men gorged and stuffed with every sort of comfort, while their halves stood begging at their doors, gaunt with hunger and poverty; and they found it strange that these needy halves could endure such injustice without taking the others by the throat or setting fire to their houses.

I talked with one of them a long while; but my interpreter was so bad, and so hampered by his own stupidity from grasping what I meant, that I could get hardly anything worthwhile out of him. When I asked the man what benefit he got from his rank among his own people — for he was a captain, and our sailors called him king — he said: to march first into war. Asked how many men followed him, he pointed to a stretch of ground, meaning as many as that space would hold — perhaps four or five thousand men. Asked whether his authority lapsed when the war ended, he said this much of it remained: when he visited the villages that depended on him, they cut paths for him through the thick of their woods, so that he could travel at his ease.

None of that sounds bad at all — but what of that? They don't wear pants.
