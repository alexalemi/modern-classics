Of Age

I can't accept the way we settle, in advance, how long our lives are supposed to last. I notice the wise men cut the figure much shorter than popular opinion does. When the younger Cato's friends tried to stop him from killing himself, he asked them: "Am I now at an age where I can be accused of leaving the world too soon?" He was forty-eight. He considered that a ripe, advanced age, given how few people ever reach it.

As for those who soothe themselves with some notion of "the natural course of things" and promise themselves years beyond that — well, if they could be exempted from the endless accidents we're all naturally exposed to, maybe they'd have a case. But what an idle fantasy it is to expect to die of sheer worn-out strength, the kind of death that comes only at the furthest extreme of old age, and to set the lease of our life by that measure — when it is, of all deaths, the rarest and least often seen. We call that one alone a natural death. As if it were against nature for a man to break his neck in a fall, drown in a shipwreck, or get carried off by pleurisy or plague — as if our ordinary condition didn't expose us to exactly these hazards. Let us stop flattering ourselves with these fine words. Perhaps we ought instead to call natural whatever is general, common, and universal.

Dying of old age is a death that is rare, extraordinary, singular — and therefore less natural than the others, not more. It is the last and furthest kind of dying; the more remote it stands, the less we should count on it. It is, true enough, the boundary stone beyond which we cannot pass, the limit the law of nature has fixed. But it is also a privilege she very rarely grants — to last all the way there. It's a lease she signs only as a special favor, maybe to one person in two or three centuries, and even then she throws in a safe-conduct pass to get him past all the obstacles and ambushes she has scattered along that long road.

So here is my opinion: once we reach forty, we should regard it as an age very few attain. Since men don't usually get that far, it's a sign we are already well along. And once we've passed the ordinary bounds — which are the true measure of life — we shouldn't expect to go much further. Having slipped past so many precipices of death, the same ones we've watched so many other men tumble into, we ought to admit that a run of luck this extraordinary — the luck that has rescued us from all those dangers and kept us alive beyond the usual term — is not likely to hold much longer.

Even our laws keep this error alive. They say a man isn't capable of managing his own estate until he's twenty-five — when he'll be hard pressed to manage his life that long. Augustus shaved five years off the old Roman standard and declared that thirty was old enough to be a judge. Servius Tullius excused knights over forty-seven from the hardships of war; Augustus released them at forty-five. Though sending men to the fireside before fifty-five or sixty strikes me as a bit unreasonable. My own view is that our work and our usefulness should be stretched as far as possible, for the public good. The fault I find is on the other side: they don't put us to work early enough. This same emperor was running the whole world at nineteen — and yet he wanted a man to be thirty before he was fit to settle a dispute about a gutter.

For my part, I believe our souls are as grown up at twenty as they will ever be, as capable then as ever. A soul that hasn't given clear proof of its force by that age never will. Our natural qualities and powers show whatever vigor and beauty they have within that term, or never. As they say in Dauphiné, "if the thorn doesn't prick when it's born, it will hardly ever prick at all."

Of all the great human actions I've ever heard or read of, of whatever kind, in past ages and in our own, more were done before the age of thirty than after — and often in the lives of the very same men. May I not point with confidence to Hannibal, and to his great rival Scipio? They spent the better half of their lives living off the glory they had won in their youth. Great men afterward, yes — compared with everyone else. But not compared with themselves.

As for me personally, I'm certain that since that age both my mind and my body have shrunk rather than grown — retreated rather than advanced. It's possible that in those who use their time well, knowledge and experience keep increasing with the years. But quickness, readiness, steadiness, and the other parts of us that matter far more, and are far more essentially our own — these droop and decay. As Lucretius says, "when the body is shaken by the heavy blows of time and the limbs sag with blunted force, the judgment limps, the tongue and the mind wander."

Sometimes the body surrenders to age first, sometimes the mind. And I've seen plenty of men whose brains went weak before their legs or their stomach did. It's a disease that causes its sufferer little pain and shows obscure symptoms — which makes it all the more dangerous. This is why I complain about our laws: not that they keep us at work too long, but that they set us to work too late. Considering how fragile life is, and how many ordinary, natural rocks it can wreck on, we should not hand over so large a share of it to childhood, idleness, and apprenticeship.
