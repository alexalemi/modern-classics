Of Coaches
(Part 1 of 2)

It's easy to show that when the great authors write about causes, they don't use only the ones they believe to be true. They also use the ones they don't believe, as long as those have some beauty and invention in them. They speak truly enough, and usefully enough, if they speak ingeniously. We can never be certain of the master cause, so we pile up a whole heap of them, hoping the real one might happen to be somewhere in the pile. As Lucretius says, "it is not enough to give one cause; we must give many, though only one of them is true."

You ask me where the custom comes from of blessing people who sneeze? Well: we break wind three ways. The wind that escapes from below is too filthy to mention. The wind that comes out of the mouth carries a whiff of gluttony with it. The third is the sneeze, and because it comes from the head and gives no offense, we grant it this polite reception. Don't laugh at the distinction — they say it's Aristotle's.

I think I read in Plutarch — who, of all the authors I know, best mixed art with nature and judgment with learning — that seasickness is caused by fear; he had first worked out some argument proving that fear could produce such an effect. Now, I am very prone to seasickness, and I know perfectly well that this cause has nothing to do with me. I know it not by argument but by hard experience. I'll set aside what I've been told — that animals get seasick too, especially hogs, who have no notion whatsoever that they're in danger. And I'll set aside what an acquaintance told me about himself: that though he's very subject to it, the urge to vomit left him three or four times when he was badly frightened in a violent storm. He was like that ancient who said, "I was too frightened to be sick," as Seneca tells it.

I was never afraid on the water — nor in any other danger, and I've had enough of them in front of my eyes to qualify, if death qualifies — never so afraid, I mean, as to be stunned out of my judgment. Fear springs sometimes from a lack of judgment as much as from a lack of courage. Every danger I've ever been in, I looked at without blinking, with an open, sound, and whole sight. Besides, it takes courage to be afraid. That courage once served me better than anything else: it let me order and manage my retreat so that it was, if not without fear, at least without panic and without astonishment. I was shaken, yes, but not dazed or stupefied.

Great souls go much further than this, and show us retreats that are not just steady and composed but downright lofty. Let me give you the account Alcibiades left of Socrates, his comrade in arms. "I found him," he says, "after the rout of our army, him and Laches, among the last of those retreating. I could study him at my leisure and in safety, because I was on a good horse and he was on foot, as he had fought. First I noticed how much judgment and resolve he showed compared with Laches. Then I noticed the boldness of his walk — no different from his everyday stride — and his gaze, firm and level, weighing and judging everything that passed around him, looking now at one group, now at another, friends and enemies alike. To his friends, that look gave heart; to his enemies, it announced that he would sell his life dearly to anyone who tried to take it. And so they came off safe — because people don't like to attack a man like that. They chase the ones who look frightened."

That is the testimony of a great captain, and it teaches what we see confirmed every day: nothing throws us into danger like a frantic eagerness to get clear of it. As Livy puts it, "where there is least fear, there is usually least danger."

Our people are wrong to say that a man "is afraid of death" when what they mean is that he thinks about it and sees it coming. Foresight is equally useful for whatever concerns us, good or bad. To consider a danger and weigh it is, in a way, the very opposite of being stunned by it.

I do not find myself strong enough to stand against the force of this passion of fear, or of any other violent passion. If it once conquered me and knocked me flat, I would never get up again entirely sound. Whoever made my soul lose her footing would never set her upright after. She probes and re-probes herself too deeply, too much to the quick; she would never let the wound she'd received close and scar over. It's been lucky for me that no illness has yet unhinged her. Every time I'm charged, I meet it with all the resistance and defense I have — which means the first assault that routed me would leave me no way to rally. I have no second line. Wherever the flood breaks through my banks, I lie open, and I drown without remedy. Epicurus says a wise man can never become a fool. I hold the reverse opinion: that whoever has once been a thorough fool will never afterward be thoroughly wise. God sends me cold according to my coat, and passions in proportion to my means of withstanding them. Nature, having left me exposed on one side, has covered me on the other: having disarmed me of strength, she has armed me with insensibility — with a composure that is steady, or, if you prefer, dull.

Now, I cannot long endure — and when I was young I could endure even less — a coach, a litter, or a boat, and I hate every kind of travel but horseback, in town and country alike. But I can bear a litter even less than a coach, and by the same logic I can bear rough water, which frightens people, better than the motion of a calm. At those little jolts the oars give, stealing the vessel out from under us, I feel — I don't know how — my head and my stomach both go wrong; just as I can't sit on a wobbly chair. When the sail or the current carries us evenly along, or we're being towed, that smooth motion doesn't bother me at all. It's interrupted movement that offends me, and worst of all when it's slow. I can't describe it any better than that. The doctors prescribed squeezing and binding my lower belly with a towel to fix this trouble — which I haven't tried, being accustomed to wrestling with my own defects and beating them myself.

If my memory could serve me, I wouldn't think my time wasted in setting down here the endless variety that history offers on the use of chariots in war — varied by nation and by age, and in my opinion of great necessity and effect, so that it's a wonder we've lost all knowledge of them. I'll say only this: quite recently, in our fathers' time, the Hungarians used them to great advantage against the Turks. Each one carried a man with a shield and a musketeer, plus a stack of harquebuses ready loaded, the whole thing screened with canvas like a war-galley. They formed the front of their battle line with three thousand such coaches, and after the cannon had spoken, sent the whole volley pouring into the enemy — who had to swallow that taste of things to come, which was no small head start. Then the chariots charged into the enemy squadrons to break them open and clear a way for the rest. Besides that, they could shield the troops on a dangerous flank while marching, or cover a position and fortify it in a hurry. In my own time, a gentleman on one of our frontiers — too bulky a man for any horse to carry — having a quarrel on his hands, rode about the country in a chariot of this sort, and got on very comfortably. But let's leave these chariots of war.

As if the worthlessness of coaches weren't already proven well enough, the last kings of our first dynasty traveled in a chariot drawn by four oxen. Mark Antony was the first man in Rome to have himself drawn in a coach by lions — with a singing girl beside him. Heliogabalus later did the same, calling himself Cybele, the mother of the gods; he was also drawn by tigers, playing the god Bacchus; sometimes he harnessed two stags to his coach, another time four dogs, and another time four naked girls, having himself drawn by them in full pomp, stark naked himself. The Emperor Firmus had his chariot drawn by ostriches of such prodigious size that it seemed to fly rather than roll.

The strangeness of these inventions puts another notion in my head: that it is a kind of small-mindedness in monarchs, and proof that they don't quite understand what they are, when they labor to make themselves honored and to look great through excessive spending. It would be excusable in a foreign country; but among their own subjects, where they hold sovereign power and can do whatever they please, it actually cheapens their dignity — the highest degree of honor they can reach. Just as, it seems to me, it's superfluous for a private gentleman to dress up finely at home: his house, his servants, and his kitchen answer for him well enough.

The advice Isocrates gives his king seems grounded in good sense: be splendid in plate and furniture, since that's a durable expense that passes down to your successors; and avoid all magnificence that will be forgotten in short order.

I loved dressing finely when I was a younger son, having no other ornament, and it suited me. There are some men on whom their rich clothes sit and weep. We have remarkable stories of the frugality of our kings about their own persons and in their gifts — kings who were great in reputation, in valor, and in fortune. Demosthenes fought hard against his city's law assigning public money to the pomp of its plays and festivals; he wanted its greatness shown in numbers of well-equipped ships and well-provided armies. And there's good reason to condemn Theophrastus, who in his book on riches takes the contrary position and maintains that this sort of expense is the true fruit of abundance. Such delights, says Aristotle, please only the basest sort of people; they vanish from memory as soon as the crowd has had its fill, and no serious, judicious man can hold them in any esteem.

The money would, in my opinion, be spent far more royally — being more profitably, justly, and durably spent — on ports, harbors, walls, and fortifications; on handsome buildings, churches, hospitals, colleges, and the improvement of streets and highways. In that line Pope Gregory XIII will leave an honorable memory to future ages, and in that line our Queen Catherine would prove her natural generosity to long posterity, if her means matched her inclination. Fortune did me a real spite when she interrupted the noble structure of the Pont-Neuf of our great city and robbed me of the hope of seeing it finished before I die.

Besides, it seems to subjects watching these triumphs that it's their own wealth being paraded before them — that they're being entertained at their own expense. For the people tend to assume about kings what we assume about our servants: that their job is to provide everything we need in abundance, and to touch none of it themselves. That's why the Emperor Galba, pleased with a musician who played for him at supper, sent for his money-box and gave him a handful of crowns out of it with these words: "This is not the public money, but my own." Still, it usually turns out that the people have reason on their side, and that princes feed the people's eyes with what their bellies need.

Even generosity doesn't show its true luster in a sovereign's hand; private men have the better right to it. For, taking it strictly, a king has nothing that is properly his own; he owes his very self to others. Authority is not given for the benefit of the magistrate but for the benefit of the people. A superior is never made superior for his own profit but for the profit of those beneath him — a doctor exists for the sick, not for himself. Every office, like every art, has its purpose outside itself.

So the tutors of young princes who make it their business to stamp this virtue of open-handedness into them — preaching that they should refuse nothing, and count nothing so well spent as what they give away (a doctrine I have seen in high credit in my time) — either care more for their own profit than their master's, or don't understand whom they're talking to. It's far too easy to preach generosity to someone with unlimited means of practicing it at other people's expense. And since a gift is measured not by its size but by the means of the giver, in such mighty hands it comes to nothing: they find themselves wastrels before they can be counted generous. Besides, it's a minor virtue compared with the other royal ones — and the only one, as the tyrant Dionysius said, that sits comfortably with tyranny itself. I would rather teach a prince the old farmer's verse: that whoever wants a good crop must sow with the hand, not pour from the sack. He must scatter the seed, not dump it in one heap. And since he has to give — or, to put it more accurately, to pay and to restore to so many people according to what they've earned — he ought to be a loyal and discerning steward of it. If a prince's generosity has neither measure nor discrimination, I would rather have him a miser.

Royal virtue seems to consist most of all in justice; and of all the branches of justice, the one that best marks a king is the justice that goes with giving — because that's the part kings have reserved to perform in person, while every other kind they hand off to be administered by others. Bounty without measure is a weak way to win goodwill; it puts off more people than it attracts. As Cicero says, "the more men you spend it on, the fewer you can spend it on later; and what greater folly than to make sure you can no longer do the very thing you love to do?" And if it's handed out without regard to merit, it embarrasses the man who receives it, and is received without grace. Tyrants have been sacrificed to the people's hatred by the hands of the very men they unjustly raised up — buffoons, panders, fiddlers, and ragamuffins of that stamp — who reckon they can secure their ill-gotten gains by showing hatred and contempt for the man they got them from, signing on to the common verdict.

The subjects of a prince who is excessive in gifts grow excessive in asking; they peg their demands not to reason but to precedent. Honestly, we often have cause to blush at our own shamelessness. By any standard of justice we are overpaid the moment the reward equals the service — do we owe our princes no natural obligation at all? If a prince carries our costs entirely, he does too much; it's enough that he contributes to them. The surplus is called a benefit, and a benefit cannot be demanded — the very word "liberality" has liberty in it.

The way we do it, it never ends. What we've already received doesn't count; only future generosity counts. So the more a prince exhausts himself in giving, the poorer he grows in friends. How could he satisfy appetites that grow with every feeding? Whoever has his mind on getting no longer thinks about what he's gotten. Nothing belongs to greed so properly as ingratitude.

The example of Cyrus fits well here, as a touchstone for the kings of our day to test whether their gifts are well or badly placed — and to see how much better that emperor placed his than they do, who are reduced to borrowing from subjects they don't know, and sooner from the people they've wronged than from the people they've showered with benefits, receiving "aid" in which nothing is voluntary but the name. Croesus once needled Cyrus about his open hand, and calculated what his treasury would be worth if he'd been a little tighter-fisted. Cyrus wanted to vindicate his generosity, so he sent letters in every direction to the great men of his realm whom he had particularly advanced, asking each of them to send him as much money as he could for a pressing need, and to send an account of what he could provide. When the answers came in, every one of his friends — judging it not enough to offer merely what they'd received from his bounty, and adding plenty of their own — it turned out the total came to far more than Croesus's reckoning. Whereupon Cyrus said: "I am no less in love with riches than other princes; I am merely a better manager of them. You see at how small a cost I have acquired the priceless treasure of so many friends, and how much more faithful treasurers they are to me than hired men with no obligation and no affection would be — and my money better stored than in chests, which would bring down on me the hatred, envy, and contempt of other princes."

The emperors excused the extravagance of their games and public spectacles on the grounds that their authority depended in some sort — at least in outward show — on the will of the Roman people, who from time immemorial had been used to being courted with such shows and excesses. But it was private citizens who had fed that custom, gratifying their fellow citizens chiefly out of their own purses with that sort of profusion and magnificence. It had quite another taste when the masters came to imitate it. As Cicero says, "the handing of money from its rightful owners to strangers should not be counted generosity."

Philip, seeing that his son was trying to win the affection of the Macedonians with presents, reprimanded him in a letter along these lines: "What! Do you want your subjects to look on you as their cashier and not their king? Do you want to buy their affections? Then buy them with the benefits of your virtue, not the benefits of your money-box."

And yet, no doubt about it, it was a fine thing to bring into the amphitheater and plant there a great number of huge trees, fully branched and fully green, a great shady forest arranged in beautiful order — and on the first day to turn loose into it a thousand ostriches, a thousand stags, a thousand boars, and a thousand fallow deer, to be killed and carried off by the people; on the second day, to have a hundred huge lions, a hundred leopards, and three hundred bears slaughtered before the crowd; and on the third day, three hundred pairs of gladiators fighting to the finish — as the Emperor Probus did. It was a fine thing, too, to see those vast amphitheaters faced all over with marble on the outside, worked with figures and statues, and glittering inside with rare ornament — "a balustrade studded with gems, a portico overlaid with gold," as Calpurnius says — every side of that enormous space filled and ringed, bottom to top, with sixty or eighty rows of seats, marble too, and laid with cushions. "Let him go out," says Juvenal, "if he has any shame, and get up off the equestrian cushion, since his fortune doesn't meet the legal mark." A hundred thousand men could sit there at their ease.

And the floor below, where the games were played: they made it open and split apart by machinery into chasms, like caves vomiting out the beasts for the spectacle. Then they flooded it into a deep sea, full of sea monsters and loaded with warships, to stage a naval battle. Then they drained it dry and level again for the gladiators. And for the fourth act they strewed it with vermilion and fragrant resin instead of sand, and laid out a solemn feast there for that whole infinite crowd of people — the final act of a single day. As Calpurnius describes it: how often have we seen the arena floor sink and split, and wild beasts climb out of the chasm, and from those same depths a grove of gilded trees rise up in enameled blossom; nor were the marvels only of the forest — I have watched sea-calves fighting with bears, and an unlovely sort of cattle that might be called sea-horses.

Sometimes they raised up a high mountain, covered with fruit trees and other green growth, sending streams of water down from its summit as if from a spring's mouth. Sometimes a great ship came rolling in, opened and split apart by itself, disgorged four or five hundred fighting beasts from its hold, closed up again, and vanished without anyone's help. At other times they made jets of perfumed water shoot upward from the floor, high enough to sprinkle that whole vast multitude. To shelter the crowd from the weather, they had that immense space covered now with embroidered purple curtains, now with silk of one color or another, drawn off or on in a moment, just as they pleased — though, as Martial jokes about a notorious thief, "however fiercely the sun scorches the spectators, the awnings are drawn in when Hermogenes arrives."
