Of the Inequality Among Us

Plutarch says somewhere that he doesn't find as much difference between one beast and another as he finds between one man and another. He means the inner qualities — the furnishings of the soul. And honestly, I find such a gulf between Epaminondas, as I judge him, and certain men I know — men of good sense, mind you — that I'd happily go Plutarch one better: there is more difference between this man and that man than between that man and a beast. As Terence says, "ah, how far one man can outstrip another!" There are as many degrees of mind as there are yards between here and heaven, and just as countless.

But when it comes to valuing men, here is a strange thing: we are the only creature appraised by what isn't ours. Every other animal is rated on its own qualities. We praise a horse for its strength and the sureness of its feet — as Juvenal says, it's the swift horse we applaud, the one that sets the whole hoarse circus roaring as it wins — not for its fancy harness. We praise a greyhound for its speed, not its collar; a hawk for its wing, not its hood and bells.

Why, then, don't we value a man for what is actually his own? He has a grand household, a beautiful palace, plenty of credit, so many thousand a year in income. All of that is around him. None of it is in him.

You don't buy a cat in a bag. If you're bargaining for a horse, you have its blankets stripped off so you can see it bare and open to your eye — or if it's draped, as horses used to be when presented to princes, only the unimportant parts are covered, so that you won't be distracted by a glossy coat or a broad rump and will look instead at the legs, the eyes, the feet: the parts that do the work. Horace says this was the custom of kings — when they bought horses, they inspected them uncovered, so a handsome head standing on a weak foot wouldn't tempt some gaping buyer.

So why, when you size up a man, do you take him wrapped and bundled in his packaging? He shows you nothing then but the parts that aren't remotely his own, and he hides exactly the parts by which his value can be judged. It's the price of the blade you want, not the scabbard. Strip him, and maybe you wouldn't bid a penny. Judge him by himself, not by his outfit. As one of the ancients put it very neatly: "Do you know why you think he's tall? You're counting the height of his shoes." (That's Seneca.) The pedestal is no part of the statue.

Measure him without his stilts. Make him set aside his income and his titles and present himself in his shirt. Is his body sound and lively, fit for its work? What kind of soul does he have? Is it a fine one, a capable one, fully equipped? Is it rich in what's its own, or only in what it has borrowed? Has luck had a hand in the matter? Can it look drawn swords in the eye without blinking? Does it care whether life leaves by the mouth or by the throat? Is it settled, even, content? That is what needs examining. That is how you measure the enormous differences between man and man.

Is he, as Horace describes, "wise and master of himself, a man whom neither poverty nor death nor chains can frighten; strong enough to tell his appetites no and to look down on honors; complete in himself, smooth and round, so that nothing outside can get a grip on him — a man against whom fortune always charges and always limps away"? A man like that stands five hundred yards above kingdoms and duchies. He is an absolute monarch — of himself, in himself. "The wise man," says Plautus, "shapes his own fortune."

What is left for such a man to want? As Lucretius asks, doesn't nature cry out for nothing more than this — a body free of pain, and a mind enjoying itself, clear of fear and worry?

Now compare him with the general run of mankind: stupid, small-souled, servile, unstable, endlessly tossed about by the storm of one passion after another, hanging on others for everything — and the distance between them is greater than the distance between heaven and earth. Yet such is the blindness of habit that we take little or no notice of it. Meanwhile, put a peasant next to a king, a nobleman next to a vassal, a magistrate next to a private citizen, a rich man next to a poor one, and everybody sees an enormous gap — though they differ, you might say, only in their trousers.

In Thrace the king was set apart from his people in a particularly entertaining way: he had a religion all to himself, a private god — Mercury — whom his subjects weren't allowed to worship, while he, in turn, wouldn't lower himself to theirs, Mars, Bacchus, and Diana. But these are only stage paintings; they make no real difference in the thing itself. Watch actors play a duke or an emperor up on the stage, then go back, a moment later, to being the valets and porters they really are. Just so with the emperor whose pomp dazzles you in public — all those great emeralds set in gold, as Lucretius says, flashing green, and the sea-blue silk robe. Peek behind the curtain and you'll find nothing but an ordinary man, possibly a more contemptible one than the least of his subjects. As Seneca says, one man is happy on the inside; the other's happiness is gold leaf.

Cowardice, indecision, ambition, spite, and envy churn in him as much as in anyone. Horace says it: neither treasure chests nor the consul's bodyguard can clear away the mind's miserable riots, or the cares that flutter around paneled ceilings. And worry and fear grab him by the throat even in the middle of his armies — for as Lucretius says, the fears and gnawing anxieties of men are not scared off by the clash of weapons or the points of spears; they move boldly among kings and the masters of the world, and the glitter of gold doesn't awe them a bit.

Do fevers, gout, and strokes spare him any more than they spare one of us? When old age settles on his shoulders, can the yeomen of his guard lift it off? When the fear of death turns him white, can the gentlemen of his bedchamber talk him out of it? When jealousy or some other obsession is swimming in his head, do our bows and compliments restore his good humor? That canopy stitched with pearls and gold over his bed has no power whatsoever against a bad attack of colic. Burning fevers, as Lucretius says, leave you no sooner when you toss on embroidered tapestry under royal purple than when you lie in a common blanket.

Alexander the Great's flatterers had him convinced he was the son of Jupiter. But one day he was wounded, and watching the blood run from the wound he said, "Well, gentlemen, what do you say now? Isn't this blood crimson, and entirely human? It's hardly the kind Homer has flowing from the wounds of gods." The poet Hermodorus wrote verses in honor of Antigonus calling him the son of the sun. Antigonus said: "The man who empties my chamber pot knows better."

A king is a man, no more — and if he's defective or ill-made from birth, the empire of the whole universe can't fix him. Let the girls fight over him, says Persius; let a rose spring up wherever he steps — what good is any of it if he's a fool? Even pleasure and good luck can't be tasted without vigor and sense. As Terence says, things are worth what the mind of their owner is worth: good to the man who knows how to use them, bad to the man who uses them badly.

Whatever fortune hands you, it takes a palate to taste it. It's enjoying things, not owning them, that makes us happy. It isn't lands, as Horace says, or heaps of bronze and gold that drive fevers from a sick owner's body or cares from his mind; the possessor has to be healthy if he plans to get any good from his pile. To a greedy man or a frightened one, house and estate are what a painting is to a blind man — what a hot compress is to gout.

A dull man's taste is flat and dead. He no more enjoys what he has than a man with a head cold enjoys fine wine — than a horse appreciates its expensive saddle. Plato has it right: health, beauty, strength, riches, and everything else we call goods are exactly as bad for the unjust man as they are good for the just one, and the evils the other way around. Where body and mind are out of order, what use are all the external conveniences? The smallest pinprick, the smallest passion of the soul, is enough to strip the pleasure out of being monarch of the whole world. At the first twinge of gout, what comfort is it to be called Sire and Your Majesty?

Made all of silver, as Tibullus says, made all of gold — doesn't he forget his palaces and his grandeur anyway? If he loses his temper, does being a prince keep him from going red, going pale, and grinding his teeth like a madman? And on the other hand, if he's a capable man of sound nature, kingship adds almost nothing to his happiness. If your belly is well, says Horace, and your sides and your feet, all the wealth of kings can add nothing more. He can see the whole apparatus for what it is: counterfeit and trickery. Maybe he'd even agree with King Seleucus, that a man who knew the real weight of a scepter wouldn't bother to pick one up if he found it lying on the ground — so heavy and painful are the duties of a good king.

It can be no small job to govern others when we find it so hard to govern ourselves. And as for ruling, which looks so attractive: considering how feeble human judgment is, and how hard it is to choose among new and doubtful things, I'm strongly of the opinion that it is far easier and far pleasanter to follow than to lead. It is a great rest for the mind to have only one road to walk and no one to answer for but yourself. As Lucretius says, far better to obey in peace than to crave the reins of power. Add to that Cyrus's saying, that no man is fit to command unless he's worth more than the people he commands.

But King Hiero, in Xenophon, goes further: even in the enjoyment of pleasure itself, he says, kings are worse off than private men, because having everything available on command takes away the sharp delight ordinary people get from it. Love that is too rich and too easy, as Ovid says, turns to boredom, and like sweets on the stomach, it sickens.

Do we imagine the choirboys take any great delight in music? Surfeit makes it tedious to them. Feasts, balls, masquerades, tournaments — these thrill people who see them rarely and long to see them; to someone who attends constantly, the flavor goes flat and stale. Women don't much excite the man who has them whenever he likes. The man who never gives himself time to be thirsty will never know the real pleasure of drinking. Farces and acrobatics delight the audience; to the performers they're a grind. The proof is that princes sometimes amuse themselves by going in disguise, stepping down from their rank for a while to live, briefly, in the poor plain style of the humblest of their people. The rich enjoy a change, as Horace says: a clean simple supper under a poor man's small roof, no tapestries, no purple, has smoothed many an anxious forehead.

Nothing clogs and sickens like abundance. What appetite wouldn't quail at three hundred women all at its mercy, as the Grand Signor has in his seraglio? And what relish for the hunt did that ancestor of his keep for himself who never went hawking without seven thousand falconers?

Beyond all this, I suspect the very glare of greatness brings real trouble into the enjoyment of even the most tempting pleasures. The great stand in too bright a light; they live exposed to every eye. And I don't know why we demand, more than of anyone else, that they hide their faults — since what counts as mere indiscretion in us, the public brands in them as tyranny and contempt for the law. People are inclined to believe that, on top of their natural appetite for vice, princes get an extra thrill out of trampling public decencies. Plato in his Gorgias actually defines a tyrant as one who has license, in a city, to do whatever he pleases. And often, because of that impunity, the open display of a prince's vices does more damage than the vice itself. Every man dreads being watched and pried into; princes are watched down to their gestures, their glances, and their thoughts, the whole populace believing it has the right and title to judge them. Besides, blemishes grow with the eminence and brightness of the place where they sit: a mole or a wart on them shows larger than a gaping wound on other men. That's why the poets imagine Jupiter conducting his love affairs in so many borrowed shapes — of all the escapades they charge him with, there is only one, as I remember, where he appears in his own majesty and grandeur.

But back to Hiero. He also complains of what his royalty cost him in this: he couldn't go abroad and travel freely, being practically a prisoner inside the borders of his own country, and in everything he did he was hemmed in by an importunate crowd. Truthfully, when I see our kings sitting all alone at table, ringed by so many people talking at them and so many strangers staring at them — as they always are — I have often felt more pity than envy.

King Alfonso used to say that in this respect donkeys were better off than kings: their masters let them feed at their ease, a favor kings can't get from their servants. It has never struck me as any great advantage to a man of sense to have twenty people chattering around him while he sits on the toilet. Nor can I see why the services of a man with ten thousand a year, a man who took Casale or held Siena, should be more comfortable or more welcome to him than those of a good valet who knows his job.

The advantages of being a sovereign are mostly imaginary. Every level of fortune contains some little image of kingship. Caesar called all the lords of France who held free rights on their own lands "kinglets" — petty kings. And in truth, apart from the title "Sire," they go a long way toward royalty. Look at the provinces far from court — Brittany, say. Consider the retinue, the vassals, the officers, the duties, the service, the ceremony, the whole state kept up by a lord living away from court in his own house among his own tenants and servants — and watch where his imagination flies. Nothing could be more royal. He hears talk of his actual master once a year, the way one hears of the king of Persia, and acknowledges him only through some distant family tie his secretary keeps filed away in a register somewhere. Our laws sit lightly enough, truth be told: a French gentleman feels the weight of the crown pinch his shoulders maybe twice in his life. Real, effective subjection falls only on those of us who volunteer for it — who stick their necks under the yoke hoping to profit by it in wealth and honors. A man who is content at his own fireside, and can run his household without lawsuits and quarrels with the neighbors, is as free as the Doge of Venice. Servitude binds few men, as Seneca says; many men bind themselves to it.

But what bothers Hiero most is finding himself stripped of all friendship, cut off from all mutual companionship — the thing in which the truest and most perfect enjoyment of human life consists. For what testimony of affection can I squeeze out of a man who owes me, like it or not, everything he's capable of giving? Can I read anything into his humble speech and his bowing and scraping, when those courtesies aren't his to withhold? The honor we receive from people who fear us is not honor. Those respects are paid to my royalty, not to me. As Seneca has it in his tragedy, the greatest advantage of kingship is that the people are forced not only to endure their master's deeds but to praise them.

Don't I see that the bad king and the good one, the hated and the beloved, get exactly the same reverence? My predecessor was served with the same ceremony, and my successor will be. If my subjects do me no harm, that's no proof of affection — why should I take it for one, when they couldn't harm me if they wanted to? No one follows me or obeys me out of any friendship between him and me; friendship can't form where there is so little in common and so little exchange. My height has lifted me clean out of human fellowship: there's too much disparity, too much distance. They follow me out of form and habit — or rather, they follow my fortune, not me, hoping to grow their own. Everything they say to me and do for me is surface paint. Their freedom is curbed on every side by the great power I hold over them, so I see nothing around me that isn't masked and disguised.

The Emperor Julian, being applauded one day by his courtiers for his exact justice, said: "I'd be proud of this praise if it came from people who dared to condemn or criticize the opposite, were I to do it."

All the real advantages princes have, they share with men of middling condition. It's for the gods to ride winged horses and dine on ambrosia. Kings have no other sleep than ours and no other appetite. The steel they arm themselves with is no better tempered than the steel we use. Their crowns keep off neither the rain nor the sun.

Diocletian, who wore as fortunate and revered a crown as any, resigned it to retire into the happiness of private life. Some time later, when public affairs demanded that he take it up again, he answered the men who came to urge him: "You would never try to persuade me to this if you had seen the fine order of the trees I've planted in my orchard, and the beautiful melons I've sown in my garden."

In Anacharsis's opinion, the happiest state would be one in which, all else being equal, rank was measured out according to men's virtues, and disgrace according to their vices.

When King Pyrrhus was preparing his expedition into Italy, his wise counselor Cyneas, wanting to make him feel the vanity of his ambition, asked him: "Well, sire — what is all this great preparation for?" "To make myself master of Italy," the king replied. "And when that's done?" said Cyneas. "Then I'll cross into Gaul and Spain." "And then?" "Then I'll go conquer Africa; and at last, when I've brought the whole world under me, I'll sit down and rest, and live content and at ease."

"Then for God's sake, sire," said Cyneas, "tell me what stops you from being in that condition right now, if you please? Why not settle yourself this instant in the state you're aiming at, and spare yourself all the labor and danger you're putting in between?"

The trouble, as Lucretius says, was that he never learned where acquiring should stop, or how far real pleasure can actually grow.

I will close with an old verse that I find very fit for the purpose — Cornelius Nepos's line: "Every man frames his own fortune."
