Of the Education of Children
(Part 2 of 4)

It's also the general opinion that a child should not be raised in his mother's lap. Mothers are too tender. Their natural affection makes even the most sensible of them so overfond that they can't bring themselves to correct their children's faults, or to let them be toughened by hardship and risk, as they ought to be. A mother cannot bear to see her boy come home dusty and sweating from his exercise, or drink something cold when he's hot, or climb onto an unruly horse, or face a rough fencer with a foil in his hand, or so much as fire a gun. But there's no help for it: whoever wants to raise a boy to be worth anything as a man must spare him nothing while he's young, and must regularly break the rules of medicine. As Horace says, let him live under the open sky, and in the thick of danger.

It isn't enough to toughen his soul; you must toughen his muscles too. The soul is crushed if the body doesn't help carry the load — it's too much work to manage both jobs alone. I know to my own cost how mine groans under the burden, saddled as it is with a body so delicate and so quick to complain, forever leaning its whole weight on her. And I often notice in my reading that our masters pass off as greatness of soul and strength of mind what is really toughness of skin and hardness of bone. I have seen men, women, and children born with constitutions so hard and insensible that a sound beating means less to them than a flick of the finger would mean to me — people who won't cry out, flinch, or shrink under a serious thrashing. When wrestlers imitate the philosophers in endurance, it's strength of sinew, not stoutness of heart. Now, being trained to work hard is being trained to bear pain: labor, as Cicero says, hardens us against suffering.

A boy must be broken in to the toil and roughness of exercise — trained up to bear dislocations, colic, cautery, even prison and the rack. For by bad luck he may come to the worst of these; the way the world goes, such things fall on the good as well as the bad. We have the proof in front of us: in our present civil war, whoever draws his sword against the laws threatens the honestest men with the whip and the noose.

There is this too: at home, the tutor's authority — which ought to be absolute over the boy in his charge — is constantly checked and undermined by the presence of the parents. Add to that the deference the whole household pays the boy as the master's son, and his awareness of the estate and the greatness he stands to inherit, and you have, in my opinion, no small handicaps at that tender age.

And yet even in this conversing with men that I spoke of just now, I've noticed a vice: instead of gathering observations from others, we make it our whole business to put ourselves on display. We're more concerned with showing off our own merchandise than with stocking up on new goods. Silence and modesty are very useful qualities in conversation. So train the boy to be sparing with his knowledge once he has it — a careful manager of it — and not to object to or correct every silly remark or ridiculous story told in his presence. It is rude and unbecoming to carp at everything that doesn't suit our own taste. Let him be content with correcting himself, and not seem to condemn in others everything he wouldn't do himself, or to set himself against common custom. As Seneca says, one can be wise without showing off, wise without arousing envy.

Let him avoid those overbearing airs of authority, and that childish ambition of wanting to seem more polished and accomplished than he is — by which carriage he will only expose himself — and of treating every chance to interrupt and correct as too precious to pass up, hoping to come away with a reputation for being something special. Just as only great poets get to take poetic license, only great and illustrious souls get to set themselves above custom. If Socrates and Aristippus sometimes acted against the manners of their time, Cicero warns, no one should think he has the same permission: they earned that liberty by great and divine merits.

Teach him not to enter an argument or a debate except against an opponent worthy of him — and even then, not to use every little trick that might happen to serve, but only the arguments that serve him best. Teach him to be choosy in selecting his reasons, to despise irrelevance, and therefore to prize brevity. Above all, teach him to surrender and submit to truth the moment he spots it, whether it appears in his opponent's argument or in his own second thoughts. He's not auditioning for a professor's chair, where one performs a set racket of words and syllogisms; he is bound to no argument except as his own judgment approves it. Nor is arguing a trade where the freedom to recant and change your mind on reflection gets sold for cash. As Cicero says, no necessity compels him to defend everything that has been prescribed and ordered.

If his tutor shares my temperament, he will shape the boy's will to be a very good and loyal subject to his prince, deeply attached to him and brave in his quarrel — but he will also cool in him any desire to be tied to that service by anything beyond public duty. Setting aside the other costs to the liberty every honest man ought to keep, a judgment bought and mortgaged by personal obligations is either blinded and less free to do its work, or stained with ingratitude and indiscretion. A man who is purely a courtier can have neither the power nor the will to speak or think anything but well of a master who, out of so many millions of other subjects, picked him by hand to feed and advance. That favor, and the profit flowing from it, cannot help corrupting his freedom and dazzling him — and not without some show of reason. You can hear it: these people speak a different language from everyone else in the nation, and not much of what they say in that courtly dialect is to be believed.

Let his conscience and his virtue shine through his speech, with reason as their only guide. Make him understand that admitting an error he has discovered in his own argument — even if no one but himself has noticed it — is an act of judgment and honesty, which are the main things he is after. Obstinacy and contentiousness are common qualities, most at home in small souls. To revise and correct yourself, to abandon a bad position at the very height and heat of the argument — these are rare, great, philosophical qualities.

Advise him, when he's in company, to keep his eyes and ears in every corner. I find that the seats of greatest honor are usually grabbed by the men with the least in them, and that great fortune and great ability rarely travel together. I've been at tables where, while the people at the head of the room were discussing the beauty of the tapestries or the flavor of the wine, plenty of fine things said at the lower end were lost and thrown away. Let him sound out the talent of every man — a peasant, a bricklayer, a passing stranger. Something can be learned from each of them in his own line, and something from their talk will turn out useful one day or another. Even the folly and foolishness of others will teach him something: by noticing everyone's graces and manners, he'll build a taste for the good ones and a contempt for the bad.

Plant in his mind an honest curiosity to inquire into everything. Whatever is singular or rare near wherever he happens to be, let him go and see it — a fine house, a noble fountain, an eminent man, the field of an ancient battle, the routes of Caesar and Charlemagne. Let him learn, as Propertius has it, which land lies locked in frost, which crumbles with heat, which wind sets fairest for Italy. Let him look into the manners, the revenues, and the alliances of princes — things very pleasant to learn and very useful to know.

In this conversing with men, I mean also — and chiefly — those who live only in the records of history. Through books he will keep company with the great and heroic souls of the best ages. It's an idle and empty study for those who make it so by going at it carelessly; but for those who do it with care and attention, it's a study of priceless fruit and value — the only study, Plato tells us, that the Spartans kept for themselves. What profit won't he reap, where the business of men is concerned, from reading the Lives of Plutarch? But let my tutor remember what his teaching is really aimed at, and stamp on his pupil's memory not so much the date of the fall of Carthage as the characters of Hannibal and Scipio; not so much where Marcellus died as why it was unworthy of his duty that he died there. Let him teach him not so much the narrative of history as how to judge it.

History, in my opinion, is the subject we each take to with the most different measures. I have read a hundred things in Livy that another man hasn't read, or hasn't noticed; and Plutarch read a hundred more there than I could ever find — more, perhaps, than the author ever put in. For some it's purely a grammar exercise; for others it's the very anatomy of philosophy, by which we get into the most hidden parts of our nature. There are in Plutarch many long discussions richly worth careful reading, for in that kind of writing he is, to my mind, the greatest master of all. But there are a thousand other matters he has only brushed against — where he merely points his finger to show which way we may go if we like, or contents himself with one sharp hit at the very nerve of the question, leaving us to grope out the rest. Take the place where he says that the people of Asia served a single master because they could not pronounce one syllable: No. That remark may well have given La Boétie the matter and the occasion for his "Voluntary Servitude." Even watching Plutarch pick out some slight action in a man's life, or a single word that hardly seems worth that much, is a whole discourse in itself. It's our loss that men of understanding are so immoderately fond of brevity; their reputation gains by it, no doubt, but in the meantime we are the poorer. Plutarch would rather we praised his judgment than his knowledge; he would rather leave us hungry to read more than stuffed with what we've already read. He knew very well that you can say too much even on the best subject — and that Alexandridas was right to rebuke the man who made excellent but endless speeches to the Spartan magistrates: "Stranger, you say what you should say, but not the way you should say it." People with thin, spare bodies pad themselves out with clothes; people thin on matter pad themselves out with words.

The human understanding is wonderfully brightened by daily conversation with men, for otherwise we sit squeezed and heaped up inside ourselves, with our sight reaching no farther than the end of our own nose. When someone asked Socrates where he was from, he did not answer "Athens" but "the world." He, whose imagination was fuller and wider than ours, embraced the whole world as his city and extended his acquaintance, his society, and his affection to all mankind — not like us, who look no farther than our feet. When the vines of my village are nipped by frost, my parish priest concludes at once that the wrath of God has gone out against the entire human race, and that the cannibals must already have caught the pip. Seeing our civil wars, who doesn't cry out that the machinery of the world is coming apart and the Day of Judgment is at hand — never considering that far worse has been seen before, and that meanwhile, in a thousand other parts of the earth, people are having a perfectly lovely time? For my part, considering the license and impunity that always ride along with such upheavals, I'm amazed the wars are so moderate, and that there isn't more mischief done. To the man with hailstones rattling about his ears, the whole hemisphere seems one storm and tempest. It's like the ridiculous Savoyard who said, very gravely, that if that simpleton of a French king had only managed his fortune properly, he might in time have risen to be steward of the household to the Duke of Savoy. The fellow's shallow imagination simply could not conceive of anything greater than a Duke of Savoy. And in truth we are all in this error without noticing it — an error of great weight and great harm. But whoever can call up before his mind's eye, as in a painting, the great image of our mother Nature in her full majesty and luster; whoever reads in her face her endless, constant variety; whoever finds himself in that picture — and not just himself but a whole kingdom — as no more than the prick of a fine pencil point against the whole: that man alone sizes things at their true scale and grandeur.

This great world — which some multiply further, as species under one genus — is the mirror in which we must look to know ourselves from the right angle. In short, this is the book I would have my young gentleman study with the most attention. So many temperaments, so many sects, so many judgments, opinions, laws, and customs teach us to judge our own sanely, and train our understanding to recognize its imperfection and its natural weakness — which is no small lesson. So many upheavals of states and kingdoms, so many turns and revolutions of public fortune, teach us not to make any great marvel of our own. So many great names, so many famous victories and conquests drowned and swallowed in oblivion, make ridiculous our hope of immortalizing our name by capturing ten cavalrymen or some henhouse known to history only because it fell. The pride and pomp of so many foreign courts, the swollen majesty of so many grandeurs, steady our eyes to bear the luster of our own without blinking. So many millions of men buried before us encourage us not to be afraid of going to find such good company in the other world. And so on with everything else. Pythagoras used to say that our life is like the great crowded gathering at the Olympic games: some exercise their bodies to carry off the glory of the prize; others bring merchandise to sell for profit; and some — by no means the worst sort — come for no other advantage than to watch, to consider how and why everything is done, and to be spectators of other men's lives, the better to judge and steer their own.

To these examples may fitly be joined all the most profitable lessons of philosophy, which all human actions ought to take as their rule. A pupil shall be taught — as Persius puts it — what it is right to wish for; what use coined money really has; how much we owe to our country and our dear ones; what God commanded us to be, and what post we hold in the human scheme; what we are, and to what purpose we were born. He shall be taught what it is to know and what it is to be ignorant; what the aim of study ought to be; what valor, temperance, and justice are; the difference between ambition and greed, servitude and obedience, license and liberty; by what marks a man may recognize true, solid contentment; how far death, pain, and disgrace really deserve our fear; and, as Virgil says, how to shun each hardship — or how to bear it.

He shall be taught by what secret springs we move, and what drives our shifting moods and indecisions. For it seems to me that the first ideas his understanding should be steeped in ought to be the ones that regulate his conduct and his sense — that teach him to know himself, and how to die well and how to live well. Among the liberal arts, let's begin with the art that makes us free. They all serve life in some measure, as everything else does in some measure too; but let's choose the one that serves it directly and by profession. If we could once confine the duties of human life within their just and natural limits, we'd find that most of the sciences in circulation are of no use to us — and that even in the useful ones there are useless stretches and hollows we'd do better to leave alone, keeping to Socrates' direction and limiting our studies to what has real and honest utility. Dare to be wise, says Horace — begin! The man who keeps putting off the hour of living well is like the bumpkin waiting for the river to flow past before he crosses: but the river flows, and will roll on flowing, to the end of time.

It's great foolishness to teach our children what influence Pisces has, or fierce Leo, or Capricorn washed by the western sea — the science of the stars and the motion of the eighth sphere — before teaching them the science of themselves. What do I care, says Anacreon, for the Pleiades or the stars of Taurus? Anaximenes wrote to Pythagoras: "Why should I trouble myself hunting out the secrets of the stars, with death or slavery always before my eyes?" — for the kings of Persia were just then preparing to invade his country. Everyone ought to say the same: "Assaulted as I am by ambition, greed, rashness, and superstition, with so many other enemies of life inside me, shall I sit and ponder the wobbles of the world?"

After he has been taught what will make him wiser and better, then you may entertain him with the elements of logic, physics, geometry, and rhetoric; and whichever science he then leans to, his judgment being already formed and fit to choose, he will quickly master. His instruction should come sometimes by conversation, sometimes by reading. Sometimes his tutor will put the author himself into his hands — whichever he judges right for him — and sometimes only the marrow and substance of it. And if the tutor isn't bookish enough himself to find all the fine passages the books hold for his purpose, some man of learning can be attached to him, to supply on every occasion the provisions he needs and pass them along to the pupil. Who can doubt that this way of teaching is easier and more natural than the method of Gaza, where the precepts are so tangled and harsh, and the words so empty, so fleshless and meaningless, that there is nothing to take hold of — nothing that quickens and lifts the mind — whereas here the mind has something to bite on and digest? This fruit is not only far fairer beyond comparison; it also ripens far earlier.

It's a thousand pities that matters stand as they do in our age: that philosophy, even among intelligent men, has become an empty and fantastic name, a thing of no use and no value either in opinion or in fact. I think the cause is those quibbles and petty sophistries that have camped in all the approaches to her. People are badly wrong to paint her for children as inaccessible, with a frowning, grim, terrifying face. Who put that false mask on her — that pale, haggard, ghostly look? There is nothing gayer, brisker, merrier — I almost said friskier. She preaches nothing but holiday and good cheer; a sad, anxious face is proof she doesn't live in that house. When the grammarian Demetrius found a knot of philosophers sitting and chatting together in the temple at Delphi, he said to them, "Either I'm much mistaken, or by your cheerful, pleasant faces you're in no very deep discussion." To which one of them, Heracleon of Megara, replied: "Knitting your brows belongs to people puzzling over whether some verb doubles its letter, or hunting the derivation of comparatives and superlatives. Philosophical talk delights and cheers those who have it; it never sours them or casts them down." As Juvenal says, you can read the hidden torments of a mind in a sick body — and read its joys too: the face takes either look from the mind.

The soul that houses philosophy ought to be so healthy that it makes the body healthy too. Her peace and contentment should shine all the way to the surface; her ease should mold the outward bearing into her own shape, arming it with a graceful confidence, a lively and cheerful carriage, a calm and contented face. The most unmistakable sign of wisdom is constant cheerfulness; her state, like everything above the moon, is always clear and serene. It is Baroco and Baralipton — those barbarous passwords of school logic — that leave their disciples so grubby and smoke-stained, not she; they don't know her except by hearsay. What! She is the one who calms the storms and tempests of the soul, who teaches famine and fever to laugh and sing — and not by some imaginary celestial machinery, but by natural, visible reasons. She has virtue for her goal; and virtue is not, as the schoolmen claim, perched on the summit of a steep, rugged, unclimbable cliff. Those who have actually approached her find just the opposite: she lives on a fair, fertile, flowering plain, from which she sees everything spread below her; and anyone can reach her who knows the way, by shady, green, sweetly blooming avenues, down a slope as gentle and smooth as the curve of the sky. It's because they have never visited this supreme virtue — beautiful, triumphant, loving, as delicious as she is courageous, the sworn and implacable enemy of anxiety, sorrow, fear, and constraint, with nature for her guide and fortune and pleasure for her companions — that they have gone and invented, out of their own weakness, this ridiculous image of her: sad, quarrelsome, sulky, threatening, scowling. They've set her on a rock, off by herself among thorns and brambles, and made of her a hobgoblin to frighten people.
