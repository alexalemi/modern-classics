To the Reader

Reader, this is an honest book. It warns you right at the door that I wrote it for private, family reasons and nothing more. I gave no thought to being useful to you, or to winning any glory for myself — my powers aren't up to a project like that. I wrote it for the particular benefit of my relatives and friends, so that when they've lost me (which must happen soon), they can recover here some traces of my character and my moods, and so keep their knowledge of me more whole and more alive.

If I'd been out to court the world's favor, I would have dressed myself up in borrowed beauty. Instead, I want to be seen here in my simple, natural, everyday manner, without strain or artifice — because it is myself I'm painting. My faults will show up here as they really are, along with my native shape, as far as public decency allows. If I had lived among those nations that are said to dwell still under the sweet liberty of nature's first laws, I assure you I would very gladly have painted myself entire, and entirely naked.

So, reader: I am myself the matter of my book. There's no reason you should spend your free time on a subject so frivolous and so empty.

Therefore farewell.

Montaigne, this first of March, 1580.
