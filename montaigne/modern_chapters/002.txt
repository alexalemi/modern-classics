Of Liars

There is no man alive with less business talking about memory than me. I can find hardly a trace of one in myself, and I doubt there's another in the world so spectacularly unreliable as mine. All my other faculties are ordinary and average; but in this one I think I'm so rare and singular that I deserve to be famous for it.

Besides the natural inconvenience I suffer from it — and considering how necessary memory is, Plato was right to call it a great and powerful goddess — there's this: where I come from, when people want to say a man has no sense, they say he has no memory. So when I complain about the defect of mine, they correct me and refuse to believe me, as if I were accusing myself of being a fool. They can't see the difference between memory and understanding — which makes my case worse. But they do me wrong. Experience shows us daily, if anything, the opposite: that an excellent memory tends to come paired with shaky judgment.

They do me another wrong too. I am better at friendship than at anything else, and yet the very words that describe my infirmity make me out to be ungrateful. They question my heart on the evidence of my memory, and turn a natural defect into a defect of conscience. "He forgot this request," someone says, "or that promise. He doesn't remember his friends anymore. He forgot to say this, or do that, or keep quiet about the other thing, for my sake." True enough, I forget plenty. But neglect something a friend has actually entrusted to me? Never. It should be enough that I feel the misery and inconvenience of the thing, without being branded with malice on top of it — a vice that couldn't be more foreign to my temperament.

Still, I get a few comforts out of my infirmity. First, it's a flaw that has helped me correct a worse one that would easily have grown on me: ambition. A failing memory is unbearable in anyone who takes up public business. Also — as nature shows us in similar cases — she has strengthened my other faculties in proportion as she left this one bare. Otherwise I'd have been content to let my mind and judgment rest lazily on other people's reports, without ever putting them to work on their own strength, if other men's ideas and opinions had always been at my fingertips by courtesy of memory. And it keeps me from being talkative — for the warehouse of memory is always better stocked than the warehouse of invention. If my memory had stayed faithful to me, I'd have deafened every friend I have with chatter by now, since the subjects themselves would keep rousing what little gift I have for handling and arranging them, warming me up and pulling me along. That would have been a pity. I've watched it happen to some of my closest friends. Their memories hand them the whole subject, entire and fully present, so they start their story too far back and load it with so many useless details that if the story is good, they smother the good in it — and if it isn't, you're left cursing either the strength of their memory or the weakness of their judgment. It's hard to close off a story and cut it short once you're rolling; nothing shows the strength of a horse like a clean, sudden stop. Even speakers who stay on point — I see them wanting to stop and not managing it. While they hunt around for a graceful exit line, they keep rambling on, dragging through trivialities like men staggering on weak legs. Old men are the most dangerous company of all: they remember the past but forget how many times they've told you about it. I've watched stories from the mouth of a very great lord — genuinely entertaining stories — become unbearable by being repeated a hundred times to the same audience.

The second comfort is that I'm slower to remember the injuries people do me. As one ancient put it, I'd need an official register of insults — or a prompter, like Darius, who, so he wouldn't forget the offense the Athenians had given him, had a page repeat in his ear three times every time he sat down to dinner: "Sir, remember the Athenians." And then, too, the places I revisit and the books I reread always smile at me with a fresh novelty.

It's said with good reason that anyone without a sound memory should never take up the trade of lying. I know the grammarians draw a line between telling an untruth and telling a lie. To tell an untruth, they say, is to say something false that we ourselves believe is true; but to lie — by the definition of the Latin word our French comes from — is to say something we know in our conscience to be untrue. It's only this second kind of liar I'm talking about here. These people either invent their falsehoods wholesale, or they disguise and alter a true story until it ends up a lie.

When they disguise and alter a story, telling it differently each time to suit themselves, it's very hard for them to escape getting caught sooner or later. The true version of the thing took possession of the memory first — it got lodged there by way of real knowledge — so it keeps presenting itself to the imagination and shoulders out the falsehood, which can never get such firm and settled footing. The circumstances of the true version keep running through the mind and make them forget the fake ones, which have no parentage but their own invention.

When they invent the whole thing from scratch, there's no true impression to collide with the lie, so they would seem to be in less danger of tripping. But even so, an invention is a hollow body with nothing to grip; it slips easily out of any memory that isn't very sure of itself. I've had some amusing proof of this, at the expense of those professionals who shape everything they say to fit the business at hand — or to please whichever great man they're talking to. The circumstances they're so willing to enslave their word and conscience to keep changing, so their story has to change with them: they tell one man a thing is this, another that it's that, painting the same fact different colors for different audiences. And if those men ever compare notes and discover the trick, what becomes of the whole fine art? Besides, they trip themselves up, ridiculously, all the time — for what memory could possibly be big enough to hold all the different shapes they've forged for one and the same subject? I've known plenty of men in my day who envied the reputation this kind of cleverness brings. What they don't see is that once you have the reputation, the trick no longer works.

In plain truth, lying is an accursed vice. We are men, and hold together as men, only by our word. If we really saw the horror and the weight of lying, we would hunt it down with fire and sword — and more justly than we hunt other crimes. I notice that parents routinely punish their children, and not very sensibly, for innocent little mistakes, tormenting them over thoughtless pranks that leave no mark and carry no consequence. Meanwhile lying — and a step below it, stubbornness — are the faults that ought to be fought from the moment they appear and at every stage of growth, because they grow up right along with the child. Once a tongue gets the knack of lying, you can't imagine how nearly impossible it is to break it of the habit. That's how it happens that otherwise honest men end up enslaved to this vice. I have a perfectly decent lad as my tailor, and I have never once caught him telling the truth — not even when it would have worked in his favor.

If falsehood, like truth, had only one face, we'd be on better terms with it: we could simply take the opposite of whatever the liar said as certain. But the reverse of truth has a hundred thousand faces, and an open field without edge or limit. The Pythagoreans make good finite and certain, and evil infinite and uncertain. There are a thousand ways to miss the target; there's only one way to hit it. For my own part, I hold this vice in such horror that I'm not sure I could bring my conscience to save me even from obvious, extreme danger by telling one brazen, deliberate lie.

One of the old church fathers says that a dog we know is better company than a man whose language we don't understand — so true is it, as Pliny adds, that a foreigner who shares no tongue with us hardly counts as a fellow man. And how much less sociable than silence is false speech?

King Francis I used to boast that he had used exactly this weapon to corner Francesco Taverna, ambassador of Francesco Sforza, Duke of Milan, and a man famous in his day for his skill in talking. Taverna had been sent to make his master's excuses to His Majesty over an affair of very great consequence, which was this. The King, wanting to keep some channel into Italy — from which he had recently been driven out — and especially into the duchy of Milan, had decided to keep a gentleman of his own at the Duke's court: an ambassador in fact, but in appearance a private person, pretending to be there on his own personal business. For the Duke depended far more on the Emperor — especially just then, while negotiating a marriage with the Emperor's niece, daughter of the King of Denmark and now the dowager of Lorraine — and couldn't be seen dealing and conferring with us without serious damage to himself. A Milanese gentleman named Merveille, one of the King's own equerries, was judged right for the job, and was sent off with secret credentials and the instructions of an ambassador, along with letters of recommendation to the Duke about his own private affairs, to mask and color the business. But he stayed at that court so long that the Emperor eventually got wind of his real role there — which, as we suppose, is what caused what followed. On the pretext of a murder charge, the Duke had Merveille's trial dispatched in two days, and his head struck off at night, in prison.

Messire Francesco arrived with a long, counterfeit account of the affair all prepared — for the King had appealed to every prince in Christendom, and to the Duke himself, demanding satisfaction — and was given his audience at the morning council. To support his case, he laid out several plausible defenses of the deed: his master had never taken Merveille for anything but a private gentleman and his own subject, there purely on his own business; he denied absolutely ever having heard that the man belonged to the King's household, or that the King even knew him — let alone took him for an ambassador. The King, in his turn, pressed him with objections and questions, challenged him from every side, and finally cornered him with this: why, then, was the execution carried out at night, and as if by stealth? At which the poor flustered ambassador, trying to wriggle out gracefully, answered that the Duke, out of respect for His Majesty, would have been very sorry to see such an execution performed in daylight. You can guess the dressing-down he got when he came home, for tripping so clumsily in front of a prince with as fine a nose as King Francis.

Pope Julius II sent an ambassador to the King of England to stir him up against King Francis. The ambassador had his audience, and the King, before giving an answer, dwelt on the difficulties he would face in mounting a force big enough to attack so powerful a king, and gave some reasons for them. The ambassador, very unwisely, replied that he had considered the same difficulties himself, and had said as much to the Pope. From this remark — so squarely opposed to the whole point of his errand, which was to push England straight into war — the King of England drew the first hint of what he afterward found to be true: that this ambassador, in his own heart, leaned toward the French side. The King advised the Pope; the ambassador's estate was confiscated when he got home, and he came very close to losing his head.
