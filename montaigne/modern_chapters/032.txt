Of Experience
(Part 4 of 5)

There is no profession as pleasant as the soldier's. It is noble in its practice — for valor is the sturdiest, proudest, most generous of all the virtues — and noble in its cause: no service is more universal or more just than defending the peace and greatness of your country. You enjoy the company of so many noble, young, active men; the daily sight of great and tragic spectacles; the freedom of plain, artless conversation; a masculine, unceremonious way of living; the variety of a thousand different actions; the brave music of war that fires both your ears and your soul; the honor of the work — even its hardships and difficulties, which Plato rates so lightly that in his Republic he gives women and children a share of them. You volunteer for particular exploits and dangers according to your own judgment of their brilliance and importance, and you find that even life itself may be excusably spent there — for, as the poet says, it is a fine thing to die with sword in hand.

To fear dangers shared by such a multitude of men — not to dare what so many kinds of souls dare, what a whole people dares — is for a heart poor and mean beyond all measure. Company gives courage even to children. If other men outdo you in knowledge, in grace, in strength, in fortune, you have outside causes to blame; but if you give way to them in steadiness of soul, you have no one to blame but yourself. Death is more abject, more lingering, more painful in bed than in battle; fevers and catarrhs are as agonizing and as fatal as a musket shot. Whoever has braced himself to bear the accidents of ordinary life has no need to screw up extra courage to be a soldier. To live, my Lucilius, as Seneca says, is to be a soldier.

I don't remember ever having had the itch — and yet scratching is one of nature's sweetest gratifications, and always within reach. But repentance follows too close behind. I practice it mostly on my ears, which itch from time to time on the inside.

I came into the world with all my senses entire, almost to perfection. My stomach is conveniently good, and so are my head and my lungs; they generally hold up even at the height of a fever. I have passed the age that some nations — not without reason — fixed as so proper a limit of life that they allowed no one to exceed it. And yet I still have stretches, short and inconstant as they are, so clean and sound that they fall little short of the health and cheerfulness of my youth. I'm not talking about vigor and sprightliness; it isn't reasonable to expect those to follow me past their boundary. As Horace says, I can no longer stand waiting at a lady's door in the rain.

My face and eyes immediately give my condition away. All my changes begin there, and they look somewhat worse than they really are; my friends often pity me before I feel the cause in myself. My mirror doesn't frighten me, because even in my youth it happened more than once that I wore a sickly complexion of bad omen without any serious consequence — so that the doctors, finding nothing inside to answer for the change outside, put it down to the mind, to some secret passion gnawing at me within. They were wrong. If my body took orders from me as well as my mind does, we would travel a little more comfortably together. My mind at the time was not only free of trouble but full of joy and satisfaction, as it usually is — half by its constitution, half by its own design. As Ovid says, the sickness of the body does not infect the mind.

I am of the opinion that this even temper of my soul has often helped my body up from its lapses. The body is often down; but if my soul is not exactly brisk and gay, it is at least tranquil and at rest. I had a quartan fever for four or five months that made me look completely wretched; my mind the whole time was, if not calm, at least pleasant. If the pain stays outside me, the weakness and languor don't much depress me. I can name various bodily collapses that horrify me even to mention, and yet I fear them less than a thousand passions and agitations of the mind that I see all around me. I have made up my mind that I am done with running; it's enough that I can crawl along. Nor do I complain of the natural decay I feel in myself — who is surprised, asks Juvenal, to find a goiter in the Alps? — any more than I regret that my term will not be as long and entire as an oak's.

I have no complaint against my imagination. Few thoughts in my life have so much as broken my sleep, except thoughts of desire — which woke me without afflicting me. I dream seldom, and then of chimeras and fantastic things, usually spun out of pleasant thoughts, more ridiculous than sad. And I believe it is true that dreams are faithful interpreters of our inclinations — but it takes art to sort them and understand them. It's small wonder, as the old poet says, that the things men practice, think about, care for, see, and do while awake should run through their heads when they sleep.

Plato goes further and says it is the office of wisdom to draw prophetic instruction about the future from dreams. I don't know about that — except that Socrates, Xenophon, and Aristotle, men of unimpeachable authority, relate wonderful instances of it. Historians say the Atlantes never dream; they also never eat anything that has been killed, and I add that fact because it may be the very reason they don't dream — for Pythagoras prescribed a certain diet to produce the right kind of dreams. My own are very gentle, without any thrashing of the body or talking aloud. I have seen plenty of people in my time wonderfully disturbed by theirs. Theon the philosopher walked in his sleep, and so did Pericles' servant — on the tiles, on the very roof of the house.

At table I hardly ever choose my dish; I take the nearest at hand, and I'm reluctant to trade it for another. A confusion of meats and a clatter of dishes displease me as much as any other kind of confusion. I am easily satisfied with few dishes, and I'm an enemy of Favorinus's notion that at a feast they should snatch away the meat you like and set a plate of something else in front of you — that it's a pitiful supper if you haven't stuffed your guests with the rumps of assorted fowl, and that only the songbird deserves to be eaten whole. I usually eat salted meats, yet I prefer my bread without salt, and my baker, against the custom of the region, never sends any other kind to my table. In my childhood, what they mostly had to correct in me was a refusal of the things children usually love best — sugar, sweets, marzipan. My tutor fought this aversion to delicacies as a kind of delicacy itself; and in truth that's all it is: pickiness of taste, whatever it fastens on. Whoever cures a child of a stubborn attachment to brown bread, bacon, or garlic, cures him of pampering his palate. There are people who play at plainness by pining for beef and ham among the partridges. Very fine — that is the daintiness of the dainty. It's the taste of a pampered fortune that loses its relish for ordinary, familiar things; the kind of luxury, as Seneca says, by which wealth amuses itself with the tedium of riches. Refusing to enjoy what another man is enjoying, fussing over what you eat — that is the essence of this vice; as Horace puts it, if you cannot be content with herbs in a small dish for your supper.

There is, granted, this difference: it's better to bind your appetite to the things easiest to come by. But it is always a vice to bind yourself at all. I once called a kinsman of mine overdelicate because his time in our galleys had untaught him the use of a bed and the habit of undressing to sleep.

If I had sons, I would gladly wish them my upbringing. The good father God gave me — who has nothing from me but the acknowledgment of his goodness, though truly it is a hearty one — sent me from my cradle to be raised in a poor village of his, and kept me there through my nursing and beyond, training me to the lowest and most common way of living. A well-disciplined stomach, as Seneca says, is a great part of liberty. Never take the rearing of your children into your own hands — and still less hand it over to your wives. Leave their formation to fortune, under common and natural laws; leave it to custom to train them up to frugality and hardship, so that they will have to climb down from rigor rather than up to it. My father's scheme aimed at something else as well: to make me familiar with the people, and with the condition of those who most need our help. He reckoned I should be made to look toward the men who stretch out their arms to me rather than those who turn their backs on me. And for the same reason he chose people of the humblest fortune to hold me at the baptismal font — to bind and attach me to them.

His plan has not turned out badly. Whether because there is more honor in stooping, or out of a natural compassion that has enormous power over me, I lean toward the humbler sort of people. Whatever faction I would condemn in our wars, I would condemn it more sharply when it was flourishing and successful; it will go some way toward reconciling me to it when I see it miserable and crushed. How willingly I admire the fine spirit of Cheilonis, daughter and wife of kings of Sparta. While her husband Cleombrotus, in the city's upheaval, had the upper hand over her father Leonidas, she played the good daughter — stuck close to her father in all his misery and exile, set against the conqueror. But the moment the luck of war turned, she changed her allegiance with the change of fortune and bravely took her husband's side, following him wherever his ruin carried him. She admitted, it seems to me, no other choice than to join whichever side needed her most, where her compassion could do the most good. I am naturally more inclined to follow the example of Flamininus, who gave his help to those who needed him rather than to those with the power to repay him, than that of Pyrrhus, whose disposition was to grovel before the great and lord it over the poor.

Long sittings at table both annoy me and do me harm — perhaps because I formed the habit as a child, but I keep eating the whole time I sit. So at my own house, though our meals are on the short side, I usually sit down a little after the others, in the manner of Augustus. But I don't imitate him in rising before the rest. On the contrary, I love to sit on a long while after and listen to the talk — provided I take no part in it. Talking on a full stomach tires me and does me harm, though I find it thoroughly wholesome and pleasant to argue and exercise my voice before dinner.

The ancient Greeks and Romans had better sense than we do. Unless some extraordinary business prevented them, they set aside for eating — which is one of the principal actions of life — many hours, and the better part of the night. They ate and drank more deliberately than we do, who rush through everything at a gallop, and they stretched this natural pleasure out into more leisure and better use, seasoning it with profitable conversation.

The people whose business it is to look after me could very easily keep me from anything they think will hurt me, for in such matters I never crave or miss what I don't see. But once a thing is in front of my eyes, persuading me to abstain is a waste of breath. So when I mean to fast, I have to be kept away from the supper table entirely and given just enough for a prescribed light meal; if I sit down at table, I forget my resolution. When I order my cook to dress some dish a different way, my whole household knows what it means: my stomach is out of order, and I won't touch a thing.

I like most meats — those that can stand it — barely boiled or roasted, and very well hung, even, for some, downright gone. Nothing offends me, generally, except toughness; toward every other quality I am as patient and indifferent as any man I've known. So, contrary to the common taste, even fish often strikes me as too fresh and too firm. Not for lack of teeth — mine have always been good, even excellent, and age hasn't yet begun to threaten them; I've always rubbed them with a napkin every morning, and before and after dinner. God is kind to those whom He makes die by degrees. That is the one benefit of old age: the final death will be that much less complete and less painful — it will kill only half a man, or a quarter. One tooth just fell out, without pulling and without pain; it had reached the natural end of its term. That part of my being, and several others, are already dead — others half dead — and they were among the most active, in the front rank during my vigorous years. This is how I melt and slip away from myself. What folly it would be in my understanding to feel the height of this fall, already so far along, as if it were from the very top! I hope I won't. Truly, I draw one principal consolation from thinking on my death: that it will be just and natural, and that from now on I can neither ask nor hope of Destiny any favor in this matter that isn't an unlawful one. Men persuade themselves that in former ages lives were longer, as men were taller. But they deceive themselves: Solon, who belonged to those elder times, sets the limit of life at seventy years. And I — who have so thoroughly and so universally worshipped that golden mean of the time gone by, and concluded that the most moderate measure is the most perfect — shall I now claim an immeasurable, prodigious old age? Whatever runs against the course of nature may be troublesome, but whatever comes according to her should always be pleasant. All things done according to nature, says Cicero, are to be counted good.

And so, says Plato, the death that comes by wounds or disease is violent, but the death that comes upon us with old age leading the way is of all deaths the easiest — in a certain sense, even delicious. Young men, says Cicero, have their lives taken from them by force; old men, by ripeness.

Death mixes and blends itself with life all the way through. Decay runs ahead of its hour and shoulders its way into the very course of our growth. I have portraits of myself at twenty-five and at thirty-five. I compare them with one painted recently: how many times over it is no longer me! How much further is my present image from those than from the image of my dying! It is abusing nature to make her trot so far that she is forced to quit us — to abandon our conduct, our eyes, teeth, legs, and all the rest to the mercy of a foreign, haggard countenance, and hand us over into the keeping of art, being weary of following us herself.

I'm not excessively fond of either salads or fruit — except melons. My father hated every kind of sauce; I love them all. Eating too much hurts me; but as for the quality of what I eat, I don't yet know for certain that any food disagrees with me, nor have I noticed that full moon or new, autumn or spring, has any effect on me. We have movements in us that are fickle and unaccountable. Radishes, for example, I first found agreeable to my stomach, then nauseating, and now agreeable again. In several other things I find my stomach and appetite shifting the same way: I've changed back and forth from white wine to claret, from claret to white.

I am a great lover of fish, and so I make my fast-days feasts and my feast-days fasts. And I believe what some people say, that fish is easier to digest than meat. As I make it a matter of conscience not to eat meat on fish-days, so my taste makes it a matter of conscience not to mix fish and meat: the difference between them seems too wide.

From my youth I have sometimes skipped a meal — either to sharpen my appetite for the next day (for as Epicurus fasted and ate lean to teach his pleasure to do without abundance, I do it the other way round, to train my pleasure to make better and merrier use of abundance); or I fasted to keep my vigor fresh for some work of body or mind, for both of those are cruelly dulled in me by a full stomach. And above all I hate that stupid coupling of so healthy and sprightly a goddess with that little belching god, bloated with the fumes of his liquor. Or I fasted to settle a sick stomach; or for lack of fit company — for I say, as that same Epicurus did, that one should not so much consider what one eats as with whom one eats it. And I commend Chilo for refusing to commit himself to Periander's feast until he learned who the other guests would be. No dish is so sweet to me, no sauce so appetizing, as the one drawn from good company. I think it healthier to eat at more leisure and eat less, and to eat more often. But I would have appetite and hunger consulted: I would take no pleasure in being fed three or four meager, rationed meals a day, on a doctor's schedule. Who will guarantee me that the good appetite I have in the morning will still be there at supper? Let us old fellows especially seize the first opportune hour of eating, and leave the hopes and forecasts to the almanac-makers. The ripest fruit of my health is pleasure; let us hold fast to the present and the known. I avoid rigid consistency in these laws of fasting. Whoever wants a habit to serve him, let him avoid continuing it: we harden in it, our strength falls asleep there; six months later you'll find your stomach so broken in that all you've gained is the loss of your liberty to do otherwise — except at your own cost.

I keep my legs and thighs no warmer in winter than in summer: one simple pair of silk stockings, nothing more. I have allowed myself, for relief of my colds, to keep my head warmer, and my belly on account of my colic. But my ailments got used to these measures in a few days and turned up their noses at my ordinary provisions. From a coif we soon climb to a kerchief over it, from a plain cap to a quilted hat; the padding of the doublet must no longer serve as mere ornament; you must add a hare's skin or a vulture's skin, and a skullcap under the hat. Follow this staircase and you'll go a fine way. I will do nothing of the sort, and would gladly undo the little I've begun. If you fall into some new trouble, all that gear is wasted — you're used to it; go find something else. This is how men ruin themselves who let themselves be pestered with these enforced, superstitious regimens: they must add something more, and then something more after that. There is no end to it.

For the sake of our affairs and our pleasures, it is far more convenient to do as the ancients did — skip dinner and put off good cheer until the hour of retirement and rest, so as not to break up the day; and that's what I used to do. For health, on the other hand, I have since found by experience that it's better to dine, and that digestion goes better while we're awake. I'm seldom thirsty, well or sick; my mouth is apt to go dry, but without thirst. Usually I drink only with the thirst that eating creates, well along in the meal. I drink pretty well for a man of my size: in summer, at a tasty meal, I not only exceed the limit of Augustus, who drank precisely three times and no more, but — so as not to offend the rule of Democritus, who forbade stopping at four as an unlucky number — I proceed at need to a fifth glass, about three half-pints. The little glasses are my favorites, and I like to drain them dry, which other people avoid as bad manners. I dilute my wine, sometimes by half, sometimes by a third, with water; and when I'm at home, by an old custom my father's doctor prescribed both for him and for himself, what is meant for me is mixed in the buttery two or three hours before it's brought in. They say Cranaus, king of Attica, invented this custom of watering wine; whether it does any good or not, I've heard argued both ways. I think it more decent and more wholesome for children to drink no wine until sixteen or eighteen. The most usual and common way of living is the most becoming; all peculiarity, in my opinion, is to be avoided. I would hate a German who put water in his wine as much as a Frenchman who drank it pure. Public usage lays down the law in these matters.

I dread fog, and flee from smoke as from the plague. The first repairs I rushed into at my own house were the chimneys and the privies — the common, insufferable defects of all old buildings. And among the hardships of war I count the choking dust they kept us riding in for whole days together. My breathing is free and easy, and my colds mostly pass off without troubling the lungs and without a cough.

The heat of summer is more my enemy than the cold of winter. Besides the discomfort of heat, which is harder to remedy than cold, and besides the beating of the sun's rays on the head, all glittering light hurts my eyes — so that nowadays I couldn't sit at dinner facing a blazing fire.

To dull the whiteness of the paper, back in the days when I read more, I used to lay a piece of glass over my book, and it relieved my eyes considerably. To this hour — at fifty-four — I am a stranger to spectacles, and I can see as far as I ever did, as far as anyone. It's true that toward evening I begin to feel a little dimness and weakness in my sight when I read — an exercise that has always strained my eyes, especially at night. There's one step backward, and a plain one. I shall retreat another; from the second to the third, from the third to the fourth, so softly that I'll be stone blind before I feel my sight aging and failing. So cunningly do the Fates untwist the thread of our lives. So too I suspect my hearing is starting to thicken; and you'll see that when I've half lost it, I'll still be blaming the voices of those who speak to me. You must wind the soul up to a high pitch to make it feel how it is ebbing away.

My walk is quick and firm, and I couldn't say which of the two, my mind or my body, I've had more trouble holding in one place. The preacher who can fix my attention through a whole sermon is a good friend of mine indeed. In ceremonious places, where every face is starched, where I have seen the ladies hold even their eyes perfectly still, I have never managed to keep some part of me from breaking loose. Seated I may be; settled, never. And as for fidgeting — I am never without a switch in my hand, walking or riding. As the philosopher Chrysippus's maid said of her master, that he was only drunk in his legs — for he had the habit of forever kicking them about wherever he sat, and she said it at a time when wine had made all his companions drunk and he felt no change at all — so it might have been said of me from infancy that I had either folly or quicksilver in my feet, so much fidgeting and restlessness is in them wherever I put them.

It is unmannerly, besides being bad for the health and even for the pleasure of eating, to eat as greedily as I do. In my haste I often bite my tongue, and sometimes my fingers. Diogenes, meeting a boy eating that way, gave the boy's tutor a box on the ear. There were men at Rome who taught people to chew, as well as to walk, with a good grace. Eating this way, I lose the leisure of talking, which gives such relish to the table — provided the talk suits it, that is, pleasant and short.

There is jealousy and envy among our pleasures; they cross and hinder one another. Alcibiades, a man who well understood good cheer, banished even music from the table so that it wouldn't disturb the pleasure of conversation — because, as Plato tells us, it is the custom of ordinary people to call in fiddlers and singers to their feasts for lack of the good talk and pleasant discourse with which men of understanding know how to entertain one another. Varro asks this of a dinner party: people of graceful presence and agreeable conversation, neither mute nor chattering; cleanness and delicacy in the food and in the place; and fair weather. The art of dining well is no small art, and its pleasure no small pleasure: neither the greatest generals nor the greatest philosophers have disdained the practice or the science of eating well. My imagination has handed three meals over to the keeping of my memory, which fortune made supremely sweet to me, each at a different moment of my more flourishing years. My present state shuts me out of such things; for every guest, according to the happy temper of body and mind he brings with him, furnishes his own share of the grace and savor.

I, who only crawl upon the earth, hate that inhuman wisdom that would have us despise and resent all care of the body. I count it as much an injustice to turn up our noses at natural pleasures as to take them too much to heart. Xerxes was a blockhead — wrapped in every human delight, he offered a reward to anyone who could invent him new ones. But hardly less a blockhead is the man who cuts off any of the pleasures nature has provided him. We should neither chase them nor flee them; we should receive them. I receive them, I admit, a little too warmly and gladly, and let myself follow my natural inclinations easily enough. There is no need for us to exaggerate their emptiness; they make us feel it quite enough on their own, thanks to our sickly, kill-joy mind, which puts us out of taste with them as it does with itself. The mind treats both itself and everything it takes in now better, now worse, according to its insatiable, wandering, ever-shifting nature.
