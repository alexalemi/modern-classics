Of Idleness

You've seen what happens to fields left fallow. If the soil is rich, it erupts with a hundred thousand kinds of weeds and useless wild growth; to get any honest work out of it, you have to break it up and sow it with the seeds you actually want. And women's bodies, they say, can sometimes produce on their own mere shapeless lumps of flesh, but for a true and natural birth they have to be sown with a different seed. Minds are the same. Unless you give a mind some definite subject to rein it in and hold it steady, it tumbles off into a thousand wild excursions, roving endlessly here and there through the vague fields of imagination — like light reflected off trembling water in a bronze bowl, as Virgil describes it, thrown by the sun or the shining moon, flickering across every surface and darting up to strike the highest ceiling.

And in that restless agitation, there is no piece of nonsense too empty for an idle mind to fill with a thousand fantasies and chimeras — "like a sick man's dreams, conjuring empty phantoms," as Horace says. A mind without a fixed aim loses itself — because, as they say, to be everywhere is to be nowhere.

Not long ago I retired to my own house, resolved, as far as I possibly could, to stay clear of all business and spend in privacy and rest the little time I have left to live. I thought the kindest favor I could do my mind was to leave it in complete leisure to entertain and amuse itself — which I hoped it could manage easily by now, having grown more settled and mature with time. But I find that "leisure always breeds a restless mind," as Lucan says. Quite the contrary of settling down, my mind has bolted like a horse that's thrown its rider, running a far wilder course on its own than any horseman would ever have put it to. It births so many chimeras and fantastic monsters, one on top of another, without order or purpose, that — the better to study their strangeness and absurdity at my leisure — I have begun to set them down in writing. These are my attempts: my essais, as we say in French. And I'm hoping, in time, to make my mind ashamed of itself.
