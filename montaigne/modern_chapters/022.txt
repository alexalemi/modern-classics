Of Cruelty

I think virtue is something other — something nobler — than the good nature we're born with, the simple inclination toward decency. Souls that are well-made and well-bred follow the same path virtue does, and wear the same face in their actions. But the word virtue implies something greater and more active than letting yourself be led, gently and quietly, by a happy disposition down the road of reason.

A man who shrugs off insults out of natural sweetness does a fine and praiseworthy thing. But a man who is stung to the quick by an offense, who arms himself with reason against the raging appetite for revenge, and who after a hard fight finally masters his own passion — that man does far more. The first acts well; the second acts virtuously. Call one action goodness and the other virtue. The very name of virtue presupposes difficulty and struggle; it cannot exist without an opponent. That may be why we call God good, mighty, generous, and just — but we do not call Him virtuous. His works are natural and cost Him nothing.

Many philosophers have held this view — not only Stoics but Epicureans too. And before anyone objects to my lumping them together: I'm borrowing the common opinion of the Epicureans, which is false, whatever the clever quip of Arcesilaus may suggest. When someone needled him that plenty of students left his school for Epicurus's, but never one came the other way, he answered, "Naturally — you can make capons out of roosters, but never roosters out of capons." In truth the Epicurean sect yields nothing to the Stoic in firmness or in the rigor of its opinions and rules. One Stoic — showing more honesty than those debaters who, to score points against Epicurus, put words in his mouth he never thought, twisting his sentences by grammatical hairsplitting into meanings they knew were foreign to his mind and his morals — declared that he had quit the Epicurean school for this reason among others: he found their road too lofty and too steep. For those who are called lovers of pleasure are in fact lovers of honor and justice, and cultivate and practice every virtue — so says Cicero.

These philosophers say it is not enough to have the soul well-placed, well-tempered, and well-disposed to virtue. It is not enough to have our resolutions and our reasoning fixed beyond fortune's reach. We must go looking for occasions to put them to the test. They would seek out pain, hardship, and contempt on purpose, to wrestle with them and keep the soul in training — for, as Seneca says, virtue grows stronger by being attacked.

This is one reason Epaminondas — who belonged to yet a third school — refused riches that fortune offered him by perfectly lawful means. I have poverty to wrestle with, he said; and he held that extreme to the end of his life. Socrates, it seems to me, set himself an even harder drill: he kept a thoroughly bad-tempered, scolding wife for daily practice, which is fencing with sharpened blades.

Metellus, alone of all the Roman senators, undertook by sheer force of virtue to withstand Saturninus, the tribune of the people, who was determined to ram through an unjust law in favor of the commons. Having thereby incurred the death penalty that Saturninus had decreed for dissenters, Metellus told the men leading him to execution something like this: that doing evil was too easy and too base; that doing good where there was no danger was commonplace; but that doing good where there was danger was the proper work of a man of virtue.

These words of Metellus show exactly what I'm getting at: virtue refuses to keep company with ease. The smooth, gentle, downhill path that a sweet natural disposition travels is not the path of true virtue. Virtue demands a rough and stormy passage. She wants either external difficulties to wrestle with, like Metellus had — fortune delighting to break her stride — or internal ones, supplied by the unruly appetites and imperfections of our condition.

I've come this far at my ease. But here it occurs to me that the soul of Socrates, the most perfect that has ever come to my knowledge, would by this rule deserve very little credit. I cannot detect in that man the slightest stirring of a vicious inclination. I cannot imagine any difficulty or constraint in the course of his virtue. I know his reason was so powerful and so sovereign in him that it would never have allowed a vicious appetite even to sprout. To a virtue as elevated as his, I have nothing to oppose. I seem to see him marching at a victorious, triumphant pace, in state and at his ease, with nothing in his way.

If virtue can only shine by fighting contrary appetites, shall we say she cannot exist without the help of vice — that she owes vice her reputation and her honor? And what would become of that brave and generous Epicurean pleasure, which prides itself on nursing virtue tenderly in its lap, giving her shame, fevers, poverty, death, and tortures for toys to play with? If I assume that perfect virtue shows itself in struggling, in patiently enduring pain, in bearing the worst extremities of gout without being shaken from her seat; if I make trouble and difficulty her necessary diet — then what becomes of a virtue raised so high that she not only despises pain but rejoices in it, that finds the stabbing of a sharp colic positively delightful? For that is the virtue the Epicureans established, and many of them proved it by their actions. So have others, who I find outdid the very rules of their own school.

Witness the younger Cato. When I see him dying, tearing out his own bowels, I am not content to believe merely that his soul was free of trouble and horror. I cannot think he simply held the steadiness the Stoic rules prescribed — calm, unmoved, imperturbable. There was something in that man's virtue, it seems to me, too lively and too fresh to stop there. I believe — I have no doubt — that he felt pleasure and delight in so noble an action, and enjoyed it more than anything else he ever did. He quit life rejoicing that he had found a reason to die, as Cicero says. I believe it so thoroughly that I wonder whether he would have wanted to be deprived of the occasion for so brave a deed. And if the goodness that made him care more for the public's interest than his own didn't hold me back, I could easily slip into the opinion that he was grateful to fortune for putting his virtue to so splendid a trial — grateful to that brigand Caesar for trampling underfoot the ancient liberty of his country. I seem to read in that act a kind of exaltation of soul, an extraordinary and manly thrill of pleasure, as he looked on the nobility and height of his enterprise: fiercer for having resolved on death, as Horace puts it.

Not spurred by any hope of glory, as some have shallowly and feebly concluded — that consideration was too small to touch a heart so generous, so proud, so determined — but for the beauty of the thing in itself, which he, who handled the springs of it, saw far more clearly and in its perfection than we can. Philosophy has obliged me by ruling that so brave an action would have been indecently placed in any life but Cato's: that only his life was entitled to end that way. And yet, following reason, he ordered his son and the senators who were with him to manage their affairs differently. Cato, says Cicero, to whom nature had given incredible dignity, and who had strengthened it by lifelong constancy, never wavering in his resolve, was bound to die rather than look upon the face of a tyrant.

Every death ought to be in proportion to the life that went before it. We do not become someone else just because we are dying. I always interpret a death by the life that preceded it. If anyone tells me of a death that looked strong and steadfast, attached to a feeble life, I conclude it came from some feeble cause, in keeping with the life. The ease of Cato's death, the facility in dying that he had acquired by the vigor of his soul — should that dim the luster of his virtue?

And who, with even a drop of true philosophy in his head, can be content to imagine Socrates merely free of fear and passion under arrest, in chains, condemned? Who does not recognize in him not just firmness and constancy — that was his everyday condition — but some new satisfaction, a playful cheerfulness, in his final words and acts? In that little start of pleasure he felt scratching his leg when his irons were taken off, doesn't he show the same serenity and joy of a soul released from past discomforts and on the point of entering into knowledge of things to come?

Cato will pardon me, if he pleases. His death is more tragic, more drawn-out; but this one is — I don't quite know how — finer. Aristippus, hearing people lament that death, said, "May the gods send me one like it."

In the souls of these two men, and of their imitators (for I very much doubt they ever had equals), one sees so perfect a habit of virtue that it became their very constitution. It is no longer a laborious virtue, no longer reason's precepts, held up by a soul straining at the load — it is the very essence of their soul, its natural and ordinary bearing. They made it so by long practice of philosophy's precepts working on a rich, fine nature. The vicious passions that spring up in us find no entrance into them; the force and vigor of their souls stifle and snuff out unruly desires the moment they begin to stir.

Now I don't think it can be doubted that it is nobler, by a high and godlike resolution, to prevent temptations from being born at all — to be so formed to virtue that the very seeds of vice are rooted out — than to stop their progress by main force; and that this second way, catching ourselves surprised by the first stirrings of passion and arming ourselves to stand firm and beat them back, is itself far more generous than simply being equipped with an easy, affable nature that happens to dislike debauchery and vice. For this third and lowest sort of virtue seems to make a man innocent, but not virtuous; free from doing evil, but not strong enough to do good. Besides, this condition sits so close to imperfection and weakness that I hardly know where to draw the line between them. The very names of goodness and innocence have, for this reason, become almost terms of contempt.

I know very well that several virtues — chastity, sobriety, temperance — can come to a man through personal defects. Steadiness in danger (if it deserves the name), contempt of death, patience in misfortune, can often be found in men simply because they fail to judge such events properly and don't grasp them for what they are. Dullness and stupidity sometimes counterfeit virtuous deeds. I have often seen men praised for what deserved blame.

An Italian lord once said this in my presence, at the expense of his own nation: that the Italians' subtlety and quickness of mind were so great, and they foresaw dangers from so far off, that no one should wonder if in war they often provided for their safety before they had even sighted the peril. We French and the Spaniards, not so cunning, went a step further: we had to see and feel the danger before taking alarm — and even then we couldn't hold steady. But the Germans and the Swiss, heavier and slower, hadn't the wit to look around them even when the blows were landing on their heads. Perhaps he was only joking. Yet it is quite true that raw soldiers rush into danger more recklessly than men who have taken their beatings — knowing well, as Virgil says, how much fresh glory in arms and the sweetness of honor can do in a first battle.

This is why, when we judge a particular action, we must consider the circumstances, and the whole man who performed it, before we give it a name.

Now a word about myself. I have sometimes heard my friends call prudence in me what was merely luck, and credit me with courage and patience where it was only judgment and opinion — giving me one title when another was due, sometimes to my advantage, sometimes not. As for the rest, I am so far from having reached that first and most perfect degree of excellence, where virtue becomes habit, that I've barely given proof of the second. I have not worked very hard at curbing the desires that have pressed me. My virtue is a virtue — or rather an innocence — that is casual and accidental. If I'd been born with a more disorderly temperament, I'm afraid the result would have been shabby, for I have never noticed much steadiness in my soul for resisting passions, even mildly vehement ones. I don't know how to nurse quarrels and conflicts inside my own chest. So I owe myself no great thanks for being free of several vices. If my nature is flawed by only a few minor vices and otherwise sound, as Horace says, it is like faulting moles on a handsome body.

I owe it to my fortune rather than my reason. She had me born of a family famous for integrity, and of a very good father. Whether he passed some of his temperament into me, or whether the examples at home and the good upbringing of my childhood worked on me without my noticing, or whether I was simply born this way — whether the Scales or dread Scorpio presided over my birth hour, as Horace has it, or Capricorn, tyrant of the western sea — I cannot say. But this much is so: I have a natural horror of most vices. The answer of Antisthenes, asked what was the best thing to learn — "to unlearn evil" — seems to point at this. I hold them in horror, I say, with a detestation so natural and so much my own that the same instinct and impression I brought away from my nurse I keep still; no temptation has ever had the power to alter it. Not even my own reasonings, which, straying off the common road in some things, might easily seem to license me for actions that my natural inclination makes me hate.

I will say a monstrous thing, but I will say it anyway: I find my conduct, in many matters, more under control than my opinions, and my appetite less debauched than my reason. Aristippus laid down opinions so bold in favor of pleasure and riches that he set all the philosophers against him. But as for his conduct: when the tyrant Dionysius presented three beautiful women to him and told him to choose, he answered that he would choose all three — Paris got into trouble for preferring one over the other two. But having brought them home, he sent them back untouched. And when his servant, carrying his money behind him on the road, found the load too heavy, Aristippus told him to pour out and throw away whatever was troubling him. Epicurus, whose doctrines were supposedly so irreligious and soft, lived a life laborious and devout. He wrote to a friend that he lived on nothing but biscuit and water, and asked him to send a little cheese to keep on hand for when he wanted to throw a feast.

Must it be true, then, that to be perfectly good we must be so by some hidden, natural, universal property — without law, reason, or example? The excesses I've been mixed up in are not, thank God, of the worst sort. I have condemned them in myself fairly — my judgment was never infected by them; on the contrary, I accuse them more severely in myself than in anyone else. But that is all. Beyond that, I put up too little resistance and let myself lean too easily to the other side of the scale — except that I keep my vices in bounds and stop them from mixing with other vices, which mostly cling together in a cluster if a man isn't careful. I have trimmed mine back and kept them as single and as simple as I could, and I do not carry the wrong any further, as Juvenal says.

As for the Stoics' opinion that the wise man, when he acts, acts by all the virtues at once, though one shows most, according to the nature of the action — the human body might lend them some analogy there, since anger cannot work unless all the humors assist it, even if choler predominates. But if they want to draw the parallel conclusion, that when the wicked man does wickedly, he does it by all the vices at once, I don't believe it, or else I don't understand them, for I find the opposite in practice. These are sharp, airless subtleties that philosophy sometimes amuses itself with. I indulge some vices, but I flee others as thoroughly as any saint could. The Peripatetics likewise reject this all-or-nothing bundling, and Aristotle holds that a prudent and just man can still be intemperate and unrestrained. Socrates admitted to people who spotted a certain inclination to vice in his face that it was indeed his natural bent — but that he had corrected it by discipline. And friends of the philosopher Stilpo said that, born with a weakness for wine and women, he had made himself by study thoroughly abstinent from both.

What good there is in me, I have, on the contrary, by the chance of my birth. I do not hold it from law, precept, or any other schooling. The innocence in me is a simple innocence: little vigor, and no art.

Among all the vices, I cruelly hate cruelty, both by nature and by judgment, as the extreme of all vices. And with such softness that I cannot watch a chicken's neck wrung without distress, and cannot bear to hear a hare crying in my dogs' teeth — even though the chase is a violent pleasure.

Those who have sensuality to fight against like to use this argument, to show that it is altogether vicious and unreasonable: that at its height it masters us so completely that reason has no access. And they cite our own experience in the act of love — that moment, as Lucretius describes it, when the body already has its foretaste of joy and Venus is on the point of finishing her work. They conceive that the pleasure so transports us that our reason cannot do its job while we are in that ecstasy. But I know very well it can be otherwise; a man may sometimes, if he chooses, win this point over himself and turn his soul, even at the critical moment, to other thoughts — though he must bend it to that purpose deliberately. I know the utmost force of this pleasure can be mastered. I have experienced it in myself, and have not found Venus so imperious a goddess as many men, far more virtuous than I am, declare her to be. I do not count it a miracle, as the Queen of Navarre does in one of the tales of her Heptameron (a very pretty book of its kind), nor even a thing of extreme difficulty, to spend whole nights with a long-desired mistress, with every freedom and opportunity a man could want, and still keep one's pledged word to be content with kisses and simple caresses, pressing no further.

I think the pleasure of the hunt would make a better example. There the pleasure is smaller, but the surprise is sharper, leaving reason no time to brace itself for the encounter — when, after a long search, the animal bursts up suddenly where we least expected it. That jolt, and the heat of the shouting, strike us so hard that those who love this lesser chase would find it impossible to wrench their thoughts elsewhere at that instant. And the poets make Diana triumph over Cupid's torch and arrows: in such delights, asks Horace, who does not forget the anxious cares of love?

But back to my point. I am tenderly compassionate of other people's afflictions, and would readily cry for company, if I could cry at all, for any occasion whatever. Nothing tempts my tears but tears — and not only real ones, but tears of every kind, feigned or painted. The dead I scarcely pity; I would sooner envy them. But I deeply pity the dying. Savages who roast and eat the bodies of the dead offend me less than men who torment and persecute the living. I cannot even watch the ordinary executions of justice, however reasonable, with a steady eye.

Someone wanting to give evidence of Julius Caesar's clemency wrote: "He was mild in his revenges. Having forced the surrender of the pirates who had earlier captured and ransomed him, and having sworn to crucify them, he did condemn them to the cross — but only after they had first been strangled. His secretary Philemon, who had tried to poison him, he punished with nothing harsher than plain death." Without naming the Latin author who dares offer it as proof of mercy that a man merely killed those who had wronged him — it's easy to guess he was haunted by the horrid, inhuman examples of cruelty the Roman tyrants had set.

For my part, even in justice itself, anything beyond a simple death looks to me like pure cruelty. Let them kill, but not torture. This holds especially for us, who ought to have some care for souls, and dismiss them in a good and tranquil state — which cannot be done when we have shaken them with unbearable torments.

Not long ago, a soldier being held prisoner noticed from his tower that crowds were gathering at the place of execution and carpenters were busy putting up a scaffold. Concluding the preparations were for him, he resolved to kill himself, but could find nothing to do it with except an old rusty cart-nail that fortune put in his way. With it he gave himself two great wounds in the throat; finding these were not enough, he gave himself a third in the belly, and left the nail buried up to the head. The first of his keepers to come in found him like this — still alive, but sunk down and drained by his wounds. To use what time remained before he died, they hurried to read him his sentence. When he heard he was only to be beheaded, he seemed to take new courage, accepted the wine he had refused before, and thanked his judges for the unhoped-for mildness of their sentence. He had resolved to dispatch himself, he said, for fear of some more terrible and unbearable death — the preparations he'd seen had convinced him they meant to torture him with some horrible execution — and he counted himself delivered from death, now that it was changed from the one he had dreaded.

I would advise that these displays of severity, meant to keep the people in line, be practiced on the dead bodies of criminals. To see them denied burial, to see them boiled and cut in quarters, would work on the crowd nearly as much as the pain inflicted on the living — which in effect amounts to little or nothing, as God himself says: they kill the body, and after that there is no more they can do. And the poets dwell with special force on the horror of that picture as something worse than death — alas, cries Cicero's verse, that the half-burnt remains of the king, his bones laid bare, should be dragged foully through the dirt and gore.

I happened to be in Rome one day just as they were executing Catena, a notorious robber. He was strangled without any stir among the spectators; but when they began to cut him in quarters, the hangman could not strike a blow without the people following it with a wailing cry, as if every one of them had lent his own sense of feeling to that miserable carcass. Such inhuman excesses should be spent on the bark, not on the quick. Artaxerxes, in a somewhat similar spirit, softened the old laws of Persia by ordaining that noblemen who had committed a fault, instead of being whipped as they used to be, should be stripped and their clothes whipped in their place; and instead of having their hair torn out, should only have their tall headdress taken off. The Egyptians, devout as they were, thought they satisfied divine justice well enough by sacrificing pigs in image and effigy — a bold invention, to pay God in picture and show for so essential a substance.

I live in a season when unbelievable examples of this vice flourish, through the license of our civil wars. You will find nothing in the ancient histories more extreme than what we witness every day. But it has not in the least reconciled me to it. I could hardly persuade myself, before I saw it with my own eyes, that there existed souls so monstrous that they would commit murder for the sole pleasure of it; that they would hack and chop off other men's limbs; that they would sharpen their wits to invent unheard-of tortures and new kinds of death — without hatred, without profit, with no other end than to enjoy the pleasant spectacle of the gestures and writhings, the pitiful groans and cries, of a man dying in anguish. For this is the farthest point cruelty can reach: that a man should kill a man, not in anger, not in fear, but only to watch, as Seneca says.

For my own part, I cannot without grief see even an innocent animal pursued and killed — a creature with no defense, that has done us no harm. And it often happens that a stag we are hunting, finding himself spent and out of breath, with no escape left, surrenders himself to us, his pursuers, imploring mercy with his tears — bleeding, as Virgil says, and like a creature begging for grace. That has always been a deeply unpleasant sight to me. I hardly ever take an animal alive that I don't turn loose again. Pythagoras used to buy them from fishermen and fowlers to do the same. It was the slaughter of wild beasts, says Ovid, that first warmed man's steel with blood.

Natures that are bloodthirsty toward animals show a native bent toward cruelty. At Rome, once they had accustomed themselves to spectacles of slaughtered animals, they proceeded to slaughtered men — to gladiators. Nature herself, I fear, has stamped in man some instinct toward inhumanity. Nobody takes pleasure in watching animals play with and caress one another; everybody enjoys watching them tear each other to pieces.

And so that no one laughs at this sympathy of mine for animals: theology itself commands us some kindness on their behalf. Considering that one and the same master has lodged us together in this palace for his service, and that they, like us, belong to his household, it has reason to enjoin us some affection and regard for them. Pythagoras borrowed the transmigration of souls from the Egyptians, but it was afterward received by many nations, our Druids among them — souls never die, says Ovid, but leave their old seat and live on, received into new homes. The religion of our ancient Gauls held that souls, being eternal, never stop moving and shifting from one body to another. And they mixed into this notion a thought about divine justice: according to how the soul had behaved while it was in Alexander, they said, God assigned it another body to live in, more or less painful, suited to its condition. He makes them wear the mute chains of brutes, says Claudian — the bloodthirsty he shuts up in bears, robbers in wolves, deceivers in foxes — until, after years and a thousand shapes, purged in Lethe's stream, he restores them at last to human form. If the soul had been valiant, they lodged it in a lion's body; if voluptuous, in a hog's; if cowardly, in a deer's or a hare's; if treacherous, in a fox's — and so on, until, purified by this punishment, it entered the body of some other man. For I myself remember, says Ovid's Pythagoras, that in the days of the Trojan war I was Euphorbus, son of Panthous.

As for that kinship between us and the beasts, I don't put much stock in it — nor in the practice of several nations, among the oldest and noblest, who not only welcomed animals into their society and company but ranked them far above themselves, treating them sometimes as familiars and favorites of their gods, holding them in more than human reverence; others recognized no god and no divinity except them. Beasts were made sacred by the barbarians, says Cicero, for the benefits they conferred. And Juvenal: one region adores the crocodile; another trembles before the ibis, glutted on serpents; here gleams the golden image of the sacred ape; here men venerate a river fish; there whole towns worship a dog.

Even the interpretation Plutarch gives this error — and it is a well-conceived one — does the animals credit. He says it was not the cat or the ox, for instance, that the Egyptians worshipped; rather they worshipped, in those beasts, some image of the divine faculties — in this one, patience and usefulness; in that one, liveliness; or, as with our neighbors the Burgundians and all the Germans, impatience at being shut up — by which they represented liberty, which they loved and adored above every other divine attribute. And so on through the rest. But when, among the more moderate opinions, I meet arguments that try to demonstrate the close resemblance between us and the animals — how large a share they have in our greatest privileges, how plausibly we can be compared — truly, I knock a good deal off our presumption, and I willingly resign that imaginary sovereignty we are supposed to hold over the other creatures.

But suppose all of that were false. There remains, even so, a certain respect, and a general duty of humanity, that binds us not only to animals, which have life and feeling, but even to trees and plants. We owe justice to men, and kindness and gentleness to the other creatures capable of receiving it. There is a certain exchange between them and us, a mutual obligation.

I am not ashamed to confess how childishly tender my nature is on this point: I can hardly refuse my dog when he pesters me to play at the most inconvenient moments. The Turks have alms and hospitals for animals. The Romans made the feeding of geese a public charge, in honor of the vigilance that had saved their Capitol. The Athenians decreed that the mules which had worked on the building of the temple called Hecatompedon should be set free, to graze wherever they pleased, unhindered. The people of Agrigentum made a solemn custom of burying the animals they had loved — horses of rare quality, dogs, useful birds, even those kept only to amuse their children — and the magnificence they showed in everything else appeared particularly in the splendor and number of the monuments raised for this purpose, which stood in their beauty for centuries afterward. The Egyptians buried wolves, bears, crocodiles, dogs, and cats in sacred ground, embalmed their bodies, and wore mourning at their deaths. Cimon gave honorable burial to the mares that had three times won him the prize at the Olympic Games. The elder Xanthippus had his dog buried on a headland by the sea, which has kept its name ever since. And Plutarch says he himself could never quite bring himself, for a small profit, to sell off to the butcher an ox that had served him a long time.
