Of Custom, and Not Easily Changing an Accepted Law
(Part 2 of 2)

If everyone who heard a good maxim did what those of us who study ourselves have learned to do — stopped at once to ask how it touches his own private case — he would discover that it was less a fine saying than a sharp lash across the everyday stupidity of his own judgment. But people take the precepts and warnings of truth as addressed to the public at large, never to themselves. Instead of applying them to their own conduct, they file them away in memory — ignorantly, and to no profit at all. But back to the empire of custom.

People raised in liberty, who have answered to no authority but their own will, look on every other form of government as monstrous and against nature. People used to monarchy do exactly the same. And whatever chance fortune gives them to change — even when they have just escaped, at enormous cost, from one master who weighed on them — they run, at the same cost, to set up another. They cannot manage to hate subjection itself.

It is custom's doing that everyone is content with the spot where nature planted him. The Highlanders of Scotland do not pine for Touraine, and the Scythians do not pine for Thessaly. When Darius asked certain Greeks what it would take to get them to adopt the Indian custom of eating their dead fathers — for that was the Indian practice, in the belief that the noblest tomb they could give them was inside their own bodies — the Greeks answered that nothing on earth could hire them to do it. But when he then tried to persuade the Indians to give up their custom and burn their fathers' bodies in the Greek manner, they were even more horrified at the suggestion. Everyone does the same, for habit hides from us the true face of things. As Lucretius says, nothing is at first so grand or so marvelous that, little by little, we do not come to wonder at it less.

I once took it on myself to defend one of our own practices — one accepted with absolute authority for many leagues around — and not content, as people usually are, to prop it up with law and precedent, I dug down to its origin. I found the foundation so weak that I, whose job was to convince others, very nearly talked myself out of it. This is the recipe Plato relies on, in his Laws, to cure the unnatural loves of his age — and he rates it sovereign: that public opinion condemn them, that the poets and every other kind of writer tell horrible stories about them. By the power of that recipe, the most beautiful daughters no longer tempt their fathers' lust, nor the handsomest brothers their sisters' desire; the mere fables of Thyestes, Oedipus, and Macareus, sung into the tender brains of children, have planted the wholesome belief there. Chastity is, in truth, a great and shining virtue, and its usefulness is well enough known. But to argue for it from nature, at its true value, is exactly as hard as it is easy to argue for it from custom, laws, and precepts. The fundamental, universal reasons are dark and difficult to dig out, and our masters either skim over them or, not daring to touch them at all, dive straight into the shelter of custom, where they puff themselves up and crow to their hearts' content. Yet those who refuse to be drawn from the original source commit a still greater error and land in wild opinions — witness Chrysippus, who scattered through so many of his writings how little he made of incestuous unions, however close the relation.

Whoever would shake himself free of custom's violent prejudice will find a good many things accepted with total, unquestioning conviction that have no support at all except the hoary head and wrinkled face of ancient usage. But once the mask is off and things are referred to the verdict of truth and reason, he will feel his judgment turned completely upside down — and yet set back on much surer footing. For example, I will ask him: what could be stranger than to see a whole people bound to obey laws they have never understood? Tied, in everything domestic — marriages, gifts, wills, sales, purchases — to rules they cannot possibly know, since the rules are neither written nor published in their own language, so that they must buy both the interpretation and the use of them? This is not the clever advice Isocrates gave his king — to make his subjects' trade and business free, open, and profitable, and their quarrels and lawsuits costly, loaded with heavy fees and penalties. It is something far more monstrous: putting reason itself up for sale, and turning the laws into merchandise. I count myself lucky that, as our historians tell it, the first man to stand up to Charlemagne when he tried to impose Latin and imperial laws on us was a Gascon gentleman — a countryman of mine.

And what could be more savage than a nation where, by lawful custom, the office of judge is bought and sold, where verdicts are paid for in cash, and where justice may be legitimately denied to anyone who cannot afford it? Where this trade stands in such repute that it creates, alongside the three ancient estates of church, nobility, and people, a fourth estate of wrangling lawyers — who, holding the laws in their hands and sovereign power over men's lives and fortunes, form a body apart from the nobility. From which it follows that we have double laws — the laws of honor and the laws of justice — flatly opposed on many points. The nobles condemn a man who swallows an insult as harshly as the courts condemn a man who avenges one. By the code of arms, whoever puts up with an affront is stripped of rank and honor; by the civil law, whoever takes revenge to clear his name incurs the death penalty. Go to court to repair an injury to your honor, and you disgrace yourself; refuse to go, and the law censures and punishes you. Of these two so different systems, both answering to one sovereign, the one is in charge of peace, the other of war; one takes the profit, the other the honor; one the wisdom, the other the virtue; one the word, the other the deed; one justice, the other valor; one reason, the other force; one wears the long robe, the other the short — and so they split the world between them.

As for indifferent things, like clothes — anyone wishing to call them back to their true purpose, which is the service and comfort of the body, and on which their original grace and fitness depend, will find plenty to work with. For the most fantastical inventions imaginable, I give you, among others, our flat caps; that long tail of velvet hanging from our women's heads with its particolored trimmings; and that vain and useless model of a member which we cannot decently so much as name — and which we nevertheless parade in public. Still, none of these considerations will persuade a man of understanding to break with the common fashion. On the contrary, it seems to me that all singular, private fashions are marks of folly and vain affectation rather than of sound reason, and that a wise man ought, inwardly, to withdraw his soul from the crowd and keep it free there, with full power to judge things as they are — but outwardly, to follow the fashion of his time completely. Public society has no claim on our thoughts; but everything else — our actions, our labors, our fortunes, our very lives — we are to lend and surrender to its service and to common opinion. So did that good and great Socrates, who refused to save his life by disobeying the magistrate, even a thoroughly wicked and unjust magistrate. For it is the rule of rules, the general law of laws, that everyone observe those of the place where he is. As the Greek line has it, it is good to obey the laws of one's country.

And now to another point. It is very doubtful whether any change in an accepted law, whatever it may be, can bring a benefit as manifest as the danger and trouble of altering it. A government is a structure of many different parts and members, joined and fitted together so tightly that you cannot stir a single brick or stone without the whole body feeling it. The lawgiver of the Thurians ordained that whoever proposed to abolish an old law or establish a new one should present himself to the people with a halter around his neck — so that if the innovation was not approved by everyone, he could be hanged on the spot. And the lawgiver of Sparta spent his life extracting from his citizens a faithful promise that none of his laws would be broken. The Spartan magistrate who so roughly cut the two strings Phrynis had added to the lyre never stopped to ask whether the addition improved the harmony or made the instrument fuller and more complete. It was enough to condemn the invention that it was a novelty — an alteration of the old way. That is also the meaning of the old rusty sword carried before the magistracy of Marseilles.

For my own part, I have a great distaste for novelty, whatever face or pretense it wears, and I have reason — I have been an eyewitness of the great evils it produces. The evils that have pressed on us for so many years are not all its doing; but one may fairly say it has accidentally fathered and bred the mischief and ruin that have come since, even the mischief done without it and against it. It is innovation, chiefly, that we must accuse for these disorders. Alas, as Ovid says — I suffer wounds made by my own weapons.

Those who give the first shake to a state are nearly always the first to be swallowed in its ruin. The fruits of public upheaval are rarely enjoyed by the man who set it in motion; he beats and stirs the water for another man's net. The unity and weave of this monarchy, this grand edifice, having been ripped and torn in its old age by this thing called innovation, has lain open ever since — a rent giving easy entrance to every injury. Royal majesty comes down with difficulty from the summit to the middle; from the middle to the bottom, it falls and tumbles headlong. But if the inventors do the greater mischief, the imitators are the more vicious — throwing themselves into examples whose horror and punishment they have felt themselves. And if there is any degree of honor in doing harm, the latecomers must concede to the first movers the glory of the invention and the courage of the first attempt. Every kind of new disorder draws, from that primal and ever-flowing spring, its examples and precedents for unsettling our government. In the very laws made to remedy the first evil, we can read the beginnings and the pretexts of every sort of wicked enterprise. What Thucydides said of the civil wars of his time is happening to us: to flatter the public vices, men christened them with new and milder names, sweetening and disguising their true titles — and all this, if you please, to reform our conscience and our belief. "Fine words, truly," as Terence says. But the fairest pretext for innovation is of very dangerous consequence; as Livy puts it, no change from the ancient ways deserves the benefit of the doubt.

And to speak my mind freely: it argues a strange self-love and a vast presumption to prize one's own opinions so highly that the public peace must be overthrown to establish them — to usher in so many certain evils, and so dreadful a corruption of manners, as civil war and political upheaval always bring in their train, and to usher them into the bowels of one's own country. Is there worse housekeeping than to set up so many certain, known vices against errors that are merely contested and debatable? Are there worse kinds of vice than those committed against a man's own conscience and the natural light of his own reason? The Roman Senate, in its dispute with the people over the administration of religion, was bold enough to pay them off with this evasion: that such matters concerned the gods more than the Senate, and the gods themselves would see that their rites were not profaned. It matches the oracle's answer to the people of Delphi who, fearing a Persian invasion in the Median war, asked Apollo what to do with the holy treasure of his temple — hide it, or carry it elsewhere? He answered that they should move nothing, and look after themselves; he was sufficient to take care of what belonged to him.

The Christian religion bears every mark of the highest usefulness and justice, but none more plainly than its strict command to all alike: obey the civil magistrate; uphold and defend the laws. And what a wonderful example divine wisdom has left us of this — that to accomplish the salvation of mankind, and to conduct its glorious victory over death and sin, it would do so no other way than at the mercy of our ordinary forms of justice, submitting the progress and outcome of so high and so saving a work to the blindness and injustice of our customs and usages, sacrificing the innocent blood of so many of the elect, and the long loss of so many years, to the ripening of that priceless fruit. There is a vast difference between the man who follows the forms and laws of his country and the man who undertakes to regulate and change them. The first pleads simplicity, obedience, and example in his excuse; whatever he does cannot be charged to malice — at worst, it is misfortune. For who is there, asks Cicero, whom antiquity cannot move, attested and sealed by the fairest monuments? And as Isocrates says, falling short is nearer to moderation than overshooting. The other man is playing a far riskier game. Whoever takes it on himself to choose and to alter usurps the authority of judging, and had better look hard at what he is doing — had better be sure he sees clearly the defect in what he would abolish and the virtue in what he would introduce.

This very ordinary consideration is what settled me in my station and kept even my most extravagant, unruly youth under the rein: not to load my shoulders with so great a weight as to make myself answerable for a science of that importance — not to dare, in this, what in my better and riper judgment I would not dare in the easiest matters I had been taught, where rashness of judgment costs nothing. It seems to me deeply unjust to try to subject public, established customs and institutions to the weakness and instability of a private and particular fancy — for private reason has only a private jurisdiction — and to attempt against divine law what no government would tolerate against the civil laws. Human reason has far more dealings with civil laws, and yet even they are sovereignly judged by their own proper judges; the utmost skill can do no more than expound and extend the law and custom received — never twist it, never innovate upon it. If divine providence has sometimes gone beyond the rules to which it has necessarily bound us, that is no license for us to do the same. Those are masterstrokes of the divine hand — not for us to imitate, but to admire: extraordinary examples, marks of express and particular purpose, of the order of miracles, set before us as displays of its almighty power, equally beyond our rules and our strength. It would be folly and impiety to try to reproduce them. We are not to follow them, but to contemplate them with the deepest reverence — acts of God's own person, and not for us. Cotta puts it very aptly: in matters of religion, he says, I follow the high priests Coruncanius, Scipio, and Scaevola — not Zeno, Cleanthes, or Chrysippus.

God knows, in our present quarrel — this civil war, where there are a hundred articles to strike out and put in, great and weighty ones — how many men can truly boast of having exactly and thoroughly weighed the grounds and reasons of the two parties? It is a number, if it makes a number at all, that would hardly trouble our peace. But all the rest — where are they headed? Under what flags do they march, in what quarter do they camp? Their remedies work like other weak and badly applied medicines: the humors they meant to purge they have only heated and aggravated by the struggle, and left in us still. The potion was too weak to purge, but strong enough to weaken us. It does not work; we keep it in our bodies; and all we reap from the operation is internal cramps and aches.

So it is, nevertheless, that Fortune — who keeps her authority above anything we can do or say — sometimes confronts us with a necessity so urgent that the laws simply must give a little ground. And when you are resisting the spread of an innovation that forces its way in by violence, holding yourself everywhere and in everything within bounds and rules — against men who have the power, men for whom anything is lawful that advances their design, who recognize no law or rule except what serves their purpose — that is a dangerous obligation and an intolerable inequality. For as Seneca says, putting faith in the treacherous opens the door to harm.

The ordinary discipline of a healthy state makes no provision for these extraordinary accidents. It presupposes a body that stands firm in its principal members and functions, and a common consent to obedience and observance. Proceeding lawfully is a cold, heavy, constrained business, and no match for proceeding headstrong and unbridled. To this day it is thrown in the teeth of those two great men, Octavius and Cato — in the civil wars of Sulla and of Caesar — that they would rather let their country suffer the last extremities than rescue their fellow citizens at the expense of its laws, or be guilty of any innovation. For in truth, in those final necessities where there is no other remedy, it would perhaps be wiser to stoop and yield a little, to take the blow, than to resist with no possibility of doing good and so give violence the occasion to trample everything underfoot. Better to make the laws do what they can, when they cannot do what they would. So did the Spartan king who suspended them for twenty-four hours; so did the man who, for once, shifted a day in the calendar; so did Alexander the Great, who made a second May out of the month of June. The Spartans themselves, those most religious observers of their country's laws, found themselves pinched by one of their own edicts, which expressly forbade choosing the same man twice as admiral — while their affairs absolutely required that Lysander take that command again. So they made one Aratus admiral; true — but Lysander went along as general of the navy. By the same subtlety, one of their ambassadors, sent to Athens to obtain the repeal of a certain decree, was told by Pericles that it was forbidden to take down the tablet on which a law had once been posted. He advised Pericles simply to turn it around — for that was not forbidden. And Plutarch praises Philopoemen for this: that being born to command, he knew how to command not only according to the laws, but to command the laws themselves, when the public necessity required it.
