Of the Power of the Imagination

"Fortis imaginatio generat casum," say the schoolmen — a strong imagination creates the event itself.

I am one of the people most susceptible to the power of imagination. Everyone gets jostled by it; some get knocked flat. It cuts right through me, and since I haven't the strength to resist it, my whole strategy is to avoid it. I could live on nothing but cheerful, healthy company. The mere sight of another man's suffering causes me real physical pain, and I often find I've borrowed someone else's sensations for my own. A man coughing nonstop in my presence sets my own lungs and throat itching. I'm more reluctant to visit the sick I love and feel bound to than the sick I don't care about and barely look at — because I catch the disease I'm worrying over and take it home with me. So it doesn't surprise me at all that imagination hands out fevers, and sometimes death, to people who give it too much room and entertain it too willingly.

Simon Thomas was a great physician in his day. I remember running into him once at Toulouse, at the house of a rich old man with bad lungs. Discussing the cure with his patient, Thomas told him that one excellent remedy would be to make my visits so pleasant that I'd come often — and then to fix his eyes on the freshness of my face, and his thoughts on the liveliness and vigor glowing out of my youth, and to fill all his senses with the flourishing age I was in; by which means his constitution might possibly improve. What he forgot to add was that mine, at the same time, might get worse.

Gallus Vibius strained his mind so hard to understand the essence and workings of madness that he wrenched his own judgment out of its seat and could never get it back — and could boast that he'd become a fool by too much wisdom. There are men whose fear beats the executioner to the job: like the prisoner whose blindfold was removed so his pardon could be read to him, and who was found stone dead on the scaffold — killed by nothing but the stroke of his own imagination. We startle, tremble, go pale, and blush under imagination's pushes; lying in bed we feel our bodies shaken by its power, sometimes nearly to the point of dying of it. And boiling youth heats up so far in its sleep that it satisfies its desires in a dream — so that, as Lucretius says, as if the whole act were really completed, young men pour out the flood and stain their bedclothes.

It's nothing new to see horns sprout overnight on a forehead that had none at bedtime — but what happened to Cippus, king of Italy, is still worth remembering. He had spent a day as a delighted spectator at a bullfight, dreamed all night of horns on his head, and by the sheer force of imagination grew real ones there. Passion gave the son of Croesus the voice nature had denied him. Antiochus fell into a fever because the beauty of Stratonice had stamped itself too deep into his soul. Pliny claims he saw Lucius Cossitius, who turned from a woman into a man on her own wedding day. Pontanus and others report the same metamorphosis happening in Italy in more recent times — and through the intense longing of the person concerned and of his mother, as Ovid has it, "Iphis the boy paid the vows he had made as a girl."

I myself, passing through Vitry-le-François, saw a man whom the Bishop of Soissons had confirmed under the name Germain, but whom everyone in town had known as a girl named Mary until the age of twenty-two. He was, when I saw him, heavily bearded, old, and unmarried. He told us that as he was straining in a jump, his male organs emerged — and to this day the girls of that town have a song warning each other not to take big strides, or they'll turn into men like Mary Germain. It's no great wonder if this kind of accident happens often. If imagination has any power in such matters, it is so constantly and so fiercely bent on this one subject that — rather than have it relapse again and again into the same thought and the same violence of desire — it might be simpler to give these young women the thing they long for, once and for all.

Some attribute the scars of King Dagobert and of Saint Francis to the force of imagination. It is said to lift bodies right off the ground, sometimes. Celsus tells of a priest whose soul would be carried off into such ecstasy that his body lay a long while without sense or breath. Saint Augustine mentions another man who, at the sound of any sorrowful or mournful cries, would immediately fall into a faint, so far out of himself that it was useless to call him, shout in his ears, pinch him, or burn him until he came back on his own. Then he'd say he had heard the voices as if from far away, and had felt the pinching and the burning. And to prove this was no stubborn act of defiance against his own senses, the man had, all that while, neither pulse nor breath.

It's very likely that visions, enchantments, and all extraordinary effects of that kind owe their credit mainly to the power of imagination working on ordinary, more pliable souls — minds whose belief is so easily captured that they think they see what they do not see.

And I'm still not satisfied that those famous "knots" — the wedding charms our whole age is so obsessed with that people talk of almost nothing else, the spells supposed to tie up a bridegroom and keep him from consummating his marriage — are anything more than the voluntary workings of apprehension and fear. I know this by experience, in the case of a particular friend of mine, a man I can vouch for as I would for myself, who could not possibly fall under any suspicion of weakness in this department, and just as little of being bewitched. He had heard a companion of his tell the story of an extraordinary failure that had overtaken him at the worst possible moment. Later, finding himself in the same situation, the horror of that story suddenly seized his imagination so violently that he met the same fate. And from then on, the miserable memory of his disaster kept running through his mind and tyrannizing over him, and he was prone to relapse into the same misfortune.

He found a remedy for this fantasy in another fantasy: by frankly confessing the weakness in advance, declaring it to the woman before anything began, the agitation of his soul was somewhat relieved — since the failure was now expected, the pressure on him weighed less. Later, at times when he was in no such fear, when his thoughts were free and disentangled and his body in its true and natural state — being then at leisure to have the part examined, so to speak, and the fact communicated to the other party — he was completely cured of the trouble. Once a man has acquitted himself properly with a woman a single time, he is never again in danger of failing with her, except from some genuine and excusable weakness.

This disaster, in fact, is only to be feared in encounters where the soul is strained beyond measure with desire or with reverence — and especially when the opportunity comes unexpected and urgent. There's no way for a man to defend himself from a surprise like that; it throws him entirely out of order. I have known men who protected themselves from the mishap by arriving half-satisfied from elsewhere, on purpose to take the edge off their ardor; and others who, having grown old, find themselves less impotent for being less able; and one man who was cured by a friend's assurance that he carried a counter-charm guaranteed to protect him from enchantments. That story is not a bad one, so you shall have it.

A count of a very great family, with whom I was close, married a beautiful lady who had previously been courted by a man who was present at the wedding. All the count's friends were worried — especially an old lady, his kinswoman, who was presiding over the festivities and hosting them in her own house. She suspected the rival would try foul play with these sorceries, and she told me her fear. I told her to leave it to me.

I happened to have with me a flat plate of gold engraved with celestial figures, a charm against sunstroke and headaches — to work, it had to sit exactly on the seam of the skull, held in place by a ribbon tied under the chin. A piece of nonsense first cousin to the very nonsense we're discussing. Jacques Pelletier, who was living in my house, had given it to me as a great rarity, and it struck me I could put it to some use. I privately warned the count that he might well meet the same bad luck other bridegrooms had met, especially with a certain person in the house who would be delighted to do him that kind of favor — but he should go to bed boldly. I would do him the office of a friend, and would not, at need, spare a miracle that lay in my power, provided he swore on his honor to keep it absolutely secret. He was simply to give me an agreed sign, when they came in during the night to bring him his wedding posset — the custom here is to carry the bridegroom a hot drink in the middle of his wedding night — if things had gone badly.

His ears had been so battered, and his mind so thoroughly worked over by the eternal chatter about this business, that when the moment came he really did find himself tied up by the trouble of his imagination, and gave me the sign. I whispered to him to get up, as if to chase us all out of the room, and — in a joking way — to snatch my nightgown off my shoulders (we were nearly the same height), throw it over his own, and keep it on until he'd done what I told him. Which was this: when we had all left, he was to withdraw as if to relieve himself, repeat certain words three times and perform certain motions three times, and at each of the three, tie the ribbon I'd put into his hand around his waist, setting the medal that hung from it — figures arranged just so — exactly over his kidneys. Then, having on the third time cinched the ribbon so well that it could neither come loose nor slip, he was to go back to his business with full confidence — and not forget to spread my gown over the bed so that it covered them both.

These monkey tricks are the heart of the whole effect. Our fancy, once seduced, believes that means so strange must surely flow from some secret science: their very emptiness gives them weight and authority. In short, it is certain my figures proved themselves more venereal than solar — more active than protective. It was a sudden whim, mixed with a little curiosity, that drew me into an act so contrary to my nature; for I am an enemy of all subtle and counterfeit dealings and hate every kind of trickery, even in sport, even to someone's advantage. The act may not be vicious, but the manner of it is.

Amasis, king of Egypt, married Laodice, a very beautiful Greek girl, and though he was perfectly capable everywhere else, found himself quite another man with his wife — he could not enjoy her at all. He was so enraged that he threatened to kill her, suspecting witchcraft. As usual in matters that live in the fancy, she steered him toward religion; he made his vows to Venus, and found himself divinely restored the very first night after his offerings and sacrifices.

Now women are wrong to greet us with that disdainful, coy, scowling face that puts out our fire even as it lights our desire. The daughter-in-law of Pythagoras said that a woman who goes to bed with a man should take off her modesty with her petticoat, and put it back on with the same. The soul of the attacker, rattled by alarm after alarm, easily loses the power to perform. And whoever imagination has once played this trick on, and humiliated with the shame of it — and she plays it only at first acquaintance, because then a man is most ardent and eager, and most afraid of failing at this first account he gives of himself — having begun badly, he falls into such fever and spite over the accident that the trouble tends to stay with him on every occasion after.

Married people, with all their time before them, should never force the thing or even attempt it unless they find themselves fully ready. It is less shameful to fail to christen the wedding sheets — when a man feels himself all agitation and trembling — and to wait for another, more private, more composed opportunity, than to make himself permanently miserable over having stumbled and been routed in the first assault. Until possession is fairly taken, a man who knows himself subject to this weakness should feel his way gradually, with small trials and light advances, rather than stubbornly stake everything on conquering, once and for all, his own mutinous and unwilling faculties. Those who know their member to be naturally obedient need only take care to out-maneuver their fantasies.

We have good reason to remark on the ungovernable liberty of this member: so inconveniently insistent when we have no use for it, and so inconveniently failing when we need it most; contesting authority with the will so imperiously, refusing every solicitation of hand and mind with such proud obstinacy. And yet — though his rebellion is universally complained of, and the evidence used to condemn him — if he had hired me to plead his case, I might well bring his fellow members under suspicion of having framed him, out of pure envy of the importance and sweetness of his particular office: of having conspired to arm the whole world against him by maliciously charging him alone with a crime they all commit.

Because consider: is there one part of our bodies that doesn't regularly refuse to work when the will commands, and work away when the will forbids? Every one of them has passions of its own that wake it or put it to sleep without asking our leave. How often do the involuntary movements of our face betray the thoughts we meant to keep secret, and expose us to the people in the room? The same cause that animates this member also animates — without our knowledge — the lungs, the pulse, and the heart; the sight of a pleasing object spreads a flame through us, a small fever, before we know it's happening. And is it only these veins and these muscles that rise and fall without the consent not just of our will, but of our very knowledge? We don't order our hair to stand on end or our skin to shiver with fear or desire. The hands often wander where we haven't sent them. The tongue seizes up, and the voice freezes, at its own hour. Even when we have nothing to eat and would gladly forbid it, the appetite for food stirs up the parts subject to it, exactly like that other appetite — and abandons us, just as unseasonably, whenever it sees fit.

The vessels that serve to empty the belly have their own dilations and contractions, beyond and without our consent, just like those that empty the bladder. And as for the case Saint Augustine cites to defend the prerogative of the will — he saw a man who could command his rear end to produce as many discharges as he pleased — his commentator Vives caps it with a contemporary example of a man who could break wind in tune. None of which proves that organ purely obedient; for is anything usually more tumultuous or more indiscreet? And let me add one I know myself, so rude and unruly that for forty years it has marched its master toward one continuous, unbroken eruption, and looks likely to keep at it till he dies of it. I could wish I knew only from books how often a man's belly, denied a single puff of escape, brings him to the very edge of an agonizing death; and that the emperor who gave us liberty to break wind anywhere had also given us the power to do it.

But our will, on whose behalf this whole indictment is brought — with how much better cause might we charge her with mutiny and sedition, with irregularity and disobedience? Does she always will what we would have her will? Doesn't she often will exactly what we forbid — and to our obvious harm? Does she let herself be governed by the conclusions of our reason any more than the rest do?

To finish: I would ask, on behalf of the gentleman my client, that the court consider this. In the matter at hand, his cause is inseparably and indistinguishably joined with an accessory — and yet he alone is prosecuted, on arguments and charges that cannot possibly be brought against her; since her business, though it is sometimes to invite inopportunely, is never to refuse, and her invitations are, moreover, tacit and quiet ones. The malice and injustice of his accusers is therefore plain to see. But however the lawyers and judges may quarrel, nature will go on her own way in the meantime — and she would have done no more than justice if she had granted this member some special privilege: author of the only immortal work of mortals. A divine work, according to Socrates; and love, the desire for immortality, itself an immortal spirit.

One man, perhaps, by just such an effect of imagination, leaves his scrofula — the king's evil — behind him here, while his companion, coming after, carries his back into Spain. And this is why, in such matters, the mind has to be prepared in advance for the thing that's to be done. Why do physicians work so hard, beforehand, on their patients' credulity, with so many false promises of a cure, if not so that the effect of imagination can make up for the fraud in their potions? They know very well — one of the masters of their trade has left it in writing — that there are men for whom the mere sight of medicine does the work.

All these fancies have come into my head from a story told me by an apothecary in my late father's household — a blunt Swiss, of a nation not much given to vanity or lying — about a merchant he had long known at Toulouse: a sickly man, much afflicted with kidney stones, who often needed enemas and had his physicians prescribe various kinds, depending on the turns of his disease. When the enema was brought, none of the usual formalities was skipped — he tested it himself, to feel whether it was too hot. There he lay, flat on his front, everything performed with full ceremony — except the injection itself. The apothecary would withdraw, the patient would be settled as if he had truly received it, and the effect was identical to the real thing. And if the physician judged the operation insufficient, he would administer two or three more doses, in exactly the same fashion. My witness swore that — since the man paid as if he'd really had the medicine — the sick man's wife once tried to save money by having plain warm water put in; the effect exposed the trick, that kind proving useless, and they had to return to the original method.

A woman convinced she had swallowed a pin in a piece of bread cried and wailed with an unbearable pain in her throat, where she thought she felt it sticking. But a clever fellow who was brought in, seeing no swelling and nothing wrong from the outside, judged it was only a fancy taken from some crust that had scratched her on the way down. He made her vomit, and secretly tossed a bent pin into the basin. The moment the woman saw it, believing she had thrown it up, she felt her pain vanish. And I myself knew a gentleman who, three or four days after entertaining a large company at his house, bragged as a joke — for there was nothing to it — that he had served them baked cat. One young lady who'd been at the feast took such horror at this that she fell into violent vomiting and fever, and nothing could be done to save her.

Even the animals are subject to the force of imagination as much as we are. Look at dogs, who die of grief for their lost masters, and bark and tremble and twitch in their dreams; horses kick and whinny in their sleep.

Now all of this can be set down to the close kinship of soul and body, trading their fortunes back and forth. But it's quite another thing when imagination works not on one's own body but on someone else's. Just as an infected body passes its sickness to those nearby — as we see with plague, smallpox, and sore eyes, which run through whole households and whole cities, and as Ovid says, "look at sore eyes and your own eyes grow sore; many ills pass from body to body" — so imagination, violently stirred, lets fly an infection that can strike a target outside itself. The ancients held that certain women of Scythia, when roused to fury against a man, could kill him with a look alone. Tortoises and ostriches hatch their eggs just by gazing at them — which suggests their eyes send out some projecting power. And the eyes of witches are said to be aggressive and harmful: "some eye, I know not whose, is bewitching my tender lambs," as Virgil's shepherd complains.

Magicians are not much of an authority with me. But we do see by experience that women stamp the marks of their fancy on the children they carry in the womb — witness the one who gave birth to a Moor; and the girl from near Pisa, presented to the Emperor Charles, King of Bohemia, all rough and covered with hair, whom her mother said she had conceived that way because of a picture of John the Baptist hanging inside her bed-curtains.

It's the same with animals: witness Jacob's sheep, and the hares and partridges that the snow turns white in the mountains. At my own house, a little while ago, a cat was seen watching a bird at the top of a tree; they fixed their eyes on each other for some time, and at last the bird dropped, dead, straight into the cat's claws — either dazzled by the force of its own imagination or drawn by some attracting power in the cat. Those who enjoy field sports have surely heard the story of the falconer who fastened his gaze on a kite in the air and wagered that he could bring it down by the sheer power of his sight — and did, so it was said.

For the tales I borrow, I send the bill to the consciences of those I had them from. The reasonings are my own, and they stand on the proofs of reason, not of experience. Everyone is free to add his own examples; and whoever has none should still believe — considering the number and variety of accidents in this world — that there are plenty of them. If I don't apply them well, let someone else apply them for me. In the subject I'm treating here — our habits and our impulses — testimony and instances, however fabulous, serve as well as true ones, provided they're possible. Whether it really happened or not, at Rome or at Paris, to John or to Peter, it is still some turn the human mind can take, and that is what I'm usefully studying. I see it and I profit from it, in shadow as well as in substance. And among the various readings the histories offer, I pick out the rarest and most memorable for my own use. There are authors whose whole purpose is to relate what has happened. Mine, if I could manage it, would be to relate what can happen. The schools quite reasonably permit you to invent comparisons when you have none at hand. I don't use that license, though; on this point my scruples outdo all historical authority. In the examples I bring in here — of what I've heard, read, done, or said — I have forbidden myself to alter even the smallest, most trivial circumstance. My conscience does not falsify one jot. What my ignorance may do, I cannot answer for.

This is what makes me wonder, sometimes, whether a theologian or a philosopher — men of such exact and tender conscience and prudence — is fit to write history at all. How can they stake their good name on the faith of the crowd? How can they answer for the opinions of men they've never met, or pass off their guesses as solid coin? They would refuse to give sworn testimony before a judge about actions performed under their own eyes, with several witnesses present; and there's no man alive they know so intimately that they'd stand full guarantee for his intentions. For my part, I think it less risky to write about the past than the present — exactly because the writer then only has to account for borrowed truth that everyone knows he took on trust.

Some people urge me to write the events of my own time. They fancy I see them with an eye less blinded by passion than most, and at closer range, because fortune has given me access to the heads of the various factions. What they don't consider is that I would not take the trouble for the glory of Sallust himself — sworn enemy that I am of obligation, of diligence, of perseverance; that nothing is so contrary to my style as extended narrative, when I keep breaking off in my own writing for sheer want of breath; that I have no gift for composition or development worth anything, and am more ignorant than a child of the phrases and even the words used for the most ordinary things. That is why I have undertaken to say only what I can say, and have cut my subject to fit my strength. Were I to take some guide and follow his pace, I might not be able to keep up; and in the freedom of my own liberty I might publish judgments which, on better reflection and by reason's lights, would be illegitimate and punishable.

Plutarch would tell us, of what he has handed down, that the work of making his examples true and accurate belongs to others; but that they are useful to posterity, and presented with a luster that lights our way to virtue — that is his own work. In an old story, unlike a dose of medicine, there's no great danger in whether it happened this way or that.
