Of Experience
(Part 3 of 5)

Is life worth that much? To keep ourselves alive we are made to give up the very things we live by — to live, we stop living. As the poet asks, can you call a man alive when the air he breathes and the light he steers by have been turned into burdens?

If the doctors' regimens do no other good, they do at least this much: they get their patients ready for death early, by undermining the use of life and trimming it away little by little.

Sick or well, I have always willingly obeyed the appetites that pressed on me. I give my desires and inclinations a long rein. I don't like curing one disease with another, and I hate remedies that are more trouble than the sickness itself. To suffer from colic and to be forbidden oysters is two evils instead of one: the disease torments you from one side, the diet from the other. Since we're always in danger of guessing wrong anyway, let's at least risk our mistake after we've had the pleasure. The world goes the opposite way and counts nothing as beneficial unless it hurts; ease makes it deeply suspicious.

My appetite, in any number of things, has fit itself to the health of my stomach quite happily on its own. Sharp, spicy sauces pleased me when I was young; when my stomach later turned against them, my taste followed at once. Wine is bad for the sick — and it's the first thing my mouth rejects when I'm ill, with an unconquerable distaste. Whatever I take against my inclination harms me; nothing I eat with appetite and delight ever does. I have never been hurt by any action that gave me real pleasure, and so I've made every medical pronouncement yield, generously, to my enjoyment.

When I was young — back when Cupid fluttered about me, as the poet says, splendid in his saffron tunic — I gave myself the rein as freely and recklessly as any man alive to whatever desire had hold of me. I soldiered in those wars not without glory, as the line goes — though my strength lay more in lasting out the campaign than in the charge: I can scarcely remember six bouts in a night.

It is a misfortune and a marvel at once to confess how young I was when I first came under love's power. It was pure chance — long before the age of choice or knowledge. I can't remember myself back that far. My case might be paired with Quartilla's, who couldn't remember ever having been a maid. Hence, as the poet says, the early hair, and the beard that astonished my mother.

Doctors bend their rules to fit the violent cravings of their patients, and usually with good success; no craving can be so strange and wrong-headed that nature doesn't have some hand in it. And besides, how small a thing it is to satisfy the imagination! In my opinion that faculty matters most of all — at least more than any other. The heaviest and most common evils are the ones imagination loads onto us. There's a Spanish saying I like from several angles: Defienda me Dios de mí — "God defend me from myself."

When I'm sick, I'm sorry not to have some craving I could enjoy satisfying; all the rules of medicine would hardly keep me from it. I do the same when I'm well: I can see little more left to hope or wish for. It would be pitiful to be so weak and faded that even wishing is beyond you.

The art of medicine is not so settled that we can't find authority for whatever we choose to do. It changes with climates and changes with moons; it changes from one famous doctor to the next — Fernel says one thing, Scaliger another. If your physician thinks it bad for you to sleep, to drink wine, or to eat such-and-such, don't worry: I'll find you another who disagrees with him. The variety of medical arguments and opinions covers every shape a thing can take. I saw one poor sick man panting and burning with thirst — denied water in order to be cured — and laughed at afterward for his pains by a second physician, who condemned the first one's advice as harmful. Hadn't he tortured himself to good purpose? A man of that profession died lately of kidney stones, having used extreme fasting to fight his disease; his fellow physicians say the fasting was exactly what dried him up and baked the gravel in his kidneys.

I've noticed that in wounds and in sickness alike, talking unsettles and hurts me as much as any excess I could commit. My voice is loud and effortful, and it pains and tires me — so much so that when I've gone to whisper in some great man's ear about serious business, he has often asked me to lower my voice.

Here's a story worth the detour. Someone in a Greek school was speaking loudly, the way I do, and the master of ceremonies sent word for him to speak more softly. "Then have him send me," the man replied, "the tone he wants me to use." The answer came back: take your tone from the ears of the person you're speaking to. That was well said — if it meant "suit your voice to the business you have with your listener." But if it meant "it's enough that he hears you; let him set your volume," I don't see the sense of it. The tone and movement of my voice carry a great part of my meaning, and I'm the one who has to govern them to make myself understood. There is a voice for teaching, a voice for flattering, a voice for scolding. I want my voice not just to reach a man but, on occasion, to strike him and pierce him. When I'm dressing down my valet in sharp and bitter language, it would be a fine thing for him to say, "Speak lower, master — I hear you perfectly well." There is a voice fitted to the hearing, as the rhetorician says, not by its loudness but by its rightness. Speech belongs half to the speaker and half to the listener. The listener should prepare to receive it the way it comes — as in tennis, where the man receiving the ball shifts and sets himself according to the motions of the server and the character of the stroke.

Experience has also taught me this: we ruin ourselves by impatience. Evils have their life spans and their limits — their sicknesses and their recoveries.

Diseases are built on the pattern of animals: they have their fortunes and their allotted days from birth. Whoever tries to bully one short by force in the middle of its course only lengthens and multiplies it, and provokes it instead of calming it. I'm of Crantor's opinion: we should neither stubbornly stonewall our evils nor collapse under them for lack of spine, but give way to them naturally, according to their condition and ours. We should grant diseases free passage. I find they stay a shorter time with me because I let them alone; I have shed some of the most stubborn and tenacious kinds, the ones with the worst reputations, by their own decay — without help, without art, and against all of art's rules. Let nature have her way for a while; she understands her business better than we do. But so-and-so died of it! So will you — if not of that disease, then of another. And how many people have failed to escape dying with three doctors at their tails? Example is a vague and universal mirror, reflecting in every direction. If a medicine is delicious, take it: that much, at least, is present good. I will never balk at the name or the color if the thing is pleasant going down — pleasure is one of the chief varieties of profit.

I have allowed colds, gouty discharges, looseness, palpitations of the heart, migraines, and other ailments to grow old in me and die a natural death. I lost them just as I was getting half-fond of keeping them. Courtesy persuades them away faster than bluster. We have to bear the laws of our condition patiently. We are born to grow old, to grow weak, and to be sick, despite all medicine. It's the first lesson the Mexicans teach their children: the moment they come out of the womb they greet them with, "Child, you have come into the world to endure. Endure, suffer, and say nothing." It is unjust to complain that what may happen to anyone has happened to you. Be angry, says Seneca, when something is decreed unjustly against you alone.

Look at an old man begging God to keep his health whole and vigorous — that is, asking to be made young again. Fool, why these childish wishes, doomed in advance? His condition no longer allows it. Gout, kidney stones, indigestion — these are the symptoms of long years, the way heat, rain, and wind are the symptoms of long journeys. Plato doesn't believe Aesculapius bothered prescribing regimens to drag out life in a weak and wasted body, useless to its country and its calling, incapable of fathering strong and healthy children. He doesn't think such fussing fits the justice and prudence of God, which directs all things toward use. My good friend, your business is done; nobody can restore you. At most they can patch you and prop you a little, and stretch your misery out an hour or two — like a man who shores up a collapsing house with prop after prop, until the appointed day when house, props, and all come down together.

We must learn to endure what we cannot escape. Our life is composed of contrary things, like the harmony of the world — different tones, sweet and harsh, sharp and flat, lively and solemn. What could a musician do who loved only some of them? He has to know how to use them all and mix them. And so must we mix the goods and evils that are of one substance with our life. Our being cannot survive without that mixture, and the one half is no less necessary to it than the other. Trying to fight natural necessity is to reenact the folly of Ctesiphon, who got into a kicking contest with his mule.

I consult almost nobody about the changes I feel in myself, because these doctors take advantage of you. Once they have you at their mercy, they stuff your ears with prognoses. Catching me once weakened by sickness, they handled me insultingly with their dogmas and their schoolmaster scowls — threatening me now with great pains, now with approaching death. I was shaken by it, I admit, but not beaten, not knocked off my ground; my judgment was neither changed nor confused, but it was disturbed. And that is always agitation and struggle.

Now I handle my imagination as gently as I can, and would relieve it, if I could, of all strain and conflict. You have to help it along, flatter it, fool it if you can. My mind is well suited to the job — it never lacks for plausible arguments. If it could persuade as well as it preaches, it would set me up perfectly.

Would you like an example? My mind tells me that it's for my own good that I have kidney stones. It says that buildings my age must naturally suffer some leakage — it is time they began to loosen and admit a breach. It's a common necessity; there is nothing miraculous or new in it. I am paying old age its due, and I cannot expect a better bargain. It says the company should comfort me, since I've fallen into the most ordinary ailment of men my age: everywhere I look I see men tormented by the same disease, and the fellowship is honorable, since it most often strikes men of quality. It's a noble disease, a dignified one. It says that of the men struck by it, few get off with less pain than I do — and even they pay for it with strict diets and daily doses of disgusting medicines, while I owe my better condition purely to good luck. The few ordinary broths of eryngo and burst-wort I've taken — twice or three times, to oblige certain ladies who, with more kindness than my pain was sharp, insisted on giving me half of theirs — seemed to me equally easy to swallow and useless in effect. Other men must pay a thousand vows to Aesculapius and as many crowns to their physicians to pass a little gravel that I pass often with nature's help alone. Even my composure in company is undisturbed; I can hold my water ten hours, as long as any man in health.

"The fear of this disease," my mind goes on, "used to terrify you, back when you didn't know it — the cries and despairing groans of those who make it worse by their impatience gave you a horror of it. It's an affliction that punishes the very organs you sinned with most. You are a man of conscience; we are only entitled to complain of punishment we haven't earned. Look at this chastisement: it's gentle compared to others, and laid on with a father's tenderness. Notice how late it came — it occupies and inconveniences only that stretch of your life which is sterile and lost anyway, having, as if by contract, left the license and pleasures of your youth their full term.

"The fear and pity people feel for this disease serve your vanity, too — a quality you may have purged from your judgment and half-cured from your reason, but your friends can still spot the tincture in your complexion. There is pleasure in hearing people say of you: now that is strength of mind, that is patience! They see you sweat with pain, turn pale and turn red, tremble, vomit blood, suffer strange contractions and convulsions, drip great tears from your eyes, pass urine thick, black, and dreadful, or have it stopped by some sharp, jagged stone that cruelly pricks and flays the neck of your bladder — and all the while you keep an ordinary face, chatting with your people in the gaps, holding up your end of a conversation, excusing your pain now and then and underselling your suffering. Do you remember the men of the old days who hunted out diseases on purpose, to keep their virtue in training? Suppose nature has driven and pushed you into that glorious school — one you would never have entered on your own.

"If you tell me it's a dangerous, mortal disease — which ones aren't? It's a doctor's trick to exempt any disease at all from the road to death. What does it matter whether they go there directly or slide and slip toward the path that leads us to it? You do not die because you are sick; you die because you are alive. Death kills you perfectly well without sickness's help — and sickness has actually postponed death for some men, who lived longer precisely because they thought they were always dying. Besides, there are diseases, as there are wounds, that are medicinal and good for us.

"Your stones are often as long-lived as you are. There are men who've kept them company from childhood into extreme old age; if the men hadn't quit first, the stones were ready to stay longer. You kill them more often than they kill you. And even if the disease keeps the image of approaching death before you — is it not a kindness, at your age, to be reminded of your end? And here's the worst of it: you no longer have anything left that should make you want to be cured. One way or another, the common necessity will call for you soon. Consider how skillfully and gently this disease puts you out of love with life and weans you from the world — not bullying you with a tyrant's grip, like so many other infirmities you see in old men, which hold them in continual torment and unbroken weakness and pain, but by warnings and instructions spaced out at intervals, with long rests in between, as if to give you the chance to meditate on its lesson and chew it over at your own leisure. And to give you the means to judge sanely, and to summon the resolve of a brave man, it sets your whole condition before you, the good and the bad — a thoroughly cheerful life and an unbearable one, in one and the same day. If you don't embrace death, at least you shake hands with it once a month. So you have all the more reason to hope it will take you one day without warning — that having been led so often down to the water's edge, still assuming the usual terms apply, you and your confidence will some morning be ferried across before you know it. A man cannot reasonably complain of a disease that splits its time fairly with health."

I am grateful to Fortune for attacking me so often with the same weapons. She shapes and trains me by repetition, hardens me, habituates me, so that I know now within a little what it will cost me to be quit of an attack. Lacking a natural memory, I make one out of paper: whenever some new symptom appears in my disease, I write it down. So by now, having run through nearly every kind of episode, whenever something alarming threatens me I leaf through these little loose notes like the Sibyl's leaves — and I never fail to find, somewhere in my past experience, a favorable precedent to comfort me. Habit also makes me hope better of the future: this clearing-out has gone on so long that nature is unlikely to change her course now, and nothing worse is likely to come than what I already feel.

Besides, the temperament of this disease suits my quick and sudden nature. When it attacks me gently, I get frightened — that means a long siege. But its natural style is the brisk, vigorous flare-up: it claws me thoroughly for a day or two. My kidneys held out a whole age without trouble; I have now lived almost another age since they changed their state. Evils have their seasons, like benefits; perhaps this infirmity is drawing to a close. Age cools the heat of my stomach; its digestion, less perfect now, sends crude matter on to my kidneys. Why shouldn't the heat of my kidneys, in their own turn and season, cool as well, so that they can no longer petrify my phlegm, and nature finds some other route of purging? The years have visibly helped me dry up certain discharges. Why not these residues too, that supply the matter for gravel?

And is there any pleasure on earth like that sudden change — when after extreme pain, by the passing of a stone, I recover, fast as a flash of lightning, the beautiful light of health, free and full, the way it happens in our sharpest and most sudden colics? Is there anything in the pain endured that can outweigh the joy of so swift a recovery? How much fairer health looks to me after sickness — sickness so close, so nearly touching it, that I can see the two of them face to face in their full dress, squaring off like rivals to dispute the field! The Stoics say vices were usefully introduced to give value and luster to virtue. We can say, with better reason and a less reckless guess, that nature has given us pain for the honor and the service of pleasure and ease. When Socrates was relieved of his chains and felt the delicious itch in his legs where their weight had been, he rejoiced to consider the close alliance between pain and pleasure — how the two are yoked by a necessary connection, taking turns following and begetting one another — and he called out to good old Aesop that he should have made a fine fable out of exactly that.

The worst thing I see in other diseases is that they are less grievous in their action than in their aftermath. You spend a whole year recovering, full of weakness and fear the entire time. There's so much hazard, so many stages on the way back to safety, that there's no end to it. Before they've unwrapped you of your kerchief, and then of your cap — before they'll let you walk out for air, drink wine, lie with your wife, eat melons — the odds are you've relapsed into some new misery. Kidney stones have this privilege: they carry themselves clean away. Other maladies always leave some imprint and alteration behind, which makes the body prone to the next disease — they lend each other a hand. The excusable ones are content to possess us without spreading or ushering in their followers; but courteous and kind are those whose passing leaves us some profit. Since the stones came, I find myself free of my other ailments — more so, it seems to me, than I was before — and I haven't had a fever since. I reason that the extreme and frequent vomiting I'm subject to scours me out, while my loss of appetite for this and that, and the strange fasts I'm forced to keep, digest away my unhealthy humors, and nature voids in those stones whatever is excess and harmful in me. Don't ever tell me it's medicine bought too dear. What about all the stinking potions, the cauteries, incisions, sweats, drainage-cords, diets — all the apparatus of cure that, because we can't withstand its violence and persistence, so often brings us to the grave instead? So when I have the stone, I count it as medicine; when I'm free of it, as outright deliverance.

And here is another particular favor my disease does me: it pretty much plays its own game by itself and lets me play mine, if I have the courage to take it up. In its greatest fury I have held out ten hours on horseback. Just endure — you need no other regimen. Play, run, dine, do this and that if you can; your indulgence will do you more good than harm. Try saying that to a man with the pox, the gout, or a hernia! Other diseases bind you everywhere — they rack everything you do, throw your whole order into disarray, and drag your entire way of life into their orbit. This one only pinches the skin. It leaves your understanding and your will entirely at your own disposal, along with your tongue, your hands, and your feet. It wakes you up rather than stupefies you. The soul is struck by the burning of a fever, crushed by an epilepsy, knocked off her seat by a fierce migraine — in short, staggered by every disease that injures the whole mass or the noblest parts. This one never touches the soul. If anything goes wrong with her, it's her own fault — she has betrayed, unhorsed, and abandoned herself. Only fools let themselves be persuaded that this hard, solid body baked in our kidneys can be dissolved by drinks. Once it stirs, there is nothing to do but give it passage; and for that matter, it will make one for itself.

I notice one more particular convenience in it: it's a disease that leaves us little to guess at. We're spared the trouble that other diseases throw us into through the uncertainty of their causes, conditions, and course — a trouble that is infinitely painful. There's no need of consultations and doctoral interpretations: the senses tell us perfectly well what it is and where it is.

With arguments like these, strong and weak — like Cicero with the disease of his old age — I try to rock my imagination to sleep and dress its wounds. If they look worse tomorrow, I'll provide new stratagems. And here's proof it's true: lately I've reached the point where the slightest movement forces pure blood out of my kidneys. So what? I move about exactly as before, and ride after my hounds with a young man's insolent ardor. I count it a very good settlement for an affliction of that importance, when all it costs me is a dull heaviness and unease in that region. It's some large stone wasting and consuming the substance of my kidneys — and of my life, which I drain away little by little, not without a certain natural pleasure, as an excretion that is from now on superfluous and a nuisance.

And if I feel something stirring, don't imagine I run to consult my pulse or my urine in order to brace myself with some annoying precaution. I'll feel the pain soon enough, without lengthening it in advance with the disease of fear. He who fears he will suffer already suffers what he fears. Add to that the doubts and ignorance of those who presume to expound nature's designs and inner workings, and the endless false forecasts of their art, and we ought to conclude that her ways are inscrutable and utterly unknown. There is great uncertainty, variety, and obscurity in what she promises and what she threatens. With the exception of old age — which is an unmistakable sign of death's approach — in everything else I see few signs of the future we could ground a prediction on. I judge myself only by actual sensation, not by reasoning. What would be the use, since I'm resolved to bring nothing to it but waiting and patience? Would you like to know what I gain by that? Look at the people who do otherwise, who hang on so many different persuasions and counsels — how often imagination presses on them without any pain in the body at all! Many times, while feeling well and safe and entirely free of these dangerous attacks, I've amused myself by describing them to physicians as just beginning in me. I sat through the verdict of their dreadful conclusions in perfect comfort — and came away that much more indebted to the favor of God, and that much better instructed in the vanity of this art.

There is nothing youth should be urged to more than activity and alertness. Our life is nothing but movement. I get myself moving with difficulty, and I'm slow at everything — rising, going to bed, eating. Seven in the morning is early for me; where I'm in charge, I never dine before eleven or sup before six. I used to blame the fevers and other illnesses I fell into on the sluggishness that too much sleep had laid on me, and I have always regretted falling back asleep in the morning. Plato is angrier at excess of sleep than at excess of drink. I like to sleep on a hard bed, and alone — even without my wife, in the royal style — and fairly well covered. My bed is never warmed for me, though since I've grown old they give me, when I need them, cloths to warm my feet and stomach. People found fault with the great Scipio for being a heavy sleeper — for no other reason, in my opinion, than that it annoyed them that he alone should have nothing in him to find fault with. If I'm particular about anything in my way of living, it's about my bed more than anything else; but in general I give way and adapt to necessity as well as anyone. Sleep has taken up a great part of my life, and even at my present age I still sleep eight or nine hours at a stretch. I am weaning myself, with profit, from this leaning toward sloth, and I'm clearly the better for it. The change is a little hard, true, but in three days it's done. And I know few men who can live on less sleep when they need to, who keep up their exercise more steadily, or who feel long journeys less.

My body is up to steady exertion, but not violent or sudden effort. These days I steer clear of strenuous exercise, the kind that puts me in a sweat: my limbs tire before they warm. I can stay on my feet a whole day, and walking never wearies me; but from my youth I've preferred to ride when the road is paved — on foot you're up to your haunches in mud, and small fellows like me get elbowed and jostled in the streets for want of presence. And I have always loved to rest, sitting or lying, with my heels as high as my seat or higher.
