Of Experience
(Part 2 of 5)

"Nothing is more shameful," as Cicero says, "than to let assertion and approval run ahead of knowledge and perception."

Aristarchus said that in ancient times you could scarcely find seven wise men in the world, and in his own day scarcely as many fools. Don't we have more reason to say that of our age than he had of his? Assertion and stubbornness are sure signs of stupidity. Here is a fellow who has fallen flat on his nose a hundred times in one day, and there he is back at his therefores, as resolute and sturdy as ever. You'd think some new soul, some fresh vigor of understanding had been poured into him since — that he was like that ancient son of the earth who, as Lucan tells it, recovered fresh strength in his broken limbs every time he touched his mother ground. Does this incorrigible blockhead imagine he picks up a new mind by picking up a new argument?

It's from my own experience that I accuse human ignorance — and that, in my opinion, is the surest lesson in the whole school of the world. Those who won't conclude it in themselves from so empty an example as mine, or their own, let them take it from Socrates, the master of masters. The philosopher Antisthenes used to tell his disciples, "Come, let's go hear Socrates; there I'll be a pupil along with you." And while he held the Stoic doctrine that virtue alone was enough to make a life completely happy, needing nothing else whatever, he added: "except the strength of Socrates."

That long attention I spend studying myself trains me to judge other people tolerably well, too — and there are few things I speak of better, or with a better excuse. I often manage to see my friends' qualities more precisely than they do themselves. I have astonished some of them with the accuracy of my description, and put them on notice about themselves. Because I've been used since childhood to seeing my own life mirrored in other people's, I have acquired a studious bent that way; and when I'm paying attention, I let little around me escape that serves the purpose — faces, moods, talk. I study it all: what I should avoid and what I should follow.

In my friends, then, I discover their inward inclinations by what they produce outwardly. But I don't sort this endless variety of scattered, disconnected actions into species and chapters, distributing my parcels neatly under known headings and classes — for, as Virgil says, there's no counting how many kinds there are, or what their names might be.

The wise deliver their ideas more specifically, piece by piece. I, who can see no further into things than experience shows me, present mine loosely, without rules, by trial — in disjointed installments, as something that cannot be said all at once and in a lump. Coherence and consistency are not to be found in low, common souls like ours. Wisdom is a solid, complete building, every piece in its place, every piece bearing its mark; "wisdom alone is wholly turned in upon itself," as Cicero says. I leave it to the experts — and I'm not sure even they can pull it off, in a business so tangled, so minute, and so governed by chance — to marshal this infinite diversity of faces into distinct bodies, to pin down our inconstancy and set it in order. I find it hard not only to connect our actions one to another, but even to label each one by itself with any single ruling quality — that's how ambiguous and many-colored they look under different lights.

What was remarked as rare in Perseus, king of Macedon — that his mind, fixing itself to no one condition, wandered through every kind of life, displaying manners so wild and erratic that neither he nor anyone else knew what sort of man he was — seems to me to fit nearly the whole world. And I have known one man especially, of his very stamp, to whom the conclusion applies even better: no middle setting anywhere, always careering from one extreme to the other for reasons nobody could guess; no path without crossings and astonishing contradictions; no single quality pure and unmixed. The best guess anyone will ever make about him is that he worked at making himself known by being unknowable.

You need sound ears to hear yourself frankly judged. And since few of us can stand to hear it without being stung, those who risk doing it for us show a rare effect of friendship. It is genuinely loving someone to be willing to wound and offend him for his own good. I find it harsh to judge a man whose bad qualities outnumber his good ones. Plato requires three things in anyone who would examine another man's soul: knowledge, goodwill, boldness.

I used to be asked, sometimes, what I would have thought myself fit for, if anyone had wanted to make use of me while I was still the right age — "while better blood gave me vigor," as Virgil has it, "before envious old age frosted my temples."

"For nothing," I said. And I'm happy to excuse myself from knowing anything that enslaves me to other people. But I would have told my master the truth, and regulated his conduct, if he'd allowed it — not wholesale, by schoolroom lessons, which I don't understand (and from which I see no true reform spring in those who do understand them), but by watching him at leisure, on every occasion, judging him simply and naturally, as an eyewitness, point by point; letting him know plainly how he stood in common opinion, against the chorus of his flatterers. There's not one of us who wouldn't be worse than kings if we were corrupted as constantly as they are by that breed of riffraff. How could it be otherwise, if even Alexander, that great king and philosopher, couldn't defend himself from them?

I would have had fidelity enough for the job, and judgment, and independence. It would have to be an office without a name — otherwise it loses its grace and its effect — and it's not a part just anyone can play. Even truth doesn't have the privilege of being spoken at all hours and in every manner; noble as its use is, it has its limits and discretions. It often happens, the way the world goes, that a man drops truth into a prince's ear not just uselessly but harmfully, even unjustly. No one will convince me that a virtuous rebuke cannot be viciously delivered, or that the interest of the substance should never give way to the interest of the form.

For such work I'd want a man content with his fortune — "pleased with what he is, and wanting nothing more," as Martial puts it — and of middling rank. On the one hand, he wouldn't be afraid to touch his master's heart to the quick for fear of losing his place; on the other, being of no great station, he'd have easier dealings with all sorts of people. And I would confine this office to one man only; to spread the privilege of such liberty and familiarity among many would breed a damaging irreverence. Of that one man, above all, I would require the fidelity of silence.

A king is not to be believed when he brags of his firmness in standing the shock of the enemy for his glory's sake if, for his own profit and improvement, he can't stand the freedom of a friend's advice — which has no power beyond pinching his ear, everything else remaining in his own hands. Now, no class of men needs true and frank advice as badly as kings do. They sustain a public life; they have to satisfy the opinion of a great crowd of spectators; and since everyone around them hides whatever might turn them from their course, they find themselves, without noticing, sunk in the hatred and loathing of their people — often over things they could have avoided at no cost even to their pleasures, if only someone had warned and corrected them in time. Their favorites usually look out for themselves more than for the master; and it pays them to, since in truth most offices of real friendship, when tried on a sovereign, are rough and dangerous experiments. What's needed there is not only great affection and frankness, but courage too.

In short, this whole hodgepodge I'm scribbling here is nothing but a register of the trials of my own life — which, as far as inward health goes, is exemplary enough, provided you take its instruction against the grain. But as for bodily health, no man can offer more useful experience than I can, because I present it pure, not corrupted or doctored by art or theory. In the matter of medicine, experience is on its own dunghill, and reason yields it the whole field. Tiberius said that anyone who had lived twenty years should be answerable to himself for what hurt or helped him, and should know how to manage himself without medicine. He might have learned that from Socrates, who advised his disciples to make their health a chief study, adding that it would be strange if a man of sense, attending to his exercise and his diet, didn't understand better than any doctor what was good or bad for him.

And medicine itself professes that experience is the test of all its operations. So Plato was right to say that to be a true physician, a man should first have passed through all the diseases he means to cure, and all the accidents and conditions he is to judge of. It's only fair they should catch the pox if they want to know how to cure it. I would honestly trust myself to such hands. The others merely guide us, like the man who paints seas and rocks and harbors while sitting at his table, sailing his model ship there in perfect safety — but put him to the real work and he doesn't know which end to begin at. They describe our diseases the way a town crier describes a lost horse or dog: such a coat, such a height, such an ear — but bring the animal to him and he can't pick it out for all that. If medicine ever gives me some real, visible relief, I will cry out in good earnest, with Horace, "show me a science that works, and I'll take its hand."

The arts that promise to keep our bodies and souls in health promise the most of all — and none keep their promise less. And in our time, those who profess these arts among us show their effects less than any other sort of men. The most you can say of them is that they sell medicinal drugs; that they are physicians, you cannot say.

I have lived long enough to give an account of the regimen that has carried me this far. For anyone who wants to try it, I've been his taster: I have run the experiment. Here are a few of the articles, as memory supplies them. I have no habit that hasn't varied with circumstances; I only record the ones I've known best, the ones that have held the most possession of me up to now.

My way of life is the same in sickness as in health. The same bed, the same hours, the same food, even the same drink serve me in both conditions; I add nothing but a little more or less moderation, according to my strength and appetite. Health, for me, means keeping my accustomed state undisturbed. I see that sickness knocks me off it on one side, and that if I let the doctors have their way, they'll knock me off it on the other — so that between fortune and art, I'm forced clean off my road. The thing I believe most certainly is this: that I cannot be hurt by the use of things I have been so long accustomed to.

It's custom's business to give a man's life whatever shape she pleases; in that, she is everything — she is Circe's potion, changing our nature however she likes. How many nations, only three steps from us, think the fear of the evening damp — which so obviously hurts us — a ridiculous fancy; and our own boatmen and peasants laugh at it too. You make a German sick by laying him on a mattress, an Italian by laying him on a featherbed, and a Frenchman by leaving him without curtains and a fire. A Spanish stomach can't hold out at eating the way we do, nor ours at drinking like the Swiss.

A German amused me greatly at Augsburg by attacking our open hearths with the very arguments we always use against their stoves. To tell the truth, that smothered heat, and the smell of the heated stuff that gives it off, do bother people who aren't used to them — though not me. And since their heat is even, constant, and spread everywhere, without flame, without smoke, and without the wind that comes pouring down our chimneys, their stoves stand up to comparison with our fires in plenty of ways. Why don't we imitate the Roman architecture? They say that in the old days fires were built not inside the house but outside, at its foot, and the heat was carried through the whole building by pipes laid inside the walls, winding around the rooms to be warmed — I have seen this plainly described somewhere in Seneca. This German, hearing me praise the conveniences and beauties of his city — which it truly deserves — began pitying me for having to leave; and the first hardship he named was the heaviness of head the chimneys elsewhere would bring on me. He had heard somebody make that complaint, and fastened it on us — custom having robbed him of the means of noticing it at home. Any heat that comes from a fire weakens and dulls me. Evenus said fire is life's best seasoning; I'd rather warm myself any other way.

We are afraid of wine from the bottom of the cask; in Portugal those fumes are counted delicious — it's the drink of princes. In short, every nation has plenty of customs and usages that are not merely unknown to other nations, but savage and miraculous in their sight.

What should we do with people who accept no evidence unless it's in print, who won't believe men unless they're in a book, or truth unless it's of a respectable age? We dignify our foolishness when we send it to the press. "I have read such a thing" carries a great deal more weight than "I have heard such a thing." But I don't distrust a man's mouth any more than his pen; I know men write as carelessly as they speak; I value this age as if it were already a past one; and I quote a friend as readily as Aulus Gellius or Macrobius, and what I've seen as readily as what they wrote. As they hold of virtue that it is not greater for having lasted longer, so I hold of truth that it is none the wiser for being older. I often say it's pure folly that sets us chasing foreign and bookish examples; the supply is just as rich now as it was in the days of Homer and Plato. Isn't the truth that we want honor from the citation rather than truth in the matter at hand — as if proof borrowed from the print shops of Vascosan or Plantin counted for more than what's there to be seen in our own village? Or else we simply lack the wit to sift what's in front of us and judge it clearly enough to make an example of it. For if we say we lack the authority to make our testimony believed, we're missing the point. In my opinion, the most ordinary, common, familiar things — if we could only find their light — would furnish the greatest miracles of nature and the most wonderful examples, especially on the subject of human actions.

Now, on that subject — setting aside what I've gathered from books, and what Aristotle says of Andron the Argive, who crossed the parched sands of Libya without drinking — a gentleman who has acquitted himself well in several offices said, in company where I was present, that he had ridden from Madrid to Lisbon in the heat of summer without drinking at all. He is healthy and vigorous for his age, and there is nothing unusual in his way of living except this: he can go two or three months, even a whole year, so he has told me, without drinking. He feels thirsty sometimes, but he lets it pass; he holds that thirst is an appetite which fades easily on its own, and he drinks more from whim than from need or pleasure.

Here is another. Not long ago I found one of the most learned men in France — a man of considerable fortune, too — studying in a corner of a hall they had partitioned off for him with a tapestry, while a rabble of his servants carried on around him at full license. He told me — and Seneca says nearly the same of himself — that he turned the racket to profit: battered by the noise, he gathered and withdrew into himself all the more for contemplation, and the storm of voices drove his thoughts inward. As a student at Padua he had kept his study so long amid the rattle of coaches and the tumult of the square that he trained himself not just to ignore the noise but to use it, in the service of his studies. Socrates, when Alcibiades marveled at how he could endure his wife's perpetual scolding, answered: "the way men do who are used to the constant creak of the water-wheel." I am just the opposite. I have a tender head, easily ruffled; when it's bent on something, the least buzzing of a fly murders it.

Seneca in his youth, having warmly taken up the example of Sextius and resolved to eat nothing that had been killed, went a whole year without such food — with pleasure, he said — and gave it up only so he wouldn't be suspected of borrowing the rule from certain new religions that prescribed it. He likewise adopted, from the precepts of Attalus, the custom of never lying on bedding that yielded under his weight, and even into old age used the kind that doesn't give. What the usage of his day made men count as hardness, the usage of ours makes us count as softness.

Just look at the difference between my laborers' way of living and my own: the Scythians and the Indians are no more remote from my capacity and my form. I have taken on charity boys as servants, who quit my kitchen and my livery soon after, just to get back to their old life; I found one of them afterwards picking mussels out of the sewer for his dinner, and neither pleading nor threats could pull him away from the sweetness he found in poverty. Beggars have their splendors and their luxuries, just like the rich — and, people say, their ranks and their politics.

These are the effects of custom. She can mold us not only into whatever form she pleases — the sages say we should apply ourselves to the best one, and she will soon make it easy for us — but also into change and variety, which is the noblest and most useful lesson she teaches. The best of my bodily qualities is that I'm flexible and not very stubborn. I have inclinations that are more my own, more usual, and more agreeable to me than others; but it takes very little to turn me from them, and I slip easily into the opposite course. A young man ought to cross his own rules now and then, to wake up his vigor and keep it from going stale and rusty. There is no way of life so weak and silly as one run entirely by rule and discipline — like Juvenal's man, who consults the almanac before letting himself be carried out to the first milestone, and if the corner of his eye itches, won't reach for ointment until he has checked his horoscope.

If he'll take my advice, a young man should even throw himself into excess once in a while; otherwise the smallest indulgence will lay him flat, and he'll make himself tiresome and disagreeable in company. The worst quality in a well-bred man is fussiness — being bound to one particular way of doing things; and it is particular, if it isn't pliable and supple. It's a kind of disgrace not to be able, or not to dare, to do what you see everyone around you doing — people like that should stay home. In any man it's unbecoming; in a soldier it's a vice, and intolerable: a soldier, as Philopoemen said, should accustom himself to every variety and unevenness of life.

Though I was brought up, as far as possible, for liberty and independence, still — growing old, and having settled by sheer indifference into certain forms (my age is past schooling now; it has nothing left to do but hold itself together as best it can) — custom has already stamped her character into me, in certain things, so deeply that I'd call it excess to give them up. Without doing violence to myself, I cannot sleep in the daytime, or eat between meals, or eat breakfast, or go to bed without a long gap — three hours or so — between supper and sleep; cannot make children except before sleep, and never standing; cannot bear my own sweat; cannot quench my thirst with plain water or with wine unmixed; cannot keep my head bare for long, or get my hair cut after dinner. I'd be as uncomfortable without my gloves as without my shirt, or going unwashed when I rise from the table or my bed; and I couldn't sleep without canopy and curtains, as if they were necessities. I could dine without a tablecloth; but without a clean napkin, German style, only with real discomfort — I soil napkins more than the Germans or the Italians do, making little use of either spoon or fork. I'm sorry we never kept up the fashion, begun on the example of kings, of changing napkins with each course the way we change plates. We're told of Marius, that hard-bitten old soldier, that as he aged he grew fussy about his drink and would only use one particular cup of his own. I have let myself develop the same fancy for a certain shape of glass, and I don't like drinking from a common glass any more than from a common hand. All metal offends me next to clear, transparent material: let my eyes have their taste too, as far as they're able.

Several such daintinesses I owe to custom. Nature, for her part, has contributed some of her own: I cannot handle more than two full meals a day without overloading my stomach, nor skip one of them entirely without filling up with wind, drying out my mouth, and deadening my appetite. I take real harm from too much evening air. These last few years, on night marches — and they often run the whole night — after five or six hours my stomach turns queasy, my head starts pounding violently, and I never make it to daybreak without vomiting. When the others go to breakfast, I go to sleep; and when I get up, I'm as brisk and cheerful as before.

I had always been told that the night damp rises only at nightfall; but in recent years the long and familiar company of a lord — a man convinced that the damp is sharpest and most dangerous as the sun declines, an hour or two before it sets, and who carefully avoids that hour while shrugging off the night's — nearly stamped on me not so much his reasoning as his sensations. What — shall mere doubt and inquiry strike our imagination hard enough to change us? Those who give way to these tendencies all at once and headlong bring total ruin on themselves. And I am sorry for the several gentlemen who, through the folly of their doctors, have shut themselves up indoors while still young and healthy. Better to endure a cough than to lose, forever and through disuse, your commerce with common life in things of such great use. Malignant science, that forbids us the pleasantest hours of the day! Let us keep possession to the last. Mostly, a man toughens himself by being stubborn, and corrects his constitution — as Caesar did his falling sickness — by sheer contempt. A man should commit himself to the best rules, but not enslave himself to them, except to the kind (if any such exist) where obligation and servitude actually pay.

Kings and philosophers go to stool, and ladies too. Public lives are bound to ceremony; mine, obscure and private, enjoys every natural dispensation — and soldier and Gascon, besides, are qualities a little prone to indiscretion. So I'll say this about the act of relieving nature: it's good to assign it to certain set, nighttime hours, and to force yourself to that by custom, as I have done. But don't enslave yourself, as I have done in my declining years, to a particular comfort of place and seat for the purpose, making it a burden through long sitting. And yet, in the fouler offices, isn't there some excuse for demanding extra care and cleanliness? "Man is by nature a clean and delicate animal," as Seneca says. Of all the actions of nature, that is the one I am most impatient to have interrupted. I've seen many a soldier troubled by an unruly belly; mine and I never miss our appointment, which is at the leap out of bed — unless some urgent business or some sickness intrudes.

So, as I was saying, I think sick men can put themselves nowhere safer than sitting tight in the course of life they were bred and trained to. Change, whatever it is, unsettles and damages. Do you believe chestnuts can hurt a man of Périgord or of Lucca, or milk and cheese the mountain folk? Yet we prescribe them a way of life that's not just new but contrary — a change the healthy themselves couldn't endure. Order water for a seventy-year-old Breton; shut a sailor in a stove-heated room; forbid a Basque footman to walk. You are stripping them of motion, and in the end of air and of light.
