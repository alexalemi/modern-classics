To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die
(Part 1 of 2)

Cicero says that to philosophize is nothing else than to prepare ourselves for death. And he has a point. Study and contemplation draw the soul partway out of us, set it working apart from the body — which is a kind of apprenticeship to death, a first resemblance of it. Or else it is because all the wisdom and reasoning in the world comes down, in the end, to one lesson: do not be afraid to die.

Honestly, either reason is mocking us, or it can have no other aim than our contentment. Its whole business is to make us live well and, as Scripture says, at our ease. Every opinion in the world agrees on this much: pleasure is our goal. The schools take different roads to it, but that is where they are all headed. Otherwise we would show them the door at once — who listens to a man who proposes misery and suffering as the destination?

On this point the squabbles of the philosophical sects are just words. Let us skip past such clever trifles, as Seneca says. There is more stubbornness and point-scoring in them than suits so sacred a profession. But whatever role a man takes on, he always mixes some of himself into the performance.

Let the philosophers say what they like: even in virtue, the thing we are all aiming at is pleasure. I enjoy rattling their ears with that word — they gag on it so. And if it means some supreme delight and contentment, that delight belongs to virtue more than to any other helper. This pleasure, being livelier, more sinewy, more robust, more vigorous, is only the more seriously pleasurable for it. We ought to have given virtue the name of pleasure — the kinder, gentler, more natural name — instead of the name for force we hung on it.

As for the lower sort of pleasure, if it deserved that lovely name at all, it should have had to win it in open competition, not hold it by privilege. I find it less free of troubles and crossings than virtue itself. Besides being momentary, fluid, and fragile, it has its vigils, its fasts, its labors, its sweat and its blood. It comes with so many kinds of sharp, wounding passions, and drags such a heavy boredom behind it, that it equals the harshest penance.

And we are wrong to think those discomforts act as spur and seasoning to its sweetness, the way one contrary quickens another in nature — and then to turn around and say, when we come to virtue, that the same difficulties make her grim, austere, and out of reach. In virtue, far better than in indulgence, they ennoble, sharpen, and heighten the divine and perfect pleasure she wins for us. A man who weighs her cost against her fruit makes himself unworthy of her; he understands neither the blessing nor how to use it. Those who keep preaching that the pursuit of virtue is steep and laborious, but the enjoyment of her delightful — what are they telling us, except that she is never any fun? What human effort ever arrives at the enjoyment? The most perfect men have had to settle for aspiring to her, approaching without ever possessing. But they are mistaken, because in every pleasure we know, the pursuit itself is pleasant. The attempt takes its flavor from the thing it reaches for; it is a good part of the result, the same flesh and blood as the result. The happiness that glitters in virtue shines through all her approaches and avenues, from the first gate to the farthest wall.

Now, of all the gifts virtue gives us, contempt for death is one of the greatest. It is what furnishes human life with a soft and easy tranquility, what gives us a pure and friendly taste for living — and without it every other pleasure goes dark. That is why all the rules of philosophy meet and agree on this one article. The philosophers do also teach us, with one voice, to despise pain, poverty, and the other accidents human life is exposed to, but not with the same urgency. Partly because those accidents are not so inevitable — most of mankind goes through life without ever tasting poverty, and some without sickness or sorrow, like Xenophilus the musician, who lived a hundred and six years in perfect, uninterrupted health. And partly because, at the very worst, death can cut all the other troubles short whenever we please. But death itself is inevitable. As Horace says, we are all herded the same way; everyone's lot tumbles from the urn sooner or later, and puts us aboard the boat for everlasting exile.

So if death frightens us, it is a perpetual torment, and one that nothing relieves. There is no direction it cannot come from. We can keep twisting our heads this way and that, as in enemy country, but it hangs over us always — like the stone over Tantalus, as Cicero says.

Our courts often send condemned criminals back to be executed at the scene of the crime. Lead them past fine houses on the way; lay on the best dinner you can. Sicilian delicacies will not coax out their flavor, says Horace, and no song of birds and harps will bring back sleep. Do you think the condemned can enjoy any of it? Do you think that with the journey's end standing in plain sight, the taste of all those treats would not curdle in their mouths? He counts the days, says Claudian; he measures his life by the length of the road, and is tortured by the blow to come.

The end of our race is death. It is the necessary target of our course, and if it terrifies us, how can we take a single step without trembling? The remedy ordinary people use is simply not to think about it. But what brutish stupidity can supply blindness that thick? That is bridling the donkey by the tail — setting a man, as Lucretius says, to plan his steps walking backwards. No wonder he keeps falling into the ditch.

People are frightened by the mere mention of death; most cross themselves at the word, as if it were the name of the devil. And because making a will has to do with dying, no one can be persuaded to pick up the pen until the doctor has passed final sentence on him — and then, between the pain and the terror, God knows what state of mind he is in to do it.

The Romans, because that poor syllable death grated so harshly on their ears and sounded so ominous, found a way to soften it and spin it out. Instead of saying "he is dead," they said "he has lived," or "he has ceased to live." As long as life got a mention, even life that was over, it carried some note of consolation. From them we borrowed our expression "the late Monsieur So-and-so."

Maybe, as the saying goes, the time we have lived is worth our money. I was born between eleven and noon on the last day of February, 1533, counting the year as we do now, from the first of January. It is just two weeks since I turned thirty-nine, and I reckon to live at least as long again. Meanwhile, to fret about a thing so far off would be folly. But then — the young and the old leave life on exactly the same terms. No one exits it any differently than if he had just walked in. And there is no man so old and decrepit that, with Methuselah in mind, he does not figure he has another twenty good years in him.

You fool — who has guaranteed you the term of your life? You are leaning on doctors' tales. Look at facts and experience instead. By the ordinary run of things, you have been living a long while now on special favor. You have already outlasted the usual span of life. Count your acquaintances if you doubt it: how many died before reaching your age, against how many got there? Even among the men who made their lives famous, take a list, and I will wager you find more who died before thirty-five than after. It is reasonable, and pious too, to take our example from the humanity of Jesus Christ himself — and he ended his life at thirty-three. The greatest of merely mortal men, Alexander, died at the same age.

And how many different ways death has of catching us off guard! As Horace says, no man can guess, hour to hour, what danger to dodge. Set aside fevers and pleurisies: who would ever have imagined that a duke of Brittany would be crushed to death in a crowd, as one was at the entry of Pope Clement, my neighbor, into Lyons? Have you not seen one of our own kings killed at a tournament? And did not one of his ancestors die of being jostled by a hog? Aeschylus, warned that a falling house would kill him, took every precaution — for all the good it did, since an eagle dropped a tortoise on his head out of the open sky. Another man choked on a grape seed. An emperor died of a scratch from a comb while having his hair done; Aemilius Lepidus from stumbling over his own threshold; Aufidius from bumping against the door on his way into the council chamber. And between the very thighs of women: Cornelius Gallus the praetor; Tigillinus, captain of the watch at Rome; Ludovico, son of Guido di Gonzaga, Marquis of Mantua; and — a worse example still — Speusippus, a philosopher of Plato's school, and one of our Popes. Poor judge Bebius granted one party in a lawsuit eight days' adjournment — and in the meantime death adjourned him; his own stay of life had run out. Caius Julius, a physician, was anointing a patient's eyes when death closed his own. And if I may bring in an example from my own blood: my brother, Captain Saint-Martin, twenty-three years old and already well proven in courage, was playing a match of tennis when the ball struck him a little above the right ear. There was no sign of wound or bruise; he did not even sit down to rest. But five or six hours later he was dead of the stroke that blow had caused.

With examples this frequent and this ordinary passing before our eyes every day, how can a man possibly shake off the thought of death, or stop feeling that it has us by the throat at every moment?

What does it matter, you will say, how it comes, so long as a man does not torment himself waiting for it? I am of that mind myself. If there were any way at all to duck the blow — even by crawling under a calfskin — I am not the man who would be ashamed of the trick. All I want is to pass my time at ease, and whatever recreations get me there, I take hold of, however inglorious and unexemplary you please. As Horace says, I would rather seem crazy and lazy, so long as my faults please me or at least escape my notice, than be wise and snarling about it.

But it is folly to think you can get there by that road. They come, they go, they trot, they dance — and of death, not a word. All very fine. Yet when it does come — to them, to their wives, their children, their friends — surprising them unguarded and unprepared, then what torments, what cries, what madness and despair! Have you ever seen anything so crushed, so changed, so confounded? We have to provide for it earlier than that. And this brutish carelessness, even if it could lodge in the head of a man of sense (which I think utterly impossible), sells us its merchandise far too dear. If death were an enemy we could dodge, I would advise borrowing the weapons of cowardice. But it is not. It catches the runaway and the coward as surely as the honest man standing his ground — it hounds the fleeing soldier too, as Horace says, and has no mercy on the soft knees and timid back of unwarlike youth. And no armor is tempered well enough to protect us: let a man hide, wary, under iron and brass, says Propertius, and death will still pull his head out of its shell.

So let us learn to stand our ground and fight him. And to begin stripping him of his greatest advantage over us, let us take exactly the opposite road from the usual one. Let us rob death of his strangeness. Let us keep him company, get used to him, have nothing more often in our minds than death. At every moment let our imagination picture him in all his shapes. When a horse stumbles, when a tile falls, at the smallest prick of a pin, let us say it at once — "Well, and what if that had been death itself?" — and stiffen and steady ourselves on the spot. In the middle of our feasting and fun, let us keep the refrain of our frail condition playing, never letting pleasure carry us so far away that we forget how many ways this jollity of ours is death's target, and how many fists he shakes at it. The Egyptians did just that: at the height of their feasts, with the good cheer at its best, they had the dried skeleton of a man carried into the room as a reminder to the guests. Believe each day that dawns to be your last, says Horace, and the unhoped-for hour will be all the more welcome when it comes.

Where death waits for us is uncertain; let us wait for him everywhere. To think about death in advance is to think about freedom in advance. He who has learned to die has unlearned to be a slave. There is nothing evil in life for the man who has fully grasped that losing life is not an evil. Knowing how to die frees us from all subjection and constraint. When the miserable king of Macedon, his prisoner, sent to beg Paulus Aemilius not to parade him in his triumph, Paulus answered: "Let him make that request to himself."

In truth, in everything, if nature does not lend a hand, it is very hard for art and effort to get anywhere. By nature I am not melancholy, but I am a brooder, and there is nothing I have entertained myself with longer than thoughts of death — even in the most reckless season of my life, when my green youth, as Catullus has it, was running riot in its pleasant spring. Among the ladies, in the thick of the games, more than one onlooker probably thought I was chewing on some jealousy, or the uncertainty of some hope. In fact I was remembering some man or other who, a few days before, had come home from a party just like this one — his head as full of idleness, love, and good times as mine was then — and been carried off by a burning fever. For all I knew, the same fate was hanging at my ear. Soon the present will have been, says Lucretius, never to be called back.

Yet that thought wrinkled my forehead no more than any other. It is impossible not to feel the sting of such imaginings at first. But handle them, turn them over, come back to them, and in time they grow so familiar they are no trouble at all. Otherwise I, for one, would live in perpetual fright and frenzy — for no man ever trusted his life less, no man ever counted less on its length. Health, which I have so far enjoyed strong, vigorous, and rarely interrupted, does not stretch my hopes of living, and sickness does not shrink them. Every minute I feel myself slipping away. And one thought runs through my head constantly: whatever can be done tomorrow can be done today. Hazards and dangers, in truth, do little or nothing to hurry our end. If we consider the thousands of other threats still hanging over our heads, beyond the one that looks most pressing, we will find that healthy or sick, at sea or by the fireside, in battle or idle at home, we are all exactly as close to it as each other. No man is frailer than another, says Seneca, and no man surer of his tomorrow.

For anything I still have to do before I die, the longest stretch of leisure would look too short — even if the whole business would take an hour. Someone leafing through my notebooks the other day found a memo about something I wanted done after my death. I told him, and it was the plain truth, that though I had been only a league from home, healthy and cheerful, I had hurried to write it down on the spot, because I could not be sure of living until I got back. Since I am forever brooding over my own thoughts and settling them within myself, I am at every hour about as ready as I am ever going to be; and death, whenever he comes, can bring nothing with him that I have not expected for a long time. We should always be booted and spurred, as far as we can manage, and ready to go — and above all take care, when the time comes, to have no business left with anyone but ourselves. Why, asks Horace, do we tease so short a life with so many big projects? We will find work enough there without adding more.

One man complains, more than of death itself, that it cuts him off from a glorious victory. Another, that he must move out before marrying off his daughter or finishing his children's education. One mourns the loss of his wife's company, another his son's, as the chief comfort of his existence. For my part, I am — thank God — at this moment in such a state that I am ready to move out whenever it pleases Him, without regret for anything at all. I am loosening every worldly tie; my goodbyes are already half said to everyone but myself.
