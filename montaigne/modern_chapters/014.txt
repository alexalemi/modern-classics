Of Solitude

Let's skip the long old debate between the active life and the solitary one. And as for the fine sayings that ambition and greed hide behind — that we are not born for ourselves but for the public — let's appeal boldly to the people actually in public life. Let them put their hands on their hearts and tell us whether titles, offices, and the bustle of the world aren't really pursued for private advantage at public expense. The corrupt methods men use nowadays to climb to the heights their ambition aims at show plainly enough that the ends can't be worth much.

Tell ambition this: she's the one who gives us a taste for solitude in the first place. What does she avoid more than company? What does she crave more than elbow room?

A man can behave well or badly anywhere. But if Bias is right that the greater part of men is the worse part, or the Preacher that there's not one good man in a thousand — good men, as Juvenal says, being scarcer than the gates of Thebes or the mouths of the Nile — then the contagion in a crowd is very dangerous. You must either imitate the vicious or hate them, and both are risky: to grow like them because they are many, or to hate the many because they are unlike you.

Merchants who go to sea are right to be careful that the people who board with them aren't blasphemers or scoundrels — they consider such company unlucky. That's why Bias said cheerfully to some shipmates who were calling on the gods in the middle of a dangerous storm, "Quiet — keep your voices down, so they don't realize you're here with me." And a more striking case: Albuquerque, viceroy of the Indies for King Emmanuel of Portugal, in extreme danger of shipwreck, hoisted a young boy onto his shoulders for one reason only — that in their shared danger the boy's innocence might serve as his protection and recommend him to God's favor, and so get him safely to shore.

It's not that a wise man can't live content anywhere, alone even in the thick of a palace crowd. But given the choice, the philosopher will tell you he'd flee the very sight of the crowd. He'll endure it if he must; left to himself, he'll choose to be alone. He can't feel sufficiently rid of vice while he still has to wrestle with it in other men. Charondas punished as wicked those convicted of keeping wicked company. There is nothing so unsociable and so sociable as man: unsociable by his vice, sociable by his nature. And Antisthenes, I think, gave a poor answer to the man who reproached him for keeping bad company, when he said that physicians live well enough among the sick. For if physicians do help the sick get healthy, they certainly damage their own health through the contagion, the constant sight of disease, and the daily handling of it.

Now the goal, as I take it, is always the same: to live more at leisure and at one's ease. But people don't always take the right road to it. They often think they've quit business altogether when they've only traded one job for another. There's hardly less trouble in governing a private family than a whole kingdom. Wherever the mind is tangled up, it's tangled entirely; and domestic chores are no less bothersome for being less important. Besides, by shaking off the court and the marketplace, we haven't shaken off the chief torments of life. As Horace says, it's reason and prudence that banish care, not a house with a commanding view of the sea. Ambition, greed, indecision, fear, and runaway desires don't stay behind just because we leave the country — black care, as Horace also says, sits right behind the horseman. They follow us into cloisters and philosophy schools. Neither deserts, nor caves, nor hair shirts, nor fasting can shake them off: the fatal arrow stays lodged in our side.

Someone told Socrates that a certain man hadn't been improved at all by his travels. "I believe it," said Socrates, "for he took himself along with him." Why do we go looking for lands warmed by a different sun? What exile from his country ever escaped from himself too?

If a man doesn't first unload himself and his mind of the burden weighing on him, moving around will only make it press harder and sit heavier — the way a ship's cargo is less trouble when it's stowed snug and settled. You do a sick man more harm than good by moving him from place to place; you drive the disease in deeper by jostling it, just as stakes sink deeper and stand firmer the more you work them up and down in the hole where they're meant to stay. So it's not enough to get away from the public; it's not enough to change your ground. A man must get away from the crowd's habits that have taken up residence inside him. He must sequester himself and take possession of himself again. You say you've broken your chains? The dog who finally snaps his chain after a long struggle still drags a good length of it from his neck as he runs. We carry our chains with us. Our liberty isn't absolute; we keep glancing back at what we've left behind, and our imagination is still full of it. Unless the mind is purged, as Lucretius says, what battles and dangers we take on in spite of ourselves — what bitter anxieties, what terrors, what wreckage from pride, lust, anger, luxury, and sloth!

Our disease is in the mind, and the mind cannot escape from itself — the fault, says Horace, lies in a mind that never gets away from itself. So the mind must be called home and shut up inside itself. That is true solitude, and it can be enjoyed even in crowded cities and the courts of kings — though more comfortably apart.

Now, since we're undertaking to live alone and do without company, let's arrange it so that our contentment depends entirely on ourselves. Let's dissolve every tie that binds us to others. Let's win this much from ourselves: to be able to live alone in earnest, and to live at our ease doing it.

Stilpo escaped the burning of his city, where he lost his wife, his children, and everything he owned. Demetrius Poliorcetes, seeing him walk out of so great a ruin with an untroubled face, asked whether he hadn't suffered any loss. "No," he answered — thank God, nothing of his was lost. The philosopher Antisthenes meant the same thing when he said, neatly, that a man should equip himself with goods that float, and can swim out of a shipwreck with their owner. Certainly a wise man never loses anything, as long as he has himself. When the city of Nola was sacked by the barbarians, Paulinus, the bishop there, lost everything he had and was taken prisoner; and he prayed like this: "Lord, keep me from feeling this loss; for Thou knowest they have not yet touched anything that is mine." The riches that made him rich and the goods that made him good were still intact. That is what it means to choose treasures that can't be plundered, and to hide them in a place no one can enter — a place that can be betrayed by no one but ourselves.

A man should have a wife, children, property, and above all health, if he can get them. But we must not fasten our hearts to them so tightly that our happiness depends on them. We must reserve a back shop all our own, entirely free, where we keep our real liberty — our principal retreat and solitude. There our daily conversation should be with ourselves, and so private that no outside business or company gets in. There we should laugh and talk as if we had no wife, no children, no property, no staff, no servants — so that when the day comes that we lose them, it will be nothing new to do without them. We have a soul that can turn in on itself; it can keep itself company. It has the means to attack and to defend, to give and to receive. No need to fear that solitude will leave us slumped in boring emptiness — in solitude, as Tibullus says, be a crowd to yourself.

Virtue is satisfied with herself: no rules, no words, no audience. In our ordinary actions, not one in a thousand actually concerns ourselves. That man you see scrambling up the rubble of that wall, furious, beside himself, with all those gunshots aimed at him; and that other one, covered in scars, pale, faint with hunger, resolved to die rather than open the gates to him — do you think they're there for their own sake? No — most likely for the sake of someone they've never seen, who never gives a thought to their pain and danger, and is wallowing in idleness and pleasure the whole time. And this drooling, bleary-eyed, unwashed fellow you see shuffling out of his study after midnight — do you think he's been digging through books to learn how to be a better, wiser, happier man? No such thing. He'll die at that desk, but he'll teach posterity the meter of Plautus's verses and the correct spelling of a Latin word. Who doesn't willingly trade health, rest, and life itself for reputation and glory — the most useless, frivolous, counterfeit coin in circulation? As if our own death weren't frightening enough, we take on the deaths of our wives, children, and household. As if our own affairs didn't supply enough worry, we take up our neighbors' and friends' affairs too, the better to rack our brains and torment ourselves. Ah, says Terence — can any man let anything become dearer to him than himself?

Solitude seems to me to sit best on those who have already given their most active and flourishing years to the world, after the example of Thales. We have lived enough for others; let us live what little is left for ourselves. Let's bring our thoughts and intentions home, to ourselves and our own ease. Arranging a safe retreat is no small job; it will keep us busy enough without mixing in other projects. Since God gives us time to organize our departure, let's pack up, say our goodbyes early, and pry ourselves loose from those gripping obligations that drag us elsewhere and pull us away from ourselves.

We must untie the knot of our obligations, however strong. We may still love this person or that thing, but we should wed ourselves to nothing but ourselves. That is: let the rest be ours, but not glued so close that it can't be pulled away without flaying us or tearing out a piece of us. The greatest thing in the world is to know how to belong to yourself.

It's time to wean ourselves from society when we can no longer contribute to it. A man who's in no condition to lend should forbid himself to borrow. Our strength is failing; let's call it in and concentrate it on ourselves. Whoever can fold the duties of friendship and company back into himself, let him do it. In this decay of age, which makes a man useless, burdensome, and tiresome to others, let him take care not to become useless, burdensome, and tiresome to himself. Let him indulge and pamper himself, and above all govern himself with such respect for his own reason and conscience that he'd be ashamed to stumble in their presence — for it is rare, as Quintilian says, that a man holds himself in sufficient reverence.

Socrates says that boys should get themselves educated, grown men should practice doing good, and old men should withdraw from all civil and military employment and live at their own discretion, bound to no office. Some temperaments suit these precepts of retirement better than others. People of a soft, slow disposition, with delicate attachments and a will that doesn't readily submit to harness — and I am one, by nature and by conviction — will take to this advice more easily than active, busy souls who embrace everything, sign up for everything, run hot for everything, who offer and give themselves to every occasion. We should use these accidental, external goods as far as they please us, but never make them our main foundation. They aren't one; neither nature nor reason allows it. Why then, against their laws, should we enslave our contentment to a power outside ourselves?

But to leap ahead of fortune's accidents — to strip ourselves in advance of the comforts within our reach, as some have done out of devotion and some philosophers out of reasoning; to wait on yourself hand and foot, to sleep on boards, to put out your own eyes, to throw your wealth in the river, to go hunting for pain — some chasing bliss in the next life through misery in this one, others lying flat on the ground to be safe from falling — all that is virtue run to excess. Let the sturdiest, toughest natures make even their hiding glorious and exemplary. As Horace puts it: when money is short, I praise the safe little life, tough enough on slim rations — but the moment things get richer and easier, I declare that the only wise men, the only ones living well, are those whose money shows in handsome country villas.

A great deal less would do for me. It's enough, while fortune favors me, to prepare for the day she turns; enough, sitting comfortably, to picture trouble coming as far as my imagination will stretch — the way we stage mock combats at jousts and tournaments in the middle of perfect peace. I don't think the philosopher Arcesilaus any less temperate or virtuous for knowing he ate off gold and silver plate when his fortune allowed it; in fact I think better of him than if he had refused what he used with generosity and moderation. I see the true limits of natural necessity. Noticing the poor man begging at my door, often merrier and healthier than I am, I put myself in his place and try to fit my mind to his cut. Running through other examples the same way — imagining death, poverty, contempt, and sickness hard on my heels — I find it easy to resolve not to be terrified of what a lesser man than I accepts with such patience. I can't believe a meaner understanding can do more than a stronger one, or that the effects of reasoning can't reach as high as the effects of habit. And knowing how flimsy these accidental comforts are, I never fail, in the middle of enjoying them, to make it my chief prayer to God that He make me content with myself and with the condition I'm in. I see healthy young men who nevertheless keep a stash of pills in their trunk at home, to take when a cold strikes — which they fear the less because they think the remedy is within reach. That's the way to do it — and further, if you find yourself prone to some more violent disease, stock yourself with the medicines that numb and deaden the part.

The occupation a man picks for this retired life should be neither laborious nor boring; otherwise there's no point in having retired at all. Which occupation depends on each man's taste. Mine doesn't bend at all toward farming, and those who love it should pursue it in moderation — trying, as Horace says, to make circumstances bow to me, not me to circumstances. Farming is otherwise a very servile business, as Sallust calls it — though some branches are more forgivable than the rest, like the care of gardens, which Xenophon attributes to Cyrus. And a middle way can be found between the grubbing, anxious absorption of men who make farming their whole life and study, and the slack, total negligence of others who let everything run wild — like Democritus, whose cattle, as Horace says, ate up his crops and fields while his soaring mind ranged abroad without its body.

But let's hear the advice the younger Pliny gives his friend Caninius Rufus on the subject of solitude: "I advise you, in that full and prosperous retreat of yours, to leave the farm work to your hired hands and devote yourself to the study of letters, so as to extract from them something entirely your own." He means reputation — the same spirit as Cicero, who says he wants to use his retirement from public affairs to win immortal life through his writings. Is your knowing nothing, asks Persius, unless somebody else knows that you know it?

It seems only reasonable, when a man talks of leaving the world, that he should look entirely beyond it. These men do it by halves. True, they set their affairs up for the time when they'll no longer be in the world; but the fruit of their project they still claim to draw from the world after they've left it — a ridiculous contradiction.

The notion of those who seek solitude for devotion's sake, filling their hopes and courage with the certainty of divine promises in the next life, is far more soundly built. They set God before themselves, an object infinite in goodness and power; the soul has room there to satisfy her desires in perfect freedom. Afflictions and sufferings turn to profit, being spent to purchase eternal health and joy; death becomes a thing to wish for, the passage to so perfect a state. The harshness of their rules is quickly softened by habit, and the appetites of the flesh are baffled and beaten down simply by being refused — since nothing keeps them alive but use and exercise. This single end — another, happily immortal life — genuinely deserves that we abandon the comforts and pleasures of this one. And whoever can truly and steadily set his soul ablaze with the fire of that living faith and hope builds himself, in solitude, a life more delicious and luxurious than any other kind of existence.

So neither the goal nor the method of Pliny's advice pleases me; we often jump out of the frying pan into the fire. This business with books is as laborious as any other, and just as much an enemy of health — which ought to be our first consideration. And we shouldn't let ourselves be lulled by the pleasure of it: it's the same pleasure that destroys the frugal man, the miser, the sensualist, and the ambitious man.

The sages warn us often enough to beware the treachery of our appetites, and to tell true, whole pleasures apart from those laced with more pain than they're worth. Most of our pleasures, they say, hug and flatter us only to strangle us, like the thieves the Egyptians called Philistae. If the hangover arrived before the drunkenness, we'd take care not to drink too much; but pleasure, to deceive us, walks in front and hides what's trailing behind her. Books are pleasant — but if poring over them ends up costing us our health and our good humor, the best things we have, let's drop them. I'm one of those who think no fruit they yield can make up for that loss. Like men long worn down by illness who finally surrender to medicine and accept a regimen of living they must never again break, so the man who retires, weary and sick of the common way of life, should design his new one by the rules of reason, and set it up with forethought and reflection. He should have said goodbye to every kind of labor, whatever it promises, and shaken off all the passions that disturb the peace of body and soul — and then choose the road that suits his own temper best: let each man know his own way.

In farming, study, hunting, and every other pursuit, we should go to the outer limit of pleasure, and take care not to push past the point where trouble starts to mix in. We should keep only as much business as is needed to keep us in shape and protect us from the troubles that the other extreme — dull, slack idleness — drags along with it. There are barren, knotty sciences, mostly hammered out for the crowd; leave those to the people still in the world's service. Personally, I care for no books except the pleasant, easy kind that tickle me, or the kind that console me and teach me how to manage my life and my death — strolling silently in the healthy woods, as Horace says, pondering whatever is worthy of a wise and good man.

Wiser men, with strong and vigorous souls, can fashion themselves a rest that is purely spiritual. But I, who have a perfectly ordinary soul, need the help of the body's comforts to hold myself up; and since age has lately robbed me of the pleasures I liked best, I train and sharpen my appetite for the ones that remain and suit this season better. We must hang on with tooth and nail to the use of life's pleasures, which the years wrench out of our hands one after another. Let's pluck the sweets, says Persius — the living is ours; soon enough you'll be ashes, a ghost, a story people tell.

Now as for the goal Pliny and Cicero hold out to us — glory — it's nowhere near my reckoning. Ambition is, of all moods, the most contrary to solitude. Glory and repose cannot lodge in the same house. As far as I can see, these men have only their arms and legs out of the crowd; their soul and intention are stuck in it deeper than ever — does an old man, asks Persius, really gather tidbits for other people's ears? They've stepped back only to get a better running start and charge harder into the crowd. Want to see how short they shoot? Set against them the advice of two philosophers, from two very different schools — one writing to Idomeneus, the other to Lucilius — urging their friends to leave honors and affairs for solitude.

"Until now," they say, "you have lived swimming and floating; come now and die in the harbor. You have given the first part of your life to the light; give what remains to the shade. It's impossible to give up the work unless you also give up the fruit of it; so strip yourself of every concern for name and glory. There's a danger the shine of your past deeds will throw too much light on you and follow you right into your hiding place. Quit, along with the other pleasures, the pleasure that comes from other people's approval. As for your knowledge and talents, don't worry — they won't lose their effect if you yourself are the better for them. Remember the man who was asked why he worked so hard at an art that hardly anyone would ever appreciate. 'A few are enough for me,' he replied; 'one is enough; none at all is enough.' He spoke truly. You and one companion are theater enough for each other — or you for yourself. Let the whole people be as one person to you, and be yourself a whole people to one. It's a shabby ambition to try to wring glory out of one's idleness and seclusion. Do as the hunted animals do, and brush out your tracks at the mouth of your den. Your business is no longer what the world says of you, but what you say to yourself. Withdraw into yourself — but first prepare yourself to receive yourself. It would be madness to trust yourself to your own keeping if you can't govern yourself. There are ways to fail in solitude just as in company. Until you have made yourself into someone you wouldn't dare stumble in front of, and until you feel a certain shame and reverence before yourself — keep honest images ever before the mind, as Cicero says — hold in your imagination Cato, Phocion, and Aristides, in whose presence even fools would hide their faults, and make them auditors of all your intentions. If your intentions go crooked, reverence for those men will set them straight again. They will keep you on the road of being content with yourself, of borrowing nothing from anyone but yourself, of steadying and fixing your soul on definite, bounded thoughts where she can take her pleasure — and, having understood the true goods, which we enjoy in proportion as we understand them, of resting satisfied, with no craving for more life or more name."

That is the counsel of true and natural philosophy — not of a boastful, chattering philosophy like that of the other two.
