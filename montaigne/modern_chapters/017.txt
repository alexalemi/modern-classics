Of Smells

It's been said of certain people — Alexander the Great, for one — that their sweat gave off a sweet smell, thanks to some rare and extraordinary constitution. Plutarch and others went looking for the cause. But the ordinary human body works quite the other way, and the best it can hope for is to smell of nothing at all. Even the sweetness of the purest breath has no greater perfection than to carry no offensive smell whatsoever, like the breath of healthy children. That's what made Plautus say of a woman: she smells sweetest who smells of nothing.

And people who go around in fine exotic perfumes deserve to be suspected of some natural flaw underneath — something they're using all that scent to cover up. To smell good is to stink. As Martial teases a friend: "You laugh at us, Coracinus, for wearing no scent; I'd rather smell of nothing than smell sweet." And elsewhere he says it again: the man who always smells good doesn't smell good.

That said, I'm a great lover of good smells myself, and I detest bad ones — and I catch them at a greater distance, I think, than other men. As Horace puts it, my nose is quicker to sniff out a rank armpit or a festering sore than a keen hound is to find the hidden sow.

Among smells, the simple and natural ones please me most. The ladies should take note, since this is chiefly their department. Even in the depths of barbarism, the Scythian women used to powder and crust their faces and whole bodies after bathing with a certain fragrant drug that grew in their country; when they scrubbed it off to be intimate with their men, they came away perfumed and sleek.

You wouldn't believe how strangely smells of every kind cling to me, how readily my skin drinks them in. Whoever complains that nature gave us no vehicle for carrying smells to the nose has it wrong — smells carry themselves, especially to me. My mustache, which is full, does the job nicely. If I so much as brush it with my gloves or my handkerchief, the smell stays a whole day. It tells on me — it reports where I've been. And the close, luscious, devouring, clinging, melting kisses of youthful passion, back in my wilder days, would leave a sweetness on my lips for hours afterward.

And yet I've always been remarkably untouched by epidemic diseases — the kind caught at a sick man's bedside or bred from bad air — and I've escaped every one of my time, and there have been several, in our cities and in our armies. We read of Socrates that he never once left Athens through all the plagues that ravaged the city, and he alone was never infected.

Doctors could, I believe, get far more use out of odors than they do. I've often noticed that smells change me — they work on my spirits, each according to its nature. Which makes me approve of the old idea that incense and perfume in churches — a practice so ancient and so universal across all nations and religions — was meant to cheer us, to rouse and purify the senses, and so make us fitter for contemplation.

To judge the matter better, I'd love to have tasted the work of those cooks who had the rare art of seasoning exotic scents into the flavor of food. It was noticed especially in the household of the king of Tunis, who in our own day landed at Naples to meet the Emperor Charles. His dishes were stuffed with aromatic drugs at such expense that dressing one peacock and two pheasants in their fashion came to a hundred ducats; and when the carver cut them open, not just the dining hall but every room of his palace and the neighboring streets filled with a sweet vapor that took a long while to fade.

My chief concern in choosing where to stay is always to avoid thick and stinking air. And those beautiful cities, Venice and Paris, lose a good deal of my affection for them — the one by the foul smell of her marshes, the other by the smell of her mud.
