Of the Education of Children
(Part 1 of 4)

To Madame Diane de Foix, Comtesse de Gurson

I have never met a father who would disown his son, however stunted or misshapen the boy turned out. That doesn't mean the father can't see the defects — unless he's completely drunk on paternal affection, he sees them perfectly well. But the boy is still his.

It's the same with me. I see better than anyone that what I write here amounts to the daydreams of a man who only nibbled the outer crust of learning in his youth, and kept nothing but a vague, shapeless picture of it. A little of everything and nothing of the whole — the French way. I know, in general terms, that medicine exists, and law; that mathematics has four branches; and roughly what each of these is aiming at. Maybe I even know, in a general way, what the sciences claim to do for our lives. But to dig deeper than that — to have worn out my brains studying Aristotle, the reigning monarch of modern learning, or to have devoted myself seriously to any single branch of knowledge — I have never done it. There is no art whose basic outlines I could sketch. The lowest schoolboy in the lowest form can call himself more learned than I am; I couldn't even quiz him on his first lesson. If I'm ever forced to try, I have to save face by asking him some broad, clumsy questions about general things — testing his natural intelligence with a lesson as foreign to him as his lessons are to me.

I have never settled down seriously with any book of solid learning except Plutarch and Seneca. And with those, like the Danaides with their leaking jars, I am forever filling up and forever draining out. Some of it drips onto this paper; almost none of it stays in me. History is my real hunting ground when it comes to reading. Poetry too, which I love with a special tenderness — because, as Cleanthes said, just as a voice forced through the narrow tube of a trumpet comes out sharper and stronger, so a thought packed into the harmony of verse springs out harder and hits the mind with a cleaner, more pleasing blow.

As for the natural faculties I have — and these essays are exactly that, an attempt to test them — I feel them bending under the load. My imagination and judgment only grope along in the dark, tripping and stumbling. And when I have gone as far as I can go, I am never satisfied; I can still see more country ahead of me, vast and blurred, wrapped in clouds I cannot get through. I write about whatever comes into my head, using nothing but my own natural equipment. So when I happen — as I often do — to stumble in some good author across the very subjects I've tried to handle (as I did just recently in Plutarch, on the power of imagination), and I see how weak and limp I look next to those better writers, so heavy, so flat — I pity myself, or despise myself, take your pick.

Still, I take comfort in this: my opinions often have the honor and good luck of matching theirs. I'm walking the same road, even if I'm a long way behind, and at least I can say, "Yes — that's exactly it." I also have one ability not everyone is blessed with: I can see the enormous distance between them and me. And despite that, I let my own inventions run their course, low and feeble as they are, without patching or plastering over the flaws the comparison exposes. Frankly, you need a strong back to keep pace with those people.

The reckless scribblers of our age do the opposite. Into their laborious nothings they paste whole passages and pages of ancient authors, hoping the borrowed brilliance will dress up their own work. It does the reverse. The contrast is so glaring that their own writing looks more sallow and deformed beside it — they lose far more than they gain.

The philosophers Chrysippus and Epicurus took the two opposite extremes here. Chrysippus stuffed his books not just with other authors' sayings but with entire works — in one of them, the whole Medea of Euripides — which led Apollodorus to remark that if you removed everything that wasn't his, you'd be left with blank paper. Epicurus, on the other hand, left behind three hundred volumes without a single quotation in any of them.

The other day I had a little adventure of this kind. I was reading a French book, dozing my way through a long stretch of words so dull, so insipid, so empty of wit or common sense that they were French words and nothing more. After this long, tedious slog I suddenly hit a passage that was lofty, rich, soaring up to the clouds. If the slope up to it had been gradual, or the climb gentle, there might have been some excuse. But it was a sheer cliff, so completely cut off from everything around it that the first six words launched me into another world — and from up there I could see how deep and low the valley was that I'd come from. I have never had the heart to go back down into it since. If I decked out one of my own pieces with rich plunder like that, it would only spotlight the dullness of everything else I'd written.

Criticizing in others a fault I commit myself seems no more unreasonable to me than condemning — as I often do — other people's faults when I find them in me. Faults should be called out wherever they live; no sanctuary. I know perfectly well how boldly I keep trying to rise to the level of what I steal, to make my own style march alongside it — even with the rash hope of fooling my reader's eye so they can't tell the difference. But when I manage it, it's as much by careful placement as by any invention or strength of my own. Besides, I don't take on the whole army of these champions, or even fight any one of them hand to hand. I engage them in quick raids and light skirmishes. I don't grapple; I test their strength and never push as far as I pretend to. If I could actually hold my own with them, I'd be doing well — because I only ever attack them where they are strongest.

But to cover yourself head to toe in another man's armor, as I've seen some do, so that not even your fingertips show; to build a whole work out of old inventions patched together here and there with your own rubbish (easy enough for anyone with a bit of schooling, on an ordinary subject), and then to try to hide the theft and pass it off as your own — that is first of all dishonest and small-minded. Having nothing of their own worth a reputation, these people try to buy one with goods they have no title to. And second, it's ridiculous: they settle for the ignorant applause of the crowd, won by a pitiful con, while cheapening themselves in the eyes of intelligent readers — who turn up their noses at all this borrowed plaster, and whose praise is the only praise worth having. For my part, there is nothing I would not rather do than that. I only say so much about others to better explain myself.

None of this is aimed at the writers of centos — the patchwork poems — who announce themselves as exactly that. I have known some very clever ones in my time, particularly one writing under the name Capilupus, not to mention the ancients. These are genuinely intelligent men who show it both in that form and in others — Lipsius, for instance, in the learned and laborious weave of his Politics.

However that may be, and however trifling these foolish efforts of mine are, I never meant to hide them — any more than I'd hide a portrait of myself, bald and graying, in which the painter has given you not some ideal face but my own. These are my personal opinions and fancies. I offer them as what I believe, not as what anyone else ought to believe. My only aim here is to reveal myself — and tomorrow's self may be a different one, if some new lesson happens to change me. I have no authority to be believed, and I don't want any; I'm too aware of how badly educated I am to go instructing anyone else.

Now — someone who had read the previous chapter told me at my house the other day that I should have said more about the education of children.

Well, madam, if I had any competence on this subject, I couldn't put it to better use than as a gift to the little man who will soon make his happy arrival (you are too generous to begin with anything but a boy). Having played such a part in arranging your marriage, I have a certain personal stake in the greatness and prosperity of everything that comes of it. Besides, you have had first claim on my service for so long that I'm bound to wish honor and advantage on everything that concerns you. But truthfully, all I understand about the matter is this: the greatest and hardest problem in human knowledge is the raising of children.

It's like farming. The work before planting, and the planting itself, is sure, plain, and well understood. But once the thing planted comes to life — that's when the real work begins: more skill, more care, far more difficulty in cultivating it and bringing it to perfection. So with people. Getting children is easy; once they're born, then comes the trouble, the worry, the care of training them up properly. At that tender age the signs of their inclinations are so faint, the promises so unreliable, that it's nearly impossible to build any solid judgment on them. Look at Cimon, look at Themistocles, and a thousand others who completely defied the expectations people had of them. Bear cubs and puppies show their natural temperament right away. But people, the moment they grow up, take on habits, sign up for opinions, conform to laws and customs, and easily alter — or at least disguise — their real disposition. And yet nature's bent is hard to force. The result is that, having picked the wrong road, we often spend enormous effort and years of a child's life training him for things he is by his nature completely unsuited for. Even so, in this difficulty, my opinion is clear: aim them at the best and most rewarding studies, and don't fuss too much over those faint childhood omens — to which Plato, in his Republic, gives far too much weight, I think.

Madam, learning is a great ornament and a marvelously useful tool, especially for people raised to your level of fortune. In truth it can't do its proper work in mean and lowly hands. It is far readier to lend its strength to conducting a war, governing a people, or negotiating the friendship of princes and foreign nations than to constructing a syllogism, arguing a lawsuit, or prescribing a dose of pills. So, madam — confident that you won't leave this essential piece out of your children's education, you who have tasted its sweetness yourself and come from a learned line (we still have the writings of the old Counts of Foix, the ancestors of both you and your husband the Count, and your uncle, Monsieur de Candale, brings out new ones every day, extending your family's claim on this quality for ages to come) — I will presume to offer your ladyship one particular notion of my own, contrary to the usual method. It is all I can contribute to your service in this business.

The duties of the tutor you choose for your son — and the whole success of his education hangs on that choice — involve many other important parts that I won't touch, since I have nothing worthwhile to add to the standard advice. And on the point where I do presume to advise, he should follow me only as far as it seems sensible to him.

So: for a boy of good family who pursues learning not for profit (an aim too low to deserve the grace and favor of the Muses, and one that puts a man in service and debt to others), and not so much for show as for his own inner use — to furnish and enrich himself within, wanting to turn out an accomplished gentleman rather than a mere scholar — for such a boy, I would have his people take care to choose him a tutor with a well-made head rather than a well-filled one. Look for both, certainly; but of the two, value character and judgment above raw learning. And let this man do his job by a new method.

Tutors ordinarily never stop thundering in our ears, as if they were pouring knowledge into a funnel, while our job is simply to repeat back what we've been told. I'd have the tutor correct this from the start. According to the capacity of the mind in his hands, he should put it through its paces — letting the boy taste things for himself, choose and discern for himself; sometimes clearing the path for him, sometimes letting him clear it alone. I don't want the tutor doing all the inventing and all the talking; I want him to listen to his pupil talk in turn. Socrates, and Arcesilaus after him, had their students speak first and spoke to them afterward. As Cicero says, the authority of the teacher is very often an obstacle to those who want to learn.

It's good to make the boy trot out in front, like a young horse, so the tutor can judge his gait and figure out how much to slow his own pace to match the pupil's strength. Get that proportion wrong and we ruin everything. Knowing how to hit it, and stay within it, is one of the hardest things I know; it takes a lofty and very well-tempered soul to stoop to a child's pace and guide it. I walk more firmly and surely uphill than down.

Those who follow our usual practice — one lesson, one pace, for a roomful of boys with completely different minds and abilities — are profoundly mistaken. No wonder that out of a whole crowd of pupils, barely two or three get anything worthwhile from their schooling.

Let the master demand an account not just of the bare words of the lesson but of its meaning and substance; and let him judge the boy's progress not by the testimony of his memory but by the testimony of his life. Make him take what he has just learned and turn it into a hundred different shapes, fit it to a hundred different subjects, to see whether he has truly grasped it and made it his own — pacing the instruction by Plato's method of teaching. Spitting food back up exactly as you swallowed it is proof of rawness and indigestion. The stomach hasn't done its work unless it changes the form and condition of what it was given to digest.

Our minds work only on credit, tied and bound to the lead of someone else's fancy, captive to the authority of someone else's lesson. We've been so broken to the harness that we have no free stride of our own. Our vigor and liberty are gone. As Seneca says, they never come out of wardship.

I was privately taken, in Pisa, to meet a worthy man — but such an Aristotelian that his standing thesis was this: the touchstone and measure of all sound thinking and all truth is conformity to Aristotle's teaching; outside of that, everything is emptiness and chimera, since Aristotle saw everything and said everything. That position, interpreted a bit too broadly and unfairly, once landed him in serious and lasting danger from the Inquisition in Rome.

Let the tutor make the boy sift everything he reads, and lodge nothing in his head on bare authority or on trust. Aristotle's principles should be no more sacred to him than those of Epicurus or the Stoics. Set the whole range of opinions in front of him: he will choose if he can — and if he can't, he'll remain in doubt. As Dante says, "doubting pleases me no less than knowing."

If he embraces the opinions of Xenophon and Plato through his own reasoning, they will no longer be theirs; they will be his. Whoever follows another follows nothing. He finds nothing — he isn't even looking for anything. As Seneca says, we are under no king; let each man claim himself.

Let him at least know that he knows. He should absorb the spirit of these thinkers, not memorize their precepts. And let him cheerfully forget where he got it all, so long as he knows how to make it his own. Truth and reason belong to everyone; they're no more the property of the first man who said them than of the last man to repeat them. It is no more "according to Plato" than according to me, since he and I see and understand it the same way. Bees raid the flowers here and there, but afterward they make honey of it, and the honey is all their own — no longer thyme or marjoram. In the same way, the boy will transform and blend his borrowings into a work that is entirely his: namely, his judgment. His education, his labor, his study aim at nothing but forming that.

He owes no one a list of his sources; he owes only what he has made of them. Plunderers and borrowers parade their houses and their purchases — not how they got the money. You never see the fees and kickbacks of a lawyer; you see the alliances he builds, the honors won for his family. No one publishes his income; everyone publishes what he's acquired.

The whole profit of our study is to become better and wiser. It is the understanding, Epicharmus said, that sees and hears; the understanding that turns everything to use, that arranges everything, that acts and rules and reigns. Everything else is blind, deaf, and soulless. And we make the understanding timid and servile by never letting it do anything on its own. Who ever asked his pupil what he thinks of rhetoric or grammar, or of this or that sentence of Cicero? Our masters jam these things into our memories fully feathered, like oracles where the letters and syllables are the very substance of the matter. Knowing by heart is not knowing; it is only holding on to what you've handed your memory for safekeeping. What a man truly knows, he can deploy at will, without checking with the author or fumbling through the pages of his book.

A purely bookish learning is a poor, paltry learning. It may serve as decoration, but it is no foundation to build on — as Plato says when he calls constancy, faith, and sincerity the true philosophy, and the other sciences, aimed at other ends, mere face paint. I'd love to see Paluel or Pompey, those two famous dancers of my day, teach us capers just by performing them while we sat still in our seats — which is exactly how these men propose to train the understanding without ever setting it in motion. Or to see us learn to ride, handle a pike, play the lute, or sing without the bother of practice — just as they want to make us judge well and speak well without ever exercising us in judging or speaking.

In this kind of education, everything that crosses our path is textbook enough: a page's prank, a servant's blunder, a joke at the table — each one a fresh subject.

For the same reason, mixing with people is enormously useful, and so is travel in foreign countries. Not to come home — as most of our young gentlemen do — with a report on the exact circumference of the Pantheon, or the richness of Signora Livia's petticoats, or how much longer and broader Nero's face is on some ruined statue than on some old medal. Come home instead able to describe the temperaments, the manners, the customs, and the laws of the nations you visited — and to have whetted and sharpened your wits by rubbing them against other people's. I would send a boy abroad very young, and first of all — to kill two birds with one stone — to those neighboring nations whose language is furthest from our own, because if the tongue isn't trained to it early, it grows too stiff to bend.
