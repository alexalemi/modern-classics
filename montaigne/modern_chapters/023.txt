All Things Have Their Season

People who compare Cato the Censor with the younger Cato — the one who killed himself — are comparing two beautiful natures that resemble each other closely. The elder built his reputation in several arenas, and he stands out for his military exploits and his useful public service. But the younger's virtue — and it would be blasphemy to compare anyone to him for sheer vigor — was far more pure and unblemished. For who could clear the Censor of envy and ambition, when he dared to attack the honor of Scipio, a man infinitely beyond him, or anyone else of his time, in goodness and every other excellent quality?

One of the stories they tell about the Censor is that in his extreme old age he threw himself into learning Greek with a greedy appetite, as if quenching a long thirst. To me, that does not say much for his honor. It is, properly speaking, what we call falling into second childhood. All things have their season — even good ones. I can say my Lord's Prayer at the wrong moment: they accused Titus Quintius Flaminius of exactly that, because as general of an army he was seen off praying by himself in the middle of a battle. A battle, mind you, that he won.

The wise man, as Juvenal says, sets a limit even on honest things.

Eudemonidas, watching Xenocrates still bent over his school lectures in old age, asked, "When will this man be wise, if he is still learning?" And when people praised King Ptolemy for drilling himself in arms every day, Philopoemen said, "It is nothing to admire in a king of his age to still be practicing these things. By now he ought to be using them." The young should make their preparations, say the sages, and the old should enjoy them. The greatest vice they find in us is that our desires keep growing young again. We are always beginning to live over.

Our studies and our desires ought to feel their age at some point. Yet here we are, one foot in the grave, and our appetites and projects keep springing up fresh every day. As Horace puts it, you contract for marble to be cut when you are at the edge of the funeral, and build houses forgetful of the tomb.

The longest of my plans does not stretch beyond a year. I think of nothing now but finishing. I am ridding myself of all new hopes and undertakings, taking my last leave of every place I depart from, and letting go, every day, of something I own. As Seneca says, henceforth I will neither lose anything nor gain it — I have more left to pay for the journey than I have journey left. And with Virgil's Dido I can say: I have lived, and finished the course Fortune set before me.

It is, in fact, the only comfort I find in my old age: it kills off in me a number of the cares and desires that have disturbed my life — care about how the world is going, care about riches, grandeur, knowledge, health, myself. There are men learning to speak just when they ought to be learning to be silent forever. A man may always study, but he must not always go to school. What a contemptible thing, an old man at his ABCs!

Different things suit different men, as the poet says; all things are not for all ages.

If we must study, let us study something suited to our present condition, so we can answer like the man who, asked what use study was at his decrepit age, said: "That I may leave better, and more at ease." Such a study was the younger Cato's, when he felt his end approaching and found it in Plato's discourse on the eternity of the soul. Not — let us be clear — that he was not long since furnished with everything needed for such a departure. Of assurance, settled will, and instruction he had more than Plato had in all his writings; his knowledge and his courage were, in this respect, above philosophy. He took up that reading not as a service to his death, but as a man whose sleep was never troubled by a matter of such weight: without choosing it or changing anything, he simply continued his studies along with the other accustomed actions of his life. The night he was denied the praetorship, he spent at play. The night he was to die, he spent reading. Losing his life or losing an office — to him it was all one.
