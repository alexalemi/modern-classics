Of Experience
(Part 5 of 5)

As Horace says, "unless the vessel is clean, whatever you pour into it turns sour."

I, who brag about how carefully and particularly I embrace the comforts of life, find them, when I look at them closely, to be little more than wind. But so what? We are all wind, through and through. And the wind itself, wiser than we are, is content to bluster and shift from corner to corner, doing its proper work without wishing for stability and solidity — qualities that are not its own.

The pure pleasures of the imagination, some say, are the greatest, just as its pure pains are — that was the verdict of Critolaus's famous scales. No wonder: imagination makes its pleasures to order, cuts them out of whole cloth. I see notable examples of this every day, maybe even enviable ones. But I am of a mixed and earthbound constitution. I cannot snap at so simple and single a bait. I let myself be carried along, lazily, by the present pleasures available under the general human law — pleasures intellectually sensual and sensually intellectual.

The Cyrenaic philosophers held that bodily pleasures, like bodily pains, are the more powerful — being double, and being more honest. There are some, as Aristotle says, who reject them out of a kind of savage dullness; and I know others who do it out of ambition. Why don't they go further and give up breathing? Why not live on their own resources, and refuse light because it is free and costs them neither invention nor effort? Let Mars or Pallas or Mercury feed them, instead of Venus, Ceres, and Bacchus, and we shall see how they get on. These swaggering moods can fake a kind of contentment — what won't fancy do? But of wisdom there is not a touch in them. Will they be working on squaring the circle while lying with their wives?

I hate being told to keep my mind in the clouds while my body is at the table. I do not want the mind nailed there, or wallowing there either; I want it to take its seat there — to sit, not to lie down. Aristippus defended only the body, as if we had no soul; Zeno took account only of the soul, as if we had no body. Both were wrong. Pythagoras, they say, followed a philosophy that was all contemplation; Socrates, one that was all conduct and action; Plato found the middle way between them. But people only say that for the sake of talking. The true balance is found in Socrates; and Plato is far more Socratic than Pythagorean, and it suits him better.

When I dance, I dance; when I sleep, I sleep. And when I walk alone in a beautiful orchard, if my thoughts drift off for a while to other matters, for another while I bring them back to the walk, to the orchard, to the sweetness of that solitude, and to myself.

Nature, like a mother, has seen to it that the actions she requires of us for our needs should also give us pleasure; she invites us to them not only through reason but through appetite, and it is unjust to break her rules. When I see Caesar and Alexander, in the thick of their greatest business, enjoying human and bodily pleasures so fully, I do not say they were relaxing their souls. I say they were strengthening them — using sheer force of spirit to make all that violent work and laborious thinking submit to ordinary daily life. Wise men, if they had believed the second was their ordinary occupation and the first the extraordinary one.

We are great fools. "He has spent his life in idleness," we say; "I have done nothing today." What — have you not lived? That is not only the most fundamental of your occupations, it is the most illustrious. "If I had been put in charge of great affairs, I would have shown what I could do." Have you known how to think about your life and manage it? Then you have done the greatest work of all. Nature needs no grand stage to show herself; she shows herself equally at every level, behind a curtain as well as in front of one. Have you known how to order your own conduct? Then you have done more than the man who has written books. Have you known how to take your rest? Then you have done more than the man who has taken cities and empires.

The glorious masterpiece of man is to live to the point. Everything else — ruling, hoarding, building — is at best a little appendix, a prop. I love to see a general of an army, at the foot of a breach he is about to assault, give himself up whole and free to his dinner, laughing and talking among his friends. And Brutus, with heaven and earth conspiring against him and against Roman liberty, stealing an hour from his night rounds to read Polybius at his ease. It is the little souls, buried under the weight of business, who cannot cleanly step out of it — who cannot set it down and pick it back up. As Horace says, "Brave hearts, who have borne worse with me — drown your cares in wine today; tomorrow we put out again upon the vast sea."

Whether it is meant in jest or in earnest that "theological wine" and Sorbonne feasts have become a proverb, I find it reasonable that the doctors dine all the more comfortably and cheerfully for having spent the morning profitably and seriously at their lessons. The consciousness of having used the other hours well is the right and savory sauce of the dinner table. That is how the sages lived. Even that inimitable straining after virtue that astonishes us in both Catos — a temper so severe it becomes almost a nuisance — submitted itself gently to the laws of the human condition, and to Venus and to Bacchus. For their own school's precepts require the perfect sage to be as expert and skilled in the use of natural pleasures as in every other duty of life: a discerning heart should come with a discerning palate.

Ease and flexibility, it seems to me, are wonderfully honorable in a strong and generous soul, and become it best. Epaminondas did not think that joining — and joining heartily — in the songs and games and dances of the young men of his city took anything away from the honor of his glorious victories or from the perfect purity of his character. And among all the admirable actions of the elder Scipio, a man worthy to be thought of heavenly descent, nothing gives him more grace than to see him idling like a child, picking up and sorting seashells on the beach, playing run-and-fetch games, amusing himself writing comedies about the commonest doings of ordinary people — and, with his head full of that tremendous campaign against Hannibal and Africa, touring the schools of Sicily and sitting in on philosophy lectures, to the point of arming the blind envy of his enemies back in Rome.

Nor is there anything more remarkable in Socrates than that, old as he was, he found time to take lessons in dancing and in playing instruments, and counted the time well spent. This is the same man who stood in an ecstasy a whole day and night on his feet, before the eyes of the entire Greek army, seized and carried off by some profound thought. The same man who, first among so many brave soldiers, ran to the rescue of Alcibiades when the enemy had him surrounded, covered him with his own body, and pulled him out by main force of arms. The same who, in the Delian battle, lifted up and saved Xenophon after he was thrown from his horse; who, when all Athens was enraged at the shameful spectacle, was the first to step forward to rescue Theramenes as the Thirty Tyrants' guards led him to execution — and gave up that bold attempt only at the urging of Theramenes himself, though just two men in all had followed him. He was known, when a beauty he loved courted him, to hold to a strict abstinence when it mattered. He was known to march to war and to walk on ice barefoot; to wear the same robe winter and summer; to outlast all his companions in bearing hardship; to eat no more at a banquet than at his own plain table. For twenty-seven years he endured, with the same face, hunger, poverty, the unruliness of his children, and the claws of his wife — and at the end, slander, tyranny, prison, chains, and poison. Yet if civility required this man to drink his share, he was also the man in the whole army who came out best from the drinking. And he never refused to play nuts with children, or to ride the hobby-horse with them, and it suited him well; for all actions, says philosophy, equally become a wise man, and equally honor him.

We have material enough, and we should never tire of holding up the image of this great man as the pattern and model of every kind of perfection. There are very few full, clean examples of how to live; and our teaching is wronged every day when we set before ourselves weak and imperfect ones, hardly good for a single use — examples that pull us backward, corrupters rather than correctors. People deceive themselves. It is in fact much easier to travel along the edges, where the extreme serves as boundary, stop, and guide, than by the middle way, wide and open — easier to go by art than by nature. But it is also much less noble, and much less commendable. Greatness of soul does not consist so much in climbing and pressing forward as in knowing how to govern and limit oneself. It counts as great whatever is enough, and it shows its height by preferring moderate things to eminent ones. There is nothing so fine, and so legitimate, as to play the man well and properly; no knowledge so hard to come by as knowing how to live this life well and naturally. And of all our diseases, the most barbarous is to despise our own being.

Whoever wants to set his spirit apart when the body is sick, to keep it from the contagion — let him do it by all means, if he can. But otherwise, let him take the body's side: let him assist it, favor it, not refuse to share in its natural pleasures, but delight in them like a fond spouse — bringing moderation along, if he is the wiser partner, for fear that through carelessness pleasure should get mixed up with pain. Intemperance is the plague of pleasure; and temperance is not its scourge but its seasoning. Eudoxus, who made pleasure the sovereign good, and his companions, who prized it so highly, tasted it in its most charming sweetness by means of temperance — which in them was singular and exemplary.

I order my soul to look on pain and on pleasure with the same steady eye — for, as Cicero says, it is as much a defect for the soul to spill over in joy as to shrink in sorrow — and with the same firmness: but gaily at the one, sternly at the other, and, so far as it can manage, as eager to snuff out the one as to stretch out the other. Seeing good sanely brings with it seeing evil sanely. Pain has something unavoidable in its mild beginnings, and pleasure something avoidable in its excessive endings. Plato couples them together and holds that it is equally the job of fortitude to stand against pain and to stand against the seductive charms of pleasure. They are two fountains: whoever draws from them — city, man, or beast — when he should and as much as he needs, is very fortunate. From the first we should drink as medicine, only of necessity, and sparingly; from the second for thirst, but not to drunkenness. Pain, pleasure, love, and hatred are the first things a child feels; if, when reason comes, those feelings take reason as their guide — that is virtue.

I have a vocabulary all my own. I "pass the time" when the time is bad and disagreeable; when it is good, I do not want to pass it — I taste it again, I hold onto it. One should run across the bad and settle on the good. The common phrases "pastime" and "passing the time" express the habit of those clever people who think they can do nothing better with their life than let it run out and slide away — to pass over it, sidestep it, and, as far as they can, ignore it and shun it, as if it were some tiresome, contemptible thing. But I know life to be something else entirely. I find it valuable and pleasant, even in this last decline of it, where I now hold it; and nature has put it into our hands dressed in such favorable circumstances that we have only ourselves to blame if it weighs on us or escapes us uselessly. As Seneca says, "It is the fool's life that is joyless and anxious, and given over wholly to the future."

Even so, I am composing myself to lose mine without regret — but as a thing that is by its nature losable, not as a thing that is a burden and a torment. Not minding death really suits only those who have enjoyed living. There is a good husbandry in enjoying life. I enjoy it twice as much as others do, for the measure of enjoyment depends on the attention we give it. Especially now, when I see my remaining time is short, I want to deepen its weight. I will stop the swiftness of its flight by the swiftness of my grip, and make up for the speed of its running out by the vigor of my use of it. The shorter my lease on life, the deeper and fuller I must make it.

Others feel the sweetness of contentment and prosperity; I feel it as much as they do, but not as something gliding past. It should be studied, savored, chewed over, so that we can give fitting thanks to Him who grants it. Other people enjoy their pleasures the way they enjoy sleep — without knowing it. So that even sleep should not escape me so stupidly, I used once to have mine interrupted, so that I could catch a glimpse of it. I deliberate with myself over a contentment; I do not skim it, I sound its depths — and I bend my reason, grown cranky and peevish with age, to take it in. Do I find myself in some calm and settled state? Is there some pleasure tickling me? I do not let my senses pilfer it alone; I bring my soul in on it — not to get entangled there but to take delight there, not to lose itself but to find itself there. And I set the soul, for its part, to look at itself in this prosperous moment, to weigh its happiness, appraise it, and amplify it.

It reckons how much it owes to God that its conscience and its inner passions are at peace; that the body is in its natural condition, enjoying in an orderly and adequate way the soft and soothing functions by which He is graciously pleased to balance out the sufferings His justice inflicts on us in its turn. It considers what a benefit it is to be so placed that wherever it casts its eye, the sky around it is calm — no desire, no fear, no doubt troubles the air; no difficulty, past, present, or future, that its imagination cannot pass over without injury. This consideration takes on great luster by comparison with conditions different from mine. So I summon up, in a thousand forms, those whom fortune or their own error tosses and torments — and also those nearer my own case, who accept their good fortune so carelessly, so incuriously. Those are people who truly "pass" their time: they skip over the present, and over what they actually possess, to wait on hope — chasing shadows and vain images that fancy dangles in front of them, like the phantoms said to flit about after death, or the dreams that mock our sleeping senses, which speed up and stretch out their flight the harder they are chased. The fruit and goal of such pursuit is more pursuit — as Alexander said that the purpose of his labor was to labor, thinking nothing done while anything remained to do.

As for me, then: I love life, and cultivate it, just as it has pleased God to grant it to us. I do not go wishing it were free of the need to eat and drink; indeed I would think it at least as pardonable a fault to wish that need were doubled — for, as Seneca says, the wise man is the keenest seeker after natural riches. Nor do I wish we could keep ourselves going by merely placing on the tongue a little of that drug by which Epimenides killed his appetite and stayed alive; nor that we could dully produce children with our fingers or our heels — rather, speaking with reverence, I wish we could also produce them voluptuously with our fingers and heels; nor that the body should be without desire and without its ticklings. These complaints are ungrateful and unfair. I accept with a glad and grateful heart what nature has done for me; I am well pleased with it, and proud of it. We do wrong to that great and all-powerful Giver when we refuse His gift, cancel it, or deface it. Himself all goodness, He has made everything good; as Cicero says, all things that are according to nature are worthy of esteem.

Of philosophy's opinions, I prefer to embrace the most solid ones — that is, the most human, the most our own. My talk, matching my conduct, is low and humble. Philosophy is playing the child, to my mind, when it gets up on its hind legs of logic to preach to us that it is a barbarous match to wed the divine to the earthly, the reasonable to the unreasonable, the strict to the indulgent, the honest to the dishonest; that pleasure is a brutish thing, unworthy of a wise man's taste; that the only enjoyment he gets from his fair young wife is the conscience-pleasure of performing a proper act in its proper order — like pulling on his boots for a useful journey. May philosophy's followers have no more right, nerve, or vigor with their brides than there is in its lesson!

That is not what Socrates says — and he is philosophy's master and ours. He values bodily pleasure as it deserves, but he prefers the pleasure of the mind, as having more force, more constancy, more ease, more variety, more dignity. The mind's pleasure, by his account, by no means walks alone — he is not so fanciful as that — it merely walks first. For him temperance is the moderator of pleasures, not their enemy.

Nature is a gentle guide, but no more gentle than prudent and just. As Cicero says, "we must enter into the nature of things, and see thoroughly what she requires." I hunt for her track everywhere. We have muddled it over with artificial trails, and that is why the sovereign good of the Academics and the Peripatetics — "to live according to nature" — becomes so hard to pin down and explain; and likewise its neighbor, the Stoic good, "to consent to nature." Is it not an error to count any actions less worthy because they are necessary? They will never knock it out of my head that the marriage of pleasure with necessity is a most suitable match — a match, as an ancient says, in which the gods always conspire. Why do we tear apart, by divorce, a structure woven of such close and brotherly correspondence? On the contrary, let us bind it tighter with mutual services. Let the mind rouse and quicken the heaviness of the body, and the body steady and fix the lightness of the mind. As Augustine says, he who praises the soul's nature as the supreme good and condemns the flesh as evil shows that he desires the soul carnally and flees the flesh carnally — for he judges by human vanity, not divine truth.

In this gift God has made us, there is nothing unworthy of our care. We are accountable for it down to a single hair. And it is no token errand for a man to conduct mankind's nature according to its condition: it is express, plain, and the principal commission of all, and the Creator has given it to us seriously and sternly. Authority alone has power over common understandings, and weighs more in a foreign tongue — so let us have one more charge at it here. As Seneca says, "who will deny that it is folly's own trademark to do what must be done sluggishly and grudgingly — to push the body one way and the mind another, and be torn apart between utterly opposite motions?"

To see this for yourself, ask any one of these men, some day, to tell you what notions and speculations he was stuffing into his head when they made him turn his thoughts away from a good meal and grudge the time spent eating. You will find there is nothing so insipid among all the dishes on your table as that fine meditation of his (most of the time we would do better to sleep outright than to stay awake for what we stay awake for), and that all his thoughts and reasonings are not worth your worst plate of stew. Even if they were the raptures of Archimedes himself — what of it? I am not here speaking of, and I do not mix in with our rabble of ordinary men and the vanity of the desires and musings that distract us, those venerable souls raised by ardent devotion and religion to constant and conscientious meditation on divine things: souls that, by the force of a vivid and vehement hope, taste in advance the eternal nourishment which is the final aim and last step of Christian desire, the only constant and incorruptible pleasure — and so disdain to attend to our needy, fluid, ambiguous comforts, and easily hand over to the body the care and use of its sensual, temporal feed. That is a privileged study. Between ourselves, I have always observed that supercelestial opinions and subterranean morals go together with singular harmony.

Aesop, that great man, saw his master pissing as he walked. "What next," he said, "must we shit as we run?" Let us manage our time as well as we can; plenty of it will still lie idle or badly spent. The mind hardly admits it has enough other hours for its own business that it must refuse to keep the body company for the little stretch the body requires for its needs.

These people want to get outside themselves; they would escape from the man. It is madness: instead of transforming themselves into angels, they transform themselves into beasts; instead of raising themselves, they sink. These transcendental humors frighten me, like high and unreachable places. Nothing in the life of Socrates sits harder with me than his ecstasies and his dealings with his daemon; nothing is so human in Plato as the very things for which they call him divine. Of our sciences, the ones that have climbed highest seem to me the most earthbound and low; and I find nothing so humble and so mortal in the life of Alexander as his fantasies about his own immortality. Philotas pricked that bubble nicely in a letter, congratulating him on the oracle of Jupiter Ammon that had enrolled him among the gods: "For your sake I am glad of it; but I pity the men who will have to live with a man, and obey a man, who exceeds the measure of a man and is not content with it." As Horace says, "because you carry yourself below the gods, you rule."

The graceful inscription with which the Athenians honored Pompey's entry into their city says just what I mean: "You are a god in so far as you confess yourself a man."

It is an absolute perfection, and as it were divine, for a man to know how to enjoy his being rightfully. We go looking for other conditions because we do not understand the use of our own, and step outside ourselves because we do not know what it is like within. Yet it is no use climbing onto stilts, for even on stilts we must still walk on our own legs; and on the loftiest throne in the world, we are still sitting only on our own behind.

The fairest lives, to my mind, are those that fall into the common human pattern, with order, but without miracle and without extravagance. Now old age needs to be treated a little more tenderly. Let us commend it to that god who is the protector of health and of wisdom — but of a gay and sociable wisdom. As Horace prays:

Grant me, Apollo, to enjoy what I have,
in good health; leave my mind sound and whole;
let my old age be without dishonor,
and not without music.
