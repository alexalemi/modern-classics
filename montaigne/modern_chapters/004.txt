To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die
(Part 2 of 2)

"Wretch that I am," the dying man cries, "one disastrous day has stripped me of every prize life had to offer." And the builder laments: "My works stand unfinished — the great walls' proud towers left unmade."

We should plan nothing that takes so long to finish — or at least, nothing we burn so hotly to see completed. We are born for action. As the poet says, "when I die, let me be caught in the middle of my work."

I want a man to be doing things — to stretch out the business of living as far as he possibly can. And let death find me planting my cabbages, not caring a whit about death and even less about my unfinished garden. I watched a man die who, at his last gasp, complained of nothing so bitterly as that destiny was cutting the thread of the history he was writing — he had only gotten as far as our fifteenth or sixteenth king. What such men never add is that once you are dead, you will no longer want any of those things.

We need to rid ourselves of these common, harmful moods. This is why men first put burial grounds next to churches and in the busiest parts of town — to accustom ordinary people, women and children included, says Lycurgus, not to flinch at the sight of a corpse, and so that the constant spectacle of bones, graves, and funerals would keep reminding us of our fragile condition. The ancients went further: there was once a custom of livening up banquets with bloodshed — mixing the meal with the grim spectacle of armed men fighting to the death, some of them falling across the very cups and splashing the tables with blood.

The Egyptians, after their feasts, used to have a great image of death carried in before the company, while a man cried out: "Drink and be merry, for when you are dead you will look like this." In the same spirit, I keep death not only in my imagination but continually in my mouth. There is nothing I am so curious about, nothing I love learning more, than how men die — their words, their faces, their bearing — and no passages in history I read more closely. It shows in how I crowd my pages with examples; I have a particular fondness for the subject. If I were in the business of making books, I would compile an annotated register of the various deaths of men. He who would teach men to die would teach them to live. Dicaearchus did write one with a title like that, but he wrote it to another, less useful purpose.

Someone may object that the pain and terror of dying so far outrun anything we can imagine that the best fencer in the world will be out of his game when it comes to the real thrust. Let them say what they like: thinking it through in advance is still, beyond any doubt, an enormous advantage. And is it nothing to get even that far — to the threshold itself — without trembling, without fever? Besides, Nature herself lends us a hand and gives us heart. If the death is sudden and violent, we have no time to fear it. If it is slow, then I notice that as I sink deeper into a sickness, I naturally begin to feel a certain weariness and disdain for life. I find this resolution to die much harder to swallow when I am in good health than when I am burning with fever. The less grip I have on life's comforts, as I begin to lose the use and pleasure of them, the less terrifying death looks. Which gives me hope that the farther I travel from the one and the nearer I come to the other, the more easily I will make the trade. Caesar said that things often look bigger at a distance than up close, and I have found the same: in full health, I dreaded sickness far more than I ever felt it once it arrived. The vigor and cheerfulness I live in now make the opposite condition look so out of proportion to this one that in imagination I inflate every misery to half again its true size, and dread it as far heavier than it turns out to be when it actually lands on me. I hope to find death the same.

Just watch how, through the ordinary changes and declines we suffer daily, nature hides from us the feel of our own decay. What does an old man have left of his youth and his better days? As the line goes, "alas — how small a share of life is left to the old!" When a worn-out old soldier of Caesar's guard came up to him to ask permission to kill himself, Caesar looked at his withered body and broken walk and answered, cheerfully: "So you think you're still alive, do you?"

If a man fell into old age all at once, I do not believe human nature could endure the change. But nature leads us by the hand, down a gentle, almost imperceptible slope, step by step, until she has eased us into that miserable state and made us at home in it. We feel no jolt when our youth dies in us — though that is, in truth, a harder death than the final death of a languishing body, the death of old age. The leap from a weary existence to no existence is not nearly so great as the leap from a sweet, flourishing life to a painful and troublesome one.

A body bent and bowed has less strength to carry a load; so does a soul. We have to raise her up firm and straight against the power of this adversary. As long as she fears death, she can never be at rest. But once she has steadied herself, she can boast of something almost beyond the human condition: that anxiety, anguish, fear — even the smallest disquiet — cannot live in her. No tyrant's glare can shake her settled calm; neither can the storm-wind that rules the wild Adriatic, nor the mighty hand of thundering Jove.

She has become sovereign over her own passions and appetites, mistress of necessity, shame, poverty, and every other injury of fortune. So let those of us who can, seize this advantage. This is the true and sovereign liberty here on earth — the one that arms us to defy violence and injustice and to laugh at prisons and chains. "I will keep you in shackles and chains," says the master, "under a savage jailer." — "A god will set me free the moment I choose." That god, I think, is death. Death is the finish line of all things.

Our religion itself has no surer human foundation than contempt for life. Reason alone invites us to it — for why fear losing a thing which, once lost, cannot be missed? And since so many kinds of death threaten us, isn't it worse to fear them all forever than to suffer one of them once? What does it matter when it comes, since it cannot be avoided? When someone told Socrates, "The thirty tyrants have sentenced you to death," he answered: "And nature, them."

What nonsense, to torment ourselves over the one step that delivers us from all torment! As our birth brought us the birth of all things, our death will bring the death of all things. So it is just as foolish to weep that we will not be alive a hundred years from now as to weep that we were not alive a hundred years ago. Death is the beginning of another life. We wept like this once before; entering this life cost us just as dearly; we stripped off our old veil then, too, when we came in.

Nothing that happens only once can be a grievance. Is it reasonable to spend so long fearing a thing so quickly over? Long life and short life are made all one by death — there is no long or short for things that no longer exist. Aristotle says there are little creatures on the banks of the river Hypanis that live only a single day. The one that dies at eight in the morning dies in its youth; the one that dies at five in the evening dies of decrepit old age. Which of us wouldn't laugh to see that flicker of a lifespan weighed out in happiness and misery? Yet our own — measured against eternity, or even against the duration of mountains, rivers, stars, trees, and some animals — is every bit as laughable.

But nature compels us to it. "Leave this world," she says, "as you entered it. The same passage you made from death to life, without suffering and without fear, make again from life to death. Your death is one piece of the order of the universe; it is a piece of the life of the world. Mortals live by lending life to one another, and like runners in a relay, they hand on the torch of life.

"Shall I unmake this beautiful arrangement of things for you? Death is the condition of your creation; death is a part of you; in running from it, you run from yourselves. This very being you enjoy is divided equally between life and death. The day of your birth was your first step toward the grave. The first hour that gave you life took an hour of it away. Being born, you began dying; the end hangs from the beginning.

"All the time you live, you steal from life; you live at life's own expense. The perpetual work of your life is to build death. You are in death the whole time you are in life — for when you are no longer in life, you are after death. Or, if you like it better this way: you are dead after life, but dying all the while you live — and death handles the dying far more roughly than the dead, more sharply, closer to the quick.

"If you have made your profit of life, you have had your fill of it. Go your way satisfied. Why not leave life the way a well-fed guest leaves a feast? But if you have not known how to use it — if it did you no good — what do you care about losing it? Why would you want to keep it longer? Why ask for more days, only to spend them as badly and lose them all over again?

"Life in itself is neither good nor evil. It is the stage where good and evil play, depending on what you make of it. And if you have lived one day, you have seen everything. One day is equal and alike to all days. There is no other light, no other darkness. This very sun, this moon, these stars, this whole arrangement of things — it is the same your ancestors enjoyed, and the same that will entertain your great-grandchildren. Your fathers saw nothing different; your descendants will see nothing new.

"And at the very worst, the whole cast and plot of my comedy plays itself out in a single year. If you have watched my four seasons turn, you have seen the infancy, the youth, the manhood, and the old age of the world. Then the year has played its part, and it knows no other trick but to begin again. It will always be exactly this. We circle in the same round, forever confined to it; the year turns endlessly in its own footprints. I have no plans to invent new entertainments for you. There is nothing more I can devise to please you — it is the same show, over and over.

"Make room for others, as others made room for you. Equality is the soul of fairness. Who can complain of being included in a destiny that includes everyone? Live as long as you like; you will not shave one moment off the time you must spend dead. It is all for nothing: you will be every bit as long in that condition you fear so much as if you had died in your nurse's arms. Outlast as many centuries as you please — death will still be eternal.

"And yet I will put you in a condition where you have nothing left to complain about. Don't you know that when you are dead there will be no other you, still alive, to stand at your grave and mourn you lying in it? You will not even miss the life you are so worried about now. No one, then, longs for himself or for living; no regret for ourselves touches us at all. Death is less to be feared than nothing — if anything could be less than nothing. It is much less to you, if less is possible, than what you can see to be nothing at all.

"Death does not concern you, dead or alive. Alive, because you are; dead, because you are no more.

"And no one dies before his hour. The time you leave behind you was no more yours than the time that passed before you were born — it concerns you just as little. Look back: that whole eternity of time before you existed was nothing to you. Wherever your life ends, it is complete there. The value of living lies not in the length of days but in the use you make of them. A man may have lived long and yet lived little. Use time while you have it. Whether you have lived enough depends on your will, not on your count of years. Did you imagine you would never arrive at the place you were walking toward without rest? There is no road without an end. And if company eases the way, isn't the whole world traveling the very same road? All things, when your life is over, will follow you.

"Doesn't everything move in the same dance you dance? Is there anything that does not grow old along with you? A thousand men, a thousand animals, a thousand other creatures die in the very instant that you die. No night has ever followed day, no dawn has ever followed night, that did not hear, mingled with the wails of infants, the cries of grief that keep company with death and dark funerals.

"Why shrink back, if there is no way to retreat? You have seen plenty of men who were glad to die, since dying delivered them from great miseries. But have you ever seen one man who was sorry he had died? It is pure foolishness to condemn a thing you have never experienced — not in your own person, nor in anyone else's.

"Why do you complain of me, and of destiny? Do we do you any wrong? Should you govern us, or should we govern you? Your age may not be finished, but your life is. A small man is as much a man as a giant; neither men nor their lives are measured by the yard. Chiron refused immortality when his father Saturn — the god of time and duration himself — told him what its conditions were. Honestly consider how much more unbearable and painful an everlasting life would be to man than the life I have given him. If you did not have death, you would curse me eternally for keeping it from you. I have deliberately mixed a little bitterness into it, to keep you, once you saw how convenient it is, from embracing it too greedily and too rashly. And to settle you in the moderation I ask — neither sick of living nor in a hurry to die — I have tempered both of them between sweetness and pain.

"I taught Thales, the most eminent of your sages, that living and dying are matters of indifference. So when someone asked him, 'Why don't you die, then?' he answered, very wisely: 'Because it makes no difference.'

"Water, earth, air, fire, and the other parts of this structure of mine are no more the instruments of your life than they are the instruments of your death. Why fear your last day? It carries you no closer to your death than any day before it did. The last step does not cause the weariness — it only declares it. Every day travels toward death; the last one arrives."

These are the good lessons of our mother Nature.

I have often wondered why the face of death, whether we meet it in ourselves or in others, looks so much less dreadful to us in war than it does at home in our own houses — otherwise an army would be a parade of doctors and whimpering invalids — and why, since death is everywhere the same, peasants and plain working people meet it with so much more composure than everyone else. I honestly believe it is the terrible faces and trappings we dress it in that frighten us more than the thing itself: a whole new way of living; the cries of mothers, wives, and children; the visits of stunned and grieving friends; a crowd of pale, blubbering servants; a darkened room ringed with burning candles; our bedside besieged by doctors and preachers — in short, nothing but horror and ghostliness all around us. We are shrouded and buried before we are dead. Children are frightened even of their best friends when they see them in a mask, and so are we. The mask must be pulled off things as well as off people. Once it is removed, we will find underneath only the very same death that a servant or a poor chambermaid died the other day, without any fear and without any fuss. Happy the death that leaves us no time to arrange such ceremonies.
