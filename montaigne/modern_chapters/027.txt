Of Coaches
(Part 2 of 2)

Even the netting stretched in front of the spectators to protect them from the beasts turned loose in the arena was woven of gold — "the woven nets glitter with gold," as Calpurnius says.

If anything can excuse such excesses, it is when the novelty and invention produce more wonder than the expense. Even in these vanities we can see how fertile those ages were in minds of a kind quite different from ours. This sort of fertility works like everything else nature produces. It is not that she spent her whole force then and there. We do not advance; we scurry up and down, wheel this way and that, double back the way we came. I am afraid our knowledge is feeble in every direction. We see neither far forward nor far backward. Our understanding takes in little and lives only a little while — short in reach of time and short in reach of matter. As Horace says, many brave men lived before Agamemnon, but all of them lie pressed under the long night, unwept and unknown. And Lucretius asks why the poets sang nothing earlier than the Theban war and the fall of Troy.

And Solon's account of what he learned from the Egyptian priests — the long life of their state, and their way of learning and preserving the histories of other nations — is not, I think, a testimony to be brushed aside here. Cicero puts it this way: if we could see the unlimited stretch of regions and of times on every side, a vastness the mind can wander through so far and wide that it never finds an edge to rest its eye on, we would discover in that infinite immensity an infinite multitude of forms.

Even if everything that has come down to us by report about past ages were true, and known by somebody, it would be less than nothing next to what is unknown. And of this very image of the world that glides away while we live on it, how wretched and limited is the knowledge of even the most curious! Not just of particular events, which fortune often makes weighty and exemplary, but of the condition of great governments and nations — a hundred escape us for every one that reaches us. We make a mighty fuss over the invention of artillery and printing, which other men at the other end of the world, in China, had a thousand years ago. If we could see as much of the world as we do not see, we would perceive, we may well believe, a perpetual multiplication and turnover of forms. Nothing is single and rare in nature's eyes — only in the eyes of our knowledge, which is a wretched foundation for our rules, and gives us a thoroughly false picture of things. Just as we nowadays foolishly conclude that the world is declining into decrepitude from the arguments we draw out of our own weakness and decay — "our age is broken, and the earth worn out," says Lucretius — so he just as foolishly concluded that the world was newborn and young, from the vigor he saw in the minds of his own time, teeming with novelties and new arts: the world is fresh and recent, he says, which is why some arts are still being polished and still growing, and why shipping gains new improvements every day.

Our world has just discovered another one — and who can promise us it is the last of its brothers, since the spirits, the Sibyls, and we ourselves were ignorant of this one until now? — a world as large, as populous, as fruitful as the one we live on, and yet so raw and so childish that we are still teaching it its ABCs. Fifty years ago it knew nothing of letters, weights, measures, clothing, wheat, or vines. It was still naked in its mother's lap, living only on what she gave it. If we are right about our own old age, and that poet was right about the youth of his era, then this other world will only be coming into the light as ours makes its exit. The universe will fall into paralysis — one limb withered, the other in full vigor.

I am very much afraid we have hurried along its decline and ruin by our contagion, and sold it our opinions and our arts at a very steep price. It was an infant world, and yet we have not whipped it into our discipline by any superiority of natural worth and strength, nor won it by our justice and goodness, nor subdued it by our greatness of soul. Most of their answers, and most of the dealings we have had with them, show that they were not one inch behind us in clarity and natural good sense. The astonishing magnificence of the cities of Cusco and Mexico; the king's garden, among many other things, where every tree, fruit, and plant stood excellently formed in gold, in the same order and size they would have in a real garden; his cabinet, stocked with golden likenesses of every animal bred in his lands and in his seas; and the beauty of their work in jewels, feathers, cotton, and painting — all of it proves they were no whit beneath us in industry. But as for devotion, obedience to law, goodness, liberality, loyalty, and plain dealing, it served us well that we had less of these than they did. That advantage is what lost them, sold them, and betrayed them.

As for boldness and courage — for firmness, constancy, and resolution against pain, hunger, and death — I would not hesitate to set the examples I find among them against the most famous examples of the ancient world recorded on our side of it. As for the men who subdued them: take away the tricks and swindles they used to gull them. Take away the perfectly natural astonishment of those nations at the sudden, unannounced arrival of bearded men — different in language, religion, shape, and face, from a part of the world so remote they had never heard it was inhabited at all — mounted on great unknown monsters, against people who had never seen a horse, never seen any beast trained to carry a man or a load of any kind. Men shelled in hard, shining skin, with sharp, glittering weapons in their hands, against people so dazzled by the gleam of a mirror or a knife that they would trade great treasures of gold and pearl for them, and who had neither the knowledge nor the material to pierce our steel even at their leisure. Add the lightning and thunder of our cannons and muskets — enough to rattle Caesar himself, caught off guard with as little experience of them — turned on people who were naked except for some quilted cotton where the art had reached, with no weapons beyond bows, stones, sticks, and wooden shields. People taken by surprise under cover of friendship and good faith, drawn in by their own curiosity to see strange and unknown things. Take away that disparity, I say, from the conquerors, and you take away the whole occasion of so many victories.

When I consider that unconquerable ardor with which so many thousands of men, women, and children threw themselves, over and over, into certain danger for the defense of their gods and their liberty; that noble stubbornness in bearing every extremity and hardship, and death itself, rather than submit to the dominion of the men who had so shamefully cheated them — some of them, once captured, choosing to starve themselves to death rather than accept food from the hands of enemies who had won so basely — I am convinced that anyone who had attacked them on equal terms of weapons, experience, and numbers would have had as hard a war on his hands, and maybe harder, than any we have ever seen.

Why didn't so noble a conquest fall to Alexander, or to the ancient Greeks and Romans? Why didn't so vast a revolution, the transformation of so many empires and nations, fall into hands that would have gently polished and cleared away whatever was rough and wild in them, and cherished and encouraged the good seeds nature had planted there — not only blending the arts of our world into their farming and the adornment of their cities, where these were needed, but blending the Greek and Roman virtues with the virtues native to the country? What a repair of their condition that would have been, and what a benefit to the whole world, if our first examples and conduct over there had drawn those peoples into the admiration and imitation of virtue, and built between them and us a brotherly fellowship and understanding! How easy it would have been to make something of souls so innocent, so hungry to learn, and for the most part starting with such fine natural inclinations!

Instead we did the opposite. We used their ignorance and inexperience to bend them all the more easily toward treachery, luxury, greed, and every sort of inhumanity and cruelty, by the model and pattern of our own manners. Whoever drove up the price of trade goods to such a rate? So many cities razed, so many nations exterminated, so many millions of people put to the sword, and the richest and most beautiful part of the world turned upside down — for the traffic of pearls and pepper! Mechanic victories! Never did ambition, never did public hatreds, drive men against one another into such miserable hostilities and such miserable calamities.

Certain Spaniards, coasting along in search of their mines, landed in a fruitful, pleasant, well-peopled country and made the inhabitants their usual declarations: that they were peaceable men, come from a very distant country, sent on behalf of the King of Castile, the greatest prince of the habitable world, to whom the Pope, God's representative on earth, had given the principality of all the Indies; that if the inhabitants would pay him tribute, they would be very gently and courteously treated. Meanwhile they demanded food for themselves, and gold — for some medicine they pretended to need — and preached the belief in one God and the truth of our religion, which they advised them to embrace, throwing in a few threats for good measure.

This was the answer they got. As for being peaceable, they didn't look it, if they were. As for their king, since he was reduced to begging, he must be poor and needy; and the man who had made him this gift must love quarrels, to give away what wasn't his and bring it into dispute against its ancient owners. As for food, they would supply it. Gold they had little of, since it was a thing they held in low esteem, useless for the actual business of living, which they cared only to pass happily and pleasantly; but whatever they could find, except what was used in the service of their gods, the strangers might freely take. As for one single God, the idea pleased them well — but they would not change their religion, having lived in it so long and so happily, and being in the habit of taking advice only from friends and people they knew. As for the threats, it showed poor judgment to threaten men whose nature and strength you knew nothing about. So the strangers had better hurry up and clear off their coast, since they were not accustomed to take the courtesies and professions of armed foreigners in good part — otherwise they would be dealt with like these others. And they pointed to the heads of executed men around the walls of their city.

There's a fair sample of the babbling of these children. And note this: neither in that place nor in several others where the Spaniards did not find the merchandise they were after did they make any stay or any attempt, whatever other attractions the country offered — witness my essay Of Cannibals.

Of the two most powerful monarchs of that world — and perhaps of this one, kings over so many kings — the last two they deposed: the king of Peru was taken in battle and put to a ransom so enormous it passes belief. He paid it faithfully, and showed in his conversation every sign of a frank, generous, steady spirit and a clear, settled understanding. The conquerors, having already squeezed out of him one million, three hundred and twenty-five thousand, five hundred weight of gold, plus silver and other goods worth no less — so much that their horses went shod in solid gold — still wanted to find out, at whatever cost in disloyalty and injustice, what the rest of this king's treasure might amount to, and to get their hands on that too. So they trumped up a false accusation, with false witnesses, charging that he was plotting to raise his provinces in rebellion and free himself. And by the virtuous verdict of the very men who had conspired his ruin by this treachery, he was sentenced to be publicly hanged and strangled — having first been made to buy his way out of being burned alive by accepting the baptism they gave him at the moment of execution. A horrid, unheard-of barbarity, which he nevertheless endured without flinching in word or look, with a bearing truly grave and royal. And afterwards, to soothe a people stunned and shaken by so strange a thing, the conquerors counterfeited great grief at his death and staged a sumptuous funeral.

The other, the king of Mexico, had long defended his besieged city, and showed in that siege everything that endurance and perseverance can do, if ever a prince and people did. When his misfortune delivered him alive into his enemies' hands, on terms that he would be treated like a king, he did nothing in his captivity unworthy of the title. After their victory, the conquerors did not find as much gold as they had promised themselves; so when they had searched and ransacked everything, they set about extracting information by the cruelest tortures they could devise on the prisoners they held. Gaining nothing — the prisoners' courage proving stronger than the torments — they arrived at last at such a pitch of fury that, against their pledged word and against the law of nations, they condemned the king himself, and one of the chief noblemen of his court, to the rack, each in the other's sight. The lord, overcome with pain, ringed around with burning coals, turned his dying eyes pitifully toward his master, as if to beg his pardon for being able to bear no more. The king fixed him with a fierce, stern look, rebuking his cowardice and faintness of heart, and said only this, in a harsh, steady voice: "And what do you think I am suffering? Am I in a bath? Am I more comfortable than you?" The lord, at that, sank under his torment and died on the spot. The king, half roasted, was carried away — not out of pity (what pity ever touched souls so barbarous that, on a doubtful rumor of some golden vessel to be looted, they would broil a man, and not just a man but a king so great in fortune and in merit, before their eyes?), but because his constancy made their cruelty more and more shameful. They hanged him later, for having nobly attempted to free himself by arms from so long a captivity and subjection; and he died with a courage worthy of so great-souled a prince.

Another time they burned alive, in one fire, four hundred and sixty living men — the four hundred common people, the sixty among the chief lords of a province — simply prisoners of war. We have these accounts from the conquerors themselves: they not only admit it, they boast of it and publish it. Could it be as a testimony to their justice? To their zeal for religion? Surely these ways run too contrary to so holy an end. If their aim had been to spread our faith, they would have remembered that faith grows not by the possession of territory but by the winning of souls, and they would have been more than content with the killing that war makes necessary, without adding a massacre as indiscriminate as if it were of wild beasts, as universal as fire and sword could make it — sparing, by deliberate choice, only as many as they meant to keep for miserable slavery in the work and service of their mines. So it was that many of the captains were put to death on the very ground of their conquests, by order of the kings of Castile, justly outraged by the horror of their conduct; and almost all of them died hated and despised. God, in fitting justice, allowed all that great plunder to be swallowed up by the sea in transport, or in the civil wars in which they devoured one another; and most of the men themselves were buried in foreign soil with no fruit of their victory.

As for the revenue from those countries — the fact that even in the hands of so thrifty and careful a prince as the present King of Spain, it falls so far short of what his predecessors were led to expect, and of that first abundance of riches found at the original landing in those newly discovered lands (for though a great deal is brought home, we can see it is nothing next to what should have been expected) — the explanation is that the use of money was wholly unknown there, so that all their gold sat gathered in one place, serving no purpose but ornament and display, a treasure handed down from father to son by many powerful kings who kept draining their mines to build that vast heap of vessels and statues for the decoration of their palaces and temples. Our gold, by contrast, is all in motion and in trade. We cut it into a thousand little pieces, cast it into a thousand forms, scatter and disperse it a thousand ways. Imagine our kings hoarding up all the gold they could lay hands on for several centuries and letting it lie idle.

The people of the kingdom of Mexico were somewhat more civilized and more advanced in the arts than the other nations around them. So they judged, as we do, that the world was nearing its end, and took the desolation we brought among them as a sure sign of it. They believed the world's existence was divided into five ages, the lives of five successive suns, of which four had already run their course; and the sun that gave them light was the fifth. The first perished, with every other creature, in a universal flood. The second perished when the sky fell upon the earth and smothered every living thing — that was the age they assigned to the giants, and they showed the Spaniards bones by whose proportions men would have stood twenty feet tall. The third perished by fire, which burned and consumed everything. The fourth by a churning of air and wind that flattened whole mountains; in that one men did not die but were turned into baboons. (What impressions will the weakness of human belief not accept!) After the death of this fourth sun, the world spent twenty-five years in perpetual darkness. In the fifteenth of those years a man and a woman were created who restored the human race. Ten years later, on a certain day, the sun appeared, newly created; and they count their years from that day. On the third day after its creation the old gods died, and new ones have been born since, day by day. How they think this last sun will perish, my author could not learn. But their count for that fourth change matches the great conjunction of stars which, eight hundred-odd years ago, by the astrologers' reckoning, produced great upheavals and novelties in the world.

As for pomp and magnificence — which is how I got into this discussion in the first place — neither Greece nor Rome nor Egypt can compare any of its works, for usefulness, difficulty, or grandeur, with the highway to be seen in Peru, built by the kings of that country from the city of Quito to the city of Cusco: three hundred leagues, straight, even, twenty-five paces wide, paved, and lined on both sides with fine high walls, and along the inside of the walls two streams that never fail, bordered with the beautiful plants they call moly. Where they met rocks and mountains, they cut through and leveled them, and filled in pits and valleys with lime and stone. At the end of each day's journey stand beautiful palaces stocked with provisions, clothing, and weapons, for travelers and for armies that have to pass that way. In rating this work I have counted the difficulty, which weighs especially heavily in that place. They built with no stones smaller than ten feet square. They had no means of carriage but hauling their loads by the strength of their own arms. They did not even know the art of scaffolding, and knew no other trick for standing to their work than to pile earth against the building as it rose, and take it away again when they were done.

Let us come back to our coaches. In place of coaches, and of every other kind of carriage, they had themselves carried on men's shoulders. That last king of Peru, on the day he was taken, was carried like this — seated on a chair of gold, borne on staves of gold, in the middle of his army. As many of his bearers as were killed to bring him down (for the Spaniards wanted him alive), just as many others — and they competed for it — stepped into the places of the dead, so that no matter what slaughter was made of those people, he could never be toppled, until a horseman seized hold of him and wrestled him to the ground.
