Of Repentance

Other writers set out to shape mankind. I only describe a man — and a particular one at that, badly enough made, whom I would certainly build quite differently if I had it to do over. But it's done; there's no recalling it.

Now, though the lines of my portrait keep shifting and changing, the likeness holds. The world is one perpetual see-saw. Everything in it moves without rest — the earth, the rocks of the Caucasus, the pyramids of Egypt — both with the general motion and with a motion of their own. Even steadiness is nothing but a slower, more languid swaying. I cannot pin my subject down. He goes staggering and reeling along with a natural drunkenness. I take him as he is at the moment I turn my attention to him. I do not portray being; I portray passing. Not the passage from one age of life to another — or, as people say, from one seven-year stretch to the next — but from day to day, from minute to minute. My history has to be fitted to the hour. I may change presently, not only by accident but by intention. This book is a register of varied and changing events, of ideas that are unsettled and, when it happens so, contradictory — whether because I am then a different self, or because I catch my subjects under different circumstances and from different angles. So yes, I may well contradict myself now and then; but the truth, as Demades said, I never contradict. If my soul could ever get a solid footing, I would stop making attempts and start making decisions. But it is forever in its apprenticeship, forever on trial.

The life I am setting out is an ordinary one, without luster. No matter. You can tie the whole of moral philosophy to a common private life just as well as to one of richer stuff: every man bears the whole form of the human condition.

Authors present themselves to the public by some special, external badge. I am the first ever to do it by my whole being — as Michel de Montaigne, not as a grammarian or a poet or a lawyer. If the world complains that I talk too much about myself, I complain that the world does not so much as think about itself.

But is it reasonable that someone so private in his way of living should presume to make himself publicly known? Is it reasonable to bring before the world — where polish and craftsmanship carry so much credit and authority — the raw, simple products of nature, and a fairly weak nature at that? Isn't writing books without learning and without art like building a wall without stone or brick? The fancies of music are guided by art; mine, by chance.

Still, I have this much going for me, by the rules: no man ever handled a subject he understood better than I understand the one I have taken up — in this field I am the most learned man alive. And second: no man ever went deeper into his material, sifted its parts and connections more finely, or arrived more exactly at the goal he set himself. To finish the job, all I need to bring is fidelity — and it is here, the purest and most sincere fidelity to be found anywhere. I tell the truth, not as much as I would like to, but as much as I dare; and I dare a little more as I grow older, since custom seems to allow old age more freedom to chatter, and more indiscretion in talking about oneself.

What I often see elsewhere cannot happen here — the craftsman and his work contradicting each other. "Can a man whose conversation is so sensible have written such a foolish book?" Or, "How do such learned writings come from a man whose talk is so feeble?" When a man's conversation is ordinary and his writing remarkable, it means his capacity is borrowed, not his own. A learned man is not learned in everything; but a whole man is whole everywhere — even in his ignorance. Here, my book and I walk hand in hand, keeping one pace. In other cases you can praise or blame the work apart from the workman; not here. Whoever touches the one touches the other. Whoever judges the book without knowing the man wrongs himself more than me; whoever does know him gives me all the satisfaction I want. I will be lucky beyond what I deserve if I win just this much public agreement: that intelligent people see I was capable of profiting from learning, if I had had any — and that I deserved a better memory.

Allow me here to repeat what I have said often: that I rarely repent, and that my conscience is content with itself — not the conscience of an angel, not the conscience of a horse, but the conscience of a man. And I always add this clause — not as a formality but as real and honest submission — that I speak as one inquiring and doubting, and refer myself, purely and simply, to the common and accepted beliefs for the final answer. I do not teach; I relate.

There is no vice that is truly a vice which doesn't offend, and which a sound judgment doesn't condemn. Its ugliness and wrongness are so obvious that perhaps the people are right who say vice is mainly bred from stupidity and ignorance — it is that hard to imagine a man knowing it without hating it. Malice sucks up most of its own venom and poisons itself with it. Vice leaves repentance in the soul the way an ulcer sits in the flesh, forever scratching and tearing at itself. Reason wipes out other griefs and sorrows, but it breeds the grief of repentance — which is all the harder to bear because it springs from within, just as the chills and fevers that rise inside us bite sharper than the ones that strike only the skin. I count as vices (each in its own measure) not only the ones that reason and nature condemn, but also the ones that human opinion has made vices — even false and mistaken opinion — once law and custom have given it authority.

Likewise there is no virtue that doesn't gladden a well-born nature. There is a kind of — I don't know what — congratulation in doing well, an inward satisfaction, and a generous boldness that keeps company with a good conscience. A boldly vicious soul may perhaps armor itself with confidence, but it cannot supply itself with this contentment. It is no small satisfaction to feel yourself preserved from the contagion of so rotten an age, and to say to yourself: "Anyone who could see straight into my soul would not find me guilty of anyone's affliction or ruin, or of revenge or envy, or of any public offense against the laws, or of stirring up trouble, or of breaking my word. And though the license of these times permits and even teaches everyone to do it, I have not laid hands on any Frenchman's goods or money; I have lived on my own purse, in war as in peace; and I have never used a man's labor without paying him his wage." These testimonies of conscience are a pleasure; and this natural rejoicing is a great benefit to us — the only payment that can never fail us.

To rest the reward of virtuous deeds on other people's approval is to build on far too uncertain a foundation — especially in an age as corrupt and ignorant as this one, when the good opinion of the crowd is an insult. Whom would you trust to show you what deserves praise? God keep me from being a good man by the descriptions I see people giving every day of themselves in their own honor. As Seneca says, what used to be vices are now customs.

Some friends of mine have at times taken it on themselves to school me and scold me with great frankness — sometimes of their own accord, sometimes at my invitation, as a service which, to a well-made soul, surpasses all the other offices of friendship, not just in usefulness but in kindness. I have always welcomed it with the wide-open arms of courtesy and gratitude. But to speak honestly between us, I have often found so much false measure in their blame and in their praise alike that I would hardly have gone wrong to do wrong, rather than do right by their notions.

Those of us who live private lives, on display to no one but ourselves, need above all to have a standard set up within, by which to test our actions — and, by that standard, sometimes to pat ourselves on the back and sometimes to correct ourselves. I have my own laws and my own court to judge myself, and I appeal to them more than anywhere else. I do restrain my actions out of regard for others; but I extend them only by my own rule. You alone know whether you are cowardly and cruel, or loyal and devout. Others never see you; they only guess at you by uncertain conjectures. They see your art, not your nature. So do not cling to their verdict; cling to your own. As Cicero says, it is your own judgment you must use on yourself; the weight of your own conscience in the discovery of your virtues and vices is immense — take that away, and everything collapses.

But the saying that repentance follows hard on the heels of sin doesn't seem to apply to sin in its full dress, the kind that lodges in us as in its own house. We can disown and retract the vices that take us by surprise, the ones passion sweeps us into. But the vices that long habit has rooted in a strong and deliberate will are not open to being contradicted. Repentance is nothing but a recanting of our will, a turning against our own fancies — and our fancies drive us whichever way they please. Look at this man, whom repentance makes disown his former virtue and self-control; as Horace has it, why didn't I have the mind I have today when I was a boy — or why, now that I have it, can't I have my smooth cheeks back?

It is a rare life that keeps its order even in private. Anyone can play his part in the puppet show and act the honest man on the boards. The real point is to be ruled inside, in your own chest, where everything is permitted to you and everything is hidden. The next step down is to be orderly at home — in your own house, in your own chimney corner, in your everyday doings, for which you answer to no one, and where there is no rehearsal and no act. That is why Bias, describing the excellent state of a household, said: the master must be the same man within it, of his own free virtue and temper, that he is outside it for fear of the law and what people will say. And it was a fine answer Julius Drusus gave the builders who offered, for three thousand crowns, to remodel his house so that his neighbors could no longer see into it as they did: "I will give you six thousand," he said, "to make it so everyone can see into every room." It is recorded to the honor of Agesilaus that on his journeys he always took lodging in temples, so that the people — and the gods themselves — could look in on his most private doings. Many a man has been a miracle to the world in whom his wife and his valet never saw anything even remarkable. Few men have been admired by their own households. No one was ever a prophet, not merely in his own house but in his own country — so says the experience of history. It is the same with trifles; and in the following small example you can see the image of the great ones. In my home country of Gascony they think it's comic to see me in print. The farther from my own doorstep my readers are, the higher my stock. In Guienne I pay the printers; elsewhere they pay me. That is the hope men bank on who hide themselves while alive and present, to get themselves a name when they are dead and gone. I would rather have less of a name and take none but what is owed me while I'm here to collect it. When I leave the world, I waive the rest.

Watch this official, whom the people escort in pomp, with wonder and applause, right to his door: he sheds the pageant with his robe, and falls as low as he was hoisted high. Inside himself, all is tumult and squalor. And even if there were order in there, it would take a quick and finely chosen judgment to detect it in those low, private motions. Add to that the fact that order is a dull and somber virtue. Storming a breach, conducting an embassy, governing a nation — these are glittering deeds. Scolding, laughing, selling, paying, loving, hating; dealing gently and justly with your own household and with yourself; not slacking off, not giving yourself the lie — that is rarer, harder, and less noticed. So whatever people say, retired lives carry duties as stiff and strenuous as other lives do, or stiffer. Private men, Aristotle says, serve virtue at a harder and higher rate than men in office, because we gear ourselves up for the grand occasions more out of glory than out of conscience. The shortest road to glory would be to do for conscience what we now do for glory. Alexander's virtue, on his enormous stage, looks to me a good deal less vigorous than the virtue of Socrates in his lowly, obscure routine. I can easily picture Socrates in Alexander's place; Alexander in Socrates' place, I cannot. Ask the one what he can do, and he answers, "Subdue the world." Ask the other, and he answers, "Live a human life in keeping with its natural condition" — a far more general, weighty, and legitimate science.

The worth of a soul lies not in soaring high but in walking in order. Its greatness is exercised not in greatness but in ordinary things. Just as those who judge us from inside set little store by the shine of our public actions — seeing that these are only threads and jets of clear water spurting from a thick and muddy bottom — so those who judge us by the brave outward show conclude that the inside matches, and cannot believe that faculties as common as their own could live in the same skin as these other faculties that astonish them, so far out of their range. That is why we give demons such monstrous shapes. Who doesn't picture Tamerlane with huge eyebrows, flaring nostrils, a terrible face, and a giant's frame — the size of the image his name has stamped on our imagination? If someone had brought me, back in the day, to meet Erasmus, I could hardly have believed that anything he said to his servant or his landlady was not all adages and maxims. We find it much easier to imagine a craftsman on the toilet, or on his wife, than a great chief justice — so venerable in his bearing and his learning. It seems to us that men like that never stoop from their lofty benches so far as to actually live. And just as vicious souls are sometimes spurred to do good by some outside push, so virtuous souls are spurred to do wrong. They must be judged, then, by their settled state, when they are at home with themselves — if they ever are — or at least when they are nearest to rest and to their native position.

Natural inclinations get help and reinforcement from education, but they are hardly ever changed or overpowered by it. A thousand natures in my own day have slipped off toward virtue or toward vice straight through schooling that pushed the opposite way. As Lucan says, wild beasts caged and grown unused to the woods turn tame, put off their fierce looks, and submit to the rule of man; but let one small taste of blood reach their mouths, and their rage and fury come back — their throats swell at the remembered savor, and their anger barely spares the trembling keeper. You do not root out these original qualities; you cover them up, you hide them. Latin is like a native tongue to me; I understand it better than French. But I haven't spoken it, and have hardly written it, for forty years. Yet in the sudden, extreme emotions I have fallen into two or three times in my life — once when I saw my father, in perfect health, collapse on top of me in a faint — the first words that burst up from the bottom of me have always been Latin: nature surging out and forcing her own expression, against forty years of disuse. And the same thing is told of plenty of others.

Those who in my time have tried to correct the world's morals with new opinions reform the surface vices; the essential ones they leave where they were — when they don't actually swell them. And swelling is the danger: we put off all other good conduct on the strength of these external, cut-rate, showy reforms, and through them we pay off cheaply for the natural vices, the ones bred in the bone, the ones inside. Look a little at your own experience. There is no man who, if he listens to himself, does not discover in himself a form all his own, a ruling form, which fights against his education and against the storm of passions that oppose it. For my part, I rarely feel myself jolted by shocks; I am nearly always found in my place, like a heavy, lumbering body. If I am not at home, I am always close by. My excesses do not carry me very far. There is nothing extreme or strange about the case. And still I have my sound and vigorous moments of turning back.

The true condemnation, the one that hits the common practice of mankind, is this: that their very retirement is full of filth and corruption; that their idea of reform is smudged; that their repentance is nearly as sick and lame as their sin. Some are so glued to vice by natural bent or long habit that they no longer see its ugliness. Others — and I am of this tribe — do feel the weight of vice, but they balance it against the pleasure or some other consideration, and they put up with it and rent themselves out to it at a certain price — viciously, though, and basely. Still, you might imagine a disproportion so extreme that the pleasure could justly excuse the sin, as we say usefulness sometimes does — not only when the pleasure is incidental, outside the sin itself, as in theft, but when it lies in the very exercise of the sin, as with women, where the urge is violent and, they say, sometimes beyond overcoming.

The other day, at Armagnac, on the estate of a kinsman of mine, I met a peasant whom everyone calls the Thief. He gave this account of his life: born a beggar, and concluding that earning his bread by the sweat of his hands would never get him clear of want, he decided to turn thief, and being a strong young man, he plied the trade through his whole youth in perfect safety — for he harvested and gleaned in other men's fields, but far from home, and in such quantities that nobody could imagine one man carrying off so much on his shoulders in a night. And he took care, besides, to spread the damage around evenly, so the loss fell lighter on each individual. He is now old, and rich for a man of his station — thanks to his trade, which he admits to anyone openly. To square his accounts with God, he says he is making daily restitution, through good turns, to the heirs of the people he robbed; and if he doesn't finish (for he can't do it all at once), he will leave the duty to his own heirs, in proportion to the wrong that only he knows he did to each. By this account, true or false, the man regards theft as a dishonest act and hates it — but he hates it less than poverty. He repents of it in itself, simply enough; but insofar as it was counterbalanced and compensated this way, he does not repent. This is not the habit that fuses us into vice and bends our very understanding to its shape; nor is it that whirlwind of passion that swamps and blinds the soul in gusts and hurls us for the moment, judgment and all, into the power of sin.

What I do, I customarily do completely, in one piece and one stride. I rarely have an impulse that hides itself and sneaks past my reason; nearly every move I make is made with the consent of all my faculties, without split or inward mutiny. My judgment gets all the blame or all the praise for it — and the blame it gets once, it keeps forever, for almost since infancy it has been one and the same judgment: the same bent, the same track, the same force. And in the matter of general convictions, I settled in childhood into the spot where I meant to stand.

Some sins are violent, quick, sudden; set those aside. But these other sins, repeated so many times, deliberated, planned — sins of temperament, or sins of profession and trade — I cannot conceive how they could sit so long in one and the same resolve unless the reason and conscience of the man who keeps them is constant in willing them. And the repentance he claims comes over him at some appointed instant — that I find very hard to imagine or believe. I do not follow the Pythagorean idea that men take on a new soul when they approach the images of the gods to receive their oracles — unless the sect meant that the soul must indeed be a foreign one, new, loaned for the occasion, since our own shows so little sign of the purity and cleanliness the office calls for.

These men do exactly the opposite of the Stoic teaching, which tells us, yes, to correct the imperfections and vices we find in ourselves, but never to let them disturb the peace of our souls. These men would have us believe they carry great grief and remorse inside; but of amendment, of correction, of stopping, they show us nothing. Yet there is no cure if the disease is not thrown off. If repentance sat in one pan of the scales, it would outweigh the sin. I know no quality so easy to counterfeit as piety, when a man's life and morals are not made to match it: its essence is hidden and secret; its appearances are easy and showy.

For my own part, I may wish in a general way to be different than I am; I may condemn my whole form and be displeased with it, and beg God to remake me entirely and to pardon my natural weakness. But this I ought not to call repentance, it seems to me — no more than my displeasure at not being an angel or Cato. My actions are in order; they match what I am and match my condition. I can do no better. And repentance does not properly reach the things that are not in our power — regret reaches those. I can imagine numberless natures loftier and better ordered than mine; for all that, I do not improve my own faculties, any more than my arm or my mind grows stronger because I conceive of someone else's being stronger. If imagining and wishing for a nobler way of acting than ours bred repentance for our own, we would have to repent of our most innocent works — since we may rightly suppose that in a more excellent nature they would have been done with greater dignity and perfection, and we would wish ours had been. When I compare the conduct of my youth with the conduct of my old age, I find I have generally kept order in both, by my own lights; that is all my powers of resistance can do. I do not flatter myself: in the same circumstances, I would do the same things again. It is not a spot on me; it is a dye that stains me through and through. I know no repentance that is superficial, halfway, and ceremonial. It must sting me on every side before I will call it by that name; it must grip and wring my bowels as deeply and as universally as God sees into me.

As for business — many fine opportunities have slipped through my fingers for lack of good management. And yet my deliberations were sound enough, given what was in front of me at the time; their method is always to take the easiest and safest course. I find that in my old resolutions I proceeded sensibly, by my own rule, according to the state of the matter proposed; and in the same situation I would do the same a thousand years from now. I don't ask what it looks like now, but what it looked like then, when I was weighing it. The force of any counsel lies in its timing; occasions and circumstances roll and change without end. I have made some heavy mistakes in my life — important ones — not for lack of good judgment but for lack of good luck. There are secret parts in the matters we handle, parts no one can guess — especially in human nature: mute conditions that never show themselves, unknown sometimes even to their owner, which are sprung and startled into life by occasions. If my prudence could not see into them or predict them, I hold no grudge against it; its commission only runs so far. If the outcome beats me — if it favors the side I rejected — there's no help for that. I don't blame myself; I accuse my fortune, not my work. And that is not to be called repentance.

Phocion once gave the Athenians a piece of advice that was not followed; and when the affair went off successfully against his prediction, someone said to him, "Well, Phocion, are you glad things are going so well?" "Very glad," he said, "that this has happened — but I do not repent of advising the other." When friends come to me for advice, I give it freely and clearly, without stalling — the way almost everyone else does — over the chance that the thing may turn out against my opinion and leave me blamed for the counsel. That worries me not at all. They will be in the wrong for having consulted me; I could not decently refuse them the service.

I, for my part, can rarely blame anyone but myself for my blunders and misfortunes, because in practice I rarely ask anyone else's advice — only as a courtesy of form, or where I need information, technical knowledge, or the facts of the matter. In things where I have nothing to supply but judgment, other people's reasons may back mine up, but they seldom turn me around. I listen to them all graciously and patiently; but as far as I can remember, I have never trusted any but my own. To my mind they are only flies and motes that flit about my will. I set little store by my own opinions — but I set just as little by other people's, and fortune pays me out accordingly: I take little advice, and I am asked for little. I am rarely consulted, and even more rarely believed; I know of no undertaking, public or private, that my advice ever restored or improved. Even the people fortune had tied, more or less, to my management have let themselves be run by anyone's counsel sooner than mine. And being a man every bit as jealous of his peace as of his authority, I prefer it that way. By leaving me alone they treat me according to my professed creed, which is to be settled and contained entirely within myself. To me it is a pleasure to be uninvolved in other men's affairs and clear of standing surety for what they do.

About all things past, however they fell out, I have little regret. This thought spares me the pain of it: that's how they were bound to fall out; they sit now in the great revolution of the universe, in the chain of Stoical causes. Your fancy cannot, by wish or imagination, shift one jot of it without overturning the whole order of things, and the past, and the future.

For the rest, I hate that accidental repentance that old age brings in its baggage. The ancient who said he was obliged to his years for weaning him off pleasure held a different opinion from mine. I can never think myself beholden to impotence for any good it may do me. Nor, as Quintilian says, will Providence ever be so hostile to her own work that feebleness should be ranked among the best things. Our appetites are scarce in old age; a deep satiety settles on us after the act. In that I see nothing of conscience. Sourness and weakness stamp us with a drowsy, crabbed, rheumatic virtue. We must not let natural decay carry us so far that it corrupts our judgment along the way. Youth and pleasure never so mastered me, back then, that I failed to see the face of vice in the pleasure; and the distaste the years have brought me does not so master me now that I cannot see the pleasure in the vice. Now that I am no longer in my prime, I judge these things as if I were. As the old poet says, old as I am, unfit now for ladies' love, I still remember what beauty can do.

I, who examine my reason narrowly and strictly, find it the very same it was in my most licentious years — except perhaps weaker and dimmer for having grown old. And I find that whatever pleasure it now refuses me out of regard for my bodily health, it would refuse me no more than it ever did out of regard for the health of my soul. I do not rate it the more valiant for being out of combat. My temptations are so broken and crippled that they are not worth its opposing: I push them off with outstretched hands, nothing more. But set the old concupiscence in front of my reason, and I fear it would have less strength to resist than it once had. I cannot see that, of itself, it judges anything differently now than it did then, or that it has come into any new light. So if there is a convalescence here, it is a bewitched one. A wretched sort of cure, to owe your health to your disease!

It is not for our misfortune to perform that office; it is for the good fortune of our judgment. Blows and afflictions get nothing out of me but curses. They are for people who can't be wakened without a whip. My reason runs far more freely in prosperity. It is much more distracted and put upon digesting pains than digesting pleasures. I see best in clear weather. Health counsels me more cheerfully, and to better effect, than sickness does. I did all that lay in me to reform and regulate myself in the matter of pleasures while I still had the health and vigor to enjoy them. I would be ashamed and resentful if the misery and bad luck of my decrepitude were to be rated above my good, healthy, lively, vigorous years — if men were to take my measure not by what I have been, but by what I have ceased to be.

In my opinion it is living happily, and not — as Antisthenes said — dying happily, that makes human felicity. I have made no effort to fix a philosopher's tail onto the head and body of a dissolute man; I will not have this wretched remainder of me give the lie to the pleasant, sound, and long part of my life. I want to present myself uniform throughout. Were I to live my life over again, I would live it just as I have lived it. I neither complain of the past nor fear the future; and unless I am badly fooled, things have gone inside me about as they have gone outside. One of my chief debts to my fortune is that my bodily life has run its course with each thing in its natural season. I have seen the grass, the flower, and the fruit; now I see the withering — happily, because naturally. I bear my present infirmities the more gently because they came on schedule, and because they make me remember all the more kindly the long happiness of my past life. My wisdom may have been the same size in both seasons; but it performed better, and with better grace, when it was green and gay than now that it is broken down, peevish, and labored.

So I renounce these accidental, painful reformations. God must touch our hearts. Our consciences must amend of themselves, by the strengthening of our reason — not by the failing of our appetites. Pleasure is not, in itself, something pale and colorless, fit to be made out only by dim, bleary eyes.

We ought to love temperance for its own sake, and out of reverence for God, who has commanded it, and chastity too. But what catarrhs reduce us to — what I owe to the favor of my kidney stones — is neither chastity nor temperance. A man cannot brag of despising and resisting pleasure if he cannot see it, if he doesn't know what it is — its graces, its powers, its most winning beauties. I know both sides; I have the right to say it. But it seems to me that in old age our souls suffer more troublesome ailments and defects than in youth. I said so when I was young and got laughed at for my beardless chin; and I say so now that my grey hair gives me some standing. We call our peevish tempers, and our distaste for whatever is in front of us, wisdom. But the truth is we don't so much abandon our vices as trade them — and, in my opinion, for worse ones. Besides a silly, threadbare pride, a tedious itch to talk, prickly and unsociable moods, superstition, and a ridiculous appetite for riches just when we have lost the use of them, I find in old age more envy, more injustice, more malice. It plants more wrinkles in our minds than on our faces; and you will seldom or never see a soul that doesn't, in aging, come to smell sour and musty. The whole man moves together, toward his ripening and toward his rot.

Looking at the wisdom of Socrates, and several details of his condemnation, I would venture to believe he lent himself to it on purpose, in a way, by playing along — at seventy, with the prospect just ahead of feeling the high motions of his mind cramped and his accustomed brightness dimmed.

What strange transformations I watch old age work, every day, in so many of my acquaintances! It is a powerful disease, and it creeps in naturally and invisibly. It takes a vast stock of study, and great precaution, to dodge the defects it loads onto us — or at least to slow their advance. I can feel that, for all my fortifications, it gains on me foot by foot. I hold out as well as I can. But where it will finally bring me, I do not know. Come what may, I am content that the world should know, when I have fallen, from what height I fell.
