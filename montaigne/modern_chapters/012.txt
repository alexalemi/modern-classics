Of Friendship

Watching a painter who works for me, I had a mind to imitate his method. He picks out the fairest spot, the middle of each wall, and puts there a picture labored over with all his skill; and the empty space around it he fills with grotesques — odd, fantastic figures with no charm except their variety and the extravagance of their shapes. And honestly, what are these things I scribble but grotesques and monstrous bodies, patched together from assorted parts, with no definite shape, and no order, sequence, or proportion except by accident? As Horace says, a lovely woman ends, below the waist, in a fish.

In this second part I keep pace with my painter well enough; but I fall far short of him in the first and better part, because my skill doesn't stretch to attempting a rich, polished picture, finished according to the rules of art. So I decided to borrow one from Étienne de La Boétie — one that will honor and adorn all the rest of this work. I mean a discourse he called Voluntary Servitude, though those who never knew him have since renamed it, aptly enough, Against One. He wrote it in his youth, as an exercise, in honor of liberty against tyrants. It has long since passed through the hands of men of learning and judgment, and won high and deserved praise, for it is finely written, and as full as a thing can be.

And yet one can fairly say it falls far short of what he could do. If, in the riper age when I had the good luck to know him, he had taken up a plan like mine — to put his thoughts in writing — we would have seen many rare things, things that would have come very near rivaling the best of the ancients. In natural gifts especially, I know no one comparable to him. But he left nothing behind except that treatise — and even that by chance; I believe he never saw it again after it first left his hands — and some observations on that Edict of January which our civil wars have made famous, which may yet find their place elsewhere. That was all I could recover of his remains — I, to whom, with so loving a remembrance, on his deathbed, he willed his library and his papers — besides the little volume of his works that I have since had printed.

And I owe this treatise a particular debt, because it was the occasion of our first acquaintance. It was shown to me long before I ever saw him, and gave me my first knowledge of his name; and so it set going the friendship which, for as long as God was pleased to leave us together, we kept up so perfect, so inviolate, and so entire that you will hardly read of its like — and among the men of our own day there is no sign or trace of any such thing in practice. So much has to come together to build it that it is much if fortune manages it once in three centuries.

There is nothing nature seems to have made us for so much as society; and Aristotle says the good lawgivers took more care of friendship than of justice. Now the highest point of its perfection is this: that it exist for its own sake. Generally, all the attachments that pleasure, profit, or public or private need creates and feeds are so much the less beautiful and noble — so much the less friendships — to the degree that they mix into friendship some other cause and aim and reward than friendship itself. Nor do the four ancient kinds — natural, social, hospitable, erotic — make up a true and perfect friendship, whether taken separately or together.

That of children for parents is really respect. Friendship feeds on a communication that cannot exist between them, the gap being too great; and it might even offend against the duties of nature. Not all a father's secret thoughts are fit to share with his children, for fear of breeding an unbecoming familiarity; and the warnings and corrections, which are among the chief offices of friendship, cannot properly be performed by son toward father. There have been nations where custom had the children kill their fathers, and others where the fathers killed their children, to keep one from standing in the other's way in life; and naturally what the one expects depends on the other's ruin. There have been great philosophers who made nothing of this natural tie. Aristippus, for one: when they pressed him about the affection he owed his children, seeing they had come out of him, he started spitting, and said that this had come out of him too — that we also breed worms and lice. And there was that other man, whom Plutarch tried to reconcile with his brother, who said: "I don't value him the more for having come out of the same hole."

Brother is, in truth, a fine name and full of affection — which is why he and I made it our name for each other. But the tangling of interests, the dividing of estates, the fact that one's wealth must be the other's poverty — these strangely loosen and weaken the brotherly tie. Brothers pushing their fortunes and advancement along the same path can hardly help jostling and blocking each other at every step. Besides, why should the harmony of character, mind, and inclination that breeds true and perfect friendship be found in these relations? Father and son may be of entirely opposite temperaments, and brothers too. He is my son, he is my brother — but he is hot-tempered, ill-natured, or a fool. And then, the more these are friendships that law and natural obligation lay upon us, the less there is in them of our own choice and free will; and our free will produces nothing more promptly and properly its own than affection and friendship. Not that I haven't tasted, in my own person, everything that kind of bond can give. I had the best and most indulgent father that ever was, even into his extreme old age, and he came of a family famous and exemplary for generations in the matter of brotherly concord — he was himself, as Horace says of himself, known for a father's love toward his brothers.

Nor can we bring in here the love we bear to women, though it is an act of our own choice, or set it in this rank. Its fire, I confess — and the goddess who mixes a sweet bitterness into love is no stranger to me, as Catullus admits — is more active, more scorching, more sharp. But it is a rash and fickle flame, wavering and inconstant, a fever that comes and goes in fits, and that holds us only by one corner. The fire of friendship is general and universal, but temperate and even — a constant, settled warmth, all gentleness and smoothness, with nothing harsh or stinging in it. What's more, love is nothing but a frantic desire for what flees from us. It is Ariosto's hunter, who chases the hare through heat and cold, over mountain and shore, prizes it nothing once he sees it caught, and only runs hard after the thing that runs away. As soon as love crosses into the territory of friendship — that is, into an agreement of wills — it droops and goes out. Enjoyment destroys it, because its end is bodily, and bodies get their fill. Friendship, on the contrary, is enjoyed in exact proportion as it is desired. It rises, grows, and feeds only on enjoyment, because it is spiritual, and the soul grows finer with use. Under this perfect friendship those fleeting affections found a place in me too, in my younger years — to say nothing of him, who confesses more than enough of them in his verses. So I had both passions in me, but always so that I could tell them apart; and they were never in any degree of comparison — the first holding its course in so lofty and proud a flight that it looked down with disdain at the other beating along far below it.

As for marriage: besides being a bargain where only the entrance is free — its continuance is forced and compelled, hanging on something other than our will — and a bargain ordinarily struck for other ends, it comes with a thousand foreign tangles to unravel, enough to snap the thread and trouble the course of a living affection. Friendship has no business or traffic with anything but itself. Add to this, to tell the truth, that the ordinary capacity of women is not up to the kind of converse and communication that feeds this sacred bond; nor do their souls seem firm enough to bear the strain of so hard and lasting a knot. And certainly, if it were not for that — if there could be such a free and willing relationship in which not only the souls had this entire enjoyment, but the bodies also shared in the union, so that the whole person was engaged — the friendship would no doubt be fuller and more complete. But there is no example yet of that sex arriving at it, and by the common consent of the ancient schools it is excluded.

And that other Greek licence is justly abhorred by our morals. Besides, since their practice required so necessary a disparity of age and difference of office between the lovers, it answered no better than the love of women to the perfect union and harmony we are asking for here. For what is this love of friendship, Cicero asks — why does no one love an ugly young man, or a handsome old one? Even the picture the Academy paints of it will not, I think, contradict me when I say this: that the first frenzy, breathed by the son of Venus into the lover's heart at the sight of the flower and prime of a budding youth — a frenzy to which they allow all the insolent and passionate excesses that an immoderate ardor can produce — was founded simply on outward beauty, the false image of bodily generation. It could not be founded on the soul, which was still hidden from sight, only just springing up, too young to show what it would become. If that frenzy seized a base spirit, the means of his courtship were rich presents, favor and advancement to office, and other such trash — which they by no means approve. If it fell on a nobler soul, the means were noble in proportion: instruction in philosophy; precepts to revere religion, to obey the laws, to die for the good of one's country; examples of valor, prudence, and justice — the lover laboring to make himself acceptable by the grace and beauty of his soul, that of his body being long since faded, and hoping by this fellowship of mind to establish a firmer and more lasting bond.

When this courtship came to its effect in due season — for what they do not require in the lover, namely leisure and discretion in his pursuit, they strictly require in the one loved, since he has to judge an inner beauty, hard to know and slow to discover — then there was born in the beloved the desire of a spiritual conception, by the mediation of a spiritual beauty. In him, this was the principal thing; the bodily part was accidental and secondary — the exact reverse of the lover. For this reason they rank the beloved higher, and maintain that the gods do too; and they greatly blame the poet Aeschylus for giving the lover's part, in the loves of Achilles and Patroclus, to Achilles, who was in the first beardless flower of his youth and the handsomest of all the Greeks. After this general communion was established, the sovereign and worthiest part presiding and performing its proper offices, they say great benefit came of it, both to private men and to the public; that it made the strength of the countries where it was received, and was the chief safeguard of liberty and justice — witness the wholesome loves of Harmodius and Aristogiton. So they called it sacred and divine, and held that nothing was its enemy but the violence of tyrants and the baseness of the common people. In the end, all that can be granted to the Academy is that this was a love which finished in friendship — which agrees well enough with the Stoic definition of love, as Cicero reports it: love is the attempt to form a friendship out of the appearance of beauty.

I come back to my own description, which is juster and truer: only those friendships deserve the name, as Cicero says, that are fortified and confirmed by mature judgment and length of time.

For the rest, what we commonly call friends and friendships are nothing but acquaintances and familiarities, knit by some chance or convenience, by means of which our souls hold a little conversation. But in the friendship I am speaking of, the souls mix and blend into each other so completely that they wear away the seam that joined them, and cannot find it again. If you pressed me to say why I loved him, I feel it could only be expressed by answering: Because it was he; because it was I.

There is, beyond all my reasoning and beyond everything I can say in particular, some inexplicable and fated force that brokered this union. We were seeking each other before we ever met, on the strength of what we heard of each other — which worked on our affections more than, in reason, mere reports should. I think it was some ordinance of heaven. We embraced each other in our names. And at our first meeting, which happened by chance at a great town feast, we found ourselves so taken with each other, so well known to each other, so bound to each other, that from then on nothing was as near to us as each other. He wrote an excellent Latin satire, since published, in which he excuses and explains the haste of our understanding, so quickly grown to its perfection. Having so little time to last, and having begun so late — for we were both grown men, and he some years the older — it had no time to lose, and no reason to pattern itself on those slow, regular friendships that need so many precautions of long preliminary acquaintance.

This friendship has no model but itself, and can be compared only to itself. It was not one special consideration, nor two, nor three, nor four, nor a thousand. It was some quintessence of the whole mixture which, having seized my entire will, led it to plunge and lose itself in his; which, having seized his entire will, led it to plunge and lose itself in mine, with an equal hunger and an equal striving. I say lose, in all truth, for we kept back nothing for ourselves — nothing that was either his or mine.

When Laelius, in the presence of the Roman consuls — who, after condemning Tiberius Gracchus, were prosecuting everyone who had been close to him — came to ask Caius Blosius, Gracchus's dearest friend, how much he would have done for him, Blosius answered: "Everything." "What, everything?" said Laelius. "And what if he had commanded you to set fire to our temples?" "He would never have commanded me that," Blosius replied. "But what if he had?" Laelius pressed. "I would have obeyed him," said the other. If he was as perfect a friend to Gracchus as the histories report, he had no need to provoke the consuls with that last bold confession; though he might well have stood firm in the assurance he had of Gracchus's will. But those who condemn his answer as seditious do not really understand the mystery, and fail to presuppose what was true: that he held Gracchus's will in his sleeve, both by the power of a friend and by his perfect knowledge of the man. They were friends more than citizens — friends more than friends or enemies of their country, or friends of ambition and upheaval. Having given themselves absolutely to each other, each held absolutely the reins of the other's inclination. Suppose this team driven by virtue and guided by reason — and without that it cannot be harnessed at all — and Blosius's answer is exactly what it ought to have been. If their actions ever flew out of the handle, then by my measure of friendship they were neither friends to each other nor friends to themselves.

Besides, that answer sounds no worse than mine would, if someone asked me, "If your will commanded you to kill your daughter, would you kill her?" and I answered yes. That implies no consent to the deed, because I have no shadow of doubt about my own will — and just as little about the will of such a friend. All the eloquence in the world has no power to dislodge me from the certainty I have of his intentions and judgments. Not one action of his could be put before me — whatever face it wore — without my finding its motive at first sight. Our souls pulled so exactly together, they regarded each other with so ardent an affection, and with the same affection laid open to each other the very bottom of our hearts, that I not only knew his soul as well as my own, but in any business of mine I would sooner have trusted myself to him than to myself.

Let no one set those other, common friendships in the same rank as this. I know them as well as the next man, and the most perfect of their kind too; but I advise against mixing up their rules — you would be badly deceived. In those other friendships you must walk with the bridle in your hand, with prudence and caution; the knot is not tied so tight that there is no cause to mistrust it. "Love him," said Chilo, "as if you must one day hate him; and hate him as if you must one day love him." This precept, which would be an abomination in the sovereign and ruling friendship I am describing, is perfectly sound in the practice of ordinary and customary ones — to which you may also apply the saying Aristotle had so often in his mouth: "O my friends, there is no friend."

In this noble commerce, the services and benefits that feed other friendships do not even deserve to be counted. The reason is the complete fusion of our wills. For just as the kindness I have for myself gets no increase from the help I give myself in time of need — whatever the Stoics say — and just as I feel no gratitude to myself for any service I do myself, so the union of such friends, being truly perfect, makes them lose the very feeling of such duties, and hate and banish from between them these words of division and difference: benefit, obligation, gratitude, request, thanks, and the rest. Everything being actually common between them — wills, thoughts, judgments, goods, wives, children, honor, and life — and their agreement being that of one soul in two bodies, by Aristotle's very apt definition, they can neither lend nor give anything to each other. That is why the lawmakers, to honor marriage with some imagined resemblance to this divine bond, forbid gifts between husband and wife: to infer by it that everything should belong to both of them, and that they have nothing to divide or share out.

In the friendship I am speaking of, if one could give to the other, it is the one receiving the kindness who would oblige his friend. For since each of them, above everything, is looking for the chance to do the other good, the one who supplies the matter and the occasion is the generous one — giving his friend the satisfaction of doing for him the very thing he most desires. When the philosopher Diogenes was short of money, he used to say he asked his friends to give it back, not to give it.

And to show how this works in practice, here is a singular old example. Eudamidas of Corinth had two friends, Charixenus of Sicyon and Areteus of Corinth. Coming to die a poor man, his two friends being rich, he made his will like this: "I bequeath to Areteus the keeping of my mother, to support and provide for her in her old age. To Charixenus I bequeath the marrying of my daughter, and the giving of as large a dowry as he can manage. And in case one of them should die, I substitute the survivor in his place." Those who first saw this will had a good laugh at it. But the heirs, once they were told, accepted it with extraordinary satisfaction; and when Charixenus died five days later, and both charges fell to Areteus alone, he kept the old woman with great care and tenderness, and of the five talents he had in his estate, he gave two and a half in marriage with his own only daughter, and two and a half in marriage with the daughter of Eudamidas — and held both weddings on the same day.

This example is very full — except for one objection: the plurality of friends. For the perfect friendship I am speaking of is indivisible. Each gives himself so entirely to his friend that he has nothing left to share elsewhere. On the contrary, he is sorry he is not double, triple, quadruple — sorry he does not have several souls and several wills, so as to confer them all on this one object. Common friendships can be divided up: you can love the beauty of this man, the easy temper of that one, the generosity of a third, a father's affection in a fourth, a brother's in a fifth, and so on. But this friendship that possesses the whole soul, and rules there with absolute sovereignty, cannot possibly be doubled. If two friends called for your help at the same moment, which would you run to? If they asked contrary services of you, how could you serve both? If one trusted to your silence a thing it mattered for the other to know, how would you get clear? A single, ruling friendship dissolves every other obligation. The secret I have sworn to reveal to no other, I can impart without perjury to him who is not another: he is myself. It is a great enough miracle for a man to double himself; those who talk of tripling do not know the height of the thing. Nothing is extreme that has its match. And whoever supposes that of two friends I love one as much as the other, and that they love each other and love me just as I love them, multiplies into a brotherhood the most single and unified thing in the world — the thing of which even one example is the hardest in the world to find.

The rest of that story fits very well with what I was saying. For Eudamidas grants it to his friends, as a kindness and a favor, to be of use to him in his need. He leaves them heirs to his own generosity, which consists in putting into their hands the means of doing him good. And without doubt the force of friendship shows far more richly in his act than in Areteus's. In short, these are effects beyond the imagining of anyone who has not tasted them. They make me honor beyond measure the answer of that young soldier to Cyrus, who asked him how much he would take for a horse that had just won him the prize in a race — whether he would trade it for a kingdom: "No indeed, Sire. But I would gladly let him go to gain a true friend by it, if I could find a man worthy of such an alliance." He did not speak badly when he said "if I could find." You can easily enough find men fit for a superficial acquaintance. But in this kind, where a man deals from the very bottom of his heart and holds nothing back, you can be sure that every spring of the works must be perfectly true and perfectly sure.

In partnerships that hold only by one end, we need only guard against the flaws that touch that particular end. It cannot matter to me what religion my doctor or my lawyer holds; that consideration has nothing in common with the services of friendship they owe me. And in the household dealings between me and my servants, I take the same line. I hardly ask whether a footman is chaste; I ask whether he is diligent. A gambling muleteer troubles me less than a weak one, a foul-mouthed cook less than an ignorant one. I do not set up to tell the world what it should do — plenty of others meddle with that — only to give an account of what I do in it. As Terence has it: this is my way; as for you, do as you find needful.

At table I prefer the witty to the wise; in bed, beauty before goodness; in conversation, the best talker, sincere or not. And so with everything else. There was a man caught astride a stick, playing horses with his children, who asked the one who surprised him to say nothing of it until he was a father himself — judging that the fondness which would then be born in his own soul would make him a fair judge of the sight. So I, too, could wish I were speaking only to people who have experienced what I am telling. But knowing how far such a friendship lies from common practice, and how rare it is, I do not expect to meet a single good judge of it. For even the discourses antiquity has left us on the subject seem flat and weak to me beside the feeling I have of it. And in this point the facts outdo the very precepts of philosophy. As Horace says, while I have my senses, there is nothing I would set against an agreeable friend.

Old Menander called that man happy who had managed to meet even the shadow of a friend. He certainly had reason to say so, especially if he spoke from experience. For in truth, if I compare all the rest of my life — though by God's grace I have passed it pleasantly enough, comfortably, and, except for the loss of such a friend, free of any heavy affliction, in great tranquility of mind, content with my natural and original advantages without hunting after others — if I compare the whole of it, I say, with the four years I was given to enjoy the sweet company and society of that excellent man, it is nothing but smoke, nothing but a dark and tedious night.

Since the day I lost him — a day forever bitter to me, forever honored, for so, you gods, you willed it, as Virgil says — I have only dragged out a languishing life. Even the pleasures that offer themselves to me, instead of consoling me, double my grief at his loss. We went halves in everything; it seems to me now that by outliving him I am stealing his share. I had resolved, as Terence's character says, that it was not right for me to enjoy any pleasure while the partner of all my pleasures was gone.

I was already so accustomed to be always two, everywhere and in everything, that it seems to me now I am no more than half of myself. If an untimely blow carried off that half of my soul, as Horace asks, why do I linger here, the other half — not so dear now, and not whole? The same day was the ruin of us both.

There is no action or thought of mine in which I do not miss him — as I know he would have missed me. For just as he surpassed me by infinite degrees in virtue and every other ability, so he did in the duty of friendship. What shame or measure can there be, Horace asks, in mourning a head so dear? And Catullus's lament for his brother says it for me: O brother, torn from me in my misery — with you all our joys have gone, the joys your sweet love fed while you lived. Dying, brother, you shattered all my happiness; my whole soul is buried with you; since your death I have driven from my mind these studies and every delight of the mind. Shall I never speak with you, never hear you speak again? Never see you again, brother dearer than life itself? At least I will love you always.

But let us hear this boy of sixteen speak for a while.

Because I have found that this work has since been brought out, and to a bad end, by men who aim to disturb and change the state of our government without troubling themselves whether they will improve it — and because they have mixed it in among writings of their own making — I have changed my mind about placing it here. And so that the author's memory may take no harm with those who could not know his opinions and his conduct at close hand, I tell them that he treated this subject in his boyhood, only by way of exercise, as a common theme worked over in a thousand books. I have no doubt he believed what he wrote, for he was conscientious enough not to lie even in jest. And I know besides that, had the choice been his, he would rather have been born in Venice than in Sarlat — and with reason. But he had another maxim sovereignly imprinted on his soul: to obey, and most religiously to submit to, the laws under which he was born. There never was a better citizen, or one who cared more for his country's quiet, or a sharper enemy of the commotions and innovations of his time. He would far sooner have used his talents to put out those civil fires than to give them anything to feed on. His mind was cast in the mold of better ages than ours.

So, in exchange for that serious work, I will substitute another from the same hand, written in the same season of his life — but of a gayer and more playful air.
