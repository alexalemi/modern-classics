Of Custom, and Not Easily Changing an Accepted Law
(Part 1 of 2)

Whoever invented the story of the village woman and the calf understood the power of custom. She got into the habit of carrying a young calf in her arms, and kept it up every day as the animal grew — until, by sheer custom, she could carry it as a full-grown ox. For custom truly is a violent and treacherous schoolmistress. Little by little, slyly and unnoticed, she slips in the foot of her authority. Then, once time has helped her settle and fix it with that gentle, humble beginning, she pulls off the mask and shows a furious, tyrannical face — and by then we no longer have the courage, or even the strength, to lift our eyes against her. At every turn we catch her forcing and violating the rules of nature. As Pliny says, custom is the most effective master of all things.

I would point to Plato's cave in his Republic, and to the doctors, who so often surrender the reasoning of their own art to custom's authority. And to that king who trained his stomach, by habit, to live on poison; and the girl Albertus tells of, who lived on spiders. In that new world of the Indies, great nations were found, in very different climates, who ate spiders too — kept stocks of them, raised them for the table, along with grasshoppers, mice, lizards, and bats. In a time when such delicacies were scarce, a toad sold for six crowns. They cook all this and serve it up with a choice of sauces. Other peoples were found there for whom our diet, and the meat we eat, were poisonous and deadly.

The power of custom is enormous, as Cicero says: hunters will spend whole nights in the snow, or let the mountain sun roast them; boxers, battered by the leaded glove, never let out a groan.

These strange examples will not seem so strange if we consider what we experience every day: how much custom numbs our senses. We don't even need the stories about the people who live by the cataracts of the Nile, or what philosophers believe about the music of the spheres — that the heavenly bodies, being solid and smooth, touch and rub against one another as they turn, and can't help producing a marvelous harmony, whose changes and cadences drive the rounds and dances of the stars; and yet the ears of every creature down here, deafened and dulled like the Egyptians' by the constant noise, cannot perceive it, however great it is. Blacksmiths, millers, pewterers, forgemen, and armorers could never live inside the perpetual racket of their own trades if it struck their ears with the violence it strikes ours.

My perfumed jacket pleases my own nose at first; after I've worn it three days running, only the bystanders enjoy it. Stranger still, custom can fix its impression on our senses even across long gaps and interruptions — ask anyone who lives near church towers and the frequent noise of the bells. I sleep at home in a tower where a great bell rings out the Ave Maria every morning and evening. The noise shakes the tower itself, and at first it seemed unbearable to me; now I'm so used to it that I hear it without the least annoyance, and often without waking at all.

Plato once scolded a boy for playing at nuts. "You're scolding me for a very small thing," said the boy. "Custom," Plato replied, "is no small thing."

I find that our worst vices take their first bent in our most tender infancy, and that the heart of our education is in the nurse's hands. Mothers are delighted to watch a child wring a chicken's neck, or amuse itself by hurting a dog or a cat. And there are wise fathers in this world who take it as a fine mark of a martial spirit when they hear a son insult a poor peasant or a servant who doesn't dare answer back, or see him lord it over them — and a great sign of cleverness when he cheats a playmate with some piece of malicious trickery. Yet these are the true seeds and roots of cruelty, tyranny, and treason. They sprout there, and afterward shoot up vigorously and grow to monstrous size, cultivated by custom.

It is a very dangerous mistake to excuse these ugly inclinations because the child is young and the stakes are small. First, it is nature speaking — and her voice is all the more honest, her inward thoughts all the more undisguised, while she is weak and young. Second, the ugliness of cheating does not depend on the difference between gold coins and pins. I'd rather draw the conclusion the other way: since he cheats at pins, why wouldn't he cheat at coins? — instead of saying, as people do, "it's only pins; he would never do it for money." Children should be carefully taught to hate vices for their own substance. The natural deformity of those vices should be shown to them so plainly that they not only avoid them in their actions but loathe them in their hearts — so that the very thought of them, under whatever mask, is hateful.

I know this much about myself. Because I was brought up from childhood to deal plainly and straight, and took an aversion to every kind of trickery even in my childish games (and note this: the games of children are not games; they should be judged as their most serious actions), there is no game so small in which I don't feel — from inside, naturally, without study or effort — an extreme aversion to cheating. I shuffle and cut and make as much clatter with the cards, and keep as strict an account of farthings, as if they were gold doubloons. Winning or losing against my wife and daughter is the same to me as when I play in earnest with others, for serious money. At all times and in all places, my own eyes are enough to keep watch on my fingers. No one watches me more closely, and there is no one whose opinion I respect more.

The other day I saw, at my own house, a little fellow from Nantes, born without arms, who has trained his feet so well to do the work his hands should have done that they've half forgotten their natural job. In fact, he calls them his hands. He cuts things with them, loads and fires a pistol, threads a needle, sews, writes, takes off his hat, combs his hair, plays cards and dice — all with as much dexterity as anyone with the usual, more suitable limbs to help him. The money I gave him — he earns his living by showing off these feats — he took in his foot, the way we take it in our hand. I've seen another, still a boy, who flourished a two-handed sword and, if I may put it this way, handled a halberd with nothing but the movements of his neck and shoulders, having no hands; tossed them into the air and caught them again, threw a dagger, and cracked a whip as well as any coachman in France.

But custom shows her effects far more plainly in the strange impressions she stamps on our minds, where she meets less resistance. What can't she impose on our judgments and beliefs? Is there any opinion so fantastic — leaving aside the gross impostures of religions, which have so strangely besotted so many great nations and so many intelligent men; for that lies beyond the reach of human reason, and any error is more excusable in those who haven't been granted, by divine bounty, an extraordinary light from above — is there any opinion so extravagant, I say, that custom hasn't planted it and established it as law in whatever part of the world she pleased to exercise her power? So that old exclamation hits the mark exactly: as Cicero says, isn't it a shame for a natural philosopher — an observer and hunter of nature — to go looking for the testimony of truth in minds soaked through with custom?

I believe no fancy so absurd or ridiculous can enter the human imagination that it doesn't meet with the example of some public practice somewhere — and consequently get a footing and backing from our reason. There are peoples where the fashion is to turn your back on the man you greet, and never look at the person you mean to honor. There is a place where, whenever the king spits, the greatest ladies of his court hold out their hands to receive it; and another nation where the most eminent persons around him stoop to take up his excrement in a linen cloth.

Let me steal a little room here for a story. A French gentleman always blew his nose with his fingers — a thing very much against our fashion — and in defending himself for it (he was a man famous for his repartee), he asked me what privilege that filthy excretion enjoyed, that we should keep a fine handkerchief ready to receive it, and then — even better — fold it up carefully and carry it around in our pockets all day. Surely, he said, that was far more nauseating than seeing it thrown away, as we throw away everything else the body discards. I found he wasn't entirely without a point; and after I'd been in his company a while, his slovenly habit had grown familiar to me — though we make a face at it when we hear it reported of another country.

Miracles are miracles according to our ignorance of nature, not according to nature's essence. Being continually accustomed to a thing blinds the eye of our judgment. Barbarians are no more a wonder to us than we are to them, and with no more reason — as everyone would admit if, after touring those remote examples, we could settle down to reflect on our own and compare the two fairly. Human reason is a dye soaked almost equally into all our opinions and manners, whatever form they take — infinite in matter, infinite in diversity.

But back to my point. There are peoples where no one but his wife and children speaks to the king except through a tube. In one and the same nation, the virgins display openly the parts that modesty should tell them to hide, while the married women carefully cover and conceal them. A custom somewhere else is a kind of cousin to that one: there, chastity matters only in marriage, for unmarried women may give themselves to as many men as they please, and if they become pregnant, they may lawfully take medicine, in the sight of everyone, to destroy the child.

And in another place, if a tradesman marries, all the wedding guests of his own condition lie with the bride before he does — and the more of them there are, the greater her honor, and the higher the opinion of her capacity and strength. If an officer marries, it's the same; the same with a laborer or a man of low condition — except then it falls to the lord of the place to perform that office. And yet a severe fidelity is strictly required during the marriage afterward.

There are places where brothels of young men are kept for the pleasure of women; where the wives go to war alongside their husbands, and take their share not only of the dangers of battle but of the honors of command. Places where they wear rings not only through their noses, lips, cheeks, and toes, but heavy links of gold thrust through their nipples and buttocks; where at dinner they wipe their fingers on their thighs, their genitals, and the soles of their feet.

Places where children don't inherit — brothers and nephews do; and elsewhere nephews only, except in the succession of the prince. Where, to maintain their system of common property, sovereign magistrates take universal charge of the farming and distribute the produce according to each person's need. Where they mourn the death of children and feast at the death of old men. Where ten or twelve sleep in one bed, men and their wives together. Where women whose husbands die violent deaths may marry again, and the others may not. Where the condition of women is held in such contempt that they kill their female infants and buy wives from their neighbors to supply the need. Where husbands may repudiate their wives without showing any cause, but wives cannot leave their husbands for any cause whatever. Where husbands may sell their wives if they prove barren. Where they boil the bodies of their dead, then pound them to a pulp, mix it with their wine, and drink it. Where the most coveted burial is to be eaten by dogs — elsewhere, by birds.

Where they believe the souls of the blessed live in perfect freedom, in delightful fields stocked with every delicacy, and that it is these souls, repeating the words we utter, that we call Echo. Where they fight in the water and shoot their arrows with deadly accuracy while swimming. Where, as a sign of submission, they hunch their shoulders up and hang their heads down — and take off their shoes when they enter the king's palace. Where the eunuchs who guard the sacred women have their lips and noses cut off besides, so that no one can love them. Where the priests put out their own eyes, the better to know their demons and receive their oracles.

Where every man makes himself a god of whatever he likes best — the hunter of a lion or a fox, the fisherman of some fish — with idols for every human action and passion; and there the sun, the moon, and the earth are the chief deities, and the way to swear an oath is to touch the ground while looking up at the sky. Where both meat and fish are eaten raw. Where the greatest oath is to swear by the name of some dead person of good reputation, laying a hand on his tomb. Where the new year's gift the king sends each year to the princes who are his vassals is fire: when it is brought, all the old fire is put out, and the neighboring peoples are bound to come fetch the new fire, each for themselves, on pain of high treason. Where, when the king retires from governing to give himself wholly to devotion (which happens often), his next successor is obliged to do the same, and the right to the kingdom passes to the third in line. Where they vary the form of government as affairs seem to demand: depose the king when they think fit, set up certain elders to govern in his place, and sometimes hand it over to the common people. Where men and women alike are both circumcised and baptized. Where the soldier who, in one battle or several, has managed to present seven enemy heads to the king is made a noble. Where they live in that rare and unsociable belief that the soul is mortal. Where the women give birth without pain or fear. Where the women wear copper plates on both legs; and if a louse bites them, they are bound, as a point of courage, to bite it back; and they dare not marry until they have first offered their virginity to the king, if he cares to accept it.

Where the ordinary greeting is to touch a finger to the ground and then point it up toward the sky. Where the men carry loads on their heads and the women on their shoulders. Where the women make water standing and the men squatting. Where they send their blood as a token of friendship, and offer incense, as to gods, to the men they wish to honor. Where kindred may not marry — not just to the fourth degree, but to any degree however remote. Where children nurse for four years, and often twelve — and in that same place it is held fatal to give a child the breast on its first day of life. Where correcting the boys belongs especially to the fathers, and the girls to the mothers — the punishment being to hang them by the heels in the smoke. Where they circumcise the women. Where they eat every kind of herb, with no scruple but the badness of the smell. Where everything stands open — the finest houses, furnished in the richest style, without doors, windows, trunks, or chests to lock — and a thief there is punished double what he would be anywhere else. Where they crack lice with their teeth like monkeys, and find it horrible to see one killed with a fingernail. Where in all their lives they never cut their hair or pare their nails; and in another place, they pare the nails of the right hand only, letting the left grow for ornament and show. Where they let the hair on the right side grow as long as it will and shave the other side; and in the neighboring provinces, some let their hair grow long in front and some behind, shaving the rest close.

Where parents rent out their children to their guests, and husbands their wives. Where a man may get his own mother with child, and fathers make use of their own daughters or sons, without scandal. Where, at their solemn feasts, they lend their children to one another, with no regard for nearness of blood. In one place men feed on human flesh; in another, it is counted a pious duty for a man to kill his father at a certain age. Elsewhere, fathers decide the fates of their children while they are still in their mothers' wombs — these to be kept and carefully raised, those to be abandoned or done away with. Elsewhere the old husbands lend their wives to young men; and in another place wives are held in common, without offense; and in one place in particular, the women count it a mark of honor to wear as many gaily fringed tassels on the hem of their dress as they have had lovers.

Moreover, hasn't custom made a whole republic of women, by themselves? Hasn't she put weapons in their hands, raised them armies, and made them fight battles? And doesn't she teach the most ignorant common folk, by her bare precept, lessons that all the philosophy in the world could never beat into the heads of the wisest men? For we know of entire nations where death was not only despised but welcomed with the greatest celebration; where children of seven let themselves be whipped to death without changing expression; where riches were in such contempt that the poorest citizen would not have stooped to pick up a purse of gold crowns. And we know of regions rich in every kind of provision where, all the same, the most ordinary diet — and the one they liked best — was nothing but bread, cress, and water. And didn't custom work that miracle in Chios, that in seven hundred years no maid or wife was ever known to commit an act against her honor?

To sum up: in my opinion, there is nothing custom does not do, or cannot do; and Pindar had every reason to call her the ruler of the world.

The man who was seen beating his father answered, when reproached for it, that it was the custom of his family: his father had beaten his grandfather the same way, his grandfather his great-grandfather — "and this boy," he said, pointing to his son, "will beat me when he comes to my age." And the father whom his son was dragging and hauling through the streets ordered him to stop at a certain door, because he himself had dragged his own father only that far — that being the fixed limit of the hereditary outrage the sons of that family traditionally practiced on the fathers. It is as much by custom as by infirmity, says Aristotle, that women tear their hair, bite their nails, and eat coal and earth — and more by custom than by nature that men abuse one another's bodies.

The laws of conscience, which we claim are born of nature, are born of custom. Each of us holds an inward reverence for the opinions and manners approved and received among his own people; he cannot break from them without great reluctance, or conform to them without applauding himself. In times past, when the people of Crete wanted to curse someone, they prayed the gods to entangle him in some bad custom.

But the principal effect of custom's power is to seize and grip us so completely that it is hardly in us to wrestle free of her grasp, or to come back to ourselves enough to consider and weigh what she commands. The truth is, because we drink her in with our mother's milk, and because the face of the world presents itself in this posture to our first sight, we seem born on the condition of following this track. And the common notions we find in repute all around us, poured into our minds with our fathers' seed, appear to be the universal and natural ones. From which it comes to pass that whatever is off the hinges of custom is believed to be off the hinges of reason — how unreasonably, for the most part, God knows.
