Of Three Kinds of Company

We should not bolt ourselves so tightly to our own moods and temperaments. The best skill we have is knowing how to apply ourselves to many different things. To keep yourself tied and chained to one single course is to exist, not to live. The finest souls are the ones with the most variety and flexibility in them. There is an admiring report of the elder Cato along these lines — as Livy says, his mind was so adaptable to everything that whatever he happened to be doing, you would swear he had been born to do exactly that.

If I were free to shape myself by my own design, there is no way of being — however graceful — that I would want to be fixed in so firmly that I could not break loose from it. Life is an uneven, irregular, many-shaped motion. To be led around by the nose by yourself, endlessly, so stuck in your own grooves that you cannot turn aside or twist your neck out of the collar — that is not being a friend to yourself, much less a master. It is being a slave.

I say this now, at this point in my life, because I find I cannot easily shake off the nagging of my own soul. It usually cannot entertain itself except with narrow things, and it cannot work except entirely and with all its strength. Give it the lightest subject and it will swell and stretch the thing to the point where its full power is engaged. For that reason, idleness is hard labor for me, and bad for my health. Most minds need outside material to wake them up and get them moving; mine needs it rather to settle down and rest — because, as Seneca says, the vices of leisure have to be shaken off by being busy. The chief and hardest study of my mind is studying itself. Books, for it, are the kind of work that distracts it from that study. The first thoughts that strike it set it bustling, testing its strength in every direction — sometimes flexing its force, sometimes steadying, moderating, and putting itself in order out of a feeling for grace and proportion. It has plenty of its own to work on. Nature has given it, as she gives everyone, enough material of its own to use, and subjects enough for inventing and judging.

Meditation is a powerful and rich study for anyone who can really taste it and apply himself to it. I would rather shape my soul than furnish it. There is no occupation either weaker or stronger — depending on the soul in question — than entertaining your own thoughts. The greatest men make it their whole business: those, as Cicero says, for whom to live is to think. Nature has favored this activity with a special privilege: there is nothing we can do for so long, and no activity we give ourselves to more often or more easily. It is the business of the gods, says Aristotle, and the source of their happiness and of ours.

The main use of reading, for me, is that the variety of its subjects wakes up my reason and exercises my judgment — not my memory. Few conversations can hold me without force and effort. It is true that beauty and elegance of speech win me over as much as the weight and depth of the subject, or more. And since I tend to drift off in every other kind of talk, lending only the rind of my attention, it often happens that in flat, pitiful exchanges — mere chatter — I either give drowsy, meaningless answers, unworthy of a child and ridiculous, or, more foolishly and rudely still, hold an obstinate silence. I have a brooding streak that pulls me into myself, and alongside it a heavy, childish ignorance of many perfectly ordinary things. Between those two qualities, I have earned this much: a man could truthfully tell five or six stories about me as ridiculous as any told about anyone.

But back to my point. This difficult temperament of mine makes me picky about the company I keep — I have to cull and select men for my purpose — and it makes me unfit for ordinary society. We live and do business with people. If their conversation annoys us, if we refuse to lower ourselves to plain and common souls — and the plain and common are often as sound as the finest-spun, and all wisdom is folly that does not adapt itself to the common ignorance — then we must stop meddling in other people's affairs and in our own, because business, public and private alike, gets done with exactly these people. The least forced and most natural motions of the soul are the most beautiful; the best occupations are the least strained. My God, what a good turn wisdom does for those whose desires it cuts to fit their power! There is no more useful knowledge. "According to what a man can" was the favorite saying and motto of Socrates — a motto of great solidity.

We must steer our desires toward the things nearest and easiest to get. Isn't it a foolish humor of mine to cut myself off from a thousand people my fortune has joined me to, people I cannot live without, in order to cling to one or two who are out of my reach — or rather, to a fantastical longing for something I cannot have? My gentle, easy manners, the enemies of all sourness and harshness, have probably kept me clear of envy and hostility; no man ever gave less occasion for being hated — I do not say for being loved. But the coldness of my conversation has, reasonably enough, cost me the goodwill of many people, who can be excused for reading it another and worse way.

I am very capable of forming and keeping rare and exquisite friendships. Since I seize so hungrily on the acquaintances that suit my taste, I throw myself at them with such force that I rarely fail to stick, and to make an impression where I hit — as I have happily proved more than once. In ordinary friendships I am somewhat cold and shy, for I do not move naturally except under full sail. Besides, having been given a taste, in my youth, of one single and perfect friendship, fortune has honestly left me with a kind of distaste for the others, and stamped too deep in my imagination the old saying that man is a companionable animal, but not a herd animal. And I have a natural difficulty about sharing myself by halves, with the hedging and the servile, wary caution required for keeping up many imperfect friendships — and that caution is demanded of us especially in this age, when you cannot talk about the world except dangerously or falsely.

Yet I see perfectly well that a man whose aim is the comforts of life — the essential comforts, I mean — as mine is, should run from these refinements and delicacies of temperament like the plague. What I would praise is a soul with several floors to it: one that knows how to stretch and to slacken; that is at ease wherever fortune carries it; that can talk with a neighbor about his building, his hunting, his lawsuits; that can chat with a carpenter or a gardener with pleasure. I envy those who can get familiar with the humblest of their own staff and make conversation in their manner. And I dislike Plato's advice that we should always speak to our servants, men or women, in the tone of a master, never playful, never familiar. For besides the reasons I have given, it is inhuman and unjust to set such a price on this pitiful accident of fortune; the societies that allow the least distance between masters and servants seem to me the fairest.

Other men study how to raise and elevate their minds; I study how to lower mine and bring it down. It is only faulty when it stretches. As Horace says, you tell us long tales of the line of Aeacus and the battles fought under sacred Troy — but what a cask of good wine costs, who will heat the bath, whose house will shelter me from the cold and when, about that you say nothing.

The Spartans' courage needed moderating — needed the sweet, harmonious sound of flutes to soften it in battle, so they would not throw themselves into recklessness and fury — while every other nation uses harsh, shrill sounds and loud fierce cries to whip its soldiers' courage to the limit. In the same way, it seems to me, and against the usual view, our minds mostly need lead rather than wings — coolness and calm rather than heat and agitation. Above all, in my opinion, it is playing the fool magnificently to put on the grave airs of a lofty intellect among people who are nothing of the kind, and to speak forever by the book — "talking with the point of a fork," as the Italians say of affected speech. You must come down to the level of the people you are with, and sometimes even affect ignorance. Set aside force and subtlety in everyday conversation; keeping things decent and orderly is enough. Crawl along the ground, if that is what they want.

The learned trip over this stone constantly. They are forever parading their pedantic learning and strewing their books over everything. These days they have so stuffed the boudoirs and the ears of the ladies with it that even if the ladies have missed the substance, they have at least kept the words — so that in every discussion, on any subject however plain and ordinary, they speak and write in a new and learned style. As Juvenal says, in this language they pour out their fears, their anger, their joys, their cares, and every secret of their souls — what more? they even make love learnedly. They quote Plato and Aquinas on matters the first passerby could settle just as well. The learning that cannot reach their souls stays sitting on the tongue.

If women of quality will take my advice, they will content themselves with displaying their own natural treasures. They hide and bury their beauties under beauties that are not theirs. It is great folly to snuff out your own light so as to shine by a borrowed one — to be buried, as Seneca says, under art, painted and packaged from head to foot. The reason is that they do not know themselves well enough, or do themselves justice. The world has nothing fairer than they are; it is for them to do honor to the arts, and to put paint itself to shame. What do they need beyond living loved and honored? They have, and know, more than enough for that. All they need do is rouse and warm a little the faculties that are already their own.

When I see them dabbling in rhetoric, law, logic, and similar drugs — so useless and unnecessary for their purposes — I begin to suspect that the men who push these fancies on them do it in order to govern them by that route. What other excuse can I find? It is enough that without any instruction from us they can tune the grace of their eyes to gaiety, severity, or sweetness; season a refusal with sharpness, suspense, or favor; and need no interpreter for the speeches we make in courting them. With that knowledge they hold the whip hand and rule the schoolmasters and the schools.

But if it nevertheless galls them to yield to us in anything at all, and they want their share of books out of curiosity, then poetry is the entertainment suited to them: a wanton, subtle, sly, chattering art, all pleasure and all show, like themselves. They can also draw various profits from history. And in philosophy — the moral part of it — they can pick out the lessons that will teach them to judge our humors and our characters, to defend themselves against our treacheries, to manage the heat of their own desires, to husband their freedom, to stretch out the pleasures of life, and to bear with grace the inconstancy of a lover, the rudeness of a husband, and the nagging of years and wrinkles. That is the most I would allow them in the sciences.

Some particular natures are private and withdrawn. My own native manner is made for communication and apt to put me on display; I am all outside and in plain view, born for company and friendship. The solitude I love and recommend is mainly nothing more than drawing my thoughts and affections back into myself — restraining and reining in not my steps but my cares and desires, shedding all outside worry, and avoiding like death all servitude and obligation: fleeing not so much the crowd of men as the crowd of business. Solitude of place, to tell the truth, rather gives me more room and sets me at large. I throw myself into affairs of state and into the world more readily when I am alone. At the Louvre, in the bustle of the court, I fold up inside my own skin; the crowd presses me back into myself. I am never so wanton, so free, so much my own man in my musings as in places of dignity and ceremonious caution. Our follies do not make me laugh — our wisdom does.

I am no natural enemy of court life; I have spent part of my own life there, and my temperament takes cheerfully to great company — provided it is in doses, and at times of my choosing. But this softness of judgment I have been describing chains me, like it or not, to solitude. Even at home, in a numerous household, in a house that sees plenty of visitors, I see people enough, but rarely the people I delight in talking with. And I reserve there, for myself and for everyone else, an unusual liberty. My house has no ceremony — no ushering, no escorting guests out to their coach, none of the other tiresome rituals our code of courtesy commands (oh, that servile, nagging custom!). Everyone there runs himself by his own method. Whoever wants to may speak his thoughts; I sit silent, musing, shut up in my study, and no guest takes offense.

The men whose society and familiarity I seek out are those we call sincere and able men. The image of these men puts me off all the rest. Theirs is, rightly considered, the rarest of our forms — and a form owed chiefly to nature. The end of this kind of company is simply intimacy, frequenting one another, and conversation: the exercise of souls, with no other profit. In our talk any subject will do for me; I do not care if it has neither weight nor depth — charm and aptness are always there. Everything in it is colored by ripe, steady judgment, and mixed with goodness, openness, gaiety, and friendship. It is not only on questions of kings and state that our minds show their force and beauty; they show it every bit as much in private talk. I know my men even by their silences and their smiles, and discover them better, perhaps, at the table than in the council chamber. Hippomachus said very well that he could spot the good wrestlers just by watching them walk down the street. If learning cares to join our conversation, it will not be turned away — provided it is not magisterial, imperious, and tiresome, as it usually is, but a junior partner, and teachable itself. We are only after passing the time; when we want to be instructed and preached at, we will go find learning on its throne. Let it lower itself to us for the occasion, please; for useful and profitable as it is, I imagine that at a pinch we could manage perfectly well without it and do our business unassisted. A well-born soul, practiced in the company of men, makes herself agreeable on her own. Art is nothing but the catalogue and record of what such souls produce.

The company of beautiful and honorable women is, for me, another sweet kind of association — for, as Cicero says, we too have educated eyes. If the soul has less to enjoy here than in the first kind, the bodily senses, which have a larger share in this one, bring it up to a level close to the other — though not, in my opinion, equal to it. But it is a commerce in which a man must keep a little on his guard, especially a man in whom the body has great power, as in me. I scalded myself there in my youth, and suffered all the torments the poets say fall on men who throw themselves into love without order or judgment. It is true that the whipping has made me wiser since — like Ovid's sailor, whoever escaped the wreck on the Capharean rocks steers wide of those waters forever after.

It is folly to fix all your thoughts on it and to plunge in with furious, reckless passion. But on the other hand, to go into it without love and without desire, like actors, playing a stock part, putting in nothing of your own but the lines — that is to look after your safety, true, but in as cowardly a way as a man who would abandon his honor, his profit, or his pleasure out of fear of danger. For it is certain that the men who set up such an arrangement can expect no fruit from it that could please or satisfy a noble soul. A man must have honestly desired what he honestly expects to enjoy. I say this even when fortune unjustly favors the play-acting — which happens often, because there is not one woman in the world, be she as ugly as the devil, who does not think herself well worth loving, and who does not prefer herself to other women on the strength of her youth, the color of her hair, or her graceful walk. There are no more universally ugly women than universally beautiful ones; the Brahmin girls who have nothing else to recommend them, when the town crier has called the people together for the purpose, go down to the marketplace and display their matrimonial parts to the public, to see whether those, at least, are temptation enough to fetch them a husband. So there is not one woman who will not readily let herself be won by the first vow of devotion a man makes her.

Now from this common, everyday treachery of the men of our time, the result we already see in practice was bound to follow: either the women band together and withdraw among themselves to escape us, or else they take their schooling from the example we set, play their side of the farce as we play ours, and enter the game without passion, care, or love — answerable, as Tacitus says, neither to their own affections nor to anyone else's — believing, with Lysias in Plato, that they can give themselves to us all the more usefully and conveniently the less we love them. And so it goes as in the theater: the audience gets as much pleasure as the actors, or more.

For my part, I no more recognize a Venus without a Cupid than a mother without children: they are things that lend and owe their being to each other. So the cheat bounces back on the cheater. The game costs him next to nothing, but he gets next to nothing out of it. The people who made Venus a goddess noticed that her principal beauty was bodiless and spiritual; but the Venus these men chase is not even human — not even animal. The beasts themselves would not take it so gross and earthbound. We see that imagination and desire heat them before the body does; we see that in both sexes there is choosing and preferring within the herd, and long affectionate attachments among them. Even those whom old age has cut off from the act still tremble, whinny, and twitter with love. We see them before the act full of hope and ardor, and after the body has played its game, still savoring the sweet memory of the pleasure — some strutting with pride afterward, others, tired and satisfied, trumpeting their triumph aloud. An animal that merely needs to relieve the body of a natural necessity does not trouble others with such elaborate preparations. This is no food for a gross, coarse appetite.

Not wanting to be thought better than I am, I will say this here about the sins of my youth. Partly for the danger to health (though I was not careful enough to escape two light mishaps), and even more out of contempt, I rarely went in for paid and mercenary embraces. I wanted the pleasure sharpened by difficulty, by desire, and by a certain kind of glory. I was of the emperor Tiberius's mind, who in his loves was taken as much by modesty and good birth as by any other quality, and of the courtesan Flora's humor, who never lent herself to anyone below a dictator, a consul, or a censor, and relished the rank of her lovers. No doubt pearls and brocade, titles and retinue, add something to it.

Beyond that, I set a high price on intelligence — provided the body passed muster. For to confess the truth, if one or the other of these two attractions had to be missing, I would sooner have given up the intelligence: it has its uses in better things. But in the matter of love — a matter that mainly concerns the senses of sight and touch — something can be done without the graces of the mind; without the graces of the body, nothing. Beauty is the true prerogative of women, so peculiarly their own that ours, though it calls for somewhat different features, is only at its peak when youthful and beardless — a blurred copy of theirs. They say that those who serve the Grand Turk on the strength of their beauty, and they are an infinite number, are dismissed at twenty-two at the latest. Reason, prudence, and the offices of friendship are better found among men; that is why men run the affairs of the world.

These two kinds of company are matters of luck, and depend on other people. The first is troublesome because it is rare; the second withers with age. So they could never have been enough for the business of my life. The company of books, which is the third, is far more certain and far more our own. It concedes every other advantage to the first two, but it has for its own share the constancy and the ease of its service. It walks beside me along my whole road and helps me everywhere. It comforts me in old age and in solitude. It relieves me of the weight of a tedious idleness, and frees me, at any hour, from company I dislike. It blunts the edge of grief, as long as the grief is not extreme and has not taken full possession of my soul. To shake off a troublesome thought, I have only to run to my books: they fix me at once and steal the other thing out of my mind. And they do not mutiny at seeing that I only come to them for lack of other amusements, more real, natural, and alive; they always receive me with the same welcome.

A man may well go on foot, they say, who leads his horse by the bridle. Our James, King of Naples and Sicily — handsome, young, and healthy, yet having himself carried about the country on a stretcher, lying on a shabby mattress, in a worn robe of grey cloth with a cap to match, all the while attended by a royal retinue of litters and led horses of every kind, gentlemen and officers — was practicing an authority still tender and wobbly: the sick man has nothing to complain of who carries his cure in his sleeve. All the benefit I draw from books lies in the experience and practice of that maxim, which is a very true one. In fact, I make scarcely more actual use of them than people who have never opened one. I enjoy them the way misers enjoy their money — in knowing I can enjoy them whenever I please; my mind is satisfied with the right of possession. I never travel without books, in peace or in war, and yet I will pass days, sometimes months, without looking at them. I will read by and by, I tell myself, or tomorrow, or whenever I feel like it — and meanwhile the time slips past and does me no harm. For I cannot tell you how much I rest and settle in the thought that they are there beside me, to give me pleasure whenever I choose; they are always at my call, and I have only to remember what a refreshment they are to my life. It is the best provision I have found for this human journey, and I deeply pity any man of understanding who travels without it. I will accept any other sort of amusement, however slight, precisely because this one can never fail me.

At home I retreat a little more often to my library, from which I oversee all the workings of my household at a glance. It stands over the entrance to my house: from there I look down on my garden, my courtyard, my farmyard, and into almost every part of the building. There I leaf through one book, then another, on all sorts of subjects, without method or plan. One hour I muse; another I jot down or pace back and forth dictating these whimsies of mine that you are reading now. It is on the third floor of a tower. The ground floor is my chapel; the second floor a bedroom with a dressing room and a closet, where I often sleep when I want to be more alone; and above is a great wardrobe. In the old days it was the most useless spot in the house. I pass most of the days of my life there, and most of the hours of each day. I am never there at night. Beside it is a rather handsome study with a fireplace very conveniently placed, and plenty of light; and if I were not more afraid of the trouble than the expense — the trouble that scares me off every project — I could easily add on each side, at the same level, a gallery a hundred paces long and twelve wide, since walls built for some other purpose already stand at the right height. Every place of retreat needs a walk. My thoughts fall asleep if I sit still; my mind will not go unless my legs move it — and everyone who studies without a book is in the same condition.

The shape of my library is round, with no flat wall except what my table and chair require, so the whole curve of the circle offers me all my books at once, ranged on five tiers of shelves all around me. It has three fine, open views, and is sixteen paces across. In winter I am there less constantly, for my house sits on a hill — that is what its name says — and no part of it is more exposed to wind and weather than this. That suits me well enough: the awkward climb gives me exercise and keeps the crowd away. There is my throne. There I try to make my rule absolute, and to withdraw this one corner from all society — conjugal, filial, and civil. Everywhere else my authority is merely verbal, and muddled in substance. The man who has nowhere at home to be by himself, to pay court to himself in private, to hide — that man, in my opinion, is miserable. Ambition pays its servants the fitting wage of keeping them always on display, like a statue in the public square. As Seneca says, a great fortune is a great slavery. They cannot even be private in the privy. I have found nothing in the austere life our monks profess harder than the rule I have seen in some of their orders: perpetual company, with many persons present at everything they do whatsoever. It seems to me far more bearable to be always alone than never to be able to be.

If anyone tells me that using the Muses only as a toy and a pastime is to cheapen them, he does not know, as I do, the worth of the toy, the pleasure, and the pastime. I can hardly keep from adding that every other aim is ridiculous. I live from day to day and — with all due reverence — I live only for myself; my plans go no further. In my youth I studied to show off; later, to make myself a little wiser; now, for my amusement — never for gain. The vain, spendthrift taste I once had for this kind of furniture — books acquired not just to supply my needs but to dress the walls and make a show — I have long since cured myself of entirely.

Books have many charming qualities for those who know how to choose them. But every good has its evil. This pleasure is no purer or cleaner than the others; it has its drawbacks, and weighty ones. The soul gets its exercise there, but the body — whose care I must never neglect either — sits motionless all the while, and grows heavy and dull. I know no excess that does me more harm, or that I should avoid more carefully in these my declining years.

These, then, have been my three favorite and personal occupations. I say nothing of the ones I owe to the world by civil obligation.
