Of Experience
(Part 1 of 5)

There is no desire more natural than the desire to know. We try every road that might lead us there. When reason fails us, we fall back on experience — as Manilius says, "through trial and error, experience created art, with example showing the way" — which is a weaker and cheaper instrument. But truth is so great a thing that we should not turn up our nose at any means of reaching it.

Reason has so many shapes that we don't know which one to grab. Experience has no fewer. And the conclusions we try to draw by comparing one event with another are shaky, because no two events are ever alike. Nothing in the whole catalogue of things is so universal as diversity and variety.

The Greeks, the Romans, and we ourselves all reach for eggs when we want the perfect example of sameness. And yet there have been men — one at Delphi in particular — who could spot the differences between eggs so well that he never mistook one for another. He kept many hens, and he could tell you which one had laid which egg.

Unlikeness forces its way into our works all by itself. No art can achieve perfect similarity. Neither Perrozet nor anyone else can polish and whiten the backs of his playing cards so carefully that some gambler won't tell them apart just from watching them shuffled in another man's hands. Resemblance does not make things one so much as difference makes them other. Nature has bound herself to make nothing that is not unique.

So I am not much impressed by the fellow who thought he could rein in the authority of judges by multiplying laws — cutting their meat into little pieces for them. He didn't realize that there is just as much liberty and elbow room in interpreting laws as in writing them. And people are fooling themselves when they think they can shrink and settle our disputes by sending us back to the exact words of the Bible. The mind finds the field no less spacious when it's quarreling over someone else's meaning than when it's delivering its own — as if there were less spite and bitterness in commentary than in invention.

We can see how wrong he was. France has more laws than all the rest of the world put together — more than would be needed to govern all the worlds of Epicurus. As Tacitus says, "once we suffered from our crimes; now we suffer from our laws." And yet we have left so much to the opinions and discretion of our judges that there was never such liberty, or such license.

What have our lawmakers gained by picking out a hundred thousand particular cases and attaching a hundred thousand laws to them? That number bears no proportion at all to the infinite diversity of human actions. Multiply our inventions as we like, they will never catch up with the variety of real examples. Add a hundred times as many more: it will still never happen that some future event, out of all those millions of selected and recorded cases, will match any one of them so exactly — pair up with it so perfectly — that no detail remains to demand a different judgment. There is very little relation between our actions, which are in perpetual change, and laws, which are fixed and unmoving. The most desirable laws are the rarest, simplest, and most general. And frankly I think we would be better off with no laws at all than with the prodigious heap of them we have.

Nature always gives us better and happier laws than the ones we make for ourselves. Witness the Golden Age the poets paint, and the condition of nations we see living with no other code. Some peoples take as their only judge the first traveler passing through their mountains, and let him settle their case. Others, on market day, pick out somebody on the spot to decide their quarrels. What would be the danger if the wisest among us settled ours the same way — case by case, at sight, without being chained to precedent and consequence? For every foot its own shoe. When King Ferdinand sent colonists to the Indies, he wisely provided that no students of law should go along, for fear lawsuits would take root in that new world — law being by its very nature a breeder of quarrels and division. He judged, with Plato, that lawyers and doctors are a bad provision for a country.

Why is it that our ordinary language, so handy for every other purpose, turns dark and unintelligible in wills and contracts? Why does a man who expresses himself perfectly clearly in everything else he says or writes find no way of declaring himself there that doesn't collapse into doubt and contradiction? Unless it's that the princes of that art, applying themselves with special care to choosing solemn words and constructing artificial clauses, have weighed every syllable and sifted every kind of finicky connection so thoroughly that they are now tangled and lost in an infinity of figures and tiny subdivisions — beyond the reach of any rule, any prescription, any certain understanding. As Seneca says, whatever is chopped down to dust becomes indistinguishable.

Have you watched children trying to divide a blob of quicksilver into a set number of parts? The more they press it and work it and try to force it to their will, the more they provoke the independence of that noble metal: it slips away from them and scatters into more little beads than anyone can count. It's the same here. By subdividing these subtleties, we teach men to multiply their doubts. We set them on the path of stretching and diversifying difficulties, of lengthening them and scattering them. Sowing questions and cutting them up for retail, we make the world ripen and teem with uncertainty and quarrels — the way soil is made fertile by being crumbled and dug deep. Learning begets difficulty, as Quintilian says.

We had our doubts about Ulpian; Bartolus and Baldus have left us more perplexed than ever. We ought to erase the trail of this endless diversity of opinions, not dress ourselves up in it and stuff posterity's head with quibbles. I don't know what to say about it — but experience makes it plain that so many interpretations scatter the truth and break it to pieces. Aristotle wrote to be understood; if he couldn't manage it, a lesser man won't do better, nor a third man removed yet further from Aristotle's own thoughts. We open up the matter and spill it in pouring it out. From one subject we make a thousand, and by multiplying and subdividing we land back in Epicurus's infinity of atoms. No two men ever judged the same thing the same way; and it is impossible to find two opinions exactly alike — not just in two different men, but in the same man at different hours. I often find room for doubt in passages the commentary didn't think worth a note. Like certain horses I've known that stumble most on the smoothest road, I trip most easily on level ground.

Who can deny that glosses multiply doubt and ignorance? You will not find one book, human or divine, of all the books the world busies itself with, whose difficulties have been cleared up by interpretation. The hundredth commentator hands the text on to the next one knottier and thornier than he found it. When have we ever agreed among ourselves, "This book has had enough; there is nothing more to say about it"?

This shows most plainly in the law. We give the force of law to an infinity of doctors, an infinity of rulings, and as many interpretations. Do we find any end to the need for interpreting? Is there any visible progress toward peace? Do we need any fewer advocates and judges than when this great mass of law was still in its infancy? On the contrary — we darken and bury understanding; we can no longer find it except behind a maze of fences and barriers. Men do not recognize the mind's natural disease: it does nothing but ferret and hunt, endlessly circling, contriving, tangling itself in its own work like a silkworm — and then suffocating in it. A mouse in a pitch barrel. It thinks it spots in the distance some glimmer of light, some imaginary truth; but while it runs that way, so many difficulties and obstacles and fresh questions cut across its path that they lose it the road and leave it drunk with the motion. Not unlike Aesop's dogs, who saw something like a dead body floating far out at sea and, since they couldn't reach it, set about drinking the water dry to open a path — and choked themselves. And what Crates said of the writings of Heraclitus fits perfectly here: they needed a reader who was a good swimmer, so that the depth and weight of the learning wouldn't pull him under and drown him.

It is nothing but personal weakness that makes us settle for what others — or we ourselves — have found in this hunt after knowledge. An abler mind will not settle. There is always room for someone to go further — yes, even for ourselves — and always another road. There is no end to our inquiries; our end is in the other world. When the mind is satisfied, that is a sign it has grown shortsighted, or grown tired. No generous mind stops inside itself. It keeps pressing on past its strength; it leaps beyond what it can actually do. If it doesn't advance and push and back up and crash about and turn, it is only half alive. Its pursuits have no limit and no form. Its food is wonder, the chase, ambiguity — which Apollo showed clearly enough by always speaking to us in a double, obscure, and sideways sense: not feeding us, but keeping us busy and guessing. It is an irregular, perpetual motion, without model and without aim. Its inventions kindle one another, chase one another, breed one another. As my friend Étienne de La Boétie wrote: in a running stream you watch one wave roll after another without end, each pursuing the one ahead, each fleeing the one behind — water forever flowing into water, always the same brook, but always different water.

There is more business in interpreting the interpretations than in interpreting the things themselves, and more books about books than about any other subject. We do nothing but write comments on one another. The whole place swarms with commentaries; of authors there is a great scarcity. Isn't the chief and most celebrated learning of our age the learning of how to understand the learned? Isn't that the common and final goal of all study? Our opinions are grafted one upon another. The first serves as rootstock for the second, the second for the third, and so we climb the ladder rung by rung — which is how it happens that the man perched highest often has more honor than merit, since he has climbed only one inch up the shoulders of the man below him.

How often, and perhaps how foolishly, have I stretched my own book to make it talk about itself? Foolishly, if only because it should remind me of what I say about others who do the same thing: that all those loving glances they keep throwing at their work prove their hearts are pounding with self-love, and that even the disdainful severity with which they whip their books is just a mother's fussing and fondling in disguise — like Aristotle, in whom valuing himself and undervaluing himself often spring from the same air of arrogance. My excuse is that I ought to have more liberty here than others, since I write specifically about myself and my writings, just as I do about my other actions — my subject turns back upon itself. But I don't know whether everyone will accept the excuse.

I noticed in Germany that Luther has left behind as many quarrels and disputes over the uncertainties of his opinions as he ever raised about Holy Scripture — and more. Our argument is an argument about words. I ask what nature is, what pleasure is, what a circle is, what substitution is. The question is made of words, and it is paid in the same coin. A stone is a body. But press further — and what is a body? A substance. And what is a substance? — and so on, until you chase the answerer to the back page of his dictionary. We swap one word for another, and often for one less understood. I know better what "man" is than I know what "animal" is, or "mortal," or "rational." To resolve one doubt, they hand me three: it's the Hydra's head. Socrates asked Meno what virtue was. "There is," said Meno, "the virtue of a man and of a woman, of a magistrate and of a private citizen, of an old man and of a child." "Wonderful," cried Socrates. "We went looking for one virtue, and you've brought us a whole swarm." We ask one question and they hand us back a hive.

Just as no event and no face is exactly like another, none is entirely different either: an ingenious mixture on nature's part. If our faces were not alike, we couldn't tell man from beast; if they were not unlike, we couldn't tell one man from another. All things hold together by some resemblance; every example limps; and any rule drawn from experience is always lame and imperfect. Still, comparisons can be fastened together at one corner or another — and that is how the laws are made to serve, stretched to fit each of our affairs by some twisted, biased, forced interpretation.

Since the ethical laws — the ones that concern each person's private duty to himself — are so hard to frame, as we see they are, it's no wonder the laws that govern multitudes of people are harder still. Just look at the form of this justice that rules over us: it is a standing testimony of human weakness, it is so full of error and contradiction. What we find in justice by way of leniency here and harshness there — and we find so much of both that I doubt the middle ground turns up as often — these are diseased and unjust limbs of the very body and essence of justice.

Some peasants have just come running to tell me that a moment ago they left, in a forest of mine, a man with a hundred wounds on him, still breathing, who begged them for water for pity's sake and for help to carry him somewhere. They tell me they didn't dare go near him and ran away, for fear the officers of justice would catch them there — for, as happens to anyone found near a murdered man, they would have been called to account for the incident, to their total ruin, having neither money nor friends to defend their innocence. What could I say to these people? It is certain that this act of humanity would have gotten them into trouble.

How many innocent people have we known to be punished — and through no fault of the judge — and how many that we never heard about? Here is something that happened in my own time. Certain men were condemned to death for a murder; the sentence was, if not pronounced, at least settled and decided. At just that moment, the judges are informed by the officers of a lower court nearby that they are holding some prisoners who have flatly confessed to that murder and shed unmistakable light on every detail of the deed. And yet the court gravely deliberated whether to suspend the execution of the sentence already passed on the first defendants. They weighed the novelty of the precedent, and the consequences of reversing judgments; they noted that the sentence had been passed and the judges were beyond the reach of second thoughts. In the end, these poor devils were sacrificed to the forms of justice. Philip, or some other king, handled a similar bind better. He had condemned a man to pay another man a heavy fine, in a final judgment. The truth came out later, and he found he had judged wrongly. On one side stood the merits of the case; on the other, the claims of judicial form. He satisfied both, more or less: he let the sentence stand and made up the condemned man's loss from his own purse. But he was dealing with a fixable wrong. My men were unfixably hanged. How many condemnations have I seen that were more criminal than the crime?

All this brings to mind the old opinions: that a man is forced to do wrong in the small change if he wants to do right in the large, and to commit injustice in little things if he wants to achieve justice in great ones; that human justice is built on the model of medicine, by which whatever works is also just and honest; that — as the Stoics held — Nature herself acts against justice in most of her own works; and — as the Cyrenaics held — that nothing is just in itself, but customs and laws make justice; and — as the Theodorians had it — that theft, sacrilege, and every kind of debauchery are just in a wise man, if he knows they will profit him.

There's no help for it: I stand where Alcibiades stood. I will never, if I can help it, put myself into the hands of a man who can decide about my head — where my life and honor depend more on the skill and diligence of my attorney than on my own innocence. I'd take my chances with a justice that would take note of my good deeds as well as my bad, where I had as much to hope for as to fear. Not being punished is poor pay for a man who has done better than merely not doing wrong. Our justice offers us only one of her hands, and the left one at that. Whoever you are, you come away with a loss.

In China — a kingdom whose government and arts, without any commerce with ours or knowledge of ours, outdo our best examples in several ways, and whose history teaches me how much bigger and more various the world is than either the ancients or we have managed to grasp — the officers sent by the prince to inspect the state of his provinces punish those who have abused their office, but they also generously reward those who have conducted themselves better than the run of men, beyond the bare requirements of duty. People come before these inspectors not just to be cleared but to gain something; not merely to be paid, but to be given a present.

No judge, thank God, has ever yet addressed me as a judge, in any cause whatever, my own or a third party's, criminal or civil. No prison has ever received me, even for a stroll. Imagination makes the very outside of a jail unpleasant to me. I am so in love with liberty that if someone forbade me access to one corner of the Indies, I would live a little less at ease for it. And as long as I can find earth or open air anywhere else, I will never sit hiding in a place where I have to hide. My God, how badly I would bear the condition I see so many people in — nailed to one corner of the kingdom, barred from the principal cities and the courts and the use of the public roads, for having quarreled with our laws! If the laws I live under so much as wagged a finger at me in threat, I would immediately go find others, wherever they might be. All my little prudence in these civil wars of ours goes into making sure they don't cut off my freedom to come and go.

Now, laws keep up their credit not because they are just, but because they are laws. That is the mystic foundation of their authority; they have no other — and it serves them well enough. They are often made by fools; more often by men who, hating equality, fail in equity; but always by men — vain and wavering authors. Nothing is so grossly, so widely, or so regularly faulty as the laws. Whoever obeys them because they are just does not obey them for the reason he ought. Our French laws, by their irregularity and deformity, rather lend a hand to the disorder and corruption that show up in their application and enforcement. The command is so confused and inconsistent that it goes some way toward excusing both disobedience and the faults of those who interpret, administer, and observe it.

Whatever fruit we may pluck from experience, the kind we draw from foreign examples will do little for our education if we make so poor a use of the experience we have of ourselves — which is more familiar to us, and certainly sufficient to teach us what we need. I study myself more than any other subject. That is my metaphysics; that is my physics. Let others ask what god steers this great house of the world, where the moon goes when it wanes and how its horns fill out again, where the winds get their power over the sea, where the clouds find their endless supply of water, and whether a day is coming that will bring the world's towers down — as Propertius wonders. "Ask that," says Lucan, "you whom the cares of the world torment."

In that great universal scheme, I let myself be carried along, ignorantly and carelessly, by the general law of the world. I will know that law well enough when I feel it. My learning cannot make it change course; it will not alter itself for my sake. It is folly to hope it might, and greater folly to fret about it, since the law is necessarily the same for everyone, public and common. The goodness and capacity of the governor ought to release us, completely, from worrying about the government. Philosophical probings and speculations serve no purpose but to feed our curiosity. The philosophers, quite rightly, refer us back to the rules of nature; but those rules have no use for such exalted knowledge. The philosophers falsify them, showing us nature's face painted in colors too high and too artificial — which is why we get so many different portraits of so uniform a subject. As nature has given us feet to walk with, so she has given us prudence to guide us through life: not the clever, muscular, showy prudence of the philosophers' invention, but a prudence that is easy, quiet, and healthy, and that does very well what the other only promises — in anyone lucky enough to know how to use it honestly and steadily, which is to say, naturally. To entrust yourself to nature most simply is to entrust yourself most wisely. Oh, what a soft, easy, wholesome pillow is ignorance and incuriosity, for resting a well-made head!

I would rather understand myself well in myself than in Cicero. In the experience I have of myself I find enough to make me wise — if only I were a good student. Whoever calls to mind the last excess of his anger, and how far that fever carried him, sees the ugliness of the passion better than in Aristotle, and hates it more justly. Whoever remembers the troubles he has been through, and the ones that merely threatened him, and the trivial causes that have moved him from one state to another, prepares himself by it for the changes to come and for knowing his own condition. The life of Caesar holds no greater lesson for us than our own. Emperor's life or common man's, it is still a life subject to every human accident. Let us only listen to it: we tell ourselves everything we chiefly need to hear.

A man who remembers how many, many times he has been wrong in his own judgment — isn't he a fool if he doesn't distrust it forever after? When someone else's reasoning convinces me I've held a false opinion, what I learn isn't so much the new thing he's told me, or my ignorance of that particular point — that would be a small purchase. I learn, in general, my own weakness and the treachery of my understanding — and from that I draw the reformation of the whole mass. With all my other errors I do the same, and I find the rule of great use in life. I don't treat the species or the instance as one stone I happened to trip over; I learn to distrust my footing everywhere, and I take care to plant it well. To learn that you have said or done a foolish thing is nothing; you must learn that you are nothing but a fool — a far broader and more important lesson.

The false steps my memory has so often made, even when it was most sure of itself, are not wasted on me. Let it swear and promise now till it's blue; I shake my ears. The first objection raised against its testimony throws me into doubt, and I wouldn't dare trust it in anything serious, or vouch for it in anyone else's business. And if it weren't that what I do from lack of memory, others do still more often from lack of good faith, I would always, in matters of fact, take the truth from another man's mouth rather than my own.

If everyone would watch closely the workings and the circumstances of the passions that rule him, as I have watched the ones I'm most prone to, he would see them coming and would slow their charge a little. They don't always leap at our throat without warning; there are threats first, and stages — as Virgil says, the sea first whitens under the wind, then swells little by little, lifting its waves higher, until it rises from the bottom of the deep to the sky.

In me, judgment occupies the magistrate's bench — or at least it tries hard to. It lets my appetites run their own course, including hatred and friendship — even the friendship I bear myself — without altering or corrupting them. If it can't reform the other parts to match its own model, at least it doesn't let itself be corrupted by them. It plays its game apart.

The advice to each of us to know ourselves must be important advice, since the god of wisdom and light had it written across the front of his temple at Delphi, as if it contained everything he had to tell us. Plato says, too, that prudence is nothing but the carrying out of that command, and Socrates proves it point by point in Xenophon. In any science, only those who have entered into it can see its difficulties and its dark places, for it takes a certain degree of intelligence to be able to know that you don't know — you must push on a door to find out whether it's bolted against you. Hence the Platonic subtlety: that those who know need not inquire, since they know; and those who don't know cannot inquire, since to inquire you must know what you're inquiring after. So with this matter of knowing yourself. The fact that everyone is seen to be so decided and so satisfied on the point, that everyone thinks he understands himself well enough, shows that no one understands anything about it — as Socrates teaches Euthydemus.

I, who profess nothing else, find in myself a depth and a variety so endless that all the fruit of my learning is to make me feel how much remains to learn. To my weakness, so often confessed, I owe my leaning toward modesty, toward obedience to the beliefs prescribed for me, toward a steady coolness and moderation in my opinions — and my hatred of that obnoxious, quarrelsome arrogance that believes and trusts wholly in itself, the capital enemy of discipline and truth.
