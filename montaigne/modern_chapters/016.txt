Of Democritus and Heraclitus

Judgment is a tool that works on any subject, and it pokes its nose into everything. That's why, in these essays of mine, I grab whatever occasion comes along. If it's a subject I don't understand at all, so much the better — I sound the ford from a good distance, and if I find it too deep for my height, I stay on the bank. Knowing you can go no further is itself an act of judgment, and one of the acts it's proudest of.

Sometimes I take an empty, trivial subject and see whether I can find anything in it to build a body out of, something to prop up and support. Sometimes I wander into a noble subject, one that's been worked over by a thousand hands, where nothing new can be said and the road is so beaten on every side that you have no choice but to walk in someone else's footsteps. There, judgment's job is to pick the route that seems best — to decide, out of a thousand paths, that this one or that one is the right choice.

I take the first argument fortune offers me. They're all equally good to me, and I never plan to cover any of them completely — because I never see the whole of anything. Neither do the people who promise so confidently to show it to us. Of the hundred limbs and faces every subject has, I take one. Sometimes I just glance it over; sometimes I lift the skin a little; sometimes I pinch it to the bone. I jab in — not as wide as I can, but as deep as I can — and most of the time I'm tempted to take a subject up by some new light I've found glinting in it.

If I knew myself less well, I might risk handling some topic to the bottom and discover my inability the hard way. Instead I scatter a word here, a word there — samples snipped from various pieces of cloth, strewn around without any design and without committing myself too far. I'm not answerable for them, and not obliged to stick to my subject. I can vary it whenever I like, and surrender to doubt and uncertainty — and to my own ruling method, ignorance.

Every motion reveals us. The same soul of Caesar that showed itself so brilliantly in marshaling the battle of Pharsalia also showed itself in arranging his idle hours and his love affairs. You judge a horse not only by watching it show off its paces, but by its ordinary walk — even by seeing it standing in the stable.

Among the soul's functions, some are low and humble. Whoever does not see her in those small offices as well as the grand ones never sees her whole — and she may show best moving at her plain, everyday pace. The winds of passion catch her most strongly at her highest altitudes. Besides, she throws herself entirely into each subject she touches, handles only one thing at a time — and handles it not according to what it is, but according to what she is.

Things in themselves may have their own weights, measures, and qualities. But once inside us, the soul cuts them to fit her own taste. Death is terrifying to Cicero, desirable to Cato, a matter of indifference to Socrates. Health, conscience, authority, knowledge, riches, beauty, and their opposites — all of them strip off at the door when they enter us, and receive a new robe from the soul, in whatever color she chooses: brown, bright, green, dark; and whatever quality: sharp, sweet, deep, or shallow. Each soul decides for herself, since none of them have agreed on any common standard of rules or procedures — each one is queen in her own realm.

So let us stop blaming the external qualities of things. The reckoning is ours to give. Our good and our ill depend on no one but ourselves. That is where our offerings and our vows belong — not to fortune, who has no power over our character. On the contrary: our character drags fortune along in its train and casts her in its own mold.

So why shouldn't I judge Alexander at the dinner table, ranting and drinking at the prodigious rate he sometimes did? Or over a chess board? What string of his soul does that idle, childish game leave untouched? I hate it and avoid it myself, precisely because it isn't enough of a game — it's too grave and serious a pastime, and I'm ashamed to spend on it an amount of attention that would serve for better things. Alexander didn't rack his brains harder over his glorious expedition into India; another man sweats no harder untangling a problem on which the salvation of mankind depends. Look how this ridiculous game torments the soul, all her faculties summoned for such a trivial cause — and what a fine chance it gives everyone to know himself and judge himself rightly. There is no posture in which I sift myself more thoroughly. What passion doesn't it stir up? Anger, spite, hatred, impatience, and a burning ambition to win — in a contest where the more honorable ambition would be to lose. Because excelling, standing out above the common run in frivolous things, hardly suits a man of honor.

What I say of this example can be said of all the others. Every particle of a man, every occupation, reveals him just as well as any other.

Democritus and Heraclitus were two philosophers. The first, finding the human condition ridiculous and vain, never went out in public except with a mocking, laughing face. Heraclitus, pitying that same condition of ours, wore a perpetually sad expression and tears in his eyes. As Juvenal puts it, the one laughed every time he set foot outside his door; the other wept.

I prefer the first temper — not because laughing is more pleasant than weeping, but because it is more disdainful, and condemns us more than the other does; and it seems to me we can never be despised as much as we deserve. Pity and lamentation imply that the thing lamented has some value. What we laugh at, we declare worthless. I do not think there is so much unhappiness in us as vanity; we are not so much wicked as silly; we are not so full of evil as of emptiness; we are not so miserable as we are cheap.

That's why Diogenes — who passed his time rolling around in his tub and thumbing his nose at the great Alexander, rating us no higher than flies or bladders puffed with air — was a sharper, more penetrating judge, and therefore in my opinion a fairer one, than Timon, the one they called the Man-hater. Because whatever a man hates, he takes to heart. Timon wished us ill; he passionately desired our ruin; he shunned our company as dangerous, the company of wicked and depraved creatures. Diogenes valued us so little that contact with us could neither disturb him nor infect him. He dropped our company not from fear, but from contempt — he figured we were incapable of doing either good or harm.

In the same key was Statilius's answer when Brutus invited him into the conspiracy against Caesar. He agreed the enterprise was just; he just didn't think mankind was worth a wise man's trouble. Which matches the teaching of Hegesias, who said a wise man should do nothing except for himself, since no one else is worth doing anything for; and the saying of Theodorus, that it isn't reasonable for a wise man to risk himself for his country — to endanger wisdom for the sake of fools.

Our condition is as laughable as it is laughed at.
