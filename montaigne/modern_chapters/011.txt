Of the Education of Children
(Part 4 of 4)

Horace describes a writer of this kind: a man with a fine nose for things but rough at building verses. Take such a work, Horace says, and strip away the rhythm and meter — put the first word last and the last word first — and you will still find the scattered limbs of a poet. The pieces will be beautiful all by themselves. That is what Menander meant when a friend scolded him: the day was approaching when he had promised a comedy, and he hadn't even started it. "It's finished and ready," Menander said. "All but the verses." Having worked out the subject and arranged the scenes in his head, he counted the rest as nothing.

Since Ronsard and Du Bellay raised the reputation of our French poetry, every little dabbler, as far as I can see, puffs up his words as grandly and rolls out his cadences almost as harmoniously as they do. More sound than sense, as Seneca says. For the common run of writers, there have never been so many poetasters as there are now. They find it easy enough to copy the rhymes; what they fall infinitely short of copying is the rich descriptions of the one master and the delicate invention of the other.

But what will become of our young gentleman if someone attacks him with the sophistic subtlety of a syllogism? "A Westphalia ham makes a man drink; drinking quenches thirst; therefore a Westphalia ham quenches thirst." Why, let him laugh at it. Laughing is wiser than trying to answer it. Or let him borrow that pleasant dodge of Aristippus: "Why should I bother to untie it, when it gives me this much trouble tied?" When someone tried this dialectical juggling on Cleanthes, Chrysippus cut him short: "Save those toys for playing with children, and stop distracting the serious thoughts of a grown man with such foolery." If these absurd subtleties — twisted and prickly sophisms, Cicero calls them — are designed to plant a falsehood in him, they are dangerous. But if they amount to nothing more than making him laugh, I don't see why a man needs to be fortified against them.

Some writers are so ridiculous they will go a mile out of their way to chase down a fine word — the kind, as Quintilian says, who don't fit words to their subject, but go hunting for subjects to fit their words. And as Seneca puts it, the kind who are lured by some pretty-sounding phrase into writing about something they never meant to touch. For my own part, I would rather drag in a good sentence by the head and shoulders to serve my purpose than bend my purpose to go hunting for a sentence. Words exist to serve; they should follow a man's meaning — and let Gascon step in where French won't do the job. I want the matter to be so compelling, so completely possessing the imagination of the listener, that he has no attention left over for the words.

The way of speaking I love is natural and plain, the same on paper as in the mouth — a sinewy, muscular way of expressing yourself, short and pithy; not so much elegant and polished as prompt and forceful. The speech with real savor, says an old epitaph, is the speech that strikes home. Rather hard than tiresome; free of affectation; irregular, loose-jointed, and bold, where every piece stands as a body of its own. Not the style of a pedant, a preacher, or a lawyer, but rather a soldier's style — which is what Suetonius calls Julius Caesar's, though I confess I can't see why he calls it that.

I have always been ready to imitate that careless dress you still see among the young men of our day — cloak slung over one shoulder, cap tilted to one side, a stocking sagging — which expresses a kind of proud disdain for foreign frills and a contempt for the artificial. But I find that same carelessness even better employed in a way of speaking. All affectation is ungraceful in a courtier, especially in our French gaiety and freedom; and in a monarchy every gentleman ought to be shaped to the court's model. So an easy, natural negligence wears well. I no more like a fabric where you can see every knot and seam than a fine figure so thin you can count the bones and veins. Language in the service of truth, says Seneca, should be plain and unstudied; and again: who speaks with elaborate care, unless he wants to sound affected?

Eloquence that draws all the attention to itself cheats the very subject it was meant to advance. Just as it is a ridiculous vanity in dress to make ourselves conspicuous by some private, peculiar fashion, so in language the hunt for novel phrases and unheard-of words comes from a childish, schoolmasterish ambition. Let me be bound to use no other words than the ones spoken in the markets of Paris! Aristophanes the grammarian completely missed the point when he criticized Epicurus for his plain way of speaking, since the whole aim of his oratory was simply to be understood.

The imitation of words is so easy that a whole people picks it up at once; the imitation of inventing, and of applying words fitly, comes much more slowly. Most readers, finding two robes alike, mistakenly believe the bodies inside are alike too. But force and sinew cannot be borrowed; only the gloss and the outward finery — the words and delivery — can. Most of the people I talk with speak the same language I am writing here; whether they think the same thoughts, I couldn't say.

The Athenians, Plato says, cultivate fullness and elegance of speech, the Spartans brevity, and the Cretans fertility of thought rather than fertility of language — and the Cretans have it right. Zeno used to say he had two sorts of students: one sort curious to learn things — these were his favorites — and the other caring for nothing but words. Not that fine speaking isn't a good and admirable quality. But it is not so excellent or so necessary as some make it out to be, and it scandalizes me that we should spend our whole lives on nothing else. I would first want to understand my own language, and the language of my neighbors, with whom I do most of my business and most of my talking.

Greek and Latin are great ornaments, no doubt, and of great use. But we buy them too dear. I will tell you here of one way to get them cheaper — a way tested on my own person — and whoever likes may use it. My late father made the most careful inquiry a man could make, among men of the greatest learning and judgment, after an exact method of education. They warned him about the drawback of the usual practice: they had him believe that the tedious years we spend learning languages that cost the ancients nothing were the sole reason we could never reach the grandeur of soul and the perfection of knowledge of the old Greeks and Romans. I don't believe that's the only reason. Still, the expedient my father hit upon was this. In my infancy, before I could speak at all, he put me in the care of a German — a man who later died a famous physician in France — who knew not a word of our language and was fluent and expert in Latin. This man, whom my father had brought from his own country and kept on a great salary for this one purpose, had me constantly in his hands. Two others of lesser learning were joined to him, to attend me and relieve him; and these spoke to me in no language but Latin.

As for the rest of the household, it was an unbreakable rule that neither my father himself, nor my mother, nor manservant, nor chambermaid would speak anything in my presence except whatever scraps of Latin each had managed to learn in order to chatter with me. It is hard to imagine how much the whole family got out of this. My father and mother picked up enough Latin to understand it perfectly well, and to speak it as much as ordinary needs required; so did the servants who were most about me. In short, we Latined it at such a rate that the language spilled over into the neighboring villages, where to this day you can find Latin names for craftsmen and their tools, planted there by custom.

As for me, I was more than six years old before I understood French or Périgordin any better than Arabic. And without art, book, grammar, or rule — without the whip, and without the cost of a single tear — I had learned by then to speak Latin as pure as my master's own, for I had no other language to mix in with it. If, for instance, they wanted to give me a written exercise in the college manner, where other boys received it in French, mine had to be given in bad Latin, for me to turn into good. And Nicolas Grouchy, who wrote a book on Roman assemblies; Guillaume Guerente, who wrote a commentary on Aristotle; George Buchanan, that great Scottish poet; and Marc Antoine Muret, whom both France and Italy acknowledged as the best orator of his time — all of them my private tutors — often told me afterward that as a small child I had that language so fluent and ready that they were afraid to strike up a conversation with me. Buchanan in particular, whom I later saw in the suite of the late Marshal de Brissac, told me he was planning to write a treatise on education and meant to take his model from mine; he was then tutor to that Comte de Brissac who later proved so valiant and gallant a gentleman.

As for Greek, of which I have barely a smattering, my father planned to have me taught that too by a device — but a new one, by way of sport, tossing declensions back and forth like those games of counters by which people learn geometry and arithmetic. For among the other advice he had received, he had been told to make me relish learning and duty by my own free will and desire, and to bring up my soul in complete liberty and delight, without severity or constraint. And he observed this to the point of superstition, if I may put it that way. Some people hold that it troubles the tender brains of children to be jerked suddenly and violently out of sleep in the morning — sleep being a thing they are sunk in far more deeply than we are — so my father had me wakened every day by the sound of a musical instrument, and was never without a musician for the purpose. This one example will let you judge the rest, and it is enough by itself to commend both the prudence and the affection of so good a father — who is in no way to blame if he harvested no fruit answering to so exquisite a cultivation.

Two things were the cause of that. First, a sterile and unsuitable soil. Though I had a strong, healthy constitution and a disposition gentle and tractable enough, I was at the same time so heavy, slack, and drowsy that they couldn't pull me out of my idleness even to play. What I saw, I saw clearly enough; and underneath that sluggish temperament I nursed a bold imagination, and opinions beyond my years. But my mind was slow, and would move no faster than it was led; my understanding tardy, my invention limp; and on top of everything, an incredible lack of memory. No wonder nothing much could be extracted from all that. And second: like those people who, impatient with a long and steady cure, will try every prescription and remedy going, the good man — terribly afraid of failing in a thing he had set his whole heart on — finally let himself be overruled by the common opinion, which always trails after its leader like a flight of cranes. He fell in with the custom of the day, no longer having around him the men from Italy who had given him his first model of education, and sent me at six years old to the College of Guienne, then the best and most flourishing in France. And there, nothing could be added to the care he took — choosing me the ablest private tutors, securing every other advantage, reserving several special arrangements contrary to the usual college practice. But for all these precautions, it was a college still. My Latin promptly went bad, and since then, through disuse, I have lost all command of it. All that this novel education did for me was to vault me, on arrival, into the upper forms. By thirteen, when I left the college, I had finished my whole course, as they call it — and in truth with nothing to show for it that I can honestly brag of now.

My first taste for books came from the pleasure of reading the fables of Ovid's Metamorphoses. At about seven or eight years old I gave up every other amusement to read them — partly because that language was my own mother tongue, and it was the easiest book I knew, and by its subject the best suited to my age. As for Lancelot of the Lake, Amadis of Gaul, Huon of Bordeaux, and that whole heap of trash that children love best, I had never so much as heard their names — and to this day I don't know what's in them — so strict was the discipline I was raised under. But Ovid was enough to make me neglect my assigned lessons. And here I had the enormous luck of dealing with an understanding tutor, who knew how to look the other way, discreetly, at this and other truancies of the same kind. By that means I ran through Virgil's Aeneid, then Terence, then Plautus, then some Italian comedies, drawn on by the sweetness of the subject. If he had been fool enough to break me of this diversion, I truly believe I would have carried nothing away from school but a hatred of books, as almost all our young gentlemen do. He managed the business cleverly — pretending to see nothing, and sharpening my appetite by letting me devour those books only in the time I could steal from my regular studies. For the chief qualities my father wanted from the men he had entrusted with my education were good nature and good humor; and to tell the truth, my character had no vices in it but sloth and want of mettle. The danger was not that I would do ill, but that I would do nothing. Nobody predicted I would turn out wicked — only useless. They foresaw idleness, not malice.

And so it has turned out. The complaints I hear about myself run like this: "He is idle; cold in the duties of friendship and kinship, and in public duties; too private; too disdainful." Even the most hostile do not say, "Why did he take that? Why didn't he pay this man?" — but, "Why won't he part with anything? Why doesn't he give?" I would take it as a favor if people expected from me no extra works of generosity beyond those. But they are unjust: they demand from me what I do not owe far more rigorously than they demand from others what they do owe. In condemning me to it ahead of time, they wipe out the pleasure of the act and rob me of the gratitude that would be its proper reward — whereas any good deed from my hands ought to count for more, precisely because I have never received any. I can dispose the more freely of my fortune the more it is mine, and of myself the more I am my own. All the same, if I were any good at publishing my own actions, I could perhaps answer these reproaches very well — and could give certain people to understand that they are not so much offended that I do too little, as that I could do a great deal more than I do.

Yet for all this heavy disposition of mine, my mind, when it withdrew into itself, was not without strong movements, and sound, clear judgments about whatever objects it could reach — and it could digest them alone, without any help. And among other things, I genuinely believe it would have been completely impossible to make it submit to violence and force. Shall I tell you about one faculty of my youth? I had great confidence of face, and flexibility of voice and gesture, in throwing myself into whatever part I undertook to act. Before I had well entered my twelfth year — as Virgil puts it — I was playing the leading roles in the Latin tragedies of Buchanan, Guerente, and Muret, which were staged with great dignity in our College of Guienne. Our principal, Andreas Goveanus, was in this, as in every other part of his office, beyond comparison the best in France; and I was reckoned one of the best actors.

It is an exercise I see nothing wrong with in young people of good family. I have since watched our princes, following the example of some of the ancients, perform in person, handsomely and creditably; in Greece it was even allowed to persons of quality to make a profession of it. Livy tells how a secret was entrusted to Aristo the tragedian — a man of good family and fortune, neither of which was tarnished by his profession, since nothing of the kind was held a disgrace among the Greeks.

I have always called it impertinence to condemn these entertainments, and injustice to bar comedians worth seeing from our good towns, grudging the people that public pleasure. Well-governed states take care to assemble their citizens not only for the solemn duties of devotion but for sports and spectacles too. Fellowship and friendship grow from it. And besides, what diversion could be more orderly and regular than one performed in full view of everyone, often in the presence of the magistrate himself? For my part, I think it reasonable that a prince should sometimes treat his people at his own expense, out of a kind of fatherly goodness and affection; and that in populous cities there should be theaters built for such entertainments — if only to draw people away from worse and private doings.

To come back to my subject: there is nothing like coaxing the appetite and the affections. Otherwise all you produce is so many donkeys loaded down with books. By dint of the whip, you hand them their pocketful of learning to carry. But to do the job properly, learning shouldn't merely be lodged with them; it should be married to them.
