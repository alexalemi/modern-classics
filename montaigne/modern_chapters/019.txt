That Our Actions Are Inconsistent

Those who make it their business to study human behavior never run into anything that stumps them more than fitting our actions together into one consistent picture. Our deeds contradict one another so strangely that it seems impossible they came from the same person. The younger Marius shows up sometimes as a son of Mars, sometimes as a son of Venus. Pope Boniface VIII, they say, came into his office like a fox, ruled in it like a lion, and died like a dog. And who would believe it was Nero — the very portrait of cruelty — who, when the death warrant of a condemned man was brought to him to sign, as the custom was, cried out, "I wish I had never learned to write!" That is how much it pained him to send a man to his death.

History is full of such examples, and every man can supply so many from his own experience that I sometimes wonder at intelligent people taking the trouble to sort these pieces into a matching set — since indecision strikes me as the most common and obvious defect of our nature. Remember the famous line of the comic player Publius: "A plan that cannot be changed is a bad plan."

There is some sense in judging a man by the general run of his life. But given how naturally unstable our habits and opinions are, I've often thought that even the best authors are a little off in stubbornly trying to weave us into one solid, consistent fabric. They settle on a general image of a man and then interpret all his actions to fit it — and whatever they can't bend into uniformity, they write off as play-acting. Augustus has escaped them: there was such an obvious, sudden, and continual variety in everything he did, his whole life long, that even the boldest critics have had to let him slip away whole and unjudged. Constancy is the virtue I find hardest of all to believe in, and inconstancy the easiest. Whoever judges a man in detail, piece by piece, will hit the truth more often.

In all of antiquity it's hard to pick out a dozen men who set their lives on one fixed and certain course — which is the main point of wisdom. To sum it all up in a word, says one of the ancients, and to gather every rule of human life into one: it is to will, and not to will, always the same thing. I won't bother to add, he says, "provided the will is just" — because if it isn't just, it cannot possibly stay the same. I learned long ago that vice is nothing but disorder and lack of measure, and so constancy can never be fastened to it. Demosthenes is supposed to have said that the beginning of all virtue is reflection and deliberation — and its end and perfection, constancy. If we chose our course by reason, we would choose the best one; but nobody has thought of it. As Horace says, "what he sought, he scorns; what he just let go, he chases again. He seethes, and his whole life is out of order."

Our usual practice is to follow the pull of our appetite — left, right, up, down, wherever the breeze of occasion carries us. We never think about what we want until the instant we want it; and we change like that little creature that takes its color from whatever you set it on. What we decided a moment ago we immediately reverse, and then reverse again. It is all swaying and inconsistency. As Horace says, we are wooden puppets, moved by strings in other people's hands.

We do not go; we are carried — like floating things, drifting now gently, now violently, as the current runs calm or rough. As Lucretius asks, don't we see them all, not knowing what they want, forever seeking a change of place, as if that could set down the load? Every day a new whim, and our moods move with the weather — as Cicero has it, "men's minds are like the light that father Jupiter sends across the fruitful earth."

We float among our various leanings. We will nothing freely, nothing absolutely, nothing constantly. If a man had fixed laws and a settled plan of conduct in his head, we would see an evenness shine through his whole life — an order, an unfailing fit of each action to the next. Empedocles spotted the flaw in the people of Agrigentum: they threw themselves into pleasures as if every day were their last, and built as if they were going to live forever. The younger Cato is easy to judge: strike one note and you have the whole instrument — a harmony of perfectly tuned sounds that cannot clash. But with us it's the opposite: every single action needs its own separate judgment. The surest way, in my opinion, is to judge each act by the circumstances closest around it, without digging any further and without drawing any larger conclusion from it.

During the civil disorders of our poor kingdom, I was told that a young woman, close by where I was then staying, had thrown herself out of a window to escape the advances of a soldier quartered in her house. The fall didn't kill her, so she tried again, and would have cut her own throat if she hadn't been stopped — though not before wounding herself badly enough to show she meant it. And then she freely admitted that the soldier had so far pressed her with nothing worse than courtship, persistence, and presents — she was only afraid he would come to force in the end. And all this she said with such a look, such a tone, and her own blood still on her to vouch for her virtue, that she seemed a second Lucretia. Yet I have since learned, on good authority, that both before and after, she drove no such hard bargain. As the host says in Ariosto's tale: be as handsome a man and as fine a gentleman as you please, but don't conclude too much about your lady's unshakable chastity just because you've been turned down. It may only mean the muleteer suits her better.

Antigonus had taken one of his soldiers into high favor for his courage, and ordered his physicians to cure the man of a deep, lingering disease that had tormented him for years. After the cure, Antigonus noticed him going about his fighting far more coolly than before, and asked what had so tamed him. "You did, sir," the soldier answered, "by relieving me of the pains that made my life not worth keeping." A soldier of Lucullus had been stripped of everything by the enemy, and in revenge pulled off a magnificent stroke against them that left him richer than before. Lucullus, who had formed a high opinion of him from that exploit, set about recruiting him for some extremely dangerous mission, with all the handsome speeches and promises he could think of — the kind of words, as Horace says, that might put heart into a coward. "Send some poor soldier who's been plundered," the man answered — for, as Horace has it, "the fellow who has lost his purse will go wherever you like" — and he flatly refused to go.

We read that Mehmed furiously berated Hassan, the commander of his Janissaries, for letting the Hungarians break into his squadrons and for fighting feebly himself. Hassan's only answer was to charge alone, scimitar in hand, into the first body of enemies he met, where he was promptly cut to pieces. That was perhaps not so much vindication as a swing of mood — not natural bravery so much as a flash of wounded pride. The man you saw yesterday so daring and bold — don't be surprised to find him just as great a coward the day after. Anger, necessity, company, wine, or the sound of a trumpet had set his blood up. That is not courage formed and settled by reason; it is courage thrown together by circumstances — and it's no wonder if different circumstances turn it into something else entirely.

These supple shifts and contradictions in us have led some to imagine that we have two souls, and others that two powers attend us, each pulling its own way — one toward good, the other toward ill — since such jerky variety could never, they think, flow from a single source.

As for me, every gust of every accident not only carries me along with it; I also unsettle and trouble myself by the sheer instability of my own posture. Whoever looks closely into himself will hardly ever find himself twice in the same state. I give my soul one face or another, depending on which side I turn her to. If I speak of myself in different ways, it's because I see myself in different ways. Every contradiction can be found in me, in some corner or other, in some form or other: bashful, insolent; chaste, lustful; talkative, silent; hardworking, delicate; clever, dull; gloomy, cheerful; lying, truthful; learned, ignorant; generous, greedy, and wasteful. I find all of it in myself, more or less, depending on which way I turn. And whoever sifts himself to the bottom will find this same flux and discord in himself — even in his own judgment. I have nothing to say about myself that is whole, simple, and solid, without mixture and confusion. Distinguo — "I draw a distinction" — is the most universal clause in my logic.

Though I always mean to speak well of what's good, and to put the better interpretation on things rather than the worse, still our condition is so strange that vice itself often pushes us into doing good — if doing good were judged by anything but intention. So one gallant deed should not prove a man brave. If he were truly brave, he would be brave always, on every occasion. If his courage were a habit and not a single outburst, it would make him equally steady in every emergency: the same alone as in company, the same in the tournament lists as on the battlefield. For say what they will, there is not one courage for the street and another for the field. He would bear an illness in his bed as bravely as a wound in battle, and fear death no more in his own house than in an assault. We would not see the same man charge into a breach with superb confidence and then torment himself over a lost lawsuit or the death of a child. When a man who is a notorious coward stands firm under poverty — when he flinches at the barber's razor and yet runs head-on at enemy swords — the action deserves praise; the man does not.

Many Greeks, says Cicero, cannot bear the sight of an enemy yet endure sickness with courage; the Cimbrians and Celtiberians are exactly the reverse — for, as he adds, nothing can be steady that does not spring from a fixed principle.

No valor was more extreme in its kind than Alexander's; but even his is of one kind only — not full enough at every point, not universal. Incomparable as it is, it has its blemishes. We see him losing his head at every faint suspicion that his captains were plotting against his life, and throwing himself into those investigations with a violence and reckless injustice, and a fear, that overturned his natural reason. The superstition that ran so deep in him carries its own trace of cowardice. And his extravagant remorse over the murder of Clytus is one more mark of how unevenly his courage ran.

Everything we do is a patchwork of separate pieces, and we want to win honor under a false flag. Virtue can only be followed for her own sake; if we sometimes borrow her mask for some other purpose, she promptly snatches it back off our face. Virtue is a vivid, fast dye: once the soul is truly steeped in it, it will not come out except with the cloth itself. So to judge a man rightly, we must follow his track a long way and watch it closely. If constancy does not stand firm there on its own foundation — "if the path of his life has been thought through and laid out in advance," as Cicero puts it — if the variety of events makes him change his pace (his path, I mean; the pace may run faster or slower), then let him go: that man runs wherever the wind blows, as the motto of our Talbot has it.

It's no wonder, says one of the ancients, that chance has such power over us, since we live by chance. Whoever has not aimed his life at some definite goal cannot possibly arrange the individual acts. Whoever does not carry the whole shape in his head cannot fit the pieces together. What good is a supply of paints to a man who doesn't know what he means to paint? No one draws up a definite plan for his life; we decide it only piece by piece. The archer must first know what he is aiming at, and then fit his hand, bow, string, arrow, and movement to the target. Our plans go astray because they have no address and no destination. No wind works for the sailor who has no port in mind.

I can't accept the verdict given in favor of Sophocles, when the reading of one of his tragedies was taken as proof — against his own son's accusation — that he was fit to manage his household affairs. Nor do I think the Parians, sent to straighten out the Milesians, drew a sound conclusion from what they saw. Visiting the island, they took note of the best-tended fields and the best-run country houses, wrote down the owners' names, called an assembly of the citizens, and appointed those farmers the new governors and magistrates — reasoning that men so careful of their private business would be just as careful of the public's.

We are all patchwork, and so shapeless and diverse in composition that every piece plays its own game every moment. And there is as much difference between us and ourselves as between us and other people. "Consider it a great thing," says Seneca, "to play one and the same man."

Since ambition can teach men valor, and temperance, and generosity — yes, and even justice; since greed can take a shop boy raised in shade and idleness and give him the nerve to leave the fireside and trust himself, in a frail boat, to the waves and the anger of Neptune, and can teach him discretion and prudence along the way; since Venus herself puts boldness and resolve into boys still under the schoolmaster's rod, and steels the hearts of tender girls in their mothers' laps — as Tibullus tells it, the girl, with love for her guide, slips past the sleeping guards and steals through the dark, alone, to her young man — given all this, it is not enough for a sound judgment to weigh us simply by our outward actions. It must probe right down to the soul, and discover there what springs set the motion going. But since that is a high and hazardous undertaking, I wish fewer people would attempt it.
