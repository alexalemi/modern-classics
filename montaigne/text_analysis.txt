TEXT ANALYSIS AND TRANSLATION STRATEGY
Selected Essays — Michel de Montaigne, 1580-1588
Source: Charles Cotton's English translation (1685-86), Hazlitt edition
(Gutenberg #3600). Twenty-four essays + the preface, selected from 107.

================================================================================
1. WHAT THIS BOOK IS
================================================================================

Montaigne invented the essay — essai, "attempt" — and the genre has never
escaped him. Retired at 38 to the tower library of his family estate in
the middle of France's wars of religion, he set out to describe the only
subject he claimed to know: himself. The result is the most companionable
book in world literature — digressive, skeptical, funny, humane, and
startlingly honest about everything from his kidney stones to his

memory lapses to what he actually thinks about while doing anything.

This selection: the canon hits (Of Cannibals, Of Friendship, Of the
Education of Children, To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die, Of Repentance,
Of Books, Of Cripples, Of Experience) plus the short gems (Of Idleness,
Of Smells, Of Liars, Of Democritus and Heraclitus, All Things Have Their
Season). Grouped under Book I / Book II / Book III as in the original.

================================================================================
2. MONTAIGNE'S STYLE — AND THE COTTON LAYER
================================================================================

- CONVERSATION, NOT ARGUMENT. Montaigne thinks on the page. He digresses,
  doubles back, contradicts himself and notices it cheerfully. NEVER
  straighten the path — the wandering IS the essay. Keep transitions
  loose and natural ("But to my purpose..." → "But back to my point...").
- THE SELF-PORTRAIT IN MOTION. First person everywhere, concrete and
  bodily. He tells you about his slow memory, his height, his bowels,
  his fear of death and how he talked himself out of it. Render the
  frankness exactly — intimate, wry, never confessional-dramatic.
- SKEPTICAL WARMTH. His doubt is friendly. He mocks pedants, torturers,
  colonizers, and himself in the same easy tone. The register is a
  brilliant friend talking over dinner — smart but never showing off.
- QUOTATION POLICY (important — Cotton gives Latin verse + bracketed
  English + citation everywhere): render each quotation as ONE graceful
  modern English sentence or verse-fragment, integrated into the flow,
  with a light attribution — e.g.: As Horace says, "grant me what I
  have, and health to enjoy it." DROP the Latin except for short famous
  tags (a few words) where the Latin itself carries the punch. Never
  keep both Latin and translation for long passages.
- EDITORIAL BRACKETS: the source is full of --[...]-- editor's notes
  (Hazlitt/Le Clerc citations, edition notes). DROP THEM ALL. They are
  not Montaigne.
- Cotton's long colon-chained sentences → break into modern sentences;
  Montaigne's own rhythm is quick and speech-like, so this is a
  restoration, not a loss.

================================================================================
3. FORMAT CONVENTIONS
================================================================================

- One essay per file (long essays split into "(PART n OF k)" parts).
  Heading = the essay's modernized title on the first line (no chapter
  numbers): "Of Idleness", "To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die",
  "Of the Education of Children", "Of Three Kinds of Company" (for "Of
  Three Commerces"), "Of the Inequality Among Us", "A Custom of the
  Island of Cea", "Of the Power of the Imagination" (for "Of the Force
  of Imagination"), "That Our Actions Are Inconsistent" (for "Of the
  Inconstancy of Our Actions") — modernize gracefully, keep "Of ..."
  shape. The preface file heading: "To the Reader".
- No invented subheadings within essays.
- Part files: parts 2+ continue mid-essay directly, no re-introduction.

================================================================================
4. VOCABULARY MAP
================================================================================

  essay/essai                  -> where Montaigne refers to his essais,
                                  "these attempts of mine" or "my essays"
                                  (gloss the pun once, in the preface
                                  file or first use: essai = attempt)
  "the vulgar"                 -> ordinary people / the crowd
  "science"                    -> learning, knowledge (16th-c. sense)
  "wit"                        -> mind, intelligence (by context)
  "humours"                    -> moods, temperament
  "physic"                     -> medicine
  "commerce" (III.3)           -> company/association (the essay is
                                  about books, friends, and women as
                                  three kinds of company)
  cannibals                    -> keep the word (it is the essay's title
                                  and pivot) but render the people as
                                  Montaigne does — with admiration; "the
                                  people of the New World" / "these
                                  nations" in running text per source
  Sebond, Epaminondas, La Boétie -> keep names; La Boétie identified
                                  once ("Étienne de La Boétie, the friend
                                  of the great friendship essay")
  kidney stones / "the stone"  -> kidney stones

================================================================================
5. FAMOUS PASSAGES — MANDATED MODERN PHRASINGS (must_contain checks these)
================================================================================

- Preface: "...an honest book" opening kept; and the famous line rendered
  exactly: "I am myself the matter of my book." Closing "Therefore
  farewell" kept with the date line.
- Of Friendship, on La Boétie — render exactly: "Because it was he;
  because it was I."
- Of Cannibals — render the thesis as: "everyone calls barbarism
  whatever is not his own practice."
- Of Repentance — render exactly: "I do not portray being; I portray
  passing."
- Of Cripples (on witch trials) — render as: "it is putting a very high
  price on our conjectures to roast a man alive on their account."
- Of Experience — render exactly: "When I dance, I dance; when I sleep,
  I sleep." And close the book with the Horace prayer as a short verse
  rendering (Apollo, health, a sound mind, an old age with music in it).
- To Philosophize Is to Learn to Die — keep the turn: "he who would
  teach men to die would teach them to live."

================================================================================
6. CONTENT NEEDING CAREFUL HANDLING
================================================================================

- OF CANNIBALS: Montaigne's Brazilians are the essay's heroes; the
  barbarians are the Europeans torturing each other alive in the wars of
  religion. Render his admiration and the satirical reversal at full
  strength; "savage" appears in his mouth as a term he is dismantling —
  keep the dismantling visible ("wild, the way we call fruits wild that
  nature produces by herself").
- A CUSTOM OF THE ISLE OF CEA: a catalogue of classical suicides and
  arguments for and against — 16th-century moral philosophy; measured,
  complete, no modern editorializing.
- OF THE POWER OF THE IMAGINATION: includes frank impotence/sexual
  anecdotes — matter-of-fact, complete, wry as Montaigne is.
- OF CRIPPLES: the witch-trial skepticism is the point; also includes a
  bawdy proverb about lame women — render plainly.
- Wars-of-religion violence (torture, burnings) appears as Montaigne's
  contemporary reality — measured register, full force where he gives it
  ("I live in a season when unbelievable examples of this vice of
  cruelty flourish").

================================================================================
7. VOICE TARGET — SAMPLE TRANSFORMATION
================================================================================

Cotton (Of Idleness):
  "...and there is no folly or vain subject from which they do not
  bring forth a thousand fancies and chimeras... the mind that has no
  fixed point loses itself; for, as they say, to be everywhere is to be
  nowhere."

Modern target:
  "...there is no piece of nonsense too empty for an idle mind to fill
  with a thousand fantasies and chimeras. A mind without a fixed aim
  loses itself — because, as they say, to be everywhere is to be
  nowhere."

Register: 8th grade–high school, but never dumbed down — Montaigne's
intelligence must feel effortless. Contractions freely; this is the most
conversational book in the collection. Self-deprecation lands dry.
