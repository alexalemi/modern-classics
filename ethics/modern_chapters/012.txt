Chapter 9

But we may still wonder whether we have really said enough about what it is to do injustice and to suffer it. Start with those strange lines of Euripides:

  A: I killed my mother — that is the whole of my story.
  B: But tell me: did both of you will the deed, or neither?

The question they raise is this: is it actually possible to suffer injustice — to be wronged — voluntarily? Or is being wronged always involuntary, in the way that doing wrong is always voluntary?

And is it always one or the other? Doing injustice is always voluntary; is suffering it always involuntary, or is it sometimes voluntary and sometimes not? The same question comes up on the positive side, with having justice done to you. Doing justice is always voluntary, just as doing injustice is; so you might expect the active and the passive to match up in both cases — that being wronged and having justice done to you would each be always voluntary or always involuntary. But it would surely be absurd to claim that having justice done to you is always voluntary; some people have justice done to them and certainly do not will it.

We can also put the question in a more general form: whenever a man has something unjust done to him, is he wronged? Or does suffering injustice, like doing it, require some further conditions?

Both what I do and what I have done to me can be just "accidentally," as we saw; and the same goes for unjust. Doing something unjust is not the same as doing injustice, and suffering something unjust is not the same as being wronged — and likewise for doing justice and having justice done to you. For to be wronged, there must be someone doing the wrong; to have justice done to you, someone doing the justice.

Now, if doing injustice means simply hurting a man voluntarily, and "voluntarily" means with knowledge of the person, the instrument, and the manner, then the weak-willed man, who voluntarily hurts himself, would voluntarily suffer injustice — and it would be possible for a man to wrong himself, which is itself one of the questions in dispute.

Again, a man might, through weakness of will, voluntarily let himself be hurt by another man acting voluntarily; so here too a man would be voluntarily wronged.

I think, rather, that the definition is faulty. To "hurting with knowledge of the person, the instrument, and the manner" we must add: "against his wish." On that definition, a man can voluntarily be hurt and suffer what is unjust, but no one can voluntarily be wronged. For no one wishes to be hurt — not even the weak-willed man, who acts contrary to his own wish. No one wishes for what he does not think good, and what the weak-willed man does is not what he thinks he ought to do. And the man who gives away what is his, as Glaucus in Homer gives to Diomede —

  gold armor for bronze, a hundred oxen's worth for nine —

is not wronged; for the giving rests with him, but being wronged does not rest with oneself: there has to be someone doing the wrong.

Clearly, then, suffering injustice cannot be voluntary.

Two questions remain of those we set out to discuss: (1) when an unfairly large share is assigned, who does the injustice — the man who assigns it or the man who receives it? and (2) is it possible to do injustice to yourself?

Take the first. If it is the assigner and not the receiver who does the injustice, then a man who knowingly and voluntarily gives another too much and himself too little does injustice to himself. And this is exactly what moderate people are often thought to do; the equitable man is inclined to take less than his due. But the case is not so simple. Perhaps he took a larger share of some other good — of reputation, say, or of the noble itself.

Besides, the difficulty dissolves under our definition of doing injustice: nothing is done to this man against his wish, so no injustice is done to him — at most, harm.

It is also plain that it is the man who makes the unjust award who does injustice, and not always the man who gets more than his share. A man does not do injustice whenever what he does can be called unjust, but only when he voluntarily does what is unjust — and that can be said only of the one in whom the action originates, which here is the distributor, not the receiver.

Again, "do" has many senses. In a certain sense an inanimate tool "kills"; so does my hand; so does my slave, acting under my orders. These can be said to do what is unjust, but not to act unjustly or to commit an act of injustice.

Again, if a judge gives an unjust judgment unknowingly, he does not commit injustice in the sense of violating what is legally just, and his judgment is not unjust in that sense — though in a certain sense it is unjust; for what is just according to law and what is just in the primary sense are different things. But if he gives unjust judgment knowingly, then he himself is grasping at more than his share — a share of favor with one party, or of revenge on the other. A judge who gives unjust judgment on those grounds takes more than his due exactly as if he split the unjust award itself; even in that case, a judge who awards a piece of land takes his cut not in land but in money.

Men imagine that since acting unjustly is in their power, being just must be easy. It is not. To sleep with your neighbor's wife, to strike your neighbor, to pass certain coins from your hand to his — all easy, and always in your power. But to do these acts as the expression of a certain character is not easy, and not always in your power.

In the same way men think that knowing what is just and what is unjust takes no great wisdom, since anyone can look up what the law prescribes (though what the law prescribes is only accidentally just, not essentially). But to know how such acts must be done, and how such distributions must be made, in order to be just — that is a harder matter than knowing what is good for health, and even that is no easy matter. It is easy enough to know what honey is, and wine, and hellebore, and cautery, and the knife; but to know how they must be applied, and to whom, and when, in order to produce health — that is so far from easy that to have this knowledge is to be a doctor.

For the same reason, some people think the just man is as capable of acting unjustly as justly — indeed more capable than another of performing the acts themselves, of sleeping with a woman or landing a blow — just as the courageous man is more capable than another of throwing away his shield, turning his back, and running for it. But playing the coward and acting unjustly do not consist merely in doing such acts (though the doer might be said to act unjustly "accidentally"); they consist in doing them in a certain frame of mind — just as practicing medicine and healing do not consist simply in cutting or not cutting, in giving a drug or withholding it, but in doing so in a particular fashion.

Lastly, justice presupposes persons who share in the things that are, generally speaking, good, but who can have too much of them or too little. For some beings — the gods, perhaps — no amount of such things is too much; for others — the incurably vicious — no amount does any good, everything hurts them; but for the rest of us they are useful within limits. Justice, therefore, is an essentially human affair.

Chapter 10

Next we must speak of equity and the equitable, and ask how equity is related to justice and the equitable to the just. On inspection, they seem neither simply identical nor different in kind. Sometimes we praise what is equitable and the equitable man — we even borrow the word as a general term of praise meaning "good," as if the more equitable a thing is, the better it is. At other times we stop and find it strange that the equitable should deserve praise if it is something other than the just. For if they differ, then either the just is not good or the equitable is not; and if both are good, they are the same thing.

These are the reflections that create the difficulty about equity. And in a way they are all correct, and not really in conflict. For the equitable, though better than one kind of just, is itself just; it is not better than the just as if it were something different in kind. The just and the equitable, then, are the same in kind, and both are good, though the equitable is the better.

What obscures the matter is this: the equitable is just, but it is not the legally just — it is a correction of it.

The reason is that every law speaks in general terms, while there are some matters about which it is impossible to speak correctly in general terms. Where one must speak generally but cannot do so correctly, the law takes the case that holds for the most part — fully aware that it does not hold for every case.

And the law is no less correct for that. The defect lies not in the law, nor in the lawgiver, but in the nature of the subject matter; it is built into the very conditions of human action.

So when the law lays down a general rule and a case then arises that the rule does not fit, it is right — where the legislator has fallen short and erred by speaking without qualification — to correct the omission: to say what the lawgiver himself would say if he were standing there, and what he would have written into the law if he had known of the case.

The equitable, then, is just, and better than the just in one sense of the word — not better than absolute justice, but better than the error that comes of stating things without qualification. And this is the very essence of the equitable: it is an amendment of the law where the law fails through the generality of its language.

The reason the law cannot cover every case is that some matters simply cannot be caught in a law, and so require a special decree. For what is variable needs a variable standard — like the leaden rule used in Lesbian masonry. That rule has no fixed shape of its own; it bends to fit the contour of each stone. Just so, the decree bends to fit the case.

We have found, then, what the equitable is: it is just, and it is better than what is just in a certain sense of the word. And from this it is easy to see what the equitable man is. He is the man disposed to choose such a course and follow it — who does not press his rights to the last penny at other people's expense, but is ready to take less than his due even with the law on his side. This is the equitable man, and the character is equity: a kind of justice, not some different character altogether.

Chapter 11

The foregoing discussion lets us answer the question whether it is possible for a man to act unjustly toward himself.

Justice in one sense of the word, we found, covers those exercises of the several virtues which the law prescribes. For example, the law does not order a man to kill himself, and what the law does not order it forbids. Further, when a man voluntarily and without provocation hurts another contrary to law — "voluntarily" meaning with knowledge of the person and the instrument — he acts unjustly. Now, the man who kills himself in a fit of rage does this voluntarily, against right reason, and does what the law forbids; therefore he acts unjustly.

But unjustly toward whom? Toward the state, surely, not toward himself: he suffers voluntarily, and no one can be wronged voluntarily. And this is in fact the ground on which the state punishes it — a particular loss of civic standing is pronounced upon the man who destroys himself, as one who has acted unjustly toward the state.

Again, take "unjust" in the other sense, in which it names not badness in general but one particular form of vice. In this sense too it is impossible to act unjustly toward yourself. (This sense, we found, differs from the first: the unjust man here is bad in the way the coward is bad — as having one specific vice, not a wholly vicious character; and in calling his act unjust we are not charging him with complete badness.) For if a man could wrong himself, the same thing could be taken from and given to the same person at the same time — which is impossible. In fact, the just and the unjust always require more than one person.

Further, an act of injustice must be voluntary and deliberate, and it must come first — the man who merely pays back the hurt he received is not held to act unjustly. But the man who hurts himself inflicts and suffers the very same hurt at the very same moment.

Again, if a man could wrong himself, it would be possible to be wronged voluntarily.

Further, no one acts unjustly without committing some particular act of injustice; and no man commits adultery with his own wife, or breaks into his own house, or steals his own property.

But the whole question of wronging oneself is settled, without going into detail, by our answer to the question whether a man can be wronged voluntarily.

(It is plain that suffering injustice and doing it are both bad — the one is having less, the other having more, than the mean amount, which plays the role here that health plays in medicine or good condition in athletic training. Still, doing injustice is the worse of the two. Doing injustice is blamable and implies vice — either complete, fully formed vice, what we call vice without qualification, or something well on the way to it; for a voluntary act of injustice does not always imply an unjust character. But having injustice done to you implies no vice and no injustice in you at all.

In itself, then, being wronged is the lesser evil, though accidentally it can be the greater. Science does not bother with such accidents: it calls pleurisy a worse ailment than a stumble, even though the stumble might on occasion turn out worse — if, say, stumbling made you fall and the enemy caught or killed you.)

Though we cannot speak strictly of justice between a man and himself, we can speak of it metaphorically, in virtue of a certain resemblance — justice not between a man and himself but between certain parts of him. Not justice in every sense, though: only the kind that holds between master and slave, or husband and wife. For that is the sort of relation in which the rational part of the soul stands to the irrational part.

And it is just this division into parts that makes people fancy there is such a thing as injustice toward oneself: one part of a man can have something done to it by another part against its desires, and so people suppose there is a kind of justice between these parts, as between ruler and ruled.

Let this, then, conclude our examination of justice and the other moral virtues.
