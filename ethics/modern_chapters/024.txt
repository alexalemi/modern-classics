Chapter 8

The life that consists in the activity of the other kind of virtue — moral virtue — is happy only in a secondary sense; for the activities of moral virtue are emphatically human, not divine. Justice, I mean, and courage and the other moral virtues show themselves in our dealings with one another: in contracts and services and outward acts of every sort, where we observe what is due in each case, and in our inward feelings too. And all of this is emphatically human business.

Again, moral virtue seems in some respects to be actually a product of our physical constitution, and in many respects to be closely bound up with the passions.

Again, practical wisdom is inseparably yoked to moral virtue, and moral virtue to practical wisdom, since the moral virtues fix the principles from which practical wisdom starts, while practical wisdom fixes what is right in morals.

But the moral virtues, being bound up with the passions, must belong to our compound nature — the union of soul and body — and the virtues of the compound nature are emphatically human. Therefore the life that expresses them, and the happiness that consists in that life, must be emphatically human too.

The happiness that consists in the activity of reason, on the other hand, stands apart from our lower nature. (So much we may be allowed to assert about it; a detailed discussion would take us beyond our present purpose.)

Further, this happiness would seem to need only a small supply of external goods — certainly less than the moral life needs. Both need the necessaries of life to the same extent, let us say; for though the statesman does in fact take more care of his body than the philosopher does, the difference will be trifling. But in what they need for their activities the difference will be great. The generous man will need money, if he is to act generously; the just man will need it to pay what he owes — for mere wishes cannot be tested: even unjust men pretend they wish to act justly. The courageous man will need strength, if he is to perform any act of courage; and the man of self-control will need the liberty to indulge — for how else can he, or the possessor of any other virtue, show what he is?

Again, people dispute whether the purpose or the action is more essential to virtue, since virtue implies both. Plainly, completeness requires both. But action needs many things, and the greater and nobler the action, the more it needs.

The man engaged in contemplation, by contrast, needs none of these things for his work; indeed, one may even say they get in the way of his contemplating. But insofar as he is a man living among men, he chooses to act virtuously; and so he will need such things in order to live a human life.

That perfect happiness is some kind of contemplative activity may also be shown in the following way.

We always suppose the gods to be the most blessed and happy of all beings; but what kind of actions shall we ascribe to them? Acts of justice? Surely it is ridiculous to picture the gods making contracts and returning deposits and so on. Acts of courage, then — enduring terrors and facing danger because it is noble to do so? Or acts of generosity? But to whom are they to give? And is it not absurd to imagine that they have money, or anything of that kind? And what could acts of self-control possibly mean for them? Surely it would be an insult to praise the gods for having no evil desires. Run through the whole list, and you will find that everything about action is petty and unworthy of gods.

And yet everyone supposes that the gods live, and therefore that they are active; we can hardly suppose they lie asleep like Endymion.

Now, if a being lives, and action cannot be ascribed to it, still less production, what is left but contemplation? It follows, then, that the divine life, which surpasses all others in blessedness, is a life of contemplation.

Of all human activities, therefore, the one most akin to this will be capable of the greatest happiness.

And this is further confirmed by the fact that the other animals have no share in happiness, being wholly incapable of this kind of activity. The life of the gods is blessed through and through; the life of man is blessed just so far as it attains some likeness of this activity; but none of the other animals is happy, since none of them can contemplate at all.

Happiness, then, extends exactly as far as contemplation does: the more contemplation in a life, the more happiness — not by accident, but because happiness necessarily accompanies contemplation; for contemplation is precious in itself.

Our conclusion, then, is that happiness is a kind of contemplation.

But since we are men, we shall need external good fortune as well; for our nature does not by itself supply everything contemplation requires. The body must be healthy, and fed, and otherwise cared for. Still, we must not suppose that because happiness is impossible without external goods, the man who is to be happy will need many of them, or much. Independence does not depend on a superabundance of good things, and neither does action; a man can do noble deeds without being ruler of land and sea. A moderate equipment of fortune is enough to give a man scope for virtuous action — as we can easily see, for private citizens seem to do what is right not less than princes, but rather more — and that much is all he needs; for that man's life will be happy who has virtue and exercises it.

Solon too, I think, described the happy man well when he said that, in his view, it was a man moderately supplied with the gifts of fortune who had done the noblest deeds and lived with self-control; for a man of modest means can do his duty.

Anaxagoras also seems to have held that the happy man is neither a rich man nor a prince; he said he would not be surprised if the happy man struck the masses as a strange choice, for they judge by externals, which are all they can appreciate.

The opinions of the wise, then, seem to agree with our theory. Such opinions carry some weight; but in practical matters the test of truth lies in the facts of life, for it is there that the supreme authority resides. So we should test everything we have said against the facts of life: if it agrees with the facts, we may accept it; if it clashes with them, we must set it down as mere theory.

But, once more, the man who exercises his reason and cultivates it, and keeps it in the best condition, seems also to be the most beloved of heaven. For if the gods take any care for human affairs, as they are thought to do, it is reasonable to suppose that they delight in what is best in us and most akin to themselves — that is, in reason — and that they reward those who love and honor it most, as men who care for what is dear to the gods and act rightly and nobly. And plainly all this belongs, above all, to the wise man. The wise man, therefore, is the most beloved of heaven — and therefore, we may conclude, the happiest.

In this way too, then, the wise man will be happier than anyone else.

Chapter 9

Now that we have said enough — in summary form, at least — about these matters, and about the virtues, and about friendship and pleasure too, are we to suppose that we have reached the end we proposed? Surely not. The old saying holds good: in practical matters the end is not to know what should be done, but to do it. It is not enough to know about virtue, then; we must try to possess it and to use it, or take whatever other road may make us good.

Now, if theories had power by themselves to make us good, then, in Theognis' words, "many and great would be the rewards they deserved," and we ought to provide them. But in fact, though theories seem strong enough to guide and stimulate generous-minded young men — though a well-bred character with a sincere love of the noble may be opened by them to the influence of virtue — they are powerless to turn the mass of men toward goodness. For most men are naturally governed by fear rather than by reverence: they abstain from evil not because it is foul but because of the punishment it brings. Living under the sway of their passions, they pursue the pleasures that suit their nature and the means to those pleasures, and avoid the opposite pains; while of the noble and the truly pleasant they have not so much as a notion, since they have never tasted it.

What theory or argument, then, could bring men like these to order? It is impossible, or at least very difficult, to argue away what has long been ingrained in the character. For my part, I think we must be well content if, when everything that seems to make men good is present, we get some modicum of virtue.

Now, what makes men good is held by some to be nature, by others habit — that is, training — by others instruction.

As for the goodness that comes by nature, it is plainly not in our power; it is bestowed by some divine agency on those who truly deserve to be called fortunate.

As for theory and instruction, I fear they cannot avail in every case. The hearer's soul must first have been prepared by training — taught to feel delight and aversion on the right occasions — just as soil must be prepared if the seed is to thrive. A man who lives at the mercy of his passions will not listen to the arguments meant to turn him back, and will not even understand them. When he is in that state, how is argument to change his mind? To put it bluntly, passion seems to yield not to reason but only to force. The character, then, must already be there, somehow akin to virtue — loving what is noble and hating what is base.

But it is hard to get right guidance toward virtue from our youth up unless we are brought up under suitable laws; for a life of self-control and steadiness is not pleasant to most men, least of all to the young. Our upbringing, then, should be fixed by law, and our whole way of life with it; it will stop being painful as it becomes habitual. And I venture to think it is not enough to get the right nurture and training while we are young: since we must keep up the same way of life as grown men, and confirm the habits, we need the intervention of the law in these matters too — and indeed, to put it bluntly, over the whole of life. For most men obey compulsion more readily than reason, and fear of punishment more readily than desire for the noble.

That is why some hold that the legislator should first invite men to virtue, exhorting them for the nobility of it — those who have been well trained will listen; but that when men will not listen, or are of baser nature, he should apply correction and punishment, and banish the incorrigible altogether. The good man, they say, whose guide is the noble, will obey reason; but the man who is not good, whose desires are set on pleasure, must be corrected by pain, like a beast of burden. And for the same reason, they say, the pains applied must be those most contrary to the pleasures the offender loves.

As we have said, then, the man who is to be good must be well nurtured and trained, and must afterward continue in the same excellent way of life, doing nothing vile either willingly or unwillingly; and this can be brought about only if men live subject to some kind of reason and right order — backed by force.

Now, a father's rule has neither the force nor the power of compulsion required, nor does the rule of any individual, unless he is a king or something like one. But law has compulsory power, and at the same time it is a rational ordinance proceeding from a kind of practical wisdom, from reason. And whereas we resent individuals who oppose our inclinations, even when they are right to oppose them, we do not feel aggrieved when the law bids us do what is right.

Yet Sparta is the only state, or nearly the only one, where the legislator seems to have attended to the nurture and way of life of the citizens. In most states these matters are wholly neglected, and every man lives as he pleases, laying down the law for wife and children like the Cyclops.

The best thing, then, would be for the state to take these matters in hand and manage them rightly. But since the state neglects them, it would seem to fall to each of us individually to help our own children and friends along the road to virtue — and we should have the power to do it, or at least the will.

From what has been said, it would seem that our best plan for this is to learn the art of legislation. Training on the public scale is plainly carried on by means of laws, and it is good where the laws are good; and it would seem to make no difference whether the laws are written or unwritten, or whether they regulate the education of one person or of many — no more than it matters in music or gymnastics or any other course of training. For just as law and custom prevail in the state, so in the household the father's word and the family's ways prevail — no less, indeed even more, because of the ties of kinship and of benefits received; for affection and obedience are implanted in the members of a family by nature from the start.

Moreover — despite what has just been said — individual treatment is better than treatment in the mass, in education no less than in medicine. As a general rule, rest and fasting are good for a fever patient; in a particular case they may not be. A boxing master, I suppose, does not prescribe the same style to every pupil. It would seem, then, that individuals are educated more precisely under private instruction; for then each gets exactly what he needs.

But you will treat an individual case best — whether as doctor, or trainer, or anything else — when you know the general rule: "such-and-such is good for all men," or "for all men of a certain temperament." For the sciences are said to deal, and do deal, with what is common to many individuals.

I do not deny that a particular person may be treated quite well by someone with no scientific knowledge at all, if experience has taught him precisely how particular causes affect that one man — just as some men seem to be their own best doctors, though quite unable to prescribe for anyone else.

Nevertheless, I venture to say that a man who wishes to master an art, or to grasp it scientifically, must go on to its general principles and get to know them by the proper method; for, as we have said, it is with universals that the sciences deal.

And so the man who wants to make men better by training — whether many men or few — should try to acquire the art or science of legislation, assuming that men can be made good through law. To mold rightly the character of whoever presents himself is not a thing that just anybody can do; if anyone can, it is the man who knows — just as in medicine, and in every other profession that calls for careful treatment and practical wisdom.

Our next business, then, is to ask: from whom, and by what means, are we to learn the art or science of legislation?

"As we learn the other arts," someone will say — "from the men who practice it, the politicians; for we found that legislation is a branch of statesmanship."

But I think the case of statesmanship is not quite like that of the other sciences and arts. In the others, the same men plainly teach the art and practice it — physicians and painters, for instance. But with statesmanship, the sophists profess to teach it, while it is never they who practice it, but the statesmen. And the statesmen would seem to act by some instinctive gift, proceeding by experience rather than by reasoning. For plainly they never write or speak about these matters (though that might be a better use of their time than composing speeches for the courts and the assembly), and they have never made statesmen of their own sons or of any of their friends. Yet we should expect them to have done so if they could; for they could have left no better legacy to their country, and there is nothing they would sooner have chosen for themselves — and therefore for those dearest to them — than this power.

Experience, however, we must allow, does seem to be of great service here; otherwise men would never become statesmen by mere familiarity with politics. Those who want a knowledge of statesmanship, then, seem to need experience as well as theory.

But those sophists who profess to teach statesmanship turn out to be ludicrously far from doing it. To put it bluntly, they do not even know what statesmanship is or what it deals with. If they knew, they would not treat it as identical with rhetoric, or as something lower than rhetoric; nor would they think it easy to frame a code of laws by collecting the laws that are most admired. "It is only a matter of picking out the best," they say — ignoring the fact that the selection itself requires understanding, and that to judge correctly is here, as in music, a matter of the greatest difficulty. In every field it is the men of special experience who judge the products rightly, who understand how and by what means each is brought about, and which combinations are harmonious; the inexperienced must be content if they can tell whether the result is good or bad — as with painting. Now, laws are, so to speak, the products of statesmanship. How, then, should a collection of laws make a man able to legislate — or even to pick out the best of them?

Even medicine, it seems, cannot be taught from handbooks. And yet the medical handbooks try to tell you not only the remedies but how to apply them, and how to treat each class of patient, sorting them by temperament. All this may be serviceable to men of experience; to those who know nothing of medicine it would seem to be useless.

So too, I think we may say, collections of laws and constitutions may be very serviceable to those who are able to examine them with a discriminating eye — to judge which ordinances are good and which bad, and which fit together with which. But those who go through such compendia without the trained faculty cannot judge them properly (unless a correct judgment comes of itself), though they may perhaps sharpen their wits on these matters.

Since our predecessors, then, have left the subject of legislation unexamined, it will perhaps be better to look into it ourselves — and indeed into the whole question of the management of a state — so that our philosophy of human life may be completed as far as it lies in our power to complete it.

Let us try, then, first of all, to review whatever sound observations our predecessors have made on this or that branch of the subject; and then, with our collection of constitutions before us, let us ask what things preserve and what things destroy states, what preserves and destroys each kind of constitution, and why some states are governed well and others badly. When we have gained an insight into all this, we shall perhaps be better able to see what the best kind of constitution is, and how each kind should be arranged, and what system of laws and customs suits each best.

Let us begin, then.
