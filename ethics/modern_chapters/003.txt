Book II

(Part 2 of 2)

Chapter 6

We have found, then, the genus to which virtue belongs. But we want to know not merely that virtue is a habit, but what kind of habit it is.

We may safely say this much: the virtue of a thing both puts the thing itself in good condition and makes it perform its function well. The virtue of the eye, for instance, makes both the eye and its work good; it is by the eye's virtue that we see well. In the same way the proper virtue of a horse makes it what a horse should be — good at running, at carrying its rider, at standing firm against a charge.

If this holds in every case, then the proper virtue of man will be the habit that makes a man good and makes him perform his function well.

How this comes about we have already said; but the same conclusion can be reached another way, by asking what the nature of this virtue is.

Now take any quantity, continuous or discrete: it is possible to take a larger amount, a smaller amount, or an equal amount — and each either in relation to the thing itself or relative to our own needs.

By an equal or fair amount I mean a mean amount — one that lies between excess and deficiency.

By the absolute mean — the mean of the thing itself — I mean the point equally distant from both extremes; and that is one and the same for everybody.

By the mean relative to us I mean the amount that is neither too much nor too little for us; and that is not one and the same for everybody.

For instance, if ten is too large and two is too small, then six is the mean of the thing itself — the arithmetic mean — for six exceeds one extreme by exactly as much as it is exceeded by the other. That is the mean by arithmetical proportion.

But the mean relative to us cannot be found that way. If ten pounds of food is too much for a given man to eat, and two pounds too little, it does not follow that the trainer will order him six; for six may itself be too much for this particular man, or too little — too little for Milo the wrestler, too much for the beginner. The same holds in running and wrestling.

So we may say generally that a master of any art avoids what is too much and what is too little; he searches out the mean and chooses it — not the absolute mean, but the mean relative to us.

Every art or science, then, perfects its work in this way: it looks to the mean and brings its work up to that standard. That is why people say of a good piece of work that nothing could be added to it and nothing taken away — meaning that excess or deficiency destroys its goodness, while the mean preserves it. And good artists, as we say, do in fact keep their eyes fixed on the mean in everything they do. Now if this is so, and if virtue, like nature, is more exact and better than any art, it follows that virtue too must aim at the mean. I mean moral virtue; for it is moral virtue that deals with feelings and actions, and it is these that admit of excess, deficiency, and the mean. It is possible to feel fear, confidence, desire, anger, pity — pleasure and pain generally — too much or too little, and both are wrong. But to feel them at the right times, about the right things, toward the right people, for the right end, and in the right way — that is the mean and the best course, and that is the mark of virtue. In the same way our outward actions also admit of excess, deficiency, and the due amount.

Virtue, then, deals with feelings and with actions, where excess is wrong and deficiency is blamed, while the mean amount is praised and hits the mark — and both being praised and being right belong to virtue.

Virtue, then, is a kind of mean state, since it aims at the mean.

Again, there are many ways of going wrong — for evil belongs to the unlimited, as the Pythagoreans pictured it, and good to the limited — but only one way of going right. That is why the one is easy and the other hard: easy to miss the target, hard to hit it. And this is one more reason why excess and deficiency belong to vice, and hitting the mean belongs to virtue:

  Good men are good in one way; bad men are bad in every way.

Virtue, then, is a habit of choice, lying in the mean relative to us, determined by reason — that is, by the reason by which the man of practical wisdom would determine it. And it is a mean, first, because it lies in the middle between two vices, one of excess and one of deficiency; and second, because while the vices fall short of or overshoot the due amount in feeling and in action, virtue finds the mean and chooses it.

In its essence, therefore — by the definition of what it is — virtue is a mean; but measured by what is best and right, it is an extreme.

Not every action or feeling admits of a mean, however. Some have names that already imply badness: among feelings, spite, shamelessness, envy; among actions, adultery, theft, murder. All of these are blamed because they are bad in themselves, not because of their excess or deficiency. With these it is impossible ever to go right; they are always wrong. Right and wrong in such things does not depend on doing them with the right person, at the right time, in the right manner — with adultery, say — the mere doing of any of them is wrong.

It would be equally absurd to look for a mean, an excess, and a deficiency in unjust or cowardly or self-indulgent conduct; for then there would be a mean of excess and a mean of deficiency, an excess of excess and a deficiency of deficiency.

The fact is this: just as there is no excess or deficiency of self-control or courage — because the mean is there, in a sense, an extreme — so in these kinds of conduct there is no mean, no excess, no deficiency: however they are done, they are wrong. For, to put it generally, there is no mean of excess or deficiency, and no excess or deficiency of the mean.

Chapter 7

But it is not enough to state all this in general terms; we must go on and apply it to the particulars — the several virtues and vices. For in reasoning about conduct, general statements are too vague and carry less truth than particular ones. Conduct deals with particular cases, and it is against these that our statements must be found to hold good.

Let us take the particulars, then, from the following table.

In the feelings of fear and confidence, the mean is courage. Of those who exceed, the man who exceeds in fearlessness has no name — as happens often in this table — while the man who exceeds in confidence is foolhardy, and the man who exceeds in fear and falls short in confidence is a coward.

In certain pleasures, and to a lesser extent in certain pains, the mean is self-control, and the excess is self-indulgence. People who fall short in the matter of these pleasures hardly exist, so they too have received no name; let us put them down as "insensible."

In giving and taking money, the mean is generosity; the excess and deficiency are extravagance and stinginess. These two vices exceed and fall short in opposite ways: the extravagant man exceeds in spending but falls short in taking, while the stingy man exceeds in taking but falls short in spending. (For the present we are only giving an outline or summary — nothing more; these points will be treated in detail later.)

Besides these there are other dispositions in the matter of money. There is a mean called magnificence — for the magnificent man is not the same as the generous man: he deals with large sums, the generous man with small — an excess called vulgarity or bad taste, and a deficiency called cheapness. These vices differ from the ones opposed to generosity; how they differ will be explained later.

With respect to honor and disgrace, the mean is greatness of soul; the excess may be called vanity, and the deficiency smallness of soul.

And just as we said that generosity is related to magnificence, differing only in that it deals with small sums, so here there is a virtue related to greatness of soul, differing only in that it is concerned with small honors instead of great ones. A man may desire honor as much as he ought, or more, or less: the man who carries the desire to excess is called ambitious, the man who lacks it unambitious, while the man with the due amount has no name. Nor are there abstract names for the characters, except "ambition" for the ambitious man's. Because of this, the men at the extremes both lay claim to the middle ground. Indeed, in everyday speech the man in the middle is sometimes called ambitious and sometimes unambitious; and sometimes the ambitious man is praised, sometimes the unambitious. Why this is so we will explain later; for now let us carry out our plan and run through the remaining types of character.

In the matter of anger, too, there is excess, deficiency, and the mean. The characters here scarcely have recognized names; but since we call the man in the middle gentle, let us call the mean gentleness. Of those at the extremes, let the man who exceeds be called wrathful and his vice wrathfulness, and the man who falls short wrathless and his character wrathlessness.

Besides these there are three other means, resembling one another and yet different. All of them have to do with our dealings with one another in word and action, but they differ in this: one concerns the truthfulness of those dealings, the other two their pleasantness — one of the two, pleasantness in amusement, the other, pleasantness in all the business of life. We must speak of these as well, so that we may see more plainly how in every case the mean is praiseworthy, while the extremes are neither right nor praiseworthy, but blamable.

Here too names are mostly lacking, and we must try, as elsewhere, to coin them ourselves, to keep the argument clear and easy to follow.

In the matter of truth, then, let us call the man who observes the mean truthful, and the mean truthfulness. Pretence that exaggerates is boasting, and the man a boaster; pretence that understates is irony, and the man ironical.

With regard to pleasantness in amusement, the man who observes the mean is witty, and his character wittiness; the excess is buffoonery, and the man a buffoon; the man who falls short is a boor, and his character boorishness.

With regard to pleasantness in the other affairs of life, the man who makes himself pleasant in the right way is friendly, and his mean state is friendliness; the man who overdoes it is obsequious if he has no ulterior motive, and a flatterer if he is angling for his own advantage; the man who falls short and makes himself disagreeable at every turn is a quarrelsome and sour sort of fellow.

There are also ways of observing the mean in mere emotions, and in our conduct with regard to them. Shame, for instance, is not a virtue, and yet the modest man is praised. For here too we speak of one man as observing the mean and another as exceeding it — like the bashful man, whom the least thing makes shy — while the man who is deficient in the feeling, or lacks it altogether, is shameless; the man who observes the mean is modest.

Righteous indignation, again, hits the mean between envy and spite. These have to do with the pleasure and pain we feel at what happens to our neighbors. The righteously indignant man is pained by the sight of undeserved prosperity; the envious man goes beyond him and is pained by the sight of anyone prospering; while the spiteful man, so far from being pained, actually rejoices in his neighbors' misfortunes.

But there will be another opportunity to discuss these matters.

As for justice, the term is used in more senses than one; so after settling the questions above, we will distinguish its various senses and show how each kind of justice is a mean.

And then we will treat the intellectual virtues in the same way.

Chapter 8

There are, then, as we said, three kinds of disposition: two kinds of vice, one marked by excess and the other by deficiency, and one kind of virtue, the observance of the mean. Now each is in a way opposed to each; for the extreme dispositions are opposed both to the mean and to one another, while the mean is opposed to both extremes. Just as the equal is greater when compared with the less, and less when compared with the greater, so the mean dispositions are excessive compared with the deficient ones, and deficient compared with the excessive ones, in feeling and in action alike. The brave man looks foolhardy next to the coward, and cowardly next to the foolhardy man; the self-controlled man looks self-indulgent next to the insensible man, and insensible next to the self-indulgent man; the generous man looks extravagant beside the stingy man, and stingy beside the extravagant man.

And so each extreme character tries to shove the middle character away from the middle, representing him as belonging to the opposite extreme: the coward calls the brave man foolhardy, the foolhardy man calls him a coward, and so on in the other cases.

But though mean and extremes are opposed to one another in this way, the extremes are strictly contrary to each other rather than to the mean; for they lie farther from each other than from the mean, just as the greater lies farther from the less, and the less from the greater, than either lies from the equal.

Sometimes, moreover, an extreme bears a certain resemblance to the mean — foolhardiness to courage, extravagance to generosity — whereas between the extremes there is the greatest possible unlikeness.

And "things as far removed from each other as possible" is the accepted definition of contraries; so the farther apart things are, the more contrary they are.

Compared with the mean, however, it is sometimes the deficiency that is more opposed and sometimes the excess. Foolhardiness, an excess, is not so opposed to courage as cowardice, a deficiency, is; but insensibility, a deficiency of feeling, is not so opposed to self-control as self-indulgence, an excess, is.

There are two reasons for this. One is drawn from the nature of the thing itself: since one extreme is in fact nearer to the mean and more like it, we naturally do not set it against the mean so strongly as the other. Foolhardiness seems nearer to courage and more like it, cowardice more unlike; so it is cowardice we call the opposite of courage — for what lies farther from the mean seems more contrary to it.

That, then, is the one reason, drawn from the thing itself. The other lies in ourselves, and it is this: whatever we are more prone to by nature appears more opposed to the mean. Our natural inclination runs toward indulgence in pleasure, and so we slip more easily into self-indulgence than into disciplined habits. The directions in which we tend to run to lengths, then, are the ones we speak of as more opposed to the mean; and thus self-indulgence, the excess, is more opposed to self-control than the deficiency is.

Chapter 9

We have now explained sufficiently that moral virtue is a mean, and in what sense: first, that it lies in the middle between two vices, one of excess and one of deficiency; and second, that it aims at the mean in feeling and in action.

And this is why it is a hard thing to be good; for finding the middle — the mean — in each case is hard, just as finding the center of a circle is not something everyone can do, but only the man who knows how.

So too anyone can get angry — that is easy — and anyone can give money away or spend it. But to do these things to the right person, to the right extent, at the right time, for the right end, and in the right way — that is not something everyone can do, and it is by no means easy. That is why doing it well is rare and praiseworthy and noble.

The man who aims at the mean, then, should first of all steer away from the extreme that is more opposed to it, as Calypso advises Odysseus:

  Keep your ship clear of that smoke and surf.

For of the two extremes one is more dangerous, the other less. Since hitting the mean precisely is hard, we must, as the proverb has it, take to the oars when we cannot sail, and choose the lesser of two evils; and we shall do that best in the way just described.

Second, we must each consider what we ourselves are most prone to — for different natures incline toward different things — and we can learn this from the pleasure and pain we feel. Then we must drag ourselves in the opposite direction; for by pulling well away from the wrong side we shall arrive at the middle, just as we straighten a bent stick by bending it back the other way.

But in everything we must be most on our guard against pleasant things — against pleasure — for we can scarcely judge her impartially. We should treat her, then, as the old counselors of Troy proposed to treat Helen, and repeat their saying in every case: if we send her away, we shall be less likely to go wrong.

This, in outline, is the course by which we shall best be able to hit the mean.

It is a hard task, we must admit, above all in the particular case. It is not easy to determine, for instance, how one ought to be angry, and with whom, and on what grounds, and for how long; for public opinion sometimes praises those who fall short and calls them gentle, and sometimes calls a harsh temper manly.

In fact a slight slip from the mean, whether toward excess or toward deficiency, is not blamed — only a considerable one; for that no one can mistake. But how far and to what extent a man must go wrong to earn blame is hardly possible to fix by reasoning; nothing that falls within the scope of perception can be so fixed. Such things lie in the region of particulars, and the judgment lies with perception.

This much, then, is plain: the middle character is in all cases to be praised, but we ought to lean sometimes toward excess and sometimes toward deficiency; for that is how we shall most easily hit the mean and get things right.
