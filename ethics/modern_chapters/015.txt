Book VII

(Part 1 of 3)

Characters Other Than Virtue and Vice

Chapter 1

Let us now make a fresh start and say this: the forms of moral character to avoid are three in number — vice, weakness of will, and brutishness. For two of these the opposites are obvious: the opposite of vice we call virtue, and the opposite of weakness of will we call self-restraint. But for the opposite of the brutish character, the best candidate is an excellence that is beyond us — the excellence of a hero or a god. So Homer has Priam say of Hector that he was surpassingly good:

  He seemed no child of mortal man,
  But of a god.

If, then, superlative excellence turns men into gods, as the stories tell us, it is evident that the opposite of the brutish character must be some excellence of that order. For just as a brute has neither virtue nor vice, neither does a god: what belongs to a god is something higher than virtue, and what belongs to a brute is something different in kind from vice.

Now, a godlike man is rare — to borrow the phrase the Spartans use; when they admire a man intensely, they call him seios aner, "a divine man" — and the brutish character is just as rare among human beings. It is found most often among the barbarians; sometimes disease or bodily injury produces it; and, thirdly, we use the name as a term of abuse for people who carry vice to an extreme pitch.

We shall have to say something about this disposition further on, and vice we have already discussed. So let us now speak of weakness of will, softness, and love of luxury, and also of self-restraint and endurance — for we must treat these as names of states of character that are neither identical with virtue and vice nor different from them in kind.

Here we must follow our usual method: first set down the appearances — the current opinions about these conditions — then work through the puzzles they raise, and finally establish, if we can, the truth of all the common opinions on the subject, or, failing that, of most of them and the most important. For if the difficulties are resolved and the popular notions left standing, we shall have attained as much certainty as the subject allows.

What, then, is commonly thought? (1) That self-restraint and endurance are good and praiseworthy things, while weakness of will and softness are bad and blameworthy. (2) That the self-restrained man is simply the man who stands by his reasoned conclusions, and the weak-willed man the one who abandons them. (3) That the weak-willed man knows that an act is bad and is driven to it by passion anyway, while the self-restrained man knows his desires are bad and is held back from following them by reason. (4) That the man of self-control is self-restrained and enduring — though as to the converse, some hold that every self-restrained and enduring man is self-controlled, and others deny it; and while some say the self-indulgent man is weak-willed and the weak-willed man self-indulgent, using the terms interchangeably, others draw a distinction between them. (5) As for the man of practical wisdom, people sometimes say it is impossible for him to be weak-willed, and at other times say that some men who are practically wise, and clever too, are weak-willed. (6) Lastly, people are called weak-willed even about anger, honor, and gain.

These, then, are the things people say.

Chapter 2

But an objection arises at once: in what sense can a man be judging rightly when he acts against his own judgment?

Some maintain that he cannot act that way if he really knows what is right. It would be strange, Socrates thought, if knowledge were actually in a man and something else mastered it and dragged it about like a slave. In fact Socrates fought the whole position root and branch: there is no such thing as weakness of will, he held; no one acts against what is best while judging it to be best — when a man acts that way, it is through ignorance.

Now this theory plainly contradicts experience. And if the passion that sways the weak-willed man really is due to ignorance, we must ask what kind of ignorance it is. For this much is clear: the man who acts against his better judgment does not think the act good before the passion is upon him.

There are others who agree with Socrates in part and disagree in part. They grant that nothing can prevail against knowledge, but they do not grant that men never act against what seems best; and so they say that when the weak-willed man gives in to pleasure, what he has is not knowledge but only opinion.

But if it really is opinion and not knowledge — if what opposes the desires is not a strong conviction but a weak one, like the belief of a man in doubt — then we excuse a man for not standing by it in the face of strong desires. But in fact we do not excuse vice, or anything else we call blameworthy.

Is it practical wisdom, then, that opposes desire in the cases where we blame a man for yielding? It is, after all, the strongest form of conviction. But surely that would be absurd: the same man would then be practically wise and weak-willed at once, and no one would say that a man of practical wisdom would voluntarily do the vilest acts. Besides, we have already shown that practical wisdom is essentially a capacity that issues in action — it deals with the last step, the thing to be done — and that it implies the possession of all the moral virtues.

Again: if a man cannot be self-restrained without having strong and bad desires, then the man of self-control will not be self-restrained, nor the self-restrained man self-controlled; for it is no part of the self-controlled character to have desires that are either violent or bad.

Yet in the self-restrained man the desires must be both strong and bad. If they were good, the habit that keeps him from following them would be bad, and self-restraint would not always be a good thing. If they were weak and not bad, resisting them would be nothing to respect; and if they were bad but weak, it would be nothing to admire.

Again: if self-restraint makes a man stand by any opinion whatsoever, it is a bad thing — as when it makes him stand by a false one. And if weakness of will makes a man abandon any opinion whatsoever, then there will be a good kind of weakness of will. Take Neoptolemus in Sophocles' Philoctetes: he deserves praise for failing to stick to the plan Odysseus had talked him into — and what stopped him was the pain he felt at telling a lie.

Again, the well-known argument of the sophists, fallacious as it is, creates a genuine difficulty. Wanting to establish a paradox — so that they will look clever if they pull it off — they build a syllogism that traps the hearer: his reason is fettered, since he is unwilling to rest in a conclusion that revolts him, but unable to move forward, since he cannot find the flaw in the argument. Thus it can be argued that folly combined with weakness of will is virtue: because of his weakness of will, a man does the opposite of what he judges to be good; but he judges that the good is bad and not to be done; the result is that he will do the good and not the bad.

Again, on this showing the man who pursues and does what is pleasant from conviction — who deliberately chooses these things — would seem to be better than the man who does so not on calculation but from weakness of will. For the first man is easier to cure: change his convictions and you change him. But the weak-willed man invites the proverb, "If water chokes you, what will you wash it down with?" If he were convinced that what he does is good, a change of conviction might stop him; but as it is, he is already convinced that something else is good — and he does this anyway.

Again, if weakness of will and self-restraint can show themselves in anything whatever, who is the man we call weak-willed simply? No one man unites all the various forms of it, yet there are people to whom we apply the term with no qualification at all.

Such, roughly, are the puzzles that arise. Some of them we must clear away, and some we must leave standing; for the solving of a puzzle is the discovery of the truth.

Chapter 3

Our questions, then, are these: (1) Does the weak-willed man act with knowledge or not — and what does "knowledge" mean here? (2) What is the field in which self-restraint and weakness of will operate — all pleasures and pains, or certain definite classes of them? (3) Are the self-restrained man and the man of endurance the same or different? And so on with the other questions that belong to this inquiry.

(Though the proper starting point is to ask whether the self-restraint and the weakness of will we are discussing are marked off from other kinds by their field or by their manner — I mean, whether a man is weak-willed in this special sense merely because his reason loses control in certain matters, or because it loses control in a certain way, or on both grounds; and, connected with this, whether this weakness of will and this self-restraint can show themselves in everything. Our answer will be: the man who is called weak-willed simply, with no qualification, does not show his character in everything, but only in the matters where the self-indulgent man shows his. Nor is it simply an uncontrolled disposition toward those matters that makes him what he is — that would make weakness of will identical with self-indulgence — but a particular kind of uncontrolled disposition. For the self-indulgent man is carried along by his own deliberate choice, holding that the pleasure of the moment is always to be pursued; the weak-willed man thinks otherwise — and pursues it all the same.) Now to question (1).

As for the argument that it is true opinion, not knowledge, against which men act when they act weak-willedly — it makes no difference here. Some people who merely hold opinions are in no doubt whatever; they think they have exact knowledge.

If the reply is that those with mere opinion act against their judgment more readily because their belief is weak, we answer that knowledge and opinion do not differ in that way: some people believe their bare opinions as strongly as others believe what they really know. Heraclitus is proof enough of that.

But we use the word "know" in two different senses. A man who has knowledge he is not now using is said to know a thing, and so is a man who is now using it. It will make a difference, then, whether a man has knowledge of what he ought not to do without that knowledge being present to his mind, or has it actually present. Only the latter seems strange — acting against knowledge you are attending to; acting against knowledge you merely possess does not.

Again, since reasoning of this kind involves two sorts of premises — a universal proposition for the major and a particular for the minor — nothing prevents a man from acting against his knowledge while having both, if he is using only the universal and not the particular; for it is the particular that gets acted on.

Again, there are different kinds of universal: one may concern the agent himself, another the thing. For instance, a man may reason, "dry food is good for every man, and I am a man," or "food of such-and-such a kind is dry" — but whether this food here is of that kind is something he may not know, or may know without the knowledge being awake in him.

These distinctions make an enormous difference — so much so that acting against knowledge in the one way seems not at all strange, while in the other way it does seem strange.

But there is yet another way of "having knowledge" besides those just mentioned. Within "having without using," we can see, there are different modes of having, so that a man may in one sense have knowledge and in another sense not have it — a man who is asleep, for instance, or mad, or drunk. And people in the grip of passion are in just such a state; for anger, sexual desire, and the like visibly alter the condition of the body, and in some cases actually produce madness. Clearly, then, we must say that the weak-willed man has knowledge in the same sort of way as the sleeping, the mad, and the drunk.

And saying the words of knowledge is no proof of really having it. Even in the grip of these passions, people recite demonstrations and verses of Empedocles — just as beginners string sentences together before they understand what they mean; the meaning has to grow into them, and that takes time. So we must suppose that the weak-willed say the words the way actors do.

Again, one may look for the cause of the phenomenon in a way proper to its own nature, as follows. There is (1) the universal judgment, and (2) the judgment about particular facts, which falls to sense-perception. When the two are joined into one, the soul must — in theoretical matters — affirm the conclusion, and in practical matters must carry it straight out into action. For instance, if you judge "everything sweet should be tasted" and "this thing before me is sweet" — a particular fact — then, if you have the power and are not prevented, you cannot help but act on the conclusion at once: taste it.

Now suppose there is on one side the universal judgment forbidding you to taste, and on the other side the universal "everything sweet is pleasant," with its particular "this thing before me is sweet" — and it is the particular judgment that is operative — and appetite happens to be present. The first chain of reasoning tells you to avoid the thing; appetite drives you toward it, for appetite can set each of the bodily parts in motion.

So it turns out that when people act weak-willedly, it is in a way under the influence of reason — of opinion, that is — but of opinion that is opposed to right reason not in itself, only incidentally. For it is the desire, not the opinion, that is opposed to right reason.

And this is why brutes cannot be weak-willed: they have no universal judgments, only images and memories of particular facts.

As for how the weak-willed man gets out of his ignorance and recovers his knowledge, the account is the same as for the man who is drunk or asleep; nothing about it is peculiar to this phenomenon, and for that account we must go to the students of natural science.

But since the last premise — the minor — is an opinion about something perceived, and is what determines action, this is the one the weak-willed man in his passion either does not have, or has only in the sense in which, as we explained, having means not knowing in the full sense but saying the words, as a drunk man recites the sayings of Empedocles.

And because the last term is not universal, and does not seem to be an object of knowledge in the way the universal is, even what Socrates was trying to establish seems to come out true. For when passion carries a man away, what it drags about is not what we regard as knowledge in the strict sense — nor is it such knowledge that gets perverted by passion — but only the knowledge of perception.

Chapter 4

So much, then, for whether the weak-willed man knows or not, and in what sense it is possible to act weak-willedly with knowledge. We must next ask whether a man can be weak-willed simply, or only weak-willed in some particular respect — and, if the former, what the field is in which the character shows itself.

It is evident that it is in pleasures and pains that the self-restrained and the enduring, and the weak-willed and the soft, show what they are.

Of the sources of pleasure, some are necessary, and others are desirable in themselves but admit of excess. The necessary ones are the bodily processes — nutrition, the propagation of the species, and generally the bodily functions we said self-indulgence and self-control are concerned with. The others are not necessary but are desirable in themselves: victory, honor, wealth, and good and pleasant things of that kind.

Now, those who go to excess in this second class against their own better reason we do not call weak-willed simply, but with a qualifier: weak-willed about money, or gain, or honor, or anger — not simply, because they are different characters and get the name only by resemblance. (Compare the man who won at the last Olympic games: he was called Man, for that was his name; the word as applied to him meant almost, but not quite, what it ordinarily means.)

Here is the proof: weakness of will is blamed not merely as a mistake but as a kind of vice — either vice simply or some particular vice — but the people just described are not blamed that way.

Among the characters concerned with the bodily enjoyments, though — the ones we say self-control and self-indulgence deal with — take the man who goes to excess in pursuing what is pleasant and avoiding what is painful, in the matter of hunger and thirst, heat and cold, and everything that reaches us through touch or taste, and who does this not from deliberate choice but against his choice and his reasoning. Him we call weak-willed with no addition — not "weak-willed about this particular thing, say anger," but simply weak-willed.

A proof of this: people are also called soft in these latter matters, but not in any of the former — not about honor or gain.

And this is why we group the weak-willed man with the self-indulgent, and the self-restrained with the self-controlled — but none of those who are only metaphorically called self-restrained and weak-willed — because they are in a way concerned with the same pleasures and pains. They deal with the same matters, but they behave differently: the other three deliberately choose what they do; the weak-willed man does not.

And so a man who pursues excessive pleasure with no desire, or only a moderate one, and who runs from even slight pains, is more properly called self-indulgent than one who is driven to it by violent desires. For what would the first man do if the violent passions of youth were added — if going without his natural appetites were violent pain to him?

Now, some of our desires and pleasures belong to the class of the noble and good (for some of the things that please us are naturally desirable), others to the opposite class, and others lie in between, as we distinguished before — money, gain, victory, and honor falling in the first class. With regard to all of these, the first class and the intermediate alike, men are blamed not for being affected by them, or desiring them, or caring for them, but for doing so in certain ways and beyond the due amount. So we blame those who are moved by some good and noble object, or pursue it, past the bounds of reason — people who care too much about honor, say, or about their children or parents. These are noble objects, and men are praised for caring about them; but even here one can go too far — if, like Niobe, one were to fight against the gods themselves, or carry things as far as Satyrus, nicknamed Philopator, "father-lover," whose devotion to his father seemed to reach the pitch of outright folly.

In these matters, then, there is no room for vice or wickedness, for the reason given: each of these things is in itself desirable, and only the excess in them is bad and to be avoided.

Similarly, there is no room here for weakness of will in the strict sense — for weakness of will is not only to be avoided; it is positively blameworthy. But because the condition looks similar, we do use the term with a qualification, saying "weak-willed in this" or "in that" — just as we call a man a "bad doctor" or a "bad actor" whom we would never call bad flat-out. In those cases the term "badness" applies not simply but with a qualification, because each of these qualities is not strictly a vice but only analogous to one; and likewise here, it is plain, we must count as weakness of will (or self-restraint) in the strict sense only what shows itself in the matters that self-control and self-indulgence deal with, while what shows itself about anger gets the name only by metaphor. That is why we add the qualifier and call a man "weak-willed in anger," as "in honor" or "in gain."
