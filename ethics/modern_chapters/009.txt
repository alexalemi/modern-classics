Book IV

(Part 3 of 3)

Chapter 6

In social life — living alongside others, sharing their conversation and their common business — some people show themselves obsequious: to please you they praise everything, never object to anything, and think their whole duty is to avoid giving pain to anyone they meet. Those who take the opposite line, who object to everything and never stop for a moment to think what pain they may cause, are called quarrelsome and sour.

It is plain enough that both these habits deserve blame, and that the habit which takes the middle course between them deserves praise — the habit that makes a man go along with what he should, in the right way, and likewise refuse to go along. This type of character has no recognized name, but it looks most like friendliness. For the man who shows this moderation is just the sort of man we mean when we speak of an upright friend — except that a friend also feels affection. The difference from friendship is that this quality involves no emotion, no affection for the people we deal with. The man who has it goes along when he should, not because he loves or hates, but because that is his character. He will behave the same way toward people he knows and people he does not, toward intimates and strangers — only, in each case, as is fitting; for we do not owe strangers the same consideration we owe our intimates, or the same care not to pain them.

We have already said in general terms that such a man will behave as he ought with others; but we must add that, while he tries to add to their pleasure and to avoid giving them pain, he always steers by what is noble and fitting. The pleasures and pains of social life seem to be his particular field. Whenever he finds it would not be noble — or would be positively harmful to himself — to help along one of these pleasures, he will refuse to go along and will choose to give pain instead. And if a pleasure would bring discredit on the man producing it, and no small discredit, or would do him some injury, while opposing it costs only a little pain, he will not go along with it; he will set his face against it. He will behave differently in the company of important people and of ordinary people, of close friends and of mere acquaintances, and so on, giving each his due — preferring, other things being equal, to promote pleasure, and reluctant to give pain, but letting the consequences govern his conduct when the consequences are serious: by consequences I mean what is noble and fitting. So for the sake of a great pleasure later, he will inflict a small pain now.

The man who hits the mean here, then, is something of this sort, but he has no recognized name.

The man who always makes himself pleasant, if he aims simply at pleasing and has nothing further in view, is obsequious; but if he does it to get some profit for himself, in money or in what money buys, he is a flatterer.

And the man who sets his face against everything is, as we have said, quarrelsome and sour.

Here the extremes seem to be opposed to one another rather than to the mean, because the mean has no name.

Chapter 7

The mean that lies between boasting and irony — this virtue, too, lacks a name — shows itself in almost the same field.

It will be worth examining these qualities as well; for we shall know more about human character when we have gone through each of its forms, and we shall be more fully convinced that the virtues are ways of observing the mean when we have surveyed them all and found that this holds of every one.

We have spoken of the characters that social life displays where pleasure and pain are concerned; let us now speak in the same way of those who show themselves truthful or untruthful — in what they say, in what they do, and in the claims they put forward.

First of all, then, the boaster seems fond of claiming the things people admire when he does not have them, or not to the degree he pretends; the ironical man, on the other side, disclaims what he has, or plays it down; while the man who observes the mean — the man who is "always himself" — is truthful in word and deed, admitting the plain facts about himself and neither inflating nor shrinking them.

Now each of these lines of conduct can be pursued with some further object in view, or with none.

When a man has nothing further in view, he speaks and acts and lives according to his character.

But falsehood is in itself vile and blamable, and truth is noble and praiseworthy in itself.

And so the truthful man, who observes the mean, deserves praise, while both the untruthful characters deserve blame — the boaster more than the ironical man.

Let us speak of each of them, then, and first of the truthful character.

Remember that we are not talking about the man who tells the truth in business dealings, or in matters that fall under justice and injustice — those belong to another virtue. The man we mean is the one who, where nothing so weighty is at stake, is truthful in his speech and in his life because that is his character.

Such a man would seem to be a good man. For he who loves truth, and tells it where nothing hangs on it, will tell it all the more surely where serious interests are involved; he will shun falsehood there as something base, having already shunned it elsewhere for its own sake — and such a man deserves praise.

He leans toward understating the truth rather than overstating it, and this seems the more fitting course, since all exaggeration is offensive.

The man who claims more than he has with nothing further in view — the boaster proper — seems not to be a good character (a good man would take no pleasure in falsehood), but he seems silly rather than bad.

Of boasters who do have a further object, the one whose object is reputation or honor is not censured very severely (no more than the boaster proper is); but the one whose object is money, or the means of making money, is held in greater disgrace.

Notice, though, what marks off the boaster proper from the other kinds of boasters: not his ability to boast, but his preference for boasting. He is a boaster by habit, because that is his character — just as there is, on one side, the liar proper, who delights in the falsehood itself, and on the other the liar who lies out of desire for honor or gain.

Those who boast for reputation claim the things that win a man praise or get him counted happy; those whose motive is gain claim the things that benefit others and whose absence can escape detection — skill in magic, say, or in medicine. And so it is usually things of this sort that men claim and boast of, since they meet both conditions.

Ironical people, on the other hand, with their way of talking themselves down, seem the more refined characters; for their motive seems to be not love of gain but a wish to avoid parade. And what they disclaim is, notably, exactly what people admire — Socrates was an instance of this.

But those who disclaim small advantages they obviously possess are called affected humbugs, and are the more easily despised. And sometimes this self-deprecation is barely distinguishable from boasting — dressing like a Spartan, for instance; for there is something boastful in extreme depreciation just as in exaggeration.

Those who use irony in moderation, and are ironical about matters not too obvious and plain, come across as men of refinement.

Finally, the boaster seems to be the special opposite of the truthful man; for he is worse than the ironical man.

Chapter 8

Again, since relaxation has its place in our life, and one form of relaxation is amusing conversation, it seems that here too there is a proper way of mixing with others — that there are things it is right to say, and a right way of saying them; and the same holds for listening. And here also it will make a difference what sort of people you are speaking in front of, or listening to.

Plainly, in these matters too, one can go beyond the mean or fall short of it.

Now those who go to excess in ridicule seem to be buffoons and vulgar fellows, straining for a laugh at any cost, and more set on raising the laugh than on keeping their jokes elegant and painless to their target. While those who will never say anything funny themselves, and scowl at those who do, are considered boorish and morose. But those who joke gracefully are called witty — men of ready wit, as it were quick and versatile men.

For a man's character seems to reveal itself in these playful sallies, and so we judge his moral makeup by them, just as we judge his body by its movements.

But because ridiculous things are everywhere, and most people take more delight in fun and joking than they should, the buffoon is often called witty because he delights his audience. That there is a difference between the two, and no small one, is plain from what we have said.

One element of the character that observes the mean here is tact. The man of tact will say, and listen to, only the sort of thing that suits an honest man and a gentleman; for there are things it is quite becoming for such a man to say and to hear by way of jest, and the jesting of a gentleman differs from that of a slavish nature, the jesting of an educated man from that of an uneducated one.

You can see this in the difference between the old comedy and the new: the earlier writers got their laughs from obscenity, the later ones from innuendo — and as decency goes, that is no small difference.

Can we define good jesting, then, as making jokes that befit a gentleman? Or jokes that do not pain the hearer — or that even give him pleasure? Surely that last is hopelessly indefinite, for different things are hateful and pleasant to different people.

But the things he will listen to will be of the same sort as the things he will say: the jokes a man can listen to, we think, he can make himself.

So there are jokes he will not make — though we cannot define them exactly; for to make a joke of a man is to vilify him in a way, and the law forbids certain kinds of vilification, and perhaps ought to forbid certain kinds of jesting too.

The refined and gentlemanly man, therefore, will regulate his wit accordingly, being as it were a law to himself.

Such, then, is the character of the man who observes the mean, whether we call him a man of tact or a man of ready wit.

The buffoon, on the other hand, cannot resist an opening for a joke; if he can just raise a laugh, he will spare neither himself nor anyone else, and will say things no man of refinement would say — some of which he would not even listen to.

The boor, lastly, is wholly useless in this kind of company: he contributes nothing and takes everything badly. And yet relaxation and amusement seem to be necessary ingredients in our life.

The ways of observing the mean in social life that we have described, then, are three in number, and all have to do with conversation or shared activity of some kind. They differ in that one is concerned with truth, while the other two are concerned with what is pleasant; and of the two concerned with pleasure, one has its field in our amusements, the other in all the rest of social life.

Chapter 9

Shame — the modesty of our earlier list — cannot properly be called a virtue, for it is more like a feeling or emotion than a habit. At any rate, it is defined as a kind of fear of disgrace, and it works much like the fear that danger excites: men blush when they are ashamed, while the fear of death turns them pale. Both, then, seem to be in a way physical, and that is held to be the mark of a feeling or emotion rather than of a habit.

Again, this feeling does not suit every time of life, but only youth. We think it proper for young people to be ready to feel shame because, living as they do by their emotions, they often go wrong, and shame holds them back from wrong actions.

And so we praise young men who are ready to feel shame, but no one would praise an older man for being apt to be ashamed; for we think he ought not to do anything that could make him ashamed of himself in the first place.

Indeed, shame is not the mark of a good man, since it is what vile acts occasion — and such acts should simply not be done. (Nor does it matter that some acts are really shameful and others shameful only in public opinion; neither kind should be done, and so a man ought not to be feeling shame.) It is the mark of a worthless man to be the sort who would do something shameful.

And if a man's character is such that he would be ashamed if he did one of these shameful acts, it is absurd for him to imagine that this makes him a good man; for shame attaches only to voluntary acts, and a good man will never voluntarily do vile acts.

At most, shame would be good conditionally: if he were to do the act, a good man would be ashamed. But there is nothing conditional about the virtues.

Again, granted that it is bad to be shameless — to feel no shame at doing shameful things — it does not follow that it is good to do them and be ashamed of it.

Self-restraint, in the same way, is not a virtue either, but something between virtue and vice.

But we will explain this point about self-restraint later; let us now treat of justice.
