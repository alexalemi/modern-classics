Book III

(Part 1 of 3)

The Will

Chapter 1

Virtue, as we have seen, has to do with feelings and actions. Now, praise and blame attach only to what is voluntary; what is involuntary gets pardon, and sometimes even pity.

So anyone investigating the nature of virtue needs to draw a clear line between the voluntary and the involuntary — and lawmakers will find the distinction useful too, when they hand out rewards and punishments.

The general view is that an act is involuntary when it is done under compulsion or through ignorance.

"Done under compulsion" means the cause is outside the person, who contributes nothing to it — as when someone is carried off by a whirlwind, or by men too strong to resist.

But there is a real question about acts done to avoid a greater evil, or to gain some noble end. Suppose a tyrant orders you to do something disgraceful while he holds your parents or your children in his power: do it and they live, refuse and they die. Whether such acts are voluntary or involuntary is a matter of genuine dispute.

Throwing cargo overboard in a storm is a similar case. No one dumps his property over the side for nothing; but any sensible person will do it to save his own life and the lives of the crew.

Acts of this kind, then, are mixed — but they come closer to being voluntary. For they are wanted and chosen at the moment they are done, and the end of an act is whatever the agent has in view at the time. So in applying the terms voluntary and involuntary, we must look at the agent's state of mind at the moment of action. And at that moment he does will the act; for in cases like these the cause that sets his limbs moving lies inside him — and where the cause lies in the agent, it is up to him to act or not to act.

Such acts, then, are voluntary — though in themselves, apart from the circumstances, we may grant they are involuntary, since no one would choose anything of the kind for its own sake.

In fact, people are sometimes praised for actions of this sort — for enduring something disgraceful or painful in order to secure some great and noble result. In the opposite case they are blamed; for no decent person would endure utter disgrace with no noble result in view, or only a trivial one.

In some cases we do not praise, but we do pardon: when someone is driven to do wrong by pressure too strong for human nature, pressure no one could withstand. Though there are some cases, I think, where the plea of compulsion cannot be allowed — where a man ought to accept death in its most painful form rather than do the deed. The circumstances that supposedly "compelled" Alcmaeon, in Euripides' play, to kill his mother look simply absurd.

It is sometimes hard to decide whether we should do this deed to avoid that evil, or endure that evil rather than do this deed; and it is harder still to stand by our decision. For usually the evil we want to avoid is painful, while the deed we are being pushed toward is disgraceful — and that is why we are blamed or praised, according to whether we let ourselves be compelled or not.

What kinds of acts, then, should be called compulsory?

Our answer, I think, must be this. First: when the cause lies outside and the agent contributes nothing, the act is compulsory without qualification — and therefore involuntary. Second: when an act that would never be done voluntarily for its own sake is chosen now, in preference to a given alternative, and the cause lies in the agent — such an act is "involuntary in itself," in the abstract, but "voluntary here and now, in preference to this alternative." And an act of this second kind is closer to being voluntary; for acts belong to the realm of particulars, and the particular thing done here is done voluntarily.

It is hardly possible, though, to lay down rules for which of two alternatives should be preferred; the particular cases differ too much.

Someone might argue that acts whose motive is something pleasant or something noble are compulsory too — for here also, the claim goes, something outside us constrains us.

But if that were true, every act would be compulsory; for pleasure and the noble are the motives of every act of every human being.

Again, acting under compulsion, against one's will, is painful; but acting for the sake of something pleasant or noble brings pleasure. And it is absurd to blame things outside us instead of our own readiness to be caught by their lures — absurd to claim our noble acts as our own while charging our disgraceful ones to "the pleasant things outside us."

Compulsory, then, it appears, means this: the cause is external, and the person compelled contributes nothing.

What is done through ignorance is always "not-voluntary," but it is "involuntary" only when the agent is pained afterwards — sorry when he discovers what he has done. For when a man who acted through ignorance feels no distress at the deed, you cannot say he did it voluntarily, since he did not know what he was doing; but you cannot say he did it involuntarily or unwillingly either, since he is not sorry.

So a man who has acted through ignorance and is sorry afterwards is held to have acted involuntarily, unwillingly; if he is not sorry, we may say — to mark the difference — that he acted "not-voluntarily." Since the case is different, it is better to have a different name for it.

Acting through ignorance, moreover, seems to be a different thing from acting in ignorance. A man who is drunk or in a rage is not thought to act through ignorance but through drunkenness or rage — yet he acts not knowingly, but in ignorance.

Every vicious man, to be sure, is ignorant of what he ought to do and ought not to do, and it is exactly this kind of error that makes men unjust and bad in general. But the word "involuntary" does not properly apply where a man is merely ignorant of what is right. The ignorance that makes an act involuntary is not ignorance of the principles that should guide our choices — that is vice; not, I say, ignorance of the universal, for that we blame — but ignorance of the particulars: the persons and things the act affects. These are the grounds for pity and pardon; a man who is ignorant of any of these particulars acts involuntarily.

It may be worth spelling out, then, what these particulars are and how many. They are: first, who is acting; second, what he is doing; third, the object or person the act affects; sometimes also the instrument — what he is doing it with; the purpose — what he is doing it for, say, to protect someone; and the manner — gently, for instance, or violently.

Now, no one short of a madman could be ignorant of all of these. Obviously a man cannot be ignorant of who is acting — how could he not know himself?

But a man can be ignorant of what he is doing. Someone who has said something will plead that the words slipped out before he knew it, or that he did not know the subject was forbidden — as Aeschylus pleaded about the Mysteries. Or a man might plead that in discharging the weapon he only meant to demonstrate how it worked, as the defendant did in the catapult case. Again, a man might mistake his own son for an enemy, as Merope does; or a sharp spear for a blunted practice one; or a heavy stone for a pumice-stone. One might kill a man with a drug meant to save him, or land a hard blow when one only meant to touch — as boxers do when they spar with open hands.

Since ignorance is possible about all these circumstances, a man who is ignorant of any of them is held to have acted involuntarily — above all when he is ignorant of the most important ones; and the most important seem to be the persons affected and the result.

Beyond this, though, the agent must be grieved and sorry for what he has done, if an act committed in this kind of ignorance is to count as involuntary and not merely not-voluntary.

But now, having found that an act is involuntary when done under compulsion or through ignorance, we may conclude that a voluntary act is one the doer originates himself, with knowledge of the particular circumstances.

For I venture to say it is wrong to call acts done from anger or desire involuntary.

In the first place, if that were right, we could no longer say that any of the other animals act voluntarily — nor even children.

Again, what does the claim mean? That none of the acts we do from desire or anger is voluntary? Or that the noble ones are voluntary and the disgraceful ones involuntary? Taken the second way, it is surely ridiculous, since the cause of both is one and the same. Taken the first way, it is absurd too, I think: absurd to say that we ought to desire a thing and, in the same breath, that pursuing it is involuntary. And in fact there are things we ought to be angry at, and things we ought to desire — health, for instance, and learning.

Again, what is done unwillingly seems to be painful, while what is done from desire is pleasant.

Again, as far as involuntariness goes, what is the difference between wrong done by calculation and wrong done in anger? Both are to be avoided; but the unreasoning passions seem to belong to a man just as much as his reason does, so acts done from the impulse of anger or desire are the man's own acts too. To call them involuntary, then, would be too absurd.

Chapter 2

Now that we have distinguished the voluntary from the involuntary, our next task is to discuss choice. For choice seems to be bound up with virtue most intimately of all, and to be a surer test of character than actions themselves.

Choosing is evidently a kind of willing, but the two terms are not identical: willing is the wider one. Children and the other animals have will, but not choice; and acts done on the spur of the moment are called voluntary, but not chosen.

Those who say that choice is appetite, or anger, or wish, or some sort of opinion do not seem to describe it correctly.

In the first place, choice is not shared by irrational creatures; appetite and anger are.

Again, the weak-willed man acts from appetite and not from choice; the self-restrained man acts from choice and not from appetite.

Again, appetite can run contrary to choice; but one appetite cannot run contrary to another appetite.

Again, the object of appetite — or of aversion — is the pleasant or the painful; but the object of choice, as such, is neither.

Still less can choice be anger; for acts done in anger seem to be, of all acts, the least chosen.

Nor is choice the same as wish, though the two look very alike. We cannot choose the impossible — a man who said he did would be thought a fool — but we can wish for the impossible, to escape death, for example.

Again, we may wish for things that could never come about through our own doing — that a particular actor or athlete should win, say — but we never choose such things; we choose only what we think our own action can bring about.

Again, strictly speaking we wish for the end and choose the means. We wish to be healthy, but we choose the things that will make us healthy; we wish to be happy, and we admit the wish, but it would not be correct to say we choose to be happy. For, in a word, choice deals with what is in our power.

Nor can choice be opinion. For one thing, anything at all can be a matter of opinion — the unalterable and the impossible no less than what is in our power. For another, we sort opinions by whether they are true or false, not by whether they are good or bad, which is how we sort choices.

Choice, then, is not the same as opinion in general — and no one, in fact, claims it is.

But neither is it identical with some particular kind of opinion. For it is our choosing good or evil that makes us morally good or bad; holding certain opinions does not.

Again, we choose to take or avoid something good or evil; we opine what a thing is, or what it is good for, or in what way — but we cannot opine to take or to avoid.

Again, we commend a choice for being right or correct, an opinion for being true.

Again, we choose a thing when we know well that it is good; we can hold an opinion about a thing we know nothing about.

Again, it seems the people best at choosing are not always the best at forming opinions: some have excellent judgment but, through depravity, fail to choose what they ought.

It may be said that choice must be preceded or accompanied by an opinion or judgment. But that makes no difference; our question is not that, but whether the two are identical.

What, then, is choice, since it is none of these?

It seems, as we said, that what is chosen is willed, but what is willed is not always chosen.

The missing mark, I think, is "after previous deliberation." For choice implies calculation and reasoning. The name itself seems to point the same way: something is chosen before other things — picked out in preference to them.

Chapter 3

Now for deliberation. Do we deliberate about everything — is anything whatever a possible subject of deliberation — or are there some things about which deliberation is impossible?

By "a matter for deliberation" we should mean, I think, not what a fool or a madman might deliberate about, but what a rational being would.

Now, no one deliberates about things eternal and unalterable — the system of the heavenly bodies, say, or the incommensurability of the side and the diagonal of a square.

Again, no one deliberates about things that change but always change in the same way, whether the cause is necessity or nature or anything else — the solstices, for instance, and the sunrise; nor about things wholly irregular, like drought and rain; nor about matters of chance, like finding a treasure.

Again, even human affairs are not all matters for deliberation: what constitution would be best for Scythia is a question no Spartan would deliberate about.

The reason we deliberate about none of these things is that none of them can be brought about by us.

What we deliberate about is conduct — the things within our own control. And these are all that is left; for besides nature, necessity, and chance, the only remaining cause of change is reason and human agency in general. Though we should add that each person deliberates about what he himself can do.

One further limit: where knowledge is exact and complete, there is no room for deliberation. Take writing: there is no doubt about how the letters should be formed.

We deliberate, then, about things our own agency brings about — but not about all of them equally. We deliberate about medicine and moneymaking; about navigation more than about gymnastics, since navigation has not yet been worked up into so exact a system; and so on — more about matters of art than matters of science, because there is more doubt about them.

Matters of deliberation, then, are matters where there are rules that generally hold good but where the outcome cannot be predicted — where there is an element of uncertainty. In important matters we call in advisers, distrusting our own judgment.

We deliberate not about ends but about means. A doctor does not deliberate whether he shall heal, nor an orator whether he shall persuade, nor a statesman whether he shall produce a good system of laws — no one in any profession deliberates about his end. Rather, having set the end before us, we consider how and by what means it can be reached. If it can be reached by several means, we consider which is easiest and best; if by only one, we consider how it is to be reached by that means, and how that means itself is to be secured, and so on — until we come to the first link in the chain of causes, which is the last thing we discover.

For in deliberating we seem to inquire and analyze in just this way, as we analyze a geometrical figure in order to learn how to construct it. (Not all inquiry is deliberation — mathematical inquiry is not — but all deliberation is inquiry.) And what comes last in the analysis comes first in the construction.

If we run into an impossibility, we drop the plan — if the scheme needs money, say, and no money can be raised. But if the thing looks possible, we set to work. By "possible" I mean what can be done by us; and what our friends can do, we can in a sense do ourselves, since it is we who set our friends to work.

Sometimes we are working out what instruments we need, sometimes how to use them; and so throughout: sometimes what agency will produce the effect, sometimes how, or through whom, that agency is to be set going.

It appears, then — as we said before — that a man originates his own acts; that he deliberates about what he can do himself; and that what he does, he does for the sake of something else. For he cannot deliberate about the end, only about the means to it; nor, again, can he deliberate about particular facts — whether this is a loaf, or whether it is properly baked. Those are matters of immediate perception. And if a man goes on deliberating forever, he will never reach a conclusion.

The object of deliberation and the object of choice are the same, except that the object of choice is already fixed and settled: when we say "this is chosen," we mean it has been selected after deliberation. For we always stop the inquiry into how to do a thing once we have traced the chain of causes back to ourselves — to the commanding part of ourselves; for that is the part that chooses.

The old constitutions Homer describes illustrate the point: there the kings announce to the people what they have chosen.

Since, then, a thing is said to be chosen when, being in our power, it is desired after deliberation, we may define choice as deliberate desire for something in our own power. For we first deliberate, then make our decision, and then desire in accordance with the deliberation.

Let this stand, then, as our outline account of choice — of what it is, and of what it deals with, namely means to ends.
