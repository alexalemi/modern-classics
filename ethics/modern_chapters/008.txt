Book IV

(Part 2 of 3)

Chapter 3

Greatness of soul, as its very name says, has to do with great things. Let us first pin down what those things are.

It will make no difference whether we examine the quality itself or the man who has it.

By a great-souled man we mean one who claims much and deserves much. The man who claims much without deserving it is a fool, and a man who possesses virtue is never a fool. So the man we have just described is the great-souled man.

The man who deserves little and claims little is modest and sensible, but not great-souled; for greatness of soul implies greatness, just as beauty implies stature. Small men may be trim and well proportioned, but they cannot be called beautiful.

The man who claims much without deserving it is vain — though not everyone who claims more than he deserves is vain.

The man who claims less than he deserves shows smallness of soul, whether his deserts are great or moderate, or small and he claims still less. And the fault seems greatest in the man whose deserts are great; for what would he do if he deserved less than he does?

The great-souled man, then, stands at an extreme in the greatness of what he deserves, but at the mean in how he carries himself; for he claims exactly what he deserves, while all the others claim too much or too little.

Now if he deserves much and claims much — and most of all deserves and claims the greatest things — there will be one thing above all that concerns him. For desert is measured in external goods. And the greatest of external goods, we may fairly say, is the one we render to the gods as their due, the one that people in high positions want most, and the one set as the prize for the noblest deeds. The thing that fits this description is honor — safely the greatest of all external goods. Honor and dishonor, therefore, are the field in which the great-souled man behaves as he ought.

Indeed we can see, without any argument, that honor is what great-souled men care about; honor is what they claim, above everything else, and what they deserve.

The small-souled man falls short, whether you measure his claims against his own deserts or against what the great-souled man claims for himself.

The vain or conceited man exceeds what is due to himself, though he does not out-claim the great-souled man.

But the great-souled man, since he deserves the greatest things, must be a perfectly good man; for the better man always deserves the greater things, and the best man the greatest. The truly great-souled man, therefore, must be good. In fact, greatness in every virtue would seem to be built into being great-souled.

It would be equally out of character for the great-souled man to run away swinging his arms and for him to commit an injustice. For what could he love enough to do something shameful for it, when nothing counts for much in his eyes?

Survey him point by point and you will find that the idea of a great-souled man who is not a good man is completely absurd. If he were not good, he could not even be worthy of honor; for honor is the prize of virtue, and it is paid to the good as their due.

Greatness of soul, then, seems to be a kind of crown of the virtues: it makes them greater, and it cannot exist without them. That is why true greatness of soul is a hard thing; it is impossible without the union of all the virtues.

The great-souled man, then, shows his character above all in the matter of honor and dishonor. At great honor from good men he will be moderately pleased, as merely getting his due — or even less than his due, since no honor can measure up to complete virtue. Still, he will accept it, since they have nothing greater to give him. But honor from ordinary people, on trivial grounds, he will flatly despise; that is not what he deserves. Dishonor he will likewise shrug off; he will never have earned it.

But though it is chiefly in the matter of honor, as we have said, that the great-souled man shows himself, he will also hold the mean in his feelings about wealth and power and every kind of good and bad fortune, whatever happens to him. He will be neither much elated by prosperity nor much cast down by adversity — after all, not even honor strikes him as a very great thing. Power and wealth are desirable for honor's sake (at least, those who have them want to be honored for them); so the man who thinks lightly of honor must think lightly of these too.

And so great-souled men seem to look down on everything.

Now the gifts of fortune are also commonly thought to contribute to greatness of soul. The well born are thought worthy of honor, and so are the powerful and the wealthy; they are in a position of superiority, and whatever is superior in some good thing is always held in greater honor. So these things do, in a sense, make people more great-souled, since such people are honored by some. But strictly, only the good man is worthy of honor — though the man who has goodness and good fortune together is commonly thought worthier still. Those who have these goods without virtue have no just claim to great things and are not properly called great-souled; neither is possible without complete virtue.

Instead, people who have these goods easily become arrogant and insolent. Without virtue it is not easy to carry the gifts of fortune gracefully; and so, unable to carry them, and thinking themselves better than everyone else, such people look down on others while doing whatever happens to please them. They imitate the great-souled man without really being like him, and they imitate him where they can: they do not show virtue in their actions, but they do look down on people. The great-souled man never looks down on anyone unjustly — his estimates are accurate — while most people who do it have no relevant grounds at all.

The great-souled man is not one to run into petty dangers, and he does not love danger, since there are few things he values much. But he is ready to face a great danger, and when he does, he does not spare his life; life is not worth keeping at every cost.

It is his nature to confer benefits, but he is ashamed to receive them; conferring is what a superior does, receiving is what an inferior does. And when he has received a favor, he tends to repay it with a greater one — that way his creditor becomes his debtor, and ends up on the receiving side.

Such men also seem to remember the benefits they have given better than those they have received (for the recipient of a benefit is the inferior of the benefactor, and this man wants to be the superior); they like being reminded of the one, and dislike being reminded of the other. That is why we read that Thetis would not mention to Zeus the services she had done him, and why the Spartans, negotiating with the Athenians, brought up the benefits Sparta had received rather than the ones she had conferred.

It is characteristic of him, again, never to ask favors, or only reluctantly, but to be ready to grant them; to be lofty toward the powerful and the fortunate, but unassuming toward people of middle rank. To assert superiority over the great is difficult and dignified; to assert it over the humble is easy. A haughty manner among the great is perfectly good breeding; among the lowly it is brutal — like showing off your strength on a cripple.

Another mark of him: he does not rush in wherever honor is on offer, or go where others hold first place. He stays aloof and holds back, except where there is great honor to be won or a great work to be done — not many things, but great and notable ones.

Again, he must be open in his hatreds and open in his loves (concealing your feelings — caring less for truth than for what people will think — is cowardly), and open in word and deed. His sense of his own superiority makes him outspoken, and he is truthful, except insofar as he takes an ironic tone with the masses. And he cannot shape his life to suit someone else, unless it is a friend; that is servile — which is why all flatterers and hangers-on of great men are slavish by nature, and why men of low nature become flatterers.

He is not easily moved to admiration; nothing is great to him.

He readily forgets injuries. Brooding on the past, especially on past wrongs, is not in his character; he prefers to overlook them.

He is no gossip. He will not talk about himself or about others, for he does not care to be praised, or to have others blamed (though he is not quick to hand out praise either). So he is not given to speaking ill of people, not even his enemies — except deliberately, to insult them.

When something happens that cannot be helped, or that matters little, he is the last man alive to complain or beg for help; that is how a man behaves who thinks such things very important.

He would rather own beautiful things that bring no profit than useful things that pay; that is the mark of a man whose resources are within himself.

Further, the character of the great-souled man seems to call for a slow step, a deep voice, and measured speech. A man is not likely to hurry when few things deeply interest him, or to get worked up when nothing strikes him as very important — and hurry and excitement are what produce a shrill voice and rapid movements.

This, then, is the great-souled man.

The man who falls short of this quality is small-souled; the man who overshoots it is vain or conceited.

Now these two do not seem to be bad men — they do no harm — but they are in error.

The small-souled man deserves good things and robs himself of what he deserves. He seems the worse for not claiming them, and for misjudging himself; if he judged rightly, he would desire what he deserves, since it is good. Not that such people seem to be fools — rather, too retiring. But a misjudgment of this kind really does seem to make them worse; for men strive for what they think they deserve, and these men shrink back from noble deeds and undertakings, thinking themselves unworthy of them, just as they shrink from external goods.

Vain men, on the other hand, are fools as well as ignorant of themselves, and they make it obvious. They take on honorable offices they are unfit for, and are promptly exposed as incapable. They dress in fine clothes and put on fine airs and all the rest of it; they want the whole world to know their good fortune; they talk about themselves, as if that were the road to honor.

But smallness of soul is more opposed to greatness of soul than vanity is; it is both commoner and worse.

Greatness of soul, then, as we have said, has to do with honor on the grand scale.

Chapter 4

But it appears (as we said at the outset) that there is also a virtue concerned with honor which stands to greatness of soul as generosity stands to magnificence. Both this virtue and generosity have nothing to do with great things; they make us behave properly in matters of moderate or trifling importance. Just as in the taking and giving of money it is possible to hit the mean, and also to exceed it or fall short of it, so in the desire for honor it is possible to go too far or not far enough — or, again, to desire honor from the right source and in the right manner.

We call a man ambitious as a term of reproach, meaning that he desires honor more than he ought and from the wrong sources; and we call a man unambitious as a term of reproach, meaning that he does not want to be honored even for noble deeds.

But sometimes we call a man ambitious as a term of praise, meaning that he is manly and loves noble things; and sometimes we call a man unambitious as a term of praise, meaning that he is moderate and sensible (as we said at the outset).

Plainly, then, "fond of" a thing is said in several senses, and the term "fond of honor" does not always mean the same: as praise it means fonder than most men are, as reproach it means fonder than is right. And since there is no recognized name for the observance of the mean, the extremes fight, so to speak, over what looks like an empty seat. But wherever there is excess and deficiency there is also a mean; and honor is in fact desired more than is right, and less; therefore it can also be desired to the right degree. This character, then, is praised — it is observance of the mean in the matter of honor — though it has no recognized name. Compared with ambition it looks like lack of ambition; compared with lack of ambition it looks like ambition; compared with both at once it looks, in a way, like both at once. We can see the same thing happening with the other virtues. But in this case the extreme characters seem to be opposed to one another, rather than to the middle character, because the character who observes the mean has no recognized name.

Chapter 5

Gentleness is the observance of the mean with respect to anger. But note that we have no recognized name for the mean here, and scarcely any recognized names for the extremes. So the term gentleness — which strictly leans toward the deficiency in anger (itself nameless) — gets applied to the mean.

The excess may be called wrathfulness; for the emotion involved is wrath, or anger, though the things that provoke it are many and various.

The man who is angry on the right occasions and with the right persons, and also in the right manner, at the right season, and for the right length of time, is praised. Let us call him gentle, then, since gentleness is used as a term of praise. For the man we call gentle wants to keep his balance — not to be carried off by his emotions, but to be angry only in the manner, on the occasions, and for the length of time that reason prescribes. If he errs, he seems to err on the side of deficiency: he is slow to take revenge and quick to forgive.

The deficiency, though — call it wrathlessness or what you will — is blamed. People who are not angered by what ought to anger them seem to be fools, and so do those who are not angry in the way, at the time, and with the persons they ought to be. Such a man seems to feel nothing and to be pained by nothing, and, never being angered, to lack the spirit to defend himself. And to let yourself be insulted, or to stand quietly by while your friends are insulted, is slavish.

It is possible to exceed on every point: to be angry with people one ought not to be angry with, at things one ought not to be angry at, more than one ought, more quickly, and for longer. Not that all these errors show up in the same person — that would be impossible; for evil destroys itself, and if it appears complete, it becomes unbearable.

So we find that wrathful men get angry fast, with the wrong people and at the wrong things, and more than they ought; but they get over it just as fast, and that is the best point in their character. The reason is that they do not hold their anger in: their quick temper makes them retaliate at once, so that what is in them comes out into the open — and then they are done with it.

People called choleric are excessively quick-tempered, flaring up at anything and on any occasion; hence the name, which in Greek suggests boiling over.

Sulky men are hard to appease, and their anger lasts a long time, because they keep it in. For the moment we retaliate, we are relieved: vengeance ends our anger, replacing pain with pleasure. But the sulky man gets no such relief; he carries the weight of his wrath around with him. No one even tries to talk him out of it, since he does not show it, and it takes a long time to digest one's anger inside oneself. Such men are a great trial to themselves and to their closest friends.

Lastly, hard is the name we give to those who are offended by things that ought not to offend them, and more than they ought, and for longer — and who will not be appeased without vengeance or punishment.

Of the two extremes, the excess is the more opposed to gentleness. It is commoner (human beings incline more naturally to vengeance), and a hard-tempered person is worse to live with than one who is too easygoing.

What we said some time ago is made abundantly clear by what we have just been saying: it is not easy to define how, with whom, at what, and for how long one ought to be angry — how far one may rightly go, and at what point the fault begins. The man who strays a little from the right course is not blamed, whether toward excess or toward deficiency; sometimes we praise those who fall short and call them gentle, and sometimes we call the harsh-tempered manly, as men fit to rule. But how large and what kind of error makes a man blamable can hardly be defined; it depends on the particular circumstances of each case, and only immediate perception can decide it.

But this much at least is clear: the habit that observes the mean is to be praised — the habit that makes us angry with the right persons, at the right things, in the right manner, and so on — while all the habits of excess and deficiency deserve censure: slight censure if the error is trifling, graver if it is considerable, severe if it is great.

It is evident, therefore, that we must strive for the habit that observes the mean.

Let this stand, then, as our account of the habits that have to do with anger.
