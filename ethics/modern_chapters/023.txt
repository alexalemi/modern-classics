Chapter 5

From this it seems to follow that pleasures differ in kind. For things that differ in kind are, we believe, completed by things that differ in kind. That is how it stands with the products of nature and of art — animals and trees, paintings, sculptures, houses, furniture. In the same way, then, activities that differ in kind are completed by things that differ in kind.

Now the activities of the intellect differ in kind from the activities of the senses, and the several kinds of each differ from one another; therefore the pleasures that complete them differ too.

The same conclusion follows from the close bond between each pleasure and the activity it completes. For an activity is heightened by its proper pleasure. People who enjoy a thing understand it better and press deeper into it: those who delight in geometry become geometricians, and grasp its propositions better than anyone else; and likewise lovers of music, or of architecture, or of anything else, make progress in their own line of work because they delight in it. The pleasures, then, help the activities grow. But what helps a thing grow must belong closely to it; and where things differ in kind, whatever belongs closely to them must differ in kind as well.

The point comes out still more clearly, perhaps, from the fact that the activity of one faculty is blocked by the pleasure proper to another. A lover of the flute cannot attend to an argument if he hears someone playing nearby; he takes more delight in the flute-playing than in the business at hand, and so the flute-player's pleasure ruins the activity of his reason.

The same thing happens in other cases too, whenever a man is active on two fronts at once: the pleasanter business crowds out the other, and the greater the gap in pleasantness, the harder it crowds — even to the point of shutting the other down altogether. That is why, when something delights us intensely, we cannot do anything else at all; and when we turn to a second thing, it is because we no longer care much about the first. People who eat sweets in the theater do it most when the actors are bad.

Since its proper pleasure sharpens an activity — makes it last longer and go better — while an alien pleasure spoils it, plainly these two pleasures stand far apart. An alien pleasure, in fact, does almost exactly what the activity's own pain does. For an activity is ruined by the pain that arises from it: if a man finds it disagreeable and painful to write or to calculate, he stops writing in the one case and calculating in the other, because the activity hurts. An activity, then, is affected in opposite ways by its proper pleasure and its proper pain — and by "proper" I mean what arises from the activity itself. An alien pleasure, as we have already said, does nearly what the proper pain does: it wrecks the activity, though not to the same degree.

Again, activities differ in goodness and badness — some are to be sought, some shunned, some are neither — and their pleasures differ in just the same way; for each activity has its proper pleasure. The pleasure proper to a good activity is good, and the pleasure proper to a bad one is bad: desire for noble things is praised, desire for base things blamed. And the pleasures that ride along with our activities belong to them even more intimately than the desires do; for the desires are separate from the activity both in time and in nature, while the pleasures come at almost the same moment, and are so hard to pull apart from the activity that men debate whether the activity is not simply identical with the pleasure.

It seems, though, that pleasure is not the same thing as thinking or perceiving — that is impossible; it is because the two cannot be separated that some people fancy they are identical.

As the activities vary, then, so do their pleasures. Sight is purer than touch, hearing and smell purer than taste; and their pleasures differ accordingly. The pleasures of the intellect are purer than these pleasures of sense, and within each class some are purer than others.

Each kind of being, moreover, seems to have its proper pleasure, just as it has its proper function — namely, the pleasure that accompanies the activity of its faculties, the realization of its nature. Look at the animals one by one and you will see it: the pleasures of a horse, a dog, and a man are all different. As Heraclitus says, a donkey would rather have hay than gold; fodder gives a donkey more pleasure than gold does.

The pleasures of beings that differ in kind, then, differ in kind; and we might naturally expect the pleasures of beings of the same kind not to differ. Yet among men, at least, they vary widely. The same things delight one man and pain another; what is grievous and hateful to one is pleasant and lovable to another. It happens even with sweet things: a man in a fever has a different notion of what is sweet from a man in health, and a feeble man a different notion of what is hot from a robust man. And so it goes in other matters too.

But in all matters of this kind we hold that things are what they appear to be to the good man.

If this is right, as we hold it to be — if, that is, the measure of everything is virtue, or the good man as such — then what appears to him to be pleasure will be pleasure, and what he delights in will be pleasant.

If what he finds disagreeable strikes someone else as pleasant, there is nothing astonishing in that; men are corrupted and warped in many ways. Such things are not pleasant — they are pleasant only to these men, in this condition. Plainly, then, the pleasures that everyone admits are base should not be called pleasures at all, except for the corrupt.

But among the pleasures that are considered good, which, or what kind, are to be called the proper pleasures of man? The answer follows from his proper activities, for the pleasures ride along with these. Whether, then, the activities of the complete and happy man are one or many, the pleasures that complete those activities will be the pleasures of man in the full sense of the words; the rest will be so only in a secondary sense and with a fraction of the meaning — just like the activities themselves.

Conclusion

Chapter 6

Now that we have discussed the kinds of virtue and friendship and pleasure, it remains to give a summary account of happiness, since we set that down as the end of everything man does. And it will shorten the work if we first recapitulate what we said above.

We said that happiness is not a habit — not a settled state. If it were, it could belong to a man who slept through all his days and lived the life of a vegetable, or to a man crushed by the greatest misfortunes. Since we cannot accept that, we must place happiness among activities, as we said before. And since some activities are necessary — that is, desirable for the sake of something else — while others are desirable in themselves, plainly happiness must be placed among the activities desirable in themselves, not those desirable for the sake of something else. For happiness lacks nothing; it is self-sufficient.

Now, an activity is desirable in itself when nothing is looked for from it beyond the activity itself.

Two kinds of activity are held to be of this sort: first, virtuous actions, for doing what is noble and excellent must count as desirable for its own sake; and second, the amusements that please us, for these are not chosen for the sake of anything else — indeed men are more often harmed than helped by them, neglecting their health and their fortunes.

Now most of the people whom the world calls happy fill their days with pastimes of this sort. That is why the man with a ready wit at such pastimes is a favorite at a tyrant's court: he makes himself pleasant in exactly what the tyrant wants, and what the tyrant wants is amusement. So amusements pass for ingredients of happiness, because princes spend their leisure on them. But princes, we may venture to say, prove nothing. Princely rank does not carry virtue or reason with it, and virtue and reason are the springs of all excellent activity. And if these men, never having tasted pure and refined pleasure, run to the pleasures of the body, that is no reason to think those pleasures the more desirable; children, too, are sure that what they prize is better than anything else. It is only natural, then, that as children and men differ in their estimate of what is valuable, so do bad men and good.

Therefore — as we have said many times — what is truly valuable and truly pleasant is what is valuable and pleasant to the good man. Now what each man finds most desirable is the activity that fits his own trained character; and so what the good man finds most desirable is activity in accordance with virtue.

Happiness, therefore, does not consist in amusement. Indeed, it would be absurd if the end were amusement — if we toiled and labored our whole life long for the sake of amusing ourselves. For we choose everything, one may say, for the sake of something else — everything except happiness, for happiness is the end. To be serious and to work hard for the sake of amusement seems silly, downright childish; but to amuse ourselves so that we can get back to serious work — that, as Anacharsis put it, seems right. For amusement is a kind of rest, and we need rest because we cannot work continuously.

Rest, then, cannot be the end; for we take it as a means to activity.

Again, the happy life is thought to be the life in accordance with virtue; and such a life is a serious business, not a matter of amusement.

Again, we call serious things better than funny and amusing things, and the better the part or the man, the more serious the function; but we have already said that the activity of the better is higher, and more of the nature of happiness.

Again, bodily pleasures are within anyone's reach — a slave's no less than the best man's; but no one supposes that a slave shares in happiness, seeing that he does not share in the life proper to a man. For happiness does not lie in pastimes of this sort, but in activities in accordance with virtue, as we have already said.

Chapter 7

But if happiness is an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue — or, if there is more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete of them — then it is reasonable to expect that it will be the activity of the highest virtue; and that will be the virtue of the best part of us.

Now, that part or faculty — call it reason or what you will — which seems by nature to rule and to lead, and to grasp things noble and divine, whether it is itself divine or only the most divine element in us: the activity of this part, in accordance with its proper virtue, will be perfect happiness.

That this activity is contemplation we have already said.

And this conclusion would seem to agree both with what we said above and with the truth.

For this activity is the highest possible: reason is the highest thing in us, and of all knowable things, those that reason deals with are the highest.

Again, it is the most continuous; for we can contemplate more continuously than we can do anything else whatever.

We think, too, that pleasure must be one of the ingredients of happiness; and by common consent the pleasantest of all virtuous activities is the activity of wisdom. Philosophy, at any rate, is held to offer pleasures wonderful in their purity and steadiness; and it stands to reason that those who possess knowledge pass their time more pleasantly than those who are still hunting for it.

Again, what we call self-sufficiency will belong most of all to the contemplative life. The necessities of life, true enough, the wise man needs as much as the just man and the rest; but once these are adequately supplied, the just man still needs people toward whom, and along with whom, he can act justly — and so do the self-controlled man and the courageous man and each of the others — while the wise man can contemplate all by himself, and the wiser he is, the more he can do it. He would do it better, we may grant, with fellow workers; but for all that, he is the most self-sufficient of men.

Again, this life alone seems to be desired purely for its own sake. It yields nothing beyond the contemplating itself, while from the practical activities we look to gain something, more or less, beyond the action.

Again, happiness is thought to require leisure; for we work in order to have leisure, just as we make war in order to have peace. Now the practical virtues get their exercise in politics or in war, and these are anything but leisurely occupations.

War, indeed, is the very opposite of leisurely. No one chooses to fight for fighting's sake, or stirs up a war for that purpose; a man who turned friends into enemies just to bring on battles and slaughter would be counted a bloodthirsty monster.

But the politician's life is not leisurely either; and beyond the political work itself, it aims at power and honors — or at least at happiness for the man and his fellow citizens, which is something different from the political activity; for we, in asking what happiness is, also ask what politics is, plainly treating the two as different things.

If, then, among actions in accordance with virtue, the statesman's and the soldier's stand first in nobility and grandeur, and yet they are unleisurely, aim at some further end, and are not desired simply for themselves; while the activity of reason seems to surpass them in seriousness, since it is contemplation of truth, and to aim at no end beyond itself, and to have its proper pleasure — which itself heightens the activity — and to be self-sufficient, and leisurely, and unwearying so far as anything human can be, and to have every other mark that we assign to happiness: then this, the activity of reason, will be the complete happiness of man — provided it is granted a complete span of life; for nothing incomplete has a place in happiness.

But a life of this kind would be something more than human. A man will live it not insofar as he is a man, but insofar as there is something divine in him; and as far as this divine element surpasses our compound human nature, so far does its activity surpass the activity of the other kind of virtue — the practical, moral kind.

If, then, reason is divine compared with man, the life of reason is divine compared with human life. And we should not listen to those who advise us, being men, to think human thoughts, and, being mortal, mortal thoughts. We ought, so far as in us lies, to put on immortality, and strain every nerve to live by the best thing in us; for small though it may be in bulk, in power and in worth it far surpasses all the rest.

Indeed, this part would even seem to be each man's true self, since it is the sovereign and better part of him. It would be strange, then, if a man chose to live not his own life, but the life of something else.

And here what we said before applies again: what is proper to each being by nature is best and pleasantest for it. For man, then, the life of reason is best and pleasantest, since reason more than anything else is the man. And this life, therefore, is also the happiest.
