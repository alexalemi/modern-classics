Book VII

(Part 2 of 3)

Chapter 5

Some things are pleasant by nature — some of them pleasant in themselves, others pleasant to particular kinds of animals or people. Other things are not naturally pleasant at all, but come to be pleasant in one of three ways: (1) through physical damage, (2) through habit, or (3) through an originally corrupt nature. And each of these three classes has a character type to match.

Take the third class first: the brutish characters. There is the creature in a woman's form that is said to tear open pregnant women and devour their unborn children; there are the peoples around the Black Sea who are said to delight in things like eating raw flesh or human flesh, or handing over their children to one another for the feast; and there are the things reported of Phalaris.

These are what we call brutish natures. In other cases the disposition comes from disease or madness — like the man who killed and ate his mother, or the slave who ate the liver of his fellow slave. These belong to the first class.

Other habits are either symptoms of a morbid state or the products of habituation, and so fall under the first class or the second: plucking out one's hair, for instance, or biting the nails, or eating cinders and earth, or unnatural sexual practices. These come sometimes from nature, sometimes from habit — as with those who have been abused from childhood.

Now, wherever nature is the cause of such morbid habits, no one would think of using the term "weakness of will" — any more than we would call women weak-willed for the part they play in reproduction. Nor would we use the term of those who have brought themselves into a morbid state by habitual indulgence.

Having habits of this kind, then, falls outside the boundaries of vice, just as brutishness does; and when a man who has such impulses masters them, or is mastered by them, that is not self-restraint or weakness of will in the strict sense, but only by a metaphor — just as the man who is mastered by his temper cannot strictly be called weak-willed. For even folly, cowardice, self-indulgence, and bad temper, whenever they run beyond a certain pitch, are themselves either brutish or morbid. A man so constituted by nature that he is frightened of everything, even the squeak of a mouse — his cowardice is brutish, inhuman; in the well-known case of the man who was terrified of a weasel, disease was the cause. So too with irrational human beings: those who lack reason by nature and live by their senses alone are brutish, like some races of remote barbarians, while those whose irrationality comes from disease (epilepsy, say) or insanity are morbidly irrational.

Again, a man may on occasion have one of these impulses without being mastered by it — if Phalaris, say, had felt the desire to eat a child's flesh, or some monstrous lust, and held himself back. And it is equally possible not merely to have the impulse but to be mastered by it.

To sum up, then: just as with vice there is one sort that is human and is called vice simply, and another sort that carries a qualifying label — "brutish" or "morbid" vice, not vice simply — in the same way there is plainly a brutish weakness of will and a morbid weakness of will, while only the kind that pairs with human self-indulgence is weakness of will simply.

We have now shown that weakness of will and self-restraint proper have to do only with the things that self-indulgence and self-control have to do with, and that in other matters there is a kind of weakness of will so called only metaphorically, and with a qualifying label.

Chapter 6

The next point to consider is that weakness of will about anger is less disgraceful than weakness of will about appetite.

Anger, it seems, listens to reason a little, but mishears it — like a hasty servant who dashes off before you have finished speaking and so gets the errand wrong, or like a dog that barks the moment it hears a noise, without waiting to find out whether it is a friend. Just so anger, hot and hasty by nature, hears something — but not what reason actually orders — and rushes off to take revenge. For when reason or imagination announces an insult or a slight, anger draws the inference, so to speak, that the offender is to be treated as an enemy, and instantly boils over. Appetite, on the other hand, needs only for reason or the senses to announce "this is pleasant," and it rushes to enjoy it. So anger, in a way, obeys reason, and appetite does not. Appetite is therefore the more disgraceful; for the man who is weak-willed about anger gives in, in some sense, to reason, while the other gives in not to reason but to appetite.

Again, it is more excusable to follow impulses that are natural — even with our appetites, it is more pardonable to follow the ones common to all men, and the more common they are, the more pardonable. And anger and bad temper are more natural than the desire for excessive and unnecessary pleasures. Recall the man who defended himself for beating his father: "He beat his own father," he said, "and that father beat his; and this boy here" — pointing to his son — "will beat me when he is grown. It runs in the family." And there is the other story of the man being dragged out of the house by his son, who told him to stop at the doorway — for he had dragged his own father exactly that far, and no further.

Again, the more a man is given to calculated malice, the more unjust he is. Now the hot-tempered man is not given to calculated malice, and anger is not underhanded — it asserts itself in the open. Appetite is another matter: say of it what the poets say of Aphrodite, "craft-weaving daughter of Cyprus," or what Homer says of her embroidered belt,

  whose charm steals away the reason even of the wise.

If, then, this weakness of will is the more unjust, it is also more disgraceful than weakness of will about anger; it is weakness of will simply, and a sort of vice.

Again, when a man commits a wanton outrage he feels no pain in the doing — rather pleasure — while a man who acts in anger always feels pain as he acts. If, then, the acts that most justly rouse our indignation are the more unjust, it follows that weakness of will about appetite is the more unjust; for outrage of that kind is never committed in anger.

Plainly, then, weakness of will about appetite is more disgraceful than weakness of will about anger, and self-restraint and weakness of will proper are concerned with bodily appetites and pleasures.

But now let us look at the differences among these bodily appetites and pleasures.

As we said at the outset, some are human and natural in kind and degree; others are marks of a brutish nature; others still are the result of injury or disease.

Now, self-control and self-indulgence have to do only with the first class. That is why we call animals neither self-controlled nor self-indulgent — except metaphorically, if some whole species stands out from the rest by its lewdness and wantonness and greed. For there is no choice or deliberate calculation in what animals do; they stand outside the rational order, like madmen.

Brutishness is less dangerous than vice, but more horrifying. For in the brutish man the noble part is not corrupted, as it is in a man who is vicious in the merely human way — it is absent altogether. So asking which is worse is like comparing the inanimate with the animate: the badness of a thing that lacks the governing principle is always less destructive, and here reason is the governing principle — and the brutish man lacks it. It is like comparing injustice with an unjust man: each, in its own way, is the worse. For a bad man will do ten thousand times the harm a brute will.

Chapter 7

Now, with regard to the pleasures and pains of touch and taste, and the desires and aversions that go with them — the field we earlier marked out as belonging to self-indulgence and self-control — it is possible to be so disposed that you give in to temptations most people resist, or so disposed that you resist temptations most people give in to. Where pleasures are concerned, the first of these characters is the weak-willed man and the second the self-restrained; where pains are concerned, the first is soft and the second enduring. The character of most people lies between the two — leaning, perhaps, toward the worse.

Now some pleasures are necessary and others are not, and the necessary ones are necessary only in certain quantities — not too much and not too little; and the same holds of appetites and pains. The man who pursues pleasures beyond the pale of legitimate pleasure, or pursues any pleasure to excess, and does so by deliberate choice, for their own sake and not for any further consequence — that man is self-indulgent. Such a man is necessarily incapable of remorse, and therefore incurable; for the man who feels no remorse cannot be cured. At the opposite extreme is the man who falls short (while the man who hits the mean is self-controlled). The same goes for the man who runs away from bodily pains not because he is overcome in the moment, but by deliberate choice.

Of those who act without deliberate choice, one sort is driven by the pleasure itself, the other by the wish to escape the pain of unsatisfied desire; so we must distinguish these from one another.

But everyone would agree that a man who does something disgraceful with no desire, or only a mild one, is worse than one who does it under violent desire — that a man who strikes another in cold blood is worse than one who strikes in anger. For what would he do if passion had hold of him? That is why the self-indulgent man is worse than the weak-willed.

Of the characters we have described, then, we must mark off softness from self-indulgence.

The self-restrained character is the opposite of the weak-willed, and the enduring the opposite of the soft. For endurance means holding out, while self-restraint means overcoming — and holding out is a different thing from overcoming, as avoiding defeat is different from winning a victory. So self-restraint is the better of the two.

But the man who caves in where most men can and do hold out is soft and luxurious (for luxury, too, is a kind of softness) — the sort of man who lets his cloak trail on the ground rather than take the trouble to lift it, who plays the invalid without supposing himself wretched, though a wretched man is exactly what he is imitating.

It is the same with self-restraint and weakness of will. If a man gives way to pleasures or pains that are violent and excessive, we are not surprised; we are ready to forgive him, if he struggled — like Philoctetes bitten by the viper in Theodectes' play, or Cercyon in the Alope of Carcinus, or like people who try to hold in a laugh and burst out in one violent explosion, as happened to Xenophantus. What does surprise us is when a man gives way to, and cannot resist, what most people are able to hold out against — unless the cause is inherited constitution or disease, as softness is hereditary in the Scythian kings, and as the female is naturally softer than the male.

The man who is devoted to amusement is generally thought to be self-indulgent, but in fact he is soft. For amusement is relaxation, a rest from work; and the man devoted to amusement is one of those who take too much of it.

Weakness of will comes in two kinds: the impetuous and the weak. The weak deliberate, but under the influence of passion do not stand by the results of their deliberation; the impetuous are swept off by passion because they never deliberated at all. For — just as you cannot easily tickle a man who has just been tickling you — some people, when they see what is coming, forewarned, rouse themselves and their reason in advance, and so are able to resist the impulse, whether it is pleasant or painful. It is people of quick feeling, and people of a melancholic temperament, who are most prone to the impetuous kind of weakness of will: the first through the speed of their impressions, the second through their intensity, they do not wait to hear the voice of reason, but follow their imagination.

Chapter 8

Again, the self-indulgent man, as we said, is not given to remorse, for he stands by his deliberate choice; but the weak-willed man is always apt to feel remorse. So the matter does not stand as it was put in one of the difficulties we listed: the self-indulgent man is incurable, the weak-willed man curable. For full-formed vice is like diseases such as dropsy or consumption, while weakness of will is like epilepsy: the one is chronic badness, the other comes in fits.

Indeed, we may say flatly that weakness of will and vice are different in kind; for the vicious man does not know the nature of his acts, and the weak-willed man does.

Among the weak-willed themselves, those who momentarily lose their reason are not as bad as those who keep their reason and disobey it; for the latter give way to a slighter impulse, and cannot, like the former, claim to have acted without deliberating. The weak-willed man is like someone who gets drunk quickly and on little wine — on less than it takes most men.

We have seen that weakness of will is not vice; and yet in a manner of speaking it perhaps is. The difference is that the vicious man acts by deliberate choice and the weak-willed man against it. But for all that, their acts look alike — as Demodocus said against the Milesians: "The Milesians are not fools, but they do just what fools would do." So the weak-willed man is not unjust, but he will act unjustly.

It is the mark of the weak-willed man to pursue bodily pleasures that are excessive and contrary to right reason without being convinced that they are good; the self-indulgent man is convinced they are good, because it is his character to pursue them. The first, then, can easily be brought to a better mind; the second cannot. For virtue preserves the principle — the starting point — while vice destroys it; and in conduct the motive, the end we act for, is the principle of action, holding the same place here that the hypotheses hold in mathematics. In mathematics, no reasoning or proof can teach us the starting points; and here, likewise, it is not reasoning but virtue — whether natural or acquired by training — that teaches us to hold right opinions about the principle of action. The man of this character is self-controlled; the man of the opposite character is self-indulgent.

But there is a class of men who are apt to be momentarily carried out of their right senses by passion — swayed by it far enough that they do not act according to reason, but not so far that it has become part of their nature to believe such pleasures should be pursued without limit. These are the weak-willed. They are better than the self-indulgent, and not absolutely bad; for the best part of their nature, the principle of right conduct, still survives in them.

Opposed to them is another class of men: those who stand by their resolutions and are not carried out of their senses by passion, at least. From all this it is plain that self-restraint is a good state of character and weakness of will a bad one.

Chapter 9

Now, who is to be called self-restrained — the man who stands by reasoning of any kind and a choice of any kind, or only the man who stands by the right choice? And who is to be called weak-willed — the man who abandons any kind of choice and any kind of reasoning, or the man who abandons true reasoning and right choice? This was a difficulty we raised before. Is not the answer this: incidentally it may be any kind, but essentially it is true reasoning and right choice that the one stands by and the other abandons? For if you choose or pursue A for the sake of B, it is B that you pursue and choose essentially, and A only incidentally. And by "essentially" we mean "absolutely" or "simply." So in a certain sense it can be opinion of any kind; but speaking absolutely, it is true opinion that the one holds to and the other deserts.

But there is another class of people who are apt to stick to their opinions no matter what — the ones we call stubborn — because they resist persuasion and are not easily talked into changing their minds. These bear some resemblance to the self-restrained man, as the extravagant man resembles the generous one and the foolhardy man the courageous; but on many points they differ. What the self-restrained man refuses is to change his mind under pressure of passion and appetite; when occasion calls, he yields to reason readily enough. But it is precisely to reason that the stubborn man will not listen — and he is quite capable of conceiving a passion, and is often led around by his pleasures.

The opinionated, the ignorant, and the boorish are all stubborn — the opinionated from motives of pleasure and pain: they delight in the sense of victory when no argument can budge them, and are pained when their opinions are overruled, like decrees struck down. So they resemble the weak-willed man rather than the self-restrained.

Sometimes, too, people abandon their resolutions from something other than weakness of will — as Neoptolemus does in Sophocles' Philoctetes. Pleasure, it is true, was his motive for abandoning his resolution — but a noble pleasure; for telling the truth was a fine thing in his eyes, and Odysseus had persuaded him to lie. For not everyone who acts with pleasure as his motive is self-indulgent, or worthless, or weak-willed — only the man whose motive is a base pleasure.

Again, there is a character that takes too little delight in the pleasures of the body, and swerves from reason in that direction; and the self-restrained man stands between this character and the weak-willed. For the weak-willed swerve from reason through an excess, and these others through a deficiency, while the self-restrained man holds fast and is not pulled aside by either.

And if self-restraint is a good thing, both of the opposed characters must be bad — as in fact they evidently are. But since the second extreme shows itself rarely and in few people, weakness of will comes to be regarded as the only opposite of self-restraint, just as self-indulgence comes to be regarded as the only opposite of self-control.

We often apply names metaphorically, and so we come to speak — metaphorically — of the self-restraint of the self-controlled man. For both the self-restrained man and the self-controlled man are so made that they never do anything against reason for the sake of bodily pleasure; but the self-restrained man has bad desires and the self-controlled man has none; and the self-controlled man is so constituted that he takes no delight in anything contrary to reason, while the self-restrained man does take delight in such things — but is not carried away by them.

The weak-willed and the self-indulgent resemble each other too, though they are different: both pursue bodily pleasures, but the self-indulgent man pursues them on principle, and the weak-willed man does not.
