Book III

(Part 2 of 3)

Chapter 4

Wish, we have already said, is for the end. But here opinion splits: some hold that what we wish for is the good; others, that it is whatever seems good.

Each side runs into trouble. Those who say the object of wish is the good must admit that when a man chooses badly, what he wishes for is not an object of wish at all — for if it were, it would have to be good, and in fact it may be bad. Those who say the object of wish is whatever seems good must admit that nothing is an object of wish by nature; each man simply wishes for what seems good to him — and different things, even contrary things, seem good to different people.

Since neither answer quite satisfies us, perhaps we had better say this: the good, plainly and without qualification, is the real object of wish; but for each man, the object of wish is what seems good to him. The good man, then, wishes for what is really worth wishing for; what the bad man wishes for might be anything at all. It is just the same with the body: people in sound condition find healthy the things that really are healthy, while the sick find other things healthy — and likewise with what tastes bitter or sweet, what feels hot or heavy, and so on. For the good man judges each case correctly: in each case, what is true is what seems true to him.

Every trained capacity has its own special form of the noble and the pleasant, and perhaps nothing marks the good man so much as this: his power to discern these in each case, being himself, so to speak, their standard and measure.

What misleads most people is pleasure. It seems to be a good thing even when it is not. So they choose the pleasant as if it were good, and shun pain as if it were evil.

Chapter 5

We have seen that we wish for the end, while we deliberate about and choose the means to it.

Actions concerned with means, then, will be guided by choice, and so will be voluntary.

But the acts in which the virtues show themselves are concerned with means.

Therefore virtue is in our power — and so, equally, is vice. For where it is in our power to act, it is in our power not to act; where we can say no, we can say yes. If doing a deed is noble and lies with us, then not doing it, which is disgraceful, also lies with us; and if refraining is noble and lies with us, then doing it, which is disgraceful, lies with us too. But if doing and not doing noble or base deeds lies with us, and if this, as we found, is exactly what it is to be good or bad, then it follows that it lies with us to be decent men or worthless ones.

So the saying

  "No one is wicked by his own will, and no one is blessed against it"

is partly false and partly true. No one, certainly, is blessed against his will; but vice is voluntary.

If we deny this, we must quarrel with what we just established, and contend that a man is not the source and parent of his actions as he is of his children.

But if those statements stand, and we cannot trace our acts back to any sources other than ones within ourselves, then whatever has its source in us depends on us and is voluntary.

Everyday life bears witness to this, and so do the lawgivers. They correct and punish those who do evil — except where the act was done under compulsion, or through ignorance the agent was not responsible for — and they honor those who do noble deeds, plainly meaning to encourage the one and discourage the other. But no one encourages us to do what is not in our power and not voluntary: there would be no point in persuading a man not to feel heat or pain or hunger, since he will feel them all the same.

I say "ignorance the agent was not responsible for," because the law punishes the ignorance itself when the agent appears responsible for it. For an offense committed while drunk, the penalty is doubled: the source of the offense lies in the man himself, for he could have avoided getting drunk, and the drunkenness was the cause of his ignorance. Again, ignorance of any point of law that a man ought to know, and easily could know, does not save him from punishment. And so in other cases: where the ignorance seems to come from negligence, the offender is punished, since it lay with him to remove that ignorance — he could have taken the trouble.

But, someone may object, it was the man's character not to take the trouble.

We reply that men make themselves responsible for acquiring such a character, by living carelessly — for becoming unjust through repeated acts of wrongdoing, or self-indulgent through spending their days drinking and the like. It is repeated acts of a particular kind that produce a particular character.

You can see this in the way men train for any contest or performance: they practice, continually.

Not to know, then, that repeated acts of a given kind produce the corresponding character or habit is to show a complete want of sense.

Moreover, it is absurd to say that a man who acts unjustly does not wish to be unjust, or that a man who behaves self-indulgently does not wish to be self-indulgent.

If a man knowingly does the acts that must make him unjust, he is unjust voluntarily. It does not follow that if he wishes it, he can stop being unjust and become just — any more than a sick man can become well just by wishing it. He may be voluntarily sick, through living without restraint and disobeying his doctors. There was a time when it was open to him not to be sick; but once he has thrown his health away, that option is gone. When you have thrown a stone, you can no longer call it back — yet the throwing was in your power, for the start of its flight depended on you.

Just so, the unjust or the self-indulgent man was free at the beginning not to acquire this character, and therefore he is unjust or self-indulgent voluntarily; but now that he has acquired it, he is no longer free to put it off.

And it is not only vices of soul that are voluntary; bodily defects are sometimes voluntary too, and then we censure them. Nobody censures natural ugliness, but we do censure the ugliness that comes from negligence and want of exercise. So too with weakness and disability: no one would reproach a man born blind, or blinded by illness or a blow — we would sooner pity him; but everyone would censure a man who had blinded himself with heavy drinking or some other excess.

So among the defects of the body, it is those that depend on us that are censured, and those that do not depend on us that are not. And if that is so, then in other fields as well, the vices that get blamed must be the ones that depend on us.

Some people may still object.

"All men," they may say, "desire what appears good to them, but they have no control over how things appear. A man's character, whatever it is, decides what the end will look like to him."

I answer: if each man is in some way responsible for his own habits and character, then he must in some way be responsible for how things appear to him as well.

But suppose he is not. Then no man is responsible for, or the cause of, his own wrongdoing; he does evil through ignorance of the end, imagining that this is the way to secure the greatest good. On this view, aiming at the true end is not something we choose: a man must be born with a kind of eyesight, so to speak, if he is to judge rightly and choose what is really good. The truly wellborn man is the one whom nature has richly endowed with this gift — for it is the greatest and finest of gifts, one we cannot acquire or learn from anyone else but must keep all our lives just as nature gave it, and to be born well and nobly endowed in this respect would be wellborn in the truest and fullest sense.

Now, granting all that — how will virtue be any more voluntary than vice?

For whether it is nature or something else that fixes how the end appears, it is fixed the same way for both alike, for the good man and the bad; and both alike refer everything they do to it.

So take either horn. Say that it is not nature alone that decides how the end appears to each man, but that the man himself contributes something; or say that the end is fixed by nature, but virtue is voluntary because the good man voluntarily takes the steps toward it. Either way, vice will be exactly as voluntary as virtue; for the bad man's own self is just as active as the good man's — in choosing the particular acts, at least, if not in setting the end.

If, then, the virtues are voluntary, as everyone allows (for we do in fact help to make our own character, and by being of a certain character we give a certain cast to the end as we see it), then the vices must be voluntary too. Everything we have said applies equally to them.

We have now described in outline the nature of the virtues in general: they are modes of observing the mean; they are habits — trained states of character; they show themselves in the very acts that produce them; they depend on us and are voluntary; and they follow the guidance of right reason. But our particular acts are not voluntary in the same sense our habits are. We are masters of our acts from beginning to end, so long as we know the particular circumstances; but of our habits we are masters only of the beginnings — their growth creeps forward step by step, imperceptibly, like the growth of a disease. Still, since it lay with us to use our powers this way or not, the characters that result are on that account voluntary.

Now let us take up the virtues one by one and say what each is, what its field is, and how it operates there; in doing so we shall also see how many there are. And first, courage.

The Several Moral Virtues and Vices

Chapter 6

We have already said that courage is observance of the mean with respect to feelings of fear and confidence.

Now, fear is plainly excited by fearful things, and these are, roughly speaking, evils; which is why fear is sometimes defined as "expectation of evil."

Fear, then, is excited by evil of every kind — disgrace, poverty, disease, friendlessness, death — but not every kind gives scope for courage. Some things we actually ought to fear; fearing them is noble and not fearing them is base. Disgrace is one: the man who fears disgrace is honorable, a man with a proper sense of shame, while the man who does not fear it is shameless (though some stretch the word "courageous" to cover him, since he does bear a certain resemblance to the courageous man — courage also being a kind of fearlessness). Poverty, on the other hand, we probably ought not to fear, nor disease, nor in general the things that do not come from vice and do not depend on us. But being fearless about these is still not courage in the strict sense — though here too the term gets applied by resemblance. There are men, for instance, who are cowards in the dangers of war and yet free and bold in spending money.

Nor, on the other side, is a man a coward for fearing violence to his children or his wife, or for dreading envy and things of that kind; nor is he courageous for staying calm at the prospect of a whipping.

In what terrors, then, does the courageous man show his quality? Surely in the greatest, for no one is better able to endure what is terrible. And of all things the most terrible is death; for death is our limit, and once a man is dead it seems there is no longer anything, good or evil, for him.

Yet even death, it seems, does not give scope for courage on every occasion — not death by drowning, say, or by disease.

On what occasions, then? Surely the noblest; and those are the occasions of war, for they carry the greatest and the noblest danger.

The honors paid to courage in free states and by monarchs confirm this.

Strictly speaking, then, the courageous man is the one who fearlessly faces an honorable death, and all the sudden emergencies that threaten death — and such emergencies come mostly in war.

Of course the courageous man is fearless in illness too, and at sea — but not in the sailors' way. The sailors, because of their experience, stay hopeful while the landsmen have already despaired of their lives and shudder at the thought of such a death.

Besides, the occasions that especially call out courage are those where a man can show his prowess, or where death is noble; and in these deaths there is neither nobility nor any room for prowess.

Chapter 7

The same things do not frighten all men alike; yet we do speak of some terrors as beyond human power to face. Such things frighten every man in his senses. But the terrors a man can face differ in size — some are greater, some less fearful — and the same goes for the things that inspire confidence. Now, the courageous man keeps his presence of mind, so far as a man can. He will fear these fearful things, yes; but he will endure them as he ought and as reason bids, for the sake of the noble — for that is the end and aim of virtue.

But it is possible to fear these things too much or too little, and possible, too, to take as fearful what really is not. So men go wrong sometimes by fearing the wrong things, sometimes by fearing in the wrong manner or at the wrong time, and so on.

And all of this applies equally to the things that inspire confidence.

The man, then, who endures and fears what he ought, from the right motive, in the right manner, and at the right time — and who feels confidence the same way — is courageous.

For the courageous man governs both his feeling and his action by the merits of each case, as reason bids.

Now, the end of every exercise of a habit is the same as the end of the habit itself.

To the courageous man, courage is essentially a noble thing.

Therefore its end is noble too; for everything takes its character from its end.

It is for the sake of the noble, then, that the courageous man endures and acts courageously in each particular case.

As for the characters that run to excess: the man who exceeds in fearlessness has no name (this often happens, as we said before) — but a man would have to be a maniac, or numb to pain, to fear nothing at all, not even earthquakes and breaking waves, as they say is true of the Celts.

The man who is overconfident in the face of fearful things is called foolhardy. But the foolhardy man is generally thought to be really a braggart, putting on a courage he does not have. What he wants is to seem, in the presence of danger, what the courageous man actually is; so he imitates him where he can. That is why your foolhardy man is usually a coward underneath: he blusters as long as it is safe, and turns tail when real danger comes.

The man who fears too much is a coward: he fears what he ought not, and as he ought not, and so through the whole list.

He is deficient in confidence as well; but what shows most is his excess of fear in the presence of pain.

The coward is also despondent, for he is frightened of everything. The courageous man is just the opposite: confidence implies hopefulness.

So the coward, the foolhardy man, and the courageous man all show their characters in the same circumstances, but behave differently in them. The first two exceed or fall short, while the courageous man holds the mean and behaves as he ought; and the foolhardy are headlong and eager before the danger comes but fall away in its presence, while the courageous are keen in the act and quiet beforehand.

Courage, then, as we have said, is observance of the mean with regard to things that excite confidence or fear, in the circumstances we have specified; and it chooses its course and holds its post because doing so is noble, or because failing to is disgraceful.

But to seek death as a refuge from poverty, or from love, or from anything painful, is not the act of a brave man but of a coward. It is softness to run from hardship; and a man who dies this way accepts death not because it is noble, but simply to escape an evil.
