Book IV

(Part 1 of 3)

The Several Moral Virtues and Vices—Continued

Chapter 1

Generosity, which we will take up next, seems to be moderation in the matter of wealth. What we praise in a generous man is not his conduct in war, nor in the situations where self-control is praised, nor in passing judgment, but his conduct in the giving and taking of wealth — and above all in the giving. By "wealth" we mean everything whose value can be measured in money.

Extravagance and stinginess are the excess and the deficiency in the matter of wealth.

Stinginess always means caring about money more than one should. Extravagance, on the other hand, sometimes stands for a whole bundle of vices at once. Weak-willed people who blow their money on wild living get called extravagant, and so the extravagant are held to be thoroughly worthless characters, since they combine several vices in one.

But we should note that this is not the proper use of the term. The word itself — asotos, literally "a lost man" — is meant to pick out a man with one particular vice: wasting his own substance. He is "lost" because he is being destroyed by his own fault, and wasting what you live on is a kind of self-destruction, since your life depends on it. This, then, is what we will take "extravagance" properly to mean.

Now, anything that has a use can be used well or badly.

Riches are simply an abundance of useful things.

But each thing is best used by the man who has the virtue that deals with that thing.

Therefore riches will be best used by the man who has the virtue that deals with wealth — that is, the generous man.

The ways of using wealth are spending and giving; taking and keeping are rather ways of acquiring it. So it is more distinctive of the generous man to give to the right people than to take from the right sources and refuse the wrong ones. For it is more distinctive of virtue to do good than to have good done to you, and to act nobly than merely to avoid acting basely; and clearly doing good and acting nobly go with the giving, while receiving good and not acting basely go with the taking.

Again, our gratitude goes to the man who gives, not to the man who declines to take — and so does our praise.

Again, it is easier not to take than to give; we are more reluctant to part with what is our own than to take what belongs to someone else.

Again, it is the givers who are commonly called generous. Those who merely abstain from taking are praised not so much for generosity as for justice, and those who take are not praised at all.

Again, of all virtuous characters the generous man is perhaps the best loved, because he is useful — and his usefulness lies in his giving.

Now, virtuous acts, as we said, are noble and are done for the sake of the noble. The generous man, then, like the others, will give for the sake of the noble, and give rightly: to the right people, to the right extent, and at the right time — in short, his giving will have every mark of right giving.

Moreover, he will give with pleasure, or at least without pain; for virtuous acts are always pleasant or painless — certainly they are never painful.

The man who gives to the wrong people, or gives from some motive other than love of the noble, is not generous; he must be called something else.

Nor is the man generous who gives with a pained heart; that shows he would rather have the money than the noble act — which is not how the generous man feels.

Nor will the generous man take from the wrong sources; that kind of taking does not fit a man who sets no store by wealth.

Nor is he quick to ask favors; a man in the habit of doing good to others is not usually in a hurry to have good done to him.

But from the right sources he will take — from his own property, for instance — not because there is anything noble in taking, but simply because he cannot give without it. For the same reason he will not neglect his property, since he means to use it to help people. And he will refuse to give to just anyone who comes along, precisely so that he has something left to give to the right people, at the right times, and where giving is noble.

It is very characteristic of the generous man to overshoot in giving, leaving too little for himself; disregard of self is part of the character.

In calling a man generous we must take his means into account. What makes a gift generous is not the size of the sum but the settled character of the giver, and that character scales the gift to the giver's fortune. So it is entirely possible that the man who gives the smaller amount is the more generous man, if he gives it out of less.

Those who inherit a fortune seem to be more generous than those who made one. For one thing, they have never known want; for another, everyone is especially fond of what they made themselves — look at parents, or poets.

It is not easy for a generous man to be rich. He is not inclined to take or to keep, but very inclined to spend, and he values money not for its own sake but only for the sake of giving it away.

Hence the complaint people bring against fortune, that the men who most deserve wealth have the least of it. But this is natural enough: you cannot have wealth without taking trouble over it, any more than you can have anything else.

Still, the generous man will not give to the wrong people or at the wrong times. If he did, he would no longer be practicing generosity, and having spent that way he would not have enough left for the occasions that matter. For, as we said, the generous man is the one who spends in proportion to his fortune and on the proper objects; the man who overshoots that measure is extravagant. This is why kings are not called extravagant: it is not easy for them to outrun their possessions with their gifts and expenses.

Generosity, then, being moderation in the giving and taking of wealth, the generous man will give and spend the proper amounts on the proper objects, in small matters and great alike, and do it with pleasure; and he will also take the proper amounts from the proper sources. For since the virtue is moderation in both giving and taking, the man who has it will do both rightly. Right taking goes together with right giving; any other kind of taking is contrary to it. The givings and takings that go together are found in the same person; those that are contrary to each other obviously are not.

If it happens that the generous man spends something in a way that is contrary to what is right and noble, he will be pained — but moderately and in due measure; for it is characteristic of virtue to feel pleasure and pain on the right occasions and in due measure.

The generous man is also easy to deal with in money matters. He is not hard to cheat, since he does not value wealth, and he is more likely to be annoyed at having failed to spend where he ought than pained at having spent where he ought not — the sort of man Simonides would not approve of.

The extravagant man goes wrong on these points too: he is neither pleased nor pained on the right occasions or in the right way. But this will become clearer as we go on.

We have already said that extravagance and stinginess are each an excess and a deficiency at once, and in two things: giving and taking (spending counts under giving). Extravagance exceeds in giving and in not taking, and falls short in taking; stinginess falls short in giving and exceeds in taking — in small things, we should add.

Now, the two sides of extravagance are not often combined in one person. It is not easy to keep giving forever while never taking: private citizens soon run through their means, and it is private citizens who generally get the name.

The extravagant man of this pure type — the one who combines both elements — should be considered a good deal better than a stingy man. He is easily cured, both by age and by running out of money, and he can be brought round to the middle course. For he has the essentials of the generous character: he gives and he does not take; he simply does neither well nor as he ought. If he can be trained to that, or changed in any other way, he will be generous — he will give to the right people and refuse to take from the wrong sources. This is why he is generally felt to be no bad character: going too far in giving and in not taking is the mark not of a vicious or ignoble nature, but of a foolish one.

The extravagant man of this sort, then, seems much better than the stingy man — for the reasons just given, and also because he does good to many people, while the stingy man does good to no one, not even himself.

But most extravagant men, as we said, not only give wrongly but take from the wrong sources, and in that respect they are stingy. They become grasping because they want to spend and cannot easily do it — their supplies run out fast — and so they are forced to draw from elsewhere. And since they care nothing for the noble, they take quite recklessly, from any source at all; they long to give, and it does not matter a whit to them how the money goes or where it comes from.

That is why their gifts are not generous. They are not noble, not given for the sake of the noble, not given in the right way. Sometimes such men make rich the very people who ought to be poor: they will give nothing to a man of sober character, and a great deal to flatterers or to anyone who supplies them some other pleasure. Hence most of them are dissolute as well: being quick to part with money, they lavish it on wild living, and since they do not shape their lives toward the noble, they slide easily into the pursuit of pleasure.

The extravagant man, then, if he finds no guidance, ends up here; but with training he can be brought to the moderate and right course.

Stinginess, by contrast, is incurable — old age, and every kind of decline, seems to make men stingy.

It also runs deeper in human nature than extravagance: most men are fonder of money than of giving.

And it reaches far and comes in many forms, for there are, it seems, many ways of being stingy.

It has two parts — falling short in giving and going too far in taking — but it does not always appear whole; sometimes the parts come separately, one man exceeding in taking while another falls short in giving. All those who go by names like the tightwad, the skinflint, the miser fall short in giving, but they do not covet other people's goods or want to take them.

Some are driven to this by a kind of honesty — a wish to steer clear of disgrace. Some of them seem to save (or at least say they save) precisely so that they will never be forced to do anything disgraceful; the "cheeseparer" belongs here, along with his kind, so named for the extreme lengths to which he carries his refusal to give.

Others keep their hands off their neighbors' goods only out of fear, reckoning that it is no easy thing to take other people's property without having your own taken in turn; so they settle for neither taking nor giving.

Others again go to such excess in taking that they will make any gain they can, from anywhere at all — those who ply degrading trades, brothel-keepers and the like, and loan sharks who lend small sums at steep rates. All of these make money from the wrong sources and beyond the right amount.

What these last have in common is plainly the pursuit of base gain: every one of them puts up with disgrace for the sake of gain, and a small gain at that. For the men who make improper gains improperly on a grand scale are not called stingy — tyrants who sack cities and strip temples are called wicked, impious, unjust instead. But the dice-sharper, the man who steals clothes at the baths, the common thief — these count among the stingy, for they all make base gains. Both the thief and the sharper ply their trade for gain and endure disgrace for it: the thief runs the gravest dangers for his haul, and the sharper makes his profit off his friends — the very people he ought to be giving to. Both, then, wanting to make gain in the wrong ways, are seekers of base gain; and all money-making of this sort is stingy.

Stinginess is rightly named as the opposite of generosity. It is a worse evil than extravagance, and men more often go wrong in this direction than in the extravagance we described.

Let this stand, then, as our account of generosity and of the vices opposed to it.

Chapter 2

Our next task, it seems, is to examine magnificence, for this too appears to be a virtue concerned with wealth.

But it does not, like generosity, cover the whole field of money dealings — only those involving large outlays; and there it goes beyond generosity in sheer scale. For as the name itself suggests (megaloprepeia — "greatness that fits"), magnificence is suitable expenditure on a grand scale. But grandeur is relative: what it is suitable to spend when you are fitting out a warship is not what it is suitable to spend when you are heading a sacred embassy.

What is suitable, then, is relative to the person, the occasion, and the business at hand. And the man who spends fittingly on trivial or middling occasions is not called magnificent — the man who can say, with the poet,

  To many a wandering beggar did I give —

but rather the man who spends fittingly on great ones. The magnificent man is generous; but a man may be generous without being magnificent.

The deficiency of this quality is called cheapness; the excess is called vulgarity, bad taste, and so on. The mark of the excess is not spending too much on the proper objects, but making a show — spending on the wrong objects and in the wrong style. We will come to these presently.

The magnificent man is like a skilled artist: he can see what an occasion calls for, and he can spend great sums with taste. For as we said at the outset, a habit — a settled state of character — takes its stamp from the acts it issues in and the things it produces. The magnificent man's outlays, then, must be great and suitable.

And what he produces must be of the same kind, for only so will the expense be at once great and suited to the result.

The result, then, must be worthy of the expenditure, and the expenditure worthy of the result — or even beyond it.

Moreover, the magnificent man's motive in spending this way will be love of the noble; for that is common to all the virtues.

Further, he will spend gladly and with a free hand, since minute reckoning of costs is cheap. He will ask how the work can be made most beautiful and most fitting, not what it will cost and how it can be done most cheaply.

So the magnificent man must be generous as well. The generous man, too, will spend the right amount in the right manner; but it is in the "great" of great and right that magnificence goes beyond generosity, which deals with the same field — I mean in the actual size of the sum spent. And further, where the sum spent is the same, the magnificent man's outlay will produce the more magnificent result.

For the excellence of a possession is not the same as the excellence of a work. As a possession, the most prized thing is what is worth most — gold, say; as a work, what is great and beautiful, for the sight of such a work stirs admiration, and what is magnificent is always admirable. Excellence of work on a great scale — that is magnificence.

Now, there is a class of expenditures we call honorable in a special sense: expenditure on the worship of the gods — offerings, temples, sacrifices — and likewise everything spent on the worship of heroes, and again every public service undertaken from noble ambition, as when a man decides to equip a chorus or a warship, or to give a public feast, in handsome style.

But in every case, as we have said, we must look at the spender — who he is and what he has — for the outlay should be proportionate to the circumstances, and should suit not only the result but the man behind it.

Hence a poor man cannot be magnificent. He lacks the means to spend great sums suitably, and if he tries he is a fool, for he spends out of proportion and in the wrong way, and an act must be done in the right way to be virtuous. Such spending befits those who have the means — earned by their own efforts, or through ancestors or connections — and who have birth, reputation, and the rest; for all these things carry a certain greatness and standing with them.

The magnificent man, then, is properly a man of this sort, and magnificence shows itself most properly in expenditures of the kind we named, for these are the greatest and most honorable. But it can also appear on private occasions — the kind that come once in a lifetime, like a wedding; or the kind that concern the whole city or its leading men, like receiving foreign guests and sending them on their way, or giving them presents and returning theirs. For the magnificent man lavishes money not on himself but on public objects, and gifts to strangers have something in common with offerings to the gods.

A magnificent man will also build his house in a style that matches his wealth, for even a fine house is a kind of public ornament. And he will spend more readily on things that last, since these are the noblest. And on each occasion he will spend what suits it — for what suits the gods is not what suits men, nor a temple what suits a tomb.

And since every expenditure can be great of its kind — great outlay on a great occasion being the most magnificent, and after that what is great for the particular occasion, whatever it is — and since the greatness of the result is not the same as the greatness of the expense (the most beautiful ball or the most beautiful flask that can be had is a magnificent present for a child, though its price is small and cheap), it follows that it is the mark of the magnificent man to do magnificently whatever he does, of whatever kind — for work of that sort is not easily surpassed — and to produce a result worthy of the expense.

Such, then, is the magnificent man. The man of excess — the vulgar man, as we call him — exceeds, as we said, by spending improperly. He spends great sums on small occasions and makes an unseemly show of it: entertaining his club members with a wedding feast; bringing his chorus onstage in purple, when he is producing a comedy, as they did at Mesara. And he will do all this not from any love of the noble and beautiful, but to parade his wealth, hoping to be admired for it — spending little where he ought to spend much, and much where he ought to spend little.

The cheap man, on the other hand, will fall short on every occasion. Even when he is spending very large sums, he will ruin the beauty of the whole by penny-pinching over a trifle — never doing anything without hesitating over it, forever calculating how it can be done for the least possible cost, groaning even at that, and convinced that everything he does is on a needlessly grand scale.

Both these characters, then, are vicious; but they do not bring disgrace, since they neither harm anyone else nor are especially offensive in themselves.
