Book VI

(Part 2 of 2)

Chapter 9

Inquiry and deliberation are not the same thing: deliberation is one particular kind of inquiry. But we need to pin down what good deliberation is — is it a kind of science, or opinion, or lucky guessing, or something else altogether?

It is not science; for we do not inquire into what we already know, and good deliberation is a kind of deliberation, and when we deliberate we are inquiring and calculating.

Nor is it lucky guessing. A lucky guess comes without calculation and in a flash, while deliberation takes time. Hence the common saying: be quick to carry out what you have decided, but slow to decide.

Shrewdness, again — quickness at hitting the mark — is not the same as good deliberation; shrewdness is a kind of lucky guessing.

Nor is good deliberation any sort of opinion.

But consider: when we deliberate badly we go wrong, and when we deliberate well we go right. So good deliberation is clearly some kind of correctness — but a correctness neither of science nor of opinion. Science does not admit of correctness, since it does not admit of error; and correctness of opinion is simply truth. Besides, whatever we hold an opinion about is already something settled.

Still, good deliberation is impossible without calculation.

The only option left, then, is that it is correctness of thinking; for thinking is not yet assertion. Opinion is not an inquiry but already a definite assertion, whereas a person deliberating — well or badly — is still inquiring and calculating.

But good deliberation is a certain correctness in deliberation; so we must first ask what deliberation is and what its field is.

Now, correctness comes in several kinds, and clearly not every kind of correctness in deliberating counts as deliberating well. A weak-willed man or a bad man may, by careful calculation, arrive at exactly the goal he has set himself; he will have deliberated correctly, though what he wins is a great evil. But to have deliberated well is thought to be a good thing; so it is only one particular kind of correctness in deliberation that is called good deliberation — the kind that arrives at what is good.

Further, one can reach what is good by a false syllogism: reach the right conclusion about what to do, but by the wrong route and on the wrong grounds, the middle term being false. So this too falls short of good deliberation — hitting the right conclusion, but not on the right grounds.

Again, one man may reach the right conclusion only slowly, another quickly. So we must add one more condition: good deliberation means coming to a right conclusion about what is useful and ought to be done, and coming to it in the right manner and at the right time.

Once more, we speak of deliberating well without qualification, and of deliberating well toward a particular kind of end. Good deliberation without qualification is what reaches right conclusions about the means to the end without qualification; a particular kind of good deliberation is what reaches right conclusions about the means to a particular kind of end. And so, since deliberating well is the mark of people of practical wisdom, good deliberation will be correctness in judging what is useful toward that end of which practical wisdom has a true conception.

Chapter 10

Understanding — the faculty we credit when we call someone understanding, or a person of good understanding — is not the same as science in general, nor as opinion (if it were, everyone would have it). Nor is it identical with any particular science, the way medicine deals with health or geometry with magnitudes. For understanding is not concerned with what is eternal and unchangeable, nor with events of every kind, but only with the things a person might puzzle over and deliberate about. Its field, then, is the same as that of practical wisdom; but the two are not identical. Practical wisdom issues orders — its business is what should be done or not done — while understanding only judges. (Understanding and good understanding come to the same thing here; so do a man of understanding and a man of good understanding.)

Understanding is neither the possession of practical wisdom nor its acquisition. Rather, just as a student is said to show understanding when he puts to use the science he hears from his teacher, so in the sphere of practical wisdom a man shows understanding when he uses the opinions he hears from others to judge — and to judge fittingly; for judging "well" here means judging fittingly.

And it is from this use of the word in learning that its use for the faculty comes — the faculty we mean when we call a man understanding; for we often speak of a learner's understanding.

Chapter 11

Judgment — what we mean when we speak of a considerate judge of others, or say that a man "has judgment" — is the correct discernment of what is equitable. Here is the sign: we think the equitable man above all shows consideration in his judgments, and we count it equitable to judge some things with consideration. And consideration — sympathetic judgment — is simply judgment that correctly discerns what is equitable; and correctly means truly.

Now all these formed faculties we have listed tend, naturally enough, toward the same point. We apply all of these terms — judgment, understanding, practical wisdom, intuitive reason — to the same people: we say that someone has, by a certain age, come to have judgment and reason, and is practically wise and understanding. For all four faculties deal with ultimate and particular facts; and it is by having discernment in the matters practical wisdom deals with that a person counts as understanding, or as a man of sound or considerate judgment — for the equitable is common ground in all our good dealings with others.

But what is to be done is always some particular thing, something ultimate. Knowing it, as we have seen, is the business of the man of practical wisdom; and understanding and judgment also concern things to be done — that is, ultimates.

And the intuitive reason — the last of the four — also deals with ultimates, in both senses of the word. Both first principles and ultimate particular facts are grasped by intuitive reason, not by demonstration. On one side, in demonstrations, reason grasps the unalterable first principles; on the other, in practical calculations, reason grasps the ultimate, alterable fact — the minor premise of the practical syllogism. These particular facts are our starting points for the end we aim at: the universal principle is drawn out of the particulars. Of these particulars, then, we must have a kind of perception — and this perception is intuitive reason.

That is why these faculties are thought to be natural gifts: nature never makes a man wise, but she does, we think, endow men with judgment, understanding, and reason. A sign of this is that we believe these powers keep pace with our time of life — that a certain age brings reason and judgment — as though nature were the cause.

(Intuitive reason, then, is both beginning and end; for demonstrations start from these ultimate truths and terminate in them.)

And on this account we ought to attend to the undemonstrated sayings and opinions of experienced and older people, and of people of practical wisdom, no less than to their demonstrations. Experience has given them an eye, and with it they see correctly.

We have now said, then, what practical wisdom is and what wisdom is, what each deals with, and that each is the virtue of a different part of the soul.

Chapter 12

But here an objection may be raised. "What is the use of them?" someone may ask. Wisdom does not study any of the things that make a man happy, for it does not ask how anything comes about. Practical wisdom does — but why do we need it? Practical wisdom deals with what is just and noble and good for man, the very things it is the good man's part to do; but knowing them makes us no readier to do them, if — as we have said — the virtues are habits. It is just as with things called "healthy" or "sound," meaning not what produces health but what flows from a healthy state: knowing medicine or gymnastics makes us no more able to do such things.

"But suppose we say a man should have practical wisdom not in order to do these acts but in order to become able to do them — then it is of no use to those who are already good, nor even to those who are not. For it will not matter whether they have it themselves or take the advice of others who do. It will be enough to do here as we do about health: we wish to be healthy, but we do not go off and learn medicine.

"Again, it seems strange that practical wisdom, though inferior to wisdom, should nevertheless govern it — since in every field the practical faculty holds sway and issues orders."

We must now take up these points; so far we have only been stating the difficulties.

First, then, we may say that both practical wisdom and wisdom must be desirable in themselves, since each is the virtue of one of the parts of the soul — even if neither of them produced anything at all.

Next, they do produce something.

On one hand, wisdom produces happiness — not in the sense in which medicine produces health, but in the sense in which health produces health. Wisdom is a part of complete virtue, and so possessing it and exercising it make a man happy.

On the other hand, in the sphere of action, a man performs his function perfectly when he acts in accordance with both practical wisdom and moral virtue: virtue makes the end we aim at right, and practical wisdom makes the means to it right.

(The fourth part of the soul, the vegetative part or faculty of nutrition, has no such virtue; for nothing is in its power to do or not to do.)

As for the objection that practical wisdom makes us no readier to do what is noble and just, let us go a little deeper, beginning here.

We allow, on one hand, that some who do just acts are not yet just — those, for instance, who do what the laws command unwillingly, or in ignorance, or for some outside motive and not for the sake of the acts themselves (though they do what they ought, and everything a good man should do). And on the other hand, it seems that when a man does the several acts in a certain condition, he is good — namely, when he does them from choice, and for the sake of the acts themselves.

Now, virtue makes the choice right; but deciding what should be done to carry out the choice belongs not to virtue but to another power. Let us dwell on this point a moment and make it quite clear.

There is a power we call cleverness: the power to carry out the steps toward any proposed goal and so achieve it. If the goal is noble, the power deserves praise; if the goal is base, it is the power of a villain. That is why we call both the practically wise man and the villain clever.

Practical wisdom is not this power, but it does not exist without it. And this eye of the soul, as we may call it, does not reach its full development without moral virtue — as we said before, and as can be shown. All syllogisms about what is to be done have as their starting point "the end — the supreme good — is such-and-such" (whatever it may be; any definition of the good will do for the argument). But it is only to the good man that this appears as the good; for vice perverts us and makes us go wrong about the starting points of action. So it is plain, as we said, that it is impossible to be practically wise without being morally good.

Chapter 13

This suggests a further look at moral virtue; for here the case is closely parallel. As practical wisdom stands to cleverness — not identical, but closely akin — so full virtue stands to natural virtue.

Everyone grants that in a certain sense the several types of character are given us by nature: justice, a tendency toward self-control, courage, and the rest show themselves from the moment of birth. Nevertheless we look for developed goodness as something different from this, and expect to find these same qualities in another form. Even children and animals have the natural dispositions, but without reason to guide them these are plainly harmful. This much, at least, seems clear: just as a strong-bodied creature that cannot see stumbles heavily when it moves, because it has no sight, so it is with natural virtue. But when it is lit by reason, it acts surpassingly well; and the natural virtue — which before only resembled virtue — will then be virtue fully developed.

We find, then, that just as the calculative part has two forms, cleverness and practical wisdom, so the moral qualities have two forms, natural virtue and full virtue — and full virtue is impossible without practical wisdom.

That is why some people say that all the virtues are forms of practical wisdom — why Socrates in particular held this view. His inquiry was partly right and partly wrong: wrong in thinking that all the virtues actually are forms of practical wisdom, right in saying that they are impossible without it.

Here is a confirmation: even now, everyone defining virtue will name its field and then add that it is a habit "in accordance with right reason" — and right means in accordance with practical wisdom. Everyone, it seems, has a sort of inkling that a settled character of this kind — one in accordance with practical wisdom — is virtue.

Only a slight change is needed in the expression. Virtue is not simply a habit in accordance with right reason, but a habit that implies right reason. And right reason in these matters is practical wisdom.

So where Socrates held that the virtues are forms of reason (for he held they are all modes of knowledge), we hold that they imply reason.

It is evident, then, from what has been said, that it is not possible to be good in the strict sense without practical wisdom, nor practically wise without moral virtue. And this lets us answer an objection that may be urged. "The virtues," someone may say, "are found apart from each other: a man strongly disposed by nature to one virtue is not equally drawn to all the others, so he will have acquired this one while he still lacks that one." We may answer that this can happen with the natural virtues, but not with the virtues that make a man good without qualification. For where the single virtue of practical wisdom is present, all the moral virtues are present with it.

And so it is plain, first, that even if it did not bear on practice, we would still need practical wisdom, because it is the virtue of a part of our nature; and second, that choice cannot be right without both practical wisdom and moral virtue — for virtue makes us desire the end, and practical wisdom makes us take the right means to it.

Nevertheless, practical wisdom is not the mistress of wisdom or of the better part of our nature, any more than medicine is the mistress of health. Practical wisdom does not employ wisdom in its service; it provides for wisdom's coming to be. It does not rule wisdom, but rules in wisdom's interest. To assert the contrary would be like asserting that statesmanship rules the gods, because it issues orders about everything in the city — including the worship of the gods.
