Book II

(Part 1 of 2)

Moral Virtue

Chapter 1

Virtue, then, is of two kinds, intellectual and moral. Intellectual virtue owes its birth and growth mainly to teaching, and so it needs time and experience. Moral virtue is the product of habit — which is why its Greek name, êthikê, is a slight variation on ethos, the word for habit.

From this one thing is immediately plain: none of the moral virtues is implanted in us by nature, for nothing that exists by nature can be trained to behave otherwise. A stone naturally falls downward, and you could not train it to rise — not if you threw it up ten thousand times. You could not train fire to burn downward. Nothing that naturally behaves one way can be habituated into behaving another.

The virtues, then, come neither by nature nor against nature. Nature gives us the capacity to acquire them, and training develops that capacity.

Again, wherever nature gives us something, we get the power first and put it to use afterward. The senses show this clearly: we did not acquire sight by repeatedly seeing, or hearing by repeatedly hearing. On the contrary, we had the powers first and then used them; we did not get them by using them. But the virtues we acquire by first doing the acts — just as with the crafts. We learn a craft by doing the very thing we will do once we have learned it: we become builders by building, and lyre-players by playing the lyre. And so by doing just acts we become just, and by doing self-controlled and courageous acts we become self-controlled and courageous.

What happens in cities attests to this. Legislators make their citizens good by training them — at least, that is every legislator's wish; those who fail at it miss their mark, and this is what separates a good constitution from a bad one.

Again, every virtue is produced, and every vice too, by the same acts — and here also the crafts run parallel. Playing the lyre produces good lyre-players and bad ones alike; so with builders and all the rest: build well and you become a good builder, build badly and you become a bad one. If it were otherwise, nobody would need a teacher; we would all simply be born good or bad at our trades. And it is exactly the same with the virtues. By how we act in our dealings with other people, we become just or unjust; by how we act in the face of danger, training ourselves to feel fear or confidence, we become courageous or cowardly. So too with our appetites and with anger: by behaving one way or the other in the situations that engage these feelings, some of us become self-controlled and even-tempered, others self-indulgent and hot-tempered. In a word: acts of a certain kind produce habits of the same kind.

Hence we must make sure our acts are of the right kind, for as the acts vary, so does the character that results. It makes no small difference, then, whether a person is trained from youth in this way or in that — it makes a great difference; or rather, it makes all the difference.

Chapter 2

But our present inquiry does not aim, as our others do, at theory alone. We are not asking what virtue is merely in order to know; we are asking in order to become good — otherwise the inquiry would profit us nothing. So we must examine acts and ask what kind they should be; for, as we said, it is the acts that determine the character that results.

First, then, that our acts must accord with right reason is common ground, and we shall take it for granted here, postponing for later the question of what right reason is and how it relates to the other virtues.

But let one thing be understood before we go on: all reasoning about practical matters can be given in outline only, not with scientific exactness. As we said at the start, the precision to demand depends on the subject in hand, and in matters of conduct and questions of what is useful there are no fixed laws — any more than in questions of health.

And if our general account is inexact, our reasoning about particular cases is more inexact still. Particular cases fall under no science and no book of rules; the agent must always look at the occasion itself and judge what it requires, just as in medicine or in navigation.

Still, though this is so, we must try to give what help we can.

First, then, observe that in matters of this kind, falling short and going too far are equally fatal. This is plain — to illustrate what we cannot see by what we can — in the case of strength and health. Too much exercise destroys strength, and so does too little; too much food and drink ruins health, and so does too little — while the fitting amount produces, increases, and preserves them. It is just the same with self-control and courage and the other virtues. The man who runs from everything, fears everything, and never makes a stand becomes a coward; the man who fears nothing at all and will walk into anything becomes foolhardy. Likewise the man who takes his fill of every pleasure and abstains from none becomes self-indulgent, while the man who shuns every pleasure — a "boor," as we say — ends up insensible, dead to enjoyment. Self-control and courage, then, are destroyed by excess and by deficiency, and preserved by the mean.

But habits are not only produced, preserved, and destroyed by the same occasions and the same means; they also show themselves in the same circumstances. This is how it works with something as palpable as strength. Strength is produced by taking plenty of nourishment and doing plenty of hard work — and it is the strong man who is most capable of doing exactly that. So it is with the virtues: by abstaining from pleasures we become self-controlled, and once we have become self-controlled we are best able to abstain. And so with courage: by habituating ourselves to make light of danger and to stand up to it, we become courageous; and once we have become courageous, we are best able to face danger.

Chapter 3

The pleasure or pain that accompanies a man's acts should be taken as the test of his formed character.

The man who abstains from bodily pleasures and enjoys the abstaining is self-controlled; the man who abstains but resents it is self-indulgent. The man who faces danger with pleasure — or at least without pain — is courageous; the man to whom it is painful is a coward.

For moral virtue is deeply concerned with pleasures and pains. It is pleasure that moves us to do what is base, and pain that moves us to shrink from what is noble. That is why, as Plato says, we need to be trained from childhood to feel pleasure and pain at the right objects. This is what sound education means.

Another reason virtue has to do with pleasure and pain is that it has to do with actions and feelings — and every feeling and every action is accompanied by pleasure or pain.

Punishment attests to it as well, for punishment works through pleasure and pain. It is a kind of cure, and a cure works by administering the opposite of the disease.

Again, as we said before, every state of character is essentially bound up with the very things that form it, for better or for worse; and it is through pleasures and pains that characters go bad — through pursuing and avoiding the wrong ones, or at the wrong time, or in the wrong manner, or going wrong in whatever other way can be distinguished.

Hence some people go so far as to define the virtues as states of impassivity — a kind of calm freedom from feeling. But they go wrong in stating it absolutely, without adding "in the right or wrong manner, at the right or wrong time," and so on.

We may lay it down, then, that moral virtue makes us do what is best in matters of pleasure and pain, and vice the contrary. The following considerations will throw further light on the point.

There are three kinds of things that move us to choose, and three that move us to avoid: on one side the noble, the useful, and the pleasant; on the other their opposites — the shameful, the harmful, and the painful. About all of these the good man tends to go right and the bad man to go wrong, but especially about pleasure. For pleasure we share with the animals, and it accompanies everything we pursue or choose; even the noble and the useful appear pleasant to us.

Again, the feeling of pleasure has been bred into all of us from infancy by our upbringing; it is so ingrained in our life that it can scarcely be scrubbed out. And we all, more or less, make pleasure our measuring stick in judging actions. For this reason too, then, our whole inquiry must deal with these matters; for feeling pleasure and pain in the right way or the wrong way has no small influence on our acts.

Again, it is harder to fight pleasure than to fight anger — and Heraclitus says fighting anger is hard. But virtue, like craft, is always concerned with the harder case, for success is worth more when the task is harder. For this reason also, both virtue and the science of the state must always concern themselves with pleasures and pains; for the man who handles them rightly will be good, and the man who handles them badly will be bad.

Let this stand as established, then: virtue has to do with pleasures and pains; the acts that produce it also develop it — and, done differently, destroy it; and it shows itself in the very acts that produced it.

Chapter 4

But here someone may ask what we mean by saying that men can become just and self-controlled only by doing just and self-controlled acts. Surely, the objection runs, if their acts are just and self-controlled, they are already just and self-controlled — just as people who do what is grammatical and musical are already grammarians and musicians.

We may answer, first, that this is not quite true even of the crafts. A man may write something correctly by chance, or because someone else prompted him; he is not a grammarian until he not only writes correctly but writes as a grammarian would — that is, from his own knowledge of grammar.

But second, the virtues are not parallel to the crafts on this point anyway. What a craft produces has its excellence in itself, so it is enough if the product comes out with a certain quality. But with the virtues, an act does not count as done justly or with self-control merely because the act itself is of a certain sort. The doer must also be in a certain state of mind when he does it: first, he must know what he is doing; second, he must choose the act, and choose it for its own sake; and third, the act must flow from a formed and stable character. Of these conditions, only one — the knowledge — matters for possessing a craft. But for possessing the virtues, knowledge counts for little or nothing, while the other conditions, which come only from repeatedly doing what is just and self-controlled, count not a little but for everything.

An act, then, is called just or self-controlled when it is the sort of act the just or self-controlled man would do. But the man who does it is not thereby just or self-controlled — not unless he also does it in the spirit of the just or self-controlled man.

It is right, then, to say that a man becomes just by doing what is just, and self-controlled by doing what is self-controlled; and without doing these things, no one has any chance of ever becoming good.

But most men, instead of doing them, take refuge in theory. They imagine they are philosophizing, and that philosophizing will make them good — like a sick man who listens attentively to everything his doctor says and then follows none of his orders. That kind of philosophizing will no more produce a healthy soul than that kind of treatment will produce a healthy body.

Chapter 5

We must ask next what virtue actually is.

A quality of the soul is one of three things: (1) a feeling, (2) a capacity, or (3) a habit — a settled state of character. Virtue, then, must be one of these three. By feelings I mean appetite, anger, fear, confidence, envy, joy, love, hate, longing, emulation, pity — in general, whatever is accompanied by pleasure or pain. By a capacity I mean that in virtue of which we are said to be capable of these feelings — capable, for instance, of being angered or pained or moved to pity. And by a habit I mean that in virtue of which we are well or badly disposed in the matter of our feelings: in the matter of anger, for instance, we are badly disposed if we run too violent or too slack, and well disposed if we keep to the moderate course. And so with the rest.

Now, the virtues are not feelings, and neither are the vices. (1) We are not called good or bad on account of our feelings, but we are called so on account of our virtues and vices. (2) We are neither praised nor blamed for our feelings — a man is not praised for being afraid, nor blamed simply for being angry, but only for being angry in a particular way — whereas we are praised and blamed for our virtues and vices. (3) We can be angered or frightened without any deliberate choice, but the virtues are a kind of deliberate choice, or at least are impossible without it. And (4) with respect to our feelings we are said to be moved, but with respect to our virtues and vices we are not said to be moved; we are said to be disposed this way or that.

For these same reasons they are not capacities either. We are not called good or bad merely for being capable of feeling, nor are we praised or blamed for it. And besides, nature gives us our capacities, and nature does not make us good or bad — but that point we have already dealt with.

If, then, the virtues are neither feelings nor capacities, only one possibility remains: they are habits.
