Book I

(Part 1 of 2)

The End

Chapter 1

Every art and every inquiry, and likewise every action and every choice, seems to aim at some good; and so it has been well said that the good is that at which everything aims.

But notice a difference among these aims, or ends. Sometimes what we are after is the activity itself; sometimes it is a result over and above the activity. And wherever there is an end beyond the act, the result is better than the activity that produces it.

Now since there are many kinds of action and many arts and sciences, there are many ends too: health is the end of medicine, a ship of shipbuilding, victory of the art of war, wealth of household management.

But often several of these are grouped under one master art or science — bridle-making and the other equipment trades under horsemanship; horsemanship, along with everything else a soldier does, under the art of war; and so on. In every such case the end of the master art is more desirable than the ends of the arts beneath it, since those are pursued for its sake. And this holds equally whether the end in question is the activity itself or something beyond it, as in the cases just mentioned.

Chapter 2

Suppose, then, that there is some end of our actions that we want for its own sake, choosing everything else as a means to it — and suppose we do not choose everything for the sake of something further, since then the chain would run on forever and desire would be empty and pointless. Clearly this end would be the good, the best of all things. And surely, from a practical standpoint, knowing it matters enormously: like archers with a definite mark to shoot at, we will be far more likely to hit what we want.

If this is right, we must try to sketch, at least roughly, what this good is — and first of all, which of the arts or sciences it belongs to.

It would seem to belong to the supreme science, the one that most of all deserves to be called the master science.

Politics looks like the answer. It is Politics that prescribes which sciences a state needs, which ones each citizen is to study, and how far; and we see even the most prestigious arts — household management, rhetoric, the art of war — placed under its authority.

Since Politics makes use of the other practical sciences, and since it further lays down what people are to do and what they are to refrain from doing, its end must embrace the ends of all the rest; so its end must be the proper good of man.

For even though this good is the same for a single person and for a state, the good of the state seems the grander and more complete thing, both to achieve and to preserve. It is a fine thing to secure it for one person; but to secure it for a whole people, and for whole states, is nobler and more divine.

This, then, is what the present inquiry aims at — and it is a kind of political inquiry.

Chapter 3

We must be satisfied with as much precision as our subject allows; the same exactness should no more be expected in every kind of reasoning than in every kind of craftsmanship.

Now the noble and the just — the things Politics deals with — vary so much, and are so uncertain, that some people conclude they exist only by convention, not by nature.

There is a similar uncertainty about good things, because good things often do people harm: men have been ruined by wealth before now, and have lost their lives through courage.

Since our subject and our starting materials are like this, we must be content to indicate the truth roughly and in outline. When we are dealing with things that admit of no fixed and unchanging rules, and reasoning from premises that are only probable, we must settle for conclusions that are only probable too.

The reader, for his part, should take each of my statements in the same spirit. It is the mark of an educated mind to expect, in each kind of inquiry, only as much exactness as the subject allows: it is just as absurd to accept probable reasoning from a mathematician as to demand rigorous proof from an orator.

Now each of us judges well what he knows, and that is what earns the name "a good judge" — a good judge of some special field when he has been educated in that field, a good judge without qualification when he has had a broad education. Hence a young man is not a fit student of Politics: he lacks experience of the business of life, and that experience is precisely the data and subject matter of Politics.

Besides, since he tends to be swept along by his feelings, the study will do him no good; for its aim is not knowledge but practice.

And here it makes no difference whether a man is young in years or merely young in character. The young man's disqualification is not a matter of time; it comes from living by feeling and chasing every desire as it arises. For people like that, knowledge turns out useless in practice — just as it does for those we call weak-willed. But for those who order their desires and their actions by reason, knowledge of these matters will be a great benefit.

So much, then, by way of preface: about the student, about the spirit in which what we say must be received, and about what we propose to do.

Chapter 4

To resume: since all knowledge and every choice aims at some good, what is this good that we say Politics aims at — what is the highest of all the goods that action can achieve?

As far as its name goes, nearly everyone agrees. Ordinary people and cultivated people alike call it happiness, and they take "living well" and "doing well" to mean the same thing as "being happy."

But they disagree about what happiness actually is, and the many do not give the same account of it as the wise.

The many take it to be something obvious and tangible — pleasure, or wealth, or fame. One man says this, another says that; often the same man says different things at different times: when he is sick, it is health; when he is poor, it is wealth. And when people feel the sting of their own ignorance, they admire most whoever says something grand that sails over their heads.

Some philosophers, on the other hand, have held that beyond all these particular good things there is an "absolute" good, which is the cause of the goodness in all the rest.

It would hardly be worthwhile to review every opinion ever held; we will confine ourselves to those that are most widespread, or that seem to have some reason on their side.

But we must not overlook the distinction between arguments that proceed from first principles and arguments that work up toward them. Plato was right to keep raising this question and asking which way the road runs — from the starting points or toward them — just as on a racecourse you may run from the judges to the far post, or back again.

Well: we must start from what is known.

But "what is known" means two things: what is known to us, which is one thing, and what is known absolutely, which is another.

I think it is safe to say that we must start from what is known to us.

And this is why nothing but a good moral upbringing can prepare a man to study the noble and the just — in a word, to study Politics. For here the undemonstrated fact is the starting point, and if the fact is sufficiently clear to a man, he will not need the reason why on top of it. Now the man with a good upbringing either already has the starting points of action or will easily accept them when they are pointed out. As for the man who neither has them nor will accept them, let him hear Hesiod:

  Best of all is the man who sees the truth himself;
  good, too, is the one who listens to wise advice;
  but the man who neither sees it for himself nor takes
  another's words to heart — he is of no use at all.

Chapter 5

Let us pick up the discussion at the point where we digressed.

It seems that people take their notions of the good, or happiness, from the lives actually lived — and not unreasonably. The many, the least refined, suppose it to be pleasure, which is why the life they aim at is the life of enjoyment.

For the most conspicuous kinds of life are three: this life of enjoyment, the life of the statesman, and, third, the life of contemplation.

The mass of men show themselves utterly slavish by preferring a life fit for cattle; but their view gets a hearing, because many of the people in high places share the tastes of Sardanapalus.

Cultivated men with a practical bent prefer honor; for honor, I suppose we may say, is the aim of the statesman's life.

But honor seems too shallow to be the good we are seeking. It appears to depend on the people who give it rather than on the man who receives it, while we have a hunch that the good is something peculiarly a man's own — something that can scarcely be taken away from him.

Moreover, men seem to pursue honor in order to reassure themselves of their own worth: at any rate, they want to be honored by sensible people, by people who know them, and on the ground of their virtue — their excellence. So plainly, in their own view at least, virtue is better than honor; and perhaps we should take virtue, rather than honor, to be the end of the statesman's life.

But even virtue looks too incomplete to be our answer. A man could possess virtue and yet be asleep, or inactive his whole life long; worse, he could meet with the greatest disasters and misfortunes. And no one would call such a man happy — except to defend a thesis for argument's sake. But we need not dwell on this now; it is discussed well enough in the popular writings.

The third kind of life is the life of contemplation. We will deal with it further on.

As for the moneymaking life, it is something forced and unnatural; and wealth is obviously not the good we are searching for, since it is merely useful — a means to something else. One would sooner take the earlier candidates — pleasure, honor, virtue — for ends, since they are prized for their own sake. But not even they are the end, though a great deal of breath has been wasted trying to show that they are.

Chapter 6

Setting these views aside, we now have to examine the "universal good" and state the difficulties it raises — not a pleasant task, given our friendship for the authors of the doctrine of Ideas. But we venture to think this is the right course: for the sake of truth we ought to sacrifice even what is nearest to us, especially since we call ourselves philosophers. Both are dear to us, but it is a sacred duty to give the preference to truth.

First, then: the very men who put forward this theory did not posit a common Idea for things of which one is prior to the other — which is why they held no common Idea of numbers. But the predicate "good" is applied to substances, and also to qualities and relations. Now what exists independently — what we call substance — is logically prior to what is relative; for the relative is a kind of offshoot, an accident of a thing or substance. So by their own principles there cannot be one common Idea covering all these goods.

Second, "good" is used in as many different senses as "is" or "being." We apply it to substances, or independent existences — God, reason; to qualities — the virtues; to quantity — the moderate or due amount; to relatives — the useful; to time — the opportune moment; to place — the right habitation; and so on. Clearly, then, the word "good" cannot stand for one and the same notion in all these applications; if it did, it could not be applied in all the categories, but in one only.

Third, if the notion were one, then — since there is one science for all the things that come under one Idea — there would be one science of all goods. But in fact there are many sciences even for goods within a single category. The science of the opportune moment in war is strategy; in disease, medicine. The science of the due amount in food is medicine; in exercise, gymnastics.

Fourth, one might ask what they even mean by this "absolute." In "absolute man" and "man," the word "man" has one and the same sense; as far as manhood goes, there is no difference between them. And if that is so, neither will there be any difference in goodness between "absolute good" and "good."

Fifth, they do not make the good any more good by making it eternal: a white thing that lasts a long while is no whiter than one that lasts a day.

There seems to be more plausibility in the doctrine of the Pythagoreans, who place the One in their column of goods rather than reducing all goods to unity; and even Speusippus seems to follow them here.

But these points can wait for another occasion. To what I have said, however, someone may object that the Platonists do not talk this way about all goods indiscriminately. Goods that are pursued and welcomed for their own sake, they hold, are called good by reference to one common Form, while things that tend to produce or preserve these goods, or to ward off their opposites, are called good only as means to them, in a different sense.

Evidently, then, goods will fall into two classes: things good in themselves, and things good as means to the former. So let us separate the goods-in-themselves from the merely useful, and ask whether the first class is called good by reference to one common Idea.

Now what kind of things would one call "good in themselves"?

Surely the things we pursue even apart from their consequences — wisdom, say, and sight, and certain pleasures and certain honors. For although we sometimes pursue these as means, no one could refuse to rank them among the things that are good in themselves.

If these are excluded, nothing is good in itself except the Idea — and then the Form will be empty and meaningless.

If, however, these things do belong among the goods-in-themselves, then it will have to be shown that the goodness of all of them answers to one and the same account, the way "white" means the same thing applied to snow and applied to white lead.

But in fact we have to give a separate and different account of the goodness of honor and wisdom and pleasure.

"Good," then, is not a term applied to all these things alike, in one sense, by reference to one common Idea or Form.

But then how do they all come to be called good? Surely not by a mere accident of naming. Perhaps because they all proceed from one source, or all contribute to one end; or perhaps rather by some analogy — just as we call reason the eye of the soul, because reason stands to the soul as the eye stands to the body, and so on.

But we may drop these questions for now; working them out in detail belongs more properly to another branch of philosophy.

And for the same reason we may drop any further consideration of the Idea. For even granting that this term "good," applied to so many different things, has one and the same meaning throughout — or that there exists an absolute good apart from all the particulars — it is evident that this good would not be anything a man can act on or attain. And it is a good of exactly that kind we are now seeking.

It might be thought, perhaps, that the universal good would still be worth knowing with a view to the goods that are attainable and achievable: with it as a pattern, the argument runs, we shall more readily discern our own good, and discerning it, achieve it.

There is certainly some plausibility in this argument, but it seems at odds with how the sciences actually work. All of them aim at some good and strive to fill their gaps, yet they leave the knowledge of this universal good alone. And it is hardly likely that every practitioner of every art and science would fail to know — would not even look for — something that would help them so much.

Indeed, I am at a loss to see how the weaver or the carpenter would be furthered in his own craft by knowing this absolute good, or how contemplating the pure Form would make anyone better at healing the sick or commanding an army. For it seems to me the doctor does not even seek health in this abstract way: he seeks the health of man — or rather of some particular man. For it is individuals he has to heal.

Chapter 7

Leaving these matters, then, let us return once more to the question: what can this good be that we are searching for?

It seems to be different in different actions and different arts — one thing in medicine, another in war, and so on. What, then, is the good in each of these? Surely that for the sake of which everything else is done. In medicine that is health; in war, victory; in building, a house — a different thing in each case, but always, in whatever we do and whatever we choose, it is the end. For it is always for the sake of the end that everything else is done.

If, then, there is one end of everything man does, this end will be the good achievable by action — or if there are several, then these.

By this route the argument comes around to the same point as before; and this point we must now try to state more clearly.

We see that there are many ends. But some of them are chosen only as means — wealth, flutes, instruments generally. So plainly not all ends are final.

But the best of all things must, we conceive, be something final.

If there is only one final end, then, this will be what we are seeking; if there are several, the most final of them.

Now what is pursued as an end in itself is more final than what is pursued as a means to something else; and what is never chosen as a means is more final than the things chosen both as ends in themselves and as means; in short, that is strictly final which is always chosen as an end in itself and never as a means.

Happiness, more than anything else, seems to answer to this description. Happiness we always choose for itself and never for the sake of anything else; while honor and pleasure and reason and every virtue we choose partly for themselves — for apart from any result, we would still choose each of them — but partly for the sake of happiness, on the supposition that they will help make us happy. Whereas no one chooses happiness for the sake of these things, or as a means to anything else at all.

We seem to be led to the same conclusion if we start from the notion of self-sufficiency.

The final good is thought to be self-sufficient. And by self-sufficient we do not mean sufficient for a man on his own, leading a solitary life; we take account of parents, children, wife — in short, friends and fellow citizens generally, since man is by nature a social being. Some limit has to be set here, of course; extend the circle to ancestors and descendants and friends of friends, and it never comes to a stop. But that question can wait. For now, let us take "self-sufficient" to mean: what by itself makes life desirable and lacking in nothing. And happiness is believed to be exactly that.

Further, happiness is believed to be the most desirable thing in the world — and not merely as one good thing among others. If it were merely one among others, then plainly the addition of even the least of other goods would make it more desirable; for the addition makes a surplus of good, and of two goods the greater is always the more desirable.

Happiness, then, appears to be something final and self-sufficient — the end of everything man does.

But perhaps the reader will say that while no one disputes that happiness is the best thing in the world, a still more precise definition of it is wanted.

The best way to get one, I think, is to ask: what is the function of man? For just as the goodness and excellence of a piper or a sculptor, or the practitioner of any art — of anyone, in general, who has a function or business to perform — lies in that function, so man's good would seem to lie in his function, if he has one.

But can we really suppose that while a carpenter and a cobbler each have a function and a business of their own, man himself has no business, no function assigned him by nature? Surely not. Just as his separate parts — eye, hand, foot — plainly each have their own function, so we must suppose that man too has some function over and above all these.

What, then, is it?

Merely being alive he evidently shares even with the plants, and we are looking for what is peculiar to him. So we must set aside the life of mere nutrition and growth.

Next comes the life of the senses; but this too he plainly shares with horses and cattle and animals of every kind.

There remains, then, the life of action — the life of his rational nature, which has two sides: one rational in the sense of obeying reason, the other rational in the sense of having and exercising reason.

But since this phrase is still ambiguous, let us be clear that we mean the life that consists in the active exercise of these powers; for that seems the truer sense of the term.

The function of man, then, is an activity of his soul: on one side obeying reason, on the other having and using it.

Now the function of a man in any line of work and the function of a good man in that line are generically the same — a harper and a good harper, for instance — and this holds in every case without exception; the good man simply adds his superior excellence at the work. A harper's function is to play the harp; a good harper's, to play it well.

(Man's function, then, being as we say a kind of life — namely an activity of the soul, and actions of various kinds carried out with reason — the good man's function is to do all this well and nobly. And the function of anything is done well when it is done in accordance with that thing's proper excellence.)

If this is so, the result is that the good of man is an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue — or, if there is more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete of them.

But we must add: over a full span of years. One swallow does not make a spring, nor does one fine day; and in the same way one day, or any short stretch of time, does not make a man blessed or happy.

Let this stand, then, as our rough outline of the good. For this, I think, is the proper method: first sketch the outline, then fill in the details. And once the outline is fairly drawn, it would seem that anyone can carry on the work and fit in the particulars that time reveals to us or helps us find. That, indeed, is how the arts and sciences have grown; filling in the gaps takes no extraordinary genius.

We must remember, however, what was said above, and not demand the same degree of accuracy in every branch of study, but in each case only as much as the subject matter allows and as suits that kind of inquiry. The carpenter and the geometer both look for the right angle, but in different ways: the carpenter wants only as close an approximation as his work requires, while the geometer wants to know what a right angle is and what its special character is — his aim is the truth. We must follow the same course everywhere else, so that side issues do not end up taking more of our time than the real business at hand.

Nor must we demand the reason why in every case alike. Sometimes it is enough that the undemonstrated fact be fairly pointed out, as with the starting points, or first principles, of a science. The undemonstrated fact always forms the first step of a science; and these starting points are reached in different ways — some by induction, some by perception, some by a kind of training. In each case we must try to grasp them in the proper way, and take pains to define them clearly; for they carry great weight in everything that follows. The start, I think, is more than half the race; and once our starting point is found, it clears up a good number of our difficulties.
