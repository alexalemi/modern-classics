Book X

(Part 1 of 3)

Pleasure

Chapter 1

Our next business, I think, is to discuss pleasure. For nothing seems more intimately bound up with our nature than pleasure; which is why, in educating the young, we use pleasure and pain as the rudders by which we steer them. And to delight in what we ought to delight in, and to hate what we ought to hate, seems to matter as much as anything for the formation of a virtuous character. These feelings run through the whole of life, and they carry weight enough to pull a man toward virtue and happiness — for we choose what pleases us and shun what pains us.

Surely, then, we cannot pass over these questions, especially since they are so hotly disputed. Some people say that pleasure is the good. Others say the very opposite — that pleasure is thoroughly bad: some of them, perhaps, because they are convinced it really is, others because they think it has a good effect on men's lives to declare pleasure a bad thing even if it is not. Most men, they say, lean toward pleasure and are slaves to it, and so they ought to be pulled in the opposite direction — that way they will be brought to the middle.

But I cannot think this is right. Where feeling and conduct are concerned, words carry less weight than deeds; and when what a man says is contradicted by the plain facts, his words fall into contempt and drag the truth down with them. When a man who denounces pleasure is caught, now and then, desiring it himself, his very attraction to it is taken as proof that he really considers all pleasure desirable — for most men do not draw fine distinctions. True statements, then, seem to be the most useful ones, for practice as well as for theory: because they square with the facts, they are believed, and so they persuade those who understand them to regulate their lives by them. But enough of this; let us now go through the current opinions about pleasure.

Chapter 2

Eudoxus held that pleasure is the good, because he saw that all beings, rational and irrational alike, strive after it. In every case, he said, what is desirable is good, and what is most desirable is best; so the fact that all beings incline toward one and the same thing shows that this thing is best for all. (Each being, after all, finds out what is good for itself — its own food, for instance.) But what is good for all, and what all strive after, is the good.

His arguments won acceptance more because of the excellence of his character than on their own merits; for he was known as a man of remarkable self-control, and so people concluded that he was not saying all this out of love of pleasure — what he said must really be true.

Eudoxus also thought his point could be proved just as clearly from pleasure's opposite: pain, in itself, is something all beings shun; therefore its opposite is desirable for all.

Again, he argued, the most desirable thing is what we choose not on account of something else, but for its own sake; and pleasure is admitted to be like this. Nobody ever asks a man his motive for feeling pleasure — we take it for granted that pleasure is desirable in itself.

Again, he argued that any good thing whatever — just or self-controlled conduct, for instance — becomes more desirable when pleasure is added to it; and only the good can increase the good.

Now, this last argument does seem to show that pleasure is a good thing, but not that it is one bit better than any other good thing; for any good becomes more desirable when another good is added to it than it is by itself.

Indeed, Plato uses an argument of exactly this shape to show that pleasure is not the good. The pleasant life, he says, is more desirable with wisdom than without; but if the combination of the two is better, pleasure itself cannot be the good — for nothing can be added to the good to make it more desirable. And it is equally clear that nothing else can be the good either, if it becomes more desirable when combined with anything that is good in itself. What good is there, then, that cannot be improved by addition, and that men can actually share in? For that is the sort of good we are looking for.

But those who insist, on the other side, that what all beings desire is not good are surely talking nonsense. What everyone thinks, that, we say, is true; and to the man who tells us to put no trust in the opinion of mankind, we reply that we can hardly put more trust in his. If it were only irrational creatures that desired these things, there might be something in the objection; but since rational beings desire them too, how can it be anything but nonsense? And perhaps even in the lower creatures there is some natural principle of good, stronger than they are, that strives after their proper good.

Nor does what Eudoxus' opponents say against his argument from the opposite seem sound. They say that even if pain is bad, it does not follow that pleasure is good; for one bad thing can be opposed to another bad thing, and both of them to some third thing that is neither. Now, this is not a bad point in itself, but it does not hold in the present case. If both were bad, both alike ought to be shunned; if neither were bad, neither should be shunned — or at any rate one no more than the other. But in fact men plainly shun the one as bad and choose the other as good. That, then, is how the two are opposed.

Chapter 3

Again, even if pleasure is not a quality, it does not follow that it is not a good thing. The activity of virtue is not a quality either; neither is happiness itself.

It is objected, next, that the good is determinate while pleasure is indeterminate, because it admits of more and less.

Now, if the evidence for this is that one can be more or less pleased, then the same can be said of justice and the other virtues. We plainly speak of people as being, and showing themselves, more or less virtuous: one man is more just or more brave than another, and it is possible to act more or less justly and with more or less self-control.

But if what they mean is that one pleasure can be more or less of a pleasure than another, I suspect they miss the real reason when they say it is because some pleasures are pure and some mixed. Why should it not be with pleasure as it is with health? Health is something determinate, yet it admits of more and less. The balance of elements that constitutes health is not the same for everyone, nor always the same for the same person; it can vary within certain limits without ceasing to be health, being now more and now less truly health. The same may hold of pleasure.

Again, assuming that the good is complete while motions and comings into being are incomplete, they try to show that pleasure is a motion and a coming into being.

But they do not seem to be right even in calling it a motion. Every motion, it seems, is necessarily quick or slow — either absolutely, like the motion of the universe, or relatively; but pleasure is neither quick nor slow. One can become pleased quickly, just as one can become angry quickly; but the feeling itself cannot be quick — not even relatively, as walking and growing can be. The passage into pleasure, then, may be quick or slow; but the active state itself — the feeling of pleasure — cannot.

And how could pleasure be a coming into being?

It seems that not just anything comes out of just anything; rather, what a thing comes out of is what it dissolves back into. So if pleasure is the coming into being of something, pain must be that thing's dissolution. And indeed they say just this: that pain is a falling short of the natural state, and pleasure its replenishment.

But these are bodily processes. If pleasure is the replenishment of the natural state, then the thing in which the replenishment happens — that is, the body — must be the thing that feels pleasure. And that does not seem to be the case. Pleasure, then, is not the replenishment; rather, while the replenishing goes on we may feel pleasure, as while the depleting goes on we may feel pain.

This whole view of pleasure seems to have been suggested by the pleasures and pains of eating; for there we really do first run short, suffering pain, and then find pleasure in being filled again. But it is not so with all pleasures. The pleasures of the mathematician involve no antecedent pain; nor, among the pleasures of sense, do those of smell; nor do many sights and sounds; nor memories and hopes. Of what, then, are these the coming into being? Nothing has run short here that could be replenished.

To those who point to the disgraceful pleasures as proof that pleasure is not good, we may reply that these things are not pleasant. Granted they are pleasant to ill-conditioned people, we should no more conclude that they are pleasant — except to those people — than we conclude that what is wholesome or sweet or bitter to a sick man is really so, or that a thing is white because it looks white to a man with diseased eyes.

Or we might reply this way: the pleasures are desirable, but not from these sources — just as wealth is desirable, but not at the price of treachery, and health is desirable, but not at the price of eating any abominable food.

Or we might say that pleasures differ in kind. The pleasures that come from noble sources are different from those that come from base ones; you cannot feel the just man's pleasure without being just, or the musical man's pleasure without being musical, and so on through the rest.

The distinction between the true friend and the flatterer seems to show either that pleasure is not good, or else that pleasures differ in kind. The friend, in his company, is thought to have our good in view; the flatterer, our pleasure. And we blame the flatterer, while we praise the friend for having a different aim in keeping our company.

Again, no one would choose to live his whole life with the intellect of a child, even on condition of enjoying a child's pleasures to the fullest possible degree; and no one would choose to get enjoyment by doing some utterly disgraceful act, even if he were never to feel a moment's pain for it.

And there are many things we should care about even if they brought no pleasure — sight, memory, knowledge, the moral and intellectual virtues. Even if we grant that pleasure necessarily accompanies them, it changes nothing; for we should choose them even if no pleasure came of them.

It seems plain, then, that pleasure is not the good, and that not every pleasure is desirable, but that some pleasures are desirable — differing in kind, or in their sources, from those that are not. Let this stand, then, as a sufficient account of the current opinions about pleasure and pain.

Chapter 4

What pleasure is, and what sort of thing it is, we shall discover more readily if we make a fresh start, as follows.

Seeing seems to be complete at any moment: it lacks nothing that could come along afterward to complete its nature. And pleasure seems to resemble seeing in this respect. It is something whole and entire, and at no moment could you find a pleasure that would be made complete by lasting longer.

Therefore pleasure is not a motion. Every motion takes time and aims at an end — the motion of building, for instance — and is complete when it has produced what it was aiming at: complete, that is, in the whole time, or at the final moment of it. While the work is in progress, all the motions are incomplete, and different in kind from the whole motion and from one another. Fitting the stones together is a different thing from fluting the column, and both are different from building the temple. The building of the temple is complete — nothing more is needed to carry out the plan; but the building of the foundation, or of the triglyph, is incomplete, for each is the building of a part only. These motions, then, differ in kind, and you cannot find a motion that is complete in its nature at any given moment — it is complete, if at all, only in the whole time.

The same goes for walking and the other forms of locomotion. All locomotion is motion from one place to another, but there are distinct kinds of it — flying, walking, leaping, and so on. More than that: within walking itself there are differences. The starting point and the destination are not the same for the whole course as for a stretch of it, nor for this stretch as for that; and crossing this line is not the same as crossing that one — for you do not cross a line in the abstract, but a line that lies in a particular place, and this line lies in a different place from that one. For a detailed discussion of motion I must refer you to my other works; but the upshot seems to be that motion is not complete at any moment — its several parts are incomplete and different in kind from one another, the starting point and destination being what makes the difference in kind.

Pleasure, on the other hand, is complete in its nature at any moment. Plainly, then, pleasure and motion must be distinct from each other, and pleasure must belong to the class of things whole and complete. The same conclusion follows from this too: duration is necessary for motion, but not for pleasure — a moment's pleasure is something whole and entire.

From all this it is clear that people are wrong to call pleasure a motion or a coming into being. Those terms do not apply to everything, but only to things that are divisible into parts and are not wholes. There is no coming into being of seeing, or of a mathematical point, or of a unit; none of these is a motion or a coming into being. And the terms are just as inapplicable to pleasure; for pleasure is a whole.

Every sense is active upon its proper object, and its activity is complete when the sense is in good condition and its object is the noblest that falls within its range — for this seems to be what complete activity means. (And let us assume it makes no difference whether we speak of the sense being active or of the person who has it.) For each sense, then, the activity is best when the faculty on the one side is in the finest condition and the object on the other is the finest that falls within that faculty's range.

And this activity will be not only the most complete but also the pleasantest. Every sense has its pleasure in being active, and so do thought and contemplation; and the activity is pleasantest when it is most complete, and most complete when the faculty is in good condition and the object is the worthiest of those that fall within its scope.

Further, the pleasure completes the activity. But it does not complete it in the way the object and the faculty complete it, when both are as they should be — just as health and the doctor are not in the same way the cause of a man's being healthy.

(That every sense has its pleasure is evident: we speak of pleasant sights and pleasant sounds. And it is evident too that the pleasure is greatest when both the faculty and the object it works upon are as good as they can be; when that is true of object and perceiver alike, there will always be pleasure — so long, at least, as there is something to act and something to be acted upon.)

Now, the pleasure completes the activity not in the way the trained habit does, by being present in a man already, but as a sort of completeness laid on top — like the bloom of youth on those in their prime.

So long, then, as the object of thought or of sense is what it should be, and likewise the subject that perceives or contemplates it, there will be pleasure in the activity. For while the thing acted upon and the thing acting remain the same and stand in the same relation to each other, the same result naturally follows.

Why is it, then, that no one can feel pleasure continuously? Probably because we tire. No human faculty is capable of continuous activity; so pleasure cannot be continuous either, since it accompanies the activity. For the same reason some things please us when they are new, and please us less afterward. At first the mind is stimulated and works away at them intensely — just as we strain our eyes to look hard at something; later the effort slackens and relaxes, and the pleasure loses its keenness along with it.

We should expect the desire for pleasure to be shared by all men, seeing that all men desire to live.

For life is a kind of activity, and each man is active with the faculties he loves best upon the things he loves best: the musical man is active with his hearing upon melodies, the lover of learning with his mind upon the objects of study, and so on with the rest.

But pleasure completes these activities, and therefore completes life — which is what men desire.

Naturally, then, men desire pleasure too; for each man finds in it the completion of his life, and life is desirable.

Whether we desire life for the sake of pleasure or pleasure for the sake of life is a question we may set aside for the present. For the two seem to be yoked together and to admit of no separation: without activity there is no pleasure, and pleasure completes every activity.
