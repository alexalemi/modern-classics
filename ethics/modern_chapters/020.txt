Book IX

(Part 1 of 2)

Friendship or Love—Continued

Chapter 1

In all friendships between unlike partners, it is proportionate exchange that keeps things equal and keeps the friendship alive — we have said this already. It works just as it does in the association of citizens, where the shoemaker gets, in exchange for his shoes, a return proportionate to what they are worth, and so with the weaver and everyone else.

In those civic cases, a common measure is ready to hand: money. Money is the standard everything is referred to and measured against.

In erotic friendships, though, there is no such standard, and so the lover sometimes complains that he loves intensely and gets no love back — when, perhaps, there is simply nothing lovable about him. And often the beloved complains that the lover once promised everything and now delivers nothing.

Complaints like these tend to arise when one party is in the friendship for pleasure and the other for profit, and neither is getting what he wants. A friendship built on those motives dissolves the moment the partners stop getting the very thing they became friends for; neither one loved the other's self — each loved only something the other had, and something not built to last. Which is why these friendships are not built to last either. But friendship based on character is its own reward, and so, as we said, it tends to endure.

Friends also quarrel when they find they are getting something different from what they wanted — for when you fail to get what you want, it is as if you got nothing at all. There is a story about a harper that makes the point. A man promised him that the better he played, the more he would be paid; but when dawn came and the harper claimed what was promised, the man replied that he had already paid — pleasure for pleasure. Now, if pleasure was what both of them wanted, that would settle it. But if one wanted pleasure and the other wanted profit, and one has what he wants while the other does not, the bargain has not been fairly carried out; for what a man happens to want is what he sets his heart on, and it is for the sake of that — and only that — that he agrees to render his particular service.

But who should set the value of the service — the one who gives first, or the one who receives first? The one who gives first seems to leave the pricing to the other. They say this was the practice of Protagoras: after teaching a subject, he would tell the pupil to estimate what the knowledge was worth to him, and that is the fee he would take.

Some prefer the rule of the proverb: "Let a friend be content with his stated wage."

But a man who takes his money up front and then delivers nothing — because he promised more than he could perform — is rightly held accountable; he has not fulfilled his contract. (The sophists, perhaps, are forced to take payment in advance, since nobody would pay for what they know once they had heard it.)

So: whoever fails to do what he has already been paid for is rightly charged with it. But where there is no explicit agreement about the service, two cases arise. (a) When one man helps another freely, for that other's own sake, no accusation can arise — as we said, that is the very nature of friendship based on virtue. Here the return should be measured by the purpose of the man who did the first service, for purpose is the mark of both the friend and virtue. The same rule seems to apply between a philosopher and his students: the worth of what he gives cannot be measured in money, and no honor could fully repay him; but here, as with gods and parents, what is possible is accepted as enough. (b) If, however, the first gift was made not in that spirit but on the understanding that there would be a return, then the return should, ideally, be one that both parties agree is proportionate to what was given. And if they cannot agree, it seems not only necessary but just that the one who received the first benefit should set its value: whatever advantage he actually got from it, or whatever he would have paid for the pleasure of it, that amount repays the giver in full. This is plainly how it works in sales; and in some states the law gives no remedy at all in voluntary contracts, on the principle that once you have given a man credit, you must finish the bargain in the same spirit in which you began it. It is held fairer for the service to be valued by the man who was trusted than by the man who trusted him. For most things are valued differently by those who have them and those who want them: what belongs to us, and what we give away, always looks precious to us. All the same, the return must be measured by the value the receiver sets on the service — though he ought, perhaps, to price it not at what it seems worth now that he has it, but at the value he put on it before he had it.

Chapter 2

Some further questions come up here. Should a father's claims on his son be unlimited, so that the son obeys him in everything — or should a sick son obey the doctor, and should a citizen electing a general choose the man skilled in war? Similarly: should you help your friend rather than a good man, and repay a benefactor rather than do a favor for a comrade, if you cannot do both?

Perhaps we may say that laying down precise rules for all such cases is scarcely possible; the cases differ in every possible way — in how much is at stake, and in whether the act is noble or merely necessary. But this much is fairly clear: no single person's claims override everyone else's; and as a general rule we should repay benefits received before doing favors for comrades — just as, if we had borrowed money, we ought to pay our creditors before giving presents to our friends.

But even this rule may not hold in every case. Suppose a man has been ransomed from bandits. Should he in turn ransom his ransomer, whoever that may be — or repay him when he asks, even though he has not been captured — in preference to ransoming his own father? It would seem a man ought to ransom his father even before himself.

So, as we said: generally speaking, we should repay what we owe; but if giving instead is nobler, or meets a more pressing need, we should lean that way. Sometimes it is not even fair to repay the original service — for instance, when one man helped another knowing him to be good, while the repayment would mean helping a man he believes to be bad. Sometimes, likewise, you are not bound to lend in turn to a man who once lent to you: A may have lent to B fully expecting repayment, because B is honorable, while B has no hope of ever being repaid by A, who is a scoundrel. If that is the true state of things, A's demand for a return loan is not fair; and even if the facts are otherwise, so long as they think this of each other, their conduct will seem natural enough.

As we have said many times, statements about human feelings and actions can only be as definite as their subject matter allows.

It is fairly plain, then, that on the one hand not everyone's claims are the same, and on the other hand the father's claims do not override all others — just as Zeus does not receive all our sacrifices. The claims of parents, brothers, comrades, and benefactors are all different, and to each we must render what is properly his own and his due.

And this is how people actually behave. To a wedding they invite their kin, for kin share in the family and so in everything that concerns it; and for the same reason it is thought that kin have first claim to be invited to funerals.

Parents would seem to have a special claim on us for their support: we owe it to them, and it is nobler to sustain the lives of those who gave us our own than to sustain ourselves.

Honor, too, we should pay our parents, as we do the gods — but not every kind of honor. The honor due a father is not the same as the honor due a mother, nor do we owe either of them the honor due a wise man or a good general, but precisely a father's honor and a mother's honor.

To all our elders, again, we should pay the honor their age is due — rising when they enter, giving them the place of honor at the table, and so on. Between comrades and brothers, on the other hand, there should be frank speech and everything in common. And to kin, to fellow tribesmen, to fellow citizens, and to everyone else, we should always try to give what is due — assigning to each what properly belongs to him, according to how close he is to us and how good or useful he is. When the people involved are of one kind, the assignment is fairly easy; when they are of different kinds, it is harder. But that is no reason to shirk the task; we must draw the distinctions as best we can.

Chapter 3

Another hard question: should we or should we not break off friendship with people who are no longer what they were?

Where the friendship was based on profit or pleasure, perhaps there is nothing puzzling: when those give out, the friends naturally part. It was the profit or the pleasure they loved; when that is gone, it is only to be expected that the love goes too. But complaints are likely if a man who loved another for profit or pleasure pretended to love him for his character. As we said at the outset, quarrels between friends most often arise when the real basis of the friendship is not what it was supposed to be. If a man deceives himself — assuming he is loved for his character when the other's behavior gives no ground for it — he has only himself to blame. But if he is taken in by the other's pretense, he has fair ground for complaint against the impostor — more, even, than against a counterfeiter of the coinage, since what has been tampered with is more precious.

But suppose you take a man into your friendship as a good man, and he becomes — and shows himself to be — a bad one. Should you still love him? Surely that is impossible: not everything is lovable, only the good. What is bad is not lovable and should not be loved; we ought not to love what is bad, nor make ourselves like what is worthless — and as we said before, like makes friends with like.

Should the friendship, then, be broken off at once? Perhaps not in every case — only where the badness is incurable. Where reform is possible, we are more bound to help a friend's character than his fortune, since character is the nobler thing and has more to do with friendship. Still, a man who does withdraw his friendship in such a case does nothing unnatural: it was not this man he made friends with. His friend has become someone else, and since he cannot restore him, he keeps his distance.

But suppose one friend stays as he was while the other improves and comes to far surpass him in virtue: should the better man still treat the other as a friend? It is hardly possible. We see this most plainly when the gap grows wide — take a boyhood friendship. If one of the two remains a child in understanding while the other has become a man in the fullest sense of the word, how can they still be friends, when the things that please them, the things they rejoice and grieve over, are no longer the same? They will not even agree in their tastes about each other; and without that, we found, people cannot be friends, for they cannot live together. (But this point has already been discussed.)

Shall we say, then, that the better man should treat his old friend as no more than a stranger, exactly as if they had never been friends? Perhaps we can go further: he should not entirely forget the old intimacy. Just as we hold that we should serve friends before strangers, so former friends retain some claim on us for the friendship that once was — unless it was extraordinary depravity that parted us.

Chapter 4

Our friendly relations toward others — all the marks by which friendship is defined — seem to be derived from our relations toward ourselves. A friend is sometimes described as one who wishes and does what is good, or seems good, for the other person's own sake; or as one who wishes his friend to exist and to live, for the friend's sake. (This is what mothers feel toward their children, and what quarreling friends still feel for one another.) Others describe a friend as one who spends his life with another and chooses what he chooses, or as one who shares his friend's griefs and joys. (This, too, is found above all in mothers.) And friendship is regularly defined by one or another of these marks.

Now, every one of these marks is found in the good man's relation to himself — and in other men just so far as they suppose themselves good; but, as we have said, virtue and the good man seem to be the standard in everything. For the good man is of one mind with himself: he desires the same things with his whole soul. He wishes for himself what is good, and what seems good, and he does it — for it is the mark of the good man to work out what is good — and he does it for his own sake, that is, for the sake of the rational part of him, which seems to be what a man's self really is. He wishes himself to live and be preserved, and above all the part of him with which he thinks; for to the good man, existence is a good. And it is for himself that each of us wishes what is good: no one would choose to have every good in the world (as, say, God is in complete possession of the good) on condition of becoming someone else — only on condition of remaining just who he is. And a man's reason would seem to be his self, or at least to be his self more truly than anything else in him.

Such a man also wishes to spend time with himself; his own company is pleasant to him. The memory of his past life is sweet, and his hopes for the future are good — and such hopes are pleasant. His mind is well stocked with matter for contemplation. And he shares his own griefs and joys: the same things pain and please him at every season, not one thing now and another thing later — he is, so to speak, not a man who changes his mind.

Since, then, all these marks are found in the good man's relation to himself, and since he stands to his friend as he stands to himself — for his friend is another self — friendship is defined by one or another of these marks, and those in whom they are found are called friends.

Whether friendship toward oneself is possible we may set aside for now; though it would seem possible insofar as a man is two or more selves — that follows from what has been said, and from the fact that the extreme of friendship for another is likened to friendship for oneself.

But the marks we have listed appear to be found in the general run of men, who are not good. Perhaps we should say that men share in them just so far as they are satisfied with themselves and believe themselves good. The utterly worthless and impious never have them — never even seem to. Indeed we might almost say flatly that no one who is not good has them; for such men are not at one with themselves. They desire one thing and wish another, like the weak-willed, who choose what is pleasant but harmful instead of what they themselves hold to be good. Others, through cowardice or laziness, shrink from doing what they believe is best for them. And those who have done many terrible things out of wickedness come to hate life, wish to be rid of it, and sometimes actually destroy themselves.

Bad men hunt for company to pass their time in and run from their own; alone, they remember much that is painful and expect more of the same, but other people's company distracts them from it. There is nothing lovable in them, so they have no friendly feeling toward themselves.

The man who is not good, then, cannot share his own griefs and joys, for his soul is divided against itself. One part of him, because of its viciousness, is pained at holding back from something while another part is pleased; one part pulls this way, another that, as if tearing him to pieces between them. Or if it really is impossible to be pained and pleased at the same moment, then at least a little while later he is pained that he was pleased, and wishes those pleasures had never come his way — for men who are not good are full of remorse.

So we may say flatly: the man who is not good has no friendly feeling even toward himself, since there is nothing lovable in him. And if to be in this state is utter misery, we ought to strain every nerve to avoid vice and to try to be good; for that is how a man comes to be a friend to himself, and comes to be a friend to others.

Chapter 5

Goodwill looks like friendship, but it is not friendship. We can have goodwill toward people we do not know, and who have no idea we wish them well — and there can be no friendship there. But this we have said already.

Nor is goodwill the same as affection, for it has none of the intensity and desire that go with affection.

Affection, moreover, requires close acquaintance, while goodwill can spring up in a moment — as it does toward athletes competing for a prize. We become well-wishers of a competitor and are eager for him to win, though we would not lift a finger to help him; for, as we said, our goodwill in such cases is sudden, and the fondness is only skin-deep.

The truth seems to be that goodwill is the germ of friendship, just as pleasure in the sight of a person is the germ of erotic love. No one falls in love without first being delighted by visible beauty; but delighting in someone's beauty is not yet being in love — not unless you also ache in his absence and long for his presence. In the same way, people cannot be friends without first becoming well-wishers, but well-wishers are not yet friends on that account: they merely wish good to those they favor, and would not actually help them in anything, or put themselves out for them. So one might say — stretching the term — that goodwill is friendship lying idle, which with time and close acquaintance may become friendship proper. Not the friendship whose motive is profit, though, nor the one whose motive is pleasure, for goodwill has no place in those. The man who has received a benefit does return goodwill to his benefactor, and justly so; but the man who wishes another prosperity in hopes of the good things he can get through him does not really wish the other well — he wishes himself well, just as a man is no real friend if his service has one eye on profit.

Generally speaking, goodwill is founded on some kind of excellence or goodness: it arises when someone strikes us as beautiful, or brave, or endowed with some other fine quality — as we said in the case of the athletes.

Chapter 6

Concord — unity of mind — also seems to be a mark of friendship. That is exactly why it is not mere agreement in opinion, which is possible even between total strangers.

Nor do we speak of concord when people agree in judgment on just any subject — on astronomy, say; being of one mind about that has nothing to do with friendship. We say concord prevails in a state when the citizens agree about what is in the common interest, choose the same course, and carry out what they have decided together. Concord, then, is about practical matters — and among those, matters of importance, where both parties, or all parties, can get what they want. A state is of one mind, for instance, when all the citizens agree that offices shall be elective, or that an alliance shall be made with Sparta, or that Pittacus shall govern — Pittacus himself being willing to take the office. But when each man wants the power for himself, like the brothers in Euripides' Phoenissae, there is discord. Being of one mind is not just each thinking of the same thing, whatever it may be, but thinking of it under the same terms — as when the common people and the upper classes both agree that the best men should govern; for then everyone gets what he wants.

Concord, then, appears to be — just as it is called — the friendship of states: it concerns the common interest, and things that bear heavily on how we live.

This kind of concord is found among good men. They are of one mind with themselves and with each other, standing, so to speak, always on the same ground; their wishes hold steady and do not ebb and flow like the Euripus. They wish for what is just and for the common interest, and they work for it together. But men who are not good cannot be of one mind, except to a small degree — just as they cannot be friends except briefly. They strive to take more than their share of the profits while taking less than their share of the labors and public burdens; and each man, while aiming at this for himself, keeps a sharp eye on his neighbor and blocks him from doing the same — for if they are not on guard against each other, the community is ruined. The result is discord: each compelling the other to do what is just while refusing to do it himself.

Chapter 7

Benefactors seem to love the people they have benefited more than the beneficiaries love them back. This looks irrational, and people hunt for the explanation.

Most people think the reason is that the one is a debtor and the other a creditor. Just as, with a loan, the debtor would be glad to see his creditor out of the way while the lender positively worries over his debtor's safety, so — the theory goes — the benefactor wants his beneficiary to stay alive in hopes of a return favor, while the beneficiary is in no hurry to repay.

Epicharmus might perhaps say that people who talk this way are "looking from the cheap seats," but it sounds true to life; most men do have short memories, and are more eager to receive benefits than to confer them.

But the real cause, it would seem, lies deeper in the nature of things, and the creditor's case is not even a true parallel. Creditors feel no affection for their debtors — only a wish that they stay safe long enough to pay. But those who have done a kindness feel real love and affection for those they have benefited, even if the beneficiaries are of no use to them and never will be.

The same thing can be seen in craftsmen: every craftsman loves the work of his own hands more than the work would love him if it came to life. Poets perhaps carry it furthest: they love their own poems to excess, doting on them as if they were their children.

The benefactor's case is like theirs. The person he has benefited is, so to speak, his handiwork; and so he loves his work more than the work loves its maker. The reason is this: existence is something we all desire and love; but we exist in activity — in living and doing; and what a man makes is, in a way, its maker in activity. So he loves his work because he loves existence. And this is simply the nature of things: what a thing is potentially, its work exhibits in activity.

Besides, for the benefactor there is something noble in his act, so that he delights in the person who embodies it; but for the beneficiary there is nothing noble in the benefactor — at most something useful, and that is less pleasant and less lovable.

Again, what is pleasant is the activity of the present, the hope of the future, the memory of the past; and the most pleasant — and most lovable — is what we are actively engaged in. For the benefactor, his deed endures (for the noble is lasting); for the beneficiary, the advantage soon passes away.

Again, the memory of noble deeds is pleasant, the memory of merely profitable ones hardly pleasant at all, or less so — though with expectation the reverse seems true.

Again, loving is like doing something; being loved is like having something done to you. And the ones who have the doer's part in the transaction naturally feel, and show, the more love.

Again, we are all fonder of what has cost us effort — those who made their money love it more than those who inherited it. Now, receiving a benefit costs no labor, while conferring one takes trouble. This is also why mothers love their children more than fathers do: bearing them costs the mother more, and she knows better that they are her own. And that would seem to be the mark of benefactors too.
