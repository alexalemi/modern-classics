Book VIII

(Part 1 of 2)

Friendship or Love

Chapter 1

After what has gone before, a discussion of friendship naturally comes next; for friendship is a sort of virtue, or at least it goes hand in hand with virtue, and besides that, it is one of life's absolute necessities. No one would choose to live without friends, even if he had every other good thing. In fact, it is precisely when a man is rich, when he holds power and authority, that he seems to need friends most of all. What is the use of all that prosperity if he has no chance to do good with it? And doing good is shown most often, and most admirably, toward friends. Or how could such a position even be kept safe without friends? The greater it is, the more danger it stands in. And in poverty, and in every other misfortune, we treat our friends as our one refuge. We need friends when we are young, to keep us from going wrong; when we are old, to care for us and to carry out the plans we no longer have the strength to execute ourselves; and in the prime of life, to help us do noble deeds — "two going together," as Homer says; for two together are stronger both in thought and in action.

Love seems to be planted by nature in the parent for the offspring and in the offspring for the parent — not only among human beings, but among birds and most animals too. It is planted in members of the same species for one another, and among human beings especially; that is why we praise those who love their fellow men. And anyone who travels can see how every human being is akin to every other, and dear to him.

Again, friendship seems to be the bond that holds states together, and lawgivers seem to care even more about securing it than about securing justice. For concord looks like a close cousin of friendship, and concord is what lawgivers want most to preserve, while faction is what they want most to drive out as an enemy. If citizens are friends, they have no need of justice; but even if they are just, they still need friendship or love as well — indeed, justice in its completest form seems to be a matter of friendship or love too.

More than that: friendship is not only necessary, it is a noble thing. We praise those who love their friends; having many friends is thought to be something noble; and some people even think that a good man and a friend are one and the same.

But opinions about friendship differ on quite a few points. Some hold that it is a kind of likeness — that people who are like one another are friends. Hence the sayings "Like to like" and "Birds of a feather flock together," and others of the same sort. Others say the opposite: "Two of a trade never agree."

Still others dig deeper into these questions and look for the causes behind the appearances. Euripides, for instance, says:

  The parched earth loves the rain,
  and the high heaven, heavy with moisture,
  loves to fall to earth.

Heraclitus says, "Opposites fit together," and "From discordant elements comes the fairest harmony," and "It is by strife that all things come into being." Others take the opposite view — Empedocles above all — and say that like desires like.

Now, the difficulties that concern the constitution of the universe we may set aside; they do not really belong to our present inquiry. But the ones that concern human nature, and are bound up with human character and feeling, we will discuss. For instance: can friendship arise in all men, or is it impossible for bad men to be friends? And is there one form of friendship, or many? Those who suppose there is only one kind, on the ground that friendship admits of more and less, are relying on insufficient evidence; for things that differ in kind can also differ in degree. (But we have spoken about this point already.)

Chapter 2

Perhaps these difficulties will clear up once we determine what it is that is lovable. For it seems we do not love just anything, but only what is lovable, and the lovable is either good or pleasant or useful. But "useful" would seem to mean whatever helps us get some good or some pleasure — so that, in the end, only the good and the pleasant are loved as ends.

Now, do men love what is good, or what is good for themselves? For the two sometimes come apart.

The same question can be asked about the pleasant.

It seems that each man loves what is good for himself: the good is lovable in itself, but what is lovable to each man is what is good for him. Someone may object that each man loves not what is really good for him but what seems good for him. It makes no difference; the lovable we are talking about will then simply be the apparently lovable.

The motives of love, then, are three. Now, love for inanimate things is not called friendship; for there is no return of affection there, and no wishing for the thing's own good. It would be absurd to wish well to wine, for instance — at most we wish that it may keep well, so that we can have it. But it is commonly said that we must wish our friend's good for his own sake. A man who wishes another's good in this way is called a well-wisher when the wish is not returned; when the well-wishing is mutual, it is called friendship.

But shouldn't we add that each must be aware of the other's goodwill? A man often wishes well to people he has never seen but supposes to be good or useful, and one of those people may feel the same way about him. These two are plainly well-wishers of one another — but who could call them friends, when neither knows how the other feels?

To be friends, then, two people must wish each other's good — from one of the three motives named above — and each must be aware of the other's feelings.

Chapter 3

But these three motives differ from one another in kind; therefore the affections and friendships based on them will differ in kind too. The kinds of friendship, accordingly, are three — equal in number to the motives of love; for any one of the three can be the basis of a mutual affection of which each party is aware.

Now, those who love one another wish each other's good with respect to the very thing that is the motive of their love. So those whose love for one another rests on the useful do not love each other for what they are, but only insofar as each gets some good from the other.

It is the same with those whose affection rests on pleasure: people are fond of a wit, for instance, not for what he is, but as a source of pleasure to themselves.

Those who love on the ground of the useful, then, care for each other for the sake of their own good, and those who love on the ground of pleasure care for each other for the sake of what pleases them — each loving the other not as being the man he is, but as useful or pleasant.

These friendships, then, are "accidental"; for the person is loved not as being the person and character he is, but as a source of some good or some pleasure. Friendships of this kind are easily dissolved, since the parties do not stay the same: when they cease to be pleasant or useful to each other, their love ceases. And the useful is nothing permanent — it shifts from one time to another. So when the motive of the friendship disappears, the friendship dissolves with it, since it existed only with a view to that motive.

Friendship of this kind seems to be found especially among the elderly (for at that age men pursue the useful rather than the pleasant), and among those middle-aged and young men who keep a sharp eye on profit. Friends of this sort do not generally even live in each other's company; sometimes they are not even pleasant to each other — and they have no need of such constant company unless it pays. They make themselves pleasant exactly as far as they have hopes of getting something good out of it.

With these friendships people generally class the kind that exists between host and guest.

The friendship of young men is thought to rest on pleasure. Young men live by impulse, and mostly pursue what is pleasant to them here and now. But as they grow older, the things that please them change. That is why they are quick to make friendships and quick to drop them: the friendship changes along with the thing that pleases, and pleasure of that sort turns over fast.

Young men are also prone to falling in love; for erotic love is mostly a matter of impulse and rests on pleasure. So they fall in love, and soon fall out of it, passing from one state to the other many times in a single day.

Friends of this kind do want to spend their days together and live together; for that is how they get what their friendship is for.

But the perfect kind of friendship is the friendship of good men who are alike in virtue. Each alike wishes the other well as a good man, and each is a good man in his own essential character. And those who wish well to their friends for the friends' own sake are friends in the truest sense; for they feel this way toward each other because of what each is, not by accident. So their friendship lasts as long as their virtue lasts — and virtue is a lasting thing.

Again, each of them is both good simply and good for his friend; for good men are both good without qualification and useful to one another.

In the same way they are pleasant too; for good men are pleasant both in themselves and to each other. Every character delights in the acts that are proper to it and in acts of the same kind, and the acts of good men are the same or similar.

A friendship like this, as we should expect, is lasting, since it unites in itself every condition of true friendship. Every friendship has as its motive some good or some pleasure — whether in itself, or relative to the one who loves — and rests on some likeness. But in this case everything required belongs to the friends in their own nature: here there is likeness, and the rest — what is good simply and pleasant simply — and these are the most lovable things of all. So it is between people of this sort that love and friendship are found in their truest and best form.

Naturally, such friendships are rare, because such people are rare. And a friendship of this kind needs time and long acquaintance besides. As the proverb has it, people cannot know one another until they have eaten the requisite amount of salt together. Nor can they accept one another as friends, or be friends, until each has shown himself to the other, and been approved, as worthy of love. Those who are quick to treat each other as friends may wish to be friends, but they are not really friends unless they are lovable and know each other to be so. The wish for friendship can spring up quickly; friendship cannot.

This kind of friendship, then, is complete — complete in duration and in every other respect; and in it each gets from the other the same or nearly the same as he gives, which is how it should be between friends.

Chapter 4

The friendship whose motive is pleasure bears some resemblance to this one; for good men, too, are pleasant to each other. So does the friendship whose motive is the useful; for good men are useful to one another as well. And in these cases too, the friendship is most likely to last when what each gets from the other is the same — pleasure, say — and not only the same, but drawn from the same source: a friendship between two wits, for instance, rather than one between a lover and his beloved. For in the latter case the pleasure does not come from the same source for both: the lover delights in gazing at his beloved, while the beloved likes receiving attentions. And when the bloom of youth fades, the friendship sometimes vanishes with it — the one no longer finds the beauty that pleased him, the other no longer gets the attentions. Yet they often do remain friends: namely, when their time together has brought them to care for each other's characters, and their characters are alike.

Those who in love affairs exchange not pleasure but profit are friends in a lesser degree, and less lastingly. The friendship whose motive is profit ends when the advantage ends; for it was never each other that they loved, but the profit.

For pleasure or for profit, then, even bad men can be friends with one another, and good men with bad, and men who are neither with anyone at all. But clearly only good men can be friends for each other's own sake; for bad men take no delight in one another unless there is something to be gained.

The friendship of good men is also the only one that is proof against slander; for a man is not quick to believe anyone's word against a friend he has tested for himself over the years. Between such friends there is trust, and the certainty that neither would ever wrong the other, and everything else that true friendship demands. In the other kinds, there is no such security.

Now, since men do apply the name "friends" to those who love each other for profit's sake — as states do, for expediency is thought to be the ground on which states form alliances — and also to those who love each other for pleasure's sake, as children do, perhaps we too should call such people friends, and say that there are several kinds of friendship. First, friendship in the primary and strict sense: the friendship of good men as good men. Second, the other kinds, which are called friendships by resemblance to this one; for those people are friends insofar as their relation involves some element of good, and that is the resemblance — the pleasant, after all, is a good to those who love pleasure. But these two lesser kinds do not often coincide: the same people do not usually become friends for the sake of profit and of pleasure both, since accidental properties are not often combined in one subject.

Now that we have sorted friendship into these kinds, we can say: bad men will be friends for pleasure or profit, being alike in that respect, while good men, when they are friends, love each other for what they are — that is, as good men. Good men, then, are friends simply; the others are friends accidentally, and by resemblance to them.

Chapter 5

Just as with the virtues we distinguish the settled character or capacity from its exercise, so it is with friendship. When friends are living in each other's company, they take pleasure in each other and do each other good; when they are asleep, or far apart, they are not exercising their friendship, but they still have the disposition that would issue in friendly acts if it were exercised. Distance does not dissolve friendship itself, only its exercise. But if the absence lasts long, it is thought to wear away even the friendship itself. Hence the saying:

  Full many a friendship has been loosed
  for want of talk together.

Old men do not seem apt for friendship, nor do sour-tempered men; there is little in them to give pleasure, and no one can spend his days in the company of what is painful, or even of what fails to please. For our nature seems to shun the painful and seek the pleasant above all things.

Those who approve of each other but do not live in each other's company seem to be well-wishers rather than friends. Nothing is so characteristic of friendship as living together. The needy seek help that way; but even the happy desire company — a solitary life suits them least of all men. And people cannot spend their lives together unless they are pleasant to each other and delight in the same things, which seems to be what comradeship requires.

The truest friendship, then, is the friendship of good men, as we have said again and again. For what is lovable and desirable, it seems, is what is good or pleasant in itself; but to each man, what is good or pleasant for him — and the friendship of good men for each other rests on both grounds at once.

But it seems that while love is a feeling, friendship is a habit. For love can just as well be directed at inanimate things, but the love of friends for one another involves choice, and choice proceeds from a habit. And when friends wish well to those they love for their own sakes, they are moved not by feeling but by habit. Again, in loving a friend, each loves what is good for himself; for whoever gains a good man as a friend gains a good for himself. So each loves what is good for himself, and each gives back the equal of what he gets, in good wishes and in pleasure. For, as the old saying joins them, "friendship is equality" — and both are found in the highest degree in the friendship of good men.

Chapter 6

Sour-tempered men and old men are the less apt for friendship the harsher their temper is and the less pleasure they take in company; for delight in company seems to be, more than anything else, the mark of friendship and the thing that produces it. That is why the young make friends quickly and the old do not — people do not make friends with those who give them no pleasure — and neither do the sour-tempered. Such men may indeed be well-wishers: they wish each other good, and help each other in need. But they are hardly friends, since they do not spend their days together or delight in each other — and those, more than anything else, are thought to be the marks of friendship.

It is impossible to be a friend, in the full sense, to many people at the same time, just as it is impossible to be in love with many people at once. Friendship of that sort is something intense, and intense feeling takes a single object. Besides, it is not easy for one man to find many people at once who deeply please him — perhaps not many good ones either. And the friends must test each other and grow used to each other, which is a very hard thing to accomplish. In the way of profit or pleasure, on the other hand, it is quite possible to please many people; men of that sort are not rare, and their services take little time.

Of these lesser kinds, the one that comes nearer true friendship is the friendship for pleasure, when each renders the same to the other and both take pleasure in each other, or in the same things — the friendships of the young are typically like this, for a generous spirit is commoner in the young. The friendship whose motive is the useful, by contrast, is the friendship of petty traders. The happy have no need of useful friends, but they do need pleasant ones: what they want is people to live with. They may put up with what is painful for a little while, but no one could endure anything continually if it pained him — not even the good itself. So they require their friends to be pleasant. But they ought, we may add, to require them to be good as well as pleasant — and good for them; then everything a friend should be will be combined in one person.

Men in high places seem to keep their friends in separate compartments. Some of their friends are useful and others are pleasant, but rarely is the same man both; for they are not looking for people who are at once agreeable and virtuous, or people useful for noble ends. When they want pleasure they seek out wits, and for everything else they choose men who are clever at carrying out orders — and those two qualities are seldom found in one person.

The good man, as we have said, is both pleasant and useful at once. But such a man does not become the friend of a man above him in station unless the superior is also superior in virtue; otherwise the good man cannot establish proportionate equality — inferior in one respect exactly as he is superior in another. And great men are not much in the habit of conceding this.

In the friendships discussed so far, the parties are equals: they do the same for each other and wish the same for each other, or else they exchange equal amounts of different things — pleasure for profit, say. (We have already explained that these last are friendships in a lesser degree, and less lasting. Being at once like true friendship and unlike it, they seem both to be and not to be friendships. Insofar as they resemble the friendship based on virtue — the one involving pleasure and the other profit, both of which true friendship contains — they seem to be friendships; but insofar as true friendship is proof against slander and lasting, while they change quickly and differ in many other ways, they seem not to be friendships at all. The unlikeness shows.)

Chapter 7

But besides these there is another kind of friendship — friendship between unequals: of a father for a son, and generally of an older person for a younger, of a man for a woman, of a ruler of any kind for a subject.

These, too, differ among themselves. The friendship of parent for child is not the same as that of ruler for subject; nor, for that matter, is the friendship of father for son the same as that of son for father, nor that of man for woman the same as that of woman for man. For each of these persons has a different virtue and a different function, and the grounds of their affection differ; so their love and their friendship differ too. What each does for the other, then, is not the same, and they should not expect it to be the same. But when children give their parents what is owed to those who gave them life, and parents give their children what is owed to children, then the friendship between them will be lasting, and what it ought to be. And in every friendship between unequals, the love on each side should be proportional: the better of the two — and the more useful, and so on in each case — should receive more love than he gives. For when love is proportioned to desert, a sort of equality is established; and equality seems to be a necessary condition of friendship.

But equality does not seem to work the same way in friendship as it does in justice. In the sphere of justice, "equal" means first of all "proportionate to merit," and "equal in quantity" only secondarily; in friendship it is the reverse — "equal in quantity" comes first, "proportionate to merit" second.

You can see this plainly wherever a great gap opens between two people in virtue, or vice, or wealth, or anything else: they no longer are friends, and no longer even expect to be. It is plainest of all in the case of the gods, whose superiority in every good thing is greatest. But it shows in the case of kings as well: men far beneath them do not claim their friendship, nor do men of no account expect to be friends with the best and wisest in the state. There is no drawing an exact line in such cases, no fixing the point up to which friendship can survive. Much can be taken away and the friendship remain; but when one party is removed to a vast distance — as God is — it can no longer exist.

This has prompted an objection: that in the end a friend does not really wish his friend the greatest of all goods — namely, to become a god — for then he would lose him as a friend, and so lose a good; for a friend is a good thing. If, then, we were right to say that a friend wishes his friend's good for the friend's own sake, we must add: "the friend remaining what he is." He will wish him the greatest goods a man can have — though perhaps not all of them; for every man wishes good most of all to himself.

Chapter 8

Most people, out of a desire for honor, seem to want to be loved rather than to love; and that is why most men are fond of flatterers. For the flatterer is a friend in an inferior position — or pretends to be one, and pretends to love more than he is loved; and being loved is thought to come close to being honored, which is what most men are striving for.

But it seems they desire honor not for its own sake, but incidentally. Most men delight in being honored by those in power because of what they expect: they hope to get from them whatever they happen to want, and so they enjoy the honor as a token of good things to come. Those, on the other hand, who want the respect of good men, of men who know, are seeking to confirm their own opinion of themselves; they take joy in the assurance of their worth, trusting the judgment of those who declare it.

Being loved, however, men delight in for its own sake. It would seem to follow that being loved is better than being honored, and that friendship is desirable in itself.

But friendship seems to lie more in loving than in being loved. The proof is the delight mothers take in loving. Some mothers give their children to others to bring up, and love them, knowing them for their own, without seeking love in return, when they cannot have both — content to see the children doing well, and loving them even though the children, in their ignorance, give back nothing of what is due a mother.

Since friendship lies more in loving than in being loved, and since we praise those who love their friends, it seems that loving is the virtue of friends. So when people love each other in proportion to their worth, they are lasting friends, and theirs is a lasting friendship.

This is also how unequals can be friends most truly; for in this way they are made equal. And equality and likeness are what make friendship — above all the likeness of those who are alike in virtue. For such men, being little liable to change, stay as they were, in themselves and toward each other; they never ask anything unworthy of one another, or do anything unworthy for one another — rather, they hold each other back from anything of the sort. For it is the mark of a good man neither to go wrong himself nor to let his friend go wrong.

Bad men, as friends, have no such steadiness; they do not even stay like themselves. They become friends for a short spell, delighting in each other's wickedness.

Friends who are useful or pleasant to each other last longer than that: as long, that is, as they keep supplying pleasure or profit.

The friendship whose motive is the useful seems, more than any other kind, to be a union of opposites — rich and poor, ignorant and learned; for when a man wants something, he will give something else in exchange to get it. And perhaps we could pull the lover and his beloved, the beautiful person and the ugly one, into this class. This is why lovers sometimes make themselves ridiculous by demanding to be loved as they love. If they were equally lovable, the claim might stand; but when there is nothing lovable about them, it is absurd.

But perhaps nothing desires its opposite as such — only accidentally, the desire being really for the mean between the two, since that is what is good. For the dry, for instance, the good is not to become wet, but to reach the intermediate state; and so with the hot, and the rest of these opposites. But let us set these questions aside; they are, in truth, somewhat foreign to our present purpose.
