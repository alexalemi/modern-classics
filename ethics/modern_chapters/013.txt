Book VI

(Part 1 of 2)

The Intellectual Virtues

Chapter 1

We said earlier that what we should choose is neither too much nor too little but "the mean," and that the mean is what "right reason" prescribes. Now we have to explain what that means.

Every virtue we have discussed implies — as every habit of mind implies — some target that the rational man keeps his eye on as he tightens or slackens his effort. In other words, there must be some standard that fixes the various forms of moderation we have been describing, the states that we say lie between excess and deficiency and accord with right reason. All of this is quite true — but it is not precise enough. In any occupation that can be reduced to rational principles, it is perfectly true to say that we must strain ourselves neither too much nor too little, but "in moderation," "as right reason orders." Yet by itself that tells you almost nothing. A man would hardly learn how to treat a patient by being told to treat him as the art of medicine prescribes, and as someone versed in that art would treat him.

So too with the habits and characters of the soul: it is not enough that the rule we have laid down is correct. We need to know precisely what this right reason is, and what standard it gives us.

The virtues of the soul, you will remember, we divided into two classes, calling one moral and the other intellectual. The moral virtues we have already discussed in detail; let us now examine the other class, the intellectual virtues — after a few preliminary remarks about the soul.

We said before that the soul consists of two parts, the rational and the irrational. Let us now divide the rational part in the same way, and assume that it contains two rational faculties: one by which we know the things that rest on invariable principles — things that cannot be otherwise — and one by which we know the things that vary. For generically different objects must answer to generically different faculties, if, as we hold, our faculties are able to know their objects through some kind of likeness or kinship with them.

Call the first the scientific or demonstrative faculty, and the second the calculative or deliberative faculty; for deliberating and calculating are the same thing, and no one deliberates about what cannot be otherwise. So one division of the rational faculty may fairly be called the calculative.

Our problem, then, is to find what each of these faculties is in its full development, its best state; for that will be its virtue.

And its virtue will refer directly to its proper function.

Chapter 2

Now, three things in us guide action and the grasp of truth: sense, reason, and desire.

The first of these originates no action. We can see that from the fact that the animals have sense but take no part in action.

Take the other two, and you find two modes of reasoning — affirmation and denial, assent and dissent — and two corresponding modes of desire — pursuit and avoidance, attraction and repulsion.

Now, moral virtue is a habit of choice, and choice is deliberate desire — desire that follows on deliberation.

It follows that if choice is to be everything it should be, the reasoning must be true and the desire right, and the very same things the reasoning affirms, the desire must pursue.

This, then, is the kind of reasoning — and the kind of truth — that has to do with action.

Speculative reasoning, which has to do with neither action nor production, is good or bad simply as it is true or false; for the function of the intellect as such is always the grasp of truth. But the function of the practical intellect is truth in agreement with right desire.

Choice, then, is the cause of action — not the final cause, the end it aims at, but the efficient cause, the source that sets it going — and the source of choice, in turn, is desire together with reasoning about means. So choice necessarily involves, on one side, the faculty of reason in exercise, and on the other a certain moral character, a certain state of the desires; for right action and its contrary are alike impossible without both reasoning and character.

Reasoning by itself, however, sets nothing in motion. Only reasoning that aims at an end and issues in action does that — what we may call practical reasoning. (Practical reasoning governs production too; for whenever you make something, you make it with a further object in view. The thing you make is not desired as an end in itself, but only as a means to, or condition of, something else. But the thing you do is an end in itself, for doing well — right action — is the end, and that is what desire aims at.)

Choice, then, may be called either reason that desires, or desire that reasons; and this power of originating action is what a man essentially is.

No past event can be chosen: no one chooses to have sacked Troy. We deliberate not about what is past but about what is to come and can be otherwise; the past cannot be undone. So Agathon is right when he says:

  One power even God must go without —
  to make undone what has once been done.

We have found, then, that both divisions of the reason — both intellectual faculties — have the attainment of truth as their function; and the developed state in which each best attains truth will be its virtue.

Chapter 3

Let us describe these virtues, then, starting fresh from the beginning.

Assume that the ways in which the mind arrives at truth, by affirming or denying, are five in number: art, science, practical wisdom, wisdom, and intuitive reason. Conception and opinion are left off the list, because they can be mistaken.

What science is we can learn from the following considerations (we want a precise account here, and must not settle for metaphors). We all suppose that what we know scientifically cannot be otherwise; whereas of what can be otherwise, we cannot say, the moment it is out of sight, whether it still exists or not. The object of science, then, is necessary — and therefore eternal, for whatever is necessary by its own nature is eternal, and the eternal neither comes to be nor passes away.

Further, all science is held to be teachable, and what can be known scientifically can be learned. But all teaching starts from something already known, as we explained in the Analytics; for it proceeds either by induction or by syllogism. Induction leads the learner up to universal principles; syllogism starts from them. So there are principles from which syllogism starts that syllogism itself cannot reach — and which must therefore be reached by induction.

Science, then, may be defined as a habit of demonstration — with all the further qualifications listed in the Analytics. We must add those, because a man properly has scientific knowledge only when the principles behind his knowledge are accepted and known to him in a particular way; unless those principles are better known to him than the conclusions drawn from them, his knowledge is merely accidental.

Let that stand, then, as our account of science.

Chapter 4

The variable — what can be otherwise — includes both what man makes and what man does; and making, or production, is different from doing, or action (here we follow the popular distinction). So the reasoned habit of acting is different from the reasoned habit of producing. Neither includes the other; for action is not production, and production is not action.

Now, the builder's skill is one of the arts, and can be described as a certain reasoned habit of producing; there is no art that is not a habit of this kind, and no habit of this kind that is not an art. An art, then, is the same thing as a habit of producing with correct reasoning.

And every art is concerned with bringing something into being — with contriving and calculating how to bring into being some thing that can either be or not be, and whose cause lies in the producer, not in the thing produced. For art has nothing to do with what is or comes to be by necessity, nor with the products of nature; those carry the cause of their coming-to-be within themselves.

Since production and action are different, art of course belongs with production, not with action. And in a certain sense art and chance work the same ground, as Agathon says:

  Art waits on fortune, fortune waits on art.

Art, then, as we said, is a habit of production with correct reasoning; and its contrary — lack of art — is a habit of production with incorrect reasoning; and the field of both is the variable.

Chapter 5

To find out what practical wisdom is, let us first ask who the people are that we call practically wise.

The mark of a man of practical wisdom seems to be that he can deliberate well about what is good and useful for himself — not in some particular department, such as what makes for health or strength, but about well-being, about living well as a whole.

Our usage confirms this: we apply the name to people who deliberate well in some particular field when they calculate well the means to some particular good end in matters that no art covers. So we may say generally that the man who can deliberate well is practically wise.

But no one deliberates about what cannot be altered, or about what is not in his power to do.

Now science, as we saw, involves demonstration; but things whose principles or causes are variable do not admit of demonstration — for everything that depends on such principles is variable too — and things that are fixed by necessity admit of no deliberation. It follows that practical wisdom can be neither a science nor an art: not a science, because the sphere of action is what can be altered; not an art, because production and action are generically different.

What remains is this: practical wisdom is a reasoned and true habit of mind that issues in action, in the field of human good and evil. For production has an end beyond itself, but action does not — good action, doing well, is itself the end.

This is why Pericles and men like him are considered practically wise: they are able to see what is good for themselves and for men in general; and that, we take it, is the character of those who can manage a household or a state.

This, too, is why the Greeks gave self-control its name, sophrosyne — the word suggests "that which preserves practical wisdom." And what self-control preserves is precisely this kind of judgment. For pleasure and pain do not destroy or distort every judgment — not, for instance, the judgment that the angles of a triangle are equal to two right angles — but only judgments about matters of practice. The principles of action are the ends for whose sake we act; and the man corrupted by pleasure or pain straightaway loses sight of the principle: he no longer sees that this is the end for whose sake, and as a means to which, each particular act should be chosen and done. For vice is a destroyer of principles.

Our conclusion, then: practical wisdom is a reasoned and true habit that issues in action, in the field of human good.

Moreover, art has its excellence — its perfect development — in something beyond itself; not so practical wisdom. Again, in art the man who errs on purpose is better than the man who errs against his will; in practical wisdom the deliberate error is worse, as it is in all the virtues. Plainly, then, practical wisdom is itself a virtue, and not an art.

And since the rational parts of the soul are two, practical wisdom will be the virtue of the second — the calculative part, the faculty of opinion; for opinion deals with what can be otherwise, and so does practical wisdom.

Yet it is something more than merely "a reasoned habit of grasping truth." We can see that from this: a merely reasoned habit can be forgotten and lost, but practical wisdom, once acquired, is never lost.

Chapter 6

Science is a mode of judgment that deals with universal and necessary truths; and demonstrable truths depend on principles — since science proceeds by demonstration, every science has its principles. The principles on which scientific truths depend, then, cannot be the business of science itself, nor of art, nor of practical wisdom: for what is known scientifically can be demonstrated, while art and practical wisdom deal with what can be otherwise.

Nor are these principles the business of wisdom; for it is characteristic of the wise man to have demonstrative knowledge of certain things — and demonstration is not how principles are reached.

Now, the habits of mind by which we grasp truth without any admixture of error, whether about things invariable or things variable, are science, practical wisdom, wisdom, and intuitive reason. If none of the first three — practical wisdom, science, wisdom — can be the faculty that grasps first principles, only one conclusion is left: first principles are grasped by intuitive reason.

Chapter 7

The word "wisdom" is sometimes applied, in the domain of the arts, to the consummate masters of a craft: Phidias is called wise as a master of sculpture, Polyclitus for his skill in portrait statues; and in this use it means nothing more than excellence of art, the artistic faculty brought to perfection.

But there are also men we consider wise not in one department, not in any particular thing — as Homer says in the Margites:

  The gods gave him no skill with spade or plow,
  nor made him wise in anything at all —

but wise generally. In this general sense, then, wisdom will plainly be the most perfect of the sciences.

The wise man, then, must not only know what follows from the first principles of knowledge; he must also know the truth about the principles themselves. Wisdom, therefore, will be intuitive reason joined with demonstrative scientific knowledge — scientific knowledge of the noblest objects with its crowning perfection, so to speak, added to it. For it would be absurd to suppose that statesmanship, or practical wisdom, is the highest of our faculties — unless man is the best thing in the universe.

Now, "wholesome" and "good" mean one thing for men and another for fish, while "white" and "straight" mean the same thing always. In the same way, we must all allow that "wise" means one thing always, while "practically wise" means different things for different beings; for we call practically wise whatever sees clearly to its own affairs, and we judge it fit to be trusted with them. (This is why we sometimes apply the word even to animals — the ones that show foresight about their own lives.)

It is plain, too, that wisdom cannot be the same as statesmanship. If we call knowledge of our own advantage "wisdom," there will be many wisdoms; for there is no single knowledge of the good of all animals — each species has its own — any more than there is a single art of healing for every living thing.

It makes no difference that man may be superior to all the other animals; for there are things far more divine in their nature than man — to take the most conspicuous instance, the heavenly bodies.

After all this it is plain that wisdom is scientific, demonstrative knowledge joined with intuitive reason, about objects of the noblest nature.

And this is why people call Anaxagoras and Thales and men of that sort wise, but not practically wise — seeing that they are ignorant of their own advantage — and say that they know things that are extraordinary, wonderful, difficult, and divine, but useless, because it is no human good that they seek.

Practical wisdom, on the other hand, deals with human affairs, with matters that admit of deliberation. For the special function of the man of practical wisdom, as we conceive him, is to deliberate well; and no one deliberates about what cannot be otherwise, or about matters with no end in view — no achievable good. And the man who deliberates well, without qualification, is the man who can arrive, by reasoning and calculation, at the best thing attainable by man in action.

Nor does practical wisdom deal only in general propositions. It must know particular facts as well; for it issues in action, and the field of action is the field of particulars.

This is why some men who lack the theory are more effective in practice than others who have it — above all, men of wide experience. If you know that light meat is digestible and wholesome, but do not know which meats are light, you will not cure anyone; the man who only knows that chicken is light and wholesome will do better.

But practical wisdom is concerned with practice; so it needs knowledge of both kinds — general truths and particular facts — but especially the particular.

Here too, however, there must be a supreme form of the faculty; and to it we now turn.

Chapter 8

Statesmanship and practical wisdom are in fact the same habit of mind, though they show themselves differently.

Of this faculty as applied to the state, the supreme form is the legislative; the form that deals with particular cases is called by the generic name, statesmanship. This latter is concerned with action and deliberation — for a decree bears directly on action, as the last link in the chain. And this is why only men engaged in this work are said to take part in politics: they alone act, the way craftsmen do.

But the faculty is most of all regarded as practical wisdom when it is applied to the individual, to one's own affairs; and it is this form that receives the generic name, practical wisdom — the others being household management, legislation, and statesmanship in the narrower sense, which divides in turn into the deliberative and the judicial.

Knowing one's own good, then, would seem to be one kind of knowledge — though a very various one. In the general opinion, the man who knows and minds his own affairs is practically wise, while statesmen are busybodies — as Euripides has it:

  Call that wise? I could have lived carefree,
  one man in the ranks among the many,
  an equal share with every other man ...
  but those who reach too high, who do too much —

For men generally seek their own good, and suppose that this is what they ought to do; and from this opinion comes the notion that such men are the practically wise ones.

And yet, perhaps, a man cannot manage his own affairs well without managing a household — and even taking part in the management of a state.

Moreover, how a man ought to manage his own affairs is not obvious; it needs looking into. Here is the evidence: a young man may become expert in geometry or mathematics, and wise in such matters, but nobody thinks a young man can have practical wisdom. The reason is that practical wisdom deals with particular facts, and particular facts become familiar only through experience — which a young man cannot have, for experience is the fruit of years.

We might ask, while we are here, why a boy can be a mathematician but not wise, and no expert in nature either. Surely the answer is that mathematics is an abstract science, while the principles of wisdom and of natural science are drawn only from long experience. A young man can repeat the propositions of the latter, but he does not really believe them; whereas the meanings of mathematical terms he grasps without trouble.

Again, error in deliberation may lie either in the universal judgment or in the particular one: you may be wrong in judging that all water that weighs heavy is unwholesome, or wrong in judging that this water weighs heavy. But practical wisdom, for all its universal judgments, is plainly not science; for, as we said, it deals with the ultimate particular fact — the last link in the chain — since that is what can actually be done.

And so it stands, in a way, opposite to intuitive reason as well. Intuitive reason grasps the primary principles, for which no demonstration can be given; practical wisdom grasps the ultimate particular, which cannot be scientifically proved but is perceived — not by one of the special senses, but by the kind of perception by which, in mathematics, we see that this particular figure before us is a triangle; for there too our reasoning must come to a stop. Still, this faculty of grasping particulars is really perception rather than practical wisdom, though it is perception of another kind.
