Chapter 5

Some people go further and think that justice is simply reciprocity — that whatever a man has done to another should be done to him in return. That was the teaching of the Pythagoreans; their whole definition of the just came down to this: what you did, you suffer.

But simple reciprocity does not fit either distributive justice or corrective justice — though people try to read it into the rule of Rhadamanthus: "To suffer what you have done is justice." In many cases the two come apart. If a man holding public office strikes someone, he ought not to be struck back; but if someone strikes an officer, he ought not merely to be struck in return — he ought to be punished on top of it.

Further, it makes a great difference whether what was done to the other man was done with his consent or against it.

And yet, in the exchange of services, reciprocity of a kind is exactly the rule of justice that holds society together — not simple reciprocity, though, but proportionate reciprocity: not equal repaid with equal, but repayment in proportion. The very existence of a state depends on proportionate return. When men have been done evil, they look to return it — if a man cannot repay an injury, we count his condition slavish. And when men have been done good, they look to repay that too; for without repayment there is no exchange of services, and it is the exchange of services that binds us together in society.

This is why we set up a temple of the Graces — the spirits of grace and gratitude — where everyone can see it: to remind people to repay what they have received. For that is the special mark of grace: to return the kindness of those who have been kind to us, and then to take the lead in kindness ourselves.

Proportionate exchange is produced by what we may call cross-conjunction — a crosswise pairing.

For instance: let A stand for a builder, B for a shoemaker, C for a house, and D for shoes.

The builder must take some of the shoemaker's work, and give the shoemaker his own work in exchange.

Now, the result we want will follow if the exchange takes place after proportionate equality has first been established between the goods.

If it is not, there is no equality, and the dealing collapses; for there is no reason why the work of the one should not be worth more than the work of the other. Their products, then, must first be brought to an equality — appraised by some common standard of value.

And this holds for every other art and profession just as much as for building and shoemaking. None of them could survive if what the customer receives did not correspond, in quantity and in quality, to what the producer supplies.

For exchange does not happen between two physicians. It happens between a physician and a farmer — in general, between people of different professions whose work is of unequal worth. These unequal parties, then, have to be reduced to equality, measured by a common standard.

So everything to be exchanged — every good, every service — must somehow be reducible to a common measure.

This is what money was invented for. It serves as a medium of exchange; with it we can measure everything, and so we can measure how much one kind of work is worth against another — how many shoes, for instance, are equivalent to a house, or to a given quantity of food.

What we need, then, is for a certain number of shoes to stand to a house (or to a measure of grain) in the same ratio that a builder (or a farmer) stands to a shoemaker. Unless this adjustment is made, no dealing or exchange of services can happen at all; and it cannot be made unless the goods can somehow be made equal.

We need, in short, one common measure of value, as we said before.

And in truth that measure is demand — our need of one another's services — which is what holds the members of a society together in the first place. If men had no needs, or no needs in common, there would either be no exchange, or a very different sort of exchange from the one we know.

But money has been introduced by convention as a sort of representative of demand. That is why the Greek word for it is nomisma: its value comes not from nature but from law — nomos — and it lies with us to change it or to withdraw it from use.

Exchange, then, will take place once the wares have been equated — once prices have adjusted them — so that the quantity of the shoemaker's work stands to the quantity of the farmer's work in the same ratio that farmer stands to shoemaker. But this adjustment must be made not at the moment of exchange (for then one of the two parties would pocket both advantages), but while each still holds his own wares. Do it then, and the two are put on an equal footing and can deal with each other, because this kind of equality can be established between them.

If A stands for a farmer and C for a certain quantity of his work (or his grain), then B will stand for a shoemaker and D for the quantity of shoemaker's work that is valued as equal to C.

If people could not reciprocate in this way, there would be no exchange of services at all.

That it is need which forms, as it were, the common bond holding society together is plain from this: people do not exchange unless they need one another's services — both parties, or at least one of them. That is why, when one people has something others need — wine, say — the others offer to export grain in return; and this grain must then be made equal in value to the wine.

But even if we happen to need nothing at the moment, money is a sort of guarantee that we will be able to make an exchange at any future time when we do need something; for whoever brings money must always be able to take goods away in exchange.

Money, it is true, is subject to the same conditions as everything else: its value does not always stay the same. Still, it tends to be more constant than the value of anything else.

Everything, then, must be assessed in money; for this is what lets men exchange their services at all times, and so makes society possible.

Money, as a standard, reduces things to a common measure so that equal amounts of each can be taken. For there would be no society without exchange, no exchange without equality, and no equality without a common measure.

Strictly speaking, of course, no truly common measure can be found for things so wildly different. But our needs supply a standard that is accurate enough for practical purposes.

There must, then, be some one common unit, and it must be fixed by convention — which is why we call it money, nomisma, from nomos. Money makes all things commensurable, for everything is valued in money. For instance: let A stand for a house, B for ten minae (a sum of money), and C for a bed. Let A equal half of B — taking a house to be worth five minae — and let C, the bed, equal one tenth of B. Then we see at once how many beds equal one house: five.

Clearly this is how all exchange must have worked before money came into use; for it makes no difference whether you give five beds for a house, or the price of five beds.

So much for what is unjust and what is just. Now that these are settled, we can see that just conduct is a mean between doing injustice and suffering it: the one is having too much, the other having too little, of one's due.

We can see, too, that the virtue justice is a kind of observance of the mean — but not in quite the same way as the virtues discussed so far. It does aim at a mean; but here both extremes fall under a single vice, injustice.

Justice, then, is the habit in virtue of which the just man is said to be disposed to do deliberately what is just: that is, in dealings between himself and another (or between two other parties), to apportion things so that he does not grab more of what is desirable for himself and leave his neighbor less — and the reverse with what is burdensome — but so that each gets his fair share, meaning his proportionate share; and likewise when he is judging between two other parties.

Injustice, on the contrary, is the character that chooses what is unjust — a disproportionate amount: too much or too little of what is advantageous or disadvantageous, as the case may be.

That is why injustice is both an excess and a deficiency: it chooses both. In a man's own affairs, it chooses an excess of what is (as a general rule) advantageous and a deficiency of what is disadvantageous; in the affairs of others, it makes a similarly disproportionate assignment, though which way the proportion gets violated will depend on circumstances.

And of the two sides of an unjust deed, to suffer the injustice is a lesser evil than to do it.

Let this stand, then, as our account, in general terms, of the nature of justice and injustice, and of what is just and what is unjust.

Chapter 6

But since a man can do an unjust act without yet being an unjust man, we may ask: are there acts of injustice such that a single one stamps the doer at once as unjust in this or that way — as a thief, say, or an adulterer, or a robber?

Perhaps the answer is that the difference does not lie in the acts at all. A man might commit adultery knowing perfectly well what he was doing, and yet act not from deliberate choice but from a moment's passion. In that case he acts unjustly, but he is not an unjust man — not a thief, though he committed a theft; not an adulterer, though he committed adultery; and so on down the line.

We have already explained how reciprocity is related to what is just. But we must not lose sight of what we are actually looking for: justice in the simple, unqualified sense — and that is political justice, justice between citizens. This kind of justice exists among men who share a common life in order to supply their needs, men who are free and on a footing of equality, whether absolute equality or proportionate.

Where men are not on this footing, there is no political justice between them — only something that can be called justice by a stretch, metaphorically. For the term "just" strictly applies only where men have law to appeal to; and the existence of law implies the existence of injustice, since the administration of law is precisely the sorting of the just from the unjust.

And where there is injustice, there is unjust dealing (though not every unjust act implies an unjust man) — that is, taking too much of the goods of life and too little of its burdens. This is why we do not allow an individual man to rule over us, but rather reason — that is, law; for an individual is prone to take more for himself and turn tyrant.

The magistrate's job is to guard what is just, and if what is just, then what is equal and fair. And if he is a just man, it seems he gets no profit out of his office — he does not take a larger share of the good things of life, except where a larger share is proportionate to his worth. He works, in other words, for the benefit of others; which is why justice is sometimes called "another's good," as we remarked before. So some salary must be given him, and this he receives in the form of honor and privilege. It is when magistrates are not content with these that they make themselves tyrants.

What is just between master and slave, or between father and child, is not the same as political justice, though it resembles it. There can be no injustice, strictly speaking, toward what is part of oneself — and a man's property, and his children until they reach a certain age and set up for themselves, are as it were a part of him. No one deliberately chooses to injure himself; and so a man cannot be unjust toward himself.

Here, then, there is no political justice or injustice. That, we found, is a matter of law, and it exists between people whose situation implies law — people who share equally, or fairly, in ruling and being ruled.

The term "just," therefore, applies more properly to a man's relations with his wife than to his relations with his children and his property. This is household justice; but even it is not the same as justice between citizens.

Chapter 7

Now, political justice is partly natural and partly conventional. Natural justice is what has the same force everywhere and does not depend on our accepting or rejecting it. Conventional justice is what could originally have been settled one way or another indifferently, but once settled is indifferent no longer — for instance, that a prisoner's ransom be one mina, or that a sacrifice be a goat rather than two sheep; and, again, all the rules made for particular occasions, like the sacrifice decreed in honor of Brasidas at Amphipolis, and everything that has the character of a decree.

Now, some people think all justice is conventional, on this reasoning: what is natural never varies and has the same force everywhere — fire burns both here and in Persia — while what is just plainly does vary.

But this is not quite right, though there is something in it. Among the gods, perhaps, it is not true at all; but among us, part of what is just is natural, even though all of it is subject to change. All of it can change — and yet, I repeat, part is natural and part is not.

Nor is it hard to tell, among things that can be otherwise than they are, which are natural and which rest instead on law and convention — even though both kinds vary. The same distinction holds in other fields. The right hand, we say, is naturally the stronger; yet in any given man the left may become just as strong.

So with justice: the conventional part, the part prescribed with a view to some particular purpose, varies the way measures vary. Measures for wine and grain are not the same everywhere — they run larger where dealers buy and smaller where they sell. Likewise, what is just not by nature but by human ordinance is not the same everywhere, any more than constitutions are the same everywhere — though there is only one constitution that is by nature the best everywhere.

Each of the terms "just" and "lawful," in its several senses, stands for a universal that covers many particulars: the acts are many, but the notion is one, applying to them all alike.

Notice, too, that "what is unjust" is different from "an act of injustice," and "what is just" from "an act of justice." A thing is unjust by nature or by ordinance; but this same thing, once done, is called an act of injustice — before it was done, it was merely unjust. The same holds for an act of justice; though here Greek usage prefers dikaiopragema as the general term for a just act, and reserves dikaioma for the correcting of an act of injustice. But the several species of just and unjust acts — their nature, their number, the ground they cover — we must postpone for the present.

Chapter 8

Now that we know what is just and what is unjust, we can say this: a man acts unjustly or justly when he does these things voluntarily. When he does them involuntarily, he acts neither unjustly nor justly, strictly speaking, but only "accidentally" — he does something that happens to be just or unjust. For whether a deed counts as an act of injustice (or of justice) depends on whether it is voluntary or involuntary. If it is voluntary, the doer is blamed, and only then does the deed become an act of injustice. Something unjust may be done, then, without there being any act of injustice at all — if the voluntariness is missing.

By a voluntary act I mean, as I explained before, anything within the doer's control that is done knowingly — knowing the person, the instrument, and the result: whom he is striking, what he is striking with, and what the blow will do — with no accident and no compulsion intervening at any point. If someone else seizes your hand and strikes a third person with it, that is no voluntary act of yours: it was not in your control. Again, the man you strike may be your father, while you know only that he is a man, or one of the company, but not that he is your father. The same distinction applies to the result, and, in a word, to the whole act. What is done in ignorance, then — or done not in ignorance but outside our control, or under compulsion — is involuntary. (There are also many natural processes, like growing old and dying, in which we knowingly take part, actively or passively, and which are neither voluntary nor involuntary.)

An accidentally unjust act is just as possible as an accidentally just one. A man might return a deposit unwillingly, out of fear of the consequences: we cannot say he did what is just, or acted justly, except accidentally. And likewise a man who is forcibly prevented, against his will, from returning a deposit acts unjustly, or does what is unjust, only accidentally.

Voluntary acts divide in turn into those done from choice and those done without choice — that is, those done with previous deliberation and those done without it.

Now, there are three ways in which we may hurt our neighbor.

First, a hurt done in ignorance is generally called a mistake, when there is a misconception about the person affected, or the thing done, or the instrument, or the result: I did not think I would hit at all, or not with this instrument, or not this person, or not with this effect — but something followed other than what I had in mind. I meant a prick, not a wound; or I did not mean to wound this man; or not to deal a wound of this kind.

But we can draw the line more precisely. When the hurt comes about contrary to all reasonable expectation, it is a misadventure. When it is not contrary to reasonable expectation but still involves no vicious intent, it is a mistake. For a man makes a mistake when the origin of the trouble lies in himself — he set the train of events in motion; he meets with misadventure when it comes from outside.

Second, when the doer acts knowingly but without previous deliberation, it is an act of injustice — as when a man is driven by anger, or by any of the other passions that human beings are naturally and unavoidably subject to. In doing such hurts and committing such wrongs, the doer acts unjustly, and the acts are acts of injustice; but they do not stamp him as an unjust or wicked man, for the hurt was not done out of wickedness.

But third, when the hurt is done from choice, the doer is unjust and wicked.

This is why acts done in anger are rightly judged not to be done with malice aforethought: it was the man who gave the provocation who started it, not the man who struck in a passion.

In cases of this last kind, moreover, what people dispute is usually not whether the deed was done, but where the justice of the case lies; for it is the appearance of injustice that stirs the striker's anger. This is different from disputes about contracts. There the question is one of fact, and one of the two parties must be a vicious character — unless his memory is simply at fault. But here they agree about the facts and differ about which side is in the right (whereas the deliberate aggressor knows perfectly well where the right lies): the one thinks he has been wronged; the other thinks not.

But if a man hurts another from choice, he acts unjustly; and acts of injustice of this kind — violations of what is proportionate and fair — stamp the doer as an unjust character.

In the same way, a man is a just character when he acts justly from choice; but he is said merely to act justly whenever he voluntarily does what is just.

Of involuntary injuries, finally, some are pardonable and some are not. Wrongs committed not only in ignorance but because of ignorance are pardonable. Wrongs committed in ignorance, but not because of it — because of some passion neither natural nor human — are not.
