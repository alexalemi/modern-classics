Book I

(Part 2 of 2)

Chapter 8

We should not be content, then, to test this starting point of ours only against our premises and our conclusion; we must also test it against what people actually say about happiness. For everything in experience chimes with a true principle, while a false one soon collides with the facts.

Now, good things have long been sorted into three classes: external goods on one side, and on the other goods of the soul and goods of the body. And goods of the soul are commonly held to be goods in the fullest sense — more truly good than any of the others.

But "actions and activities of the soul" surely count as goods "of the soul." So our account is confirmed by this opinion — an old one, and endorsed by everyone who occupies himself with philosophy.

Indeed, our account secures the support of this opinion simply by saying that certain actions and activities are the end; for that ranks the end among the goods of the soul, not among external goods.

Our account also agrees with the common saying that the happy man "lives well" and "does well"; for happiness, as we have described it, just is a kind of living well and doing well.

More than that: everything people look for in happiness seems to be contained in happiness as we define it.

Some hold that happiness is virtue, or some particular virtue; some say practical wisdom; others a kind of wisdom; others again say all or some of these, with pleasure added, either as an ingredient or as a necessary accompaniment; and some even work external prosperity into their account.

Now, some of these views are held by many people and have age on their side; others by a few, but those few carry weight. And it is likely that neither camp is entirely wrong — that each is right on at least one point, if not on most.

First, then, the view that happiness is virtue, or some kind of virtue, harmonizes with our account; for "activity in accordance with virtue" surely belongs to virtue.

But I think it makes no small difference whether we conceive the good as mere possession or as use — as a habit or as the active exercise of that habit. For the habit can be present and yet produce nothing good, as when a man is asleep or otherwise kept from his function; the activity cannot. It must show itself in acts, and in good acts. Just as at the Olympic games it is not the finest and strongest bodies that are crowned but those who compete — for the victors come from among these — so in life too, the prizes go to those who not only have the virtues but put them into action.

Further, the life of such men is pleasant in itself. Pleasure is a state of the soul, and each man finds his pleasure in whatever he is said to love: the lover of horses in horses, the lover of sights in sightseeing — and in the same way, the lover of justice in just acts, and generally the lover of virtue in virtuous acts.

Now with most men, the things they take pleasure in are perpetually at war with one another, because those things are not pleasant by nature. But those who love the noble take pleasure in what is naturally pleasant; and virtuous acts are naturally pleasant, so that such acts are pleasant both to these men and in themselves.

Their life, then, has no need of pleasure fastened onto it as an accessory; it carries its pleasure within itself.

Indeed — to add one more point — a man is not even good unless he takes pleasure in noble deeds. No one would call a man just who did not enjoy acting justly, or generous who took no pleasure in acts of generosity; and so on down the list.

If this is so, then virtuous acts are pleasant in themselves. But they are also good and noble, and each of these in the highest degree — if the good man's judgment about them is right, and his judgment is as we have said.

Happiness, then, is at once the best and noblest and pleasantest thing in the world; and these three do not come apart, as the inscription at Delos would have it:

  Noblest is what is most just; best of all is health;
  but pleasantest is to win your heart's desire.

For all of them together belong to the best activities; and it is these activities — or the one among them that is best of all — that we identify with happiness.

And yet happiness plainly needs external goods too, as we said. For it is impossible, or at least not easy, to act nobly without some equipment of fortune. Many things can be done only with instruments, so to speak — friends, wealth, political influence. And there are some things whose absence takes the bloom off our happiness: good birth, good children, personal beauty. A man is not very likely to be happy if he is downright ugly, or of low birth, or alone in the world and childless — and perhaps less likely still if his children or friends are worthless, or if he had good ones and lost them.

As we said, then, happiness seems to require this kind of prosperity as well; which is why some identify happiness with good fortune, just as others identify it with virtue.

Chapter 9

This raises a further question: is happiness attained by learning, or by forming habits, or by some other kind of training? Or does it come by some divine dispensation — or even by chance?

Well, if the Gods give any gifts to men, happiness is likely to be among them — likelier, indeed, than anything else, in proportion as it is better than everything else that is human.

But this belongs more properly to another inquiry. Even if happiness is not heaven-sent, though — even if it comes through virtue or some kind of learning or training — it still seems to be one of the most divine things there is; for the prize and end of virtue is evidently something supremely good, something divine and blessed.

Again, if it is acquired in this way, it will be open to many; for then everyone not crippled for virtue will be able to win it by study and care.

And if it is better that men come to happiness this way rather than by chance, it is reasonable to suppose things really are so — since in nature everything is arranged as well as it can possibly be, and likewise in art, and in every kind of causation, above all in the highest kind. To hand over the greatest and noblest of things to chance would be far too jarring a note.

But our definition itself clears up the difficulty. Happiness was defined as an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue — or, if there is more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete of them. Of the remaining goods — the goods other than happiness itself — some must be present as necessary conditions, while others are aids, useful instruments of happiness. And this agrees with what we said at the start. We laid it down that the end of Politics is the best of ends; but the chief business of Politics is to make the citizens people of a certain character — good, and apt to do what is noble. It is no accident, then, that we call no ox or horse or any other animal happy; for none of them can share in activity of this kind.

For the same reason, no child is happy either: his age keeps him, as yet, from doing such things. When we do call a child happy, it is because we hope he will do them. For, as we said, happiness requires not only complete virtue but also a full term of years for its exercise. Our circumstances are subject to many turns and to every sort of chance, and it is possible that the most prosperous man alive will meet with great disasters in his old age, as the tales of Troy tell of Priam; and a man who has been dealt fortune like that, and has come to a miserable end, no one calls happy.

Chapter 10

Are we, then, to call no man happy while he lives? Must we look to the end, as Solon said, and call no man happy until he is dead?

And if we do grant that, do we mean that a man actually is happy once he is dead? Surely that is absurd — especially for us, who say that happiness is a kind of activity, a kind of life.

But suppose we do not call the dead man happy, and suppose Solon meant not that, but only that when a man is dead you can at last pronounce him happy safely, as being now out of reach of evil and misfortune. Even this can be disputed. For people think that good and evil can, in some fashion, touch a dead man — just as they can touch a living man without his being aware of them — honors paid to him, for instance, and disgraces, and the successes and misfortunes of his children and descendants generally.

But this raises difficulties of its own. A man may have lived happily into old age and ended as he lived, and yet all sorts of reversals may come upon his descendants — some of them may be good people who meet the fortune they deserve, others the opposite. And clearly the descendants may stand at any degree of remove from their ancestor. It would be strange if the dead man changed along with all this, becoming happy and miserable by turns; but it would also be strange if the fortunes of his line did not reach the ancestors at all, even for some limited time.

But let us go back to our earlier question; its solution may clear up this difficulty too.

Suppose Solon's saying means that we should look to the end and only then call a man happy — not as being happy now, but as having been happy once.

Surely it is strange that at the very time when he is happy we should refuse to say of him what is true. We decline to call the living happy because their fortunes may change, and because we take happiness to be something enduring and hard to shake, while the wheel of fortune keeps turning for one and the same man. Obviously, the argument runs, if we track a man's fortunes, we shall call him happy and then miserable over and over, making the happy man "a sort of chameleon, one who rests on no sound foundation."

Our reply is that tracking fortune like this cannot be right. For our good and ill do not lie in fortune. Human life needs good fortune added to it, as we said; but it is virtuous activity that constitutes a man's happiness, as the contrary activity constitutes his misery.

In fact, the difficulty we have been discussing turns into further confirmation of our account. For nothing human is as stable as activity in accordance with virtue; such activities seem more lasting even than the sciences. And the highest of them are the most lasting of all, because the happy spend their lives in them most fully and most continuously — which seems to be exactly why we do not forget how to do them.

The happy man, then, as we define him, will have the permanence we are looking for, and will keep his character all through life. For he will always, or with the least possible interruption, be occupied in virtuous deeds and virtuous thinking; and whatever his fortune, he will bear it in the noblest fashion and carry himself fittingly always and in everything, since he is truly good — "foursquare, without a flaw."

But fortune deals out many things, some great, some small. The small ones, good or bad, plainly carry no weight in the scale. The great ones, when they come in numbers, will make life happier if they are good — for they add a grace of their own to life, and the use a man makes of them is itself noble and good; but if they are evil, they crush and maim happiness, for they bring pain and get in the way of many activities.

And yet even here nobility shines through — when a man bears many great misfortunes with calm, not because he feels nothing, but out of nobility and greatness of soul. And if it is a man's activities that set the character of his life, as we said, then no happy man can become miserable; for he will never do what is hateful and base. The truly good and sensible man, we hold, bears whatever fortune sends with dignity, and always makes the best of his circumstances — as a good general makes the most warlike use of the army he has, and a good shoemaker makes the best shoe that can be made from the leather he is given, and so on through all the crafts.

If this is so, the happy man will never become miserable — though neither will he be truly happy if he meets the fate of Priam.

Nor, on the other hand, is he shifting and changeable. He will not be dislodged from his happiness easily, or by ordinary misfortunes, but only by many heavy ones; and after such blows he will not recover his happiness quickly, but — if at all — only over a long and complete stretch of time, in which he wins his way to great and noble things.

We shall meet every objection, then, if we say that the happy man is "one whose activity accords with complete virtue, and who is adequately furnished with external goods, not for some chance stretch of time but for a full term of years" — adding, perhaps, "and who will go on living so, and will die as he lived." For the future is hidden from us, and happiness we take to be an end, something in every way final and complete.

If this is so, then those among the living who have these things, and will keep them, we shall call blessed — but blessed as men are blessed.

Chapter 11

So much for that question. Turning now to the fortunes of a man's descendants, and of his friends generally: the doctrine that none of it touches the dead at all seems too cold, and too much at odds with what people believe. But the things that happen to descendants and friends are many and various — some come home more, some less — and to work through them one by one would be a long business, indeed an endless one. It will be enough to speak in general terms, in outline only.

Now, just as some of a man's own misfortunes have a certain weight and pull on his life while others sit more lightly, so it is with what happens to any of his friends. And it always makes far more difference whether those whom an event touches are alive or dead — far more than it matters, in a tragedy, whether the terrible crime is committed on the stage or is merely presupposed to have happened before the play begins. So we must take this difference into our reckoning — and still more, perhaps, the standing doubt whether the dead share in any good or evil at all. For it appears that even if something does get through to them, good or bad, it is something faint and slight, either in itself or to them; or if not that, then at least of a size and kind that cannot make happy those who are not, nor take happiness from those who are.

The conclusion, then, seems to be this: the good fortune of friends, and likewise their misfortune, does affect the dead in some measure — but not in such a way, or to such a degree, as to make the happy unhappy, or anything of that kind.

Chapter 12

These points settled, we may next ask whether happiness belongs among the goods we praise, or rather among those we revere; for it is plainly not a mere capacity, but a good actually possessed.

Whatever we praise, we seem to praise as being of a certain quality and as standing in a certain relation to something. We praise the just man and the courageous man, and the good man generally, and virtue itself, because of the actions and the works they produce; and we praise the strong man and the swift runner and the rest because each has a certain natural gift, standing in a certain relation to something good and admirable.

This becomes obvious when we consider the praises offered to the Gods: the Gods are made ridiculous by being measured against our standard — and that happens because, as we said, praise always involves reference to something else.

If praise, then, belongs to things of that sort, it is clear that for the best things what is due is not praise but something greater and better — as, indeed, our usage shows. The Gods we call blessed and happy, and "blessed" is what we call the most godlike men.

So too with good things: no one praises happiness the way he praises justice; we call it blessed, as something better and more divine than the goods we praise.

Eudoxus is thought to have argued well on these lines when he pleaded the case for pleasure's claim to first prize. The fact that pleasure, though a good, is not praised shows — he maintained — that it stands above the goods that are praised, as God and the good stand above them; for these are the standards by which we judge everything else. Praise we give to virtue, since virtue makes us apt to do what is noble; encomiums we compose for its works, whether of body or of soul.

But refining these distinctions belongs more properly to those who have made a study of encomiums. For us it is plain from what has been said: happiness belongs among the goods that we revere and count as final.

And this seems to follow, further, from the fact that happiness is a first principle or starting point. It is for its sake that we do everything else we do; and the principle and cause of all goods we hold to be something divine and worthy of reverence.

Chapter 13

Since happiness is an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue — or, if there is more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete of them — we should now examine virtue; for that will probably throw light on our inquiry about happiness.

And indeed the true statesman seems to be occupied with virtue above all; for he wants to make the citizens good and obedient to the laws. We have examples in the lawgivers of Crete and Sparta, and any others who have been like them. And if this inquiry belongs to Politics, then plainly pursuing it fits the plan we laid down at the start.

The virtue we are to examine is, of course, human virtue; for it was the good of man and the happiness of man that we set out to find. And by human virtue we mean virtue not of the body but of the soul; for happiness, on our account, is an activity of the soul.

If this is so, then evidently the statesman must know something about the soul, just as the man who is to heal the eye, or the whole body, must know something about them — all the more so as Politics is a higher and better science than medicine. And the better sort of physicians take great pains to know about the body.

As statesmen, then — as students of Politics — we must inquire into the soul; but we must inquire with our own purpose in view, and go only as far as that purpose requires. To push into finer detail would be more labor than our undertaking needs.

Now, certain doctrines about the soul are stated well enough in our published discussions, and we shall use them.

Two parts of the soul are distinguished: an irrational part and a rational part.

Whether these are separated the way the parts of the body are, or the parts of anything divisible — or whether they are two only in thought, and in fact inseparable, like the concave and the convex in the curve of a circle — makes no difference for our present purpose.

Of the irrational part, one division seems to be common to everything that lives, plants included: I mean the part that causes nutrition and growth. For we must credit everything that takes nourishment with a faculty of this kind — in the embryo as in the full-grown creature, since it is more reasonable to suppose the faculty is the same than that it changes.

The virtue of this faculty, then, plainly belongs to man in common with other living things; it is not specifically human.

This is confirmed by the fact that in sleep this part of the soul is thought to be most active, while the good man and the bad are indistinguishable when they are asleep — whence the saying that for half their lives the happy differ not at all from the miserable. And this is just what we should expect; for sleep is the soul's holiday from the very functions that make it called good or bad — except insofar as some of what goes on in the body filters through, which is why the dreams of good men are better than other people's.

But enough of this. We may dismiss the nutritive part, since by nature it has no share in human virtue.

There seems, however, to be another principle in the soul that is irrational and yet in some way partakes of reason. In the self-restrained man and the weak-willed man alike, we praise the reason — the rational part — for it exhorts them rightly and urges them toward what is best. But there is plainly something else in them besides reason, something that fights against reason and pulls the other way. Just as a paralyzed limb, when you will to move it to the right, swings instead to the left, so it is with the soul: the weak-willed man's impulses run counter to his reason. The difference is only that in the body we see the disobedient member, while in the soul we do not. But we must hold, I think, that in the soul too there is something besides reason, which opposes it and runs against it. (In what sense it is distinct from reason does not matter here.)

Yet this something partakes of reason too, as we said. In the self-restrained man, at least, it obeys reason; and in the self-controlled and courageous man it is more obedient still, for in him it is in complete harmony with reason.

The irrational part, then, turns out to be twofold. There is the vegetative faculty, which has no share of reason at all; and there is the faculty of appetite — of desire in general — which does share in reason after a fashion, in that it listens to reason and submits to its rule. It is "rational" in the sense in which we say a man listens to reason when he heeds his father or his friends — not in the sense in which we speak of reasoning out mathematical truths. And that the irrational part is somehow open to reason is attested by all our advising, all our rebuking and exhorting.

If we choose to say that this part, too, has reason, then the rational part will likewise have two divisions: one that has reason in the strict sense, in itself; and one that listens to reason as a man listens to his father.

Now, on this division of the soul's faculties the division of virtue is based. For we speak of intellectual virtues and moral virtues: wisdom, understanding, and practical wisdom we call intellectual; generosity and self-control we call moral. When we are describing a man's moral character we do not say that he is wise or intelligent, but that he is gentle or self-controlled. Yet we praise the wise man too for his habit of mind; and habits that deserve praise are what we call virtues.
