Book V

(Part 1 of 3)

Justice

Chapter 1

We come now to justice and injustice. We have to ask what sorts of acts they are concerned with, in what sense justice observes the mean, and what the extremes are between which the just lies. And in this inquiry we will follow the same method as before.

We see that when people speak of justice, they all mean the sort of habit or character that makes a man apt to do just things — that makes him act justly and wish for what is just. And by injustice they mean, in the same way, the character that makes a man act unjustly and wish for what is unjust. Let us set this down, then, as our outline to work from.

Notice that we can start this way with justice, because a habit — a trained state of character — differs in one respect from a science or a faculty. Both members of a pair of opposites fall under one and the same science or faculty: medicine deals with sickness as well as health. But a habit that produces one result does not also produce the opposite result. Health produces only healthy performances, never unhealthy ones; we say a man walks healthily when he walks the way a healthy man walks.

Not that the two opposites are unconnected. In the first place, a habit is often known from its opposite habit, and often from its causes and results. If we know what good condition is, we can learn from it what bad condition is; and from the things that produce good condition we can infer what good condition itself is, and the other way around. If good condition is firmness of flesh, for instance, then bad condition must be flabbiness of flesh, and whatever tends to produce firmness of flesh tends to produce good condition.

In the second place, if one of a pair of opposite terms has more than one sense, then as a general rule the other has more than one sense too. So if "just" has several senses, "unjust" will have several as well.

And in fact it seems that both "justice" and "injustice" do have several senses. But because the different things covered by the common name are so closely related, the difference escapes notice. It does not strike us the way it does when the things are far apart — different in outward appearance, say. Everyone sees at once, for instance, that the kleis that lies under an animal's neck — the collarbone — is a different thing from the kleis we fasten the door with — the key — though the Greek word is the same.

Let us ask, then, in how many different senses a man is called unjust.

First, the man who breaks the laws is considered unjust; and second, so is the man who takes more than his share — the unfair man.

Plainly, then, the just man will be (1) the law-abiding man and (2) the fair man.

And a just thing will be (1) what is lawful and (2) what is fair; an unjust thing will be (1) what is unlawful and (2) what is unfair.

Now since the unjust man, in the second sense of the word, takes more than his share, his sphere of action will be goods — not all goods, but the ones good and bad fortune deal in: things that are always good in themselves, but not always good for us. These are what we humans pray for and chase after. We ought to do otherwise — pray that what is good in itself may also be good for us, and choose what is actually good for us.

But the unjust man does not always take more than his share. Sometimes he takes less — less, that is, of things that are bad in themselves. Since the lesser evil counts in a way as a good, though, and since grasping for more means grasping for more good, he is still said to take more than his share. In any case he is unfair; that is the wider term, and it covers both.

We found that the lawbreaker is unjust and the law-abiding man just. It follows that whatever is lawful is just in one sense of the word. And this is in fact how we talk; for whatever the legislator prescribes is lawful, and we call every such prescription just.

Now, the laws pronounce on all manner of things, aiming at the common interest of all, or of the best men, or of those who hold power in the state (a position determined by personal excellence or by some other such standard). So in one sense we apply the term just to whatever tends to produce and preserve the happiness of the political community, and the parts of that happiness. The law commands us to do the acts of a brave man — not to desert our post, not to run, not to throw away our weapons; and the acts of a self-controlled man — not to commit adultery or outrage; and the acts of a gentle man — not to strike or insult our neighbors; and so on through all the other virtues and vices, enjoining some acts and forbidding others — rightly, when the law is a good one, less rightly when it is a hasty improvisation.

Justice in this sense, then, is complete virtue — with this addition, that it is shown toward others. That is why it is often called the chief of the virtues, greater than any other; why the poet says "neither evening star nor morning star is so wonderful"; and why the saying has become a proverb: "Justice sums up all virtues in itself."

It is complete virtue, first, because it is the exercise of complete virtue. And it is complete because the man who has it can use his virtue in dealing with his neighbors, and not merely in his own affairs. Plenty of people manage to be virtuous at home and fail the moment they deal with their neighbors.

This is why people approve the saying of Bias: "Office will show the man." The man who holds office stands, by that very fact, in relation to others and has dealings with them.

This is also why justice, alone among the virtues, is thought to be "another's good": it implies this relation to others. What justice aims at is someone else's interest — the interest of a ruler, or of our fellow citizens.

The worst man, then, is the one who exercises his vice both toward himself and toward his friends. And the best man is not the one who exercises his virtue in his own affairs only, but the one who exercises it toward others — for that is the hard thing to do.

Justice in this sense, then, is not a part of virtue but the whole of it; and the injustice opposed to it is not a part of vice but the whole of it.

How virtue differs from justice in this sense is plain from what we have said. They are one and the same character, differently viewed: seen in relation to others, that character is justice; seen simply as a certain kind of character, it is virtue.

Chapter 2

But what we are looking for is the justice that is a part of virtue — for we maintain there is such a thing — and likewise the corresponding injustice, the one that is a part of vice.

That the word is used this way is easy to show. In the case of the other kinds of badness, the man who displays them acts unjustly, in one sense of the word, but he does not take more than his share. When a man throws away his shield out of cowardice, or reviles someone out of bad temper, or refuses to help a man with money out of stinginess, he takes nothing extra. But when a man does take more than his share, he often displays none of those vices — certainly not all of them — and yet he does display some kind of badness, since we blame him for it: namely injustice, in the second sense of the word.

So there is another sense of "injustice," in which it names a part of that whole injustice which is coextensive with badness; and another sense of "unjust," in which it applies to only a part of the things called unjust in the broad sense of "contrary to law."

Again, suppose one man commits adultery for gain and makes money by it, while another does it out of appetite, spending money and losing by it. The second man would be called not grasping but self-indulgent; the first would be called not self-indulgent but unjust, in the narrow sense. Clearly, then, it is because of the gain that he is called unjust.

Once more: every other unjust act is referred back to some particular vice. If a man commits adultery, we refer the act to self-indulgence; if he deserts his comrade in arms, to cowardice; if he strikes someone, to anger. But when a man makes an unjust gain, the act is referred to no other vice than injustice.

It is plain, then, that besides the injustice which is coextensive with vice there is a second kind of injustice — a particular vice. It bears the same name as the first because the same general notion underlies both definitions: both show themselves in our dealings with others. But the second is limited to things like honor, wealth, and security (perhaps a single name could be found to cover the whole class), and its motive is the pleasure of gain; while the sphere of the first is everything the good man's action covers.

We have established, then, that there is more than one kind of justice — that besides the kind identical with complete virtue there is another kind — and we now have to find what it is and what it is like.

We have already distinguished two senses of "unjust" — (1) the unlawful and (2) the unfair — and two senses of "just" — (1) the lawful and (2) the fair.

The injustice we have already dealt with corresponds to the unlawful.

But the unfair is not the same as the unlawful; they differ as part from whole. Everything unfair is unlawful, but not everything unlawful is unfair. So the unjust and injustice that correspond to the unfair are not the same as those that correspond to the unlawful, but differ from them as a part from the whole: this injustice is a part of complete injustice, and this justice a part of complete justice. It is justice and injustice in this limited sense, then — and the just and the unjust in this limited sense — that we must now discuss.

So let us set aside the justice that coincides with complete virtue, which is the exercise of complete virtue toward others, and the corresponding injustice, the exercise of complete vice.

It is also easy to see how the just and the unjust corresponding to them are to be defined — the lawful and the unlawful. The great bulk of lawful acts, we may say, are the acts the law commands with a view to complete virtue; for the law orders us to live out all the virtues and forbids us to live out any of the vices.

And the acts that tend to produce complete virtue are those lawful acts prescribed with a view to educating a man as a citizen. As for the education of the individual as such — the education that makes him simply a good man — we may set aside for now the question whether it belongs to the science of the state or to some other; for possibly being a good man is not the same as being a good citizen of any state whatever.

Now, of justice as a part of virtue — particular justice — and of the just in the corresponding sense, one kind is shown in the distribution of honor, wealth, and the other things that get divided among the members of the political community; for there one man's share can be fair or unfair compared with another's. The other kind is corrective: it sets things right in private transactions.

This corrective kind is subdivided in turn, for private transactions are of two sorts: (1) voluntary and (2) involuntary.

Voluntary transactions, or contracts, are things like selling, buying, lending at interest, pledging, lending without interest, depositing, and hiring. They are called voluntary because the parties enter into them of their own free will.

Involuntary transactions are of two kinds. Some involve secrecy: theft, adultery, poisoning, procuring, luring away slaves, assassination, false witness. Others involve open violence: assault, imprisonment, murder, robbery, rape, maiming, slander, insult.

Chapter 3

The unjust man, in this limited sense of the word, is unfair, we say, and the unjust thing is unfair.

Now, plainly there is a mean lying between the unfair on one side and the unfair on the other. And this is the fair, or equal; for in any act that admits of a too much and a too little, there is also a fair amount.

If the unjust is the unfair, then, the just is the fair — which everyone in fact admits without further argument.

And since the fair or equal is a mean between two extremes, it follows that the just is a mean.

But equality — fairness — implies at least two terms.

It follows that the just is at once a mean quantity and a fair amount, relative to something and to certain persons. As a mean, it lies between certain other quantities, a more and a less; as an equal or fair amount, it involves two shares; and as a just amount, it involves certain persons.

The just, then, implies at least four terms: two persons for whom it is just, and two things.

And the same "equality" — the same ratio — must hold between the persons as between the things: as the things are to one another, so must the persons be. For if the persons are not equal, their shares will not be equal; and this is exactly where disputes and accusations come from — when equals receive unequal shares, or unequals receive equal shares.

The point is plain, too, from the common phrase "according to merit." Everyone agrees that just distribution must go by merit or worth of some kind; what they do not agree on is the standard of worth. In democratic states the standard is free birth; in oligarchic states, wealth, or in some, noble birth; in the true aristocratic state, virtue or personal merit.

The just, then, is something proportionate. For proportion is not a property of abstract numbers only but of anything that can be counted; proportion means equality of ratios, and it requires at least four terms.

That a discrete proportion requires four terms is obvious at once. But a continuous proportion requires four terms too, for it uses one term as two and repeats it. For instance: a is to b as b is to c. The term b is repeated; counting b twice over, we find the terms of the proportion are four in number.

The just, then, requires at least four terms, and the ratio between the two pairs must be the same: the persons must stand to one another in the same ratio as the things.

Let us say, then: as a is to b, so c is to d — or, rearranging the terms, as a is to c, so b is to d.

The sums of the new pairs will then stand to one another in the original ratio: a plus c is to b plus d as a is to b, or as c is to d.

But these pairs are exactly the pairs the distribution joins together; and if the things are assigned in this way, the assignment is just.

This joining of a to c and of b to d, then, is the just in distribution; and the just in this sense is a mean quantity, while the unjust is what breaks the proportion. For the proportionate is a mean, and the just, as we said, is proportionate.

Mathematicians call this kind of proportion a geometrical proportion; for it is when four terms are in geometrical proportion that the sum of the first and third stands to the sum of the second and fourth in the original ratio of the first to the second, or the third to the fourth.

But this proportion, as it applies in justice, cannot be a continuous one; for no single term can stand for both a person and a thing.

The just in this sense, then, is the proportionate, and the unjust is the disproportionate. In the unjust case one quantity becomes more, or too much, and the other less, or too little. And this is what we actually see in practice: the man who wrongs another gets too much of the good in question, and the man who is wronged gets too little. With evil it runs the other way; for the lesser evil, compared with the greater, counts as a good — the lesser evil is more desirable than the greater, what is desirable is good, and what is more desirable is a greater good.

This, then, is one form of the just.

Chapter 4

It remains to treat the other form: corrective justice, the just in the way of redress, whose sphere is private transactions, voluntary and involuntary alike.

This differs in kind from the form we have just discussed.

For the just in the distribution of a common stock of goods always follows the proportion described above — even when it is a common fund being divided, the amounts the several partners take must stand in the same ratio as the amounts they put in — and the unjust in the corresponding sense is what violates this proportion.

But the just in private transactions, though it too is a sort of equality (and the unjust a sort of inequality), follows not geometrical proportion but arithmetical.

For here it makes no difference whether a good man has defrauded a bad man or a bad man a good one, or whether the man who committed adultery is good or bad. The law looks only to the difference the injury makes; it treats the parties as equals, and asks only whether one has done a wrong and the other suffered it — whether one inflicted damage and the other received it.

The unjust here, then, is something unequal, and the judge tries to make it equal. For even when one man strikes and another is struck, or one kills and another is killed, the doing and the suffering are divided unequally; the judge tries to restore equality by the penalty or loss he lays on the offender, subtracting it from his gain.

For in such cases, though the terms do not always quite fit, we generally speak of the doer's "gain" — the striker's, say — and the sufferer's "loss"; at any rate, once the court has assessed the injury, what the doer gets is called his "loss" or penalty, and what the sufferer gets is called his "gain."

The equal or fair, then, is a mean between more, or too much, and less, or too little. But gain and loss are each a more and a less in opposite ways: gain is more good and less evil, and loss the opposite.

And in the mean between them lies, as we found, the equal or fair — which we say is just.

Corrective justice, then, the just in the way of redress, is the mean between loss and gain. That is why, when disputes arise, men go to the judge; and going to the judge is going to justice, for the judge is meant to be, as it were, living justice. Men look for the middle from a judge — they ask that he be moderate, that he observe the mean — and some even call judges "mediators" (mesidioi, middle-men), meaning that if they hit the mean, they will hit the just.

The just, then, must be a sort of mean, if the judge is a "mediator."

What the judge does is restore equality. It is as if he found a line divided into two unequal parts, and cut off from the greater part the amount by which it exceeds the half, and added that amount to the lesser.

When the whole has been divided evenly, the parties say they "have their own" — each has now received an equal, fair amount.

And the equal or fair amount here is the arithmetic mean between the more, or too much, and the less, or too little. That, by the way, is why it is called dikaion (just): because the division is in half — dicha — as if the word were dichaion; and the dikastes (judge) is a dichastes, a halver.

For if you cut a part off one of two equal lines and add it to the other, the second line now exceeds the first by two such parts. (Had you only cut the part off the first without adding it to the second, the second would exceed it by just one part.) The second exceeds the mean by one part, and the mean exceeds the shortened first line by one part.

This tells us how much to take from the man who has more, or too much, and how much to add to the man who has less, or too little: to the latter's portion we must add the amount by which it falls short of the mean, and from the former's portion we must take away the amount by which it exceeds the mean.

To draw the picture: let AA′, BB′, and CC′ be three equal lines. From AA′ cut off the segment AE; and to CC′ add the segment D, equal to AE. Then the whole line DCC′ exceeds EA′ by CD plus CZ (each equal to AE, or CD), and it exceeds BB′ by CD.

And this holds not only in geometry but in the arts as well; they could not exist unless the material being worked on received an impression matching, in kind and quantity and quality, the work the artist puts in.

These terms "loss" and "gain," by the way, are borrowed from voluntary exchange. In voluntary exchange, ending up with more than your own is called gaining, and ending up with less than you started with is called losing — in buying and selling, I mean, and in the other transactions where the law gives free play. And when the result for each party is neither more nor less but exactly the amount he started with, then they say they "have their own," and are neither losers nor gainers. The just, then, is a mean between a gain and a loss, neither of which was intended, and consists in having after the transaction the equivalent of what you had before it.
