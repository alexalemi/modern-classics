TEXT ANALYSIS — Nicomachean Ethics (Aristotle, c. 340 BC)
Source: F. H. Peters' translation (1893), Standard Ebooks edition;
SE endnotes (Peters' apparatus) stripped in prep. 10 books, 116
chapters in 25 files; Peters' thematic part titles ("The End", "Moral
Virtue", "The Will"…) kept as subheading lines.

WHAT THIS IS

Aristotle's lecture notes on how to live: happiness as the goal of
life, virtue as a trained habit aiming at the mean, the analysis of
choice and responsibility, the catalogue of virtues, justice,
practical wisdom, weakness of will, two books on friendship, and the
final ascent to the contemplative life. The single most influential
ethics book ever written, and a founders' staple.

STYLE AND TONE

- These are LECTURE NOTES, not dialogue: compressed, businesslike,
  signposted ("Let us begin…", "This point being settled…", "But this
  will be clearer if…"). The modern voice is a brilliant lecturer
  talking through the argument — plain, direct, moving briskly, using
  "we" as Aristotle does. Do NOT novelize; the clipped professional
  rhythm IS the style.
- Aristotle constantly flags method: what precision to expect, what
  the many say vs. the wise, working from endoxa. Keep the signposts;
  they are the reader's handrails.
- Examples are homely and concrete (the lyre-player, the doctor, the
  archer, wine and Milo the wrestler) — keep every one.
- Peters is accurate but Victorian ("noble and beautiful", "meanness",
  "liberality") — de-archaize per the glossary below; untangle his
  longest sentences but keep the argument's logical skeleton visible
  (therefore / but / for / since).

LOCKED GLOSSARY — the book's argument depends on rigid consistency

- eudaimonia → "happiness" (Peters' choice; keep it, with I.4's own
  discussion doing the defining work).
- arete → "virtue" ("excellence" allowed once in apposition at first
  use).
- hexis → "habit" — glossed once as "a settled state of character".
- mesotes → "the mean" (locked); the vices are "excess" and
  "deficiency".
- phronesis → "practical wisdom" (project-wide term); sophia →
  "wisdom"; theoria → "contemplation"; nous → "intuitive reason"
  (Peters' term, keep).
- sophrosyne → "self-control" (project-wide); its vice akolasia →
  "self-indulgence".
- enkrateia → "self-restraint"; akrasia → "weakness of will" (NEVER
  Peters' "continence/incontinence" — Book VII depends on this pair).
- to kalon → "the noble" (matches the Memorabilia's noble/shameful).
- energeia → "activity"; ergon → "function" (the function argument,
  I.7).
- eleutheriotes → "generosity" (never "liberality");
  megalopsychia → "greatness of soul" (its discussion defines it);
  mikropsychia → "smallness of soul".
- philia → "friendship" throughout Books VIII–IX (even where modern
  usage strains — Aristotle's point).
- The good for man, I.7, near-verbatim: "an activity of the soul in
  accordance with virtue" (and if virtues are many, "the best and
  most complete of them").

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Natural-slavery adjacent remarks, the status of women, and Greek
  class attitudes appear in passing — render plainly as his, per
  project policy, no softening, no amplification.
- Suicide (V.11), pederasty-adjacent friendship remarks — measured
  historical register.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- I.1 opening: "…the good is that at which everything aims."
- I.7: "One swallow does not make a spring" (keep the whole sentence's
  shape: nor does one fine day; nor one day of happiness).
- II.1: "by doing just acts we become just" (the habituation thesis).
- IV.5: "angry with the right persons, at the right things, in the
  right manner" (the compressed mean-of-anger formula).
- IX.4/IX.9: the friend as "another self".

FORMAT

25 files across 10 books; heading "Book N" exactly; "(Part n of k)"
markers; Peters' part titles as standalone subheading lines where they
fall; "Chapter N" standalone headings (per-book numbering). Word ratio
target 0.85–1.15 (English→modern English). Parts 2+ never re-introduce
a book; non-final parts never conclude it.
