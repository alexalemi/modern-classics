The inhabitants of Flatland are typically around eleven of your inches in length or width, with a maximum of twelve inches. 

Our women are straight lines.

Our soldiers and lowest-class workers are isosceles triangles, with two equal sides around eleven inches long and a very short base, often not exceeding half an inch. This gives them a sharp and formidable angle at their vertices. In fact, when their bases are extremely small (less than an eighth of an inch), they can hardly be distinguished from straight lines or women, their vertices are so pointed. We call these triangles "isosceles," and I will refer to them as such in the following pages.

Our middle class consists of equilateral or equal-sided triangles.

Our professionals and gentlemen are squares (which is the class I belong to) and pentagons.

Above these come the nobility, with several ranks, starting at hexagons and increasing the number of sides until they receive the honorable title of "polygonal" or many-sided. Finally, when the number of sides becomes so numerous and the sides themselves so small that the figure can no longer be distinguished from a circle, they are included in the "circular" or priestly order, which is the highest class of all.

It is a law of nature for us that a male child will have one more side than his father, so that each generation rises (as a rule) one step in the scale of development and nobility. Thus, the son of a square is a pentagon, the son of a pentagon is a hexagon, and so on.

However, this rule does not always apply to the tradespeople, and even less often to the soldiers and workers, who can hardly be considered true human figures, as they do not have all their sides equal. For them, the law of nature does not hold, and the son of an isosceles (that is, a triangle with two sides equal) remains isosceles.

Nevertheless, all hope is not lost, even for the isosceles, that their descendants may ultimately rise above their degraded condition. For after a long series of military successes or diligent and skilled labors, it is generally found that the more intelligent among the artisan and soldier classes show a slight increase in their third side or base, and a shrinkage of the two other sides. Marriages arranged by the priests between the sons and daughters of these more intellectual members of the lower classes often result in offspring that approximate even more closely to the type of the equilateral triangle.

Rarely - in proportion to the vast numbers of isosceles births - is a genuine and certified equilateral triangle produced from isosceles parents. Such a birth requires, as its antecedents, not only a series of carefully arranged intermarriages, but also a long-continued exercise of frugality and self-control on the part of the would-be ancestors of the coming equilateral, and a patient, systematic, and continuous development of the isosceles intellect through many generations.
