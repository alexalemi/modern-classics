How I Tried to Teach the Concept of Three Dimensions to My Grandson, and with What Success

I woke up feeling elated and began to reflect on the glorious path ahead of me. I thought, "I will go out immediately and spread the gospel of Three Dimensions throughout Flatland. Even to Women and Soldiers, the revelation of the Third Dimension shall be proclaimed." I decided to start with my wife.

Just as I had settled on my plan of action, I heard many voices in the street commanding silence. Then a louder voice followed – it was a herald's proclamation. Listening carefully, I recognized the words of the Council's Resolution, ordering the arrest, imprisonment, or execution of anyone who would corrupt people's minds with delusions and claims of revelations from another world.

I reflected. This danger was not to be taken lightly. It would be better to avoid it by omitting any mention of my revelation and proceeding instead with a demonstration – which, after all, seemed so simple and convincing that nothing would be lost by discarding the former approach. "Upward, not Northward" – that was the key to the entire proof. It had seemed fairly clear to me before I fell asleep, and when I first woke up, fresh from my dream, it had appeared as obvious as arithmetic; but somehow, it did not seem quite so obvious to me now. Though my wife entered the room at that very moment, I decided, after we had exchanged a few ordinary remarks, not to begin with her.

My Pentagonal sons were men of character and standing, respected physicians, but not particularly gifted in mathematics, and therefore unfit for my purpose. But it occurred to me that a young and docile Hexagon, with a talent for mathematics, would make the most suitable pupil. Why not, then, make my first experiment with my little precocious grandson, whose casual comments on the meaning of 33 had met with the Sphere's approval? Discussing the matter with him, a mere boy, I would be in perfect safety; for he would know nothing of the Council's Proclamation, whereas I could not be certain that my sons – so greatly did their patriotism and reverence for the Circles outweigh mere affection – might not feel compelled to hand me over to the Prefect if they found me seriously advocating the seditious heresy of the Third Dimension.

But the first thing to be done was to satisfy my wife's curiosity in some way. She naturally wanted to know the reasons for which the Circle had desired that mysterious interview and the means by which he had entered the house. Without going into the details of the elaborate account I gave her – an account, I fear, not entirely consistent with the truth, as my readers in Spaceland might desire – I must be content with saying that I succeeded at last in persuading her to return quietly to her household duties without eliciting any reference from me to the World of Three Dimensions. This done, I immediately sent for my grandson; for, to confess the truth, I felt that all I had seen and heard was slipping away from me, like the image of a half-grasped, tantalizing dream, and I longed to test my skill in making a first disciple.

When my grandson entered the room, I carefully secured the door. Then, sitting down by his side and taking our mathematical tablets – or, as you would call them, Lines – I told him we would resume the lesson from yesterday. I taught him once more how a Point by motion in One Dimension produces a Line, and how a straight Line in Two Dimensions produces a Square. After this, forcing a laugh, I said, "And now, you rascal, you wanted to make believe that a Square may in the same way by motion 'Upward, not Northward' produce another figure, a sort of extra square in Three Dimensions. Say that again, you young scoundrel."

At that moment, we heard the herald's "Hear ye! Hear ye!" once more outside in the street, proclaiming the Council's Resolution. Young as he was, my grandson – who was unusually intelligent for his age and raised with the utmost reverence for the authority of the Circles – grasped the situation with an acuity for which I was quite unprepared. He remained silent until the last words of the Proclamation had died away, and then, bursting into tears, said, "Dear Grandpapa, that was only my fun, and of course I meant nothing at all by it; and we didn't know anything then about the new Law; and I don't think I said anything about the Third Dimension; and I'm sure I didn't say one word about 'Upward, not Northward,' for that would be such nonsense, you know. How could a thing move Upward, and not Northward? Upward and not Northward! Even if I were a baby, I couldn't be so absurd as that. How silly it is! Ha! ha! ha!"

"Not at all silly," said I, losing my temper. "Here, for example, I take this Square" – and, as I spoke, I grasped a movable Square that was lying nearby – "and I move it, you see, not Northward but – yes, I move it Upward – that is to say, Northward, but I move it somewhere – not exactly like this, but somehow –" Here I brought my sentence to a meaningless conclusion, shaking the Square about in a purposeless manner, much to the amusement of my grandson, who burst out laughing louder than ever and declared that I was not teaching him, but joking with him. And so saying, he unlocked the door and ran out of the room. Thus ended my first attempt to convert a pupil to the Gospel of Three Dimensions.
