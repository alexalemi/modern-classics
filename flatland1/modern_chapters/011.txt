§ 11 About Our Priests

It's high time I move on from these brief and meandering observations about things in Flatland to the central event of this book - my initiation into the mysteries of Space. That is my true subject; everything that has come before is merely an introduction.

For this reason, I must omit many matters that I believe would be of interest to my readers: for example, our method of propelling and stopping ourselves, even though we lack feet; the means by which we give stability to structures of wood, stone, or brick, although of course we have no hands, nor can we lay foundations as you can, nor take advantage of the lateral pressure of the earth; the way in which the rain originates in the gaps between our various zones, so that the northern regions do not intercept the moisture falling on the southern; the nature of our hills and mines, our trees and plants, our seasons and harvests; our alphabet and writing method, adapted to our linear tablets. I must pass over these and a hundred other details of our physical existence, not because I have forgotten them, but out of respect for the time of the Reader.

Yet before I proceed to my main subject, my readers will no doubt expect some final remarks on those pillars and mainstays of the Constitution of Flatland, the controllers of our conduct and shapers of our destiny, the objects of universal reverence and almost worship: I need hardly say that I mean our Circles or Priests.

When I call them Priests, let me not be understood as meaning no more than the term denotes with you. With us, our Priests are the Administrators of all Business, Art, and Science; Directors of Trade, Commerce, Generalship, Architecture, Engineering, Education, Statesmanship, Legislation, Morality, and Theology; they do nothing themselves, yet they are the Causes of everything worth doing, that is done by others.

Although everyone popularly called a Circle is deemed a Circle, yet among the better-educated classes it is known that no Circle is truly a Circle, but only a Polygon with a very large number of very small sides. As the number of the sides increases, a Polygon approaches a Circle; and, when the number is very great indeed, say for example three or four hundred, it becomes extremely difficult for even the most delicate touch to detect any polygonal angles. I should say it would be difficult - for, as I have shown above, Recognition by Feeling is unknown among the highest society, and to feel a Circle would be considered a most audacious insult. This habit of abstaining from Feeling in the best society allows a Circle to more easily sustain the veil of mystery with which, from his earliest years, he is wont to shroud the exact nature of his Perimeter or Circumference. With an average Perimeter of three feet, it follows that in a Polygon of three hundred sides, each side will be no more than one-hundredth of a foot in length, or little more than one-tenth of an inch; and in a Polygon of six or seven hundred sides, the sides are little larger than the diameter of a Spaceland pin-head. It is always assumed, out of courtesy, that the Chief Circle for the time being has ten thousand sides.

The advancement of the Circles' descendants in the social scale is not restricted, as it is among the lower Regular classes, by the Law of Nature which limits the increase of sides to one in each generation. If it were so, the number of sides in the Circle would be a mere matter of pedigree and arithmetic, and the four hundred and ninety-seventh descendant of an Equilateral Triangle would necessarily be a polygon with five hundred sides. But this is not the case. Nature's Law prescribes two opposing decrees affecting Circular propagation; first, that as the race climbs higher in the scale of development, so development shall proceed at an accelerated pace; second, that in the same proportion, the race shall become less fertile. Consequently, in the home of a Polygon of four or five hundred sides, it is rare to find more than one son; more than one is never seen. On the other hand, the son of a five-hundred-sided Polygon has been known to possess five hundred and fifty, or even six hundred sides.

Art also intervenes to assist the process of higher Evolution. Our physicians have discovered that the small and tender sides of an infant Polygon of the higher class can be fractured, and its entire frame re-set, with such precision that a Polygon of two or three hundred sides sometimes - by no means always, for the process is attended with serious risk - but sometimes, leaps over two or three hundred generations, and as it were doubles at a stroke, the number of its ancestors and the nobility of its descent.

Many a promising child is sacrificed in this way. Scarcely one out of ten survives. Yet so strong is the parental ambition among those Polygons who are, as it were, on the fringe of the Circular class, that it is very rare to find a Nobleman of that position in society who has neglected to place his firstborn in the Circular Neo-Therapeutic Gymnasium before the child has reached the age of a month.

One year determines success or failure. At the end of that time, the child has, in all probability, been added to the tombstones that crowd the Neo-Therapeutic Cemetery; but on rare occasions, a joyful procession bears the little one back to his exultant parents, no longer a Polygon, but a Circle, at least by courtesy: and a single instance of so blessed a result induces multitudes of Polygonal parents to submit to similar domestic sacrifices, which have a dissimilar outcome.
