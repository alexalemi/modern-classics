The Matter of Irregular Figures

Throughout the previous pages, I have been assuming - what perhaps should have been stated clearly from the beginning as a fundamental principle - that every person in Flatland is a Regular Figure, meaning they have a regular, symmetrical shape. By this, I mean that a Woman must not only be a line, but a straight line; that a Craftsman or Soldier must have two equal sides; that Tradespeople must have three equal sides; Lawyers (of which class I am a humble member) must have four equal sides, and generally, that in every Polygon, all the sides must be equal.

The lengths of the sides would, of course, depend on the individual's age. A newborn Female would be about an inch long, while a tall adult Woman might reach a foot in length. As for the Males of every class, it can be roughly said that the combined length of an adult's sides is around two feet or a little more. But the actual size of our sides is not the issue here. I am speaking of the equality of the sides, and a moment's reflection will show that the entire social structure of Flatland rests on the fundamental fact that Nature wills all Figures to have equal sides.

If our sides were unequal, our angles might also be unequal. Instead of simply feeling or visually estimating a single angle to determine an individual's form, it would be necessary to carefully measure each angle. But life would be too short for such tedious assessment. The entire science and art of Visual Recognition would perish; Touch, as an art, would not long survive; interaction would become perilous or impossible; there would be an end to all trust and foresight; no one would feel safe making even the simplest social arrangements; in a word, civilization might relapse into barbarism.

Am I moving too quickly for my Readers to follow me to these obvious conclusions? Surely a moment's thought, and a single example from everyday life, must convince everyone that our social system is based on Regularity, or Equality of Angles. You might meet two or three Tradespeople on the street, whom you immediately recognize as such by a glance at their angles and slightly dimmed sides, and invite them to your home for lunch. You do this now with complete confidence, because everyone knows the approximate area occupied by an adult Triangle. But imagine if your Tradesman dragged behind his regular, respectable vertex a parallelogram of twelve or thirteen inches in diagonal - what would you do with such a misshapen creature stuck fast in your doorway?

But I am insulting the intelligence of my Readers by dwelling on details that must be obvious to anyone who enjoys the advantages of living in Spaceland. Clearly, measuring a single angle would no longer be sufficient in such extraordinary circumstances; one's entire life would be consumed by feeling or surveying the perimeter of one's acquaintances. Already, the difficulties of avoiding collisions in a crowd tax the sagacity of even a well-educated Square; but if no one could calculate the Regularity of a single figure in the company, all would be chaos and confusion, and the slightest panic would cause serious injuries, or - if Women or Soldiers happened to be present - perhaps even loss of life.

Therefore, both Practicality and Nature have stamped their approval on Regularity of form. And the Law has not been slow in reinforcing their efforts. "Irregularity of Figure" carries with us the same meaning as, or even more than, a combination of moral depravity and criminality where you live, and is treated accordingly. It is true that there are some who propound paradoxes, claiming that there is no necessary connection between geometrical and moral Irregularity. "The Irregular," they say, "is from birth shunned by their own parents, mocked by siblings, neglected by servants, scorned and suspected by society, and excluded from all positions of responsibility, trust, and useful employment. Their every movement is closely watched by the police until they come of age and present themselves for inspection; then they are either destroyed if found to exceed the fixed margin of deviation, or assigned to a tedious, unrewarding occupation for a meager wage; compelled to live and board at the office, and to take even their vacation under close supervision. What wonder that human nature, even in the best and purest, is embittered and corrupted by such surroundings!"

This very plausible reasoning does not convince me, any more than it has convinced the wisest of our Statesmen, that our ancestors erred in declaring it an axiom of policy that the tolerance of Irregularity is incompatible with the safety of the State. Doubtless, the life of an Irregular is hard, but the interests of the Greater Good require that it be so. If a man with a triangular front and a polygonal back were permitted to exist and propagate an even more Irregular posterity, what would become of the arts of civilization? Are the houses, doors, and churches in Flatland to be altered to accommodate such monstrosities? Are our ticket-collectors to be required to measure every person's perimeter before allowing them to enter a theatre or take their seat in a lecture hall? Is an Irregular to be exempted from military service? And if not, how can we prevent them from bringing ruin into the ranks of their comrades? Furthermore, what irresistible temptations to fraudulent deceptions must such a creature face! How easy for them to enter a shop with their polygonal front first, and order goods to any extent from a trusting Tradesperson! Let the advocates of a falsely called Philanthropy plead as they may for the abolition of the Irregular Penal Laws, I have never known an Irregular who was not also what Nature evidently intended them to be - a hypocrite, a misanthrope, and, to the limits of their power, a perpetrator of all manner of mischief.

Not that I would recommend (at present) the extreme measures adopted by some States, where an infant whose angle deviates by even half a degree from the correct angularity is summarily destroyed at birth. Some of our highest and most capable men, true geniuses, have in their earliest days laboured under deviations as great as, or even greater than, forty-five minutes; and the loss of their precious lives would have been an irreparable injury to the State. The art of healing has also achieved some of its most remarkable triumphs in the compressions, extensions, trepannings, bindings, and other surgical or dietary treatments by which Irregularity has been partially or wholly corrected. Therefore, advocating a Middle Way, I would not set a fixed or absolute line of demarcation; but at the time when the frame is just beginning to stabilize, and the Medical Board has reported that recovery is unlikely, I would suggest that the Irregular offspring be painlessly and mercifully terminated.
