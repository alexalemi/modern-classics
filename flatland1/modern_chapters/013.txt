It was the last day before the end of the 1999th year of our era, and the first day of the long summer break. After enjoying my favorite pastime of geometry until late in the evening, I had retired to bed with an unsolved problem weighing on my mind. That night, I had a dream.

I saw before me a vast multitude of thin, straight lines (which I assumed to be women) interspersed with even smaller, shimmering points—all moving back and forth along a single straight line, and at roughly the same speed.

A cacophony of confused chirping and twittering issued from them at intervals as they moved; but sometimes they would stop, and then all was silent.

Approaching one of the larger "women," I addressed her, but received no response. I tried again, and then a third time, but my attempts were equally ineffective. Losing patience at what seemed like unacceptable rudeness, I brought my mouth directly in front of hers to block her path, and loudly asked, "Madam, what is the meaning of this gathering, this strange and jumbled chirping, and this monotonous back-and-forth motion along a single straight line?"

"I am no woman," replied the thin line. "I am the monarch of this world. But you, how dare you intrude into my realm of Lineland?" 

Taken aback by this abrupt reply, I begged pardon if I had in any way startled or bothered his royal highness. Describing myself as a stranger, I implored the king to tell me about his domains. However, I had the utmost difficulty in obtaining any meaningful information, for the monarch constantly assumed that whatever was familiar to him must also be known to me, and he believed I was feigning ignorance as a jest. Nevertheless, by persisting with my questions, I was able to elicit the following facts:

It seemed this poor, ignorant monarch—as he called himself—was convinced that the straight line he called his kingdom, and in which he existed, constituted the entire world and, indeed, all of space. Being unable to move or see beyond his single line, he had no concept of anything outside of it. Although he had heard my voice when I first addressed him, the sounds had reached him in a manner so foreign to his experience that he had not responded, "seeing no one," as he put it, "and hearing a voice as if from my own insides." Until the moment I placed my mouth in his world, he had neither seen me nor heard anything except confused noises beating against what he called his "inside" or "stomach"; nor did he even now have the slightest notion of the place from which I had come. Outside his line, or world, all was a blank to him; no, not even a blank, for a blank implies space—rather, all was non-existent.

His subjects—the thin lines being men and the points being women—were all similarly confined in motion and sight to that single straight line, which was their universe. It need hardly be added that their entire horizon was limited to a single point; nor could anyone ever see anything but a point. Man, woman, child, object—each appeared as a mere point to the eye of a Linelander. Only the sound of the voice could distinguish sex or age. Moreover, as each individual occupied the entirety of the narrow path that constituted their universe, and no one could move to the side to make way for others, it followed that no Linelander could ever pass another. Once neighbors, always neighbors. Neighborhood for them was akin to marriage for us—neighbors remained neighbors until death parted them.

Such a life, with all vision limited to a point and all motion confined to a straight line, seemed to me inexpressibly dreary; and I was surprised to note the vivacity and cheerfulness of the king. Wondering whether it was possible, amid such unfavorable circumstances for domestic relations, to enjoy the pleasures of conjugal union, I hesitated for some time to question his royal highness on this delicate subject; but eventually I broached it by abruptly inquiring about the health of his family. "My wives and children," he replied, "are well and happy."

Staggered by this answer—for in the immediate proximity of the monarch (as I had observed in my dream before entering Lineland) there were only men—I ventured to respond, "Pardon me, but I cannot imagine how your royal highness can at any time either see or approach their majesties, when there are at least half a dozen individuals between you? Is it possible that in Lineland, proximity is not necessary for marriage and the procreation of children?"

"How can you ask such an absurd question?" replied the monarch. "If it were indeed as you suggest, the universe would soon be depopulated. No, no; proximity is unnecessary for the union of hearts, and the birth of children is too important a matter to have been left to chance. You cannot be ignorant of this. Yet since you profess ignorance, I will instruct you as if you were the veriest babe in Lineland. Know, then, that marriages are consummated through the faculty of sound and the sense of hearing.

"You are, of course, aware that every man has two mouths or voices—as well as two eyes—a bass at one end and a tenor at the other. I should not mention this, but I have been unable to distinguish your tenor during our conversation." I replied that I had but a single voice, and had not known that his royal highness possessed two. "That confirms my suspicion," said the king, "that you are not a man, but a feminine monstrosity with a bass voice and an utterly uneducated ear. But to continue.

"Nature has ordained that every man shall wed two wives—"
"Why two?" I interrupted.
"You carry your affected simplicity too far," he cried. "How can there be a completely harmonious union without the combination of the four in one: the bass and tenor of the man, and the soprano and contralto of the two women?"
"But supposing," said I, "that a man should prefer one wife or three?"
"It is impossible," he said. "It is as inconceivable as that two and one should make five, or that the human eye should see a straight line." I would have interrupted him, but he proceeded as follows:

"Once in the middle of each week, a law of nature compels us to move to and fro with a rhythmic motion of more than usual violence, which continues for the time it would take to count to one hundred and one. In the midst of this choral dance, at the fifty-first pulsation, the inhabitants of the universe pause in full career, and each individual sends forth their richest, fullest, sweetest strain. It is in this decisive moment that all our marriages are made. So exquisite is the adaptation of bass to treble, of tenor to contralto, that oftentimes the betrothed, though twenty thousand leagues apart, recognize at once the responsive note of their destined lover; and, penetrating the paltry obstacles of distance, love unites the three. The marriage thus consummated results in a threefold male and female offspring, which takes its place in Lineland."

"What! Always threefold?" said I. "Must one wife then always have twins?"

"You bass-voiced monstrosity, yes," replied the king. "How else could the balance of the sexes be maintained, if two girls were not born for every boy? Would you ignore the very alphabet of nature?" He ceased, speechless with fury; and some time elapsed before I could induce him to resume his narrative.
