As with you, so also with us, there are four directions: north, south, east, and west.

Since there is no sun or other celestial bodies, it is impossible for us to determine north in the usual way. But we have a method of our own. By a law of nature, there is a constant pull towards the south. Although in temperate climates this is very slight—so that even a reasonably healthy woman can travel several miles northward without much difficulty—the restraining effect of the southward pull is sufficient to serve as a compass in most parts of our land. Moreover, the rain (which falls at set intervals) always coming from the north is an additional aid. And in the towns, we have the guidance of the houses, which of course have their side walls running mostly north and south, so that the roofs may shelter us from the rain in the north. In the countryside, where there are no houses, the trunks of the trees provide some sort of guide. Altogether, we do not have as much trouble as one might expect in determining our bearings.

Yet in our more temperate regions, where the southward pull is hardly felt, I have sometimes been compelled to remain stationary for hours, waiting for the rain to come before continuing my journey, when walking in a perfectly barren plain with no houses or trees to guide me. This pull affects the weak, the elderly, and especially delicate females much more heavily than the robust males. So it is considered polite, when meeting a lady on the street, to always give her the northbound side—no easy task when one is in vigorous health and cannot easily tell north from south.

There are no windows in our homes, for the light reaches us equally within our dwellings and outside them, by day and night, at all times and in all places, from sources unknown. In the old days, it was an intriguing and oft-investigated question among our scholars, "What is the origin of light?" Repeated attempts to solve this mystery have only served to fill our asylums with would-be solvers. Hence, after futile efforts to suppress such investigations indirectly by heavily taxing them, the government eventually prohibited them altogether. I—alas, I alone in Flatland—now know the true solution to this puzzling problem, but my knowledge cannot be made intelligible to a single one of my countrymen. I am mocked as the maddest of the mad, I who possess the truths of space and the theory of light's introduction from the three-dimensional world.

The most common form for a house is pentagonal, with the two northern sides forming the roof, usually without doors. There is a small door on the east for the women, and a much larger one on the west for the men; the south side or floor is typically doorless.

Square and triangular houses are not allowed, for this reason: the angles of a square (and even more so, an equilateral triangle) being much sharper than those of a pentagon, and the lines of inanimate objects being dimmer than the outlines of people, there is considerable danger that the points of a square or triangular house might seriously injure an inattentive or absent-minded traveler who runs into them. Therefore, as early as the eleventh century, triangular houses were universally forbidden by law, with the only exceptions being fortifications, powder magazines, barracks, and other state buildings, which the general public should not approach without caution.

At this time, square houses were still permitted, though discouraged by a special tax. But about three centuries later, the law decided that in all towns with a population over ten thousand, the smallest house angle allowed, consistent with public safety, would be that of a pentagon. The good sense of the community has supported the efforts of the legislature, and now, even in the countryside, the pentagonal construction has replaced every other. It is only occasionally, in some very remote and backward farming district, that an antiquarian may still discover a square house.
