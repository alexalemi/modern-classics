§ 5 How We Recognize One Another

You, who are blessed with the ability to see in three dimensions and perceive depth and color, you who can actually see angles and the complete circumference of a circle - how can I make you understand the extreme difficulty we in Flatland face in recognizing one another's shape?

As I told you before, all beings in Flatland, whether animate or inanimate, appear to our eyes as a straight line. So how can one be distinguished from another when they all look the same?

There are three main ways we can recognize each other. First is the sense of hearing, which is much more developed in us than in you. This allows us not only to identify the voices of our friends, but also to distinguish between the different social classes - at least the three lowest ranks of Equilaterals, Squares, and Pentagons. (I do not consider the Isosceles in this.) But as we move up the social ladder, voice recognition becomes more difficult, partly because our voices become more similar, and partly because the ability to discern voices is seen as a plebeian skill, not valued by the aristocracy. And wherever there is a risk of deception, we cannot rely on this method alone. Among our lower classes, vocal development is so advanced that an Isosceles can easily imitate the voice of a Polygon, or even that of a Circle.

Therefore, a second method is more commonly used: the sense of touch. For our women and lower classes - I will speak of the upper classes shortly - feeling is the primary way to recognize someone, especially when meeting a stranger and needing to identify their social rank. What "introduction" is to the higher classes in your Spaceland, "feeling" is to us in Flatland. The traditional formula is, "Allow me to introduce you to my friend Mr. So-and-so, and let you feel and be felt by him." In towns and among businessmen, the words "be felt by" are often omitted, and it is simply, "Let me introduce you to Mr. So-and-so, so you may feel him." And among our more modern, fashionable gentlemen, the phrase is further shortened to "Mr. Smith, permit me to feel Mr. Jones" - a rather crude abbreviation, but one that is commonly accepted in polite society.

However, do not think that "feeling" is as tedious a process for us as it would be for you. Through long practice and training, beginning in our schools and continued in daily life, we can quickly discern the angles of an equilateral triangle, square, or pentagon by touch alone. And the blunt vertex of an acute isosceles triangle is obvious even to the dullest sense of touch. As a rule, we only need to feel a single angle to know the class to which a person belongs, unless they are from the higher nobility, where the difficulty is much greater. Even a highly educated Master of Arts at our University of Wentbridge has been known to mistake a ten-sided for a twelve-sided polygon, and there is hardly a Doctor of Science who could promptly and confidently distinguish between a twenty-sided and a twenty-four-sided member of the aristocracy.

Those readers who recall the laws I mentioned earlier regarding our treatment of women will understand that the process of introduction by touch requires great care and discretion. The angles could easily inflict serious harm on the unwary "feeler." It is essential for the safety of the one doing the feeling that the one being felt remains perfectly still. Any sudden movement, fidgeting, or even a violent sneeze has been known to prove fatal to the careless, ending many a promising friendship - especially among the lower classes of triangles, whose eyes are situated so far from their vertex that they can scarcely perceive what is happening at that end of their body. Moreover, these lower classes are of a coarse, rough nature, not sensitive to the delicate touch of the highly organized polygons. Is it any wonder, then, that an involuntary toss of the head has before now deprived the state of a valuable life?

I have heard that my excellent grandfather - one of the least irregular of his unfortunate isosceles class, who even obtained four out of seven votes from the Sanitary and Social Board to be reclassified as an equilateral shortly before his death - often lamented with a tear in his venerable eye a tragic mishap that occurred to his great-great-great-grandfather, a respectable working man with a brain angle of 59°30'. According to the story, my ill-fated ancestor, afflicted with rheumatism, was being felt by a polygon when he suddenly started, accidentally impaling the great man through the diagonal, partly due to the long imprisonment and degradation my ancestor had endured, and partly because of the moral shock that rippled through his entire family. This set the family's progress back a degree and a half, and it took five generations to recover the lost ground and finally achieve the full 60° angle required for the ascent from the isosceles class.
