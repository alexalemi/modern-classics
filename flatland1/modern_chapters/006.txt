§ 6 Recognizing Others by Sight

I'm about to seem quite inconsistent. In the previous sections, I said that all figures in Flatland appear as straight lines, and that it's impossible to distinguish individuals of different classes by sight. But now I'll explain to my Spaceland critics how we can recognize one another visually.

However, if the reader refers back to the passage about universal recognition by touch, they'll find this qualification: "among the lower classes." It's only among the upper classes and in our more temperate regions that visual recognition is practiced.

This ability exists in some areas and for some classes due to the fog that prevails most of the year, except in tropical zones. What is an unmixed evil for you in Spaceland, blotting out the landscape, depressing spirits, and weakening health, is recognized by us as a blessing almost as valuable as air itself - the nurse of the arts and parent of the sciences. But let me explain the meaning without further praise of this beneficial element.

If fog didn't exist, all lines would appear equally and indistinguishably clear. This is the case in those unfortunate countries with perfectly dry and transparent atmospheres. But wherever there is abundant fog, objects just a few feet away appear dimmer than those only slightly closer. By carefully and constantly observing these differences in brightness and clarity, we can infer the configuration of the object with great accuracy.

An example will make this clearer.

Suppose I see two individuals approaching whose rank I want to determine. Let's say they are a Merchant (an equilateral triangle) and a Physician (a pentagon). How can I tell them apart?

It's obvious to any child in Spaceland who has started geometry that if I position my eye so its gaze bisects an angle (A) of the approaching stranger, my view will be evenly between the two sides nearest to me (CA and AB). I'll see both sides impartially, and they'll appear the same size.

Now for the Merchant (1), what will I see? I'll see a straight line DAE, with the middle point (A) very bright because it's closest to me. But on either side, the line will rapidly fade into dimness, as the sides AC and AB recede quickly into the fog. The Merchant's extremities, D and E, will appear very dim indeed.

On the other hand, for the Physician (2), I'll also see a line (D'A'E') with a bright center (A'), but it will fade to dimness less rapidly, because the sides (A'C' and A'B') recede less quickly into the fog. The Physician's extremities, D' and E', won't be as dim as the Merchant's.

The reader will probably understand from these two examples how - after extensive training and constant practice - the educated classes among us can fairly accurately distinguish the middle and lower orders by sight. If my Spaceland patrons can conceive of this possibility and not entirely dismiss my account, I will have achieved all I can reasonably expect. Going into further details would only confuse matters.

However, for the sake of the young and inexperienced, who may wrongly infer from the simple examples given that visual recognition is easy, I must point out that in real life, most sight recognition problems are much more subtle and complex. For instance, if my father the Triangle presents his side to me instead of his angle, I may momentarily doubt whether he is a Straight Line, or in other words, a Woman. And when contemplating one of my hexagonal grandsons' sides (AB) full-on, I'll see one bright full line (AB) and two dimmer, fading lines (CA and BD).

But I must resist the temptation to expand on these topics. The least skilled mathematician in Spaceland will readily believe me when I assert that the problems of life faced by the well-educated - when they are themselves in motion, rotating, advancing or retreating, while trying to visually distinguish numerous high-ranking Polygons moving in different directions, as in a ballroom or social gathering - must be of a nature to tax the most intellectual's powers of angularity, and amply justify the rich endowments of the esteemed Geometry professors, both in statics and kinematics, at the illustrious University of Wentbridge, where the science and art of visual recognition are regularly taught to the elite.
