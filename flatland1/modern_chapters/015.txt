Concerning a Stranger from Spaceland

From dreams, I move to facts.

It was the last day of the 1999th year of our era. The patterns of the rain had long ago signaled the arrival of nightfall, and I was sitting with my wife, reflecting on the events of the past and the possibilities of the coming year, the coming century, the coming millennium.

My four sons and two orphaned grandchildren had retired to their rooms, and my wife was the only one remaining with me to see the old millennium end and the new one begin.

I was deep in thought, pondering the words that had casually come from the mouth of my youngest grandson, a very promising young hexagon of unusual brilliance and perfect angularity. His uncles and I had been giving him his usual practical lesson in shape recognition, rotating ourselves rapidly and then more slowly, and questioning him about our positions. His answers had been so satisfactory that I had decided to reward him by giving him a few insights into arithmetic as applied to geometry.

Taking nine squares, each an inch on each side, I had put them together to make one large square with a side of three inches, and I had then proved to my little grandson that - though it was impossible for us to see the inside of the square - we could still determine the number of square inches in the square by simply squaring the number of inches in the side: "and thus," I said, "we know that 32, or 9, represents the number of square inches in a square whose side is 3 inches long."

The little hexagon pondered this for a while and then said to me, "But you've been teaching me to raise numbers to the third power. I suppose 33 must mean something in geometry; what does it mean?"

"Nothing at all," I replied, "at least not in geometry, for geometry has only two dimensions." And then I began to show the boy how a point, by moving through a length of three inches, makes a line of three inches, which may be represented by three; and how a line of three inches, moving parallel to itself through a length of three inches, makes a square of three inches on each side, which may be represented by 32.

Upon this, my grandson, returning to his previous suggestion, suddenly spoke up and exclaimed, "Well, then, if a point by moving three inches makes a line of three inches represented by three; and if a straight line of three inches, moving parallel to itself, makes a square of three inches on each side, represented by 32; it must be that a square of three inches on each side, moving somehow parallel to itself (but I don't see how) must make something else (but I don't see what) of three inches on each side - and this must be represented by 33."

"Go to bed," I said, a little irritated by this interruption. "If you would talk less nonsense, you would remember more sense."

And so my grandson had disappeared in disgrace, and there I sat by my wife's side, trying to reflect on the year 1999 and the possibilities of the year 2000, but not quite able to shake off the thoughts suggested by the prattle of my bright little hexagon. Only a few grains of sand remained in the half-hour glass. Rousing myself from my reverie, I turned the glass northward for the last time in the old millennium, and in the act, I exclaimed aloud, "The boy is a fool."

Immediately, I became aware of a presence in the room, and a chill ran through me. "He is no such thing," cried my wife, "and you are breaking the commandments by dishonoring your own grandson." But I paid no attention to her. Looking around in every direction, I could see nothing, yet I still felt a presence and shivered as the cold whisper came again. I stood up. "What's the matter?" said my wife, "there's no draft; what are you looking for? There's nothing." There was nothing, and I resumed my seat, again saying, "The boy is a fool; 33 can have no meaning in geometry." At once, there came a distinctly audible reply, "The boy is not a fool, and 33 has an obvious geometrical meaning."

Both my wife and I heard the words, although she did not understand their meaning, and we both rushed forward in the direction of the sound. To our horror, we saw a figure before us. At first, it appeared to be a woman seen from the side, but a moment's observation showed me that the extremities passed into dimness too rapidly to represent a female form, and I would have thought it a circle, if it hadn't seemed to change its size in a manner impossible for a circle or any regular figure I had experienced.

But my wife, lacking my experience and the necessary calmness, immediately jumped to the conclusion that a woman had somehow entered the house through a small opening. "How comes this person here?" she exclaimed, "you promised me, my dear, that there would be no ventilators in our new house."

"Nor are there any," I said, "but what makes you think the stranger is a woman? I can see by my power of shape recognition—"

"Oh, I have no patience with your shape recognition," she interrupted, "'Feeling is believing' and 'A straight line to the touch is worth a circle to the sight' - two proverbs very common with the more delicate sex in Flatland."

"Well," I said, hoping to calm her, "if it must be so, demand an introduction." Assuming her most gracious manner, my wife approached the stranger, "Permit me, madam, to feel and be felt by—" Then, suddenly recoiling, "Oh! it is not a woman, and there are no angles either, not a trace of one. Can it be that I have so misbehaved towards a perfect circle?"

"I am indeed, in a certain sense, a circle," replied the voice, "and a more perfect circle than any in Flatland; but to speak more accurately, I am many circles in one." Then, more gently, "I have a message, dear madam, for your husband, which I must not deliver in your presence; and, if you would allow us to retire for a few minutes—" But my wife would not hear of our august visitor inconveniencing himself, and after many apologies for her recent indiscretion, she finally retreated to her room.

I glanced at the half-hour glass. The last grains of sand had fallen. The third millennium had begun.
