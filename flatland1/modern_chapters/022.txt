How I Tried to Spread the Theory of Three Dimensions in Other Ways, and the Result

My failure with my grandson did not encourage me to share my secret with others in my household. Yet, I did not lose hope of success. I realized I could not solely rely on the catchphrase "Upward, not Northward," but rather needed to seek a clear demonstration by presenting the public with a thorough understanding of the entire subject. For this purpose, it seemed necessary to resort to writing.

So, I devoted several months in private to composing a treatise on the mysteries of Three Dimensions. However, to evade the law if possible, I spoke not of a physical dimension, but of a realm of thought where, in theory, a Figure could look down upon Flatland and see the insides of all things simultaneously, and where it was possible that a Figure might be imagined as being surrounded by six Squares, containing eight terminal points. But in writing this book, I found myself sadly hindered by the impossibility of drawing the diagrams necessary for my purpose. After all, in our country of Flatland, there are no tablets but Lines, and no diagrams but Lines, all in one straight line and only distinguishable by difference in size and brightness. So, when I had finished my treatise (which I entitled "Through Flatland to Thoughtland"), I could not be certain that many would understand my meaning.

Meanwhile, my wife was troubled. All pleasures paled for me; all sights tempted and taunted me to outspoken treason, as I could not compare what I saw in Two Dimensions to what it truly was in Three, and I could hardly refrain from making such comparisons aloud. I neglected my clients and my own business to devote myself to the contemplation of the mysteries I had once beheld, yet which I could impart to no one, and which I found increasingly difficult to reproduce even in my own mind's eye. One day, about eleven months after my return from Spaceland, I tried to envision a Cube with my eyes closed, but failed; and though I succeeded later, I was not then, nor have I ever been since, entirely certain that I had precisely recreated the original.

This made me more melancholy than before and determined me to take some action, though I knew not what. I felt I would have been willing to sacrifice my life for the Cause, if thereby I could have produced conviction. But if I could not convince my own grandson, how could I convince the highest and most developed Circles in the land?
