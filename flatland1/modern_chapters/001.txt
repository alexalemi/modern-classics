The Nature of Flatland

I call our world Flatland, not because we use that name, but to help you, my dear readers who live in the wondrous realm of Space, better understand its nature.

Imagine a vast sheet of paper on which straight Lines, Triangles, Squares, Pentagons, Hexagons, and other shapes move freely about on or within the surface, unable to rise above or sink below it - much like shadows, only solid with luminous edges. This is a faithful representation of my country and its people. Alas, only a few years ago, I would have called it my "universe," but now my mind has been opened to greater truths.

In such a place, you'll immediately realize that there can be no true "solidity" as you understand it. But I suspect you may think we can at least distinguish the various Triangles, Squares, and other shapes as they move around. Not so - we can see nothing of the kind, at least not enough to tell one figure from another. All that is visible, or even possible to see, are Straight Lines. I will soon demonstrate the necessity of this.

Place a coin on the middle of one of your tables in Space, and lean over it - it will appear as a circle. Now, step back to the edge of the table and slowly lower your gaze (thus assuming the perspective of a Flatlander), and you will see the coin becoming more and more oval in your view. Eventually, when your eye is exactly level with the table's edge (as if you were a true Flatlander), the coin will appear as a straight line.

The same would happen if you treated a Triangle, Square, or any other flat shape in the same way. As soon as your eye is at the edge of the table, the shape will cease to appear as a figure and will instead look like a straight line. Consider an equilateral Triangle, which represents a respectable Tradesman in our world. Figure 1 shows how you would see the Tradesman from above, while Figures 2 and 3 depict how he would appear if your eye were close to or at the level of the table - a mere straight line.

When I visited Spaceland, I learned that your sailors have similar experiences while traversing the seas, discerning distant islands or coastlines on the horizon. The faraway land may have bays, headlands, and angles without number, yet from a distance, you see none of these (unless your sun shines brightly, revealing the projections and indentations through light and shadow) - just an unbroken grey line upon the water.

That is precisely what we see when one of our triangular or other acquaintances approaches us in Flatland. Since we have neither sun nor any light that casts shadows, we lack the visual aids you enjoy in Spaceland. As our friend draws nearer, his line appears larger; as he departs, it becomes smaller. But a straight line he remains, be he a Triangle, Square, Pentagon, Hexagon, or even a Circle.

You may wonder how, in these disadvantageous circumstances, we are able to distinguish our friends from one another. The answer to this very natural question will be better addressed when I describe the inhabitants of Flatland. For now, let me turn to the climate and houses in our country.
