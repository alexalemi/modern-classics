How I Came to the Land of Three Dimensions and What I Saw There

An indescribable horror seized me. There was darkness, then a dizzy, sickening sensation like nothing I had ever experienced before; I saw a Line that was not a Line, a Space that was not Space. I was myself, and yet not myself. When I found my voice, I cried out in anguish, "This is either madness or hell."

"It is neither," the calm voice of the Sphere replied. "It is knowledge; it is the Three Dimensions. Open your eyes and look steadily."

I looked, and behold, a new world! Before me stood, visibly realized, all that I had previously only inferred, conjectured, or dreamed of in the perfect beauty of a Circle. Yet I could see no heart, lungs, or arteries - only a harmonious Something for which I had no words, but which you, my Spaceland readers, would call the surface of the Sphere.

Prostrating myself mentally before my Guide, I asked, "How is it, O divine ideal of consummate loveliness and wisdom, that I see your exterior, yet cannot discern your internal organs?"

"What you think you see, you do not truly see," he replied. "It is not given to you, nor to any other Being, to behold my inner parts. I am of a different order of Beings from those in Flatland. Were I a Circle, you could see my intestines, but I am a Being composed of many Circles, the Many in the One, called in this country a Sphere. And just as the outside of a Cube is a Square, the outside of a Sphere appears as a Circle."

Bewildered as I was by his enigmatic words, I no longer chafed against them, but worshipped him in silent awe. He continued, speaking more gently, "Do not distress yourself if you cannot at first understand the deeper mysteries of Spaceland. They will dawn upon you by degrees. Let us return for a while to the region whence you came, and I will show you that which you have often reasoned about, but never seen with your own eyes - a visible angle."

"Impossible!" I cried, but the Sphere led the way, and I followed as if in a dream, until his voice arrested me once more: "Look below, and behold your own Pentagonal house and all its inhabitants."

I gazed downward and saw with my physical eye all that I had hitherto merely inferred - my four sons peacefully sleeping in the northwest rooms, my two orphaned grandsons to the south, the servants, the butler, my daughter, all in their respective apartments. Only my dear wife, alarmed by my absence, had left her room and was anxiously pacing the hall, awaiting my return. The page, roused by my cries, had also left his room and was now investigating the cabinet in my study, under the pretense of checking on my welfare.

All this I could now see, not merely infer, and as we drew nearer, I could discern even the contents of my cabinet, the two chests of gold, and the tablets the Sphere had mentioned.

Moved by my wife's distress, I would have leaped downward to reassure her, but found myself incapable of motion. "Trouble not yourself about your wife," said my Guide. "She will not be long left in anxiety; meanwhile, let us take a survey of Flatland."

Once more I felt myself rising through space. As the Sphere had said, the further we receded from the object, the larger became the field of vision. My native city, with the interior of every house and every creature therein, lay open to my view in miniature. We mounted higher, and the secrets of the earth, the depths of the mines, and the innermost recesses of the hills were unveiled before my unworthy eyes.

Awestruck at the sight of these mysteries, I said to my Companion, "Behold, I am become as a God. For the wise men in our country say that to see all things, or as they express it, 'omniscience,' is the attribute of God alone."

There was a touch of scorn in my Teacher's voice as he replied, "Is that so indeed? Then the very pickpockets and murderers of my country are to be worshipped by your wise men as being Gods, for there is not one of them who does not see as much as you see now. But trust me, your wise men are wrong."

"Then is omniscience the attribute of others besides Gods?" I asked.

"I do not know," he answered. "But if a thief or a murderer in our land can see everything that is in your land, surely that is no reason why the thief or murderer should be accepted by you as a God. This 'omniscience,' as you call it - it is not a common word in Spaceland - does it make you more just, more merciful, less selfish, more loving? Not in the least. Then how does it make you more divine?"

"More merciful, more loving!" I exclaimed. "But these are the qualities of women! And we know that a Circle is a higher Being than a Straight Line, in so far as knowledge and wisdom are more to be esteemed than mere affection."

"It is not for me to classify human faculties according to merit," replied the Sphere. "Yet many of the best and wisest in Spaceland think more of the affections than of the understanding, more of your despised Straight Lines than of your belauded Circles. But enough of this. Look yonder. Do you know that building?"

I looked, and in the distance, I saw an immense Polygonal structure, which I recognized as the General Assembly Hall of the States of Flatland, surrounded by dense lines of Pentagonal buildings at right angles to each other, which I knew to be the streets of the great Metropolis.

"Here we descend," said my Guide. It was now morning, the first hour of the first day of the two thousandth year of our era. Acting in strict accordance with precedent, the highest Circles of the realm were meeting in solemn conclave, as they had done on the first hour of the first day of the year 1000, and also on the first hour of the first day of the year 0.
