The Suppression of the Color Rebellion

The agitation for the Universal Color Bill continued for three years, and up until the very end it seemed that anarchy was destined to triumph.

A whole army of Polygons, who fought as common soldiers, was utterly annihilated by a superior force of Isosceles Triangles—with the Squares and Pentagons remaining neutral the entire time.

Worse still, some of the most capable Circles fell prey to spousal fury. Infuriated by political animosity, the wives in many noble households pestered their husbands to give up their opposition to the Color Bill; and some, finding their pleas fruitless, turned on and slaughtered their innocent children and spouses, perishing themselves in the act of carnage. It is recorded that during that three-year upheaval no less than twenty-three Circles were killed in domestic conflicts.

The peril was indeed great. It seemed the Priests had no choice between submission and extermination, when suddenly the course of events was completely changed by one of those picturesque incidents that statesmen should never ignore, often anticipate, and occasionally even orchestrate, given their absurdly disproportionate power to sway the sympathies of the common people.

It happened that a low-ranking Isosceles, with a brain little if at all above four degrees—accidentally stained by the colors of some Tradesman whose shop he had plundered—painted himself, or had himself painted (for the story varies), with the twelve colors of a Dodecagon. Going into the market, he accosted in a disguised voice a maiden, the orphaned daughter of a noble Polygon, whose affection he had pursued in vain in the past. Through a series of deceptions—aided on one side by a string of fortunate accidents too long to recount, and on the other by an almost unimaginable gullibility and carelessness on the part of the bride's relatives—he succeeded in consummating the marriage. The unhappy girl committed suicide upon discovering the fraud perpetrated against her.

When news of this catastrophe spread across the land, the minds of the Women were violently agitated. Sympathy for the wretched victim and fears of similar deceptions for themselves, their sisters, and their daughters made them now view the Color Bill in an entirely new light. Not a few openly declared themselves converted to opposition; the rest needed only a slight push to make a similar declaration. Seizing this favorable opportunity, the Circles hastily convened an extraordinary Assembly of the States, and besides the usual guard of Convicts, they secured the attendance of a large number of reactionary Women.

Amidst an unprecedented gathering, the Chief Circle of those days—named Pantocyclus—arose to find himself hissed and booed by a hundred and twenty thousand Isosceles. But he silenced the uproar by declaring that henceforth the Circles would adopt a policy of Concession, yielding to the wishes of the majority and accepting the Color Bill. The tumult immediately gave way to applause, and he invited Chromatistes, the leader of the Rebellion, into the center of the hall to receive the submission of the hierarchy in the name of his followers. Then followed a speech, a masterpiece of rhetoric, which occupied nearly a day in the delivery and to which no summary could do justice.

With an appearance of impartiality, he declared that since they were now irrevocably committing themselves to Reform or Innovation, it was desirable that they take one last comprehensive view of the whole subject, its flaws as well as its advantages. Gradually introducing the mention of the dangers to the Tradesmen, the Professional Classes, and the Gentlemen, he silenced the rising murmurs of the Isosceles by reminding them that, despite all these defects, he was willing to accept the Bill if it was approved by the majority. But it was clear that all except the Isosceles were moved by his words and were either neutral or opposed to the Bill.

Turning now to the Workmen, he asserted that their interests must not be neglected, and that if they intended to accept the Color Bill, they ought at least to do so with full awareness of the consequences. Many of them, he said, were on the verge of being admitted to the class of Regular Triangles; others anticipated a distinction for their children that they could not hope for themselves. That honorable ambition would not have to be sacrificed. With the universal adoption of Color, all distinctions would cease; Regularity would be confused with Irregularity; progress would give way to regression; the Workman would in a few generations be degraded to the level of the Military, or even the Criminal Class; political power would be in the hands of the largest group, that is to say the Criminal Classes, who were already more numerous than the Workmen and would soon outnumber all the other Classes combined when the usual Compensative Laws of Nature were violated.

A subdued murmur of agreement ran through the ranks of the Artisans, and Chromatistes, in alarm, attempted to step forward and address them. But he found himself surrounded by guards and forced to remain silent while the Chief Circle in a few impassioned words made a final appeal to the Women, exclaiming that if the Color Bill passed, no marriage would henceforth be safe, no woman's honor secure; fraud, deception, and hypocrisy would pervade every household; domestic bliss would share the fate of the Constitution and pass to speedy ruin. "Sooner than this," he cried, "come death."

At these words, which were the pre-arranged signal for action, the Isosceles Convicts fell upon and killed the wretched Chromatistes; the Regular Classes, opening their ranks, made way for a band of Women who, under the direction of the Circles, moved swiftly and unerringly upon the unsuspecting soldiers; the Artisans, imitating the example of their betters, also opened their ranks. Meanwhile, bands of Convicts occupied every exit with an impenetrable phalanx.

The battle, or rather slaughter, was of short duration. Under the skillful leadership of the Circles, almost every Women's charge was fatal, and many extracted their sting unharmed, ready for a second strike. But no second blow was needed; the rabble of the Isosceles did the rest of the work for themselves. Surprised, leaderless, attacked from the front by invisible foes, and finding their escape cut off by the Convicts behind them, they immediately—as is their nature—lost all presence of mind and raised the cry of "treachery." This sealed their fate. Every Isosceles now saw and felt an enemy in every other. Within half an hour, not one of that vast multitude was left alive, and the fragments of seven score thousand of the Criminal Class slain by one another's angles attested to the triumph of Order.

The Circles did not hesitate to push their victory to the fullest extent. The Working Men were spared but decimated. The Militia of the Equilaterals was immediately called out, and every Triangle suspected of Irregularity on reasonable grounds was destroyed by Court Martial, without the formality of exact measurement by the Social Board. The homes of the Military and Artisan classes were inspected over the course of more than a year; and during that period, every town, village, and hamlet was systematically purged of the excess of the lower orders that had arisen through neglect to pay the tribute of Criminals to the Schools and University, and the violation of other natural Laws of the Constitution of Flatland. Thus, the balance of classes was restored.

Needless to say, the use of Color was henceforth abolished, and its possession prohibited. Even the utterance of any word denoting Color, except by the Circles or by qualified scientific teachers, was punished by severe penalty. Only at our University, in some of the very highest and most esoteric classes—which I myself have never been privileged to attend—is it understood that the sparing use of Color is still sanctioned for the purpose of illustrating some of the deeper problems of mathematics. But of this I can only speak from hearsay.

Elsewhere in Flatland, Color no longer exists. The art of making it is known to only one living person, the Chief Circle for the time being; and by him, it is passed down on his deathbed to none but his Successor. One manufactory alone produces it; and, lest the secret be betrayed, the Workmen are annually consumed, and fresh ones introduced. So great is the terror with which even now our Aristocracy looks back to the far-distant days of the agitation for the Universal Color Bill.
