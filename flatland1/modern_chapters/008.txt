§ 8 The Ancient Art of Painting

If my readers have followed me so far, they will not be surprised to hear that life in Flatland is somewhat dull. I do not mean that there are no battles, conspiracies, uprisings, or factions - the kinds of events that are thought to make history interesting. Nor would I deny that the strange blend of life's problems and mathematical problems, which constantly invite speculation and provide opportunities for immediate verification, gives our existence a zest that you in Spaceland can hardly comprehend. I speak now from an aesthetic and artistic point of view when I say that life with us is dull, very dull indeed.

How could it be otherwise, when all one's views, all one's landscapes, historical scenes, portraits, flowers, and still-life paintings consist of nothing but a single line, with only varying degrees of brightness and darkness?

It was not always this way. Color, if tradition is to be believed, once brought a fleeting splendor to the lives of our ancestors in the distant past. Some private individual - a Pentagon whose name is variously reported - is said to have accidentally discovered the components of the simpler colors and a basic painting method. He began by decorating his own home, then his servants, and finally his father, sons, and grandsons, and himself. The convenience and beauty of the results appealed to everyone. Wherever this Chromatist, as the most reliable authorities agree he was called, turned his multicolored form, he immediately drew attention and commanded respect. No one now needed to "feel" him; no one mistook his front for his back; his neighbors could easily discern all his movements without straining their powers of calculation; no one jostled him or failed to make way for him; his voice was spared the exhausting effort by which we colorless Squares and Pentagons are often forced to proclaim our identities when moving through a crowd of ignorant Isosceles.

The fashion spread like wildfire. Within a week, every Square and Triangle in the district had copied the example of Chromatist, and only a few of the more conservative Pentagons held out. A month or two found even the Dodecagons infected with the innovation. A year had not passed before the habit had spread to all but the very highest nobility. Needless to say, the custom soon made its way from Chromatist's district to surrounding regions; and within two generations, no one in all Flatland was colorless except the Women and the Priests.

Here, Nature herself seemed to erect a barrier and plead against extending the innovations to these two classes. Many-sidedness was almost essential as a pretext for the Innovators. "Distinction of sides is intended by Nature to imply distinction of colors," such was the sophistry that in those days spread from mouth to mouth, converting whole towns at a time to a new culture. But manifestly, this maxim did not apply to our Priests and Women. The latter had only one side and therefore - to speak pedantically - _no sides_. The former, if they would assert their claim to be readily and truly Circles, and not merely high-class Polygons with an infinitely large number of infinitesimally small sides, were in the habit of boasting (what Women confessed and deplored) that they also had no sides, being blessed with a perimeter of only one line, or, in other words, a Circumference. Hence, these two classes could see no merit in the so-called axiom about "Distinction of Sides implying Distinction of Color," and while all others had succumbed to the allure of bodily decoration, the Priests and the Women alone remained untainted by paint.

Immoral, licentious, anarchic, unscientific - call them by whatever names you will - yet, from an aesthetic perspective, those ancient days of the Color Revolt were the glorious childhood of Art in Flatland, a childhood that sadly never matured into adulthood, nor even reached the blossom of youth. To live then was itself a delight, because living implied seeing. Even at a small gathering, the company was a pleasure to behold; the richly varied hues of an assembly in a church or theater are said to have more than once proved too distracting from our greatest teachers and actors; but most ravishing of all is said to have been the indescribable magnificence of a military review.
