Chapter 4

Where justice was concerned, he made no secret of his opinion — and he did not merely state it, he demonstrated it in action. In private life he was law-abiding and helpful to everyone he dealt with; in public he obeyed the magistrates in everything the laws required, in the life of the city and on military service alike, so that he stood out as a model of loyalty for everyone else. Three occasions in particular proved it. First, when he was serving as president of the Popular Assembly, he refused to let the sovereign people take an illegal vote, and stood on the side of the laws against a surge of popular feeling that, I think, would have broken any other man. Again, when the Thirty tried to give him orders that ran contrary to the laws, he refused to obey — when they forbade him to converse with the young, and when they ordered him and certain other citizens to arrest a man to be put to death, he stood out alone, on the ground that the orders they were giving him were against the laws. And finally, when he stood trial on the charge Meletus brought, he refused to play the usual courtroom game. Everyone knew that defendants won their acquittals by flattering the judges, by begging and appealing for pity in ways the laws forbade, and that many a man owed his life to exactly those methods. But Socrates would not do a single thing in court, however customary, that was not strictly legal; and though he could easily have been acquitted with only a slight bending of the rules, he chose to abide by the laws and die rather than break them and live.

He argued this position in conversation again and again, with one person after another; and one discussion in particular, with Hippias of Elis, on the subject of justice, has come to my knowledge.

Hippias had just arrived back in Athens after a long absence, and happened to be present while Socrates was telling some listeners how astonishing it was that if a man wanted someone taught to be a shoemaker or a carpenter or a metalworker or a horseman, he would know exactly where to send him. "They say," he added, "that if you want your horse or your ox trained properly, the world is full of instructors. But if you want to learn justice yourself, or have your son or your slave taught it, you have no idea where to find such instruction."

Hippias caught the words and needled him in his urbane way: "What, Socrates — still saying the same old things I used to hear from you years ago?"

"Yes," Socrates answered, "and what is stranger still, not only the same old things but on the same old subjects. You, I daresay, with your wide and versatile learning, never say the same thing twice on any subject."

"Naturally," he answered. "I always try to say something new."

"Even about things you know?" Socrates asked. "Suppose someone asks you how many letters there are in 'Socrates' and in what order — do you try to run off one list today and a different one tomorrow? Or in arithmetic, if someone asks whether twice five makes ten, does today's answer differ from yesterday's?"

"No," said Hippias, "on those subjects I do just what you do and repeat myself. But to come back to justice — I flatter myself I can now tell you something about it that neither you nor anyone else will be able to refute."

"By Hera!" Socrates exclaimed, "what a blessing you've discovered! Judges will stop splitting their votes; citizens will stop wrangling over right and wrong; no more lawsuits, no more party feuds; states will settle their differences, and wars will come to an end. For my part, I don't see how I can tear myself away from you until I've heard all about this grand discovery from your own lips."

"You shall hear it, all in good time," Hippias answered, "but not before you state your own belief plainly. What is justice? We've had quite enough of you ridiculing the rest of the world, questioning and cross-examining first one man and then another, while never rendering an account to anyone yourself or stating a plain opinion on a single subject."

"Why, Hippias," Socrates retorted, "haven't you noticed that I never stop proclaiming what I hold to be just?"

"And what exactly is this doctrine of yours? Let's have it in words."

"If I don't proclaim it in words," said Socrates, "I proclaim it in deeds and in fact. Or don't you think a deed is worth more as evidence than a word?"

"Worth far more," Hippias answered, "for many a man with justice on his lips commits injustice, but no man who does what is just can be unjust."

"Then have you ever heard of me, or seen me, or in any way caught me bearing false witness, or playing the informer, or setting friends quarreling, or stirring up faction in the city, or doing any other unjust thing?"

"No, I can't say I have."

"And don't you consider it just to abstain from injustice?"

"Now you're caught, Socrates — plainly trying to wriggle out of a plain statement. Asked what you believe justice to be, you keep telling us not what the just man does but what he doesn't do."

"I would have thought," Socrates answered, "that refusing to do injustice was credential enough of justice. But if you don't agree, see whether this pleases you better: I say that what is lawful is just."

"Do you mean," he asked, "that 'lawful' and 'just' are the same thing?"

"I do."

"I ask because I don't quite see what you mean by 'lawful,' or what you mean by 'just.'"

"You know what is meant by the laws of a city?"

"I do."

"And what do you take them to be?"

"The rules the citizens have enacted by agreement, about what should be done and what left undone."

"Then a citizen who runs his life by those enactments is law-abiding, and one who violates them is lawless?"

"Certainly."

"And the law-abiding man does what is just, while the lawless man does what is unjust?"

"Certainly."

"And the man who does what is just is just, and the man who does what is unjust is unjust?"

"Of course."

"It would appear, then, that the law-abiding man is just, and the lawless man unjust."

Then Hippias: "But laws, Socrates — how can anyone take the laws seriously, or take obeying them seriously, when the very people who made them are forever rejecting and rewriting them?"

"The same is true of war," Socrates replied. "Cities are forever undertaking wars and then making peace again."

"Perfectly true."

"Then what's the difference between belittling obedience to the laws because the laws may one day be repealed, and belittling discipline in wartime because peace will one day be made? Or perhaps you object to men fighting wholeheartedly for their homes and their country?"

"No indeed I don't! I heartily approve of it."

"Then take to heart the lesson Lycurgus taught the Spartans. Have you grasped that if he made Sparta stand out above every other state, it was by instilling in her, beyond everything else, a spirit of obedience to the laws? And among the magistrates and rulers of the various states, wouldn't you give first prize to those who do most to make their fellow citizens obey the laws? And wouldn't you admit that the state whose citizens are most distinguished for obedience to the laws thrives best in peacetime and is irresistible in war? But of all the blessings a state can enjoy, none stands higher than concord among its citizens. That is the constant theme of the councils of elders and of the best men in every community; everywhere throughout Greece it is established law that the citizens swear an oath of concord, and everywhere they actually swear it. Not, of course, that the citizens are to vote for the same choruses, or applaud the same flute-players, or choose the same poets, or confine themselves to the same pleasures — only that they are to obey the laws. For in the end the state whose citizens abide by the laws proves strongest and most prosperous; but without concord no state can be well governed and no household well managed.

"And in private life, what better protection can a man have than obedience to the laws? It is his safeguard against penalties and his guarantee of honors from the community; it lets him thread his way through the law courts without confusion, secure against defeat and assured of victory. It is the law-abiding man that people trust with their most sacred deposits — money, or their sons and daughters. In the eyes of the whole state he, and no one else, is the trustworthy man, the one who can be counted on to render everyone their due — parents and kinsmen and servants, friends and fellow citizens and foreigners. He is the man the enemy will soonest trust to arrange an armistice, a truce, a treaty of peace; the man they would rather have as an ally and fight beside. He is the man his country's allies will most confidently entrust with the command of their forces, or a garrison, or their very cities. He is the man who can be counted on to repay kindness with gratitude, and who therefore receives more kindness than anyone else. The most desirable of friends, the last man anyone wants for an enemy — no foreign state would choose to go to war with him. Surrounded by friends and free of foes, his very character draws men into friendship and alliance, and before him hatred and hostility melt away.

"And there, Hippias, I have done my part: that is my proof that the lawful and the just are one and the same. If you hold a contrary view, teach us."

"Upon my soul, Socrates," said Hippias, "I don't believe I hold any opinion contrary to what you've said about justice."

"But now, Hippias, do you know of certain unwritten laws?"

"Yes," he answered, "the ones observed in every country, and in the same sense everywhere."

"Could you say that men made them?"

"How could they have?" he answered. "All men could never have gathered together from the ends of the earth — and they don't even speak one language."

"Then who do you believe made these laws?"

"For my part, I think the gods must have made them for men. And I take this as proof: everywhere among men, the first law and custom is to worship and revere the gods."

"And honoring parents — that is the custom everywhere too?"

"That too."

"And so is the prohibition against parents having children with their children, and children with their parents?"

"No — there, Socrates, I stop. That one doesn't look to me like a law of God."

"Why not?"

"Because I notice that it is sometimes broken."

"People break plenty of other laws too," said Socrates. "But those who transgress the laws laid down by the gods pay a penalty no man can possibly escape — unlike the men who break human laws and escape punishment, some by slipping away unseen, some by force."

"And what is this inescapable penalty, Socrates, that parents and children who breed with one another have to pay?"

"The greatest of all. What worse can happen to human beings in producing children than to produce them badly?"

"But why should they produce them badly, when nothing prevents good parents from breeding good children?"

"Because to produce good children it is not enough that the two parents be good and of good stock. They must both also be in the prime and vigor of their bodies. Do you suppose the seed of those at their prime is like the seed of those who haven't yet reached it, or who are past it?"

"No — it stands to reason the seed would differ."

"And which is better?"

"That of those at their prime, clearly."

"So the seed of those not at their prime is not good?"

"Most likely not."

"Then that is not the right way to produce children?"

"It is not."

"Then children produced that way are produced as they ought not to be?"

"So it appears to me."

"Then who will be badly born, if not these?"

"I grant you that opinion too," Hippias replied.

"Again — isn't it a custom universally respected to return good for good and kindness for kindness?"

"A custom, yes — but that one, too, gets broken."

"Then the man who breaks it pays his penalty in finding himself deserted: stripped of good friends, and driven to chase after people who don't love him. Isn't it so? The man who treats me well when I deal with him is a good friend to me; but if I fail to repay his kindness, my ingratitude makes him hate me — and yet I must keep pursuing him and clinging to him, because his company is worth so much to me."

"Yes, Socrates. In every one of these cases, I admit, the hand of the gods shows through. A law that carries its own penalty for breaking it suggests to my mind a lawgiver better than human."

"And in your opinion, Hippias, do the gods legislate what is just, or the opposite of what is just?"

"Not the opposite, Socrates — heaven forbid! If God himself could not legislate justly, hardly anyone else could."

"It would seem, then, Hippias, that the gods themselves are well pleased that the lawful and the just should be one and the same."

By such words and such conduct, teaching by example and by precept alike, he helped make those who came near him more upright and more just.

Chapter 5

And now I want to show how he made his companions more capable in action. First of all, since his creed was that self-control is the indispensable foundation of any noble achievement, he showed himself to his companions as a man disciplined beyond all others; and then, by conversation and discussion, he encouraged them toward self-control above everything else. He kept the things that lead to virtue constantly in his own mind, and kept reminding everyone he met of them. One conversation of his with Euthydemus on the subject of self-control has come to my knowledge, and it will serve to illustrate.

"Tell me, Euthydemus," he began, "do you believe freedom is a noble and magnificent possession, whether for a man or for a state?"

"I can't conceive of a nobler or more magnificent one," he answered.

"Then do you believe a man is free who is ruled by the pleasures of the body and, because of them, cannot do what is best?"

"Certainly not."

"No — presumably doing what is best looks like freedom to you, and having masters over you who prevent it looks like the loss of freedom?"

"Absolutely."

"So it seems clear to you that the man with no self-control is the very opposite of free?"

"Upon my word, I strongly suspect so."

"And does it seem to you that the man without self-control is merely prevented from doing what is noblest, or that he is actually driven to do what is most shameful?"

"Driven to the one, I think, as surely as he is blocked from the other."

"And what sort of lords and masters are they, do you think, who block what is best and enforce what is worst?"

"Goodness knows — the very worst masters there could be."

"And what do you take to be the worst kind of slavery?"

"Slavery to the worst masters," he answered.

"It would seem, then, that the man without self-control is bound over to the very worst kind of slavery?"

"So it appears."

"And doesn't this same lack of self-control shut wisdom — the best of all things — out of a man's life, and plunge him into its opposite? Doesn't it drag him off toward what is pleasant, and keep him from attending to the things that would truly benefit him and from learning to understand them? And often, though he can see the good and the bad perfectly well, doesn't it confound his wits and make him choose the worse in place of the better?"

"Yes — that is how it goes."

"And a sound and temperate mind — who has less claim to that than the man without self-control? The works of a temperate spirit and the works of self-indulgence are exact opposites, surely?"

"That too I admit."

"And when it comes to caring for the things a man ought to care about and giving himself to them — can you think of anything that fights against it harder than this same lack of self-control?"

"Nothing that I can think of."

"And can anything worse befall a man? Can he fall under a more ruinous influence than the one that makes him choose the harmful in place of the helpful, coaxes him to devote himself to the bad and neglect the good, and forces him — like it or not — to do what every man in his sober senses would shrink from?"

"I can imagine nothing worse."

"And self-control, it is reasonable to suppose, works the opposite effects on men from the lack of it?"

"It is."

"And what works the opposite of the very worst is presumably the very best?"

"That follows naturally."

"So it looks, Euthydemus, doesn't it, as if self-control is the best thing a man can have?"

"It does indeed, Socrates."

"But now, Euthydemus, has one fact ever struck you?"

"What fact?"

"That in the end, lack of self-control cannot even deliver us to that realm of sweetness some people think is its special territory. It is self-control, and nothing else, that holds the passport to the highest pleasures."

"How so?" he asked. "In what way?"

"This way," Socrates answered. "Lack of self-control will not let us hold out against hunger or thirst or desire or lack of sleep — and enduring those is the only road to any real pleasure in eating and drinking, in love, in rest and in sweet sleep: waiting patiently, holding out, until each pleasure arrives at its sweetest moment. And so lack of self-control cuts us off from any full enjoyment of the most obvious and most constantly recurring pleasures. Only self-control enables us to endure the pains I've named, and so only self-control gives us any pleasure worth remembering in these everyday cases."

"That is the plain truth," he answered.

"Furthermore, if there is any joy in learning something beautiful and good, or in the patient discipline that lets a man keep his body in shape, manage his household well, make himself useful to his friends and his city, and master his enemies — pursuits that bring not only the greatest advantages but the deepest satisfactions — it is the self-controlled who reap those fruits in the very doing of them, while the man with no self-control has no share in any of them. For who, we may fairly say, has less claim to such things than the man least able to do them — tied down as he is to chasing whatever pleasure lies nearest to hand?"

Euthydemus replied: "Socrates, you seem to me to be saying that a man who is mastered by the pleasures of the body has nothing to do with virtue at all."

"And what is the difference, Euthydemus," he asked, "between a man without self-control and the dullest of beasts? If a man abandons every high aim, gives up searching for what is best, and strives only to gratify his sense of pleasure, how is he better than the silliest of cattle? No — it is given to the self-controlled alone to search out the things of highest worth, and, sorting them out by kinds in word and in deed, to choose the good deliberately and hold back from the bad. That is how a man becomes best and happiest," he added, "and most capable of reasoning and discussion. The very word 'discussion' — dialectic — comes from people getting together to deliberate in common, sorting things by kinds. So a man ought to prepare himself for that as thoroughly as he can, and pursue it with earnest resolution beyond everything else; for that is the road to excellence, and that is what makes a man fittest to lead his fellows and strongest in debate."
