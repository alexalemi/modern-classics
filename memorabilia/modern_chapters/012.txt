Book IV

(Part 1 of 6)

Chapter 1

That was Socrates: so helpful in every situation and in every way that nobody with ordinary sensitivity who watched him could miss the fact that time spent in his company — anywhere, under any circumstances — was a priceless gain. Even the memory of him, once he was gone, was no small benefit to those who had grown used to his company and welcomed him. And he was just as helpful to his friends in his playful moods as in his serious ones.

Take that saying so often on his lips: "I am in love with so-and-so." It was obvious to everyone that what drew his soul was never a beautiful body in its bloom, but a soul unfolding toward virtue. And he spotted these "good natures" by certain signs: quickness to learn whatever they turned their attention to; a memory that held on to what they had learned; and a real passion for the studies that help a person manage a household or a state — for dealing well, in general, with people and human affairs. People like that, he maintained, needed only an education to become happy themselves, to run their own households happily, and to be able to make other people — whole states or single individuals — happy as well.

He had a different approach for different kinds of people. Those who thought their natural gifts were so good they could look down on learning, he taught this lesson: it is precisely the most gifted nature that needs training and education most. Look at horses, he would say. It is the spirited, fiery thoroughbred that, if properly broken in as a colt, grows into a superb and serviceable animal — but left unbroken, turns out completely unmanageable and good for nothing. Or take dogs: a puppy that shows the appetite for hard work and the eagerness to go after wild game that mark a fine breed will, if raised well, prove excellent for the hunt or any other useful work; but neglect his training and he turns into a stupid, frantic brute who cannot obey the simplest command. It is exactly the same with human beings. The young man with the best natural endowment — the strongest spirit, the firmest determination to carry through whatever he takes in hand — will, if trained and taught what is right to do, become a supremely good and useful man. He does what is best, on the grandest scale. But leave that same young man ignorant and untrained, and he becomes not just very bad but very dangerous. Lacking the knowledge to see what is right to do, he will often put his hand to villainy, and the very size and force of his character make it impossible to rein him in or turn him off his evil course. So in his case too the achievements come on the grandest scale — but the worst.

Then there was the type so swollen with pride in his riches that he considered himself excused from any further education — since "money makes the man," and his wealth would be quite enough to get him whatever he wanted and win him honors from an admiring world. Socrates would bring such people back to their senses. Wasn't it folly, he would point out, to suppose that without instruction a man could draw the line between what helps and what harms in conduct? And folly again to suppose that, without being able to draw that line, a man could use wealth alone to get whatever he liked, or find the useful path lying plain in front of him? Wasn't it the height of simplicity to think that a man who cannot work to any profit can be doing well, or be equipped in any adequate way for the business of life? And the height of simplicity again to think that mere wealth, without real knowledge, could buy a reputation for some excellence — or that without such a reputation a man could win distinction and fame?
