Chapter 7

Charmides, the son of Glaucon, was, as Socrates could see, a man of real ability and influence — far more capable, in fact, than most of the people active in politics at the time. But he was also a man who shrank from speaking before the people or taking any hand in the business of the state. So Socrates put this to him:

"Tell me, Charmides — suppose a man were good enough to win a victory in the great games and take the crown, bringing honor to himself and making his country more glorious throughout Greece, and yet he refused to compete. What kind of person would you call him?"

"Soft and cowardly, obviously," he answered.

"And if another man had it in him, by devoting himself to public affairs, to lift up his city and win honor for himself in the process, and yet he hesitated and hung back — wouldn't it be fair to call him a coward too?"

"Maybe," he said. "But why are you asking me these questions?"

"Because," Socrates replied, "I think you have exactly that power, and yet you hesitate to take up work that you are bound to share in, if only because you are a citizen."

"And where have you detected this power of mine," said Charmides, "that you pass so harsh a sentence on me?"

"In those gatherings where you meet the men who run the city's politics," said Socrates. "I've watched: whenever they consult you on anything, you always have good advice to give, and whenever they make a mistake, you put your finger on the weak spot at once."

"Talking things over in private is one thing, Socrates," said Charmides. "Fighting it out in a packed assembly is another."

"And yet a man who can count," said Socrates, "counts just as well in a crowd as he does sitting alone; and it's the best harp player in private who wins the prize in public."

"But surely you see," said Charmides, "that shyness and stage fright are planted deep in human nature — and they grip us far harder in a crowd than among our close friends."

"Yes — and that's exactly what I'm determined to teach you," said Socrates. "You feel no such shyness before the wisest and most powerful men, yet you're ashamed to open your mouth in front of the weakest and dullest. Who is it among them that overawes you? The fullers? The cobblers? The carpenters, the coppersmiths, the merchants, the farmers, the traders in the marketplace calculating how to buy cheap and sell dear? Because those are the individual atoms the Popular Assembly is made of. How is your behavior any different from a champion athlete quailing before a crowd of amateurs? You can argue comfortably with the leading statesmen of the city — some of whom look down on you — and you stand head and shoulders above the practiced public speakers; and yet the moment you face a set of people who have never given politics a thought in their lives, and who certainly never dreamed of looking down on you, you're afraid to open your mouth for terror of being laughed at."

"Well, you'll admit," he answered, "that the Popular Assembly does quite often laugh at sound argument."

"So do the others," said Socrates. "And that's precisely what astonishes me: you handle those grand people easily enough when they turn to mockery, and yet you've convinced yourself you can't stand up to a crowd of ordinary men. My good friend, don't be ignorant of yourself! Don't fall into the commonest mistake there is — the mistake of people who rush off to investigate everyone else's business and never turn to examine their own. Don't shirk that duty like a coward. Brace yourself to pay real attention to who you are; and as for public affairs, if there is any way they can be improved through you, don't neglect them. When politics goes well, it isn't only the mass of your fellow citizens who profit — it's your personal friends, and, not least, yourself."

Chapter 8

Once Aristippus set out to cross-examine Socrates the way Socrates had once cross-examined him. Socrates, wanting the discussion to benefit the people around him, answered not like a debater guarding his argument against tricks, but like a man convinced that nothing matters more than doing what is right.

Aristippus asked him whether he knew of anything good. His plan was this: if Socrates said yes and named some particular good — food, drink, money, health, strength, courage — Aristippus would show that the thing named was sometimes bad. But Socrates knew that when something troubles us, what we want is whatever will end the trouble, and he answered in exactly the best way.

"Are you asking me," he said, "whether I know something good for fever?"

"No," he replied, "that's not my question."

"For eye inflammation, then?"

"No, not that either."

"For hunger?"

"No, not for hunger."

"Well," said Socrates, "if you're asking me whether I know of any good thing that is good for nothing, I don't know of it — and I don't want to."

Aristippus came back at him and asked whether he knew of anything beautiful.

"Yes," he answered, "many things."

"Are they all like one another?"

"On the contrary — often as unlike as they could possibly be."

"Then how can something be beautiful," he asked, "when it's unlike the beautiful?"

"Easily," said Socrates. "A man who's a beautiful runner can be quite unlike a man who's a beautiful boxer; a shield, which is a beautiful thing for defense, is utterly unlike a javelin, which is a beautiful thing for swift, sure throwing."

"Your answers are no better now," said Aristippus, "than when I asked whether you knew anything good. They're cut from the same cloth."

"And so they should be," said Socrates. "Do you imagine that good is one thing and beautiful another? Don't you know that all things are beautiful and good relative to the same standard? Virtue, to start with, isn't good by one standard and beautiful by another. Human beings are called 'beautiful and good' in the same sense and by the same standard; men's bodies are seen to be beautiful and good by the same measure; and in general everything people use is considered beautiful and good by the same test — namely, what it is useful for."

"Then I suppose," said Aristippus, "even a dung basket is a beautiful thing?"

"Certainly," he answered, "and a golden spear an ugly one — if for their respective purposes the basket is well made and the spear badly."

"Are you telling me the same things can be both beautiful and ugly?"

"Of course — and by the same reasoning, both good and bad. What's good for hunger is often bad for fever, and what's good for fever bad for hunger; what's beautiful for wrestling is often ugly for running. Everything is good and beautiful when it's well suited to its purpose, and bad and ugly when it's ill suited."

In the same way, when he argued about houses — that the same house must be at once beautiful and useful — I couldn't help feeling he was giving a fine lesson in how a house ought to be built. He approached it like this:

"If you mean to build the perfect house, shouldn't you plan to make it as pleasant and as useful to live in as possible?" And once that was admitted: "It's pleasant to have the house cool in summer and warm in winter, isn't it?" And when that was granted too: "Now, in a house facing south, the winter sun slants in under the porch, while in summer, when it passes right overhead, the roof gives us welcome shade. If that's what we want, then we should build the south side higher, to catch the winter sun, and the north side lower, to keep out the cold winds. To put it in a word: the pleasantest and most beautiful home is the one where the owner can find the most agreeable retreat in every season and store his belongings in the greatest safety."

Paintings and ornamental moldings, he said, rob you of more pleasure than they give.

The best site for a temple or an altar, he maintained, was a spot visible from far off and untrodden by human feet: it does the worshipper good to lift up his eyes from a distance and offer his prayer, and good to approach the holy place clean and undefiled.

Chapter 9

Asked once whether courage could be taught or came by nature, he answered: "I think that just as one body is naturally stronger than another for hard work, so one soul is naturally tougher than another in the face of danger — I certainly notice that people raised under the same laws and customs differ widely in daring. But I believe that every nature can be strengthened toward courage by learning and practice. It's obvious that Scythians and Thracians wouldn't dare take up shield and spear and fight Spartans; and it's just as obvious that Spartans would refuse to face Thracians armed only with light shields and javelins, or Scythians with nothing but bows and arrows. And as far as I can see, the same holds everywhere: men differ from one another by nature, but they improve by practice and attention. Which makes one thing clear — everyone, quick-witted or slow, should learn and practice the things he wants to excel in."

Between wisdom and self-control he drew no line. If a man could recognize the things that are beautiful and good well enough to live by them, and knew the shameful things well enough to avoid them, Socrates judged him wise and self-controlled at once.

When he was pressed further — did he consider people wise and self-controlled who know what is right but don't do it? — he answered: "No more than I consider them unwise and out of control. Everyone, I take it, chooses from the options open to him whatever he thinks serves his interest best, and then does it. So I hold that people who act wrongly and crookedly are neither wise nor self-controlled."

He said, too, that justice, and every other virtue, is wisdom. Just actions, and all actions done with virtue, are beautiful and good; people who know these things will never choose anything else in their place, and people who don't know them cannot do them — even if they try, they miss the mark and fail. So the wise do what is beautiful and good, and the unwise cannot; even when they try, they fail. And since all just actions, and all beautiful and good actions generally, are done with virtue, it's clear that justice and every other virtue is wisdom.

Madness, he maintained, was the opposite of wisdom — though he didn't count simple ignorance as madness. What came very near madness, he argued, was for a man not to know himself: to imagine and assume he knows what he doesn't know. Most people, of course, talk differently: if a man is completely astray about something most of mankind knows nothing about, they don't call him mad; but the same derangement about matters of common knowledge they do call madness. If a man thinks himself too tall to walk through a gate in the Long Wall without stooping, or strong enough to lift a house, or attempts any other plain impossibility, they say he's insane; but if his delusions are confined to small matters, popular opinion doesn't call him mad at all. In fact, just as ordinary speech reserves the word "passion" for strong desire, so it reserves the word "madness" for derangement on a grand scale.

Asked what envy was, he found it to be a certain kind of pain — not the sorrow we feel at a friend's misfortune or an enemy's success; that isn't envy. "Envy," he said, "is felt only by people who are annoyed at the successes of their friends." When someone expressed astonishment that anyone who liked another person could be pained at his doing well, he pointed to a common tendency: when a man is doing badly, people's sympathies are stirred and they rush to help him; but when fortune smiles on him, they somehow feel hurt. "I don't say this could happen to a thoughtful person," he added, "but it's not an uncommon condition in a silly mind."

Asked what leisure was, he said: "I find that most men are always doing something. The dice player, the gambler, the clown — they're all doing something, and they all have leisure, because they're free to turn and do something better. But nobody is at leisure to turn from better to worse; and if he turns that way, having no leisure for it, he is simply doing badly."

Kings and rulers, he said, are not the men who merely hold the scepter, or get elected by any passers-by, or drawn by lot, or who seize power by force or fraud — but the men who know how to rule. Once someone granted that it's the ruler's job to command what ought to be done and the job of the ruled to obey, he would point out example after example: on a ship, the one who rules is the man who knows — the captain — and the shipowner and everyone else on board obey him as the expert. In farming, the landowners obey the one who knows; in sickness, the patients; in athletic training, the young men in training. In general, anyone with something at stake that needs care will attend to it himself, if he thinks he has the knowledge; and if he doesn't trust his own expertise, he will eagerly obey any expert on hand — or even send for one from a distance — and follow that expert's guidance, doing what he's told.

And in the art of spinning wool, he liked to point out, women rule men — because women know the art and men don't.

If anyone objected that a tyrant has the option of not listening to good advice, he would answer: "How does he have that option, with a penalty hanging over everyone who disobeys wise counsel? In whatever matter he disobeys good advice, he will go wrong, I presume — and going wrong, he will pay for it." And to the suggestion that the tyrant could simply have the wise man killed: "Do you think a man who destroys his best ally gets off free, or suffers only some slight, passing loss? Is that the way to save himself, do you think — or the fastest way to his own destruction?"

When someone asked him what the best pursuit for a man was, he answered: "Doing well." Asked next whether he counted good luck a pursuit, he said: "Quite the opposite — luck and doing are poles apart, in my view. To stumble into something desirable without seeking it, I call good luck; to do a thing well through learning and practice, I call doing well — and the people who make that the serious business of their lives seem to me to be doing well indeed."

The best men and the dearest to God, he went on, are those who do their work well — in farming, the work of the farm; in medicine, the work of healing; in politics, the work of the state. A man who does nothing well, he added, is good for nothing and dear to no god.

Chapter 10

Then again, whenever chance brought him into conversation with someone who possessed an art and practiced it for a living, he never failed to be useful to that kind of person too. Stepping one day into the studio of Parrhasius the painter, he fell into conversation with him.

"Parrhasius," he said, "painting is a representation of visible things, isn't it? With your colors you painters copy and reproduce, as closely as you can, hollows and heights, dark and light, hard and soft, rough and smooth, the freshness of youth and the wrinkles of age — don't you?"

"That's right," he answered. "So we do."

"And when you're portraying ideal beauty, since it isn't easy to find any one human being without a flaw, you gather from many models the most beautiful features of each, and so make your figures beautiful throughout?"

"Yes, that's how we work."

"But wait," Socrates went on. "Do you also capture the character of the soul — the thing that is most winning, most sweet, most lovable, most longed-for, most desirable? Or can't that be depicted at all?"

"How could it be, Socrates?" he answered. "It has no proportion, no color, none of the qualities you just listed — it isn't even visible."

"Well — does a person's look ever show kindness toward someone, or hatred?"

"I'd say it does."

"Then that much, at least, can be captured in the eyes?"

"Certainly."

"And do people wear the same expression over their friends' good fortune as over their misfortune?"

"By no means. Over good fortune the face is radiant; over bad, clouded."

"So these looks, too, can be represented?"

"Decidedly."

"And more than that: nobility and generosity, meanness and servility, self-control and good sense, insolence and vulgarity — they show through a person's face and posture, whether he's standing still or moving, don't they, as if through a chink in a wall?"

"You're right."

"Then these can be imitated too?"

"No doubt."

"And which do you think is the pleasanter face to look at — one stamped with a beautiful, good, lovable character, or one that bears the marks of what is ugly, bad, and hateful?"

"There's no comparison, Socrates."

Another time he went into the workshop of Cleiton the sculptor, and in the course of conversation said to him: "You have a gallery of handsome figures here, Cleiton — runners, wrestlers, boxers, all-in fighters. I can see that. But the quality that most draws the soul of the viewer through his eyes — the look of life — how do you produce that in your statues?"

Cleiton stood there puzzled and didn't answer at once, so Socrates went on: "Is it by modeling your work closely on living bodies that you make your statues appear alive?"

"No doubt it is," he answered.

"By faithfully copying how the parts of the body pull up or down with a pose — squeezed together or stretched apart, tensed or relaxed — that's how you make your statues look like real bodies, make them 'breathe,' as people say?"

"Exactly."

"And doesn't a faithful rendering of what a body in action is feeling give the viewer a particular pleasure?"

"I'd say so."

"Then the menace in the eyes of soldiers in battle ought to be copied, and the radiance of a victor in his moment of triumph imitated?"

"Absolutely."

"It seems, then, that the sculptor's job is to build into his form the workings of the soul as well."

Visiting Pistias the armor-maker, who showed him some finely made breastplates, Socrates exclaimed: "By Hera, Pistias, a splendid invention — the breastplate covers the parts of a man that need protecting, and still leaves his arms and hands free. But tell me," he added, "why do you charge more for your breastplates when they're no stronger and no more expensively made than other men's?"

"Because mine, Socrates," he answered, "are better proportioned."

"Proportioned! And how do you show that quality to a customer, to justify the higher price — by measure, or by weight? Because I assume you don't make them all identical, to one pattern — not if you make them fit, as of course you do."

"Fit! I most certainly do," he answered. "Take my word for it — a breastplate is no use without that."

"But aren't the wearers' bodies themselves," asked Socrates, "some well proportioned and some badly?"

"Decidedly so."

"Then how do you make a well-proportioned breastplate to fit a badly proportioned body?"

"The same way I make it fit," said Pistias. "What fits is well proportioned."

"So you're using 'well proportioned' not in an absolute sense," said Socrates, "but relative to the wearer — the way you might call a shield well proportioned for the man it suits, or a military cloak, and so on with everything else, in your way of speaking. But maybe there's another considerable advantage in this fitting?"

"Teach me, Socrates, if you have something in mind."

"A breastplate that fits galls the wearer less with its weight than one that doesn't. The ill-fitting one either drags on the shoulders like a dead weight or presses hard on some other part of the body, and turns into a nuisance and a torment. But the one that fits spreads its weight — some along the collarbone and shoulder blades, some over the shoulders and chest, some on the back and belly — until it feels less like a load to carry than like a second skin."

"You've named the very quality," said Pistias, "that I think gives my work its special value. Though I have to say, some customers want something else in a breastplate — decoration, or gold inlay."

"For all that," replied Socrates, "if they end up buying one that doesn't fit, all they own is a finely worked, gilded nuisance, it seems to me. But tell me this," he added. "The body doesn't hold one fixed position — it bends at one moment and straightens at the next. How can a breastplate that's exactly modeled to it ever fit?"

"It can't."

"You mean," Socrates continued, "that what fits is not the exactly modeled breastplate, but the one that doesn't gall the wearer in use?"

"You've hit the very point, Socrates," said Pistias. "You understand the matter exactly."
