Book I

(Part 2 of 4)

Chapter 2

Just as surprising to me is the belief that Socrates corrupted the young. Here was a man who, beyond everything I have already said, kept his appetites and passions under the strictest control; who could endure winter's cold, summer's heat, and every kind of hard work better than almost anyone; who had trained himself to need so little that even on the scantiest means he never lacked enough. Is it believable that such a man made others irreverent, or lawless, or self-indulgent, or soft in the face of hard work? Far from it. He was the saving of many, by kindling in them a passion for virtue and the hope that if they managed themselves carefully they could grow truly beautiful and good. Not that he ever set up as a teacher of virtue. But by being so visibly good himself, he made the people who spent time with him hope that by imitating him they might one day come to resemble him.

Nor should anyone conclude from this that he neglected his own body, or approved of those who neglected theirs. Stuffing yourself and then sweating it off with overwork was a regimen he condemned; the right plan, he thought, was to eat as much as the appetite genuinely welcomed and work it off with moderate exercise — a habit healthy for the body and no obstacle to the care of the soul. And there was nothing showy or affected about him: no dandyism of cloak or shoes, no soft living of any kind.

Least of all did he make his companions greedy for money. He curbed every other craving in them, and he refused to make a profit from the one craving they had for his company. By taking no payment, he believed, he was guarding his own freedom. People who charged fees for their conversation he branded as sellers of their own persons, since the fee obliges them to talk for the benefit of whoever pays. What amazed him was that anyone possessing virtue would demand money as its price, instead of counting the real profit — an honest friend gained — as reward enough. As if a soul newly awakened to honor could ever forget its debt to its greatest benefactor!

For his own part, without ever making any such promise, he was content to trust that those who took his teaching to heart would be good and true friends to him and to one another for life. How, then, would a man of this character corrupt the young? Unless the careful cultivation of virtue is corruption.

"But," says the accuser, "Socrates taught his companions to despise the established laws, by harping on the folly of appointing state officials by lot — a method no one would dream of using to choose a ship's pilot or a flute-player or anyone else in that line of work, where a mistake costs far less than a mistake in politics." Talk like this, the accuser claims, incited the young to hold the constitution in contempt and made them violent and headstrong. My own view is the opposite: people who train their minds, and who believe they can teach their fellow citizens where their true interests lie, are the last people to turn to violence. They know too well that violence breeds hatred and danger, while persuasion gets the same results safely and with goodwill. The man who is forced hates you, like a man who has been robbed of something precious; the man who is persuaded is grateful, as if you had done him a favor. Compulsion is not the method of the man who cultivates wisdom; it is the method of the man who holds power without judgment. And consider this too: a man who resorts to force needs plenty of allies to fight his battles; a man whose strength is persuasion needs no one, because he is confident he can win consent single-handed. What would such a man want with bloodshed? How absurd to kill a man rather than have him alive, willing, and useful.

"But," the accuser answers, "the two men who did the state more harm than anyone in its history — Critias and Alcibiades — were both companions of Socrates: Critias the oligarch and Alcibiades the democrat. Where will you find a greedier thief, a more savage killer, than the one? Where such a monument of insolence, self-indulgence, and high-handedness as the other?" For my part, insofar as those two harmed the state, I have no wish to appear as the defender of either. I will only explain what their connection with Socrates really was.

They were the two most ambitious men Athens ever produced. Ambition was in their blood: if they got their way, all power would be in their hands, and their fame would eclipse everyone else's. About Socrates they knew three things: first, that he lived a completely independent life on almost nothing; next, that he was disciplined to the last degree in the face of every pleasure; and last, that in debate he could do whatever he liked with any opponent. Given those facts, and given the character of the pair, which is more likely — that they sought his company because they were drawn to his way of life and his self-mastery, or because they calculated, "If we attach ourselves to him, we will become masters of statecraft, unmatched in speech and action"? My own belief is this: if heaven had offered them the choice between living out their whole lives as they saw Socrates living his, or dying, they would both have chosen death.

Their conduct is the proof. The moment they felt themselves the masters of everyone around them, they sprang away from Socrates and plunged into politics — the only prize for which they had ever sought his company in the first place.

Here someone may object: before giving his companions lessons in politics, Socrates should have taught them self-control. I do not dispute the principle. But I observe that every teacher shows his pupils, by example, how he practices what he preaches, and backs it up with argument. And I know that Socrates showed himself to his companions as a beautiful and noble character, and reasoned with them about virtue and every other human concern in the noblest way. I know, too, that even those two practiced self-control as long as they were with him — not from any fear that Socrates would fine them or beat them, but because at the time they were genuinely persuaded that such conduct was best.

Perhaps some self-styled philosophers will answer: "A truly just man can never become unjust; a self-controlled man can never lose control; nothing once learned can ever be unlearned." I do not agree. The soul works like the body. If the body goes without exercise, it can no longer do its work; if the soul goes without exercise, the same thing happens — we can no longer do what we should, or hold back from what we shouldn't. That is why fathers keep their sons away from bad men, however steady the sons may be: the company of the good is training in virtue, and the company of the bad is its undoing.

One poet testifies to this: "From the noble you will learn noble things; mix with the base, and you will lose even the sense you have." Another says: "The good man is base at one hour and noble at another." And I add my own testimony to theirs. I see that no one can remember a long poem without practice and repetition; in the same way, whoever stops rehearsing his teacher's words of instruction forgets them. And with the words of warning, a man forgets the very state of soul in which he once longed for goodness — and having forgotten that, is it any wonder he lets goodness itself slip away too? I see also that a man who slides into drunkenness, or plunges headlong into a love affair, loses his old power of doing right and refraining from wrong. Many a man who found thrift easy while passion was cold loses the knack the moment he falls in love; and once he has poured out his money, he no longer keeps his hands off gains he would once have been ashamed to touch. So where is the difficulty in supposing that a man can be self-controlled today and not tomorrow — that a man who once had the power to act rightly can lose it? To me, at any rate, it is clear that everything beautiful and noble is the fruit of constant practice and training, and self-control most of all. For the desires are planted in the same body as the soul, right alongside her, and they never stop whispering in her ear: "Drop this self-restraint. Hurry up and gratify us — gratify the body."

But back to Critias and Alcibiades. I repeat: as long as they lived with Socrates, they were able, with his support, to master their base appetites. Then they were separated from him. Critias fled to Thessaly, where he kept company with men better versed in lawlessness than in justice. Alcibiades fared no better. His beauty made him the quarry of fashionable women who hunted him as fair game, while his influence in the city and among the allies delivered him into the hands of expert flatterers. Honored by the democracy, stepping effortlessly to the front rank, he behaved like an athlete so sure of victory in the games that he lets his training slide — and so he forgot the duty he owed himself.

Such were the fortunes of these two. Is what followed really so strange? Puffed up by their ancestry, exalted by their wealth, inflated by power, worked on by a whole company of human tempters, and separated from Socrates for years on end — is it any wonder they grew arrogant through and through? And for the offenses of these two, the accuser holds Socrates responsible! Yet for the fact that in their early days, when they were young and at the age when recklessness and lack of restraint come most naturally, this same Socrates kept them modest and well behaved — for that, the accuser has not one word of praise. That is not how justice is measured anywhere else. What teacher of the harp or the flute, what teacher of anything, who has turned out competent pupils, is held to account because one of them goes off to another teacher and turns out a failure? What father, whose son stays honest in the company of one friend but goes to the bad in the company of another, blames the earlier friend? Doesn't he rather praise the first one all the more, the worse the boy turns out with the second? Fathers themselves, who share their own homes with their sons, are not blamed for their sons' offenses, so long as their own conduct is good. That is the fair test to apply to Socrates. Was he guilty of any base conduct himself? If so, call him a scoundrel. But if he never once faltered in self-control from beginning to end, how, in the name of justice, can he be blamed for a vice that was never in him?

I will go further. Even if he had done no wrong himself, but had watched others do wrong and approved, he would deserve blame. Listen, then. Socrates was well aware that Critias was in love with Euthydemus, and was trying to use him the way men do whose desire is for the body. He tried to talk him out of it, arguing that it was degrading, and unworthy of an honorable man, to appear as a beggar before the very person he loved and wanted to think well of him — forever pleading for something shameful to give and shameful to get.

When this reasoning fell on deaf ears and Critias refused to give it up, Socrates — so the story goes — remarked in front of a whole company, Euthydemus included, that Critias seemed to be suffering from a pig's itch: why else this urge to rub himself against Euthydemus, like piglets scraping against stones?

That, no doubt, is where Critias' hatred of Socrates began. He stored it up; and later, when he was one of the Thirty — the oligarchy that seized power in 404 — and sat with Charicles as the regime's official lawgiver, he remembered it. He wrote a law against teaching the art of words purely to injure Socrates. Having no other handle on him, he reached for the smear the public levels at philosophers in general, hoping to poison opinion against him — a charge quite unfounded in Socrates' case, so far as I ever heard from his own lips or learned about him from anyone else. But Critias' hatred soon showed itself plainly. At the time when the Thirty were putting citizens to death wholesale — respectable citizens — and pushing one man after another into crime, Socrates let fall a remark: "It would be strange enough for a herdsman who keeps thinning and starving his cattle to deny that he is a bad herdsman. But for a ruler of a city who keeps thinning and impoverishing the citizens to feel no shame, and deny that he is a bad ruler — that is stranger still." The remark was reported to the government. Critias and Charicles summoned Socrates, showed him the law, and forbade him to converse with the young.

Socrates asked whether he was permitted to put questions, in case anything in their commands was unclear to him.

"Certainly," the two of them said.

"Well then," said Socrates, "I am prepared to obey the laws. But to keep from breaking this one out of ignorance, I need instruction. Do you order us to keep away from the art of words because you think it makes speech correct, or because it makes speech incorrect? If correct, then apparently we must give up speaking correctly. If incorrect, then obviously we should be trying to mend our speech."

Charicles lost his temper. "Since you're so slow to understand, Socrates, we'll frame the order in language better suited to your intelligence: you are not to hold any conversation whatsoever with the young."

"Then to remove all ambiguity," said Socrates, "so there's no chance of my doing anything other than what you command — define for me up to what age a human being counts as young."

"For as long," Charicles answered, "as he is too young to sit on the Council — that is, before he has reached maturity of judgment. You will not converse with anyone under the age of thirty."

"And if I'm buying something," said Socrates, "and the seller happens to be under thirty, am I not even to ask what he's charging?"

"Oh, things of that sort, fine," said Charicles. "But you know perfectly well, Socrates, that you have a way of asking questions when you already know exactly how matters stand. None of that."

"And no answers either, I suppose," he said, "if some young man asks me something I happen to know — where Charicles lives, say, or where Critias is to be found?"

"Things of that kind, yes, of course," Charicles replied; and Critias put in: "But you'll have to be done with your shoemakers and carpenters and blacksmiths, Socrates. They must be worn quite down at the heel by now, the circulation you've given them."

"Then I must also give up what goes along with them," said Socrates, "— the just, the holy, and the rest?"

"Most assuredly," answered Charicles, "and cowherds in particular. Otherwise, take care you don't thin the herd yourself."

And there the secret was out. The remark about the cattle had reached their ears, and they could not forgive the man who made it.

That, perhaps, is enough to show what kind of intimacy existed between Critias and Socrates, and how they stood toward each other. But I will add this: no one gets an education from a teacher he does not like. And it cannot be said that Critias and Alcibiades kept Socrates' company because they liked him. From the very first, their eyes were fixed on one goal: leadership of the state. Even while they were with him, there were no debating partners they were more eager to take on than the leading politicians.

So there is a story told of Alcibiades — how, before he was twenty, he engaged his own guardian Pericles, then the first man of the state, in a discussion about laws.

"Tell me, Pericles," he said, "can you teach me what a law is?"

"Certainly I can," Pericles replied.

"Then please do. One so often hears men praised as 'law-abiding,' and it strikes me that nobody really earns that compliment who doesn't know what a law is."

"There's a ready answer to that, Alcibiades. You want to know what a law is? Laws are what the majority, meeting in assembly, approves and enacts, declaring what ought to be done and what ought not to be done."

"Enacts on the assumption that one ought to do what is good, or what is bad?"

"What is good, of course, young man — not what is bad."

"And if it is not the majority but a minority — as in an oligarchy — that meets and enacts rules of conduct, what are those?"

"Whatever the ruling power of the state, after deliberation, enacts as what must be done — that too is called law."

"Then if a tyrant, holding supreme power in the state, enacts rules of conduct for the citizens, are those enactments law?"

"Yes," said Pericles. "Whatever a tyrant enacts as head of the state — that also goes by the name of law."

"But force, Pericles, and lawlessness — how do we define those? Isn't it when the stronger compels the weaker to do what he pleases — not by persuasion, but by force?"

"So I would say," said Pericles.

"Then whatever a tyrant compels the citizens to do by decree, without persuading them — that is lawlessness?"

"You're right," said Pericles. "I take back what I said: measures a tyrant passes without persuading the citizens are not law."

"And measures a minority passes, not by persuading the majority but by the strength of its position — should we call that force, or not?"

"Anything one man compels another to do without persuasion," said Pericles, "whether he writes it into a decree or not, seems to me force rather than law."

"Then whatever the whole people, being stronger than the men of property, enacts without persuading them — that too would be force rather than law?"

"No doubt," answered Pericles — and he added: "At your age, Alcibiades, we were clever hands at this sort of thing ourselves. These are just the quibbles we used to sharpen our wits on — much as you're doing now, if I'm not mistaken."

To which Alcibiades replied, "Ah, Pericles — if only I could have known you when you were at your cleverest!"

Well then: as soon as they thought themselves superior to the politicians of the day, Critias and Alcibiades stopped coming to Socrates. His company held no attraction for them — and besides, when they did come, there was the annoyance of being cross-examined about their own shortcomings. Off they went to the affairs of state, the only reason they had ever come near him at all.

No — if you want to see Socrates' true companions, look at Crito, Chaerephon, Chaerecrates, Hermogenes, Simmias and Cebes, Phaedondes and the rest. They clung to him not to become star speakers in the Popular Assembly or the law courts, but with a nobler ambition: to become beautiful and good in soul, and so able to do their duty by household and family, by relatives and friends, by fellow citizens and the state at large. And of all these true followers, not one, in youth or in old age, ever did an evil deed — or was even accused of one.

"But for all that," the accuser insists, "Socrates taught sons to treat their fathers with contempt. He persuaded his young followers that he could make them wiser than their fathers, and he pointed out that the law allows a son to have his father confined if he proves him insane — evidence, he claimed, that it is lawful for the wiser to imprison the more ignorant."

What Socrates actually held was this: a man who imprisons another for nothing worse than ignorance could justly be imprisoned in his turn by those who know more than he does. Questions of that kind occupied him constantly — for instance, where exactly the line falls between madness and ignorance. His conclusion came to this: if a madman may be confined, as a practical matter, for his own good and his friends', then in all justice the man who does not know what he ought to know should be content to sit at the feet of those who do know, and learn.

"But it wasn't only fathers," the accuser goes on. "Socrates dishonored the rest of a man's kin in the eyes of his followers, saying that the sick man and the man with a lawsuit get no help from their relatives — one goes to his doctor, the other to his legal adviser. And listen to his language about friends: 'What good is their kindly feeling unless they can actually be of use to you? Good intentions by themselves are nothing; the only people worth honoring are those who know what is right and can explain it.' So by bringing the young to see him as supremely wise, and supremely gifted at making others wise, he so worked on his followers that in their eyes the rest of the world counted for nothing next to Socrates."

Now, I admit he said those things about fathers and the rest of a man's relatives. I can go further and add more. He said that when the soul — the only seat of intelligence in us — has gone out of a man, we carry out the body of even our nearest and dearest and bury it out of sight as fast as we can. He used to say that even in life, every man is ready to part with pieces of his own body — his dearest possession — wherever they are useless and unprofitable: he removes them himself, or lets someone else do it for him. Men cut their own nails and hair and corns; they let surgeons cut them and cauterize them, with pain and aching, and are so grateful for the service that they pay the surgeon a fee besides. And a man spits his saliva as far from him as he can, because inside the body it does him no good and some harm.

But in saying all this, his object was not to teach anyone to bury his father alive or carve himself to pieces. His point was that whatever lacks intelligence lacks worth; and so he urged his hearers to make themselves as sensible and as useful as they could — so that a man who wanted the esteem of a father, a brother, or anyone else would not sit back in careless self-satisfaction, trusting to mere kinship, but would work at being useful to the people whose esteem he wanted.

"But," the accuser continues, "Socrates carefully culled the most immoral passages from the famous poets and used them as evidence, teaching his companions to be criminals and tyrants." The line of Hesiod, for instance — "Work is no disgrace; it is idleness that is the disgrace" — the accuser says Socrates interpreted as the poet's command to shrink from no work, however wicked or shameful, but to do anything for gain.

Now, Socrates would have agreed entirely that it is a blessing and a benefit for a man to be a worker, and that a lazy do-nothing is a curse — that work is good and idleness an evil. But whom did he mean by workers? In his vocabulary, only those doing good work counted as workers at all. Dice-players, gamblers, and everyone else in a base and ruinous trade he called idle drones. Take it that way, and the line from Hesiod stands beyond reproach: "Work is no disgrace; only idleness is disgrace."

Then there was a passage from Homer that, the accuser tells us, was forever on his lips — the one about Odysseus during the rout: whenever he came upon a prince or a man of note, he would stand beside him and restrain him with gentle words — "Good sir, it isn't right for you to run like a coward. Stand fast yourself, and steady the rest of the men" — but any commoner he found shouting and breaking ranks, he struck with his staff and rebuked: "Sit still, wretch, and listen to your betters. You are weak and unwarlike, worth nothing in council or in war. We cannot all be kings here."

The accuser claims Socrates read these lines as the poet's approval for beating commoners and the poor. Socrates never said anything of the kind — by that reading, he would have been voting to have himself beaten. What he did say was that people who are useless in word and deed alike — incapable of helping the army or the city or the people itself in time of need — ought to be restrained, however rich they may be, and especially if they add insolence to their uselessness.

Socrates himself was the very opposite of all that. He was plainly a lover of the people — indeed, of all mankind. Though he had many devoted admirers, citizens and foreigners alike, he never charged anyone a fee for his company, but gave freely to all from the riches of his soul. Some of his hearers took the fragments they had received from him for nothing and sold them at high prices to everyone else; and they were no lovers of the people as he was, for they refused to talk with anyone who could not pay. In the eyes of the whole world, Socrates brought his city more honor and richer luster than Lichas, whose name is a byword, ever brought Sparta. Lichas made himself famous by feasting and entertaining the foreign visitors at the Gymnopaediae, Sparta's great festival; Socrates spent a lifetime pouring out his substance in the greatest of benefits, given to everyone who cared to receive them. He made those who kept his company better people, and sent them on their way rejoicing.

I can come to no other conclusion, then, but this: being so good a man, Socrates deserved honor from the state rather than death. And that, I would argue, is the strictly legal view of the case. What does the law demand? If a man is proved a thief, a clothes-stealer, a pickpocket, a housebreaker, a kidnapper, a robber of temples, the penalty is death. And of all men, no one stood further from those crimes than Socrates.

To the state he was never the cause of any harm — no disaster in war, no civil strife, no treason, no mischief of any kind. And his private life was as blameless as his public one. He never hurt a single soul, never deprived anyone of any good or inflicted any evil, and no one ever so much as accused him of doing so. Where, then, is his liability under the indictment? Far from refusing to recognize the gods, as the indictment claimed, he was conspicuous above all men for his service to heaven. Far from corrupting the young — the charge the prosecutor pressed hardest — he was known for laboring to free his companions from base desires, and to kindle in them a passion for that loveliest and most queenly of virtues, the one without which states and families crumble. Conducting himself so, did he not deserve high honor from Athens?
