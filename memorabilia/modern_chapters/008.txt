Book III

(Part 1 of 4)

Chapter 1

Men who set their hearts on honorable distinction got the same kind of help from Socrates: in each case he spurred them to work steadily at the thing they aimed for. The stories that follow will show what I mean.

He heard one day that Dionysodorus had arrived in Athens, advertising himself as a teacher of the whole art of generalship. Now, one of the young men in his company made no secret of wanting to be elected general, so Socrates said to him:

"It would be outrageous, wouldn't it, for a man who wants to be a general to throw away the slightest chance of learning the duties of the office? The city ought to fine and punish a man like that far more severely than the imposter who takes a contract to carve a statue without ever having learned sculpture. During a war the whole fortune of the state is placed in the general's hands, with all the danger that entails; enormous good or enormous harm follows, depending on whether he succeeds or bungles. So I put it to you, young man: wouldn't a candidate who works hard at getting elected but never bothers to learn the job richly deserve that fine?"

Arguments like this persuaded the young man to go and take the course. When he had finished it and come back, Socrates began to tease him.

"Look at our young friend, gentlemen. Homer says Agamemnon had a majestic bearing — doesn't he seem to move more majestically himself, now that he has studied generalship? A man who has learned the harp is a harpist even if he never touches the instrument; a man who has studied medicine is a doctor even if he never practices. In the same way our friend here is a general from this day forward and forever, whether or not he receives a single vote at the elections. The ignoramus without the science, on the other hand, is neither general nor doctor — not even if the whole world appoints him. But," he went on, turning to the young man, "since some of us may end up serving as captain or colonel under you, give us a taste of the science of war. Where did the professor begin his instruction in generalship? Tell us, please."

"Where he ended," the young man said. "He taught me tactics — tactics and nothing else."

"But surely," Socrates replied, "tactics are only a tiny fraction of generalship. A general must be able to furnish the equipment of war and keep his troops supplied; he must be inventive and full of practical resource; nothing can escape his eye or wear out his endurance; he must be shrewd and quick-witted, gentle and fierce at once, straightforward and cunning; a watchman and a thief; open-handed as a spendthrift one moment and tight-fisted as a miser the next, as generous in giving as he is grasping in getting; solid in defense and a daredevil in attack. These qualities and many more, whether they come by nature or by training, are what make a good general and master of war. Of course it's a fine thing to be a tactician too — there is all the difference in the world between an army properly arranged for battle and the same army in disorder. It's like building a house: stones, bricks, timber, and tiles tumbled in a heap are no use at all; but arrange them in order — the materials that won't rot or crumble, the stones and tiles, at the bottom and the top, and the bricks and timber in the middle, the way a builder does — and you end up with something valuable: a house."

"That's exactly the parallel, Socrates," said the youth. "In battle, too, the rule is to draw up your best men in the front and the rear, with the poorer ones in between, so the ones ahead can lead them and the ones behind can push them on."

"Very good — if the professor taught you to tell good men from bad. If he didn't, what use was the lesson? If someone told you to stack coins with the best coins at the top and bottom and the worst in the middle, that would hardly help you unless you'd first been taught to tell genuine coin from counterfeit."

"Well, no," said the youth, "he certainly didn't teach us that. We'd have to distinguish the good from the bad ourselves."

"Then shall we see how we might best avoid blundering between them?"

"I'm ready," he replied.

"Well then! Suppose we were raiders, and the job was to carry off a load of silver. Would we be arranging our forces rightly if we put the greediest men in the vanguard?"

"I'd say so."

"And when there's danger to be faced? Should the vanguard then be the men hungriest for honor?"

"Those are the ones, at any rate, who will face danger for the sake of praise and glory. And fortunately such men don't hide themselves in corners — they shine out everywhere, and they're easy to spot."

"But tell me, did he teach you how to draw up troops in general, or where and how to apply each particular formation?"

"Nothing of the kind."

"And yet there are countless situations in which the same order of march or battle simply won't do."

"I assure you, he drew none of those fine distinctions."

"He didn't, did he? Good heavens! Then go back to him and ply him with questions. If he really has the science, and has any shame left in him, he'll blush to have taken your money and sent you away empty-handed."

Chapter 2

Another time he fell in with a man who had been elected general and master of war, and greeted him with this:

"Why do you suppose Homer calls Agamemnon 'shepherd of the people'? Could it be to show that just as a shepherd must care for his sheep — keep them safe, see they have everything they need, and secure the purpose they're raised for — so a general must care for his soldiers: keep them safe, keep them supplied, and achieve the purpose they soldier for? And that purpose is to get the mastery of the enemy and so add to their own good fortune and happiness. Or tell me, why does Homer praise Agamemnon by calling him 'both a good king and a mighty warrior'? Doesn't 'mighty warrior' mean not merely a man who stands and fights bravely himself, but one who inspires the same fighting spirit in his whole army? And 'good king' — not merely a man who defends his own life gallantly, but one who is the source of happiness for everyone he rules? A man isn't chosen king so that he can take good care of himself, however nobly; he's chosen so that the people who chose him may find happiness through him. And why do men go to war at all, if not to make life better? Why do they elect generals, if not to have guides toward that goal? So the man who takes on the office is bound to provide, for the people who elected him, the very thing they elected him to provide. It would be hard to find a nobler ambition than that — or anything more shameful than its opposite."

That was how he handled the question, what is the virtue of a good leader? — and by stripping away everything superficial, he laid bare the kernel of the matter: the job of every leader is to make the people he leads happy.

Chapter 3

The following conversation I can also vouch for — with a young man who had just been elected hipparch, commander of the cavalry.

"Can you tell us, young man, what made you want the cavalry command?" Socrates asked. "What were you after? Surely not just the honor of riding at the head of the knights — even the mounted archers get that; they ride out in front of the commanders themselves."

"You're right."

"Nor just to be talked about, since even a madman can boast of that sort of fame."

"Right again."

"Then is this the explanation — you think you can improve the cavalry? Your aim is to hand it over to the state in better condition than you found it, and if the cavalry is ever called out, to do Athens some good at its head?"

"Exactly."

"And a noble ambition it is, upon my word — if you can pull it off. The command you've been given covers horses and riders, doesn't it?"

"It does."

"Come then, tell us first: how do you propose to improve the horses?"

"Ah, I don't think that will be any part of my business. Each trooper is personally responsible for the condition of his own horse."

"But suppose that when they muster, you find some have brought animals with bad feet or bad legs or some other weakness; others, beasts so underfed they can't keep up on the march; others, brutes so badly broken they won't hold their place in the ranks; and others, such desperate plungers they can't be put in the ranks at all. What becomes of your cavalry then? How will you charge at the head of a troop like that and win glory for the state?"

"You're right. I'll look after the horses as well as I possibly can."

"And won't you take the men in hand too?"

"I will."

"The first thing will be to make them expert at mounting their horses?"

"Certainly — that way, if a man is thrown, he'll have a better chance of saving himself."

"And when it comes to the hazard of a real engagement, what then? Will you order the enemy down onto the sandy ground where you're used to drilling, or try beforehand to train your men on the kind of ground where battles are actually fought?"

"The second would be better, no doubt."

"And will you make it part of your duty to see that as many of your men as possible can aim and shoot from horseback?"

"That would be better too, certainly."

"And have you thought about how to whet your troopers' courage — how to kindle in them a fighting fury against the enemy? Those are the things that make stout hearts stouter."

"If I haven't until now, I'll try to make up for lost time."

"And have you troubled your head at all about how to make your men obey you? Because without that, all your horses and troopers, however brave and strong, are worth nothing."

"That's true. But what's the best way to bring them to it, Socrates?"

"I suppose you know that in any business whatever, people most willingly follow the lead of whoever they consider the expert. In sickness, they obey the man they think the best doctor; on a voyage, the best pilot; on a farm, the best farmer — and so on."

"Yes, certainly."

"Then in cavalry matters too, we may reasonably expect that the man who is seen to know his business best will command the readiest obedience."

"So if I can show my troopers that I'm better than any of them, will that be enough to win their obedience?"

"Yes — if along with it you teach them that obeying you means more glory and surer safety for themselves."

"And how am I to teach them that?"

"Upon my word! How are you to teach them that? Far more easily, I should think, than if you had to teach them that bad things are better than good ones, and more profitable besides."

"You mean that a cavalry commander, on top of his other qualifications, needs command of speech and argument?"

"Did you imagine he was to command in silence? Has it never occurred to you that everything custom requires us to learn — the noblest things we know, the very knowledge that teaches us how to live — we learned through speech and reason? That whatever other noble learning a man acquires, he acquires it by that same reason? That the best teachers are the ones with the freest command of thought and language, and the men who best understand the most serious matters are the most brilliant speakers about them? Or notice this: whenever this city of ours fits out a chorus — like the one it sends to Delos — nothing from any other quarter of the world can compete with it, and no other state can gather such a flower of manhood as Athens."

"That's true."

"And yet it isn't sweetness of voice that sets the Athenians apart from everyone else, or size or strength of body. It's ambition — the love of honor, which more than anything puts a keen edge on the spirit in pursuit of whatever is beautiful and held in high esteem."

"That's true too."

"Then don't you think that if a man devoted himself to our cavalry the same way, we would leave the rest of the world far behind — in the furnishing of arms and horses, in discipline of array, in readiness to face danger against the enemy — if only our men could be persuaded that doing so would bring them honor and distinction?"

"It stands to reason."

"Then don't hesitate. Try to guide your men onto that road. You'll profit by it yourself, and so, through you, will your fellow citizens."

"I will certainly try," he answered.

Chapter 4

Another time, seeing Nicomachides on his way back from the elections, he asked him, "Who were elected generals, Nicomachides?"

"Isn't it just like them, these Athenians?" he said. "Just like them, I tell you! They don't elect me — me, who have worn myself out with military service ever since my name first went on the muster-roll, as captain, as colonel, with all these wounds from the enemy to show for it" — and here, suiting the action to the word, he bared his arms and displayed the scars of old wounds — "no, they pass over me and elect Antisthenes! A man who has never served a day in the infantry, who never did one brilliant thing in the cavalry that I ever heard of, and who has no science at all, as far as I can tell, except piling up money."

"Well, isn't that one point in his favor?" Socrates returned. "He ought to be able to keep the troops supplied."

"Merchants are good at collecting stores," said Nicomachides. "It doesn't follow that a merchant can command an army."

"But Antisthenes hates to lose," Socrates rejoined. "He insists on winning, and that's a very necessary quality in a general. Haven't you noticed that every time he has sponsored a chorus, that chorus has won?"

"Good heavens, yes — but there's a wide difference between standing at the head of a band of singers and dancers and standing at the head of a troop of soldiers."

"And yet Antisthenes has no practical skill in singing or in training a chorus — he simply had the knack of finding the best experts in both."

"So on campaign, by the same reasoning, he'll find experts too — some to marshal the troops for him and others to fight his battles?"

"Just so. And if in military matters he shows the same skill at picking out the best men that he has shown in chorus matters, he'll very likely carry off the victory there as well. He spent freely to win a chorus competition with a single tribe behind him; he'll presumably spend more freely to win a war with the whole state behind him."

"Do you really mean, Socrates, that the same man who manages a good chorus can serve as commander-in-chief?"

"I mean this: whatever a man is put in charge of — a household, a city, an army — if he knows what it needs and has the skill to provide it, he will be a good chief and director of it."

"Honestly, Socrates," said Nicomachides, "I never expected to hear you say that a good housekeeper and manager of an estate would make a good general."

"Come then, let's examine the duties of each and settle whether they're the same or different."

"Let's."

"Well, isn't it a duty common to both to make the people under them prompt to obey orders?"

"Certainly."

"And to assign each task to the people best qualified to perform it?"

"That belongs to both alike," he answered.

"And punishing the bad and rewarding the good — that belongs to both too, I think?"

"Decidedly."

"And winning the goodwill of their subordinates — surely that's the noble ambition of both?"

"That too."

"And is it in the interest of both alike to attract supporters and allies?"

"Beyond a doubt."

"And doesn't it closely concern them both to be good guardians of what's in their charge?"

"Very much so."

"Then it concerns them both equally to be painstaking, and to spare no labor in everything they do?"

"Yes — all these duties belong to both alike. But the parallel ends when it comes to actual fighting."

"Yet both are certain to meet with enemies?"

"No doubt about that."

"And isn't it in the interest of both to get the upper hand of those enemies?"

"Certainly. But you're leaving something out: what good will organization and the art of management be when it comes to actual fighting?"

"That is exactly where they'll be of most use," said Socrates. "The good manager knows that nothing pays so well as victory in battle, and nothing is so ruinous and expensive as defeat. So he will eagerly seek out and provide everything that makes for victory, and painstakingly discover and guard against everything that leads to defeat. When he sees his forces ready and ripe for victory, he will fight with all his energy — and, just as important, while they are not yet ready, he will be careful not to fight. Don't look down on men with a genius for management, Nicomachides. Handling private business differs from handling the business of the state only in scale. In every other respect the parallel holds, and above all in this: neither can be done without human beings, and the human beings who conduct private affairs and public affairs are of one and the same type. The men who prosper in either sphere are those who know the secret of dealing with people; the men who lack it strike a false note in both."
