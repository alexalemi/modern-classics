Book II

(Part 1 of 4)

Chapter 1

If talk like that steered his listeners away from fakery and false pretenses, then I am just as sure that talk like the following pushed his companions to practice self-control and endurance: self-control in eating, drinking, sleep, and sexual desire; endurance of cold, heat, hard work, and pain. He had noticed that one of his companions had gotten far too loose with himself in all these areas, so he took the question straight to him.

"Tell me, Aristippus," said Socrates, "suppose two boys were handed over to you to educate — one to be trained for ruling, the other with no claim to rule anything at all. How would you bring each of them up? Shall we start our inquiry at the very beginning, with the basic matter of food?"

"Food is certainly the place to start," said Aristippus. "It's a first necessity. No man alive can do without it."

"Then we can expect the urge to reach for food to show up in both boys when mealtime comes around?"

"We can."

"So which of the two should be trained to put urgent business first and his belly second — of his own free will?"

"The one being trained to rule, obviously, unless we want the state's affairs neglected while he's in charge."

"And the same boy must also be given the strength to hold out against thirst when the craving hits him?"

"Certainly he must."

"And which of the two gets the self-control over sleep — the discipline to stay up late, rise early, or keep watch all night if the need arises?"

"That endurance goes to the same one."

"And self-control in sexual matters, strong enough that no desire ever keeps him from his duty — which of them claims that?"

"The same one again."

"And which of the two receives, as a further gift, the willingness to face hard work head-on rather than turn and run from it?"

"That too belongs by rights to the one being trained to rule."

"And which of them is the fitter candidate for learning every skill that helps in mastering an opponent?"

"Our future ruler, without question. Without that kind of knowledge, everything else he can do is simply wasted."

"And won't a man educated this way be harder to trap than other creatures? You know how some animals get caught — through their own greed. Some of them are naturally shy, but appetite pulls them toward the bait anyway, and they're hooked. Others are snared just the same way with drink."

"Undoubtedly."

"And others fall victim to lust — quails, for instance, or partridges, which rush toward the hen's call, hot with desire and dreaming of pleasure, lose all power to calculate the danger, and fly straight into the hunter's nets."

Aristippus agreed.

"And isn't it a disgraceful thing for a man to be caught exactly like the silliest bird or beast? Think of the adulterer who sneaks into the women's quarters of another man's house knowing precisely what he risks — the penalties the law threatens, the trap he might be caught in, the humiliation that follows if he is. With all those horrors hanging over the adulterer's head, and with so many honest ways available to relieve his desire safely, to drive headlong onto the rocks anyway — what do we call that but madness? A man who behaves that way must surely be possessed by some evil spirit?"

"So it seems to me."

"And doesn't it strike you as strangely careless that, while most of life's essential business — war, farming, more than half of everything else — has to be carried on under the open sky, most men never train themselves at all to handle cold and heat?"

Aristippus agreed again.

"And don't you agree that the man who is going to rule must train himself to bear all these things lightly?"

"Absolutely."

"So if we rank the people with self-control in all these matters as fit to rule, shouldn't we rank the people incapable of it as having no claim to rule whatsoever?"

"Agreed."

"Well then — since you know where each of the two classes stands, has it ever occurred to you to ask which of them you're entitled to belong to?"

"It has," replied Aristippus, "and I don't dream for a moment of placing myself among those who want to rule. Providing for your own private needs is serious work as it is; to my mind only a fool refuses to be content with that and saddles himself with supplying a whole community with whatever it happens to want on top of it. For a man to give up so much of his own enjoyment in order to stand at the head of a state — and then, if he fails to deliver every last item on that state's wish list, to be held criminally accountable for it — that seems to me the very height of folly. Why, states expect to treat their rulers exactly the way I treat my household slaves. I expect my servants to keep me generously supplied with everything I need, and never to lay a finger on any of it themselves. And that's just how states see the ruler's job: provide them with every good thing imaginable, and keep his own hands off the whole pile. So here's my offer. If anyone wants a heap of trouble for himself and the privilege of being a nuisance to everyone else, I'll educate him along your lines and enroll him among the ones fit to rule. As for me, put me down with the people who want to spend their days as easily and pleasantly as possible."

"Then shall we turn at this point," said Socrates, "and ask which of the two actually lives more pleasantly — the rulers or the ruled?"

"By all means, let's."

"Start with the nations and peoples we know. In Asia, the Persians rule, while the Syrians, Phrygians, and Lydians are ruled. In Europe, the Scythians rule and the Maeotians are ruled. In Africa, the Carthaginians rule and the Libyans are ruled. Which of each pair leads the happier life, in your opinion? Or come closer to home — you're a Greek yourself. Among the Greeks, who do you think is better off, the dominant cities or the subject ones?"

"Now hold on," exclaimed Aristippus. "Understand me: I'm just as far from enrolling myself among the slaves. There is, I take it, a middle road between the two, and that's the one I intend to walk — avoiding rule and slavery alike, straight through freedom, the high road that leads to happiness."

"If only your road could avoid human beings," said Socrates, "the way it avoids rule and slavery, there might be something in what you say. But you live in the middle of human beings. If you refuse to rule and refuse to be ruled, and won't willingly pay court to the men who do rule, I think you can see that the strong know very well how to make the weak sit and weep for it — in public and in private — and to use them as slaves. Surely you've noticed the standard pattern: one group of people sows and plants, and another group marches in, cuts their grain, fells their fruit trees, and lays siege to them from every side, because the weaker party wouldn't pay proper respect — until at last they're persuaded to accept slavery rather than fight a war against stronger men. And in private life the same, as you'll admit: the bold and powerful are known to reduce the helpless and timid to servitude, and to live very comfortably off their victims."

"Ah, but I have a simple remedy against all such misfortunes," said Aristippus. "I don't lock myself into any single community. I roam the whole world as a foreigner."

"Now that," said Socrates, "is a masterstroke, I must say. Ever since Sinis and Sciron and Procrustes died, of course, nobody has laid a hand on a traveler. And yet — think about it — the men who live in their own states today still pass laws to protect themselves against wrongdoing. Beyond their families and personal connections they gather crowds of friends around them; they ring their cities with walls and battlements; they collect weapons to fend off evildoers; and to make security doubly sure, they line up allies from foreign states as well. With all that defensive machinery in place, these free citizens still occasionally fall victim to injustice. But you — with none of those protections; you, who spend half your days on the open roads, where more crimes are committed than anywhere else; you, who in whatever city you enter rank below the least of its citizens; you, exactly the sort of person that anyone bent on mischief singles out for attack — you, on the strength of a foreigner's passport, expect to be exempt from harm? Is that your comfort? Will the city authorities send a herald around on your behalf — 'The person of this man Aristippus is secure; let his comings and goings be free from danger'? Or does your real confidence rest on something else — the knowledge that you'd make the kind of slave no master would care to keep? Because who would want a man in his house who refuses to work and has such expensive tastes? But let's stop and consider that very point: how do masters deal with a slave of that sort? Don't they discipline his appetites with starvation? Block his thieving with bars and bolts wherever there's anything to steal? Stop him from running off with chains and confinement? Drive the laziness out of him with the whip? Isn't that how it goes? Or how do you proceed when you find those tendencies in one of your own servants?"

"I punish him with every misery there is," said Aristippus, "until I force him to serve me properly. But Socrates — back to your pupil, the one educated in the royal art of ruling, which you apparently take to be happiness. How exactly is he better off than the people who suffer all these hardships against their will? He'll go hungry and thirsty, shiver with cold, lie awake through the night, and take every other punishment on the list — voluntarily. For my part, I can't see what difference it makes to the bare back that takes the whipping whether the whipping was requested or not. Nor, for that matter, does it make any difference to my body in general — provided it's my body — whether the whole army of such evils besieges me with my consent or without it. Except for one thing: the folly that comes attached to suffering by choice."

"Really, Aristippus?" said Socrates. "Do you see no difference at all between voluntary and involuntary hardship? The man who goes hungry by choice can eat whenever he likes, and the man who goes thirsty of his own free will can drink, and so on down the whole list — while the man who suffers those things under compulsion can't stop suffering when he feels like it. Besides, the man who takes hardship on willingly faces his troubles cheerfully, because hope keeps him afloat — the way a hunter chasing wild game finds the work a pleasure, because he hopes to make the catch. And game is a cheap prize for all that labor. What shall we say, then, of the men who work to win themselves good friends, or to defeat their enemies, or to gain the strength of body and soul to manage their households well, to help their friends and serve the country that raised them? Won't they work toward those high goals gladly, and live with real satisfaction — carried along not only by their own good conscience but by the praise and admiration of everyone around them? And one thing more: lazy habits and the fleeting pleasures of the moment can never produce a fit body, as the athletic trainers tell us, and they plant no knowledge worth having in the soul. But disciplined effort in pursuit of fine and noble ends brings men through, in the end, to the goal — so the good men tell us. Hesiod says it somewhere:

"Wickedness you can gather easily and in bulk: the road is smooth, and she lives close by. But in front of virtue the immortal gods have set toil and sweat. The path to her is long and steep, and rough at the start — yet when you reach the summit, then the path grows easy, hard as it was.

"And Epicharmus gives the same testimony when he says:

"The gods sell us every good thing at the price of our labors.

"And in another place he cries:

"Don't set your heart on the soft things, fool, or you'll end up with the hard ones.

"And the wise Prodicus says much the same about virtue in that composition of his about Heracles — the one he has recited to such crowds. As near as I can remember it, it goes like this.

"When Heracles was passing from boyhood into the bloom of youth — the age when the young, standing on the edge of independence, first show plainly whether they will take the path of virtue or the path of vice — he went out to a quiet place and sat down, debating with himself which of the two roads he should take. And as he sat there thinking, two women of great stature seemed to approach him. One was beautiful to look at and naturally noble: her body graced by purity, her eyes by modesty; her bearing was sober, and her dress was white. The other was fed soft and fleshy; her skin was made up to look whiter and rosier than it really was, and her figure padded to look taller than nature had made her. Her eyes were wide open and staring, and her dress was cut to show off every ripening charm. She kept inspecting herself, and glancing around to see whether anyone else had noticed her, and looking back again and again at her own shadow.

"When the two came near Heracles, the first kept walking toward him at the same even pace, but the other, desperate to get there ahead of her, ran up to the young man and cried: 'I can see, Heracles, that you're torn about which road through life to choose. Make me your friend, and I will lead you down the pleasantest and easiest road of all. Here is my promise: you will taste every one of life's sweets and escape every one of its bitters. First of all, you won't trouble your head with wars or responsibilities. Your only study will be what food or drink might please your palate; what sight or sound might delight you; what scent, what touch; which lover's company would thrill you most; how to pillow yourself in the softest sleep; and how to come by every single one of these pleasures with the least possible effort. And if ever the suspicion creeps in that the stream of good things might one day run dry, don't be afraid — I won't lead you anywhere you'd have to restock it by working and suffering in body and soul. No: others will do the laboring, and you will harvest the fruit of their labor, and you'll keep your hands off nothing that can turn you a profit. To all my companions I give license to help themselves from every side.'

"When Heracles heard that, he asked, 'Lady, what is your name?' And she said, 'My friends call me Happiness. The people who hate me have their own nicknames for me — Vice and Mischief.'

"And just then the other woman came forward and spoke. 'I too have come to you, Heracles. I know your parents, and I have watched your nature through your upbringing; and so I have good hope that if you take the road that leads to me, you will make yourself a great doer of fine and noble deeds, and that I myself will stand higher in honor because of you, made brighter by the shine of what you accomplish. I will not trick you with little preludes of pleasure. I will tell you the truth about how the gods have actually arranged the world. Of all that is good and admirable, the gods give nothing to men without effort and discipline. If you want the gods' favor, you must serve the gods. If you want to be loved by your friends, you must do your friends good. If you desire honor from a city, you must be useful to that city. If you expect all of Greece to admire your excellence, you must try to do Greece some good. If you want the earth to yield you crops in abundance, you must tend the earth. If you mean to grow rich from flocks and herds, you must care for the flocks and herds. If your ambition is to rise through war — to have the power to free your friends and master your enemies — you must learn the arts of war from those who know them, and practice using them in the field. And if you want a body that is strong and capable, you must teach your body to obey your mind, and train it with work and sweat.'

"At this point, as Prodicus tells it, Vice broke in. 'Do you see, Heracles, how long and hard the road is that this woman would march you down to reach her festivities? I will take you to happiness by a short and easy road.'

"Then Virtue spoke. 'You wretched creature — what good thing do you have? What do you know of pleasure, when you won't lift hand or foot to earn it? You don't even wait for desire to arrive: you're stuffed before you're hungry and drunk before you're thirsty. To make eating possible at all you have to invent armies of cooks and confectioners; to make drinking possible you lay down the costliest wines and run around hunting for ice in the middle of summer. To help you sleep, soft blankets aren't enough — you need feather beds and rockers to rock you, because your drowsiness comes not from work but from having absolutely nothing to do. Even the natural appetite of love you force before its time, by every trick you can invent, using men as if they were women. That is how you educate your friends: dragging them through outrages by night and sleeping them through the most precious hours of the day. You are immortal, but the gods have cast you out of their company, and good men despise you. The sweetest sound of all — the sound of your own praise — you have never heard; and the fairest of all sights you have never seen, for you have never once beheld a fine deed done by your own hand. Who would believe a word you say? Who would give you anything you needed? What sane man would dare to join your reveling crew? Your followers are a sorry sight: while they're young their bodies are feeble, and when they're old their minds are foolish. Through their youth they're kept sleek and idle at no cost of effort; through old age they drag themselves along, withered and wretched — ashamed of everything they've done, and crushed by everything still left to do. They raced through their pleasures in their youth and stored up the bitterness for the end. But my company is with the gods, and with the good among men; no fine deed, divine or human, is done without me. I am honored first among the gods, and honored among the men whose right it is to honor me: a beloved fellow-worker to craftsmen, a faithful guardian of house and land to owners, a kindly helper to servants, a good partner in the labors of peace, a steadfast ally in the work of war, and the best of companions in friendship. My friends enjoy their food and drink with real pleasure and no fuss, because they wait until they want it; and sleep comes sweeter to them than to the idle. They aren't bitter about giving it up, either, and they never let it crowd out their duty. Among my people, the young delight in the praise of their elders and the old take pride in the honor of the young; they remember their past deeds with pleasure and are glad to do today's work well. Because of me they are dear to the gods, loved by their friends, and honored by their countries. And when the appointed end comes, they do not lie forgotten and dishonored — their memory blooms on, and their praise is sung forever. Toils like these, Heracles, child of noble parents, are yours to take up; and if you endure them, the most blessed happiness there is will belong to you.'

"That, Aristippus, is a rough sketch of how Prodicus tells the education of Heracles by Virtue — though he dressed his thoughts, I admit, in far more magnificent language than I've managed just now. It might be worth your while to take some of this to heart, and to give a little thought to the part of your life that still lies ahead."
