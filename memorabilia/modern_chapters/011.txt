Book III

(Part 4 of 4)

Chapter 11

There was in the city at that time a beautiful woman named Theodota — the kind of woman who kept company with any admirer who could win her favor. One of the group happened to mention her, saying her beauty was beyond words. "Painters flock to her to paint her portrait," he added, "and she shows them as much of that beauty as decency allows." "Then we had better go and see her," said Socrates, "since you clearly can't grasp what's 'beyond words' by listening to words." "Then follow me right now," said the man who had brought her up, and off they went to visit Theodota. They found her posing for a painter, and stood watching. When the painter had finished, Socrates spoke up.

"Gentlemen, should we be thanking Theodota for showing us her beauty, or should she be thanking us for coming to look? Doesn't it work like this — if the showing profits her more, the debt is hers; but if the looking benefits us more, then we owe her."

Someone said that was a fair way to put it.

"Well then," he went on, "she gains our praise right now, and once we've spread her fame around, she'll gain even more. But we — the immediate effect on us is a longing to touch what we've seen; we'll leave with a sting in us, and once we're gone we'll ache with wanting. So it seems we're the ones doing her a service, and she's the one receiving our attentions."

"Goodness," said Theodota, "if that's how it works, then I'm the one in your debt for the viewing."

By now Socrates had noticed that she was expensively dressed, that her mother was there with her in clothes and attendance well beyond the ordinary, that her maids were many and good-looking and anything but neglected, and that the whole house was lavishly furnished. So he asked, "Tell me, Theodota, do you own a farm in the country?"

"Not I."

"A house, then, that brings in income?"

"No, no house either."

"Workshops, perhaps, with laborers?"

"No, no workshops."

"Then where does your living come from?"

"My friends are my living," she said, "when they choose to be kind to me."

"By heaven, Theodota, that's a fine property — a flock of friends is worth far more than a flock of sheep or goats or cattle. But tell me: do you leave it to luck whether a friend lands on your hand like a stray fly, or do you use some art of your own to draw them in?"

"How would I ever come up with an art for that?"

"Far more naturally than the spiders do, I promise you. You know how they hunt for their living — they weave those gossamer webs, and whatever tumbles in becomes dinner."

"So you're advising me to weave some kind of net?"

"Come now — you don't imagine you'll snare the noblest game there is, a lover, with no more craft than that? Look how many tricks it takes to bag a mere hare. Hares feed at night, so the hunter needs night dogs. At daybreak they scatter, so he needs a second pack of scent-hounds to work out which trail they took from their feeding ground to their beds. They're so fast they vanish in the open, so he needs yet another pack, fast dogs, to run them down by their tracks. And since some will slip past even those, he sets nets across the escape paths at the end of it all, so they tumble into the mesh and are caught."

"And what contraption like that is supposed to catch me my lovers?"

"Well — what if, instead of a dog, you had a man who would hunt up a wealthy admirer of beauty for you, find his haunts, and then scheme to drive him into your nets?"

"Nets? What nets do I have?"

"One, certainly — and a close-woven one at that: your own person. And inside it sits a soul that teaches you which looks please and which words charm; that tells you to welcome real devotion with a smile and shut the door on the insolent; to hurry to a friend's bedside when he's sick, and to share his joy openly when he's done something fine; and when a man truly cares for you, to give yourself to him heart and soul. As for loving — I'm sure you know how to love not just softly but devotedly, and to convince your friends of your affection not with pretty phrases but with acts."

"Honestly," said Theodota, "I use none of these devices."

"And yet it makes all the difference to approach a human being in the right and natural way. Force certainly won't catch a friend or keep one; kindness and pleasure are the only snares for that elusive animal, man — the only things that hold him fast."

"You're right."

"So, first: ask of your admirers only what they can grant without regretting it. Then repay them in the same spirit — dispensing your favors with the same economy. That's how you'll make friends whose love lasts longest and whose generosity never runs dry. And you'll please them best if you give only when they're hungry for the gift. Notice how even the sweetest food, offered before a man wants it, seems flat — and to a man already full, it's nearly sickening. But create hunger, and even plain fare tastes like honey."

"Then how do I create this hunger in my friends?"

"First, never offer or even hint at your delicacies while they're sated — wait until the fullness has passed and the want makes itself felt. Even then, hint at it only faintly: gracious conversation, the look of someone who would like to be kind — and then the vision withdraws — until the want is at its sharpest. The same gifts are worth far more at the moment of real desire than a moment before it."

"Oh, Socrates," said Theodota, "why aren't you here at my side, my partner in the hunt, helping me catch my friends and lovers?"

"I will be — if you can persuade me to come."

"And how do I persuade you?"

"You'll find a way," he said, "if you really need me."

"Then come visit me. Come often."

And Socrates, poking fun at his own lack of paying occupations, said, "Ah, Theodota, leisure is not a commodity I deal in much. I have a hundred affairs of my own, private and public, to keep me busy — and then there are my girlfriends, my dear friends, who won't leave me alone day or night, forever studying love-charms and incantations at my lips."

"You really know about those things, Socrates?"

"Of course. Why else do you suppose Apollodorus here and Antisthenes never leave my side? Why have Cebes and Simmias come all the way from Thebes to stay with me? Believe me, that doesn't happen without a good many love-charms and incantations and magic wheels."

"Then lend me your magic wheel — I'll spin it first for you."

"Ah, but I don't want to be drawn to you," he said. "I want you to come to me."

"Then I'll come. Only — will you be at home to me?"

"Yes, I'll welcome you — unless someone dearer still has me engaged, and I have to be 'not at home.'"

Chapter 12

Noticing that one of his companions, a young man named Epigenes, was in poor physical condition, he said, "You have the body of a man who never trains, Epigenes."

"Well, I'm a private citizen, not an athlete in training."

"You are exactly as much in training," said Socrates, "as any man who ever competed at Olympia — unless you consider the contest for life itself, the one the Athenians will hold against their enemies whenever the day comes, a small matter. And it's no small number of men who die in the dangers of war because their bodies failed them, or who save their lives shamefully. Many are taken prisoner for the same reason, and if that's their luck, spend the rest of their days in slavery; or they land in desperate straits, pay out ransom worth more than everything they own, and drag through the rest of life destitute of bare necessities. Many earn a coward's reputation when the real culprit was an unfit body. Do you shrug off these penalties of bad condition? Do you think you'd bear them lightly? I imagine what a man goes through to keep himself fit is far lighter — far pleasanter, even — by comparison. Or is it your position that bad condition is healthier and generally more useful than good? Do you sneer at everything a sound body brings? Because everything that follows from fitness is the opposite of what follows from neglect. The fit man is healthy and strong. Many a man has come through the ordeal of war safely on that account alone, and walked through all its horrors with his dignity intact. Many have rescued friends with nothing else to lean on, or done real service to their country and been judged worthy of gratitude for it — winning great reputation and the highest honors the state can give, living out the rest of their lives the happier and the brighter for it, and leaving their children a better start in life.

"Just because our city doesn't require military training in public is no reason to neglect it in private — it's a reason to work at it all the harder. You can be sure there is no contest of any kind, and no undertaking whatsoever, in which you'll be worse off for having your body well prepared. Everything men do, the body is a help in doing. And in every use we make of it, it matters enormously that it be in the best possible condition. Even where you'd think the body counted least — in thinking — who doesn't know the terrible stumbles men make because their health is bad? Forgetfulness, despondency, irritability, madness — they take advantage of poor condition to assault the mind so violently that they can drive out everything a man knows. But the man in good bodily shape has ample security. He runs no such risk of breaking down; his fitness works toward the very opposite of what neglect produces — and what wouldn't a sensible man endure for results like those? Besides, it's a disgrace for a man to grow old through sheer neglect before ever seeing what he might have become with his body at its full strength and beauty. And that sight is denied to the man who neglects himself — for it does not come of its own accord."

Chapter 13

Once, when a man was furious because he had said good day to a passer-by and gotten no greeting back, Socrates said, "That's ridiculous. If you'd run into a man with a wretched body, you wouldn't be angry — but you stumble on a soul in a somewhat boorish state, and that offends you."

To another man who complained that he took no pleasure in eating, he said, "Acumenus has a good prescription for that." "What is it?" the man asked. "Stop eating," he said. "Once you do, life will be pleasanter, cheaper, and healthier."

Someone else grumbled that the drinking-water in his house was warm. "Then when you want a warm bath," said Socrates, "it will be waiting for you."

"But it's too cold for bathing," said the man.

"Do your servants seem to mind drinking it or washing in it?"

"Quite the opposite — I've often marveled how contentedly they use it for both."

"Which is warmer to drink — the water in your house or the water in the temple of Asclepius?"

"The temple of Asclepius."

"And which is colder to bathe in — yours or the water in the cave of Amphiaraus?"

"The cave of Amphiaraus."

"Then consider this: you're in danger of being harder to please than a servant or an invalid."

A man was giving the slave who attended him a savage whipping, and when Socrates asked why he was so angry with his own servant, he said the fellow was lazy and gluttonous, a good-for-nothing who loved money and hated work. "And has it ever occurred to you to consider," said Socrates, "which of the two more deserves the whipping — the master or the man?"

When someone was dreading the journey to Olympia, he said, "Why be afraid of the distance? At home you spend nearly the whole day walking around. On the way to Olympia you'll take a walk and have breakfast, take another walk and have dinner, and go to bed. Don't you see that five or six days' worth of your usual walks, laid end to end, will stretch from Athens to Olympia? I'd advise you to set out a day too early rather than a day too late. Being forced to stretch a day's journey past a reasonable length is a misery; taking one day more than you need is pure ease. Better to hurry at the start than on the road."

When another man said he was completely worn out from a long journey, Socrates asked whether he'd had baggage to carry.

"Not I," said the man. "Only my cloak."

"Were you traveling alone, or was your servant with you?"

"I had my servant."

"Empty-handed, or carrying something?"

"Carrying my bedding and the rest of the baggage, of course."

"And how did he come through the journey?"

"Better than I did, I'd say."

"Well then — suppose you had had to carry his load. How would you have fared?"

"Badly. Or rather, I couldn't have carried it at all."

"What an admission! To be capable of so much less work than a slave boy — does that sound to you like a man in good condition?"

Chapter 14

When people dined together and some brought little food while others brought a great deal, Socrates would tell the servant either to put the small contributions into the common stock or to serve a share of them around to everyone. The men with the grand provisions were then ashamed not to take their share of the common stock — and ashamed, in turn, not to throw their own supplies in as well. So in went the grand provisions with the rest; and since they now got no more than the men who brought little, they soon stopped spending money on delicacies.

At one supper Socrates noticed that a member of the party had pushed aside his bread and was working on the delicacies alone. The conversation happened to be about names — what actions each word properly applies to — so Socrates put it to the company: "Can we say why we call a man a 'relish-eater'? What exactly does he do to earn the name? Everyone adds a relish to his bread when he can get one, but surely that alone doesn't make him a relish-eater — does it?"

"Hardly," said one of the company.

"Well then," said Socrates, "what about a man who eats the relish by itself, no bread at all — not for training, but for pleasure? Is he a relish-eater or not?"

"If he isn't, it's hard to say who is," came the answer. And someone else put in, "What about the man who eats a little bread with a great deal of relish?"

"He's earned the title fairly too, I'd say," said Socrates. "And while everyone else prays to the gods for a good harvest of grain, he presumably prays, 'May my relishes multiply!'"

At this the young man, feeling the conversation was aimed his way, didn't stop eating the delicacies — but he did help himself to bread. Socrates, who missed nothing, said, "Keep an eye on our friend there, you next to him, and see whether he treats the bread as relish or the relish as bread."

Another time he saw one of the company tasting several dishes with a single piece of bread. "Could there be more extravagant cookery," he said, "or better designed to ruin the dishes, than eating so many at once — cramming in more mixtures than the cook himself would ever allow? He combines ingredients the cooks keep apart, so if they're right, he's wrong, and he's destroying their art. And really, isn't it absurd to hire the most skilled cooks you can find, and then, claiming no such skill yourself, rearrange their work? And there's something worse in store for the man who trains himself to eat many dishes at once: when there aren't many on the table, habit will make him feel deprived — while the man used to sending down his bread with a single relish will dine cheerfully however few there are."

He also used to say that in the Attic dialect, "making good cheer" was another name for eating, and that the "good" tacked on stood for eating food that troubled neither soul nor body and wasn't hard to find. So he applied "making good cheer" to modest, well-ordered living.
