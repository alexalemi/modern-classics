Chapter 6

Some conversations Socrates had with Antiphon the sophist also deserve a place in this record. Antiphon was hoping to lure Socrates' companions away from him, and he came up to Socrates once in their presence with this challenge: "Socrates, I always assumed that people who study philosophy were supposed to grow happier by the day. But you seem to have harvested a very different crop from yours. You live — no, exist — in a style no slave would put up with from his master. Your food and drink are the cheapest to be had. The one shabby cloak you own serves you summer and winter alike, and you go the whole year round without shoes on your feet or a shirt on your back. Then again, you won't take money or earn it — though the mere pursuit of money is a pleasure, and having it makes life sweeter and more independent. Now, teachers in every field generally try to shape their pupils into copies of themselves. If that's what you intend for your companions, you ought to call yourself a professor of misery."

To this challenge Socrates replied: "One thing is clear to me, Antiphon: you've painted such a vivid picture of my miserable life that I'm sure you would sooner die than live it. So let's look at what you actually find so hard about it. Is it that a man who takes payment is bound by contract to finish the work he's paid for, while I, who take nothing, am under no obligation to talk with anyone I don't choose? Do you look down on my diet because what I eat is less wholesome or less strengthening than what you eat — or because my food is scarcer and more expensive to come by? Or does what your money buys at the market simply taste better than what mine doesn't? Don't you know that the sharper your appetite, the less you need sauces, and the keener your thirst, the less you crave exotic drinks? As for clothes — people change them on account of cold or heat, and wear boots and shoes only so their feet won't blister and keep them from walking. Well, then: have you ever known me to stay indoors more than the next man because of the cold? Have you ever seen me fighting anyone for a spot in the shade because of the heat? Don't you know that even a naturally weak man can, by exercise and practice, come to outdo a natural giant who neglects his body — beat him at the very thing he's trained in, and bear the strain more easily? Yet apparently you won't accept that I, who am forever training myself to endure whatever may land on the body, can face every hardship more easily than someone like you, who perhaps hasn't practiced. And if it's a question of escaping slavery to the belly, or to sleep, or to lust — can you suggest any better protection than having something you love more: a counter-charm that delights you not only while you're using it, but with the hope of all the good it will keep on doing you? And this much you certainly do know: joy doesn't come to the man who feels he's succeeding at nothing. It belongs to the man who's convinced things are moving forward for him — in his farming, in the working of his ship, in any of the thousand and one jobs a man may turn his hand to. He gets to look at his work and think, 'I am doing well.' But is all that pleasure put together half as sweet as the consciousness of becoming a better person yourself, and of gaining better and better friends? That, for my part, is what I go on believing.

"Now suppose your friends or your country need help. Which of us has more free time to give them — the man who lives the way I live today, or the man who lives in the style you consider so fortunate? Which of us takes to a soldier's life more easily — the man who can't get by without expensive comforts, or the one for whom whatever comes to hand is enough? Which of us cracks first in a siege and begs for terms — the man of elaborate wants, or the man who gets along happily on whatever is easiest to find? You, Antiphon, seem to think happiness consists of luxury and extravagance. I hold a different creed: to want nothing at all is an attribute of God; to want as little as possible is to come nearest the divine; and since the divine is mightiest, whatever comes closest to the divine is next mightiest."

Antiphon returned to the attack on another occasion. "Socrates," he said, "for my part I believe you're a good and honest man — but a wise one? Hardly. And I don't think you'd dispute the verdict yourself. After all, you ask no fee for your company. Yet if it were your cloak, or your house, or anything else you own, you'd set a value on it — you'd never dream of giving it away, let alone of trading it for less than it's worth. The plain conclusion is that if you thought your company was worth anything, you'd charge nothing less than full value for that too. So there it is, as I said: good and honest you may be, since you don't cheat people out of greed; but wise you cannot be, since what you know isn't worth a penny."

To this assault Socrates answered: "Antiphon, among us it's held that beauty and wisdom have this in common: there is an honorable way and a shameful way to dispose of each. The man who sells his beauty buys himself an ugly name; but if a man discerns a fine soul in his admirer and makes that man his friend, we call the choice a sensible one. So it is with wisdom. The man who sells it for money to the first bidder, we call a sophist — as much as to say, a man who prostitutes his wisdom. But if that same man recognizes a noble nature in someone, teaches him every good thing he knows, and makes a friend of him, we say he's doing exactly what a good and generous citizen ought to do. I have my tastes, Antiphon, like anyone else. One man delights in a good horse, another in his hounds, another in his fighting cocks; my delight is good friends. Whatever good thing I have, I teach it to them, and I introduce them to others who I think will help them along the road to virtue. And the treasures the wise men of old have left behind, written in their books — I unroll those and read them through together with my friends, and if we spot anything good, we pick it out eagerly, and we count it great gain if we can only grow in friendship with one another."

Listening to this talk, I couldn't help reflecting that he, the teacher, was a man to be envied — and that we, his listeners, were being led by him toward beauty and nobility of soul.

On yet another occasion this same Antiphon asked Socrates how he expected to make statesmen of other people when he didn't take part in politics himself — assuming he even knew how. "Let me put a question to you, Antiphon," Socrates replied. "Which is the more statesmanlike course: to practice politics single-handed, or to make it my business to turn out as many people as possible who are fit for the work?"

Chapter 7

Let's consider, finally, whether by steering his companions away from quackery and false pretenses he wasn't also spurring them directly toward virtue. He used to say there was no better road to a good name than actually becoming good at whatever you want to be reputed good at. And he drove the truth of that home like this: "Think what a man would be forced to do if he wanted to be considered a good flute player without really being one. He'd have to copy the good players in all the externals of the art, wouldn't he? First of all, since those artists travel in style with a long train of attendants, he must have the same. Next, since they can command the applause of a crowd, he must pack the hall with hired clappers. But one thing is certain: he must never actually give a performance, or he'll be exposed on the spot and laughed at — not just as a bad flute player, but as a miserable fraud. And now look at him: a mountain of expenses to meet, not one advantage to show for them, and a rotten reputation on top of it all. What's left for him but a life stale and unprofitable, the scorn and mockery of everyone? Or take another case. Suppose a man wanted to be thought a good general, or a good ship's pilot, without being anything of the kind. Picture how it goes for him. One of two misfortunes: either, for all his longing to seem qualified in these matters, he can't convince anyone — which is bad enough — or he succeeds, which is worse. Because it stands to reason that a man appointed to steer a ship or lead an army without the knowledge for it will quickly destroy the very people he'd least want to hurt, and make a sorry exit from the stage himself."

By one example after another he would show, in the same way, that it doesn't pay to appear rich, or brave, or strong, without really being the thing you pretend. "Sooner or later," he said, "demands will be laid on you that are beyond your power to meet, and when you fail exactly where everyone believed you capable, the world will give you no sympathy." "I call that man a cheat, and a great cheat too," he would say, "who talks money or goods out of someone by persuasion and defrauds him. But the biggest imposter of all is the man who has deluded his city into believing he is fit to lead it, when all the while he is worthless."
