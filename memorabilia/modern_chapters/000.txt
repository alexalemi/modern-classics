Book I

(Part 1 of 4)

Chapter 1

I have often wondered what arguments the men who indicted Socrates could possibly have used to convince the Athenians that he deserved to die. The indictment ran like this: "Socrates is guilty of refusing to recognize the gods recognized by the state, of importing strange new divinities of his own, and of corrupting the young."

Start with the first charge. What evidence did they ever produce that Socrates refused to recognize the state's gods? Did he fail to sacrifice? Did he ignore divination? Just the opposite. He was regularly seen offering sacrifice, at home and at the public altars of the city, and he relied on divination just as openly. In fact his famous saying, "A divinity gives me a sign," was on everyone's lips — and if I had to guess, that phrase is exactly what gave rise to the charge of importing new divinities. But there was nothing newer in his case than in anyone else's who believes the gods send guidance. People everywhere rely on omens of every kind: the flight or cry of birds, a stray remark, a chance meeting, the entrails of a sacrificed animal. And even by the ordinary understanding, nobody thinks it's the bird itself, or the stranger you happen to pass, that knows what will benefit you — it's the gods who use these things as instruments to signal it. Socrates believed exactly that. The only difference was that where most people say a bird or a passerby "turned them back" or "urged them on," Socrates said what he actually meant: "The divinity," he said, "gives me a sign." He constantly advised his friends to do this or avoid that on the authority of that divine sign — and the plain fact is that those who took his advice did well, while those who ignored it lived to regret it. Now think about what that implies. Obviously he had no wish to look like a fraud or a fool in front of the people he saw every day. But he would have looked like both if he kept announcing revelations from a god and the god kept making him wrong. Clearly he would never have risked predicting anything unless he trusted that his words would come true. And where could that trust rest except on a god? And if he trusted the gods, how can anyone say he didn't recognize them?

There is another side to how he guided the people close to him. For the ordinary business of life, his advice was simple: act however you judge best. But where the outcome was genuinely beyond calculating, he told his friends to consult the oracle about whether to go ahead at all. "No one," he would say, "who wants to run a household or a city well — no one who hopes to steer the ship of state — can do without help from above." Of course, he granted, carpentry, building, metalwork, farming, the management of people, along with the theory behind these crafts and the sciences of arithmetic, economics, and strategy — all of that can be studied and mastered by the human mind. But even in these fields, he said, the gods keep the most important part for themselves, hidden from mortal eyes. A man can sow a field perfectly and still not know who will harvest the crop. He can build a beautiful house and not know who will live in it. A general cannot be sure a campaign will pay off; a politician cannot be sure his leadership will end in good or in disaster. A man who marries a lovely wife hoping for happiness cannot know whether she will bring him grief instead, and a man who builds powerful alliances in the city cannot know whether those very alliances will get him exiled. To imagine that all of this lies within human judgment, with nothing left to the gods, was, Socrates thought, madness. But it was just as absurd to run to Heaven with questions we are perfectly equipped to answer by learning — as if you should ask an oracle, "Should I hire an experienced driver for my carriage, or a man who has never held the reins?" or "Should I put a sailor in command of my ship, or a landsman?" The same goes for anything you can settle by counting, weighing, or measuring. Pestering the gods with questions like that struck him as a kind of profanity. "Our duty is plain," he would say. "Where the gods have given us the ability to work things out for ourselves, let's use it. But where the answer is hidden, let's seek it from above, through divination — for the gods give signs to anyone they favor."

Consider, too, that Socrates lived his whole life in plain view. Early in the morning he would head to the public walks or the training grounds; at midday he was in the marketplace with the crowds; and as the day wound down, wherever the biggest gathering was, that's where you would find him — talking, mostly, while anyone who wanted could stop and listen. And in all that time no one ever heard him say, or saw him do, a single impious or irreverent thing. Unlike others, in fact, he refused to speculate about the grand questions of the universe — how the "cosmos," as the professors call it, came into being, or what forces drive what happens in the heavens. Racking your brain over such things, he argued, was playing the fool. His first question was: did these theorists feel they had mastered human affairs so completely that they were free to move on to loftier matters? Or did they seriously believe that ignoring everything human to speculate about everything divine was the proper work of a man? He was amazed they couldn't see that these problems lie beyond mortal reach — since even the men proudest of their theories about them contradict one another like madmen. For just as some madmen have no fear of what is genuinely terrifying while others are terrified of what is harmless, and some will say or do anything in public without a flicker of shame while others won't set foot among their fellow men at all, and some honor neither temple nor altar nor anything else sacred while others bow down to sticks and stones and worship animals — so it goes with the thinkers who fret over the nature of the universe. One school has discovered that all being is one and indivisible; another, that it is infinite in number. One announces that everything is in constant flux; another answers that nothing can ever move at all. One says everything is born and everything dies; another counters that nothing could ever be born or ever die.

Sometimes his questioning of these speculators took a different tack. A student of human affairs, he pointed out, expects to put what he learns to use, for himself or for others as he chooses. So do these investigators of the divine machinery expect that, once they've discovered what forces produce the various phenomena, they will make wind and rain and good harvests on demand? Will they manage all that to suit their needs? Or has no such idea ever crossed their minds — will they be satisfied just to know how it all happens? That was how he talked about the people who meddle in such questions. He himself never tired of examining human ones. What is piety, and what is impiety? What is beautiful, and what is ugly? What is noble, and what is shameful? What do "just" and "unjust" mean? What is self-control, and what is madness? What is courage, and what is cowardice? What is a state, and what is a statesman? What is rule over men, and what makes a person fit to rule? These and questions like them were his subject — and understanding them, as he put it, is what makes a person truly free and noble, while those who don't understand them deserve to be called slaves.

Now, where Socrates' opinions were unknown to the public, it's no great surprise that the jury got him wrong. But that they ignored facts everyone knew — that is astonishing.

At one point Socrates served on the Council, and he had sworn the councilor's oath to act in accordance with the laws. It so happened that he was presiding over the Popular Assembly on the day it was seized by the urge to condemn the nine generals — Thrasyllus, Erasinides, and the rest — to death by a single collective vote, which was illegal. And despite the fury of the crowd and the threats of several powerful men, he refused to put the question to a vote. Keeping faith with his oath mattered more to him than gratifying the people in their injustice or shielding himself from the threats of the mighty. The truth is that his belief about how the gods watch over men was very different from most people's. The majority seem to imagine that the gods know some things and are ignorant of others. Socrates was convinced that the gods know everything — what is said, what is done, and what is deliberated in the silent chambers of the heart — that they are present everywhere, and that they give signs to men about everything that concerns men.

So I come back to where I began. It amazes me that the Athenians ever let themselves be persuaded that Socrates was anything but sound-minded about the gods. This was a man who never once said or did an impious thing regarding them — a man whose every word and every act concerning the gods matched, word for word and deed for deed, everything we recognize as the deepest piety.
