Book IV

(Part 6 of 6)

Chapter 8

Now suppose someone were to set Socrates' claim — that the divine sign told him what he should and should not do — against the fact that the jury sentenced him to death, and conclude that this proved him guilty of lying, or of deluding himself, about this "divinity" of his. Let such a person consider two things. First, Socrates was by then so far advanced in years that if he had not died when he did, his life would have reached its natural end soon afterward. Second, as things turned out, he escaped the bitterest part of life — the years that dull every mind — and in exchange he got to display the full strength of his soul and win glory besides: partly by a defense unmatched for its truthfulness, its frankness, and its integrity, and partly by bearing the sentence of death with perfect gentleness and courage. For it is admitted on all sides that no man in living memory ever met death more nobly. After the sentence he had to live on for thirty days, because it was the month of the Delia — Apollo's festival — and the law allows no one to die at the public executioner's hand until the sacred embassy returns from the island of Delos. Through the whole of that month, as every one of his friends can testify, his life went on exactly as before. There was nothing to mark any difference between those days and the days that came before them, in the steady evenness of his courage — and his had always been a life of marvelous cheerfulness and calm content.

(Stop and ask: how could a man die more nobly or more beautifully than that? Or put it this way: dying so, his death was the noblest and most beautiful a man could die; being the most beautiful, it was also the most fortunate and heaven-blessed; and being the most blessed, it was the most precious in the sight of God.)

Now let me add some things about him that I heard from Hermogenes, the son of Hipponicus. He said that even after Meletus had filed the indictment, he used to hear Socrates conversing about anything and everything rather than the trial hanging over him, and he ventured to suggest that Socrates ought to be thinking about his line of defense. Socrates' first answer was: "Don't you think I have been practicing it my whole life long?" And when Hermogenes asked how, he explained that he had passed his days doing nothing else but examining what is just and what is unjust — right and wrong — doing the right and holding back from the wrong; "and that conduct," he added, "I count the finest practice for a defense a man could ask for." Hermogenes came back to the point and pleaded with him: "Don't you see, Socrates, how often an Athenian jury, swayed by clever argument, condemns innocent people to death and acquits real criminals?" "I assure you, Hermogenes," Socrates replied, "every time I have tried to turn my thoughts to the defense I am to make before the court, the divine sign has opposed me." "How strange!" Hermogenes exclaimed. "Do you find it strange," Socrates went on, "that God should judge it better for me to close my life now? Don't you know that up to this very moment I would not concede to any man that he has lived a better or a happier life than mine? The best life, I hold, belongs to those who work hardest at becoming as good as they can be, and the happiest to those who feel most keenly that they are growing in goodness — and so far, that is exactly the good fortune I see fallen to my lot. I have found it so not just in casual encounters with others, but by strict comparison between myself and others, and in that conviction I stand to this day. And not I alone: my friends hold the same conviction about me — not for the weak reason that they are my friends and love me, or every man's friends would feel the same about theirs, but because they are persuaded that keeping my company makes them better. But if I am destined to live on, I suppose I shall have to pay the penalties of old age in full: to see and hear less keenly, to fail in force of mind, to leave school — as it were — a duller student than I came, less learned and more forgetful; to fall, in short, from whatever I once stood highest in, and grow daily worse at the very things I once did best. If I couldn't feel the change, the life left over would scarcely be worth living; but given that I must feel it, then the existence left to me could only turn insipid and joyless — a death in life, stripped of everything that makes life sweet. And if it is reserved for me to die unjustly, then the shame lies on those who unjustly kill me. If injustice is shameful, how can any unjust act whatever fail to be shameful? But where is the disgrace to me if others fail to judge me justly or treat me rightly? I see before me a long line of men who have walked this road, and I see the reputations they left among posterity — how differently people speak of those who did the wrong and those who suffered it. And I know that though I die today, mankind will remember me very differently from the men who put me to death. The witness that outlives me will say this: that I never at any time wronged any man or made him worse, but always tried to make the people who kept my company better."

Such were the things he said in conversation with Hermogenes and the rest. And among those who knew Socrates and understood what kind of man he was, all who make virtue their pursuit still, to this day, miss him with the bitterest regret, as the one helper in the pursuit of virtue whom nothing can replace.

To me, personally, he was what I have tried throughout to describe: so pious and devout that he would take no step apart from the will of Heaven; so just and upright that he never did even a trifling injury to any living soul; so self-controlled, so temperate, that he never once chose the sweeter over the better; so sensible, so wise, so prudent, that in telling better from worse he never went wrong — and he needed no helper, for in these matters his own judgment was sure and sufficient. He could set out a moral question and define it with perfect clarity; he could test others, and where they went wrong, cross-examine and convict them, and so drive and guide them toward virtue and true nobility. That is my estimate of him. If the verdict fails to satisfy, I would ask anyone who disagrees to set the character of any other man beside this portrait — and then pass sentence. For of all whom I have known, he was the best and the happiest of men.
