Book IV

(Part 3 of 6)

Chapter 3

Socrates, it should be clear by now, was in no hurry for his companions to become skilled speakers, men of action, or clever inventors — not until they had first laid a solid foundation of self-control. Men who had those abilities without that saving virtue, he believed, only became worse men, with greater power to do harm. So his first concern was to instill in his companions a wise attitude toward the gods. Others who were present on particular occasions have recorded how he talked with people on this theme; I will confine myself to one discussion with Euthydemus that I heard myself.

"Tell me, Euthydemus," Socrates began, "has it ever struck you how carefully the gods have provided for everything human beings need?"

"No," he said, "I can't say it ever has."

"Well, you hardly need reminding that first of all we need light — and light is what the gods supply us."

"Very true," said Euthydemus. "If we didn't have it, then for all the good our eyes could do us we might as well have been born blind."

"And since we also need rest and relaxation, they give us the blessing of night, the balm of quiet rest."

"Yes," he answered, "we owe them a great deal for that gift too."

"And then, since the sun in its brightness marks out the hours of the day for us and lights up everything we do, while night in its darkness blurs every distinction — haven't they set the stars overhead, which tell us the watches of the night, so that we can take care of many of our needs even in the dark?"

"So they have," he said.

"And let's not forget the moon: doesn't she show us not only the divisions of the night but the divisions of the month as well?"

"Certainly," he answered.

"And what about this — that we need food, and the heavenly powers supply that too? They make good things spring up out of the earth for our benefit, and for our benefit they provide the right seasons in their turn, furnishing us not only with the many different things we need but with the sources of our joy and gladness besides."

"Yes," he answered eagerly, "all of that truly bears witness to a love for man."

"And what of another priceless gift — water? It works together with the earth and the seasons to give birth and growth to everything useful to us; more than that, it helps nourish our very bodies, and mixed into everything that feeds us it makes our food easier to digest, healthier, and better-tasting. And notice this: the more of it we need, the more abundantly it is supplied. What do you say to a gift like that?"

"There again," said Euthydemus, "I see the mark of providential care."

"And then there is fire, which the same heavenly power has provided — our defense against cold, our helper in darkness, our fellow worker in every craft and in every tool that men invent or make for their own use. To put it briefly: nothing worth mentioning that serves human life gets made without fire. What do you say to that?"

"Yes," said Euthydemus, "that is generosity by design if anything ever was."

"Then consider the movements of the sun. When it has made its turn in winter, it draws near to us again, ripening some crops and drying out others whose season is past; and when it has finished that work, it comes no closer, but turns away, as if taking care not to scorch us and do us harm; and then, when in its retreat it reaches the point past which we would plainly freeze to death from the cold, notice how it turns about once more and comes back, traveling through exactly that region of the sky where it can do us the most good."

"Yes, honestly," he answered, "these things certainly look as if they were ordered for man's sake."

"And again, since it's obvious we could endure neither scorching heat nor freezing cold if either came on us suddenly, notice how gradually the sun approaches and how gradually it withdraws, so that we arrive at each extreme without ever noticing the change."

"For my part," he replied, "I'm beginning to wonder whether the gods have any occupation at all except taking care of man. The one thing that keeps me from saying so outright is that the other animals seem to share these benefits along with us."

"Why, of course — and isn't it plain that the animals themselves are born and raised for man's sake? No other living creature gets so much good out of sheep and goats, horses and cattle and donkeys and the rest of the animals as man does. He depends on them, I should think, even more than on plants and crops; at any rate he uses both alike for food and for trade, and a large part of the human race doesn't live off the soil at all, but on the milk and cheese and meat of their flocks and herds. And everywhere men tame and domesticate the more useful kinds of animals and turn them into fellow workers in war and in everything else."

"I can't disagree with that," he answered, "when I see animals far stronger than man become so obedient to his hand that he can use them however he pleases."

"And as we reflect on the endless beauty and usefulness and variety of nature, what are we to say of the fact that man has been given senses matched to all that diversity, through which we take our fill of every good thing? Or of the power of reasoning planted in us, which lets us draw conclusions about what we perceive, and with the help of memory work out how each kind of thing can be turned to our good, and devise countless contrivances for enjoying what is good and fending off what is bad? Or, last of all, of the gift of speech, through which we teach one another, share in all the blessings I've named, form communities, establish laws, and live a civilized life? What are we to think of all that?"

"It certainly does appear, Socrates, that the gods show great regard for humankind — tender care, even."

"And what do you make of the fact that where we are powerless to plan wisely for our own future, at exactly that point they lend us their help — telling the inquirer through divination what is about to happen, and teaching him how it may best be turned to good account?"

"And you, Socrates," said Euthydemus, "they seem to treat as an even closer friend than the rest of men, if they show you by signs in advance, without even waiting to be asked, what you should and should not do."

"And you will discover for yourself that I'm telling the truth, if you don't wait to see the visible forms of the gods themselves, but are content to see their works — and, with those before your eyes, to worship and honor the divine makers of them. Reflect that the gods themselves point us toward this lesson. Every one of them gives us his blessings freely, yet not one of them ever steps out from behind the veil to hand over a single gift. And above all, the one who orders and holds together the whole universe — in which all things are beautiful and good; who keeps it forever fresh for our use, sound and ageless, free of sickness and decay, serving his will unerringly and faster than thought — this God is seen performing the mightiest works of all, yet in the actual running of them he remains invisible to mortal eyes.

"Consider, too, that the sun over our heads, which seems so plainly visible to everyone, will not let a man look at him too closely; stare at him shamelessly, and he snatches your eyesight away. And if the gods themselves are unseen, you will find their servants hidden as well. The thunderbolt is plainly hurled from the height of heaven, and it overpowers everything it meets — yet no eye can catch it coming or going at the moment it strikes. The winds too are themselves invisible, though what they do is plain to see, and we know them by their approach. And let's not forget the soul of man himself, which shares in the divine if anything human does: it rules within us as plainly as anything can, and yet it, like the rest, is hidden from our sight. Keep all this in mind, and don't look down on the invisible powers. Learn to recognize their strength from what they visibly accomplish, and honor the Divinity."

"Believe me, Socrates," Euthydemus replied, "there's no danger of my neglecting the divine power, even a little. That's not what worries me. What discourages me is the thought that no human soul could ever repay the kindness of the gods with the gratitude it deserves."

"Don't let that discourage you," he said. "You know what answer the god at Delphi gives to everyone who comes asking, 'How shall I return thanks to heaven?' — 'According to the law and custom of your city.' And the law and custom everywhere, I take it, is that a man should please the gods with offerings according to the ability that is in him. How could a man honor the gods more beautifully or more devoutly than by doing exactly what they themselves command? Only he must not slacken or fall short of his ability — because when a man does that, it's plain enough that at that moment he is not honoring the gods at all. So honor the gods not grudgingly but according to your ability; and when you have done that, be of good cheer and expect the greatest blessings. For where else should a sensible man look for great blessings, if not from those with the greatest power to help him? And how else can he hope to win them, except by pleasing his helpers? And how can he please them better than by obeying them as fully as he can?"

By words like these — and by conduct that matched his words — Socrates shaped and molded the hearts of his companions, making them at once more devout and more virtuous.
