Book II

(Part 2 of 4)

Chapter 2

He once noticed that Lamprocles, the elder of his sons, was in a foul temper with his mother, and he took the boy aside.

"Tell me, son," he said, "have you ever heard of people being called ungrateful?"

"Of course," the young man replied.

"And do you understand what a person does to earn that name?"

"Yes. When someone has been treated kindly, and could repay the kindness but doesn't, people call him ungrateful."

"So the ungrateful get counted among wrongdoers?"

"They do."

"And have you ever asked yourself whether ingratitude might work the way enslaving prisoners does — wrong when done to friends, but defensible when done to enemies?"

"I have thought about that. My view is that whoever the kindness comes from, friend or enemy, the person who receives it ought to try to repay it, and if he doesn't, he's in the wrong."

"Then if that's how the matter stands, ingratitude is wrongdoing pure and simple?"

Lamprocles agreed.

"And it follows that the greater the benefit a person receives, the greater the wrong if he fails to repay it?"

He agreed again.

"Then where," Socrates went on, "will we ever find greater benefits than the ones children receive from their parents? Their father and mother brought them out of nothing into existence, gave them the sight of all this beauty and a share in all the blessings the gods give humankind — things we prize so highly that every one of us shudders at the thought of leaving them, and states have made death the penalty for the worst crimes because there is no greater evil to hold wrongdoing in check.

"Don't imagine people have children just for the physical pleasure of it. The streets and the brothels are full of ways to satisfy that. No — what we plainly look for is the kind of wife who will give us the finest children; we marry her, and we carry on the family. The man's duty is double: to provide for the woman who is going to raise children alongside him, and to lay up for those children, before they are even born, everything he thinks will help them live well — as much of it as he can. The woman carries her precious burden through discomfort and pain, at the risk of her own life, sharing with the child inside her the very food that feeds her. And when she has carried it to the end and brought it into the world through hard labor, she feeds it and watches over it tenderly — not in return for any good it has done her, because a baby doesn't know who is being kind to it and can't even signal what it wants. She has to guess what it needs and what will comfort it, and try to supply both; and she feeds it night and day for months on end, putting up with the drudgery without any idea what thanks she will ever see for it. And the care doesn't stop at nursing. When the children are old enough to learn, the parents teach them whatever useful knowledge they have as a guide to life; and where they think someone else is better qualified, they send the children to him and pay for the lessons — doing everything in their power to see their children turn out as good as possible."

"Fine," the young man answered. "Suppose she has done all that, and twenty times more. Nobody on earth could stand my mother's temper."

"Which do you think is harder to bear," said Socrates, "a wild animal's savagery or a mother's?"

"A mother's — at least when the mother is mine."

"Really! And has this mother ever actually injured you, the way animals injure people — bitten you, say, or kicked you?"

"No, but she says things a person would sooner die than listen to."

"And how many annoyances do you suppose you have caused her — whining and fussing in word and deed, night and day, since you were a little boy? How much grief and worry when you were sick?"

"Well, I never said or did anything to make her ashamed of me."

"Oh, come now. Do you think it's harder for you to listen to your mother than for one actor to listen to another on the tragic stage, when the floodgates of abuse really open?"

"No — because the actor knows it's all talk. The character cross-examining him won't fine him; the one hurling threats won't hurt him. That's why they take it so easily."

"Then why should you fly into a rage? You know perfectly well that whatever your mother says, she not only means you no harm — she is wishing more blessings down on your head than on anyone else alive. Or do you actually believe your mother wants the worst for you?"

"No, I don't believe that."

"So here is a mother who wants the best for you, who nurses you when you're sick to make you well again and to see that you lack nothing, and who on top of all that is forever praying to heaven for blessings on you and paying vows on your behalf — and you call her harsh and impossible? My own view is that if you can't stand a mother like that, you can't stand your own good luck.

"Tell me this," he went on. "Is there anyone alive you think you owe service to? Or are you prepared to stand entirely alone — to please nobody, try to please nobody, follow nobody, obey no general and no ruler of any kind? Is that your position, or do you admit you owe allegiance to somebody?"

"Of course I owe allegiance."

"Then take your neighbor. You're willing to keep on his good side, so that he'll kindle a fire for you when you need one, lend a ready hand when things go well, and stand by you like a friend if you stumble into trouble?"

"I am."

"And the stranger you happen to travel with, by land or sea — the chance acquaintances you fall in with — is it nothing to you whether these people become friends or not, or do you admit their goodwill is worth taking some trouble to earn?"

"It's worth it."

"So that's where we've landed: you're prepared to pay attention to this stranger and that one, but to your mother, who loves you more than anyone else does, you owe no service and no allegiance? Don't you know that the state ignores ordinary ingratitude — it brings no charges against people who fail to return a kindness — but for one case it saves its penalties? If a man fails to give his parents the service and respect he owes them, the law lays its finger on him: his name is struck from the roll, and he is barred from holding the archonship — as much as to say that sacrifices offered for the state by such a man would be no offerings at all, being tainted with impiety, and that nothing else he did could be done 'well and justly' either. More than that: if a man neglects to tend his dead parents' grave, the state looks into it when it examines its magistrates for office. So, my son, if you have any sense, you will beg your mother's pardon — before the gods take you for an ungrateful man and refuse to do you good, and before your fellow men notice how you neglect your parents and despise you for it with one voice, leaving you stranded in a desert without a friend. Because once people get the idea that here is a man ungrateful to his own parents, nobody will believe that a kindness done to him would be anything but thrown away."

Chapter 3

Two brothers he knew well, Chaerephon and Chaerecrates, had fallen out, and this had caught his attention. When he next saw the younger of the two, he raised the matter with him.

"Tell me, Chaerecrates — you're not one of those strange people, surely, who believe property is better and more precious than a brother? Property is a senseless thing that needs protecting; a brother is a sensible, feeling being who can do the protecting. Besides, a man has plenty of property and only one of him. And here's another marvel: a man counts his brother a loss because his brother's goods aren't his own — yet he doesn't count his fellow citizens a loss, though their possessions aren't his either. In their case he has the wit to see that living securely among many people, with enough for himself, beats owning a whole city's wealth and living in dangerous isolation; but apply the same reasoning to brothers, and suddenly nobody's listening. Again, a man of means will buy slaves because he wants help with his work, and he'll go out and acquire friends because he needs their support — but his brother? Who cares about brothers? Apparently a friend can be found in any ordinary citizen, but not in your own flesh and blood. And yet coming from the same parents and being nursed at the same breast is a great head start toward friendship — even animals feel a natural pull toward the creatures they were raised with. On top of all that, the world respects a man with brothers more than a man who stands alone and has to fight his own battles."

"I grant you, Socrates," Chaerecrates answered, "that when the quarrel is a small one, a man ought to bear with his brother and not shun him over some trifle. A brother of the right sort is a blessing, just as you say. But when he's the exact opposite of that, why should a man attempt the impossible?"

"Tell me, then — is there nobody Chaerephon can please, any more than he can please you? Or do some people find him perfectly agreeable?"

"That's exactly why I have a right to detest him. He can be pleasant enough to other people; but let him come anywhere near me, and everything about him is the opposite of a blessing."

"Might it be like a horse?" said Socrates. "A horse is no use to the rider who doesn't know how to handle him — and a brother 'kicks,' too, when someone tries to deal with him without knowing how."

"But how could I not know how to deal with my own brother, when I know how to return a kind word and a good deed in kind? A man who does everything he can to needle me, in word and deed — him I can neither speak well of nor treat well, and what's more, I won't try."

"That's an astonishing thing to say, Chaerecrates. If your dog — a good one, useful with the flocks — fawned on your shepherds but growled and bared his teeth whenever you came near, you wouldn't waste time on his bad temper; you'd try to win him over with kindness. But your brother? A brother who acted as a brother should would be a great blessing to you — you admit that; and you confess you know the secret of kind words and kind deeds; and yet you won't set yourself to make him the best friend you have."

"I'm afraid, Socrates, I simply don't have the skill to make Chaerephon treat me as he should."

"Yet there's no elaborate or newfangled machinery required. Bait your hook the way you know best, and you'll catch him — and once caught, he'll be your devoted friend."

"If you know I possess some love-charm without being aware of it," said Chaerecrates, "hurry up and enlighten me."

"Answer me this, then. If you wanted an acquaintance to invite you to dinner the next time he holds a feast, what would you do?"

"Obviously I'd set the example by inviting him first."

"And if you wanted a friend to look after your affairs while you were abroad?"

"Obviously I'd first undertake to look after his when he traveled."

"And if you wanted a foreign host to take you under his roof when you visited his country?"

"Obviously I'd start by offering him my own roof when he came to Athens, and show myself eager to further whatever business brought him — so that he'd be just as eager to do the same for me."

"Well then! It turns out you know every charm known to man — you've simply been hiding the knowledge all this time. Or are you shrinking from the first move because it would look bad to make friendly advances to your own brother? A man is generally thought to deserve praise for beating an enemy to the blow and beating a friend to the kindness. If Chaerephon had seemed to me the fitter of you two to lead the way toward friendship, I would have tried to persuade him to make the first move. As it is, I'm convinced the first step belongs to you — and so does the final victory."

"That's a startling thing to hear from you, Socrates, and most unlike you — telling me, the younger, to take the lead ahead of my elder brother. Everywhere in the world it's the other way around: the elder leads, in word and in deed."

"How so? Isn't it the custom everywhere for the younger to step aside when he meets his elder in the street, to stand and offer him his seat, to honor him with the soft couch, to yield to him in argument? Don't stand there dithering, my good friend. Reach out your hand gently, and you'll see how quickly the worthy soul responds. Look at your brother's character — proud, open, quick where honor is concerned. Mean little scoundrels are best caught with a bribe; that's the way with riffraff. But a fine nature needs finer handling. You win natures like his with affection."

"And suppose I do all that," said Chaerecrates, "and for all my trying he's no better?"

"At the worst, you will have shown yourself a good, honest, brotherly man, and he will stand exposed as a sorry creature that kindness is wasted on. But I don't expect anything of the sort. My belief is that the moment he hears your challenge, he'll leap into the contest, and out of sheer competitive pride he'll strain to outdo you in kindness of word and deed.

"Right now the two of you are like a pair of hands that God made to help each other but that have quit their work and taken to hindering each other instead — like a pair of feet fashioned on the divine plan to work together, which have given that up in order to trip each other. Isn't it plain mindless stupidity to use for harm what was made for help? And in making two brothers, it seems to me, God intends more benefit for each than he gets from two hands, or two feet, or two eyes, or any of the other pairs a man is born with. Ask your hands to work together at two points more than a fathom apart — six feet — and they can't do it; your feet can't stretch across even a single fathom; your eyes, for all the range we claim for them, can't see the front and back of an object at the same time even up close. But two brothers bound in friendship can work for each other's good with whole seas between them."

Chapter 4

I once heard him discuss the related subject of friendship itself, in terms that seemed to me exactly calculated to help a man choose his friends well and use them well.

He had heard it said, times without number, that of all possessions none equals a good and honest friend; and yet, as far as he could see, most people troubled themselves about anything sooner than acquiring friends. Houses, fields, slaves, cattle, furniture of every sort — these they worked hard to get and strove hard to keep; but the greatest blessing of all, which by their own admission a friend is, hardly one man in a hundred ever thought about gaining, or about keeping the friends he already had. Let a friend and a household slave fall sick at the same time, and watch what happens: the master promptly fetches the doctor for his slave and spares no pains or expense on his recovery, while the friend in the same condition gets barely a thought. If both die, he is visibly upset about the slave — that, as he reckons it, is a real loss — while his friend's death leaves his position, as he sees it, untouched. And so, though he would never dream of leaving his other possessions neglected and uncared for, the friend, who asks for nothing, gets nothing.

And the crowning example, he said, is this: however much ordinary property people own, most of them at least know how much of it there is. But ask a man to count his friends — not so very long a list, after all — and he can't do it; and if, to oblige you, he starts naming names, he'll soon take back some he had already put down. That is how much thought people spend on their friends.

And yet set any other possession beside a good friend, and which comes off better? A horse has its worth; so does a yoke of oxen; but who can measure the worth of a worthy friend? Kinder and more constant than the most faithful of slaves, he is the one possession that truly deserves the name "good for everything." Look at the post he assigns himself: wherever his friends' welfare falls short, he steps in to make up the lack; their interests, private and public alike, are his concern. Is a kindness wanted somewhere? He throws in his full weight. Does some terror loom? He is on hand to help and defend — spending his money, spending his energy, joining in persuasion or, if it comes to that, in force. He is the man who gladdens the prosperous in their hour of success and steadies those whose footing has nearly gone. Whatever a man's own hands can do for him, whatever his eyes can see and his ears hear and his feet can cover, a friend's good offices will match; and often enough, what a man has failed to accomplish for himself, has missed seeing or hearing or getting, a friend acting on his behalf will achieve for him. And still — though people will carefully tend a tree for the sake of its fruit — more than half the world pays only a lazy, drifting attention to this richest of all possessions, a friend.

Chapter 5

I remember another argument of his whose whole effect was to push the listener into self-examination — to make him ask himself, "What am I actually worth to my friends?" It came about like this. He had noticed that one of his companions was neglecting a friend who was hard pressed by poverty. So, in front of the neglectful man and several others, he put his questions to the poor man himself, Antisthenes.

"Tell me, Antisthenes," he said, "do friends have their values, the way household slaves do? One slave may be worth two minae, another only half a mina, a third five, a fourth as much as ten; and they say that Nicias, the son of Niceratus, paid a whole talent for a superintendent for his silver mines. So I've been putting the question to myself: do friends, like slaves, have their market prices?"

"No doubt about it," Antisthenes replied. "I know a man I would rather have as my friend than be paid two minae, and another I wouldn't value at half a mina, and a third I wouldn't part with for ten — and then there's one whose friendship would be cheap even if it cost me all the money and effort in the world to win it."

"Well then," Socrates went on, "if that's so, wouldn't it be a good thing for every man to examine himself and ask what he happens to be worth to his friends — and then try to make himself as valuable as possible, so that his friends won't be tempted to give him up? How often I hear the complaint: 'My friend So-and-so has dropped me,' or 'A man I counted a friend sold me out for a mina.' And every time I hear it, the question comes to me: if the owner of a worthless slave is ready to sell him to any buyer for whatever he'll fetch, isn't there at least a strong temptation to let a worthless friend go when there's something to be gained on the exchange? What I never see is good slaves being sold off that way — or good friends lightly given up."
