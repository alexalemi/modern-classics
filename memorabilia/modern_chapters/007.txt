Chapter 8

At another time he ran into an old friend he had not seen in years, and greeted him: "Where in the world have you appeared from, Eutherus?"

"From abroad, Socrates — I got back just before the war ended," he answered. "But at the moment, from right here in the city. Since we lost our holdings across the border, and my father left me nothing in Attica, I have to stay home now and earn my bread with my own hands. It seems better than begging from other people — especially since I own nothing I could borrow against."

"And how long do you expect your body to hold up, working for wages like that?"

"Honestly, Socrates — not long."

"And when you find yourself an old man, your expenses won't shrink, but no one will pay for the labor of your hands anymore."

"That's true."

"Then wouldn't it be better to take up work right now that will still support you when you're old? Go to some large landowner who needs help managing his estate. Supervise his projects, help bring in his harvest, look after his property — you'd be a benefit to the estate, and the estate would be a benefit to you."

"I couldn't stand the harness of slavery, Socrates!" he burst out.

"And yet the men who run the departments of a city aren't considered slaves for managing public property. If anything, they're considered freer than most."

"To put it plainly, Socrates, I simply can't stomach being answerable to anyone."

"And yet, Eutherus, it's hard to find any work that doesn't make you answerable to somebody. It's difficult to do anything at all without some mistake, and even if you managed to do it flawlessly, it would be difficult to escape unfair criticism. I wonder whether you get through your present work entirely free of complaints. No? Then here's what you should do. Avoid the fault-finders and attach yourself to fair-minded, considerate people. Take on what you can do with a good grace, and decline what you feel you can't. And whatever you do take on, throw your whole heart into it and make it your finest work. That is the way to silence the critics and ease your own difficulties at a stroke. Life will run more smoothly, the risks will shrink, and your old age will be provided for."

Chapter 9

At another time, I know, he heard Crito remark that life in Athens was no easy thing for a man who wanted to mind his own business.

"Right at this moment," Crito went on, "there's a set of men threatening me with lawsuits — not because they have any wrongdoing to charge me with, but simply because they've concluded I would rather pay them off than be bothered any further."

"Tell me, Crito," Socrates replied, "you keep dogs to drive the wolves off your sheep, don't you?"

"Certainly — it more than pays for itself."

"Then why not keep a man, willing and able, to drive off this pack that's trying to prey on you?"

"I'd gladly do it," he answered, "if I weren't afraid he'd turn and savage his own keeper."

"What! Don't you see that a man like that would find it far pleasanter — and far more profitable — to gratify a man like you than to quarrel with you? Believe me, there are men in this city who would take real pride in having you for a friend."

So they sought out Archedemus — a practical man with a clever tongue in his head, but poor. He wasn't the sort to make money by hook or by crook; he loved honesty, and he was too good-natured himself to earn a living shaking people down. Crito would take the opportunity at harvest time to set aside small presents for Archedemus — grain, oil, wine, wool, whatever staples the farm produced — and he would invite him to a sacrificial feast, and show him attention in other ways. Archedemus came to feel that he had a harbor of refuge in Crito's house, and he could not do enough for his patron. Before long he had hunted up a whole list of offenses that could be lodged against Crito's shakedown artists — and not only their many crimes but their many enemies as well. Presently he prosecuted one of them in a public suit, the kind where the court decides what the defendant must suffer or what he must pay. The man, knowing how much he had to answer for, tried everything to get free of Archedemus — but Archedemus would not let go. He held on until the informer not only dropped his grip on Crito but paid Archedemus a sum of money on top of it.

Once Archedemus had won this victory and a few others like it, what followed is easy to guess. It was just like when a shepherd gets an especially good dog: the other shepherds all want to pasture their flocks nearby, so they can get the benefit of the dog too. A number of Crito's friends came begging him to let Archedemus be their guardian as well, and Archedemus was overjoyed to do Crito the favor. So it came about that not only Crito lived in peace, but his friends did too. If anyone Archedemus was on bad terms with threw it in his face that he took his patron's favors and paid for them in flattery, he had his answer ready: "Which is more shameful — to accept kindnesses from good and honest men and repay them, so that I make such men my friends and the scoundrels my enemies? Or to wrong decent, honorable men and make enemies of them, on the off chance of winning a few rogues to my side — and the certainty of ruining the company I keep?"

From then on Archedemus was Crito's right hand, and the rest of Crito's friends held him in honor.

Chapter 10

I can also cite, from my own knowledge, the following discussion; the arguments were addressed to Diodorus, one of his companions.

"Tell me, Diodorus," Socrates said, "if one of your slaves runs away, do you take trouble to recover him?"

"More than that," Diodorus answered. "I call in others to help, and I post a reward for bringing him back."

"And if one of your household servants falls sick, do you look after him and call in doctors to save his life?"

"I certainly do."

"Then if an acquaintance — someone worth far more to you than any slave in your household — were about to perish from want, wouldn't you think it your business to take trouble to save his life? Now, you know without my telling you that Hermogenes is not made of wood or stone. If you helped him, he would be ashamed not to repay you in kind. And think what you would be getting: a willing, kindly, trustworthy helper, well suited to doing what you ask — and not just that, but capable of coming up with useful ideas on his own, a man with real foresight and judgment. A man like that is worth dozens of slaves. Good managers tell us that when something valuable can be had at a low price, that is the time to buy. And with times as bad as they are now, good and honest friends can be had exceedingly cheap."

"You're quite right, Socrates," Diodorus answered. "Tell Hermogenes to come to me."

"Tell Hermogenes to come to you? Not I! As far as I can see, you have no better right to summon him than to go to him yourself — and the gain is no more his than yours."

So Diodorus went off at once to find Hermogenes, and at no great cost he won himself a friend — a friend whose one concern from then on was to find some way, by word or deed, to help Diodorus and make him glad.
