Chapter 3

I have said that Socrates helped the people around him partly by the example he set and partly by what he said. To back that up, let me write down what I remember, beginning with religion and everything that concerns the gods.

In word and deed alike, he followed the rule the Pythia, Apollo's priestess at Delphi, gives to anyone who asks how to handle a sacrifice, or honoring ancestors, or anything of that kind. Her answer is: "Follow the law and custom of your own city, and you will be acting piously." That is how Socrates behaved, and that is what he urged others to do; people who acted on any other principle he considered fussy and full of themselves.

His prayer was simple: "Give me what is best for me." The gods, he said, know best what the good things actually are. Praying for gold or silver or absolute power made no more sense than praying for a particular throw of the dice, or a particular turn in a battle — things whose consequences no one can foresee.

His means were small, and so were his sacrifices, but he never felt this put him a step below people who offered great quantities from great wealth. It would be a sorry thing for the gods themselves, he thought, if they took more delight in big offerings than in small ones — for then the gifts of bad men would often please them more than the gifts of good men. And from our side, life would hardly be worth living if Heaven welcomed a villain's offering over a righteous man's. No: he believed the gods' joy is measured by the holiness of the giver. He was always quoting the line of Hesiod that says,

According to your ability, sacrifice to the immortal gods.

"Yes," he would say, "and in our dealings with friends and strangers, and in everything life demands of us, there's no better motto than that: let a man do according to his ability."

Or take another point. If he believed a sign had been given him from heaven, nothing on earth could make him go against it. You could sooner have talked him into taking a blind man who didn't know the road as his guide, in place of a sighted man who did. He said the same about others: it is folly to act against the warnings of God just to dodge some embarrassment in front of men. He himself held every human aid cheap next to counsel from above.

The way of life he trained his soul and body to was one that would let almost anyone who adopted it face existence cheerfully and pass his days in peace — and it certainly cost nothing to speak of. It was so frugal that I doubt any man could work so little as not to earn what contented Socrates. He ate just enough for eating to be a pleasure, and he came to the table with the only sauce he needed — an appetite. Any drink tasted good to him, because he never drank unless he was thirsty. If he accepted a dinner invitation, he had no trouble avoiding the trap most people fall into, of eating too much; and his advice for those who couldn't manage it was to stay away from anything that tempts you to eat when you're not hungry or drink when you're not thirsty. That sort of thing, he said, ruins your stomach, your head, and your soul. "It must have been by feasting men on so many fancy dishes," he liked to say, half joking, "that Circe turned them into pigs. Odysseus kept his self-control — that, and a timely warning from Hermes — and didn't gorge on them, and so he never turned into a swine." That was his way with the subject: light, and dead serious.

As for the affairs of Aphrodite, his advice was to stay well clear of beautiful people: touch that fire once, and it is not easy to keep a sound head and a sober mind. Take one case in particular. He had heard that Critobulus had kissed a good-looking boy — the son of Alcibiades. So with Critobulus standing right there, Socrates put a question to me.

"Tell me, Xenophon," he said, "haven't you always taken Critobulus for a sensible man rather than a rash one — prudent, not thoughtless or reckless?"

"Certainly," I said, "that's what I'd have said of him."

"Well, from now on consider him the opposite — a hot-headed daredevil. This is the kind of man who would turn somersaults over drawn knives, or jump into a fire."

"And what have you seen him do," I asked, "that you give him such a reputation?"

"Do? Hasn't the man dared to kiss the son of Alcibiades — the best-looking boy in his prime?"

"Well," I said, "if that's the daredevil stunt in question, I think I could face that danger myself."

"You poor soul," he said. "And what do you think happens to you after that kiss? Let me tell you. That instant, you lose your freedom — the papers of your slavery are as good as signed. You'll be forced to spend a fortune on pleasures that hurt you. You'll have hardly a free moment for anything worth studying. You'll find yourself chasing, with all your energy, things no man would choose to chase — not even a madman."

"Heracles!" I said. "What a terrible power to live in one kiss!"

"Does that surprise you?" he said. "Don't you know the tarantula, no bigger than a small coin, has only to touch your mouth and it racks its victim with pain and drives him out of his mind?"

"Yes," I said, "but the tarantula injects something when it bites."

"You fool — and you think these beautiful creatures inject nothing with a kiss, just because you can't see the poison? Don't you know that this wild beast people call 'beauty in its bloom' is deadlier than any tarantula? The spider at least has to touch you. This thing needs no contact at all — let a man merely look at it, even from far off, and it injects something into him that drives him mad. Maybe that's why the Loves are called 'archers': because beauty wounds from a distance. My advice to you, Xenophon, is that whenever you catch sight of one of these beautiful faces, you run for your life and don't look back. And to you, Critobulus: go abroad for a year. Even that may barely be long enough for the wound to heal."

That, he said, is how anyone on unsteady footing should handle the affairs of Aphrodite, just as with food and drink: keep to what the soul could do without entirely, if the body didn't require it — and even then, nothing that stirs up trouble. But he himself, plainly, was armed at every point and could not be touched. He found it easier to keep away from beauty in its fullest bloom than most men find it to refuse weeds and garbage. To sum up: in eating and drinking and all these temptations of the senses, the equipment of his soul made him independent. He could honestly claim that his modest pleasures were no smaller than the pleasures of people who work so hard to get theirs — and his pains were far fewer.

Chapter 4

There is a view in circulation, based on what certain people say and write about Socrates — guesswork in both cases — that however good he was at firing men up about virtue in theory, he was incapable of actually leading them to it. Those who hold this view should weigh carefully not just the way he used cross-examination to take down people who imagined they knew everything, but also his everyday conversation with the friends who spent their time in his company. Then let them decide whether he was incapable of making his companions better.

I will start with a conversation I once heard him have about divinity with Aristodemus, "the little," as he was called. Socrates had noticed that Aristodemus neither sacrificed to the gods nor paid any attention to divination — in fact, he made fun of people who did.

"Tell me, Aristodemus," he began, "are there any human beings you admire for their genius?"

"There are," he said.

"Give us their names."

"In epic poetry, the one I admire most is Homer. In dithyrambs — choral hymns — Melanippides. Sophocles in tragedy, Polyclitus in sculpture, Zeuxis in painting."

"And which do you consider more admirable: someone who fashions senseless images that cannot move, or someone who could fashion living creatures with minds and motion of their own?"

"The latter, by far — provided the living creatures came about by design and weren't just an accident."

"Now suppose you had two kinds of things: one gives no clue what it's for, and the other obviously serves some useful purpose. Which would you judge the work of chance, and which of design?"

"Clearly the one made for a useful end is the work of design."

"Then doesn't it strike you that whoever made human beings in the first place gave them each of their senses for a useful end? Eyes to see the visible world, ears to catch sound. What good would smells be if we hadn't been given nostrils? What sense of sweet and sharp and every pleasure of the palate, if a tongue hadn't been fashioned in us to report on them? And beyond that — doesn't this look like foresight: that the delicate eyeball is shuttered with eyelids like folding doors, which open wide when we need to see and close firmly in sleep? That eyelashes are planted as a screen, so even the wind can't strike the eye too roughly? That the brow above is finished with a ledge of eyebrow, so sweat running off the head can't get in and do damage? That the ear catches every sound and never fills up? That the front teeth of every animal are made for cutting, and the back ones for taking the food and grinding it down? That the mouth, the intake gate for everything the creature needs, is set close to the eyes and nose — while the exits for waste, which is unpleasant, are turned away behind and routed as far as possible from the senses? When you see all this built with such evident foresight, can you really be in doubt whether it's the product of chance or of intelligence?"

"No — put that way, certainly not. It looks like the handiwork of some wise craftsman who loves living things."

"And what about the passion planted in man to beget children, and in the mother to rear her baby, and in the creature itself, once born, this deep desire to live and this fear of death?"

"No doubt those look like the contrivances of someone who deliberately planned for living creatures to exist."

"And you — you'd say you have a spark of wisdom in you yourself?"

"Ask your questions and I'll answer."

"And yet you imagine wisdom exists nowhere else? You know your body holds only a tiny fragment of the vast earth, a little drop of all the waters, a small portion of each of the other elements, huge as they are, that went into assembling your frame. Mind alone, apparently — which is found nowhere — you had the luck to snatch up out of nothing, you can't say how. And these things around us, enormous in size and countless in number, keep their perfect order, you suppose, through some absence of intelligence?"

"Maybe so — because I can't see the master agents behind them, the way I can see the makers of things produced here on earth."

"You can't see your own soul either, and it is the master agent of your body. By that logic you're entitled to say you do nothing by intelligence, and everything by chance."

At this point Aristodemus said, "I assure you, Socrates, I don't look down on the divine power. On the contrary — I believe the Divinity is too grand to need any service of mine."

"Then the grander the power that condescends to attend to you, the more you owe it honor."

"Believe me," he said, "if I could believe the gods take any thought for human beings, I would not neglect them."

"How can you suppose they don't? Who, in the first place, gave man alone among the animals his upright posture — letting him see farther ahead, gaze more freely at what is above, and come to less harm than the creatures that share his eyes and ears and mouth? Consider next: to the beasts of the field they gave feet, good only for getting around, but to man they gave hands as well — the hands that have done more than anything to lift us above the animals in happiness. And the tongue: every animal has one, but in man they fashioned it to touch different parts of the mouth at different moments, so that we can produce articulate speech and signal everything we want to one another. Or consider the pleasures of sex: in the rest of the animal kingdom, confined to fixed seasons; in man, continuing unbroken into old age. And it did not content the Godhead merely to look after man's body. Far more important, he planted in man the noblest and most excellent kind of soul. What other creature has a soul that can even recognize the existence of the gods who arranged this grand and beautiful universe? What other tribe of animals serves the gods at all? What spirit is better than man's at guarding against hunger and thirst, cold and heat, at relieving sickness and building strength? What is better suited to hard work for the sake of learning, or more capable of storing away in memory everything it has heard or seen or understood? Isn't it perfectly obvious to you that, beside the other animals, human beings live like gods — supreme by nature in body and soul alike? A creature with a man's mind in an ox's body couldn't carry out any of its intentions; and hands attached to a witless brute are useless. You have received both of these priceless gifts — and your considered opinion is that the gods take no thought for you? What exactly do you want them to do before you'll believe you are in their thoughts?"

"When they treat me the way you say they treat you," said Aristodemus, "and send me counselors to tell me what to do and what to avoid — then I'll believe."

"Send you counselors! And when the people of Athens consult the oracle and the gods answer — you're an Athenian, aren't you? Don't you think the answer is addressed to you too? When they send portents to warn the cities of Greece, or all mankind — you're a man, a Greek. Aren't those meant for you as well? Or are you the one person singled out for divine neglect? Again: do you suppose the gods could have planted in human hearts the belief that they have power to do us good or harm, if they had no such power? Wouldn't men have found out the fraud in all this time? Don't you see that the wisest and longest-lasting of human institutions — cities and whole peoples — are always the most god-fearing, and that in the individual, the riper the age and judgment, the deeper the religion? My good man," he went on, "take this to heart. Your own mind, inside you, turns and directs your body as it pleases; in the same way you ought to believe that the wisdom dwelling in the whole universe disposes of all things as it sees fit. Your eye can range over many miles — is the eye of God powerless to take in everything at a glance? Your soul can dwell in thought on affairs here, or in Egypt, or in Sicily — is the thought of God not sufficient to care for everything at once? With human beings, you learn who is willing to return your kindness by doing them acts of service; you get at the secret of a man's wisdom by asking his advice. Try the gods the same way: serve them, and see whether they will counsel you in matters hidden from mortal sight. You will discover that the Godhead is of such nature and such greatness as to see all things and hear all things at once, to be present everywhere, and to let nothing escape its watchful care."

To my mind, talk like this made his companions hold back from anything unholy, shameful, or unjust — not only when other men could see them, but even when they were alone, since they had to believe that nothing they did could escape the eye of Heaven.

Chapter 5

I suppose everyone will grant that self-control is a noble thing for a man to possess. If so, consider whether talk like the following was likely to lead his listeners toward it. "Gentlemen," he would say, "if a war broke out and we wanted to choose the man best able to save us and defeat the enemy, would we pick someone we knew to be a slave to his belly, to wine, to lust — a man who folds at the first hardship or nods off on watch? Could we expect a man like that to save us, or to beat anybody? Or suppose a man near the end of his life wanted to find someone to educate his sons, guard his unmarried daughters, and keep his property safe — would he consider a man without self-control fit for any of those trusts? Why, no one would put his flocks and herds, his storehouses and barns, or the oversight of his work in the hands of a slave with no self-control. If someone offered us a steward or an errand boy of that character as a free gift, we'd turn him down. And if a man won't accept a slave without self-control, how hard should the master work to avoid deserving the same label himself? It's not with the intemperate as with the greedy and the grasping. Those men, whatever they take from others, can at least be said to enrich themselves. But the man with no self-control can't claim to be a blessing to himself while he's a curse to his neighbors. The harm he does others is nothing next to the harm that falls back on himself — for the height of harm is to ruin not just your own house and property but your own body and your own soul. And in company: who enjoys a guest who obviously cares more for the wine and the food than for the friends beside him — who shortchanges his comrades of their due affection to fawn over a mistress? Doesn't it come down to this: every honest man should treat self-control as the very cornerstone of virtue, and lay it first as the foundation of his soul? Without it, who can learn anything good or practice it to any degree worth mentioning? What slave of pleasure won't end up degraded in body and soul together? By Hera, every free man should pray to be spared having such a slave — and every man enslaved to such pleasures should beg Heaven to send him good masters, because that is the one hope of rescue left to him."

Well-tempered words — but his self-control showed even more in his actions than in his talk. He was master not only of the pleasures of the body but of those that money feeds, too. His belief was that a man who takes money from any chance donor sets up a master over himself, and binds himself to a slavery as degrading as any there is.
