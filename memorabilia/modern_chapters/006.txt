Chapter 6

Then there is the question of testing — how do you tell whether a friend is worth winning before you commit? On that subject, I think what Socrates said to Critobulus could not fail to be instructive.

"Tell me," he said, "suppose we found ourselves in need of a good and honest friend — how would we go about finding one? First, I suppose, we would look for a man who is master of his appetites: not ruled by his belly, not addicted to wine or sex or sleep or laziness. No one enslaved to tyrants like those could do his duty by himself or by his friends, could he?"

"Certainly not," Critobulus answered.

"Then you agree we should keep our distance from anyone under that kind of rule?"

"Absolutely."

"Well then," Socrates went on, "what about the spendthrift, who can never stand on his own feet and is forever begging from his neighbors — who can't repay what he gets, and hates you when you refuse to give? Don't you think he too would make a disagreeable friend?"

"Certainly."

"So we keep away from him as well?"

"We must."

"And what about the man whose great talent is money dealings? His one craving is to pile up cash, which makes him an expert at driving a hard bargain — delighted to take in, very reluctant to pay out."

"In my opinion he's an even worse specimen than the last."

"And the man whose passion for making money is so consuming that he has no time for anything that doesn't add to his gains?"

"Keep away from him too, I say. There's no good to be had from him or his company."

"And the quarrelsome, factious type, whose chief accomplishment is saddling his friends with a crowd of enemies?"

"For God's sake, let's avoid him as well."

"Now imagine a man free of every one of those defects — a man with no objection to receiving kindness, but it never enters his head to return any."

"There's no use in him either. But Socrates, what kind of man should we try to make our friend? What does he look like?"

"The exact opposite of all the above, I'd say. He has control over the pleasures of the body; he is kind, and fair in all his dealings; and he is fiercely determined never to be outdone in kindness by anyone who treats him well, so that his friends actually profit from knowing him."

"But how do we test those qualities, Socrates, before we know the man?"

"How do we test a sculptor? Not by listening to the artist talk. We look at what he has already made: if his earlier statues were beautifully done, we trust the next ones will be too."

"You mean if we find a man who has treated his old friends well, we can take it as proved that he'll treat his new ones just as well?"

"Certainly. When I see a man who has handled horses skillfully before, I expect him to handle the next ones skillfully too."

"Good. And once we've found a man whose friendship is worth having, how do we make him our friend?"

"First we should ask the will of Heaven whether it's advisable to make him our friend at all."

"Well, and suppose the gods approve our choice — how do we go about capturing him? Will you tell me that?"

"Not by running him down like a hare," said Socrates, "not by decoying him like a bird, and not by force like a wild boar. Capturing a friend against his will is heavy labor, and chaining him up like a slave is no easy business either. Men treated that way tend to become enemies, not friends."

"Then how do we turn them into friends?"

"They say there are incantations that those who know them need only recite to make a friend of anyone they please — and love potions too, which those who hold the secret can slip to whomever they like and win their affection."

"And where would we learn them?"

"You needn't go any farther than Homer for the one the Sirens sang to Odysseus. It begins, I think, like this: 'Come hither, come, Odysseus, famous man, great glory of the Achaeans!'"

"And did that spell work on everyone, Socrates? Did the Sirens only have to sing that one incantation, and every listener was rooted to the spot?"

"No. That was the incantation reserved for souls hungry for fame and in love with virtue."

"Which is as much as to say we must fit the incantation to the listener, so that when he hears it he doesn't suspect the enchanter is laughing at him behind his hand. I certainly can't imagine a better recipe for hatred and disgust than going up to a man who knows he is small and ugly and weak, and murmuring in his ear that he is tall and handsome and strong. But do you know any other love-charms, Socrates?"

"I can't say I do. Though I've heard that Pericles knew quite a few, which he poured into the ear of our city until she loved him."

"And how did Themistocles win the city's love?"

"Ah, that was no incantation at all. What he did was hang around her neck an amulet of real protection."

"You're implying, aren't you, Socrates, that if we want to win the love of a good man, we must be good ourselves — in what we say and what we do?"

"Did you imagine a bad man could make good friends?"

"Well," said Critobulus, "I could swear I've seen sorry public speakers on the closest terms with great and noble statesmen, and men utterly incapable of command who were boon companions of born generals."

"But to stick to the point we were discussing: do you know of anyone who can attach a useful friend to himself without being useful in return? Can service and disservice make an alliance?"

"Honestly, no. But grant that a worthless man can't be friends with the beautiful and good — then here is what worries me next: can a man who is himself beautiful and good attach himself, with a wave of the hand as it were, to every other beautiful and good nature?"

"What perplexes you, Critobulus, is that so often men of noble conduct, men whose souls are far above anything base, are not friends at all but at strife with one another — and treat each other more harshly than they would treat the most worthless of mankind."

"Yes, and it isn't only private individuals. Whole cities — the very ones most devoted to noble policy and least tolerant of anything base — are frequently at war with one another. When I think about that, my heart sinks, and the whole question of how friends are won fills me with despair. The bad, as I see it, can't be friends with each other. How could the ungrateful, the reckless, the greedy, the faithless, the self-indulgent hold together as friends? Without hesitation I set the bad down as born to be enemies, not friends — it's stamped on them from birth. But then, as you suggest, neither can they harmonize with the good. How should men who do evil be friends with men who hate all evil-doing? And if, on top of everything, the very men who cultivate virtue tear each other apart fighting for first place in their cities, envying one another, hating one another — then who is left to be friends? Where in the world are goodwill and loyalty to be found?"

"The truth is, Critobulus, there's some subtlety in the weave of these things. Nature planted the seeds of friendship in human beings. We need one another; we feel pity; we help each other by joining forces, and we're grateful when we're helped. But nature planted seeds of war as well. Since we all find the same things beautiful and pleasant, we fight over their possession; disagreement creeps in, and men range themselves in opposite camps. Quarreling and anger sound the note of war; the passion for always having more is a hostile force; and envy is a hateful fiend.

"And yet through every one of those barriers friendship steals her way and binds the beautiful and good together. Such is their virtue that they would rather own a modest living in peace than rule an empire won by war. Hungry and thirsty as they may be, they can share their food and drink without a pang. Neither the bloom of youth nor the delights of love can crack their self-control or tempt them to cause pain where none should be. They don't merely renounce greed and divide wealth justly and lawfully — they supply what is missing from one another's needs. They can settle quarrels, not just harmlessly but to everyone's advantage. They can keep anger from running to lengths a man would repent of later. And envy they sweep away entirely: a good man treats what he owns as belonging to his friends, and what his friends own as belonging to him. Is it really so improbable, then, that the beautiful and good should share the honors of the state not only without injuring each other, but to their mutual advantage?

"Men who covet honors and offices for the license they give — to embezzle public money, to bully their fellow men, to wallow in luxury — such men are unjust and rotten, and incapable of harmony with anyone. But if a man wants those same honors so that he can be secure against wrongdoing himself, help his friends in what is right, and, once raised to high position, try to do his country some good — what is there to stop him from working in harmony with another man of the same spirit? Will he be less able to help his friends with the beautiful and good at his side? Will his power to benefit the community shrink because the flower of the community are his fellow workers? Even in the athletic games it's obvious that if the strongest competitors were allowed to combine against the weakest, that team would win every event and carry off every prize. The rules of the arena forbid it — but in politics, where the beautiful and good hold the field and no one stops you from joining with anyone you please for the city's benefit, surely it's pure gain for a public man to make the best men his friends: partners and collaborators in his work instead of rivals and obstacles. And this much is obvious too: if it comes to war with a foreign power, a man will need allies — and all the more if the men lined up against him are the flower of the enemy. Besides, those willing to fight your battles must be treated well, so that goodwill can quicken into real enthusiasm; and one good man is a better investment than a dozen scoundrels, because a little kindness goes a long way with the good, while the base only ask for more the more you give them.

"So keep your spirits up, Critobulus. Just try to become good yourself, and once you've managed that, set your hand to capturing the beautiful and good. Perhaps I can even help you in the hunt, being something of an expert in the lore of love. Whenever my heart is set on someone, my whole soul leaps toward him: I love, and I demand to be loved in return; I long for him, and need him to long for me; I thirst for his company, and need him to thirst for mine. You will need all the same things, I can see, whenever you're seized by the desire for a friendship. So don't hide from me whom you'd like as a friend. I take such pains to please anyone who pleases me that I'm not entirely unpracticed, I think, in the art of catching men."

"Why, Socrates," Critobulus replied, "those are exactly the lessons I've been thirsting for — especially if this same lore of love will let me capture men who are good in soul and beautiful in person."

"Now hold on, Critobulus. Nothing in my science makes the beautiful stand still while somebody lays hands on them. I'm convinced men fled from Scylla precisely because she laid hands on them; the Sirens laid hands on nobody — they sat far off and sang their spells into every ear, and so, the story goes, all men stayed to listen and were charmed."

"I promise I won't lay hands on anyone. So if you have a good device for winning friends, teach your pupil."

"And if there's to be no laying on of hands, no touching of lips either — agreed?"

"Agreed. I won't put my lips to anyone's — who isn't beautiful."

"There you go, Critobulus — you can't open your mouth without some luckless remark. The beautiful won't stand for that kind of liberty, however eagerly the ugly invite it, flattering themselves that some quality of soul makes them beautiful too."

"Be of good cheer, then. Let our compact stand: kisses for the beautiful, and for the good, a rain of kisses. Now teach us the art of catching friends."

"Well then. When you want to win someone's affection, will you allow me to inform on you — to report to him that you admire him and want to be his friend?"

"File the charge, with all my heart. I never heard of anyone who hated his admirers."

"And if I add a second charge — that because you admire him, you feel real goodwill toward him — you won't think I'm blackening your character?"

"No. For my part, I know a warm feeling springs up in me toward anyone I believe feels warmly toward me."

"All that, then, I'll feel free to say about you to the men whose friendship you want. And I can promise further help — on one large condition: that you authorize me to say you are devoted to your friends; that nothing delights you like a good and honest friend; that you take as much pride in your friends' fine deeds as in your own, and as much pleasure in their good fortune as in yours; that you never tire of scheming and planning to bring them a rich harvest of both; and that you have discovered a man's virtue is to outdo his friends in kindness and his enemies in harm. Authorize that report, and I think you'll find me a useful fellow hunter in the quest for friends — which is the conquest of the good."

"Why ask my permission — as if you weren't free to say whatever you like about me?"

"No, that I deny, on the authority of Aspasia. I have it from her own lips. Good matchmakers, she told me, are wonderfully effective at bringing people together — so long as the good qualities they vouch for are truthfully reported. But lies she could not recommend: the poor deluded victims end up hating each other and the go-between as well. I'm so thoroughly persuaded she's right that I don't believe I have the power to say anything in your praise that I can't say truthfully."

"What a wonderfully good friend you are to me, Socrates! If I have any merit that entitles me to win a friend, you'll lend me a hand — otherwise, you'd rather not invent even the smallest fiction for my benefit."

"And which way do you think I'd help you more — by praising you falsely, or by persuading you to try to be a good man? If it isn't obvious, look at it by the light of a few examples. Suppose I want to make a shipowner your friend, and I start by praising you to him falsely: 'You'll find him a good pilot.' He takes me at my word and hands his ship over to you — you, who have no idea how to steer one. What can you expect but to wreck the ship and yourself together? Or suppose by the same sort of lying I persuade the whole city to entrust its fortunes to you — 'a born commander,' I say, 'a practiced lawyer, a natural statesman.' The odds are that you and the city both come to grief through you. Or take a case from everyday life: by my lies I persuade some private citizen to hand you his affairs as 'a careful, businesslike man with a real head for management.' When the test came, wouldn't your administration be ruinous, and the figure you cut ridiculous? No, my dear friend, there is one road only — the shortest, the safest, the best — and it is simply this: whatever you want to be thought good at, try to be good at. Run through all the virtues men can name, and you'll find there isn't one that can't be increased by learning and practice. Those, Critobulus, are the principles I think we should hunt on. If you see it differently, teach me — I'm all attention."

"Really, Socrates," said Critobulus, "I'd be ashamed to argue against a word of it. If I did, what I said would be neither noble nor true."

Chapter 7

He had two ways of dealing with his friends' difficulties. Where ignorance was the trouble, he treated it with a dose of common sense; where want and poverty were to blame, he taught them to help one another according to their means. Let me set down some incidents I know of firsthand. Once, for instance, he came across Aristarchus wearing the look of a man with a black cloud over him, and greeted him like this:

"You seem to have something weighing on your mind, Aristarchus. If so, you should share the load with your friends. Perhaps together we can lighten it a little."

"I'm in desperate straits, Socrates, and that's the truth," Aristarchus answered. "Ever since the civil strife broke out in the city, what with the rush of people down to Piraeus and all the banishments, my poor stranded female relatives have descended on me. Sisters, nieces, cousins — they've all come flocking to me for protection. I have fourteen free-born souls under my one roof, I tell you, and how are we supposed to live? We can get nothing from the land — the enemy holds it. Nothing from my houses in town — the city is nearly empty. No one will buy the furniture, and there's no money to be borrowed anywhere; you'd have better luck finding it lying in the road than getting it out of a banker. It's a hard thing, Socrates, to stand by and watch your relatives starve — and impossible to feed so many in times like these."

Socrates heard him out, then asked, "How is it that Ceramon, with just as many mouths to feed, not only manages to supply himself and his people with the necessities of life but clears a handsome surplus, while you, in the same fix, are afraid you'll all starve for lack of them?"

"Bless you — because he feeds slaves, and I have free-born souls to feed!"

"And which would you say are the better human beings — the free people in your house, or Ceramon's slaves?"

"The free souls under my roof, without a doubt."

"Then isn't it a disgrace that he should prosper on the strength of his inferior household while you, with your far superior one, are in trouble?"

"Of course it is — but he supports a set of craftsmen, and I support people with a liberal education."

"And what is a craftsman? Doesn't the word cover anyone who knows how to make something useful?"

"Certainly."

"Is barley meal useful?"

"Extremely."

"And loaves of bread?"

"No less."

"What about cloaks for men and women — tunics, mantles, vests?"

"All highly useful things."

"And the people in your house don't know how to make any of them?"

"On the contrary, I believe they can make them all."

"Then are you not aware that from just one of those trades — his barley-meal business — Nausicydes not only maintains himself and his household staff but keeps pigs and cattle besides, and clears such profits that he regularly makes voluntary contributions to the state? That Cyrebus feeds his whole establishment from a bakery and lives in comfort? That Demeas of Collytus makes his living from cloaks, Menon from mantles, and more than half of Megara from the making of vests?"

"Yes — but those men buy foreign slaves and keep them, and can force them to make whatever pays. My kinswomen are free-born ladies."

"So because they are free-born and your relatives, you think they should do nothing but eat and sleep? Is that what you observe of free people generally — that the ones who live that way are happier and more enviable than the ones who put their time into the useful skills they actually possess? Is that how the world works — that for learning what's worth knowing, and remembering what you've learned, and keeping the body healthy and strong, and getting and keeping the things that make life worth living, idleness and inattention are helpful, while work and study are a dead loss? When your relatives learned the skills you say they know, did they learn them as useless ornaments, never to be put to practical account — or as something they were meant to take seriously one day and profit by? Which makes human beings more sensible: idling, or attending to useful work? Which helps a man more toward justice and uprightness: doing, or sitting with folded hands calculating how to pay for the next meal? As things stand, if I'm not mistaken, there's no love lost in your house. You can't help feeling that they're costing you dearly, and they can see that you find them a burden. That is a dangerous state of affairs: bitterness on both sides is likely to grow, and the old affection is likely to snap.

"But give them free scope for their energies, and everything changes. When you see how useful they are, you'll grow genuinely fond of them; and when they see they can please you, they'll cling to their benefactor warmly. The memory of old kindnesses will grow sweeter, and the gratitude flowing from new ones will multiply tenfold; you'll be bound together closer, more like a family. Now, if the work in question were anything shameful, they should choose death before it. But as it is, the things they know are exactly the arts regarded as the loveliest and most fitting for women; and what any of us knows, we do easily and quickly — it's a joy to do, and the result is beautiful. So don't hesitate to propose to them what will benefit them and you alike. They will probably be glad to accept."

"Honestly, Socrates," Aristarchus answered, "what you say is so good that although until now I haven't been willing to borrow — knowing that once the money was spent I'd have no way to repay it — I think I can bring myself to do it now, to raise a fund for the work."

So capital was raised and wool was bought. The women worked while they ate their breakfast and worked on until supper ended the day, and the house grew cheerful instead of gloomy. Frowns gave way to smiles; instead of eyeing one another sideways with suspicion, they looked each other happily in the face. The women loved their kinsman for his kindness; he grew attached to them as helpers. In the end he came to Socrates and told him with delight how matters stood — "and now," he added, "they accuse me of being the only drone in the house, eating the bread of idleness."

"Then why not tell them the fable of the dog?" said Socrates. "Once upon a time, when animals could talk, the story goes, a sheep said to her master, 'It's a strange thing, master: we sheep give you fleeces and lambs and cheese, and you give us nothing we don't crop from the ground ourselves — while this dog of yours, who gives you nothing of the kind, gets shares of the very food from your own mouth.' The dog heard her and answered promptly, 'And so I should — for I'm the one who keeps you safe and sound, so that you aren't stolen by men or carried off by wolves. If I didn't stand guard over you, you couldn't so much as graze in the field for fear of being killed.' And at that, so the tale goes, the sheep admitted the dog was rightly honored above them. So tell your flock that you are the dog in the fable — their guardian and overseer — and it's thanks to you that they can do their work and live their lives in safety, with no one to harm them."
