(Part 2 of 6)

Chapter 2

Then there was a third type: the people convinced they had already received the best education there is, and proud of their wisdom. Let me describe how he dealt with these.

Euthydemus "the handsome" had, Socrates learned, collected a large library of the most celebrated poets and philosophers, and on the strength of it he already considered himself more than a match for anyone his age in wisdom — and fully expected, before long, to outshine them all in his capacity for speech and action alike. At first, being too young to enter the marketplace, he would settle himself in a saddler's shop nearby whenever he had business to conduct. So that is where Socrates went, taking a few of his companions along. Someone opened by raising a question: was it through keeping company with the wise, or by his own unaided talent, that Themistocles had risen so far above his fellow citizens that the whole city instinctively turned to him whenever it needed a capable man? Socrates, aiming to stir up Euthydemus, answered that it was charmingly naive to believe that the minor arts couldn't be mastered without qualified teachers, while leadership of the state — the greatest concern of all — was supposed to drop into a man's lap by itself, like a windfall.

On a later visit, Euthydemus was there but visibly keeping his distance, as though the last thing he wanted was to appear to admire Socrates for wisdom. So Socrates remarked, "Gentlemen, it's obvious from how our friend Euthydemus spends his time that once he comes of age, he won't hold back his advice when the state puts a question up for debate. And you can imagine the elegant opening he has prepared for his speeches, given how anxious he is not to be suspected of ever learning anything from anybody. Clearly he'll begin like this: 'Men of Athens, I have never at any time learned anything from anyone. When I heard of a man able in speech and action, I made no attempt to meet him. I never troubled to find myself a teacher among those who know. Quite the reverse: I have steadily avoided not merely learning from others, but even the faintest appearance of it. However, whatever occurs to me on its own, I will gladly share with you.' Wouldn't that opening suit a candidate for city physician just as well? He could begin, 'Men of Athens, I have never studied medicine under anybody, and I never sought out any doctor as my teacher. In fact I have been constantly on my guard, not only against learning anything from the profession, but against the least appearance of knowing medicine at all. Nevertheless, if you'll grant me the post, I promise to do my best to pick up the skill — by experimenting on you.'" Everyone present laughed at that opening speech, and the matter dropped there.

In time it was plain that Euthydemus had begun to pay attention to what was said, though he was still careful never to utter a sound himself — as if silence might earn him a reputation for depth. Wanting to cure him of that, Socrates said, "Isn't it strange? People who want to learn the harp or the flute, or to ride, or to master any skill of that kind, aren't content to work away unceasingly in private at the thing they want to excel in. They seek out the best-regarded teachers they can find and do everything, endure everything, to follow those teachers' judgment in all things — as if there were no other way to amount to anything. Yet among those who hope to become powerful speakers and statesmen, there are some who see no reason they shouldn't be able to do everything politics demands at a moment's notice, by sheer inspiration, with no preparation whatever. And surely statecraft is the harder of the two — there are more competitors in that field and fewer who ever reach the prize, which can only mean it demands more sustained effort, not less."

Those were the kinds of things Socrates said in Euthydemus's hearing in the early days. When he saw that the young man was not only tolerating the conversation more readily but had become an eager listener, he went to the saddler's shop alone, and with Euthydemus seated beside him, this conversation took place.

"Tell me, Euthydemus, is it true what I hear — that you've collected a great many books by the men called wise?"

"Quite true, Socrates. And I mean to keep collecting until I own every book I can lay my hands on."

"By Hera! I admire you for it — for choosing to store up treasures of wisdom rather than silver and gold. Clearly you don't believe money makes men better; you believe the thoughts of the wise enrich their owners with virtue."

Euthydemus was delighted to hear this, thinking to himself, "In Socrates' eyes, I'm well on the road to wisdom." But Socrates, seeing him pleased with the praise, went on: "And what is it you want to excel at, Euthydemus, that you're collecting all these books?"

While Euthydemus sat silent, considering his answer, Socrates offered, "A great doctor, perhaps? The medical treatises alone would fill a good-sized library."

"No indeed. Not I!"

"An architect, then? That calls for a well-stocked mind too."

"No, that's not my ambition."

"A mathematician, maybe, like Theodorus?"

"No, not a mathematician either."

"An astronomer, then?" And when the young man shook his head at that too — "A rhapsodist, a professional reciter? I'm told you own the complete works of Homer."

"God forbid! Not that! Rhapsodists know their epic poetry down to the letter, of course — but they're empty-headed creatures themselves."

At last Socrates said, "Can it be, Euthydemus, that you're after the excellence that makes men statesmen and managers — fit to rule, able to benefit everyone else and themselves besides?"

"Yes," he replied. "That is the excellence I want — more than anything."

"Upon my word!" said Socrates. "Then you've set your heart on the noblest of virtues and the greatest of the arts. It is the property of kings; they call it 'royal.' But tell me — have you considered whether a man can excel at it without being just and upright?"

"Certainly I have. Without justice, no one can even be a good citizen."

"And no doubt you've taken care of that first step?"

"Socrates, I think I could hold my own against anyone as a just man."

"And do just men have their own characteristic works, the way carpenters and shoemakers do?"

"Of course they do."

"And just as a carpenter can show you his products, a just man should be able to describe and display his?"

"I see!" said Euthydemus. "You're afraid I can't describe the works of justice. Well, believe me, I can — and the works of injustice too, since there are plenty of those to see and hear every single day."

"Then suppose we write a J on this side and a U on that," Socrates proposed, "and whatever strikes us as a work of justice we'll file under the J, and whatever seems a work of injustice under the U?"

"By all means, if you think it will help."

So Socrates wrote the letters as he had suggested, and continued. "Lying exists among men, does it not?"

"Certainly."

"Which column does it go in?"

"Under injustice, obviously."

"Deceit exists too?"

"Very much so."

"Which side for deceit?"

"Injustice, clearly."

"And doing people harm — mischief of any sort?"

"That too."

"And selling free men into slavery?"

"That too."

"And none of these can be allowed to sit on the side of justice, can it, Euthydemus?"

"That would be monstrous."

"Very well. But suppose a man is elected general and enslaves an unjust, wicked, and hostile city. Are we to say he's doing wrong?"

"Certainly not."

"We'll admit he's doing what is just?"

"Certainly."

"And suppose he deceives the enemy while at war with them?"

"That's fair and just too."

"And if he steals and plunders their property — isn't he doing what is just?"

"Certainly. When you started, I assumed you meant friends only."

"So everything we set down under injustice will now have to be entered under justice as well?"

"Apparently."

"Then let's enter them there, and draw a new line: it is just to do such things to an enemy, unjust to do them to a friend — with a friend, one should be as straightforward as possible."

"I agree completely," said Euthydemus.

"So far so good," said Socrates. "But now suppose a general sees his troops losing heart, and invents a story that reinforcements are on the way, and with that lie restores their courage. Which column does that piece of deceit belong in?"

"Under justice, I'd say."

"Or suppose a man's son is sick and needs medicine but refuses to take it, and the father tricks him — slips the medicine into his food — and by that lie restores the boy to health. Where do we file that fraud?"

"The same place, in my judgment."

"Or suppose your friend is in black despair, and you're afraid he'll do away with himself, so you steal his knife or anything else of the kind. Which side does that theft go on?"

"That too must go under justice, surely."

"You're telling me, then, that even with friends, straight dealing isn't the rule in every case?"

"Heaven forbid!" the young man exclaimed. "I take back what I said before, if I'm allowed."

"Allowed! Of course you are — far better that than filing a false entry. But there's one more point we shouldn't leave unexamined. When a man deceives a friend to the friend's harm, which is more unjust — to do it deliberately or without meaning to?"

"Honestly, Socrates, I've stopped trusting my own answers. Everything I said before looks different to me now than it did then. Still, let me risk one more opinion: the deliberate deceiver is worse than the unintentional one."

"And do you think there's a study, a science, of justice, just as there is of letters?"

"I do."

"And which man would you call more literate — one who misspells and misreads on purpose, or one who does it without meaning to?"

"The one who does it on purpose — because he can do it correctly whenever he chooses."

"So the deliberate misspeller may be a literate man, while the involuntary one is simply illiterate?"

"He must be. I don't see any way out of it."

"And which of the two knows what is just — the man who lies and deceives on purpose, or the one who does it without meaning to?"

"The deliberate one, clearly."

"Now, you say the man who knows letters is more literate than the man who doesn't?"

"Yes."

"And the man who knows what is just is more just than the man who doesn't?"

"I suppose so — but for the life of me I can't make head or tail of my own admission."

"Well, look at it this way. Suppose a man wants to tell the truth but can never hold the same story for two minutes together. First he says the road runs east, then no, it runs west; he adds up a column of figures and gets one total, then another — now more, now less. What do you make of a man like that?"

"Heaven help us — clearly he doesn't know what he thought he knew."

"And you know the name people give certain men — 'slavish,' or 'little better than a slave'?"

"I do."

"Is it a name for their wisdom, or their ignorance?"

"Their ignorance, clearly."

"Ignorance of smithing, say?"

"Certainly not."

"Ignorance of carpentry, then?"

"No, not that either."

"Of shoemaking?"

"No, none of those. If anything, the opposite — most of the people who know those trades are the very ones called little better than slaves."

"So it's the name reserved for those who are ignorant of the beautiful, the good, and the just?"

"That's my opinion."

"Then we must strain every nerve, in every way, to escape being slaves."

"Socrates," the young man burst out, "by all that's holy, I flattered myself that I was studying philosophy — that I was on the right road to everything a man needs who wants to make beauty and goodness his pursuit. So you can imagine my despair when, after all my labor, I can't even answer questions about the things a man most needs to know — and there's no path of progress open to me, no way left to improve."

At that Socrates said, "Tell me, Euthydemus, have you ever been to Delphi?"

"Yes, certainly — twice."

"And did you notice an inscription somewhere on the temple: KNOW THYSELF?"

"I did."

"And did you pay it no attention? Or did you take it to heart and try to discover who and what you are?"

"I certainly didn't," he answered. "That much, at least, I was sure I already knew. If I didn't know even myself, what in the world did I know?"

"And does a man know himself, do you think, if he knows his own name and nothing more? Or must he go to work the way a buyer of horses does? A buyer doesn't consider that he knows the animal he's after until he has examined it point by point: is it obedient or stubborn, strong or weak, fast or slow — and how does it stand on everything else that makes a horse useful or useless for a horse's work? Shouldn't a man examine his own nature the same way, measured against what a human being needs, and learn his own capacities?"

"Yes, I see it now: the man who doesn't know his own ability doesn't know himself."

"And isn't this plain too — that self-knowledge brings men countless blessings, and ignorance of themselves countless evils? The man who knows himself knows what is good for him. He sees the limits of his powers; by doing what he knows, he supplies himself with what he needs and does well; by keeping clear of what he doesn't know, he avoids mistakes and so avoids misfortune. And now that he has a measure to judge other men by as well, he makes use of them too, to gain what is good and steer around what is bad. But the man who doesn't know himself, who is deceived about his own capacity, is in the same fog about everyone and everything else. He knows neither what he wants, nor what he is doing, nor the people he is dealing with; wrong about all of it, he misses the good and stumbles into the bad.

"The man who knows what he's doing succeeds at it, and wins fame and honor. His equals are glad to work with him, while those who are failing in their own affairs beg for his advice, his leadership, his protection; they pin their hopes of happiness on him, and love him best of all men for it. The man who doesn't know what he's doing chooses badly, fails at whatever he attempts, and not only suffers loss and punishment for his blunders — he loses his reputation step by step, becomes a laughing-stock, and ends his life in dishonor and contempt.

"And what is true of individuals is true of states. A city that doesn't know its own strength, and goes to war with a stronger one, ends up uprooted — or enslaved."

"Believe me, I entirely agree," said Euthydemus. "The precept KNOW THYSELF can't be prized too highly. But where does one begin the self-examination? That's what I look to you to explain, if you'd be so kind."

"Well," said Socrates, "I take it you know perfectly well which things are good and which are bad? We can rely on you that far?"

"Why, yes, surely — if I couldn't tell that much, I'd be worse than any slave."

"Come then, explain them to me."

"That's easy enough. First, health in itself I count a good and disease in itself an evil; and next, the sources of each — food, drink, daily habits — I count good or bad according to whether they lead toward health or toward disease."

"Then health and disease themselves are good when they lead to something good, and bad when they lead to something bad?"

"But when could health lead to something bad, or disease to something good?"

"Why, bless me, often enough!" replied Socrates. "Take a doomed expedition, or a disastrous voyage, or any calamity of that kind — and there are plenty to choose from. The men whose health and strength let them take part are lost; the ones left behind, too sick or too feeble to go, are saved."

"That's true. But you'll admit there are advantages that come with strength and losses that come with weakness."

"Even so — should we call things that benefit us one moment and injure us the next good, strictly speaking, any more than bad?"

"No — certainly not, by that argument. But wisdom, Socrates — you must at least grant that wisdom is indisputably a good. What is there a wise man wouldn't do better than a fool?"

"Really? Have you never heard of Daedalus — how Minos seized him precisely because of his wisdom, and forced him to be his slave, robbed at one stroke of his homeland and his freedom? How, trying to escape with his son, he caused the boy's death, couldn't even save himself, and was carried off among barbarians and enslaved all over again?"

"I know the old story."

"And haven't you heard of 'the woes of Palamedes,' the commonest theme of the poets — how Odysseus envied him his wisdom, and destroyed him for it?"

"That tale's told too."

"And how many others, do you suppose, have been seized for their wisdom, shipped off to the Great King, and made slaves at his court?"

"Well then — happiness, prosperity," Euthydemus exclaimed. "Surely that is a blessing, and the most unquestionable of all!"

"It might be," Socrates replied, "if it weren't itself compounded of questionable blessings."

"What component of happiness could possibly be questionable?"

"None — unless, of course, we're going to count beauty in it, or strength, or wealth, or reputation, or anything of that sort."

"By heaven, of course we're going to count them! What would happiness be without them?"

"By heaven, then we'll be counting in the commonest sources of human misery! How many people have been ruined by their good looks — undone by admirers driven half-mad at the sight of beauty in bloom! How many, trusting their strength, have attempted things beyond their power and come to grief! How many have been softened by their wealth, then plotted against and destroyed! How many, through fame and political power, have suffered a world of misery!"

"Well," the young man said, "if I'm not even right to praise happiness, then I confess I don't know what a man ought to pray to the gods for."

"These are matters," Socrates went on, "that you've perhaps never examined, because you were so confident you knew them already. But since the state you're preparing to lead is a democracy, of course you know what a democracy is?"

"I certainly presume so."

"And can a man know what a government of the people is without knowing who the people are?"

"Certainly not."

"And whom do you take the people to be?"

"The poor citizens, I'd say."

"So you know who the poor are?"

"Of course I do."

"And who the rich are, presumably?"

"As surely as I know the poor."

"And what do you mean by poor and rich?"

"The poor are those who don't have enough to cover their needs; the rich are those who have more than enough."

"Have you noticed, though, that some people with a mere pittance find it not only sufficient but manage to save a surplus out of it — while others can't make a large fortune stretch far enough?"

"I have indeed — and thank you for the reminder," said Euthydemus. "One hears of kings and tyrants driven by want to crimes like the neediest paupers."

"Then if that's how things stand, we'll have to class those kings among the people — and some owners of small estates, provided they manage them well, among the rich?"

"My own dull wits force me to admit it," said Euthydemus. "I begin to think my best course is to keep my mouth shut altogether. A little more of this and I'll be proved to know absolutely nothing." And he went away crestfallen, in an agony of self-contempt, convinced that he was truly no better than a slave.

Many of those whom Socrates reduced to this same condition refused ever to come near him again — and those, for his part, he wrote off as blockheads. But Euthydemus had the wit to see that his one hope of becoming a man worth anything was to spend as much time with Socrates as he possibly could. From that day on he never left his side except when he had to, and even began imitating some of his teacher's habits and practices. And Socrates, once he saw how matters stood with him, stopped rattling him and began explaining, in the simplest and plainest way, what he believed a man most needed to know and to practice.
