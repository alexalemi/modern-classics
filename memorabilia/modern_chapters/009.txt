Book III

(Part 2 of 4)

Chapter 5

It is worth setting down here a conversation Socrates had with Pericles — the son of the great statesman Pericles, not the father himself. Socrates opened it:

"I must tell you, Pericles, I'm expecting great things for our military affairs once you're elected general. Athens' standing should rise, and we should get the upper hand over our enemies."

"I wish with all my heart it would turn out as you say, Socrates," Pericles replied, "but how any of that is supposed to happen, I honestly can't see."

"Then shall we talk it through together," Socrates continued, "weigh the arguments on both sides, and see how far it's actually possible?"

"By all means."

"Well then, you know that in sheer numbers the Athenians are no smaller a people than the Boeotians?"

"Yes, I'm aware of that."

"And do you think the Boeotians could field a finer, healthier pick of men than Athens could?"

"I think we'd hold our own there too."

"And which of the two would you call the more united people — the friendlier among themselves?"

"The Athenians, I'd say. Whole sections of Boeotia resent the way Thebes runs things for its own benefit, and they bear that city ill will. At Athens I see nothing of the kind."

"Add this, too: no people is hungrier for honor or prouder in spirit — and those are no weak spurs. They'll drive even a sluggish nature to risk everything for glory's sake and for country."

"There's not much fault to find with Athenians on that score either."

"And if we turn to the great deeds of our ancestors, no people on earth has inherited a richer store of them than we have — memories that stir so many of us to take excellence seriously and to prove ourselves, in our turn, as brave as our fathers were."

"All of that is perfectly true, Socrates. But have you noticed that ever since the disaster at Lebadeia, where Tolmides lost his thousand men, and the one at Delium under Hippocrates, everything has been reversed? Athens' standing against the Boeotians has sunk, and the confidence of Thebes against Athens has swelled to match. The Boeotians, who in the old days wouldn't risk a battle with Athenians even on their own soil unless they had the Spartans and the rest of the Peloponnesians backing them up, now threaten to invade Attica all by themselves. And the Athenians, who used to ravage Boeotia at will whenever the Boeotians stood alone, are now afraid the Boeotians may one day come and ravage Attica."

"Yes," said Socrates, "I see that's how things stand. But it seems to me the city was never more manageable, never so ripe for a really good leader, than it is today. Boldness breeds carelessness, slackness, and disobedience; fear makes people attentive, obedient, and orderly. You can see the proof of that aboard ship. As long as the weather is calm and there's nothing to fear, the crew is a picture of disorder. But the moment a storm threatens or an enemy comes in sight, the scene changes: not only is every order obeyed, but the sailors fall silent and wait for the next signal like a chorus with its eyes on the leader."

"Well," said Pericles, "granted that now is the moment when they'd actually obey — then it's high time to talk about how we rekindle the old fire in them: the longing for the old-fashioned courage, the glory, the prosperity of the days gone by."

"Well," Socrates went on, "if we wanted them to reclaim some material wealth that others now hold, the best way to spur them on would be to show them it was their ancestors' property and theirs by right. Since what we actually want is for them to strive for first place in excellence, we have to show them that this leadership, joined with virtue, is their oldest inheritance — theirs beyond any other people's — and that if they pursue it in earnest they'll soon surpass the world."

"How do we teach them that?"

"By reminding them, I think, of what they already carry in their minds: that the oldest of our ancestors whose names have come down to us were also the bravest of heroes."

"You mean the judgment of the gods that Cecrops and his court were chosen to decide, because of their virtue?"

"That, yes, and the birth and rearing of Erechtheus, and the war fought in his day to hold back invasion from the whole neighboring continent; and the war against the Peloponnesians in the days of the Heraclidae, the descendants of Heracles; and all the battles fought in Theseus' time. In every one of them, our ancestors showed themselves the best men of their age. And if you like, we can come down to later times, to what their descendants did — men not so far removed from ourselves — in the struggle against the masters of Asia, and of Europe too as far as Macedonia: a power with resources and weapons beyond anything that had come before, with the mightiest deeds to its name. The Athenians met it partly on their own and partly sharing the honors with the Peloponnesians, by land and by sea. Those men were heroes too, and tradition says they far outshone the peoples of their day."

"So the story of their heroism runs."

"And that is why, through all the upheavals and migrations that have swept over Greece wave after wave, they alone kept their own land, unmoved — so that other peoples routinely turned to Athens as a court of appeal in disputes over rights, or fled to her as a harbor of refuge from oppressors."

"And the wonder to me, Socrates," said Pericles, "is how our city ever came to decline from that."

"I think we're the victims of our own success. Like an athlete whose easy dominance in the arena lulls him into slacking off until he finally loses to weaker opponents, we Athenians, at the height of our superiority, neglected ourselves and went soft."

"Then what should we do now to recover the old virtue?"

"There's no mystery about it, I think. We can rediscover our forefathers' institutions and practice them with something like their exactness — in which case we may well match their success. Or we can imitate the people who lead the field today, adopt their way of life, and, if we live by it as strictly as they do, be at least their equals — or, if we follow it more conscientiously still, their betters."

"You seem to be saying," Pericles answered, "that the spirit of brave and beautiful manhood has simply flown from our city. When will Athenians revere their elders the way the Spartans do — Athenians, who start practicing contempt for gray hairs on their own fathers? When will they train their bodies like Spartans — when they not only neglect fitness themselves but sneer at those who care about it? When will they obey their magistrates like Spartans — when they take actual pride in despising authority? When will they be united like Spartans — when instead of working together for common interests they tear at each other's reputations, envy each other more than they envy the rest of mankind, quarrel in private and in public more than any people alive, drag each other endlessly through the courts, and would rather turn a profit on a neighbor's trouble than lend a hand? They treat the city's business like some foreign country's — a bone to fight over — and rejoice in nothing so much as having the means to fight over it. From all this the state breeds blindness and cowardice, and the citizens' hearts fill with such hatred and hostility toward each other that I often shudder to think some disaster will fall on the city too great for her to bear."

"Don't," Socrates replied, "please don't let yourself believe the Athenians are sick beyond curing. Don't you see how disciplined they are in everything to do with the fleet? How promptly and precisely they obey the officials at the athletic games? How they follow their trainers in the choruses better than anyone alive?"

"Yes — and that's the wonder of it," he answered. "All those people obey their leaders; but our heavy infantry and our cavalry, supposedly the pick of the citizens for manly excellence, are the most undisciplined of all."

"Well," said Socrates, "the council that sits on the Areopagus — isn't it made up of men of proven character?"

"Certainly."

"Can you name any body, judicial or executive, that tries cases or conducts business with more honor, stricter legality, greater dignity, or fairer justice?"

"No, I have no complaint there."

"Then we shouldn't despair, as if all sense of order and discipline had died out of our countrymen."

"And yet," he answered, "not to keep harping on one string — in military service, where sobriety, order, and discipline are needed if they're needed anywhere, none of these things gets any attention at all."

"Maybe," said Socrates, "because there they're commanded by the men who know least. Take harpists, chorus performers, dancers: have you noticed that nobody ever dreams of leading them without knowing the art? The same goes for wrestlers and pancratiasts, the all-in fighters. All those leaders can tell you where they learned the skill they preside over — but most of our generals are amateurs, improvising as they go. Now, I don't for a moment suppose you're that sort. I believe you could account for your schooling in generalship as readily as your schooling in wrestling. You've preserved your father's rules of strategy at first hand, and you've collected plenty more from every quarter where a future general could pick up anything useful. I'm sure, too, that you take pains never to be ignorant, even unknowingly, of anything that will serve you in so high an office — and that wherever you catch yourself lacking, you go to the people who know, sparing neither gifts nor thanks, so their knowledge can fill your gaps and their help can be counted on."

"I'm not blind, Socrates," said Pericles. "You don't say all this because you think I actually do these things. You're teaching me that a man who means to be a general must do them. And I grant every word."

Socrates went on: "Have you ever noticed, Pericles, that a great mountain barrier runs along the front of our country toward Boeotia, like a rampart — cut only by narrow, steep passes, the sole roads into the heart of Attica, which sits ringed by natural fortresses?"

"Certainly I have."

"And have you heard of the Mysians and Pisidians in the Great King's territory — how they hold their mountain strongholds with light arms, sweep down to raid and ravage the king's land, and keep their own freedom?"

"Yes, I know of them."

"Then don't you think a corps of young, able-bodied Athenians, equipped with lighter arms and holding that mountain rampart of ours, would be a thorn in the enemy's side and a splendid bulwark for our own countryside?"

"I think all of these would be useful measures, Socrates, decidedly."

"Then if they please you," Socrates replied, "try, best of men, to make them real. Whatever part of them you carry out will be an honor to you and a blessing to the state; and wherever you fall short, you'll do no harm to the city and no discredit to yourself."

Chapter 6

Glaucon, the son of Ariston, had set his heart so fiercely on leading the state that nothing could stop him from giving public speeches — though he was not yet twenty. His friends and relatives tried and failed to keep him from making himself ridiculous and getting hauled down from the speaker's platform. The one man who succeeded was Socrates, who took a kindly interest in the young man for the sake of Charmides, his uncle, and of Plato, his brother. Here is how it happened. Socrates ran into him, and first, to get him to listen at all, held him with an opening like this:

"Ah, Glaucon — so you've decided to become the leading man of the state?"

"I have, Socrates."

"And what a noble ambition, if anything human deserves the name! Succeed, and you'll be able to get whatever you desire; you'll be in a position to help your friends; you'll raise up your father's house and exalt your fatherland; you'll make a name for yourself first in the city, then throughout Greece, and perhaps, like Themistocles, even among the barbarians. Wherever you go, all eyes will be on you."

Glaucon's heart swelled as he drank this in, and he gladly stayed to listen.

Presently Socrates continued: "Then this much is clear, isn't it, Glaucon — since you want the city's honors, you'll have to benefit the city?"

"Undoubtedly."

"Then by all that's sacred, don't keep us in the dark. Tell us: how do you propose to benefit the state first? Where will you begin?"

Glaucon sat silent, as if he were considering for the very first time where he would begin. So Socrates went on: "I imagine, if you wanted to improve a friend's estate, you'd try to make it wealthier. Will you try the same with the state — to make it wealthier?"

"Absolutely."

"And the state grows wealthier as its revenues increase?"

"That seems likely, at any rate."

"Then tell us, please: what are the state's revenues at present, and what do they come from, and how large are they? No doubt you've gone into the question carefully, so that if any source runs dry you can make up the shortfall, and if any has been neglected you can restore it."

"To tell the truth," said Glaucon, "those are matters I haven't really gone into."

"Never mind," he said, "if you've skipped that point. Just run through the expenditures for us. Obviously you plan to cut the unnecessary ones."

"Well — no. Honestly, I haven't had time to look into that side of it yet either."

"Then we'll postpone the project of making the state wealthier. Without knowing what goes out and what comes in, there's obviously no way to handle it seriously."

"But Socrates," Glaucon put in, "it's also possible to enrich the state out of its enemies' pockets!"

"Very much so," Socrates answered, "provided you beat them. But if you're beaten, it's also possible to lose what you already have."

"True enough."

"And therefore," Socrates went on, "before deciding whom to make war on, a statesman must know the strength of his own city and the strength of the enemy — so that if his side is the stronger, he can throw his advice behind the war, and if it's the weaker, he can urge caution."

"You're right."

"Then would you list for us the land and naval forces of Athens first, and then those of our opponents?"

"Forgive me — I couldn't recite them off the top of my head."

"If you have them written down somewhere," Socrates added, "do fetch them. I can't tell you how eager I am to hear."

"No," he said. "To be honest, I haven't got them written down either."

"Then we'll hold off on advising war or peace — in your maiden speech, at any rate. The questions are enormous, and you're just starting out; I quite understand you haven't gotten through them yet. But the defense of the country — that, I'm sure, you've studied. You know exactly how many forts and outposts are worth keeping and how many aren't, which garrisons are strong enough and which are undermanned; and you'll argue for reinforcing the useful outposts and scrapping the superfluous ones."

"Scrap them all — that's my advice," said Glaucon. "For all the good they do! Defenses, indeed — kept up so well that the property of the countryside gets stolen anyway."

"But if you scrap the outposts," Socrates asked, "won't something worse follow? Won't any thug who likes be free to plunder openly? And by the way — is this judgment based on personal inspection? Have you gone out and examined the defenses yourself? How do you know they're as badly kept as you say?"

"I'm guessing that they are."

"Then shall we defer our advice on this too, until we've gotten past guessing? It will be time enough when we know the facts."

"Perhaps that would be better," Glaucon replied.

"Then there are the silver mines. You haven't visited those in person, I know, so you can't say why they produce less than they used to."

"No — I've never been out there."

"No, indeed. Heaven help us, it's an unhealthy district, by all accounts — so when the moment comes for advice on that subject, you'll have your excuse ready."

"Now you're making fun of me," Glaucon answered.

"But here's a point I'm sure you haven't neglected. No, this one you'll have gone into thoroughly, and you can tell us: how long can the grain grown in our own countryside feed the city? How much does the city need for a single year? That way it can never run short of this basic necessity without your knowing, and with your full command of the facts you'll be able to advise on a truly vital question — to the aid, maybe even the rescue, of your country."

"It's a colossal business," Glaucon answered, "if I'm expected to attend to all these details."

"On the other hand," said Socrates, "a man can't even run his own household well without knowing, first, what it needs, and then taking pains to supply it item by item. Now, this city consists of more than ten thousand households, and it's not easy to look after so many at once — so why didn't you start by practicing on one of them, and try to build up your own uncle's? It could use the help. If your strength holds for that one case, you can take on more; but if you can't relieve one household, how could you possibly hope to manage thousands? If a man can't carry fifty pounds, it's absurd for him to try to carry a hundred."

"Oh, I'd be glad enough to help my uncle's house," said Glaucon, "if only my uncle would be persuaded to listen to me."

"So," said Socrates, "you can't persuade your uncle — and you imagine you'll be able to make the whole Athenian people, uncle included, obey you? Be careful, Glaucon, that in your thirst for glory and reputation you don't end up with the opposite. Don't you see how dangerous it is to say or do things beyond the range of your knowledge? Think of the people you know whose talk or conduct plainly overshoots what they understand: do they earn more praise for it, or more blame? Are they admired, or despised? Then think of the people who do know what they're saying and doing, and I think you'll find that in every field, the ones who win reputation and admiration come from the ranks of the most knowledgeable, while the disreputable, the mean, and the contemptible rise out of some depth of ignorance and dullness. So if reputation and admiration as a statesman is what you thirst for, make sure of one accomplishment above all: knowledge, as thorough as you can get, of the work you mean to do. If you enter public life with that to set you apart from everyone else, I wouldn't be at all surprised if you reached your ambition quite easily."
