TEXT ANALYSIS — The Memorabilia (Xenophon, c. 371 BC)
Source: H. G. Dakyns' translation (1897), Gutenberg #1177. prep.py
strips Dakyns' footnote apparatus and Greek transliterations; Book III's
mislabeled fourteenth chapter is renumbered.

WHAT THIS IS

Xenophon's defense and portrait of Socrates — the practical, sociable,
useful Socrates, remembered by a soldier-historian rather than a
metaphysician. Four Books, 39 chapters: Books I–II open as a legal
defense (answering the indictment: refusing to recognize the state's
gods, importing new divinities, corrupting the young) and shade into
scenes of Socrates giving concrete advice — to friends, sons, brothers,
would-be generals, artists, even a courtesan. This is the Socrates the
American founders read alongside Plato's: less irony, more usefulness.

The project already has Plato's complete dialogues; this volume is the
COUNTERPOINT. Where Plato's Socrates dazzles and unsettles, Xenophon's
teaches you how to manage a household, pick a friend, and stay useful
to your city. Let that plainness be a feature, not a flaw to punch up.

STYLE AND TONE

- Xenophon narrates in an earnest first person ("I have often
  wondered…", "I shall endeavour to show…") interleaved with dialogue
  scenes. Keep the memoirist's voice: warm, loyal, a touch defensive —
  a veteran vouching for his teacher.
- Dialogue is the engine. Render exchanges as natural modern speech.
  Do NOT use the Plato-dialogues convention of a speaker name on its
  own line — Dakyns embeds speech in narration with quotation marks;
  keep that embedded style, with modern punctuation.
- Socrates' method: homely analogies (flute-players, horses, farming,
  shoemakers), chains of short questions, sudden reversals. Keep every
  analogy concrete; keep question-chains as real questions.
- Dakyns is ornate-Victorian ("vouchsafe", "never so well", "whereas").
  De-archaize aggressively; the underlying Greek is plain soldierly
  prose and the modern text should read that way.

TRANSLATION CHALLENGES

- The daimonion: Socrates' "divine sign". Locked rendering: "the
  divine sign" / his saying "A divinity gives me a sign." Do not
  modernize into "inner voice" or "conscience" — the religious charge
  depends on it being a divinity.
- Greek institutions, locked glosses (use on first occurrence, then the
  plain term): "the Thirty" (the oligarchy of 404 BC), "sophist"
  (professional teacher of rhetoric — keep the word), "talent"/"mina"
  (keep, money), "agora" → "the marketplace", "symposium" → "dinner
  party" where informal, "hoplite" → "infantry soldier" (heavy),
  "dithyramb" and such may take a two-word gloss in apposition.
- Proper names: use familiar modern spellings (Meletus, Critias,
  Alcibiades, Aristippus, Chaerephon, Prodicus, Aspasia). Consistency
  matters more than system — the ledger records any judgment calls.
- Second-person plural addresses inside speeches: modern "you all" is
  too casual; plain "you" carries it.

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- The trial and execution of Socrates, and the crimes of Critias and
  the Thirty (mass killings, property seizures), are discussed as
  historical fact — measured register, neither softened nor dramatized.
- I.2 discusses the accusation that Socrates taught tyrants; II.1
  includes enslaved people among household property; III.11 is a
  friendly scene with Theodota, a courtesan — render all of it
  faithfully, in a neutral historical voice, as the author's world and
  argument. No bowdlerizing, no modern editorializing.
- Greek pederastic conventions surface occasionally (e.g. I.2 on
  Critias and Euthydemus, IV.1). Translate what the text says, plainly
  and without elaboration, in the same historical register.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- I.1 the indictment: "Socrates is guilty of refusing to recognize the
  gods recognized by the state, of importing strange new divinities of
  his own, and of corrupting the young." (lock: "corrupting the young")
- I.1: his saying "A divinity gives me a sign." (lock: "gives me a
  sign")
- II.1 the Choice of Heracles: keep the fable's frame intact — young
  Heracles at the crossroads, the two women, Virtue and Vice (Vice
  first calls herself Happiness). (locks: "Heracles", "two roads")
  Render the two speeches at full length; this is the book's most
  famous set piece and was a founders' favorite.
- IV.8 the closing eulogy: end with "…of all whom I have known, he was
  the best and the happiest of men." (lock: "the best and the happiest
  of men")

FORMAT

- 18 files across 4 Books; each file: heading "Book N" exactly, then
  "(Part n of k)" marker (all books are multi-part), then "Chapter N"
  standalone heading lines with the chapter's paragraphs after each.
- Chapter numbering restarts each Book (standard citation is by
  Book + chapter, e.g. II.1).
- Parts 2+ of a book never re-introduce it; non-final parts never
  conclude it. Word ratio target: 0.8–1.2 (Dakyns pads; the modern text
  will often be slightly shorter than his Victorian English).
