CHAPTER 009 TRANSLATION NOTES -- Synopsis of the Six Following Meditations
===========================================================================

CHANGES AND RATIONALE
---------------------
- The original is structured as six dense paragraphs, one per Meditation, with
  extremely long sentences (some running 10+ lines). Broke these into shorter,
  clearer sentences while preserving the logical flow of each summary.

- The Second Meditation paragraph is the longest by far -- Descartes goes on an
  extended digression about why he doesn't prove the soul's immortality here.
  Split this into multiple focused paragraphs: (1) what the meditation
  accomplishes, (2) why the immortality proof is deferred, (3) the chain of
  prerequisites, (4) the divisibility argument, (5) why full proof requires
  all of physics. This makes the logical structure visible rather than buried.

- "corporeal nature" -> "physical nature" per vocabulary mapping.

- "viz." -> removed or replaced with "that is" / natural phrasing.

- "objective reality" -- kept as Descartes' technical term but immediately
  glossed in parentheses ("that is, represents so high a degree of being and
  perfection"), following Descartes' own parenthetical gloss in the original.

- "the act of the understanding (intellectio) is distinguished from that of the
  imagination (imaginatio)" -> "I distinguish the act of understanding from the
  act of imagining" -- dropped the Latin terms since they add nothing for a
  modern reader and the English equivalents are perfectly clear.

- "a highly perfect machine" -> "an incredibly sophisticated machine" and
  "workman" -> "engineer" -- modernized the analogy while keeping its force.

- "men are possessed of bodies" -> "we have bodies" -- simpler, same meaning.

- "no one of sound mind ever seriously doubted" -> "no one in their right mind
  has ever seriously doubted" -- natural modern phrasing.

- "the brutes" does not appear in this chapter.

- Footnote reference "10" in the original (after "objective reality") was
  integrated: the bracketed gloss in the original was kept as a parenthetical
  explanation in the modernized text.

- The final sentence of the synopsis, which is a single enormous sentence in the
  original (about 8 lines), was broken into four shorter sentences. The key
  rhetorical move -- that knowledge of mind and God is MORE certain than
  knowledge of the physical world -- is preserved and given its own sentence
  for emphasis, since this is the central thesis of the entire Meditations.

TONE AND APPROACH
-----------------
- This synopsis functions as a roadmap for readers. Descartes wrote it to be
  helpful, and the modernized version leans into that: clear, organized,
  inviting. The reader should feel oriented and curious about what's ahead.

- The voice is slightly more systematic and less personal than the Discourse
  chapters -- appropriate for the Meditations' more formal register -- but still
  accessible and direct.

- The long digression about immortality is Descartes being transparent about
  his method: "Here's why I'm not proving this yet, and here's what I'd need."
  This is actually a strength -- it shows intellectual honesty. The translation
  preserves this quality while making the reasoning much easier to follow.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR MEDITATION I (CHAPTER 010)
-------------------------------------------------
- "The senses" -- used consistently (not "our senses" or "sense experience").
- "Clear and distinct" -- has appeared as a paired technical term. Keep using it
  exactly this way throughout.
- "Substance" -- used in its philosophical sense (that which exists independently).
  Established in the synopsis discussion of mind vs. body as distinct substances.
- "Extension" -- not yet used directly, but "physical nature" has been established
  as the modernized term for "corporeal nature." When "extension" appears as
  Descartes' specific technical term (the essential property of physical things),
  keep it and explain briefly on first use.
- "Objective reality" -- has been introduced and glossed. If it reappears in
  Meditation III, the reader will have seen it before.
- The synopsis establishes that Meditation I is about doubt -- radical, sweeping
  doubt of everything. The translation of Meditation I should make this feel
  urgent and real, not just abstract. The dream argument and the evil genius
  should land with force.
- "Prejudice" -- used in Descartes' sense of "preconceived opinion" or
  "assumption we haven't examined." Not the modern sense of social bias.
  Consider glossing on first significant use in Meditation I.
- First person ("I") throughout. Descartes in the Meditations is a thinker
  working through problems in real time -- more intense and introspective
  than the Discourse's storytelling mode.
- The synopsis mentions that doubt "delivers us from all prejudice" and makes
  the mind "withdraw itself from the senses." Meditation I should deliver
  on both of these promises dramatically.
