CHAPTER 022 TRANSLATION NOTES
Appendix -- Definitions, Postulates, Axioms, and Propositions
==============================================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
----------------------------

1. STRUCTURE: The original has a clear logical architecture -- Definitions (I-X),
   unnumbered Postulates (1st-7th), Axioms (I-X), Propositions (I-IV with
   demonstrations), and a Corollary. This structure was preserved exactly, with
   section headers (DEFINITIONS, POSTULATES, AXIOMS, PROPOSITION I, etc.) added
   for clarity. The original text implies these divisions but does not always
   label them explicitly.

2. DEFINITIONS -- APPROACH: These are precise philosophical definitions. Kept
   them tight and clear, modernizing syntax without changing meaning. The key
   challenge is that Descartes is defining technical terms (thought, idea,
   objective reality, formal reality, substance, mind, body, God) that he uses
   with very specific meanings. Each definition was translated to be understandable
   on first reading while remaining philosophically precise.

3. "AWANTING" -> "lacking" (Definitions context, Proposition III). Per vocabulary
   mapping. Appears multiple times in the proofs.

4. "CORPOREAL FANTASY" -> "physical imagination." Definition II distinguishes
   between ideas as they exist in the brain (physical) and ideas as they inform
   the mind. "Corporeal fantasy" is Descartes' term for the brain's image-making
   faculty considered as a physical organ.

5. "ENTITY OR BEING" -> "being or existence." Definition III's "objective reality"
   is one of Descartes' most important and most confusing technical terms. The
   translation keeps it clear: the objective reality of an idea = the being of
   what the idea represents, insofar as that being exists in the idea (i.e., as
   a representation). The bracketed gloss "[or by representation]" from the
   original was integrated naturally.

6. "FORMALLY" vs. "EMINENTLY" -> kept both terms as technical vocabulary with
   brief contextual explanation in Definition IV. "Formally" = in exactly the way
   we conceive it. "Eminently" = not in that exact form, but in something so
   great it can account for it. These terms recur throughout the proofs.

7. "MENS" vs. "ANIMA" (Definition VI): The original preserves the Latin to
   explain why Descartes prefers "mind" (mens) over "soul" (anima). Dropped the
   Latin but kept the substantive point: "soul" is ambiguous because it often
   refers to something physical (e.g., Aristotle's vegetative and animal souls
   are tied to the body).

8. POSTULATES -- APPROACH: These are numbered requests to the reader -- each one
   is an exercise or mental discipline Descartes considers prerequisite for
   understanding the proofs that follow. Changed "1st," "2nd," etc. to "First,"
   "Second," etc. for consistency, and changed "I request" to "I ask" for a more
   natural register while preserving the sense of polite instruction.

9. "IN FINE" (Postulate 1) -> omitted. Per vocabulary mapping. The phrase "in
   fine, they may acquire the habit" was rendered as "they eventually develop
   the habit."

10. "ADVERT TO" (Postulate 7) -> "noting." Per vocabulary mapping.

11. "REPUGNANT" -> not present in this chapter, but the related concept of
    contradictions appears in Postulate 3 ("the same thing cannot at once be
    and not be"). Translated straightforwardly.

12. AXIOM V -- EXTENDED EXPLANATION: This axiom has a long parenthetical
    explanation (the sky example) that functions as a mini-argument. Broke this
    out into its own paragraph for clarity, since it is genuinely illuminating:
    Descartes is showing that even our most basic belief (the sky exists) depends
    on the principle that ideas must have real causes.

13. "FORRNALLY" in original (line 145) -> clearly a typo for "formally." Corrected
    silently.

14. THE THREE PROOFS OF GOD'S EXISTENCE: The Appendix contains three distinct
    proofs, each presented as a formal demonstration:
    - Proposition I: The ontological argument (God's nature includes existence)
    - Proposition II: From the idea of God in us (causal argument)
    - Proposition III: From our own existence (cosmological argument)
    Each was translated to make the logical steps as transparent as possible,
    preserving the "premise, premise, therefore conclusion" format.

15. PROPOSITION III -- CHAIN ARGUMENT: This is the most complex proof, with
    multiple intermediate conclusions building on each other. The original is
    extremely dense. Broke it into clearly separated steps, each on its own
    line or paragraph, so the reader can follow the chain: I can't give myself
    perfections -> therefore I can't preserve myself -> therefore something else
    preserves me -> that thing has everything I have -> that thing has the idea
    of perfections -> that thing must actually have those perfections -> that
    thing is God.

16. "A FORTIORI" -> "even more easily." This Latin logical term means "with even
    greater reason." Replaced both occurrences with natural English that conveys
    the same logical force.

17. THE MIND-BODY DISTINCTION (Proposition IV): This is the capstone argument of
    the entire Appendix and one of the most famous arguments in the history of
    philosophy. The structure is: (1) God can make anything we clearly conceive;
    (2) we clearly conceive mind without body and body without mind; (3) therefore
    God could make them exist separately; (4) things that can exist separately are
    really distinct; (5) therefore mind and body are really distinct.

    Translated to make each step maximally clear. The closing note -- where
    Descartes explains why he invoked God's omnipotence (not because separation
    requires special power, but because God was the only foundation available in
    the argument so far) -- is philosophically important and was preserved in full.

18. "SKY" vs. "HEAVENS" -- In the Corollary, "the sky and the earth" was changed
    to "the heavens and the earth" to match the biblical/theological register of
    the passage (Descartes is echoing Genesis). In Axiom V's sky example, kept
    "sky" since it is used as an everyday empirical example, not a theological
    reference.

TONE
----
The Appendix has the most formal, structured tone of anything in the collection.
It reads like a mathematical proof -- definitions, axioms, propositions, QED.
The translation preserves this logical architecture while making each step readable.
The postulates are somewhat warmer (Descartes is directly addressing and coaching
his readers), and the translation reflects this slight tonal shift.

The overall effect should be: precise, rigorous, but followable. A reader who has
made it through the Meditations and the Principles should be able to follow each
proof step by step.

CONSISTENCY NOTES (END OF VOLUME)
----------------------------------
This is the final chapter in the collection. Key terms that have been maintained
throughout:
- "Clear and distinct" -- Descartes' criterion of truth, used as a paired phrase
- "Substance" -- that which exists independently
- "Mode" / "attribute" -- properties or ways of being of a substance
- "Extension" -- the essential property of physical things (taking up space)
- "Objective reality" -- the reality of what an idea represents
- "Formal reality" -- the reality something has in itself
- "Eminently" -- containing something in a higher or more excellent form
- "The senses" -- consistent throughout
- "Physical" for "corporeal" -- except where "corporeal" appears in a direct
  philosophical distinction (e.g., "corporeal fantasy" in Definition II, rendered
  as "physical imagination")
- "I think, therefore I am" -- the foundational certainty (not directly stated
  in this chapter but implied throughout)
- God's existence proofs treated with full philosophical seriousness
- Mind-body distinction presented as the culminating argument
