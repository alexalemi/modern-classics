CHAPTER 001 TRANSLATION NOTES -- Discourse on the Method, Part I
=================================================================

CHANGES AND RATIONALE
---------------------

SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
- The original chapter is built from enormously long sentences, many running
  5-10 lines with multiple semicolons, nested clauses, and chains of "and."
  Nearly every sentence was broken into 2-5 shorter ones. For example, the
  opening paragraph is a single sentence in the original; the translation
  breaks it into seven sentences while preserving the logical flow.

- The long catalog of school subjects (languages, fable, history, eloquence,
  poetry, mathematics, morals, theology, philosophy, law, medicine) was kept
  as a single flowing passage with "That..." clauses to preserve Descartes'
  rhetorical buildup, but each clause was simplified internally.

VOCABULARY:
- "good sense" -> "common sense" in most places. Descartes uses "bon sens"
  which maps more naturally to "common sense" in modern English. The opening
  line keeps it as "common sense" since that's the colloquial term.
- "the brutes" -> "animals" (per vocabulary mapping).
- "inasmuch as" -> "the thing that" / rephrased (per vocabulary mapping).
- "avow" -> "freely admit" (per vocabulary mapping).
- "in fine" -> "finally" / "and finally" (per vocabulary mapping).
- "certitude" -> "certainty" (per vocabulary mapping).
- "disquisitions" -> "writings" (per vocabulary mapping).
- "superstructure" -> "anything more ambitious" (architectural metaphor
  preserved loosely; "superstructure reared on them" -> "build anything
  more ambitious on top of them").
- "the schools" -> "the schools" (kept in the first instance since context
  makes the meaning clear; elsewhere "what was taught in the schools").
- "precipitancy" does not appear in this chapter.
- "letters" (meaning scholarly/literary study) -> "books and learning" or
  "the study of books" depending on context.
- "knight-errants of romance" -> "knights-errant in old romances" (modernized
  the hyphenation, kept the reference which is vivid and fun).
- "Lower Brittany" kept as-is -- it's a specific regional reference
  (a rural, unfashionable part of France) and works in context.
- "fictitious titles" -> "bogus credentials" (Descartes means academic
  titles/honors that don't reflect real knowledge).
- "parricide" kept as "cruelty" -- the original Latin-via-Veitch word is
  jarring in modern English and the broader point is about mislabeled virtues.

TONE:
- Leaned into Descartes' self-deprecating humor throughout. His famous
  line about copper and glass / gold and diamonds is preserved almost
  verbatim -- it's already funny and vivid.
- The "tale" framing (where he says this is just a story, not a lesson)
  is given extra warmth. Descartes is performing modesty but also genuinely
  being open about his limitations.
- The passage about his school is translated with mild exasperation --
  Descartes is clearly frustrated that the best education in Europe left
  him feeling ignorant, and the translation lets that come through.
- The closing image of "the great book of the world" is preserved -- it's
  one of Descartes' best metaphors and needs no modernization.

STRUCTURAL CHOICES:
- Paragraph breaks follow the original closely. Each paragraph in the
  source corresponds to one in the translation.
- The chapter heading follows the format from 000: "DISCOURSE ON THE METHOD"
  then "Part I" on the next line.

CONTENT NOTES:
- Footnote reference numbers in the original were omitted (there were none
  in this chapter's text).
- "One of the most celebrated schools in Europe" = La Flèche, the Jesuit
  college where Descartes studied. Not named explicitly in the original;
  left as-is per Descartes' own choice not to name it.
- The catalog of disciplines is Descartes' systematic demolition of his
  entire education. Each subject gets praised and then subtly undermined.
  The translation preserves this structure -- especially the sly dig at
  philosophy ("gives us a way to talk about absolutely anything with an
  air of authority, impressing people who don't know better").

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (Part II)
---------------------------------------------
- First person "I" maintained throughout. Direct, conversational tone.
- "Method" capitalized when referring to Descartes' specific Method.
- "Common sense" / "reason" used interchangeably (as Descartes does).
- "Animals" not "brutes."
- "Clear and distinct" has not appeared yet as a technical term -- it will
  likely appear in Part II when Descartes lays out his four rules.
- Part II picks up where Part I ends: Descartes is traveling, stationed
  with an army in Germany, and begins developing his Method. He'll describe
  the stove-heated room where he spent a day thinking, and lay out the
  four famous rules. The tone should remain autobiographical/narrative but
  will shift toward more philosophical content.
- Key metaphors to watch for in Part II: the city-planning metaphor
  (comparing building a philosophy to rebuilding a city), the house
  metaphor (tearing down and rebuilding), the forest metaphor (choosing
  a direction and sticking with it). These should be translated vividly.
- "The sciences" remains lowercase (general reference).
- The scholar-in-his-study vs. practical-person contrast established in
  Part I may recur -- keep the same framing.
- Descartes' modesty performance continues into Part II (he insists he's
  not telling anyone else what to do, just describing his own path).
  Maintain the same warm, self-aware tone.
