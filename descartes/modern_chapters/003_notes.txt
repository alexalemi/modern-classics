CHAPTER 003 TRANSLATION NOTES -- Discourse on the Method, Part III
====================================================================

CHANGES AND RATIONALE
---------------------
- The enormous opening sentence (one of the longest in the Discourse) was broken
  into several shorter sentences while preserving the house-renovation analogy.
  The original packs the analogy, the problem, and the solution into a single
  breathless construction; the modern version separates them for clarity.

- The three maxims are set off with horizontal rules and bold labels (**The first
  rule**, **My second rule**, **My third rule**) to make the structure immediately
  visible. Descartes' original text buries these in running prose.

- "provisory code of morals" -> "provisional moral code" / "set of three or four
  rules to live by" -- "provisory" is archaic and awkward; "provisional" is the
  standard modern translation of Descartes' "morale par provision."

- The forest analogy (Maxim 2) was kept intact and made more vivid. It's one of
  Descartes' best-known illustrations and works well in modern English with only
  light touch-up.

- "thenceforward" -> "from then on" or restructured away entirely per vocabulary
  mappings.

- "in fine" -> "finally" / "to round out" per vocabulary mappings.

- "viz." -> replaced with a colon or restructured, per vocabulary mappings.

- "betook me" -> "set out" / "settled in" per vocabulary mappings.

- "incertitude" -> "uncertainty" -- straightforward modernization.

- "the vulgar" (referring to common/popular philosophy) -> "the commonly accepted
  ones" -- "vulgar" in its archaic sense of "common" would be badly misread today.

- "holding intercourse with mankind" -> "engaging with the world" -- "intercourse"
  in its archaic sense of "social dealings" would be distracting for modern readers.

- "prosecuting my design" -> "pursuing my project" -- simple modernization.

- "ennui" -> "boredom" -- while "ennui" survives in English, "boredom" is clearer
  for the target reading level.

- Footnote 2 (referring to mathematical examples in the volume) was integrated as
  a parenthetical: "(The many examples throughout this volume will make this clear.)"

- Footnote 3 (identifying "this country" as Holland) was integrated directly into
  the text: "this country -- Holland --" to avoid leaving the reader guessing.

- The Stoic philosophy passage (Maxim 3) about ancient philosophers who could
  "enjoy a happiness which their gods might have envied" was kept largely intact
  because it's genuinely powerful rhetoric. Only the syntax was simplified.

- "the act of mind by which a thing is believed is different from that by which
  we know that we believe it" -- this remarkably modern psychological insight was
  preserved clearly. It's a precursor to the concept of metacognition and deserves
  to land with full force.

- Descartes' careful political positioning (obeying laws, holding to his faith)
  was translated faithfully. These statements were partly sincere and partly
  protective -- he was writing in a period when heterodox thinkers faced real
  danger. The translation preserves them without editorial comment.

STRUCTURAL DECISIONS
--------------------
- Added horizontal rule separators (---) between the maxims and the concluding
  sections to make the five-part structure of this chapter visible:
  1. Introduction (the house analogy)
  2. Maxim 1 (follow moderate customs)
  3. Maxim 2 (be decisive)
  4. Maxim 3 (master yourself, not fortune)
  5. Conclusion (the Method as vocation) + nine years of travel + move to Holland

- Used *italics* for emphasis in two places where Descartes' original clearly
  stresses certain words: "did" vs "said" and "improve" vs "deteriorate."

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (Part IV -- The Cogito)
----------------------------------------------------------
- First person "I" maintained throughout, conversational tone sustained.
- "Method" capitalized when referring to Descartes' specific Method.
- "Clear and distinct" appeared briefly in Maxim 2 context ("clear and determinate
  principle of choice") -- in Part IV it becomes a central technical term. Use
  "clear and distinct" as the paired phrase going forward.
- "The senses" should be consistent (Part IV will discuss how the senses deceive).
- Descartes' characteristic modesty and self-deprecation are well established;
  Part IV shifts to a more confident, argumentative tone as he presents the cogito.
  Let the voice shift naturally -- he's moving from practical rules to his greatest
  philosophical discovery.
- "I think, therefore I am" -- this is the big moment. Use the famous English
  formulation. The Veitch translation has "I think, hence I am" -- modernize to
  the universally recognized version.
- "Substance," "essence," "nature" -- these technical terms will become more
  frequent in Part IV. Keep them as-is per the text analysis.
- The dream argument appears in Part IV -- make it vivid and immediate per the
  text analysis guidance.
- Holland has been identified by name; Descartes is now settled there. Part IV
  begins with him in his heated room (poele), continuing the autobiographical
  frame.
- God proofs begin in Part IV -- translate with full seriousness per text analysis.
- "Good sense" / "reason" / "the light of reason" -- these have been used
  interchangeably in the Discourse so far. Part IV will lean more on "reason"
  and "natural light." Keep "natural light" as a recognizable Cartesian term.
