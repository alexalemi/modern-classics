CHAPTER 007 TRANSLATION NOTES
Meditations on the First Philosophy -- Dedication to the Sorbonne
=================================================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: This chapter contains some of the most extreme sentence
   construction in the entire volume. The original has sentences running 10-15
   lines with multiple semicolons, nested parenthetical clauses, and chains
   of subordinate reasoning. The very first sentence of the body text is a
   single sprawling construction. Almost every sentence was broken into
   multiple shorter ones while preserving the logical chain of argument.

2. OPENING ADDRESS: Changed "Gentlemen" (which Veitch uses for the formal
   Latin address) to "To the Distinguished Faculty of Theology at the
   Sorbonne" as a header, with "Gentlemen" retained as the salutation.
   This makes the recipient and context immediately clear.

3. "INFIDELS" -> "NONBELIEVERS": The original uses "infidels" throughout,
   which carries strong negative connotations in modern English. Changed to
   "nonbelievers" which is more neutral and matches Descartes' actual tone --
   he's not hostile toward them, he's trying to find ways to persuade them.

4. CIRCULAR REASONING: The original's explanation of the circularity problem
   (believe God because of Scripture, believe Scripture because of God) was
   tangled in nested clauses. Broke it into a clear statement of the circle
   followed by the explicit observation that nonbelievers would spot it.

5. SCRIPTURE QUOTATIONS: Kept the biblical quotations (Wisdom 13, Romans 1)
   intact and clearly attributed, as they are central to Descartes' argument
   that philosophy is doing what Scripture itself demands.

6. THE LATERAN COUNCIL REFERENCE: Kept the historical reference to the
   Lateran Council under Leo X, Session VIII, as it's crucial context --
   Descartes is saying the Church itself has ordered philosophers to prove
   these things. This is a strategic move, not mere citation.

7. "SCIOLISTS" -> "MORE PRETENTIOUS THAN TRULY KNOWLEDGEABLE OR INTELLIGENT":
   "Sciolist" (a person who pretends to be knowledgeable) is completely
   unknown to modern readers. Expanded to convey the meaning clearly.

8. "PERSPICUITY" -> "CLARITY"/"RIGOR": This archaic term appears twice in
   the original. Used "clarity" and "intellectual rigor" depending on context.

9. GEOMETRY ANALOGY: Descartes' comparison of his metaphysical proofs to
   geometric demonstrations (Archimedes, Apollonius, Pappus) is central
   to his argumentative strategy. Kept the named mathematicians and made
   the analogy's logic clearer by breaking it into shorter steps.

10. POLITICAL STRATEGY: This dedication is fundamentally a political document.
    Descartes is asking the Sorbonne for three things: (a) correct his errors,
    (b) fill in gaps, and (c) officially endorse his arguments. The translation
    preserves the rhetorical escalation toward (c), which is what he really
    wants. The flattery of the Sorbonne's authority is deliberate and
    strategic.

11. FOOTNOTE REFERENCE: The original text has a bare "7" footnote marker near
    the end referring to bracketed text. Integrated the bracketed content
    directly into the sentence and omitted the footnote number per the
    text_analysis guidance.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS
--------------------
- "Natural reason" -- kept as-is; it's clear enough and is Descartes' key term
  for reason unaided by faith/revelation
- "Demonstrations" -- kept; it's Descartes' technical term for proofs that
  carry certainty (as opposed to mere arguments or persuasion)
- "Clear and distinct" -- used "certainty and clarity" in this context since
  the full technical phrase appears in the Meditations proper
- "The commerce of the senses" -> "the influence of the senses"

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (008 -- Preface to the Reader)
-----------------------------------------------------------------
- The Meditations section has now begun. The tone shifts from the Discourse's
  autobiographical narrative to something more formal and argumentative.
- This dedication establishes the key framing: Descartes is seeking to prove
  God's existence and the soul's distinction from the body using reason alone.
  The Preface (008) will continue this framing.
- "Demonstrations" is Descartes' preferred term for his proofs -- maintain
  this throughout the Meditations.
- "Nonbelievers" is the term chosen for those who don't accept God's existence
  (replacing the original's "infidels" and "atheists"). However, "atheists"
  appears in the original near the end and was kept there since Descartes
  uses it specifically and pointedly. Use judgment on which term fits in
  future chapters.
- The Sorbonne faculty are addressed with deference -- this tone of strategic
  politeness should carry into the Preface, which also addresses an audience
  Descartes wants to win over.
- "The senses" -- established as the consistent term (per text_analysis).
- The geometry analogy (proofs as demonstrations comparable to mathematical
  ones) will recur. Maintain the same framing.
