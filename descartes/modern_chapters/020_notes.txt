CHAPTER 020 TRANSLATION NOTES
Principles of Philosophy -- Part III: Of the Visible World
============================================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
----------------------------

1. STRUCTURE: The original consists of three unnumbered propositions presented as
   continuous paragraphs. Unlike Parts I and II (which have numbered sections with
   Roman numerals), Part III in this selection has only three brief passages.
   Preserved the three-paragraph structure but added a transitional paragraph
   ("But before we can properly investigate this, two things must be kept in mind")
   to make the logical organization clearer.

2. SENTENCE LENGTH: Each of the three original passages is essentially a single
   enormous sentence. The first passage runs roughly 100 words in one sentence;
   the second is similar; the third is even longer. Broke each into multiple
   clear sentences while maintaining the logical flow.

3. "PREJUDICES OF THE SENSES" -> "biases of the senses." "Prejudice" in Descartes
   means "preconceived opinion" or "unexamined assumption," consistent with earlier
   chapters. "Biases" captures this sense naturally.

4. "PHILOSOPHIZING ARIGHT" -> "properly investigate." The original "in order to
   our philosophizing aright regarding this" is extremely awkward in modern English.

5. "THE INFINITY OF THE POWER AND GOODNESS OF GOD" -> "the infinite power and
   goodness of God." Small grammatical modernization.

6. "PLAINLY RIDICULOUS AND INEPT IN PHYSICAL REASONING" -> "plainly absurd and
   foolish when it comes to understanding the physical world." "Inept" in the
   original means "unsuitable/foolish" (its older sense), not "clumsy."

7. THEOLOGICAL CONTENT: This chapter contains an important methodological point:
   God's creation may exceed our understanding, and we should not assume
   anthropocentric purposes in nature. This is a surprisingly modern and humble
   stance. Translated with full seriousness -- this is Descartes the scientist
   arguing for intellectual humility.

TONE
----
This is textbook-mode Descartes: declarative, systematic, less personal than the
Discourse. The three passages function as methodological ground rules before the
investigation of the physical world. The voice is that of a careful professor
setting up the framework before diving into specifics.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (021 -- Principles Part IV: Of the Earth)
----------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Part IV returns to numbered sections (Roman numerals) covering the earth and
  physical phenomena: the senses, perception, pain, pleasure, the five external
  senses, how the soul perceives through the brain.
- Key terms to maintain: "clear and distinct," "substance," "extension," "mode."
- "The senses" (not "our senses") -- consistent with earlier chapters.
- "Physical" for "corporeal" -- established vocabulary mapping.
- The content becomes much more concrete and scientific: nerves, brain, sensory
  organs, specific medical examples. Translate with clarity and vividness.
- Descartes uses parenthetical Latin terms (sensuum perceptiones, sensus, etc.)
  -- handle these as brief parenthetical notes or integrate naturally.
- The phantom limb example at the end of the perception section is a powerful
  concrete illustration -- make it vivid and immediate.
