TEXT ANALYSIS — Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea
(Jules Verne, 1870; Vingt mille lieues sous les mers)
Source: the ORIGINAL FRENCH from Project Gutenberg (#5097), the Hetzel
text. TRANSLATED FROM THE FRENCH. The from-the-Latin/Italian pipeline
pattern applies: translate from Verne's own French; there is no bundled
crib, and the notorious public-domain English (Lewis Mercier, 1873) is
exactly what we are replacing — see below.

WHY THIS BOOK IS THE PROJECT'S THESIS

For a century English readers met Verne through Lewis Mercier's 1873
translation, which cut roughly a fifth of the text, mangled the science
(Mercier didn't understand it), flattened Verne's prose, and even
softened Captain Nemo's anti-imperial politics. Verne wrote a thrilling,
scientifically serious adventure for a broad audience; the English-
speaking world got a garbled boys'-own abridgment. Retelling the COMPLETE
book in vivid, accurate modern English restores the novel Verne actually
wrote — and it should read like the page-turner it is.

WHAT THIS IS

A first-person adventure narrated by Professor Pierre Aronnax. In 1866 a
mysterious sea "monster" terrorizes shipping; Aronnax joins the hunt,
is thrown overboard with his servant and a harpooner, and the three are
captured by Captain Nemo, commander of the submarine Nautilus. What
follows is a ten-month, 20,000-league voyage under every sea, half
captivity and half wonder, ending in the maelström off Norway.

THE FOUR VOICES — get these right and the book works

- PROFESSOR ARONNAX — the narrator: erudite French marine biologist,
  endlessly curious, morally torn between his captivity and the
  once-in-history spectacle Nemo shows him. Warm, reflective, occasionally
  wry. His narration carries the science; keep it lucid, never dry.
- CONSEIL — Aronnax's Flemish manservant: unflappable, utterly devoted, a
  compulsive classifier of animals who freely admits he can name a species
  only once it's dead on a table. SIGNATURE TRAIT: he addresses and refers
  to Aronnax in the third person as "monsieur" — never "you." Render this
  consistently as "Master" ("Just as Master pleases," "Master called?").
  It is his defining comedy and dignity; do not modernize it away.
- NED LAND — Canadian master harpooner: blunt, hot-tempered, practical,
  hungry (literally — he wants fresh meat, not fish), and desperate for
  freedom. The book's id and its comic engine, and Aronnax's foil. Give
  him plain, punchy, salty speech — but he is shrewd, not a buffoon.
- CAPTAIN NEMO — brilliant, cultured, wounded, vengeful; a genius who has
  renounced humanity for the sea yet weeps over it. Byronic and formal.
  His dignity must never tip into camp. "Nemo" is Latin for "No one."

STYLE AND TONE

- ADVENTURE FIRST. This is a page-turner; the modern voice is clear,
  propulsive, cinematic. Verne's long periodic French sentences break into
  natural modern rhythm. Contractions welcome in dialogue and fine in
  narration. Keep the wonder and the menace vivid.
- COMPLETE, never abridged — this is the whole point. The long passages of
  natural history (Conseil's and Aronnax's species catalogues), the
  Nautilus's engineering, oceanography, and geography STAY. Make them read
  with a naturalist's delight rather than a textbook's drone: they are the
  texture of the wonder. Do not summarize a list into "many kinds of fish."
- THE SCIENCE STAYS RIGHT and stays as Verne wrote it (including where he
  is dated or wrong — no silent corrections; this is a historical text).
  Keep Latin species names as Verne gives them, italicized in sense.
  Keep metric units (meters, kilometers, leagues, atmospheres, degrees
  Celsius) — Verne is proudly metric; add a light gloss only where a
  modern reader would otherwise lose the scale, and only once.
- Nautical and period vocabulary rendered accurately but readably.

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY

- Names kept: the Nautilus; Captain Nemo; Professor Aronnax (Pierre
  Aronnax); Conseil; Ned Land. The frigate Abraham Lincoln; Commander
  Farragut; the ship Scotia, the Moravian.
- "the Nautilus" (italic sense), "Captain Nemo", "the Professor".
- Conseil -> Aronnax address = "Master" (third person), always.
- Nemo's motto/monogram: "Mobilis in Mobili" kept in Latin (Verne's title
  spells it "Mobilis in mobile"; the ship's device reads "Mobilis in
  Mobili" — keep the Latin, gloss once: "moving within the moving
  element"). The monogram is a letter N.
- The mysterious onboard language phrase "Nautron respoc lorni virch"
  kept verbatim (untranslatable by design).
- "leagues" for lieues (a league ≈ 4 km; the title's 20,000 leagues is
  DISTANCE TRAVELED, not depth — never imply depth). "knots" for nœuds.
- Units: meters/metres, atmospheres (pressure), Celsius. Depth in meters.
- Species: keep the common French/English common names Verne uses, plus
  his Latin binomials. "cachalots" = sperm whales; "baleines" = (right/
  baleen) whales; "poulpe(s)" = the great cephalopod of Ch. XVIII —
  Verne's animal is a giant octopus/"devilfish"; render the creature
  consistently (the chapter title is "The Giant Squid" for recognizability,
  but follow Verne's actual descriptions in the body).

SENSITIVE / EDITORIAL

- Nemo's anti-imperial fury (against oppression, against a great maritime
  power never named but understood) is central and was softened by
  Mercier. Render it in full force, as Verne wrote it — it is the
  character's soul, not a flaw to manage.
- Period-dated attitudes and ethnographic descriptions (the Papuan
  "savages" of the Torres Strait, etc.) are of 1870: translate the
  author's meaning faithfully in a measured register, neither
  sensationalized nor bowdlerized, as scholarly modernization of a
  canonical text.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — must survive vividly (checked by must_contain)

- The opening: the 1866 panic over the phantom sea-monster.
- "Mobilis in Mobili" — Nemo's motto, first revealed on the tableware.
- Nemo: "The sea is everything... the Living Infinite" (la mer est tout;
  the great hymn to the sea in Part One).
- The giant-squid / devilfish battle (Part Two, Ch. XVIII).
- The South Pole: Nemo plants his black flag with the gold N.
- The final maelström off the Lofoten coast.
