A Vanished Continent

The next morning, February 19, I saw Ned come into my cabin. I had been expecting him. He looked thoroughly crestfallen.

"Well, Professor?" he said.

"Well, Ned, our luck was out yesterday."

"Right! That cursed captain had to stop just at the very hour we were about to slip away from his boat."

"Yes, Ned. He had business with his banker."

"His banker!"

"Or rather his banking house. By which I mean that Ocean out there, where his riches are safer than they'd be in the vaults of any government."

I then told the Canadian about the previous night's events, in the secret hope of nudging him back toward the idea of not abandoning the captain; but the only result of my account was Ned's forcefully expressed regret at not having managed to take a little stroll of his own across the battlefield of Vigo.

"Well," he said, "it's not over yet! It's just one harpoon thrown and missed! Next time we'll pull it off — this very evening, if we have to..."

"Which way is the Nautilus heading?" I asked.

"No idea," Ned answered.

"All right. At noon we'll get our position."

The Canadian went back to Conseil. As soon as I was dressed, I stepped into the salon. The compass was anything but reassuring. The Nautilus's course lay south-southwest. We were turning our backs on Europe.

I waited with a certain impatience for the fix to be marked on the chart. Toward half past eleven the ballast tanks emptied, and our vessel rose to the surface of the Ocean. I rushed up to the platform. Ned Land had beaten me there.

No more land in sight. Nothing but the immense sea. A few sails on the horizon — bound, no doubt, all the way out to Cape San Roque in search of the favorable winds for rounding the Cape of Good Hope. The sky was overcast. A gale was brewing.

Ned, seething, tried to pierce the hazy horizon. He was still hoping that behind all that fog stretched the land he wanted so badly.

At noon the sun showed itself for a moment. The first mate seized on the break in the clouds to take its altitude. Then, as the sea grew heavier, we went back down and the hatch was shut.

An hour later, when I consulted the chart, I saw that the Nautilus's position was marked at 16°17′ longitude and 33°22′ latitude, a hundred and fifty leagues from the nearest coast. There was no thinking of escape now, and I leave you to imagine the Canadian's fury when I gave him the news.

For my own part, I didn't take it too hard. I felt as though a weight that had been pressing on me had lifted, and I was able to go back to my usual work with something like relative calm.

That evening, around eleven o'clock, I had a most unexpected visit from Captain Nemo. He asked me very graciously whether I felt tired after sitting up the night before. I answered that I did not.

"Then, Professor Aronnax, I have a curious excursion to propose."

"Propose away, Captain."

"So far you've only seen the sea floor by day, under the light of the sun. Would it suit you to see it on a dark night?"

"Very gladly."

"This walk will be tiring, I warn you. We'll have to march a long way and climb a mountain. The roads aren't very well kept."

"What you tell me, Captain, only sharpens my curiosity. I'm ready to follow you."

"Come along, then, Professor. We'll go put on our diving suits."

When I reached the changing room, I saw that neither my companions nor any member of the crew was to come with us on this excursion. Captain Nemo hadn't even offered to bring Ned or Conseil.

In a few moments we had climbed into our gear. The air tanks, generously charged, were set on our backs, but the electric lamps were not made ready. I pointed this out to the captain.

"They'd be of no use to us," he answered.

I thought I must have misheard him, but I couldn't repeat my remark, because the captain's head had already vanished into its metal sheath. I finished harnessing myself up, felt an iron-tipped staff placed in my hand, and a few minutes later, after the usual maneuver, we set foot on the bottom of the Atlantic, at a depth of three hundred meters.

Midnight was near. The waters were profoundly dark, but Captain Nemo pointed out to me, far off, a reddish point, a kind of broad glow, shining about two miles from the Nautilus. What that fire was, what fed it, why and how it kept renewing itself within the mass of water, I couldn't have said. In any case, it lit our way — dimly, it's true, but I soon grew used to those peculiar shadows, and I understood, in these circumstances, why the Ruhmkorff lamps were useless.

Captain Nemo and I walked side by side, straight toward the beacon fire. The flat ground rose imperceptibly. We took long strides, helping ourselves along with the staff; but our pace was slow, all in all, for our feet kept sinking into a kind of mud kneaded through with seaweed and strewn with flat stones.

As I went, I heard a sort of crackling overhead. Now and then the noise doubled, becoming a continuous sputter. I soon understood the cause. It was rain, falling hard and pattering on the surface of the waves. Instinctively the thought came to me that I was about to get soaked! By water, in the middle of water! I couldn't help laughing at the absurd idea. But to tell the truth, under the thick coat of a diving suit, you no longer feel the liquid element; you imagine yourself in the middle of an atmosphere only a little denser than the one on land — and that's all there is to it.

After half an hour's walk, the ground turned rocky. Jellyfish, microscopic crustaceans, and sea pens lit it faintly with phosphorescent glimmers. I made out heaps of stones covered by a few million zoophytes and tangles of algae. My foot kept slipping on those slimy carpets of wrack, and without my iron-tipped staff I'd have fallen more than once. When I turned around, I could still see the whitish beacon of the Nautilus, beginning to pale in the distance.

Those piles of stone I've just mentioned were arranged on the ocean floor with a certain regularity I couldn't account for. I made out gigantic furrows that lost themselves in the far darkness, their length beyond any reckoning. Other oddities presented themselves too, things I couldn't bring myself to admit. It seemed to me that my heavy lead soles were crushing a litter of bones that snapped with a dry crack. What, then, was this vast plain I was crossing? I would have liked to question the captain, but his sign language — which let him talk with his companions when they followed him on his undersea excursions — was still incomprehensible to me.

Meanwhile the reddish light that guided us grew stronger and set the horizon ablaze. The presence of this furnace beneath the waters intrigued me to the highest degree. Was it some electrical discharge making itself known? Was I walking toward a natural phenomenon still unknown to the scientists of the land? Or even — for the thought crossed my mind — was a human hand at work in this conflagration? Was it a human hand fanning that blaze? Was I to meet, beneath these deep waters, companions and friends of Captain Nemo, living as he did this strange existence, and to whom he was paying a visit? Would I find down there a whole colony of exiles who, weary of the world's miseries, had sought and found their independence in the deepest reaches of the Ocean? All these wild, inadmissible notions pursued me, and in that state of mind, endlessly overexcited by the parade of marvels passing before my eyes, I would not have been surprised to come upon, at the bottom of that sea, one of those undersea cities Captain Nemo dreamed of!

Our road grew brighter and brighter. The whitening glow radiated from the summit of a mountain some eight hundred feet high. But what I saw was only a reflection, thrown up by the crystal of the layers of water. The furnace, the source of this inexplicable radiance, lay on the far slope of the mountain.

Through the stony labyrinths that seamed the floor of the Atlantic, Captain Nemo advanced without hesitation. He knew this dark road. He had traveled it often, no doubt, and could not lose his way on it. I followed him with unshakable confidence. He seemed to me one of the sea's own genies, and when he strode ahead of me, I admired his tall figure, cut out in black against the glowing background of the horizon.

It was one in the morning. We had reached the first slopes of the mountain. But to tackle them, we had to venture along the difficult paths of a vast thicket.

Yes! A thicket of dead trees, leafless and sapless, trees turned to stone under the action of the water, with here and there a gigantic pine towering above them. It was like a coalfield still standing upright, holding to the sunken ground by its roots, its branches, like the fine cutout of black paper, drawn sharply against the ceiling of the waters. Picture a forest in the Harz Mountains, clinging to the flanks of a slope — but a forest swallowed by the sea. The paths were choked with seaweed and wrack, among which swarmed a whole world of crustaceans. On I went, scaling the rocks, striding over the fallen trunks, snapping the sea vines that swung from tree to tree, startling the fish that flitted from branch to branch. Swept along, I no longer felt tired. I followed my guide, who never tired at all.

What a spectacle! How to convey it? How to paint the look of these woods and rocks in that liquid element — their undersides dark and savage, their tops washed with red tones under a light doubled by the reflecting power of the water? We climbed rocks that then broke away in enormous slabs, tumbling down with the muffled roar of an avalanche. To the right and left yawned shadowy galleries where the eye lost itself. Here vast clearings opened up, as though a human hand had cleared them, and I wondered at times whether some inhabitant of these undersea regions might not suddenly appear before me.

But Captain Nemo climbed on and on. I did not want to fall behind. I followed him boldly. My staff was a real help. A false step would have been dangerous on those narrow ledges hollowed out along the flanks of the chasms; but I trod them with a firm foot, feeling none of the dizzy pull of vertigo. Now I leaped a crevasse whose depth would have made me draw back among the glaciers on land; now I ventured out onto the swaying trunk of a tree flung from one abyss to another, never looking beneath my feet, my eyes only for the wild scenery of this region. Here monumental rocks, tilted on their irregularly cut bases, seemed to defy the laws of balance. Between their stone knees, trees sprang up as though shot out under tremendous pressure, and held up the very rocks that held them. Then natural towers, broad sheer faces cut like the curtain walls of a fortress, leaned at an angle that the laws of gravity would never have permitted on the surface of the land.

And did I not feel that difference myself, owing to the powerful density of the water, when — despite my heavy garments, my copper head, my metal soles — I climbed slopes of impossible steepness, clearing them, so to speak, with the lightness of a mountain goat or a chamois!

In telling the story of this excursion beneath the waves, I feel well enough that no one will believe me! I am the historian of things that seem impossible and yet are real, beyond dispute. I did not dream it. I saw and felt it!

Two hours after leaving the Nautilus, we had crossed the tree line, and a hundred feet above our heads rose the peak of the mountain, its shadow thrown across the dazzling glow of the opposite slope. A few petrified shrubs straggled here and there in grimacing zigzags. Fish rose in masses beneath our steps, like birds startled in tall grass. The rocky mass was pitted with impenetrable crevices, deep grottoes, unfathomable holes, at the bottom of which I could hear formidable things stirring. My blood ran back to my heart when I glimpsed some enormous feeler barring my path, or some terrifying claw snapping shut with a clack in the shadow of the hollows! Thousands of luminous points glittered amid the darkness. They were the eyes of gigantic crustaceans crouched in their dens — giant lobsters rearing up like halberdiers and clashing their legs with a rattle of old iron, titanic crabs trained like cannon on their carriages, and hideous octopuses interlacing their tentacles like a living bramble of serpents.

What was this extravagant world I still knew nothing of? To what order did these jointed creatures belong, for whom the rock formed a kind of second shell? Where had nature found the secret of their sluggish, plantlike existence, and for how many centuries had they lived like this in the lowest layers of the Ocean?

But I could not stop. Captain Nemo, familiar with these dreadful animals, no longer paid them any heed. We had reached a first plateau, where still other surprises awaited me. There stood picturesque ruins that betrayed the hand of man, and no longer that of the Creator. They were vast heaps of stone in which one could make out the vague forms of castles and temples, clothed in a whole world of flowering zoophytes, and over which the algae and wrack, in place of ivy, spread a thick vegetal mantle.

But what was this portion of the globe, swallowed up by cataclysms? Who had set these rocks and stones in place like the dolmens of prehistoric times? Where was I? Where had Captain Nemo's whim carried me?

I longed to ask him. Unable to, I stopped him. I seized his arm. But he, shaking his head and pointing to the last summit of the mountain, seemed to say to me:

"Come! Come on! Come along!"

I followed him in one last effort, and in a few minutes I had scaled the peak, which rose some ten meters above the whole rocky mass.

I looked back at the side we had just crossed. On this face the mountain rose only seven or eight hundred feet above the plain; but on its opposite slope it towered to twice that height above the sunken floor of this part of the Atlantic. My gaze stretched far and took in a vast space lit by a violent blaze. For this mountain was, in fact, a volcano. Fifty feet below the peak, amid a rain of stones and cinders, a broad crater vomited torrents of lava that scattered in a cascade of fire through the mass of water. Set there, this volcano, like an immense torch, lit the plain below all the way to the farthest limits of the horizon.

I said the undersea crater threw up lava, but not flames. Flames need the oxygen of the air, and cannot develop underwater; but streams of lava, which carry within themselves the principle of their own incandescence, can heat to white heat, do battle victoriously with the liquid element, and turn it to vapor on contact. Swift currents swept away all these gases in diffusion, and the lava torrents slid down to the foot of the mountain, like the discharges of Vesuvius over some second Torre del Greco.

And there, indeed, before my eyes, ruined, wrecked, cast down, lay a destroyed city — its roofs caved in, its temples toppled, its arches wrenched apart, its columns lying on the ground, where you could still feel the solid proportions of a kind of Tuscan architecture; farther off, some remains of a gigantic aqueduct; here the silted-up rise of an acropolis, with the floating outlines of a Parthenon; there the vestiges of a quay, as though some ancient harbor had once sheltered, on the shores of a vanished ocean, the merchant ships and the war triremes; farther still, long lines of crumbled walls, broad deserted streets — a whole Pompeii buried beneath the waters, which Captain Nemo was raising again before my eyes!

Where was I? Where was I? I had to know at any price, I wanted to speak, I wanted to tear off the copper sphere that imprisoned my head.

But Captain Nemo came over and stopped me with a gesture. Then, picking up a piece of chalky stone, he stepped toward a rock of black basalt and traced this single word:

ATLANTIS

What a flash of lightning shot through my mind! Atlantis — the ancient Meropis of Theopompus, the Atlantis of Plato, that continent denied by Origen, Porphyry, Iamblichus, d'Anville, Malte-Brun, and Humboldt, who set its disappearance down to the tales of legend, and admitted by Posidonius, Pliny, Ammianus Marcellinus, Tertullian, Engel, Scherer, Tournefort, Buffon, and d'Avezac — there it was, beneath my very eyes, still bearing the undeniable evidence of its catastrophe! So this was that sunken region that had existed beyond Europe, beyond Asia, beyond Libya, out past the Pillars of Hercules, where that mighty people the Atlanteans had lived — against whom the first wars of ancient Greece were waged!

The historian who set down in his writings the great deeds of those heroic times is Plato himself. His dialogue of Timaeus and Critias was traced, so to speak, under the inspiration of Solon, the poet and lawgiver.

One day Solon was talking with some wise old men of Saïs, a city already eight hundred years old, as its annals engraved on the sacred wall of its temples bore witness. One of these old men told the story of another city, a thousand years older still. This first Athenian city, ninety thousand years old, had been invaded and partly destroyed by the Atlanteans. These Atlanteans, he said, occupied an immense continent larger than Africa and Asia combined, covering an area that ran from the twelfth degree of latitude to the fortieth degree north. Their dominion reached even to Egypt. They tried to force it as far as Greece, but they had to fall back before the indomitable resistance of the Hellenes. Centuries passed. A cataclysm struck — floods, earthquakes. A single night and day were enough to blot out this Atlantis, whose highest peaks — Madeira, the Azores, the Canaries, the Cape Verde islands — still rise above the waves.

Such were the historical memories that Captain Nemo's inscription set throbbing in my mind. And so, led here by the strangest of destinies, I was treading one of the mountains of that continent! My hand was touching those ruins a thousand times ancient, contemporaries of the geological ages! I was walking on the very spot where the contemporaries of the first man had walked! Under my heavy soles I was crushing the skeletons of animals from fabled times, animals that these trees, now turned to stone, had once covered with their shade!

Ah! Why did I not have more time! I would have liked to descend the steep slopes of this mountain, to cross this whole immense continent — which no doubt once joined Africa to America — and to visit those great cities of before the Flood. There, perhaps, spread out beneath my eyes, lay Machimos the warlike and Eusebes the pious, whose giant inhabitants lived for whole centuries, and who had strength enough to heap up these blocks that still resisted the action of the water. One day, perhaps, some eruptive upheaval will bring these sunken ruins back to the surface! Many undersea volcanoes have been reported in this part of the Ocean, and many ships have felt extraordinary tremors as they passed over these tormented depths. Some have heard hollow rumblings that told of the deep struggle of the elements; others have gathered volcanic ash flung up out of the sea. All this ground, right down to the Equator, is still being worked by the plutonic forces. And who knows whether, in some distant age, swollen by volcanic discharges and by successive layers of lava, the peaks of fire-vomiting mountains may not one day break the surface of the Atlantic!

While I mused like this, while I tried to fix in my memory every detail of that magnificent landscape, Captain Nemo — leaning on a moss-grown pillar — stood motionless, as if turned to stone, in a mute rapture. Was he thinking of those vanished generations, asking them the secret of human destiny? Was this the place where this strange man came to steep himself again in the memories of history, and to relive that ancient life — he who wanted nothing of the modern one? What would I not have given to know his thoughts, to share them, to understand them!

We stayed in that spot for a full hour, gazing at the vast plain under the glow of the lava, which now and then flared to a startling intensity. The seething deep within sent quick shudders running across the crust of the mountain. Deep sounds, transmitted with perfect clarity through this liquid element, echoed back with a majestic breadth.

At that moment the moon appeared for an instant through the mass of the waters and cast a few pale rays over the sunken continent. It was only a glimmer, but the effect was beyond describing. The captain rose, took one last look at the immense plain; then with his hand he signaled me to follow him.

We came down the mountain quickly. Once past the mineral forest, I caught sight of the Nautilus's beacon shining like a star. The captain walked straight toward it, and we were back aboard just as the first tints of dawn were whitening the surface of the Ocean.
