The South Pole

(Part 2 of 2)

"They aren't dangerous animals?" Conseil asked me.

"No," I answered, "unless you attack them. When a seal defends its pup, its fury is terrible, and it isn't rare for it to smash a fisherman's boat to pieces."

"It's within its rights," Conseil replied.

"I won't say otherwise."

Two miles farther on, we were brought up short by the headland that sheltered the bay against the south winds. It dropped sheer to the sea and foamed under the surf. Beyond it came bursts of tremendous bellowing, the kind a herd of cattle might produce.

"Well now," said Conseil, "a concert of bulls?"

"No," I said, "a concert of walruses. Are they fighting?"

"They're fighting or they're playing."

"With Master's leave, we ought to see this."

"We ought to, Conseil."

And so there we were, scrambling across the blackish rocks amid unexpected landslides, over stones the ice made dangerously slippery. More than once I went down, at the expense of my back. Conseil, more careful or more sure-footed, barely stumbled at all, and would haul me up, saying:

"If Master would be so good as to spread his legs a little, Master would keep his balance better."

Reaching the upper ridge of the headland, I saw a vast white plain covered with walruses. The animals were playing among themselves. Those were howls of joy, not of anger.

Walruses resemble seals in the shape of their bodies and the arrangement of their limbs. But they lack canines and incisors in the lower jaw, and as for the upper canines, these are two tusks eighty centimeters long that measure thirty-three around at the socket. These teeth, made of a compact, unstreaked ivory harder than an elephant's and slower to yellow, are much sought after. And so the walruses are the target of a reckless hunt that will soon destroy them down to the last one, since the hunters, slaughtering pregnant females and young indiscriminately, kill off more than four thousand of them every year.

Passing close by these curious creatures, I could examine them at my leisure, for they did not stir. Their hide was thick and rough, a tawny tone verging on red, their coat short and sparse. Some measured four meters in length. Calmer and less fearful than their northern cousins, they posted no chosen sentinels to watch the approaches to their camp.

After examining this city of walruses, I thought about turning back. It was eleven o'clock, and if Captain Nemo found conditions favorable for an observation, I wanted to be present for it. Yet I had little hope the sun would show itself that day. Clouds crushed low against the horizon hid it from our eyes. It was as if that jealous star refused to reveal this unreachable point of the globe to human beings.

Still, I made up my mind to head back to the Nautilus. We followed a narrow path that ran along the top of the cliff. At half past eleven we had reached the landing place. The beached dinghy had set the captain ashore. I spotted him standing on a block of basalt, his instruments beside him. His gaze was fixed on the northern horizon, near which the sun was tracing its long, drawn-out arc.

I took my place beside him and waited without a word. Noon came, and, just as the day before, the sun did not appear.

It was a stroke of bad luck. The observation had failed once more. If it was not accomplished tomorrow, we would have to give up for good on fixing our position.

For we were precisely at March 20. Tomorrow, the 21st, the day of the equinox, refraction not counted, the sun would drop below the horizon for six months, and with its disappearance the long polar night would begin. Since the September equinox it had risen out of the northern horizon, climbing in long spirals until December 21. At that point, the summer solstice of these austral regions, it had begun to descend again, and tomorrow it would cast down its last rays.

I shared my observations and my fears with Captain Nemo.

"You were right, Professor Aronnax," he told me. "If tomorrow I don't get the sun's altitude, I won't be able to try again for six months. But then, precisely because the chances of my navigation have brought me into these seas on March 21, my position will be easy to fix — if, at noon, the sun shows itself to us."

"Why is that, Captain?"

"Because when the sun traces such long spirals, it's difficult to measure its height above the horizon exactly, and the instruments are apt to make serious errors."

"So how will you proceed?"

"I'll use nothing but my chronometer," Captain Nemo answered. "If tomorrow, March 21, at noon, the sun's disk — allowing for refraction — is cut exactly in half by the northern horizon, then I'm at the South Pole."

"True enough," I said. "But that claim isn't mathematically rigorous, because the equinox doesn't necessarily fall at noon."

"No doubt, Professor, but the error won't be a hundred meters, and we need no better than that. Until tomorrow, then."

Captain Nemo returned aboard. Conseil and I stayed until five o'clock, pacing the beach, observing and studying. I gathered nothing curious, except for a penguin egg, remarkable for its size, that a collector would have paid more than a thousand francs for. Its isabelline color, the streaks and markings that adorned it like so many hieroglyphs, made it a rare trinket. I placed it in Conseil's hands, and the careful, sure-footed lad carried it back intact to the Nautilus, holding it like a precious piece of China porcelain.

There I set that rare egg beneath one of the museum's display cases. I had a hearty supper of an excellent slice of seal liver, whose taste recalled that of pork. Then I went to bed, not without invoking, like a Hindu, the favors of the radiant star.

The next morning, March 21, at five o'clock, I climbed up onto the platform. There I found Captain Nemo.

"The weather is clearing a little," he told me. "I have good hopes. After breakfast we'll go ashore to choose an observation post."

That settled, I went to find Ned Land. I would have liked to bring him with me. The stubborn Canadian refused, and I could plainly see that his silence, like his foul temper, was growing worse by the day. All in all, I didn't regret his obstinacy in this instance. There truly were too many seals ashore, and it wouldn't do to subject that rash hunter to such temptation.

Breakfast over, I went ashore. The Nautilus had risen a few more miles during the night. It lay well out, a good league from a coast dominated by a sharp peak four or five hundred meters high. The dinghy carried, along with me, Captain Nemo, two of the crew, and the instruments — that is, a chronometer, a telescope, and a barometer.

During our crossing I saw numerous whales belonging to the three species peculiar to the southern seas: the right whale — the "right-whale" of the English — which has no dorsal fin; the humpback, a rorqual with a pleated belly and vast whitish flippers that, despite their name, do not form wings; and the fin-back, a brownish-yellow whale, the liveliest of the cetaceans. This powerful animal can be heard from far off when it hurls up to a great height its columns of air and vapor, which look like whirls of smoke. These various mammals were frolicking in herds through the calm waters, and I could plainly see that this basin at the Antarctic pole now served as a refuge for the cetaceans too fiercely hunted by the whalers.

I also noticed long whitish strings of salps, a kind of clustered mollusk, and large jellyfish that swayed among the swell of the waves.

At nine o'clock we made land. The sky was brightening. The clouds were fleeing to the south. The mists were abandoning the cold surface of the waters. Captain Nemo made for the peak, which he no doubt meant to use as his observatory. It was a grueling climb over sharp lava and pumice stones, through an atmosphere often saturated with the sulfurous fumes of the fumaroles. The captain, for a man unused to treading on land, scaled the steepest slopes with a suppleness and an agility I couldn't match, and that a chamois hunter would have envied.

It took us two hours to reach the summit of this peak, half porphyry, half basalt. From there our eyes took in a vast sea that, toward the north, drew its terminal line sharply against the backdrop of the sky. At our feet, fields dazzling with whiteness. Over our heads, a pale blue, cleared of mist. To the north, the sun's disk like a ball of fire already notched by the edge of the horizon. From the bosom of the waters rose magnificent sheaves of liquid jets, hundreds of them. In the distance, the Nautilus, like a sleeping whale. Behind us, toward the south and east, an immense land, a chaotic heaping-up of rocks and ice whose limit could not be seen.

Reaching the summit of the peak, Captain Nemo carefully took its altitude by means of the barometer, since he would have to account for it in his observation.

At a quarter to noon the sun, now seen by refraction alone, showed itself as a disk of gold and scattered its last rays over this abandoned continent, over these seas that man has never yet furrowed.

Captain Nemo, equipped with a telescope fitted with crosshairs that corrected the refraction by means of a mirror, watched the sun sink little by little below the horizon, following a long-drawn-out diagonal. I held the chronometer. My heart was pounding. If the disappearance of the sun's half-disk coincided with noon on the chronometer, we were at the pole itself.

"Noon!" I cried out.

"The South Pole!" answered Captain Nemo in a grave voice, handing me the telescope, which showed the sun cut precisely into two equal portions by the horizon.

I watched the last rays crown the peak and the shadows climb slowly up its slopes.

At that moment Captain Nemo, resting his hand on my shoulder, said to me:

"Professor, in 1600 the Dutchman Ghéritk, carried off by currents and storms, reached 64° south latitude and discovered the New Shetlands. In 1773, on January 17, the illustrious Cook, following the thirty-eighth meridian, arrived at 67°30′ latitude, and in 1774, on January 30, on the one hundred and ninth meridian, he reached 71°15′ latitude. In 1819 the Russian Bellingshausen found himself on the sixty-ninth parallel, and in 1821 on the sixty-sixth at 111° west longitude. In 1820 the Englishman Brunsfield was stopped at the sixty-fifth degree. That same year the American Morrell, whose accounts are doubtful, working up the forty-second meridian, discovered open sea at 70°14′ latitude. In 1825 the Englishman Powell could get no farther than the sixty-second degree. That same year a simple seal-hunter, the Englishman Weddell, rose to 72°14′ latitude on the thirty-fifth meridian, and to 74°15′ on the thirty-sixth. In 1829 the Englishman Forster, commanding the Chanticleer, took possession of the Antarctic continent at 63°26′ latitude and 66°26′ longitude. In 1831 the Englishman Biscoe, on February 1, discovered Enderby Land at 68°50′ latitude; in 1832, on February 5, Adelaide Land at 67° latitude, and on February 21, Graham Land at 64°45′ latitude. In 1838 the Frenchman Dumont d'Urville, stopped before the ice pack at 62°57′ latitude, charted Louis-Philippe Land; two years later, on a fresh thrust south, he named — at 66°30′, on January 21 — Adélie Land, and eight days after that, at 64°40′, the Clarie Coast. In 1838 the Englishman Wilkes advanced as far as the sixty-ninth parallel on the hundredth meridian. In 1839 the Englishman Balleny discovered Sabrina Land, on the very edge of the polar circle. Finally, in 1842, the Englishman James Ross, aboard the Erebus and the Terror, on January 12, at 76°56′ latitude and 171°7′ east longitude, found Victoria Land; on the 23rd of the same month he charted the seventy-fourth parallel, the highest point reached until then; on the 27th he was at 76°8′, on the 28th at 77°32′, on February 2 at 78°4′, and in 1842 he came back to the seventy-first degree, which he could not pass. Well then — I, Captain Nemo, on this March 21, 1868, have reached the South Pole at the ninetieth degree, and I take possession of this part of the globe, equal to one-sixth of the known continents."

"In whose name, Captain?"

"In my own, Professor!"

And so saying, Captain Nemo unfurled a black flag, bearing a gold N quartered upon its field. Then, turning back toward the sun, whose last rays were licking the horizon of the sea:

"Farewell, sun!" he cried. "Vanish, radiant star! Set beneath this open sea, and let a night six months long spread its shadows over my new domain!"
