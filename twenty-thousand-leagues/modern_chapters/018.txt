Vanikoro

That terrible spectacle was only the first in a whole series of maritime disasters that the Nautilus was to encounter along its course. Ever since it had begun following more frequented waters, we often caught sight of wrecked hulls rotting away between the surface and the deep, and, lower down, cannons, cannonballs, anchors, chains, and a thousand other iron objects being devoured by rust.

Meanwhile, still carried along by the Nautilus — where we lived as though cut off from the world — on December 11 we came within sight of the Pomotou archipelago, Bougainville's old "dangerous group," which stretches across a span of five hundred leagues from east-southeast to west-northwest, between 13°30′ and 23°50′ south latitude, and 125°30′ and 151°30′ west longitude, from Ducie Island to Lazareff Island. The archipelago covers an area of three hundred and seventy square leagues and is made up of some sixty clusters of islands, among which one notes the Gambier group, over which France has imposed its protectorate. These islands are coral formations. A slow but ceaseless upheaval, driven by the labor of the polyps, will one day link them all together. Then this new island will later fuse with the neighboring archipelagos, and a fifth continent will stretch from New Zealand and New Caledonia all the way to the Marquesas.

The day I laid out this theory before Captain Nemo, he answered me coldly:

"It isn't new continents the earth needs, but new men!"

The chances of its navigation had led the Nautilus straight toward Clermont-Tonnerre Island, one of the most curious of the group, discovered in 1822 by Captain Bell of the Minerve. I was able, then, to study the madrepore system to which the islands of this ocean owe their existence.

Madrepores — which one must be careful not to confuse with corals — have a tissue coated in a calcareous crust, and the variations in its structure led Monsieur Milne-Edwards, my illustrious teacher, to classify them into five sections. The tiny animalcules that secrete this coral stone live by the billions at the bottom of their cells. It is their calcareous deposits that become rocks, reefs, islets, islands. In one place they form a circular ring surrounding a lagoon or small inner lake, which openings connect with the sea. In another they take the shape of barrier reefs like those along the coasts of New Caledonia and various islands of the Pomotou. Elsewhere, as at Réunion and Mauritius, they raise up fringing reefs — high, sheer walls, alongside which the ocean plunges to considerable depths.

Skirting the sheer flanks of Clermont-Tonnerre Island by only a few cable-lengths, I marveled at the gigantic work accomplished by these microscopic laborers. These walls were specifically the work of the madrepores known as millepores, porites, astraea, and meandrina. These polyps develop above all in the churning surface layers of the sea, and so it is by their upper part that they begin these substructures, which sink little by little along with the debris of secretions that support them. Such, at least, is the theory of Monsieur Darwin, who explains the formation of atolls this way — a theory superior, in my view, to the one that gives the madrepore works a foundation of mountaintops or volcanoes submerged a few feet below sea level.

I could observe these curious walls at very close range, for the sounding line marked more than three hundred meters of depth right at their base, and our electric sheets set the brilliant limestone glittering.

Answering a question Conseil put to me about how long it took these colossal barriers to grow, I astonished him considerably by telling him that scientists reckoned that growth at one-eighth of an inch per century.

"So then," he said to me, "to raise these walls, it took...?"

"A hundred and ninety-two thousand years, my good Conseil, which stretches the biblical days out rather a lot. Besides, the formation of coal — that is, the mineralization of forests swallowed up by floods — required a far greater span of time still. But I would add that the days of the Bible are only epochs, and not the interval that passes between two sunrises, because, according to the Bible itself, the sun does not date from the first day of creation."

When the Nautilus rose back to the surface of the ocean, I could take in the whole spread of this island of Clermont-Tonnerre, low and wooded. Its madrepore rocks had plainly been made fertile by waterspouts and storms. One day some seed, torn from the neighboring lands by a hurricane, fell onto the calcareous layers, mixed with the decomposed remains of fish and sea plants that formed the vegetable soil. A coconut, driven by the waves, arrived on this new coast. The germ took root. The tree, growing tall, held back the water vapor. A stream was born. Vegetation gained ground little by little. A few animalcules, worms, insects, landed on trunks wrenched from the windward islands. Turtles came to lay their eggs. Birds nested in the young trees. In this way animal life developed, and, drawn by the greenery and the fertility, man appeared. And so were formed these islands, the immense works of microscopic animals.

Toward evening, Clermont-Tonnerre melted into the distance, and the Nautilus's course shifted noticeably. After touching the Tropic of Capricorn at the one hundred and thirty-fifth degree of longitude, it headed west-northwest, working its way back up the whole intertropical zone. Though the summer sun was lavish with its rays, we suffered no discomfort from the heat, for thirty or forty meters below the surface the temperature never rose above ten or twelve degrees.

On December 15 we left to the east the alluring Society archipelago and graceful Tahiti, the queen of the Pacific. That morning I glimpsed, a few miles to leeward, the lofty peaks of that island. Its waters supplied the tables aboard with excellent fish — mackerel, bonito, albacore, and varieties of a sea serpent called munerophis.

The Nautilus had covered eight thousand one hundred miles. Nine thousand seven hundred and twenty miles were logged when it passed between the Tonga-Tabou archipelago, where the crews of the Argo, the Port-au-Prince, and the Duke-of-Portland met their end, and the Navigators' archipelago, where Captain de Langle, La Pérouse's friend, was killed. Then it came within sight of the Viti archipelago, where the savages massacred the sailors of the Union and Captain Bureau, of Nantes, commander of the Aimable-Joséphine.

This archipelago, which extends over a span of a hundred leagues from north to south and ninety leagues from east to west, lies between 6° and 2° south latitude and 174° and 179° west longitude. It is made up of a number of islands, islets, and shoals, among which one notes the islands of Viti-Levou, Vanoua-Levou, and Kandubon.

It was Tasman who discovered this group in 1643 — the very year Torricelli invented the barometer and Louis XIV ascended the throne. I leave you to decide which of these events was of greater use to humanity. Then came Cook in 1714, d'Entrecasteaux in 1793, and finally Dumont d'Urville, in 1827, who untangled the whole geographical chaos of this archipelago. The Nautilus drew near the bay of Wailea, the scene of the terrible adventures of that Captain Dillon who was the first to shed light on the mystery of La Pérouse's shipwreck.

This bay, dredged several times over, yielded excellent oysters in abundance. We ate them immoderately, having opened them at our very table, following the precept of Seneca. These mollusks belonged to the species known as ostrea lamellosa, which is very common in Corsica. This Wailea bank must have been enormous, and certainly, were it not for the many causes of destruction, these accumulations would end up filling in the bays, since as many as two million eggs are counted in a single individual.

And if Master Ned Land had no cause to regret his gluttony on this occasion, it is because the oyster is the one dish that never brings on indigestion. Indeed, it takes no fewer than sixteen dozen of these headless mollusks to furnish the three hundred and fifteen grams of nitrogenous matter needed for one man's daily nourishment.

On December 25 the Nautilus was sailing through the middle of the New Hebrides archipelago, which Quiros discovered in 1606, Bougainville explored in 1768, and Cook gave its present name in 1773. This group is made up chiefly of nine large islands and forms a band a hundred and twenty leagues long from north-northwest to south-southeast, lying between 15° and 2° south latitude and between 164° and 168° longitude. We passed fairly close to the island of Aurou, which, at the moment of the midday observations, appeared to me as a mass of green woods crowned by a peak of great height.

That day was Christmas, and Ned Land seemed to me to miss keenly the celebration of "Christmas," the true family festival that Protestants keep with a fervor bordering on fanaticism.

I hadn't seen Captain Nemo for a week or so when, on the morning of the 27th, he came into the grand salon with the air, as always, of a man who had left you five minutes before. I was busy tracing the Nautilus's route on the planisphere. The captain came over, laid a finger on a point on the chart, and spoke this single word:

"Vanikoro."

The name was magic. It was the name of the islets on which La Pérouse's ships had been lost. I sprang to my feet.

"The Nautilus is taking us to Vanikoro?" I asked.

"Yes, Professor," the captain replied.

"And I'll be able to visit those famous islands where the Boussole and the Astrolabe were wrecked?"

"If it would please you, Professor."

"When will we reach Vanikoro?"

"We are there now, Professor."

Followed by Captain Nemo, I climbed up onto the platform, and from there my eyes swept eagerly across the horizon.

To the northeast rose two volcanic islands of unequal size, ringed by a coral reef that measured forty miles around. We were facing the island of Vanikoro proper, to which Dumont d'Urville gave the name Île de la Recherche — Search Island — and we lay directly before the little harbor of Vanou, situated at 16°4′ south latitude and 164°32′ east longitude. The land seemed carpeted in green from the beach up to the interior summits, over which towered Mount Kapogo, four hundred and seventy-six fathoms high.

The Nautilus, after clearing the outer belt of rocks through a narrow pass, found itself inside the breakers, where the sea ran thirty to forty fathoms deep. Under the verdant shade of the mangroves I made out a few natives, who showed extreme surprise at our approach. In this long, blackish body advancing at water level, did they not see some formidable sea creature they had every reason to distrust?

Just then Captain Nemo asked me what I knew of La Pérouse's shipwreck.

"What everyone knows, Captain," I answered him.

"And could you tell me what everyone knows?" he asked, in a slightly ironic tone.

"Very easily."

I recounted for him what the latest work of Dumont d'Urville had brought to light — work of which here is a very brief summary.

La Pérouse and his second-in-command, Captain de Langle, were sent by Louis XVI in 1785 to carry out a voyage of circumnavigation. They sailed aboard the corvettes the Boussole and the Astrolabe, which were never seen again.

In 1791, the French government, rightly troubled by the fate of the two corvettes, fitted out two large storeships, the Recherche and the Espérance, which left Brest on September 28 under the command of Bruni d'Entrecasteaux. Two months later, word came through the deposition of a certain Bowen, commander of the Albermale, that the wreckage of shipwrecked vessels had been seen off the coasts of New Georgia. But d'Entrecasteaux, unaware of this communication — which was uncertain enough, at any rate — steered for the Admiralty Islands, named in a report by Captain Hunter as the site of La Pérouse's shipwreck.

His searches came to nothing. The Espérance and the Recherche even passed straight in front of Vanikoro without stopping, and, all in all, the voyage was a most unhappy one, for it cost the lives of d'Entrecasteaux, two of his officers, and several sailors of his crew.

It was an old hand of the Pacific, Captain Dillon, who was the first to find indisputable traces of the shipwrecked men. On May 15, 1824, his ship, the Saint-Patrick, passed near the island of Tikopia, one of the New Hebrides. There, a lascar who came alongside in a canoe sold him a silver sword hilt bearing the imprint of characters engraved with a graver. This lascar claimed, moreover, that six years earlier, during a stay at Vanikoro, he had seen two Europeans who belonged to ships that had run aground years before on the island's reefs.

Dillon guessed that these must be La Pérouse's ships, whose disappearance had stirred the whole world. He tried to reach Vanikoro, where, according to the lascar, a great deal of wreckage from the disaster was to be found; but the winds and currents kept him from it.

Dillon returned to Calcutta. There he managed to interest the Asiatic Society and the East India Company in his discovery. A ship, which was given the name Recherche, was placed at his disposal, and he set out on January 23, 1827, accompanied by a French agent.

The Recherche, after putting in at several points in the Pacific, anchored off Vanikoro on July 7, 1827, in that same harbor of Vanou where the Nautilus was floating at this very moment.

There, it gathered up numerous remains of the wreck — iron implements, anchors, pulley straps, swivel guns, an eighteen-pound cannonball, fragments of astronomical instruments, a piece of taffrail, and a bronze bell bearing the inscription "Bazin made me," the mark of the foundry at the Brest Arsenal around 1785. Doubt was no longer possible.

Dillon, rounding out his information, stayed at the site of the disaster until the month of October. Then he left Vanikoro, headed for New Zealand, anchored at Calcutta on April 7, 1828, and returned to France, where he was very warmly received by Charles X.

But at that same time, Dumont d'Urville, without any knowledge of Dillon's work, had already set out to search elsewhere for the scene of the shipwreck. And in fact, word had come through the reports of a whaler that medals and a cross of Saint-Louis were in the hands of the natives of the Louisiade and New Caledonia.

Dumont d'Urville, commanding the Astrolabe, had therefore put to sea, and, two months after Dillon had just left Vanikoro, he was anchoring off Hobart Town. There he learned of the results Dillon had obtained, and, on top of that, he heard that a certain James Hobbs, second officer of the Union, of Calcutta, having gone ashore on an island situated at 8°18′ south latitude and 156°30′ east longitude, had noticed iron bars and red cloth in use among the natives of those parts.

Dumont d'Urville, rather perplexed, and unsure whether he ought to put any faith in these accounts carried by newspapers of little credibility, nevertheless resolved to launch himself onto Dillon's trail.

On February 10, 1828, the Astrolabe presented itself off Tikopia, took on as guide and interpreter a deserter settled on the island, made for Vanikoro, came within sight of it on February 12, skirted its reefs until the 14th, and only on the 20th anchored inside the barrier, in the harbor of Vanou.

On the 23rd, several of the officers made a circuit of the island and brought back a few unimportant pieces of debris. The natives, adopting a policy of denials and evasions, refused to lead them to the site of the disaster. This very shady conduct suggested that they had mistreated the shipwrecked men, and, indeed, they seemed to fear that Dumont d'Urville had come to avenge La Pérouse and his ill-fated companions.

Nevertheless, on the 26th, won over by gifts and understanding that they had no reprisals to fear, they led the second officer, Monsieur Jacquinot, to the scene of the wreck.

There, under three or four fathoms of water, between the Pacou and Vanou reefs, lay anchors, cannons, and pigs of iron and lead, encrusted in calcareous concretions. The Astrolabe's launch and whaleboat were directed to this spot, and, not without long and exhausting labor, their crews managed to haul up an anchor weighing eighteen hundred pounds, a cast-iron eight-pounder, a pig of lead, and two copper swivel guns.

Questioning the natives, Dumont d'Urville also learned that La Pérouse, after losing his two ships on the island's reefs, had built a smaller vessel in which he went off to wreck a second time... Where? No one knew.

The commander of the Astrolabe then had a cenotaph raised, under a clump of mangroves, to the memory of the famous navigator and his companions. It was a simple four-sided pyramid, set on a base of coral, into which went no ironwork that might tempt the greed of the natives.

Then Dumont d'Urville wished to leave; but his crews were sapped by the fevers of that unhealthy coast, and, very ill himself, he was unable to set sail until March 17.

Meanwhile the French government, fearing that Dumont d'Urville might not be aware of Dillon's work, had sent to Vanikoro the corvette the Bayonnaise, commanded by Legoarant de Tromelin, who was on station off the west coast of America. The Bayonnaise anchored off Vanikoro a few months after the Astrolabe's departure, found no new document, but established that the savages had respected La Pérouse's memorial.

Such is the substance of the account I gave Captain Nemo.

"So then," he said to me, "it's still not known where this third vessel — the one the shipwrecked men built on the island of Vanikoro — went to perish?"

"It is not known."

Captain Nemo said nothing in reply, and signaled me to follow him to the grand salon. The Nautilus sank a few meters beneath the waves, and the panels slid open.

I rushed to the window, and there, beneath the coral incrustations, sheathed in fungia, siphonules, alcyons, and caryophyllia, through myriads of charming fish — rainbow wrasse, glyphisodons, pompherids, snappers, and squirrelfish — I recognized certain remains the dredges had not been able to tear loose: iron stirrups, anchors, cannons, cannonballs, the fittings of a capstan, a stem-post — all objects come from the wrecked ships and now upholstered in living flowers.

And while I gazed at these forlorn wrecks, Captain Nemo said to me in a grave voice:

"Commander La Pérouse set out on December 7, 1785, with his ships the Boussole and the Astrolabe. He first anchored at Botany Bay, visited the Friendly archipelago and New Caledonia, made for Santa Cruz, and put in at Namouka, one of the islands of the Hapaï group. Then his ships arrived at the uncharted reefs of Vanikoro. The Boussole, which was in the lead, ran onto the southern coast. The Astrolabe came to her aid and grounded in the same way. The first ship was destroyed almost at once. The second, wedged fast to leeward, held out a few days. The natives gave the castaways a fairly good welcome. They settled on the island and built a smaller vessel out of the debris of the two large ones. A few sailors stayed at Vanikoro of their own free will.

"The rest, weakened and ill, set off with La Pérouse. They steered for the Solomon Islands, and they perished, ship and all, on the western coast of the group's main island, between Capes Deception and Satisfaction!"

"And how do you know this?" I cried.

"Here is what I found on the very site of that last wreck!"

Captain Nemo showed me a tin box, stamped with the arms of France and entirely corroded by the salt water. He opened it, and I saw a bundle of yellowed papers, still legible.

They were the very instructions of the Minister of the Navy to Commander La Pérouse, annotated in the margins in the hand of Louis XVI!

"Ah! It's a fine death for a sailor!" said Captain Nemo then. "A coral tomb is a peaceful grave, and may heaven grant that my companions and I never have any other!"
