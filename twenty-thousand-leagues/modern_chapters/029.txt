The Arabian Tunnel

That same day I reported to Conseil and Ned Land the part of the conversation that concerned them directly. When I told them that within two days we would be in the waters of the Mediterranean, Conseil clapped his hands, but the Canadian only shrugged.

"An underwater tunnel!" he cried. "A passage between the two seas! Who ever heard of such a thing?"

"Friend Ned," Conseil replied, "had you ever heard of the Nautilus? No! And yet here it is. So don't shrug so lightly, and don't dismiss a thing just because you've never heard of it."

"We'll see about that!" Ned Land shot back, shaking his head. "After all, I'd like nothing better than to believe in this captain and his passage, and heaven grant that he really does take us into the Mediterranean."

That very evening, at 21°30′ north latitude, the Nautilus, riding on the surface, drew near the Arabian coast. I made out Jeddah, that important trading port for Egypt, Syria, Turkey, and the Indies. I could see fairly clearly the whole sweep of its buildings, the ships moored along the quays, and those whose draft forced them to anchor out in the roadstead. The sun, rather low on the horizon, struck the houses of the town full on and set off their whiteness. Beyond them, a few huts of wood and reeds marked the quarter where the Bedouins lived.

Soon Jeddah faded into the shadows of evening, and the Nautilus slipped back beneath the faintly phosphorescent water.

The next day, February 10, several ships appeared, running on a course opposite to ours. The Nautilus resumed its underwater navigation; but at noon, at the moment of taking our position, the sea being empty, it rose again to its waterline.

With Ned and Conseil, I came and sat on the platform. The coast to the east showed only as a mass barely sketched in a damp haze.

Leaning against the sides of the dinghy, we were chatting about this and that when Ned Land, stretching his hand toward a point on the sea, said to me:

"Do you see something out there, Professor?"

"No, Ned," I answered, "but I don't have your eyes, as you know."

"Look hard," Ned went on. "There, off the starboard bow, about level with the beacon! You don't see a mass that seems to be moving?"

"Yes," I said, after watching closely, "I can make out a sort of long, blackish body on the surface of the water."

"Another Nautilus?" said Conseil.

"No," answered the Canadian, "unless I'm badly mistaken, that's some sea animal."

"Are there whales in the Red Sea?" Conseil asked.

"Yes, my boy," I replied, "you come across them now and then."

"That's no whale," Ned Land put in, never taking his eyes off the thing he'd spotted. "Whales and I are old acquaintances, and I couldn't mistake the look of one."

"Let's wait," said Conseil. "The Nautilus is heading that way, and before long we'll know what to make of it."

Sure enough, that blackish object was soon no more than a mile from us. It looked like a great reef stranded in the open sea. What was it? I still couldn't say.

"Ah! It's moving! It's diving!" cried Ned Land. "A thousand devils! What kind of animal can it be? Its tail isn't forked like a whale's or a sperm whale's, and its fins look like stumps of limbs."

"But then—" I began.

"There now," the Canadian went on, "it's rolled onto its back, and it's lifting its breasts into the air!"

"It's a mermaid," cried Conseil, "a real mermaid, saving Master's presence."

That word mermaid put me on the right track, and I understood that this animal belonged to that order of sea creatures which legend has turned into mermaids, half woman and half fish.

"No," I told Conseil, "it's not a mermaid, but a curious creature of which barely a few specimens survive in the Red Sea. It's a dugong."

"Order Sirenia, group Pisciforma, subclass Monodelphia, class Mammalia, phylum Vertebrata," answered Conseil.

And when Conseil had spoken like that, there was nothing left to add.

Meanwhile Ned Land kept watching. His eyes were shining with greed at the sight of the animal. His hand seemed ready to harpoon it. You would have said he was waiting for the moment to throw himself into the sea and attack it in its own element.

"Oh, Professor," he said to me in a voice trembling with emotion, "I've never killed one of those."

The whole harpooner was in that one word.

At that instant Captain Nemo appeared on the platform. He saw the dugong. He read the Canadian's whole attitude, and, addressing him directly:

"If you were holding a harpoon right now, Master Land, wouldn't it be burning a hole in your hand?"

"Just as you say, sir."

"And it wouldn't displease you to take up your fisherman's trade again for a day, and add this cetacean to the list of those you've already struck?"

"It wouldn't displease me in the least."

"Well then, you may try."

"Thank you, sir," Ned Land replied, his eyes blazing.

"Only," the captain went on, "I urge you not to miss this animal — and that's for your own sake."

"Is this dugong dangerous to attack?" I asked, in spite of the Canadian's shrug.

"Yes, sometimes," the captain answered. "The animal turns on its attackers and capsizes their boat. But for Master Land, there's no fear of that danger. His eye is quick, his arm is sure. If I tell him not to miss this dugong, it's because the creature is rightly considered fine game, and I know Master Land is no enemy of a good cut of meat."

"Ah!" said the Canadian. "So this beast treats itself to the luxury of being good to eat as well?"

"Yes, Master Land. Its flesh — real meat — is extremely prized, and all through Malaysia it's reserved for the tables of princes. That's why this excellent animal is hunted so relentlessly that, like the manatee, its cousin, it grows rarer and rarer."

"Then, Captain," said Conseil in all seriousness, "if by chance this one were the last of its kind, wouldn't it be right to spare it in the interest of science?"

"Perhaps," retorted the Canadian, "but in the interest of the kitchen, it's better to hunt it down."

"Have at it, then, Master Land," answered Captain Nemo.

At that moment seven crewmen, mute and impassive as always, climbed onto the platform. One carried a harpoon and a line like those used by whale fishermen. The dinghy was unshipped, hauled from its socket, and launched into the sea. Six oarsmen took their places on the benches, and the coxswain manned the tiller. Ned, Conseil, and I sat down in the stern.

"Aren't you coming, Captain?" I asked.

"No, sir, but I wish you a good hunt."

The dinghy shoved off and, driven by its six oars, headed quickly toward the dugong, which was floating then some two miles from the Nautilus.

When we had come within a few cable-lengths of the creature, the boat slowed, and the oars dipped noiselessly into the calm water. Ned Land, harpoon in hand, went to take his stand upright at the bow of the dinghy. The harpoon used to strike a whale is ordinarily attached to a very long rope that pays out quickly when the wounded animal drags it along. But here the rope measured no more than ten fathoms or so, and its end was simply made fast to a little barrel that, floating, would mark the dugong's course beneath the water.

I had risen and could clearly study the Canadian's adversary. This dugong — which also goes by the name halicore — closely resembled the manatee. Its oblong body ended in a very long tail fin, and its side flippers ended in what were truly fingers. Its difference from the manatee lay in this: its upper jaw was armed with two long, pointed teeth that formed diverging tusks on either side.

This dugong that Ned Land was preparing to attack had colossal dimensions; its length was at least seven meters. It wasn't moving, and it seemed to be asleep on the surface of the waves — a circumstance that made its capture easier.

The dinghy edged cautiously to within three fathoms of the animal. The oars hung suspended in their rowlocks. I half rose. Ned Land, his body thrown a little back, brandished his harpoon with a practiced hand.

Suddenly a hiss was heard, and the dugong vanished. The harpoon, flung with all Ned's force, had no doubt struck nothing but water.

"A thousand devils!" cried the Canadian in a fury. "I missed it!"

"No," I said, "the animal's wounded — there's its blood — but your weapon didn't stay lodged in its body."

"My harpoon! My harpoon!" cried Ned Land.

The sailors bent to their oars again, and the coxswain steered the boat toward the floating barrel. The harpoon fished up, the dinghy set off in pursuit of the animal.

The dugong came back to the surface from time to time to breathe. Its wound hadn't weakened it, for it shot along with extreme speed. The boat, worked by vigorous arms, flew after it. Several times it closed to within a few fathoms, and the Canadian held himself ready to strike; but the dugong would slip away with a sudden dive, and there was no reaching it.

You can judge the rage that boiled up in the impatient Ned Land. He hurled the most forceful oaths in the English language at the wretched animal. For my part, I'd gotten no further than vexation at seeing the dugong foil all our tricks.

We chased it without letup for an hour, and I was beginning to think it would be very hard to take, when the animal was seized by an unlucky notion of revenge that it would soon have cause to repent. It turned back on the dinghy to attack us in its turn.

This maneuver didn't escape the Canadian.

"Look out!" he said.

The coxswain spoke a few words in his strange language, no doubt warning his men to be on their guard.

The dugong, having come within twenty feet of the dinghy, stopped, snuffed the air sharply through its vast nostrils — pierced not at the tip but at the upper part of its muzzle — then, gathering its momentum, hurled itself at us.

The dinghy couldn't avoid the shock. Half overturned, it shipped a ton or two of water that had to be bailed out; but thanks to the coxswain's skill, taken at an angle instead of head-on, it didn't capsize. Ned Land, clinging to the stem, jabbed harpoon-blow after harpoon-blow into the gigantic animal, which, its teeth sunk into the gunwale, lifted the boat out of the water the way a lion lifts a young deer. We were thrown one on top of another, and I hardly know how the adventure would have ended if the Canadian, relentless against the beast, hadn't at last struck it to the heart.

I heard the grinding of teeth on the metal plating, and the dugong disappeared, dragging the harpoon down with it. But soon the barrel bobbed back to the surface, and a few moments later the animal's body appeared, rolled over on its back. The dinghy came up to it, took it in tow, and made for the Nautilus.

It took powerful tackle to hoist the dugong onto the platform. It weighed five thousand kilograms. It was cut up under the eyes of the Canadian, who insisted on following every detail of the operation. That same day the steward served me at dinner a few slices of that flesh, skillfully prepared by the ship's cook. I found it excellent, and even superior to veal, if not to beef.

The next day, February 11, the Nautilus's larder was enriched again with delicate game. A flock of sea swallows settled on the Nautilus. They were a species of Sterna nilotica, peculiar to Egypt, with a black beak, a gray dappled head, the eye ringed with white spots, the back, wings, and tail grayish, the belly and throat white, and red feet. We also caught a few dozen Nile ducks, wild birds of a high flavor, whose neck and crown are white and speckled with black.

The Nautilus's speed was moderate now. It was moving along at a saunter, so to speak. I noticed that the water of the Red Sea grew less and less salty the closer we came to Suez.

Toward five in the evening, we sighted Cape Ras Muhammad to the north. It's this cape that forms the tip of Arabia Petraea, held between the Gulf of Suez and the Gulf of Aqaba.

The Nautilus entered the Strait of Jubal, which leads to the Gulf of Suez. I could clearly see a high mountain dominating Ras Muhammad between the two gulfs. It was Mount Horeb — that Sinai on whose summit Moses saw God face to face, and which the mind pictures forever crowned with lightning.

At six o'clock, the Nautilus, now floating, now submerged, passed off Tor, set at the bottom of a bay whose waters looked tinged with red — an observation Captain Nemo had already made. Then night fell, in the midst of a heavy silence broken now and then by the cry of a pelican and a few night birds, by the sound of the surf fretted against the rocks, or by the distant groan of a steamer beating the waters of the gulf with its ringing blades.

From eight to nine o'clock, the Nautilus stayed a few meters beneath the surface. By my reckoning, we had to be very near Suez. Through the panels of the salon, I could see the rocky bottom brilliantly lit by our electric light. It seemed to me that the strait was growing narrower and narrower.

At a quarter past nine, the boat having come back to the surface, I climbed onto the platform. Too impatient to pass through Captain Nemo's tunnel, I couldn't keep still, and I sought to breathe the fresh night air.

Soon, in the darkness, I made out a pale fire, half washed out by the haze, glowing a mile from us.

"A floating lighthouse," said a voice near me.

I turned and recognized the captain.

"That's the floating light of Suez," he went on. "It won't be long before we reach the mouth of the tunnel."

"The entrance can't be easy?"

"No, sir. And so I make it my habit to stay in the helmsman's cage and direct the maneuver myself. And now, if you'll go below, Professor Aronnax, the Nautilus is going to sink beneath the waves, and it won't come back to the surface until we've passed through the Arabian Tunnel."

I followed Captain Nemo. The hatch closed, the water tanks filled, and the vessel sank some ten meters.

Just as I was about to head back to my room, the captain stopped me.

"Professor," he said, "would you care to come with me into the pilot's cage?"

"I didn't dare ask you," I answered.

"Come along, then. This way you'll see everything there is to see of a voyage that's at once underground and underwater."

Captain Nemo led me toward the central stairway. Halfway up the steps, he opened a door, followed the upper gangways, and reached the pilot's cage, which, as we know, rose at the end of the platform.

It was a cabin measuring six feet on each side, much like those the helmsmen occupy on the steamboats of the Mississippi or the Hudson. In the middle a wheel was worked, set vertically, geared to the wheel ropes of the rudder that ran all the way to the stern of the Nautilus. Four portholes of lens-shaped glass, set into the walls of the cabin, let the man at the helm look out in every direction.

The cabin was dark; but soon my eyes grew used to the gloom, and I made out the pilot, a powerful man whose hands rested on the rim of the wheel. Outside, the sea appeared vividly lit by the beacon that shone behind the cabin, at the other end of the platform.

"Now," said Captain Nemo, "let's look for our passage."

Electric wires linked the helmsman's cage with the engine room, and from there the captain could signal to his Nautilus both direction and speed at once. He pressed a metal button, and at once the speed of the propeller dropped sharply.

I watched in silence the high, sheer wall we were skirting just then — the immovable base of the sandy massif of the coast. We followed it that way for an hour, only a few meters off. Captain Nemo never took his eyes off the compass hanging in the cabin between its two concentric rings. At a mere gesture from him, the helmsman altered the Nautilus's course from one instant to the next.

I had placed myself at the port porthole, and I could see magnificent substructures of coral, zoophytes, algae, and crustaceans waving their enormous claws, which reached out from the crevices of the rock.

At a quarter past ten, Captain Nemo took the helm himself. A wide gallery, black and deep, opened before us. The Nautilus plunged boldly into it. An unfamiliar rushing sound made itself heard along its flanks. It was the waters of the Red Sea that the slope of the tunnel was hurling toward the Mediterranean. The Nautilus followed the torrent, swift as an arrow, despite the efforts of its engine, which, to resist, was beating the water with its propeller reversed.

On the narrow walls of the passage I could see nothing now but blazing streaks, straight lines, furrows of fire traced by our speed under the glare of the electricity. My heart was pounding, and I pressed it down with my hand.

At ten thirty-five, Captain Nemo let go of the wheel, and turning to me:

"The Mediterranean," he said.

In less than twenty minutes, the Nautilus, carried along by that torrent, had crossed the Isthmus of Suez.
