For and Against

At the time these events took place, I was on my way home from a scientific expedition into the Badlands of Nebraska, in the United States. In my capacity as assistant professor at the Museum of Natural History in Paris, I had been attached to the expedition by the French government. After six months in Nebraska, laden with precious collections, I reached New York toward the end of March. My departure for France was set for the first days of May. In the meantime, then, I was busy sorting and classifying my mineralogical, botanical, and zoological treasures, when the incident of the Scotia occurred.

I was thoroughly up to date on the question of the hour — and how could I not have been? I had read and reread every American and European newspaper without getting any closer to the truth. The mystery intrigued me. Unable to form an opinion, I drifted from one extreme to the other. That there was something out there could not be doubted, and the skeptics were cordially invited to lay a finger on the wound in the Scotia's side.

By the time I arrived in New York, the question was at fever pitch. The hypothesis of a floating island, an uncharted reef, championed by a few not especially competent minds, had been thoroughly abandoned. And indeed — unless this reef had an engine in its belly, how could it move about with such prodigious speed?

Dismissed for the same reason was the idea of a floating hull, an enormous piece of wreckage — again on account of the speed of its movements.

That left two possible solutions to the puzzle, and they created two very distinct camps of believers: on one side, those who held out for a monster of colossal strength; on the other, those who held out for a "submarine" boat of extraordinary driving power.

Now, this second hypothesis, admissible after all, could not survive the inquiries pursued in both the Old World and the New. That some private individual should have such a machine at his disposal was hardly likely. Where and when had he had it built, and how could he have kept its construction a secret?

Only a government could possibly own such an engine of destruction, and in these disastrous times, when men rack their brains to multiply the power of their weapons of war, it was conceivable that some state might be testing this formidable device without the others' knowledge. After the chassepot rifles, the torpedoes; after the torpedoes, the underwater rams; and then — the recoil. At least, so I hope.

But the theory of a war machine collapsed too, before the governments' formal denials. Since a matter of public interest was at stake — for transoceanic communications were suffering — the good faith of the governments could not be questioned. Besides, how could one accept that the construction of such a submarine boat had escaped the public eye? Keeping a secret under such circumstances is very difficult for a private individual, and certainly impossible for a state whose every act is doggedly watched by rival powers.

And so, after inquiries carried out in England, France, Russia, Prussia, Spain, Italy, America, and even Turkey, the hypothesis of an underwater Monitor was definitively rejected.

Upon my arrival in New York, several people had done me the honor of consulting me about the phenomenon in question. I had published in France a two-volume quarto work entitled The Mysteries of the Great Ocean Depths. This book, particularly well received in scholarly circles, had made me something of a specialist in that rather obscure branch of natural history. My opinion was sought. So long as I could deny the facts, I took refuge in flat denial. But soon, pinned against the wall, I had to explain myself in no uncertain terms. And indeed, "the honorable Pierre Aronnax, professor at the Paris Museum," was called upon by the New York Herald to formulate some opinion or other.

So I complied. I spoke because I could no longer keep silent. I discussed the question from every angle, politically and scientifically, and here I give an extract from a well-stuffed article I published in the issue of April 30.

"And so," I wrote, "having examined the various hypotheses one by one, and every other supposition being rejected, we are necessarily driven to admit the existence of a marine animal of tremendous power.

"The great depths of the ocean are entirely unknown to us. The sounding line has never reached them. What goes on in those remote abysses? What creatures inhabit, and can inhabit, twelve or fifteen miles beneath the surface of the waters? What is the makeup of these animals? We can scarcely even guess.

"Yet the solution to the problem put before me may take the form of a dilemma.

"Either we know every variety of creature that populates our planet, or we do not.

"If we do not know them all — if nature still keeps secrets from us in matters of ichthyology — then nothing is more reasonable than to admit the existence of fish, or cetaceans, of new species or even new genera, of an essentially 'bottom-dwelling' constitution, that inhabit the strata beyond the reach of the sounding line, and that some event or other — a whim, a caprice, if you like — brings up at long intervals toward the upper level of the ocean.

"If, on the contrary, we do know every living species, then we must necessarily look for the animal in question among the marine creatures already catalogued, and in that case I would be inclined to admit the existence of a giant narwhal.

"The common narwhal, or sea unicorn, often reaches a length of sixty feet. Multiply that dimension by five, even by ten; give this cetacean a strength proportional to its size; enlarge its offensive weapons — and you have the animal we are looking for. It will have the proportions determined by the officers of the Shannon, the instrument required by the puncturing of the Scotia, and the power needed to breach a steamer's hull.

"For the narwhal is armed with a sort of ivory sword — a halberd, as certain naturalists put it. It is a great tooth as hard as steel. Some of these teeth have been found buried in the bodies of whales, which the narwhal always attacks successfully. Others have been wrenched — not without difficulty — from the hulls of ships that they had pierced clean through, the way a drill bores through a barrel. The museum of the Paris Faculty of Medicine possesses one of these tusks, two meters and twenty-five centimeters long and forty-eight centimeters wide at its base!

"Well then! Suppose the weapon ten times stronger, and the animal ten times more powerful; launch it at a speed of twenty miles an hour; multiply its mass by its velocity, and you obtain an impact capable of producing the catastrophe in question.

"So, until further information comes to light, I would cast my vote for a sea unicorn of colossal dimensions, armed no longer with a mere halberd, but with a genuine ram like the ironclad frigates or the war 'rams,' whose bulk and driving power it would combine.

"That is how this inexplicable phenomenon could be explained — unless there is nothing there at all, in spite of everything that has been glimpsed, seen, felt, and sensed — which is also possible!"

Those last words were a piece of cowardice on my part; but up to a point I wanted to protect my professorial dignity, and not lay myself too open to the mockery of the Americans, who laugh heartily when they do laugh. I was leaving myself an escape hatch. Deep down, I admitted the existence of the "monster."

My article was hotly debated, which won it a wide echo. It rallied a fair number of supporters. The solution it proposed, moreover, left free rein to the imagination. The human mind delights in these grandiose conceptions of supernatural beings. And the sea is precisely their best vehicle — the one element in which such giants (beside whom the land animals, elephants or rhinoceroses, are mere dwarves) can be produced and can grow. The liquid masses carry the largest known species of mammals, and perhaps they harbor mollusks of incomparable size, crustaceans frightful to behold — lobsters a hundred meters long, say, or crabs weighing two hundred tons! Why not, indeed? In former ages, the land animals, contemporaries of the geological epochs — the quadrupeds, the quadrumanes, the reptiles, the birds — were built on gigantic patterns. The Creator had cast them in a colossal mold, which time has gradually reduced. Why should the sea, in its unknown depths, not have preserved these vast specimens of the life of another age — the sea that never changes, while the earth's core is almost ceaselessly transformed? Why should it not hide in its bosom the last varieties of these titanic species, whose years are centuries and whose centuries are millennia?

But I let myself be carried away into daydreams that are no longer mine to entertain! Enough of these fancies, which time has turned, for me, into terrible realities. I repeat: opinion had by then made up its mind about the nature of the phenomenon, and the public accepted without dispute the existence of a prodigious being that had nothing in common with the fabled sea serpents.

But while some saw in it only a purely scientific problem to be solved, others — more practical, especially in America and England — were of a mind to purge the ocean of this fearsome monster, in order to make the sea lanes safe again. The industrial and commercial papers treated the question chiefly from this angle. The Shipping and Mercantile Gazette, the Lloyd, the Paquebot, the Revue maritime et coloniale — every sheet devoted to the insurance companies, which were threatening to raise their premium rates — were unanimous on this point.

Public opinion having declared itself, the states of the Union were the first to act. Preparations were made in New York for an expedition to hunt down the narwhal. A fast frigate, the Abraham Lincoln, made ready to put to sea as soon as possible. The arsenals were thrown open to Commander Farragut, who pressed briskly ahead with the arming of his frigate.

And, precisely as always happens, the very moment it was decided to give chase to the monster, the monster ceased to appear. For two months no one heard a word of it. Not a single ship crossed its path. It was as if this Unicorn had gotten wind of the plots being hatched against it. There had been so much talk of it — even over the transatlantic cable! And so the wits maintained that this sly creature had intercepted some passing telegram and was now turning it to its own advantage.

So there was the frigate, fitted out for a long cruise and equipped with formidable fishing gear, and no one knew where to point it. Impatience was mounting, when, on July 2, word came that a steamer of the San Francisco to Shanghai line had sighted the animal again three weeks earlier, in the northern seas of the Pacific.

The excitement this news caused was extreme. Commander Farragut was not granted twenty-four hours' respite. His provisions were aboard. His bunkers were bursting with coal. Not a man was missing from the crew rolls. He had only to light his furnaces, build up steam, and cast off! He would not have been forgiven half a day's delay. But then, Commander Farragut asked for nothing better than to be off.

Three hours before the Abraham Lincoln left the Brooklyn pier, I received a letter worded as follows:

Professor Aronnax, of the Paris Museum, Fifth Avenue Hotel.

New York.

"Sir,

If you would care to join the expedition of the Abraham Lincoln, the government of the Union will be pleased to see France represented by you in this undertaking. Commander Farragut has a cabin at your disposal.

Very cordially yours, J. B. HOBSON, Secretary of the Navy."
