Captain Nemo's Last Words

The panels had slid shut over that terrifying sight, but the light had not been given back to the salon. Inside the Nautilus there was nothing but darkness and silence. It was leaving that place of desolation, a hundred feet below the surface, with prodigious speed. Where was it going? North or south? Where was this man fleeing to, after his horrible act of vengeance?

I had gone back to my cabin, where Ned and Conseil were sitting in silence. I felt an unconquerable horror for Captain Nemo. Whatever he had suffered at the hands of men, he had no right to punish like this. He had made me, if not the accomplice, then at least the witness of his revenge! That was already too much.

At eleven o'clock the electric light came back on. I went through to the salon. It was deserted. I consulted the various instruments. The Nautilus was fleeing northward at a speed of twenty-five miles an hour, sometimes at the surface, sometimes thirty feet beneath it.

Taking our bearings on the chart, I saw that we were passing the mouth of the English Channel, and that our course was carrying us toward the northern seas at an incomparable speed.

I could barely catch, as they flashed past, the long-nosed sharks, the hammerheads, the dogfish that haunt those waters, great sea eagles, clouds of seahorses like the knights on a chessboard, eels writhing like the little serpents of a fireworks display, whole armies of crabs fleeing sideways with their claws crossed over their shells, and finally schools of porpoises racing the Nautilus for speed. But of observing, studying, classifying — there was no longer any question.

By evening we had covered two hundred leagues of the Atlantic. Shadow fell, and the sea was overrun with darkness until the moon rose.

I went back to my cabin. I could not sleep. I was besieged by nightmares. The horrible scene of destruction kept playing over and over in my mind.

From that day on, who could ever say how far the Nautilus carried us into that basin of the North Atlantic? Always at that unmeasurable speed! Always in the middle of the hyperborean fogs! Did it touch the headlands of Spitsbergen, the sheer cliffs of Novaya Zemlya? Did it range across those uncharted seas, the White Sea, the Kara Sea, the Gulf of Ob, the Liakhov archipelago, and those unknown shores of the Asian coast? I could not say. I had lost all reckoning of the passing time. The hour had been suspended on the ship's clocks. It seemed that night and day, as in the polar lands, no longer followed their regular course. I felt myself drawn into that realm of the strange where the overwrought imagination of Edgar Allan Poe moved so freely. At every moment I expected to see, like the fabulous Gordon Pym, "that shrouded human figure, of proportions far vaster than those of any dweller upon the earth, thrown across the cataract that guards the approaches to the pole"!

I estimate — though perhaps I am mistaken — I estimate that this reckless dash of the Nautilus went on for fifteen or twenty days, and I do not know how long it might have lasted, but for the catastrophe that put an end to the voyage. Of Captain Nemo there was no further sign. Of his first officer, none either. Not a single member of the crew was visible for one instant. Almost without pause, the Nautilus stayed submerged. When it came up to the surface to renew its air, the panels opened and closed automatically. No more positions marked on the planisphere. I had no idea where we were.

I will say too that the Canadian, at the end of his strength and his patience, no longer showed himself. Conseil could not draw a single word out of him, and feared that in some fit of delirium, under the grip of a terrible homesickness, he might kill himself. So he watched over him with a devotion that never let up for a moment.

You can understand that, under these conditions, the situation had become impossible.

One morning — on what date, I could not say — I had dozed off toward the first hours of the day, a heavy, sickly sort of sleep. When I woke, I saw Ned Land leaning over me, and I heard him say in a low voice:

"We're going to make our escape!"

I sat up.

"When?" I asked.

"Tomorrow night. All watch seems to have vanished from the Nautilus. It's as if a stupor has settled over the whole ship. You'll be ready, Professor?"

"Yes. Where are we?"

"In sight of land I just made out this morning through the fog, twenty miles to the east."

"What land is it?"

"I've no idea, but whatever it is, we'll take shelter there."

"Yes, Ned. Yes, we'll escape tonight, even if the sea swallows us up!"

"The sea's rough, the wind's fierce, but twenty miles to cover in that light little boat of the Nautilus doesn't scare me. I've managed to carry some food and a few bottles of water aboard it without the crew knowing."

"I'll follow you."

"What's more," the Canadian added, "if I'm caught, I'll fight, I'll let them kill me."

"Then we'll die together, Ned my friend."

I was ready for anything. The Canadian left me. I made my way up to the platform, where I could barely keep my feet against the pounding of the waves. The sky was threatening, but since land was there in that thick fog, we had to run for it. We could not afford to lose a single day, or a single hour.

I went back to the salon, dreading and longing all at once to run into Captain Nemo, wanting and not wanting to see him. What would I have said to him? Could I have hidden from him the involuntary horror he now inspired in me? No! Better not to find myself face to face with him! Better to forget him! And yet!

How long that day was — the last I would spend aboard the Nautilus! I kept to myself. Ned Land and Conseil avoided speaking to me, for fear of giving themselves away.

At six o'clock I had dinner, but I had no appetite. I forced myself to eat, in spite of my revulsion, not wanting to weaken myself.

At half past six Ned Land came into my cabin. He said to me:

"We won't see each other again before we leave. At ten o'clock the moon won't be up yet. We'll take advantage of the dark. Come to the dinghy. Conseil and I will be waiting for you there."

Then the Canadian went out, without giving me time to answer him.

I wanted to check the Nautilus's heading. I went to the salon. We were running north-northeast at a frightening speed, at a depth of fifty meters.

I cast a last look over these marvels of nature, these treasures of art heaped up in this museum, this collection without rival, destined to perish one day at the bottom of the sea along with the man who had assembled it. I wanted to fix one final impression in my mind. I stayed there for an hour, bathed in the glow of the luminous ceiling, passing in review those treasures shining under their glass cases. Then I went back to my cabin.

There I put on stout sea clothes. I gathered up my notes and packed them carefully against my body. My heart was pounding hard. I could not still its beating. Surely my agitation, my distress, would have betrayed me to Captain Nemo's eyes.

What was he doing at that moment? I listened at the door of his cabin. I heard the sound of footsteps. Captain Nemo was there. He had not gone to bed. At every movement it seemed to me he was about to appear and ask me why I wanted to flee! I was seized by one alarm after another. My imagination magnified them. The feeling grew so agonizing that I asked myself whether it might not be better to walk into the captain's cabin, see him face to face, defy him with a look and a gesture!

It was a madman's impulse. Luckily I held myself back, and I stretched out on my bed to quiet the trembling of my body. My nerves calmed a little, but with my brain overexcited, I saw again, in one swift rush of memory, my whole life aboard the Nautilus — all the incidents, happy and unhappy, that had crossed it since I vanished from the Abraham Lincoln: the underwater hunts, the Torres Strait, the savages of Papua, the grounding, the coral cemetery, the passage through Suez, the island of Santorini, the Cretan diver, Vigo Bay, Atlantis, the ice barrier, the South Pole, the imprisonment in the ice, the battle with the devilfish, the storm in the Gulf Stream, the Avenger, and that horrible scene of the ship sunk with all her crew!... All these events passed before my eyes, like the painted backdrops that unroll behind the stage of a theater. And then Captain Nemo swelled to monstrous size in that strange setting. His figure grew sharper and took on superhuman proportions. He was no longer a fellow man, he was the man of the waters, the genie of the seas.

It was by then half past nine. I was gripping my head in both hands to keep it from bursting. I closed my eyes. I did not want to think anymore. Half an hour more to wait! Half an hour of a nightmare that might drive me mad!

At that moment I heard the vague strains of the organ, a mournful harmony beneath some indefinable chant, the true lament of a soul that wants to break its earthly bonds. I listened with every sense at once, barely breathing, plunged like Captain Nemo himself in those musical raptures that carried him away beyond the limits of this world.

Then a sudden thought terrified me. Captain Nemo had left his cabin. He was in that salon I had to cross in order to escape. There I would meet him one last time. He would see me, he might speak to me! A single gesture of his could annihilate me, a single word chain me to his ship!

Still, ten o'clock was about to strike. The moment had come to leave my cabin and rejoin my companions.

There was no hesitating, even if Captain Nemo should rise up before me. I opened my door carefully, and yet it seemed to me that as it turned on its hinges it made a frightful noise. Perhaps that noise existed only in my imagination!

I crept forward along the dark passageways of the Nautilus, stopping at every step to still the beating of my heart.

I reached the angled door of the salon. I opened it gently. The salon was sunk in deep darkness. The strains of the organ echoed faintly. Captain Nemo was there. He did not see me. I believe that even in full light he would not have noticed me, so completely was he absorbed in his rapture.

I dragged myself across the carpet, avoiding the least contact whose sound might have betrayed my presence. It took me five minutes to reach the far door that opened onto the library.

I was about to open it when a sigh from Captain Nemo froze me where I stood. I realized he was rising. I even caught a glimpse of him, for a few rays from the lighted library filtered through to the salon. He came toward me, arms folded, silent, gliding rather than walking, like a specter. His laboring chest heaved with sobs. And I heard him murmur these words — the last that ever struck my ear:

"Almighty God! Enough! Enough!"

Was it the confession of remorse escaping in this way from the conscience of that man?...

Beside myself, I rushed into the library. I climbed the central staircase, and, following the upper passageway, I reached the dinghy. I climbed in through the opening that had already let my two companions through.

"Let's go! Let's go!" I cried.

"Right now!" the Canadian answered.

The opening cut into the plating of the Nautilus was first closed and bolted shut with a wrench Ned Land had provided himself with. The opening of the dinghy was closed the same way, and the Canadian began to unscrew the bolts that still held us to the submarine boat.

Suddenly a noise from inside reached us. Voices were answering one another sharply. What was wrong? Had our escape been discovered? I felt Ned Land slip a dagger into my hand.

"Yes!" I murmured, "we'll know how to die!"

The Canadian had stopped his work. But one word, repeated twenty times over, one terrible word, revealed to me the cause of this commotion spreading through the Nautilus. It was not us that its crew was worried about!

"Maelström! Maelström!" they were crying.

The Maelström! Could a more terrifying name ring in our ears in a more terrifying situation? Were we then in those dangerous waters off the Norwegian coast? Was the Nautilus being dragged into that whirlpool, at the very moment our dinghy was about to break away from its flank?

As everyone knows, at the moment of the flood tide the waters squeezed between the Faroe Islands and the Lofotens are hurled forward with irresistible violence. They form a whirlpool from which no ship has ever managed to escape. From every point of the horizon come rushing monstrous waves. They form that gulf so aptly called the "Navel of the Ocean," whose power of attraction reaches out to a distance of fifteen kilometers. There are swallowed down not only ships, but whales, and even the white bears of the polar regions.

It was into this that the Nautilus — involuntarily, or perhaps deliberately — had been steered by its captain. It was tracing out a spiral whose radius grew smaller and smaller. And along with it, the dinghy, still hooked to its side, was carried along at a dizzying speed. I could feel it. I felt that sickening whirl that comes after a spinning motion carried on too long. We were in terror, in horror pushed to its utmost pitch, our blood halted in our veins, all nerve force annihilated, running with cold sweat like the sweat of the death agony! And what a roar around our frail little boat! What bellowings the echo flung back from miles away! What an uproar from those waters shattering on the sharp rocks of the bottom, there where the hardest bodies are broken, where tree trunks are worn away and take on "a coat of fur," as the Norwegian expression has it!

What a situation! We were pitched about horribly. The Nautilus was defending itself like a human being. Its steel muscles cracked. At times it reared up, and we with it!

"We have to hold on," said Ned, "and screw the bolts back down! By staying fastened to the Nautilus, we can still save ourselves...!"

He had not finished speaking when there came a splintering crack. The bolts gave way, and the dinghy, torn from its socket, was flung like a stone from a sling into the middle of the whirlpool.

My head struck an iron rib, and with that violent shock I lost consciousness.
