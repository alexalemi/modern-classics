The South Pole

(Part 1 of 2)

I rushed up to the platform. Yes! Open sea. Only a few scattered chunks of ice here and there, a handful of drifting icebergs; far off, a wide expanse of water; a whole world of birds in the air, and myriads of fish beneath the waves, which — depending on the depth — shaded from deep blue to olive green. The thermometer read three degrees Celsius above zero. It was like a kind of relative springtime, sealed away behind that ice barrier whose distant masses stood out along the northern horizon.

"Are we at the Pole?" I asked the captain, my heart pounding.

"I don't know," he answered. "At noon we'll take our bearings."

"But will the sun show itself through these mists?" I said, glancing at the grayish sky.

"However little of it appears, that will be enough for me," the captain replied.

Ten miles from the Nautilus, to the south, a solitary islet rose to a height of two hundred meters. We steered toward it, cautiously, for these waters might be strewn with reefs.

An hour later we had reached the islet. Two hours after that we had finished circling it. It measured four or five miles around. A narrow channel separated it from a considerable stretch of land — a continent, perhaps — whose limits we could not make out.

The existence of this land seemed to bear out Maury's hypotheses. The American engineer had noticed, in fact, that between the South Pole and the sixtieth parallel the sea is covered with floating ice of enormous size, the like of which is never met with in the North Atlantic. From this he drew the conclusion that the Antarctic Circle encloses considerable landmasses, since icebergs cannot form in the open sea but only along coasts. According to his calculations, the mass of ice that wraps the southern pole forms a vast cap whose width must reach four thousand kilometers.

Meanwhile the Nautilus, for fear of running aground, had halted three cable-lengths off a beach dominated by a magnificent heap of rocks. The dinghy was launched. The captain, two of his men carrying the instruments, Conseil, and I climbed aboard it. It was ten o'clock in the morning. I had not seen Ned Land. No doubt the Canadian did not want to eat his own words in the presence of the South Pole.

A few strokes of the oars brought the dinghy onto the sand, where it ran aground. Just as Conseil was about to leap ashore, I held him back.

"Captain," I said to Nemo, "the honor of setting foot first on this land is yours."

"Yes, sir," the captain answered, "and if I do not hesitate to tread this polar soil, it is because, until now, no human being has ever left the print of a footstep here."

With that, he sprang lightly onto the sand. A keen emotion set his heart beating hard. He climbed a rock that ended in an overhang above a little promontory, and there, arms folded, his gaze burning, motionless, silent, he seemed to take possession of these southern regions. After five minutes spent in this rapture, he turned back toward us.

"Whenever you're ready, sir," he called to me.

I stepped ashore, followed by Conseil, leaving the two men in the dinghy.

Over a long stretch, the ground showed a reddish tufa, as though it were made of crushed brick. Scoria, streams of lava, and pumice stones covered it. There was no mistaking its volcanic origin. In certain spots, faint wisps of steam gave off a sulfurous smell, proof that the inner fires still kept their power to erupt. And yet, having climbed a high escarpment, I could see no volcano within a radius of several miles. It is known that in these Antarctic regions James Ross found the craters of Erebus and Terror in full activity on the one hundred and sixty-seventh meridian, at 77°32′ of latitude.

The vegetation of this desolate continent struck me as extremely sparse. A few lichens of the species Usnea melanoxantha spread over the black rocks. Certain microscopic plantlets, rudimentary diatoms — a sort of cell set between two quartz shells — and long fronds of crimson and purple wrack, buoyed up by little swim bladders and flung ashore by the surf, made up the whole meager flora of the region.

The shore was strewn with mollusks: small mussels, limpets, smooth heart-shaped cockles, and above all with clios, their bodies oblong and membranous, the head formed of two rounded lobes. I also saw myriads of those boreal clios, three centimeters long, of which a whale swallows a whole world at every mouthful. These charming pteropods — true butterflies of the sea — brought the open water to life all along the edge of the shore.

Among the other zoophytes there appeared, in the shallows, a few coral-like growths of the kind that, according to James Ross, live in the Antarctic seas down to a depth of a thousand meters; then small soft corals of the species Procellaria pelagica, and a great number of the starfish peculiar to these climates, sea stars that studded the ground like constellations.

But it was in the air that life overflowed. There, by the thousands, flew and fluttered birds of every kind, deafening us with their cries. Others crowded the rocks, watching us pass without fear and pressing familiarly under our feet. These were penguins, as nimble and supple in the water — where they have sometimes been mistaken for darting bonitos — as they are clumsy and heavy on land. They let out strange, harsh cries and gathered in great assemblies, sparing of gesture but lavish with clamor.

Among the birds, I noticed sheathbills, of the wader family, as big as pigeons, white in color, with short conical beaks and eyes ringed with red. Conseil laid in a supply of them, for these fowl, properly prepared, make an agreeable dish. Through the air passed sooty albatrosses with a wingspan of four meters, rightly called the vultures of the Ocean; giant petrels, among them the quebrantahuesos, or bone-breakers, with their arched wings, great eaters of seals; cape petrels, a kind of little duck black and white on the upper body; and finally a whole run of petrels, some whitish with brown-edged wings, others blue and peculiar to the Antarctic seas — the latter "so oily," I said to Conseil, "that the islanders of the Faroes need only fit them with a wick before lighting them."

"A little more," Conseil replied, "and they'd be perfect lamps! After that, we can hardly demand that nature should have gone and fitted them with a wick in advance!"

Half a mile on, the ground was riddled with the nests of penguins, a sort of burrow arranged for laying eggs, out of which streamed great numbers of birds. Captain Nemo later had several hundred of them hunted, for their dark flesh is very edible. They brayed like donkeys. These creatures, the size of a goose, slate-gray on the body, white underneath, and collared with a band of lemon yellow, let themselves be killed by the throw of a stone without even trying to flee.

Still the mist did not lift, and by eleven o'clock the sun had not yet appeared. Its absence troubled me more than a little. Without it, no observations were possible. How then were we to determine whether we had reached the Pole?

When I rejoined Captain Nemo, I found him silently leaning on a piece of rock, gazing up at the sky. He seemed impatient, vexed. But what could be done? This bold and powerful man did not command the sun as he commanded the sea.

Noon came without the day-star's having shown itself for a single instant. One could not even make out the place it occupied behind the curtain of mist. Before long the mist dissolved into snow.

"Until tomorrow," the captain said to me simply, and we made our way back to the Nautilus through swirling gusts of falling snow.

During our absence, the nets had been let out, and I studied with interest the fish that had just been hauled aboard. The Antarctic seas serve as a refuge for a great many migratory species that flee the storms of the lower latitudes only to fall, it is true, into the teeth of porpoises and seals. I noted a few southern cottus, a decimeter long, a kind of whitish cartilaginous fish crossed with livid bands and armed with spines; then some Antarctic chimaeras, three feet long, their bodies very elongated, the skin white, silvery, and smooth, the head rounded, the back fitted with three fins, the snout ending in a trunk that curves back toward the mouth. I tasted their flesh, but found it insipid, despite the opinion of Conseil, who made a very good meal of it.

The snowstorm lasted until the next day. It was impossible to stay on the platform. From the salon, where I was writing up the incidents of this excursion onto the polar continent, I could hear the cries of the petrels and albatrosses sporting in the middle of the tempest. The Nautilus did not stay still; running along the coast, it pushed another ten miles or so to the south, through that half-light the sun left as it grazed the edge of the horizon.

The next day, March 20, the snow had stopped. The cold was a little sharper. The thermometer read two degrees below zero. The fogs lifted, and I hoped that, on that day, our observation could be carried out.

Captain Nemo not having yet appeared, the dinghy took Conseil and me and set us ashore. The nature of the ground was the same — volcanic. Everywhere traces of lava, scoria, and basalt, without my catching sight of the crater that had spewed them out. Here as there, myriads of birds brought this part of the polar continent to life. But this realm they now shared with vast herds of marine mammals that watched us with their gentle eyes. They were seals of various species, some stretched out on the ground, others lying on drifting ice floes, several coming out of the sea or going back into it. They did not flee at our approach, never having had any dealings with man, and I counted enough of them there to stock a few hundred ships.

"My word," said Conseil, "it's a good thing Ned Land didn't come along with us!"

"Why is that, Conseil?"

"Because the mad hunter would have killed everything in sight."

"'Everything' is saying a lot, but I do think, in fact, that we couldn't have kept our friend the Canadian from harpooning a few of these magnificent creatures. Which would have displeased Captain Nemo, for he does not shed the blood of harmless beasts for nothing."

"He's right."

"Certainly, Conseil. But tell me — haven't you already classified these superb specimens of marine fauna?"

"Master knows perfectly well," Conseil replied, "that I'm not very strong on the practical side. Once Master has taught me the name of these animals..."

"They're seals and walruses."

"Two genera belonging to the family of pinnipeds," my learned Conseil hastened to say, "order of the carnivores, group of the unguiculates, subclass of the monodelphians, class of the mammals, phylum of the vertebrates."

"Well done, Conseil," I answered, "but these two genera, seals and walruses, divide into species, and if I'm not mistaken, we'll have the chance to observe them here. Let's walk on."

It was eight o'clock in the morning. We had four hours left to fill before the sun could be usefully observed. I directed our steps toward a broad bay that scooped into the granite cliff of the shore.

There, I can say, as far as the eye could see all around us, the land and the ice floes were crowded with marine mammals, and I found myself instinctively looking for old Proteus, the mythological herdsman who watched over these immense flocks of Neptune. They were seals, above all. They formed distinct groups, males and females, the father keeping watch over his family, the mother nursing her young, a few of the juveniles, already strong, striking out on their own a few paces off. When these mammals wanted to move, they went by little hops produced by the contraction of their bodies, helping themselves along rather awkwardly with their imperfect flipper, which, in the manatee — their cousin — forms a true forearm. I should say that in the water, their element above all others, these animals with their supple spine, narrow pelvis, short close-set fur, and webbed feet swim admirably. At rest and on land, they took on extremely graceful attitudes. And so it was that the ancients, observing their gentle expressions, their eloquent gaze that not even the loveliest gaze of a woman could surpass, their velvety limpid eyes, their charming poses, and poeticizing them in their own fashion, transformed the males into tritons and the females into sirens.

I pointed out to Conseil the considerable development of the cerebral lobes in these intelligent creatures. No mammal, man excepted, has richer brain matter. And so seals are capable of receiving a certain education; they are easily domesticated, and I believe, along with certain naturalists, that suitably trained they could render great service as fishing dogs.

Most of these seals were sleeping on the rocks or on the sand. Among these true seals — which have no external ears, differing in this from the sea lions whose ear is prominent — I observed several varieties of sea leopards, three meters long, white-furred, with bulldog heads, armed with ten teeth in each jaw, four incisors above and below and two great canines cut in the shape of a fleur-de-lis. Among them slid sea elephants, a kind of seal with a short mobile trunk, the giants of the species, which over a girth of twenty feet measured a length of ten meters. They made no movement at our approach.
