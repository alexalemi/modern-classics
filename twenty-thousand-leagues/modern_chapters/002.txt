As Master Pleases

Three seconds before J.-B. Hobson's letter arrived, I no more dreamed of chasing the Unicorn than of attempting the Northwest Passage. Three seconds after reading the letter from the honorable Secretary of the Navy, I understood at last that my true calling, the single purpose of my life, was to hunt down this troubling monster and rid the world of it.

And yet I had just come back from a grueling expedition, worn out and hungry for rest. I longed for nothing but to see my own country again, my friends, my little lodging at the Jardin des Plantes, my dear and precious collections! But nothing could hold me back. I forgot everything — the fatigue, the friends, the collections — and without another moment's thought I accepted the American government's offer.

"Besides," I told myself, "every road leads back to Europe, and the Unicorn will be kind enough to draw me toward the coasts of France! The worthy creature will let itself be caught in European waters — purely for my personal convenience — and I mean to bring back no less than half a meter of its ivory halberd for the Museum of Natural History."

But in the meantime, I would have to go looking for this narwhal in the northern Pacific — which, as a way of getting home to France, meant taking the road to the antipodes.

"Conseil!" I called, impatience in my voice.

Conseil was my manservant. A devoted young fellow who came with me on all my travels; a good-hearted Fleming I was fond of, and who returned the feeling in full — a man phlegmatic by nature, methodical on principle, dutiful out of habit, seldom rattled by whatever surprises life sprang on him, very clever with his hands, ready for any task, and, in spite of his name, one who never gave advice — not even when nobody asked him for any.

From rubbing shoulders with the scientists of our little world at the Jardin des Plantes, Conseil had ended up picking up a thing or two. In him I had a genuine specialist, thoroughly grounded in natural-history classification, who could scramble up and down the whole ladder of branches, groups, classes, subclasses, orders, families, genera, subgenera, species, and varieties with the agility of an acrobat. But his science stopped right there. Classifying was his life, and he knew nothing beyond it. Deeply versed in the theory of classification, and hardly at all in its practice, he could not, I believe, have told a sperm whale from a right whale! And yet, what a fine and worthy young fellow!

For ten years now, Conseil had followed me wherever science dragged me. Never once a word from him about the length of a journey or how tired it left him. Never an objection to buckling up his suitcase for whatever country you cared to name — China or the Congo, however far off it lay. Off he went, there as here, and asked for nothing more. On top of that, he enjoyed splendid health that defied every illness; solid muscles, but no nerves — not the least trace of nerves, morally speaking, that is.

The young man was thirty years old, and his age stood to his master's as fifteen to twenty. I hope I may be forgiven for admitting, in this roundabout way, that I was forty.

Conseil had just one flaw. A rabid stickler for form, he never spoke to me except in the third person — to the point of being downright irritating.

"Conseil!" I said again, my hands already flying feverishly through my preparations for departure.

I had no doubt whatever about this devoted young man. As a rule I never asked whether it suited him or not to come along on my travels; but this time we were talking about an expedition that might drag on indefinitely, a hazardous undertaking, in pursuit of an animal that could send a frigate to the bottom like a walnut shell! There was matter for reflection here, even for the most unflappable man alive. What would Conseil say?

"Conseil!" I cried a third time.

Conseil appeared.

"Master called?" he said as he came in.

"Yes, my boy. Get me ready, get yourself ready. We leave in two hours."

"Just as Master pleases," Conseil answered calmly.

"Not a moment to lose. Pack into my trunk all my traveling gear, clothes, shirts, socks — don't bother counting them, just as many as you can manage — and be quick about it!"

"And Master's collections?" Conseil observed.

"We'll deal with those later."

"What! The archaeotherium, the hyracotherium, the oreodons, the cheiropotamus, and Master's other carcasses?"

"They'll be kept at the hotel."

"And Master's live babirusa?"

"It'll be fed while we're away. In fact, I'll leave orders to have our whole menagerie shipped off to France."

"So we're not going back to Paris?" Conseil asked.

"Yes... certainly..." I answered evasively, "only by way of a small detour."

"Whatever detour Master pleases."

"Oh, it's a trifling one! A slightly less direct route, that's all. We're taking passage aboard the Abraham Lincoln."

"Just as it suits Master," Conseil answered placidly.

"You see, my friend, it's about the monster... the famous narwhal... We're going to rid the seas of it! The author of a two-volume quarto work on The Mysteries of the Great Ocean Depths can hardly excuse himself from sailing with Commander Farragut. A glorious mission, but — a dangerous one too! There's no knowing where we're bound! These creatures can be very unpredictable! But we'll go all the same! We have a commander who doesn't scare easily!"

"Whatever Master does, I'll do," Conseil answered.

"And think it over carefully! Because I don't want to hide a thing from you. This is one of those voyages a man doesn't always come back from!"

"Just as Master pleases."

A quarter of an hour later, our trunks were ready. Conseil had done it in a flash, and I was certain nothing was missing, for the young man classified shirts and coats every bit as well as birds or mammals.

The hotel elevator set us down in the great vestibule of the mezzanine. I went down the few steps that led to the ground floor. I settled my bill at that vast counter forever besieged by a considerable crowd. I left orders to ship my bales of stuffed animals and dried plants to Paris (France). I opened a line of credit sufficient to keep the babirusa fed, and, with Conseil at my heels, I jumped into a carriage.

The vehicle — twenty francs the trip — went down Broadway as far as Union Square, followed Fourth Avenue to its junction with Bowery Street, turned into Catherine Street, and stopped at Pier 34. There the Catherine ferryboat carried us — men, horses, and carriage — over to Brooklyn, that great annex of New York on the left bank of the East River, and in a few minutes we reached the wharf beside which the Abraham Lincoln was belching torrents of black smoke from its two funnels.

Our baggage was hauled aboard the frigate's deck at once. I hurried on board. I asked for Commander Farragut. One of the sailors led me up to the poop deck, where I found myself face to face with a fine-looking officer who held out his hand to me.

"Monsieur Pierre Aronnax?" he said.

"The very man," I answered. "Commander Farragut?"

"In person. Welcome aboard, Professor. Your cabin is waiting for you."

I bowed, and leaving the commander to the business of getting under way, I had myself shown to the cabin set aside for me.

The Abraham Lincoln had been perfectly chosen and fitted out for its new assignment. It was a fast frigate, equipped with superheating apparatus that let it drive its steam pressure up to seven atmospheres. Under that pressure the Abraham Lincoln reached an average speed of eighteen and three-tenths miles an hour — a considerable pace, yet still not enough to keep up with the gigantic cetacean.

The interior arrangements of the frigate matched its nautical qualities. I was very pleased with my cabin, set in the stern, opening onto the officers' wardroom.

"We'll be comfortable here," I said to Conseil.

"As comfortable, if Master doesn't mind my saying so," Conseil replied, "as a hermit crab in the shell of a whelk."

I left Conseil to stow our trunks properly and went back up on deck to watch the preparations for getting under way.

Just then Commander Farragut was ordering the last moorings cast off that still held the Abraham Lincoln to the Brooklyn pier. And so, a quarter of an hour's delay — even less — and the frigate would have sailed without me, and I would have missed this extraordinary, supernatural, altogether improbable expedition, whose truthful account may yet find a few skeptics.

But Commander Farragut had no intention of losing a single day, or a single hour, in reaching the waters where the animal had just been sighted. He sent for his engineer.

"Are we up to pressure?" he asked.

"Yes, sir," the engineer answered.

"Go ahead," cried Commander Farragut.

At this order, relayed to the engine room by compressed-air devices, the mechanics set the starting wheel in motion. Steam whistled as it rushed into the half-open valves. The long horizontal pistons groaned and shoved the connecting rods of the shaft. The blades of the propeller beat the waves with mounting speed, and the Abraham Lincoln moved off majestically amid a hundred or so ferryboats and tenders loaded with spectators, forming an escort in its honor.

The wharves of Brooklyn and all that part of New York bordering the East River were packed with the curious. Three cheers, torn from five hundred thousand throats, burst out one after another. Thousands of handkerchiefs waved above the dense mass and saluted the Abraham Lincoln until it reached the waters of the Hudson, at the tip of that long peninsula that forms the city of New York.

Then the frigate, following along the New Jersey side the admirable right bank of the river, all lined with villas, passed between the forts, which saluted it with their heaviest guns. The Abraham Lincoln answered by lowering and raising three times the American flag, its thirty-nine stars blazing at the mizzen peak; then, altering course to take the buoyed channel that curves around into the inner bay formed by the point of Sandy Hook, it skimmed that sandy spit where a few thousand spectators cheered it one more time.

The procession of boats and tenders kept following the frigate, and left it only when they came abreast of the lightship whose two beacons mark the entrance to the New York channels.

Three o'clock was striking just then. The pilot climbed down into his boat and rejoined the little schooner waiting for him to leeward. The fires were stoked; the propeller beat the waves faster; the frigate ran along the low yellow coast of Long Island, and at eight o'clock in the evening, having lost the lights of Fire Island to the northwest, it steamed at full speed out onto the dark waters of the Atlantic.
