A New Proposal from Captain Nemo

On February 28, when the Nautilus rose to the surface at noon at 9°4′ north latitude, it came in sight of land lying eight miles to the west. The first thing I made out was a cluster of mountains, about two thousand feet high, whose shapes had been molded into the most fanciful outlines. Once the reading was taken, I went back into the salon, and when the bearing had been plotted on the chart, I recognized that we stood off the island of Ceylon — that pearl hanging from the lower lobe of the Indian peninsula.

I went to the library to look for some book about this island, one of the most fertile on the globe. As it happened, I found a volume by Sirr H. C., esq., titled Ceylon and the Cingalese. Back in the salon, I first noted down the bearings of Ceylon, on which antiquity had lavished so many different names. It lay between 5°55′ and 9°49′ north latitude, and between 79°42′ and 82°4′ longitude east of the Greenwich meridian; its length was two hundred and seventy-five miles; its maximum width, one hundred and fifty miles; its circumference, nine hundred miles; its area, twenty-four thousand four hundred and forty-eight miles — in other words, a little smaller than Ireland.

At that moment Captain Nemo and his second officer appeared.

The captain glanced at the chart. Then, turning to me:

"The island of Ceylon," he said, "a land famous for its pearl fisheries. Would it please you, Professor Aronnax, to visit one of them?"

"Without a doubt, Captain."

"Good. That will be easy enough. Only, if we see the fisheries, we won't see the fishermen. The annual season hasn't opened yet. No matter. I'll give the order to make for the Gulf of Manaar, where we'll arrive during the night."

The captain said a few words to his second officer, who left at once. Soon the Nautilus sank back into its watery element, and the pressure gauge showed that it was holding at a depth of thirty feet.

With the chart before me, I searched for this Gulf of Manaar. I found it on the ninth parallel, off the northwest coast of Ceylon. It was formed by a long line running out from the little island of Manaar. To reach it, we would have to work our way up the entire western shore of Ceylon.

"Professor," Captain Nemo said to me then, "pearls are fished in the Bay of Bengal, in the Indian Sea, in the seas of China and Japan, in the seas of southern America, in the Gulf of Panama, in the Gulf of California; but it is at Ceylon that the fishing yields the finest results. We're arriving a bit early, no doubt. The fishermen don't gather in the Gulf of Manaar until the month of March, and then, for thirty days, their three hundred boats give themselves over to this lucrative harvest of the sea's treasures. Each boat is crewed by ten rowers and ten fishermen. The fishermen, divided into two teams, dive in turns and go down to a depth of twelve meters by means of a heavy stone that they grip between their feet and that a rope ties back to the boat."

"So," I said, "it's still this primitive method that's in use?"

"Still," Captain Nemo answered, "even though these fisheries belong to the most industrious people on the globe — the English, to whom the Treaty of Amiens ceded them in 1802."

"It seems to me, though, that the diving suit, such as you use it, would render great service in an operation like this."

"Yes, for these poor fishermen can't stay underwater for long. The Englishman Perceval, in his voyage to Ceylon, does speak of an African diver who could stay five minutes without coming up, but I find that hard to believe. I know that some divers reach fifty-seven seconds, and very skilled ones as much as eighty-seven; but they're rare, and once back aboard, these wretched men bleed from the nose and ears, the water tinged with blood. I believe the average time the fishermen can bear is thirty seconds, during which they hurry to cram into a little net all the pearl oysters they can wrench loose; but generally these fishermen don't live to old age. Their eyesight weakens; ulcers break out on their eyes; sores form on their bodies; and often they're even struck down by apoplexy at the bottom of the sea."

"Yes," I said, "it's a sad trade, and one that serves nothing but the satisfaction of a few whims. But tell me, Captain — how many oysters can a boat fish in a day?"

"Forty to fifty thousand or so. It's even said that in 1814, when the English government had fishing done on its own account, its divers, in twenty days of work, brought up seventy-six million oysters."

"At least," I asked, "are these fishermen decently paid?"

"Barely, Professor. In Panama they earn only a dollar a week. Most often, they get a single sou for an oyster that holds a pearl — and how many do they bring up that hold none!"

"A sou to these poor people who make their masters rich! It's disgraceful."

"And so, Professor," Captain Nemo said to me, "you and your companions will visit the bank of Manaar, and if by chance some early fisherman is already there, well, we'll watch him at work."

"Agreed, Captain."

"By the way, Professor Aronnax — you're not afraid of sharks?"

"Sharks?" I cried.

The question struck me as, to say the least, rather pointless.

"Well?" Captain Nemo went on.

"I'll admit, Captain, that I'm not yet very well acquainted with that kind of fish."

"We're used to them, we are," Captain Nemo replied, "and in time you will be too. Besides, we'll be armed, and along the way we might even hunt a shark or two. It's an interesting sport. So then — until tomorrow, Professor, and bright and early."

Having said this in an offhand tone, Captain Nemo left the salon.

If someone invited you to hunt bear in the mountains of Switzerland, you'd say, "Very good! Tomorrow we go bear-hunting." If someone invited you to hunt lion on the plains of the Atlas, or tiger in the jungles of India, you'd say, "Aha! So it seems we're off to hunt tiger or lion!" But if someone invited you to hunt shark in its natural element, you might well ask leave to think it over before accepting the invitation.

As for me, I passed my hand across my forehead, where a few beads of cold sweat had gathered.

"Let's think this over," I told myself, "and take our time. Hunting otters in the underwater forests, as we did in the forests of Crespo Island — that's one thing. But roaming the bottom of the sea when you're all but certain to meet sharks down there — that's another thing entirely! I know that in certain countries, in the Andaman Islands especially, the native divers don't hesitate to attack a shark with a dagger in one hand and a noose in the other; but I also know that many of those who face these formidable animals don't come back alive! Besides, I'm no native diver, and even if I were, I think a slight hesitation on my part, in this case, wouldn't be out of place."

And there I was dreaming of sharks, thinking of those vast jaws bristling with multiple rows of teeth, capable of cutting a man in two. I could already feel a certain ache around my kidneys. And I couldn't stomach the casual ease with which the captain had made this deplorable invitation! You'd have thought he was proposing to go track down some harmless fox in the underbrush.

"Well!" I thought. "Conseil will never agree to come, and that'll excuse me from going along with the captain."

As for Ned Land, I confess I didn't feel quite so sure of his good sense. A danger, however great, always held a certain appeal for his combative nature.

I went back to reading Sirr's book, but I leafed through it absentmindedly. Between the lines I kept seeing jaws opened terrifyingly wide.

Just then Conseil and the Canadian came in, looking calm and even cheerful. They had no idea what was in store for them.

"Well, Professor," Ned Land said to me, "your Captain Nemo — the devil take him! — has just made us a very kind proposal."

"Ah!" I said. "So you know—"

"Begging Master's pardon," Conseil answered, "the commander of the Nautilus has invited us to visit tomorrow, in Master's company, the magnificent fisheries of Ceylon. He did it in excellent terms and behaved like a true gentleman."

"He said nothing more to you?"

"Nothing, Professor," the Canadian answered, "except that he'd already spoken to you about this little outing."

"So he did," I said. "And he gave you no details about—"

"None, Professor. You'll be coming with us, won't you?"

"Me? Of course! I see you've taken a liking to the idea, Master Land."

"Yes! It's curious — very curious."

"Dangerous, perhaps!" I added in an insinuating tone.

"Dangerous," Ned Land answered, "a simple excursion onto an oyster bank!"

Captain Nemo had clearly decided it was pointless to plant the idea of sharks in my companions' minds. I looked at them with a troubled eye, as though they were already missing a limb or two. Should I warn them? Yes, surely, but I hardly knew how to go about it.

"Professor," Conseil said to me, "would Master be willing to give us some details about pearl fishing?"

"About the fishing itself?" I asked. "Or about the incidents that—"

"About the fishing," the Canadian said. "Before setting foot on the ground, it's good to know it."

"Well then! Sit down, my friends, and I'll teach you everything the Englishman Sirr has just taught me."

Ned and Conseil settled onto a divan, and the first thing the Canadian said to me was:

"Professor, what exactly is a pearl?"

"My good Ned," I answered, "to the poet, the pearl is a tear of the sea; to the peoples of the East, it's a drop of dew turned solid; to the ladies, it's a jewel of oblong shape, of a glassy luster, of a mother-of-pearl substance, which they wear on the finger, at the neck, or in the ear; to the chemist, it's a mixture of phosphate and carbonate of lime with a little gelatin; and finally, to the naturalist, it's simply a diseased secretion of the organ that produces mother-of-pearl in certain bivalves."

"Phylum of mollusks," said Conseil, "class of acephalans, order of testaceans."

"Precisely, learned Conseil. Now, among these testaceans, the iridescent sea-ear, the turban shells, the giant clams, the pinna shells — in a word, all those that secrete mother-of-pearl, that is, the blue, bluish, violet, or white substance lining the inside of their shells — are capable of producing pearls."

"Mussels too?" asked the Canadian.

"Yes! The mussels of certain streams of Scotland, Wales, Ireland, Saxony, Bohemia, and France."

"Good! We'll keep an eye out for those from now on," the Canadian answered.

"But," I went on, "the mollusk above all others that distills the pearl is the pearl oyster, the Meleagrina margaritifera, the precious pintadine. The pearl is nothing but a nacreous concretion arranged in a globular form. Either it sticks to the oyster's shell, or it embeds itself in the folds of the animal. On the valves, the pearl is fixed; on the flesh, it's loose. But it always has for its nucleus some small hard body, either a sterile ovule or a grain of sand, around which the nacreous matter is deposited over several years, layer by layer, in thin concentric coats."

"Do you find several pearls in a single oyster?" Conseil asked.

"Yes, my boy. There are certain pintadines that form a veritable jewel-case. People have even cited one oyster — though I take the liberty of doubting it — that contained no fewer than a hundred and fifty sharks."

"A hundred and fifty sharks!" cried Ned Land.

"Did I say sharks?" I exclaimed quickly. "I mean a hundred and fifty pearls. Sharks would make no sense."

"Quite so," said Conseil. "But will Master now teach us the means by which these pearls are extracted?"

"They go about it in several ways, and often, when the pearls stick to the valves, the fishermen tear them loose with pincers. But most commonly, the pintadines are spread out on esparto-grass mats that cover the shore. There they die in the open air, and after ten days they're in a satisfactory state of putrefaction. They're then plunged into vast reservoirs of seawater, after which they're opened and washed. This is where the sorters' double task begins. First, they separate the layers of mother-of-pearl known in the trade as silver-true, white-bastard, and black-bastard, which are delivered in cases of a hundred and twenty-five to a hundred and fifty kilograms. Then they strip out the flesh of the oyster, boil it, and sift it in order to extract even the smallest pearls."

"Does the price of these pearls vary according to their size?" Conseil asked.

"Not only according to their size," I answered, "but also according to their shape, according to their water — that is, their color — and according to their orient — that is, that shimmering, iridescent gleam that makes them so lovely to the eye. The finest pearls are called virgin pearls, or paragons; they form in isolation within the tissue of the mollusk; they're white, often opaque, but sometimes of an opaline transparency, and most commonly spherical or pear-shaped. The spherical ones make bracelets; the pear-shaped ones, pendants — and, being the most precious, they're sold one by one. The others stick to the oyster's shell and, being more irregular, are sold by weight. Finally, in a lower order come the little pearls known as seed pearls; they're sold by measure and serve especially for working embroidery onto church ornaments."

"But this work of separating the pearls by size must be long and difficult," said the Canadian.

"No, my friend. This work is done by means of eleven sieves, or riddles, pierced with a varying number of holes. The pearls that stay in the sieves with twenty to eighty holes are of the first order. Those that don't slip through the riddles pierced with a hundred to eight hundred holes are of the second order. And finally, the pearls for which they use the sieves pierced with nine hundred to a thousand holes make up the seed pearls."

"That's ingenious," said Conseil, "and I see that the dividing and sorting of pearls is done mechanically. And can Master tell us what the working of the pearl-oyster banks brings in?"

"To go by Sirr's book," I answered, "the fisheries of Ceylon are leased out annually for the sum of three million sharks."

"Francs, you mean!" Conseil put in.

"Yes, francs! Three million francs," I went on. "But I believe these fisheries no longer bring in what they once did. The same is true of the American fisheries, which under the reign of Charles the Fifth produced four million francs, now reduced to two-thirds of that. All told, one may put the overall yield of the pearl trade at nine million francs."

"But," Conseil asked, "aren't there a few famous pearls that have been valued at a very high price?"

"Yes, my boy. It's said that Caesar gave Servilia a pearl valued at a hundred and twenty thousand francs in our currency."

"I've even heard tell," said the Canadian, "of a certain lady of ancient times who used to drink pearls in her vinegar."

"Cleopatra," Conseil shot back.

"It must have tasted foul," Ned Land added.

"Detestable, friend Ned," Conseil answered; "but a little glass of vinegar that costs fifteen hundred thousand francs is a tidy price."

"I'm sorry I didn't marry that lady," said the Canadian, working his arm in a rather unsettling way.

"Ned Land the husband of Cleopatra!" cried Conseil.

"Well, I did nearly get married, Conseil," the Canadian answered seriously, "and it's not my fault the business fell through. I'd even bought a pearl necklace for Kat Tender, my fiancée — who married someone else, as it happens. Well, that necklace cost me no more than a dollar and a half, and yet — the Professor will kindly take my word for it — the pearls that made it up wouldn't have passed through the twenty-hole sieve."

"My good Ned," I answered, laughing, "those were artificial pearls, mere glass globules coated on the inside with essence of Orient."

"Essence of Orient?" asked the Canadian. "And what's that worth?"

"Next to nothing! It's nothing but the silvery matter of the bleak's scales, gathered in water and preserved in ammonia. It has no value at all."

"That's maybe why Kat Tender married someone else," Master Land replied philosophically.

"But," I said, "to come back to the pearls of high value, I don't believe any sovereign ever owned one superior to Captain Nemo's."

"This one," said Conseil, pointing to the magnificent jewel enclosed under its glass case.

"Certainly. I'm not mistaken in assigning it a value of two million—"

"Francs!" Conseil said quickly.

"Yes," I said, "two million francs, and no doubt it cost the captain nothing but the trouble of picking it up."

"Ha!" cried Ned Land. "Who's to say we won't run across its equal tomorrow, during our little walk!"

"Bah!" went Conseil.

"And why not?"

"What use would millions be to us aboard the Nautilus?"

"Aboard, none," said Ned Land, "but... elsewhere."

"Oh! Elsewhere!" went Conseil, shaking his head.

"Come to think of it," I said, "Master Land is right. And if we ever bring back to Europe or America a pearl worth a few million, that at least will lend great authenticity — and, at the same time, great value — to the account of our adventures."

"I believe it," said the Canadian.

"But," said Conseil, who always came back to the instructive side of things, "is this pearl fishing dangerous?"

"No," I answered quickly, "especially if one takes certain precautions."

"What's the risk in the trade?" said Ned Land. "Swallowing a few mouthfuls of seawater!"

"Just as you say, Ned. By the way," I said, trying to take on Captain Nemo's offhand tone, "are you afraid of sharks, brave Ned?"

"Me?" the Canadian answered. "A harpooner by trade! It's my job to laugh at them!"

"It's not a matter," I said, "of catching them with a swivel-hook, hoisting them onto a ship's deck, chopping off their tails with an axe, slitting open their bellies, tearing out their hearts, and throwing them back into the sea!"

"Then it's a matter of...?"

"Yes, precisely."

"In the water?"

"In the water."

"My word — with a good harpoon! You know, Professor, these sharks are pretty poorly built beasts. They have to roll over onto their bellies to snap you up, and in the meantime..."

Ned Land had a way of pronouncing the word "snap" that sent a chill down your spine.

"Well, and you, Conseil — what do you think of these sharks?"

"Me?" said Conseil. "I'll be frank with Master."

"That's the spirit," I thought.

"If Master faces the sharks," said Conseil, "I don't see why his faithful servant shouldn't face them right alongside him!"
