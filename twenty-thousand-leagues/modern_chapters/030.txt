The Greek Archipelago

The next day, February 12, at daybreak, the Nautilus rose to the surface of the waves. I rushed up onto the platform. Three miles to the south the hazy silhouette of Pelusium took shape. A torrent had carried us from one sea to the other. But that tunnel, easy enough to run down, must surely be impossible to climb back up.

Toward seven o'clock, Ned and Conseil joined me. These two inseparable companions had slept soundly, without troubling themselves in the least over the exploits of the Nautilus.

"Well, Professor," asked the Canadian, in a tone of light mockery, "and this Mediterranean of yours?"

"We're floating on the surface of it, friend Ned."

"What!" said Conseil. "This very night?"

"Yes, this very night, in the space of a few minutes, we crossed that uncrossable isthmus."

"I don't believe a word of it," the Canadian answered.

"Then you're wrong, Master Land," I replied. "That low coast curving away to the south is the coast of Egypt."

"Tell that to someone else, Professor," retorted the stubborn Canadian.

"But since Master says so," Conseil told him, "then Master must be believed."

"What's more, Ned, Captain Nemo did me the honors of his tunnel himself, and I was standing right beside him, in the pilot's cage, while he steered the Nautilus through that narrow passage with his own hands."

"You hear that, Ned?" said Conseil.

"And you, with those sharp eyes of yours," I added, "you can make out the jetties of Port Said stretching out into the sea."

The Canadian looked closely.

"You're right at that," he said. "You're right, Professor, and that captain of yours is a master among men. We're in the Mediterranean. Good. Then let's talk, if you please, about our own little affairs — but in such a way that no one can overhear us."

I saw well enough what the Canadian was driving at. In any case, I thought it was better to talk, since he wanted to, and the three of us went and sat down near the beacon, where we were less exposed to the damp spray of the waves.

"Now then, Ned, we're listening," I said. "What do you have to tell us?"

"What I have to tell you is very simple," the Canadian answered. "We're in Europe, and before Captain Nemo's whims drag us off to the bottom of the polar seas or carry us back to Oceania, I'm asking to leave the Nautilus."

I admit this discussion with the Canadian always put me in an awkward spot. I had no wish whatsoever to hinder my companions' freedom, and yet I felt not the slightest desire to leave Captain Nemo. Thanks to him, thanks to his machine, I was completing my underwater studies day by day, rewriting my book of the ocean depths in the very midst of its element. Would I ever again find such a chance to observe the wonders of the sea? No, surely not! And so I couldn't reconcile myself to the idea of abandoning the Nautilus before our cycle of investigations was done.

"Friend Ned," I said, "answer me honestly. Are you bored aboard? Do you regret that fate threw you into Captain Nemo's hands?"

The Canadian went a few moments without answering. Then, folding his arms:

"Honestly," he said, "I don't regret this voyage under the seas. I'll be glad to have made it. But to have made it, it has to end. That's how I feel."

"It will end, Ned."

"Where and when?"

"Where? I have no idea. When? I can't say — or rather, I suppose it will finish when these seas have nothing more to teach us. Everything that begins in this world necessarily has an end."

"I think as Master does," Conseil put in, "and it's quite possible that after sailing across all the seas of the globe, Captain Nemo will give the three of us the boot."

"The boot!" cried the Canadian. "You mean a boot, I hope!"

"Let's not exaggerate, Master Land," I went on. "We have nothing to fear from the captain, but I don't share Conseil's ideas either. We're masters of the Nautilus's secrets, and I don't expect its commander, just to give us back our freedom, will resign himself to letting those secrets roam the world with us."

"Then what do you hope for?" asked the Canadian.

"That circumstances will arise which we can — which we must — take advantage of, just as well six months from now as this minute."

"Oh ho!" said Ned Land. "And where will we be six months from now, if you please, Professor?"

"Perhaps here, perhaps in China. You know the Nautilus is a swift traveler. It crosses the oceans the way a swallow crosses the air, or an express train crosses the continents. It has no fear of frequented waters. Who's to say it won't come alongside the coasts of France, England, or America, where an escape could be attempted just as favorably as here?"

"Professor Aronnax," the Canadian answered, "your arguments are rotten at the root. You speak in the future: 'We'll be there! We'll be here!' Me, I speak in the present: 'We are here, and we'd better make the most of it.'"

I was hard pressed by Ned Land's logic, and I felt myself beaten on that ground. I no longer knew what arguments to bring forward in my own favor.

"Professor," Ned went on, "let's suppose — impossible as it is — that Captain Nemo offers you your freedom this very day. Would you accept?"

"I don't know," I answered.

"And if he adds that this offer he's making you today, he won't renew it later on, would you accept then?"

I didn't answer.

"And what does friend Conseil think?" asked Ned Land.

"Friend Conseil," the worthy fellow answered calmly, "friend Conseil has nothing to say. He is entirely disinterested in the question. Like his master, like his comrade Ned, he is a bachelor. No wife, no relatives, no children wait for him back home. He is in Master's service, he thinks as Master thinks, he speaks as Master speaks, and, much to his regret, no one can count on him to make a majority. Only two people are here in contention: Master on the one side, Ned Land on the other. That said, friend Conseil is listening, and he stands ready to keep the score."

I couldn't help smiling to see Conseil annihilate his own personality so completely. Deep down, the Canadian must have been delighted not to have him against him.

"Then, Professor," said Ned Land, "since Conseil doesn't exist, let's argue it out between the two of us. I've spoken, you've heard me. What have you got to answer?"

Clearly I had to come to a conclusion, and evasions were repugnant to me.

"Friend Ned," I said, "here is my answer. You're right and I'm wrong, and my arguments can't stand up against yours. There's no counting on Captain Nemo's goodwill. The most ordinary prudence forbids him to set us free. By the same token, prudence demands that we seize the first chance to leave the Nautilus."

"Well said, Professor Aronnax. Now that's talking wisely."

"Only," I said, "one observation, just one. The chance has to be a serious one. Our first attempt to escape has to succeed; because if it fails, we won't get another opening to try again, and Captain Nemo won't forgive us."

"That's all quite true," the Canadian answered. "But your observation applies to any escape attempt, whether it happens in two years or in two days. So the question is always this: if a favorable chance presents itself, we have to seize it."

"Agreed. And now, Ned, will you tell me what you mean by a favorable chance?"

"It would be one that, on some dark night, brings the Nautilus a short distance off a European coast."

"And you'd try to save yourself by swimming for it?"

"Yes, if we were close enough to a shore, and if the ship were floating on the surface. No, if we were far out, and if the ship were running underwater."

"And in that case?"

"In that case, I'd try to get hold of the dinghy. I know how it's handled. We'd slip inside, and with the bolts drawn, we'd rise to the surface without so much as the helmsman, stationed up forward, noticing our escape."

"Good, Ned. Then keep watch for that chance; but don't forget that a failure would be the end of us."

"I won't forget it, Professor."

"And now, Ned, do you want to know my whole thought on your plan?"

"Gladly, Professor Aronnax."

"Well, I think — I won't say I hope — I think that this favorable chance will never present itself."

"Why is that?"

"Because Captain Nemo can't hide from himself that we haven't given up hope of recovering our freedom, and he'll stay on his guard, above all in the seas and within sight of the coasts of Europe."

"I'm of Master's opinion," said Conseil.

"We'll see about that," answered Ned Land, shaking his head with a determined air.

"And now, Ned Land," I added, "let's leave it there. Not another word on any of this. The day you're ready, warn us and we'll follow you. I put myself completely in your hands."

That was how this conversation ended — a conversation that was to have such grave consequences later on. And I must say now that events seemed to confirm my predictions, to the Canadian's great despair. Did Captain Nemo distrust us in these frequented seas, or did he simply want to keep out of sight of the countless ships of every nation that furrow the Mediterranean? I don't know, but most of the time he kept us between two waters and well out from the coasts. Either the Nautilus surfaced, showing nothing but the pilot's cage, or it slipped away to great depths, for between the Greek archipelago and Asia Minor we found no bottom at two thousand meters.

And so it was only through a line of Virgil that I came to know of the island of Karpathos, one of the Sporades, when Captain Nemo quoted it to me, laying his finger on a point of the planisphere:

Est in Carpathio Neptuni gurgite vates Coeruleus Proteus...

It was indeed the ancient dwelling of Proteus, the old shepherd of Neptune's flocks — now the island of Scarpanto, lying between Rhodes and Crete. I saw nothing of it but the granite foundations through the glass of the salon.

The next day, February 14, I resolved to spend a few hours studying the fishes of the Archipelago; but for some reason or other, the panels stayed hermetically shut. Taking the bearings of the Nautilus's course, I noticed that it was heading toward Candia, the ancient island of Crete. At the moment when I had embarked on the Abraham Lincoln, that island had just risen up in its entirety against Turkish despotism. But what had become of the insurrection since that time, I had absolutely no idea, and it certainly wasn't Captain Nemo, cut off from all communication with the land, who could have told me.

So I made no allusion to that event when, that evening, I found myself alone with him in the salon. Besides, he struck me as taciturn, preoccupied. Then, contrary to his habits, he ordered both panels of the salon opened, and, moving from one to the other, he studied the mass of water attentively. To what end? I couldn't guess, and for my part I spent my time studying the fish that passed before my eyes.

Among others, I noticed those aphysa gobies mentioned by Aristotle and commonly known as "sea loaches," which one meets particularly in the salt waters bordering the delta of the Nile. Near them unrolled some half-phosphorescent pagres, a kind of sea bream that the Egyptians ranked among their sacred animals, and whose arrival in the river's waters — announcing its fertile flooding — was celebrated with religious ceremonies. I noted as well some cheilines three decimeters long, bony fish with transparent scales, their livid color mixed with red spots; they are great eaters of marine plants, which gives them an exquisite flavor; and so these cheilines were much sought after by the gourmets of ancient Rome, and their entrails, dressed with the milt of moray eels, the brains of peacocks, and the tongues of flamingos, made up that divine dish which so delighted Vitellius. Another inhabitant of these seas caught my attention and brought back to my mind all the memories of antiquity. This was the remora, which travels attached to the belly of sharks; according to the ancients, this little fish, clinging to the keel of a ship, could stop it in its course, and one of them, holding back Antony's vessel during the Battle of Actium, thus helped Augustus to victory. On what slender threads the destinies of nations hang! I also observed some admirable anthias, which belong to the order of the lutjanids, fish sacred to the Greeks, who credited them with the power to drive sea monsters from the waters they frequented; their name means "flower," and they justified it by their shimmering colors, their shades ranging across the whole scale of red from the paleness of rose to the blaze of ruby, and by the fleeting reflections that shot moiré patterns across their dorsal fins. My eyes couldn't tear themselves away from these marvels of the sea, when suddenly they were struck by an unexpected apparition.

In the midst of the waters, a man appeared — a diver, carrying a leather pouch at his belt. This was no corpse abandoned to the waves. This was a living man, swimming with a vigorous stroke of one arm, vanishing now and again to go breathe at the surface, then plunging back down at once.

I turned toward Captain Nemo, and in a stirred voice:

"A man! A castaway!" I cried. "We must save him at all costs!"

The captain didn't answer me and went to lean against the glass.

The man had come closer, and, his face pressed to the panel, he was looking in at us.

To my utter astonishment, Captain Nemo made him a sign. The diver answered him with his hand, rose immediately toward the surface of the sea, and did not reappear.

"Don't be alarmed," the captain told me. "That's Nicolas, of Cape Matapan, nicknamed the Pesce. He's well known throughout the Cyclades. A bold diver! Water is his element, and he lives in it more than on land, forever going from one island to another, all the way to Crete."

"You know him, Captain?"

"Why not, Professor Aronnax?"

That said, Captain Nemo walked over to a cabinet standing near the left-hand panel of the salon. Beside this cabinet I saw an iron-bound chest, its lid bearing on a copper plate the monogram of the Nautilus, with its motto, Mobilis in Mobili.

At that moment the captain, without concerning himself over my presence, opened the cabinet — a sort of safe that held a great number of ingots.

They were ingots of gold. Where did this precious metal come from, this metal that represented an enormous sum? Where was the captain gathering this gold, and what was he going to do with it?

I didn't utter a word. I watched. Captain Nemo took these ingots one by one and arranged them methodically in the chest, which he filled completely. I estimated that it held then more than a thousand kilograms of gold — that is, close to five million francs.

The chest was securely closed, and the captain wrote an address on its lid in characters that must have belonged to modern Greek.

This done, Captain Nemo pressed a button whose wire connected with the crew's quarters. Four men appeared, and, not without difficulty, they pushed the chest out of the salon. Then I heard them hoisting it by means of tackle up the iron staircase.

At that moment Captain Nemo turned to me:

"You were saying, Professor?" he asked.

"I was saying nothing, Captain."

"Then, Professor, you'll permit me to wish you good evening."

And with that, Captain Nemo left the salon.

I went back to my cabin greatly intrigued, as one can imagine. I tried in vain to sleep. I kept searching for some connection between the appearance of that diver and this chest full of gold. Soon, from certain movements of rolling and pitching, I felt that the Nautilus was leaving the lower layers and coming back to the surface of the waters.

Then I heard the sound of footsteps on the platform. I understood that the dinghy was being unfastened and launched into the sea. It knocked for an instant against the flanks of the Nautilus, and then all sound ceased.

Two hours later, the same sound, the same comings and goings repeated themselves. The boat, hoisted aboard, was fitted back into its socket, and the Nautilus plunged once more beneath the waves.

So those millions had been carried to their destination. To what point on the continent? Who was Captain Nemo's correspondent?

The next day I recounted to Conseil and the Canadian the events of that night, which excited my curiosity to the highest degree. My companions were no less surprised than I was.

"But where does he get these millions?" asked Ned Land.

To that, no answer was possible. I went into the salon after breakfast and set to work. Until five in the evening, I was writing up my notes. At that moment — was I to put it down to some personal disposition? — I felt an extreme heat, and I had to take off my coat of byssus. An incomprehensible effect, for we were not at high latitudes, and besides, the Nautilus, submerged, should have felt no rise in temperature at all. I looked at the pressure gauge. It showed a depth of sixty feet, a depth the warmth of the atmosphere could never have reached.

I went on with my work, but the temperature rose to the point of becoming intolerable.

"Could there be a fire aboard?" I asked myself.

I was about to leave the salon when Captain Nemo came in. He went up to the thermometer, consulted it, and, turning back to me:

"Forty-two degrees," he said.

"I feel it, Captain," I answered, "and if this heat increases even a little, we won't be able to bear it."

"Oh, Professor, this heat won't increase unless we want it to."

"So you can moderate it at will?"

"No, but I can move away from the source that produces it."

"So it's external?"

"Of course. We're floating in a current of boiling water."

"Is it possible?" I cried.

"Look."

The panels opened, and I saw the sea gone entirely white all around the Nautilus. A smoke of sulfurous vapors was unrolling through the middle of the waves, which bubbled like the water of a boiler. I laid my hand against one of the glass panes, but the heat was such that I had to pull it away.

"Where are we?" I asked.

"Near the island of Santorini, Professor," the captain answered, "and precisely in that channel which separates Nea Kameni from Palea Kameni. I wanted to give you the curious spectacle of an undersea eruption."

"I thought," I said, "that the formation of these new islands was finished."

"Nothing is ever finished in volcanic waters," answered Captain Nemo, "and the globe is forever being worked here by its underground fires. Already, in the nineteenth year of our era, according to Cassiodorus and Pliny, a new island, Theia the divine, appeared in the very place where these islets have recently formed. Then it sank beneath the waves, only to show itself again in the year sixty-nine and sink once more. From that time down to our own day, the plutonian labor was suspended. But on February 3, 1866, a new islet, which they named the islet of George, emerged amid the sulfurous vapors near Nea Kameni, and welded itself to it on the sixth of the same month. Seven days later, on February 13, the islet Aphroessa appeared, leaving a channel of ten meters between itself and Nea Kameni. I was in these seas when the phenomenon occurred, and I was able to observe all its phases. The islet Aphroessa, round in shape, measured three hundred feet in diameter by thirty feet in height. It was made of black glassy lavas, mixed with fragments of feldspar. Finally, on March 10, a smaller islet, called Reka, showed itself near Nea Kameni, and since then these three islets, welded together, form no more than a single, selfsame island."

"And the channel where we are at this moment?" I asked.

"Here it is," answered Captain Nemo, showing me a map of the Archipelago. "You see that I've marked the new islets on it."

"But this channel will fill in one day?"

"That's probable, Professor Aronnax, for since 1866, eight little islets of lava have surged up facing the port of Saint Nicholas on Palea Kameni. It's therefore obvious that Nea and Palea will join together before long. If, in the middle of the Pacific, it's the infusoria that build the continents, here it's the eruptive phenomena. Look, Professor, look at the labor being accomplished beneath these waves."

I came back toward the glass. The Nautilus had stopped moving. The heat was becoming intolerable. From white as it had been, the sea was turning red, a coloration due to the presence of an iron salt. Despite the hermetic sealing of the salon, an unbearable sulfurous odor was escaping, and I could make out scarlet flames whose brightness killed the glare of the electric light.

I was drenched in sweat, I was suffocating, I was going to cook. Yes, truly, I felt myself cooking!

"We can't stay any longer in this boiling water," I said to the captain.

"No, that wouldn't be prudent," answered the impassive Nemo.

An order was given. The Nautilus came about and drew away from that furnace it couldn't brave with impunity. A quarter of an hour later, we were breathing at the surface of the waves.

The thought then came to me that if Ned Land had chosen these waters to carry out our escape, we would never have come out alive from this sea of fire.

The next day, February 16, we left that basin which, between Rhodes and Alexandria, counts depths of three thousand meters, and the Nautilus, passing well off Cerigo, left the Greek archipelago behind, after doubling Cape Matapan.
