Some Figures

A moment later we were seated on a divan in the lounge, cigars between our lips. The captain laid before me a set of drawings that gave the plan, section, and elevation of the Nautilus. Then he began his description in these words:

"Here, Professor Aronnax, are the various dimensions of the vessel that carries you. It's a very elongated cylinder with conical ends. It closely resembles the shape of a cigar — a shape already adopted in London for several craft of the same kind. The length of this cylinder, from tip to tip, is exactly seventy meters, and its beam, at its widest point, is eight meters. So it isn't built quite to the ratio of one to ten, like your fast steamers, but its lines are long enough and its run prolonged enough that the displaced water slips away easily and puts up no resistance to its passage.

"These two dimensions let you work out, by a simple calculation, the surface area and the volume of the Nautilus. Its surface comes to 1,011.45 square meters; its volume, 1,500.2 cubic meters — which is to say that, fully submerged, it displaces or weighs 1,500.2 cubic meters, or tons.

"When I drew up the plans for a vessel meant to travel underwater, I wanted it, when floating in equilibrium, to sit nine-tenths submerged and to show only one-tenth above the surface. Under those conditions, then, it had to displace only nine-tenths of its volume — that is, 1,356.48 cubic meters, weighing that same number of tons. So in building it to the dimensions I just gave you, I couldn't exceed that weight.

"The Nautilus is made of two hulls, one inside the other, joined by T-shaped iron beams that give it tremendous rigidity. Thanks to this cellular arrangement, it stands up to pressure like a solid block, as though it were cast in one piece. Its plating can't give way; it holds together by its own cohesion and not by the grip of rivets, and the uniformity of its construction — the result of a perfect fit between materials — lets it defy the most violent seas.

"Both hulls are built of sheet steel, whose density relative to water is 7.8. The inner hull is no less than five centimeters thick and weighs 394.96 tons. The outer shell, together with the keel — fifty centimeters high and twenty-five wide, weighing 62 tons on its own — plus the engine, the ballast, the various fittings and furnishings, the interior bulkheads and bracing, all come to 961.62 tons; add that to the 394.96 tons, and you get the required total of 1,356.48 tons. Is that clear?"

"It's clear," I answered.

"So," the captain went on, "when the Nautilus is afloat under these conditions, one-tenth of it rides above the water. Now, I've fitted it with tanks whose capacity equals that one-tenth — a volume of 150.72 tons — and if I fill them with water, the vessel then displaces, or weighs, 1,507 tons, and is completely submerged. That's exactly what happens, Professor. These tanks are set in the lower parts of the Nautilus. I open the valves, they fill, the vessel sinks, and comes to rest just level with the surface of the water."

"Fine, Captain, but here we come to the real difficulty. That you can bring the ship level with the surface of the ocean, I understand. But lower down, diving beneath that surface, won't your underwater machine meet a pressure — and therefore suffer an upward thrust — that has to be reckoned at one atmosphere for every thirty feet of water, or about one kilogram per square centimeter?"

"Precisely, Professor."

"Then unless you fill the Nautilus entirely, I don't see how you can drive it down into the heart of the waters."

"Professor," Captain Nemo replied, "you mustn't confuse statics with dynamics, or you lay yourself open to serious errors. It takes very little effort to reach the ocean's lower regions, because bodies have a tendency to become 'sinkers.' Follow my reasoning."

"I'm listening, Captain."

"When I set out to determine the added weight the Nautilus would need in order to submerge, all I had to concern myself with was the reduction in volume that seawater undergoes as its layers grow deeper and deeper."

"That's obvious," I answered.

"Now, if water isn't absolutely incompressible, it is at least very slightly compressible. In fact, according to the most recent calculations, this reduction amounts to only 436 ten-millionths per atmosphere, or per thirty feet of depth. If I mean to go down a thousand meters, I then allow for the reduction in volume under a pressure equal to that of a column of water a thousand meters high — that is, under a pressure of a hundred atmospheres. This reduction would then be 436 hundred-thousandths. So I'd have to increase the weight until the vessel weighs 1,513.77 tons instead of 1,507.2 tons. The increase, consequently, would come to only 6.57 tons."

"Only that?"

"Only that, Professor Aronnax, and the calculation is easy to check. Now, I have supplementary tanks capable of taking on a hundred tons of water. So I can descend to considerable depths. When I want to rise again and come level with the surface, I need only expel that water, emptying all the tanks completely if I want the Nautilus to ride one-tenth of its total capacity above the waves."

Against reasoning backed by figures like these, I had nothing to object.

"I accept your calculations, Captain," I answered, "and I'd be churlish to dispute them, since experience proves them right every day. But now I sense a genuine difficulty ahead of us."

"Which is, Professor?"

"When you're at a depth of a thousand meters, the walls of the Nautilus bear a pressure of a hundred atmospheres. If, at that moment, you want to empty the supplementary tanks in order to lighten your vessel and rise to the surface, your pumps have to overcome that pressure of a hundred atmospheres — which comes to a hundred kilograms per square centimeter. That calls for a power —"

"That electricity alone could give me," Captain Nemo cut in. "I repeat, Professor: the dynamic power of my machines is very nearly infinite. The pumps of the Nautilus have prodigious force — as you must have seen when their columns of water came pouring like a torrent over the Abraham Lincoln. Besides, I only use the supplementary tanks to reach middling depths of fifteen hundred to two thousand meters, and even then only to spare my equipment. So when the whim takes me to visit the depths of the ocean, two or three leagues below its surface, I use maneuvers that take longer but are no less reliable."

"Which are, Captain?" I asked.

"That brings me naturally to telling you how the Nautilus is handled."

"I can't wait to hear it."

"To steer this vessel to starboard or to port — to maneuver it, in short, along a horizontal plane — I use an ordinary broad-bladed rudder, mounted on the stern-post and worked by a wheel and tackle. But I can also drive the Nautilus up and down, in a vertical plane, by means of two inclined planes attached to its sides at the waterline — movable planes, able to take any position, worked from inside by powerful levers. Kept parallel to the vessel, these planes hold it moving horizontally. Tilted, the Nautilus — depending on the angle set and driven by the thrust of its propeller — either dives along a diagonal as long as I please, or climbs along that same diagonal. And if I want to return to the surface faster still, I throw in the propeller, and the pressure of the water sends the Nautilus rising straight up like a balloon that, swollen with hydrogen, shoots quickly into the air."

"Bravo, Captain!" I cried. "But how can the helmsman follow the course you set for him in the middle of the waters?"

"The helmsman is stationed in a glass-walled cage that projects from the upper part of the Nautilus's hull, fitted with lens-shaped panes."

"Panes able to withstand such pressures?"

"Perfectly. Crystal, fragile under a sharp blow, nonetheless offers considerable resistance. In experiments with fishing by electric light, carried out in 1864 in the northern seas, plates of this material, only seven millimeters thick, were seen to withstand a pressure of sixteen atmospheres, all the while letting through powerful heat rays that spread the warmth unevenly across them. Now, the panes I use are no less than twenty-one centimeters thick at their center — thirty times that thickness."

"Granted, Captain Nemo — but after all, to see, you need light to drive back the darkness, and I wonder how, in the blackness of the waters —"

"Behind the helmsman's cage is mounted a powerful electric reflector, whose beams light up the sea for half a mile around."

"Ah, bravo — three times bravo, Captain! Now I understand that phosphorescence of the so-called narwhal that so puzzled the scientists! And on that subject, let me ask you: was the collision of the Nautilus with the Scotia, which caused such a stir, the result of a chance encounter?"

"Purely by chance, Professor. I was cruising two meters below the surface when the shock occurred. I saw, moreover, that it had done no serious harm."

"None, sir. But as for your encounter with the Abraham Lincoln?"

"Professor, I'm sorry for one of the finest ships of that gallant American navy — but I was under attack, and I had to defend myself! I contented myself, though, with putting the frigate out of any condition to harm me; she'll have no trouble repairing her damage at the nearest port."

"Ah, Captain," I exclaimed with conviction, "your Nautilus really is a marvelous vessel!"

"Yes, Professor," Captain Nemo answered with genuine emotion, "and I love it as the flesh of my flesh! If aboard one of your ships, exposed to all the hazards of the ocean, everything is danger — if on that sea the first impression is the sense of the abyss, as the Dutchman Jansen so aptly put it — then aboard the Nautilus, and beneath its waters, a man's heart has nothing left to fear. No warping to dread, for the double hull of this vessel has the rigidity of iron; no rigging to be worn out by pitch and roll; no sails for the wind to carry off; no boilers for the steam to burst; no fire to fear, since this craft is made of sheet metal and not of wood; no coal to run out of, since electricity is its driving power; no collision to fear, since it sails alone in the deep waters; no storm to brave, since a few meters below the surface it finds absolute calm! There, Professor. There is the ship of ships! And if it's true that the engineer has more confidence in a vessel than the builder, and the builder more than the captain himself, then you can understand with what abandon I trust myself to my Nautilus — since I am at once its captain, its builder, and its engineer!"

Captain Nemo spoke with a compelling eloquence. The fire in his eyes, the passion in his gestures, transfigured him. Yes! He loved his ship the way a father loves his child.

But a question — an indiscreet one, perhaps — arose naturally, and I couldn't keep myself from putting it to him.

"You're an engineer, then, Captain Nemo?"

"Yes, Professor," he answered. "I studied in London, in Paris, in New York, back when I was a dweller on the continents of the earth."

"But how were you able to build this admirable Nautilus in secret?"

"Each of its pieces, Professor Aronnax, reached me from a different point on the globe, under a disguised destination. Its keel was forged at Le Creusot, its propeller shaft at Pen & Co. of London, the sheet-iron plates of its hull at Leard's of Liverpool, its propeller at Scott's of Glasgow. Its tanks were made by Cail & Co. of Paris, its engine by Krupp in Prussia, its ram in the workshops of Motala in Sweden, its precision instruments at Hart Brothers of New York, and so on — and each of these suppliers received my plans under a different name."

"But," I went on, "once these pieces were made, they had to be assembled, fitted together?"

"Professor, I'd set up my workshops on a deserted islet, in the open ocean. There, my workmen — that is, my brave companions, whom I've trained and formed — and I finished our Nautilus. Then, once the work was done, fire destroyed every trace of our passage on that islet, which I'd have blown up if I could have."

"Then I may assume the cost of building this craft was enormous?"

"Professor Aronnax, an iron ship costs eleven hundred and twenty-five francs per ton. Now, the Nautilus gauges fifteen hundred. So it came to 1,687,000 francs — two million counting its fittings, or four or five million with the works of art and the collections it holds."

"One last question, Captain Nemo."

"Go ahead, Professor."

"You're rich, then?"

"Infinitely rich, sir — I could pay off France's ten billion of debt without the slightest inconvenience!"

I stared hard at the strange man who spoke to me this way. Was he trading on my credulity? The future would tell.
