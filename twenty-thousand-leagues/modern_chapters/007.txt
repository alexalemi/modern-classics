Mobilis in Mobili

The abduction had been so brutally carried out, and with such lightning speed, that neither my companions nor I had had time to collect ourselves. I don't know what they felt as they were shoved into this floating prison, but for my own part a quick shudder ran cold across my skin. Who were we dealing with? Some new breed of pirate, no doubt, plundering the sea in their own fashion.

The narrow hatch had scarcely closed above me when a deep darkness swallowed me whole. My eyes, still soaked with the light outside, could make out nothing. I felt my bare feet grip the rungs of an iron ladder. Ned Land and Conseil, seized just as roughly, came down after me. At the foot of the ladder a door swung open, then shut again immediately behind us with a resounding clang.

We were alone. Where? I couldn't have said, could scarcely even imagine. Everything was black — black so utter that, even after a few minutes, my eyes had not yet caught one of those vague glimmers that drift through the deepest of nights.

Meanwhile Ned Land, furious at being handled this way, gave free rein to his indignation.

"A thousand devils!" he burst out. "These people could give the Caledonians lessons in hospitality! All that's left is for them to turn out to be cannibals. I wouldn't be surprised if they did — but I'm telling you now, no one eats me without hearing me object!"

"Easy, Ned, easy," Conseil answered calmly. "Don't work yourself up before the hour. We're not in the roasting pan just yet."

"In the roasting pan, no," the Canadian shot back, "but in the oven for certain. It's dark enough. Lucky for me my bowie knife hasn't left my side, and I can still see well enough to use it. The first of these bandits who lays a hand on me—"

"Don't get riled, Ned," I said to the harpooner, "and don't compromise us with pointless violence. For all we know, they're listening. Let's try instead to find out where we are."

I felt my way forward. After five steps I came up against an iron wall of bolted plates. Turning back, I bumped into a wooden table, and set around it were several stools. The floor of this prison was hidden beneath a thick mat of phormium that muffled the sound of footsteps. The bare walls betrayed no trace of a door or a window. Conseil, making the same tour in the opposite direction, met up with me, and we came back to the middle of this cabin, which must have measured twenty feet long by ten feet wide. As for its height, Ned Land, tall as he was, couldn't reach it.

A full half hour had already crept by with no change in our situation when our eyes passed abruptly from utter darkness to the most violent light. Our prison lit up all at once — that is, it filled with a luminous substance so intense that at first I couldn't bear its brilliance. By its whiteness, by its intensity, I recognized that electric glow which produced around the submarine boat a kind of magnificent phosphorescence. After shutting my eyes involuntarily, I opened them again and saw that the light streamed from a frosted half-globe curving out of the ceiling of the cabin.

"At last! Now we can see!" cried Ned Land, who stood on the defensive, knife in hand.

"Yes," I answered, risking the play on words, "but our situation is as dark as ever."

"Let Master be patient," said the imperturbable Conseil.

The cabin's sudden lighting had let me examine it down to the smallest detail. It held nothing but the table and the five stools. The invisible door must have been hermetically sealed. Not a sound reached our ears. Everything seemed dead inside this boat. Was it moving? Holding at the surface of the ocean? Sinking into the depths? I couldn't guess.

Still, the luminous globe hadn't been lit for nothing, so I hoped the crew would not be long in showing themselves. When you mean to forget people, you don't light up their dungeon.

I was not mistaken. The rattle of a bolt was heard, the door opened, and two men appeared.

One was short, powerfully muscled, broad-shouldered, sturdy of limb, with a strong head, a thick mane of black hair, a heavy mustache, and a quick, penetrating gaze — his whole person stamped with that southern liveliness that in France marks the people of Provence. Diderot claimed, and rightly, that a man's gestures are metaphorical, and this little man was surely living proof of it. You sensed that in his ordinary speech he must lavish personifications, metonymies, and hypallages. Not that I was ever in a position to confirm it, for in my presence he always used a peculiar and utterly incomprehensible tongue.

The second stranger deserves a more detailed description. A disciple of Gratiolet or Engel could have read his face like an open book. I recognized his dominant qualities without hesitation. Self-confidence — for his head rose nobly on the arch formed by the line of his shoulders, and his black eyes looked out with cold assurance. Composure — for his skin, pale rather than ruddy, betokened calmness of blood. Energy — shown by the swift contraction of his brow muscles. And courage, in the end — for his deep breathing told of a great vital force.

I will add that this man was proud, that his firm, calm gaze seemed to reflect lofty thoughts, and that from the whole of him — from the harmony of expression in the movements of his body and face — there resulted, by the physiognomists' reckoning, an unquestionable frankness.

I felt myself "involuntarily" reassured in his presence, and I took it as a good omen for our interview.

Whether this man was thirty-five or fifty, I couldn't have said. He was tall, with a broad forehead, a straight nose, a clearly drawn mouth, magnificent teeth, and fine, elongated hands — eminently "psychic," to borrow a term from chirognomy, that is, worthy of serving a high and passionate soul. This man was certainly the most admirable specimen I had ever met. One particular detail: his eyes, set a little wide apart, could take in nearly a quarter of the horizon at once. This faculty — I verified it later — was matched by a power of vision even keener than Ned Land's. When this stranger fixed his gaze on an object, the line of his brows knit together, his broad eyelids drew close so as to hem in the pupils and narrow the field of sight, and he looked! What a look! How it magnified objects shrunk by distance! How it pierced you to the soul! How it saw through those sheets of water so opaque to our eyes, and read into the deepest reaches of the sea!

The two strangers wore berets made of sea-otter fur and sealskin sea boots, and their clothes, of a peculiar fabric, hung loose at the waist and left great freedom of movement.

The taller of the two — clearly the master of the ship — examined us with the closest attention, without saying a word. Then, turning to his companion, he conversed with him in a language I couldn't place. It was a sonorous, harmonious, supple tongue, whose vowels seemed to carry a widely varied accent.

The other replied with a nod of the head and added two or three perfectly incomprehensible words. Then his eyes seemed to question me directly.

I answered, in good French, that I did not understand his language. But he seemed not to understand mine either, and the situation grew rather awkward.

"Let Master tell our story anyway," said Conseil. "Perhaps these gentlemen will catch a word or two."

I began again the tale of our adventures, pronouncing each syllable clearly and leaving out no detail. I gave our names and stations; then I formally introduced Professor Aronnax, his servant Conseil, and Ned Land, master harpooner.

The man with the gentle, calm eyes listened to me quietly, politely even, with remarkable attention. But nothing in his face showed that he had understood my story. When I had finished, he did not speak a single word.

There remained the resource of speaking English. Perhaps we could make ourselves understood in that nearly universal tongue. I knew it, along with German, well enough to read fluently, though not to speak correctly. And here, above all, we needed to make ourselves understood.

"Come, your turn," I said to the harpooner. "It's up to you, Master Land — pull out of your bag the finest English an Anglo-Saxon ever spoke, and try for better luck than I had."

Ned did not need to be asked twice, and he began my story over in a version I could follow well enough. The substance was the same, but the form differed. Carried away by his temper, the Canadian threw a good deal of heat into it. He complained violently of being imprisoned in defiance of all human rights, demanded to know by what law they held him this way, invoked habeas corpus, threatened to prosecute those who were confining him unlawfully, thrashed about, gesticulated, shouted, and finally made clear by an expressive gesture that we were dying of hunger.

Which was perfectly true, though we had all but forgotten it.

To his great astonishment, the harpooner seemed to have been no more intelligible than I. Our visitors didn't so much as blink. It was plain they understood neither the language of Arago nor that of Faraday.

Thoroughly at a loss, having vainly exhausted our philological resources, I no longer knew what course to take, when Conseil said to me:

"If Master will permit, I'll tell the whole thing in German."

"What! You know German?" I exclaimed.

"Like a Fleming, if it won't displease Master."

"On the contrary, it pleases me very much. Go ahead, my boy."

And Conseil, in his tranquil voice, recounted for the third time the various turns of our story. But despite the narrator's elegant phrasing and fine accent, German met with no more success.

At last, driven to the wall, I gathered up everything left to me from my early studies and undertook to tell our adventures in Latin. Cicero would have stopped his ears and packed me off to the kitchen, but I managed to get through it all the same. Same negative result.

This last attempt having definitively failed, the two strangers exchanged a few words in their incomprehensible language and withdrew, without so much as offering us one of those reassuring gestures that pass current in every country on earth. The door closed again.

"It's an outrage!" cried Ned Land, exploding for the twentieth time. "What! We speak to these rascals in French, English, German, Latin, and not one of them has the courtesy to answer!"

"Easy, Ned," I said to the seething harpooner. "Anger won't get us anywhere."

"But do you realize, Professor," our irascible companion went on, "that a man could starve to death outright in this iron cage?"

"Bah," said Conseil philosophically, "we can hold out a good while yet."

"My friends," I said, "we mustn't despair. We've been in worse spots than this. Do me the favor of waiting before you form an opinion of this boat's commander and crew."

"My opinion's already made," Ned Land retorted. "They're rascals—"

"Fine. And from what country?"

"The country of rascals!"

"My good Ned, that country isn't yet marked clearly enough on the map, and I admit the nationality of these two strangers is hard to pin down. Not English, not French, not German — that's all one can say for certain. Still, I'd be tempted to grant that this commander and his second officer were born in low latitudes. There's something southern about them. But whether they're Spanish, Turkish, Arab, or Indian, their physical type won't let me decide. As for their language, it's absolutely incomprehensible."

"That's the trouble with not knowing every language," Conseil answered, "or the drawback of not having a single universal one!"

"Which would do no good at all!" Ned Land replied. "Don't you see these people have a language of their own, a language invented to drive to despair honest men who are asking for their dinner? But in every country on earth, opening your mouth, working your jaws, snapping your teeth and lips — isn't that plain enough on its own? Doesn't it mean the same in Quebec as in the Pomotous, in Paris as at the antipodes: I'm hungry! Give me something to eat!"

"Oh," said Conseil, "there are some natures so unintelligent!"

As he spoke those words, the door opened. A steward came in. He was bringing us clothes — jackets and sea trousers of a fabric whose nature I couldn't identify. I hurried to put them on, and my companions did the same.

Meanwhile the steward — mute, perhaps deaf — had set the table and laid three places.

"Now that's something serious," said Conseil, "and it looks promising."

"Bah," answered the resentful harpooner, "what the devil do you expect to eat here? Turtle liver, shark fillet, dogfish steak!"

"We'll soon see!" said Conseil.

The dishes, covered with their silver domes, were set out symmetrically on the cloth, and we took our places at the table. Decidedly, we were dealing with civilized people, and but for the electric light flooding over us, I could have believed myself in the dining room of the Adelphi Hotel in Liverpool, or the Grand Hotel in Paris. I must say, though, that bread and wine were entirely absent. The water was fresh and clear, but it was water — which was not to Ned Land's taste. Among the dishes served to us I recognized various fish delicately prepared; but of certain others, excellent though they were, I couldn't venture an opinion, and I couldn't even have said to which kingdom, vegetable or animal, their contents belonged. As for the table service, it was elegant and in perfect taste. Each utensil — spoon, fork, knife, plate — bore a letter encircled by a motto in exergue, and here is the exact facsimile:

Mobilis in Mobili — moving within the moving element! The motto applied precisely to this underwater vessel, provided you translated the preposition in as "within" and not "upon." The letter N was doubtless the initial of the name of that enigmatic figure who reigned in command at the bottom of the seas!

Ned and Conseil weren't given to so much reflection. They were devouring their meal, and I was not slow to follow their example. I was reassured, moreover, about our fate, and it seemed clear to me that our hosts did not mean to let us die of starvation.

But everything comes to an end here below, everything passes — even the hunger of men who haven't eaten in fifteen hours. Our appetites satisfied, the need for sleep made itself imperiously felt. A natural enough reaction after the endless night through which we had struggled against death.

"My word, I could sleep well," said Conseil.

"And I'm asleep already!" answered Ned Land.

My two companions stretched out on the cabin's mat and were soon plunged into a deep sleep.

For my part, I gave in less easily to that violent need for sleep. Too many thoughts crowded my mind, too many unanswerable questions pressed upon me, too many images held my eyelids half open. Where were we? What strange power was carrying us off? I felt — or rather thought I felt — the vessel sinking toward the sea's most remote layers. Violent nightmares beset me. In those mysterious refuges I glimpsed a whole world of unknown animals, and this submarine boat seemed one of their own kind — alive, moving, formidable like them! Then my brain quieted, my imagination melted into a vague drowsiness, and I soon fell into a heavy, dismal sleep.
