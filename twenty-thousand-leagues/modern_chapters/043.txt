The Giant Squid

For several days the Nautilus kept steadily away from the American coast. Clearly it had no wish to venture into the waters of the Gulf of Mexico or the Caribbean Sea. There would have been no shortage of water beneath its keel — the average depth of those seas is eighteen hundred meters — but no doubt those waters, strewn with islands and furrowed by steamers, did not suit Captain Nemo.

On April 16 we raised Martinique and Guadeloupe, at a distance of about thirty miles. For a moment I caught sight of their high peaks.

The Canadian, who had counted on carrying out his plans somewhere in the Gulf — either by making land or by hailing one of the many boats that ply their coasting trade from island to island — was thoroughly put out. Escape would have been quite feasible if Ned Land could have gotten hold of the dinghy without the captain's knowledge. But out on the open ocean, it was no longer to be thought of.

The Canadian, Conseil, and I had a fairly long conversation on the subject. For six months now we had been prisoners aboard the Nautilus. We had covered seventeen thousand leagues, and, as Ned Land pointed out, there was no reason why it should ever end. So he made me a proposal I hadn't expected: to put the question to Captain Nemo point-blank. Did the captain mean to keep us aboard his vessel indefinitely?

Such a move went against my grain. As I saw it, it could lead nowhere. There was nothing to hope for from the commander of the Nautilus — everything, on the contrary, depended on ourselves alone. Besides, for some time now the man had been growing darker, more withdrawn, less sociable. He seemed to be avoiding me. I met him only at rare intervals. Once, he had taken pleasure in explaining the wonders of the deep to me; now he left me to my studies and no longer came to the salon.

What change had come over him? For what reason? I had nothing to reproach myself with. Perhaps our presence aboard weighed on him? Even so, I could hardly hope that he was the kind of man to give us back our freedom.

So I asked Ned to let me think the matter over before we acted. If the move produced no result, it might only revive Nemo's suspicions, make our situation harder, and jeopardize the Canadian's plans. I might add that there was no way I could plead ill health as an argument. Apart from the harsh ordeal of the ice barrier at the South Pole, none of us had ever been better — not Ned, not Conseil, not I. This wholesome food, this healthy air, this regularity of life, this uniformity of temperature, gave sickness no opening; and for a man to whom memories of the land brought no regret, for a Captain Nemo, who is at home wherever he goes, who goes where he pleases, who marches toward his goal by ways mysterious to others though not to himself — such an existence I could understand. But we had not broken with humanity. For my own part, I had no wish to bury my strange and wholly new studies along with myself. I had now earned the right to write the true book of the sea, and that book I wanted to see the light of day sooner rather than later.

There too, in those waters of the Antilles, ten meters below the surface, through the open panels, what a wealth of interesting specimens I had to enter in my daily notes! Among the zoophytes there were siphonophores known as the pelagic Physalia — great oblong bladders with a pearly sheen, spreading their membranes to the wind and letting their blue tentacles trail like threads of silk: charming jellyfish to the eye, true nettles to the touch, distilling a corrosive fluid. Among the articulates there were annelids a meter and a half long, armed with a pink proboscis and equipped with seventeen hundred organs of locomotion; they wound their way beneath the waters, casting off, as they passed, every color of the solar spectrum. In the branch of fishes there were molubar rays — enormous cartilaginous creatures ten feet long and weighing six hundred pounds, with a triangular pectoral fin, a slightly humped back, and eyes set at the outer edges of the front of the head; they floated like the wreckage of a ship and now and then clapped themselves against our window like an opaque shutter. There were American triggerfish, for which nature has ground up nothing but black and white; feathery gobies, long and fleshy, with yellow fins and a jutting jaw; mackerel of sixteen decimeters, with short sharp teeth and small scales, belonging to the albacore species. Then, in swarms, came surmullets girdled with stripes of gold from head to tail, waving their resplendent fins — true masterpieces of the jeweler's art, once dedicated to Diana, especially prized by wealthy Romans, and of which the old proverb ran: "Let him who catches them not eat them!" Finally, golden angelfish, adorned with emerald bands, dressed in velvet and silk, passed before our eyes like lords stepped out of a Veronese painting; spurred sea bream slipped away on the swift stroke of their thoracic fins; fifteen-inch clupanodons wrapped themselves in their phosphorescent glow; gray mullet beat the sea with their thick fleshy tails; red whitefish seemed to mow the waves with their knife-edged pectorals; and silvery moonfish, worthy of their name, rose over the watery horizon like so many pale-shining moons.

How many more marvelous new specimens I might have observed, had the Nautilus not gradually sunk toward the lower strata! Its inclined planes carried it down to depths of two thousand and three thousand five hundred meters. There animal life was represented only by sea lilies, starfish, lovely Medusa's-head feather stars whose straight stalks bore a little calyx, top shells, bloody-toothed shells, and keyhole limpets — littoral mollusks of the larger species.

On April 20 we had risen again to a mean depth of fifteen hundred meters. The nearest land was then the archipelago of the Lucayan Islands, scattered like a heap of paving stones over the surface of the waters. There rose high submarine cliffs, sheer walls built of rough-hewn blocks laid in broad courses, between which opened black holes that our electric beams could not light to the bottom.

These rocks were carpeted with tall grasses, giant kelp, gigantic wrack — a veritable espalier of water plants fit for a world of Titans.

From these colossal plants, Conseil, Ned, and I naturally fell to speaking of the sea's gigantic animals. The one is plainly meant to feed the other. Yet through the windows of the nearly motionless Nautilus, I could still make out on those long filaments nothing but the chief articulates of the brachyuran order — long-legged spider crabs, violet crabs, and clios peculiar to the Caribbean waters.

It was about eleven o'clock when Ned Land drew my attention to a tremendous swarming going on through the great weeds.

"Well," I said, "these are real devilfish caverns, and I wouldn't be surprised to see a few of those monsters here."

"What!" said Conseil. "Squid — plain ordinary squid — of the class of cephalopods?"

"No," I said, "devilfish of great size. But friend Land is surely mistaken, for I don't see anything."

"A pity," Conseil replied. "I'd like to meet one of those devilfish face to face — the ones I've heard so much about, that can drag ships down into the depths. Those beasts are called krak—"

"A crock is more like it," the Canadian cut in dryly.

"—Krakens," Conseil finished, not troubling himself over his companion's little joke.

"You'll never make me believe," said Ned Land, "that such animals exist."

"Why not?" answered Conseil. "We believed in Master's narwhal, after all."

"We were wrong, Conseil."

"No doubt! But others still believe in it, I'm sure."

"Very likely, Conseil. But for my part, I'm quite determined not to admit the existence of such monsters until I've dissected one with my own hands."

"So," Conseil asked me, "Master doesn't believe in giant devilfish?"

"Who the devil ever did believe in them?" cried the Canadian.

"A great many people, friend Ned."

"Not fishermen. Scholars, maybe!"

"Beg your pardon, Ned. Fishermen and scholars both!"

"But I who am speaking to you now," said Conseil, with the most serious air in the world, "remember perfectly well seeing a large boat dragged under the waves by the arms of a cephalopod."

"You saw that?" the Canadian demanded.

"Yes, Ned."

"With your own eyes?"

"With my own eyes."

"Where, if you please?"

"At Saint-Malo," Conseil answered, unruffled.

"In the harbor?" said Ned Land, mocking.

"No, in a church," Conseil replied.

"In a church!" cried the Canadian.

"Yes, friend Ned. It was a painting — a painting of the devilfish in question!"

"Good!" said Ned Land, bursting out laughing. "Monsieur Conseil, pulling my leg!"

"As a matter of fact, he's right," I said. "I've heard of that painting; but the subject it shows is drawn from a legend, and you know what to make of legends where natural history is concerned! Besides, when it comes to monsters, the imagination asks nothing better than to run wild.

"Not only has it been claimed that these devilfish could drag ships under, but a certain Olaus Magnus tells of a cephalopod a mile long that looked more like an island than an animal. There's also the tale of the bishop of Nidros, who one day set up an altar on an immense rock. When his Mass was over, the rock got under way and returned to the sea. The rock was a devilfish."

"And that's all?" asked the Canadian.

"No," I replied. "Another bishop, Pontoppidan of Bergen, likewise speaks of a devilfish on which a whole regiment of cavalry could maneuver!"

"They knew how to lay it on, those bishops of the old days!" said Ned Land.

"And finally, the naturalists of antiquity cite monsters whose maws were like a gulf, and which were too big to pass through the Strait of Gibraltar."

"Now that's more like it!" said the Canadian.

"But in all these stories," Conseil asked, "what is there that's true?"

"Nothing, my friends — nothing, at least, beyond the bounds of the plausible that mounts into fable or legend. Still, the imagination of storytellers needs, if not a cause, at least a pretext. There's no denying that devilfish and squid of very large species exist, though smaller nonetheless than the cetaceans. Aristotle recorded the dimensions of a squid five cubits long — that is, three meters ten. Our fishermen frequently see specimens more than a meter eighty in length. The museums of Trieste and Montpellier preserve skeletons of devilfish that measure two meters. Moreover, by the naturalists' reckoning, one of these animals only six feet long would have tentacles twenty-seven feet long. Which is quite enough to make a formidable monster."

"Are any caught nowadays?" asked the Canadian.

"If fishermen don't catch them, sailors at least see them. A friend of mine, Captain Paul Bos of Le Havre, has often assured me that he met one of these monsters, of colossal size, in the seas of India. But the most astonishing fact of all, the one that no longer allows the existence of these gigantic animals to be denied, took place only a few years ago, in 1861."

"What fact is that?" asked Ned Land.

"Here it is. In 1861, to the northeast of Tenerife — at just about the latitude where we are at this moment — the crew of the dispatch boat Alecton sighted a monstrous squid swimming in its waters. Commander Bouguer drew close to the animal and attacked it with harpoons and gunfire, without much success, for balls and harpoons passed through that soft flesh as through a jelly with no substance to it. After several fruitless attempts, the crew managed to slip a running knot around the mollusk's body. The knot slid down as far as the tail fins and stopped there. They then tried to haul the monster aboard, but its weight was so considerable that it tore away from its own tail under the pull of the rope, and, stripped of that ornament, it vanished beneath the waters."

"There, at last, is a fact," said Ned Land.

"An indisputable fact, my good Ned. And so it was proposed to name this devilfish 'Bouguer's squid.'"

"And how long was it?" the Canadian asked.

"Wasn't it about six meters?" said Conseil, who, posted at the window, was once more examining the crannies of the cliff.

"Precisely," I answered.

"Wasn't its head," Conseil went on, "crowned with eight tentacles that thrashed on the water like a nest of writhing serpents?"

"Precisely."

"Weren't its eyes, set flush with the head, of considerable size?"

"Yes, Conseil."

"And its mouth — wasn't it a real parrot's beak, but a formidable one?"

"Just so, Conseil."

"Well then! No offense to Master," Conseil replied calmly, "but if this isn't Bouguer's squid, here at least is one of its brothers."

I looked at Conseil. Ned Land rushed to the window.

"The frightful beast!" he cried.

I looked in my turn, and I couldn't hold back a shudder of revulsion. Before my eyes writhed a horrible monster, worthy of a place in the legends of the monster-mongers.

It was a squid of colossal dimensions, eight meters long. It was traveling backward with tremendous velocity, straight toward the Nautilus. It stared with its huge fixed eyes of a glaucous green. Its eight arms — or rather its eight feet, rooted in its head, which is what has earned these animals the name cephalopod, "head-foot" — were twice the length of its body and writhed like the hair of the Furies. One could see distinctly the two hundred and fifty suckers arranged along the inner face of the tentacles in the form of half-spherical capsules. From time to time these suckers fastened onto the salon window, drawing a vacuum against the glass. The monster's mouth — a horny beak shaped like a parrot's — opened and closed vertically. Its tongue, a horny substance itself armed with several rows of sharp teeth, came quivering out of that veritable pair of shears. What a whim of nature! A bird's beak on a mollusk! Its body, spindle-shaped and swollen in the middle, formed a fleshy mass that must have weighed twenty to twenty-five thousand kilograms. Its inconstant color, changing with extreme speed according to the creature's irritation, passed in turn from a livid gray to a reddish brown.

What was irritating this mollusk? No doubt the presence of the Nautilus, more formidable than itself, and on which its sucking arms and its mandibles could get no purchase. And yet — what monsters these devilfish are, what vitality the Creator has bestowed on them, what vigor in their movements, given that they possess three hearts!

Chance had brought us face to face with this squid, and I did not mean to let slip the chance to study this specimen of the cephalopods with care. I mastered the horror its appearance stirred in me and, taking up a pencil, I began to draw it.

"Perhaps it's the very same one the Alecton met," said Conseil.

"No," answered the Canadian, "since this one is whole and the other lost its tail!"

"That's no reason," I replied. "The arms and tail of these animals grow back by regeneration, and in seven years the tail of Bouguer's squid has surely had time to sprout again."

"Besides," Ned retorted, "if it isn't this one, maybe it's one of those!"

And in fact, other devilfish were appearing at the starboard window. I counted seven. They formed an escort around the Nautilus, and I heard the grinding of their beaks on the iron hull. We were served to our heart's content.

I went on with my work. These monsters held themselves in our waters with such precision that they seemed motionless, and I could have traced them foreshortened on the glass. In any case, we were moving at a moderate pace.

Suddenly the Nautilus stopped. A shock ran shuddering through its whole frame.

"Have we struck something?" I asked.

"In any case," the Canadian answered, "we'd already be free of it, since we're afloat."

The Nautilus was afloat, no doubt, but it was no longer moving. The blades of its propeller weren't beating the waves. A minute passed. Captain Nemo, followed by his first officer, came into the salon.

I hadn't seen him for some time. He seemed to me somber. Without speaking to us, without perhaps even seeing us, he went to the panel, looked at the devilfish, and said a few words to his officer.

The officer went out. Soon the panels closed again. The ceiling lit up.

I went over to the captain.

"A curious collection of devilfish," I said to him, in the offhand tone a hobbyist might take before the glass of an aquarium.

"Indeed, Professor," he answered me, "and we're going to fight them hand to hand."

I stared at the captain. I thought I hadn't heard him right.

"Hand to hand?" I repeated.

"Yes, Professor. The propeller has stopped. I believe the horny mandibles of one of these squid have gotten caught in the blades. That's what's keeping us from moving."

"And what do you mean to do?"

"Rise to the surface and slaughter every last one of this vermin."

"A difficult undertaking."

"So it is. The electric bullets are powerless against that soft flesh, where they don't meet enough resistance to burst. But we'll attack them with axes."

"And with the harpoon, Captain," said the Canadian, "if you won't refuse my help."

"I accept it, Master Land."

"We'll go with you," I said, and, following Captain Nemo, we made our way toward the central staircase.

There a dozen men, armed with boarding axes, stood ready for the attack. Conseil and I took up two axes. Ned Land seized a harpoon.

By now the Nautilus had returned to the surface. One of the sailors, posted on the topmost rungs, was unscrewing the bolts of the panel. But hardly were the nuts loosened when the panel flew up with tremendous violence, plainly wrenched open by the sucker of a devilfish's arm.

At once one of those long arms slid like a serpent through the opening, and twenty more thrashed above it. With one stroke of his axe, Captain Nemo cut off the formidable tentacle, which slid writhing down the rungs.

Just as we were pressing against one another to reach the platform, two other arms, lashing the air, came down on the sailor standing in front of Captain Nemo and lifted him away with irresistible force.

Captain Nemo let out a cry and hurled himself outside. We rushed after him.

What a scene! The wretched man, seized by the tentacle and glued to its suckers, was being swung through the air at the whim of that enormous trunk. He gasped, he choked, he screamed: "Help! Help me!" Those words, spoken in French, filled me with profound shock! So I had a countryman aboard — several, perhaps! That heart-rending cry I will hear for the rest of my life!

The unfortunate man was lost. Who could tear him from that powerful grip? Yet Captain Nemo had flung himself on the devilfish and, with one blow of his axe, had struck off another of its arms. His first officer struggled furiously against other monsters crawling up the flanks of the Nautilus. The crew fought with their axes. The Canadian, Conseil, and I drove our weapons into those fleshy masses. A violent smell of musk filled the air. It was horrible.

For an instant I thought the poor man, wound in the devilfish's coils, might be torn free of its powerful suction. Seven of the eight arms had been cut away. A single one, brandishing its victim like a feather, writhed in the air. But just as Captain Nemo and his officer flung themselves upon it, the animal shot out a column of blackish liquid, secreted from a pouch in its abdomen. We were blinded by it. When the cloud had cleared, the squid had vanished — and with it my luckless countryman!

What a rage then drove us against those monsters! We were beside ourselves. Ten or twelve devilfish had overrun the platform and the flanks of the Nautilus. We rolled pell-mell among those writhing serpent-stumps that leaped and jerked across the platform in floods of blood and black ink. It seemed as though those slimy tentacles grew back like the heads of the Hydra. At every stroke Ned Land's harpoon plunged into the glaucous eyes of the squid and burst them. But my daring companion was suddenly bowled over by the tentacles of a monster he hadn't been able to dodge.

Ah! how my heart did not break with terror and horror, I cannot say! The formidable beak of the squid had gaped open over Ned Land. The poor man was about to be cut in two. I threw myself to his rescue. But Captain Nemo had beaten me to it. His axe disappeared between the two enormous mandibles, and, miraculously saved, the Canadian rose to his feet and drove his harpoon clear through to the triple heart of the devilfish.

"I owed myself this revenge!" said Captain Nemo to the Canadian.

Ned bowed without answering him.

The battle had lasted a quarter of an hour. The monsters, beaten, mutilated, struck to death, at last gave way and vanished beneath the waves.

Captain Nemo, red with blood, motionless beside the beacon, gazed at the sea that had swallowed one of his companions, and great tears ran from his eyes.
