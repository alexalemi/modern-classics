The Ice Barrier

The Nautilus had resumed its unwavering course toward the south. It ran down the fiftieth meridian at a considerable speed. Did it mean, then, to reach the Pole? I didn't think so, for up to now every attempt to climb to that point of the globe had failed. And the season, besides, was already far advanced, since March 13 in the antarctic lands answers to September 13 in the northern regions — the start of the equinoctial period.

On March 14 I spotted floating ice at 55° latitude, mere pallid fragments twenty to twenty-five feet across, forming reefs on which the sea broke. The Nautilus was holding to the surface of the ocean. Ned Land, who had already fished in the arctic seas, was familiar with this spectacle of icebergs. Conseil and I were admiring it for the first time.

In the sky, off toward the southern horizon, there stretched a white band of dazzling brilliance. English whalers have given it the name "ice-blink." However thick the clouds may be, they cannot dim it. It announces the presence of a pack, or bank, of ice.

And in fact, larger blocks soon appeared, their gleam shifting with the whims of the mist. Some of these masses showed veins of green, as though copper sulfate had traced their rippling lines. Others, like enormous amethysts, let the light pass right through them. These threw back the rays of day off the thousand facets of their crystals. Those, tinted with the vivid glints of limestone, would have furnished enough stone to build a whole city of marble.

The farther south we went, the more these floating islands grew in number and in size. Polar birds nested there by the thousands. They were petrels, cape pigeons, shearwaters, and they deafened us with their cries. Some of them, taking the Nautilus for the carcass of a whale, came to settle on it and jabbed at its ringing plates with their beaks.

Through all this navigation among the ice, Captain Nemo often stayed out on the platform. He studied these forsaken waters closely. Now and then I saw his calm gaze come alive. Was he telling himself that here, in these polar seas closed to man, he was in his own domain, master of these impassable spaces? Perhaps. But he did not speak. He stood motionless, coming back to himself only when his instincts as a ship-handler took over again. Steering his Nautilus then with a consummate skill, he neatly dodged the shock of these masses, some of which measured several miles in length by a height that ran from seventy to eighty meters. Often the horizon seemed shut off entirely. At the level of the sixtieth degree of latitude, every passage had vanished. But Captain Nemo, searching carefully, soon found some narrow opening through which he slipped boldly, knowing perfectly well, all the same, that it would close again behind him.

And so it was that the Nautilus, guided by that skilled hand, passed all this ice, which Conseil classified — by shape or by size — with a precision that delighted him: icebergs, or mountains of ice; ice-fields, level and boundless expanses; drift-ice, or floating ice; packs, or broken fields, called palchs when they are circular and streams when they are made of elongated pieces.

The temperature was quite low. The thermometer, exposed to the outside air, read two to three degrees below zero. But we were warmly dressed in furs, paid for by the seals and the sea bears. The interior of the Nautilus, evenly heated by its electric apparatus, defied the most intense cold. Besides, it need only have sunk a few meters below the waves to find a bearable temperature there.

Two months earlier, we would have enjoyed perpetual daylight in this latitude; but already the night was falling for three or four hours, and later it would cast six months of shadow over these circumpolar regions.

On March 15 we passed the latitude of the New Shetland Islands and the South Orkneys. The captain told me that in the old days numerous tribes of seals had inhabited these lands; but the English and American whalers, in their rage for destruction, slaughtering the adults and the pregnant females wherever life stirred, had left the silence of death behind them.

On March 16, toward eight in the morning, the Nautilus, following the fifty-fifth meridian, crossed the antarctic polar circle. The ice hemmed us in on every side and closed off the horizon. Yet Captain Nemo went from passage to passage, still climbing.

"But where is he going?" I asked.

"Straight ahead," Conseil answered. "After all, when he can't go any farther, he'll stop."

"I wouldn't swear to it!" I replied.

And to be honest, I'll confess that this adventurous excursion was not at all displeasing to me. Words fail me to say how the beauties of these new regions astonished me. The ice took on superb attitudes. Here its whole mass formed an eastern city, with its minarets and its countless mosques. There, a fallen city, thrown flat as if by a convulsion of the earth. Scenes forever changing under the slanting rays of the sun, or lost in the gray mists amid hurricanes of snow. Then, on all sides, detonations, avalanches, great tumbling of icebergs that shifted the scenery like the landscape of a diorama.

When the Nautilus was submerged at the moment these balances broke, the noise carried under the water with a frightful intensity, and the fall of these masses set up formidable eddies down into the deep layers of the ocean. The Nautilus would then roll and pitch like a ship abandoned to the fury of the elements.

Often, seeing no way out anymore, I thought we were prisoners for good; but with instinct to guide him, on the slightest hint Captain Nemo would discover fresh passages. He was never wrong in reading the thin threads of bluish water that streaked the ice-fields. And so I had no doubt that he had already ventured the Nautilus into the midst of the antarctic seas before.

Yet in the course of that day, March 16, the ice-fields barred our way absolutely. This was not the ice barrier yet, but vast ice-fields cemented together by the cold. This obstacle could not stop Captain Nemo, and he flung himself against the ice-field with a terrible violence. The Nautilus drove in like a wedge into that brittle mass and split it apart with dreadful cracking. It was the ancient battering ram, driven by an infinite power. The shattered ice, hurled high, fell back in hail all around us. By its momentum alone our craft cut itself a channel. Sometimes, carried away by its own drive, it would ride up onto the ice-field and crush it under its weight, or now and then, wedged in beneath the ice-field, it would break the sheet apart with a simple pitching motion that tore long rifts through it.

During these days violent squalls assailed us. In certain thick fogs you could not have seen from one end of the platform to the other. The wind shifted abruptly to every point of the compass. The snow piled up in layers so hard we had to break it with pickaxes. At a temperature of a mere five degrees below zero, every outside part of the Nautilus was coated with ice. Rigging could not have been worked, for every rope would have jammed in the throats of the pulleys. Only a vessel without sails, driven by an electric motor that had no need of coal, could face latitudes as high as these.

Under these conditions the barometer generally held very low. It even dropped to 73°5′. The readings of the compass no longer offered any guarantee. Its frantic needles pointed in contradictory directions as we neared the southern magnetic pole, which does not coincide with the geographic south of the world. Indeed, according to Hansteen, this pole is situated at about 70° latitude and 130° longitude, and according to Duperrey's observations, at 135° longitude and 70°30′ latitude. We then had to take numerous readings from compasses carried to different parts of the ship and strike an average. But often we relied on dead reckoning to plot the course run — a poor method in the midst of these winding passages whose landmarks change without cease.

At last, on March 18, after twenty useless assaults, the Nautilus found itself definitively blocked. These were no longer streams, nor palchs, nor ice-fields, but an endless, motionless barrier formed of mountains welded to one another.

"The ice barrier!" the Canadian said to me.

I understood that for Ned Land, as for every navigator who had gone before us, this was the impassable obstacle. The sun having shown itself for a moment around noon, Captain Nemo obtained a fairly accurate reading that put our position at 51°30′ longitude and 67°39′ southern latitude. This was already a far-advanced point of the antarctic regions.

Of sea, of liquid surface, there was no longer any sign before our eyes. Beneath the ram of the Nautilus there stretched a vast tormented plain, tangled with confused blocks, with all that capricious jumble that marks the surface of a river some time before the ice breaks up, but on gigantic proportions. Here and there, sharp peaks, slender needles rose to a height of two hundred feet; farther off, a line of cliffs cut sheer and clothed in grayish tints, vast mirrors that reflected a few rays of sunlight half drowned in the mists. Then, over this desolate landscape, a savage silence, scarcely broken by the beating wings of the petrels or the shearwaters. Everything was frozen then, even sound.

The Nautilus was thus forced to halt in its adventurous course among the fields of ice.

"Professor," Ned Land said to me that day, "if your captain goes any farther —"

"Well?"

"He'll be a master among men."

"Why, Ned?"

"Because nobody can cross the ice barrier. He's powerful, your captain; but, a thousand devils! he's no more powerful than nature, and where she has set her boundaries, a man has to stop whether he likes it or not."

"True enough, Ned Land, and yet I would have liked to know what lies behind that ice barrier! A wall — that's what irritates me most!"

"Master is right," said Conseil. "Walls were only ever invented to annoy scholars. There ought not to be walls anywhere."

"Bah!" said the Canadian. "Behind that ice barrier we know perfectly well what's there."

"And what is that?" I asked.

"Ice, and more ice!"

"You may be sure of that fact, Ned," I answered, "but I am not. That's exactly why I'd like to go and see."

"Well, Professor," the Canadian answered, "give up the idea. You've reached the ice barrier, which is quite enough, and you'll go no farther — not you, not your Captain Nemo, not his Nautilus. And whether he likes it or not, we'll turn back north, that is, to the land of honest folk."

I have to admit that Ned Land was right, and until ships are built to navigate over fields of ice, they will have to stop before the ice barrier.

And in fact, despite its efforts, despite the powerful means employed to break the ice apart, the Nautilus was reduced to immobility. Ordinarily, one who can go no farther gets off simply by turning back. But here, to turn back was as impossible as to go forward, for the passages had closed behind us, and if our craft stayed stationary at all, it would soon be locked fast. That is exactly what happened toward two in the afternoon, and the young ice formed along its sides with astonishing speed. I had to admit that Captain Nemo's conduct was more than imprudent.

I was on the platform just then. The captain, who had been studying the situation for some minutes, said to me:

"Well, Professor, what do you think of it?"

"I think we're caught, Captain."

"Caught! And how do you mean that?"

"I mean that we can go neither forward nor back, nor to any side. That, I believe, is what is called 'caught' — at least on the inhabited continents."

"So, Professor Aronnax, you think the Nautilus won't be able to work itself free?"

"Only with difficulty, Captain, for the season is already too far gone for you to count on the ice breaking up."

"Ah, Professor," Captain Nemo replied in an ironic tone, "you'll always be the same! You see nothing but hindrances and obstacles! I tell you, not only will the Nautilus free itself, but it will go farther still!"

"Farther south?" I asked, looking at the captain.

"Yes, Professor, it will go to the Pole."

"To the Pole!" I cried, unable to hold back a movement of disbelief.

"Yes," the captain answered coldly, "to the antarctic pole, to that unknown point where all the meridians of the globe cross. You know that I make the Nautilus do whatever I wish."

Yes! I knew it. I knew this man was bold to the point of recklessness! But to conquer these obstacles that bristle around the South Pole — more inaccessible than the North Pole, which the boldest navigators had not yet reached — was that not an utterly insane undertaking, one that only the mind of a madman could conceive?

It then occurred to me to ask Captain Nemo whether he had already discovered this pole that no human foot had ever trodden.

"No, Professor," he answered me, "and we will discover it together. Where others have failed, I will not fail. Never have I taken my Nautilus so far across the southern seas; but, I repeat, it will go farther still."

"I want to believe you, Captain," I went on in a slightly ironic tone. "I do believe you! Let's go ahead! There are no obstacles for us! Let's break through this ice barrier! Let's blow it up, and if it resists, let's give the Nautilus wings, so it can pass over the top!"

"Over the top, Professor?" Captain Nemo answered calmly. "No, not over the top, but underneath."

"Underneath!" I cried.

A sudden revelation of the captain's plans had just lit up my mind. I understood. The wonderful qualities of the Nautilus were going to serve it once more in this superhuman enterprise!

"I see we're beginning to understand each other, Professor," the captain said to me with a half smile. "You already glimpse the possibility — I would say the success — of this attempt. What is impracticable with an ordinary ship becomes easy for the Nautilus. If a continent emerges at the Pole, it will stop before that continent. But if, on the contrary, it is open sea that washes the Pole, it will go to the Pole itself!"

"Just so," I said, carried along by the captain's reasoning. "If the surface of the sea is solidified by the ice, its lower layers are free, thanks to that providential fact which places the maximum density of seawater at a temperature above its freezing point. And, if I'm not mistaken, the submerged part of this ice barrier is to the emergent part as four is to one?"

"Roughly, Professor. For every foot the icebergs stand above the sea, they have three below it. Now, since these ice mountains do not exceed a height of a hundred meters, they sink only three hundred. And what are three hundred meters to the Nautilus?"

"Nothing, Captain."

"It can even go and seek, at a greater depth, that uniform temperature of the sea's waters, and there we'll defy with impunity the thirty or forty degrees of cold at the surface."

"Right, Captain, quite right," I replied, warming to it.

"The only difficulty," Captain Nemo went on, "will be to stay submerged several days without renewing our supply of air."

"Is that all?" I answered. "The Nautilus has vast tanks; we'll fill them, and they'll furnish us all the oxygen we need."

"Well thought, Professor Aronnax," the captain answered, smiling. "But not wanting to have you accuse me of recklessness, I lay all my objections before you in advance."

"Have you any more to make?"

"Just one. It's possible, if a sea exists at the South Pole, that this sea is entirely frozen over, and that consequently we won't be able to come back up to its surface!"

"Come, Captain, are you forgetting that the Nautilus is armed with a formidable ram, and can't we launch it diagonally against these fields of ice, which will open at the shock?"

"Ah, Professor, you've got ideas today!"

"Besides, Captain," I added, growing more and more enthusiastic, "why shouldn't one meet open sea at the South Pole as well as at the North Pole? The poles of cold and the poles of the earth do not coincide, either in the southern hemisphere or in the northern; and until there is proof to the contrary, we must assume either a continent or an ocean free of ice at these two points of the globe."

"I believe so too, Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo answered. "I'll only point out to you that after raising so many objections against my project, you're now crushing me with arguments in its favor."

Captain Nemo was right. I had come to outdo him in daring! It was I who was dragging him to the Pole! I was outstripping him, leaving him behind... But no! You poor fool. Captain Nemo knew better than you the pros and cons of the question, and he was amusing himself to see you swept away in dreams of the impossible!

Meanwhile, he had not lost a moment. At a signal the first officer appeared. The two men spoke rapidly in their incomprehensible language, and whether the officer had been warned of it beforehand or found the project workable, he showed no surprise.

But impassive as he was, he showed no more complete impassiveness than Conseil when I told that worthy fellow of our intention to push on to the South Pole. A "Just as Master pleases" greeted my announcement, and I had to be content with that. As for Ned Land, if ever shoulders were raised high, they were the Canadian's.

"You see, Professor," he said to me, "you and your Captain Nemo — I feel sorry for you both!"

"But we'll reach the Pole, Master Ned."

"Possibly, but you won't come back from it!"

And Ned Land withdrew into his cabin, "so as not to do something desperate," he said as he left me.

Meanwhile, the preparations for this bold attempt had begun. The powerful pumps of the Nautilus were forcing air into the tanks and storing it there at high pressure. Around four o'clock, Captain Nemo announced to me that the platform hatches were about to be closed. I threw a last look at the thick ice barrier we were about to cross. The weather was clear, the air fairly pure, the cold very sharp, twelve degrees below zero; but the wind having died down, this temperature did not seem too unbearable.

Ten or so men climbed onto the sides of the Nautilus and, armed with pickaxes, broke the ice around the hull, which was soon cleared. The operation was quickly done, for the young ice was still thin. We all went back inside. The usual tanks filled with this water left free at the waterline. The Nautilus was not slow to descend.

I had taken a place in the salon with Conseil. Through the open window we watched the lower layers of the southern ocean. The thermometer was rising. The needle of the pressure gauge swung across the dial.

At about three hundred meters, just as Captain Nemo had foreseen, we were floating beneath the rippled underside of the ice barrier. But the Nautilus dove lower still. It reached a depth of eight hundred meters. The temperature of the water, which had given twelve degrees at the surface, now registered only eleven. Two degrees already gained. It goes without saying that the temperature inside the Nautilus, raised by its heating apparatus, held at a far higher level. Every maneuver was carried out with extraordinary precision.

"We'll get through, if Master doesn't mind my saying so," Conseil said to me.

"I certainly count on it!" I answered, in the tone of a deep conviction.

Under this open sea, the Nautilus had struck straight for the Pole, without straying from the fifty-second meridian. From 67°30′ to 90°, twenty-two and a half degrees of latitude remained to be crossed — that is, a little more than five hundred leagues. The Nautilus took an average speed of twenty-six miles an hour, the speed of an express train. If it kept it up, forty hours would be enough to reach the Pole.

For part of the night the novelty of the situation held Conseil and me at the salon window. The sea shone under the electric radiance of the beacon. But it was deserted. The fish did not linger in these imprisoned waters. They found there only a passage for going from the antarctic ocean to the open sea of the Pole. Our pace was swift. You could feel it in the shudders of the long steel hull.

Toward two in the morning I went to snatch a few hours' rest. Conseil did the same. Crossing the gangways, I did not meet Captain Nemo. I supposed he was in the helmsman's cage.

The next day, March 19, at five in the morning, I resumed my post in the salon. The electric log told me that the Nautilus had slackened its speed. It was rising now toward the surface, but cautiously, slowly emptying its tanks.

My heart was pounding. Were we going to surface and find the free air of the Pole?

No. A jolt told me that the Nautilus had struck the lower surface of the ice barrier, still very thick, to judge by the dull sound. In fact, we had "touched," to use the sailors' expression, but in reverse and at a depth of a thousand feet. That gave two thousand feet of ice above us, of which one thousand emerged. The ice barrier stood then at a height greater than the one we had measured along its edges. A not very reassuring circumstance.

Through that day the Nautilus began this same experiment several times over, and each time it came up against the wall that ceilinged over it. At certain moments it met the ice at nine hundred meters, which meant twelve hundred meters of thickness, of which two hundred meters rose above the surface of the ocean. That was double its height at the moment the Nautilus had sunk beneath the waves.

I carefully noted these various depths, and in this way I obtained the underwater profile of this range that unfolded beneath the waters.

That evening no change had come in our situation. Still the ice between four hundred and five hundred meters of depth. A clear decrease, but what a thickness still between us and the surface of the ocean!

It was eight o'clock by then. For four hours already the air inside the Nautilus should have been renewed, according to the daily custom aboard. Yet I was not suffering too much, though Captain Nemo had not yet drawn on his tanks for an extra supply of oxygen.

My sleep was troubled that night. Hope and fear besieged me by turns. I got up several times. The Nautilus went on groping. Toward three in the morning I noticed that the lower surface of the ice barrier was met at only fifty meters of depth. A hundred and fifty feet separated us then from the surface of the water. The ice barrier was gradually becoming ice-field again. The mountain was turning back into plain.

My eyes never left the pressure gauge. We kept rising, following a diagonal along the resplendent surface that glittered under the electric rays. The ice barrier sloped away above and below in long ramps. It thinned out mile by mile.

At last, at six in the morning, on that memorable day of March 19, the salon door opened. Captain Nemo appeared.

"The open sea!" he said to me.
