Sperm Whales and Right Whales

During the night of March 13th to 14th, the Nautilus resumed its course toward the south. I assumed that once we reached the latitude of Cape Horn it would swing west, cross into the Pacific, and finish its voyage around the world. It did nothing of the kind. It kept climbing toward the far southern regions. Where on earth did it mean to go? The Pole? That was madness. I was beginning to think that the captain's recklessness more than justified Ned Land's fears.

For some time now the Canadian had stopped talking to me about his escape plans. He had grown less talkative, almost silent. I could see how heavily this long imprisonment weighed on him. I could feel the anger banking up inside him. Whenever he crossed paths with the captain, his eyes lit with a dark fire, and I was always afraid his natural violence would carry him to some extreme.

That day, March 14th, he and Conseil came looking for me in my cabin. I asked what had brought them.

"Just a simple question, Professor," the Canadian answered.

"Go ahead, Ned."

"How many men do you suppose there are aboard the Nautilus?"

"I couldn't say, my friend."

"It seems to me," Ned Land went on, "that running her doesn't call for a big crew."

"True enough," I replied. "In her present conditions, ten men at most should be enough to handle her."

"Well then," said the Canadian, "why should there be any more than that?"

"Why?" I echoed.

I looked hard at Ned Land, whose intentions were easy enough to guess.

"Because," I said, "if I can trust my instincts, if I've understood the captain's life at all, the Nautilus is not merely a ship. She must also be a refuge for men who, like her commander, have broken off every tie with the land."

"Perhaps," said Conseil, "but surely the Nautilus can only hold so many men. Couldn't Master estimate that maximum?"

"How would I do that, Conseil?"

"By calculation. Given the ship's capacity, which Master knows, and therefore the quantity of air she contains — and knowing, on the other hand, how much each man uses up in the act of breathing — and comparing all that with the fact that the Nautilus has to surface every twenty-four hours..."

Conseil's sentence went on and on, but I saw clearly where he was heading.

"I follow you," I said. "But that calculation, easy enough to set up, can only give a very rough figure."

"Never mind," Ned Land pressed.

"Here's the calculation, then," I answered. "Each man uses up, in one hour, the oxygen contained in a hundred liters of air — so, in twenty-four hours, the oxygen contained in two thousand four hundred liters. We only have to work out how many times over the Nautilus holds two thousand four hundred liters of air."

"Exactly," said Conseil.

"Now," I went on, "the capacity of the Nautilus being fifteen hundred tons, and that of a ton being a thousand liters, the Nautilus holds fifteen hundred thousand liters of air, which, divided by two thousand four hundred..."

I worked it out quickly in pencil.

"...gives a quotient of six hundred and twenty-five. Which is to say that the air held in the Nautilus could, strictly speaking, keep six hundred and twenty-five men alive for twenty-four hours."

"Six hundred and twenty-five!" Ned repeated.

"But you can take it as certain," I added, "that passengers, sailors, and officers all together, we don't make up a tenth of that number."

"Still too many for three men!" muttered Conseil.

"So, my poor Ned, all I can do is advise you to be patient."

"And better than patience," said Conseil, "resignation."

Conseil had found exactly the right word.

"After all," he went on, "Captain Nemo can't keep heading south forever! He'll have to stop sooner or later — if only at the ice barrier — and turn back toward more civilized seas! Then it will be time to take up Ned Land's plans again."

The Canadian shook his head, ran a hand across his forehead, said nothing, and withdrew.

"If Master will allow me a remark," Conseil said to me then, "poor Ned thinks about everything he can't have. Everything from his old life keeps coming back to him. Everything we're denied strikes him as a loss. His old memories weigh on him and his heart is heavy. You have to understand him. What is there for him to do here? Nothing. He's no man of science like Master, and he can't take the same pleasure we do in the marvelous things of the sea. He'd risk anything just to walk into a tavern back home!"

There was no denying that the monotony aboard must have felt unbearable to the Canadian, a man used to a free and active life. The events that could stir him were rare. That day, though, one incident came to remind him of his glory days as a harpooner.

Around eleven in the morning, running along the surface of the ocean, the Nautilus fell in with a pod of right whales. The encounter didn't surprise me, for I knew that these animals, hunted to the brink, have taken refuge in the basins of the high latitudes.

The part the whale has played in the world of the sea, and its influence on geographical discovery, has been considerable. It was the whale that drew after it, first the Basques, then the Asturians, the English, and the Dutch, emboldening them against the dangers of the ocean and leading them from one end of the earth to the other. Whales like to haunt the southern and northern seas. Old legends even claim that these cetaceans led fishermen to within a mere seven leagues of the North Pole. If that is not yet true, it will be one day, and it is probably in just this way — by hunting the whale into the Arctic or Antarctic regions — that men will reach that unknown point of the globe.

We were sitting on the platform in a calm sea. But October in these latitudes gave us fine autumn days. It was the Canadian — he couldn't be mistaken about it — who sighted a whale on the horizon to the east. Watching closely, you could see its blackish back rise and fall above the waves, five miles off the Nautilus.

"Ah!" cried Ned Land, "if I were aboard a whaler, there's an encounter that would do my heart good! That's a big animal! Look at the power in it — the columns of air and vapor its blowholes throw up! A thousand devils! Why must I be chained to this sheet of metal?"

"What, Ned," I answered, "you still haven't gotten over your old fishing notions?"

"How could a whaler forget his old trade, Professor? How does a man ever tire of the thrill of a hunt like that?"

"You've never fished these seas, Ned?"

"Never, Professor. Only the northern seas — the Bering Strait as much as the Davis Strait."

"Then the southern right whale is still unknown to you. What you've hunted up to now is the common right whale, and it would never risk crossing the warm waters of the Equator."

"Ah, Professor, what are you telling me?" the Canadian replied in a distinctly skeptical tone.

"I'm telling you how it is."

"Come now! Take me, the man talking to you — back in sixty-five, two and a half years ago, off Greenland, I brought in a whale that still carried in its flank the punch-stamped harpon of a Bering whaler. Now I ask you — how, after being struck west of America, could the animal have come to be killed in the east, unless, after rounding either Cape Horn or the Cape of Good Hope, it had crossed the Equator?"

"I'm of Ned's mind," said Conseil, "and I'm waiting to hear what Master will answer."

"Master will answer, my friends, that whales keep to certain seas, according to their species, and don't leave them. And if one of these animals came from the Bering Strait to the Davis Strait, it's quite simply because there's a passage from one sea to the other, whether along the coasts of America or of Asia."

"Am I supposed to believe that?" the Canadian asked, closing one eye.

"You're supposed to believe Master," said Conseil.

"In that case," the Canadian went on, "since I've never fished these waters, I don't know the first thing about the whales that frequent them?"

"That's what I told you, Ned."

"All the more reason to make their acquaintance," Conseil replied.

"Look! Look!" cried the Canadian, his voice trembling. "She's coming closer! She's heading straight for us! She's mocking me! She knows there's nothing I can do to her!"

Ned stamped his foot. His hand quivered as it brandished an imaginary harpoon.

"These cetaceans," he asked, "are they as big as the ones in the northern seas?"

"Just about, Ned."

"Because I've seen big whales, Professor — whales that measured as much as a hundred feet long! I've even been told that the Hullamock and the Umgallick of the Aleutian Islands sometimes topped a hundred and fifty feet."

"That strikes me as exaggerated," I answered. "Those animals are only rorquals, fitted with dorsal fins, and like the sperm whales they're generally smaller than the common right whale."

"Ah!" cried the Canadian, whose eyes never left the ocean, "she's coming nearer, she's coming into the Nautilus's waters!"

Then, picking up the conversation again:

"You talk about the sperm whale," he said, "as if it were some little beast! And yet there are stories of gigantic sperm whales. They're intelligent cetaceans. Some of them, they say, cover themselves with seaweed and wrack. People take them for little islands. They land on them, they set up camp, they light a fire..."

"They build houses," said Conseil.

"Yes, joker," Ned Land replied. "Then one fine day the animal dives and drags all its inhabitants down to the bottom of the abyss."

"Just like in the voyages of Sinbad the Sailor," I put in, laughing.

"Ah, Master Land, it seems you're fond of tall tales! What sperm whales you have! I hope you don't believe in them yourself!"

"Professor," the Canadian answered seriously, "when it comes to whales, you have to believe anything!"

"Look at her go, this one! Look how she slips away!"

"They say those animals can travel around the world in a fortnight."

"I won't say no."

"But what you probably don't know, Professor Aronnax, is that at the beginning of the world, whales moved faster still."

"Oh, really, Ned? And why is that?"

"Because in those days their tails ran crosswise, like a fish's — that is, the tail, flattened vertically, struck the water from left to right and right to left. But the Creator, noticing they went too fast, gave their tails a twist, and ever since, they've beaten the waves up and down, at the cost of their speed."

"Well now, Ned," I said, borrowing one of the Canadian's own phrases, "am I supposed to believe that?"

"Not too much," Ned Land answered, "and no more than if I told you there are whales three hundred feet long weighing a hundred thousand pounds."

"That is a great deal, indeed," I said. "Still, one has to admit that certain cetaceans reach a considerable size, since they're said to yield as much as a hundred and twenty tons of oil."

"That I've seen," said the Canadian.

"I readily believe it, Ned, just as I believe that some whales equal a hundred elephants in bulk. Imagine the effect of a mass like that launched at full speed!"

"Is it true," asked Conseil, "that they can sink ships?"

"Ships — I don't think so," I answered. "And yet the story goes that in 1820, in these very southern seas, a whale hurled itself at the Essex and drove it backward at a speed of four meters a second. The sea poured in through the stern, and the Essex went down almost at once."

Ned gave me a mocking look.

"For my part," he said, "I once took a whale's tail — in my boat, needless to say. My mates and I were flung six meters into the air. But next to the Professor's whale, mine was no more than a calf."

"Do these animals live a long time?" Conseil asked.

"A thousand years," the Canadian answered without hesitation.

"And how do you know that, Ned?"

"Because that's what they say."

"And why do they say it?"

"Because that's what they know."

"No, Ned, they don't know it — they suppose it, and here's the reasoning they lean on. Four hundred years ago, when fishermen first hunted whales, these animals ran larger than they grow today. So it's supposed, reasonably enough, that the smaller size of today's whales comes from their not having had the time to reach their full growth. That's what made Buffon say these cetaceans could, and even should, live a thousand years. You follow?"

Ned Land didn't follow. He was no longer listening. The whale kept coming closer. He devoured it with his eyes.

"Ah!" he cried, "it's not one whale anymore, it's ten, it's twenty, it's a whole pod! And to be able to do nothing! To be stuck here, hand and foot bound!"

"But, friend Ned," said Conseil, "why not ask Captain Nemo's permission to hunt?..."

Conseil hadn't finished his sentence before Ned Land had dropped down through the hatch and gone running in search of the captain. A few moments later, the two of them reappeared on the platform.

Captain Nemo studied the pod of cetaceans sporting on the waters a mile off the Nautilus.

"Those are southern right whales," he said. "There's a whole whaling fleet's fortune out there."

"Well then, sir," the Canadian asked, "couldn't I give chase to them, if only so as not to forget my old trade as a harpooner?"

"What would be the point," Captain Nemo answered, "of hunting merely to destroy? We have no use for whale oil aboard."

"And yet, sir," the Canadian pressed, "in the Red Sea you gave us leave to go after a dugong!"

"That was a matter of getting fresh meat for my crew. Here, it would be killing for the sake of killing. I'm well aware that's a privilege reserved to man, but I don't hold with such murderous pastimes. In destroying the southern right whale and the common right whale — harmless, good creatures — your kind, Master Land, commit a blamable act. That's how they've already emptied the whole of Baffin Bay, and how they'll wipe out a class of useful animals. So leave these poor cetaceans in peace. They have quite enough to deal with in their natural enemies — the sperm whales, the swordfish, and the sawfish — without your meddling too."

I leave you to imagine the face the Canadian made during this lecture on morals. To give reasons of that kind to a hunter was to waste one's breath. Ned Land stared at Captain Nemo and plainly didn't grasp what he was trying to tell him. And yet the captain was right. The barbarous, thoughtless zeal of the fishermen will one day make the last whale vanish from the ocean.

Ned Land whistled his Yankee Doodle through his teeth, shoved his hands in his pockets, and turned his back on us.

Meanwhile Captain Nemo went on watching the pod of cetaceans, and, turning to me:

"I was right to say that, without counting man, whales have enemies enough. These are going to have a hard fight on their hands before long. Do you see, Professor Aronnax, eight miles off to leeward, those blackish points moving about?"

"Yes, Captain," I answered.

"Those are sperm whales — terrible animals that I've sometimes met in pods of two or three hundred! As for those, cruel and destructive brutes, people are right to exterminate them."

The Canadian turned around sharply at these last words.

"Well, Captain," I said, "there's still time, in the very interest of the right whales..."

"No need to put ourselves at risk, Professor. The Nautilus will be enough to scatter these sperm whales. She's armed with a steel ram every bit as good as Master Land's harpoon, I fancy."

The Canadian didn't trouble to hide a shrug. Attack cetaceans with a ram! Who had ever heard of such a thing?

"Wait, Professor Aronnax," said Captain Nemo. "We'll show you a hunt you don't know yet. No mercy for these ferocious cetaceans. They're nothing but mouth and teeth!"

Mouth and teeth! You couldn't better describe the great-headed sperm whale — the cachalot macrocéphalus — whose length sometimes exceeds twenty-five meters. This cetacean's enormous head takes up about a third of its body. Better armed than the whale, whose upper jaw carries only baleen, it is fitted out with twenty-five massive teeth, twenty centimeters high, cylindrical and coning to a point at the tip, each weighing two pounds. It's in the upper part of that huge head, in great cavities partitioned off by cartilage, that some three or four hundred kilograms of that precious oil are stored — the stuff called spermaceti, or "whale-white." The sperm whale is an ungainly animal, more tadpole than fish, as Frédol remarks. It is poorly built, "botched," so to speak, along the whole left side of its frame, seeing hardly at all except out of the right eye.

Meanwhile the monstrous pod kept drawing nearer. It had spotted the right whales and was preparing to attack them. You could tell in advance that the sperm whales would win — not only because they're better built for attack than their harmless adversaries, but because they can stay longer beneath the waves without coming up to breathe at the surface.

There was not a moment to lose in going to the whales' rescue. The Nautilus dropped to mid-depth. Conseil, Ned, and I took our places at the windows of the salon. Captain Nemo went to stand beside the helmsman, to work his machine like an engine of destruction. Soon I felt the beat of the propeller quicken and our speed pick up.

The battle between the sperm whales and the right whales had already begun when the Nautilus arrived. She maneuvered so as to cut through the pod of great-heads. At first they showed little alarm at the sight of the new monster joining the fray. But soon they had to guard themselves against her blows.

What a fight! Even Ned Land, soon carried away, ended up clapping his hands. The Nautilus was nothing now but a formidable harpoon, wielded by her captain's hand. She flung herself at those fleshy masses and ran them clean through, leaving two writhing halves of animal in her wake. The tremendous blows of the tails against her flanks — she didn't feel them. The shocks she dealt out — no more than that. One sperm whale finished off, she raced to another, pivoting in place so as not to miss her prey, going forward, going astern, obedient to her helm, diving when the cetacean sank into the deeper layers, rising with it when it came back to the surface, striking it head-on or aslant, cutting it or tearing it, in every direction and at every gait, piercing it with her terrible ram.

What a carnage! What an uproar over the surface of the waves! What shrill whistlings, what strange snorts from those terrified animals! In the midst of those normally so peaceful waters, their tails raised veritable swells.

For a whole hour this Homeric massacre dragged on, and the great-heads could not escape it. Several times, ten or a dozen of them together tried to crush the Nautilus under their mass. Through the window you could see their enormous jaws paved with teeth, their formidable eyes. Ned Land, beside himself, threatened them and hurled abuse at them. You could feel them clinging to our machine like hounds fastening on a wild boar in the thickets. But the Nautilus, forcing her propeller, carried them off, dragged them along, or hauled them back up to the higher levels of the water, heedless of their enormous weight or their powerful grip.

At last the mass of sperm whales thinned out. The waters grew calm again. I felt us rising to the surface of the ocean. The hatch was opened, and we rushed out onto the platform.

The sea was covered with mangled corpses. A tremendous explosion could not have split, torn, and shredded those fleshy masses with more violence. We floated among gigantic bodies, bluish along the back, whitish under the belly, and all knotted with enormous protuberances. A few terrified sperm whales fled toward the horizon. The waves were stained red over a stretch of several miles, and the Nautilus floated in the middle of a sea of blood.

Captain Nemo came to join us.

"Well, Master Land?" he said.

"Well, sir," the Canadian answered — his enthusiasm had cooled now — "it's a terrible sight, right enough. But I'm not a butcher, I'm a hunter, and this is nothing but a slaughter."

"It's a massacre of destructive animals," the captain replied, "and the Nautilus is no butcher's knife."

"I'd rather have my harpoon," the Canadian shot back.

"To each his own weapon," the captain answered, looking Ned Land straight in the eye.

I was afraid Ned might let himself be carried away into some act of violence that would have had deplorable consequences. But his anger was turned aside by the sight of a whale the Nautilus was coming alongside just then.

The animal hadn't been able to escape the sperm whales' teeth. I recognized the southern right whale, with its flattened head, entirely black. Anatomically, it differs from the white whale and the Nord-Caper by the fusion of its seven cervical vertebrae, and it carries two ribs more than its kin. The wretched cetacean, lying on its side, its belly riddled with bites, was dead. From the end of its mutilated fin there still hung a little calf it hadn't been able to save from the massacre. Its open mouth let the water run out, murmuring like surf through its baleen.

Captain Nemo brought the Nautilus up close to the animal's carcass. Two of his men climbed onto the whale's flank, and I saw, not without astonishment, that they were drawing off from its teats all the milk they held — the equivalent, that is, of two or three barrels.

The captain offered me a cup of this milk, still warm. I couldn't help showing him my distaste for the drink. He assured me it was excellent, and in no way different from cow's milk.

I tasted it and agreed. So it was a useful reserve for us, since this milk, in the form of salt butter or cheese, was bound to bring a pleasant variety to our usual fare.

From that day on, I noticed with unease that Ned Land's feelings toward Captain Nemo were turning worse and worse, and I resolved to keep a close watch on the Canadian's every move.
