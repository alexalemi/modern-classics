Four Thousand Leagues Under the Pacific

The next morning, November 18, I had fully recovered from the exhaustion of the day before, and I climbed up onto the platform just as the Nautilus's first officer was pronouncing his daily phrase. It occurred to me then that it must have to do with the state of the sea — or rather that it meant: "We have nothing in sight."

And indeed, the ocean was deserted. Not a sail on the horizon. The heights of Crespo Island had vanished during the night. The sea drank in all the colors of the spectrum except the blue, reflecting that in every direction and taking on a magnificent shade of indigo. A watered-silk pattern of broad bands rippled regularly across the swelling waves.

I was admiring this splendid face of the ocean when Captain Nemo appeared. He seemed not to notice my presence, and began a series of astronomical observations. Then, his work finished, he went and leaned his elbows on the housing of the beacon-lamp, and his gaze lost itself over the surface of the sea.

Meanwhile some twenty of the Nautilus's sailors — all of them vigorous, well-built men — had come up onto the platform. They had come to haul in the nets that had been trailing behind us through the night. These sailors clearly belonged to different nations, though the European type was marked in every one of them. I recognized, beyond any doubt, Irishmen, Frenchmen, a few Slavs, a Greek or a Cretan. Still, these men were sparing with their words, and among themselves they used only that strange tongue whose origin I could not so much as guess at. So I had to give up questioning them.

The nets were hauled aboard. They were a kind of trawl, like those used off the coast of Normandy — vast pouches held half-open by a floating spar and by a chain threaded through the lower meshes. These pouches, dragged along on their iron footropes, swept the ocean floor and gathered up everything in their path. That day they brought up some curious samples from these fish-rich waters: anglerfish, whose comical movements have earned them the nickname of clowns; black commersons, fitted with their antennae; wavy triggerfish ringed with red bands; crescent puffers, whose venom is extremely subtle; a few olive-colored lampreys; long-snouted grenadiers covered in silvery scales; cutlassfish, whose electric power equals that of the electric eel and the torpedo ray; scaly featherbacks with brown transverse stripes; greenish cod; several varieties of gobies; and so on — and finally a few fish of larger proportions: a jack with a prominent head, a meter long; several fine bonito mackerel splashed with blue and silver; and three magnificent tuna whose swiftness had not been enough to save them from the trawl.

I reckoned that this cast of the net brought up more than a thousand pounds of fish. It was a fine haul, but no surprise. These nets stay in tow for hours, and they cage up an entire aquatic world in their prison of twine. So we would have no shortage of provisions, and excellent ones at that — supplies the speed of the Nautilus and the lure of its electric light could renew endlessly.

These various products of the sea were at once lowered through the hatch toward the storerooms, some destined to be eaten fresh, others to be preserved.

With the fishing done and the air supply renewed, I supposed the Nautilus was about to resume its underwater excursion, and I was getting ready to go back to my cabin, when Captain Nemo turned to me and said, without any preamble:

"Look at this ocean, Professor — isn't it endowed with a life of its own? Doesn't it have its rages and its tendernesses? Yesterday it fell asleep as we did, and now here it is, waking after a peaceful night!"

Not a good morning, not a good evening! You would have thought this strange man was simply carrying on a conversation with me that had already begun.

"Look," he went on, "it wakes beneath the sun's caresses! It's about to live its daytime life all over again. It makes for a fascinating study, to follow the workings of its organism. It has a pulse, it has arteries, it has its spasms — and I say that clever man Maury was right when he discovered in it a circulation as real as the circulation of the blood in animals."

It was clear that Captain Nemo expected no answer from me, and it seemed pointless to lavish on him the "Of courses," the "Certainlys," and the "You're quite rights." He was speaking rather to himself, taking long pauses between his sentences. It was a meditation aloud.

"Yes," he said, "the ocean has a true circulation, and to bring it about, the Creator of all things had only to multiply within it heat, salt, and microscopic animals. Heat, in fact, creates different densities, which give rise to currents and countercurrents. Evaporation — nil in the hyperborean regions, very active in the equatorial zones — sets up a permanent exchange between the tropical waters and the polar waters. What's more, I have caught these currents flowing top to bottom and bottom to top, and they make up the true respiration of the ocean. I have seen the molecule of seawater, warmed at the surface, sink back down toward the depths, reach its maximum density at two degrees below zero, then, cooling further, grow lighter and rise again. At the poles you'll see the consequences of this phenomenon, and you'll understand why, by this law of provident nature, freezing can never occur anywhere but at the surface of the water!"

While Captain Nemo was finishing his sentence, I was saying to myself: "The pole! Does this bold character mean to take us all the way there?"

But the captain had fallen silent, contemplating this element he had studied so completely and so ceaselessly. Then, resuming:

"Salts," he said, "are present in the sea in considerable quantity, Professor, and if you removed all of those it holds in solution, you would make a mass of four and a half million cubic leagues, which, spread out over the globe, would form a layer more than ten meters high. And don't imagine that the presence of these salts is due to some caprice of nature. No. They make the marine waters less liable to evaporation, and keep the winds from carrying off too great a quantity of vapor — which, condensing again, would flood the temperate zones. An immense role, the role of a stabilizer in the general economy of the globe!"

Captain Nemo stopped, even stood up, took a few steps along the platform, and came back to me:

"As for the infusoria," he resumed, "as for those billions of microscopic animals, which exist by the million in a single droplet, and of which it takes eight hundred thousand to weigh a milligram — their role is no less important. They absorb the marine salts, they assimilate the solid elements of the water, and, true makers of limestone continents, they manufacture corals and madrepores! And so the drop of water, stripped of its mineral nourishment, grows lighter, rises again to the surface, absorbs there the salts left behind by evaporation, grows heavier, sinks back down, and brings the microscopic animals fresh elements to absorb. Hence a double current, ascending and descending — and always motion, always life! Life, more intense than on the continents, more exuberant, more infinite, blossoming in every part of this ocean — an element of death for man, so they have said, but an element of life for myriads of animals, and for me!"

When Captain Nemo spoke like this, he was transfigured, and he stirred in me an extraordinary emotion.

"And so," he added, "there is where true existence lies! And I could well conceive of the founding of nautical cities, of clusters of underwater houses that, like the Nautilus, would come up to breathe each morning at the surface of the seas — free cities, if ever there were any, independent towns! And yet — who knows whether some despot..."

Captain Nemo finished his sentence with a violent gesture. Then, addressing me directly, as if to drive off a grim thought:

"Professor Aronnax," he asked, "do you know the depth of the ocean?"

"I know, at least, Captain, what the principal soundings have taught us."

"Could you cite them for me, so that I might check them if need be?"

"Here are a few," I answered, "that come back to me. If I'm not mistaken, an average depth of eight thousand two hundred meters has been found in the north Atlantic, and of two thousand five hundred meters in the Mediterranean. The most remarkable soundings were taken in the south Atlantic, near the thirty-fifth parallel, and they gave twelve thousand meters, fourteen thousand and ninety-one meters, and fifteen thousand one hundred and forty-nine meters. All in all, it's estimated that if the sea bottom were leveled off, its average depth would be about seven kilometers."

"Good, Professor," Captain Nemo replied. "We'll show you better than that, I hope. As for the average depth of this part of the Pacific, I'll tell you that it is only four thousand meters."

That said, Captain Nemo made for the hatch and disappeared down the ladder. I followed him, and went back into the grand salon. The propeller began to turn at once, and the log registered a speed of twenty miles an hour.

Over the days and weeks that followed, Captain Nemo was very sparing with his visits. I saw him only at rare intervals. His first officer regularly took the ship's position, which I would find marked on the chart, so that I could trace the Nautilus's course exactly.

Conseil and Land spent long hours with me. Conseil had told his friend all the wonders of our undersea walk, and the Canadian was sorry not to have come along. But I hoped the chance to visit the oceanic forests would present itself again.

Almost every day, for a few hours, the panels of the salon would open, and our eyes never tired of probing the mysteries of the underwater world.

The Nautilus's general heading was southeast, and it kept between a hundred and a hundred and fifty meters deep. One day, though, on some whim or other, drawn down at an angle by its inclined planes, it reached the layers of water lying at two thousand meters. The thermometer read a temperature of 4.25 degrees centigrade — a temperature that, at this depth, seems common to all latitudes.

On November 26, at three in the morning, the Nautilus crossed the Tropic of Cancer at 172° longitude. On the 27th, it passed in sight of the Sandwich Islands, where the illustrious Cook met his death on February 14, 1779. By then we had covered four thousand eight hundred and sixty leagues since our starting point. That morning, when I came up onto the platform, I made out, two miles to leeward, Hawaii, the largest of the seven islands that make up this archipelago. I distinguished clearly its cultivated fringe, the various mountain chains that run parallel to the coast, and its volcanoes, dominated by Mauna Kea, which rises five thousand meters above sea level. Among other samples from these waters, the nets brought up peacock sea-fans — flattened polyps of a graceful shape, peculiar to this part of the ocean.

The Nautilus held to its southeast heading. It crossed the Equator on December 1, at 142° longitude, and on the 4th of the same month, after a swift passage marked by no incident, we came within sight of the Marquesas group. I sighted, three miles off, at 8°57′ south latitude and 139°32′ west longitude, Martin Point on Nuku Hiva, the chief island of this group, which belongs to France. I saw only the wooded mountains outlined against the horizon, for Captain Nemo did not care to close with land. There the nets brought up fine specimens of fish: dolphinfish with azure fins and gold tails, whose flesh has no rival in the world; hologymnosus, all but scaleless yet of an exquisite taste; bony-jawed cardinalfish; and yellowish frigate mackerel worthy of the bonito — all fish deserving to be entered in the ship's larder.

After leaving these charming islands under the protection of the French flag, from December 4 to 11 the Nautilus covered about two thousand miles. This stretch of sailing was marked by an encounter with an immense host of squid, curious mollusks very close kin to the cuttlefish. French fishermen call them encornets; they belong to the class of cephalopods and to the family of the dibranchiates, which also takes in the cuttlefish and the argonauts. These animals were studied with particular care by the naturalists of antiquity, and they furnished many a metaphor to the orators of the Agora, as well as an excellent dish at the tables of wealthy citizens — if we are to believe Athenaeus, a Greek physician who lived before Galen.

It was during the night of December 9 to 10 that the Nautilus met this army of mollusks, which are especially nocturnal. You could count them by the million. They were migrating from the temperate zones toward the warmer ones, following the same route as the herrings and sardines. We watched them through the thick crystal panes, swimming backward with extreme speed, propelling themselves by means of their locomotor tube, chasing fish and mollusks, eating the small ones, eaten by the big ones, and thrashing in indescribable confusion the ten arms that nature has planted on their heads like a mane of pneumatic serpents. For all its speed, the Nautilus sailed for several hours in the midst of this troop of animals, and its nets brought up an uncountable number of them, among which I recognized the nine species that d'Orbigny has classified for the Pacific Ocean.

As you can see, throughout this crossing the sea lavished its most marvelous spectacles on us without pause. It varied them endlessly. It changed its scenery and its staging for the pleasure of our eyes, and we were called not only to contemplate the works of the Creator in the midst of the liquid element, but also to penetrate the most formidable mysteries of the ocean.

During the day of December 11, I was busy reading in the grand salon. Ned Land and Conseil were watching the luminous waters through the half-open panels. The Nautilus lay motionless. Its tanks filled, it held at a depth of a thousand meters — a scarcely inhabited region of the oceans, where only the large fish made rare appearances.

Just then I was reading a charming book by Jean Macé, The Servants of the Stomach, and savoring its ingenious lessons, when Conseil broke off my reading.

"Would Master care to come here a moment?" he said in a peculiar voice.

"What is it, Conseil?"

"Let Master have a look."

I got up, went and leaned my elbows before the pane, and looked.

There, in the full glare of the electric light, an enormous blackish mass hung motionless in the midst of the waters. I studied it carefully, trying to make out the nature of this gigantic cetacean. But a thought suddenly crossed my mind.

"A ship!" I cried.

"Yes," answered the Canadian, "a disabled vessel that's gone straight to the bottom!"

Ned Land was not mistaken. We were in the presence of a ship whose cut shrouds still hung from their chainplates. Its hull seemed to be in good condition, and its wreck was at most a few hours old. Three stumps of masts, sheared off two feet above the deck, showed that the vessel, in trouble, had been forced to sacrifice its rigging. But, lying over on its side, it had filled with water, and it still listed to port. A sorry sight, this hulk lost beneath the waves — yet sorrier still the view of its deck, where a few corpses, lashed by ropes, still lay! I counted four of them — four men, one of whom stood upright at the helm — and then a woman, half emerged through the skylight of the poop deck, holding a child in her arms. This woman was young. In the vivid glare of the Nautilus's lights, I could make out her features, which the water had not yet begun to disfigure. In one last effort, she had lifted her child above her head — poor little creature, whose arms were locked around its mother's neck! The attitude of the four sailors struck me as terrifying, twisted as they were in convulsive movements, making one final effort to tear free of the ropes that bound them to the ship. Alone, calmer, his face clear and grave, his graying hair plastered to his forehead, his hand clenched on the wheel, the helmsman seemed still to be steering his shipwrecked three-master through the depths of the ocean!

What a scene! We stood mute, our hearts pounding, before this shipwreck caught in the act and, so to speak, photographed in its final minute! And already I could see, their eyes ablaze, enormous sharks advancing, drawn by this bait of human flesh!

Meanwhile the Nautilus, maneuvering, turned around the sunken ship, and for an instant I was able to read on its stern board:

Florida, Sunderland.
