At a Venture

For a while the Abraham Lincoln's voyage passed without incident. But one occasion arose that threw Ned Land's marvelous skill into sharp relief and showed just how much confidence we ought to place in him.

Off the Falklands, on June 30, the frigate spoke with some American whalers, and we learned that none of them had seen anything of the narwhal. But one of them, the captain of the Monroe, knowing that Ned Land was aboard the Abraham Lincoln, asked for his help in hunting a whale that had come into view. Commander Farragut, eager to see Ned Land at work, gave him leave to go across to the Monroe. And fortune served our Canadian so well that, instead of one whale, he harpooned two at a single double stroke — striking one clean through the heart and taking the other after a chase of only a few minutes!

There's no doubt about it: if the monster ever finds itself on the wrong end of Ned Land's harpoon, I wouldn't bet on the monster.

The frigate ran down the southeastern coast of America at a prodigious clip. By July 3 we lay off the mouth of the Strait of Magellan, level with Cape Vírgenes. But Commander Farragut had no wish to take that winding passage, and maneuvered instead to round Cape Horn.

The crew backed him unanimously. And indeed — was it likely we'd meet the narwhal in that cramped strait? A good many of the sailors swore the monster couldn't get through it, "that it was too big to fit!"

On July 6, around three in the afternoon, the Abraham Lincoln, fifteen miles to the south, rounded that solitary islet, that lost rock at the very tip of the American continent, to which Dutch sailors gave the name of their home town — Cape Horn. The course was set to the northwest, and the next day the frigate's propeller was at last churning the waters of the Pacific.

"Keep your eyes peeled! Keep your eyes peeled!" the sailors of the Abraham Lincoln kept telling one another.

And peel them they did, enormously. Eyes and spyglasses — a little dazzled, it's true, by the prospect of two thousand dollars — never rested for a moment. Day and night we watched the surface of the ocean, and the night-sighted ones, whose ability to see in the dark boosted their chances by fifty percent, were well placed to win the prize.

As for me, though the lure of money held little appeal, I was hardly the least attentive man aboard. Giving only a few minutes to meals, a few hours to sleep, indifferent to sun or rain, I no longer left the deck. Now leaning over the bulwarks of the forecastle, now propped against the after rail, I devoured with a greedy eye the cottony wake that whitened the sea as far as it could reach. And how many times I shared the thrill of the officers and crew when some capricious whale lifted its blackish back above the waves! In an instant the frigate's deck would fill. The hatchways would spew a torrent of sailors and officers. Each one, chest heaving, eyes clouded, watched the passage of the great mammal. I looked, I looked until I wore out my retina, until I nearly went blind, while Conseil, forever phlegmatic, would say to me in his calm way:

"If Master would be so good as to open his eyes a little less wide, Master would see a great deal more!"

But it was all wasted feeling! The Abraham Lincoln would change course, run down the sighted animal — an ordinary whale or a common sperm whale — which soon vanished amid a chorus of curses!

The weather, though, held fair. The voyage was going on under the best of conditions. It was then the bad season in the southern hemisphere, for July in that zone corresponds to our European January; but the sea stayed calm, and let itself be scanned easily over a wide sweep.

Ned Land still showed the most stubborn skepticism; he even made a point of not examining the surface of the waves outside of his watch — at least when no whale was in view. And yet his marvelous powers of vision would have done us great service. But eight hours out of twelve, that pigheaded Canadian read or slept in his cabin. A hundred times I reproached him for his indifference.

"Bah!" he'd answer. "There's nothing out there, Professor Aronnax, and even if there were some animal, what chance do we have of spotting it? Aren't we just running along at a venture? People say this creature nobody can find was sighted again in the high seas of the Pacific — fine, I'll grant that — but two months have already gone by since that encounter, and going by the temperament of your narwhal, it doesn't care to sit and molder for long in the same waters! It's gifted with a prodigious knack for getting around. Now, you know better than I do, Professor, that nature does nothing without a reason, and she wouldn't give an animal that's slow by nature the power to move fast if it had no need to use it. So, if the beast exists, it's already far away!"

To that I had no answer. Plainly we were groping in the dark. But what other way was there to go about it? And so our chances were quite limited. Still, no one yet doubted our success, and not a sailor aboard would have bet against the narwhal and its next appearance.

On July 20 we cut the Tropic of Capricorn at 105° longitude, and on the 27th of the same month we crossed the equator at the hundred-and-tenth meridian. That fix taken, the frigate held a more decided course to the west and struck into the central seas of the Pacific.

Commander Farragut reasoned, rightly, that it was better to keep to deep water and stay well clear of the continents and islands whose approach the animal had always seemed to avoid — "no doubt because there wasn't enough water there for it!" as the boatswain put it. So the frigate stood off from the Pomotou group, the Marquesas, and the Sandwich Islands, cut the Tropic of Cancer at 132° longitude, and steered for the seas of China.

We were at last on the stage of the monster's latest antics! And to tell the truth, life aboard had become unlivable. Hearts pounded dreadfully, laying up incurable aneurysms for the future. The whole crew labored under a nervous overexcitement I couldn't begin to describe. No one ate, no one slept. Twenty times a day a misjudgment, an optical illusion of some sailor perched on the crosstrees, caused unbearable anguish, and these emotions, repeated twenty times over, kept us in a state of agitation too violent not to bring on a reaction before long.

And indeed, the reaction was not slow in coming. For three months — three months in which every day lasted a century! — the Abraham Lincoln plowed all the northern seas of the Pacific, chasing after every whale that was sighted, veering off course abruptly, swinging suddenly from one tack to the other, stopping dead, forcing or reversing its steam again and again at the risk of throwing its engine off its bearings, and it left not a single point unexplored from the shores of Japan to the American coast. And nothing! Nothing but the immensity of the empty waves! Nothing that looked like a gigantic narwhal, or a submarine islet, or the wreck of a foundered ship, or a fleeing reef, or anything whatever of the supernatural!

So the reaction set in. Discouragement took hold of everyone's mind first, and opened a breach for disbelief. A new sentiment sprang up aboard, made of three-tenths shame against seven-tenths fury. We had been "utter fools" to let ourselves be taken in by a fantasy — but even more furious than foolish! The mountains of arguments piled up over a year came crashing down all at once, and each man thought of nothing now but making up, at meals or in sleep, for the time he'd so stupidly sacrificed.

With the natural fickleness of the human mind, we flung ourselves from one excess to the other. The warmest champions of the enterprise inevitably became its most ardent detractors. The reaction rose up from the depths of the ship, from the stokers' quarters clear to the officers' wardroom, and certainly, without a very particular stubbornness on Commander Farragut's part, the frigate would have finally turned her head back to the south.

Still, this fruitless search could not go on much longer. The Abraham Lincoln had nothing to reproach herself with, having done everything to succeed. Never did the crew of an American naval vessel show more patience and more zeal; the failure could not be laid at their door; there was nothing left but to turn back.

A representation to that effect was made to the commander. The commander held firm. The sailors made no secret of their discontent, and the service suffered for it. I don't mean to say there was mutiny aboard, but after a reasonable spell of obstinacy, Commander Farragut — like Columbus before him — asked for three days' patience. If within a delay of three days the monster had not appeared, the helmsman would give three turns of the wheel, and the Abraham Lincoln would make for European seas.

This promise was given on November 2. Its first effect was to revive the flagging spirits of the crew. The ocean was watched with fresh attention. Every man wanted to give it that last look in which all one's memory is summed up. The spyglasses went to work with a feverish energy. It was a supreme challenge flung at the giant narwhal, and the creature could not in reason excuse itself from answering that summons "to appear!"

Two days went by. The Abraham Lincoln kept under low steam. We tried a thousand tricks to rouse the attention or stir the apathy of the animal, in case it happened to be found in these waters. Enormous quarters of bacon were streamed astern — to the great satisfaction of the sharks, I must admit. The boats fanned out in every direction around the Abraham Lincoln while she lay to, and left not a point of sea unexplored. But the evening of November 4 arrived without this undersea mystery being unveiled.

The next day, November 5, at noon, the final deadline ran out. After the fix was taken, Commander Farragut, faithful to his promise, was to give the course to the southeast and abandon for good the northern regions of the Pacific.

The frigate then lay at 31°15′ north latitude and 136°42′ east longitude. The lands of Japan were less than two hundred miles off to leeward. Night was coming on. Eight bells had just struck. Great clouds veiled the disk of the moon, then in its first quarter. The sea heaved peacefully under the frigate's stem.

At that moment I was leaning at the bow, on the starboard bulwark. Conseil, posted beside me, gazed straight ahead. The crew, perched in the rigging, scanned the horizon as it narrowed and darkened little by little. The officers, armed with their night glasses, searched the growing gloom. Now and then the somber ocean glittered under a ray the moon shot between the fringe of two clouds. Then every trace of light died away in the darkness.

Watching Conseil, I noticed that the good fellow was feeling, ever so slightly, the general influence. At least I thought so. Perhaps, and for the first time, his nerves were quivering under the touch of a feeling of curiosity.

"Come now, Conseil," I said to him, "here's a last chance to pocket two thousand dollars."

"If Master will permit me to say so," Conseil replied, "I never counted on that prize, and the government of the Union could have promised a hundred thousand dollars — it wouldn't have been any the poorer for it."

"You're right, Conseil. It's a foolish business after all, and one we threw ourselves into too lightly. So much time lost, so much wasted feeling! Six months ago we'd already be back in France —"

"In Master's little apartment," Conseil answered, "in Master's Museum! And I'd already have classified Master's fossils! And Master's babirusa would be settled in its cage at the Jardin des Plantes, drawing every curious soul in the capital!"

"Just so, Conseil, and — not to mention, I imagine — that they'll all be laughing at us!"

"Quite so," Conseil answered tranquilly. "I do think they'll laugh at Master. And — must I say it...?"

"You must say it, Conseil."

"Well then, Master will have no more than he deserves!"

"Really!"

"When one has the honor of being a scholar like Master, one doesn't lay oneself open to —"

Conseil couldn't finish his compliment. In the midst of the general silence, a voice had just made itself heard. It was the voice of Ned Land, and Ned Land was shouting:

"Ahoy! The thing in question, to leeward, off our beam!"
