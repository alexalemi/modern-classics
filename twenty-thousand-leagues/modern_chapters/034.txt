The Undersea Coal Mines

The next day, February 20, I woke very late. The exhaustion of the night had stretched my sleep until eleven o'clock. I dressed quickly. I was eager to know which way the Nautilus was heading. The instruments told me she was still running south at a speed of twenty miles an hour, at a depth of a hundred meters.

Conseil came in. I told him about our nighttime excursion, and since the panels were open, he could still catch a glimpse of that submerged continent.

The Nautilus was in fact skimming barely ten meters above the floor of the Atlantis plain. She glided along like a balloon carried on the wind above the meadows of the earth — though it would be truer to say that we sat in that salon as if in the car of an express train. The nearest features rushing past our eyes were rocks carved into fantastic shapes, forests of trees that had passed from the vegetable kingdom into the mineral, their motionless silhouettes grimacing beneath the waves. There were stony masses buried under carpets of sea squirts and anemones, bristling with tall vertical water-plants, and then blocks of lava strangely twisted, testifying to all the fury of the earth's volcanic upheavals.

While these bizarre landscapes blazed under our electric beams, I told Conseil the story of these Atlanteans, who — on the purely imaginary side of things — inspired so many charming pages in Bailly. I described to him the wars of those heroic peoples. I argued the question of Atlantis like a man who can no longer doubt. But Conseil, distracted, was hardly listening, and his indifference to this historical matter was soon explained to me.

The fact was that a great many fish were catching his eye, and whenever fish went by, Conseil, swept off into the abysses of classification, took leave of the real world. When that happened, I had nothing to do but follow him and take up our ichthyological studies alongside him.

These fish of the Atlantic did not, for that matter, differ noticeably from those we had observed so far. There were rays of gigantic size, five meters long and endowed with such muscular power that it lets them leap clear of the waves; sharks of various species, among them a blue shark fifteen feet long, with sharp triangular teeth, whose transparency made it almost invisible in the water; brown dogfish; humantins shaped like prisms and armored in a warty hide; sturgeons like their cousins of the Mediterranean; and trumpet pipefish a foot and a half long, yellow-brown, fitted with little gray fins, without teeth or tongue, filing past like slender, supple serpents.

Among the bony fish, Conseil noted blackish marlins three meters long, armed on the upper jaw with a piercing sword; weevers, brightly colored, known in Aristotle's day as sea dragons, whose dorsal spines make them very dangerous to seize; then dolphinfish, their brown backs striped with fine blue lines and framed in a border of gold; beautiful sea bream; moonfish, a kind of disc with azure gleams that, lit from above by the sun's rays, looked like patches of silver; and finally swordfish eight meters long, traveling in schools, carrying yellowish fins cut like scythes and long six-foot blades — intrepid creatures, more herbivorous than fish-eating, obeying the least sign from their females like well-trained husbands.

But even as I studied these various specimens of marine life, I did not stop examining the long plains of Atlantis. Now and then some capricious irregularity of the ground forced the Nautilus to slow down, and she would slip with the skill of a whale through narrow bottlenecks between the hills. If the maze grew impassable, the craft rose like a balloon, and once the obstacle was cleared she resumed her swift course a few meters above the floor. An admirable, delightful way to travel, calling to mind the maneuvers of a balloon voyage — with this one difference, that the Nautilus obeyed, passively and precisely, the hand of her helmsman.

Toward four in the afternoon, the terrain — generally made up of a thick ooze mixed with mineralized branches — changed little by little. It grew rockier and seemed strewn with conglomerates, with basaltic tuffs, and here and there with scatterings of lava and sulfurous obsidian. I judged that a region of mountains was about to succeed the long plains, and sure enough, as the Nautilus swung through certain maneuvers, I saw the southern horizon barred by a high wall that seemed to shut off every exit. Its summit clearly rose above the level of the ocean. It had to be a continent, or at the very least an island — one of the Canaries, perhaps, or one of the Cape Verde group. Since our position had not been taken — deliberately, perhaps — I had no idea where we were. In any case, such a wall seemed to me to mark the end of this Atlantis, of which, all told, we had crossed only a tiny portion.

Night did not interrupt my observations. I had stayed on alone. Conseil had gone back to his cabin. The Nautilus, slackening her pace, hovered above the confused masses of the sea floor, now grazing them as though she meant to settle there, now rising capriciously toward the surface of the waves. Through the crystal of the waters I would then catch sight of a few brilliant constellations, and precisely five or six of those zodiacal stars that trail at the tail of Orion.

I would have lingered a long while yet at my window, admiring the beauties of the sea and the sky, when the panels closed. At that moment the Nautilus had come up flush against the high wall. How she would maneuver, I could not guess. I went back to my room. The Nautilus was no longer moving. I fell asleep with the firm intention of waking after a few hours' rest.

But the next morning it was eight o'clock when I returned to the salon. I looked at the pressure gauge. It told me that the Nautilus was floating on the surface of the ocean. What was more, I heard the sound of footsteps on the platform. And yet not a roll betrayed the swell of the waves above.

I climbed to the hatch. It was open. But instead of the broad daylight I expected, I found myself surrounded by deep darkness. Where were we? Had I been mistaken? Was it still night? No! Not a single star was shining, and the night has no such absolute blackness.

I did not know what to think, when a voice said to me:

"Is that you, Professor?"

"Ah, Captain Nemo," I answered, "where are we?"

"Underground, Professor."

"Underground!" I cried. "And the Nautilus is still afloat?"

"She is always afloat."

"But I don't understand."

"Wait a few moments. Our beacon is about to be lit, and if you like a situation made clear, you'll be satisfied."

I set foot on the platform and waited. The darkness was so complete that I could not even make out Captain Nemo. Still, looking up at the zenith, exactly above my head, I thought I caught an uncertain gleam, a kind of half-light filling a circular hole. At that instant the beacon suddenly blazed on, and its brilliance made that vague light vanish.

I looked, after shutting for a moment my eyes dazzled by the electric flood. The Nautilus was stationary. She floated beside a bank arranged like a quay. This sea that bore her up at present was a lake imprisoned within a ring of walls measuring two miles across, or six miles around. Its level — the pressure gauge showed as much — could only be the level outside, for a passage necessarily existed between this lake and the sea. The high walls, sloping inward at their base, curved over into a vault and formed an immense inverted funnel, five or six hundred meters high. At the summit opened a circular mouth through which I had glimpsed that faint clarity, plainly the light of day.

Before examining more closely the interior arrangement of this enormous cavern, before asking myself whether it was the work of nature or of man, I went over to Captain Nemo.

"Where are we?" I said.

"At the very heart of an extinct volcano," the captain answered, "a volcano whose interior the sea invaded after some convulsion of the ground. While you were sleeping, Professor, the Nautilus entered this lagoon through a natural channel that opens ten meters below the surface of the ocean. This is her home port — a safe, convenient, secret harbor, sheltered from every point of the compass. Find me, on the coasts of your continents or your islands, a roadstead to match this refuge against the fury of the hurricanes."

"You're right," I answered. "Here you are safe, Captain Nemo. Who could reach you at the heart of a volcano? But at its summit — didn't I see an opening?"

"Yes, its crater, a crater once brimming with lava, vapors, and flame, and which now gives passage to the life-giving air we breathe."

"But what volcanic mountain is this?" I asked.

"It belongs to one of the many islets with which this sea is sown. A mere reef for ships, for us an immense cavern. Chance let me discover it, and in that, chance served me well."

"But couldn't a person climb down through that mouth that forms the crater of the volcano?"

"No more than I could climb up it. Up to about a hundred feet, the inner base of this mountain can be walked; but above that the walls overhang, and their slopes could never be scaled."

"I see, Captain, that nature serves you everywhere and always. You are safe on this lake, and no one but you can visit its waters. But what good is this refuge? The Nautilus needs no port."

"No, Professor, but she needs electricity to move, elements to produce her electricity, sodium to feed her elements, coal to make her sodium, and coal mines to dig out her coal. Now, precisely here, the sea covers whole forests that were engulfed in geological ages; mineralized now and turned to coal, they are for me an inexhaustible mine."

"So your men, Captain, work here as miners?"

"Exactly. These mines stretch out beneath the waves like the coalfields of Newcastle. It is here that, clad in the diving suit, pick and mattock in hand, my men go to dig out this coal — which I have never had to ask of any mine on land. When I burn this fuel to make sodium, the smoke that escapes through the crater of this mountain still gives it the look of a volcano in eruption."

"And will we see them at work, your crew?"

"No, not this time, at least, for I'm in a hurry to continue our tour of the underwater world. So I'll content myself with drawing on the reserves of sodium I already have. The time to load them — a single day, that is — and we'll be on our way again. If you'd like to explore this cavern and walk around the lagoon, take advantage of this day, Professor Aronnax."

I thanked the captain and went to fetch my two companions, who had not yet left their cabin. I invited them to follow me without telling them where they were.

They climbed onto the platform. Conseil, whom nothing ever astonished, thought it a perfectly natural thing to wake up beneath a mountain after falling asleep beneath the waves. But Ned Land had no other idea in his head than to see whether the cavern offered any way out.

After breakfast, around ten o'clock, we went down onto the bank.

"So here we are on dry land once more," said Conseil.

"I don't call this 'land,'" the Canadian retorted. "And besides, we're not on it, we're under it."

Between the foot of the mountain walls and the waters of the lake stretched a sandy shore that, at its widest, measured five hundred feet. Along this strand one could easily make the circuit of the lake. But the base of the high walls formed a tortured ground, on which lay, in picturesque heaps, volcanic blocks and enormous pumice stones. All these crumbled masses, coated with a polished glaze under the action of the underground fires, blazed at the touch of the beacon's electric jets. The mica-flecked dust of the shore, kicked up by our steps, flew off like a cloud of sparks.

The ground rose noticeably as we drew away from the water's edge, and we soon reached long, winding ramps — genuine steep tracks that let us climb little by little; but one had to walk carefully among these conglomerates, bound together by no cement, and the foot kept slipping on the glassy trachytes, made of crystals of feldspar and quartz.

The volcanic nature of this enormous excavation asserted itself on every side. I pointed it out to my companions.

"Can you picture," I asked them, "what this funnel must have been when it filled with boiling lava, and the level of that incandescent liquid rose to the mouth of the mountain, like molten iron up the sides of a furnace?"

"I picture it perfectly," Conseil answered. "But will Master tell me why the great smelter suspended his operation, and how it comes about that the furnace has been replaced by the peaceful waters of a lake?"

"Most likely, Conseil, because some convulsion produced, below the surface of the ocean, this opening that served as a passage for the Nautilus. Then the waters of the Atlantic rushed into the interior of the mountain. There was a terrible struggle between the two elements, a struggle that ended to Neptune's advantage. But many centuries have passed since then, and the submerged volcano has changed into a peaceful grotto."

"Very good," Ned Land replied. "I accept the explanation, but I regret, for our own sakes, that this opening the Professor speaks of wasn't made above the level of the sea."

"But, friend Ned," Conseil put in, "if this passage hadn't been underwater, the Nautilus couldn't have entered it!"

"And I'll add, Master Land, that the waters wouldn't have rushed in beneath the mountain, and the volcano would have stayed a volcano. So your regrets are wasted."

Our climb went on. The ramps grew steeper and narrower by degrees. Deep gullies cut across them now and then, which had to be crossed. Overhanging masses had to be worked around. We slid along on our knees, we crawled on our bellies. But with Conseil's agility and the Canadian's strength to help, every obstacle was overcome.

At a height of about thirty meters, the nature of the ground changed, without becoming any easier to walk. Black basalts succeeded the conglomerates and trachytes — some spread in sheets all pocked with blowholes, others forming regular prisms, arranged like a colonnade that bore up the springings of this immense vault, an admirable specimen of nature's architecture. Then, between these basalts, wound long flows of cooled lava, encrusted with streaks of bitumen, and here and there spread broad carpets of sulfur. A stronger daylight, entering through the crater above, flooded with a hazy clarity all these volcanic ejecta, forever buried in the bosom of the extinct mountain.

Before long, however, our upward march was halted, at a height of about two hundred and fifty feet, by impassable obstacles. The inner curve of the vault came back in an overhang, and the climb had to turn into a walk around the rim. At this last level the vegetable kingdom began to contend with the mineral. A few shrubs, and even certain trees, sprang from the crevices of the wall. I recognized spurges letting their caustic sap run out. Heliotropes, quite unfit to justify their name, since the sun's rays never reached them, drooped sadly their clusters of flowers with colors and scents half faded away. Here and there a few chrysanthemums pushed up timidly at the foot of long-leaved aloes, sickly and forlorn. But between the lava flows I caught sight of little violets, still perfumed with a faint scent, and I confess I breathed them in with delight. Fragrance is the soul of the flower, and the flowers of the sea, those splendid water-plants, have no soul!

We had reached the foot of a clump of sturdy dragon trees, which were prying apart the rocks with the effort of their muscular roots, when Ned Land cried out:

"Ah! Professor, a beehive!"

"A beehive!" I replied, with a gesture of perfect disbelief.

"Yes! a beehive," the Canadian repeated, "and bees buzzing around it."

I went closer and had to face the facts. There, at the mouth of a hole bored into the trunk of a dragon tree, were several thousand of these ingenious insects, so common all through the Canaries, and whose product is especially prized there.

Naturally the Canadian meant to lay in a store of honey, and it would have been churlish of me to object. A certain quantity of dry leaves mixed with sulfur was set alight under the spark of his tinderbox, and he began smoking out the bees. The buzzing died away little by little, and the gutted hive yielded several pounds of fragrant honey. Ned Land filled his knapsack with it.

"Once I've mixed this honey with breadfruit paste," he told us, "I'll be in a position to offer you a scrumptious cake."

"By heaven!" said Conseil, "it'll be gingerbread."

"Gingerbread it is," I said, "but let's get on with this interesting walk."

At certain turns of the path we were now following, the lake came into view in its whole extent. The beacon lit up entirely its tranquil surface, which knew neither ripples nor swell. The Nautilus kept a perfect stillness. On her platform and along the bank the men of her crew were moving about, black shadows sharply cut out in that luminous atmosphere.

Just then we were rounding the highest crest of these first tiers of rock that held up the vault. I saw then that the bees were not the only representatives of the animal kingdom inside this volcano. Birds of prey soared and wheeled here and there in the shadow, or fled from their nests perched on points of rock. These were white-bellied hawks, and screeching kestrels. On the slopes there also scampered off, with all the speed of their long legs, fine plump bustards. I leave you to imagine how the Canadian's appetite was kindled at the sight of this tasty game, and how he regretted not having a rifle in his hands. He tried to make stones do the work of buckshot, and after several fruitless attempts he managed to wound one of these magnificent bustards. To say that he risked his life twenty times to get hold of it is only the plain truth, but he managed so well that the creature went to join the honeycakes in his bag.

We then had to go back down toward the shore, for the crest was becoming impassable. Above us the gaping crater looked like the wide mouth of a well. From this spot the sky could be made out quite clearly, and I watched clouds go racing by, tousled by the west wind, trailing their misty rags right down to the summit of the mountain. Sure proof that these clouds hung at no great height, for the volcano rose no more than eight hundred feet above the level of the ocean.

Half an hour after the Canadian's last exploit, we had regained the inner shore. Here the flora was represented by broad carpets of that samphire, a small umbelliferous plant very good for pickling, which also goes by the names of sea-fennel and rock-samphire. Conseil gathered a few bunches of it. As for the fauna, it counted crustaceans of every sort by the thousand: lobsters, brown crabs, prawns, mysid shrimp, sand-hoppers, squat lobsters, and a prodigious number of shellfish — cowries, murex shells, and limpets.

At this spot a magnificent grotto opened up. My companions and I took pleasure in stretching out on its fine sand. Fire had polished its enameled, glittering walls, all dusted with the powder of mica. Ned Land was feeling the walls and trying to sound their thickness. I could not help smiling. The conversation then turned to his eternal projects of escape, and I felt I could, without going too far, hold out this hope to him: that Captain Nemo had gone south only to renew his supply of sodium. So I hoped that now he would head back toward the coasts of Europe and America, which would let the Canadian take up his botched attempt again with more chance of success.

We had been stretched out an hour in this charming grotto. The conversation, lively at the start, was flagging by then. A certain drowsiness was creeping over us. Since I saw no reason to resist sleep, I let myself drift into a deep torpor. I dreamed — one does not choose one's dreams — I dreamed that my existence had been reduced to the vegetative life of a simple mollusk. It seemed to me that this grotto formed the two valves of my shell...

All at once I was awakened by Conseil's voice.

"Alarm! Alarm!" the worthy fellow was shouting.

"What is it?" I asked, half rising.

"The water's coming up on us!"

I sat bolt upright. The sea was pouring like a torrent into our retreat, and since we most decidedly were not mollusks, we had to get out.

In a few moments we were safe on the very top of the grotto.

"What on earth is happening?" asked Conseil. "Some new phenomenon?"

"Why no, my friends," I answered. "It's the tide, only the tide, which nearly caught us out like the hero of Walter Scott! The ocean is swelling outside, and by a perfectly natural law of equilibrium, the level of the lake is rising too. We've got off with a half-bath. Let's go change on the Nautilus."

Three quarters of an hour later we had finished our circular walk and were back on board. The men of the crew were just then finishing loading the stores of sodium, and the Nautilus could have left at once.

Yet Captain Nemo gave no order. Did he mean to wait for night and slip out in secret through his underwater passage? Perhaps.

Be that as it may, the next day the Nautilus, having left her home port, was sailing well clear of any land, a few meters below the waves of the Atlantic.
