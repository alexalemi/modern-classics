Ned Land's Tempers

How long that sleep lasted, I have no idea; but it must have been a long one, for it left us fully rested from our exhaustion. I was the first to wake. My companions hadn't stirred yet, and lay stretched out in their corner like two inert masses.

I had barely risen from that passably hard bunk when I felt my head clear and my mind sharpen. I set about examining our cell all over again.

Nothing about its interior arrangements had changed. The prison was still a prison, and the prisoners were still prisoners. Only, the steward had taken advantage of our sleep to clear the table. So nothing pointed to any imminent change in our situation, and I began to wonder in all seriousness whether we were fated to spend the rest of our days in this cage.

That prospect struck me as all the more unpleasant because, although my brain was free of the previous night's obsessions, I now felt a curious tightness in my chest. My breathing was coming with difficulty. The heavy air was no longer enough for my lungs to work with. Roomy as the cell was, it was plain that we had used up most of the oxygen it held. In fact, each man consumes in one hour the oxygen contained in a hundred liters of air, and that air, by then loaded with a nearly equal quantity of carbon dioxide, becomes unbreathable.

It was therefore urgent to renew the atmosphere of our prison — and no doubt the atmosphere of the underwater boat as well.

That raised a question in my mind. How did the commander of this floating dwelling manage it? Did he obtain air by chemical means, driving off with heat the oxygen locked in potassium chlorate and absorbing the carbon dioxide with caustic potash? In that case he would have had to keep up some contact with the continents in order to procure the materials for the process. Did he simply store air under high pressure in tanks and then release it as his crew required? Perhaps. Or — a more convenient method, more economical, and therefore more likely — did he content himself with rising to the surface of the sea to breathe, like a cetacean, renewing his supply of atmosphere every twenty-four hours? However he did it, whatever the method, it seemed to me only prudent that he employ it without delay.

Indeed, I was already reduced to quickening my breaths to draw out what little oxygen the cell still held when, all at once, I was refreshed by a current of pure air, fragrant with a salty tang. It was the sea breeze itself, bracing and heavy with iodine! I opened my mouth wide, and my lungs drank in the fresh molecules. At the same moment I felt a swaying, a gentle roll of no great amplitude but perfectly unmistakable. The boat — the monster of sheet iron — had evidently just come up to the surface of the ocean to breathe, whale-fashion. So the ship's ventilation method was now perfectly understood.

Once I had filled my chest with that pure air, I looked for the duct — the "airway," if you like — that let this reviving flow reach us, and I soon found it. Above the door opened a ventilation hole that let through a cool column of air, renewing the cell's depleted atmosphere.

I had gotten that far in my observations when Ned and Conseil woke almost at the same instant, roused by that revitalizing draft. They rubbed their eyes, stretched their arms, and were on their feet in a moment.

"Did Master sleep well?" Conseil asked me, with his everyday courtesy.

"Very well, my good fellow," I answered. "And you, Ned Land?"

"Like a log, Professor. But unless I'm mistaken, I could swear I'm breathing a sea breeze?"

A sailor couldn't be wrong about that, and I told the Canadian what had happened while he slept.

"Well now!" he said. "That explains those bellowing noises we heard whenever the so-called narwhal came in sight of the Abraham Lincoln."

"Exactly, Ned. It was the thing breathing!"

"Only, Professor Aronnax, I've no idea what time it is — unless it happens to be dinnertime?"

"Dinnertime, my worthy harpooner? Say breakfast time, at the very least, for we've certainly reached the day after yesterday."

"Which proves," Conseil put in, "that we've had twenty-four hours' sleep."

"That's my reckoning," I answered.

"I won't argue the point," Ned Land replied. "But dinner or breakfast, the steward will be welcome whichever one he brings."

"Both," said Conseil.

"Just so," said the Canadian. "We've a right to two meals, and for my part I'll do justice to the pair of them."

"All right, Ned, let's wait," I answered. "It's clear these strangers don't mean to let us starve — otherwise last night's supper would make no sense."

"Unless they're fattening us up!" Ned shot back.

"I object," I answered. "We haven't fallen into the hands of cannibals!"

"Once is no habit," the Canadian replied gravely. "Who's to say these people haven't gone a long time without fresh meat? And in that case, three sound, well-built specimens like the Professor here, his servant, and me—"

"Get those ideas out of your head, Ned," I told the harpooner, "and above all, don't let them set you off against our hosts. That would only make matters worse."

"In any case," said the harpooner, "I've got the devil's own hunger, and dinner or breakfast, the meal's slow in coming!"

"Ned," I replied, "we have to conform to the ship's schedule, and I suspect our stomachs are running fast against the cook's dinner bell."

"Well then, we'll set them right," Conseil said calmly.

"There you are, friend Conseil, that's you all over," the impatient Canadian retorted. "You never waste your temper or your nerves! Always calm! You'd be capable of saying your thanks before you'd said grace, and starving to death rather than complaining!"

"What good would it do?" asked Conseil.

"It would do the good of complaining! That's already something. And if these pirates — I say pirates out of respect, and so as not to cross the Professor, who forbids me to call them cannibals — if these pirates imagine they're going to keep me shut up in this cage where I'm suffocating, without hearing the kind of oaths I season my rages with, they're mistaken! Come now, Professor Aronnax, speak frankly. Do you really think they'll hold us long in this iron box?"

"To tell the truth, I know no more about it than you do, Ned."

"But come, what's your guess?"

"My guess is that chance has made us masters of an important secret. Now, the crew of this underwater boat has an interest in keeping that secret, and if that interest outweighs the lives of three men, then I believe our existence is very much in danger. If not, then at the first opportunity the monster that swallowed us will hand us back to the world our own kind lives in."

"Unless it enrolls us among its crew," said Conseil, "and keeps us that way—"

"Right up until," Ned Land replied, "some frigate faster or cleverer than the Abraham Lincoln captures this nest of cutthroats and sends its crew — and us along with it — to breathe our last from the end of its mainyard."

"Well reasoned, Ned," I replied. "But so far, as far as I know, no one has made us any such offer. No use, then, debating what course to take should the occasion arise. I say again: let's wait, let's take our cue from circumstances, and let's do nothing, since there's nothing to be done."

"On the contrary, Professor!" answered the harpooner, who wouldn't let it go. "We've got to do something."

"Do what, Ned?"

"Escape."

"Escaping from a prison on land is often difficult enough, but from an underwater prison strikes me as flat-out impossible."

"Come on, friend Ned," Conseil asked, "what's your answer to Master's objection? I can't believe an American is ever at the end of his resources!"

The harpooner, plainly embarrassed, said nothing. Flight, given the circumstances chance had thrown us into, was absolutely impossible. But a Canadian is half a Frenchman, and Ned Land made that plain enough by his reply.

"So, Professor Aronnax," he went on after a few moments' thought, "you can't guess what people ought to do when they can't break out of their prison?"

"No, my friend."

"Simple enough. They fix things so as to stay in it."

"Good grief!" said Conseil. "Better to be inside than above or below!"

"But only after throwing out the jailers, the turnkeys, and the guards," Ned Land added.

"What, Ned? You'd seriously think of seizing this vessel?"

"Very seriously," answered the Canadian.

"It's impossible."

"Why so, Professor? Some lucky chance might turn up, and I don't see what could stop us from taking advantage of it. If there aren't more than a score of men aboard this machine, they won't make two Frenchmen and a Canadian back down, I should think!"

Better to accept the harpooner's proposition than to argue it. So I contented myself with answering:

"Let's wait and see how things develop, Ned, and we'll see. But until then, I beg you, hold your impatience in check. We can only act by cunning, and you won't bring lucky chances into being by flying off the handle. Promise me, then, that you'll accept the situation without too much anger."

"I promise you, Professor," Ned Land answered, in a tone that was anything but reassuring. "Not a violent word will pass my lips, not a brutal gesture will give me away — not even if the meals aren't served with all the regularity a man could wish."

"I have your word, Ned," I said to the Canadian.

Then the conversation lapsed, and each of us fell to thinking on his own. For my part, I'll admit that, despite the harpooner's assurances, I kept no illusions. I put no stock in those lucky chances Ned Land had spoken of. To be handled so surely, the underwater boat required a large crew, and consequently, in the event of a fight, we would be up against too strong a force. Besides, before anything else we would have to be free, and we weren't. I couldn't see any way at all of escaping this cell of sheet iron, sealed so tightly shut. And if this strange commander had a secret to keep — which seemed at the very least probable — he wouldn't let us move about freely aboard his ship. Now, would he rid himself of us by violence, or would he set us down some day on some corner of dry land? That was the unknown. All these hypotheses struck me as extremely plausible, and one had to be a harpooner to hope for the return of one's freedom.

I understood, moreover, that Ned Land's mood was souring as the thoughts crowded in on his brain. Little by little I could hear the curses rumbling in the back of his throat, and I saw his gestures turn menacing again. He would get up, pace like a wild beast in a cage, kick and punch at the walls. Meanwhile time was passing, hunger was making itself cruelly felt, and this time the steward did not appear. And it was leaving us castaways forgotten too long, if anyone really did have good intentions toward us.

Ned Land, tormented by the pangs of his sturdy stomach, was working himself up more and more, and, for all his promise, I genuinely feared an explosion the moment he came face to face with one of the men aboard.

For two more hours, Ned Land's fury mounted. The Canadian called out, he shouted, but in vain. The iron walls were deaf. I couldn't hear so much as a sound inside this boat, which seemed to lie dead. It wasn't moving, for I would surely have felt the hull shudder under the drive of the propeller. Plunged, no doubt, into the depths of the waters, it no longer belonged to the earth. That whole gloomy silence was terrifying.

As for how long we might be left abandoned, isolated at the bottom of this cell, I didn't dare guess. The hopes I had conceived after our meeting with the ship's commander were fading little by little. The gentleness of that man's gaze, the generous cast of his features, the nobility of his bearing — all of it was vanishing from my memory. I saw this enigmatic figure again as he must necessarily be: pitiless, cruel. I felt him to be outside humanity, closed to any feeling of mercy, an implacable enemy of his own kind, toward whom he must have sworn an undying hatred.

But this man — was he really going to let us die of starvation, shut up in this narrow prison, given over to those horrible temptations that savage hunger drives one to? That frightful thought took on a terrible intensity in my mind, and with my imagination feeding it, I felt myself seized by a senseless dread. Conseil stayed calm. Ned Land roared.

At that moment, a sound was heard outside.

Footsteps rang on the metal floor. The locks were worked, the door swung open, and the steward appeared.

Before I could make a move to stop him, the Canadian had hurled himself on the poor man; he had knocked him down; he had him by the throat. The steward was choking under that powerful grip.

Conseil was already trying to pry the harpooner's half-suffocated victim out of his hands, and I was about to join my efforts to his, when I was suddenly nailed to the spot by these words, spoken in French:

"Calm yourself, Ned Land, and you, Professor, be so good as to hear me out!"
