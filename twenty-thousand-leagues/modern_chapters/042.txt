From Cape Horn to the Amazon

How I came to be up on the platform, I couldn't say. Perhaps the Canadian had carried me there. But I was breathing — I was drinking in the living air of the sea. Beside me my two companions were drunk on those cool, fresh molecules. Poor wretches too long starved of food can't fling themselves recklessly on the first meal set before them; but we had no need to hold back. We could fill our lungs to bursting with the atoms of that atmosphere, and it was the breeze, the breeze itself, that poured this voluptuous intoxication into us.

"Ah," Conseil kept saying, "oxygen — how good it is! Master needn't be afraid to breathe. There's plenty for everyone."

As for Ned Land, he said nothing, but he opened jaws wide enough to frighten a shark. And what mighty inhalations! The Canadian "drew" like a stove in full blaze.

Our strength came back to us quickly, and when I looked around I saw that we were alone on the platform. Not a single crewman. Not even Captain Nemo. The strange sailors of the Nautilus made do with the air circulating inside; none had come up to revel in the open atmosphere.

The first words I spoke were words of thanks and gratitude to my two companions. Ned and Conseil had stretched out my life through the last hours of that long agony. No amount of gratitude could ever repay such devotion.

"Come now, Professor," Ned Land answered me, "it's not worth talking about! What was there to it, on our part? Nothing at all. It was purely a matter of arithmetic. Your life was worth more than ours. So it had to be preserved."

"No, Ned," I replied, "it wasn't worth more. No one stands above a generous, good-hearted man — and that's what you are."

"All right, all right," repeated the Canadian, embarrassed.

"And you, my brave Conseil, you suffered badly too."

"Not so very badly, if I'm to tell Master the whole truth. I was short a few mouthfuls of air, but I think I'd have gotten used to it. Besides, I kept my eyes on Master, who was fainting away, and that took away any least desire I had to breathe. It fairly cut off my — as they say — my wind..."

Confused at having stumbled into a cliché, Conseil didn't finish.

"My friends," I answered, deeply moved, "we are bound to one another forever, and you have claims on me —"

"Which I intend to abuse," the Canadian shot back.

"Eh?" said Conseil.

"Yes," Ned Land went on, "the claim of dragging you off with me the day I leave this infernal Nautilus."

"For that matter," said Conseil, "are we heading the right way?"

"Yes," I replied, "since we're heading toward the sun, and here the sun means north."

"No doubt," said Ned Land, "but the question is still whether we're making for the Pacific or the Atlantic — that is, for the busy seas or the empty ones."

To that I had no answer, and I feared Captain Nemo might rather be taking us back toward that vast ocean that washes the coasts of both Asia and America at once. He would thus complete his round-the-world voyage beneath the sea and return to those seas where the Nautilus found its most perfect independence. But if we went back to the Pacific, far from any inhabited land, what became of Ned Land's plans?

We were soon to be settled on that important point. The Nautilus was traveling fast. The polar circle was quickly crossed, and the course laid for the promontory of Cape Horn. We were abreast of the American headland on March 31, at seven in the evening.

By then all our past sufferings were forgotten. The memory of that imprisonment in the ice was fading from our minds. We thought only of the future. Captain Nemo no longer appeared, either in the salon or on the platform. The daily position, plotted on the world chart by the first officer, let me determine the exact heading of the Nautilus. And that evening it became clear, to my great satisfaction, that we were coming back north by the Atlantic route.

I told the Canadian and Conseil the result of my observations.

"Good news," the Canadian answered, "but where is the Nautilus going?"

"I couldn't say, Ned."

"Could her captain be meaning, after the South Pole, to take on the North Pole and get back to the Pacific by the famous Northwest Passage?"

"I wouldn't put it past him," said Conseil.

"Well," said the Canadian, "we'll have given him the slip before then."

"In any case," Conseil added, "he's a real master of a man, this Captain Nemo, and we'll never regret having known him."

"Especially once we've left him!" Ned Land retorted.

The next day, April 1, a few minutes before noon, when the Nautilus rose to the surface of the waves, we sighted a coast to the west. It was Tierra del Fuego — the Land of Fire — so named by the first navigators when they saw the many columns of smoke rising from the natives' huts. This Tierra del Fuego forms a vast cluster of islands stretching thirty leagues long and eighty leagues wide, between 53° and 56° of southern latitude and 67°50′ and 77°15′ of western longitude. The coast looked low to me, but far off rose high mountains. I even thought I glimpsed Mount Sarmiento, rising two thousand and seventy meters above sea level — a pyramidal block of schist with a very sharp summit which, depending on whether it is veiled or clear of mist, "foretells fair weather or foul," Ned Land told me.

"A famous barometer, my friend."

"Yes, Professor, a natural barometer that never once deceived me when I sailed the channels of the Strait of Magellan."

Just then the peak stood out sharp against the sky. It was an omen of fine weather. And fine weather it was.

Back beneath the water, the Nautilus drew close to the coast and ran along it only a few miles off. Through the salon windows I saw long lianas and giant kelp — those pear-bearing wracks of which the free polar sea had held a few specimens. With their glossy, slimy filaments, they measured up to three hundred meters in length; genuine cables, thicker than a thumb and very tough, they often serve as mooring lines for ships. Another weed, known as kelp velp, with leaves four feet long, matted into the coral concretions, carpeted the seabed. It served as nest and food to myriads of crustaceans and mollusks — crabs, cuttlefish. There the seals and otters treated themselves to splendid meals, mixing fish flesh with the vegetables of the sea, after the English fashion.

Over these rich, luxuriant depths the Nautilus passed at extreme speed. Toward evening it approached the Falkland archipelago, whose harsh peaks I was able to make out the next day. The sea there was of only moderate depth. I concluded, and not without reason, that these two islands, ringed by a great many islets, had once formed part of the Magellanic lands. The Falklands were probably discovered by the famous John Davis, who gave them the name Davis Southern Islands. Later, Richard Hawkins called them the Maiden Islands, the Isles of the Virgin. They were afterward named the Malouines, at the start of the eighteenth century, by fishermen of Saint-Malo, and finally the Falklands by the English, to whom they belong today.

In these waters our nets brought up fine specimens of seaweed, and in particular a certain kelp whose roots were laden with mussels — the best in the world. Geese and ducks dropped by the dozen onto the platform and soon took their places in the ship's larders. As for fish, I especially noted bony species belonging to the goby genus, and above all some gudgeons two decimeters long, all speckled with whitish and yellow spots.

I also admired numerous jellyfish, and the loveliest of their kind — the chrysaoras peculiar to the seas of the Falklands. At times they formed a very smooth hemispherical umbrella streaked with reddish-brown lines and fringed with twelve regular festoons; at other times they were an upturned basket from which broad leaves and long red streamers gracefully spilled. They swam by waving their four leaf-shaped arms and let their opulent mane of tentacles drift and dangle behind them. I would have liked to keep a few samples of these delicate zoophytes; but they are only clouds, shadows, mere appearances, that melt and evaporate out of their native element.

When the last heights of the Falklands had dropped below the horizon, the Nautilus submerged to between twenty and twenty-five meters and followed the American coast. Captain Nemo did not show himself.

Until April 3 we did not leave the waters of Patagonia, now beneath the ocean, now on its surface. The Nautilus passed the wide estuary formed by the mouth of the Plata and, on April 4, found itself abreast of Uruguay, though fifty miles out to sea. Its heading held to the north, following the long windings of South America. By then we had covered sixteen thousand leagues since we first came aboard in the seas of Japan.

Around eleven in the morning, the Tropic of Capricorn was crossed on the thirty-seventh meridian, and we passed well off Cape Frio. Captain Nemo, much to Ned Land's displeasure, had no liking for the neighborhood of these inhabited coasts of Brazil, for he ran at a dizzying speed. Not a single fish, not a single bird, however swift, could keep up with us, and the natural curiosities of these seas escaped all observation.

This pace held up for several days, and on the evening of April 9 we sighted the easternmost point of South America, which forms Cape São Roque. But then the Nautilus veered off again, and went hunting at greater depths for an undersea valley that gouges its way between this cape and Sierra Leone on the African coast. This valley forks at the latitude of the Antilles and ends in the north in an enormous depression nine thousand meters deep. Here the geological cross-section of the ocean shows, as far as the Lesser Antilles, a sheer cliff six kilometers high, cut straight down, and, at the latitude of the Cape Verde Islands, another wall no less formidable — the two of them thus enclosing the whole submerged continent of Atlantis. The floor of this immense valley is broken by a few mountains that lend a picturesque aspect to these submarine depths. I speak of it mainly on the strength of the manuscript charts in the Nautilus's library — charts plainly drawn by Captain Nemo's own hand and mapped from his personal observations.

For two days these deserted and deep waters were visited by means of the inclined planes. The Nautilus made long diagonal tacks that carried it to every level. But on April 11 it rose abruptly, and land reappeared to us at the mouth of the Amazon River — a vast estuary whose outflow is so tremendous that it freshens the sea over a stretch of several leagues.

The equator was crossed. Twenty miles to the west lay the Guianas, French territory where we could have found an easy refuge. But the wind was blowing a stiff gale, and the furious waves would never have let a mere dinghy face them. Ned Land no doubt understood this, for he said nothing to me about it. On my side, I made no allusion to his plans of escape, for I had no wish to push him into some attempt that would inevitably have miscarried.

I easily made up for this delay with some interesting studies. During those two days, the 11th and 12th of April, the Nautilus did not leave the surface of the sea, and its trawl brought up a whole miraculous catch of zoophytes, fish, and reptiles.

A few zoophytes had been dredged up by the trawl's dragging chain. They were, for the most part, beautiful phyctallines belonging to the family of the actinidians, and among other species the Phyctalis protexta, native to this part of the ocean — a small cylindrical trunk trimmed with vertical lines and dotted with red points, crowned by a marvelous bloom of tentacles. As for the mollusks, they consisted of creatures I had already observed: turret shells; porphyry olive shells with regularly crisscrossed lines whose russet markings stood out vividly against a flesh-colored ground; fanciful pteroceras shells like petrified scorpions; translucent hyala; argonauts; cuttlefish excellent to eat; and certain species of squid that the naturalists of antiquity classed among the flying fish, and which are used chiefly as bait for cod fishing.

Of the fish of these waters that I had not yet had occasion to study, I noted several species. Among the cartilaginous ones: pricka lampreys, a kind of eel fifteen inches long, with a greenish head, violet fins, a bluish-gray back, and a silvery-brown belly strewn with bright spots, the iris of the eye ringed with gold — curious animals that the current of the Amazon must have swept out to sea, for they inhabit fresh water; tuberculate rays with a pointed snout, a long slender tail, and armed with a long serrated spine; small sharks a meter long, gray and whitish of skin, whose teeth, set in several rows, curve backward, and which are commonly known as pantouffliers, or "slipper sharks"; bat-fish, a kind of reddish isosceles triangle half a meter across, whose pectoral fins are attached by fleshy extensions that give them the look of bats, but whose horny appendage near the nostrils has earned them the nickname sea unicorns; and finally a few species of triggerfish — the curassavian, whose stippled flanks gleam with a brilliant golden color, and the pale-violet leatherjacket, iridescent in shifting hues like a pigeon's throat.

I'll end this somewhat dry but very exact nomenclature with the series of bony fish I observed: passans, of the aptenote genus, whose snout is very blunt and snow-white, the body painted a fine black, and which are furnished with a very long, very slender fleshy thong; spiny odontognaths, long sardines three decimeters in length, resplendent with a vivid silvery gleam; guaru mackerel, provided with two anal fins; black centronotes with dark tints, caught with lighted torches — long fish two meters in length, with fatty, white, firm flesh that, eaten fresh, tastes like eel, and dried, like smoked salmon; half-red wrasses, clad in scales only at the base of the dorsal and anal fins; chrysopterans, on which gold and silver mingle their sheen with that of the ruby and the topaz; gilthead breams, whose flesh is extremely delicate and whose phosphorescent properties betray them amid the waters; pob breams, with a fine tongue and orange tints; coro croakers with golden tail fins; blackish surgeonfish; four-eyed fish of Surinam; and so on.

This "et cetera" won't keep me from mentioning one more fish that Conseil will remember for a long time, and with good reason.

One of our nets had brought up a sort of very flattened ray which, had its tail been cut off, would have formed a perfect disk, and which weighed some twenty kilograms. It was white below, reddish above, with big round spots of dark blue ringed with black, very smooth of skin, and ending in a two-lobed fin. Laid out on the platform, it thrashed about, tried to flip itself over with convulsive movements, and struggled so hard that a final jerk was about to hurl it back into the sea. But Conseil, who prized his fish, threw himself upon it, and before I could stop him, seized it with both hands.

In an instant there he was, knocked flat on his back, legs in the air, one half of his body paralyzed, and crying out:

"Oh, master! My master! Come help me!"

It was the first time the poor fellow had ever spoken to me without using the third person.

The Canadian and I picked him up, rubbed him down with all our might, and when he came to his senses, that eternal classifier murmured in a broken voice:

"Class of cartilaginous fish, order of chondropterygians with fixed gills, suborder of selacians, family of rays, genus of torpedoes!"

"Yes, my friend," I answered, "it's a torpedo ray that put you in this deplorable state."

"Ah, Master can take my word for it," Conseil shot back, "but I'll have my revenge on this animal."

"And how?"

"By eating it."

Which he did that very evening — but purely out of reprisal, for frankly it was tough.

The luckless Conseil had tangled with a torpedo ray of the most dangerous species, the cumana. This bizarre animal, in a conducting medium such as water, strikes down fish several meters away, so great is the power of its electric organ, whose two main surfaces measure no less than twenty-seven square feet.

The next day, April 12, during the daytime, the Nautilus approached the Dutch coast, toward the mouth of the Maroni. There, several groups of manatees lived in family bands. These were the manatis which, like the dugong and the Steller's sea cow, belong to the order of sirenians. These beautiful animals, peaceable and harmless, six or seven meters long, must have weighed at least four thousand kilograms. I told Ned Land and Conseil that provident nature had assigned these mammals an important role. It is they, in fact, who, like the seals, are meant to graze the submarine meadows and so destroy the masses of weed that clog the mouths of tropical rivers.

"And do you know," I added, "what has come about since men have almost wholly wiped out these useful races? The rotting weeds have poisoned the air, and the poisoned air is the yellow fever that ravages these admirable regions. The venomous vegetation has multiplied beneath these torrid seas, and the evil has spread irresistibly from the mouth of the Río de la Plata all the way to the Floridas!"

And if we're to believe Toussenel, this scourge is nothing yet beside the one that will strike our descendants when the seas are emptied of whales and seals. Then, choked with octopuses, jellyfish, and squid, they will become vast breeding grounds of infection, since their waters will no longer possess "those vast stomachs that God had charged with skimming the surface of the seas."

Meanwhile, without scorning these theories, the crew of the Nautilus took possession of half a dozen manatis. The point, in fact, was to stock the galley with an excellent meat, superior to beef and veal. This hunt was not an interesting one. The manatis let themselves be struck without defending themselves. Several thousand kilos of meat, destined to be dried, were stored aboard.

That same day, a catch made in a singular fashion further swelled the Nautilus's reserves, so teeming with game were these seas. The trawl had brought up in its meshes a number of fish whose heads ended in an oval plate with fleshy borders. These were remoras, of the third family of the subbrachian malacopterygians. Their flattened disk is made up of movable transverse cartilaginous plates, between which the animal can create a vacuum, enabling it to cling to objects the way a suction cup does.

The remora I had observed in the Mediterranean belongs to this species. But the one in question here was the bony-scaled remora, peculiar to this sea. Our sailors, as they took them, dropped them into tubs full of water.

The fishing done, the Nautilus drew nearer to the coast. In this spot a number of sea turtles were sleeping at the surface of the waves. It would have been difficult to seize these precious reptiles, for the slightest noise wakes them, and their sturdy shell is harpoon-proof. But the remora was to carry out this capture with extraordinary sureness and precision. This animal is, in fact, a living fishhook, one that would make the fortune and delight of the naive angler with his line.

The men of the Nautilus fastened to the tail of these fish a ring wide enough not to hamper their movements, and to that ring a long cord made fast at the other end to the ship.

The remoras, tossed into the sea, at once began their work and went to fix themselves to the breastplates of the turtles. Their grip was so tenacious that they would have torn apart sooner than let go. They were hauled aboard, and with them the turtles to which they clung.

In this way we took several loggerheads, a meter across, weighing two hundred kilos. Their shells, covered with large, thin, transparent, brown horny plates flecked with white and yellow, made them very valuable. Besides, they were excellent from the standpoint of eating, as were the green turtles, which are of an exquisite flavor.

This catch ended our stay in the waters off the Amazon, and, when night came, the Nautilus regained the open sea.
