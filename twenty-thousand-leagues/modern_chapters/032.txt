Vigo Bay

The Atlantic! That vast sheet of water, twenty-five million square miles across, nine thousand miles long and, on average, two thousand seven hundred wide. A great sea almost unknown to the ancients — except perhaps to the Carthaginians, those Dutchmen of antiquity, who on their trading voyages followed the western coasts of Europe and Africa. An ocean whose shores, winding in parallel curves, embrace an immense perimeter, watered by the greatest rivers in the world — the St. Lawrence, the Mississippi, the Amazon, the Plata, the Orinoco, the Niger, the Senegal, the Elbe, the Loire, the Rhine — bringing it the waters of the most civilized nations and the wildest lands alike. A magnificent plain, forever furrowed by the ships of every nation, sheltered under every flag in the world, and closed off at either end by those two dreaded headlands, the terror of navigators — Cape Horn and the Cape of Storms.

The Nautilus was cutting through its waters with the edge of its ram, having covered nearly ten thousand leagues in three and a half months — a run longer than one of the earth's great circles. Where were we bound now, and what did the future hold for us?

Out of the Strait of Gibraltar, the Nautilus had stood for the open sea. It rose back to the surface, and so our daily walks on the platform were restored to us.

I climbed up at once, with Ned Land and Conseil. About twelve miles off, Cape St. Vincent loomed vaguely — the southwestern tip of the Iberian peninsula. A fairly stiff wind was blowing from the south. The sea ran high and heavy, rolling the Nautilus in violent lurches. It was almost impossible to keep one's footing on the platform, which great masses of water swept over every few seconds. So, after a few good breaths of air, we went back down.

I returned to my cabin. Conseil went off to his, but the Canadian, looking distinctly preoccupied, followed me. Our quick dash across the Mediterranean had left him no chance to carry out his plans, and he made little effort to hide his disappointment.

When the door of my cabin had shut, he sat down and looked at me without a word.

"Ned, my friend," I said, "I understand you, but you've nothing to reproach yourself for. Given the way the Nautilus was traveling, to think of leaving it would have been madness."

Ned Land said nothing. His pressed lips and knitted brows marked a man in the grip of a single fixed idea.

"Come now," I went on, "nothing is lost yet. We're working our way up the coast of Portugal. France and England aren't far off — places where we could easily find refuge. If the Nautilus, coming out of Gibraltar, had headed south, if it had carried us off toward those regions where there are no continents, I'd share your worries. But we know now that Captain Nemo doesn't shun the civilized seas, and in a few days, I think you'll be able to act with some measure of safety."

Ned Land only stared at me harder, and at last unsealed his lips.

"It's for tonight," he said.

I sat bolt upright. I confess I was hardly prepared for this announcement. I wanted to answer the Canadian, but the words wouldn't come.

"We agreed to wait for the right circumstances," Ned Land went on. "Well, I've got them. Tonight we'll be only a few miles from the Spanish coast. It's a dark night. The wind's blowing off the sea. I have your word, Professor Aronnax, and I'm counting on you."

As I still kept silent, the Canadian rose and came closer.

"Tonight, at nine o'clock," he said. "I've warned Conseil. By then Captain Nemo will be shut up in his cabin, most likely asleep. Neither the engineers nor the crew can see us. Conseil and I will make our way to the central staircase. You, Professor Aronnax, will wait in the library, two steps from us, for my signal. The oars, the mast, and the sail are all in the dinghy. I've even managed to stow some provisions aboard. I've got hold of a wrench to unscrew the bolts that fasten the dinghy to the hull of the Nautilus. So everything's ready. Until tonight."

"The sea is rough," I said.

"I'll grant you that," the Canadian answered, "but we have to risk it. Freedom is worth the price. Besides, the boat is sound, and a few miles with a following wind are nothing to worry about. Who knows if by tomorrow we won't be a hundred leagues out to sea? Let circumstances favor us, and between ten and eleven o'clock we'll have landed somewhere on solid ground — or we'll be dead. So, God help us, and until tonight!"

With that the Canadian withdrew, leaving me all but stunned. I had imagined that, when the moment came, I'd have time to think, to talk it over. My stubborn companion wouldn't allow me that. And what, after all, would I have said to him? Ned Land was right a hundred times over. This was as good as the right circumstance, and he was seizing it. Could I go back on my word and take on myself the responsibility of ruining my companions' future for a purely personal interest? Might not Captain Nemo, tomorrow, carry us far from any land?

Just then a fairly loud hiss told me the ballast tanks were filling, and the Nautilus sank beneath the waves of the Atlantic.

I stayed in my cabin. I wanted to avoid the captain, to hide from his eyes the emotion that had taken hold of me. What a wretched day I spent that way, torn between the longing to regain my free will and the regret of abandoning this marvelous Nautilus, leaving my undersea studies unfinished! To leave this ocean like that — "my Atlantic," as I liked to call it — without having observed its deepest layers, without having stolen from it those secrets the Indian and Pacific seas had yielded to me! My novel was slipping from my hands at the first volume, my dream was breaking off at its finest moment! What miserable hours dragged by like that, now seeing myself safe ashore with my companions, now wishing — against all reason — that some unforeseen circumstance would prevent Ned Land's plans from coming off.

Twice I went to the salon. I wanted to consult the compass. I wanted to see whether the Nautilus's course was really bringing us closer to the coast, or carrying us away from it. But no. The Nautilus was still holding to Portuguese waters, pointing north as it ran along the shores of the ocean.

So there was nothing for it but to resign myself and get ready to flee. My baggage wasn't heavy. My notes, nothing more.

As for Captain Nemo, I wondered what he'd make of our escape — what anxieties, what harm perhaps it would cause him, and what he'd do if it were either discovered or bungled. Certainly I had no complaint against him; quite the opposite. Never was hospitality more open than his. In leaving him, I couldn't be charged with ingratitude. No oath bound us to him. It was on the force of circumstance alone that he counted, and not on any promise of ours, to keep us with him forever. But this openly avowed intention of holding us prisoner aboard for the rest of our lives justified every attempt we might make to get away.

I hadn't seen the captain since our visit to the island of Santorini. Would chance throw me in his path before our departure? I both wished it and feared it. I listened for the sound of him moving in his cabin, which adjoined mine. Not a sound reached my ear. The room must have been empty.

At which point I began to wonder whether that strange man was even aboard. Ever since the night the dinghy had left the Nautilus on some mysterious errand, my ideas about him had shifted a little. I believed — whatever he might have said — that Captain Nemo must have kept up some kind of relations with the land. Did he never leave the Nautilus? Whole weeks had often passed without my meeting him. What did he do during that time, and while I imagined him a prey to fits of misanthropy, wasn't he off somewhere carrying out some secret act whose nature had escaped me until now?

All these ideas and a thousand others assailed me at once. In the strange situation we were in, the field of conjecture could only be infinite. I felt an unbearable unease. This day of waiting seemed endless. The hours struck too slowly for my impatience.

My dinner was served in my cabin as usual. I ate badly, too preoccupied. I left the table at seven o'clock. A hundred and twenty minutes — I counted them — still separated me from the moment I was to join Ned Land. My agitation kept building. My pulse pounded. I couldn't stay still. I paced back and forth, hoping to calm the turmoil of my mind through movement. The idea of dying in our reckless venture was the least of my worries; but at the thought of our plan being discovered before we'd even left the Nautilus, at the thought of being hauled back before an angry Captain Nemo — or, worse still, one wounded by my desertion — my heart hammered.

I wanted to see the salon one last time. I went along the corridors and came to that museum where I'd passed so many pleasant and profitable hours. I looked at all those riches, all those treasures, like a man on the eve of an eternal exile who is leaving never to return. These wonders of nature, these masterpieces of art, among which my whole life had been concentrated for so many days — I was about to abandon them forever. I would have liked to plunge my gaze through the salon window and out into the waters of the Atlantic; but the panels were sealed tight, and a mantle of iron plate cut me off from that ocean I still didn't know.

Wandering the salon that way, I came near the door set into the cut-off corner, the one that opened onto the captain's cabin. To my great surprise, that door stood ajar. I stepped back involuntarily. If Captain Nemo were in his cabin, he might see me. But hearing no sound, I moved closer. The room was empty. I pushed the door open. I took a few steps inside. The same severe, monkish look as before.

Just then some etchings hanging on the wall, which I hadn't noticed during my first visit, caught my eye. They were portraits — portraits of those great historical figures whose whole existence had been one perpetual devotion to some great human ideal: Kosciuszko, the hero who fell with the cry of Finis Poloniae; Botzaris, the Leonidas of modern Greece; O'Connell, the defender of Ireland; Washington, the founder of the American Union; Manin, the Italian patriot; Lincoln, struck down by the bullet of a defender of slavery; and, last of all, that martyr to the emancipation of the black race, John Brown, hanging from his gallows, just as Victor Hugo's pencil so terribly drew him.

What bond existed between these heroic souls and the soul of Captain Nemo? Could I, at last, draw from this gathering of portraits the mystery of his life? Was he the champion of oppressed peoples, the liberator of enslaved races? Had he played a part in the latest political or social upheavals of the century? Had he been one of the heroes of that terrible American war — a war so lamentable and forever glorious?

All at once the clock struck eight. The stroke of the first hammer-blow on the chime tore me from my musings. I started, as though some invisible eye had been able to plunge into my most secret thoughts, and I hurried out of the cabin.

There, my gaze fell on the compass. Our heading was still north. The log showed a moderate speed, the pressure gauge a depth of about sixty feet. So circumstances were favoring the Canadian's plans.

I went back to my cabin. I dressed warmly — sea boots, an otter-skin cap, a jacket of byssus lined with sealskin. I was ready. I waited. Only the shivering of the propeller broke the deep silence that reigned aboard. I listened, straining my ears. Wouldn't some sudden burst of voices tell me that Ned Land had just been caught in his plans of escape? A mortal anxiety came over me. In vain I tried to recover my composure.

At a few minutes to nine, I pressed my ear to the captain's door. Not a sound. I left my cabin and returned to the salon, which lay in half-darkness, but empty.

I opened the door leading into the library. The same feeble light, the same solitude. I went to take up my post by the door that opened onto the cage of the central staircase. I waited for Ned Land's signal.

At that moment, the shivering of the propeller noticeably slackened, then stopped altogether. Why this change in the Nautilus's movement? Whether the halt helped or hindered Ned Land's designs, I couldn't have said.

The silence was broken now only by the beating of my own heart.

Suddenly, a slight jolt made itself felt. I understood that the Nautilus had come to rest on the ocean floor. My anxiety redoubled. The Canadian's signal didn't reach me. I felt an urge to go join Ned Land and urge him to put off the attempt. I sensed that our navigation was no longer proceeding under ordinary conditions.

At that moment the door of the grand salon opened, and Captain Nemo appeared. He caught sight of me and, without further preamble:

"Ah, Professor," he said in a friendly tone, "I was looking for you. Do you know the history of Spain?"

You could know your own country's history through and through, and in the state I was in — my mind troubled, my head lost — you couldn't have quoted a word of it.

"Well?" Captain Nemo resumed. "Did you hear my question? Do you know the history of Spain?"

"Very poorly," I answered.

"There you have your scholars," said the captain. "They don't know. Well then, sit down," he added, "and I'll tell you a curious episode from that history."

The captain stretched out on a divan, and mechanically I took a seat beside him, in the half-light.

"Professor," he said to me, "listen closely. This story will interest you in a certain respect, for it will answer a question you've no doubt been unable to solve."

"I'm listening, Captain," I said, not knowing where my companion was heading, and wondering whether this incident had anything to do with our plans for flight.

"Professor," Captain Nemo went on, "if you don't mind, we'll go back to the year 1702. You're not unaware that at that time your king, Louis XIV, believing that a mere gesture from a monarch was enough to sink the Pyrenees underground, had imposed his grandson, the Duke of Anjou, on the Spanish. This prince, who reigned more or less badly under the name of Philip V, had, abroad, a formidable adversary to deal with.

"For, the year before, the royal houses of Holland, Austria, and England had concluded a treaty of alliance at The Hague, aiming to wrest the crown of Spain from Philip V and set it on the head of an archduke, on whom they prematurely bestowed the name of Charles III.

"Spain had to resist this coalition. But she was almost entirely without soldiers or sailors. Money, however, she did not lack — provided always that her galleons, laden with the gold and silver of the Americas, made it into her ports. Now, toward the end of 1702, she was expecting a rich convoy, which France had arranged to escort with a fleet of twenty-three ships under Admiral de Château-Renault, since the coalition's navies were then roaming the Atlantic.

"This convoy was supposed to make for Cadiz, but the admiral, having learned that the English fleet was cruising in those waters, resolved to put in at a French port.

"The Spanish commanders of the convoy protested against this decision. They wanted to be taken to a Spanish port, and, failing Cadiz, to the bay of Vigo, on the northwest coast of Spain, which was not blockaded.

"Admiral de Château-Renault was weak enough to obey this injunction, and the galleons entered Vigo Bay.

"Unfortunately, this bay forms an open roadstead that cannot be defended at all. It was therefore urgent to unload the galleons before the coalition fleets arrived, and there would have been time enough for that unloading, had not a wretched question of rivalry suddenly cropped up.

"You're following the chain of events?" Captain Nemo asked me.

"Perfectly," I said, still not knowing what this history lesson was leading up to.

"Then I'll go on. Here is what happened. The merchants of Cadiz held a privilege by which all goods coming from the West Indies were to be received by them. Now, to unload the galleons' ingots at the port of Vigo was to go against their right. So they complained to Madrid, and they obtained from the feeble Philip V an order that the convoy, without proceeding to unload, should remain under seal in the roadstead of Vigo until the enemy fleets had drawn off.

"Now, while this decision was being taken, on October 22, 1702, the English ships arrived in Vigo Bay. Admiral de Château-Renault, despite his inferior forces, fought courageously. But when he saw that the convoy's riches were about to fall into the enemy's hands, he set fire to the galleons and scuttled them, and they went down with their immense treasures."

Captain Nemo had stopped. I confess I still didn't see how this story could interest me.

"Well?" I asked him.

"Well, Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo answered, "we are in that very bay of Vigo, and it rests only with you to penetrate its mysteries."

The captain rose and asked me to follow him. I'd had time to recover myself. I obeyed. The salon was dark, but through the transparent windows the waves of the sea gleamed. I looked.

Around the Nautilus, within a radius of half a mile, the waters appeared saturated with electric light. The sandy bottom was clean and clear. Crew members, dressed in diving suits, were busy clearing away half-rotted casks and staved-in crates, amid wreckage still blackened by fire. From these crates, from these barrels, spilled ingots of gold and silver, cascades of piastres and jewels. The sand was strewn with them. Then, laden with this precious plunder, the men would return to the Nautilus, set down their burden, and go back to that inexhaustible fishery of silver and gold.

I understood. This was the theater of the battle of October 22, 1702. Here was the very spot where the galleons freighted for the Spanish government's account had gone down. Here Captain Nemo came, as his needs required, to cash in the millions with which he ballasted his Nautilus. It was for him, for him alone, that America had yielded up her precious metals. He was the direct and undivided heir to those treasures wrenched from the Incas and the peoples conquered by Hernán Cortés!

"Did you know, Professor," he asked me, smiling, "that the sea held such riches?"

"I knew," I answered, "that the silver held in suspension in its waters is reckoned at two million tons."

"No doubt, but to extract that silver, the costs would outweigh the profit. Here, on the contrary, I have only to gather up what men have lost — and not only in this bay of Vigo, but at a thousand other scenes of shipwreck whose positions my underwater chart has recorded. Do you understand now why I'm rich to the tune of billions?"

"I understand, Captain. Allow me, though, to tell you that in working precisely this bay of Vigo, you've only forestalled the efforts of a rival company."

"And which one?"

"A company that received from the Spanish government the privilege of searching for the sunken galleons. Its shareholders are lured by the bait of an enormous profit, for the value of these shipwrecked riches is estimated at five hundred million."

"Five hundred million!" Captain Nemo replied. "They were here — but they're here no longer."

"Just so," I said. "So a word of warning to those shareholders would be an act of charity. Though who knows whether it would be well received. What gamblers regret above all, as a rule, is not so much the loss of their money as the loss of their wild hopes. Still, I pity them less, after all, than those thousands of unfortunates for whom such riches, fairly distributed, might have done some good — whereas for them they'll remain forever barren!"

No sooner had I voiced this regret than I felt it must have wounded Captain Nemo.

"Barren!" he shot back, kindling. "So you believe, sir, that these riches are lost when it's I who gather them up? Is it for myself, in your opinion, that I take the trouble to collect these treasures? Who tells you I don't put them to good use? Do you think I'm unaware that there are suffering beings, oppressed races on this earth — the wretched to be relieved, the victims to be avenged? Don't you understand?"

Captain Nemo stopped on these last words, regretting perhaps that he had said too much. But I had guessed. Whatever the motives that had forced him to seek independence beneath the seas, above all else he had remained a man! His heart still throbbed for the sufferings of humanity, and his vast charity went out to enslaved races as much as to individuals.

And I understood, then, for whom those millions were destined that Captain Nemo had shipped off when the Nautilus was cruising in the waters of insurgent Crete!
