Ned Land

Commander Farragut was a fine sailor, worthy of the frigate he commanded. Ship and man were one; he was her very soul. On the question of the cetacean not a shadow of doubt crossed his mind, and he would not allow the animal's existence to be argued aboard his vessel. He believed in it the way certain devout old women believe in the Leviathan — by faith, not by reason. The monster existed; he would rid the seas of it; he had sworn as much. He was a kind of Knight of Rhodes, a Dieudonné de Gozon marching out to meet the serpent that plagued his island. Either Commander Farragut would kill the narwhal, or the narwhal would kill Commander Farragut. There was no middle ground.

The ship's officers shared their chief's opinion. You should have heard them talk, debate, argue, calculate the various odds of an encounter, and scan the vast reach of the ocean. More than one volunteered for a watch up in the topgallant crosstrees who, under any other circumstances, would have cursed the chore. As long as the sun traced its daily arc, the masts were crowded with sailors who couldn't hold still — the planks of the deck fairly scorched their feet. And yet the Abraham Lincoln had not yet cut through the suspect waters of the Pacific with her prow.

As for the crew, they asked nothing better than to meet the unicorn, harpoon it, haul it aboard, and carve it up. They watched the sea with scrupulous attention. Besides, Commander Farragut had mentioned a certain sum of two thousand dollars, set aside for whoever — cabin boy or sailor, mate or officer — should be the first to sight the animal. I leave you to imagine how hard the eyes worked aboard the Abraham Lincoln.

For my own part, I was not to be outdone by the others, and I yielded to no one my share of the daily watching. The frigate would have had a hundred reasons to call herself the Argus. Alone among us all, Conseil met the question that so consumed the rest of us with sheer indifference, striking a jarring note against the general enthusiasm on board.

I've said that Commander Farragut had carefully outfitted his ship with every device suited to fishing up the gigantic cetacean. A whaler could not have been better armed. We had every known engine of the trade, from the hand-thrown harpoon to the barbed arrows of blunderbusses and the explosive shells of duck guns. Up on the forecastle stretched a perfected cannon, breech-loading, very thick in the walls and very narrow in the bore, whose model is due to appear at the Universal Exposition of 1867. This precious instrument, of American make, could throw a four-kilogram conical shell an average distance of sixteen kilometers without the least strain.

So the Abraham Lincoln lacked no means of destruction. But she had something better still. She had Ned Land, the king of the harpooners.

Ned Land was a Canadian, with a rare gift in his hands and no equal in his perilous trade. Skill and cool nerve, daring and cunning — he possessed these qualities to a superior degree, and it took an unusually crafty whale, or a singularly sly sperm whale, to escape a thrust of his harpoon.

Ned Land was about forty. He was a tall man — over six English feet — powerfully built, grave in manner, not much of a talker, at times violent, and thoroughly furious when crossed. His person drew your attention, above all the force of his gaze, which gave a striking accent to his whole face.

I think Commander Farragut had done wisely in signing this man aboard. For eye and arm, he was worth the whole crew put together. I can't compare him to anything better than a powerful telescope that was at the same time a cannon always ready to fire.

To say Canadian is to say Frenchman, and, reserved as Ned Land was, I have to admit he took a certain liking to me. My nationality drew him, no doubt. It was a chance for him to speak, and for me to hear, that old language of Rabelais still in use in a few Canadian provinces. The harpooner's family came originally from Quebec, and had already produced a whole tribe of bold fishermen back when that city belonged to France.

Little by little, Ned developed a taste for talking, and I loved to hear the tales of his adventures in the polar seas. He told the story of his fishing and his battles with a great natural poetry. His account took on an epic shape, and I felt I was listening to some Canadian Homer, singing the Iliad of the far northern regions.

I'm sketching this bold companion for you now as I know him at present. For we have become old friends, bound by that unshakable friendship that is born and cemented in the most terrifying trials. Ah, brave Ned! I ask nothing more than to live another hundred years, so I can remember you all the longer!

And now — what was Ned Land's opinion on the question of the sea monster? I have to admit he scarcely believed in the unicorn, and that, alone on board, he did not share the general conviction. He even avoided the subject, which one day I felt I had to take up with him.

On a magnificent evening — the 30th of July, that is, three weeks after our departure — the frigate lay off Cape Blanco, thirty miles to leeward of the Patagonian coast. We had crossed the Tropic of Capricorn, and the Strait of Magellan opened less than seven hundred miles to the south. Within a week the Abraham Lincoln would be furrowing the waves of the Pacific.

Seated on the poop deck, Ned Land and I chatted about this and that, gazing at that mysterious sea whose depths have so far remained beyond the reach of human eyes. Quite naturally I steered the conversation around to the giant unicorn, and weighed the various chances of success or failure for our expedition. Then, seeing that Ned let me talk without saying much of anything, I pressed him more directly.

"Come now, Ned," I asked him, "how can you not be convinced of the existence of the cetacean we're chasing? Do you have some particular reason for being so skeptical?"

The harpooner looked at me for a few moments before answering, struck his broad forehead with his hand — a habitual gesture of his — closed his eyes as if to gather his thoughts, and said at last:

"Maybe I do, Professor Aronnax."

"And yet, Ned — you, a whaler by profession, you who are on familiar terms with the great marine mammals, you whose imagination ought to accept the notion of enormous cetaceans easily enough — you of all people should be the last to doubt in a case like this!"

"That's just where you're wrong, Professor," Ned answered. "Let the common crowd believe in extraordinary comets crossing space, or in antediluvian monsters peopling the inside of the globe — fine, let it pass. But neither the astronomer nor the geologist admits such fairy tales. Same with the whaler. I've chased plenty of cetaceans, I've harpooned a great many, I've killed several, but powerful and well-armed as they were, neither their tails nor their tusks could ever have dented the sheet-iron plates of a steamer."

"Even so, Ned, people cite ships that a narwhal's tooth has run clean through, from one side to the other."

"Wooden ships, maybe," the Canadian answered. "Though even that I've never seen. So, until I have proof to the contrary, I deny that whales, sperm whales, or unicorns can produce any such effect."

"Listen to me, Ned—"

"No, Professor, no. Anything you like except that. A giant octopus, perhaps?"

"Even less likely, Ned. The octopus is only a mollusk, and the very word tells you how little firmness there is in its flesh. Even at five hundred feet long, the octopus, which does not belong to the vertebrate branch at all, is completely harmless to ships like the Scotia or the Abraham Lincoln. So the exploits of Krakens and other monsters of that sort have to be filed away among the fables."

"Then, Professor," Ned Land went on, in a fairly mocking tone, "you persist in accepting the existence of a huge cetacean...?"

"Yes, Ned, I say it again, with a conviction that rests on the logic of the facts. I believe in the existence of a mammal, powerfully built, belonging to the vertebrate branch, like the whales, the sperm whales, or the dolphins, and equipped with a horny tusk of extreme penetrating force."

"Hmph!" said the harpooner, shaking his head with the air of a man who refuses to let himself be convinced.

"Consider, my worthy Canadian," I went on, "that if such an animal exists, if it inhabits the depths of the ocean, if it frequents the liquid layers some miles below the surface of the waters, then it must necessarily possess a body whose strength defies all comparison."

"And why such a powerful body?" Ned asked.

"Because it takes an incalculable force to hold oneself in the deep layers and resist their pressure."

"Really?" said Ned, watching me with a wink.

"Really, and a few figures will prove it to you easily enough."

"Oh, figures!" Ned shot back. "You can make figures say whatever you want!"

"In business, Ned, but not in mathematics. Listen to me. Let's say the pressure of one atmosphere is represented by the pressure of a column of water thirty-two feet high. In reality the column would be a bit shorter, since we're dealing with seawater, whose density is greater than that of fresh water. Well then, when you dive, Ned, for every thirty-two feet of water above you, your body bears a pressure equal to that of the atmosphere — that is, so many kilograms for every square centimeter of its surface. It follows that at three hundred and twenty feet this pressure is ten atmospheres, a hundred atmospheres at three thousand two hundred feet, and a thousand atmospheres at thirty-two thousand feet — roughly two and a half leagues down. Which amounts to saying that if you could reach that depth in the ocean, every square centimeter of your body's surface would undergo a pressure of a thousand kilograms. Now, my good Ned, do you know how many square centimeters of surface you have?"

"I haven't the faintest idea, Professor Aronnax."

"About seventeen thousand."

"As many as that?"

"And since in reality atmospheric pressure is a little more than the weight of one kilogram per square centimeter, your seventeen thousand square centimeters are, at this very moment, bearing a pressure of seventeen thousand five hundred and sixty-eight kilograms."

"Without my noticing it?"

"Without your noticing it. And if you're not crushed by such a pressure, it's because the air penetrates the inside of your body with an equal pressure. Hence a perfect balance between the inward push and the outward push, which cancel each other out, and that lets you bear it without any trouble. But in the water, it's another matter."

"Yes, I understand," Ned answered, grown more attentive, "because the water surrounds me and doesn't penetrate me."

"Exactly, Ned. So, at thirty-two feet below the surface of the sea, you'd undergo a pressure of seventeen thousand five hundred and sixty-eight kilograms; at three hundred and twenty feet, ten times that pressure, or a hundred and seventy-five thousand six hundred and eighty kilograms; at three thousand two hundred feet, a hundred times that pressure, or seventeen hundred and fifty-six thousand eight hundred kilograms; and at thirty-two thousand feet, finally, a thousand times that pressure, or seventeen million five hundred and sixty-eight thousand kilograms — that is to say, you'd be flattened as if they'd pulled you out from between the plates of a hydraulic press!"

"The devil!" said Ned.

"Well then, my worthy harpooner, if vertebrates several hundred meters long, and thick in proportion, hold themselves at such depths — creatures whose surface is measured in millions of square centimeters — then the push they undergo has to be reckoned in billions of kilograms. Calculate, then, what the resistance of their bony framework must be, and the power of their whole organism, to withstand such pressures!"

"They'd have to be built," Ned Land answered, "out of eight-inch iron plate, like the ironclad frigates."

"Just as you say, Ned. And think, then, of the havoc such a mass could wreak, launched with the speed of an express train against a ship's hull."

"Yes... true enough... maybe," the Canadian answered, shaken by these figures but unwilling to give in.

"Well, have I convinced you?"

"You've convinced me of one thing, Professor — that if such animals exist at the bottom of the sea, they'd necessarily have to be as strong as you say."

"But if they don't exist, you stubborn harpooner, how do you explain the accident that happened to the Scotia?"

"It's maybe...," said Ned, hesitating.

"Go on!"

"Because... it just isn't true!" the Canadian answered, unknowingly reproducing a famous reply of Arago's.

But that answer proved nothing but the harpooner's obstinacy. That day I pressed him no further. The Scotia's accident could not be denied. The hole existed so plainly that it had had to be plugged, and I don't think the existence of a hole can be demonstrated more categorically than that. Now, that hole had not made itself, and since it had not been produced by underwater rocks or underwater machines, it was necessarily due to the piercing tool of some animal.

Now, in my opinion — and for all the reasons deduced above — that animal belonged to the branch of vertebrates, to the class of mammals, to the group of the fish-shaped, and finally to the order of cetaceans. As for the family in which it ranked — whale, sperm whale, or dolphin — as for the genus it belonged to, as for the species in which it ought to be classed, that was a question to be cleared up later on. To settle it, one would have to dissect this unknown monster; to dissect it, catch it; to catch it, harpoon it — which was Ned Land's affair; to harpoon it, sight it — which was the crew's affair; and to sight it, meet it — which was the affair of chance.
