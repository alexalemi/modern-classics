Conclusion

Here, then, is the conclusion of this voyage beneath the seas. What happened during that night, how the dinghy tore free of the Maelström's terrible pull, how Ned Land, Conseil, and I came out of that abyss alive — I could never say. But when I came back to myself, I was lying in a fisherman's hut on one of the Lofoten Islands. My two companions, safe and sound, were beside me, gripping my hands. We threw our arms around one another and wept.

For the moment there is no thought of getting back to France. Between northern Norway and the south the means of travel are few and far between, and so I am forced to wait for the steamer that makes its twice-monthly run from the North Cape.

It is here, then, among the good people who took us in, that I go back over the record of these adventures. It is accurate. Not a single fact has been left out, not a single detail exaggerated. This is the faithful account of that improbable expedition beneath an element closed to humankind — an element that progress will one day open to free passage.

Will anyone believe me? I don't know. And it hardly matters, in the end. What I can affirm now is my right to speak of those seas, beneath which, in less than ten months, I crossed twenty thousand leagues — of that underwater tour of the world that unveiled so many wonders to me across the Pacific, the Indian Ocean, the Red Sea, the Mediterranean, the Atlantic, and the seas of the far south and the far north.

But what has become of the Nautilus? Did it withstand the grip of the Maelström? Is Captain Nemo still alive? Is he pursuing his terrible reprisals beneath the ocean, or did he stop short at that last slaughter? Will the waves one day bring us the manuscript that holds the whole story of his life? Will I ever learn this man's name? Will the sunken warship tell us, by its nationality, the nationality of Captain Nemo?

I hope so. And I hope, too, that his powerful vessel conquered the sea in its most dreadful whirlpool, and that the Nautilus survived where so many ships have perished! If that is so — if Captain Nemo still inhabits that ocean, the country he adopted as his own — then may the hatred be stilled in that fierce heart! May the contemplation of so many wonders quench the spirit of vengeance in him! May the avenger fade away, and the man of science go on with the peaceful exploration of the seas! If his destiny is strange, it is sublime as well. Have I not understood that for myself? Did I not live ten months of that other-than-natural existence? And so, to that question posed six thousand years ago by Ecclesiastes — "Who has ever been able to sound the depths of the abyss?" — two men among all men now have the right to answer. Captain Nemo and I.
