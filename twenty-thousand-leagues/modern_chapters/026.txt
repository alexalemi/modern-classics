A Pearl Worth Ten Million

Night came on. I went to bed. I slept badly. Sharks played a leading role in my dreams, and I found it both entirely fitting and entirely unfitting, that old etymology that traces the French word for shark — requin — back to the word requiem.

The next morning, at four o'clock, I was woken by the steward Captain Nemo had assigned specially to my service. I got up quickly, dressed, and went into the salon.

Captain Nemo was waiting for me there.

"Professor Aronnax," he said, "are you ready to leave?"

"I'm ready."

"Then follow me, please."

"And my companions, Captain?"

"They've been told, and they're waiting for us."

"Aren't we going to put on our diving suits?" I asked.

"Not yet. I didn't let the Nautilus come too close to this coast, and we're a fair distance out from the Manaar bank. But I've had the dinghy made ready. It'll carry us to the exact landing point and spare us a rather long swim. It's loaded with our diving gear, which we'll put on the moment this underwater expedition begins."

Captain Nemo led me toward the central staircase, whose steps ended at the platform. Ned and Conseil were there, delighted at the "pleasure outing" being got up. Five of the Nautilus's sailors, oars ready, waited for us in the dinghy, which had been made fast against the side.

The night was still dark. Sheets of cloud covered the sky and let only a few scattered stars show through. I looked toward the land, but I saw only a blurred line closing off three-quarters of the horizon, from southwest to northwest. The Nautilus, having worked its way up the western coast of Ceylon during the night, now lay west of the bay — or rather of the gulf formed by the mainland and the island of Manaar. There, beneath the dark waters, stretched the pearl-oyster bank, an inexhaustible field of pearls more than twenty miles long.

Captain Nemo, Conseil, Ned Land, and I took our places at the stern of the dinghy. The coxswain manned the tiller; his four companions leaned into their oars; the mooring line was cast off, and we pulled away.

The dinghy headed south. The rowers were in no hurry. I noticed that their oar strokes, driven hard beneath the water, came only every ten seconds, in the manner generally used in navies. While the boat ran on under its own way, the drops of water struck the black surface of the swell with a crackle, like spatters of molten lead. A small swell rolling in from the open sea gave the dinghy a slight roll, and a few wave crests slapped against its bow.

We were silent. What was Captain Nemo thinking about? Perhaps about this land he was drawing near — too near for his liking, unlike the Canadian, to whom it still seemed too far off. As for Conseil, he was there as a simple sightseer.

Around half past five, the first tints along the horizon picked out more sharply the upper line of the coast. Fairly flat to the east, it swelled a little toward the south. Five miles still separated us from it, and its shore blurred into the misty waters. Between it and us, the sea was deserted. Not a boat, not a diver. Deep solitude over this meeting place of the pearl fishers. As Captain Nemo had pointed out to me, we were arriving in these waters a month too early.

At six o'clock, day broke all at once, with that suddenness peculiar to the tropics, which know neither dawn nor dusk. The sun's rays pierced the curtain of clouds banked along the eastern horizon, and the radiant star climbed swiftly.

I saw the land distinctly now, with a few trees scattered here and there.

The dinghy drew on toward the island of Manaar, which curved away to the south. Captain Nemo had risen from his bench and was watching the sea.

At a sign from him, the anchor was dropped, and the chain barely ran out, for the bottom lay no more than a meter down, and at this spot it formed one of the highest points of the oyster bank. The dinghy at once swung round under the pull of the ebb tide, which carried it seaward.

"Here we are, Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo said then. "You see this narrow bay. It's right here that, a month from now, the fishing fleets of the exploiters will gather in numbers, and it's these waters their divers will come to search so boldly. This bay is happily arranged for that kind of fishing. It's sheltered from the strongest winds, and the sea here is never very rough — a very favorable condition for the divers' work. Now we'll put on our diving suits, and begin our walk."

I said nothing, and while I stared at these suspect waters, I began, with the help of the boat's sailors, to pull on my heavy sea garment. Captain Nemo and my two companions were dressing as well. None of the Nautilus's men were to go with us on this new excursion.

Soon we were sealed up to the neck in the rubber suit, and straps fixed the air tanks onto our backs. As for the Ruhmkorff apparatus, there was no question of it. Before fitting my head into its copper capsule, I remarked on this to the captain.

"Those devices would be useless to us," he answered. "We won't be going to any great depth, and the sun's rays will be enough to light our way. Besides, it wouldn't be wise to carry an electric lantern down into these waters. Its brightness might unexpectedly attract some dangerous resident of these parts."

While Captain Nemo was speaking these words, I turned toward Conseil and Ned Land. But my two friends had already boxed their heads into the metal helmet, and they could neither hear nor answer.

One last question remained for me to put to Captain Nemo.

"And our weapons?" I asked. "Our rifles?"

"Rifles! What for? Don't your mountain men attack the bear with a dagger in their hand? And isn't steel surer than lead? Here's a good solid blade. Put it in your belt, and let's go."

I looked at my companions. They were armed as we were, and, on top of that, Ned Land was brandishing an enormous harpoon he'd stowed in the dinghy before leaving the Nautilus.

Then, following the captain's example, I let them fit the heavy copper sphere over my head, and our air tanks were immediately switched on.

A moment later, the sailors were setting us down one after another, and, in a meter and a half of water, we planted our feet on smooth sand. Captain Nemo gave us a wave of his hand. We followed him, and down a gentle slope we vanished beneath the waves.

There, the fears that had been haunting my brain left me. I became astonishingly calm. The ease of my movements strengthened my confidence, and the strangeness of the spectacle captured my imagination.

The sun was already sending enough light down under the water. The smallest objects stayed visible. After ten minutes of walking, we were in five meters of water, and the ground had become nearly flat.

At our steps, like flocks of snipe over a marsh, there rose up flights of curious fish of the genus Monopterus, whose members have no fin but the one on the tail. I recognized the javanese, a true serpent eighty centimeters long, with a livid belly, which you'd easily mistake for a conger eel but for the golden lines along its flanks. Among the genus Stromateus, whose body is very flattened and oval, I made out some brightly colored fiatolas carrying their dorsal fin like a scythe — edible fish that, dried and marinated, make an excellent dish known as karawade — then some Tranquebar butterflyfish, belonging to the genus Aspidophoroides, whose body is covered by a scaly armor with eight lengthwise facets.

Meanwhile, the rising sun lit the mass of water more and more. The floor gradually changed. The fine sand gave way to a veritable causeway of rounded rocks, carpeted with mollusks and zoophytes. Among the specimens of these two branches, I noticed some Placuna with thin, unequal valves — a kind of oyster peculiar to the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean — orange lucine clams with an orbicular shell, awl-shaped auger shells, some of those Persian murexes that supplied the Nautilus with such an admirable dye, horned rock shells fifteen centimeters long that reared up beneath the waves like hands ready to seize you, spiny turbinella shells all bristling with prickles, gaping lingula shells, anatina clams — edible shellfish that feed the markets of Hindustan — faintly luminous Pelagia jellyfish, and finally some splendid fan-shaped Oculina corals, magnificent fans that form one of the richest branchings of these seas.

In the midst of these living plants, and under the arbors of hydrophytes, ran clumsy legions of articulates, particularly toothed Ranina crabs, whose shell is a somewhat rounded triangle, coconut crabs special to these waters, and hideous Parthenope crabs, whose look was repellent to the eye. One animal no less loathsome, which I met with several times, was that enormous crab observed by Mr. Darwin, to which nature has given the instinct and the strength needed to feed on coconuts. It climbs the trees along the shore, knocks down the nut, which splits open in its fall, and then opens it with its powerful pincers. Here, beneath these clear waters, this crab scuttled about with unequaled agility, while green turtles, of the kind that frequent the Malabar coast, moved slowly among the shifting rocks.

Around seven o'clock, we at last were treading the pearl-oyster bank, where the oysters breed by the million. These precious mollusks clung to the rocks, held fast by that brown byssus that doesn't allow them to move — a respect in which these oysters are inferior even to the mussel, to which nature has not denied all power of locomotion.

The Meleagrina pintadina — the mother-of-pearl oyster, whose valves are roughly equal — takes the form of a rounded shell with thick walls, very rough on the outside. Some of these shells were leaved and furrowed with greenish bands radiating out from the top. These belonged to the young oysters. The others, with a rough black surface, ten years old and more, measured as much as fifteen centimeters across.

Captain Nemo pointed his hand at this prodigious heap of oysters, and I understood that this mine was truly inexhaustible, for nature's creative force outweighs man's destructive instinct. Ned Land, faithful to that instinct, was busy stuffing the finest of the mollusks into a net he carried at his side.

But we couldn't stop. We had to follow the captain, who seemed to be guiding himself along paths known to him alone. The bottom rose noticeably, and at times my arm, when I raised it, broke the surface of the sea. Then the level of the bank dipped again, capriciously. Often we rounded tall rocks tapered into little pyramids. In their dark crevices, big crustaceans, propped high on their legs like siege engines, watched us with fixed eyes, and under our feet crawled myrians, glycerids, aricians, and other annelids, stretching out their antennae and tentacle-like cirri to absurd lengths.

Just then a vast grotto opened before our steps, hollowed out of a picturesque tumble of rocks hung with all the high-warp tapestries of the underwater flora. At first this grotto looked deeply dark to me. The sun's rays seemed to die out inside it by successive degrees. Its dim transparency was no more than drowned light.

Captain Nemo went in. We went in after him. My eyes soon grew used to that relative darkness. I could make out the strangely twisting spring of the vault, borne on natural pillars set broadly on their granite bases, like the heavy columns of Tuscan architecture. Why was our unfathomable guide drawing us down into the depths of this underwater crypt? I would soon find out.

After going down a fairly steep slope, our feet trod the bottom of a kind of circular well. There Captain Nemo stopped, and with his hand he pointed out to us an object I had not yet noticed.

It was an oyster of extraordinary size, a gigantic tridacna — a holy-water basin that could have held a whole lake of blessed water, a bowl more than two meters across, and therefore larger than the one that adorned the salon of the Nautilus.

I went up to this phenomenal mollusk. By its byssus it clung to a slab of granite, and there it grew all alone in the calm waters of the grotto. I put the weight of this tridacna at three hundred kilograms. Now, such an oyster holds fifteen kilos of flesh, and it would take the stomach of a Gargantua to put away a few dozen of them.

Captain Nemo clearly knew of this bivalve's existence. This was not the first time he'd visited it, and I thought that in leading us to this spot he meant only to show us a natural curiosity. I was mistaken. Captain Nemo had a particular interest in checking the present state of this tridacna.

The mollusk's two valves were half open. The captain went up and slid his dagger between the shells to keep them from snapping shut; then, with his hand, he lifted the membranous, fringe-edged tunic that formed the animal's mantle.

There, between the leafy folds, I saw a loose pearl the size of a coconut. Its globular shape, its perfect clarity, its wonderful orient made it a jewel of priceless value. Carried away by curiosity, I reached out my hand to seize it, to weigh it, to feel it! But the captain stopped me, made a sign of refusal, and, drawing back his dagger with a quick movement, let the two valves suddenly snap shut.

Then I understood what Captain Nemo intended. By leaving that pearl buried under the tridacna's mantle, he was letting it slowly grow. With each year the mollusk's secretion added new concentric layers to it. He alone knew the grotto where this admirable fruit of nature was "ripening"; he alone raised it, so to speak, meaning one day to carry it off to his precious museum. Perhaps he'd even, following the example of the Chinese and the Indians, brought on the making of this pearl by slipping some scrap of glass or metal under the folds of the mollusk, which had little by little coated it in mother-of-pearl. In any case, comparing this pearl to those I already knew, to those that gleamed in the captain's collection, I put its value at ten million francs at the very least. A superb natural curiosity, and no luxury jewel — for I don't know what feminine ear could ever have borne it.

Our visit to the opulent tridacna was over. Captain Nemo left the grotto, and we climbed back up onto the oyster bank, among those clear waters not yet troubled by the labor of the divers.

We walked separately now, like true strollers, each of us stopping or wandering off at the whim of his fancy. For my part, I no longer had any worry about the dangers my imagination had so absurdly exaggerated. The shallows drew noticeably nearer the surface of the sea, and soon, in a meter of water, my head broke the level of the ocean. Conseil rejoined me, and pressing his big helmet against mine, he gave me a friendly greeting with his eyes. But this raised plateau measured only a few fathoms, and soon we had gone back down into our element. I believe I've now earned the right to call it that.

Ten minutes later, Captain Nemo suddenly stopped. I thought he was halting to turn back. No. With a gesture, he ordered us to crouch down beside him at the bottom of a wide crevice. His hand pointed toward a spot in the mass of water, and I looked hard.

Five meters from me, a shadow appeared and sank down to the floor. The uneasy thought of sharks crossed my mind. But I was mistaken, and once again we were not dealing with the monsters of the ocean.

It was a man, a living man, an Indian, a black man, a fisherman, a poor devil no doubt, who had come to glean ahead of the harvest. I could see the bottom of his boat, moored a few feet above his head. He was diving and coming back up, again and again. A stone cut in the shape of a sugarloaf, which he gripped between his feet while a rope tied it to his boat, served to bring him down more quickly to the sea floor. That was the whole of his equipment. Reaching the bottom, at about five meters' depth, he threw himself on his knees and filled his sack with oysters gathered at random. Then he came back up, emptied his sack, hauled his stone up again, and started over — the whole operation lasting no more than thirty seconds.

This diver didn't see us. The shadow of the rock hid us from his gaze. And besides, how would this poor Indian ever have supposed that men, beings like himself, were there beneath the waters, spying on his movements, missing no detail of his fishing?

Several times he came back up like this and dived again. He brought up no more than ten or so oysters on each dive, for they had to be torn from the bank they clung to by their tough byssus. And how many of those oysters were empty of the pearls for which he risked his life!

I watched him with deep attention. His work went along steadily, and for half an hour no danger seemed to threaten him. So I was growing used to the sight of this fascinating fishery when, all at once — at a moment when the Indian was kneeling on the floor — I saw him make a gesture of terror, spring up, and gather himself to shoot back to the surface.

I understood his horror. A gigantic shadow was rising above the wretched diver. It was a large shark, coming on diagonally, its eye ablaze, its jaws open!

I was struck dumb with horror, unable to make a move.

The ravenous animal, with a powerful stroke of its fin, hurled itself at the Indian, who threw himself to one side and dodged the shark's bite — but not the lash of its tail, for that tail, striking him in the chest, stretched him out on the floor.

This scene had barely lasted a few seconds. The shark came back, and rolling over on its back, it was getting ready to cut the Indian in two, when I felt Captain Nemo, posted beside me, rise suddenly. Then, dagger in hand, he walked straight at the monster, ready to fight it hand to hand.

The shark, at the very moment it was about to snap up the wretched fisherman, caught sight of its new adversary, and, turning belly-down again, made straight for him.

I can still see Captain Nemo's stance. Braced down on himself, he waited for the formidable shark with an admirable coolness, and when the beast rushed at him, the captain, throwing himself aside with prodigious quickness, dodged the impact and sank his dagger into its belly. But that wasn't the end of it. A terrible fight began.

The shark had roared, so to speak. Blood poured in streams from its wounds. The sea turned red, and through that opaque murk I could see nothing more.

Nothing more — until, in a clearing, I glimpsed the daring captain clinging to one of the animal's fins, fighting the monster body to body, plowing his dagger into his enemy's belly, yet unable to land the decisive blow, that is, to reach it full in the heart. The shark, thrashing, churned the mass of water with fury, and its wake threatened to knock me off my feet.

I would have liked to run to the captain's aid. But nailed in place by horror, I couldn't stir.

I watched, wild-eyed. I saw the phases of the struggle shift. The captain went down onto the floor, thrown over by the enormous bulk that bore down on him. Then the shark's jaws yawned open like a factory shears, and it would have been all over for the captain if, quick as thought, harpoon in hand, Ned Land had not rushed at the shark and struck it with his terrible point.

The waves filled with a mass of blood. They heaved under the movements of the shark, which beat them with indescribable fury. Ned Land had not missed his mark. This was the monster's death rattle. Struck to the heart, it thrashed in horrible spasms, whose backlash knocked Conseil off his feet.

Meanwhile, Ned Land had pulled the captain free. Nemo, back on his feet without a wound, went straight to the Indian, cut the rope that bound him to his stone with a quick stroke, took him in his arms, and, with a vigorous kick of his heel, rose to the surface of the sea.

We followed, all three of us, and in a few moments, miraculously saved, we reached the fisherman's boat.

Captain Nemo's first care was to call this wretched man back to life. I didn't know whether he'd succeed. I hoped so, for the poor devil hadn't been under long. But the shark's blow with its tail might have struck him a mortal wound.

Happily, under the vigorous rubbing of Conseil and the captain, I saw the drowned man little by little come back to his senses. He opened his eyes. What must his surprise have been — his terror, even — to see the four big copper heads leaning over him!

And above all, what must he have thought when Captain Nemo, drawing a bag of pearls from a pocket of his suit, put it into his hand? This magnificent act of charity, from the man of the waters to the poor Indian of Ceylon, was accepted by the latter with a trembling hand. His frightened eyes showed, moreover, that he didn't know to what superhuman beings he owed both his fortune and his life.

At a sign from the captain, we made our way back to the oyster bank, and, following the route already traveled, after half an hour's walk we came upon the anchor that held the Nautilus's dinghy to the bottom.

Once aboard, each of us, with the sailors' help, shed his heavy copper shell.

Captain Nemo's first word was for the Canadian.

"Thank you, Master Land," he said.

"It's a settling of scores, Captain," Ned Land answered. "I owed you that."

A pale smile slid across the captain's lips, and that was all.

"To the Nautilus," he said.

The boat flew over the waves. A few minutes later, we came upon the shark's carcass, floating.

By the black color marking the tips of its fins, I recognized the terrible melanopterus of the Indian sea, of the species of the true sharks. Its length exceeded twenty-five feet; its enormous mouth took up a third of its body. It was an adult, as could be seen from the six rows of teeth arranged in isosceles triangles along its upper jaw.

Conseil looked at it with a wholly scientific interest, and I'm sure he was filing it away — not without reason — in the class of cartilaginous fish, order Chondropterygii with fixed gills, family Selachii, genus Squalus.

While I was studying this inert mass, a dozen of these voracious melanopters appeared all at once around the boat; but, paying us no mind, they threw themselves on the carcass and fought over the scraps.

At half past eight, we were back aboard the Nautilus.

There I fell to reflecting on the incidents of our excursion to the Manaar bank. Two observations came out of it inevitably. One bore on the unequaled daring of Captain Nemo, the other on his devotion to a human being — one of the representatives of that race he was fleeing beneath the seas. Whatever he might say, this strange man had not yet managed to kill his heart entirely.

When I made this observation to him, he answered in a slightly moved tone:

"That Indian, Professor, is an inhabitant of the land of the oppressed, and I am still, and to my last breath I will remain, a man of that land!"
