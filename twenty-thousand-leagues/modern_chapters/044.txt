The Gulf Stream

None of us will ever be able to forget that terrible scene of April 20. I wrote it down under the pressure of a violent emotion. Since then I have gone back over the account. I read it aloud to Conseil and the Canadian. They found it accurate as to the facts, but inadequate as to the effect. To paint such scenes as those, one would need the pen of the most illustrious of our poets, the author of The Toilers of the Sea.

I have said that Captain Nemo wept as he gazed out over the waves. His grief was immense. This was the second companion he had lost since our arrival aboard. And what a death! That friend, crushed, smothered, broken in the fearsome arm of a devilfish, ground to pulp between its iron mandibles, would never rest with his shipmates in the peaceful waters of the coral cemetery!

For my own part, in the midst of that struggle, it was the cry of despair torn from the poor wretch that had ripped my heart open. That poor Frenchman, forgetting the artificial language of the crew, had gone back to speaking the tongue of his country and his mother, to hurl out one last appeal! So among this crew of the Nautilus, bound body and soul to Captain Nemo, fleeing like him from all contact with other men, I had a countryman! Was he the only one to represent France in this mysterious brotherhood, evidently made up of men of many different nationalities? It was one more of those insoluble problems that kept rising, endlessly, before my mind.

Captain Nemo went back into his cabin, and I did not see him again for some time. But how sad, how despairing, how irresolute he must have been, if I could judge by the ship itself, of which he was the soul and which received every one of his moods! The Nautilus no longer held any set course. It came and went, it drifted like a corpse at the mercy of the swells. Its propeller had been cleared, and yet it hardly used it. The ship sailed at random. It could not tear itself away from the scene of its last battle, from that sea which had devoured one of its own!

Ten days passed like this. It was only on May 1 that the Nautilus resolutely took up its course north again, after raising the Lucayas at the mouth of the Bahama Channel. We were then following the current of the sea's greatest river, a river with its own banks, its own fish, and its own temperature. I mean the Gulf Stream.

It is a river, in truth, that flows freely through the middle of the Atlantic, its waters never mingling with the waters of the ocean around it. It is a salt river, saltier than the surrounding sea. Its average depth is three thousand feet, its average width sixty miles. In certain places its current runs at a speed of four kilometers an hour. The unvarying volume of its waters is greater than that of all the rivers of the globe combined.

The true source of the Gulf Stream, identified by Commander Maury — its starting point, if you like — lies in the Bay of Biscay. There its waters, still weak in temperature and color, begin to form. It runs south, skirts equatorial Africa, warms its waves in the rays of the torrid zone, crosses the Atlantic, reaches Cape São Roque on the Brazilian coast, and forks into two branches, one of which goes off to saturate itself still further with the warm molecules of the Caribbean Sea. Then the Gulf Stream, charged with restoring the balance between the temperatures and blending the waters of the tropics with the waters of the north, begins its work as a great regulator. Heated white-hot in the Gulf of Mexico, it climbs north along the American coasts, pushes up as far as Newfoundland, is deflected under the thrust of the cold current from Davis Strait, resumes its ocean course by following the rhumb line along one of the globe's great circles, and divides into two arms around the forty-third degree — one of which, helped along by the northeast trade wind, returns to the Bay of Biscay and the Azores, while the other, after warming the shores of Ireland and Norway, runs on beyond Spitsbergen, where its temperature drops to four degrees, to form the open sea of the pole.

It was on this river of the ocean that the Nautilus was now sailing. As it comes out of the Bahama Channel, fourteen leagues wide and three hundred and fifty meters deep, the Gulf Stream moves at a rate of eight kilometers an hour. This speed decreases steadily as the current works its way north, and one can only hope that this regularity holds, for if — as some believe they have observed — its speed and its direction were ever to change, the climates of Europe would be thrown into disturbances whose consequences no one could begin to calculate.

Toward noon I was out on the platform with Conseil. I was explaining the peculiar features of the Gulf Stream to him. When my explanation was done, I invited him to plunge his hands into the current.

Conseil obeyed, and was very much surprised to feel no sensation of either heat or cold.

"That comes," I told him, "from the fact that the temperature of the Gulf Stream's waters, as they leave the Gulf of Mexico, differs very little from the temperature of the blood. This Gulf Stream is a vast heating system, one that lets the coasts of Europe deck themselves in an eternal green. And if Maury is to be believed, the heat of this current, if it could be harnessed entirely, would supply enough calories to keep a river of molten iron as large as the Amazon or the Missouri in a state of fusion."

At that moment the speed of the Gulf Stream was two meters twenty-five per second. Its current is so distinct from the surrounding sea that its compressed waters rise up above the level of the ocean, and a difference in level forms between them and the cold waters around them. Dark, moreover, and very rich in mineral salts, they stand out by their pure indigo against the green waves surrounding them. So sharp, in fact, is the line dividing them that the Nautilus, off the Carolinas, cut through the waves of the Gulf Stream with its ram while its propeller was still churning those of the open ocean.

This current swept along with it a whole world of living creatures. The argonauts, so common in the Mediterranean, traveled here in numerous shoals. Among the cartilaginous fish, the most remarkable were rays whose very slender tails made up almost a third of the body, and which formed vast diamonds twenty-five feet long; then small sharks a meter in length, with big heads, short rounded snouts, and pointed teeth arranged in several rows, their bodies seemingly covered with scales.

Among the bony fish I noted grey wrasses peculiar to these seas; synagris sea breams whose irises blazed like fire; croakers a meter long, with wide mouths bristling with small teeth, that gave off a faint cry; black centronotes, which I have mentioned before; blue dorados picked out in gold and silver; parrotfish, true rainbows of the ocean, that can rival in color the loveliest birds of the tropics; triangle-headed blennies; bluish rhombus flatfish without scales; batrachoids marked with a yellow crosswise band that forms a Greek letter T; swarms of little pointed gobies stippled with brown spots; dipterodons with silvery heads and yellow tails; various samples of salmon; slender, softly gleaming mullet that Lacépède dedicated to the gentle companion of his life; and finally a beautiful fish, the American cavalier, which — decorated with every order and bedecked with every ribbon — frequents the shores of that great nation where ribbons and decorations are held in such modest esteem.

I will add that, during the night, the phosphorescent waters of the Gulf Stream rivaled the electric glare of our beacon, especially in the stormy weather that threatened us so often.

On May 8 we were still abreast of Cape Hatteras, off the coast of North Carolina. The width of the Gulf Stream is there seventy-five miles, and its depth two hundred and ten meters. The Nautilus went on wandering at random. All watchfulness seemed to have been banished from on board. I will admit that, under these conditions, an escape might have succeeded. Indeed, the inhabited shores offered easy refuge everywhere. The sea was ceaselessly furrowed by the many steamers that run the service between New York or Boston and the Gulf of Mexico, and crossed night and day by those little schooners engaged in coastal trade along the various points of the American shore. There was hope of being picked up. It was, then, a favorable opportunity, despite the thirty miles that separated the Nautilus from the shores of the Union.

But one unfortunate circumstance thwarted the Canadian's plans completely. The weather was very bad. We were approaching those waters where storms are frequent, that homeland of waterspouts and cyclones, bred precisely by the Gulf Stream's current. To brave an often raging sea in a frail little boat was to run headlong to certain destruction. Ned Land admitted as much himself. And so he chafed at the bit, gripped by a furious homesickness that flight alone could have cured.

"Professor," he said to me that day, "this has got to end. I want to know where I stand. Your Nemo is pulling away from land and heading north. But I'm telling you plainly — I've had my fill of the South Pole, and I won't follow him to the North Pole."

"What can we do, Ned, when an escape is out of the question at the moment?"

"I keep coming back to my idea. We have to talk to the captain. You said nothing when we were in the seas off your country. I want to speak, now that we're in the seas off mine. When I think that in a few days the Nautilus is going to be off Nova Scotia, and that there, near Newfoundland, a wide bay opens up — that into that bay pours the Saint Lawrence, and that the Saint Lawrence is my river, the river of Quebec, my native city — when I think of that, the fury rises up in my face, my hair stands on end. Listen, Professor, I'd sooner throw myself into the sea! I won't stay here! I'm suffocating!"

The Canadian was clearly at the end of his patience. His vigorous nature could not adjust itself to this prolonged imprisonment. His face was altering by the day. His temper was growing darker and darker. Nearly seven months had gone by without our having had any news from the land. On top of that, Captain Nemo's withdrawal into himself, his changed mood — above all since the battle with the devilfish — his silence, all of it was making me see things in a different light. I no longer felt the enthusiasm of the early days. It took a Fleming like Conseil to accept such a situation, in this element reserved for whales and the other creatures of the sea. Truly, if that good fellow had had gills instead of lungs, I believe he would have made a distinguished fish!

"Well, Professor?" Ned Land went on, seeing that I did not answer.

"Well, Ned, you want me to ask Captain Nemo what his intentions are regarding us?"

"Yes, Professor."

"Even though he has already made them known?"

"Yes. I want to be certain one last time. Speak for me alone, in my name alone, if you like."

"But I hardly ever run into him. He even avoids me."

"That's all the more reason to go and find him."

"I'll put the question to him, Ned."

"When?" the Canadian pressed.

"When I run into him."

"Professor Aronnax, would you like me to go and find him myself?"

"No, let me handle it. Tomorrow —"

"Today," said Ned Land.

"So be it. Today I'll see him," I answered the Canadian, who, had he acted for himself, would certainly have ruined everything.

I was left alone. The request decided upon, I resolved to have done with it at once. I prefer a thing done to a thing still to do.

I went back to my cabin. From there I heard someone pacing in Captain Nemo's. I must not let this chance to meet with him slip by. I knocked at his door. I got no answer. I knocked again, then turned the knob. The door opened.

I went in. The captain was there. Bent over his worktable, he had not heard me. Determined not to leave without questioning him, I came up to him. He raised his head abruptly, knit his brows, and said to me in a fairly harsh tone:

"You, here! What do you want with me?"

"To speak with you, Captain."

"But I am busy, sir, I am working. This freedom I leave you to keep to yourself — may I not have it for myself?"

The welcome was hardly encouraging. But I was resolved to hear everything out, so as to answer everything.

"Captain," I said coldly, "I have to speak to you about a matter I cannot allow myself to put off."

"About what, sir?" he replied ironically. "Have you made some discovery that escaped me? Has the sea yielded up some new secret to you?"

We were far from any common ground. But before I could reply, he showed me a manuscript lying open on his table and said to me in a graver tone:

"Here, Professor Aronnax, is a manuscript written in several languages. It contains the summary of my studies of the sea, and, God willing, it will not perish with me. This manuscript, signed with my name, completed by the story of my life, will be sealed inside a small unsinkable container. The last of us all to survive aboard the Nautilus will throw this container into the sea, and it will go wherever the waves may carry it."

This man's name! His own history, written by his own hand! So his mystery would one day be laid bare? But at that moment I saw in this confidence nothing more than an opening.

"Captain," I answered, "I can only approve of the thought that moves you. The fruit of your studies must not be lost. But the means you are using strikes me as crude. Who knows where the winds will drive this container, into what hands it will fall? Could you not find something better? Can you not, or one of your men —?"

"Never, sir," said the captain sharply, cutting me off.

"But I, and my companions, we are ready to keep this manuscript in reserve, and if you give us back our freedom —"

"Your freedom!" said Captain Nemo, rising to his feet.

"Yes, sir, and that is the subject on which I wanted to question you. For seven months we have been aboard your vessel, and I ask you today, in the name of my companions as much as my own, whether it is your intention to keep us here forever."

"Professor Aronnax," said Captain Nemo, "I will answer you today what I answered you seven months ago: whoever enters the Nautilus must never leave it."

"But this is slavery itself that you are imposing on us!"

"Give it whatever name you please."

"But everywhere a slave keeps the right to win back his freedom! Whatever the means that offer themselves, he may hold them good!"

"That right," answered Captain Nemo, "who denies it to you? Have I ever thought of binding you by an oath?"

The captain looked at me, folding his arms.

"Sir," I said to him, "to come back a second time to this subject would be to neither your taste nor mine. But since we have begun it, let us have it out. I tell you again, it is not only myself that is at issue. For me study is a solace, a powerful diversion, a spur, a passion that can make me forget everything else. Like you, I am a man able to live unknown, obscure, in the fragile hope of one day bequeathing to the future the results of my labors, by means of some hypothetical container entrusted to the chance of the waves and the winds. In a word, I can admire you, I can follow you without displeasure in a role that I understand on certain points; but there are still other aspects of your life that let me glimpse it hedged about with complications and mysteries in which, alone here, my companions and I have no part. And even when our hearts have been able to beat for you, stirred by some of your sorrows or moved by your acts of genius and courage, we have had to choke back within ourselves the least sign of that sympathy which the sight of what is beautiful and good awakens, whether it comes from a friend or from an enemy. Well, it is this feeling — that we are strangers to everything that touches you — which makes our position something unacceptable, impossible, even for me, but impossible above all for Ned Land. Every man, simply by virtue of being a man, is worth thinking about. Have you asked yourself what schemes of vengeance the love of liberty, the hatred of slavery, might give rise to in a nature like the Canadian's — what he might be thinking, attempting, trying?"

I had fallen silent. Captain Nemo rose.

"Let Ned Land think, attempt, try whatever he likes — what is it to me? It was not I who went looking for him! It is not for my pleasure that I keep him aboard! As for you, Professor Aronnax, you are one of those who can understand anything, even silence. I have nothing more to answer you. Let this first time you have taken up this subject be also the last, for a second time I could not even bring myself to listen to you."

I withdrew. From that day on, our situation was very strained. I reported my conversation to my two companions.

"Now we know," said Ned, "that there is nothing to hope for from this man. The Nautilus is drawing near Long Island. We will make our escape, whatever the weather."

But the sky was growing more and more threatening. Symptoms of a hurricane were showing themselves. The atmosphere turned whitish and milky. To the cirrus clouds in their fine sheaves there succeeded, along the horizon, layers of nimbocumulus. Other low clouds went scudding rapidly past. The sea rose and swelled in long heaving rollers. The birds vanished, all but the storm petrels, those friends of foul weather. The barometer fell noticeably and indicated an extreme tension of vapors in the air. The mixture in the storm glass broke down under the influence of the electricity saturating the atmosphere. The struggle of the elements was near.

The storm broke on the day of May 18, just as the Nautilus was afloat off Long Island, a few miles from the New York channels. I am able to describe this struggle of the elements, because instead of fleeing it in the depths of the sea, Captain Nemo, out of some inexplicable whim, chose to face it out at the surface.

The wind was blowing from the southwest, at first a strong gale — that is, at a speed of fifteen meters a second — which rose to twenty-five meters toward three in the afternoon. That is the figure of a tempest.

Captain Nemo, unshakable under the squalls, had taken up his place on the platform. He had lashed himself around the waist to withstand the monstrous waves breaking over him. I had hauled myself up as well and tied myself down, dividing my wonder between this storm and this incomparable man who stood up to it.

The raging sea was swept by great rags of cloud that trailed down into its waves. I could no longer make out any of those small intermediate ripples that form in the bottom of the great troughs. Nothing but long soot-colored undulations, so compact that their crests did not break. Their height kept rising. They lashed each other into a frenzy. The Nautilus, now heeled over on its side, now standing up like a mast, rolled and pitched frightfully.

Toward five o'clock a torrential rain came down, and it beat down neither the wind nor the sea. The hurricane was unleashed at a speed of forty-five meters a second, that is, nearly forty leagues an hour. Under such conditions it topples houses, drives roof tiles through doors, snaps iron gratings, and shifts twenty-four-pounder cannon. And yet the Nautilus, in the midst of the tumult, bore out the words of a certain learned engineer: "There is no well-built hull that cannot defy the sea!" It was no resisting rock, which those waves would have demolished; it was a spindle of steel, obedient and mobile, without rigging, without masts, that braved their fury with impunity.

Meanwhile I studied those unleashed waves attentively. They measured up to fifteen meters in height over a length of a hundred and fifty to a hundred and seventy-five meters, and their speed of propagation, half that of the wind, was fifteen meters a second. Their volume and their power grew with the depth of the water. I understood then the role of these waves, which trap the air within their flanks and force it down to the bottom of the seas, where they carry life along with the oxygen. Their extreme force of pressure — it has been calculated — can rise as high as three thousand kilograms per square foot of the surface they beat against. It was waves like these that, in the Hebrides, shifted a block weighing eighty-four thousand pounds. It was waves like these that, in the storm of December 23, 1864, after leveling part of the town of Yedo, in Japan, and traveling seven hundred kilometers an hour, went and broke that same day on the shores of America.

The intensity of the storm grew with the night. The barometer, as in 1860 at Réunion during a cyclone, fell to 710 millimeters. At the close of day I saw a great ship pass on the horizon, laboring painfully. It was lying to under low steam, trying to hold itself head-on to the sea. It must have been one of the steamers on the lines from New York to Liverpool or Le Havre. It soon disappeared into the darkness.

At ten o'clock in the evening the sky was on fire. The atmosphere was streaked with violent flashes of lightning. I could not bear their brilliance, while Captain Nemo, looking them full in the face, seemed to breathe in the very soul of the storm. A terrible noise filled the air, a complex noise made up of the howling of the crushed waves, the bellowing of the wind, the crash of the thunder. The wind leaped to every point of the horizon, and the cyclone, setting out from the east, came back to it by way of the north, the west, and the south, in the direction opposite to the whirling storms of the southern hemisphere.

Ah, this Gulf Stream! It lived up to its name of king of storms! It is the Gulf Stream that creates these formidable cyclones, by the difference in temperature between the layers of air stacked above its currents.

The rain had given way to a downpour of fire. The droplets of water changed into blazing plumes. You would have thought that Captain Nemo, wanting a death worthy of himself, was seeking to have himself struck by lightning. In one appalling pitch of the ship, the Nautilus reared its steel ram up into the air like the rod of a lightning conductor, and I saw long sparks come streaming from it.

Broken, at the end of my strength, I dragged myself flat on my belly toward the hatch. I opened it and went back down into the salon. The storm was then reaching its maximum of intensity. It was impossible to stand upright inside the Nautilus.

Captain Nemo came back down toward midnight. I heard the ballast tanks fill little by little, and the Nautilus sank gently below the surface of the waves.

Through the open windows of the salon, I saw great terrified fish that passed like phantoms through the burning waters. Some of them were struck by lightning before my very eyes!

The Nautilus went on descending. I thought it would find calm at a depth of fifteen meters. No. The upper layers were too violently agitated. We had to go seek rest as far down as fifty meters, in the very bowels of the sea.

But there, what tranquility, what silence, what a peaceful realm! Who would have said that a terrible hurricane was raging just then on the surface of that ocean?
