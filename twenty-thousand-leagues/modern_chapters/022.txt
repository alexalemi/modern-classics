Ægri Somnia

The next day, January 10, the Nautilus resumed its course between two waters, but at a remarkable speed that I could not put at less than thirty-five miles an hour. Its propeller turned so fast that I could neither follow nor count its revolutions.

When I reflected that this marvelous electric agent, having given the Nautilus its motion, its heat, and its light, also protected the ship against attacks from outside and transformed it into a sacred ark that no profaner could touch without being struck down by lightning, my admiration knew no bounds — and from the machine it climbed at once to the engineer who had created it.

We were heading due west, and on January 11 we rounded Cape Wessel, situated at 135° longitude and 10° north latitude, which forms the eastern tip of the Gulf of Carpentaria. The reefs were still numerous, but more scattered, and marked on the chart with extreme precision. The Nautilus easily avoided the Money shoals to port and the Victoria reefs to starboard, placed at 130° longitude, along that tenth parallel we were following without deviation.

On January 13, Captain Nemo, having reached the Timor Sea, sighted the island of that name at 122° longitude. This island, whose area comes to sixteen hundred and twenty-five square leagues, is governed by rajahs. These princes call themselves sons of crocodiles — that is to say, sprung from the highest origin to which any human being can lay claim. And so their scaly ancestors swarm in the rivers of the island and are the objects of a particular veneration. People protect them, pamper them, flatter them, feed them; they offer them young girls to devour, and woe to the stranger who lays a hand on these sacred lizards.

But the Nautilus had no dealings with these ugly beasts. Timor was visible for only a moment, at noon, while the first officer took his bearings. And I caught no more than a glimpse of the little island of Rotti, part of the same group, whose women enjoy a well-established reputation for beauty in the Malay markets.

From this point, the course of the Nautilus, in latitude, bent away to the southwest. We were headed for the Indian Ocean. Where would Captain Nemo's whim carry us now? Would he sail back up toward the coasts of Asia? Would he draw near the shores of Europe? Unlikely resolutions for a man who fled the inhabited continents. Would he sail south, then? Would he go on to round the Cape of Good Hope, then Cape Horn, and push toward the Antarctic pole? Would he come back at last to his Pacific seas, where the Nautilus found its sailing easy and unhindered? The future would have to tell us.

After running along the reefs of Cartier, of Hibernia, of Seringapatam, of Scott — the last efforts of the solid element against the liquid one — by January 14 we were beyond all land. The speed of the Nautilus was strangely slackened, and, very capricious in its ways, sometimes it swam in the midst of the waters, and sometimes it floated on the surface.

During this stretch of the voyage, Captain Nemo carried out some interesting experiments on the various temperatures of the sea at different depths. Under ordinary conditions, such readings are obtained by means of fairly complicated instruments whose reports are dubious at best — whether thermometric sounding-lines, whose glass often shatters under the pressure of the water, or devices based on the varying resistance of metals to electric currents. The results obtained this way can never be checked with any real certainty. Captain Nemo, on the contrary, went to find this temperature himself, down in the depths of the sea, and his thermometer, put in contact with the various liquid strata, gave him the degree he sought immediately and reliably.

And so, whether by overloading his ballast tanks or by descending on a slant by means of his diving planes, the Nautilus reached, one after another, depths of three, four, five, seven, nine, and ten thousand meters — and the final result of these experiments was that the sea maintained a permanent temperature of four and a half degrees at a depth of a thousand meters, under every latitude.

I followed these experiments with the keenest interest. Captain Nemo brought a genuine passion to them. Often I asked myself to what end he made these observations. Was it for the good of his fellow men? That was hardly likely, for one day or another his work would perish with him in some unknown sea. Unless he meant to leave the results of his experiments to me. But that was to assume that my strange voyage would have an end — and that end I still could not see anywhere.

Be that as it may, Captain Nemo also shared with me various figures he had obtained, establishing the relative densities of the water in the principal seas of the globe. From this exchange I drew a personal lesson that had nothing to do with science.

It was during the morning of January 15. The captain, with whom I was strolling on the platform, asked me whether I knew the different densities that the waters of the sea present. I answered no, and added that science lacked rigorous observations on the subject.

"I have made them, those observations," he told me, "and I can vouch for their certainty.

"Very well," I answered, "but the Nautilus is a world apart, and the secrets of its scientists never reach the land."

"You are right, Professor," he said, after a few moments of silence. "It is a world apart. It is as foreign to the earth as the planets that accompany this globe around the sun, and the labors of the scientists of Saturn or Jupiter will never be known. Still, since chance has bound our two existences together, I can share with you the result of my observations."

"I am listening, Captain."

"You know, Professor, that seawater is denser than fresh water, but this density is not uniform. In fact, if I represent the density of fresh water by one, I find one and twenty-eight thousandths for the waters of the Atlantic, one and twenty-six thousandths for the waters of the Pacific, one and thirty thousandths for the waters of the Mediterranean —"

"Aha!" I thought. "So he ventures into the Mediterranean?"

"— one and eighteen thousandths for the waters of the Ionian Sea, and one and twenty-nine thousandths for the waters of the Adriatic."

Clearly the Nautilus did not shun the frequented seas of Europe, and I concluded from this that it would carry us back — perhaps before long — toward more civilized continents. I thought that Ned Land would learn of this detail with a very natural satisfaction.

For several days our time was taken up with experiments of every kind, bearing on the degrees of saltness of the waters at different depths, on their electrification, their coloration, their transparency — and in all these circumstances Captain Nemo displayed an ingenuity equaled only by his graciousness toward me. Then, for some days, I saw no more of him, and once again found myself as though isolated aboard his ship.

On January 16, the Nautilus seemed to fall asleep only a few meters below the surface of the waves. Its electric apparatus had stopped working, and its motionless propeller left it to drift at the mercy of the currents. I supposed the crew was busy with interior repairs, made necessary by the violence of the machine's mechanical movements.

My companions and I then witnessed a curious spectacle. The panels of the salon were open, and since the Nautilus's beacon was not lit, a vague darkness reigned in the midst of the waters.

The stormy sky, thick with heavy clouds, gave the upper layers of the ocean only an insufficient light.

I was observing the state of the sea under these conditions, and the biggest fish appeared to me only as barely sketched shadows, when the Nautilus suddenly found itself transported into full light. At first I thought the beacon had been relit, and was casting its electric brilliance through the liquid mass. I was mistaken, and after a quick look I recognized my error.

The Nautilus was floating in the midst of a phosphorescent layer that, in this darkness, became dazzling. It was produced by myriads of luminous animalcules, whose sparkle grew as they slid over the ship's metal hull. I caught, then, flashes in the midst of these luminous sheets, like streams of molten lead in a blazing furnace, or masses of metal heated white-hot — so that, by contrast, certain bright portions cast shadow in this fiery medium, from which every shadow ought to have been banished. No! this was no longer the calm radiance of our ordinary lighting. There was an uncommon vigor and movement in it. You could feel that this light was alive!

For in truth it was an infinite mass of pelagic infusoria, of miliary noctilucae, true globules of transparent jelly, furnished with a threadlike tentacle, of which as many as twenty-five thousand have been counted in thirty cubic centimeters of water. And their light was doubled again by the peculiar glow of the medusae, the starfish, the aurelia jellyfish, the date-shell pholads, and other phosphorescent zoophytes, steeped in the grease of organic matter decomposed by the sea, and perhaps in the mucus secreted by the fish.

For several hours the Nautilus floated in these gleaming waves, and our wonder grew as we watched the great sea animals frolic in them like salamanders. There, in the midst of that fire that does not burn, I saw porpoises — elegant, swift, tireless clowns of the seas — and swordfish three meters long, intelligent forerunners of hurricanes, whose formidable blades sometimes struck the salon's window. Then smaller fish appeared: varied triggerfish, leaping scomberoids, wolf-snouted filefish, and a hundred others that streaked the luminous atmosphere as they darted through it.

There was an enchantment in that dazzling spectacle! Perhaps some atmospheric condition was heightening the intensity of the phenomenon. Perhaps some storm was breaking loose on the surface of the waves. But at this depth of a few meters, the Nautilus felt nothing of its fury, and it rocked peacefully in the midst of the tranquil waters.

So we went on, endlessly charmed by some new marvel. Conseil observed and classified his zoophytes, his articulates, his mollusks, his fish. The days slipped by rapidly, and I no longer counted them. Ned, following his habit, tried to vary the fare on board. Like true snails, we had grown used to our shell, and I can affirm that it is an easy thing to become a perfect snail.

And so this existence seemed to us easy, natural, and we no longer imagined that a different sort of life existed on the surface of the terrestrial globe — when an event came to remind us of the strangeness of our situation.

On January 18, the Nautilus was at 105° longitude and 15° south latitude. The weather was threatening, the sea rough and heaving. A strong breeze was blowing from the east. The barometer, which had been falling for some days, announced an imminent battle of the elements.

I had climbed onto the platform just as the first officer was taking his measurements of hour angles. I waited, as usual, for the daily phrase to be spoken. But that day it was replaced by another phrase, no less incomprehensible. Almost at once I saw Captain Nemo appear, his eyes — fitted to a telescope — turned toward the horizon.

For a few minutes the captain stood motionless, without leaving the point framed within his lens. Then he lowered his glass and exchanged a dozen words with his first officer. The man seemed in the grip of an emotion he was vainly trying to contain. Captain Nemo, more master of himself, remained cold.

He appeared, moreover, to be raising certain objections, to which the officer replied with firm assurances. At least, that was how I understood it, from the difference in their tone and their gestures.

As for me, I had looked carefully in the direction they were observing, without seeing anything. Sky and water merged along a line of horizon of perfect clarity.

Meanwhile Captain Nemo paced from one end of the platform to the other, without looking at me — perhaps without seeing me. His step was firm, but less regular than usual. Sometimes he stopped, and, arms crossed on his chest, he watched the sea. What could he be searching for on that immense expanse? The Nautilus was then some hundreds of miles from the nearest coast.

The first officer had taken up his telescope again and was stubbornly questioning the horizon, coming and going, stamping his foot, contrasting with his chief by his nervous agitation.

In any case, this mystery would necessarily be cleared up, and before long — for on an order from Captain Nemo, the engine, increasing its propulsive power, drove the propeller into a faster rotation.

At that moment the first officer once more drew the captain's attention. Nemo broke off his pacing and pointed his telescope toward the indicated spot. He observed it a long while. For my part, seriously intrigued, I went down to the salon and brought back an excellent spyglass that I ordinarily used. Then, resting it on the cage of the beacon that jutted out at the front of the platform, I prepared to sweep the whole line of sky and sea.

But my eye had not yet been fixed to the eyepiece when the instrument was snatched violently from my hands.

I turned around. Captain Nemo stood before me — but I did not recognize him. His face was transfigured. His eye, gleaming with a somber fire, had retreated beneath his knitted brow. His teeth were half bared. His stiff body, his clenched fists, his head drawn down between his shoulders, bore witness to the violent hatred that breathed from his whole person. He did not move. My spyglass, fallen from his hand, had rolled to his feet.

Had I then, without meaning to, provoked this posture of rage? Did he imagine — this incomprehensible man — that I had surprised some secret forbidden to the guests of the Nautilus?

No! that hatred, I was not its object, for he was not looking at me, and his eye stayed obstinately fixed on that impenetrable point of the horizon.

At last Captain Nemo became master of himself again. His face, so profoundly altered, resumed its habitual calm. He addressed a few words to his first officer in the foreign language, then he turned back to me.

"Professor Aronnax," he said to me in a rather imperious tone, "I ask you to observe one of the pledges that bind you to me.

"What is it, Captain?"

"You must let yourself be confined, you and your companions, until the moment when I judge it fitting to give you back your freedom."

"You are the master," I answered, looking at him steadily. "But may I ask you a question?"

"None, sir."

At that word, I had nothing to argue, only to obey, since all resistance would have been impossible.

I went down to the cabin occupied by Ned Land and Conseil, and told them of the captain's decision. I leave it to be imagined how this news was received by the Canadian. In any case, there was no time for any explanation. Four members of the crew were waiting at the door, and they led us to that cell where we had spent our first night aboard the Nautilus.

Ned Land tried to protest, but the door closed on him for all answer.

"Will Master tell me what this means?" Conseil asked me.

I recounted to my companions what had happened. They were as astonished as I was, and no further along.

Meanwhile, I was plunged into an abyss of reflections, and the strange, apprehensive look on Captain Nemo's face would not leave my thoughts. I was incapable of linking two logical ideas together, and was losing myself in the most absurd hypotheses, when I was pulled from my mental strain by these words of Ned Land's:

"Well, look at that! Lunch is served!"

Indeed, the table had been laid. It was obvious that Captain Nemo had given this order at the same time that he had the Nautilus hurried along.

"Will Master permit me to make him a recommendation?" Conseil asked.

"Yes, my boy," I answered.

"Well then — let Master eat his lunch. It is the prudent thing, for we do not know what may happen."

"You're right, Conseil."

"Unfortunately," said Ned Land, "they've only given us the regular fare of the ship."

"Friend Ned," Conseil replied, "what would you say, then, if there had been no lunch at all?"

This reasoning cut the harpooner's grumbling short.

We sat down to table. The meal passed in near silence. I ate little. Conseil "forced himself," always out of prudence, and Ned Land, whatever his mood, did not miss a single bite. Then, lunch over, each of us settled into his corner.

At that moment the luminous globe that lit the cell went out and left us in deep darkness. Ned Land was not slow to fall asleep, and — what astonished me — Conseil too gave way to a heavy drowsiness. I was asking myself what could have brought on this imperious need for sleep in him, when I felt my own brain steeping in a thick torpor. My eyes, which I wanted to keep open, closed in spite of me. I was in the grip of a painful hallucination. Plainly, some sleeping drug had been mixed into the food we had just eaten. So prison was not enough to hide Captain Nemo's plans from us — sleep had to be added to it as well!

I heard then the panels closing again. The undulations of the sea, which had set up a slight rolling motion, ceased. Had the Nautilus, then, left the surface of the ocean? Had it gone back down into the motionless stratum of the waters?

I tried to resist sleep. It was impossible. My breathing weakened. I felt a deadly cold freeze my heavy, half-paralyzed limbs. My eyelids, true caps of lead, fell over my eyes. I could not lift them. A morbid sleep, full of hallucinations, took hold of my whole being. Then the visions faded, and left me in a total annihilation.
