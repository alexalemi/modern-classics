The Black Current

The portion of the globe covered by water has been reckoned at three million eight hundred thirty-two thousand five hundred fifty-eight square myriameters — more than thirty-eight million hectares. This liquid mass comprises two billion two hundred fifty million cubic miles, and would form a sphere sixty leagues in diameter weighing three quintillion tons. To grasp that number, you have to tell yourself that a quintillion is to a billion what a billion is to one — that is, there are as many billions in a quintillion as there are single units in a billion. And this mass of water is roughly the quantity that all the rivers of the earth would pour out over the span of forty thousand years.

During the geological ages, the age of fire was followed by the age of water. In the beginning the ocean was universal. Then, little by little, in the Silurian period, mountain peaks appeared, islands emerged and vanished again under partial floods, showed themselves once more, fused together, formed continents, and at last the land settled geographically into the shape we see today. The solid had wrested from the liquid thirty-seven million six hundred fifty-seven thousand square miles — twelve thousand nine hundred sixteen million hectares.

The configuration of the continents lets us divide the waters into five great parts: the Arctic Ocean, the Antarctic Ocean, the Indian Ocean, the Atlantic Ocean, and the Pacific Ocean.

The Pacific runs from north to south between the two polar circles, and from west to east between Asia and America across a stretch of a hundred and forty-five degrees of longitude. It is the most peaceful of the seas: its currents broad and slow, its tides moderate, its rainfall abundant. Such was the ocean that fate had first summoned me to travel — and under the strangest conditions imaginable.

"Professor," Captain Nemo said to me, "if you don't mind, we're going to fix our exact position and set the point of departure for this voyage. It's a quarter to noon. I'll bring us back up to the surface."

The captain pressed an electric bell three times. The pumps began driving the water out of the ballast tanks; on the pressure gauge the needle marked, through its shifting readings, the Nautilus's rise, then came to a stop.

"We've arrived," said the captain.

I made my way to the central stairway that led up to the platform. I climbed the metal steps and, through the open hatches, came out onto the upper part of the Nautilus.

The platform stood barely eighty centimeters above the water. The bow and stern of the Nautilus tapered into that spindle shape which had, quite rightly, earned it the comparison to a long cigar. I noticed that its sheet-metal plates, slightly overlapping, resembled the scales that clothe the bodies of the great land reptiles. So I understood perfectly well why, even through the best telescopes, this vessel had always been taken for a marine animal.

Toward the middle of the platform, the dinghy, half sunk into the hull of the ship, formed a slight swelling. Fore and aft rose two cages of modest height, with sloping walls, partly closed by thick lens-shaped panes of glass: one meant for the helmsman who steered the Nautilus, the other for the powerful electric beacon that lit its way.

The sea was magnificent, the sky clear. The long craft scarcely felt the wide swells of the ocean. A light breeze out of the east ruffled the surface of the water. The horizon, swept clear of mist, was perfect for observation.

There was nothing in sight. Not a reef, not an islet. No more Abraham Lincoln. Nothing but the empty vastness.

Sextant in hand, Captain Nemo took the altitude of the sun, which would give him his latitude. He waited a few minutes for the star to come and just graze the rim of the horizon. While he sighted, not one of his muscles so much as twitched, and the instrument could not have held stiller in a hand of marble.

"Noon," he said. "Professor, whenever you're ready."

I cast one last look at that faintly yellowish sea off the Japanese coast, and went back down to the grand salon.

There the captain worked out his position, calculated his longitude by chronometer, and checked it against earlier observations of hour angles. Then he said to me:

"Professor Aronnax, we are at a hundred thirty-seven degrees and fifteen minutes of longitude west —"

"West of which meridian?" I asked eagerly, hoping the captain's answer might give me a clue to his nationality.

"Sir," he replied, "I have several chronometers set to the meridians of Paris, Greenwich, and Washington. But in your honor I'll use the one for Paris."

The answer told me nothing. I bowed, and the commander went on:

"Thirty-seven degrees and fifteen minutes of longitude west of the Paris meridian, and thirty degrees and seven minutes of north latitude — that is, some three hundred miles off the coast of Japan. Today, the eighth of November, at noon, our voyage of exploration beneath the waters begins."

"God keep us," I answered.

"And now, Professor," the captain added, "I'll leave you to your studies. I've set our course east-northeast at a depth of fifty meters. Here are some large-scale charts where you can follow it. The salon is at your disposal, and I ask your leave to withdraw."

Captain Nemo bowed to me. I was left alone, lost in my thoughts. Every one of them turned on this commander of the Nautilus. Would I ever learn what nation this strange man belonged to — this man who boasted of belonging to none? What had provoked the hatred he had sworn against all humanity, this hatred that perhaps was hunting for some terrible revenge? Was he one of those unrecognized scholars, one of those geniuses "who had been dealt some great sorrow," as Conseil put it — a modern Galileo — or one of those men of science, like the American Maury, whose career had been broken by political upheavals? I still couldn't say. Chance had just flung me aboard his ship; he held my life in his hands; and he received me coldly, but with courtesy. Only, he had never once taken the hand I held out to him. He had never once offered me his own.

For a full hour I sat sunk in these reflections, trying to pierce a mystery that fascinated me. Then my gaze fixed on the vast planisphere spread across the table, and I laid my finger on the very spot where the observed longitude and latitude crossed.

The sea has its rivers just as the continents do. They are particular currents, recognizable by their temperature and their color, and the most remarkable of them is known as the Gulf Stream. Science has charted the courses of five principal currents around the globe: one in the North Atlantic, a second in the South Atlantic, a third in the North Pacific, a fourth in the South Pacific, and a fifth in the southern Indian Ocean. It is even probable that a sixth current once existed in the northern Indian Ocean, back when the Caspian and Aral Seas, joined to the great lakes of Asia, formed a single unbroken sheet of water.

Now, at the point marked on the planisphere, there unrolled one of these currents — the Kuro-Shivo of the Japanese, the Black River — which, issuing from the Bay of Bengal where the perpendicular rays of the tropical sun warm it, crosses the Strait of Malacca, runs along the coast of Asia, and curves through the North Pacific as far as the Aleutian Islands, carrying with it drifting trunks of camphor trees and other native products, and slicing through the ocean's waves with the pure indigo of its warm waters. This was the current the Nautilus was about to travel. I followed it with my eye, watched it lose itself in the immensity of the Pacific, and felt myself being carried along with it — when Ned Land and Conseil appeared in the salon doorway.

My two good companions stood petrified at the sight of the wonders heaped up before their eyes.

"Where are we? Where are we?" cried the Canadian. "In the museum at Quebec?"

"If Master will allow," Conseil put in, "it looks more like the Hôtel du Sommerard!"

"My friends," I answered, motioning them in, "you're neither in Canada nor in France, but very much aboard the Nautilus, fifty meters below the surface of the sea."

"We'll have to take Master's word for it, since Master says so," Conseil replied. "But frankly, this salon is enough to astonish even a Fleming like me."

"Be astonished, my friend, and look — for a classifier of your caliber, there's plenty of work here."

I hardly needed to encourage Conseil. Bent over the display cases, the good fellow was already murmuring words in the language of the naturalists: class of Gastropods, family of Buccinoids, genus of Cowries, species Cypraea madagascariensis, and so on.

Meanwhile Ned Land, no great conchologist, was questioning me about my meeting with Captain Nemo. Had I found out who he was, where he came from, where he was going, into what depths he was dragging us? In short, a thousand questions I hadn't the time to answer.

I told him everything I knew, or rather everything I didn't know, and asked him what he'd heard or seen on his side.

"Seen nothing, heard nothing!" the Canadian replied. "I haven't even caught sight of this ship's crew. You don't suppose they're electric too, do you?"

"Electric!"

"Upon my word, you'd almost be tempted to believe it. But you, Professor Aronnax," asked Ned Land, who still had his fixed idea, "can't you tell me how many men are aboard? Ten, twenty, fifty, a hundred?"

"I couldn't tell you, Master Land. And besides, take my word for it — drop this notion of seizing the Nautilus or escaping from it, at least for now. This ship is one of the masterpieces of modern industry, and I'd have hated never to see it! Plenty of people would accept the situation we're in, if only for the chance to walk through these marvels. So keep calm, and let's try to see what's going on around us."

"See!" cried the harpooner. "But there's nothing to see, there'll never be anything to see out of this iron prison! We're sailing, we're navigating, blind..."

Ned Land was just uttering these last words when darkness fell all at once — utter, absolute darkness. The luminous ceiling went out, and so abruptly that my eyes felt a stab of pain, the same you get when you pass the other way, from deep gloom into the most dazzling light.

We stood mute, not stirring, not knowing what surprise, pleasant or unpleasant, awaited us. But a sliding sound made itself heard. It was as if panels were being worked along the flanks of the Nautilus.

"This is the end of the end!" said Ned Land.

"Order of the Hydromedusae!" murmured Conseil.

Suddenly light returned on either side of the salon, through two oblong openings. The liquid masses appeared, brilliantly lit by the electric glow. Two panes of crystal separated us from the sea. At first I shuddered at the thought that this fragile wall might shatter; but strong copper frames held it in place and gave it an all but infinite resistance.

The sea was distinctly visible for a mile in every direction around the Nautilus. What a sight! What pen could describe it? Who could paint the play of light through those transparent sheets, the softness of its gradations fading down to the ocean's lower and upper reaches?

Everyone knows how transparent the sea is. Its clarity is known to surpass that of spring water. The mineral and organic substances it holds in suspension actually increase its transparency. In certain parts of the ocean, in the Antilles, a hundred forty-five meters of water let you make out the sandy bottom with astonishing sharpness, and the penetrating power of the sun's rays seems to stop only at a depth of three hundred meters. But in this fluid medium the Nautilus was crossing, the electric brilliance was being produced within the very heart of the waves. It was no longer luminous water — it was liquid light.

If one accepts the hypothesis of Ehrenberg, who believes in a phosphorescent illumination of the ocean depths, then nature has certainly reserved for the creatures of the sea one of its most prodigious spectacles — and I could judge it here by the thousand plays of this light. On either side I had a window opening onto those unexplored abysses. The darkness of the salon set off the brightness outside, and we gazed as though this pure crystal were the glass of some immense aquarium.

The Nautilus seemed not to be moving. That was because we had no landmarks. Now and then, though, the lines of water, split by its ram, streamed past our eyes at tremendous speed.

Marveling, we leaned on our elbows before those windows, and none of us had yet broken this silence of amazement when Conseil said:

"You wanted to see, friend Ned — well, now you see!"

"Amazing! Amazing!" the Canadian kept saying — for, forgetting his rage and his plans of escape, he had fallen under an irresistible spell. "A man would come from the far ends of the earth to admire a sight like this!"

"Ah!" I exclaimed. "Now I understand this man's life! He's made himself a world apart that holds its most astonishing marvels in reserve for him!"

"But the fish?" the Canadian objected. "I don't see any fish!"

"What does it matter to you, friend Ned," Conseil answered, "since you don't know them anyway?"

"Me! A fisherman!" cried Ned Land.

And on that subject an argument broke out between the two friends, for they both knew fish — but each in a very different way.

Everyone knows that fish form the fourth and last class of the vertebrate branch. They have been very properly defined as "vertebrates with double circulation and cold blood, breathing through gills and destined to live in water." They fall into two distinct series: the series of bony fish — those whose spine is made of bony vertebrae — and the cartilaginous fish, those whose spine is made of cartilaginous vertebrae.

The Canadian may perhaps have known this distinction, but Conseil knew a great deal more, and now, bound to Ned in friendship, he couldn't allow himself to seem less learned than his friend. So he said to him:

"Friend Ned, you're a slayer of fish, a very skillful fisherman. You've caught a great number of these interesting animals. But I'd wager you don't know how they're classified."

"I do," the harpooner replied gravely. "They're classified into fish you eat and fish you don't!"

"That's a glutton's distinction," Conseil answered. "But tell me — do you know the difference between bony fish and cartilaginous fish?"

"I might, Conseil."

"And the subdivisions of those two great classes?"

"I haven't the faintest idea," the Canadian admitted.

"Well then, friend Ned, listen and remember! The bony fish are subdivided into six orders. First: the acanthopterygians, whose upper jaw is complete and mobile, and whose gills take the shape of a comb. This order includes fifteen families — that is, three-quarters of all known fish. Type: the common perch."

"Fairly good eating," said Ned Land.

"Second," Conseil went on, "the abdominals, which have their ventral fins slung under the abdomen and behind the pectorals, without being attached to the shoulder bones — an order that divides into five families and takes in the greater part of the freshwater fish. Type: the carp, the pike."

"Bah!" said the Canadian with a certain scorn. "Freshwater fish!"

"Third," said Conseil, "the subbrachians, whose ventral fins are attached beneath the pectorals and hung directly from the shoulder bones. This order contains four families. Type: plaice, dab, turbot, brill, sole, and so on."

"Excellent! Excellent!" cried the harpooner, who would consider fish only from the standpoint of the dinner table.

"Fourth," Conseil resumed, unruffled, "the apodes, with an elongated body, without ventral fins, clothed in a thick and often slimy skin — an order comprising just one family. Type: the eel, the electric eel."

"Mediocre! Mediocre!" answered Ned Land.

"Fifth," said Conseil, "the lophobranchs, which have complete and free jaws, but whose gills are formed of little tufts arranged in pairs along the branchial arches. This order counts only one family. Type: the seahorses, the sea dragons."

"Poor! Poor!" the harpooner shot back.

"Sixth and last," said Conseil, "the plectognaths, whose maxillary bone is fixed to the side of the intermaxillary that forms the jaw, and whose palatine arch is locked by suture to the skull, which makes it immovable — an order that lacks true ventral fins and is made up of two families. Types: the pufferfish, the sunfish."

"Fish fit to dishonor a stewpot!" cried the Canadian.

"Have you followed all that, friend Ned?" asked the learned Conseil.

"Not in the least, friend Conseil," the harpooner replied. "But go right on, because you're very interesting."

"As for the cartilaginous fish," Conseil continued imperturbably, "they comprise only three orders."

"So much the better," said Ned.

"First, the cyclostomes, whose jaws are fused into a single mobile ring and whose gills open through numerous holes — an order comprising only one family. Type: the lamprey."

"An acquired taste," Ned Land replied.

"Second, the selacians, with gills like those of the cyclostomes, but with a mobile lower jaw. This order, the most important of the class, includes two families. Types: the ray and the sharks."

"What!" cried Ned. "Rays and sharks in the same order! Well, friend Conseil, for the rays' sake I wouldn't advise putting them together in the same tank!"

"Third," Conseil answered, "the sturgeons, whose gills open, as usual, through a single slit fitted with a gill cover — an order that includes four genera. Type: the sturgeon."

"Ah, friend Conseil, you saved the best for last — in my opinion, anyway. And is that all?"

"Yes, my good Ned," Conseil replied, "and mind you, once you know all that, you still know nothing, for the families subdivide into genera, subgenera, species, varieties..."

"Well, friend Conseil," said the harpooner, leaning over the pane of the panel, "here come some varieties now!"

"Yes! Fish!" cried Conseil. "You'd think we were standing in front of an aquarium!"

"No," I said, "because an aquarium is only a cage, and those fish are as free as birds in the air."

"Well then, friend Conseil, name them, go on, name them!" said Ned Land.

"Me?" Conseil replied. "I'm not able to! That's Master's business!"

And indeed, the worthy fellow — a rabid classifier — was no naturalist, and I doubt he could have told a tuna from a bonito. In a word, the opposite of the Canadian, who named all these fish without a moment's hesitation.

"A triggerfish," I said.

"And a Chinese triggerfish!" answered Ned Land.

"Genus Balistes, family of the Sclerodermi, order of the Plectognaths," murmured Conseil.

Decidedly, between the two of them, Ned and Conseil would have made one distinguished naturalist.

The Canadian hadn't been mistaken. A troop of triggerfish, with compressed bodies and grainy skin, armed with a spine on the dorsal fin, were frolicking around the Nautilus, flicking the four rows of prickles that bristle along each side of their tails. Nothing could be more admirable than their covering — gray above, white below — its spots of gold glittering in the dark swirl of the waves. Among them undulated rays, like a sheet let loose to the winds, and among these I spotted, to my great joy, that Chinese ray, yellowish on its upper part, soft pink beneath the belly, and fitted with three spines behind its eye — a rare species, and one whose very existence was doubtful in the time of Lacépède, who had never seen it except in a collection of Japanese drawings.

For two hours a whole aquatic army escorted the Nautilus. Amid their games and their leaps, as they vied with one another in beauty, brilliance, and speed, I made out the green wrasse; the surmullet, marked with a double black stripe; the eleotris goby, with rounded tail, white in color and flecked with violet on the back; the Japanese mackerel, that admirable scomber of these seas, its body blue and its head silver; brilliant azurors whose name alone dispenses with any description; striped sea breams, their fins variegated in blue and yellow; banded sea breams, set off by a black band on the tail; girdled sea breams, elegantly corseted in their six sashes; snipefish, true flute-mouthed trumpetfish, some specimens of which reached a meter in length; Japanese salamanders; snake-eels of the moray kind, long six-foot serpents with small, lively eyes and a wide mouth bristling with teeth — and so on.

Our admiration held always at its highest pitch. Our exclamations never ran dry. Ned named the fish, Conseil classified them, and I stood enraptured before the liveliness of their movements and the beauty of their forms. Never before had I been given the chance to catch these animals alive and free in their natural element.

I will not list all the varieties that passed thus before our dazzled eyes, this whole collection of the seas of Japan and China. The fish came running, more numerous than birds in the air, drawn no doubt by the dazzling focus of the electric light.

Suddenly the salon filled with light again. The metal panels closed. The enchanting vision vanished. But for a long while I went on dreaming, until the moment my gaze fixed on the instruments hung along the walls. The compass still showed our heading as north-northeast, the pressure gauge indicated a pressure of five atmospheres, corresponding to a depth of fifty meters, and the electric log gave a speed of fifteen miles an hour.

I waited for Captain Nemo. But he didn't appear. The clock read five.

Ned Land and Conseil went back to their cabin. I returned to my own room. My dinner was waiting there, ready. It consisted of a turtle soup made from the most delicate hawksbills, a red mullet with white, slightly flaky flesh — its liver, prepared separately, made a delicious dish — and fillets of that meat of the emperor holocanthus, whose flavor struck me as superior to salmon's.

I spent the evening reading, writing, thinking. Then, as sleep crept over me, I stretched out on my bed of eelgrass and fell into a deep sleep, while the Nautilus slid along through the swift current of the Black River.
