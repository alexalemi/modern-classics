Latitude 47°24′, Longitude 17°28′

In the wake of that storm we had been thrown back to the east. Every hope of escaping onto the approaches of New York or of the St. Lawrence had vanished. Poor Ned, in despair, shut himself away as thoroughly as Captain Nemo. Conseil and I no longer left each other's side.

I said the Nautilus had been carried off to the east. I should have said, more precisely, to the northeast. For several days it wandered now over the surface of the waves, now beneath, in the midst of those fogs so dreaded by sailors. They are caused chiefly by the melting of the ice, which keeps the air here saturated with moisture. How many ships have been lost in these waters, running in to make out the uncertain lights of the coast! How many disasters owed to these dense, blinding banks of fog! How many shocks upon these reefs whose breaking surf is drowned out by the roar of the wind! How many collisions between vessels, in spite of their running lights, in spite of the warnings of their whistles and their alarm bells!

And so the bottom of these seas had the look of a battlefield, where all these vanquished of the Ocean still lay: some old and already thickly encrusted, others fresh and reflecting the glare of our beacon on their ironwork and their copper hulls. Among them, how many ships lost with all hands, with their crews and their whole crowds of emigrants, at those dangerous points flagged in the statistics — Cape Race, St. Paul's Island, the Strait of Belle Isle, the estuary of the St. Lawrence! And in these last few years alone, how many victims added to those grim records by the lines of the Royal Mail, the Inman, the Montreal — the Solway, the Isis, the Paramatta, the Hungarian, the Canadian, the Anglo-Saxon, the Humboldt, the United States, all run aground; the Arctic, the Lyonnais, sunk in collision; the President, the Pacific, the City of Glasgow, vanished for reasons never known — a scattering of dark wreckage through which the Nautilus now sailed, as though it were passing the dead in review!

On May 15 we were off the southern tip of the Grand Banks of Newfoundland. These banks are a product of the sea's own deposits, an enormous accumulation of organic debris brought either from the Equator by the current of the Gulf Stream, or from the northern pole by that cold countercurrent that runs along the American coast. Here, too, pile up the erratic blocks dragged down by the breakup of the ice. Here has formed a vast ossuary of fish, of mollusks, of zoophytes, which perish here by the billion.

The sea is not especially deep over the Grand Banks. A few hundred fathoms at most. But toward the south there suddenly opens a deep trough, a pit three thousand meters down. Here the Gulf Stream spreads out. Its waters flower wide. It loses its speed and its warmth, but it becomes a sea in its own right.

Among the fish that the Nautilus startled as it passed, I will mention the lumpfish, a meter long, blackish along the back and orange on the belly, which sets its fellows an example of conjugal fidelity they rarely follow; a large unernack, a kind of emerald moray, excellent eating; some big-eyed karraks, whose heads bear a certain resemblance to a dog's; blennies, egg-laying like the snakes; black gobies, or gudgeon, two decimeters long; and long-tailed grenadiers with a silvery gleam, swift fish that had ventured far from the hyperborean seas.

The nets also brought up a bold, brazen fish, powerful and heavily muscled, armed with spines on its head and prickles along its fins — a veritable scorpion two to three meters long, the sworn enemy of the blennies, the cods, and the salmon: it was the sculpin of the northern seas, its body knobbed and warty, brown in color, red at the fins. The fishermen of the Nautilus had some trouble laying hold of this animal, which, thanks to the structure of its gill covers, keeps its breathing organs safe from the drying touch of the air and can survive some time out of the water.

I mention now — for the record — some little boskian fish that keep company with ships for long stretches in the northern seas; some sharp-snouted bleak, peculiar to the North Atlantic; some rascasses; and I come at last to the cods, and chiefly to that species the common cod, which I caught in its favorite haunt, on this inexhaustible bank of Newfoundland.

You might call these cods mountain fish, for Newfoundland is really nothing but an underwater mountain. When the Nautilus opened itself a path through their crowded ranks, Conseil could not hold back an observation:

"That! Cod?" he said. "But I always thought cod were flat, like dabs or soles."

"You innocent!" I cried. "Cod are only flat at the grocer's, where they're shown split open and laid out. In the water they're spindle-shaped, like mullet, and perfectly built for moving."

"I'll take Master's word for it," Conseil replied. "What a swarm — what an anthill of them!"

"Ah, my friend, there'd be far more still, if it weren't for their enemies — the rascasses and men! Do you know how many eggs have been counted in a single female?"

"Let's do the thing properly," Conseil replied. "Five hundred thousand."

"Eleven million, my friend."

"Eleven million. That I will never accept — not unless I count them myself."

"Then count them, Conseil. But you'll come round to believing me a great deal faster. Besides, it's by the thousands that the French, the English, the Americans, the Danes, the Norwegians fish for cod. They're eaten in prodigious quantities, and without these fish's astonishing fertility the seas would soon be stripped bare of them. In England and America alone, five thousand ships manned by seventy-five thousand sailors are employed in the cod fishery. Each ship brings back forty thousand on average, which comes to twenty-five million. And off the coasts of Norway, the same again."

"Fine," Conseil replied. "I'll defer to Master. I won't count them."

"Count what?"

"The eleven million eggs. But I will make one remark."

"And that is?"

"That if every one of those eggs hatched, four cod would be enough to feed England, America, and Norway."

As we skimmed the bottom of the Newfoundland banks, I could see, perfectly clearly, those long lines armed with two hundred hooks apiece that each boat pays out by the dozen. Each line, dragged down at one end by a small grapnel, was held at the surface by a rope made fast to a cork buoy. The Nautilus had to maneuver deftly through the middle of this underwater web.

In any case, it did not linger long in these busy waters. It rose toward the forty-second parallel of latitude. That put us on a level with St. John's, Newfoundland, and with Heart's Content, where the far end of the transatlantic cable comes ashore.

The Nautilus, instead of holding on to the north, took a course to the east, as though meaning to follow that telegraph plateau on which the cable rests, and whose relief repeated soundings have mapped with extreme precision.

It was on May 17, some five hundred miles from Heart's Content, at a depth of two thousand eight hundred meters, that I caught sight of the cable lying on the sea floor. Conseil, whom I had not warned, took it at first for some gigantic sea serpent and was getting ready to classify it by his usual method. But I set the good fellow straight, and to console him for his disappointment I told him various particulars about the laying of this cable.

The first cable was established during the years 1857 and 1858; but after transmitting some four hundred telegrams, it ceased to function. In 1863 the engineers built a new one, measuring three thousand four hundred kilometers and weighing four thousand five hundred tons, which was loaded aboard the Great Eastern. This attempt failed as well.

Now, on May 25, the Nautilus, submerged at a depth of three thousand eight hundred thirty-six meters, found itself precisely at the spot where the break occurred that ruined the enterprise. It was six hundred thirty-eight miles from the coast of Ireland. At two in the afternoon it had been noticed that communications with Europe had just been cut off. The electricians on board resolved to cut the cable before fishing it up, and by eleven that night they had brought up the damaged section. They made a fresh joint and splice; then the cable was lowered again. But a few days later it snapped once more, and could not be recovered from the depths of the Ocean.

The Americans were not discouraged. The daring Cyrus Field, promoter of the enterprise, who was risking his whole fortune on it, called for a new subscription. It was covered at once. Another cable was made, on better principles. The bundle of conducting wires, insulated in a sheath of gutta-percha, was protected by a padding of textile material held within a metal armature. The Great Eastern put to sea again on July 13, 1866.

The operation went well. There was, however, one incident. Several times, as they were paying out the cable, the electricians noticed that nails had recently been driven into it, meant to ruin its core. Captain Anderson, his officers, and his engineers met, deliberated, and posted a notice that if the guilty man were caught aboard, he would be thrown into the sea with no further trial. From that moment the criminal attempts stopped.

On July 23, the Great Eastern was no more than eight hundred kilometers from Newfoundland when it received word by telegraph from Ireland of the armistice concluded between Prussia and Austria after Sadowa. On the 27th, through the fog, it made out the port of Heart's Content. The enterprise had come to a happy end, and in its first dispatch young America sent old Europe these wise words, so rarely understood: "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace to men of good will."

I had not expected to find the electric cable in its original state, as it was when it left the workshops that made it. The long serpent, coated with fragments of shell, bristling with foraminifera, was crusted over in a stony sheath that protected it against the boring mollusks. It lay quietly, sheltered from the movements of the sea, and under a pressure favorable to the transmission of that electric spark which passes from America to Europe in thirty-two hundredths of a second. The life of this cable will no doubt be endless, for it has been observed that the gutta-percha sheath actually improves with its long stay in seawater.

Besides, on this so happily chosen plateau, the cable is never sunk to depths great enough to break it. The Nautilus followed it down to its lowest point, four thousand four hundred thirty-one meters down, and there it still rested without any strain of tension. Then we drew near the place where the accident of 1863 had happened.

The ocean floor there formed a valley a hundred and twenty kilometers wide, on which you might have set Mont Blanc without its summit breaking the surface of the waves. This valley is closed off to the east by a sheer wall two thousand meters high. We reached it on May 28, and the Nautilus was now no more than a hundred and fifty kilometers from Ireland.

Was Captain Nemo going to rise and make land on the British Isles? No. To my great surprise, he went back down to the south and returned toward European waters. Rounding the Emerald Isle, I caught a moment's glimpse of Cape Clear and the light of Fastnet, which guides the thousands of ships that put out from Glasgow or Liverpool.

An important question then presented itself to my mind.

Would the Nautilus dare to venture into the English Channel? Ned Land, who had reappeared since we began to close with land, never stopped questioning me. How was I to answer him? Captain Nemo remained invisible. Having let the Canadian glimpse the shores of America, was he now going to show me the coasts of France?

Meanwhile the Nautilus kept dropping steadily toward the south. On May 30 it passed in sight of Land's End, between the outermost point of England and the Scilly Isles, which it left to starboard.

If it meant to enter the Channel, it needed to bear frankly to the east. It did not.

All through the day of May 31, the Nautilus described a series of circles on the sea that intrigued me deeply. It seemed to be searching for a spot it was having some trouble finding. At noon Captain Nemo came up to take the ship's position himself. He did not say a word to me. He seemed to me gloomier than ever. What could be casting him down like this? Was it his nearness to the shores of Europe? Did he feel some resurgence of memory of the country he had left behind? What was he feeling then — remorse, or regret? For a long while this thought occupied my mind, and I had a kind of presentiment that chance would soon betray the captain's secrets.

The next day, June 31, the Nautilus held to the same maneuvers. It was plain that it was trying to fix a precise point in the Ocean. Captain Nemo came up to take the height of the sun, as he had done the day before. The sea was calm, the sky clear. Eight miles to the east, a great steamship stood out on the line of the horizon. No flag flew at its gaff, and I could not make out its nationality.

A few minutes before the sun crossed the meridian, Captain Nemo took his sextant and made his observation with extreme precision. The absolute calm of the waves made his task easier. The Nautilus lay motionless, feeling neither roll nor pitch.

I was on the platform at that moment. When his reading was finished, the captain spoke these words and no more:

"It is here!"

He went back down through the hatch. Had he seen the vessel that was altering its course and seemed to be drawing nearer to us? I could not say.

I returned to the salon. The hatch closed, and I heard the hiss of water in the tanks. The Nautilus began to sink, along a vertical line, for its screw, now checked, gave it no forward motion at all.

A few minutes later it came to rest at a depth of eight hundred thirty-three meters, settling on the bottom.

Then the luminous ceiling of the salon went out, the panels opened, and through the windows I saw the sea brilliantly lit by the rays of the beacon for a radius of half a mile.

I looked out to port, and saw nothing but the immensity of the tranquil waters.

To starboard, on the bottom, there rose a heavy swelling shape that caught my attention. You would have said it was ruins buried under a crust of whitish shells, as under a mantle of snow. Studying this mass closely, I thought I could make out the thickened forms of a ship, stripped of its masts, that must have gone down bow-first. The wreck certainly dated from a distant time. To have become so caked in the limestone of the waters, it had already spent many long years down there on the floor of the Ocean.

What ship was this? Why had the Nautilus come to visit its grave? Had it really been a shipwreck that dragged this vessel under the waves?

I did not know what to think, when, close beside me, I heard Captain Nemo say in a slow voice:

"In her day this ship was called the Marseillais. She carried seventy-four guns and was launched in 1762. On August 13, 1778, commanded by La Poype-Vertrieux, she fought boldly against the Preston. On July 4, 1779, she took part with Admiral d'Estaing's squadron in the capture of Grenada. On September 5, 1781, she joined the Comte de Grasse's action in Chesapeake Bay. In 1794 the French Republic changed her name. On April 16 of that same year she joined Villaret-Joyeuse's squadron at Brest, charged with escorting a convoy of wheat coming from America under the command of Admiral Van Stabel. On the 11th and 12th of Prairial, Year Two, this squadron met the English ships. Professor, today is the 13th of Prairial, the first of June, 1868. Seventy-four years ago to the day, at this very spot, at latitude 47°24′ and longitude 17°28′, this ship, after a heroic fight, dismasted of all three of her masts, the water in her holds, a third of her crew out of action, chose to go down with her three hundred and fifty-six sailors rather than surrender — and nailing her colors to her stern, she vanished beneath the waves to the cry of: Long live the Republic!"

"The Vengeur!" I cried.

"Yes, Professor. The Vengeur! A fine name!" murmured Captain Nemo, folding his arms.
