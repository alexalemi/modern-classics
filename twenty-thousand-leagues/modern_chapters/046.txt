A Massacre

The way he had said it, the suddenness of the whole scene, the history of that patriot ship recounted so coldly at first, and then the emotion with which the strange man had spoken his last words, that name Vengeur — whose meaning could not escape me — all of it came together to strike deep into my mind. My eyes never left the captain. He, his hands stretched out toward the sea, was gazing with a burning eye at that glorious wreck. Perhaps I would never know who he was, where he had come from, where he was going — but more and more I saw the man break free of the scholar. It was no ordinary misanthropy that had shut Captain Nemo and his companions inside the hull of the Nautilus, but a hatred, monstrous or sublime, that time could not weaken.

Was that hatred still hunting for vengeance? The future would soon teach me.

Meanwhile the Nautilus was rising slowly toward the surface, and I watched the dim shapes of the Vengeur disappear little by little. Soon a slight roll told me that we were floating in the open air.

At that moment a muffled detonation reached us. I looked at the captain. The captain did not move.

"Captain?" I said.

He did not answer.

I left him and climbed up onto the platform. Conseil and the Canadian had gotten there ahead of me.

"Where did that shot come from?" I asked.

"A cannon shot," Ned Land answered.

I looked in the direction of the ship I had spotted earlier. It had drawn closer to the Nautilus, and you could see it was cramming on steam. Six miles separated it from us.

"What ship is that, Ned?"

"From her rigging, from the height of her lower masts," the Canadian replied, "I'd bet she's a warship. May she come down on us and sink this damned Nautilus, if that's what it takes!"

"Friend Ned," Conseil replied, "what harm can it do the Nautilus? Will it go and attack us under the waves? Will it cannonade us on the ocean floor?"

"Tell me, Ned," I asked, "can you make out this ship's nationality?"

The Canadian, knitting his brows, lowering his lids, crinkling his eyes at the corners, stared for a few moments at the ship with all the force of his gaze.

"No, Professor," he answered. "I can't tell what nation she belongs to. Her flag isn't hoisted. But I can swear she's a warship, because there's a long pennant streaming from the top of her mainmast."

For a quarter of an hour we went on watching the ship as it bore down on us. Still, I couldn't believe it had recognized the Nautilus at that distance, still less that it knew what this underwater craft was.

Soon the Canadian announced that this ship was a great warship, ram-bowed, a two-decked ironclad. Thick black smoke poured from its two funnels. Its furled sails blended into the line of the yards. Its gaff flew no flag. The distance still made it impossible to make out the colors of its pennant, which streamed like a thin ribbon.

It was coming on fast. If Captain Nemo let it draw near, a chance of rescue was opening for us.

"Professor," Ned Land said to me, "let that ship pass within a mile of us and I'll throw myself into the sea, and I urge you to do the same."

I made no answer to the Canadian's proposal, and I went on watching the ship as it grew larger before my eyes. English, French, American, or Russian — whatever it was, it would surely take us in if we could only reach its side.

"Master will kindly remember," Conseil said then, "that we have some experience at swimming. He can rely on me to tow him toward that ship, should it suit him to follow friend Ned."

I was about to answer when a burst of white vapor spurted from the bow of the warship. Then, a few seconds later, the water — churned up by the fall of some heavy body — splashed over the stern of the Nautilus. A moment after that, a detonation struck my ear.

"What? They're firing on us!" I cried.

"Fine fellows!" muttered the Canadian.

"So they don't take us for castaways clinging to a wreck!"

"Begging Master's pardon... Well!" said Conseil, shaking off the water that a fresh cannonball had sent splashing all the way up to him. "Begging Master's pardon, they've recognized the narwhal, and they're firing on the narwhal."

"But surely they can see," I cried, "that they're dealing with men."

"Maybe that's exactly why!" Ned Land answered, looking at me.

A whole revelation dawned on me. No doubt people now knew what to make of the supposed monster's existence. No doubt, during its clash with the Abraham Lincoln, when the Canadian struck it with his harpoon, Commander Farragut had realized that the narwhal was an underwater boat, more dangerous than any supernatural whale. Yes, that had to be it, and on every sea, no doubt, they were now hunting this terrible engine of destruction!

Terrible indeed, if — as one might well suppose — Captain Nemo was using the Nautilus for a work of vengeance! That night when he shut us up in the cell, in the middle of the Indian Ocean, hadn't he attacked some ship? That man now buried in the coral cemetery — hadn't he been a victim of the collision the Nautilus had caused? Yes, I say it again. That must be how it was. A part of Captain Nemo's mysterious existence was coming to light. And even if his identity was not known, at least the nations banded together against him were now hunting not some fabulous creature, but a man who had sworn them an implacable hatred!

That whole formidable past rose up before my eyes. Instead of finding friends aboard the approaching ship, we could find nothing there but merciless enemies.

Meanwhile the cannonballs were multiplying around us. Some, striking the surface of the water, went ricocheting off to lose themselves at considerable distances. But not one hit the Nautilus.

The ironclad was now no more than three miles off. Despite its violent bombardment, Captain Nemo did not appear on the platform. And yet one of those conical shells, striking the hull of the Nautilus square on, would have been fatal to it.

The Canadian said to me then:

"Professor, we have to try everything to get ourselves out of this fix. Let's signal them! A thousand devils! Maybe they'll understand we're honest folk!"

Ned Land took out his handkerchief to wave it in the air. But he had barely unfurled it when — struck down by an iron hand, for all his prodigious strength — he crumpled to the deck.

"Wretch!" cried the captain. "Do you want me to nail you to the ram of the Nautilus before it hurls itself against that ship?"

Captain Nemo, terrible to hear, was more terrible still to see. His face had gone white with the spasms of his heart, which must have stopped beating for an instant. His pupils had contracted horribly. His voice no longer spoke — it roared. His body bent forward, he was wrenching the Canadian's shoulders beneath his hand.

Then, letting him go and turning back toward the warship whose shells were raining down around him:

"Ah! So you know who I am, ship of an accursed nation!" he cried in his powerful voice. "I had no need of your colors to know you! Look! I'll show you mine!"

And Captain Nemo unfurled at the front of the platform a black flag, like the one he had already planted at the South Pole.

At that instant a cannonball, striking the hull of the Nautilus at an angle without piercing it, ricocheted past the captain and went off to lose itself in the sea.

Captain Nemo shrugged. Then, turning to me:

"Go below," he told me curtly. "Go below, you and your companions."

"Captain," I cried, "are you really going to attack that ship?"

"Professor, I am going to sink it."

"You can't do that!"

"I will do it," Captain Nemo replied coldly. "Do not presume to judge me, Professor. Fate is showing you what you were never meant to see. The attack has come. The answer will be terrible. Get back inside."

"That ship — what is it?"

"You don't know? Well then — so much the better! Its nationality, at least, will stay a secret to you. Go below."

The Canadian, Conseil, and I could do nothing but obey. Some fifteen of the Nautilus's sailors surrounded the captain, watching with an implacable hatred the ship that was bearing down on them. You could feel that the same breath of vengeance drove every one of those souls.

I went below just as another shell grazed the hull of the Nautilus once more, and I heard the captain cry out:

"Strike, you mad ship! Squander your useless shot! You won't escape the ram of the Nautilus. But this is not the place where you're meant to die! I won't have your ruins mingle with the ruins of the Vengeur!"

I went back to my cabin. The captain and his first officer had stayed on the platform. The propeller was set turning, and the Nautilus, drawing swiftly away, put itself out of range of the warship's shells. But the pursuit went on, and Captain Nemo was content simply to hold his distance.

Around four in the afternoon, unable to hold back the impatience and dread devouring me, I came back to the central staircase. The hatch was open. I ventured out onto the platform. The captain was still pacing there with an agitated step. He was watching the ship, which lay to leeward five or six miles off. He circled around it like a wild beast, and, drawing it eastward, let himself be pursued. And yet he did not attack. Perhaps he was still hesitating?

I wanted to step in one last time. But I had barely called out to Captain Nemo when he silenced me:

"I am the law, I am justice!" he told me. "I am the oppressed, and there is the oppressor! Through him, everything I loved, cherished, revered — country, wife, children, my father, my mother — I watched it all perish! Everything I hate is there! Be silent!"

I cast one last look at the warship, which was cramming on steam. Then I rejoined Ned and Conseil.

"We're getting away!" I cried.

"Good," said Ned. "What ship is it?"

"I don't know. But whatever it is, it will be sunk before nightfall. In any case, better to die with it than to make ourselves accomplices to a reprisal whose justice no one can measure."

"That's my thinking too," Ned Land answered coldly. "Let's wait for night."

Night came. A deep silence reigned aboard. The compass showed that the Nautilus had not changed course. I could hear the beat of its propeller striking the waves with a swift regularity. It was holding at the surface, and a slight roll carried it now to one side, now to the other.

My companions and I had resolved to make our escape the moment the ship came close enough for us to be heard, or at least seen, for the moon — which would be full three days later — was shining brightly. Once aboard that ship, even if we couldn't ward off the blow that threatened it, at least we would do everything circumstances allowed us to attempt. Several times I thought the Nautilus was making ready to attack. But it only let its adversary draw nearer, and, a little while later, resumed its fleeing pace.

Part of the night passed without incident. We watched for our chance to act. We spoke little, being too overwrought. Ned Land would have liked to fling himself into the sea. I forced him to wait. To my mind, the Nautilus would attack the two-decker at the surface, and then escape would be not only possible but easy.

At three in the morning, uneasy, I climbed up onto the platform. Captain Nemo had not left it. He stood at the bow, near his flag, which a light breeze spread out above his head. He never took his eyes off the ship. His gaze, of an extraordinary intensity, seemed to draw it in, to hypnotize it, to reel it in more surely than if he had it under tow!

The moon was just then crossing the meridian. Jupiter was rising in the east. In the midst of all that peaceful nature, sky and ocean vied with each other in tranquility, and the sea offered to the queen of night the loveliest mirror that had ever reflected her image.

And when I thought of that deep calm of the elements, set against all the fury smoldering inside the hull of the all-but-invisible Nautilus, I felt my whole being shudder.

The ship was holding two miles off. It had come closer, still steering toward that phosphorescent glow that marked the Nautilus's presence. I saw its running lights, green and red, and its white lamp hung from the forestay of the foremast. A faint glow lit up its rigging and showed that its fires were being driven to the limit. Showers of sparks, cinders of blazing coal escaping from its funnels, studded the air with stars.

I stayed like that until six in the morning, without Captain Nemo seeming to notice me. The ship stayed a mile and a half from us, and with the first gleams of daylight its bombardment began again. The moment could not be far off when, the Nautilus attacking its adversary, my companions and I would leave forever this man I dared not judge.

I was getting ready to go below and warn them when the first officer came up onto the platform. Several sailors were with him. Captain Nemo did not see them, or did not want to see them. Certain preparations were made that you might have called the Nautilus's "clearing for action." They were very simple. The rail that formed a balustrade around the platform was lowered. Likewise, the cages of the beacon and the helmsman withdrew into the hull until they were flush with it. The surface of the long cigar of sheet metal no longer offered a single projection that might hamper its maneuvering.

I went back to the salon. The Nautilus was still riding at the surface. A few gleams of morning filtered down into the liquid layer. Beneath certain swells of the waves, the windows came alive with the reddish light of the rising sun. That terrible day of June 2 was dawning.

At five o'clock the log told me that the Nautilus's speed was slackening. I understood that it was letting the enemy close in. Besides, the detonations were coming louder now, more violent. The cannonballs plowed the surrounding water and screwed down into it with a strange whistling.

"My friends," I said, "the moment has come. A handshake, and may God watch over us!"

Ned Land was resolute, Conseil calm, and I on edge, barely holding myself together.

We passed into the library. Just as I was pushing open the door that led to the well of the central staircase, I heard the upper hatch slam shut.

The Canadian dashed for the steps, but I stopped him. A hiss I knew all too well told me that water was pouring into the ship's ballast tanks. And indeed, in a matter of moments, the Nautilus dove a few meters beneath the surface of the waves.

I understood its maneuver. It was too late to act.

The Nautilus had no intention of striking the two-decker in its impenetrable armor, but below its waterline, there where the metal shell no longer protects the planking.

We were prisoners once again, forced witnesses to the sinister drama being prepared. And in any case, we scarcely had time to think. Taking refuge in my cabin, we looked at one another without a word. A deep stupor had seized my mind. The movement of thought stopped inside me. I was in that painful state that comes just before the waiting for some appalling explosion. I waited, I listened, I lived by the sense of hearing alone!

Meanwhile the Nautilus's speed increased noticeably. It was gathering its run. Its whole hull was quivering.

Suddenly I let out a cry. A shock came — but a relatively slight one. I felt the piercing force of the steel ram. I heard scrapings, gratings. But the Nautilus, carried on by the power of its propulsion, passed straight through the mass of the ship like a sailmaker's needle through canvas!

I couldn't bear it. Wild, frantic, I burst out of my cabin and rushed into the salon.

Captain Nemo was there. Silent, grim, implacable, he was looking out through the port window.

An enormous mass was sinking beneath the waters, and so as to miss nothing of its death throes, the Nautilus was going down into the abyss along with it. Ten meters from me I saw that hull torn open, the water pouring in with a sound like thunder, then the double row of cannon and the bulwarks. The deck was covered with black shapes, struggling.

The water rose. The wretched men flung themselves into the shrouds, clung to the masts, writhed beneath the water. It was a human anthill caught in the sudden flooding of a sea!

Paralyzed, rigid with anguish, my hair standing on end, my eyes stretched wide, my breath coming short — breathless, voiceless — I too was watching! An irresistible pull held me pinned to the glass!

The huge ship was sinking slowly. The Nautilus, following it down, watched its every movement. All at once there came an explosion. Compressed air blew the ship's decks apart as if fire had reached the magazines. The surge of water was so violent that the Nautilus was thrown off course.

Then the doomed ship went down faster. Its tops, crowded with victims, came into view, then the crosstrees, bending under clusters of men, and finally the very peak of its mainmast. Then the dark mass vanished, and with it that crew of corpses, dragged down by a tremendous whirlpool...

I turned back toward Captain Nemo. That terrible dispenser of justice, that veritable archangel of hatred, was still watching. When it was all over, Captain Nemo went to the door of his cabin, opened it, and went in. I followed him with my eyes.

On the far bulkhead, beneath the portraits of his heroes, I saw the portrait of a woman, still young, and of two little children. Captain Nemo gazed at them for a few moments, stretched out his arms to them, and, sinking to his knees, broke into sobs.
