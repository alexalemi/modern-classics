The Red Sea

(Part 2 of 2)

On February 9 the Nautilus was cruising the widest part of the Red Sea, the stretch that runs between Suakin on the western shore and Quonfodah on the eastern, a hundred and ninety miles across.

That day at noon, after taking our position, Captain Nemo came up onto the platform, where I happened to be. I promised myself I would not let him go back down without at least sounding him out about his further plans. He came over the moment he saw me, graciously offered me a cigar, and said:

"Well, Professor, does this Red Sea please you? Have you seen enough of the wonders it hides — its fish and its zoophytes, its flower beds of sponge and its forests of coral? Have you caught sight of the towns scattered along its shores?"

"Yes, Captain Nemo," I answered, "and the Nautilus has lent itself marvelously to the whole study. Ah, she's an intelligent boat!"

"Yes, sir — intelligent, daring, and invulnerable! She fears neither the terrible storms of the Red Sea, nor its currents, nor its reefs."

"Quite so," I said. "This sea is reckoned among the worst of them all, and if I'm not mistaken, in the time of the ancients its reputation was detestable."

"Detestable, Professor Aronnax. The Greek and Latin historians have nothing good to say of it. Strabo tells us it is particularly rough during the season of the Etesian winds and the rains. The Arab geographer Edrisi, who describes it under the name of the Gulf of Colzoum, reports that ships perished in great numbers on its sandbanks, and that no one dared sail it by night. It is, he claims, a sea prone to frightful hurricanes, strewn with inhospitable islands, and one that 'offers nothing good' either in its depths or on its surface. And indeed, that is the opinion you find in Arrian, in Agatharchides, and in Artemidorus."

"You can tell," I replied, "that those historians never sailed aboard the Nautilus."

"True," the captain answered, smiling, "and in that respect the moderns are no further along than the ancients. It took mankind whole centuries to discover the mechanical power of steam! Who knows whether, a hundred years from now, the world will see a second Nautilus? Progress is slow, Professor Aronnax."

"That's true," I said. "Your ship is a century ahead of its time — several centuries, perhaps. What a tragedy that such a secret should die with the man who invented it!"

Captain Nemo made no reply. After a few minutes' silence, he said:

"You were telling me about the opinion the old historians held of the dangers of sailing the Red Sea?"

"I was," I answered. "But weren't their fears exaggerated?"

"Yes and no, Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo replied — and he struck me as a man who knew "his Red Sea" through and through. "What is no longer dangerous for a modern vessel — well rigged, solidly built, master of its own course thanks to obedient steam — offered every kind of peril to the ships of the ancients. Picture those first navigators venturing out in boats made of planks sewn together with palm-fiber rope, caulked with powdered resin, and smeared with the grease of sea-dogs. They hadn't even the instruments to take their bearings; they sailed by dead reckoning through currents they barely knew. Under those conditions, shipwrecks were bound to be many. But in our day the steamers running between Suez and the southern seas have nothing more to fear from the rages of this gulf, monsoons and all. Their captains and passengers no longer prepare for the voyage with propitiatory sacrifices, and on their return they no longer go, decked in garlands and gilded ribbons, to thank the gods in the temple nearby."

"I grant you that," I said, "and steam does seem to have killed off gratitude in the hearts of sailors. But Captain, since you appear to have made a special study of this sea, can you tell me the origin of its name?"

"There are numerous explanations on that score, Professor Aronnax. Would you care to hear the opinion of a fourteenth-century chronicler?"

"Gladly."

"This fanciful fellow claims the name was given after the passage of the Israelites, when Pharaoh perished in the waters that closed again at the voice of Moses:

To mark that marvel, the whole sea
Turned red, and ran with crimson dye;
And ever after, none knew how
To name it but the Red Sea now.

"A poet's explanation, Captain Nemo," I answered, "but I couldn't be satisfied with it. I'll ask you, then, for your own opinion."

"Here it is. To my mind, Professor Aronnax, this name 'Red Sea' should be seen as a translation of the Hebrew word Edrom, and if the ancients gave it that name, it was on account of the peculiar coloring of its waters."

"So far, though, I've seen nothing but clear water, with no particular tint at all."

"No doubt. But as you push toward the head of the gulf, you'll notice that singular appearance. I remember seeing the Bay of Tor entirely red, like a lake of blood."

"And you attribute that color to the presence of a microscopic alga?"

"Yes. It's a purple, mucilaginous matter produced by those tiny plantlets known as trichodesmia, of which it takes forty thousand to fill the space of a single square millimeter. Perhaps you'll come across some when we reach Tor."

"So then, Captain Nemo, this isn't the first time you've traveled the Red Sea aboard the Nautilus?"

"No, sir."

"Then, since you spoke a moment ago of the passage of the Israelites and the disaster of the Egyptians, may I ask whether you've found, beneath these waters, any trace of that great historical event?"

"No, Professor — and for an excellent reason."

"Which is?"

"That the very spot where Moses crossed with all his people is now so silted up that the camels can barely wet their legs there. You'll understand that my Nautilus wouldn't have enough water under her."

"And that spot?" I asked.

"That spot lies a little above Suez, in the arm that once formed a deep estuary, back when the Red Sea reached all the way to the Bitter Lakes. Now, whether the crossing was miraculous or not, the Israelites nonetheless passed there to reach the Promised Land, and Pharaoh's army perished at that exact place. So I believe that excavations carried out in those sands would turn up a great quantity of weapons and implements of Egyptian origin."

"That's obvious," I answered, "and one can only hope, for the archaeologists' sake, that such digs will be made sooner or later, once new towns spring up on that isthmus after the cutting of the Suez Canal. A canal quite useless for a ship like the Nautilus!"

"No doubt, but useful to the whole world," said Captain Nemo. "The ancients understood very well how valuable it would be to their commerce to establish a link between the Red Sea and the Mediterranean; but they never thought of digging a direct canal, and took the Nile as their go-between. In all likelihood the canal joining the Nile to the Red Sea was begun under Sesostris, if we're to believe tradition. What is certain is that, six hundred and fifteen years before Jesus Christ, Necho undertook the works of a canal fed by the waters of the Nile, across that plain of Egypt which faces Arabia. It could be traveled up in four days, and it was wide enough for two triremes to pass abreast. It was continued by Darius, son of Hystaspes, and probably completed by Ptolemy II. Strabo saw it in use for navigation; but the slightness of its gradient between its starting point, near Bubastis, and the Red Sea made it navigable only during a few months of the year. This canal served commerce until the age of the Antonines; then it was abandoned, silted up, restored by order of the Caliph Omar, and finally filled in for good in the year 761 or 762 by the Caliph Al-Mansor, who wanted to cut off supplies to Mohammed-ben-Abdoallah, then in revolt against him. During the Egyptian campaign, your General Bonaparte found traces of these works in the desert of Suez, and, caught by the tide, he nearly perished a few hours before reaching Hadjaroth — the very place where Moses had camped three thousand three hundred years before him."

"Well, Captain, what the ancients didn't dare undertake — this joining of the two seas, which will cut nine thousand kilometers off the route from Cadiz to the Indies — Monsieur de Lesseps has done, and before long he'll have turned Africa into one immense island."

"Yes, Professor Aronnax, and you have every right to be proud of your countryman. He is a man who does a nation more honor than the greatest of its captains! Like so many others, he began amid frustrations and rebuffs, but he triumphed, for he has the genius of willpower. And it is sad to think that this work, which should have been an international undertaking, which would have been enough to make a reign illustrious, will have succeeded only through the energy of a single man. So — all honor to Monsieur de Lesseps!"

"Yes, all honor to that great citizen," I answered, quite taken aback by the tone in which Captain Nemo had just spoken.

"Unfortunately," he went on, "I can't take you through this Suez Canal. But you'll be able to make out the long jetties of Port Said the day after tomorrow, when we're in the Mediterranean."

"In the Mediterranean!" I exclaimed.

"Yes, Professor. Does that surprise you?"

"What surprises me is to think we'll be there the day after tomorrow."

"Really?"

"Yes, Captain — though I ought to be used to being surprised by nothing since I came aboard your ship!"

"But surprised at what, exactly?"

"At the appalling speed you'll have to drive the Nautilus to, if she's to find herself in the open Mediterranean the day after tomorrow, having rounded all of Africa and doubled the Cape of Good Hope!"

"And who told you she'll round Africa, Professor? Who said anything about doubling the Cape of Good Hope?"

"And yet, unless the Nautilus sails on dry land and passes over the isthmus —"

"Or under it, Professor Aronnax."

"Under it?"

"Just so," Captain Nemo replied calmly. "Long ago, nature made beneath this tongue of land what men are only now making on its surface."

"What! There's a passage?"

"Yes, an underground passage that I've named the Arabian Tunnel. It begins below Suez and comes out in the Gulf of Pelusium."

"But this isthmus is nothing but shifting sand?"

"Down to a certain depth. But at only fifty meters you strike an unshakable bed of rock."

"And was it by chance that you discovered this passage?" I asked, more astonished by the moment.

"Chance and reasoning, Professor — and reasoning even more than chance."

"Captain, I'm listening, but my ear resists what it hears."

"Ah, sir! Aures habent, et non audient — that has been true in every age. Not only does this passage exist, but I've made use of it several times. Otherwise I'd never have ventured today into this dead end of the Red Sea."

"Is it indiscreet to ask how you discovered this tunnel?"

"Sir," the captain answered, "there can be nothing secret between people who are never to part."

I let the insinuation pass and waited for Captain Nemo's account.

"Professor," he said, "it was a simple piece of naturalist's reasoning that led me to discover this passage, which I alone know. I had noticed that in the Red Sea and in the Mediterranean there exist a certain number of fish of absolutely identical species — snake-eels, butterfish, rainbow wrasse, parrotfish, sand-smelts, flying fish. Certain of this fact, I asked myself whether there wasn't some communication between the two seas. If there was, the underground current would necessarily run from the Red Sea to the Mediterranean, simply because of the difference in their levels. So I caught a great many fish in the neighborhood of Suez. I slipped a copper ring over their tails and threw them back into the sea. A few months later, off the coast of Syria, I recovered several specimens of my fish, still wearing their telltale rings. The communication between the two seas was thus proved to me. I searched for it with my Nautilus, I found it, I ventured into it — and before long, Professor, you too will have passed through my Arabian Tunnel!"
