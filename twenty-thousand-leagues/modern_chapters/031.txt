The Mediterranean in Forty-Eight Hours

The Mediterranean — the blue sea above all others, the "Great Sea" of the Hebrews, "the sea" of the Greeks, the mare nostrum of the Romans; fringed with orange groves, aloes, cactus, and maritime pines, sweet with the scent of myrtle, framed by rugged mountains, steeped in a pure and transparent air yet endlessly worked upon by the fires of the earth — is a whole world unto itself. It is here, on its shores and its waters, Michelet says, that a man is tempered anew in one of the mightiest climates on the globe.

But beautiful as it is, I could catch no more than a hurried glimpse of this basin, whose surface covers two million square kilometers. Captain Nemo's personal knowledge failed me here as well, for the enigmatic man did not appear once during this high-speed crossing. I reckon that the Nautilus traveled some six hundred leagues beneath the waves of this sea, and it covered that distance in twice twenty-four hours. Setting out on the morning of February 16 from the waters off Greece, by the 18th, at sunrise, we had cleared the Strait of Gibraltar.

It was plain to me that this Mediterranean, pinched in among the very lands he wished to flee, was hateful to Captain Nemo. Its waves and its breezes carried too many memories back to him, if not too many regrets. Here he no longer had that freedom of movement, that independence of maneuver that the open oceans allowed him, and his Nautilus felt cramped between these close-set shores of Africa and Europe.

And so our speed ran to twenty-five miles an hour — that is, twelve leagues of four kilometers each. It goes without saying that Ned Land, to his great disgust, had to give up his plans for escape. There was no using the dinghy when it was being dragged along at twelve or thirteen meters a second. Leaving the Nautilus under such conditions would have been like jumping from a train going at that speed — a reckless move if ever there was one. Besides, our craft rose to the surface only at night, to renew its supply of air, and it steered solely by the readings of the compass and the log.

So of the interior of this Mediterranean I saw only what the traveler on an express train glimpses of the landscape fleeing before his eyes — the far horizons, and not the foregrounds that flash past like lightning. Even so, Conseil and I were able to observe a few of those Mediterranean fish that the power of their fins kept for a moment or two in the waters of the Nautilus. We kept watch at the windows of the salon, and my notes let me sketch, in a few words, the ichthyology of this sea.

Of the various fish that inhabit it, some I saw clearly, others I only half-glimpsed, to say nothing of those the Nautilus's speed stole from my eyes altogether. Let me be permitted, then, to classify them according to this improvised scheme. It will better convey the swiftness of my observations.

Through the mass of waters, brightly lit by our electric sheets, wound a few of those lampreys a meter long, common to nearly every climate. Oxyrhynchus rays, a kind of skate, five feet across, white-bellied, their backs an ashen gray and speckled, spread themselves out like great shawls swept along by the currents. Other rays flashed by so fast that I could not tell whether they deserved the name of "eagles" the Greeks gave them, or the labels of rat, toad, and bat that modern fishermen have saddled them with. Tope sharks, twelve feet long and especially dreaded by divers, raced one another for speed. Thresher sharks — "sea foxes" — eight feet long and gifted with an extraordinarily keen sense of smell, appeared like great bluish shadows. Gilthead bream, of the sparus family, some of them measuring as much as thirteen decimeters, showed themselves in their livery of silver and azure banded with ribbons, which stood out against the dark tone of their fins — fish consecrated to Venus, their eyes set in a brow of gold; a precious species, at home in every water, fresh or salt, dwelling in rivers, lakes, and oceans, living under every climate, bearing every temperature, and whose race, reaching back to the geological ages of the earth, has kept all its beauty of those first days. Magnificent sturgeons, nine to ten meters long, great travelers, struck the glass of the panels with a powerful tail, showing their bluish backs dotted with small brown spots: they resemble the sharks, though they do not match their strength, and are found in every sea; in spring they love to run up the great rivers, to fight the currents of the Volga, the Danube, the Po, the Rhine, the Loire, the Oder, and they feed on herring, mackerel, salmon, and cod; and though they belong to the class of cartilaginous fish, they are a delicacy — eaten fresh, dried, marinated, or salted, and in the old days they were borne in triumph to the table of the Luculli. But of these varied dwellers in the Mediterranean, the ones I could study to best advantage, whenever the Nautilus drew near the surface, belonged to the sixty-third genus of bony fishes. These were tunny — mackerel-tribe fish, blue-black on the back, their bellies armored in silver, their dorsal rays throwing off gleams of gold. They have a reputation for following in a ship's wake, seeking the cool of its shadow under the fires of a tropical sky, and they did not belie it, escorting the Nautilus as they once escorted the vessels of La Pérouse. For hours on end they matched their speed against our craft. I could not tire of admiring these creatures, truly built for the race — their small heads, their smooth spindle-shaped bodies, which in some ran to more than three meters, their pectoral fins of remarkable power, and their forked tails. They swam in a triangle, like certain flocks of birds whose speed they equaled — which made the ancients say that geometry and strategy were second nature to them. And yet they do not escape the pursuit of the Provençals, who prize them as highly as the peoples of the Propontis and of Italy once did; and it is blindly, heedlessly, that these precious creatures rush in and perish by the thousand in the tuna traps of Marseille.

I will mention, for the record only, those Mediterranean fish that Conseil or I did no more than half-glimpse. There were whitish cusk-eels — pearl-fish — that passed like unseizable vapors; conger-morays, snakes three to four meters long, prettied up in green, blue, and yellow; hakes of the cod family, three feet long, their livers a choice morsel; band-fish, ribbon-like, that floated like fine seaweed; gurnards, which the poets call lyre-fish and sailors call whistlers, their snouts adorned with two triangular, toothed plates that mimic old Homer's instrument; flying gurnards — "swallows" — swimming with the swiftness of the bird whose name they have taken; red-headed soldierfish, their dorsal fins fringed with filaments; shad speckled with black, gray, brown, blue, yellow, and green, sensitive to the silvery ringing of little bells; and splendid turbot — those pheasants of the sea — a kind of diamond-shape with yellowish fins, brown-stippled, whose upper side, the left, is generally marbled in brown and yellow; and, last of all, whole schools of admirable red mullet, true birds of paradise of the ocean, which the Romans paid as much as ten thousand sesterces apiece, and which they killed on their tables so as to follow with a cruel eye their changing colors, from the cinnabar red of life to the pale white of death.

And if I could not observe monkfish, triggerfish, puffers, seahorses, John Dorys, snipefish, blennies, striped mullet, wrasses, smelt, flying fish, anchovies, sea bream, bogues, sea perch, nor any of those chief representatives of the order of flatfish — the dabs, the flounders, the plaice, the soles, the brill — all common to both the Atlantic and the Mediterranean, the blame must fall on the dizzying speed that carried the Nautilus through these teeming waters.

As for the marine mammals, I believe I made out, in passing before the mouth of the Adriatic, two or three sperm whales fitted with a dorsal fin of the physeter kind; a few dolphins of the pilot-whale genus, native to the Mediterranean, the fronts of their heads streaked with little pale lines; and also a dozen seals with white bellies and black coats, known as "monks," which for all the world look like Dominican friars three meters long.

For his part, Conseil believes he caught sight of a turtle six feet across, ornamented with three ridges running lengthwise. I was sorry not to have seen the reptile, for from the description Conseil gave me, I thought I recognized the leatherback, which forms a fairly rare species. For my own part, I noticed only a few loggerheads with elongated shells.

As for the zoophytes, for a few moments I was able to admire a superb orange sea-fir that fastened itself to the glass of the port panel; it was a long slender filament, branching out into endless boughs and ending in the finest lacework the rivals of Arachne ever spun. Unfortunately, I could not fish up this admirable specimen, and no other Mediterranean zoophyte would likely have offered itself to my eyes, if the Nautilus, on the evening of the 16th, had not markedly slackened its speed. This was how it came about.

We were passing then between Sicily and the coast of Tunis. In that narrow space between Cape Bon and the Strait of Messina, the sea floor rises almost abruptly. There a veritable crest has formed, over which only seventeen meters of water remain, while on either side the depth is a hundred and seventy meters. The Nautilus therefore had to maneuver carefully so as not to strike this underwater barrier.

I showed Conseil, on the map of the Mediterranean, the place occupied by this long reef.

"But, if Master doesn't mind my saying so," Conseil observed, "it's like a genuine isthmus joining Europe to Africa."

"Yes, my boy," I answered, "it bars the whole Strait of Libya, and Smith's soundings have proven that the continents were once joined between Cape Boco and Cape Furina."

"I can well believe it," said Conseil.

"I'll add," I went on, "that a similar barrier exists between Gibraltar and Ceuta, which in the geological ages closed off the Mediterranean completely."

"Well now!" said Conseil. "What if some volcanic upheaval were to raise these two barriers back above the waves one day?"

"That's hardly likely, Conseil."

"Still, let Master allow me to finish — if it did happen, it would be a sorry business for Monsieur de Lesseps, who is going to such trouble to cut through his isthmus!"

"I grant you that. But I tell you again, Conseil, that phenomenon will not occur. The violence of the subterranean forces keeps steadily diminishing. The volcanoes, so numerous in the world's first days, are dying out little by little; the internal heat is weakening, the temperature of the globe's lower layers is dropping by an appreciable amount each century — and to our globe's cost, for that heat is its life."

"But the sun—"

"The sun is not enough, Conseil. Can it give heat back to a corpse?"

"Not that I know of."

"Well then, my friend, one day the earth will be that cooled corpse. It will become uninhabitable, and it will be uninhabited, like the moon, which lost its vital heat long ago."

"In how many centuries?" asked Conseil.

"In a few hundred thousand years, my boy."

"Then," replied Conseil, "we have time to finish our voyage — provided Ned Land doesn't meddle in it!"

And Conseil, reassured, went back to studying the shoal that the Nautilus was skimming close over at a moderate speed.

There, on a rocky, volcanic bed, a whole living flora blossomed: sponges, sea cucumbers, glassy cydippes trimmed with reddish cirri and giving off a faint phosphorescence; beroës, commonly known as sea gooseberries, bathed in the shimmerings of a solar spectrum; walking feather stars a meter across, whose crimson reddened the waters; tree-like basket stars of the greatest beauty; peacock-plumes on long stems; a great number of edible sea urchins of varied species; and green sea anemones with grayish trunks and brown disks, lost in their olive-tinted manes of tentacles.

Conseil had busied himself particularly with observing the mollusks and the articulates, and although the nomenclature is a little dry, I don't want to do this good fellow the injustice of leaving out his personal observations.

Among the branch of the mollusks, he lists numerous comb-shaped scallops; donkey's-foot spondylus heaped one upon another; triangular donax clams; three-toothed hyalaea with yellow fins and transparent shells; orange pleurobranchs; sea slugs stippled or sown with greenish points; sea hares, also known as aplysias; dolabellas; fleshy acera; umbrella-shells peculiar to the Mediterranean; sea-ears whose shell yields a much-prized mother-of-pearl; flame-marked scallops; anomias, which the Languedoc folk are said to prefer to oysters; carpet-shells so dear to the people of Marseille; double white fatty venus-clams; a few of those clams that abound on the coasts of North America and command such a considerable trade in New York; scallops with lids of varied colors; date-mussels tucked into their burrows, whose peppery flavor I greatly relished; furrowed venericardias, their shells domed at the top with jutting ribs; sea squirts bristling with scarlet tubercles; carinarias with recurved points, like light little gondolas; crowned pterotracheas; atlantas with spiral shells; gray thetys spotted with white and covered by their fringed mantles; eolids like little slugs; cavolinias crawling on their backs; ear-shells, among them the forget-me-not ear-shell with its oval shell; tawny wentletraps; periwinkles; janthinas; cinerarias; petricolas; lamellarias; cabochons; pandoras; and so on.

As for the articulates, Conseil had, in his notes, very rightly divided them into six classes, three of which belong to the marine world. These are the classes of crustaceans, of cirripedes, and of annelids.

The crustaceans subdivide into nine orders, and the first of these orders comprises the decapods — that is, the animals whose head and thorax are most often fused together, whose mouth apparatus is made up of several pairs of limbs, and which possess four, five, or six pairs of thoracic or walking legs. Conseil had followed the method of our master Milne-Edwards, who makes three sections of the decapods: the brachyurans, the macrurans, and the anomurans. These names are a shade barbarous, but they are exact and precise. Among the brachyurans, Conseil lists amathias, their brows armed with two great diverging spikes; the scorpion inachus, which — I don't know why — symbolized wisdom among the Greeks; masséna and spiny-clawed lambrus crabs, probably strayed onto this shoal, for they ordinarily live at great depths; xanthos; pilumnus crabs; rhomboid crabs; granular calappas — very easy to digest, Conseil notes; toothless corystes; ebalias; cymopolias; woolly dorippes; and so on. Among the macrurans, subdivided into five families — the armored, the burrowers, the crayfish tribe, the prawns, and the ochyzopods — he lists common spiny lobsters, whose flesh is so esteemed in the females; bear-slipper lobsters, or sea-cicadas; shore-dwelling mud-shrimps; and all sorts of edible species — but he says nothing of the crayfish-tribe subdivision that includes the true lobsters, for the spiny lobsters are the Mediterranean's only lobsters. Finally, among the anomurans, he saw common drocinas sheltered behind the abandoned shell they seize for themselves; spiny-fronted homolas; hermit crabs; porcelain crabs; and so on.

There Conseil's work broke off. He had run out of time to complete the class of crustaceans by examining the stomatopods, the amphipods, the homopods, the isopods, the trilobites, the branchiopods, the ostracods, and the entomostracans. And to round off the study of the marine articulates, he would have had to cite the class of cirripedes, which contains the cyclops and the argulus, and the class of annelids, which he would surely have divided into tube-dwellers and dorsibranchs. But the Nautilus, having passed the shoal of the Strait of Libya, resumed its accustomed speed in deeper waters. From then on, no more mollusks, no more articulates, no more zoophytes — barely a few large fish that passed like shadows.

During the night of February 16 to 17, we had entered that second Mediterranean basin, whose greatest depths run to three thousand meters. The Nautilus, driven by its propeller and gliding on its inclined planes, sank down to the sea's lowest layers.

There, in place of nature's marvels, the mass of waters offered my eyes many a moving and terrible scene. For we were now crossing that whole part of the Mediterranean so fertile in disasters. From the Algerian coast to the shores of Provence, how many ships have been wrecked, how many vessels have vanished! The Mediterranean is a mere lake compared to the vast liquid plains of the Pacific, but it is a capricious lake, with changeable waves — today kind and caressing to the frail tartan that seems to float between the double blue of the waters and the sky; tomorrow raging, tormented, torn up by the winds, breaking the strongest ships with its short seas that batter them in quick, hurried blows.

And so, in this rapid passage through the deep layers, how many wrecks I saw lying on the bottom — some already crusted over with corals, others clad only in a coat of rust: anchors, cannon, cannonballs, iron fittings, propeller blades, pieces of machinery, broken cylinders, staved-in boilers, then hulls floating between two waters, some upright, others capsized.

Of these wrecked ships, some had perished by collision, others by striking some granite reef. I saw ships that had gone straight down, their masts erect, their rigging stiffened by the water. They looked as though they were riding at anchor in some immense open roadstead, waiting for the moment to sail. When the Nautilus passed among them and wrapped them in its electric sheets, it seemed those ships were about to salute it with their colors and send it their signal number! But no — nothing but silence and death over that field of catastrophes.

I noticed that the Mediterranean bottom grew more and more cluttered with these grim wrecks as the Nautilus drew near the Strait of Gibraltar. There the coasts of Africa and Europe close in, and in that narrow space collisions are frequent. There I saw numerous iron keels, the fantastic ruins of steamers, some lying on their sides, others standing upright, like formidable beasts. One of these vessels, its flanks torn open, its funnel bent, its paddle wheels stripped down to the frames, its rudder parted from the sternpost and still held by an iron chain, its stern transom eaten away by the sea salts, presented a terrible sight! How many lives shattered in its wreck! How many victims dragged down beneath the waves! Had some sailor aboard survived to tell of that dreadful disaster, or did the waves still keep the secret of the calamity? I don't know why, but the thought came to me that this vessel buried under the sea might be the Atlas, lost with all hands some twenty years before, and never heard of since! Ah, what a sinister tale might be told of these Mediterranean depths — of this vast ossuary, where so many riches have been lost, where so many victims have met their death!

Meanwhile the Nautilus, indifferent and swift, ran at full propeller through the midst of these ruins. On February 18, toward three in the morning, it presented itself at the entrance to the Strait of Gibraltar.

There are two currents there: an upper current, long recognized, which brings the ocean's waters into the basin of the Mediterranean; and then a lower counter-current, whose existence reasoning has demonstrated today. In effect, the sum of the Mediterranean's waters, ceaselessly swelled by the flow of the Atlantic and by the rivers emptying into it, ought to raise the level of this sea year by year, for its evaporation is not enough to restore the balance. And yet it does not, and one has naturally had to admit the existence of a lower current that, through the Strait of Gibraltar, pours the Mediterranean's overflow into the basin of the Atlantic.

A true fact, indeed. And it was this counter-current the Nautilus took advantage of. It advanced rapidly through the narrow pass. For an instant I could make out the admirable ruins of the temple of Hercules, sunk — according to Pliny and Avienus — along with the low island that once bore it up, and a few minutes later we were floating on the waves of the Atlantic.
