Everything by Electricity

"Professor," said Captain Nemo, pointing to the instruments hanging on the walls of his cabin, "here are the devices the navigation of the Nautilus requires. In here, as in the salon, I have them always before my eyes, and they tell me my position and my exact heading in the middle of the Ocean. Some of them you'll recognize: the thermometer, which gives the temperature inside the Nautilus; the barometer, which weighs the pressure of the air and predicts changes in the weather; the hygrometer, which marks the degree of dryness in the atmosphere; the storm-glass, whose mixture, as it breaks down, announces the coming of a storm; the compass, which steers my course; the sextant, which by the height of the sun tells me my latitude; the chronometers, which allow me to calculate my longitude; and finally the day and night glasses, which serve me to scan every point of the horizon when the Nautilus has risen back to the surface of the waves."

"These are the ordinary instruments of any navigator," I answered, "and I know how they're used. But here are others that no doubt answer to the particular demands of the Nautilus. This dial I see, with a needle traveling across it — isn't that a pressure gauge?"

"A pressure gauge, exactly. Put in contact with the water, whose external pressure it registers, it tells me thereby the depth at which my craft is holding."

"And these soundings of a new kind?"

"They're thermometric soundings that report back the temperature of the various layers of water."

"And these other instruments, whose use I can't guess?"

"Here, Professor, I must give you a few explanations," said Captain Nemo. "Kindly hear me out."

He kept silent for a few moments, then he said:

"There is a powerful agent, obedient, quick, easy, one that bends to every use and reigns as master aboard my ship. Everything is done by it. It lights me, it warms me, it is the soul of my mechanical apparatus. This agent is electricity."

"Electricity!" I exclaimed, rather taken aback.

"Yes, Professor."

"And yet, Captain, you command an extreme swiftness of movement that sits poorly with the power of electricity. So far, its dynamic force has remained very limited, capable of producing only small amounts of power!"

"Professor," replied Captain Nemo, "my electricity is not everybody's electricity, and that is all I'll permit myself to tell you about it."

"I won't press you, Captain, and I'll content myself with being thoroughly astonished at such a result. One question only, however — which you needn't answer if it's indiscreet. The elements you use to produce this marvelous agent must wear out quickly. The zinc, for instance — how do you replace it, since you no longer have any communication with the land?"

"Your question will have its answer," replied Captain Nemo. "Let me tell you, first of all, that there exist at the bottom of the seas mines of zinc, iron, silver, and gold whose working would very certainly be practicable. But I've borrowed nothing from these metals of the earth; I wanted to ask the sea itself, and nothing else, for the means to produce my electricity."

"The sea?"

"Yes, Professor, and the means were not lacking. I could, in fact, by setting up a circuit between wires sunk to different depths, have obtained electricity from the difference in the temperatures they were exposed to; but I preferred to use a more practical system."

"And that is?"

"You know the composition of seawater. In every thousand grams you find ninety-six and a half hundredths water, and about two and two-thirds hundredths sodium chloride; then, in small quantities, chlorides of magnesium and potassium, bromide of magnesium, sulfate of magnesia, and the sulfate and carbonate of lime. You see, then, that sodium chloride is present in a considerable proportion. Now, it is this sodium that I extract from seawater, and out of which I compose my elements."

"The sodium?"

"Yes, Professor. Mixed with mercury, it forms an amalgam that takes the place of zinc in the Bunsen cells. The mercury never wears out. Only the sodium is consumed, and the sea itself supplies it to me. I'll tell you, besides, that sodium batteries must be regarded as the most powerful of all, and that their electromotive force is double that of zinc batteries."

"I quite understand, Captain, the excellence of sodium in the conditions you find yourself in. The sea contains it. Good. But you still have to manufacture it — to extract it, in a word. And how do you do that? Your batteries could obviously serve for the extraction; but, if I'm not mistaken, the sodium the electrical apparatus would consume would exceed the quantity extracted. It would turn out, then, that you'd use up more producing it than you'd ever produce!"

"And so, Professor, I don't extract it with the battery; I simply use the heat of coal from the earth."

"From the earth?" I said, pressing him.

"Say sea coal, if you like," replied Captain Nemo.

"And you can work undersea coal mines?"

"Professor Aronnax, you'll see me at work. I ask you only for a little patience, since you have time to be patient. Remember just this: I owe everything to the Ocean; it produces the electricity, and the electricity gives the Nautilus heat, light, motion — life, in a word."

"But not the air you breathe?"

"Oh, I could manufacture the air I need, but there's no point, since I go back up to the surface of the sea whenever I please. Still, if electricity doesn't supply me with breathable air, it does at least work powerful pumps that store it away in special tanks, and that lets me prolong my stay in the deep layers as long as I want, whenever the need arises."

"Captain," I answered, "I can only admire you. You've evidently found what men will no doubt find one day — the true dynamic power of electricity."

"I don't know whether they'll find it," replied Captain Nemo coldly. "Be that as it may, you already know the first application I've made of this precious agent. It's electricity that lights us, with an evenness, a steadiness, that the light of the sun doesn't have. Now, look at this clock; it's electric, and it runs with a regularity that defies the best chronometers. I've divided it into twenty-four hours, like the Italian clocks, for to me there is neither night nor day, neither sun nor moon, but only this artificial light that I carry with me to the bottom of the seas! Look — right now, it's ten in the morning."

"Exactly."

"Another application of electricity. This dial, hung here before our eyes, serves to indicate the speed of the Nautilus. An electric wire connects it to the log's propeller, and its needle shows me the craft's actual pace. And there, at this moment, we're running at a moderate speed of fifteen miles an hour."

"It's marvelous," I answered, "and I see very well, Captain, that you were right to make use of this agent, which is destined to replace wind, water, and steam."

"We're not finished, Professor Aronnax," said Captain Nemo, rising. "If you'll follow me, we'll visit the stern of the Nautilus."

In fact, I already knew the whole forward part of this underwater boat, whose exact layout, going from the center toward the ram, was as follows: the dining room, five meters long, separated from the library by a watertight bulkhead — that is, one that water couldn't get through; the library, five meters; the grand salon, ten meters, separated from the captain's cabin by a second watertight bulkhead; the said captain's cabin, five meters; mine, two and a half meters; and finally an air reservoir, seven and a half meters, that extended all the way to the bow. Total, thirty-five meters in length. The watertight bulkheads were pierced with doors that shut hermetically by means of rubber gaskets, and they guaranteed complete safety aboard the Nautilus in the event that a leak sprang somewhere.

I followed Captain Nemo through the gangways running along the sides, and came to the center of the ship. There I found a sort of well that opened between two watertight bulkheads. An iron ladder, bolted to the wall, led up to its upper end. I asked the captain what this ladder was for.

"It leads to the dinghy," he answered.

"What! You have a dinghy?" I replied, rather astonished.

"Of course. An excellent little boat, light and unsinkable, that serves for excursions and fishing."

"But then, when you want to embark, you're forced to come back to the surface of the sea?"

"Not at all. This dinghy sits against the upper part of the Nautilus's hull and occupies a cavity built to receive it. It's completely decked over, absolutely watertight, and held in place by strong bolts. This ladder leads to a manhole cut through the hull of the Nautilus, which matches a similar hole cut in the side of the dinghy. It's through this double opening that I let myself into the boat. They close one — the Nautilus's; I close the other — the dinghy's — by means of pressure screws; I release the bolts, and the boat shoots up to the surface of the sea with prodigious speed. Then I open the deck hatch, kept carefully shut until then, I step the mast, I hoist my sail or take up my oars, and off I go."

"But how do you get back aboard?"

"I don't come back, Professor Aronnax — it's the Nautilus that comes back."

"At your command!"

"At my command. An electric wire connects me to it. I send a telegram, and that's enough."

"Indeed," I said, intoxicated by these marvels, "nothing could be simpler!"

After passing the stairwell that led up to the platform, I saw a cabin two meters long, in which Conseil and Ned Land, delighted with their meal, were busy devouring it with a hearty appetite. Then a door opened onto the galley, three meters long, set between the ship's spacious storerooms.

There, electricity — more energetic and more obedient than gas itself — did all the work of cooking. The wires, running in under the stoves, delivered to spongy plates of platinum a heat that was distributed and held steady and even. It also heated distilling apparatus that, through evaporation, furnished excellent drinking water. Next to this galley opened a bathroom, comfortably fitted out, whose taps supplied cold or hot water at will.

After the galley came the crew's quarters, five meters long. But the door was shut, and I couldn't see how it was arranged — which might have told me how many men it took to run the Nautilus.

At the far end rose a fourth watertight bulkhead that separated these quarters from the engine room. A door opened, and I found myself in the compartment where Captain Nemo — a first-rate engineer, beyond any doubt — had installed his machinery for locomotion.

This engine room, brightly lit, measured no less than twenty meters in length. It was naturally divided into two parts: the first held the elements that produced the electricity, the second the mechanism that transmitted the motion to the propeller.

I was struck at first by the sui generis odor that filled the compartment. Captain Nemo noticed my reaction.

"These," he told me, "are a few emissions of gas produced by the use of sodium; but it's only a slight inconvenience. Besides, every morning we purify the ship by ventilating it in the open air."

Meanwhile, I was examining the machinery of the Nautilus with an interest easy to imagine.

"You see," Captain Nemo told me, "I use Bunsen cells, not Ruhmkorff cells. Those would have been powerless. The Bunsen cells are few in number, but strong and large, which works better, as experience has shown. The electricity produced travels to the stern, where it acts through large electromagnets on a special system of levers and gearing that transmits the motion to the propeller shaft. The propeller, six meters in diameter with a pitch of seven and a half meters, can turn as fast as a hundred and twenty revolutions per second."

"And you get, then?"

"A speed of fifty miles an hour."

There was a mystery here, but I didn't press to learn it. How could electricity act with such power? Where did this almost unlimited force take its origin? Was it in its excessive tension, obtained by coils of some new kind? Was it in its transmission, which an unknown system of levers could amplify to infinity? That was what I couldn't grasp.

"Captain Nemo," I said, "I take note of the results and I don't try to explain them. I saw the Nautilus maneuvering in front of the Abraham Lincoln, and I know what to make of its speed. But moving isn't enough. You have to see where you're going! You have to be able to steer right, left, up, down! How do you reach the great depths, where you meet a growing resistance measured in hundreds of atmospheres? How do you rise back to the surface of the Ocean? And how, finally, do you hold yourself at whatever level suits you? Am I being indiscreet in asking you?"

"Not at all, Professor," the captain answered me, after a slight hesitation, "since you must never leave this underwater boat. Come into the salon. It's our real study, and there you'll learn everything you need to know about the Nautilus!"
