The Sargasso Sea

The Nautilus had not changed course. For the moment, then, every hope of returning to European waters had to be set aside. Captain Nemo held his heading due south. Where was he taking us? I did not dare imagine it.

That day the Nautilus crossed a singular stretch of the Atlantic Ocean. No one is unaware of the great current of warm water known as the Gulf Stream. After emerging from the channels of Florida, it makes for Spitsbergen. But before it reaches the Gulf of Mexico, at about the forty-fourth degree of north latitude, this current splits into two arms; the main one heads toward the coasts of Ireland and Norway, while the second bends south at the latitude of the Azores, then strikes the shores of Africa and, tracing a long oval, comes back around toward the Antilles.

Now, this second arm — more a collar, really, than an arm — encircles with its rings of warm water that portion of the ocean that is cold, still, and motionless, the region we call the Sargasso Sea. A genuine lake in the middle of the Atlantic, whose circumference the waters of the great current take no less than three years to travel around.

The Sargasso Sea, strictly speaking, covers the entire submerged expanse of Atlantis. Certain authors have even held that the countless weeds strewn across it were torn from the meadows of that ancient continent. It is more likely, however, that this greenery — the algae and the wrack — ripped from the shores of Europe and America, is carried all the way to this zone by the Gulf Stream. That was one of the reasons that led Columbus to suppose the existence of a new world. When the ships of that bold seeker reached the Sargasso Sea, they sailed only with difficulty through the midst of these weeds, which halted their progress to the great terror of the crews, and they lost three long weeks in crossing them.

Such was the region the Nautilus was visiting at that moment — a true meadow, a dense carpet of algae, of floating wrack (Fucus natans), of tropic grapes, so thick and so compact that a vessel's prow could not have parted it without effort. And so Captain Nemo, unwilling to foul his propeller in this weedy mass, kept a few meters below the surface of the waves.

The name Sargasso comes from the Spanish word "sargazzo," meaning kelp. This kelp — the floating or berry-bearing kelp — is what chiefly forms this immense bank. And here, according to the learned Maury, author of The Physical Geography of the Globe, is why these water-plants gather in this tranquil basin of the Atlantic:

"The explanation one can offer," he says, "seems to me to follow from an experiment everyone knows. If you drop fragments of cork, or any floating bodies, into a bowl, and give the water in the bowl a circular motion, you will see the scattered fragments come together in a group at the center of the liquid surface — that is, at the least agitated point. In the phenomenon that concerns us, the bowl is the Atlantic, the Gulf Stream is the circular current, and the Sargasso Sea is the central point where the floating bodies gather."

I share Maury's opinion, and I was able to study the phenomenon in that particular setting, which ships rarely enter. Above us floated bodies of every origin, heaped up among these brownish weeds: tree trunks torn from the Andes or the Rocky Mountains and rafted down by the Amazon or the Mississippi; countless pieces of wreckage; remnants of keels and hulls; smashed-in planking so weighed down by shells and barnacles that it could no longer rise to the surface of the ocean. And time will one day bear out that other opinion of Maury's — that these materials, accumulated in this way over the centuries, will be mineralized by the action of the waters and will go on to form inexhaustible coal beds. A precious reserve that provident nature is laying up against the day when men will have exhausted the mines of the continents.

Amid this inextricable weave of weeds and wrack, I noticed charming star-shaped soft corals in shades of pink, sea anemones trailing their long tresses of tentacles, jellyfish green and red and blue, and above all those great Cuvier's rhizostomes, whose bluish umbrella is fringed with a border of violet.

That whole day of February 22 was spent in the Sargasso Sea, where fish that are fond of marine plants and crustaceans find abundant food. By the next day the ocean had resumed its accustomed look.

From that moment on, for nineteen days — from February 23 to March 12 — the Nautilus, keeping to the middle of the Atlantic, carried us along at a steady speed of a hundred leagues every twenty-four hours. Captain Nemo clearly meant to carry out his underwater itinerary, and I had no doubt that, after rounding Cape Horn, he intended to return toward the southern seas of the Pacific.

Ned Land, then, had been right to fear as much. In these wide seas, empty of islands, there could be no more thought of leaving the ship. Nor was there any way to oppose Captain Nemo's will. The only course was to submit; but what we could no longer expect from force or cunning, I liked to think we might obtain through persuasion. Once this voyage was over, might not Captain Nemo consent to give us our freedom on our sworn oath never to reveal his existence? An oath of honor that we would have kept. But this delicate question would have to be taken up with the captain. And would I even be within my rights to claim that freedom? Had he not himself declared, from the very outset and in the most formal terms, that the secret of his life required our perpetual imprisonment aboard the Nautilus? Might not my silence these four months seem to him a tacit acceptance of the situation? Would returning to the subject not simply rouse suspicions that could harm our plans, if some favorable circumstance should later present itself for taking them up again? I weighed all these reasons, turned them over in my mind, laid them out for Conseil, who was no less at a loss than I was. In short, though I am not easily discouraged, I understood that the chances of ever seeing my fellow men again were dwindling day by day — especially now, with Captain Nemo running so recklessly toward the south of the Atlantic!

During the nineteen days I have mentioned, no particular incident marked our voyage. I saw little of the captain. He was working. In the library I often came upon books he had left lying open — books of natural history above all. My own work on the ocean depths, thumbed through by him, was covered with notes in the margins, notes that sometimes contradicted my theories and my systems. But the captain contented himself with refining my work in this way, and it was rare that he discussed it with me. Sometimes I heard the melancholy strains of his organ, which he played with great feeling — but only at night, in the most secret darkness, when the Nautilus lay sleeping in the ocean's wastes.

During this stretch of the voyage we sailed whole days on the surface of the waves. The sea seemed deserted. Barely a few sailing ships, laden for the Indies, making for the Cape of Good Hope. One day we were chased by the boats of a whaler that no doubt took us for some enormous, high-priced whale. But Captain Nemo would not let these good men waste their time and their labor, and he ended the hunt by diving beneath the waters. The incident seemed to interest Ned Land keenly. I do not think I am mistaken in saying that the Canadian must have regretted that our whale of sheet metal could not be struck dead by those fishermen's harpoons.

The fish that Conseil and I observed during this period differed little from those we had already studied in other latitudes. The chief ones were a few specimens of that fearsome class of cartilaginous fish, divided into three subgenera that number no fewer than thirty-two species: broadband sharks, five meters long, with a head flattened and broader than the body, a rounded tail fin, and a back marked by seven great black bands, parallel and running lengthwise; and then perlon sharks, ash-gray, pierced with seven gill openings and furnished with a single dorsal fin set about the middle of the body.

Great dogfish passed by as well — voracious fish if ever there were any. One has every right to disbelieve the tales of fishermen, but here is what they tell. In the body of one of these animals a buffalo's head and a whole calf were found; in another, two tunas and a sailor in uniform; in another, a soldier with his saber; and in yet another, a horse and its rider. None of this, to tell the truth, is an article of faith. What is certain is that not one of these animals let itself be taken in the Nautilus's nets, so I was unable to verify their voracity.

Elegant, frolicking troops of dolphins kept us company for whole days at a stretch. They went in bands of five or six, hunting in packs like wolves out in the countryside — and no less voracious than the dogfish, if I am to believe a professor from Copenhagen who drew from a dolphin's stomach thirteen porpoises and fifteen seals. This one, it is true, was a killer whale, belonging to the largest known species, whose length sometimes exceeds twenty-four feet. This family of dolphins counts ten genera, and the ones I glimpsed belonged to the genus of bottlenose dolphins, remarkable for an exceedingly narrow snout, four times as long as the skull. Their bodies, measuring three meters, black above, were below a rosy white sown with very sparse little spots.

I will also mention, in these seas, some curious specimens of those fish of the order Acanthopterygii and the family of the drums. Certain authors — more poets than naturalists — claim that these fish sing melodiously, and that their voices together form a concert that no chorus of human voices could equal. I will not say no; but these fish gave us no serenade as we passed, and I regret it.

To finish, then, Conseil classified a great many flying fish. Nothing was more curious than to watch the dolphins give chase to them with marvelous precision. Whatever the range of its flight, whatever trajectory it traced — even above the Nautilus — the luckless fish always found the dolphin's mouth open to receive it. These were either flying gurnards or firewing sea-robins with luminous mouths, which, by night, after tracing streaks of fire across the sky, plunged into the dark waters like so many shooting stars.

Our navigation continued under these conditions until March 13. On that day the Nautilus was put to sounding experiments that interested me keenly.

By then we had traveled nearly thirteen thousand leagues since our departure in the high seas of the Pacific. Our position put us at 45°37′ south latitude and 37°53′ west longitude. These were the very waters where Captain Denham, of the Herald, had let out fourteen thousand meters of sounding line without finding bottom. Here too Lieutenant Parker, of the American frigate Congress, had been unable to reach the ocean floor at fifteen thousand one hundred forty meters.

Captain Nemo resolved to send his Nautilus down to the farthest depth in order to check these various soundings. I made ready to note down all the results of the experiment. The panels of the salon were opened, and the maneuvers began for reaching those prodigiously remote layers.

It will readily be understood that there was no question of diving by filling the ballast tanks. Perhaps they could not have increased the Nautilus's specific gravity enough. Besides, to come back up, that excess of water would have had to be forced out, and the pumps would not have been powerful enough to overcome the outside pressure.

Captain Nemo resolved to seek the ocean floor along a suitably long diagonal, by means of his side planes, which were set at an angle of forty-five degrees with the Nautilus's waterlines. Then the propeller was driven to its maximum speed, and its four blades beat the waters with indescribable violence.

Under this powerful thrust, the Nautilus's hull quivered like a sounding string and sank steadily beneath the waters. The captain and I, posted in the salon, followed the needle of the pressure gauge as it swung rapidly. Soon we passed beyond that habitable zone where most fish reside. If some of these animals can live only at the surface of seas or rivers, others, fewer in number, keep to fairly great depths. Among the latter I observed the sixgill shark, a kind of dogfish equipped with six respiratory slits; the telescope fish, with its enormous eyes; the armored sea-poacher, with its gray thoracic fins and black pectorals, protected by a breastplate of pale-red bony plates; and finally the grenadier, which, living at twelve hundred meters down, was withstanding a pressure of a hundred and twenty atmospheres.

I asked Captain Nemo whether he had observed fish at still more considerable depths.

"Fish?" he answered me. "Rarely. But in the present state of science, what does one suppose — what does one really know?"

"Here is what we know, Captain. We know that as one descends toward the lower layers of the ocean, plant life disappears faster than animal life. We know that where living creatures are still to be found, not a single water-plant grows any longer. We know that scallops and oysters live at two thousand meters of water, and that McClintock, the hero of the polar seas, drew a living starfish from a depth of two thousand five hundred meters. We know that the crew of the Bulldog, of the Royal Navy, fished up a starfish from two thousand six hundred twenty fathoms — more than a league of depth. But perhaps, Captain Nemo, you will tell me that we know nothing?"

"No, Professor," the captain replied, "I would not be so rude. Still, I will ask you how you explain that beings can live at such depths?"

"I explain it by two reasons," I answered. "First, because the vertical currents, set in motion by differences in the salinity and density of the waters, produce a movement that is enough to sustain the rudimentary life of the sea lilies and starfish."

"True," said the captain.

"And second, because if oxygen is the basis of life, we know that the quantity of oxygen dissolved in seawater increases with depth instead of diminishing, and that the pressure of the lower layers helps to compress it there."

"Ah! that is known?" replied Captain Nemo, in a slightly surprised tone. "Well, Professor, one is right to know it, for it is the truth. Indeed, I will add that the swim bladder of fish holds more nitrogen than oxygen when these animals are caught at the surface of the waters, and more oxygen than nitrogen, on the contrary, when they are drawn from the great depths. Which bears out your system. But let us go on with our observations."

My eyes turned back to the pressure gauge. The instrument showed a depth of six thousand meters. Our descent had already lasted an hour. The Nautilus, gliding on its inclined planes, kept sinking. The deserted waters were admirably transparent, of a clarity nothing could paint. An hour later we were at thirteen thousand meters — about three and a quarter leagues — and the ocean floor still gave no sign of itself.

At fourteen thousand meters, however, I made out blackish peaks rising up amid the waters. But these summits might belong to mountains as high as the Himalaya or Mont Blanc, higher even, and the depth of these abysses remained impossible to gauge.

The Nautilus went down lower still, despite the powerful pressures it was undergoing. I felt its plates trembling at the joints of their bolts; its bars bowed; its bulkheads groaned; the windows of the salon seemed to buckle under the pressure of the waters. And this solid vessel would surely have given way if, as its captain had said, it had not been capable of resisting like a solid block.

Skimming the slopes of these rocks lost beneath the waters, I still caught sight of a few shells, some tube worms, living spirorbis, and certain specimens of starfish.

But soon these last representatives of animal life vanished, and below three leagues the Nautilus passed the limits of underwater existence, as a balloon does when it rises through the air above the breathable zones. We had reached a depth of sixteen thousand meters — four leagues — and the flanks of the Nautilus were now bearing a pressure of sixteen hundred atmospheres, that is, sixteen hundred kilograms on every square centimeter of its surface!

"What a place!" I cried. "To travel through these deep regions where man has never come! Look, Captain, look at these magnificent rocks, these uninhabited grottoes, these last recesses of the globe, where life is no longer possible! What unknown sights — and why must we be reduced to keeping nothing of them but the memory?"

"Would it please you," Captain Nemo asked me, "to bring back better than the memory?"

"What do you mean by those words?"

"I mean that nothing is easier than to take a photograph of this undersea region!"

I had not had time to voice the surprise this new proposal caused me before, at a call from Captain Nemo, a lens was brought into the salon. Through the wide-open panels, the liquid medium, lit electrically, spread out with perfect clarity. Not a shadow, not the least dimming of our artificial light. The sun would have been no more favorable to an operation of this kind. The Nautilus, under the thrust of its propeller, held in check by the tilt of its planes, remained motionless. The instrument was aimed at these sites on the ocean floor, and in a few seconds we had obtained a negative of extreme purity.

It is the positive print of it that I give here. In it one sees those primordial rocks that have never known the light of the heavens, those nether granites that form the mighty foundation of the globe, those deep grottoes hollowed out of the stony mass, those profiles of incomparable sharpness whose outer edge stands out in black, as though it were owed to the brush of certain Flemish painters. Then, beyond, a horizon of mountains, an admirable undulating line that composes the backgrounds of the landscape. I cannot describe this whole array of smooth, black, polished rocks, without a trace of moss, without a stain, strangely cut in their forms and solidly set on that carpet of sand which sparkled under the jets of the electric light.

Captain Nemo, however, having finished his operation, said to me:

"Let us go back up, Professor. We must not abuse this situation, nor expose the Nautilus too long to such pressures."

"Let us go back up!" I answered.

"Hold on tight."

I had not yet had time to grasp why the captain gave me this warning when I was flung down onto the carpet.

At a signal from the captain, its propeller engaged and its planes set vertical, the Nautilus, carried up like a balloon into the air, shot upward with dizzying speed. It sliced through the mass of the waters with a resonant shudder. Not a single detail was visible. In four minutes it had covered the four leagues that separated it from the surface of the ocean, and, after breaking the surface like a flying fish, it fell back, sending the waves spurting to a prodigious height.
