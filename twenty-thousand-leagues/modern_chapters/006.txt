A Whale of Unknown Species

Startled as I was by that sudden plunge, I nonetheless kept a very clear impression of my sensations.

At first I was dragged down to a depth of about twenty feet. I am a strong swimmer — without claiming to rival Byron and Edgar Poe, who are masters of the art — and the fall did not make me lose my head. Two vigorous kicks brought me back to the surface.

My first concern was to look for the frigate. Had the crew noticed my disappearance? Had the Abraham Lincoln come about? Was Commander Farragut lowering a boat? Could I dare hope to be saved?

The darkness was deep. I caught a glimpse of a black mass vanishing away to the east, its running lights dying out in the distance. It was the frigate. I felt that I was lost.

"Help! Help me!" I shouted, swimming toward the Abraham Lincoln with desperate strokes.

My clothes weighed me down. The water plastered them to my body; they paralyzed my movements. I was sinking! I was suffocating!

"Help!"

That was the last cry I flung out. My mouth filled with water. I struggled, dragged down into the abyss —

Suddenly my clothes were seized by a powerful hand, I felt myself hauled violently back to the surface, and I heard — yes, I heard — these words spoken close to my ear:

"If Master would be so very kind as to lean on my shoulder, Master would swim a great deal more comfortably."

I grabbed the arm of my faithful Conseil with one hand.

"You!" I said. "You!"

"Myself," Conseil answered, "and at Master's orders."

"That shock threw you overboard at the same time it threw me?"

"Not at all. But being in Master's service, I followed Master."

The good fellow found that the most natural thing in the world!

"And the frigate?" I asked.

"The frigate!" Conseil answered, rolling onto his back. "I think Master would do well not to count too much on her."

"What do you mean?"

"I mean that just as I was throwing myself into the sea, I heard the men at the helm cry out, 'The propeller and the rudder are broken —'"

"Broken?"

"Yes! Broken by the monster's tooth. That's the only damage the Abraham Lincoln suffered, I believe. But it's an awkward circumstance for us — she can no longer steer."

"Then we're lost!"

"Perhaps," Conseil replied calmly. "Still, we have a few hours ahead of us, and a great deal can be done in a few hours."

Conseil's unshakable composure put fresh heart into me. I swam more vigorously; but hampered by my clothes, which gripped me like a cloak of lead, I found it extremely hard to keep myself up. Conseil noticed.

"Will Master permit me to make an incision?" he said.

And slipping an open knife under my clothes, he slit them from top to bottom with one quick stroke. Then he swiftly stripped them off me, while I swam for us both.

In my turn I did the same service for Conseil, and we went on "navigating" side by side.

Even so, our situation was no less terrible. Perhaps our disappearance had not been noticed; and even if it had, the frigate — stripped of her rudder — could not beat back upwind toward us. We could count on nothing but her boats.

Conseil reasoned this out coldly and made his plan accordingly. An astonishing nature! This phlegmatic fellow was as much at home here as in his own room.

So it was decided that, since our only chance of rescue lay in being picked up by the Abraham Lincoln's boats, we had to arrange things so as to wait for them as long as we possibly could. I resolved to divide our efforts, so as not to exhaust them both at once, and here is what we agreed on: while one of us lay stretched on his back, motionless, arms crossed and legs extended, the other would swim and push him forward. This towing job was not to last more than ten minutes, and by relieving each other in this way, we could stay afloat for several hours — perhaps until daybreak.

A slim chance! But hope is so deeply rooted in the human heart! Besides, there were two of us. And finally — I state this plainly, improbable as it may seem — even if I tried to crush every illusion in myself, even if I wanted to "give up hope," I could not do it!

The collision between the frigate and the whale had happened at around eleven o'clock at night. I therefore reckoned on eight hours of swimming until sunrise — an operation strictly feasible, so long as we relieved each other. The sea, fairly calm, tired us little. Now and then I tried to pierce those thick shadows, broken only by the phosphorescence stirred up by our own movements. I watched the luminous ripples that broke across my hand, their shimmering surface flecked with livid patches. It was as if we were plunged in a bath of quicksilver.

Toward one in the morning I was seized with extreme exhaustion. My limbs stiffened under the grip of violent cramps. Conseil had to hold me up, and the whole care of keeping us alive fell on him alone. Before long I heard the poor fellow panting; his breath came short and quick. I understood that he could not hold out much longer.

"Leave me! Leave me!" I told him.

"Abandon Master? Never!" he replied. "I fully intend to drown before he does!"

At that moment the moon broke through the fringes of a great cloud that the wind was driving eastward. The surface of the sea flashed under its light. That kindly gleam revived our strength. My head lifted. My eyes swept every point of the horizon. I spotted the frigate. She was five miles from us, no more than a dark blur, barely to be made out. But of boats — not one!

I wanted to cry out. What good would it do, at such a distance? My swollen lips let no sound pass. Conseil managed to get a few words out, and I heard him call again and again:

"Over here! Over here!"

Suspending our movements for an instant, we listened. And — perhaps it was one of those hummings with which the blood, pressed hard, fills the ear — but it seemed to me that a cry answered Conseil's.

"Did you hear that?" I whispered.

"Yes! Yes!"

And Conseil flung another desperate call into the dark.

This time there was no possible mistake! A human voice was answering ours! Was it the voice of some poor wretch abandoned in the middle of the ocean, some other victim of the blow the ship had taken? Or was one of the frigate's boats hailing us out of the shadows?

Conseil made a supreme effort, and, bracing himself on my shoulder while I resisted in one last convulsion, he raised himself half out of the water and dropped back exhausted.

"What did you see?"

"I saw —" he murmured, "I saw — but let's not talk — let's save all our strength!"

What had he seen? Then, I don't know why, the thought of the monster came into my mind for the first time! But that voice, though? The days are past when a Jonah takes refuge in the belly of a whale!

Still, Conseil went on towing me. Now and then he raised his head, looked ahead, and let out a hail of recognition, answered by a voice coming nearer and nearer. I could barely hear it. My strength was at its end; my fingers were spreading apart; my hand no longer gave me any hold; my mouth, convulsively open, was filling with salt water; the cold was creeping over me. I raised my head one last time, then I went under —

At that instant a hard body struck me. I clung to it. Then I felt myself being pulled up, brought back to the surface, my chest emptying of water — and I fainted.

I certainly came to quickly enough, thanks to the vigorous rubbing that worked up and down my body. I half opened my eyes.

"Conseil!" I murmured.

"Master rang?" answered Conseil.

At that moment, by the last light of the moon as it sank toward the horizon, I made out a face that was not Conseil's, and that I recognized at once.

"Ned!" I cried.

"In person, sir — and running after his bonus!" the Canadian replied.

"You were thrown overboard when the frigate was struck?"

"Yes, Professor, but with better luck than you: I managed to set foot almost at once on a floating island."

"An island?"

"Or, to put it more exactly, on our giant narwhal."

"Explain yourself, Ned."

"Only I soon understood why my harpoon couldn't bite into it and just glanced off, blunted, against its hide."

"Why, Ned? Why?"

"Because that beast, Professor, is made of sheet steel!"

Here I must gather my wits, refresh my memory, check my own assertions.

The Canadian's last words had produced a sudden upheaval in my brain. I hauled myself quickly up to the top of the creature — or object — half awash beneath us, which was serving as our refuge. I tested it with my foot. It was plainly a hard body, impenetrable, and not that soft substance that makes up the bulk of the great sea mammals.

But that hard body might be a bony shell, like those of the antediluvian animals, and I would be entitled to classify the monster among the amphibious reptiles, such as turtles or alligators.

Well — no! The blackish back that held me up was smooth, polished, not overlapping like scales. Struck, it rang with a metallic sound, and — incredible as it was — it seemed, I say, to be made of bolted plates.

There was no room for doubt! The animal, the monster, the natural phenomenon that had puzzled the entire scientific world, that had thrown into confusion and led astray the imaginations of sailors in both hemispheres — I had to admit it — was a still more astonishing phenomenon: a phenomenon made by human hands.

The discovery of the most fabulous, the most mythological creature in existence would not have staggered my reason to the same degree. That something prodigious should come from the Creator is simple enough. But to find, all at once, right before your eyes, the impossible mysteriously and humanly accomplished — that was enough to confound the mind!

Yet there was no hesitating. We were stretched out on the back of a kind of underwater boat, which, as far as I could judge, had the shape of an immense steel fish. Ned Land's opinion on that point was already settled. Conseil and I could only fall in with it.

"But then," I said, "this contraption must hold within it some mechanism of locomotion, and a crew to work it?"

"Obviously," the harpooner replied, "and yet, in the three hours I've lived on this floating island, it hasn't given a single sign of life."

"The boat hasn't moved at all?"

"No, Professor Aronnax. It lets itself be rocked by the swell, but it doesn't budge."

"And yet we know beyond any doubt that it's capable of great speed. Now, since it takes an engine to produce that speed, and an engineer to run the engine, I conclude — that we are saved."

"Hmph," said Ned Land in a guarded tone.

At that very moment, as if to prove my argument, a churning broke out at the stern of the strange machine — whose propeller was plainly a screw — and it began to move. We barely had time to grab hold of its upper part, which rose about eighty centimeters above the water. Very luckily its speed was not excessive.

"As long as it sails on the level," muttered Ned Land, "I have nothing to say. But if it takes a notion to dive, I wouldn't give two dollars for my hide!"

Less than that, the Canadian might have said. It was becoming urgent, then, to make contact with whatever beings were shut up inside this machine. I searched its surface for an opening, a hatch — "a manhole," to use the technical term; but the lines of bolts, firmly hammered down along the seams of the plating, were clean and uniform.

What was more, the moon then disappeared and left us in deep darkness. We had to wait for daylight to work out some way of getting inside this underwater boat.

So our safety depended entirely on the whim of the mysterious helmsmen who were steering this craft — and if they dove, we were lost! Barring that, I had no doubt we could open some communication with them. And indeed, unless they manufactured their own air, they necessarily had to come back to the surface of the ocean from time to time to renew their supply of breathable molecules. Hence there had to be some opening putting the inside of the boat in contact with the atmosphere.

As for any hope of being saved by Commander Farragut, that had to be given up completely. We were being carried westward, and I estimated that our speed, relatively moderate, reached twelve miles an hour. The screw beat the waves with a mathematical regularity, sometimes breaking the surface and sending the phosphorescent water spurting up to a great height.

Toward four in the morning the craft picked up speed. We could barely hold on against this dizzying rush whenever the waves struck us full force. Fortunately Ned's hand fell on a large mooring ring fixed to the top of the steel back, and we managed to hang on to it firmly.

At last the long night wore away. My incomplete recollection won't let me set down all its impressions. Only one detail comes back to me. During certain lulls in the sea and the wind, I thought I heard, several times, vague sounds — a kind of fleeting harmony produced by distant chords. What, then, was the mystery of this underwater navigation that the whole world was vainly trying to explain? What beings lived aboard this strange boat? What mechanical force let it travel at such a prodigious speed?

Day broke. The morning mists wrapped us round, but they were not slow to tear apart. I was about to begin a careful examination of the hull, which formed at its upper part a kind of horizontal platform, when I felt it start to sink, little by little.

"Hey! A thousand devils!" cried Ned Land, stamping his foot on the ringing steel. "Open up, you inhospitable sailors!"

But it was hard to make yourself heard over the deafening beat of the propeller. Fortunately, the sinking motion stopped.

Suddenly a noise of ironwork violently shoved came from inside the boat. A plate lifted, a man appeared, let out a strange cry, and instantly vanished.

A few moments later, eight burly fellows, their faces veiled, appeared in silence and dragged us down into their formidable machine.
