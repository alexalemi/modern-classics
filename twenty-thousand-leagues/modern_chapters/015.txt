A Walk Across the Plains

This cell was, strictly speaking, the arsenal and the changing room of the Nautilus. A dozen diving suits hung along the wall, waiting for anyone who cared to go walking.

When Ned Land laid eyes on them, he made no secret of how little he wanted to climb into one.

"But my good Ned," I said to him, "the forests of Crespo Island are nothing more than underwater forests!"

"Terrific," grumbled the harpooner, watching his dreams of fresh meat evaporate. "And you, Professor Aronnax, you're going to squeeze yourself into one of those getups?"

"I'm afraid I must, Master Ned."

"Suit yourself, Professor," he answered with a shrug. "But as for me, unless somebody forces me into it, I'll never set foot inside one of those things."

"No one is going to force you, Master Ned," said Captain Nemo.

"And Conseil means to risk it?" Ned asked.

"I go wherever Master goes," Conseil replied.

At a word from the captain, two of the crew came to help us into these heavy waterproof garments, made of seamless rubber and built to withstand tremendous pressures. They were like a suit of armor at once supple and unyielding. Each formed a pair of trousers and a jacket. The trousers ended in thick boots weighted with heavy lead soles. The fabric of the jacket was reinforced by strips of copper that plated the chest like a cuirass, shielding it against the crush of the water while leaving the lungs free to work; the sleeves ended in soft, flexible gloves that in no way hampered the movements of the hand.

There was, as you can see, a world of difference between these perfected diving suits and the shapeless contraptions — the cork breastplates, the leather overjackets, the sea-coats, the airtight chests, and the rest — that had been invented and touted in the eighteenth century.

Captain Nemo, one of his companions — a sort of Hercules who must have possessed prodigious strength — Conseil, and I were soon dressed in our diving suits. All that remained was to seat our heads inside their metal spheres. But before we came to that, I asked the captain's permission to examine the guns that had been set aside for us.

One of the Nautilus's men handed me an ordinary-looking gun whose butt, made of sheet steel and hollow inside, was of fairly large size. It served as a reservoir for compressed air, which a valve, worked by a trigger, released into the metal barrel. A magazine hollowed out within the thickness of the butt held some twenty electric bullets that, by means of a spring, loaded themselves automatically into the barrel. The moment one shot was fired, the next was ready to go.

"Captain Nemo," I said, "this weapon is perfect, and easy to handle. I ask nothing better than to try it out. But how are we to reach the bottom of the sea?"

"At this moment, Professor, the Nautilus is grounded in ten meters of water, and we have only to set out."

"But how will we get out?"

"You are about to see."

Captain Nemo thrust his head into the spherical helmet. Conseil and I did the same — though not before hearing the Canadian fling us an ironic "Happy hunting!" The top of our suits ended in a threaded copper collar onto which the metal helmet screwed. Three portholes, protected by thick glass, let us see in every direction simply by turning our heads inside the sphere. As soon as the helmet was in place, the Rouquayrol apparatus strapped to our backs began to work, and for my part I breathed easily.

With the Ruhmkorff lamp hanging at my belt and the gun in my hand, I was ready to set out. But to be honest, imprisoned in these heavy garments and nailed to the deck by my lead soles, I would have found it impossible to take a single step.

But this had been anticipated, for I felt myself being pushed into a small chamber next to the changing room. My companions, towed along in the same way, followed after me. I heard a door fitted with valves close behind us, and a deep darkness enveloped us.

After a few minutes, a sharp hissing reached my ear. I felt a distinct sensation of cold rise from my feet to my chest. Clearly, from inside the vessel someone had opened a tap and let in the water from outside, which flooded over us and soon filled the chamber. A second door, cut in the side of the Nautilus, then swung open. A dim light shone on us. An instant later, our feet were treading the floor of the sea.

And now, how could I ever retrace the impressions this walk beneath the waters left with me? Words are powerless to tell of such marvels! When the painter's own brush cannot capture the effects peculiar to the liquid element, how could the pen ever hope to reproduce them?

Captain Nemo walked ahead, and his companion followed us a few paces behind. Conseil and I kept close together, as though an exchange of words had been possible through our metal shells. Already I no longer felt the weight of my clothing, my boots, my air reservoir, or the heavy sphere in the middle of which my head rattled like an almond in its shell. Plunged in the water, all these objects lost a portion of their weight equal to that of the liquid they displaced, and I found myself the happy beneficiary of that law of physics discovered by Archimedes. I was no longer an inert mass; I had a relatively great freedom of movement.

The light, which lit up the ground to thirty feet below the surface of the ocean, astonished me by its power. The sun's rays passed easily through this watery mass and dissolved its color. I could make out objects clearly at a distance of a hundred meters. Beyond that, the depths shaded off into the fine gradations of ultramarine, then turned blue in the far distance, and faded away into a vague darkness. Truly, this water surrounding me was only a kind of air, denser than the earth's atmosphere but nearly as transparent. Above me I could see the calm surface of the sea.

We walked on fine, smooth sand, not rippled like that of the beaches, which keep the imprint of the swell. This dazzling carpet, a true mirror, hurled back the sun's rays with a startling intensity. Hence the immense glare that penetrated every molecule of the water. Will I be believed if I claim that at this depth of thirty feet I could see as if in broad daylight?

For a quarter of an hour I trod that blazing sand, strewn with an impalpable dust of seashells. The hull of the Nautilus, stretched out like a long reef, was gradually disappearing, but its beacon, once night fell in the midst of the waters, would help guide us back on board by casting its rays with perfect clearness. This is an effect hard to grasp for anyone who has only seen such whitish sheets of light on land. There, the dust with which the air is saturated gives them the look of a luminous fog; but on the sea, as beneath it, these electric beams travel with an incomparable purity.

Meanwhile we kept walking on, and the vast plain of sand seemed to stretch on without end. With my hand I parted the liquid curtains that closed again behind me, and the track of my footsteps vanished at once under the pressure of the water.

Before long, the shapes of a few objects, barely sketched in the distance, took form before my eyes. I recognized magnificent foregrounds of rocks carpeted with zoophytes of the finest kind, and I was struck at once by an effect peculiar to this element.

It was then ten in the morning. The sun's rays struck the surface of the waves at a rather oblique angle, and at the touch of their light — broken up by refraction as though through a prism — flowers, rocks, seedlings, shells, and polyps were tinged along their edges with the seven colors of the solar spectrum. It was a marvel, a feast for the eyes, this tangle of colored tones, a veritable kaleidoscope of green, yellow, orange, violet, indigo, and blue — in a word, the whole palette of a mad colorist! How I longed to share with Conseil the vivid sensations flooding into my brain, and to vie with him in cries of admiration! How I wished I knew, like Captain Nemo and his companion, how to trade my thoughts by means of agreed-upon signs! And so, for want of anything better, I talked to myself, I shouted inside the copper box that capped my head, spending on empty words perhaps more air than I ought.

Faced with this splendid spectacle, Conseil had stopped short, as I had. Plainly the worthy fellow, in the presence of these specimens of zoophytes and mollusks, was classifying, forever classifying. Polyps and echinoderms abounded on the ground. The various isis corals, the cornularians that live in isolation, tufts of virgin oculina — known long ago as "white coral" — the fungia bristling in the shape of mushrooms, the anemones clinging by their muscular disks, all made up a bed of flowers, enameled with porpitae decked in their collar of azure tentacles, with starfish that studded the sand, and with warty astrophyton — fine lace embroidered by the hands of the water-nymphs, whose festoons swayed to the faint ripples stirred up by our walking. It was a real sorrow to me to crush beneath my steps the brilliant specimens of mollusks that littered the ground by the thousand — the concentric scallops, the hammer shells, the donax clams (true leaping shells), the top shells, the red helmet shells, the angel-wing stromb shells, the sea hares, and so many other products of this inexhaustible ocean. But we had to keep walking, and on we went, while overhead there sailed troops of Portuguese men-of-war trailing their ultramarine tentacles behind them, jellyfish whose opaline or soft-pink umbrellas, fringed with a band of azure, sheltered us from the sun's rays, and pelagia panopyra that, in the dark, would have sown our path with phosphorescent gleams!

All these marvels I glimpsed within the space of a quarter of a mile, scarcely pausing, and following Captain Nemo, who beckoned me on with a gesture. Before long the nature of the ground changed. To the plain of sand there succeeded a layer of viscous mud that the Americans call "ooze," composed solely of siliceous or calcareous shells. Then we crossed a meadow of seaweed, open-sea plants that the waters had not yet torn away, and whose growth was rampant. These close-woven lawns, soft to the foot, could have rivaled the most luxurious carpets ever woven by human hands. But even as the greenery spread out beneath our steps, it did not desert our heads. A light arbor of marine plants, ranked in that exuberant family of the algae — of which more than two thousand species are known — interwove at the surface of the waters. I saw floating there long ribbons of fucus, some globular, others tubular; laurencia and cladostephus with their delicate fronds; palmate rhodymenia resembling the fans of a cactus. I noticed that the green plants kept nearer the surface of the sea, while the red ones held to a middle depth, leaving to the black or brown hydrophytes the task of forming the gardens and flower beds of the ocean's deepest layers.

These algae are truly a wonder of creation, one of the marvels of the world's flora. This one family produces at once the smallest and the largest plants on the globe. For just as forty thousand of these all-but-invisible seedlings have been counted in a space of five square millimeters, so too specimens of fucus have been gathered whose length exceeded five hundred meters.

We had left the Nautilus about an hour and a half earlier. It was nearly noon. I could tell by the perpendicular fall of the sun's rays, which no longer refracted. The magic of the colors faded away little by little, and the shades of emerald and sapphire were wiped from our firmament. We walked with a steady tread that rang out against the ground with an astonishing intensity. The faintest sounds carried with a speed the ear is not used to on land. Water, in fact, is a better conductor of sound than air, and it travels through water four times faster.

Just then the ground fell away in a marked slope. The light took on a uniform tint. We reached a depth of a hundred meters, where we came under a pressure of ten atmospheres. But my diving suit was built in such a way that I suffered nothing at all from this pressure. I felt only a certain stiffness in the joints of my fingers, and even that discomfort was not long in disappearing. As for the fatigue that ought to have come with this two-hour walk in a harness I was so little used to, there was none. My movements, helped along by the water, came with a surprising ease.

Arrived at this depth of three hundred feet, I could still perceive the sun's rays, but faintly. Their intense brilliance had given way to a reddish twilight, a halfway state between day and night. Still, we could see well enough to make our way, and there was no need yet to switch on the Ruhmkorff apparatus.

At that moment Captain Nemo stopped. He waited until I had rejoined him, and with a finger he pointed out to me some dark masses that stood out in the shadows a short distance off.

"That is the forest of Crespo Island," I thought — and I was not mistaken.
