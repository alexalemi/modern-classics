The Indian Ocean

Here begins the second part of this voyage under the seas. The first had ended with that moving scene in the coral cemetery, which left a deep impression on my mind. So this, then, was how Captain Nemo's whole life unfolded, in the heart of that immense sea — even his grave he had made ready in the most impenetrable of its depths. There, not one of the ocean's monsters would come to trouble the last sleep of those companions of the Nautilus, those friends, bound to one another in death as surely as in life! "Nor any man, either!" the captain had added.

Always that same wild, implacable distrust of human society!

For my part, I could no longer content myself with the guesses that satisfied Conseil. That worthy fellow persisted in seeing the commander of the Nautilus as nothing more than one of those unappreciated scholars who repay humanity's indifference with scorn — a misunderstood genius who, weary of the disappointments of the land, had been driven to take refuge in this inaccessible element where his instincts could work freely. But to my mind, that theory explained only one side of Captain Nemo.

For the mystery of that last night, when we had been chained up in sleep and a locked cell; the violent precaution the captain had taken in tearing from my eyes the telescope I was about to sweep across the horizon; the mortal wound of that crewman, caused by some inexplicable collision of the Nautilus — all of this was pushing me down a new road. No! Captain Nemo was not merely fleeing from mankind! His formidable machine served not only his instincts for freedom, but perhaps also the interests of some terrible reprisal — I could not say what.

At this moment nothing is clear to me; in this darkness I still catch only glimmers, and I must confine myself to writing, so to speak, at the dictation of events.

Besides, nothing binds us to Captain Nemo. He knows that escape from the Nautilus is impossible. We are not even prisoners on parole. No pledge of honor holds us. We are only captives, prisoners dressed up with the name of "guests" by a show of courtesy. Ned Land, however, has not given up hope of recovering his freedom. He is sure to seize the first chance that luck may offer him. And I would no doubt do the same. And yet it would not be without a kind of regret that I would carry away what the captain's generosity had allowed us to learn of the Nautilus's secrets. For, in the end, must one hate this man or admire him? Is he a victim or an executioner? And then, to be honest, I would like — before I leave him forever — I would like to have completed this underwater tour of the world, whose opening chapters are so magnificent. I would like to have observed the complete series of wonders heaped up beneath the seas of the globe. I would like to have seen what no man has yet seen, even if I had to pay with my life for this insatiable need to learn! What have I discovered so far? Nothing, or next to nothing, since we have crossed only six thousand leagues of the Pacific!

And yet I know very well that the Nautilus is drawing near to inhabited lands, and that if some chance of deliverance offered itself to us, it would be cruel to sacrifice my companions to my passion for the unknown. I would have to follow them — perhaps even to lead them. But would that chance ever come? The man robbed by force of his free will longs for it, that chance; but the scholar, the curious mind, dreads it.

That day, January 21, 1868, at noon, the first officer came to take the sun's altitude. I climbed onto the platform, lit a cigar, and watched him at work. It seemed clear to me that this man did not understand French, for several times I made remarks aloud that ought to have drawn from him some involuntary sign of attention, had he understood them; but he remained impassive and silent.

While he was taking his sightings with the sextant, one of the Nautilus's sailors — that powerful man who had gone with us on our first underwater excursion to Crespo Island — came to clean the glass of the beacon. I studied then the arrangement of this apparatus, whose power was multiplied a hundredfold by lens-shaped rings set like those of a lighthouse, which kept its light trained in the useful plane. The electric lamp was designed to yield its full illuminating power. Its light, in fact, was produced in a vacuum, which ensured both its steadiness and its intensity. This vacuum also spared the graphite points between which the luminous arc springs — an important economy for Captain Nemo, who could not have replaced them easily. But under these conditions their wear was almost imperceptible.

When the Nautilus prepared to resume its underwater course, I went back down to the salon. The panels closed again, and our heading was set due west.

We were now furrowing the waters of the Indian Ocean, a vast liquid plain covering five hundred and fifty million hectares, whose waters are so transparent that they make you dizzy if you lean out over their surface. The Nautilus generally floated there at depths between one and two hundred meters. So it went for several days. To anyone but me, gripped by an immense love of the sea, the hours would no doubt have seemed long and monotonous; but these daily strolls on the platform, where I steeped myself again in the invigorating air of the ocean, the spectacle of these rich waters through the salon's glass, the reading of the books in the library, the writing of my memoirs — all of it filled my time and left me not one moment of weariness or boredom.

Our health, all of us, kept up in a very satisfactory state. The shipboard diet suited us perfectly, and for my part I could easily have done without the variations that Ned Land, out of sheer protest, took pains to introduce to it. What is more, in this constant temperature there was not even a cold to fear. Besides, that madreporarian coral the Dendrophyllia — known in Provence as "sea fennel," of which a certain supply was kept on board — would have furnished, with the melting flesh of its polyps, an excellent paste against a cough.

For several days we saw a great many aquatic birds, web-footed sorts, gulls or seagulls. Some were cleverly killed, and, prepared in a certain way, they made a very acceptable dish of waterfowl. Among the great gliders, carried off to long distances from any land and resting on the waves from the fatigue of flight, I made out magnificent albatrosses with a discordant cry like the braying of a donkey, birds that belong to the family of the long-winged. The web-toed family was represented by swift frigate birds that nimbly fished the surface waters, and by numerous phaetons or tropicbirds — among others, that red-tailed tropicbird, big as a pigeon, whose white plumage is shaded with rosy tints that set off the black tone of its wings.

The Nautilus's nets brought up several sorts of sea turtles, of the hawksbill kind, with an arched back, whose shell is highly prized. These reptiles, which dive easily, can stay a long time underwater by closing the fleshy valve at the external opening of their nasal passage. Some of these hawksbills, when they were caught, were still asleep in their shells, safe from marine animals. The flesh of these turtles was generally mediocre, but their eggs made an excellent treat.

As for the fish, they never failed to rouse our admiration whenever, through the open panels, we surprised the secrets of their aquatic life. I noticed several species I had never before had the chance to observe.

I will name chiefly certain boxfish peculiar to the Red Sea, the Indian Ocean, and that part of the ocean bathing the coasts of equatorial America. These fish, like the turtles, the armadillos, the sea urchins, and the crustaceans, are protected by an armor that is neither chalky nor stony, but genuinely bony. Sometimes it takes the shape of a triangular solid, sometimes of a four-sided one. Among the triangular kinds I noted a few half a decimeter long, of wholesome flesh and exquisite taste, brown at the tail and yellow at the fins, whose acclimatization I would recommend even in fresh water, to which, for that matter, a certain number of saltwater fish grow accustomed easily. I will also mention four-sided boxfish topped along the back with four large tubercles; boxfish speckled with white points along the lower part of the body, which grow as tame as birds; trunkfish armed with spines formed by the prolongation of their bony crust, whose peculiar grunting has earned them the nickname "sea pigs"; and then "dromedaries" with big cone-shaped humps, whose flesh is hard and leathery.

I find further, in the daily notes kept by Conseil, certain fish of the pufferfish kind, peculiar to these seas: red-backed, white-breasted Spengler's puffers, distinguished by three longitudinal rows of filaments, and electric ones, seven inches long, decked out in the liveliest colors. Then, as specimens of other kinds: ovoids resembling a blackish-brown egg, striped with white bands and lacking a tail; diodons, true porcupines of the sea, armed with spines and able to swell up so as to form a ball bristling with darts; seahorses common to every ocean; flying sea moths, with an elongated snout, whose very broad pectoral fins, arranged like wings, allow them — if not to fly — at least to launch themselves into the air; spoon-tailed "pigeons," whose tail is covered with many scaly rings; long-jawed spiny eels, excellent fish twenty-five centimeters long and gleaming with the most agreeable colors; livid dragonets, whose head is rough; myriads of leaping blennies, striped with black, with long pectoral fins, gliding over the surface of the water with prodigious speed; delicious sailfin blennies, which can hoist their fins like so many sails spread to the favoring currents; splendid kurtus, on which nature has lavished yellow, sky blue, silver, and gold; trichopters, whose wings are formed of filaments; bullheads, forever smeared with mud, which produce a certain rustling sound; gurnards, whose liver is considered poison; bodians, which wear a movable blinder over the eyes; and finally trumpetfish, with a long tubular snout, true flycatchers of the ocean, armed with a gun that neither the Chassepot nor the Remington ever foresaw, and which kill insects by striking them with a single drop of water.

In the eighty-ninth genus of the fish classified by Lacépède — belonging to the second subclass of the bony fishes, marked by a gill cover and a branchial membrane — I noticed the scorpionfish, whose head is fitted out with spines and which possesses only a single dorsal fin; these animals are clad in or stripped of small scales, according to the subgenus to which they belong. The second subgenus gave us specimens of dactylopterus, three to four decimeters long, striped with yellow, but with a head of fantastic appearance. As for the first subgenus, it furnished several specimens of that bizarre fish justly nicknamed "sea toad," a fish with a large head, sometimes hollowed with deep cavities, sometimes swollen with protuberances; bristling with spines and strewn with tubercles, it carries irregular, hideous horns; its body and tail are studded with calluses; its stingers inflict dangerous wounds; it is repugnant and horrible.

From January 21 to 23 the Nautilus traveled at the rate of two hundred and fifty leagues per twenty-four hours, that is, five hundred and forty miles, or twenty-two miles an hour.

If we recognized the various kinds of fish as they passed, it was because they were drawn by the electric glare and tried to keep us company; most, outstripped by our speed, soon fell behind; a few, however, managed to hold their place for a while in the Nautilus's wake.

On the morning of the 24th, at 12°5′ south latitude and 94°33′ longitude, we sighted Keeling Island, a madreporic upheaval planted with magnificent coconut palms, which was visited by Mr. Darwin and Captain Fitz-Roy. The Nautilus skirted the steep shores of this deserted island at a short distance. Its dredges brought up numerous specimens of polyps and echinoderms, and curious shells of the mollusk branch. A few precious products of the dolphin-shell species enriched Captain Nemo's treasures, to which I added a punctiferous star coral, a kind of parasitic coral often fixed on a shell.

Soon Keeling Island vanished below the horizon, and our heading was set to the northwest, toward the tip of the Indian peninsula.

"Civilized lands," Ned Land said to me that day. "That'll be better than these Papuan islands, where you meet more savages than roebuck! On that Indian soil, Professor, there are roads, railways, English, French, and Hindu towns. You wouldn't go five miles without running into a countryman. Well now! Isn't it about time we gave Captain Nemo the slip?"

"No, Ned, no," I answered in a very firm tone. "Let it run, as you sailors say. The Nautilus is drawing near the inhabited continents. It's coming back toward Europe — let it take us there. Once we've reached our own seas, we'll see what prudence advises us to attempt. Besides, I hardly suppose Captain Nemo will allow us to go hunting on the coasts of Malabar or Coromandel the way we did in the forests of New Guinea."

"Well then, Professor — can't we do without his permission?"

I did not answer the Canadian. I had no wish to argue. At bottom, I was set on running the chances of the destiny that had thrown me aboard the Nautilus all the way to the end.

From Keeling Island on, our pace generally slowed. It also grew more erratic and often carried us to great depths. Several times we made use of the inclined planes, which interior levers could set at an angle to the waterline. We went down that way to depths of two and three kilometers, but without ever having verified the great deeps of this Indian sea, which soundings of thirteen thousand meters have not been able to reach. As for the temperature of the lower layers, the thermometer always registered, invariably, four degrees above zero. I observed only that, in the upper sheets, the water was always colder over the shallows than in the open sea.

On January 25, the ocean being utterly deserted, the Nautilus spent the day on the surface, beating the waves with its powerful screw and flinging them up to a great height. How, under such conditions, could it not have been taken for a gigantic cetacean? I spent three-quarters of that day on the platform. I watched the sea. Nothing on the horizon, except, toward four in the afternoon, a long steamer running west on an opposite tack. Its masts were visible for a moment, but it could not make out the Nautilus, which lay too flush with the water. I thought this steamship must belong to the Peninsular and Oriental line, which runs the service from Ceylon to Sydney, calling at King George's Point and Melbourne.

At five in the evening, before that swift twilight that ties day to night in the tropical zones, Conseil and I were dazzled by a curious spectacle.

There is a charming animal whose encounter, according to the ancients, foretold good fortune. Aristotle, Athenaeus, Pliny, and Oppian had studied its tastes and exhausted upon it all the poetry of the scholars of Greece and Italy. They called it Nautilus and Pompylius. But modern science has not ratified their name, and this mollusk is now known as the Argonaut.

Anyone who had consulted Conseil would have learned from that good fellow that the mollusk branch divides into five classes; that the first class, the cephalopods — whose members are sometimes naked, sometimes shelled — includes two families, the dibranchiate and the tetrabranchiate, distinguished by the number of their gills; that the family of the dibranchiates contains three genera, the argonaut, the squid, and the cuttlefish, and that the family of the tetrabranchiates contains but one, the nautilus. And if, after this nomenclature, some unruly mind had confused the argonaut, which is acetabuliferous — that is, a bearer of suckers — with the nautilus, which is tentaculiferous — that is, a bearer of tentacles — he would have been without excuse.

Now, it was a whole troop of these argonauts that was then traveling over the surface of the ocean. We could count several hundred of them. They belonged to the species of the tuberculate argonauts, which is special to the seas of India.

These graceful mollusks moved backward by means of their locomotor tube, driving out through this tube the water they had sucked in. Of their eight tentacles, six — long and slender — floated on the water, while the two others, rounded into paddle shapes, spread to the wind like a light sail. I could see perfectly their spiraling, wavy shell, which Cuvier rightly compares to an elegant little skiff. A real boat, in fact. It carries the animal that secreted it, without the animal being attached to it.

"The argonaut is free to leave its shell," I said to Conseil, "but it never does."

"Just like Captain Nemo," Conseil replied shrewdly. "That's why he would have done better to call his ship the Argonaut."

For about an hour the Nautilus floated in the midst of this troop of mollusks. Then some sudden terror seized them, I know not what. As if at a signal, all the sails were abruptly struck; the arms folded back, the bodies contracted. The shells, capsizing, shifted their center of gravity, and the whole flotilla vanished beneath the waves. It was instantaneous, and never did the ships of a squadron maneuver with more unity.

At that moment night fell all at once, and the swells, barely lifted by the breeze, stretched out peacefully beneath the Nautilus's wales.

The next day, January 26, we cut the equator on the eighty-second meridian, and we passed back into the northern hemisphere.

During that day a formidable troop of sharks escorted us. Terrible animals, which swarm in these seas and make them very dangerous. There were Philippi sharks, brown-backed and whitish-bellied, armed with eleven rows of teeth; ocellated sharks, whose neck is marked with a large black spot ringed with white that looks like an eye; Isabella sharks with a rounded snout strewn with dark points. Often these powerful animals hurled themselves against the salon's glass with a violence hardly reassuring. Ned Land could no longer control himself. He wanted to go up to the surface and harpoon these monsters — especially certain smooth-hound sharks whose mouth is paved with teeth arranged like a mosaic, and great tiger sharks five meters long, which taunted him with a quite particular insistence. But soon the Nautilus, putting on speed, easily left behind the swiftest of these sharks.

On January 27, at the mouth of the vast Gulf of Bengal, we met more than once — a sinister sight! — corpses floating at the surface. They were the dead of the Indian cities, carried by the Ganges out to the open sea, and which the vultures, the country's only undertakers, had not finished devouring. But the sharks were not lacking to help them at their funereal work.

Toward seven in the evening, the Nautilus, half submerged, sailed through a sea of milk. As far as the eye could reach, the ocean seemed turned to milk. Was it the effect of the moon's rays? No, for the moon, barely two days old, was still lost below the horizon in the sun's rays. The whole sky, though lit by the radiance of the stars, seemed black by contrast with the whiteness of the waters.

Conseil could not believe his eyes, and he questioned me about the causes of this singular phenomenon. Luckily, I was in a position to answer him.

"This is what's called a milk sea," I told him, "a vast expanse of white waves often seen off the coasts of Amboina and in these waters."

"But," asked Conseil, "can Master teach me what cause produces such an effect, for this water hasn't turned into milk, I take it!"

"No, my boy, and this whiteness that surprises you is due only to the presence of myriads of tiny infusoria, a sort of little luminous worm, gelatinous and colorless, no thicker than a hair, whose length does not exceed a fifth of a millimeter. Some of these little creatures cling together over a span of several leagues."

"Several leagues!" exclaimed Conseil.

"Yes, my boy, and don't try to reckon the number of these infusoria! You'd never manage it, for, if I'm not mistaken, some navigators have floated on these milk seas for more than forty miles."

I do not know whether Conseil heeded my advice, but he seemed to sink into deep reflection, no doubt trying to calculate how many fifths of a millimeter forty square miles contain. As for me, I went on observing the phenomenon. For several hours the Nautilus cleaved with its ram these whitish waves, and I noticed that it glided noiselessly over this soapy water, as if it were floating in those eddies of foam that the currents and countercurrents of bays sometimes leave between them.

Toward midnight, the sea suddenly took back its ordinary hue; but behind us, all the way to the limits of the horizon, the sky, reflecting the whiteness of the waves, seemed for a long time steeped in the vague glimmers of an aurora borealis.
