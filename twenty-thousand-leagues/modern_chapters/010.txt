The Nautilus

Captain Nemo rose. I followed him. A double door at the far end of the room swung open, and I stepped into a chamber the same size as the one I had just left.

It was a library. Tall bookcases of black rosewood, inlaid with copper, held on their broad shelves a great many volumes bound alike. They ran the whole curve of the room and ended along the bottom in wide divans, upholstered in brown leather, that offered the most comfortable of curves. Light reading-stands on rollers, which could be pushed away or drawn near at will, let you rest your open book on them. At the center stood a large table strewn with pamphlets, among which a few already old newspapers showed. Electric light flooded this whole harmonious ensemble, falling from four frosted globes half sunk in the scrollwork of the ceiling. I gazed with real wonder at this room so ingeniously fitted out, and I could scarcely believe my eyes.

"Captain Nemo," I said to my host, who had just stretched out on a divan, "here is a library that would do honor to more than one palace on the continents, and I'm truly amazed to think that it can follow you down into the deepest seas."

"Where would one find more solitude, more silence, Professor?" Captain Nemo answered. "Does your study at the Museum offer you as complete a peace?"

"No, Captain, and I must add that mine is a poor thing beside yours. You must have six or seven thousand volumes here—"

"Twelve thousand, Professor Aronnax. They are the only ties that still bind me to the land. But the world ended for me the day my Nautilus first dove beneath the waves. That day I bought my last books, my last pamphlets, my last newspapers, and ever since I have chosen to believe that humanity has neither thought nor written another word. These books, Professor, are at your disposal in any case, and you may use them freely."

I thanked Captain Nemo and went over to the library shelves. Books of science, of philosophy, of literature, written in every language, were there in abundance; but I did not see a single work of political economy—those, it seemed, were strictly banned from the ship. One curious detail: all these books were shelved together without distinction, whatever language they happened to be written in, and this mingling proved that the captain of the Nautilus must read fluently any volume his hand happened to pull down.

Among these works I noticed the masterpieces of the old masters and the new—that is, everything most beautiful that humanity has produced in history, poetry, fiction, and science, from Homer to Victor Hugo, from Xenophon to Michelet, from Rabelais to Madame Sand. But science, above all, was what this library had been built to hold. Books of mechanics, ballistics, hydrography, meteorology, geography, geology, and the rest took up a place no less important than the works of natural history, and I understood that they formed the captain's chief study. There I saw all of Humboldt, all of Arago, the work of Foucault, of Henri Sainte-Claire Deville, of Chasles, of Milne-Edwards, of Quatrefages, of Tyndall, of Faraday, of Berthelot, of Father Secchi, of Petermann, of Commander Maury, of Agassiz, and more. The memoirs of the Academy of Sciences, the bulletins of the various geographical societies, and—in a place of honor—the two volumes that had perhaps earned me this relatively charitable welcome from Captain Nemo. Among the works of Joseph Bertrand, his book titled The Founders of Astronomy even gave me a firm date: since I knew it had appeared in the course of 1865, I could conclude that the Nautilus had been fitted out no earlier than that. So Captain Nemo had begun his underwater existence three years ago at the most. I hoped, moreover, that still more recent works might let me pin down the date exactly; but I had time to make that search, and I did not want to hold up our stroll through the wonders of the Nautilus any longer.

"Captain," I said, "I thank you for putting this library at my disposal. There are treasures of science here, and I'll make good use of them."

"This room is not only a library," said Captain Nemo. "It's also a smoking room."

"A smoking room?" I exclaimed. "So one smokes on board?"

"Certainly."

"Then, Captain, I'm forced to conclude that you've kept up your dealings with Havana."

"None at all," the captain replied. "Take this cigar, Professor Aronnax, and though it doesn't come from Havana, you'll be pleased with it if you know your tobacco."

I took the cigar he offered me. Its shape recalled that of a Havana londres, but it seemed to be made of leaves of gold. I lit it at a little brazier resting on an elegant bronze stand, and drew my first puffs with all the pleasure of a connoisseur who hasn't smoked in two days.

"It's excellent," I said, "but it isn't tobacco."

"No," the captain answered. "This tobacco comes neither from Havana nor from the East. It's a kind of seaweed, rich in nicotine, that the sea supplies me—not without some stinginess. Do you still miss your londres cigars, Professor?"

"Captain, from this day on I scorn them."

"Then smoke as you please, and don't fret over where these cigars come from. No tax office has passed judgment on them, but they're none the worse for that, I imagine."

"On the contrary."

At that moment Captain Nemo opened a door facing the one by which I had entered the library, and I passed into an immense salon, splendidly lit.

It was a vast rectangle with cut corners, ten meters long, six wide, five high. A luminous ceiling, decorated with light arabesques, spread a clear, soft daylight over all the wonders heaped up in this museum. For it truly was a museum, in which an intelligent and lavish hand had gathered every treasure of nature and of art, with that artist's jumble that marks a painter's studio.

Some thirty masterworks, framed alike and separated by dazzling displays of arms, adorned the walls, which were hung with tapestries of a severe design. There I saw canvases of the highest value, most of which I had admired in the private collections of Europe and at the painting exhibitions. The various schools of the old masters were represented by a madonna of Raphael, a virgin of Leonardo da Vinci, a nymph of Correggio, a woman of Titian, an adoration of Veronese, an assumption of Murillo, a portrait by Holbein, a monk by Velázquez, a martyr by Ribera, a village festival by Rubens, two Flemish landscapes by Teniers, three little genre pieces by Gerard Dou, Metsu, and Paul Potter, two canvases by Géricault and Prud'hon, and a few seascapes by Backhuysen and Vernet. Among the works of modern painting were pictures signed Delacroix, Ingres, Decamps, Troyon, Meissonier, Daubigny, and others; and a few admirable scale copies of marble or bronze statues, modeled after the finest works of antiquity, stood on their pedestals in the corners of this magnificent museum. The stunned amazement the commander of the Nautilus had predicted was already beginning to take hold of my mind.

"Professor," said this strange man just then, "you'll forgive the informality with which I receive you, and the disorder that reigns in this salon."

"Captain," I answered, "without trying to learn who you are, may I be allowed to recognize in you an artist?"

"An amateur, at most, Professor. I once loved to collect these beautiful works created by the hand of man. I was an avid seeker, a tireless hunter, and I managed to gather a few pieces of great value. They are my last mementos of that land which is dead to me. In my eyes, your modern artists are already ancients; they have two or three thousand years of existence behind them, and I blur them together in my mind. The masters have no age."

"And these musicians?" I said, pointing to scores by Weber, Rossini, Mozart, Beethoven, Haydn, Meyerbeer, Hérold, Wagner, Auber, Gounod, and many others, scattered over a large-model piano-organ that filled one of the salon's panels.

"These musicians," Captain Nemo replied, "are contemporaries of Orpheus, for the differences of chronology fade away in the memory of the dead—and I am dead, Professor, as thoroughly dead as those friends of yours who rest six feet underground!"

Captain Nemo fell silent and seemed lost in a deep reverie. I studied him with keen emotion, silently reading the strangeness of his features. Leaning on the corner of a precious mosaic table, he no longer saw me; he had forgotten my presence.

I respected his meditation and went on reviewing the curiosities that enriched this salon.

Alongside the works of art, the natural rarities held a very important place. They consisted mainly of plants, shells, and other products of the Ocean, which must have been Captain Nemo's own personal finds. In the middle of the salon, a jet of water, lit by electricity, fell back into a basin made from a single giant clam. This shell, furnished by the largest of the headless mollusks, measured around six meters in circumference along its delicately scalloped rim; so it surpassed in size even those splendid giant clams the Republic of Venice gave to Francis I, and from which the church of Saint-Sulpice in Paris made two gigantic holy-water fonts.

Around this basin, under elegant glass cases fixed with copper frames, were classified and labeled the most precious products of the sea that had ever been offered to a naturalist's gaze. You can imagine my professor's delight.

The branch of the zoophytes offered very curious specimens of its two groups, the polyps and the echinoderms. In the first group there were organ-pipe corals, sea fans spread out like fans, soft sponges of Syria, sea shrubs of the Moluccas, sea pens, an admirable sea rod from the waters of Norway, various sea plumes, soft corals, a whole series of those madrepores that my teacher Milne-Edwards has so shrewdly sorted into sections, and among which I noticed lovely flabellinas, oculina corals from Réunion Island, the "chariot of Neptune" of the Antilles, superb varieties of coral—in short, every species of those curious coral-builders whose colonies form entire islands that will one day become continents. Among the echinoderms, remarkable for their spiny casing, the sea stars, the starfish, the sea lilies, the feather stars, the brittle stars, the sea urchins, the sea cucumbers, and the rest represented a complete collection of that group.

A conchologist of nervous temperament would surely have swooned before other, more numerous cases in which the specimens of the mollusk branch were arranged. There I saw a collection of inestimable value that I would not have time to describe in full. Among these treasures I'll cite, just for the record: the elegant royal hammer shell of the Indian Ocean, whose regular white spots stood out vividly against a red and brown ground; an imperial spondylus of vivid colors, bristling all over with spines, a rare specimen in the museums of Europe, whose value I put at twenty thousand francs; a common hammer shell from the seas of New Holland, hard to come by; exotic cockles from Senegal, fragile white shells with double valves that a breath would have scattered like a soap bubble; several varieties of the watering-pot shells of Java, sorts of chalky tubes fringed with leafy folds and much fought over by collectors; a whole series of top shells, some greenish-yellow, fished from American seas, others reddish-brown, partial to the waters of New Holland, some of these from the Gulf of Mexico and remarkable for their overlapping scale-like shell, others sun shells found in the southern seas, and last, the rarest of all, the magnificent New Zealand spur shell; then some admirable sulfur-yellow tellins, precious species of Cytherea and Venus clams, the trellised sundial shell from the coasts of Tranquebar, the marbled turban with its resplendent mother-of-pearl, the green parrot shells of the China seas, the almost unknown cone of the genus Coenodullus, every variety of cowrie that serves as money in India and Africa, the "Glory of the Sea," the most precious shell of the East Indies; and finally periwinkles, dolphin shells, turret shells, violet snails, egg cowries, volutes, olives, miters, helmet shells, murexes, whelks, harp shells, rock shells, tritons, cerith shells, spindle shells, conchs, wing shells, limpets, sea butterflies, sea angels—delicate, fragile shells that science has christened with its most charming names.

Set apart, in special compartments, were unrolled strings of pearls of the greatest beauty, which the electric light pricked with points of fire: pink pearls torn from the pinna shells of the Red Sea; green pearls from the iris abalone; yellow, blue, and black pearls, curious products of the various mollusks of every ocean and of certain mussels from the rivers of the North; and finally several specimens of inestimable price, distilled by the rarest of pearl oysters. Some of these pearls were bigger than a pigeon's egg; they were worth as much as, and more than, the one the traveler Tavernier sold for three million to the Shah of Persia, and they outshone that other pearl of the Imam of Muscat, which I had believed had no rival in the world.

So putting a figure on the value of this collection was, so to speak, impossible. Captain Nemo must have spent millions to acquire these various specimens, and I was wondering what source he could be drawing on to satisfy his collector's whims like this, when I was interrupted by these words:

"You're examining my shells, Professor. Indeed, they may interest a naturalist; but for me they hold one further charm, for I gathered every one of them with my own hand, and there is not a sea on the globe that has escaped my searches."

"I understand, Captain—I understand that joy of walking among such riches. You are one of those who have built their own treasure with their own hands. No museum in Europe possesses such a collection of the Ocean's products. But if I exhaust my admiration on it, what will I have left for the vessel that carries it! I have no wish to pry into secrets that are yours. Still, I confess that this Nautilus, the motive power it holds within itself, the machinery that lets it be handled, the mighty agent that drives it—all of that excites my curiosity in the highest degree. I see instruments hanging on the walls of this salon whose purpose is unknown to me. May I ask—?"

"Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo answered, "I told you that you would be free aboard my ship, and so no part of the Nautilus is off-limits to you. You may visit it in detail, and I'll be glad to be your guide."

"I don't know how to thank you, Captain, but I won't abuse your kindness. I'll only ask what these instruments of physics are meant for—"

"Professor, these same instruments are in my own room, and it's there that I'll have the pleasure of explaining their use to you. But first, come see the cabin that has been set aside for you. You need to know how you'll be housed aboard the Nautilus."

I followed Captain Nemo, who led me through one of the doors cut into each canted corner of the salon and back into the ship's passageways. He took me forward, and there I found, not a cabin, but an elegant room, with a bed, a washstand, and various other furnishings.

I could only thank my host.

"Your room adjoins mine," he said, opening a door, "and mine opens onto the salon we have just left."

I entered the captain's room. It had a severe, almost monastic look. An iron cot, a work table, a few washing furnishings. The whole lit by a dim half-light. Nothing comfortable. The strict necessities, and no more.

Captain Nemo pointed me to a seat.

"Please sit down," he said.
