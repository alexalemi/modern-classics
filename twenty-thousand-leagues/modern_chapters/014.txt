An Invitation by Letter

The next day, November 9, I woke only after a long twelve-hour sleep. Conseil came, as was his habit, to ask "how Master had passed the night" and to offer his services. He had left his friend the Canadian sleeping like a man who had never done anything else his whole life.

I let the good fellow chatter away to his heart's content without answering him much. My mind was on Captain Nemo's absence during our session the day before, and I hoped to see him again today.

I was soon back in my clothes of byssus. Their material had prompted more than one comment from Conseil. I explained to him that they were woven from the glossy, silky filaments that anchor the "fan mussels" to the rocks — a kind of shell very common along the shores of the Mediterranean. In the old days these threads were made into fine fabrics, stockings, gloves, for they were at once very soft and very warm. So the Nautilus's crew could clothe itself cheaply, without asking a thing of the cotton fields, the sheep, or the silkworms of the land.

Once dressed, I made my way to the grand salon. It was deserted.

I plunged into the study of those treasures of conchology heaped beneath the glass cases. I also went digging through the great herbariums, filled with the rarest marine plants, which, though dried, still kept their wonderful colors. Among these precious hydrophytes I noticed whorled cladostephus, peacock's-tail padinas, grape-leaved caulerpas, grain-bearing callithamnions, delicate scarlet-tinted ceramiums, agars spread out like fans, acetabularias resembling the caps of very flattened mushrooms, and long classed among the zoophytes — in short, a whole series of wracks and kelps.

The entire day went by without my being honored by a visit from Captain Nemo. The panels of the salon did not open. Perhaps they didn't want to let us grow jaded on these beautiful things.

The Nautilus held its course east-northeast, its speed at twelve miles, its depth between fifty and sixty meters.

The next day, November 10, the same neglect, the same solitude. I saw no one from the crew. Ned and Conseil spent the greater part of the day with me. They were astonished by the captain's inexplicable absence. Was this strange man ill? Did he mean to change his plans concerning us?

After all, as Conseil pointed out, we enjoyed complete liberty; we were fed delicately and abundantly. Our host was keeping to the terms of his agreement. We had nothing to complain of, and besides, the very strangeness of our fate held such splendid compensations in store that we had no right, as yet, to accuse him of anything.

That day I began the journal of these adventures — which is what has allowed me to recount them with the most scrupulous accuracy — and, a curious detail, I wrote it on paper made from marine eelgrass.

On November 11, early in the morning, the fresh air spreading through the interior of the Nautilus told me we had returned to the surface of the ocean to renew our supply of oxygen. I headed for the central stairway and climbed up onto the platform.

It was six o'clock. I found the weather overcast, the sea gray but calm. Scarcely a swell. Captain Nemo, whom I hoped to meet up there — would he come? I saw no one but the helmsman, penned in his glass cage. Seated on the ridge formed by the hull of the dinghy, I breathed in the salt air with delight.

Little by little the mist burned off under the sun's rays. The radiant star came flooding up over the eastern horizon. The sea caught fire beneath its gaze like a trail of gunpowder. The clouds, scattered high overhead, took on vivid, exquisitely shaded tones, and countless "cat's tongues" of cloud warned of wind for the whole day.

But what did the wind matter to a Nautilus that not even storms could frighten?

So I was admiring that joyous sunrise — so cheerful, so invigorating — when I heard someone climbing up toward the platform.

I was getting ready to greet Captain Nemo, but it was his second-in-command — whom I had already seen during the captain's first visit — who appeared. He advanced onto the platform and did not seem to notice my presence. His powerful telescope at his eyes, he scanned every point of the horizon with the closest attention. Then, this examination done, he came up to the panel and pronounced a sentence whose exact words are these. I've remembered it, because every morning it was repeated under identical conditions. It ran as follows:

"Nautron respoc lorni virch."

What it meant, I couldn't say.

These words spoken, the second-in-command went back down. I supposed the Nautilus was about to resume its underwater voyage. So I went back to the panel, and through the passageways I returned to my cabin.

Five days passed like this, with no change in the situation. Every morning I climbed up onto the platform. The same sentence was pronounced by the same man. Captain Nemo did not appear.

I had made up my mind that I would not see him again, when, on November 16, coming back into my cabin with Ned and Conseil, I found on the table a note addressed to me.

I opened it with an impatient hand. It was written in a frank, clear hand, though a little Gothic, calling to mind the German styles of lettering.

The note was worded as follows:

Professor Aronnax, aboard the Nautilus.

November 16, 1867.

Captain Nemo invites Professor Aronnax to a hunting party that will take place tomorrow morning in his forests on Crespo Island. He hopes that nothing will prevent the Professor from attending, and he will be glad to see the Professor's companions join him.

The commander of the Nautilus,
Captain NEMO.

"A hunt!" cried Ned.

"And in his forests on Crespo Island!" added Conseil.

"So he does go ashore, then, this odd character of ours?" Ned Land went on.

"That seems clearly stated," I said, rereading the letter.

"Well then! We have to accept," the Canadian replied. "Once we're on solid ground, we'll figure out what to do. Besides, I won't be sorry to sink my teeth into a few slabs of fresh venison."

Without trying to reconcile the plain contradiction between Captain Nemo's evident loathing for continents and islands and his invitation to go hunting in a forest, I simply answered:

"Let's first see what this Crespo Island is."

I consulted the planisphere, and at 32°40′ north latitude and 167°50′ west longitude I found a small island, sighted in 1801 by Captain Crespo, which the old Spanish charts called Rocca de la Plata — that is, "Silver Rock." So we were about eighteen hundred miles from our starting point, and the Nautilus's slightly altered course was bringing it back toward the southeast.

I showed my companions that little rock lost in the middle of the North Pacific.

"If Captain Nemo does go ashore now and then," I told them, "he at least chooses islands that are absolutely deserted!"

Ned Land shook his head without answering; then he and Conseil left me. After a supper served to me by the mute, impassive steward, I fell asleep, though not without some uneasiness.

The next day, November 17, when I awoke, I felt that the Nautilus was completely motionless. I dressed quickly and went into the grand salon.

Captain Nemo was there. He was waiting for me; he rose, bowed, and asked whether it suited me to come along with him.

Since he made no allusion whatever to his absence over these eight days, I refrained from mentioning it, and simply replied that my companions and I were ready to follow him.

"Only, sir," I added, "I'll take the liberty of putting a question to you."

"Put it, Professor Aronnax, and if I can answer it, I will."

"Well then, Captain, how does it happen that you, who have broken off all connection with the land, own forests on Crespo Island?"

"Professor," the captain answered me, "the forests I own ask the sun for neither light nor warmth. No lions, no tigers, no panthers, no quadruped of any kind sets foot in them. They are known to me alone. They grow for me alone. They are not land forests at all, but undersea forests."

"Undersea forests!" I exclaimed.

"Yes, Professor."

"And you're offering to take me there?"

"Precisely."

"On foot?"

"On foot, and dry-shod at that."

"Hunting?"

"Hunting."

"Rifle in hand?"

"Rifle in hand."

I looked at the commander of the Nautilus in a way that was hardly flattering to him.

"Decidedly, his mind is diseased," I thought. "He's had a fit that's lasted eight days, and that's lasting still. What a shame! I liked him better strange than mad!"

This thought could be read plainly on my face, but Captain Nemo merely invited me to follow him, and I followed like a man resigned to anything.

We came to the dining room, where breakfast was already served.

"Professor Aronnax," the captain said to me, "I'll ask you to share my breakfast without ceremony. We'll talk while we eat. But though I've promised you a walk in the forest, I've made no pledge to have you come across a restaurant there. So eat like a man who probably won't dine until very late."

I did justice to the meal. It was made up of various fish and slices of holothurians — those excellent zoophytes — dressed with very appetizing seaweeds, such as Porphyria laciniata and Laurentia primafetida. The drink was clear water, to which, following the captain's example, I added a few drops of a fermented liquor, extracted, in the Kamchatkan fashion, from the alga known as "palmate Rhodymenia."

Captain Nemo ate, at first, without saying a single word. Then he said to me:

"Professor, when I proposed that you come hunt in my forests of Crespo, you thought me at odds with myself. When I told you it was a matter of undersea forests, you thought me mad. Professor, one must never judge men lightly."

"But, Captain, believe me that—"

"Kindly hear me out, and you'll see whether you have any grounds to accuse me of madness or of contradiction."

"I'm listening."

"Professor, you know as well as I do that a man can live underwater provided he carries his own supply of breathable air with him. In underwater work, the laborer, dressed in a waterproof suit with his head sealed inside a metal capsule, receives air from the outside by means of force pumps and flow regulators."

"That's the diving-suit apparatus," I said.

"Just so, but under those conditions the man isn't free. He's tethered to the pump that sends him air through a rubber hose — a veritable chain riveting him to the land — and if we had to be held to the Nautilus like that, we couldn't go far."

"And the means of being free?" I asked.

"It's to use the Rouquayrol-Denayrouze apparatus, devised by two of your countrymen, but which I've perfected for my own use, and which will let you venture into these new physiological conditions without your organs suffering the least harm. It consists of a tank of thick sheet metal, in which I store air under a pressure of fifty atmospheres. This tank is fastened to the back by straps, like a soldier's pack. Its upper part forms a box out of which the air, held in by a bellows mechanism, can escape only at its normal pressure. In the Rouquayrol apparatus as it is ordinarily used, two rubber tubes run from this box to a kind of mask that seals the operator's nose and mouth; one serves to bring in the air breathed in, the other to let out the air breathed out, and the tongue closes off one or the other according to the needs of respiration. But I, who face considerable pressures at the bottom of the seas, have had to enclose my head, like the head of a diver, in a copper sphere, and it is to this sphere that the two intake and exhaust tubes lead."

"Perfect, Captain Nemo, but the air you carry must run out fast, and once it holds no more than fifteen percent oxygen, it becomes unbreathable."

"No doubt, but as I told you, Professor Aronnax, the pumps of the Nautilus let me store it under considerable pressure, and under those conditions the tank of the apparatus can supply breathable air for nine or ten hours."

"I have no further objection to make," I replied. "I'll only ask you, Captain, how you can light your way at the bottom of the ocean."

"With the Ruhmkorff apparatus, Professor Aronnax. If the first is carried on the back, the second is fastened to the belt. It consists of a Bunsen battery that I activate not with potassium bichromate, but with sodium. An induction coil gathers the electricity produced and directs it toward a specially designed lantern. In this lantern there is a glass coil containing only a residue of carbon dioxide gas. When the apparatus is working, this gas becomes luminous, giving off a whitish, continuous light. Thus equipped, I breathe and I see."

"Captain Nemo, to every one of my objections you give such crushing answers that I no longer dare doubt. Still, though I'm quite forced to accept the Rouquayrol and Ruhmkorff apparatus, I ask leave to hold some reservations about the rifle you mean to arm me with."

"But it isn't a powder rifle," the captain replied.

"An air rifle, then?"

"No doubt. How would you have me manufacture powder aboard my ship, having neither saltpeter, nor sulfur, nor charcoal?"

"Besides," I said, "to fire underwater, in a medium eight hundred fifty-five times denser than air, you'd have to overcome a considerable resistance."

"That would be no reason not to. There exist certain guns, perfected after Fulton by the Englishmen Philip Coles and Burley, by the Frenchman Furcy, by the Italian Landi, which are fitted with a special sealing system and can fire under these conditions. But I repeat, having no powder, I've replaced it with high-pressure air, which the pumps of the Nautilus supply me in abundance."

"But this air must run out quickly."

"Well, don't I have my Rouquayrol tank, which can furnish it at need? All it takes is a suitable tap. Besides, Professor Aronnax, you'll see for yourself that during these undersea hunts one uses up no great quantity of air or of bullets."

"Still, it seems to me that in this half-darkness, in the midst of a liquid so dense compared to the atmosphere, the shots can't carry far and are hardly likely to be deadly."

"Sir, with this rifle, on the contrary, every shot is deadly, and the instant an animal is hit, however lightly, it falls as if struck by lightning."

"Why?"

"Because what this rifle fires are not ordinary bullets, but little glass capsules — invented by the Austrian chemist Leniebroek — of which I have a considerable stock. These glass capsules, sheathed in a steel casing and weighted with a lead base, are true little Leyden jars, in which electricity is forced to a very high tension. At the slightest impact they discharge, and the animal, however powerful it may be, drops dead. I'll add that these capsules are no bigger than number-four shot, and that the charge of an ordinary rifle could hold ten of them."

"I'll argue no more," I replied, rising from the table, "and I have nothing left to do but take up my rifle. Wherever you go, I'll go."

Captain Nemo led me toward the stern of the Nautilus, and, passing by the cabin of Ned and Conseil, I called my two companions, who followed us at once.

Then we came to a cell located alongside, near the engine room, in which we were to put on our walking gear.
