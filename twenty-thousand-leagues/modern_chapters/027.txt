The Red Sea

(Part 1 of 2)

Over the course of January 29, the island of Ceylon dropped below the horizon, and the Nautilus, running at twenty miles an hour, slipped into that labyrinth of channels which separates the Maldives from the Laccadives. It even skirted Kittan Island, a bit of land of madreporic origin, discovered by Vasco da Gama in 1499 and one of the nineteen principal islands of that Laccadive archipelago, lying between 10° and 14°30′ north latitude and between 69° and 50°72′ east longitude.

By then we had covered sixteen thousand two hundred and twenty miles, or seven thousand five hundred leagues, since our starting point in the seas of Japan.

The next day, January 30, when the Nautilus rose back to the surface of the ocean, there was no longer any land in sight. She was steering north-northwest, heading toward that Sea of Oman which is hollowed out between Arabia and the Indian peninsula and serves as the outlet of the Persian Gulf.

This was plainly a dead end, with no way through. Where, then, was Captain Nemo taking us? I couldn't have said. And that failed to satisfy the Canadian, who that day asked me where we were going.

"We're going, Master Ned, wherever the captain's whim leads us."

"That whim," the Canadian answered, "can't take us far. The Persian Gulf has no way out, and if we go into it, we won't be long in doubling back on our own tracks."

"Well then, we'll double back, Master Land, and if after the Persian Gulf the Nautilus takes it into her head to visit the Red Sea, the Strait of Bab-el-Mandeb is always there to give her passage."

"I don't need to remind you, Professor," Ned Land replied, "that the Red Sea is just as closed off as the Gulf, since the Isthmus of Suez isn't pierced through yet, and even if it were, a mysterious boat like ours wouldn't risk itself in canals cut up by locks. So the Red Sea still isn't the road that will bring us back to Europe."

"But I never said we'd go back to Europe."

"Then what do you suppose?"

"I suppose that, after visiting these curious waters off Arabia and Egypt, the Nautilus will drop back down through the Indian Ocean, perhaps by way of the Mozambique Channel, perhaps off the Mascarenes, so as to reach the Cape of Good Hope."

"And once at the Cape of Good Hope?" the Canadian asked, with a very particular insistence.

"Well, then we'll push on into that Atlantic we don't yet know. Come now, Ned my friend — are you growing weary of this voyage under the seas? Are you getting jaded by the endlessly changing spectacle of underwater marvels? For my part, I'll see with the keenest regret the end of a voyage that so few men have ever been granted."

"But do you realize, Professor Aronnax," the Canadian answered, "that it will soon be three months we've been imprisoned aboard this Nautilus?"

"No, Ned, I don't realize it, I don't want to realize it, and I count neither the days nor the hours."

"But how does it end?"

"The end will come in its own good time. Besides, there's nothing we can do about it, and we're arguing to no purpose. If you were to come to me, my good Ned, and say, 'A chance to escape is offered to us,' then I'd discuss it with you. But that's not the case, and to speak to you frankly, I don't believe Captain Nemo will ever venture into European waters."

From this short exchange you can see that, fanatic of the Nautilus that I'd become, I had climbed right inside her commander's skin.

As for Ned Land, he ended the conversation with these words, delivered as a sort of monologue: "That's all very fine and good, but to my mind, where there's constraint, there's no more pleasure."

For four days, until February 3, the Nautilus toured the Sea of Oman at various speeds and various depths. She seemed to be moving at random, as though hesitating over which route to take, but she never crossed the Tropic of Cancer.

As we left that sea, we caught a brief glimpse of Muscat, the most important town in the land of Oman. I admired its strange look, ringed by the black rocks against which its houses and forts stand out in white. I made out the rounded dome of its mosques, the elegant point of its minarets, its cool, green terraces. But it was no more than a vision, and the Nautilus soon plunged beneath the dark waves of those waters.

Then she ran for a distance of six miles along the Arabian coasts of Mahrah and Hadhramaut, with their undulating line of mountains relieved here and there by a few ancient ruins. On February 5 we at last entered the Gulf of Aden, a true funnel driven into that neck of Bab-el-Mandeb, which pours the waters of India into the Red Sea.

On February 6 the Nautilus floated in sight of Aden, perched on a promontory joined to the mainland by a narrow isthmus, a sort of unassailable Gibraltar whose fortifications the English rebuilt after seizing it in 1839. I glimpsed the octagonal minarets of that city, which was once, according to the historian Edrisi, the richest and busiest trading depot on the whole coast.

I felt sure that Captain Nemo, having reached this point, would turn back; but I was mistaken, and to my great surprise he did nothing of the sort.

The following day, February 7, we entered the Strait of Bab-el-Mandeb, whose name means, in Arabic, "the Gate of Tears." Twenty miles wide, it is only fifty-two kilometers long, and for the Nautilus running at full speed, crossing it was scarcely the business of an hour. But I saw nothing, not even the island of Perim, whose position the British government has fortified to strengthen Aden. Too many English and French steamers of the lines from Suez to Bombay, to Calcutta, to Melbourne, to Bourbon, to Mauritius were plowing that narrow passage for the Nautilus to attempt to show herself there. So she prudently kept between two waters.

At last, at noon, we were cutting through the swells of the Red Sea.

The Red Sea — that lake famous in biblical tradition, which the rains scarcely freshen, which no important river waters, which an excessive evaporation pumps at unceasingly, and which loses each year a sheet of water a meter and a half deep! Singular gulf, which, closed off and reduced to the conditions of a lake, would perhaps have dried up entirely — inferior in this to its neighbors the Caspian or the Dead Sea, whose levels have dropped only to the point where their evaporation has come to equal exactly the sum of the waters received into them.

This Red Sea is two thousand six hundred kilometers long, with an average width of two hundred and forty. In the time of the Ptolemies and the Roman emperors it was the great commercial artery of the world, and the piercing of the isthmus will restore to it that ancient importance which the Suez railways have already partly brought back.

I would not even try to understand this caprice of Captain Nemo's that could have decided him to draw us into this gulf. But I approved without reservation of the Nautilus's having entered it. She took a moderate pace, now holding to the surface, now diving to avoid some ship, and so I was able to observe both the depths and the surface of this most curious sea.

On February 8, in the earliest hours of the day, Mocha came into view — now a ruined town whose walls fall at the mere sound of a cannon, sheltered here and there by a few green date palms. Once an important city, it had contained six public markets and twenty-six mosques, and its walls, defended by fourteen forts, formed a belt three kilometers around it.

Then the Nautilus drew nearer the African shores, where the sea is considerably deeper. There, between two waters of crystal limpidity, through the open panels, she let us gaze upon splendid thickets of dazzling coral and vast faces of rock clothed in a magnificent green fur of algae and wrack. What an indescribable spectacle, and what a variety of settings and landscapes at the flat crowns of those reefs and volcanic islets that border the Libyan coast! But it was toward the eastern shores that these branching forms appeared in all their beauty, shores the Nautilus was not slow to make for. It was off the coasts of the Tihamah — for there not only did these displays of zoophytes flower below the level of the sea, but they also formed picturesque tangles that unrolled ten fathoms above it; these last more fanciful, but less brightly colored than the ones below, whose freshness was kept up by the moist vitality of the waters.

What charming hours I spent thus at the salon window! What new specimens of the underwater flora and fauna I admired under the blaze of our electric beacon! Agaric-shaped mushroom corals, slate-colored sea anemones, among them the Thalassianthus aster; organ-pipe corals ranged like flutes and only awaiting the breath of the god Pan; shells peculiar to this sea, which settle into the madreporic hollows and whose base is twisted into a short spiral; and finally a thousand specimens of a coral animal I had not yet observed — the common sponge.

The class of sponges, first in the group of polyps, was in fact founded upon this curious product, whose usefulness is beyond dispute. The sponge is not a plant, as some naturalists still hold, but an animal of the lowest order, a coral animal lower even than coral itself. Its animal nature is not in doubt, and one cannot even adopt the opinion of the ancients, who regarded it as a being intermediate between the plant and the animal. I must say, however, that naturalists are not in agreement about the sponge's mode of organization. For some it is a coral animal; for others, such as Monsieur Milne-Edwards, it is an isolated and single individual.

The class of sponges contains about three hundred species, which are found in a great many seas, and even in certain watercourses, where they have been given the name "fluviatile." But their favorite waters are those of the Mediterranean, the Greek archipelago, the coast of Syria, and the Red Sea. There the fine soft sponges reproduce and grow, sponges whose value rises as high as a hundred and fifty francs — the blond sponge of Syria, the hard sponge of Barbary, and so on. But since I could not hope to study these zoophytes in the ports of the Levant, from which we were separated by the impassable Isthmus of Suez, I contented myself with observing them in the waters of the Red Sea.

So I called Conseil to my side, while the Nautilus, at an average depth of eight to nine meters, was slowly grazing all those lovely rocks of the eastern coast.

There grew sponges of every shape — sponges stalked, leafed, globular, fingered. They justified rather exactly those names of baskets, chalices, distaffs, elk's horns, lion's paw, peacock's tail, and Neptune's glove which the fishermen — more poets than the scholars — have given them. From their fibrous tissue, coated with a half-fluid gelatinous substance, there escaped without cease little threads of water which, after carrying life into each cell, were expelled from it by a contractile movement. This substance disappears after the death of the polyp and putrefies, releasing ammonia. Then nothing remains but those horny or gelatinous fibers of which the household sponge is composed, which takes on a reddish tint and is put to various uses, according to its degree of elasticity, permeability, or resistance to soaking.

These coral animals clung to the rocks, to the shells of mollusks, and even to the stems of water plants. They filled the smallest crevices, some spreading flat, others rising up or hanging like coral-like outgrowths. I taught Conseil that these sponges are fished in two ways, either by dredge or by hand. This last method, which requires the use of divers, is the better one, for by sparing the tissue of the coral animal it leaves it a far higher value.

The other zoophytes swarming near the sponges consisted chiefly of jellyfish of a very elegant species; the mollusks were represented by varieties of squid which, according to d'Orbigny, are peculiar to the Red Sea, and the reptiles by virgata turtles, belonging to the genus Chelonia, which furnished our table with a wholesome and delicate dish.

As for the fish, they were numerous and often remarkable. Here are the ones the Nautilus's nets most frequently brought aboard: rays, among them the limma, oval in shape and brick-colored, its body strewn with uneven blue spots and recognizable by its double toothed spine; the arnak with its silvery back; the pastinaca with its stippled tail; and the bockat, vast mantles two meters long that rippled between the waters; the aodon, utterly toothless, a kind of cartilaginous fish akin to the shark; the dromedary trunkfish, whose hump ends in a curved spine a foot and a half long; the ophidion, true morays with silvery tails, bluish backs, and brown pectoral fins bordered with a gray edging; the fiatola, a kind of butterfish zebra-striped with narrow bands of gold and decked out in the three colors of France; the garamit blennies, four decimeters long; superb caranx, decorated with seven crosswise bands of a fine black, blue and yellow fins, and gold and silver scales; centropodes; goldflame mullets with yellow heads; parrotfish, wrasses, triggerfish, gobies, and so forth; and a thousand other fish common to the oceans we had already crossed.
