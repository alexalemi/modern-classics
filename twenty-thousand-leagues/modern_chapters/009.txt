The Man of the Seas

It was the commander of the vessel who had spoken.

At those words, Ned Land sprang to his feet. The steward, nearly throttled, staggered out at a sign from his master; and such was the commander's authority aboard his own ship that not a single gesture betrayed the resentment this man must surely have felt toward the Canadian. Conseil, curious in spite of himself, and I, dumbfounded, waited in silence for the scene to reach its end.

The commander, leaning against the corner of the table, arms folded, studied us with deep attention. Was he hesitating to speak? Did he regret those words he had just let slip in French? One might well have thought so.

After a few moments of a silence none of us dreamed of breaking, he said, in a calm and penetrating voice:

"Gentlemen, I speak French, English, German, and Latin equally well. I could therefore have answered you at our very first meeting; but I wanted to know you first, and to reflect afterward. Your four accounts, absolutely alike at bottom, confirmed for me the identity of your persons. I now know that chance has brought before me Monsieur Pierre Aronnax, professor of natural history at the Museum of Paris, charged with a scientific mission abroad; Conseil, his servant; and Ned Land, a Canadian by birth, harpooner aboard the frigate Abraham Lincoln of the United States Navy."

I bowed in a gesture of assent. The commander had not put a question to me. So there was no reply to make. The man expressed himself with perfect ease, without the slightest accent. His phrasing was clear, his words exact, his fluency remarkable. And yet I did not "feel" in him a fellow countryman.

He went on in these terms:

"You have doubtless found, sir, that I was slow in paying you this second visit. It is because, once your identity was established, I wanted to weigh carefully what course to take with you. I hesitated a great deal. The most unfortunate of circumstances has brought you into the presence of a man who has broken with humankind. You came to trouble my existence—"

"Not by intention," I said.

"Not by intention?" the stranger answered, raising his voice a little. "Is it by no intention that the Abraham Lincoln hunts me across every sea? Is it by no intention that you took passage aboard that frigate? Is it by no intention that your cannonballs bounced off the hull of my ship? Is it by no intention that Master Ned Land struck me with his harpoon?"

I caught a contained irritation in these words. But to these recriminations I had a perfectly natural answer to make, and I made it.

"Sir," I said, "you are surely unaware of the debates that have taken place about you in America and in Europe. You do not know that various accidents, caused by collisions with your underwater craft, have stirred public opinion on both continents. I will spare you the countless hypotheses by which people tried to explain the inexplicable phenomenon whose secret you alone possessed. But you must understand that, in pursuing you across the high seas of the Pacific, the Abraham Lincoln believed it was chasing some powerful sea monster, one the ocean had to be rid of at any cost."

A half smile relaxed the commander's lips; then, in a calmer tone:

"Professor Aronnax," he replied, "would you dare to claim that your frigate would not have pursued and cannonaded an underwater boat just as readily as a monster?"

The question left me at a loss, for Commander Farragut would certainly not have hesitated. He would have believed it his duty to destroy a craft of this kind exactly as he would a gigantic narwhal.

"You understand, then, sir," the stranger went on, "that I have the right to treat you as enemies."

I made no answer, and with good reason. What was the use of arguing a proposition like that, when force can shatter the best of arguments?

"I hesitated for a long time," the commander resumed. "Nothing obliged me to offer you my hospitality. If I were to part with you, I had no interest in ever seeing you again. I would set you back down on the platform of this ship that had served you as a refuge. I would sink beneath the seas, and forget that you had ever existed. Was that not my right?"

"It might have been the right of a savage," I answered, "but not that of a civilized man."

"Professor," the commander shot back sharply, "I am not what you call a civilized man! I have broken with the whole of society for reasons that I alone have the right to judge. I therefore obey none of its rules, and I urge you never to invoke them in my presence again!"

This was said flatly. A flash of anger and scorn had lit the stranger's eyes, and in this man's life I glimpsed a formidable past. Not only had he placed himself outside human laws, he had made himself independent, free in the strictest sense of the word, beyond all reach! For who would dare pursue him to the bottom of the seas, when at their surface he foiled every effort mounted against him? What ship could withstand a collision with his underwater ram? What armor plating, however thick, could bear the blows of his spur? No man on earth could call him to account for his deeds. God—if he believed in one; his conscience—if he had one; these were the only judges he could depend on.

These reflections flashed swiftly through my mind while the strange personage fell silent, absorbed and as though withdrawn into himself. I regarded him with a dread mingled with fascination, much, no doubt, as Oedipus regarded the Sphinx.

After a rather long silence, the commander spoke again.

"I hesitated, then," he said, "but I concluded that my own interest could be reconciled with that natural pity to which every human being has a claim. You will remain aboard my ship, since fate has cast you here. You will be free aboard her, and in exchange for that freedom—a wholly relative freedom, I admit—I will impose only a single condition on you. Your word that you will submit to it will be enough for me."

"Speak, sir," I answered. "I take it this condition is one an honest man can accept?"

"Yes, sir, and here it is. It is possible that certain unforeseen events may oblige me to confine you to your cabins for some hours or some days, as the case may be. Wishing never to resort to violence, I expect from you in that case—more even than in any other—a passive obedience. In acting so, I shield you from all responsibility, I clear you entirely, for it falls to me to make it impossible for you to see what must not be seen. Do you accept this condition?"

So things went on aboard that were at the very least peculiar—things not to be seen by people who had not placed themselves outside the laws of society! Among the surprises the future held in store for me, this would not prove the least.

"We accept," I answered. "Only, I will ask your permission, sir, to put one question to you—just one."

"Speak, sir."

"You said that we would be free aboard your ship?"

"Entirely."

"Then I will ask what you understand by that freedom."

"Why, the freedom to come and go, to see, even to observe everything that happens here—save in a few grave circumstances—the freedom, in short, that my companions and I enjoy ourselves."

It was clear that we did not understand each other.

"Forgive me, sir," I resumed, "but such freedom is only the freedom every prisoner has to pace his cell! It cannot be enough for us."

"And yet it will have to be enough!"

"What! We must give up ever again seeing our homeland, our friends, our families?"

"Yes, sir. But giving up that unbearable yoke of the land, which men take to be freedom—perhaps that is not so painful as you suppose!"

"For one thing," cried Ned Land, "I will never give my word not to try to escape!"

"I am not asking you for your word, Master Land," the commander answered coldly.

"Sir," I said, carried away in spite of myself, "you are abusing your position over us! This is cruelty!"

"No, sir, it is mercy! You are my prisoners of war! I keep you, when with a single word I could plunge you back into the depths of the ocean! You attacked me! You came to surprise a secret that no man in the world must uncover—the secret of my whole existence! And you think I am going to send you back to that dry land that must know me no longer? Never! In holding you, it is not you I am keeping—it is myself!"

These words showed a resolve on the commander's part against which no argument would prevail.

"So, sir," I resumed, "you are simply giving us the choice between life and death?"

"Simply that."

"My friends," I said, "to a question put in such terms there is nothing to answer. But no word binds us to the master of this ship."

"No word, sir," the stranger replied.

Then, in a gentler voice, he went on:

"Now, allow me to finish what I have to say to you. I know you, Professor Aronnax. You, if not your companions, will perhaps have less cause to complain of the chance that has bound you to my fate. Among the books that serve my favorite studies you will find that work you published on the great ocean depths. I have often read it. You have carried your work as far as land-bound science allowed. But you do not know everything, you have not seen everything. Let me tell you, then, Professor, that you will not regret the time spent aboard my ship. You are about to travel through the land of wonders. Astonishment, amazement, will very likely be the habitual condition of your mind. You will not easily grow jaded with the spectacle offered ceaselessly to your eyes. In a new tour of the underwater world—the last, perhaps, who knows?—I mean to see again all that I have been able to study at the bottom of these seas I have crossed so many times, and you shall be my companion in study. From this day forward, you enter a new element; you will see what no man has yet seen—for my crew and I no longer count ourselves among the living—and our planet, thanks to me, is going to yield up to you its last secrets."

I cannot deny it: these words of the commander made a great impression on me. He had caught me by my weakness, and I forgot, for a moment, that the contemplation of these sublime things could not be worth the freedom I had lost. Besides, I was counting on the future to settle that grave question. So I contented myself with answering:

"Sir, though you have broken with humanity, I want to believe that you have not renounced all human feeling. We are castaways charitably taken in aboard your ship; we will not forget it. As for me, I do not deny that if the interest of science could absorb even the need for freedom, what our meeting promises would offer me great compensations."

I thought the commander was going to hold out his hand to seal our pact. He did nothing of the sort. I regretted it for his sake.

"One last question," I said, just as this inexplicable being seemed about to withdraw.

"Speak, Professor."

"By what name am I to call you?"

"Sir," the commander replied, "to you I am nothing but Captain Nemo, and you and your companions are nothing to me but the passengers of the Nautilus."

Captain Nemo called out. A steward appeared. The captain gave him his orders in that strange language I could not identify. Then, turning to the Canadian and Conseil:

"A meal is waiting for you in your cabin," he told them. "Kindly follow this man."

"I won't say no to that!" replied the harpooner.

At last he and Conseil left the cell where they had been shut up for more than thirty hours.

"And now, Professor Aronnax, our lunch is ready. Allow me to lead the way."

"At your service, Captain."

I followed Captain Nemo, and the moment I stepped through the door I entered a sort of corridor lit by electricity, like the gangways of a ship. After a walk of some ten meters, a second door opened before me.

I then entered a dining room, decorated and furnished in severe good taste. Tall oak sideboards, inlaid with ebony ornaments, rose at either end of the room, and on their shelves, cut in undulating lines, gleamed earthenware, porcelain, and glassware of inestimable value. The flat silver plate shone under rays poured down from a luminous ceiling, whose fine paintings softened and filtered the glare.

At the center of the room stood a table richly laid. Captain Nemo pointed out the place I was to occupy.

"Sit down," he said, "and eat like a man who must be starving."

The lunch consisted of a number of dishes whose contents the sea alone had supplied, and of a few whose nature and origin I could not guess. I will admit that it was good, though with a peculiar flavor that I grew used to easily. These various foods struck me as rich in phosphorus, and I concluded they must be of marine origin.

Captain Nemo watched me. I asked him nothing, but he read my thoughts and answered, of his own accord, the questions I was burning to put to him.

"Most of these dishes are unknown to you," he said. "Yet you may partake of them without fear. They are wholesome and nourishing. Long ago I gave up the foods of the land, and I am none the worse for it. My crew, who are a hardy lot, eat no differently than I do."

"So," I said, "all these foods are products of the sea?"

"Yes, Professor, the sea provides for all my needs. Sometimes I set my nets adrift and haul them back in, ready to burst. Sometimes I go hunting in the very midst of this element that seems beyond man's reach, and run down the game that lodges in my underwater forests. My flocks, like those of old Neptune's shepherd, graze the vast prairies of the ocean without fear. I have there a great estate that I work myself, and that is forever sown anew by the hand of the Creator of all things."

I looked at Captain Nemo with a certain astonishment, and answered him:

"I understand perfectly, sir, that your nets furnish excellent fish for your table; I understand less well that you should hunt aquatic game in your underwater forests; but I no longer understand at all how a scrap of meat, however small, could figure in your menu."

"And indeed, sir," Captain Nemo answered me, "I never make use of the flesh of land animals."

"This, however," I said, pointing to a dish where a few slices of fillet still remained.

"What you take to be meat, Professor, is nothing but fillet of sea turtle. Here, too, are some dolphin livers you would mistake for a pork stew. My cook is a skillful hand, who excels at preserving these varied products of the ocean. Taste all these dishes. Here is a preserve of sea cucumbers that a Malay would declare without rival in the world; here is a cream whose milk was furnished by the udders of whales, and its sugar by the great kelp of the North Sea; and finally, allow me to offer you some preserves of sea anemones, as good as those made from the most delicious fruits."

And I tasted, more out of curiosity than appetite, while Captain Nemo enchanted me with his improbable tales.

"But this sea, Professor Aronnax," he said to me, "this prodigious, inexhaustible nurse, does not merely feed me; it clothes me as well. The fabrics that cover you are woven from the byssus of certain shellfish; they are dyed with the purple of the ancients, shaded with violet tints that I extract from the sea hares of the Mediterranean. The perfumes you will find on the washstand of your cabin are the product of distilling marine plants. Your bed is made of the softest eelgrass of the ocean. Your pen will be a whale's baleen, your ink the liquor secreted by the cuttlefish or the squid. Everything now comes to me from the sea, as everything will one day return to it!"

"You love the sea, Captain."

"Yes! I love it! The sea is everything! It covers seven tenths of the globe. Its breath is pure and healthful. It is the vast desert where man is never alone, for he feels life quivering at his side. The sea is only the vehicle of a supernatural and prodigious existence; it is nothing but movement and love; it is the Living Infinite, as one of your poets put it. And indeed, Professor, nature reveals herself there through her three kingdoms—mineral, vegetable, animal. The last of these is broadly represented in it by the four groups of zoophytes, by three classes of articulates, by five classes of mollusks, by three classes of vertebrates—the mammals, the reptiles, and those countless legions of fish, an endless order of animals numbering more than thirteen thousand species, of which only a tenth belong to fresh water. The sea is the vast reservoir of nature. It was by the sea that the globe began, so to speak, and who knows but that it will end by it too! There lies supreme tranquility. The sea does not belong to despots. On its surface they may still exercise their iniquitous rights, do battle, devour one another, carry across it every terrestrial horror. But thirty feet below its surface, their power ceases, their influence dies out, their might disappears! Ah, sir—live, live in the bosom of the seas! There alone is independence! There I acknowledge no masters! There I am free!"

Captain Nemo fell silent all at once, in the midst of this enthusiasm that had come pouring out of him. Had he let himself be carried beyond his usual reserve? Had he said too much? For a few moments he paced back and forth, greatly agitated. Then his nerves grew calm, his face resumed its accustomed coldness, and, turning toward me:

"Now, Professor," he said, "if you wish to visit the Nautilus, I am at your service."
