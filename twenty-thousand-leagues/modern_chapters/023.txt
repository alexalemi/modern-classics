The Coral Kingdom

The next morning I woke with my head remarkably clear. To my great surprise, I was in my own room. My companions, no doubt, had been carried back to their cabin without noticing it any more than I had. What had happened during the night, they knew as little as I did myself, and to unravel that mystery I could count on nothing but the chances of the future.

Then I thought of leaving my room. Was I once more free, or still a prisoner? Entirely free. I opened the door, made my way along the passageways, and climbed the central staircase. The panels, shut the evening before, now stood open. I came out onto the platform.

Ned Land and Conseil were waiting for me there. I questioned them. They knew nothing. Sunk in a heavy sleep that had left them no memory at all, they had been very much astonished to find themselves back in their cabin.

As for the Nautilus, it seemed to us as calm and mysterious as ever. It floated on the surface of the waves at a moderate pace. Nothing on board appeared to have changed.

Ned Land, with those piercing eyes of his, studied the sea. It was empty. The Canadian could point to nothing new on the horizon — no sail, no land. A west wind blew loud and gusty, and long swells, their crests torn ragged by the wind, set the vessel rolling noticeably.

Once it had renewed its air, the Nautilus held to an average depth of fifteen meters, so as to be able to return quickly to the surface. This maneuver, contrary to habit, was carried out several times over the course of that day, the nineteenth of January. Each time, the first officer would climb up onto the platform, and the customary phrase would ring out through the interior of the ship.

As for Captain Nemo, he did not appear. Of the crew I saw no one but the impassive steward, who served me with his usual precision and his usual silence.

Toward two o'clock I was in the salon, busy organizing my notes, when the captain opened the door and came in. I greeted him. He returned an almost imperceptible nod, without a word to me. I went back to my work, hoping he might perhaps give me some explanation of the events that had marked the previous night. He did nothing of the sort. I watched him. His face struck me as exhausted; his reddened eyes had found no refreshment in sleep; his whole expression spoke of a deep sadness, a genuine grief. He paced back and forth, sat down and got up again, picked up a book at random and set it aside at once, consulted his instruments without taking his usual notes, and seemed unable to stay still for a single moment.

At last he came over to me and said:

"Are you a physician, Professor Aronnax?"

So little did I expect the question that I looked at him for some time without answering.

"Are you a physician?" he repeated. "Several of your colleagues studied medicine — Gratiolet, Moquin-Tandon, and others."

"That's true," I said. "I am a doctor; I served my residency in the hospitals. I practiced for several years before joining the Museum."

"Good, sir."

My answer had plainly satisfied Captain Nemo. But not knowing what he was driving at, I waited for further questions, meaning to answer as circumstances required.

"Professor Aronnax," the captain said, "would you consent to look after one of my men?"

"You have a sick man aboard?"

"Yes."

"I'm ready to follow you."

"Come."

I confess my heart was pounding. I don't know why I saw some connection between this man's illness and the events of the night before, and that mystery preoccupied me at least as much as the patient himself.

Captain Nemo led me to the after part of the Nautilus and showed me into a cabin next to the crew's quarters.

There, on a bed, lay a man of about forty, with strong features — a true Anglo-Saxon type.

I bent over him. This was not merely a sick man; this was a wounded one. His head, swathed in bloodstained cloths, rested on a doubled pillow. I unwound the bandages, and the wounded man, watching me with his wide, fixed eyes, let me do it without uttering a single complaint.

The wound was horrible. The skull, shattered by some blunt instrument, laid the brain bare, and the cerebral matter had suffered a deep bruising. Clots of blood had formed in the dissolving mass, which had taken on the color of wine dregs. There had been both contusion and concussion of the brain. The man's breathing was slow, and a few spasmodic twitches ran through the muscles of his face. Inflammation of the brain was complete, and it had brought on the paralysis of feeling and of movement.

I took the wounded man's pulse. It was intermittent. The extremities of the body were already growing cold, and I saw that death was coming on, without its seeming possible for me to hold it back. Having dressed the poor man's wound, I readjusted the bandages about his head and turned back to Captain Nemo.

"How did he get this wound?" I asked him.

"What does it matter?" the captain answered evasively. "A shock to the Nautilus broke one of the levers of the engine, and it struck this man. But your opinion of his condition?"

I hesitated to pronounce it.

"You may speak," the captain told me. "This man does not understand French."

I looked one last time at the wounded man, then answered:

"This man will be dead in two hours."

"Is there nothing that can save him?"

"Nothing."

Captain Nemo's hand clenched, and a few tears slid from those eyes I had not believed made for weeping.

For a few moments I went on watching the dying man, whose life was ebbing little by little. His pallor deepened still further under the electric glow that bathed his deathbed. I looked at that intelligent head, furrowed with premature wrinkles that misfortune, and perhaps misery, had carved there long ago. I searched for the secret of his life in the last words to escape his lips.

"You may withdraw, Professor Aronnax," said Captain Nemo.

I left the captain in the dying man's cabin and went back to my room, deeply shaken by the scene. All through the day I was troubled by grim forebodings. That night I slept badly, and between my frequently interrupted dreams I thought I heard distant sighs, like the chanting of a funeral dirge. Was it the prayer for the dead, murmured in that language I could not understand?

The next morning I went up on deck. Captain Nemo was there before me. As soon as he caught sight of me, he came over.

"Professor," he said, "would it suit you to make an underwater excursion today?"

"With my companions?" I asked.

"If they care to come."

"We are at your orders, Captain."

"Then be so good as to go and put on your diving suits."

Of the dying man, or the dead one, not a word was said. I rejoined Ned Land and Conseil and told them of Captain Nemo's proposal. Conseil hastened to accept, and this time the Canadian showed himself very willing to come along.

It was eight o'clock in the morning. By half past eight we were dressed for this new outing and fitted with the two devices for light and for breathing. The double door was opened, and, accompanied by Captain Nemo with a dozen crewmen behind him, we set foot at a depth of ten meters on the firm ground where the Nautilus rested.

A gentle slope led down to an uneven bottom some fifteen fathoms deep. This bottom was completely different from the one I had visited during my first excursion beneath the waters of the Pacific. Here there was no fine sand, no undersea meadows, no open-water forest. I recognized at once the marvelous region that Captain Nemo was that day doing us the honors of. It was the coral kingdom.

In the branch of the zoophytes and the class of the alcyonarians, one finds the order of the gorgonaceans, which contains the three groups of the gorgonians, the isidians, and the corallians. It is to this last that coral belongs — a curious substance that has been classified in turn among the mineral, vegetable, and animal kingdoms. A medicine to the ancients, a jewel to the moderns, it was not until 1694 that the Marseillais Peysonnel definitively assigned it to the animal kingdom.

Coral is a colony of tiny animals joined together on a brittle, stony framework called a polypary. These polyps have a single generating parent that produced them all by budding, and each possesses an existence of its own while sharing in the common life. It is, then, a kind of natural socialism. I was familiar with the latest work done on this strange zoophyte, which mineralizes even as it branches out like a tree — the naturalists' apt observation — and nothing could have been more fascinating to me than to visit one of these petrified forests that nature has planted at the bottom of the seas.

The Ruhmkorff lamps were switched on, and we followed a bank of coral still in the making, which, given time, will one day close off this whole portion of the Indian Ocean. The path was bordered by impenetrable thickets, tangles of shrubs covered with little star-shaped flowers whose rays were white. Only, contrary to the plants of dry land, these branchings, anchored to the rocks of the seafloor, all pointed downward from above.

The light produced a thousand charming effects as it played among these vividly colored boughs. I seemed to see those membranous, cylindrical tubes trembling under the undulation of the water. I was tempted to gather their fresh corollas, adorned with delicate tentacles, some newly opened, others scarcely born, while nimble little fish, quick-finned, brushed past them as they swam by, like flights of birds. But if my hand came near these living flowers, these animated sensitive plants, the alarm ran at once through the whole colony. The white corollas drew back into their red sheaths, the flowers vanished before my eyes, and the bush turned into a block of stony knobs.

Chance had set me there in the presence of the most precious specimens of this zoophyte. This coral was the equal of what is fished up in the Mediterranean, off the coasts of France, Italy, and Barbary. By its vivid tones it justified those poetic names the trade gives to its loveliest products — flower of blood, foam of blood. Coral sells for as much as five hundred francs a kilogram, and here the liquid depths covered the fortune of a whole world of coral-fishers. This precious material, often mingled with other polyparies, formed dense, inextricable masses called "macciota," on which I noticed some admirable specimens of pink coral.

But soon the bushes drew closer together, the tree-forms grew taller. Real petrified copses and long spans of fantastic architecture opened before our steps. Captain Nemo turned in under a dark gallery whose gentle downward slope led us to a depth of a hundred meters. The light from our glass coils produced magical effects at times, catching on the rough irregularities of these natural arches and on the hanging formations arranged like chandeliers, pricking them with points of fire. Among the coral shrubs I observed other polyps no less curious — melites, irises with jointed branches, then a few tufts of corallines, some green, some red, true algae encrusted in their calcareous salts, which the naturalists, after long debate, have definitively placed in the vegetable kingdom. But, as one thinker has remarked, "this is perhaps the very point where life obscurely lifts itself out of the sleep of stone, without yet detaching itself from that rough point of departure."

At last, after two hours of walking, we had reached a depth of about three hundred meters — that is, the extreme limit at which coral begins to form. But here it was no longer the isolated bush, nor the modest low-growing copse. It was the immense forest, the great mineral vegetation, the enormous petrified trees, joined by garlands of graceful plumarias — those vines of the sea — all decked out in shifting hues and reflections. We passed freely beneath their tall boughs, lost in the shadow of the waters, while at our feet the organ-pipe corals, the brain corals, the star corals, the mushroom corals, and the caryophylls formed a carpet of flowers strewn with dazzling gems.

What an indescribable spectacle! Ah, if only we could have shared what we felt! Why were we imprisoned under this mask of metal and glass? Why were words forbidden between one of us and another? If only we could have lived, at least, the life of those fish that people the liquid element — or better still, the life of those amphibious creatures that, for long hours at a stretch, can roam at their own whim through the double domain of the land and the waters!

Meanwhile Captain Nemo had come to a halt. My companions and I stopped our own march, and, turning around, I saw that his men were forming a semicircle about their chief. Looking more closely, I noticed that four of them carried on their shoulders an object of oblong shape.

We stood, at this spot, in the center of a vast clearing ringed by the tall tree-forms of the undersea forest. Our lamps cast over the space a kind of twilight glimmer that stretched the shadows out enormously across the ground. At the edge of the clearing the darkness closed in again, deep, and gathered only faint sparks caught here and there on the sharp ridges of the coral.

Ned Land and Conseil were beside me. We watched, and the thought came to me that I was about to witness a strange scene. Studying the ground, I saw that it was swollen at certain points into slight mounds crusted over with calcareous deposits, and laid out with a regularity that betrayed the hand of man.

In the middle of the clearing, on a pedestal of roughly heaped stones, rose a cross of coral, spreading its long arms that seemed made of petrified blood.

At a sign from Captain Nemo, one of his men stepped forward, and a few feet from the cross he began to dig a hole with a pick he unhooked from his belt.

I understood it all! This clearing was a cemetery, this hole a grave, that oblong object the body of the man who had died in the night! Captain Nemo and his people had come to bury their companion in this common resting place, at the bottom of that unreachable ocean!

No — never had my mind been so wrought upon! Never had more overwhelming ideas come flooding into my brain! I did not want to see what my eyes were seeing!

Meanwhile the grave was slowly deepening. Fish fled this way and that from their disturbed retreat. I could hear the iron of the pick ringing on the calcareous floor, striking sparks now and then as it met some flint lost at the bottom of the waters. The hole lengthened, widened, and soon it was deep enough to receive the body.

Then the bearers drew near. The body, wrapped in a cloth of white byssus, was lowered into its watery grave. Captain Nemo, his arms folded across his chest, and all the friends of the man who had loved them knelt in the attitude of prayer. My two companions and I had bowed reverently.

The grave was then covered over with the fragments torn from the ground, forming a slight rise above it.

When this was done, Captain Nemo and his men rose to their feet; then, drawing near the grave, all bent the knee once more, and all stretched out a hand in a sign of final farewell.

Then the funeral procession took the road back to the Nautilus, passing again beneath the arches of the forest, through the copses, along the coral bushes, and always climbing.

At last the ship's lights appeared. Their luminous trail guided us back to the Nautilus. By one o'clock we had returned.

As soon as I had changed my clothes, I went back up onto the platform, and, in the grip of a terrible obsession of thoughts, I sat down near the beacon.

Captain Nemo joined me. I rose and said to him:

"So, just as I foresaw, that man died in the night?"

"Yes, Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo answered.

"And he rests now beside his companions, in that coral cemetery?"

"Yes — forgotten by all, but not by us! We dig the grave, and the polyps take it upon themselves to seal our dead there for eternity!"

And burying his face abruptly in his clenched hands, the captain tried in vain to hold back a sob. Then he added:

"There lies our peaceful cemetery, a few hundred feet beneath the surface of the waves!"

"Your dead sleep there in peace, at least, Captain, beyond the reach of the sharks!"

"Yes, sir," Captain Nemo answered gravely, "beyond the reach of the sharks — and of men!"
