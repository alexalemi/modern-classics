A Few Days Ashore

Setting foot on land made a strong impression on me. Ned Land tested the ground with his foot, as though to take possession of it. Yet it had been only two months since we had become, in Captain Nemo's phrase, the "passengers of the Nautilus" — which is to say, in plain fact, the prisoners of her commander.

In a few minutes we were within gunshot of the shore. The soil was almost entirely made of coral, but certain beds of dried-up torrents, strewn with fragments of granite, showed that the island dated from some primordial formation. The whole horizon lay hidden behind a curtain of magnificent forest. Enormous trees, some of them reaching two hundred feet, were linked one to another by garlands of vines — real natural hammocks, rocked by a light breeze. There were mimosas, ficus, casuarinas, teaks, hibiscus, pandanus, palms, all mingled in profusion, and beneath the shelter of their green vault, at the foot of their giant trunks, grew orchids, legumes, and ferns.

But without so much as a glance at these fine specimens of Papuan flora, the Canadian abandoned the pleasant for the useful. He spotted a coconut palm, knocked down a few of its fruit, split them open, and we drank their milk and ate their meat with a satisfaction that was a standing protest against the ordinary fare of the Nautilus.

"Excellent!" said Ned Land.

"Exquisite!" answered Conseil.

"And I don't imagine," said the Canadian, "that your Nemo would object to our bringing a cargo of coconuts aboard his ship?"

"I don't suppose he would," I replied, "but he'll never want to taste them!"

"His loss!" said Conseil.

"And our gain!" Ned Land shot back. "That leaves more for us."

"Just one word, Master Land," I said to the harpooner, who was getting ready to lay waste to another coconut palm. "Coconuts are a fine thing, but before we fill the dinghy with them, it seems sensible to find out whether the island doesn't produce some other substance just as useful. Fresh vegetables would be very welcome in the Nautilus's pantry."

"Master is right," answered Conseil, "and I propose we set aside three spaces in our boat — one for fruit, one for vegetables, and the third for game, of which I haven't yet glimpsed the slightest specimen."

"Conseil, we mustn't despair of anything," answered the Canadian.

"Let's press on with our excursion, then," I said, "but let's keep our eyes open. The island may look uninhabited, but it might still harbor a few individuals who'd be less particular than we are about the kind of game they'll eat!"

"Ho! ho!" went Ned Land, with a very meaningful working of his jaw.

"Really, Ned!" cried Conseil.

"On my word," the Canadian shot back, "I'm beginning to understand the charms of cannibalism!"

"Ned! Ned! What are you saying!" retorted Conseil. "You, a cannibal! Why, I'll never be safe near you again, and I share your cabin! Am I to wake up one morning half devoured?"

"Conseil, my friend, I'm very fond of you, but not fond enough to eat you when there's no need for it."

"I don't trust it," answered Conseil. "To the hunt! We absolutely must bring down some game to satisfy this cannibal, or one of these mornings Master will find nothing left but a few scraps of servant to wait on him."

While this banter passed back and forth, we made our way beneath the dark vaults of the forest, and for two hours we roamed it in every direction.

Chance served this search for edible plants exactly as we could have wished, and one of the most useful products of the tropical zones furnished us with a precious foodstuff that was missing aboard ship. I mean the breadfruit tree, very plentiful on Gueboroar Island, and I noticed there above all that seedless variety which in Malay goes by the name of "rima."

This tree stood apart from the others by a straight trunk forty feet high. Its crown, gracefully rounded and formed of large many-lobed leaves, marked it out plainly enough to a naturalist's eye as that artocarpus which has been so happily naturalized in the Mascarene Islands. Out of its mass of greenery hung large globular fruit, a decimeter across, their outer skin covered with roughnesses arranged in a hexagonal pattern — a useful plant with which nature has favored those regions that lack wheat, and which, requiring no cultivation, bears fruit for eight months of the year.

Ned Land knew these fruit well. He had already eaten them on his many voyages, and he knew how to prepare their edible flesh. So the sight of them fired his appetite, and he could hold out no longer.

"Master," he said to me, "let me die if I don't get a taste of this breadfruit paste!"

"Taste away, Ned, my friend, taste to your heart's content. We're here to run experiments — let's run them."

"It won't take long," answered the Canadian.

And, armed with a lens, he lit a fire of dead wood that crackled away merrily. Meanwhile Conseil and I picked out the best of the artocarpus fruit. Some had not yet reached a sufficient degree of ripeness, and their thick skin covered a white pulp that was not very fibrous. Others, in great number, yellowish and gelatinous, were only waiting to be gathered.

These fruit contained no pit. Conseil brought a dozen of them to Ned Land, who cut them into thick slices and laid them on a bed of coals, all the while repeating:

"You'll see, Master, how good this bread is!"

"Especially when you've gone without it for so long," said Conseil.

"It isn't even bread anymore," added the Canadian. "It's a delicate pastry. You've never eaten it, Master?"

"No, Ned."

"Well then, get ready to take in something truly succulent. If you don't come back for more, my name's not the king of harpooners!"

After a few minutes, the part of the fruit exposed to the fire was completely charred. Inside, a white paste had appeared, a sort of tender crumb whose flavor recalled that of an artichoke.

I have to admit, this bread was excellent, and I ate it with great pleasure.

"Unfortunately," I said, "such a paste won't keep fresh, so it seems pointless to lay in a store of it for the ship."

"Come now, Master!" cried Ned Land. "There you speak like a naturalist, but I'm going to act like a baker. Conseil, gather up a harvest of these fruit that we'll take back on our way home."

"And how will you prepare them?" I asked the Canadian.

"By making a fermented paste out of their pulp that will keep indefinitely without spoiling. When I want to use it, I'll bake it in the galley aboard ship, and in spite of a slightly sour flavor, you'll find it excellent."

"Then, Master Ned, I see there's nothing lacking in this bread..."

"There is, Professor," answered the Canadian. "It lacks a bit of fruit, or at the very least a few vegetables! Let's go find the fruit and the vegetables."

Once our harvest was finished, we set out to complete this "terrestrial" dinner.

Our searches were not in vain, and by around midday we had laid in an ample supply of bananas. These delicious products of the torrid zone ripen all year round, and the Malays, who have given them the name "pisang," eat them without cooking. Along with the bananas, we gathered enormous jackfruit with a very pronounced taste, savory mangoes, and pineapples of unbelievable size. But this harvest took up a large part of our time — which, for that matter, there was no cause to regret.

Conseil kept his eye on Ned the whole while. The harpooner walked ahead, and as he strolled through the forest, he gleaned with a sure hand excellent fruit that were meant to round out his stores.

"So then," Conseil asked, "you're not missing anything now, Ned my friend?"

"Hmph!" went the Canadian.

"What! You're complaining?"

"All this greenery can't make up a meal," answered Ned. "It's the end of a meal, it's dessert. But the soup? The roast?"

"Indeed," I said, "Ned promised us cutlets that are looking rather doubtful to me."

"Professor," answered the Canadian, "the hunt isn't just unfinished — it hasn't even begun. Patience! We'll manage to come across some creature of feather or fur sooner or later, and if not in this spot, then in another..."

"And if not today, then tomorrow," added Conseil, "for we mustn't wander too far. I'd even propose that we head back to the dinghy."

"What! Already!" cried Ned.

"We ought to be back before nightfall," I said.

"But what time is it, then?" asked the Canadian.

"Two o'clock, at least," answered Conseil.

"How time flies on solid ground!" sighed Master Ned Land with a note of regret.

"Let's move," answered Conseil.

So we made our way back through the forest, rounding out our harvest with a raid on some palm cabbages that had to be gathered from the tops of the trees, some small beans I recognized as the Malays' "abrou," and some yams of superior quality.

We were loaded down when we reached the dinghy. Even so, Ned Land didn't consider his supply sufficient yet. But luck was on his side. Just as we were about to board, he spotted several trees, twenty-five to thirty feet tall, belonging to the palm family. These trees, as valuable as the artocarpus, are rightly counted among the most useful products of Malaysia.

They were sago palms, plants that grow without cultivation, propagating themselves, like mulberry trees, through their shoots and their seeds.

Ned Land knew the way to handle these trees. He took up his axe, and wielding it with great vigor, he soon had two or three sago palms lying on the ground, their ripeness betrayed by the white dust that powdered their fronds.

I watched him work more with the eyes of a naturalist than with the eyes of a hungry man. He began by stripping from each trunk a band of bark an inch thick, which covered a network of long fibers knotted in an inextricable tangle, held together by a sort of gummy flour. This flour was sago, an edible substance that serves above all to feed the populations of Melanesia.

For the moment Ned Land was content to cut these trunks into pieces, as he would have done with firewood, saving for later the job of extracting the flour, straining it through a cloth to separate it from its fibrous ligaments, evaporating its moisture in the sun, and letting it harden in molds.

At last, at five in the evening, loaded down with all our riches, we left the shore of the island, and half an hour later we drew alongside the Nautilus. No one appeared at our arrival. The enormous cylinder of sheet iron seemed deserted. Once the provisions were aboard, I went down to my cabin. I found my supper waiting for me. I ate, and then I fell asleep.

The next day, January 6, nothing new aboard. Not a sound inside, not a sign of life. The dinghy had stayed alongside, in the very spot where we had left it. We resolved to return to Gueboroar Island. Ned Land hoped to have better luck than the day before from a hunter's point of view, and wanted to explore another part of the forest.

At sunrise we were on our way. The boat, borne along by the tide that ran toward land, reached the island in no time.

We landed, and thinking it best to trust the Canadian's instinct, we followed Ned Land, whose long legs threatened to leave us behind.

Ned Land worked his way up the coast toward the west, then, wading across a few torrent beds, he reached the high plain bordered by admirable forests. A few kingfishers prowled along the watercourses, but they wouldn't let us come near. Their wariness proved to me that these birds knew what to expect from bipeds of our kind, and I concluded that, even if the island wasn't inhabited, at least human beings frequented it.

After crossing a fairly lush meadow, we came to the edge of a small wood alive with the song and flight of a great many birds.

"They're still only birds," said Conseil.

"But some of them are edible!" answered the harpooner.

"Not so, Ned my friend," retorted Conseil, "for I see nothing there but ordinary parrots."

"Conseil, my friend," Ned answered gravely, "the parrot is the pheasant of a man with nothing else to eat."

"And I'll add," I said, "that this bird, properly prepared, is well worth the fork's trouble."

And in fact, beneath the thick foliage of that wood, a whole world of parrots fluttered from branch to branch, wanting only a more careful education to speak the language of men. For the moment they chattered away in the company of parakeets of every color, of solemn cockatoos that seemed to be meditating on some philosophical problem, while lories of a brilliant red flashed past like scraps of bunting carried off by the breeze, amid loud-flying hornbills, Papuan parrots painted in the finest shades of azure, and a whole variety of charming birds, though most of them barely fit to eat.

Yet one bird peculiar to these lands, one that has never crossed beyond the limits of the Aru Islands and the Papuan islands, was missing from this collection. But fate had it in store for me to admire it before long.

After passing through a copse of no great thickness, we came again upon a plain choked with brush. There I saw rising into the air some magnificent birds whose long feathers were arranged so as to oblige them to fly against the wind. Their undulating flight, the grace of their aerial curves, the shimmer of their colors, drew and charmed the eye. I had no trouble recognizing them.

"Birds of paradise!" I cried.

"Order of passerines, section of clystomores," answered Conseil.

"Family of partridges?" asked Ned Land.

"I don't think so, Master Land. Still, I'm counting on your skill to catch one of these charming products of tropical nature!"

"We'll give it a try, Professor, though I'm more used to handling the harpoon than the gun."

The Malays, who carry on a great trade in these birds with the Chinese, have various means of taking them that we couldn't make use of. Sometimes they set snares at the tops of the tall trees the birds of paradise prefer to live in. Sometimes they capture them with a sticky birdlime that paralyzes their movements. They even go so far as to poison the springs where these birds are in the habit of drinking. As for us, we were reduced to shooting them on the wing, which left us little chance of hitting them. And in fact we wasted a good part of our ammunition to no purpose.

Toward eleven in the morning we had crossed the first range of mountains that form the center of the island, and we still hadn't killed a thing. Hunger spurred us on. The hunters had counted on the yield of their hunt, and they'd been wrong. Very luckily, Conseil, to his own great surprise, brought off a double shot and secured our lunch. He knocked down a white pigeon and a wood pigeon, which, briskly plucked and hung on a skewer, roasted before a blazing fire of dead wood. While these interesting creatures cooked, Ned prepared some artocarpus fruit. Then the pigeon and the wood pigeon were devoured down to the bone and declared excellent. The nutmeg they habitually gorge on scents their flesh and makes it delicious eating.

"It's as if the fatted hens fed on truffles," said Conseil.

"And now, Ned, what are you still missing?" I asked the Canadian.

"Some four-legged game, Professor," answered Ned Land. "All these pigeons are nothing but appetizers and little amusements for the mouth. So until I've killed an animal with cutlets on it, I won't be satisfied!"

"Nor I, Ned, if I don't catch a bird of paradise."

"Let's keep hunting, then," answered Conseil, "but working our way back toward the sea. We've reached the first slopes of the mountains, and I think it's better to regain the region of the forests."

That was sensible advice, and it was followed. After an hour's march we had reached a veritable forest of sago palms. A few harmless snakes fled from beneath our feet. The birds of paradise slipped away at our approach, and I was truly despairing of ever reaching them, when Conseil, who was walking ahead, suddenly stooped, let out a cry of triumph, and came back to me carrying a magnificent bird of paradise.

"Ah! Bravo, Conseil!" I cried.

"Master is very kind," answered Conseil.

"No, no, my boy. You've pulled off a masterstroke. To take one of these birds alive, and to take it by hand!"

"If Master will examine it closely, he'll see that I deserve no great credit."

"And why is that, Conseil?"

"Because this bird is as drunk as a quail."

"Drunk?"

"Yes, Master, drunk on the nutmegs it was gobbling under the nutmeg tree where I caught it. Look, Ned my friend, look at the monstrous effects of intemperance!"

"A thousand devils!" the Canadian shot back. "For the little gin I've drunk these past two months, you've no call to hold it against me!"

Meanwhile I was examining the curious bird. Conseil was not mistaken. The bird of paradise, intoxicated by the heady juice, was reduced to helplessness. It couldn't fly. It could barely walk. But that worried me little, and I let it sleep off its nutmegs.

This bird belonged to the most beautiful of the eight species found in Papua and the neighboring islands. It was the "great emerald" bird of paradise, one of the rarest. It measured three decimeters in length. Its head was relatively small, its eyes, set near the opening of the beak, small as well. But it offered an admirable assembly of shades — beak yellow, feet and claws brown, wings hazel with crimson tips, head and back of the neck pale yellow, throat emerald, belly and breast maroon. Two horny, downy filaments rose above its tail, which was drawn out by long, very light feathers of admirable delicacy, and they completed the ensemble of this marvelous bird, which the natives have poetically named the "sun bird."

I longed dearly to be able to bring back to Paris this superb specimen of the birds of paradise, to make a gift of it to the Jardin des Plantes, which does not possess a single living one.

"Is it really so rare, then?" asked the Canadian, in the tone of a hunter who rates game very low from the artistic point of view.

"Very rare, my good companion, and above all very hard to take alive. Even dead, these birds are still the object of an important trade. So the natives have hit on the idea of manufacturing them, the way one manufactures pearls or diamonds."

"What!" cried Conseil. "People make fake birds of paradise?"

"Yes, Conseil."

"And does Master know the natives' method?"

"Perfectly. During the east monsoon, the birds of paradise shed those magnificent feathers that surround their tail, which naturalists have named the subalar feathers. It's these feathers that the counterfeiters in birds collect, and cleverly fit onto some poor parakeet previously mutilated for the purpose. Then they dye the seam, they varnish the bird, and they ship off to the museums and collectors of Europe these products of their peculiar industry."

"Good!" said Ned Land. "If it isn't the bird, it's still its feathers, and as long as the object isn't meant to be eaten, I don't see much harm in it!"

But if my own desires were satisfied by possession of this bird of paradise, those of the Canadian hunter were not yet. Luckily, toward two o'clock, Ned Land brought down a magnificent forest hog, of the kind the natives call "bari-outang." The animal came just in time to supply us with real quadruped meat, and it was warmly welcomed. Ned Land was very proud of his shot. The hog, struck by the electric bullet, had dropped stone dead.

The Canadian skinned it and gutted it cleanly, after taking out half a dozen cutlets meant to furnish a grill for the evening meal. Then the hunt resumed, and it was to be marked yet again by the exploits of Ned and Conseil.

For in fact the two friends, beating the bushes, flushed a troop of kangaroos, which fled off bounding on their elastic legs. But these animals didn't flee so fast that the electric charge couldn't stop them in their course.

"Ah, Professor!" cried Ned Land, seized with a hunter's frenzy, "what excellent game, stewed above all! What a stock of provisions for the Nautilus! Two! Three! Five down! And to think we'll devour all this meat, and those fools aboard won't get a scrap of it!"

I believe that, in his excess of joy, if the Canadian hadn't talked so much, he'd have massacred the whole herd! But he contented himself with a dozen of these interesting marsupials, which form the first order of the aplacental mammals — so Conseil informed us.

These animals were small in size. It was one of those "kangaroo-rabbit" species that usually den in the hollows of trees, and whose speed is extreme; but if they're only middling in size, they furnish, at least, the most prized meat.

We were very pleased with the results of our hunt. Merry Ned proposed to return the next day to this enchanted island, which he meant to strip of every one of its edible quadrupeds. But he was reckoning without the events to come.

At six in the evening we had regained the beach. Our dinghy was aground in its usual spot. The Nautilus, like a long reef, emerged from the waves two miles off the shore.

Ned Land, without further delay, busied himself with the great affair of dinner. He understood this whole business of cookery admirably. The "bari-outang" cutlets, grilled over the coals, soon spread a delicious smell that perfumed the air!...

But I notice I'm following in the Canadian's footsteps. Here I am, in raptures over a grill of fresh pork! Let me be forgiven, as I forgave Master Land, and for the same reasons!

At last the dinner was excellent. Two wood pigeons rounded out this extraordinary menu. The sago paste, the artocarpus bread, a few mangoes, half a dozen pineapples, and the fermented liquor of certain coconuts put us in high spirits. I even suspect that the wits of my worthy companions were not as clear as one might have wished.

"What if we didn't go back to the Nautilus tonight?" said Conseil.

"What if we never went back?" added Ned Land.

At that moment a stone came falling at our feet and cut short the harpooner's proposal.
