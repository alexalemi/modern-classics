Captain Nemo's Thunderbolt

We had been looking toward the forest, without getting up, my hand frozen halfway to my mouth, Ned Land's completing its errand.

"A stone doesn't fall out of the sky," said Conseil, "unless it has earned the name of a meteorite."

A second stone, carefully rounded, which knocked a savory pigeon thigh clean out of Conseil's hand, lent still more weight to his observation.

All three of us on our feet, rifles to our shoulders, we were ready to answer any attack.

"Are those monkeys?" cried Ned Land.

"More or less," answered Conseil. "They're savages."

"To the boat!" I said, heading for the sea.

Retreat was the only choice, for a good twenty natives, armed with bows and slings, were emerging at the edge of a thicket that hid the horizon to our right, barely a hundred paces off.

Our dinghy lay grounded about twenty meters from us.

The savages came on, not running, but making the most hostile displays imaginable. Stones and arrows rained down.

Ned Land had refused to leave his provisions behind, and despite the imminent danger, his pig on one side, his kangaroos on the other, he made off at a fair clip.

In two minutes we were on the beach. Loading the dinghy with the provisions and weapons, shoving it into the sea, setting the two oars — all of it was the work of an instant. We hadn't gained two cable-lengths when a hundred savages, howling and gesturing, waded into the water up to their waists. I watched to see whether their appearance would draw a few of the Nautilus's men onto the platform. But no. The enormous machine, lying out in the deeper water, remained utterly deserted.

Twenty minutes later we were climbing aboard. The hatches were open. After making the dinghy fast, we went back inside the Nautilus.

I went down to the salon, from which a few chords were drifting. Captain Nemo was there, bent over his organ, lost in a musical trance.

"Captain!" I said to him.

He didn't hear me.

"Captain!" I said again, touching him with my hand.

He shuddered, and turning around:

"Ah! it's you, Professor?" he said to me. "Well! did you have good hunting? did you botanize with success?"

"Yes, Captain," I answered, "but unfortunately we've brought back a troop of bipeds whose nearness strikes me as troubling."

"What bipeds?"

"Savages."

"Savages!" answered Captain Nemo in an ironic tone. "And you're surprised, Professor, that having set foot on one of the lands of this globe, you should find savages there? Savages — where are there not any? And besides, are they any worse than the others, these people you call savages?"

"But, Captain—"

"For my part, sir, I've met them everywhere."

"Well," I answered, "if you'd rather not receive any aboard the Nautilus, you'd do well to take a few precautions."

"Set your mind at ease, Professor, there's nothing here to worry about."

"But these natives are numerous."

"How many did you count?"

"A hundred, at least."

"Professor Aronnax," answered Captain Nemo, his fingers settling back onto the keys of the organ, "if every last one of the natives of Papua were gathered on that beach, the Nautilus would have nothing to fear from their attacks!"

The captain's fingers were running over the keyboard now, and I noticed that he struck only the black keys, which gave his melodies an essentially Scottish color. Soon he had forgotten I was there, sunk in a reverie I no longer tried to break.

I went back up onto the platform. Night had already fallen, for at that low latitude the sun sets quickly and without any twilight. I could no longer make out Gueboroar Island except as a blur. But numerous fires, lit along the beach, showed that the natives had no thought of leaving it.

I stayed there alone for several hours, now musing on these natives — but without any real fear of them, for the captain's imperturbable confidence was catching — now forgetting them altogether to admire the splendors of this tropical night. My memory flew off toward France, following those zodiacal stars that would be shining over it in a few hours. The moon blazed among the constellations at the zenith. I thought then that this faithful and obliging satellite would come back the day after tomorrow, to this same spot, to lift these waters and tear the Nautilus free of its bed of coral. Toward midnight, seeing that all was quiet on the darkened waves as well as beneath the trees along the shore, I returned to my cabin and fell peacefully asleep.

The night passed without mishap. The Papuans were no doubt frightened off by the mere sight of the monster stranded in the bay, for the hatches, left open, would have offered them easy access to the interior of the Nautilus.

At six in the morning — January 8 — I went back up onto the platform. The shadows of dawn were lifting. Soon the island showed itself through the scattering mists — its beaches first, then its summits.

The natives were still there, more numerous than the day before — five or six hundred, perhaps. A few, taking advantage of the low tide, had come out onto the heads of coral, less than two cable-lengths from the Nautilus. I could make them out easily. They were true Papuans, of athletic build, men of a fine race, with broad, high foreheads, big but not flattened noses, and white teeth. Their woolly hair, dyed red, stood out against a body black and glistening like that of the Nubians. From the lobes of their ears, cut and stretched, hung strings of bone beads. These men were generally naked. Among them I noticed a few women, dressed from hip to knee in a true crinoline of grasses held up by a plant-fiber belt. Some of the chiefs had adorned their necks with a crescent and with necklaces of red and white glass beads. Almost all of them, armed with bows, arrows, and shields, carried at their shoulder a kind of net holding those rounded stones that their slings launch with such skill.

One of these chiefs, come fairly close to the Nautilus, was examining it with attention. He must have been a "mado" of high rank, for he draped himself in a mat of banana leaves, notched at the edges and set off with brilliant colors.

I could easily have brought this native down, standing as he was within short range; but I thought it better to wait for genuinely hostile moves. Between Europeans and savages, it is only right that the Europeans answer an attack and never start one.

All through the low tide these natives prowled near the Nautilus, but they did not grow loud. I heard them often repeat the word "assai," and from their gestures I understood that they were inviting me to come ashore — an invitation I felt I ought to decline.

So that day the dinghy did not leave the ship, much to the displeasure of Master Land, who could not complete his provisions. The resourceful Canadian spent his time preparing the meats and flours he had brought back from Gueboroar Island. As for the savages, they returned to shore around eleven in the morning, as soon as the heads of coral began to disappear under the rising tide. But I saw their numbers swell considerably on the beach. It seemed likely they were coming from the neighboring islands, or from Papua proper. Still, I had not glimpsed a single native canoe.

Having nothing better to do, I thought of dredging these beautiful clear waters, which showed shells, zoophytes, and sea plants in abundance. This was, moreover, the last day the Nautilus would spend in these waters — provided, that is, that it floated free on tomorrow's high tide, according to Captain Nemo's promise.

So I called Conseil, who brought me a small light dredge, rather like the ones used to fish for oysters.

"And those savages?" Conseil asked me. "With all respect to Master, they don't strike me as very wicked!"

"They're cannibals all the same, my boy."

"One can be a cannibal and a decent fellow," answered Conseil, "just as one can be a glutton and an honest man. The one doesn't rule out the other."

"Fine, Conseil, I grant you they're honest cannibals, and that they devour their prisoners honestly. But since I have no wish to be devoured, even honestly, I'll keep on my guard, for the commander of the Nautilus seems to be taking no precautions at all. And now, to work."

For two hours our dredging was actively carried on, but without bringing up anything rare. The dredge filled with Midas' ears, harp shells, melania snails, and in particular the finest hammer shells I had seen up to that day. We also took a few sea cucumbers, some pearl oysters, and a dozen little turtles that were set aside for the ship's larder.

But just when I was least expecting it, I laid my hand on a marvel — I should say on a natural deformity, very rare to come upon. Conseil had just cast the dredge, and his apparatus was coming back up loaded with various fairly ordinary shells, when all at once he saw me plunge my arm quickly into the net, draw out a shell, and let loose the cry of a conchologist — that is, the most piercing cry a human throat can produce.

"Eh! what's the matter with Master?" asked Conseil, very surprised. "Has Master been bitten?"

"No, my boy, and yet I would gladly have paid for my discovery with a finger!"

"What discovery?"

"This shell," I said, holding up the object of my triumph.

"But that's simply a porphyry olive, of the genus Oliva, order Pectinibranchia, class Gastropoda, phylum Mollusca—"

"Yes, Conseil, but instead of coiling from right to left, this olive turns from left to right!"

"Is it possible!" cried Conseil.

"Yes, my boy, it's a left-handed shell!"

"A left-handed shell!" repeated Conseil, his heart pounding.

"Look at its spire!"

"Ah! Master may believe me," said Conseil, taking the precious shell in a trembling hand, "but I've never felt an emotion like it!"

And there was reason to be moved! Everyone knows, as the naturalists have pointed out, that right-handedness is a law of nature. The stars and their satellites, in their movements of revolution and rotation, move from right to left. Man uses his right hand more often than his left, and consequently his tools and instruments, stairways, locks, watch springs, and so on, are designed to be worked from right to left. Now, nature has generally followed this same law for the coiling of her shells. They are all right-handed, with rare exceptions, and when, by some chance, their spire runs left-handed, collectors will pay for them their weight in gold.

Conseil and I were thus lost in the contemplation of our treasure, and I was already promising myself to enrich the Museum with it, when a stone, unluckily flung by a native, came and shattered the precious object in Conseil's hand.

I let out a cry of despair! Conseil threw himself on my rifle and took aim at a savage who was swinging his sling ten meters from him. I tried to stop him, but his shot went off and broke the bracelet of amulets that hung from the native's arm.

"Conseil!" I cried, "Conseil!"

"What of it! Doesn't Master see that this cannibal started the attack?"

"A shell isn't worth a man's life!" I told him.

"Ah! the wretch!" cried Conseil. "I'd sooner he had broken my shoulder!"

Conseil was sincere, but I didn't share his view. Meanwhile the situation had changed in the last few moments, and we hadn't noticed. A good twenty canoes now surrounded the Nautilus. These canoes, hollowed out of tree trunks, long, narrow, well built for speed, were kept steady by a double bamboo outrigger that floated on the surface of the water. They were handled by skillful half-naked paddlers, and I did not watch them advance without misgivings.

It was plain that these Papuans had already had dealings with Europeans, and that they knew their ships. But this long cylinder of iron stretched out in the bay, without masts, without a funnel — what were they to make of it? Nothing good, for at first they had kept a respectful distance. But seeing it motionless, they were little by little regaining confidence and trying to grow familiar with it. Now it was precisely this familiarity that had to be prevented. Our weapons, which fired without any report, could have only a middling effect on these natives, who respect nothing but noisy contrivances. Lightning without the rolling of thunder would frighten men very little, though the danger lies in the flash, not in the sound.

At that moment the canoes drew closer to the Nautilus, and a cloud of arrows came down on it.

"The devil! it's hailing," said Conseil, "and maybe a poisoned hail!"

"We must warn Captain Nemo," I said, going back in through the hatch.

I went down to the salon. I found no one there. I ventured to knock at the door that opened onto the captain's stateroom.

An "Enter" answered me. I went in, and found Captain Nemo absorbed in a calculation in which x's and other algebraic signs were not lacking.

"Am I disturbing you?" I said, out of politeness.

"Indeed you are, Professor Aronnax," the captain answered me, "but I imagine you've had serious reasons for wanting to see me?"

"Very serious ones. The natives' canoes have surrounded us, and in a few minutes we'll certainly be assailed by several hundred savages."

"Ah!" said Captain Nemo calmly. "They've come in their canoes?"

"Yes, sir."

"Well, sir, then all we need do is close the hatches."

"Precisely, and I came to tell you—"

"Nothing simpler," said Captain Nemo.

And, pressing an electric button, he transmitted an order to the crew's quarters.

"There, it's done, sir," he said to me after a few moments. "The dinghy is in place, and the hatches are closed. You don't fear, I suppose, that these gentlemen will stave in walls that the shells of your frigate couldn't dent?"

"No, Captain, but there's still one danger."

"What danger, sir?"

"It's that tomorrow, at about this same hour, we'll have to reopen the hatches to renew the air of the Nautilus—"

"No doubt about it, sir, since our vessel breathes after the manner of the cetaceans."

"Now, if at that moment the Papuans are occupying the platform, I don't see how you'll be able to keep them from coming in."

"Then, sir, you suppose they'll climb aboard?"

"I'm certain of it."

"Well, sir, let them climb aboard. I see no reason to prevent them. At bottom they're poor devils, these Papuans, and I don't want my visit to Gueboroar Island to cost the life of a single one of these unfortunate people!"

That said, I was about to withdraw; but Captain Nemo held me back and invited me to sit down beside him. He questioned me with interest about our excursions ashore, about our hunting, and seemed not to understand that craving for meat which so consumed the Canadian. Then the conversation touched on various subjects, and without being any more communicative, Captain Nemo showed himself more amiable.

Among other things, we came to speak of the Nautilus's situation, grounded as it was precisely in this strait where Dumont d'Urville had nearly been lost. Then, on that subject:

"He was one of your great seamen," the captain said to me, "one of your most intelligent navigators, that d'Urville! He's your Captain Cook, you Frenchmen. Poor, ill-fated man of science! To have braved the ice fields of the South Pole, the coral reefs of Oceania, the cannibals of the Pacific, only to perish miserably in a railway train! If that energetic man was able to reflect during the last seconds of his life, imagine what his final thoughts must have been!"

Speaking this way, Captain Nemo seemed moved, and I credit him for that emotion.

Then, chart in hand, we went back over the French navigator's labors, his voyages of circumnavigation, his twofold attempt at the South Pole that led to the discovery of the Adélie and Louis-Philippe lands, and finally his hydrographic surveys of the principal islands of Oceania.

"What your d'Urville did on the surface of the seas," Captain Nemo said to me, "I have done inside the Ocean, and more easily, more completely than he. The Astrolabe and the Zélée, tossed endlessly by the storms, could never be worth the Nautilus — a quiet study, and truly settled, in the midst of the waters!"

"Still, Captain," I said, "there's one point of resemblance between Dumont d'Urville's corvettes and the Nautilus."

"What point, sir?"

"It's that the Nautilus has run aground just as they did!"

"The Nautilus has not run aground, sir," Captain Nemo answered me coldly. "The Nautilus is made to rest on the bed of the seas, and the painful labors, the maneuvers that refloating his corvettes forced on d'Urville, I shall not undertake. The Astrolabe and the Zélée nearly perished, but my Nautilus is in no danger. Tomorrow, on the day appointed, at the hour appointed, the tide will lift it peacefully, and it will resume its voyage across the seas."

"Captain," I said, "I don't doubt—"

"Tomorrow," added Captain Nemo, rising, "tomorrow, at two forty in the afternoon, the Nautilus will float free and leave the Torres Strait without any damage."

These words spoken in a very clipped tone, Captain Nemo bowed slightly. This was his way of dismissing me, and I went back to my room.

There I found Conseil, who was eager to know the outcome of my interview with the captain.

"My boy," I answered, "when I made a show of believing that his Nautilus was threatened by the natives of Papua, the captain answered me very ironically. So I have only one thing to say: trust him, and go sleep in peace."

"Master has no need of my services?"

"No, my friend. What is Ned Land doing?"

"Begging Master's pardon," answered Conseil, "but friend Ned is cooking up a kangaroo pâté that will be a wonder!"

I was left alone; I lay down, but I slept rather badly. I could hear the noise of the savages, who were stamping on the platform and letting out deafening cries. The night passed like that, without the crew ever stirring from its usual inertia. It worried no more about the presence of these cannibals than the soldiers of an armored fort worry about the ants running over its plating.

At six in the morning I got up. The hatches had not been opened. So the air had not been renewed inside, but the tanks, charged against just such an occasion, worked in good time and released a few cubic meters of oxygen into the impoverished atmosphere of the Nautilus.

I worked in my room until noon, without having seen Captain Nemo even for a moment. No preparations for departure seemed to be under way aboard.

I waited a while longer, then made my way to the grand salon. The clock read half past two. In ten minutes the tide would reach its maximum height, and, if Captain Nemo had not made a rash promise, the Nautilus would be freed at once. Otherwise, many months would pass before it could leave its bed of coral.

But some warning shudders soon made themselves felt in the vessel's hull. I heard the calcareous roughness of the coral bottom grating along her plating.

At thirty-five minutes past two, Captain Nemo appeared in the salon.

"We're about to leave," he said.

"Ah!" I said.

"I've given the order to open the hatches."

"And the Papuans?"

"The Papuans?" answered Captain Nemo, giving a slight shrug of his shoulders.

"Won't they get inside the Nautilus?"

"And how?"

"By climbing through the hatches you've had opened."

"Professor Aronnax," Captain Nemo answered calmly, "one does not enter through the hatches of the Nautilus that way, even when they are open."

I looked at the captain.

"You don't understand?" he said to me.

"Not in the least."

"Well then! come and you'll see."

I made for the central stairway. There Ned Land and Conseil, very intrigued, were watching a few of the crew open the hatches, while cries of rage and appalling howls resounded outside.

The covers were thrown back on the outside. Twenty hideous faces appeared. But the first of these natives to lay a hand on the stair rail was flung backward by some invisible force I couldn't name, and fled, letting out horrible cries and leaping about wildly.

Ten of his companions came after him. Ten met the same fate.

Conseil was in ecstasy. Ned Land, carried away by his violent instincts, rushed onto the stairway. But the moment he seized the rail with both hands, he too was thrown down.

"A thousand devils!" he cried. "I've been struck by lightning!"

That word explained everything to me. It was no longer a rail, but a metal cable, fully charged with the ship's electricity, running up to the platform. Anyone who touched it felt a tremendous shock — and that shock would have been fatal, had Captain Nemo sent the whole current of his apparatus into this conductor! One could truly say that between himself and his attackers he had stretched an electric net that no one could cross unharmed.

Meanwhile the terrified Papuans had beaten a retreat, wild with fear. And we, half laughing, were comforting and rubbing down the wretched Ned Land, who was swearing like a man possessed.

But at that instant the Nautilus, lifted by the last swells of the tide, left its bed of coral at that fortieth minute exactly fixed by the captain. Its propeller beat the waters with a majestic slowness. Its speed built up little by little, and, sailing on the surface of the Ocean, it left behind, safe and sound, the dangerous passages of the Torres Strait.
