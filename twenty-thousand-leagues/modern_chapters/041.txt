For Want of Air

So there it was: all around the Nautilus, above and below, an impenetrable wall of ice. We were prisoners of the ice pack. The Canadian brought one of his massive fists down on a table. Conseil said nothing. I looked at the captain. His face had settled back into its usual calm. He stood with his arms crossed, thinking. The Nautilus no longer stirred.

Then the captain spoke.

"Gentlemen," he said in a level voice, "there are two ways to die in the situation we're in."

This inexplicable man had the air of a mathematics professor laying out a proof for his students.

"The first," he went on, "is to be crushed. The second is to suffocate. I leave aside the possibility of starving, because the Nautilus's stores will certainly outlast us. So let us think about our chances of being crushed — or of asphyxiation."

"As for suffocating, Captain," I answered, "that's nothing to fear. Our tanks are full."

"True," said Captain Nemo, "but they'll give us only two days of air. Now, we've been buried under the water for thirty-six hours already, and the Nautilus's heavy atmosphere is asking to be renewed. In forty-eight hours, our reserve will be exhausted."

"Then, Captain, let's be free before forty-eight hours are out!"

"We'll at least attempt it, by cutting through the wall that surrounds us."

"On which side?" I asked.

"The sounding line will tell us. I'm going to ground the Nautilus on the lower shelf, and my men, in their diving suits, will attack the iceberg where its wall is thinnest."

"Can we open the panels of the salon?"

"No harm in it. We're no longer moving."

Captain Nemo went out. Soon a hissing told me that water was pouring into the tanks. The Nautilus sank slowly and came to rest on the bed of ice at a depth of three hundred and fifty meters — the depth at which the lower ice shelf lay submerged.

"My friends," I said, "the situation is grave, but I'm counting on your courage and your energy."

"Professor," the Canadian answered, "this is no moment to pester you with my complaints. I'm ready to do anything for the good of all of us."

"Well said, Ned," I told him, holding out my hand.

"Let me add," he went on, "that I'm as handy with a pick as with a harpoon. If I can be of use to the captain, he can put me to work."

"He won't turn down your help. Come on, Ned."

I led the Canadian to the room where the Nautilus's men were pulling on their diving suits. I passed Ned's offer along to the captain, who accepted it. The Canadian climbed into his sea gear and was ready as quickly as his fellow workers. Each of them carried on his back a Rouquayrol apparatus, into which the tanks had fed a generous supply of pure air. A heavy draw on the Nautilus's reserve, but a necessary one. As for the Ruhmkorff lamps, they were useless here, in water so bright and saturated with electric light.

Once Ned was dressed, I went back into the salon, whose windows were uncovered, and, taking my place beside Conseil, I studied the surrounding layers that held the Nautilus in place.

A few moments later, we saw a dozen crewmen set foot on the ice shelf, Ned Land among them, easy to pick out by his height. Captain Nemo was with them.

Before beginning to cut into the walls, he had soundings taken to make sure the work would be aimed in the right direction. Long probes were driven into the side walls, but after fifteen meters they were still stopped by the thick barrier. There was no point attacking the ceiling above us, since that was the ice pack itself, more than four hundred meters high. Captain Nemo then had the lower surface sounded. There, ten meters of ice separated us from the water. That was the thickness of the ice field. From now on, the task was to cut out a slab equal in area to the Nautilus's waterline — about six thousand five hundred cubic meters to detach, in order to open a hole through which we could descend below the ice field.

The work began at once and was carried on with tireless stubbornness. Rather than dig all the way around the Nautilus, which would have brought greater difficulties, Captain Nemo had the enormous pit traced out eight meters off her port quarter. Then his men bored into it at several points around its circumference at the same time. Soon the picks were biting hard into that dense material, and big blocks were torn loose from the mass. By a curious effect of specific gravity, these blocks, lighter than water, flew up, so to speak, to the roof of the tunnel, which thickened above by as much as it thinned below. But that hardly mattered, as long as the lower wall was growing thinner all the while.

After two hours of hard work, Ned Land came back exhausted. He and his companions were relieved by fresh workers, whom Conseil and I joined. The Nautilus's first officer directed us.

The water struck me as strangely cold, but I warmed up quickly once I was swinging the pick. My movements were quite free, even though I was working under a pressure of thirty atmospheres.

When I came back inside after two hours of work, to take some food and some rest, I found a marked difference between the pure air the Rouquayrol apparatus fed me and the atmosphere of the Nautilus, already thick with carbon dioxide. The air hadn't been renewed in forty-eight hours, and its life-giving qualities were badly weakened. And yet, in a span of twelve hours, we had removed only a layer of ice a meter thick over the marked-out area — about six hundred cubic meters. Assuming the same amount of work were done every twelve hours, it would still take five nights and four days to bring the job to a good end.

"Five nights and four days!" I said to my companions. "And we have only two days' worth of air in the tanks."

"Not to mention," Ned put in, "that once we're out of this cursed prison, we'll still be shut in under the ice pack, with no way to reach the open air."

A fair point. Who then could foresee the least amount of time we would need to win our freedom? Wouldn't we suffocate before the Nautilus could get back to the surface of the waves? Was she doomed to perish in this tomb of ice with everyone she held? The situation looked terrible. But everyone had faced it head-on, and all were resolved to do their duty to the very end.

As I'd expected, another meter-thick layer was carved out of the vast pit during the night. But in the morning, when, sheathed in my diving suit, I moved through the liquid mass in a temperature of six or seven degrees below zero, I noticed that the side walls were slowly closing in. The layers of water farther from the pit, which weren't warmed by the men's labor and the play of the tools, were showing a tendency to solidify. Faced with this new and imminent danger, what became of our chances of rescue, and how could we stop this liquid medium from freezing — a freezing that would burst the walls of the Nautilus like glass?

I didn't tell my two companions of this new danger. What good would it do to risk breaking the spirit they were pouring into the grueling work of our rescue? But when I got back on board, I pointed out this grave complication to Captain Nemo.

"I know," he said, in that calm tone that not even the most terrible circumstances could alter. "It's one more danger, but I see no way to guard against it. Our only chance of salvation is to move faster than the freezing. We have to get there first. That's all."

Get there first! Well — by now I should have been used to this way of talking.

That day I swung the pick doggedly for several hours. The work kept me going. Besides, to work was to leave the Nautilus, to breathe directly the pure air drawn from the tanks and delivered by the apparatus — to escape, for a while, an atmosphere grown thin and foul.

Toward evening, the pit had been dug another meter deeper. When I came back on board, I was nearly asphyxiated by the carbon dioxide that saturated the air. Ah, if only we'd had the chemical means to drive off that noxious gas! Oxygen we did not lack. All this water held a considerable quantity of it, and by breaking it down with our powerful batteries, it would have given us back the life-giving element. I had certainly thought of it — but what was the use, since the carbon dioxide, the product of our own breathing, had invaded every part of the ship? To absorb it, we would have had to fill containers with caustic potash and shake them without stopping. But that substance was lacking on board, and nothing could take its place.

That evening Captain Nemo had to open the valves of his tanks and release a few columns of pure air into the interior of the Nautilus. Without this precaution, we would never have woken up.

The next day, March 26, I took up my miner's work again, starting on the fifth meter. The side walls and the lower surface of the ice pack were visibly thickening. It was plain that they would meet before the Nautilus managed to break free. Despair seized me for a moment. The pick nearly slipped from my hands. What was the use of digging, if I was only going to die smothered, crushed by this water turning to stone — a torment the cruelty of savages could never even have invented? It seemed to me that I was caught between the fearsome jaws of a monster that were closing on me, and there was no resisting them.

Just then Captain Nemo, directing the work, working himself, passed near me. I touched him with my hand and pointed to the walls of our prison. The starboard wall had advanced to less than four meters from the Nautilus's hull.

The captain understood, and signaled me to follow him. We went back on board. Once I'd taken off my diving suit, I went with him into the salon.

"Professor Aronnax," he said, "we must try some heroic measure, or we're going to be sealed into this solidified water as if in cement."

"Yes!" I said. "But what can we do?"

"Ah!" he cried. "If only my Nautilus were strong enough to bear this pressure without being crushed!"

"Well?" I asked, not grasping his idea.

"Don't you see," he went on, "that this freezing of the water would come to our aid? Don't you see that by turning solid, it would burst apart the ice fields that imprison us, the way water, in freezing, bursts apart the hardest stones? Don't you feel that it would be an agent of rescue instead of an agent of destruction?"

"Yes, Captain, perhaps. But whatever resistance to crushing the Nautilus may have, it couldn't withstand that appalling pressure. It would be flattened like a sheet of tin plate."

"I know, Professor. So we mustn't count on the help of nature, but on ourselves. We have to fight this freezing. We have to hold it back. Not only are the side walls closing in, but there's not ten feet of water left ahead of or behind the Nautilus. The freezing is gaining on us from every side."

"How long," I asked, "will the air in the tanks let us breathe on board?"

The captain looked me full in the face.

"The day after tomorrow," he said, "the tanks will be empty."

A cold sweat came over me. And yet, should I have been surprised at his answer? On March 22 the Nautilus had plunged beneath the open waters of the Pole. We were now at the 26th. For five days we had been living on the ship's reserves! And what breathable air remained had to be saved for the workers. Even as I write these things, my impression of them is still so vivid that an involuntary terror takes hold of my whole being, and the air seems to fail in my lungs!

Meanwhile Captain Nemo sat thinking, silent and still. Plainly an idea was crossing his mind. But he seemed to push it away. He kept answering himself no. At last these words escaped his lips:

"Boiling water!" he murmured.

"Boiling water?" I exclaimed.

"Yes, Professor. We're shut up in a relatively confined space. Wouldn't jets of boiling water, injected constantly by the Nautilus's pumps, raise the temperature of this medium and slow its freezing?"

"It's worth trying," I said firmly.

"Then let's try it, Professor."

The thermometer outside then read seven degrees below zero. Captain Nemo led me to the galley, where huge distilling machines were at work, supplying drinking water by evaporation. They were filled with water, and all the electric heat of the batteries was sent through the coils steeped in the liquid. In a few minutes the water had reached a hundred degrees. It was directed to the pumps while fresh water took its place as fast as it was used. The heat the batteries produced was such that cold seawater, drawn up from the sea, arrived boiling at the pump chambers after passing through the machines alone.

The injection began, and three hours later the thermometer outside read six degrees below zero. That was one degree gained. Two hours after that, it read only four.

"We're going to succeed," I said to the captain, after following and checking the progress of the operation by many observations.

"I think so," he answered. "We won't be crushed. Now we have only suffocation to fear."

During the night, the temperature of the water rose to one degree below zero. The injections couldn't carry it any higher. But since seawater doesn't freeze until minus two degrees, I was reassured at last against the dangers of solidifying.

The next day, March 27, six meters of ice had been torn from the pit. Only four meters were left to remove — still forty-eight hours of work. The air on board the Nautilus could no longer be renewed. And so that day kept going from bad to worse.

An intolerable heaviness weighed me down. Toward three in the afternoon, this feeling of anguish was carried to a violent pitch. Yawns dislocated my jaws. My lungs gasped, searching for that vital, oxygen-rich element the breath needs, which was growing scarcer and scarcer. A moral torpor took hold of me. I lay stretched out without strength, all but unconscious. My good Conseil, seized with the same symptoms, suffering the same sufferings, never left my side. He took my hand, he cheered me on, and I heard him still, murmuring:

"Ah, if only I could stop breathing, to leave more air for Master!"

Tears came to my eyes to hear him speak that way.

If our situation, for all of us, was unbearable inside, with what haste, with what joy we pulled on our diving suits to take our turn at the work! The picks rang against the icy layer. Our arms tired, our hands were rubbed raw, but what were those pains, what did those wounds matter? The air of life was reaching our lungs. We could breathe! We could breathe!

And yet no one dragged his work under the water out past his allotted time. His task done, each man handed over to his gasping companions the tank that was to pour life into him. Captain Nemo set the example and was the first to submit to this stern discipline. When his hour came, he yielded his apparatus to another and went back into the ship's foul air, always calm, without a lapse, without a murmur.

That day the usual work was done with even greater vigor. Only two meters were left to cut through over the whole surface. Only two meters separated us from the open sea. But the tanks were nearly empty of air. The little that remained had to be saved for the workers. Not one atom for the Nautilus!

When I came back on board, I was half suffocated. What a night! I couldn't paint it. Such sufferings can't be described. The next day, my breathing was labored. To my headaches were joined dizzying spells of vertigo that made a drunken man of me. My companions felt the same symptoms. Some of the crewmen were rattling in their throats.

That day, the sixth of our imprisonment, Captain Nemo, finding the mattock and the pick too slow, resolved to crush the layer of ice that still separated us from the open water. This man had kept his cool and his energy. By his moral force he mastered his physical pains. He thought, he calculated, he acted.

On his order, the vessel was lightened — that is, raised off the icy bed by a change in specific gravity. Once she floated, she was hauled into position over the huge pit that had been traced out to follow her waterline. Then, as her ballast tanks filled, she came down and fitted herself into the socket.

At that moment the entire crew came back on board, and the double communication door was shut. The Nautilus now rested on the layer of ice that was less than a meter thick, and that the soundings had pierced in a thousand places.

The valves of the tanks were then thrown wide open, and a hundred cubic meters of water rushed in, adding a hundred thousand kilograms to the weight of the Nautilus.

We waited, we listened, forgetting our sufferings, still hoping. We were staking our survival on one last throw.

In spite of the roaring that filled my head, I soon heard a shuddering under the Nautilus's hull. The ship tilted. The ice cracked with a strange, sharp sound, like paper tearing, and the Nautilus dropped.

"We're through!" Conseil murmured in my ear.

I couldn't answer him. I seized his hand. I gripped it in a helpless spasm.

All at once, carried away by her fearful overload, the Nautilus plunged beneath the water like a cannonball — that is, she fell as she would have fallen through empty air.

The whole electric power was thrown onto the pumps, which at once began driving the water out of the tanks. After a few minutes, our fall was checked. Soon the pressure gauge even showed an upward movement. The screw, running at full speed, made the plated hull shudder down to its bolts, and swept us northward.

But how long would this voyage under the ice pack last before we reached open sea? Another day? I'd be dead before then!

Half stretched out on a divan in the library, I was suffocating. My face was purple, my lips blue, my faculties suspended. I could no longer see, no longer hear. Any sense of time had vanished from my mind. My muscles couldn't contract.

How many hours passed like that, I couldn't say. But I was aware of my death agony beginning. I understood that I was going to die.

Suddenly I came to. A few puffs of air worked their way into my lungs. Had we come back up to the surface of the waves? Had we crossed the ice pack?

No! It was Ned and Conseil, my two brave friends, sacrificing themselves to save me. A few atoms of air were still left at the bottom of an apparatus. Instead of breathing it themselves, they had saved it for me, and, while they were suffocating, they poured life into me drop by drop! I tried to push the apparatus away. They held my hands, and for a few moments I breathed with rapture.

My eyes turned to the clock. It was eleven in the morning. We must be at the 28th of March. The Nautilus was traveling at the terrifying speed of forty miles an hour. She was writhing through the water.

Where was Captain Nemo? Had he gone under? Had his companions died with him?

At that moment the pressure gauge showed we were no more than twenty feet from the surface. A single field of ice separated us from the open air. Couldn't it be broken?

Perhaps! In any case, the Nautilus was going to try. I felt her take a slanting position, dropping her stern and raising her ram. A single intake of water had been enough to break her balance. Then, driven by her powerful screw, she attacked the ice field from below like a colossal battering ram. She split it little by little, drew back, charged again at full speed against the field that was tearing apart, and finally, carried by one supreme burst, she flung herself onto the icy surface and crushed it under her weight.

The panel was opened — torn open, one might say — and pure air came flooding into every part of the Nautilus.
