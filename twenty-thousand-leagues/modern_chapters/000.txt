A Runaway Reef

The year 1866 was marked by a bizarre event, an unexplained and altogether inexplicable phenomenon that no one, surely, has forgotten. To say nothing of the rumors that stirred up the populations of the ports and inflamed the public mind far inland across whole continents, it was the seafaring men above all who were shaken. Merchants and shipowners, sea captains, skippers and masters throughout Europe and America, naval officers of every country, and, in their wake, the governments of the various states on both continents — all of them took the matter with the utmost seriousness.

The fact was that, for some time now, several ships had met with "an enormous thing" out on the open sea: a long object, tapered like a spindle, sometimes glowing with a strange light, and vastly larger and faster than any whale.

The details concerning this apparition, entered in the various ships' logs, agreed rather closely on the structure of the object — or the creature — in question, on the unheard-of speed of its movements, on the astonishing power of its locomotion, and on the peculiar vitality with which it seemed endowed. If it was a member of the whale family, then it exceeded in bulk every specimen science had catalogued up to that time. Neither Cuvier, nor Lacépède, nor Monsieur Duméril, nor Monsieur de Quatrefages would have granted the existence of such a monster — not, at least, without having seen it, seen it with their own learned eyes.

Taking the average of the observations made on the various occasions — rejecting the timid estimates that gave the object a length of two hundred feet, and dismissing the wild exaggerations that made it a mile wide and three miles long — one could nonetheless assert that this phenomenal being far surpassed any dimensions admitted so far by the ichthyologists — assuming it existed at all.

And exist it did; the fact in itself could no longer be denied, and given the human brain's incurable fondness for the marvelous, one can well imagine the excitement this supernatural apparition stirred up all around the world. As for filing it away quietly among the fables, that idea had to be given up for good.

For on July 20, 1866, the steamer Governor Higginson, of the Calcutta and Burnach Steam Navigation Company, had encountered this moving mass five miles off the eastern coast of Australia. Captain Baker at first believed he was looking at some uncharted reef; he was even preparing to fix its exact position when two columns of water, hurled up by the inexplicable object, shot hissing a hundred and fifty feet into the air. So unless this reef happened to be subject to the intermittent eruptions of a geyser, the Governor Higginson had well and truly come face to face with some aquatic mammal, unknown until then, which spouted from its blowholes columns of water mixed with air and steam.

The same phenomenon was observed on July 23 of that same year, in the waters of the Pacific, by the Cristobal Colon, of the West India and Pacific Steam Navigation Company. Clearly, then, this extraordinary whale could travel from one place to another with astonishing speed, since within an interval of just three days the Governor Higginson and the Cristobal Colon had observed it at two points on the map separated by a distance of more than seven hundred nautical leagues. Two weeks later, and two thousand leagues from there, the Helvetia, of the Compagnie Nationale, and the Shannon, of the Royal Mail line, steaming in opposite directions through that stretch of the Atlantic that lies between the United States and Europe, each in turn signaled the monster at 42°15′ north latitude and 60°35′ longitude west of the Greenwich meridian. From this simultaneous sighting, the creature's length was reckoned at a minimum of more than three hundred and fifty English feet, since both the Shannon and the Helvetia were smaller than it, even though they measured a hundred meters from stem to stern. Now, the largest whales — those that frequent the waters around the Aleutian Islands, the Kulammak and the Umgullick — have never exceeded a length of fifty-six meters, if indeed they ever reach it.

These reports arriving one on top of another, along with fresh observations made aboard the transatlantic liner Pereire, a collision between the Etna, of the Inman line, and the monster, an official report drawn up by the officers of the French frigate Normandie, and a very sober sighting recorded by the staff of Commodore Fitz-James aboard the Lord Clyde — all of this stirred public opinion to its depths. In the lighter-hearted countries, people made a joke of the whole thing, but the serious, practical countries — England, America, Germany — were gravely concerned.

Everywhere in the great cities the monster became the fashion of the hour; it was sung about in the cafés, jeered at in the newspapers, and acted out on the stage. The tabloids found in it a fine opportunity to lay eggs of every color. In the papers, short as ever on copy, one saw the reappearance of every imaginary and gigantic creature you could name, from the white whale — the terrible "Moby Dick" of the far northern regions — to the monstrous Kraken, whose tentacles can wrap themselves around a five-hundred-ton vessel and drag it down into the depths of the ocean. They even dusted off the official records of ancient times: the opinions of Aristotle and Pliny, who allowed the existence of such monsters; then the Norwegian tales of Bishop Pontoppidan; the accounts of Paul Heggede; and finally the reports of Mr. Harrington, whose good faith cannot be doubted when he swears that, aboard the Castilian in 1857, he saw that enormous sea serpent which until then had frequented no waters but those of the old Constitutionnel.

Then broke out the endless quarrel between the believers and the skeptics, waged in the learned societies and the scientific journals alike. The "monster question" set minds ablaze. Journalists who make a profession of science, locked in combat with those who make a profession of wit, spilled floods of ink during that memorable campaign — and a few of them even spilled two or three drops of blood, for from the sea serpent they passed straight on to the most offensive personal attacks.

For six months the war dragged on, with fortunes shifting on both sides. To the weighty articles of the Geographical Institute of Brazil, the Royal Academy of Sciences in Berlin, the British Association, the Smithsonian Institution in Washington; to the discussions in The Indian Archipelago, in Abbé Moigno's Cosmos, in Petermann's Mittheilungen; to the science columns of the great newspapers of France and abroad — the popular press fired back with an inexhaustible verve. Twisting a saying of Linnaeus that the monster's opponents had quoted, its witty writers maintained that "nature does not make fools," and they begged their contemporaries not to give nature the lie by admitting the existence of Krakens, sea serpents, "Moby Dicks," and other ravings of delirious sailors. Finally, in an article in a much-feared satirical paper, its most popular writer, mounting the attack over them all, went at the monster like Hippolytus, dealt it one last blow, and finished it off amid a burst of universal laughter. Wit had defeated science.

Through the first months of 1867, the question seemed to be buried, and it did not look likely to rise again — when new facts were brought to the public's attention. This time it was no longer a scientific puzzle to be solved, but a real and serious danger to be avoided. The question took on a wholly different aspect. The monster became once more an islet, a rock, a reef — but a runaway reef, one you could neither pin down nor lay hold of.

On March 5, 1867, the Moravian, of the Montreal Ocean Company, finding herself during the night at 27°30′ latitude and 72°15′ longitude, struck with her starboard quarter a rock that no chart marked in those waters. Under the combined thrust of the wind and her four hundred horsepower, she was making thirteen knots. There is no doubt that, but for the superior quality of her hull, the Moravian would have been split open by the blow and gone down with the two hundred and thirty-seven passengers she was bringing back from Canada.

The accident had happened around five in the morning, just as day was beginning to break. The watch officers rushed to the stern of the ship. They examined the ocean with the most scrupulous attention. They saw nothing — nothing but a strong eddy breaking about three cable-lengths off, as though the sheets of water had been violently churned. The position was noted down exactly, and the Moravian continued on her way with no apparent damage. Had she struck a submerged rock, or some enormous piece of wreckage? No one could say; but when her hull was examined in dry dock, it was found that part of the keel had been broken.

This incident, extremely serious in itself, might perhaps have been forgotten like so many others, had it not, three weeks later, repeated itself under conditions exactly the same. Only this time, thanks to the nationality of the ship that was the new victim, and thanks to the reputation of the company to which she belonged, the event caused an enormous stir all over the world.

No one is unaware of the name of the famous English shipowner Cunard. In 1840 this shrewd businessman founded a postal service between Liverpool and Halifax, with three wooden paddle-wheel ships of four hundred horsepower and eleven hundred and sixty-two tons' burden. Eight years later, the company's fleet grew by four ships of six hundred and fifty horsepower and eighteen hundred and twenty tons, and two years after that by two more vessels of still greater power and tonnage. In 1853, the Cunard company — its contract for carrying the mails having just been renewed — added one after another to its fleet the Arabia, the Persia, the China, the Scotia, the Java, and the Russia, all ships of the first rank, and the largest that, after the Great Eastern, had ever plowed the seas. And so, by 1867, the company owned twelve ships, eight of them paddle-wheelers and four screw-driven.

If I give these very brief details, it is so that everyone may grasp just how important this maritime transport company is, known the world over for its intelligent management. No transoceanic shipping enterprise has been run with more skill; no business has been crowned with more success. In twenty-six years, Cunard ships have crossed the Atlantic two thousand times, and never once has a voyage been missed, never a delay occurred, never a letter, a man, or a vessel been lost. That is why passengers, despite the powerful competition France offers, still choose the Cunard line over any other, as is plain from a survey of the official records of recent years. Having said this, no one will be surprised at the stir caused by the accident that befell one of its finest steamers.

On April 13, 1867, with the sea calm and the breeze manageable, the Scotia lay at 15°12′ longitude and 45°37′ latitude. She was making thirteen knots and forty-three hundredths under the push of her thousand horsepower. Her paddle wheels beat the sea with perfect regularity. Her draft at the time was six meters seventy centimeters, and her displacement six thousand six hundred and twenty-four cubic meters.

At seventeen minutes past four in the afternoon, while the passengers were gathered for lunch in the grand saloon, a shock — a slight one, all things considered — struck the Scotia's hull, on the quarter and a little abaft the port paddle wheel.

The Scotia had not struck; she had been struck, and by a cutting or piercing instrument rather than a blunt one. The impact had seemed so light that no one aboard would have worried about it, but for the cry of the men down in the hold, who came scrambling up onto the deck shouting:

"We're sinking! We're sinking!"

At first the passengers were badly frightened; but Captain Anderson hastened to reassure them. In fact, the danger could not be imminent. The Scotia, divided into seven compartments by watertight bulkheads, could shrug off any single leak with impunity.

Captain Anderson went down at once into the hold. He found that the sea had flooded the fifth compartment, and the speed of the flooding proved the leak was a large one. Very fortunately, that compartment did not house the boilers, or the fires would have gone out on the spot.

Captain Anderson had the ship stopped immediately, and one of the sailors dove down to inspect the damage. A few moments later they confirmed the existence of a hole two meters wide in the steamer's hull. Such a leak could not be plugged, and the Scotia, her paddle wheels half submerged, had to continue her voyage that way. She was then three hundred miles from Cape Clear, and after three days of delay that gave Liverpool a serious fright, she entered the company's docks.

The engineers then set about examining the Scotia, which was placed in dry dock. They could not believe their eyes. Two and a half meters below the waterline gaped a clean tear, in the shape of an isosceles triangle. The break in the metal plating was of perfect sharpness — it could not have been cut more cleanly with a punch press. So the piercing tool that had made it must have been tempered to an uncommon hardness — and, after being launched with prodigious force and thus driving through a plate four centimeters thick, it had had to withdraw itself by a backward motion that was truly inexplicable.

Such was this latest incident, and its result was to fire up public opinion all over again. From this moment on, in fact, every maritime disaster that had no clearly determined cause was laid at the monster's door. This fantastic animal was made to shoulder the blame for all these shipwrecks, whose number is unfortunately considerable; for out of the three thousand ships whose loss is recorded every year at the Bureau Veritas, the number of steamships and sailing vessels presumed lost with all hands for want of news comes to no fewer than two hundred!

And so it was the "monster" that, justly or unjustly, was accused of their disappearance; and, thanks to it, communication between the various continents growing more and more dangerous, the public spoke out and demanded, in no uncertain terms, that the seas be rid at last, and at any price, of this formidable creature.
