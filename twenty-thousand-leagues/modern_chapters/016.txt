An Underwater Forest

We had reached at last the edge of this forest — surely one of the finest in all Captain Nemo's vast domain. He regarded it as his own, claiming over it the very rights the first men held in the first days of the world. And who, after all, would have disputed his ownership of this underwater estate? What bolder pioneer would ever have come, axe in hand, to clear its shadowy thickets?

This forest was made up of great tree-like plants, and the moment we passed beneath its towering arches, my eyes were struck at once by a singular arrangement of their branches — an arrangement I had never observed until then.

Not one of the grasses carpeting the ground, not one of the boughs bristling on the shrubs, crept along the bottom, or curved over, or spread out in a horizontal plane. Every one of them climbed toward the surface of the ocean. There was no filament, no ribbon, however slender, that did not hold itself straight as an iron rod. The wracks and the sea-vines developed along a rigid, perpendicular line, obeying the density of the element that had produced them. Motionless too: when I pushed them aside with my hand, these plants sprang back at once to their original position. This was the realm of the vertical.

Soon I grew used to this strange arrangement, and to the relative gloom that wrapped around us. The forest floor was strewn with sharp blocks of stone, hard to avoid. The undersea flora struck me as remarkably complete here, richer even than it would have been in the arctic or tropical zones, where its species are fewer. But for a few minutes I kept mixing up the kingdoms in spite of myself, mistaking zoophytes for water plants, animals for vegetables. And who would not have made the same mistake? Fauna and flora touch so closely in this underwater world!

I noticed that all these products of the plant kingdom held to the ground only by the shallowest of grips. Without roots, indifferent to the solid body — sand, shell, shard, or pebble — that supported them, they asked of it only a foothold, not the source of their life. These plants proceed from nothing but themselves, and the principle of their existence lies in the water that holds them up and feeds them. Most of them, instead of leaves, put out little blades of the most fanciful shapes, confined to a narrow range of colors that ran only to pink, carmine, green, olive, tawny, and brown. Here I saw again — not dried out this time, as in the Nautilus's specimens — peacock's-tail algae spread into fans that seemed to beg for a breeze, scarlet ceramium weeds, tangleweeds stretching out their young, edible shoots, thread-like sea-otters' cabbage rippling and unfurling to a height of fifteen meters, clusters of acetabularia whose stalks grow upward from the crown, and any number of other open-sea plants, all of them flowerless. "A curious anomaly," as one witty naturalist put it, "a strange element indeed, where the animal kingdom flowers and the plant kingdom does not!"

Among these various shrubs, as tall as the trees of the temperate zones, and under their damp shade, gathered real bushes of living flowers, hedges of zoophytes on which bloomed brain corals zebra-striped with twisting furrows, yellowish cup-corals with translucent tentacles, and turf-like tufts of zoanthids. And to complete the illusion, the flying gurnards darted from branch to branch like a swarm of hummingbirds, while yellow lepisacanths with bristling jaws and sharp scales, along with sea-robins and boarfish, rose up under our feet like a flock of flushed snipe.

Toward one o'clock, Captain Nemo gave the signal to halt. For my own part I was glad enough of it, and we stretched out beneath a bower of winged kelp, whose long tapering strands stood up like arrows.

That moment of rest struck me as delicious. All we lacked was the charm of conversation. But it was impossible to speak, impossible to answer. I only brought my big copper head close to Conseil's. I saw the good fellow's eyes shining with contentment, and, as a sign of his satisfaction, he wriggled inside his shell with the most comical air in the world.

After four hours of this walk, I was astonished to feel no violent hunger. What accounted for this state of my stomach, I couldn't say. But in exchange, I felt an overwhelming urge to sleep, as happens to every diver. And so my eyes soon closed behind their thick glass, and I sank into an irresistible drowsiness that the movement of walking alone had been holding off until then. Captain Nemo and his sturdy companion, stretched out in that limpid crystal, set us the example of sleep.

How long I stayed sunk in that slumber I couldn't judge; but when I woke, it seemed to me the sun was sinking toward the horizon. Captain Nemo was already on his feet, and I was beginning to stretch my limbs, when an unexpected apparition brought me abruptly upright.

A few steps off, a monstrous sea-spider, a full meter high, was watching me with its squinting eyes, poised to spring. Thick as my diving suit was — thick enough to protect me from the creature's bite — I couldn't hold back a shudder of horror. Conseil and the sailor from the Nautilus woke at that moment. Captain Nemo pointed the hideous crustacean out to his companion, who felled it at once with a blow from the butt of his rifle, and I saw the monster's horrible legs writhe in terrible convulsions.

This encounter set me thinking that other, more fearsome animals must haunt these dark depths, and that my diving suit would be no protection against their attacks. I hadn't given it a thought until then, and I resolved to stay on my guard. I supposed, moreover, that this halt marked the end of our walk; but I was wrong, and instead of turning back to the Nautilus, Captain Nemo pressed on with his daring excursion.

The ground kept dropping away, and its slope, growing steeper, led us to greater depths. It must have been around three o'clock when we reached a narrow valley, cut between high sheer walls, lying at a depth of a hundred and fifty meters. Thanks to the perfection of our equipment, we thus went ninety meters beyond the limit that nature had seemed until now to impose on the undersea excursions of man.

I say a hundred and fifty meters, though no instrument allowed me to gauge the distance. But I knew that even in the clearest seas the sun's rays could penetrate no deeper. And, sure enough, the darkness now became profound. Not an object was visible ten steps off. So I was groping my way forward when I suddenly saw a fairly vivid white light shine out. Captain Nemo had just switched on his electric apparatus. His companion did the same. Conseil and I followed their lead. By turning a screw, I established the connection between the coil and the glass spiral, and the sea, lit by our four lanterns, glowed within a radius of twenty-five meters.

Captain Nemo went on plunging deeper into the dark depths of the forest, whose shrubs were thinning out more and more. I noticed that plant life was disappearing faster than animal life. The open-sea plants had already forsaken the ground, which had turned barren, while a prodigious number of animals — zoophytes, articulates, mollusks, and fish — still swarmed over it.

As I walked, it occurred to me that the light of our Ruhmkorff apparatus was bound to draw in some of the inhabitants of these dark strata. But if any did approach us, they at least kept a distance regrettable for hunters. Several times I saw Captain Nemo stop and bring his gun to his shoulder; then, after a few moments' observation, he would straighten up and resume his march.

At last, toward four o'clock or so, this marvelous excursion came to an end. A wall of superb rock reared up before us, an imposing mass — a heaping of gigantic blocks, an enormous granite cliff hollowed with dark grottoes, but offering no practicable slope to climb. These were the coastal cliffs of Crespo Island. This was dry land.

Captain Nemo stopped short. A gesture from him brought us all to a halt, and however much I longed to scale that rampart, I had to stop. Here the domains of Captain Nemo ended. He would not go beyond them. Past this point lay that portion of the globe he was never to set foot on again.

The return began. Captain Nemo had taken the lead of his little troop once more, moving forward without a moment's hesitation. I thought I could tell that we weren't taking the same path back to the Nautilus. This new route, very steep and therefore very trying, brought us quickly nearer the surface of the sea. Still, this return to the upper layers wasn't so sudden that the decompression came too fast — which might have brought on grave disorders in our systems and caused those internal injuries so fatal to divers. Very quickly the light reappeared and grew, and, since the sun was already low on the horizon, refraction once again fringed every object with a spectral ring.

At a depth of ten meters, we walked in the midst of a swarm of little fish of every kind, more numerous than the birds of the air, and more nimble too; but no aquatic game worth a shot had yet offered itself to our eyes.

At that moment I saw the captain's weapon snap to his shoulder and track some moving object through the bushes. The shot went off, I heard a faint whistle, and an animal dropped, struck down, a few steps away.

It was a magnificent sea otter, an enhydra, the only quadruped that lives exclusively in the sea. This otter, a meter and a half long, must have been of very great value. Its coat, chestnut-brown above and silvered below, made one of those admirable furs so sought after in the Russian and Chinese markets; the fineness and sheen of its fur guaranteed it a value of at least two thousand francs. I greatly admired this curious mammal, with its rounded head trimmed with short ears, its round eyes, its white whiskers like a cat's, its webbed and clawed feet, and its bushy tail. Hunted and harried by the fishermen, this precious carnivore is becoming extremely rare, and it has taken refuge mainly in the northern reaches of the Pacific, where its species will most likely soon die out altogether.

Captain Nemo's companion came to pick up the animal, hoisted it onto his shoulder, and we set off again.

For an hour, a plain of sand unrolled before our steps. Often it rose to within less than two meters of the surface of the water. I could then see our own image, sharply reflected, sketched upside down, and above us there appeared an identical troop, reproducing our movements and our gestures, in every point exactly like us, except that it walked head-down and feet-up.

Another effect worth noting: the passing of thick clouds that formed and vanished swiftly. But on reflection I understood that these so-called clouds were due only to the varying thickness of the long ground-swells, and I could even make out the foamy "whitecaps" that their breaking crests multiplied across the water. There was nothing, right down to the shadows of the great birds passing over our heads, whose quick grazing of the sea's surface I did not catch.

On this occasion I witnessed one of the finest shots that ever set a hunter's nerves tingling. A great bird of wide wingspan, plainly visible, came gliding closer. Captain Nemo's companion took aim and fired the instant it was only a few meters above the waves. The creature dropped, struck down, and its fall carried it within reach of the deft hunter, who seized it. It was an albatross of the finest kind, an admirable specimen of the open-sea birds.

Our march had not been interrupted by this incident. For two hours we followed sometimes sandy plains, sometimes meadows of seaweed that were very hard going. Frankly, I could go no further when I caught sight of a dim glow that broke the darkness of the waters half a mile off. It was the beacon of the Nautilus. Within twenty minutes we would be aboard, and there I would breathe easy again, for it seemed to me that my tank was now supplying only air very poor in oxygen. But I had reckoned without an encounter that delayed our arrival somewhat.

I had fallen some twenty steps behind when I saw Captain Nemo come hurrying back toward me. With his powerful hand, he pressed me down to the ground, while his companion did the same to Conseil. At first I didn't know what to make of this abrupt assault, but I was reassured to see the captain lie down beside me and stay motionless.

So there I was, stretched out on the bottom, sheltered exactly beneath a bush of seaweed, when, raising my head, I saw enormous masses go noisily past, casting off phosphorescent gleams.

My blood froze in my veins! I had recognized the formidable sharks that threatened us. It was a pair of tintorea sharks, terrible creatures with enormous tails and dull, glassy stares, that distill a phosphorescent matter through holes pierced around their snouts. Monstrous fireflies, that grind a whole man to pulp in their iron jaws! I don't know whether Conseil was busy classifying them, but for my own part I studied their silvered bellies, their fearsome maws bristling with teeth, from a rather unscientific point of view — more as a victim than as a naturalist.

Very luckily, these voracious animals see poorly. They passed without noticing us, grazing us with their brownish fins, and we escaped, as if by a miracle, a danger surely greater than meeting a tiger in the depths of a forest.

Half an hour later, guided by the electric trail, we reached the Nautilus. The outer door had been left open, and Captain Nemo closed it again the moment we were back in the first cell. Then he pressed a button. I heard the pumps working inside the vessel, I felt the water sinking around me, and in a few moments the cell was completely emptied. The inner door then opened, and we passed into the changing room.

There, our diving suits were stripped off, not without some trouble, and, thoroughly worn out, faint with hunger and sleep, I made my way back to my cabin, filled with wonder at this astonishing excursion to the bottom of the seas.
