At Full Steam

At that shout the whole crew rushed toward the harpooner — commander, officers, mates, sailors, ship's boys, right down to the engineers who abandoned their engine and the stokers who left their furnaces. The order to stop had been given, and the frigate was now only coasting on her own momentum.

The darkness was deep just then, and good as the Canadian's eyes were, I wondered how he had seen anything, and what it was he could have seen. My heart was pounding fit to burst.

But Ned Land had not been mistaken, and all of us made out the object he was pointing to with his hand.

Two cable-lengths off the Abraham Lincoln's starboard quarter, the sea seemed to be lit from below. This was no mere trick of phosphorescence — there could be no mistaking it. The monster, submerged a few fathoms beneath the surface, was throwing off that intense but unexplained glow which several captains had described in their reports. This magnificent radiance had to be produced by some agent of tremendous illuminating power. The luminous patch traced across the sea an immense, elongated oval, at the center of which glowed a burning core whose unbearable brilliance faded off by successive degrees toward the edges.

"It's nothing but a cluster of phosphorescent molecules," cried one of the officers.

"No, sir," I replied with conviction. "Piddocks and salps never give off a light that powerful. This glow is electrical in nature... Besides — look, look! It's moving! It's coming forward, going back! It's charging straight at us!"

A general cry went up from the frigate.

"Silence!" ordered Commander Farragut. "Helm hard to windward! Engine astern!"

The sailors sprang to the wheel, the engineers to their engine. The steam was reversed at once, and the Abraham Lincoln, swinging to port, described a half-circle.

"Helm amidships! Engine ahead!" shouted Commander Farragut.

These orders were carried out, and the frigate pulled rapidly away from the glowing core.

Let me correct that. She meant to pull away, but the supernatural animal closed the distance at twice her speed.

We were breathless. Astonishment, far more than fear, held us silent and motionless. The animal ran us down as though it were a game. It circled the frigate, which was then making fourteen knots, and wrapped her in its electric sheets like a luminous dust. Then it drew off two or three miles, leaving behind a phosphorescent wake like the swirling clouds of steam an express locomotive flings out behind it. All at once, from the dark edge of the horizon, where it had gone to gather speed, the monster shot suddenly toward the Abraham Lincoln with terrifying swiftness, stopped short twenty feet from her wales, and went dark — not by sinking beneath the water, since its brilliance did not fade by degrees, but abruptly, as if the source of that dazzling outpouring had suddenly run dry. Then it reappeared on the other side of the ship, whether it had rounded her or slipped beneath her keel. At any instant a collision might occur that would be fatal to us.

Meanwhile, I was surprised at the frigate's maneuvers. She was fleeing, not attacking. She was being chased — she who ought to have been the hunter — and I said as much to Commander Farragut. His face, ordinarily so impassive, wore a look of indefinable bewilderment.

"Professor Aronnax," he answered me, "I don't know what formidable creature I'm dealing with, and I won't rashly risk my frigate in the middle of all this darkness. Besides, how do you attack something unknown, how do you defend against it? Let's wait for daylight, and then the roles will be reversed."

"You've no more doubt, Commander, as to the nature of the animal?"

"None, sir. It's obviously a gigantic narwhal — but an electric narwhal as well."

"Then perhaps," I added, "there's no more coming near it than there is coming near an electric eel or a torpedo ray."

"Just so," the commander replied. "And if it carries a thunderbolt's power inside it, then it's surely the most terrible animal that ever came from the hand of the Creator. That is why, sir, I intend to keep my guard up."

The whole crew stayed on their feet all through the night. No one dreamed of sleeping. The Abraham Lincoln, unable to match the creature for speed, had eased off and was holding under low steam. For its part the narwhal, imitating the frigate, let itself be rocked at the pleasure of the swell, and seemed determined not to abandon the field of battle.

Toward midnight, however, it disappeared — or, to put it more exactly, it "went out" like a big glowworm. Had it fled? That was to be feared, not hoped for. But at seven minutes to one in the morning came a deafening whistle, like the sound of a column of water driven up with extreme violence.

Commander Farragut, Ned Land, and I were on the poop deck just then, peering hungrily into the deep darkness.

"Ned Land," asked the commander, "you've often heard whales bellow?"

"Often, sir. But never a whale like this, one whose sight has earned me two thousand dollars."

"Quite right, you've a claim to the bounty. But tell me — isn't that the noise cetaceans make when they blow water out through their blowholes?"

"The same noise, sir, but this one is beyond all comparison louder. So there's no mistaking it. That's a cetacean out there in our waters, sure enough. With your permission, sir," the harpooner added, "we'll have a word or two with it tomorrow at daybreak."

"If it's in the mood to hear you out, Master Land," I said, in a tone of little conviction.

"Just let me get within four harpoon-lengths of it," the Canadian shot back, "and it'll have to hear me out!"

"But to get near it," the commander went on, "I'll have to put a whaleboat at your disposal?"

"No doubt, sir."

"That means gambling with my men's lives?"

"And with mine!" the harpooner answered simply.

Toward two in the morning the glowing core reappeared, no less intense, five miles to windward of the Abraham Lincoln. Despite the distance, despite the noise of wind and sea, we could distinctly hear the animal's formidable tail-strokes and even its panting breath. It seemed that when the enormous narwhal came up to breathe at the surface of the ocean, the air rushed into its lungs the way steam rushes into the vast cylinders of a two-thousand-horsepower engine.

"Hm!" I thought. "A whale with the strength of a cavalry regiment — that would be a fine whale indeed!"

We stayed on the alert until daybreak and made ready for battle. The fishing gear was laid out along the bulwarks. The first mate had them load those blunderbuss guns that can fire a harpoon a mile, and long duck-guns with explosive shells whose wound is mortal even to the mightiest animals. Ned Land had contented himself with sharpening his harpoon — a terrible weapon in his hand.

At six o'clock dawn began to break, and with the first gleams of daylight the narwhal's electric glow vanished. At seven the day had come up well enough, but a very thick morning mist shrank the horizon, and the best spyglasses could not pierce it. Hence disappointment and anger.

I hauled myself up to the mizzen crosstrees. A few officers had already perched themselves at the mastheads.

At eight the mist rolled heavily off the water, and its thick coils lifted little by little. The horizon widened and cleared all at once.

Suddenly, just as the evening before, Ned Land's voice rang out.

"The thing in question, off the port quarter!" cried the harpooner.

Every eye turned toward the point he indicated.

There, a mile and a half from the frigate, a long blackish body rose a meter above the waves. Its tail, lashing furiously, kicked up a considerable churn. Never did a caudal fin beat the sea with such power. An immense wake, dazzlingly white, marked the animal's passage and traced a long, sweeping curve.

The frigate drew near the cetacean. I studied it with a perfectly clear mind. The reports of the Shannon and the Helvetia had somewhat exaggerated its dimensions, and I put its length at only two hundred and fifty feet. As for its girth, I could gauge it only with difficulty; but on the whole the animal struck me as admirably proportioned in all three dimensions.

While I was observing this phenomenal creature, two jets of steam and water shot from its blowholes and rose to a height of forty meters, which settled the matter of how it breathed. From this I concluded definitively that it belonged to the branch of the vertebrates, the class of mammals, the subclass of monodelphians, the group of the fish-shaped, the order of cetaceans, family... Here I could not yet pronounce. The order of cetaceans comprises three families: the baleen whales, the sperm whales, and the dolphins — and it is among the last of these that the narwhals are classed. Each of these families divides into several genera, each genus into species, each species into varieties. Variety, species, genus, and family still eluded me, but I had no doubt of completing my classification with the help of heaven and Commander Farragut.

The crew was waiting impatiently for their chief's orders. He, after attentively studying the animal, sent for the engineer. The engineer came running.

"Sir," said the commander, "do you have your pressure up?"

"Yes, sir," the engineer answered.

"Good. Stoke your fires, and full steam ahead!"

Three cheers greeted this order. The hour of the fight had struck. A few moments later the frigate's two funnels were vomiting torrents of black smoke, and the deck shuddered under the trembling of the boilers.

Driven forward by her powerful propeller, the Abraham Lincoln headed straight at the animal. It let her come up to within half a cable-length, indifferently; then, disdaining to dive, it put on a little turn of flight and merely kept its distance.

This pursuit went on for about three quarters of an hour, without the frigate gaining a fathom on the cetacean. It was plain, then, that at this rate she would never overtake it.

Commander Farragut furiously twisted the thick tuft of hair that sprouted beneath his chin.

"Ned Land?" he called.

The Canadian came to the order.

"Well, Master Land," the commander asked, "do you still advise me to put my boats over the side?"

"No, sir," Ned Land answered, "because that beast won't be taken unless it chooses to be."

"Then what do we do?"

"Force the steam, if you can, sir. As for me — with your permission, that is — I'll go and post myself under the bobstays of the bowsprit, and if we come within harpoon-range, I harpoon."

"Go to it, Ned," Commander Farragut answered. "Engineer!" he shouted. "Get your pressure up."

Ned Land went off to his post. The fires were pushed harder; the propeller turned forty-three revolutions a minute, and the steam hissed out through the valves. The log was heaved, and it showed that the Abraham Lincoln was making eighteen and a half miles an hour.

But the cursed animal was also cruising along at eighteen and a half miles an hour.

For another hour the frigate held to that pace without gaining a single fathom. It was humiliating for one of the fastest ships in the American navy. A dull rage ran through the crew. The sailors cursed the monster, which for its part disdained to answer them. Commander Farragut no longer contented himself with twisting his little beard; he was biting it.

The engineer was called once again.

"You've reached your maximum pressure?" the commander asked him.

"Yes, sir," the engineer answered.

"And your valves are loaded?"

"To six and a half atmospheres."

"Load them to ten atmospheres."

There was an American order if ever there was one. They'd have done no better on the Mississippi to outrun a "competitor"!

"Conseil," I said to my good servant, who was standing near me, "do you realize we're probably going to blow up?"

"Just as Master pleases!" answered Conseil.

Well — I'll admit it — this was a chance I was not sorry to run.

The valves were loaded. The coal was shoveled into the furnaces. The ventilators sent torrents of air over the blazing fires. The Abraham Lincoln's speed climbed. Her masts trembled down to their very steps, and the swirling smoke could barely find a way out through the too-narrow funnels.

The log was heaved a second time.

"Well, helmsman?" asked Commander Farragut.

"Nineteen and three-tenths miles, sir."

"Stoke the fires harder."

The engineer obeyed. The pressure gauge read ten atmospheres. But the cetacean "got its steam up" too, no doubt, for without the least strain it went right on making its nineteen and three-tenths miles.

What a chase! No, I cannot describe the emotion that set my whole being trembling. Ned Land held his post, harpoon in hand. Several times the animal let itself be approached.

"We're gaining on it! We're gaining on it!" cried the Canadian.

Then, just as he was getting ready to strike, the cetacean would slip away at a speed I cannot reckon at less than thirty miles an hour. And even at our top speed, didn't it take the liberty of mocking the frigate by circling clean around her! A howl of fury broke from every chest.

By noon we were no further along than we had been at eight in the morning.

Commander Farragut then resolved to try more direct means.

"Ah!" he said. "So this animal goes faster than the Abraham Lincoln! Well then — we'll see whether it can outrun her conical shells. Master-at-arms, men to the forward gun."

The forecastle gun was loaded and trained at once. The shot went off, but the shell passed a few feet over the cetacean, which was holding at half a mile.

"Someone with a better aim!" cried the commander. "And five hundred dollars to whoever pierces that infernal beast!"

An old gunner with a gray beard — I can see him still — calm-eyed, cold of face, stepped up to his gun, brought it into position, and took a long aim. A heavy detonation burst out, mingled with the cheers of the crew.

The shell found its mark; it struck the animal, but not squarely, and glancing off its rounded surface, went to lose itself two miles out to sea.

"Well now!" said the old gunner, seething. "That rogue's plated with six-inch armor!"

"Damnation!" cried Commander Farragut.

The chase began again, and Commander Farragut, leaning toward me, said:

"I'll hound that animal until my frigate blows apart!"

"Yes," I answered, "and you'll be right to!"

We could hope the animal would wear itself out, that it wouldn't be as indifferent to fatigue as a steam engine. But nothing of the kind. The hours went by without its giving any sign of exhaustion.

Still, it must be said to the Abraham Lincoln's credit that she battled on with tireless tenacity. I reckon at no less than five hundred kilometers the distance she covered during that ill-fated day of November 6! But night came and wrapped the heaving ocean in its shadows.

At that moment I believed our expedition was over, and that we would never see the fantastic animal again. I was mistaken.

At ten fifty in the evening, the electric glow reappeared, three miles to windward of the frigate, as pure and as intense as during the previous night.

The narwhal seemed motionless. Perhaps, worn out by its day, it was sleeping, giving itself up to the swaying of the waves? Here was a chance that Commander Farragut resolved to seize.

He gave his orders. The Abraham Lincoln was held under low steam and advanced cautiously so as not to wake its adversary. It is not unusual to come upon whales fast asleep in the open ocean, and attack them then with success, and Ned Land had harpooned more than one in its sleep. The Canadian went back to take up his post under the bobstays of the bowsprit.

The frigate crept up without a sound, stopped two cable-lengths from the animal, and coasted on her own way. No one aboard breathed anymore. A deep silence reigned over the deck. We were not a hundred feet from the burning core, whose glow grew and dazzled our eyes.

At that moment, leaning over the rail of the forecastle, I saw below me Ned Land, clinging with one hand to the martingale stay, brandishing his terrible harpoon with the other. Scarcely twenty feet separated him from the motionless animal.

All at once his arm shot out violently, and the harpoon was hurled. I heard the ringing clang of the weapon, which seemed to have struck some hard body.

The electric glow went out suddenly, and two enormous waterspouts crashed down onto the frigate's deck, running like a torrent from bow to stern, knocking men off their feet, snapping the lashings of the spare spars.

A frightful shock followed, and, flung over the rail before I had time to catch hold of anything, I was hurled into the sea.
