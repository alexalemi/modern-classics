Accident or Incident?

The next morning, March 22, at six o'clock, the preparations for departure began. The last glimmers of twilight were melting away into night. The cold was biting. The constellations blazed with startling intensity. At the zenith glittered that magnificent Southern Cross, the polestar of the Antarctic regions.

The thermometer read twelve degrees below zero, and when the wind freshened it bit at us in sharp stings. The ice was thickening on the open water. The sea was beginning to freeze over on every side. Great blackish patches, spread across its surface, warned of the young ice about to form. Clearly, the southern basin, frozen through the six months of its winter, was utterly inaccessible. What became of the whales during that season? No doubt they went beneath the ice pack in search of more navigable seas. As for the seals and walruses, used to living in the harshest climates, they stayed on in these frozen waters. These animals have the instinct to dig holes in the ice-fields and keep them permanently open. They come to those holes to breathe; and once the birds, driven off by the cold, have migrated north, these marine mammals remain the sole masters of the polar continent.

Meanwhile the ballast tanks had filled, and the Nautilus was sinking slowly. At a depth of a thousand feet, it came to a stop. Its screw churned the water, and it drove due north at a speed of fifteen miles an hour. Toward evening it was already gliding beneath the immense frozen carapace of the ice pack.

The panels of the salon had been closed as a precaution, since the Nautilus's hull might strike some submerged block. So I spent the day writing up my notes. My mind was entirely given over to my memories of the Pole. We had reached that unreachable point without hardship, without danger, as though our floating railway car had simply slid along the rails of a track. And now the return had truly begun. Would it hold still more surprises like these in store for me? I believed it would, so inexhaustible is the parade of undersea marvels! Even so, in the five and a half months since chance had thrown us aboard this vessel, we had crossed fourteen thousand leagues, and along that course — longer than the earth's own Equator — how many incidents, curious or terrible, had graced our voyage: the hunt in the forests of Crespo, the grounding in the Torres Strait, the coral cemetery, the Ceylon fisheries, the Arabian tunnel, the fires of Santorini, the millions in Vigo Bay, Atlantis, the South Pole! All night long these memories, passing from dream to dream, never let my brain doze for an instant.

At three in the morning I was jolted awake by a violent shock. I had sat bolt upright in my bed and was listening in the darkness when I was flung headlong into the middle of the room. Clearly the Nautilus had struck something and heeled hard over.

I braced myself against the walls and dragged myself along the passageways to the salon, which was lit by its luminous ceiling. The furniture was overturned. Fortunately the display cases, firmly bolted at the base, had held fast. The pictures on the starboard side, following the shift in the vertical, were flattened against the tapestries, while those on the port side had swung a foot clear of the wall at their lower edge. So the Nautilus was lying over on its starboard side — and, what was more, completely motionless.

Inside I could hear footsteps and a confusion of voices. But Captain Nemo did not appear. Just as I was about to leave the salon, Ned Land and Conseil came in.

"What's happened?" I asked at once.

"I came to ask Master the very same thing," Conseil replied.

"A thousand devils!" cried the Canadian. "I know well enough what's happened! The Nautilus has struck, and judging by the list she's taking, I don't think she'll get out of it the way she did the first time, in the Torres Strait."

"But has she at least come back to the surface of the sea?" I asked.

"We don't know," Conseil answered.

"That's easy enough to find out," I said.

I checked the pressure gauge. To my great surprise, it showed a depth of three hundred and sixty meters.

"What does this mean?" I exclaimed.

"We'll have to ask Captain Nemo," said Conseil.

"But where do we find him?" asked Ned Land.

"Follow me," I told my two companions.

We left the salon. In the library, no one. At the central staircase, at the crew's quarters, no one. I supposed Captain Nemo must be stationed in the pilot's cage. The best thing was to wait. We all three went back to the salon.

I'll pass over the Canadian's grumbling. He had every excuse to lose his temper. I let him vent his ill humor to his heart's content, without answering him.

We had been waiting like this for twenty minutes, straining to catch the faintest sounds inside the Nautilus, when Captain Nemo came in. He did not seem to see us. His face, usually so impassive, betrayed a certain uneasiness. He studied the compass and the pressure gauge in silence, then laid his finger on a point of the chart, in that region representing the southern seas.

I did not want to interrupt him. Only some moments later, when he turned toward me, did I say to him — turning back on him an expression he himself had used in the Torres Strait:

"An incident, Captain?"

"No, Professor," he replied. "An accident this time."

"A serious one?"

"Perhaps."

"Is the danger immediate?"

"No."

"The Nautilus has run aground?"

"Yes."

"And this grounding came about...?"

"Through a whim of nature, not the incompetence of men. Not a single error was made in our maneuvers. And yet there's no preventing equilibrium from producing its effects. One may defy the laws of men, but one cannot resist the laws of nature."

An odd moment Captain Nemo had chosen to indulge in this philosophical reflection. In sum, his answer told me nothing.

"May I know, Captain," I asked him, "the cause of this accident?"

"An enormous block of ice, a whole mountain, has turned over," he answered. "When icebergs are undermined at their base by warmer waters or by repeated blows, their center of gravity rises. Then they flip clean over — they capsize. That is what happened. One of these blocks, as it rolled over, struck the Nautilus as she floated beneath the water. Then, sliding under her hull and lifting her with irresistible force, it carried her up into less dense layers, where she now lies on her side."

"But can't we free the Nautilus by emptying her tanks, so as to bring her back into balance?"

"That is exactly what is being done at this moment, Professor. You can hear the pumps at work. Look at the needle of the pressure gauge. It shows that the Nautilus is rising, but the block of ice is rising with her, and until some obstacle checks its upward movement, our position won't change."

Sure enough, the Nautilus was still taking the same list to starboard. No doubt she would right herself once the block itself came to a stop. But at that point, who could say whether we might not strike the underside of the ice pack — whether we might not find ourselves horribly crushed between the two frozen surfaces?

I turned over in my mind all the consequences of our situation. Captain Nemo never took his eyes off the pressure gauge. Since the iceberg's fall, the Nautilus had risen about a hundred and fifty feet, but she still held the same angle with the perpendicular.

Suddenly a slight movement made itself felt through the hull. Clearly the Nautilus was righting herself a little. The objects hanging in the salon were noticeably returning to their normal position. The walls were coming back toward the vertical. Not one of us spoke. With pounding hearts we watched, we felt the vessel come upright. The floor was leveling out beneath our feet. Ten minutes passed.

"At last, we're straight!" I exclaimed.

"Yes," said Captain Nemo, moving toward the salon door.

"But will we float?" I asked him.

"Certainly," he replied, "since the tanks aren't yet empty, and once they are, the Nautilus will have to rise back to the surface of the sea."

The captain went out, and I soon saw that, by his orders, the Nautilus's upward course had been halted. In fact she would soon have struck the underside of the ice pack, and it was better to keep her suspended between two waters.

"That was a close call!" said Conseil then.

"Yes. We might have been crushed between those blocks of ice, or at the very least imprisoned. And then, with no way to renew the air... Yes! That was a close call!"

"If it's over!" murmured Ned Land.

I had no wish to start a pointless argument with the Canadian, and I didn't answer. Besides, at that very moment the panels slid open, and the light from outside burst in through the uncovered window.

We were in open water, as I've said; but at a distance of ten meters, on either side of the Nautilus, rose a dazzling wall of ice. Above and below, the same wall. Above, because the underside of the ice pack stretched out like an immense ceiling. Below, because the capsized block, having gradually slid down, had found on the two side walls a pair of resting points that held it in this position. The Nautilus was imprisoned in a veritable tunnel of ice, roughly twenty meters wide, filled with still water. It would be easy enough for her to get out of it, then, by going either forward or astern, and to pick up again, a few hundred meters lower down, a clear passage beneath the ice pack.

The luminous ceiling had been switched off, and yet the salon still blazed with an intense light. The powerful reverberation of the ice walls was flinging the sheets of the beacon violently back into the room. I could never paint the effect of the voltaic rays on those great, whimsically cut blocks, each angle, each edge, each facet throwing off a different gleam according to the nature of the veins running through the ice. A dazzling mine of gemstones, and above all of sapphires crossing their blue jets with the green jet of emeralds. Here and there, opaline tints of infinite softness ran among burning points like so many diamonds of fire, whose brilliance the eye could not bear. The power of the beacon was multiplied a hundredfold, like that of a lamp shining through the lenticular panes of a first-order lighthouse.

"How beautiful! How beautiful!" cried Conseil.

"Yes!" I said. "It's a magnificent spectacle. Isn't it, Ned?"

"Eh! a thousand devils! yes," Ned Land shot back. "It's superb! I'm furious at being forced to admit it. No one's ever seen anything like it. But this spectacle here may cost us dear. And if I'm to say everything I think, I believe we're looking at things God meant to keep hidden from the eyes of man!"

Ned was right. It was too beautiful. All at once a cry from Conseil made me spin around.

"What is it?" I asked.

"Let Master shut his eyes! Let Master not look!"

As he said this, Conseil clapped his hands hard over his eyelids.

"But what's the matter, my boy?"

"I'm dazzled, blinded!"

My eyes turned involuntarily toward the window, but I couldn't bear the fire that was devouring it.

I understood what had happened. The Nautilus had just gotten under way at high speed. All the quiet flashes of the ice walls had suddenly turned into streaks of lightning. The fires of those myriad diamonds ran together. Carried along by her screw, the Nautilus was traveling through a sheath of lightning.

The panels of the salon then closed again. We kept our hands over our eyes, still steeped in those concentric glimmers that swim before the retina when the sun's rays have struck it too violently. It took a while for the disturbance in our sight to settle.

At last our hands came down.

"My word, I would never have believed it," said Conseil.

"And I still don't believe it!" the Canadian shot back.

"When we get back on land," Conseil added, "so jaded by all these wonders of nature, what will we think of those miserable continents and the little works turned out by the hand of man! No! the inhabited world is no longer worthy of us!"

Such words in the mouth of an unflappable Fleming showed just how far our enthusiasm had come to the boil. But the Canadian didn't fail to toss in his drop of cold water.

"The inhabited world!" he said, shaking his head. "Don't worry, friend Conseil, we won't be going back to it!"

It was then five o'clock in the morning. At that instant a shock came from the bow of the Nautilus. I understood that her ram had just struck a block of ice. It must have been a wrong maneuver, for this undersea tunnel, choked with blocks, made for no easy sailing. So I supposed that Captain Nemo, altering his course, would steer around these obstacles or follow the windings of the tunnel. In any case, the forward march could not be brought altogether to a halt. And yet, against my expectation, the Nautilus took a very pronounced backward movement.

"We're going astern?" said Conseil.

"Yes," I replied. "The tunnel must have no way out on this side."

"And so...?"

"So," I said, "the maneuver is simple enough. We'll retrace our steps and go out by the southern opening. That's all there is to it."

As I spoke this way, I wanted to appear more reassured than I really was. Meanwhile the Nautilus's backward movement was gathering speed, and driving against her screw, she was carrying us along at great pace.

"It'll mean a delay," said Ned.

"What does that matter — a few hours more or less, so long as we get out."

"Yes," Ned Land repeated, "so long as we get out!"

I paced for a while between the salon and the library. My companions sat in silence. Soon I threw myself down on a divan and picked up a book, which my eyes ran over mechanically.

A quarter of an hour later, Conseil came over to me and said:

"Is it very interesting, what Master is reading?"

"Very interesting," I replied.

"I don't doubt it. It's Master's own book that Master is reading!"

"My book?"

Sure enough, I was holding in my hand the work on The Mysteries of the Great Ocean Depths. I hadn't even realized it. I closed the book and resumed my pacing. Ned and Conseil rose to withdraw.

"Stay, my friends," I said, holding them back. "Let's stay together until we're out of this dead end."

"Just as Master pleases," Conseil replied.

A few hours went by. I often watched the instruments hanging on the salon wall. The pressure gauge showed that the Nautilus was holding at a constant depth of three hundred meters; the compass, that she was still steering south; the log, that she was moving at a speed of twenty miles an hour — an excessive speed in so cramped a space. But Captain Nemo knew he couldn't go too fast, and that now the minutes were worth centuries.

At twenty-five minutes past eight, a second shock came. From the stern, this time. I turned pale. My companions had drawn close to me. I had seized Conseil's hand. We questioned one another with our eyes, more directly than if words had put our thoughts into speech.

At that moment the captain came into the salon. I went up to him.

"The way is barred to the south?" I asked him.

"Yes, Professor. The iceberg, in turning over, has closed off every way out."

"We're blocked?"

"Yes."
