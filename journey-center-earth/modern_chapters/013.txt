Chapter 14

Stapi is a village of maybe thirty huts, built straight onto the lava under sunlight bounced back at it by the volcano. It sits at the head of a small fjord walled in by a rampart of the strangest kind.

As everyone knows, basalt is a brown rock of igneous origin, and it takes on regular shapes whose arrangement can catch you off guard. Here nature works like a geometer, building the way a human being would, as if she had wielded the square, the compass, and the plumb line. Everywhere else she makes her art out of great masses flung down without order, cones barely sketched, half-finished pyramids, and the odd, wandering run of her lines; but here, meaning to set an example of regularity, and getting there ahead of the very first architects, she created an order so severe that neither the splendors of Babylon nor the wonders of Greece ever surpassed it.

I'd certainly heard of the Giant's Causeway in Ireland and of Fingal's Cave in one of the Hebrides, but I had never yet laid eyes on a structure built out of basalt.

At Stapi, though, the phenomenon rose up in all its beauty.

The wall of the fjord, like the whole coast of the peninsula, was made of a row of vertical columns, thirty feet high. These upright shafts, of a flawless proportion, held up an arch of horizontal columns whose overhang formed a half-vault above the sea. At certain intervals, under this natural canopy, the eye caught pointed, arched openings of admirable design, through which the waves of the open water came rushing in and breaking into foam. A few chunks of basalt, torn loose by the fury of the ocean, lay stretched out along the ground like the wreckage of some ancient temple — ruins forever young, over which the centuries pass without leaving a mark.

Such was the last stage of our overland journey. Hans had guided us here with real skill, and it steadied me a little to remember that he was still coming along with us.

As we reached the door of the rector's house — a plain, low cabin, no finer and no more comfortable than its neighbors — I saw a man shoeing a horse, hammer in hand and a leather apron slung around his waist.

"Saelvertu," the hunter said to him.

"God dag," the blacksmith answered in perfect Danish.

"Kyrkoherde," said Hans, turning to my uncle.

"The rector!" my uncle repeated. "It seems, Axel, that this good fellow is the rector."

Meanwhile the guide was filling the kyrkoherde in on our situation. The man set down his work, gave a sort of shout — the kind exchanged, no doubt, between horses and horse-traders — and at once a great shrew of a woman came out of the cabin. If she didn't stand a full six feet, she came very close.

I was afraid she might come offer the travelers the Icelandic kiss; but nothing of the sort happened, and she showed rather little grace, in fact, about letting us into her house.

The strangers' room struck me as the worst in the whole parsonage — narrow, filthy, and reeking. We had to make do with it; the rector clearly did not practice the hospitality of the ancients. Far from it. Before the day was out, I could see we were dealing with a blacksmith, a fisherman, a hunter, and a carpenter, and not at all with a minister of the Lord. It was a weekday, true enough. Perhaps he made up for it on Sundays.

I don't want to speak ill of these poor priests, who are, after all, in a wretched position: the Danish government pays them a ridiculous salary and lets them collect a quarter of their parish's tithe, which comes to no more than sixty marks a year — a paltry sum. Hence the need to work in order to live; but between fishing, hunting, and shoeing horses, a man ends up taking on the manners, the tone, and the ways of hunters, fishermen, and other fairly rough sorts. That very evening I noticed that our host did not count sobriety among his virtues.

My uncle quickly grasped what kind of man he was dealing with. Instead of a decent, dignified scholar, he had found a heavy, coarse peasant, and so he resolved to begin his great expedition as soon as possible and get away from this inhospitable parish. Heedless of his own fatigue, he decided to go spend a few days on the mountain.

The preparations for departure were therefore made the very day after we arrived at Stapi. Hans hired the services of three Icelanders to take the place of the horses in carrying the baggage; but once we reached the bottom of the crater, these locals were to turn back and leave us to ourselves. That point was firmly settled.

At this juncture, my uncle had to inform the hunter that his intention was to press the exploration of the volcano to its very limits.

Hans merely nodded. Going there or going anywhere, plunging into the bowels of his island or crossing its surface — he saw no difference. As for me, distracted until now by the incidents of the trip, I had rather forgotten what lay ahead; but now I felt the dread take hold of me again, worse than ever. What could I do? If I'd ever had a chance of resisting Professor Lidenbrock, it was back in Hamburg, not here at the foot of the Sneffels.

One idea in particular gnawed at me hard — a terrifying idea, enough to rattle nerves far steadier than mine.

"Let's see," I said to myself. "We're going to climb the Sneffels. Fine. We're going to visit its crater. Good. Others have done that and lived. But that's not all. If there turns out to be a passage leading down into the bowels of the earth, if that miserable Saknussemm told the truth, we're going to lose ourselves in the volcano's underground galleries. And what says the Sneffels is really extinct? Who's to prove an eruption isn't brewing? Just because the monster has slept since 1229, does it follow that it can't wake up? And if it wakes, what becomes of us?"

That was worth thinking over, and think it over I did. I couldn't sleep without dreaming of eruptions; and the part of a bit of slag seemed a pretty brutal one to play.

At last I couldn't stand it any longer. I resolved to lay the case before my uncle as tactfully as I could, in the form of a perfectly impossible hypothesis.

I went to find him. I told him my fears, and then I stepped back to let him blow up in peace.

"I was thinking about that," he answered simply.

What did those words mean? Was he actually going to listen to reason? Was he thinking of calling off his plans? That would be too good to be true.

After a few moments of silence, during which I didn't dare question him, he went on:

"I was thinking about it. Ever since we got to Stapi, I've been turning over the serious question you've just put to me, because we mustn't act rashly."

"No," I said with feeling.

"The Sneffels has been silent for six hundred years, but it can speak again. Now, eruptions are always preceded by phenomena that are perfectly well understood. So I've questioned the people of the country, I've studied the ground, and I can tell you this, Axel: there will be no eruption."

At this pronouncement I stood dumbfounded and couldn't say a word.

"You doubt what I say?" said my uncle. "Well, then — follow me."

I obeyed mechanically. Leaving the parsonage, the Professor took a direct path that led, through a gap in the basalt wall, away from the sea. Soon we were out in open country, if you can give that name to an immense heap of volcanic debris. The land looked as though it had been crushed under a rain of enormous stones — trap, basalt, granite, and every kind of pyroxene rock.

Here and there I saw fumaroles rising into the air; these white vapors, called reykir in Icelandic, came from the hot springs, and their violence was a sign of the ground's volcanic activity. To me this seemed to justify my fears. So I was knocked flat when my uncle said:

"You see all that smoke, Axel? Well, it proves we have nothing to fear from the volcano's fury!"

"You can't be serious!" I cried.

"Get this straight," the Professor went on. "As an eruption approaches, these fumaroles redouble their activity, only to vanish completely for the duration of the event — because the elastic fluids, no longer under the necessary pressure, take the road of the craters instead of escaping through the fissures in the globe. So if these vapors hold steady in their usual state, if their energy doesn't increase, and if you add to that observation that the wind and rain aren't giving way to a heavy, still air, then you can be sure there'll be no eruption anytime soon."

"But —"

"Enough. When science has spoken, one has only to hold one's tongue."

I came back to the parsonage with my ears drooping. My uncle had beaten me with scientific arguments. Still, I had one hope left — that once we reached the bottom of the crater, it would be impossible, for lack of any gallery, to go down any deeper, in spite of all the Saknussemms in the world.

I spent the next night in one long nightmare, in the heart of a volcano and deep in the earth; I felt myself hurled off into planetary space in the form of an erupted rock.

The next day, June 23rd, Hans was waiting for us with his companions, loaded down with the provisions, the tools, and the instruments. Two iron-tipped staffs, two rifles, and two cartridge belts were set aside for my uncle and me. Hans, a cautious man, had added a full water-skin to our baggage which, together with our flasks, guaranteed us water for eight days.

It was nine o'clock in the morning. The rector and his towering shrew were waiting in front of their door. No doubt they meant to bid us the host's final farewell to the traveler. But this farewell took the unexpected form of a formidable bill, on which they'd charged us for the very air of the parsonage — foul air, I dare say. This worthy couple was fleecing us like a Swiss innkeeper and putting a fine price on their overrated hospitality.

My uncle paid without haggling. A man setting out for the center of the earth wasn't going to quibble over a few rixdales.

That settled, Hans gave the signal to leave, and a few moments later we had left Stapi behind.
