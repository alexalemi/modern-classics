Chapter 42

I suppose it must have been about ten o'clock at night. The first of my senses to work again after that last assault was hearing. Almost at once—for it truly was an act of hearing—I heard silence fall in the gallery, coming after those roars that had filled my ears for long hours. At last my uncle's words reached me like a murmur:

"We're going up!"

"What do you mean?" I cried.

"Yes, we're going up! We're going up!"

I stretched out my arm; I touched the wall; my hand came away bloody. We were rising at tremendous speed.

"The torch! The torch!" cried the professor.

Hans, not without difficulty, managed to light it, and although the flame bent downward against the upward rush, it threw enough light to show the whole scene.

"It's just as I thought," said my uncle. "We're in a narrow well, no more than four fathoms across. The water, having reached the bottom of the chasm, is finding its level again and carrying us up with it."

"Where to?"

"I don't know, but we have to be ready for anything. We're rising at a speed I'd put at two fathoms a second—that's a hundred and twenty fathoms a minute, or more than three and a half leagues an hour. At that rate, you cover ground."

"Yes—if nothing stops us, if this well has an outlet! But if it's plugged, if the air is squeezed tighter and tighter under the pressure of the column of water, if we're about to be crushed—"

"Axel," the professor answered with great calm, "our situation is almost hopeless, but there are a few chances of coming through, and those are the ones I'm weighing. If we may die at any moment, we may also be saved at any moment. So let's be ready to make the most of the slightest opening."

"But what do we do?"

"Rebuild our strength by eating."

At these words I stared at my uncle with a haggard eye. What I had not wanted to admit, I had to say at last:

"Eat?" I repeated.

"Yes, without delay."

The professor added a few words in Danish. Hans shook his head.

"What!" cried my uncle. "Our provisions are lost?"

"Yes—here's all the food that's left! One scrap of dried meat for the three of us!"

My uncle looked at me, refusing to understand my words.

"Well!" I said. "Do you still believe we can be saved?"

My question got no answer.

An hour went by. I was beginning to feel a violent hunger. My companions were suffering too, and not one of us dared touch that miserable remnant of food.

Meanwhile we kept rising fast; sometimes the air cut off our breath, the way it does for balloonists who climb too quickly. But whereas they feel a cold that grows sharper as they mount through the layers of the atmosphere, we were undergoing the exact opposite. The heat was building in an alarming way, and had surely reached forty degrees by now.

What did such a change mean? Until now the facts had borne out the theories of Davy and Lidenbrock; until now special conditions of refractory rock, of electricity, of magnetism had bent the general laws of nature, keeping the temperature around us moderate—for the theory of central fire remained, in my eyes, the only true one, the only one that could be explained. Were we now returning to a region where those forces played out in all their fury, where the heat melted rock to a state of complete fusion? I feared so, and I said to the professor:

"If we're not drowned or dashed to pieces, if we don't die of hunger, we still stand a chance of being burned alive."

He only shrugged and sank back into his thoughts.

An hour passed. And apart from a slight rise in the temperature, nothing changed the situation. At last my uncle broke the silence.

"Look," he said, "we have to make a decision."

"Make a decision?" I echoed.

"Yes. We must rebuild our strength. If we try to stretch our lives out a few more hours by rationing this last bit of food, we'll be weak right to the end."

"Yes—right to the end, which won't keep us waiting long."

"Well! Suppose a chance of rescue turns up, suppose a moment for action is called for—where will we find the strength to act, if we've let ourselves grow feeble with starvation?"

"Oh, uncle—once this scrap of meat is devoured, what will we have left?"

"Nothing, Axel, nothing; but will it feed you any better to eat it with your eyes? You're reasoning like a man without will, a creature without energy!"

"Then you don't despair?" I cried, exasperated.

"No!" the professor answered firmly.

"What! You still believe in some chance of rescue?"

"Yes! Absolutely, yes! And so long as a man's heart beats, so long as his flesh quivers, I won't allow that any creature endowed with will should give despair a place inside him."

What words! The man who spoke them under such circumstances was surely of no common temper.

"So," I said, "what do you intend to do?"

"Eat what's left of the food down to the last crumb, and rebuild our lost strength. This meal will be our last, so be it! But at least, instead of being spent, we'll have become men again."

"Well then—let's devour it!" I cried.

My uncle took the piece of meat and the few biscuits that had survived the wreck; he made three equal portions and handed them out. That came to about a pound of food apiece. The professor ate greedily, with a kind of feverish frenzy; I ate without pleasure, despite my hunger, and almost with disgust; Hans ate quietly, in moderation, chewing small mouthfuls without a sound and savoring them with the calm of a man whom no worry about the future could trouble. Rummaging carefully, he had found a flask half full of genever; he offered it to us, and that reviving liquor had the power to bring me back a little.

"Förträfflig!" said Hans, drinking in his turn.

"Excellent!" my uncle shot back.

I had recovered some hope. But our last meal was over. It was five o'clock in the morning by now.

Man is so made that his health is a purely negative thing; once the need to eat is satisfied, one can hardly picture the horrors of hunger; you have to feel them to understand them. And so, coming out of a long fast, a few mouthfuls of biscuit and meat triumphed over our past sufferings.

Still, after this meal, each of us drifted into his own thoughts. What was Hans thinking of—this man of the far West, ruled by the fatalist resignation of the Orientals? As for me, my thoughts were made of nothing but memories, and they carried me back to the surface of this globe I never should have left. The house on König-strasse, my poor Graüben, good Martha, passed before my eyes like visions, and in the dismal rumblings that ran through the rock mass I thought I caught the noise of the cities of the earth.

As for my uncle, "forever at his work," torch in hand, he was studying the nature of the ground with great attention; he was trying to make out where we stood by observing the layers stacked above one another. This calculation, or rather this rough estimate, could only be very approximate; but a scientist is always a scientist when he manages to keep his composure, and the professor Lidenbrock certainly possessed that quality to an uncommon degree.

I could hear him muttering words of geological science; I understood them, and in spite of myself I took an interest in this supreme study.

"Eruptive granite," he was saying; "we're still in the primordial epoch; but we're going up! We're going up! Who knows?"

Who knows? He still hoped. With his hand he felt the vertical wall, and a few moments later he went on:

"Here's the gneiss! Here are the mica schists! Good! Soon we'll reach the strata of the transition epoch, and then—"

What did the professor mean? Could he measure the thickness of the earth's crust hanging over our heads? Did he have any means of making that calculation? No. He had no manometer, and no rough guess could take its place.

Meanwhile the temperature was climbing steeply, and I felt myself steeped in a scorching atmosphere. I could only compare it to the heat thrown off by the furnaces of a foundry at pouring time. Little by little, Hans, my uncle, and I had had to shed our jackets and waistcoats; the slightest garment became a cause of discomfort, not to say suffering.

"Are we rising toward some blazing furnace?" I cried, at a moment when the heat redoubled.

"No," my uncle answered, "it's impossible! It's impossible!"

"And yet," I said, feeling the wall, "this wall is burning hot!"

Just as I spoke these words, my hand happened to brush the water, and I had to snatch it back as fast as I could.

"The water is boiling hot!" I cried.

The professor, this time, answered only with a gesture of anger.

Then an unconquerable terror seized my brain and never let go. I had the sense of an imminent catastrophe, one such as the boldest imagination could not have conceived. An idea, at first vague and uncertain, was hardening into a certainty in my mind. I pushed it away, but it came back stubbornly. I didn't dare put it into words. Yet a few involuntary observations settled my conviction; by the doubtful light of the torch I noticed wild, disordered movements in the granite layers; a phenomenon was clearly about to occur, one in which electricity played a part; and then this excessive heat, this boiling water!... I resolved to check the compass.

It had gone mad!
