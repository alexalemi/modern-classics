Chapter 35

Friday, August 21.—By morning the magnificent geyser had vanished. The wind had freshened and carried us swiftly away from Axel Islet. The bellowing died out little by little.

The weather, if I may put it that way, is about to change. The air is thickening with vapors, and those vapors carry with them the electricity generated by the evaporation of the saline waters. The clouds sink noticeably lower and take on a uniform olive tint; the electric rays can barely pierce this opaque curtain, dropped over the stage where the drama of the tempests is about to be played.

I feel strangely stirred, the way every creature on the surface is stirred at the approach of some cataclysm. The cumulus clouds—those great rounded masses—piled up in the south have a sinister look; they wear that "pitiless" aspect I have so often noticed at the start of a storm. The air is heavy, the sea is calm.

Far off, the clouds look like huge bales of cotton heaped up in picturesque disorder; slowly they swell, and what they gain in size they lose in number; their weight is such that they cannot tear themselves free of the horizon; but under the breath of the higher currents they gradually melt together, darken, and soon form a single layer of dreadful aspect. Now and then a wisp of vapor, still lit, bounces across that grayish carpet and is quickly lost in the opaque mass.

Clearly the atmosphere is saturated with electric fluid. I am steeped in it myself; my hair stands up on my head as it would near an electric machine. It seems to me that if my companions were to touch me at this moment, they would take a violent shock.

At ten in the morning the signs of the storm grow more decisive; the wind seems to slacken, as though only to catch its breath the better; the cloud is like an immense wineskin in which hurricanes are gathering.

I don't want to believe the sky's threats, and yet I can't stop myself from saying:

"Here's some foul weather brewing."

The Professor doesn't answer. He is in a murderous mood, watching the ocean stretch on without end before his eyes. He only shrugs at my words.

"We're in for a storm," I say, throwing out my hand toward the horizon. "Those clouds are sinking down onto the sea as though to crush it!"

Silence, all around. The wind falls still. Nature has the look of a corpse and breathes no more. On the mast, where I can already see a faint St. Elmo's fire beginning to glimmer, the slackened sail hangs in heavy folds. The raft lies motionless in the middle of a thick, unrippling sea. But if we're no longer moving, what good is it to keep this canvas up—the very thing that could send us to our doom at the first blow of the tempest?

"Let's haul it down," I say. "Let's strike the mast—that would be the prudent thing."

"No, by the devil!" my uncle cries. "A hundred times no! Let the wind seize us! Let the storm carry us off! But let me finally lay eyes on the rocks of a shore, even if our raft should smash itself to a thousand pieces against them!"

The words are barely out of his mouth when the southern horizon changes all at once. The gathered vapors dissolve into water, and the air—drawn violently in to fill the void left by the condensation—turns itself into a hurricane. It comes howling from the farthest recesses of the cavern. The darkness deepens. I can barely take a few broken notes.

The raft heaves up; it leaps. My uncle is flung from his feet. I crawl over to him. He has clamped himself hard to a length of cable and seems to be watching this spectacle of the unchained elements with something like pleasure.

Hans doesn't stir. His long hair, driven forward by the hurricane and thrown across his motionless face, gives him a strange look, for every strand of it is bristling with tiny luminous plumes. His terrifying mask is that of an antediluvian man, a contemporary of the Ichthyosaurs and the Megatheriums.

Still the mast holds. The sail stretches taut like a bubble about to burst. The raft races along at a speed I cannot begin to measure—yet slower even than the drops of water flung out beneath it, whose swiftness draws them into clean, straight lines.

"The sail! The sail!" I shout, gesturing to bring it down.

"No!" my uncle answers.

"Nej," says Hans, gently shaking his head.

But now the rain forms a roaring cataract across that horizon toward which we're rushing like madmen. Before it can reach us, though, the veil of cloud tears open, the sea boils up, and the electricity—produced by some vast chemical action working in the upper layers—is let loose. With the crashes of thunder mingle the flashing jets of the lightning; countless bolts cross and recross in the middle of the detonations; the mass of vapor turns incandescent; the hailstones that strike the metal of our tools and our weapons glow with light; the heaving waves seem so many fire-spitting hillocks, each with an inner fire smoldering beneath it, each crest plumed with a flame.

Sunday, August 23.—Where are we? Swept along with an incomparable speed.

The night was appalling. The storm does not let up. We live in a world of noise, an unbroken detonation. Our ears bleed. Not a word can be exchanged.

The lightning never stops. I see zigzags that leap upward, then double back from below and go striking the granite vault. What if it should come crashing down! Other bolts fork, or take the shape of globes of fire that burst like bombs. The general roar doesn't seem to grow any louder for it; it has passed the limit of intensity the human ear can register, and if all the powder-magazines in the world were to blow up together, we could hear nothing more.

There is a continuous emission of light from the surface of the clouds; the electric matter pours ceaselessly out of their very molecules; plainly the gaseous elements of the air have been altered; innumerable columns of water shoot up into the atmosphere and fall back foaming.

Where are we going?... My uncle lies stretched out full length at the end of the raft.

The heat redoubles. I look at the thermometer; it reads... [The figure is blotted out.]

Monday, August 24.—Will this never end? Why shouldn't this dense state of the atmosphere, once altered, be permanent?

We are broken with exhaustion. Hans is as he always is. The raft runs steadily on toward the southeast. We have covered more than two hundred leagues since Axel Islet.

At noon the fury of the hurricane doubles. We have to lash down every piece of the cargo. Each of us ties himself fast as well. The waves break clean over our heads.

For three days it has been impossible to say a single word to one another. We open our mouths, we move our lips; no sound worth the name comes out. Even mouth to ear, we cannot make ourselves heard.

My uncle has come close to me. He mouthed a few words. I think he said: "We are lost." I am not sure of it.

I decide to write these words for him: "Let's haul down our sail."

He signs that he consents.

His head has not had time to nod back up before a disk of fire appears at the edge of the raft. Mast and sail are carried off all in one piece, and I saw them lifted to a prodigious height, like the Pterodactyl, that fantastic bird of the earliest ages.

We are frozen with terror. The ball—half white, half azure, the size of a ten-inch shell—drifts slowly along, spinning with astonishing speed under the lash of the hurricane. It comes here, there, climbs onto one of the raft's timbers, leaps onto the provision sack, drops lightly down again, bounds, grazes the powder-case. Horror! We're about to be blown up! No! The dazzling disk veers away; it approaches Hans, who stares at it without flinching; my uncle, who throws himself to his knees to dodge it; me, pale and shivering under the blaze of the light and the heat; it pirouettes near my foot, which I try to pull back. I can't manage it.

A smell of nitrous gas fills the air; it gets into my throat, into my lungs. We're choking.

Why can't I pull my foot back? Is it riveted to the raft? Ah—the fall of that electric globe has magnetized all the iron aboard; the instruments, the tools, the weapons rattle and knock together with a shrill clatter; the nails of my boot cling violently to a plate of iron set into the wood. I can't pull my foot free!

At last, with one violent effort, I tear it loose just as the ball was about to seize it in its spinning course and drag me off along with it, if—

Ah! What blinding light! The globe bursts! We are drenched in jets of flame!

Then everything goes dark. I had just time to see my uncle stretched out on the raft, and Hans still at his tiller, "spitting fire" under the electricity coursing through him!
