Chapter 40

Since the beginning of the journey I had lived through no end of astonishments; I might have believed myself safe from further surprises, past being amazed by anything. And yet, at the sight of those two letters carved there three hundred years ago, I stood in a stupor that bordered on idiocy. Not only was the learned alchemist's signature there to be read on the rock, but the very stylus that had traced it lay in my hands. Short of the most stubborn bad faith, I could no longer doubt the traveler's existence or the reality of his journey.

While these thoughts whirled through my head, Professor Lidenbrock gave himself over to a fit of praise for Arne Saknussemm that was frankly a touch operatic.

"Marvelous genius!" he cried. "You forgot nothing that might open the roads of the earth's crust to other mortals, and your fellow men can pick up the traces your feet left, three centuries ago, at the bottom of these dark passages! To other eyes than yours you reserved the sight of these wonders! Your name, carved stage by stage, leads the traveler bold enough to follow you straight to his goal, and at the very center of our planet he will find it inscribed once more in your own hand. Well then! I too will go and sign my name on this last page of granite! But from this moment on, let this cape—seen by you, beside this sea discovered by you—be forever called Cape Saknussemm!"

That is what I heard, or something close to it, and I felt myself seized by the enthusiasm those words breathed. An inner fire flared to life in my chest! I forgot everything—the dangers of the journey, the perils of the return. What another man had done, I meant to do as well, and nothing that was human seemed impossible to me!

"Onward, onward!" I cried.

I was already flinging myself toward the dark gallery when the Professor stopped me, and he—the man of hot tempers—counseled me patience and a cool head.

"Let's go back to Hans first," he said, "and bring the raft to this spot."

I obeyed the order, though not without an effort, and slipped quickly among the rocks of the shore.

"You know, Uncle," I said as we walked, "that we've been remarkably well served by circumstances so far!"

"Ah! You think so, Axel?"

"No doubt about it—right down to the storm, which put us back on the right road. Bless the tempest! It carried us back to this coast that fair weather would have taken us away from! Suppose for a moment that our prow"—the prow of a raft!—"had touched the southern shores of the Lidenbrock Sea. What would have become of us? Saknussemm's name would never have appeared before our eyes, and we'd be stranded now on a beach with no way out."

"Yes, Axel, there's something providential in the fact that, sailing south, we should have come back precisely to the north and to Cape Saknussemm. I have to admit it's more than astonishing, and there's a fact here whose explanation escapes me entirely."

"Bah! What does it matter! You don't explain facts—you profit from them!"

"No doubt, my boy, but—"

"But now we're going to take up the road north again, pass beneath the northern lands of Europe—Sweden, Russia, Siberia, who knows!—instead of burrowing under the deserts of Africa or the waves of the ocean, and I don't want to know any more than that!"

"Yes, Axel, you're right, and all is for the best, since we're leaving behind this horizontal sea that could lead nowhere. We're going to descend, descend again, and keep descending! Do you realize that to reach the center of the globe there are only fifteen hundred leagues left to cross!"

"Bah!" I cried. "That's really not worth talking about! Let's go! Let's go!"

These mad speeches were still running on when we rejoined the hunter. Everything was ready for an immediate departure; not a single package remained unloaded. We took our places on the raft, and with the sail hoisted, Hans steered along the coast toward Cape Saknussemm.

The wind was not favorable to a craft that could not sail close to it. So in many places we had to push ahead with the iron-tipped poles. Often the rocks, stretching out just below the surface, forced us into long detours. At last, after three hours of sailing—that is, toward six in the evening—we reached a spot suitable for landing.

I leaped ashore, followed by my uncle and the Icelander. The crossing had not calmed me. On the contrary, I even proposed that we burn "our boats," so as to cut off any retreat. But my uncle opposed the idea. I found him singularly lukewarm.

"At least," I said, "let's set off without losing a moment."

"Yes, my boy; but first, let's examine this new gallery, so we know whether we need to get the ladders ready."

My uncle set his Ruhmkorff apparatus going; the raft, tied to the shore, was left to itself—besides, the mouth of the gallery was not twenty paces off, and our little party, with me at its head, made for it without delay.

The opening, roughly circular, was about five feet across. The dark tunnel had been cut through the living rock and carefully bored smooth by the eruptive matter it had once given passage to; its lower edge came flush with the ground, so that one could enter without the slightest difficulty.

We were following an almost level floor when, after six paces, our march was cut short by an enormous block set squarely across our path.

"Cursed rock!" I cried in a fury, seeing myself suddenly halted by an impassable obstacle.

Search as we might, right and left, high and low, there was no passage, no branching. I felt a keen disappointment, and I refused to accept that the obstacle was real. I bent down. I looked underneath the block. Not a crack. Above it. The same wall of granite. Hans played the lamp's light over every point of the surface; but it offered no break, no gap at all.

We had to give up all hope of getting through.

I had sat down on the ground; my uncle was pacing the corridor with great strides.

"But then Saknussemm?" I cried.

"Yes," said my uncle. "Was he too stopped by this door of stone?"

"No! No!" I shot back, quick and sharp. "This piece of rock, by some shock or other—or one of those magnetic phenomena that shake the earth's crust—suddenly closed this passage. Many years went by between Saknussemm's return and the fall of this block. Isn't it obvious that this gallery was once the road of the lavas, and that the eruptive matter flowed freely through it back then? Look—there are fresh fissures scoring this granite ceiling; it's made of pieces fitted together, of enormous stones, as if the hand of some giant had labored at this masonry. But one day the pressure grew too great, and this block, like a keystone that gives way, slid all the way to the floor, blocking every passage. Here's an accidental obstacle that Saknussemm never met, and if we don't bring it down, we're unworthy to reach the center of the world!"

That is how I was talking! The Professor's whole soul had passed into me. The genius of discovery inspired me. I forgot the past, I scorned the future. Nothing existed for me any longer on the surface of this spheroid into whose depths I had plunged—not the cities, not the countryside, not Hamburg, not König-strasse, not my poor Graüben, who must believe me lost forever in the bowels of the earth.

"Well then!" my uncle went on. "With pickaxe, with pick, let's cut our road and bring down these walls!"

"It's too hard for the pick," I cried.

"Then the pickaxe!"

"It's too long for the pickaxe!"

"But—!"

"Well then—powder! A mine! Let's mine it and blow up the obstacle!"

"Powder!"

"Yes! It's only a scrap of rock to break!"

"Hans, to work!" cried my uncle.

The Icelander went back to the raft and soon returned with a pick, which he used to hollow out a mine chamber. It was no small task. We had to drill a hole large enough to hold fifty pounds of guncotton, whose expansive force is four times greater than that of gunpowder.

I was in a prodigious state of overexcitement. While Hans worked, I helped my uncle busily prepare a long fuse made of wetted powder packed into a tube of cloth.

"We'll get through!" I kept saying.

"We'll get through," my uncle repeated.

By midnight our work as miners was entirely finished; the charge of guncotton lay buried in the chamber, and the fuse, unrolling along the gallery, came out into the open.

A single spark was now enough to set this formidable engine going.

"Until tomorrow," said the Professor.

I had to resign myself and wait yet another six long hours!
