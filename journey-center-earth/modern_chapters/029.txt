Chapter 30

At first I saw nothing. My eyes, unused to the light, snapped shut. When I managed to open them again, I was left even more stunned than amazed.

"The sea!" I cried.

"Yes," my uncle answered, "the Lidenbrock Sea; and I like to think no navigator will ever dispute my honor of having discovered it, or my right to name it after myself!"

A vast sheet of water — the beginning of a lake or an ocean — stretched away beyond the limits of sight. The shore, broadly notched with inlets, offered the last rippling waves a fine golden sand, strewn with those little shells that once housed the very first living things of creation. The waves broke on it with that peculiar resonant murmur you hear in enclosed, immense spaces; a light spray flew up on the breath of a steady wind, and a few flecks of it reached my face. On that gently sloping beach, about a hundred fathoms back from the line of the waves, rose the buttresses of enormous rocks that climbed, widening as they went, to an immeasurable height. Some of them, tearing at the shoreline with their sharp ridges, formed capes and promontories gnawed by the teeth of the surf. Farther off, the eye followed their mass, clearly outlined against the misty depths of the horizon.

It was a real ocean, with the capricious contours of any earthly coastline — but deserted, and frighteningly wild in its aspect.

If my gaze could roam so far out over that sea, it was because a "special" light lit up its every detail. Not the light of the sun, with its dazzling shafts and the splendid outpouring of its rays; nor the pale, vague glow of the moon, which is only a reflection, without heat. No. The illuminating power of this light, its trembling diffusion, its clear, dry whiteness, the low level of its temperature, its brilliance in truth greater than the moon's — all of it plainly declared an origin that was purely electrical. It was like an aurora borealis, a continuous cosmic phenomenon, filling this cavern that was big enough to hold an ocean.

The vault hanging over my head — the sky, if you like — seemed made of great clouds, shifting, changing vapors that, by the effect of condensation, must on certain days dissolve into torrential rains. I would have thought that under so heavy a pressure of the atmosphere the water could not evaporate at all, and yet, by some physical reason that escaped me, there were broad banks of cloud spread out in the air. But for now "the weather was fine." The electric sheets produced astonishing plays of light on the very high clouds; sharp shadows sketched themselves in the coils beneath them, and often, between two parted layers, a ray would slide down to us with remarkable intensity. But it was not the sun, after all, since its light had no heat. The effect was sad, and supremely melancholy. Instead of a firmament shining with stars, I felt above those clouds a vault of granite crushing me with all its weight — and this space, immense as it was, would not have been enough for the promenade of even the least ambitious of satellites.

I remembered then the theory of an English captain who compared the earth to a vast hollow sphere, inside which the air stayed luminous under its own pressure, while two heavenly bodies, Pluto and Proserpine, traced their mysterious orbits within. Could he have been right?

We were truly imprisoned in an enormous hollow. Its width could not be judged, since the shore widened out until it was lost from view; nor its length, for the eye was soon stopped by a slightly hazy line of horizon. As for its height, it must have exceeded several leagues. Where did that vault rest on its granite buttresses? The eye could not make it out; but there was one cloud suspended in the atmosphere whose elevation had to be reckoned at two thousand fathoms — an altitude greater than that of earthly vapors, and due, no doubt, to the considerable density of the air.

The word "cavern" plainly does not convey what I mean, in trying to paint this immense space. But the words of human language cannot serve anyone who ventures into the abysses of the globe.

Nor did I know by what geological fact to explain the existence of such a hollow. Could the cooling of the globe have produced it? I knew well enough, from travelers' accounts, of certain famous caverns, but none of them came near such dimensions.

The grotto of Guácharo, in Colombia, visited by Monsieur de Humboldt, had not yielded up the secret of its depth to the scholar who explored it over a span of two thousand five hundred feet, though it probably did not extend much beyond that. The immense Mammoth Cave in Kentucky was of gigantic proportions, to be sure, since its vault rose five hundred feet above a fathomless lake, and travelers roamed through it for more than ten leagues without reaching the end. But what were these hollows next to the one I was gazing at now, with its sky of vapors, its electric radiations, and a vast sea shut up within its flanks? My imagination felt powerless before such immensity.

All these marvels I contemplated in silence. Words failed me to express what I felt. I seemed to be witnessing, on some distant planet — Uranus or Neptune — phenomena that my "earthly" nature had no notion of. New sensations demanded new words, and my imagination would not supply them. I looked, I thought, I marveled, with a stupefaction mingled with a certain measure of dread.

The sheer surprise of the spectacle had brought the colors of health back into my face; I was treating myself with astonishment, working my own cure by means of this new therapy — and besides, the liveliness of a very dense air revived me, supplying more oxygen to my lungs.

You will easily understand that after forty-seven days shut up in a narrow gallery, it was an infinite pleasure to breathe in that breeze, laden with damp, salty emanations.

And so I had no cause to regret leaving my dark grotto behind. My uncle, already used to these marvels, was no longer astonished.

"Do you feel strong enough to walk a little?" he asked me.

"Yes, certainly," I replied. "Nothing would please me more."

"Well then, take my arm, Axel, and let's follow the windings of the shore."

I accepted eagerly, and we began to skirt this new ocean. On the left, sheer rocks, clambered one atop another, formed a titanic heap of prodigious effect. Down their sides ran countless waterfalls, spilling in limpid, ringing sheets; a few light vapors, leaping from rock to rock, marked the place of hot springs, and streams flowed gently toward the common basin, seeking on the slopes any chance to murmur more pleasantly.

Among these streams I recognized our faithful traveling companion, the Hansbach, which came to lose itself quietly in the sea, as if it had never done anything else since the beginning of the world.

"We'll miss it from now on," I said with a sigh.

"Bah!" answered the professor. "This one or that one, what does it matter?"

I thought the reply a little ungrateful.

But just then my attention was caught by an unexpected sight. Five hundred paces off, at the turn of a high promontory, a forest rose before our eyes — tall, dense, thick. It was made of trees of middling size, shaped into regular parasols, with clean, geometric outlines; the currents of the air seemed to have no grip on their foliage, and in the midst of the gusts they stood as motionless as a grove of petrified cedars.

I quickened my pace. I could not put a name to these strange growths. Did they not belong to the two hundred thousand plant species known so far — and should they be given a special place in the flora of lake-dwelling vegetation? No. When we came under their shade, my surprise turned to pure admiration.

For I found myself in the presence of products of the earth, but cut to a gigantic pattern. My uncle named them at once.

"It's only a forest of mushrooms," he said.

And he was not mistaken. Imagine the size reached by these plants, so fond of warm, damp places. I knew that the Lycoperdon giganteum grows, according to Bulliard, to eight or nine feet in circumference; but here were white mushrooms, thirty to forty feet high, with caps of an equal diameter. There were thousands of them; the light could not pierce their thick shade, and a complete darkness reigned beneath those domes ranged side by side like the round roofs of an African city.

Still, I wanted to press farther in. A deadly cold came down from those fleshy vaults. For half an hour we wandered in that clammy gloom, and it was with a genuine sense of well-being that I found the seashore again.

But the vegetation of this underground land did not stop at those mushrooms. Farther on rose, in clusters, a great number of other trees with faded, colorless foliage. They were easy to recognize; they were the humble shrubs of the earth's surface, grown to phenomenal dimensions — club mosses a hundred feet tall, giant sigillarias, tree ferns as big as the firs of the high latitudes, lepidodendrons with cylindrical forking stems, tipped with long leaves and bristling with coarse hairs like monstrous succulents.

"Astounding, magnificent, splendid!" cried my uncle. "Here is the whole flora of the second epoch of the world, the age of transition. Here are these humble plants of our gardens that grew into trees in the earliest centuries of the globe! Look, Axel, marvel at it! No botanist ever sat down to such a feast!"

"You're right, Uncle. Providence seems to have wanted to preserve, in this immense greenhouse, these antediluvian plants that the shrewdness of scholars has reconstructed so happily."

"Well put, my boy — it is a greenhouse; but you would put it better still by adding that it is perhaps a menagerie too."

"A menagerie!"

"Yes, no doubt. Look at this dust we're treading underfoot, these bones scattered over the ground."

"Bones!" I cried. "Yes — bones of antediluvian animals!"

I had flung myself upon these age-old remains, made of an indestructible mineral substance (calcium phosphate). Without hesitation I put a name to these gigantic bones, which looked like the trunks of withered trees.

"Here is the lower jaw of the Mastodon," I said; "here are the molars of the Dinotherium; here is a thighbone that can only have belonged to the largest of these animals, the Megatherium. Yes, it really is a menagerie, for these bones were certainly not carried here by some cataclysm; the animals they belonged to lived on the shores of this underground sea, in the shade of these towering plants. Look, I can see whole skeletons. And yet..."

"And yet?" said my uncle.

"I don't understand how such quadrupeds came to be in this granite cavern."

"Why not?"

"Because animal life did not exist on earth until the secondary periods, when the sedimentary ground was formed by alluvial deposits and took the place of the incandescent rocks of the primitive age."

"Well, Axel, there is a very simple answer to your objection: this ground here is sedimentary ground."

"What! At such a depth below the surface of the earth?"

"No doubt — and the fact can be explained geologically. At a certain epoch, the earth was made only of an elastic crust, subject to alternating movements up and down, by the laws of attraction. It's likely that sinkings of the ground occurred, and that a part of the sedimentary terrain was dragged down to the bottom of chasms that suddenly opened."

"That must be it. But if antediluvian animals lived in these underground regions, who's to say that one of these monsters isn't still wandering somewhere among these dark forests or behind these steep rocks?"

At the thought, I searched the various points of the horizon, not without dread; but no living creature appeared on those deserted shores.

I was a little tired, so I went and sat down at the end of a promontory, at whose foot the waves broke with a crash. From there my gaze took in the whole bay, formed by an indentation of the coast. At the back of it, a little harbor lay sheltered among the pyramid-shaped rocks. Its calm waters slept out of the wind's reach. A brig and two or three schooners could have ridden easily at anchor there. I half expected to see some ship come sailing out under full canvas, standing out to sea on the southern breeze.

But the illusion faded quickly. We were indeed the only living creatures in this underground world. During certain lulls in the wind, a silence deeper than the silences of the desert came down over the barren rocks and weighed on the surface of the ocean. I tried then to pierce the distant mists, to tear apart that curtain thrown across the mysterious back of the horizon. What questions crowded onto my lips? Where did this sea end? Where did it lead? Would we ever be able to reach its opposite shores?

My uncle, for his part, had no doubt of it. As for me, I longed for it and dreaded it at the same time.

After an hour spent contemplating this wondrous spectacle, we took the way back along the beach to regain our grotto, and it was under the sway of the strangest thoughts that I fell into a deep sleep.
