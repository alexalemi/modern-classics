Chapter 33

Saturday, August 15. The sea keeps up its monotonous sameness. No land in sight. The horizon looks impossibly far off.

My head is still heavy from the violence of my dream.

My uncle hasn't dreamed, but he's in a foul mood. He sweeps every quarter of the sky with his spyglass and folds his arms with a look of thwarted disgust.

I notice that Professor Lidenbrock is drifting back toward the impatient man he used to be, and I set the fact down in my journal. It took my near-death and my suffering to strike some spark of humanity out of him; but ever since I recovered, his nature has gotten the upper hand again. And yet — why fly into a temper? Isn't the voyage unfolding under the most favorable conditions imaginable? Isn't the raft skimming along with marvelous speed?

"You seem worried, Uncle," I said, watching him raise the glass to his eyes yet again.

"Worried? No."

"Impatient, then?"

"Anyone would be, with less cause than I have!"

"But we're moving fast —"

"What do I care? The speed isn't too slow — it's the sea that's too big!"

I remembered then that before we set out, the professor had reckoned this underground stretch of water at some thirty leagues across. Well, we'd already covered three times that distance, and the southern shores still hadn't come into view.

"We aren't descending!" the professor went on. "This is all wasted time, and, when you come down to it, I didn't travel this far to go boating on a pond!"

He calls this crossing a boating trip, and this sea a pond!

"But," I said, "since we've followed the route Saknussemm marked out —"

"That's exactly the question. Have we followed that route? Did Saknussemm ever come upon this expanse of water? Did he cross it? What if this stream we took for a guide has led us completely astray?"

"In any case, we can't regret having come this far. The sight is magnificent, and —"

"This isn't about sightseeing. I set myself a goal, and I mean to reach it! So don't talk to me about admiring the scenery!"

I took the hint and let the professor go on gnawing his lips with impatience. At six in the evening, Hans asked for his wages, and his three rix-dollars were counted out to him.

Sunday, August 16. Nothing new. Same weather. The wind shows a slight tendency to freshen. When I wake, my first concern is to check the strength of the light. I'm forever afraid that the electric phenomenon will dim and then go out. It doesn't: the shadow of the raft is sharply outlined on the surface of the waves.

Truly this sea is endless! It must be as wide as the Mediterranean, or even the Atlantic. Why not?

My uncle takes soundings several times over; he ties one of the heaviest picks to the end of a rope and pays out two hundred fathoms of it. No bottom. We have a hard time hauling our sounding line back up.

When the pick comes back aboard, Hans points out to me the marks deeply gouged into its surface. It's as though the piece of iron had been savagely clamped between two hard bodies.

I look at the hunter.

"Tänder!" he says.

I don't understand. I turn to my uncle, who is entirely lost in his own thoughts. I don't care to disturb him. I go back to the Icelander. He opens and closes his mouth several times, and makes me grasp his meaning.

"Teeth!" I say, dumbfounded, studying the iron bar more closely.

Yes! These really are the marks of teeth stamped into the metal! The jaws that carry them must have a prodigious power! Is it some monster of a lost species stirring beneath the deep layers of the water — more ravenous than the shark, more terrible than the whale? I can't tear my eyes from that half-gnawed bar. Is last night's dream about to become reality?

These thoughts churn in me all day long, and my imagination barely calms in a few hours of sleep.

Monday, August 17. I try to recall what I know of the peculiar instincts of those antediluvian animals of the Secondary period — the creatures that, coming after the mollusks, the crustaceans, and the fish, preceded the appearance of mammals on the globe. The world belonged then to the reptiles. These monsters reigned as absolute masters over the Jurassic seas (the seas of the Secondary period, which laid down the rock that makes up the mountains of the Jura). Nature had granted them the most complete organization. What a gigantic build! What a prodigious strength! Today's saurians — the alligators or crocodiles, the biggest and most fearsome of them — are only feeble scaled-down copies of their forefathers of the earliest ages!

I shudder at the picture I'm conjuring up of these monsters. No human eye has ever seen them alive. They appeared on the earth a thousand centuries before man, but their fossil bones, found again in that clayey limestone the English call the lias, have let us reconstruct them anatomically and know their colossal conformation.

I've seen at the Hamburg Museum the skeleton of one of these saurians that measured thirty feet in length. Am I really fated, I, a dweller on the surface of the earth, to find myself face to face with these representatives of an antediluvian family? No! It's impossible. And yet the mark of those powerful teeth is engraved on the iron bar, and from their imprint I can tell they're conical, like a crocodile's.

My eyes fix in terror on the sea; I dread seeing one of these inhabitants of the undersea caverns come lunging out.

I suppose Professor Lidenbrock shares my ideas, if not my fears, for after examining the pick, he sweeps his gaze across the ocean.

"Curse it," I say to myself, "this notion he took of sounding the depths! He's disturbed some sea creature in its lair, and if we aren't attacked along the way —!"

I glance at the weapons and make sure they're in good order. My uncle sees me do it and gives me an approving nod.

Already broad disturbances rippling across the surface of the waves betray the stirring of the deep layers below. The danger is near. We must keep watch.

Tuesday, August 18. Evening comes — or rather, the hour when sleep weighs down our eyelids, for this ocean has no night, and the relentless light stubbornly wears out our eyes, as though we were sailing under the sun of the arctic seas. Hans is at the tiller. During his watch I fall asleep.

Two hours later, a horrifying jolt wakes me. The raft has been heaved clear out of the water with indescribable force and flung a hundred feet away.

"What is it?" my uncle cried. "Have we run aground?"

Hans points his finger, some four hundred feet off, at a blackish mass that rises and sinks by turns. I look, and I cry out:

"It's a colossal porpoise!"

"Yes," my uncle replies, "and now there's a sea-lizard of uncommon size."

"And farther off, a monstrous crocodile! Look at its huge jaw and the rows of teeth it's armed with. Ah! It's gone under!"

"A whale! A whale!" the professor cries then. "I can see its enormous fins! Look at the air and water it's driving up through its blowholes!"

Sure enough, two columns of liquid rise to a considerable height above the sea. We stand there startled, stupefied, terrified, in the presence of this herd of sea-monsters. They're of supernatural dimensions, and the least of them would smash the raft with a single snap of its jaws. Hans tries to put the helm to the wind, to flee this dangerous company; but on the other side he spots more enemies no less fearsome: a turtle forty feet across, and a serpent thirty feet long that darts its enormous head above the waves.

Impossible to flee. These reptiles close in; they circle the raft with a speed that express trains at full throttle couldn't match; they trace concentric rings around us. I've taken up my carbine. But what effect can a bullet have on the scales that armor the bodies of these animals?

We're struck dumb with terror. Here they come, closing in! On one side the crocodile, on the other the serpent. The rest of the marine herd has vanished. I'm about to fire. Hans stops me with a gesture. The two monsters pass fifty fathoms from the raft, hurl themselves at each other, and their fury keeps them from even noticing us.

The battle is joined a hundred fathoms from the raft. We can see the two monsters distinctly, locked in combat.

But it seems to me now that the other animals are coming to take part in the fight — the porpoise, the whale, the lizard, the turtle; every moment I catch glimpses of them. I point them out to the Icelander. He shakes his head no.

"Tva," he says.

"What! Two! He's claiming that only two animals —"

"He's right," cries my uncle, whose spyglass hasn't left his eyes.

"How can that be!"

"Yes! The first of these monsters has the muzzle of a porpoise, the head of a lizard, the teeth of a crocodile — and that's what fooled us. It's the most fearsome of the antediluvian reptiles, the Ichthyosaurus!"

"And the other?"

"The other is a serpent hidden inside the shell of a turtle, the terrible enemy of the first — the Plesiosaurus!"

Hans was right. Only two monsters are churning up the surface of the sea like this, and I have before my eyes two reptiles of the primitive oceans. I can make out the bloodred eye of the Ichthyosaurus, as big as a man's head. Nature has endowed it with an optical apparatus of extreme power, capable of withstanding the pressure of the water layers in the depths where it lives. It's been rightly named the whale of the saurians, for it has both the speed and the size. This one measures no less than a hundred feet, and I can gauge its bulk when it raises the vertical fins of its tail above the waves. Its jaw is enormous, and according to the naturalists it holds no fewer than a hundred and eighty-two teeth.

The Plesiosaurus — a serpent with a cylindrical trunk and a short tail — has legs shaped like oars. Its body is entirely sheathed in a shell, and its neck, flexible as a swan's, rears thirty feet above the waves.

These animals attack each other with indescribable fury. They heave up liquid mountains that roll all the way to the raft. Twenty times we're on the verge of capsizing. Hissings of prodigious intensity ring out. The two beasts are coiled together. I can't tell one from the other! We have everything to fear from the rage of the victor.

An hour, two hours go by. The struggle goes on with the same ferocity. The combatants draw close to the raft, then move off again, by turns. We stay stock-still, ready to fire.

Suddenly the Ichthyosaurus and the Plesiosaurus vanish, carving out a genuine maelstrom in the water. Is the battle going to finish in the depths of the sea?

But all at once an enormous head shoots up out of the water — the head of the Plesiosaurus. The monster is wounded to death. I no longer see its immense shell. Only its long neck rears up, drops, rises again, curves, lashes the waves like a gigantic whip and writhes like a cut worm. The water sprays out to a considerable distance. It blinds us. But soon the reptile's death-throes near their end, its movements slacken, its contortions subside, and that long length of serpent stretches out like an inert mass on the calmed waters.

As for the Ichthyosaurus — has it gone back to its undersea cavern, or is it going to reappear on the surface of the sea?
