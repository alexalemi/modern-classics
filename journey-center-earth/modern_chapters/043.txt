Chapter 44

When I opened my eyes again, I felt the guide's powerful hand gripping me by the belt. With his other hand he was holding up my uncle. I wasn't badly hurt — just battered, aching all over. I found myself lying on the slope of a mountain, two steps from a chasm into which the slightest movement would have sent me plunging. Hans had saved me from death as I rolled down the flank of the crater.

"Where are we?" asked my uncle, who seemed thoroughly annoyed at being back on the surface of the earth.

The hunter shrugged his shoulders to signal that he had no idea.

"In Iceland?" I said.

"Nej," Hans answered.

"What! No!" cried the professor.

"Hans is mistaken," I said, pushing myself up.

After the countless surprises of this journey, one more astonishment was still in store for us. I had expected to see a cone capped with eternal snow, standing among the barren deserts of the northern regions, under the pale rays of a polar sky, beyond the highest latitudes — and instead of all that, my uncle, the Icelander, and I lay halfway down the flank of a mountain scorched by the blaze of a sun that was devouring us with its fire.

I refused to believe my own eyes; but the genuine roasting my body was undergoing left no room for doubt. We had come out of the crater half naked, and the radiant star — the one we hadn't asked a thing of for two months — was now lavishing light and heat on us, pouring down a splendid flood of brightness.

Once my eyes had grown used to a glare they'd forgotten, I put them to work correcting the errors of my imagination. At the very least I wanted to be in Spitzbergen, and I wasn't in the mood to give up the idea easily.

The professor was the first to speak.

"Well," he said, "this certainly doesn't look like Iceland."

"But the island of Jan Mayen?" I offered.

"Not that either, my boy. This is no northern volcano, with its granite hills and its snowcap."

"And yet—"

"Look, Axel — look!"

Above our heads, five hundred feet up at most, the crater of a volcano was opening, and every fifteen minutes, with a tremendous detonation, it flung out a tall column of flame mixed with pumice, ash, and lava. I could feel the convulsions of the mountain as it breathed like a whale, blowing out fire and air through its enormous vents every so often. Below us, down a fairly steep slope, sheets of erupted matter spread to a depth of seven or eight hundred feet, which gave the volcano a height of no more than a hundred fathoms. Its base vanished into a veritable basket of green trees, among which I could make out olives, figs, and vines heavy with crimson clusters of grapes.

This, plainly, was not the face of the Arctic.

When the eye cleared that ring of greenery, it quickly lost itself in the waters of a magnificent sea — or a lake — that made this enchanted land an island barely a few leagues across. To the east lay a little harbor fronted by a handful of houses, where ships of an odd design rocked on the blue swell. Beyond it, clusters of islets rose from the watery plain, so many of them that they looked like a vast anthill. To the west, distant coasts curved along the horizon; along some of them blue mountains traced their harmonious shapes, and along others, farther off, rose a prodigiously tall cone with a plume of smoke stirring at its summit. To the north an immense sheet of water glittered under the sun's rays, letting the tip of a mast or the curve of a wind-swollen sail show here and there.

The sheer unexpectedness of such a sight multiplied its marvelous beauty a hundredfold.

"Where are we? Where are we?" I kept repeating under my breath.

Hans closed his eyes with indifference, and my uncle looked on without understanding.

"Whatever this mountain is," he said at last, "it's a little warm here. The explosions aren't letting up, and it would really be a shame to have survived an eruption only to catch a chunk of rock on the head. Let's go down, and then we'll know where we stand. Besides, I'm dying of hunger and thirst."

Decidedly, the professor was no contemplative soul. For my part, forgetting hunger and exhaustion, I could have stayed in that spot for hours more — but I had to follow my companions.

The volcano's slopes were very steep. We slid down through real quagmires of ash, dodging the streams of lava that stretched out like serpents of fire. As we went, I chattered away at full speed, for my imagination was too full not to spill over into words.

"We're in Asia," I exclaimed, "on the coasts of India, in the Malay islands, in the heart of Oceania! We've crossed half the globe to end up at the antipodes of Europe!"

"But the compass?" my uncle replied.

"Yes! The compass!" I said, embarrassed. "According to it, we always traveled north."

"So it lied?"

"Oh — lied!"

"Unless this is the North Pole!"

"The Pole! No — but..."

Here was an inexplicable fact. I didn't know what to make of it.

Meanwhile we were drawing nearer to that greenery, which was a pleasure to behold. Hunger tormented me, and thirst too. Fortunately, after two hours of walking, a lovely stretch of countryside opened before us, entirely covered with olive trees, pomegranates, and vines that looked as though they belonged to everyone. In our destitution, at any rate, we weren't the sort of people to look too closely at ownership. What a joy it was to crush that delicious fruit against our lips and to bite into whole handfuls of those crimson grapes! Not far off, in the grass, in the delectable shade of the trees, I discovered a spring of cool water, into which we plunged our faces and our hands with pure delight.

While each of us was surrendering to all the sweetness of rest, a child appeared between two clumps of olive trees.

"Ah!" I cried. "An inhabitant of this happy land!"

He was a sort of small pauper, very wretchedly dressed and rather sickly, and our appearance seemed to frighten him badly. And no wonder — half naked, with our wild, unkempt beards, we looked frightful, and unless this happened to be a country of thieves, we were the sort to terrify its people.

Just as the boy was about to bolt, Hans ran after him and brought him back, despite his cries and kicks.

My uncle began by reassuring him as best he could, and said to him in good German:

"What is the name of this mountain, my little friend?"

The child didn't answer.

"Good," said my uncle. "We're not in Germany, then."

And he put the same question in English.

The child still didn't answer. I was thoroughly puzzled.

"Is he mute, then?" cried the professor, who, very proud of his gift for languages, tried the same question again in French.

The child kept the same silence.

"Then let's try Italian," my uncle went on, and he said in that tongue:

"Dove noi siamo?"

"Yes! Where are we?" I repeated impatiently.

The child said nothing.

"Ah, now — will you speak?" cried my uncle, whom anger was beginning to overtake, and he shook the boy by the ears. "Come si noma questa isola?"

"Stromboli," answered the little shepherd, who slipped out of Hans's hands and made off across the plain through the olive trees.

We hardly gave him another thought! Stromboli! What an effect that unexpected name had on my imagination! We were in the middle of the Mediterranean, in the heart of the Aeolian archipelago of mythological memory, in the ancient Strongyle where Aeolus kept the winds and the storms chained. And those blue mountains curving away to the east — they were the mountains of Calabria! And that volcano rising on the southern horizon — it was Etna, fierce Etna itself!

"Stromboli! Stromboli!" I kept repeating.

My uncle joined in with gestures and words of his own. We looked as though we were singing in a chorus!

Ah, what a journey! What a marvelous journey! We had gone in by one volcano and come out by another — and that other one stood more than twelve hundred leagues from Sneffels, from that barren land of Iceland flung out at the very edge of the world! The chances of this expedition had carried us into the midst of the most harmonious lands on earth! We had left the region of eternal snows for one of endless green, and traded the gray fog of the frozen zones over our heads for the blue sky of Sicily!

After a delicious meal of fruit and cool water, we set off again to reach the port of Stromboli. It didn't seem wise to explain how we had come to the island: the superstitious mind of the Italians would not have failed to see us as demons vomited up from the depths of hell, so we had to resign ourselves to passing for humble castaways. Less glorious, perhaps, but safer.

As we walked, I heard my uncle muttering:

"But the compass! The compass, that pointed north! How can I explain it?"

"Honestly," I said with a great air of disdain, "don't explain it — that's easier!"

"Come now! A professor at the Johannaeum who couldn't find the reason for a cosmic phenomenon — that would be a disgrace!"

And as he spoke, my uncle — half naked, his leather purse slung around his waist, his spectacles perched on his nose — became once again the terrible professor of mineralogy.

An hour after leaving the olive wood, we arrived at the port of San Vicenzo, where Hans claimed the wages of his thirteenth week of service, which were counted out to him with warm handshakes.

At that moment, though he didn't share our very natural emotion, he did at least give himself over to an extraordinary display of feeling.

With the tips of his fingers he pressed our two hands lightly, and broke into a smile.
