Chapter 19

The next morning, Tuesday the 30th of June, at six o'clock, we took up the descent again.

We were still following the lava gallery — a genuine natural ramp, gentle as those inclined slopes that still take the place of a staircase in old houses. So it went until seventeen minutes past noon, the precise instant when we caught up with Hans, who had just stopped.

"Ah!" cried my uncle. "We've reached the end of the chimney."

I looked around me. We were standing at the center of a crossroads, where two passages came to an end, both of them dark and narrow. Which one should we take? There was the difficulty.

Still, my uncle refused to appear to hesitate, either in front of me or in front of the guide. He pointed to the eastern tunnel, and before long all three of us were pushing into it.

In any case, any hesitation before that forking path would have dragged on forever, because there wasn't a single clue to settle the choice one way or the other. We had to leave it entirely to chance.

The slope of this new gallery was barely noticeable, and its cross-section wildly uneven. At times a succession of arches unrolled before our steps like the side aisles of a Gothic cathedral; medieval artists could have studied here every form of that religious architecture whose defining element is the pointed arch. A mile further on, our heads bent low beneath the flattened vaults of the Romanesque style, and thick pillars set into the rock mass sagged under the weight of the arches coming down on them. In certain places, all that gave way to low, cramped structures that looked like the work of beavers, and we crawled on our hands and knees through narrow burrows.

The heat held at a bearable level. In spite of myself I thought of how fierce it must once have been, when the lava vomited up by the Sneffels came pouring down this route that was so peaceful today. I pictured the torrents of fire smashing against the angles of the gallery, and the buildup of superheated vapors in that cramped space!

"Just so long," I thought, "as the old volcano doesn't take a late fancy to start up again!"

I said none of these reflections to my uncle. He wouldn't have understood them. His one and only thought was to press forward. He marched, he slid, he even tumbled downhill, with a conviction that, when all was said and done, you couldn't help but admire.

At six in the evening, after a not-too-tiring stroll, we had gained two leagues to the south, but barely a quarter of a mile in depth.

My uncle gave the signal to rest. We ate without much talk, and fell asleep without much thought.

Our arrangements for the night were very simple: a travel blanket to roll up in was the whole of our bedding. We had neither cold nor unwelcome visitors to fear. Travelers who plunge into the deserts of Africa, or into the heart of the New World's forests, are forced to keep watch over one another during the hours of sleep; but here, there was absolute solitude and complete safety. Savages or wild beasts — none of those dangerous breeds was anything to worry about.

We woke the next morning fresh and rested. We took up the road again. We were following a lava path as we had the day before. It was impossible to make out the nature of the ground it cut through. The tunnel, instead of sinking deeper into the bowels of the globe, was tending to become absolutely horizontal. I even thought I noticed it was climbing back up toward the surface of the earth. This became so obvious around ten in the morning — and, as a result, so tiring — that I was forced to slow our pace.

"Well, Axel?" the professor said impatiently.

"Well, I can't go on," I answered.

"What! After three hours' walk on such an easy road!"

"Easy, I won't argue — but tiring, that's for certain."

"How so, when all we have to do is go down!"

"Go up, if you don't mind!"

"Up!" said my uncle, shrugging.

"No question about it. For the last half hour the slopes have changed, and if we keep following them like this we'll certainly end up back on the soil of Iceland."

The professor shook his head like a man who has no intention of being convinced. I tried to pick the conversation back up. He didn't answer, and gave the signal to move on. I saw plainly enough that his silence was nothing but bottled-up bad temper.

Still, I had shouldered my burden again bravely, and I was keeping close behind Hans, with my uncle out in front. I was determined not to be left behind; my great worry was to never lose sight of my companions. I shuddered at the thought of getting lost in the depths of this labyrinth.

Besides, though the rising road was growing harder, I consoled myself with the thought that it was bringing me closer to the surface of the earth. That was a hope. Every step confirmed it.

At noon a change came over the walls of the gallery. I noticed it in the weakening of the electric light reflected off them. To the lava lining now succeeded bare living rock; the mass was made up of layers that were tilted, and often set vertically. We were smack in the middle of the transition era, smack in the Silurian period (so named because the ground from this period stretches widely across England, in the regions once inhabited by the Celtic tribe of the Silures).

"It's obvious!" I exclaimed. "The waters laid down these schists, these limestones and sandstones during the second era of the earth! We're turning our backs on the granite mass! We're like people from Hamburg taking the road to Hanover to get to Lübeck!"

I should have kept my observations to myself. But my geologist's temperament won out over prudence, and Uncle Lidenbrock heard my outbursts.

"What's the matter with you?" he said.

"Look!" I answered, pointing out to him the varied succession of sandstones, limestones, and the first signs of slate-bearing ground.

"Well?"

"Here we are, arrived at the very period when the first plants and the first animals appeared!"

"Ah! You think so?"

"But look, examine, observe!"

I forced the professor to play his lamp over the walls of the gallery. I expected some exclamation from him. Far from it — he didn't say a word, and went on his way.

Had he understood me or not? Was he unwilling to admit — out of the wounded pride of an uncle and a scholar — that he'd blundered in choosing the eastern tunnel? Or was he set on following this passage all the way to its end? It was clear that we had left the lava road, and that this path could not possibly lead to the furnace of the Sneffels.

Still, I asked myself whether I wasn't granting too much importance to this change in the terrain. Was I fooling myself? Were we really crossing these layers of rock stacked on top of the granite mass?

"If I'm right," I thought, "I ought to find some remnant of a primitive plant, and then I'll simply have to bow to the evidence. Let's have a look."

I hadn't gone a hundred steps before undeniable proof offered itself to my eyes. It had to be there — in the Silurian era, the seas held more than fifteen hundred species of plants and animals. My feet, used to the hard lava floor, suddenly trod on a dust made of the debris of plants and shells. On the walls you could plainly see the imprints of fucus and lycopods; Professor Lidenbrock could not possibly have been mistaken about them — but he shut his eyes, I imagine, and went on his way with a steady, unbroken step.

This was stubbornness pushed past every limit. I couldn't stand it any longer. I picked up a perfectly preserved shell, which had belonged to an animal roughly like the modern wood louse; then I caught up with my uncle and said:

"Look!"

"Well," he answered calmly, "that's the shell of a crustacean from the extinct order of trilobites. Nothing more."

"But don't you conclude from it—?"

"What you conclude yourself? Yes. Perfectly. We've left the layer of granite and the lava road behind. It's possible I made a mistake; but I won't be certain of my error until I've reached the end of this gallery."

"You're right to act this way, Uncle, and I'd back you completely — if we didn't have a more and more menacing danger to fear."

"And what's that?"

"The lack of water."

"Well then! We'll put ourselves on rations, Axel."
