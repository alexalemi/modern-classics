Chapter 20

And so it came to rationing. Our supply couldn't last more than three days. That was the grim arithmetic I worked out at supper that evening. And, worst of all, we had little hope of striking a live spring in these strata of the transition period.

All the next day the gallery unrolled its endless arches ahead of us. We walked almost without a word. Hans's silence was catching.

The path didn't rise — not noticeably, anyway; at times it even seemed to tilt downward. But that faint slope was no comfort to the Professor, because the character of the rock never changed, and the transition period only asserted itself more strongly.

The electric light struck brilliant sparks from the shale, the limestone, and the old red sandstone of the walls; you might have thought yourself in a cutting driven through the heart of Devonshire — the county that gave its name to this kind of formation. Splendid samples of marble sheathed the walls: some an agate gray shot with whimsical white veins, others flesh-pink or yellow blotched with red, and further on those dark-toned griottes in which the limestone flares up in vivid shades.

Most of these marbles carried the imprints of primitive animals; but since the day before, creation had clearly taken a step forward. Instead of the rudimentary trilobites, I could now make out the remains of a more advanced order — among them ganoid fishes and those Sauropteris in which the paleontologist's eye has learned to recognize the first forms of the reptile. The Devonian seas were home to great numbers of such creatures, and they laid them down by the thousand on these newly formed rocks.

It was becoming plain that we were climbing back up the ladder of animal life — the ladder whose summit is man. But Professor Lidenbrock didn't seem to notice.

He was waiting on one of two things: either a vertical shaft to open under his feet and let him resume his descent, or an obstacle to bar his way altogether. But evening came and neither hope was answered.

On Friday, after a night in which I began to feel the first torments of thirst, our little band plunged once more into the windings of the gallery.

After ten hours of marching, I noticed that the glow of our lamps on the walls was fading strangely. The marble, the shale, the limestone, the sandstone were giving way to a dark, dull facing. At a point where the tunnel narrowed sharply, I leaned a hand against the wall.

When I took it away, it was completely black. I looked closer. We were in the middle of a coal seam.

"A coal mine!" I cried.

"A mine without miners," my uncle replied.

"Eh — who knows?"

"I know," the Professor answered curtly, "and I'm certain this gallery, cut through these beds of coal, was not made by the hand of man. But whether it's nature's work or not, I couldn't care less. It's supper time. Let's eat."

Hans put together a little food. I barely ate, and I drank the few drops of water that made up my ration. The guide's flask, half full — that was all that was left to slake the thirst of three men.

After the meal, my two companions stretched out on their blankets and found in sleep a cure for their exhaustion. As for me, I couldn't sleep, and I counted the hours until morning.

On Saturday, at six, we set off again. Twenty minutes later we reached a vast hollow; and now I could see for certain that no human hand could have dug out this seam — the vaults would have been shored up with timbering, whereas in truth they held together by a miracle of balance alone.

This sort of cavern measured a hundred feet across and a hundred and fifty high. The ground had been violently wrenched apart by some underground convulsion. The mass of earth, yielding to a mighty upward thrust, had split open, leaving this broad void — into which inhabitants of the surface were now venturing for the very first time.

The whole history of the coal age was written on those dark walls, and a geologist could easily trace its various phases. The beds of coal were separated by strata of compact sandstone or clay, and looked as if crushed flat under the weight of the layers above.

In that age of the world, which came before the secondary era, the earth clothed itself in immense vegetation, the product of two forces working together: a tropical heat and an unrelenting damp. An atmosphere of vapors wrapped the globe on every side, still hiding the sun's rays from it.

From this comes the conclusion that the high temperatures did not come from that new source; perhaps the sun itself was not yet ready to play its dazzling part. "Climates" did not yet exist, and a scorching heat spread over the whole surface of the globe, the same at the equator as at the poles. Where did it come from? From the interior of the globe.

Whatever Professor Lidenbrock's theories might say, a violent fire smoldered in the bowels of the spheroid; its action made itself felt right up to the outermost layers of the earth's crust. The plants, robbed of the sun's kindly influence, gave neither flowers nor scent, but their roots drew a fierce vitality from the burning soil of those first days.

There were few trees — only herbaceous plants, immense grasslands, ferns, lycopods, sigillaria, asterophyllites: strange families whose species numbered in the thousands back then.

And it is precisely to this exuberant vegetation that coal owes its origin. The elastic crust of the globe obeyed the movements of the liquid mass it covered. Hence fissures, hence countless subsidences; the plants, dragged down beneath the waters, little by little formed considerable heaps.

Then natural chemistry went to work: at the bottom of the seas, the masses of vegetation turned first to peat; then, under the influence of gases and the heat of fermentation, they underwent a complete mineralization.

And so were formed these immense beds of coal that the consumption of every people on earth, through long centuries yet to come, will never manage to exhaust.

These reflections came back to me as I studied the mineral wealth heaped up in this portion of the earth's mass. This coal, no doubt, will never be brought to light. Working such remote mines would demand sacrifices far too great. And what would be the point, in any case, when coal lies practically at the surface of the earth across so many regions? So there I saw these beds, untouched — and untouched they will still be when the last hour of the world strikes.

Meanwhile we kept walking, and I alone among my companions forgot the length of the road by losing myself in geological musings. The temperature stayed roughly what it had been during our passage through the lavas and shales. Only my sense of smell was troubled by a strong odor of protocarbureted hydrogen. I recognized at once, in this gallery, the presence of a notable quantity of that dangerous gas the miners call firedamp, whose explosions have so often caused appalling catastrophes.

Luckily we had the light of Ruhmkorff's ingenious apparatus. If, by ill fortune, we had explored this gallery carelessly with a torch in hand, a terrible blast would have ended the journey by wiping out the travelers.

This excursion through the coal seam lasted until evening. My uncle could barely contain the impatience the flatness of the road stirred in him. The darkness, always deep beyond twenty paces, made it impossible to judge the length of the gallery, and I was beginning to think it endless when suddenly, at six o'clock, a wall rose unexpectedly before us. To the right, to the left, above, below, there was no way through. We had reached the bottom of a dead end.

"Well — so much the better!" cried my uncle. "At least now I know where I stand. We're not on Saknussemm's road, and there's nothing to do but turn back. Let's take a night's rest, and within three days we'll have regained the point where the two galleries fork."

"Yes," I said, "if we have the strength for it!"

"And why wouldn't we?"

"Because by tomorrow the water will be gone completely."

"And will the courage be gone too?" said the Professor, fixing me with a stern eye.

I didn't dare answer him.
