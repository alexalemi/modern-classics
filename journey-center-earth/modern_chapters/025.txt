Chapter 26

I have to admit it: so far things had been going well, and it would have been poor manners to complain. If the average level of "difficulties" didn't rise, we couldn't fail to reach our goal. And what glory then! I had actually started reasoning like Lidenbrock. Seriously. Was it the strange world I was living in that did it? Perhaps.

For several days, steeper slopes — some of them frighteningly vertical — carried us deep into the interior mass. On certain days we gained a league and a half to two leagues toward the center. Perilous descents, during which Hans's skill and his marvelous coolness were a great help to us. That impassive Icelander devoted himself with an incomprehensible ease, and thanks to him we got past more than one bad stretch we'd never have cleared on our own.

His silence, for one thing, deepened by the day. I even think it was catching. Outward things have a real effect on the brain. Shut a man up between four walls and he ends up losing the power to connect ideas and words. How many prisoners in solitary confinement have turned dull-witted, if not outright mad, from letting their thinking faculties go unused.

During the two weeks that followed our last conversation, nothing happened worth recording. I find in my memory — and for good reason — only a single event of extreme gravity. It would have been hard for me to forget the smallest detail of it.

On the 7th of August, our steady descents had brought us to a depth of thirty leagues; which is to say there were thirty leagues of rock, ocean, continents, and cities over our heads. We must by then have been two hundred leagues from Iceland.

That day the tunnel followed a gently sloping floor.

I was walking ahead. My uncle carried one of the two Ruhmkorff lamps, and I the other. I was examining the layers of granite.

Suddenly, turning around, I realized I was alone.

"Fine," I thought, "I've walked too fast, or else Hans and my uncle stopped along the way. Come on, I'd better catch up with them. Luckily the path doesn't climb much."

I retraced my steps. I walked for a quarter of an hour. I looked. No one. I called out. No answer. My voice was lost among the cavernous echoes it suddenly woke.

I began to feel uneasy. A shiver ran through my whole body.

"A little calm," I said aloud. "I'm certain I'll find my companions. There aren't two routes! Now, I was ahead, so let's go back."

I climbed back up for half an hour. I listened for some call coming to me — in an atmosphere this dense, it might reach me from a long way off. An extraordinary silence reigned in the immense gallery.

I stopped. I couldn't believe I was really alone. I was willing to be led astray, but not lost. When you're only led astray, you find your way back.

"Come now," I repeated, "since there's only one route, and since they're following it, I'm bound to meet up with them. All I have to do is keep climbing back up. Unless, not seeing me, and forgetting that I was ahead of them, they took it into their heads to turn around and go back. Well! Even in that case, if I hurry, I'll catch them. It's obvious!"

I repeated those last words like a man who isn't convinced. What's more, to put together ideas this simple and gather them into the shape of an argument, I had to take a very long time.

Then a doubt seized me. Was I really the one in front? Certainly. Hans had been following me, ahead of my uncle. He'd even stopped for a few moments to strap his baggage more firmly on his shoulder. That detail was coming back to me now. It was at that very moment that I must have kept going.

"Besides," I thought, "I have a sure way of not getting lost, a thread to guide me through this labyrinth, and one that can't break — my faithful stream. All I have to do is walk back up its course, and I'll inevitably find the traces of my companions."

This reasoning revived me, and I resolved to set out again without wasting an instant.

How I blessed my uncle's foresight then, when he'd stopped the hunter from plugging the notch cut into the granite wall! So this good spring, after quenching our thirst along the way, was going to guide me through the windings of the Earth's crust.

Before starting back up, I thought a wash would do me some good.

So I bent down to plunge my forehead into the water of the Hansbach!

Imagine my astonishment!

It was dry, rough granite I set my hands to! The stream no longer ran at my feet!
