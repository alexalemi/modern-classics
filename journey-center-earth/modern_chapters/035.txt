Chapter 36

Here ends what I called "the ship's log," so luckily saved from the wreck. I pick up my story as before.

What happened when the raft smashed against the reefs of the coast, I couldn't say. I felt myself flung into the waves, and if I escaped death, if my body wasn't torn apart on the sharp rocks, it was only because Hans's powerful arm hauled me out of the abyss.

The brave Icelander carried me beyond the reach of the waves, onto a burning sand where I found myself lying side by side with my uncle.

Then he went back toward those rocks that the furious breakers were hammering, to salvage a few scraps of the wreck. I couldn't speak; I was shattered by emotion and exhaustion; it took me a full hour to come back to myself.

Meanwhile a torrential rain kept falling, but with that final redoubling that signals the end of a storm. A few slabs of rock, one heaped on another, gave us shelter against the downpour from the sky. Hans prepared some food I couldn't touch, and each of us, worn out by three sleepless nights, dropped into a wretched, painful sleep.

The next day the weather was magnificent. Sky and sea had calmed down by mutual agreement. Every trace of the tempest had vanished. It was the cheerful words of the professor that greeted me as I woke.

"Well, my boy," he cried, "did you sleep well?"

You'd have thought we were back in the house on König-strasse, that I was coming quietly downstairs to breakfast, and that my wedding to poor Graüben was to take place that very day.

Alas! If the storm had only thrown the raft a little to the east, we would have passed under Germany, under my dear city of Hamburg, under that street where everything I loved in the world still lived. Then barely forty leagues would have separated us! But forty vertical leagues of a granite wall — and in reality, more than a thousand leagues to cross!

All these painful reflections raced through my mind before I answered my uncle's question.

"Now then!" he repeated, "won't you tell me if you slept well?"

"Very well," I answered. "I'm still battered, but that's nothing."

"Nothing at all — a little fatigue, and that's the whole of it."

"But you seem in fine spirits this morning, Uncle."

"Delighted, my boy! Delighted! We've arrived!"

"At the end of our expedition?"

"No — but at the end of that sea that never seemed to end. Now we're going to go back to traveling overland, and truly plunge into the bowels of the globe."

"Uncle, allow me one question."

"I allow it, Axel."

"And the return trip?"

"The return! Ha! You're thinking about coming back when we haven't even gotten there?"

"No, I only want to ask how it will be done."

"In the simplest way in the world. Once we've reached the center of the spheroid, either we'll find a new road to climb back up to its surface, or we'll come home in the most ordinary fashion, by the road we already traveled. I like to think it won't close up behind us."

"Then we'll have to put the raft back in good order."

"Necessarily."

"But the provisions — is there enough left to accomplish all these grand things?"

"Yes, certainly. Hans is a clever fellow, and I'm sure he saved the greater part of the cargo. Let's go make sure, anyway."

We left that grotto, which was open to every breeze. I had a hope that was at the same time a fear; it seemed impossible to me that the terrible battering of the raft hadn't destroyed everything it carried. I was wrong. When I reached the shore, I saw Hans in the middle of a whole array of objects laid out in perfect order. My uncle wrung his hand with a keen feeling of gratitude. This man, whose devotion was superhuman — of a kind there may be no other example — had worked while we slept and saved the most precious of our belongings at the risk of his life.

Not that we hadn't suffered some serious losses — our weapons, for instance; but in the end, we could do without those. The stock of powder had stayed intact, after nearly blowing up during the storm.

"Well," cried the professor, "since the guns are gone, we'll simply have to do without hunting."

"Fine — but the instruments?"

"Here's the manometer, the most useful of them all, the one I'd have given up all the others for! With it I can calculate our depth and know when we've reached the center. Without it, we'd risk going too far and coming out at the antipodes!"

This cheer of his was ferocious.

"But the compass?" I asked.

"Here it is, on this rock, in perfect condition — along with the chronometer and the thermometers. Ah, the hunter is a precious man!"

I had to admit it: as far as instruments went, nothing was missing. As for the tools and the gear, I saw them scattered across the sand — ladders, ropes, pickaxes, mattocks, and the rest.

Still, there remained the question of food to settle.

"And the provisions?" I said.

"Let's have a look at the provisions," my uncle answered.

The crates that held them were lined up along the beach in a perfect state of preservation; the sea had spared most of them, and all in all — counting biscuit, salt meat, gin, and dried fish — we could still reckon on four months' worth of food.

"Four months!" cried the professor. "We have time to go and come back, and with whatever's left I mean to throw a grand dinner for all my colleagues at the Johannaeum!"

I should have grown used long ago to my uncle's temperament, and yet this man never stopped astonishing me.

"Now," he said, "we're going to refill our water supply with the rain the storm poured into all these granite basins; so we've no fear of being caught by thirst. As for the raft, I'm going to have Hans repair it as best he can, though I don't imagine it will be of any further use to us!"

"How so?" I exclaimed.

"An idea of mine, my boy! I don't believe we'll go out the way we came in."

I looked at the professor with a certain distrust; I wondered whether he hadn't gone mad. And yet "he little knew how right he was."

"Let's go have breakfast," he went on.

I followed him up to a high headland, after he'd given his instructions to the hunter. There, some dried meat, biscuit, and tea made an excellent meal — one of the best, I have to confess, that I've had in my life. Hunger, the open air, the calm after all that upheaval — everything worked together to give me an appetite.

During breakfast, I put to my uncle the question of where exactly we were at that moment.

"That," I said, "seems hard to calculate."

"To calculate exactly, yes," he answered; "it's even impossible, since during those three days of storm I couldn't keep any record of the raft's speed or direction. But we can still work out our position by dead reckoning."

"True — the last observation was taken at the geyser islet..."

"At Axel Islet, my boy. Don't turn down the honor of having christened with your own name the first island discovered at the center of the terrestrial mass."

"All right! From Axel Islet, we'd crossed about two hundred and seventy leagues of sea, and we were more than six hundred leagues from Iceland."

"Good! Then let's start from that point and count four days of storm, during which our speed can't have been less than eighty leagues per twenty-four hours."

"I believe it. That would be three hundred leagues to add on."

"Yes, and the Lidenbrock Sea would be roughly six hundred leagues from one shore to the other! Do you realize, Axel, that it could rival the Mediterranean in size?"

"Yes — especially if we've only crossed it at its width!"

"Which is entirely possible!"

"And, curiously enough," I added, "if our calculations are right, we now have that Mediterranean over our heads."

"Really!"

"Really, for we're nine hundred leagues from Reykjavik!"

"That's a fine stretch of road, my boy; but whether we're under the Mediterranean rather than under Turkey or under the Atlantic — that can only be affirmed if our course hasn't drifted."

"No, the wind seemed steady; so I think this shore must lie to the southeast of Port Graüben."

"Good — it's easy to make sure by consulting the compass. Let's go consult the compass!"

The professor headed for the rock on which Hans had set down the instruments. He was gay, brisk; he rubbed his hands, he struck poses! A regular young man! I followed him, curious enough to know whether I'd gone wrong in my reckoning.

Reaching the rock, my uncle took the compass, laid it flat, and watched the needle, which, after swinging back and forth, settled into a fixed position under the pull of the magnetic force.

My uncle looked, then rubbed his eyes and looked again. At last he turned toward me, dumbfounded.

"What's the matter?" I asked.

He signaled me to examine the instrument. A cry of surprise escaped me. The point of the needle marked north where we assumed south to be! It pointed toward the beach instead of showing the open sea!

I shook the compass, I examined it; it was in perfect working order. Whatever position I forced the needle into, it stubbornly took up that same unexpected direction.

So there was no longer any doubt: during the storm a shift of wind had occurred without our noticing it, and it had carried the raft back toward the very shores my uncle believed he was leaving behind him.
