Chapter 1

On Sunday, the 24th of May, 1863, my uncle, Professor Lidenbrock, came rushing back to his little house at No. 19 König-strasse, one of the oldest streets in the old quarter of Hamburg.

Martha, our good housekeeper, must have thought she was terribly behind, because dinner was only just beginning to sing on the kitchen stove.

"Well," I said to myself, "if he's hungry, my uncle — the most impatient man alive — is going to howl the house down."

"Professor Lidenbrock already!" cried Martha, astonished, easing open the dining-room door.

"Yes, Martha. But dinner has every right not to be ready, because it isn't two o'clock yet. Saint Michael's has only just struck the half hour."

"Then why is Professor Lidenbrock coming home?"

"He'll no doubt tell us himself."

"Here he comes! I'm off. Master Axel, you make him see reason."

And good Martha retreated to her culinary laboratory.

I was left on my own. But making the most short-tempered of professors see reason — that was rather more than my somewhat indecisive character was equal to. So I was preparing to beat a prudent retreat up to my little room on the top floor, when the street door screamed on its hinges, a pair of big feet came cracking up the wooden staircase, and the master of the house, crossing the dining room, plunged straight into his study.

But in that quick passage he had managed to fling his nutcracker-headed cane into one corner, toss his broad, rough-napped hat onto the table, and hurl these ringing words at his nephew:

"Axel, follow me!"

I hadn't had time to stir before the professor was shouting at me again, with a sharp note of impatience:

"Well? You're not here yet?"

I bolted into the study of my formidable master.

Otto Lidenbrock was not a bad man, I'll gladly grant that; but unless something improbable changes him, he will die a terrible eccentric to the very end.

He was a professor at the Johannaeum, where he gave a course in mineralogy during which he lost his temper, as a rule, once or twice a session. Not that he cared whether his students turned up faithfully to his lessons, or how closely they paid attention, or what success they might go on to have; those little details hardly troubled him. He taught "subjectively" — to borrow a term from German philosophy — for himself, and not for anyone else. He was a selfish scholar, a well of learning whose pulley squealed whenever you tried to draw anything up out of it. In a word: a miser.

There are a few professors of this stripe in Germany.

My uncle, unfortunately, was not blessed with a great gift for pronunciation — not in private, at least, but certainly in public, and that is a regrettable flaw in a public speaker. In his lectures at the Johannaeum he would often stop dead; he'd be wrestling with some stubborn word that refused to slide out between his lips, one of those words that dig in their heels, swell up, and finally burst out in the highly unscientific form of an oath. And then: mighty rage.

Mineralogy has a great many half-Greek, half-Latin names, hard to pronounce — rough, jaw-breaking labels that would flay a poet's lips. I don't mean to run the science down. Far from it. But when a man finds himself face to face with rhombohedral crystallizations, retinasphalt resins, gehlenites, tangasites, molybdates of lead, tungstates of manganese, and titanates of zirconia, even the nimblest tongue is allowed to trip.

Now, all over town this pardonable weakness of my uncle's was well known, and people took advantage of it; they lay in wait for him at the dangerous passages, he flew into a fury, and they laughed — which is not in good taste, even for Germans. And so, if there was always a great crowd of listeners at Lidenbrock's lectures, how many of them followed the course so faithfully mainly to enjoy the professor's magnificent rages?

Be that as it may, my uncle — I can't say it too often — was a genuine scholar. Though he sometimes shattered his specimens by testing them too roughly, he combined the instinct of the geologist with the eye of the mineralogist. With his hammer, his steel point, his magnetic needle, his blowpipe, and his flask of nitric acid, he was a formidable man. From the fracture, the look, the hardness, the fusibility, the sound, the smell, or the taste of any given mineral, he would classify it without hesitation among the six hundred species that science recognizes today.

So the name of Lidenbrock rang out with honor in the schools and the learned societies. Humphry Davy, Humboldt, Captains Franklin and Sabine never failed to call on him when they passed through Hamburg. Becquerel, Ebelmen, Brewster, Dumas, Milne-Edwards all liked to consult him on the most gripping questions in chemistry. That science owed him some rather fine discoveries, and in 1853 there had appeared in Leipzig a Treatise on Transcendental Crystallography, by Professor Otto Lidenbrock — a grand folio volume with plates, which nevertheless failed to cover its own costs.

Add to all this that my uncle was curator of the mineralogical museum of Monsieur Struve, the Russian ambassador — a valuable collection with a Europe-wide reputation.

That, then, is the man who was hailing me so impatiently. Picture a tall, lean fellow, iron-healthy, with a boyish blond coloring that knocked a good ten years off his fifty. His big eyes rolled ceaselessly behind an enormous pair of spectacles; his long, thin nose was like the blade of a knife; the malicious even claimed it was magnetized and pulled in iron filings. Pure slander: it pulled in nothing but tobacco — though a great deal of that, it must be admitted, to be honest.

When I add that my uncle took mathematical strides half a fathom long, and that as he walked he kept his fists firmly clenched — the mark of an impetuous temperament — you will know him well enough not to hanker after his company.

He lived in his little house on König-strasse, a dwelling half wood, half brick, with a scalloped gable; it looked out onto one of those winding canals that crisscross the middle of Hamburg's oldest quarter, which the great fire of 1842 had mercifully spared.

The old house did lean a little, it's true, and thrust its belly out at the passersby; it wore its roof tilted over one ear, like the cap of a student of the Tugendbund; the plumb of its lines left something to be desired; but all in all it held itself up well, thanks to an old elm vigorously wedged into the front wall, which in spring pushed its budding blossoms in through the panes of the windows.

My uncle was not badly off, as German professors go. The house was his outright, contents and all. The contents were his goddaughter Graüben — a young Virland girl of seventeen — our good Martha, and me. In my double capacity as nephew and orphan, I became his assistant-preparator in his experiments.

I'll admit I bit into the geological sciences with a hearty appetite; I had mineralogist's blood in my veins, and I was never bored in the company of my precious rocks.

All in all, one could live happily in that little house on König-strasse, despite its owner's fits of impatience — because, for all his rather brutal way of going about things, he loved me no less for it. But the man simply did not know how to wait; he was in more of a hurry than nature itself.

When, back in April, he had planted seedlings of mignonette or morning glory in the china pots of his sitting room, every morning he would go and faithfully tug them by the leaves to hurry their growth along.

With an eccentric like that, there was nothing to do but obey. So I dashed into his study.
