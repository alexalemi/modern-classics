Chapter 31

The next morning I woke up completely cured. I decided a swim would do me a world of good, and I went and plunged for a few minutes into the waters of this underground Mediterranean. That name, if any sea ever earned it, this one certainly did.

I came back to breakfast with a fine appetite. Hans knew how to cook our little menu; he had water and fire at his disposal, so he was able to add a bit of variety to our usual fare. For dessert he served us a few cups of coffee, and never did that delicious drink taste better to me than it did just then.

"Now," said my uncle, "it's the hour of the tide, and we mustn't miss the chance to study the phenomenon."

"The tide!" I exclaimed.

"Of course."

"You mean the pull of the moon and the sun reaches all the way down here?"

"Why not? Aren't all bodies subject, as a whole, to universal gravitation? So this mass of water can't escape the general law either. And so, in spite of the atmospheric pressure bearing down on its surface, you're going to watch it rise just like the Atlantic itself."

At that moment we were treading the sand of the shore, and the waves were creeping little by little up the beach.

"There it is — the tide beginning to come in!" I cried.

"Yes, Axel, and from these lines of foam you can see that the sea rises about ten feet."

"It's marvelous!"

"No — it's natural."

"Say what you like, all of this seems extraordinary to me, and I can hardly believe my own eyes. Who would ever have imagined, here inside the Earth's crust, a genuine ocean, with its ebb and flow, its breezes, its storms?"

"Why not? Is there any physical reason against it?"

"I can't see one — not once we've had to give up the theory of central heat."

"So, up to now, Davy's theory holds true?"

"Obviously, and from here on nothing contradicts the existence of seas or lands inside the globe."

"No doubt — but uninhabited ones."

"Well now, why shouldn't these waters give shelter to a few fish of some unknown species?"

"In any case, we haven't spotted a single one so far."

"All right, then we can rig up some lines and see whether the hook has as much luck down here as it does in the oceans up under the moon."

"We'll try it, Axel, because we have to get at every secret of these new regions."

"But where are we, Uncle? I haven't asked you that yet — surely your instruments have answered it."

"Horizontally, three hundred and fifty leagues from Iceland."

"As far as all that?"

"I'm certain I'm not off by five hundred fathoms."

"And the compass still points southeast?"

"Yes, with a westerly declination of nineteen degrees, forty-two minutes — exactly as on the surface. As for its dip, there's a curious thing I've observed with the greatest care."

"And what's that?"

"That the needle, instead of dipping toward the pole the way it does in the northern hemisphere, tilts up instead."

"So we have to conclude that the point of magnetic attraction lies somewhere between the surface of the globe and the spot we've reached?"

"Precisely. And it's likely that if we came out under the polar regions, near that seventieth degree where James Ross discovered the magnetic pole, we'd see the needle stand straight up. So this mysterious center of attraction isn't situated at any great depth."

"Indeed — and there's a fact science never suspected."

"Science, my boy, is made of errors — but errors that are good to make, because little by little they lead to the truth."

"And what depth are we at?"

"A depth of thirty-five leagues."

"So," I said, studying the map, "the mountainous part of Scotland is above us, and up there the Grampian peaks lift their snow-covered summits to a prodigious height."

"Yes," the professor answered, laughing. "It's a bit heavy to carry, but the vault is solid. The great architect of the universe built it out of good materials, and man could never have given it such a span. What are the arches of bridges and the vaultings of cathedrals next to this nave with a radius of three leagues, under which an ocean and its storms can spread out at their ease?"

"Oh, I'm not afraid the sky will fall on my head. Now, Uncle — what are your plans? Aren't you thinking of going back to the surface of the globe?"

"Go back! The very idea! On the contrary — we'll carry on with our journey, since everything has gone so well so far."

"Still, I don't see how we're going to push on beneath this liquid plain."

"I don't intend to throw myself into it headfirst. But if oceans, strictly speaking, are really only lakes, since they're ringed by land, then all the more so this inner sea must be hemmed in by the granite mass."

"There's no doubt about that."

"Well then — on the opposite shore, I'm certain to find new ways through."

"How long do you suppose this ocean is?"

"Thirty or forty leagues."

"Ah," I said, privately suspecting that the estimate might well be wide of the mark.

"So we've no time to lose, and tomorrow we put to sea."

Without meaning to, I looked around for the ship that was supposed to carry us.

"Ah," I said, "so we're setting sail. Fine! And on what vessel do we take passage?"

"It won't be on a vessel, my boy, but on a good, solid raft."

"A raft!" I exclaimed. "A raft is as impossible to build as a ship, and I don't really see..."

"You don't see, Axel — but if you listened, you might hear!"

"Hear?"

"Yes — a certain hammering that would tell you Hans is already at work."

"He's building a raft?"

"Yes."

"What! He's already brought down trees with his axe?"

"Oh, the trees were already felled. Come and you'll see him at it."

After a quarter of an hour's walk, on the far side of the promontory that formed our little natural harbor, I caught sight of Hans at work; a few more steps and I was beside him. To my great surprise, a half-finished raft lay stretched out on the sand. It was made of beams of a peculiar wood, and a great number of planks, ribs, and frames of every sort littered the ground. There was enough there to build a whole navy.

"Uncle," I cried, "what is this wood?"

"It's pine, fir, birch — every kind of northern conifer, turned to stone under the action of the sea water."

"Is that possible?"

"It's what they call surtarbrandur, or fossil wood."

"But then, like lignite, it must be as hard as stone — it won't float!"

"Sometimes that happens; there's wood of this kind that has turned into real anthracite. But others, like these, have only begun to undergo the fossil change. Look for yourself," my uncle added, tossing one of these precious bits of driftwood into the sea.

The piece of wood, after vanishing, came back to the surface of the waves and bobbed with their swell.

"Are you convinced?" said my uncle.

"Convinced above all that it's unbelievable!"

By the evening of the next day, thanks to the guide's skill, the raft was finished. It was ten feet long by five wide; the surtarbrandur beams, lashed together with strong ropes, made a solid surface, and once launched, this improvised craft floated calmly on the waters of the Lidenbrock Sea.
