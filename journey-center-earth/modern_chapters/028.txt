Chapter 29

When I came to, I was lying in a kind of half-darkness, stretched out on a pile of thick blankets. My uncle was keeping watch, scanning my face for some last flicker of life. At my first sigh he took my hand; at my first glance he let out a cry of joy.

"He's alive! He's alive!" he shouted.

"Yes," I answered in a weak voice.

"My boy," said my uncle, crushing me against his chest, "you're saved!"

I was deeply moved by the tone in which he said those words, and even more by the care that came with them. But it took an ordeal like this to wring such tenderness out of the Professor.

Just then Hans came in. He saw my hand in my uncle's, and I dare say his eyes shone with real contentment.

"God dag," he said.

"Good morning, Hans, good morning," I murmured. "And now, Uncle, tell me — where are we at this moment?"

"Tomorrow, Axel, tomorrow. Today you're still too weak. I've wrapped your head in compresses that mustn't be disturbed. So sleep, my boy, and tomorrow you'll know everything."

"But at least," I pressed, "what time is it, what day?"

"Eleven o'clock at night. It's Sunday, the 9th of August, and I won't allow you to question me any further before the 10th of this month."

The truth was, I was very weak indeed; my eyes closed against my will. I needed a night's rest, and so I let myself drift off on the thought that my time alone had lasted four long days.

The next morning, when I woke, I looked around me. My bed — made up of all our travel blankets — had been set up in a charming grotto adorned with magnificent stalagmites, its floor carpeted with fine sand. A half-light filled the place. No torch, no lamp had been lit, and yet certain inexplicable gleams came in from outside, filtering through a narrow opening in the grotto. I could also hear a vague, indistinct murmur, like the sound of waves breaking on a beach, and now and then the whistling of a breeze.

I wondered whether I was truly awake, whether I was still dreaming, whether my brain — cracked in my fall — wasn't picking up sounds that were purely imaginary. And yet neither my eyes nor my ears could be deceived to such a degree.

"That's a ray of daylight," I thought, "slipping in through that crack in the rock! That really is the murmur of waves! That really is the whistling of the breeze! Am I mistaken, or have we come back to the surface of the earth? Has my uncle given up his expedition, then — or has he actually brought it to a happy end?"

I was putting these unanswerable questions to myself when the Professor came in.

"Good morning, Axel!" he said cheerfully. "I'd wager you're feeling well!"

"I certainly am," I said, sitting up on the blankets.

"That's just as it should be, for you've slept peacefully. Hans and I took turns watching over you, and we've seen you get steadily better."

"It's true, I do feel my old self again — and to prove it, I'll do full justice to whatever breakfast you'll be good enough to serve me!"

"You shall eat, my boy: the fever has left you. Hans rubbed your wounds with some ointment or other that the Icelanders keep the secret of, and they've healed beautifully. He's a fine fellow, our hunter!"

As he talked, my uncle was preparing a little food, which I devoured despite his warnings. All the while I pelted him with questions, and he hurried to answer them.

That was how I learned that my providential fall had carried me right to the mouth of an almost vertical gallery. Since I'd come down in the middle of a torrent of stones — the smallest of which would have been enough to crush me — it followed that part of the rock face must have slid down with me. That terrifying vehicle had ferried me all the way into my uncle's arms, where I dropped, bloody and lifeless.

"Truly," he said to me, "it's astonishing you didn't kill yourself a thousand times over. But for God's sake, let's never be separated again — or we may risk never seeing each other more."

"Let's never be separated again!" So the journey wasn't over? I opened my eyes wide in amazement, which immediately drew this question:

"What's the matter, Axel?"

"I have something to ask you. You say I'm here safe and sound?"

"No doubt about it."

"All my limbs are in one piece?"

"Certainly."

"And my head?"

"Your head, apart from a few bruises, is sitting perfectly well on your shoulders."

"Well, then, I'm afraid my brain may be out of order."

"Out of order?"

"Yes. We haven't come back to the surface of the globe?"

"Certainly not!"

"Then I must be mad, because I see daylight, I hear the sound of the wind blowing and of the sea breaking!"

"Ah! Is that all?"

"Will you explain it to me?"

"I'll explain nothing, because it can't be explained; but you'll see, and you'll understand, that geology hasn't yet said its last word."

"Then let's go out!" I cried, springing up.

"No, Axel, no! The open air might do you harm."

"The open air?"

"Yes, the wind is fairly violent. I won't have you expose yourself like that."

"But I assure you I feel perfectly fine."

"A little patience, my boy. A relapse would put us in a difficult spot, and we've no time to lose, because the crossing may be a long one."

"The crossing?"

"Yes. Rest again today, and we'll set sail tomorrow."

"Set sail!"

That last word made me leap up.

What! Set sail! So we had a river, a lake, a sea at our disposal? Was some vessel riding at anchor in an inner harbor somewhere?

My curiosity was roused to the highest pitch. My uncle tried in vain to hold me back. When he saw that my impatience would do me more harm than satisfying it, he gave in.

I dressed quickly; and, by way of extra precaution, I wrapped myself in one of the blankets and stepped out of the grotto.
