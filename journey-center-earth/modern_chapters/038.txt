Chapter 39

For another half hour our feet trod those beds of bones. We pressed on, driven by a burning curiosity. What other marvels did this cavern hold, what treasures for science? My eyes braced for every surprise, my imagination for every astonishment.

The shores of the sea had long since vanished behind the hills of the ossuary. The reckless professor, careless of losing his way, dragged me far into the distance. We advanced in silence, bathed in the electric waves. By some phenomenon I cannot explain—thanks to the light's diffusion, complete now in every direction—the glow fell evenly on every face of every object. Its source no longer sat at any fixed point in space, and it cast no shadow whatever. You would have thought yourself standing at high noon in high summer, in the heart of the equatorial regions, beneath the vertical rays of the sun. Every trace of vapor had disappeared. The rocks, the far-off mountains, a few confused masses of distant forest, took on a strange look under that equal wash of luminous fluid. We were like that fantastical figure of Hoffmann's who lost his shadow.

After a mile's march, the edge of an immense forest appeared—but no longer one of those mushroom woods that surrounded Port Graüben.

This was the vegetation of the Tertiary era in all its splendor. Great palms of species long extinct, magnificent palmacites, pines, yews, cypresses, arborvitae—these stood for the family of conifers, laced together by a net of tangled vines. A carpet of mosses and liverworts clothed the ground in softness. A few streams murmured beneath these shade trees—hardly worthy of the name, since they threw no shade. Along their banks grew tree ferns like those in the hot-houses of the inhabited world. Only, all these trees, shrubs, and plants lacked color, robbed of the sun's quickening warmth. Everything blurred into a single uniform tint, brownish and as though faded. The leaves had lost their green, and the flowers themselves—so numerous in that Tertiary age that first saw them born—colorless now and without scent, seemed cut from paper bleached by the action of the atmosphere.

My uncle Lidenbrock ventured in beneath these gigantic thickets. I followed him, not without a certain dread. Since nature had gone to the trouble of laying out a vegetable banquet here, why should the fearsome mammals not be feeding at it? In the broad clearings the fallen, time-gnawed trees had left open, I could make out legumes, maples, madder plants, and a thousand edible shrubs dear to the ruminants of every period. Then, mingled and jumbled together, rose the trees of regions utterly unlike one another on the surface of the globe—the oak growing beside the palm, the Australian eucalyptus leaning against the Norway fir, the northern birch tangling its branches with the branches of the New Zealand kauri. It was enough to confound the reason of the cleverest classifiers in earthly botany.

Suddenly I stopped. With one hand I held my uncle back.

The diffused light let me pick out the smallest objects deep in the thickets. I thought I had seen... no? Truly, with my own eyes, I was seeing immense shapes stirring beneath the trees! Yes—they were gigantic animals, a whole herd of Mastodons, no longer fossils but living, and just like those whose remains were found in 1801 in the marshes of Ohio! I saw those great elephants, their trunks writhing beneath the trees like a legion of serpents. I heard the sound of their long tusks, the ivory boring into the old trunks. Branches cracked, and leaves ripped away by the massive load vanished into the vast maws of these monsters.

So this dream, in which I had watched that whole world of prehistoric times be born again—the ages of the Tertiary and the Quaternary—was coming true at last! And there we stood, alone, in the bowels of the globe, at the mercy of its savage inhabitants!

My uncle stared.

"Come," he said all at once, seizing my arm, "forward, forward!"

"No!" I cried. "No! We have no weapons! What would we do in the middle of a herd of giant quadrupeds? Come away, uncle, come away! No human creature can defy the fury of these monsters and live."

"No human creature!" my uncle answered, dropping his voice. "You're wrong, Axel! Look—look, over there! I think I see a living being! A being like ourselves! A man!"

I looked, shrugging my shoulders, determined to push my disbelief to its very last limit. But whatever I wished, I had to yield to the evidence.

Indeed, less than a quarter of a mile off, leaning against the trunk of an enormous kauri, a human being—a Proteus of these underground lands, a new son of Neptune—was tending that countless herd of Mastodons!

Immanis pecoris custos, immanior ipse!

Yes! immanior ipse—the guardian of a monstrous herd, more monstrous still himself! This was no longer the fossil being whose corpse we had turned up in the ossuary; this was a giant capable of commanding these monsters. His height topped twelve feet. His head, big as a buffalo's, disappeared into the brush of an unkempt mane of hair. You would have called it a true mane, like that of the elephant of the earliest ages. In one hand he brandished an enormous branch—a fitting crook for this antediluvian shepherd.

We had stood frozen, dumbfounded. But we could be spotted. We had to run.

"Come, come!" I cried, dragging my uncle away—and for the first time he let himself be led!

A quarter of an hour later, we were out of sight of that fearsome enemy.

And now that I think it over calmly, now that peace has settled again in my mind, now that months have passed since that strange and supernatural encounter—what am I to think, what am I to believe? No! It is impossible! Our senses were deceived, our eyes did not see what they saw! No human creature exists in this subterranean world! No race of men dwells in these lower caverns of the globe, careless of the inhabitants of its surface, cut off from all contact with them! It is madness, deep madness!

I would sooner admit the existence of some animal whose structure comes close to the human—some ape of the earliest geological epochs, some Protopithecus, some Mesopithecus like the one M. Lartet discovered in the fossil bed of Sansan! But this one, in his sheer size, overran every measurement paleontology has ever recorded! No matter! An ape, yes, an ape, however improbable! But a man, a living man, and with him a whole race buried in the bowels of the earth—never!

Meanwhile we had left the bright, luminous forest, struck dumb with wonder, crushed under an amazement that bordered on stupor. We were running in spite of ourselves. It was a true flight, like those terrifying headlong rushes one suffers in certain nightmares. Instinctively we were making back toward the Lidenbrock Sea, and I don't know into what wild ramblings my mind might have wandered, had one practical worry not called me back to more useful observations.

Though I was certain I trod ground never before touched by our steps, I often noticed clusters of rocks whose shape recalled those of Port Graüben. Sometimes the likeness was uncanny. Streams and cascades fell by the hundred from the jutting rocks; I thought I saw again the bed of surtarbrandur—fossil wood—our faithful Hansbach, and the grotto where I had come back to life; then, a few steps on, the arrangement of the buttresses, the sudden appearance of a stream, the startling profile of a rock, would throw me back into doubt.

The professor shared my uncertainty; he could not get his bearings in this uniform panorama. I understood as much from a few words that escaped him.

"Clearly," I told him, "we haven't landed at our starting point, but surely, by following the shoreline round, we'll come back near Port Graüben."

"In that case," my uncle answered, "there's no use pushing this exploration any further, and the best thing is to go back to the raft. But are you sure you're not mistaken, Axel?"

"It's hard to say, since all these rocks look alike. Still, I think I recognize the headland at whose foot Hans built his craft. We must be near the little harbor—if this isn't the very spot," I added, studying an inlet I thought I knew.

"But no, Axel, we'd at least find our own tracks, and I see nothing—"

"But I see something!" I cried, springing toward an object glittering on the sand.

"What is it, then?"

"There!" I answered, and I showed my uncle a dagger I had just picked up.

"Well!" he said. "So you brought that weapon along with you?"

"I? Not at all—but you, I suppose?"

"Not that I know of; I have never had that object in my possession."

"And I still less, uncle."

"Now that is peculiar."

"But no, it's quite simple; the Icelanders often carry weapons of this kind, and Hans—to whom this one belongs—lost it on this beach..."

"Hans!" said my uncle, shaking his head.

Then he examined the weapon closely.

"Axel," he said to me gravely, "this dagger is a weapon of the sixteenth century, a true poniard, of the sort gentlemen wore at their belts to give the coup de grâce; it is of Spanish origin; it belongs neither to you, nor to me, nor to the hunter!"

"Do you dare to say—?"

"Look—it wasn't nicked like this from being driven into men's throats; its blade is covered with a coat of rust that dates back not a day, not a year, not a century!"

The professor was warming up, in his usual way, letting himself be carried off by his imagination.

"Axel," he went on, "we are on the trail of the great discovery! This blade has lain abandoned on the sand for one, two, three hundred years, and got these nicks on the rocks of this underground sea!"

"But it didn't come here on its own!" I cried. "It didn't twist itself out of shape! Someone came before us!"

"Yes—a man."

"And this man?"

"This man carved his name with this dagger! This man wanted, one more time, to mark the road to the center with his own hand! Let's search—search!"

And, prodigiously excited, off we went along the high wall, questioning the least crevice that might turn into a gallery.

We came at last to a place where the shore narrowed. The sea nearly bathed the foot of the buttresses, leaving a passage a fathom wide at most. Between two spurs of rock, we could make out the mouth of a dark tunnel.

There, on a slab of granite, two mysterious letters appeared, half eaten away—the two initials of the bold and fantastical traveler:

* ᛐ * ᚼ *

"A. S.!" cried my uncle. "Arne Saknussemm! Always Arne Saknussemm!"
