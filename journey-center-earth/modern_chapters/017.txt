Chapter 18

At eight in the morning a ray of daylight came to wake us. The thousand facets of lava on the walls caught it as it passed and scattered it like a shower of sparks.

The glow was strong enough to let us make out the things around us.

"Well, Axel, what do you say?" cried my uncle, rubbing his hands together. "Did you ever spend a quieter night in our house on the König-strasse? No rattle of carts, no shouting of hawkers, no bawling of boatmen!"

"No doubt we're very peaceful at the bottom of this well; but there's something frightening about the very stillness of it."

"Come now!" my uncle exclaimed. "If you're already scared, what will you be later on? We haven't gone so much as an inch into the bowels of the earth yet!"

"What do you mean?"

"I mean that we've only reached the ground level of the island! This long vertical tube, the one that opens out at the crater of the Sneffels, stops at roughly the level of the sea."

"Are you certain of that?"

"Quite certain. Check the barometer and you'll see!"

And it was true: the mercury, after climbing little by little inside the instrument as we descended, had come to rest at twenty-nine inches.

"You see," the professor went on, "we still have only the pressure of a single atmosphere, and I can't wait for the moment when the manometer takes over from this barometer."

Indeed, the barometer would become useless to us the instant the weight of the air overtook the pressure it registered at ocean level.

"But," I said, "isn't there reason to fear that this ever-increasing pressure will become quite painful?"

"No. We'll descend slowly, and our lungs will grow used to breathing a more compressed atmosphere. Balloonists end up short of air as they rise into the upper layers; we, on the contrary, may end up with too much. But I prefer it that way. Let's not lose a moment. Where's the bundle that went down into the mountain ahead of us?"

I remembered then that we'd searched for it in vain the evening before. My uncle put the question to Hans, who, after a careful look with his hunter's eyes, answered:

"Der huppe!"

"Up there."

Sure enough, the bundle was snagged on a jut of rock a hundred feet or so above our heads. At once the nimble Icelander scrambled up like a cat, and in a few minutes the bundle rejoined us.

"Now," said my uncle, "let's have breakfast — but let's breakfast like men who may have a long march ahead of them."

The biscuit and dried meat we washed down with a few mouthfuls of water laced with juniper spirit.

Breakfast over, my uncle drew from his pocket a notebook meant for his observations. He took up his various instruments one after another and jotted down the following readings:

Monday, the 1st of July.

Chronometer: 8 h. 17 m. in the morning. Barometer: 29 in. 7 lines. Thermometer: 6°. Direction: E.-S.-E.

This last observation applied to the dark gallery and was given by the compass.

"Now, Axel," the professor cried in a voice full of enthusiasm, "we are truly going to plunge into the bowels of the globe. Here, then, is the very moment when our journey begins."

So saying, my uncle took in one hand the Ruhmkorff apparatus slung around his neck, and with the other he switched the electric current into the coil of the lantern; a fairly bright light drove back the darkness of the gallery.

Hans carried the second apparatus, and it too was set going. This ingenious use of electricity let us travel for a long while by an artificial daylight of our own, even in the midst of the most flammable gases.

"On our way!" said my uncle.

Each of us shouldered his pack again. Hans took charge of shoving ahead of him the bundle of ropes and clothing, and — with me the third in line — we entered the gallery.

Just as I was about to swallow myself up in that dark passage, I lifted my head and caught one last glimpse, through the shaft of the immense tube, of that Iceland sky "which I was never to see again."

The lava, at the last eruption of 1229, had forced itself a passage through this tunnel. It coated the inside with a thick, gleaming glaze, and the electric light bounced off it a hundred times brighter.

The whole difficulty of the route lay in not sliding too fast down a slope tilted at about forty-five degrees; luckily, certain worn spots and swellings in the rock served as steps, and we had only to climb down while letting our baggage run ahead of us on the end of a long rope.

But what became steps beneath our feet turned to stalactites on the other walls. The lava, porous in places, was studded with little rounded blisters; crystals of opaque quartz, set with clear drops of glass and hanging from the vault like chandeliers, seemed to light up as we passed. You'd have thought the genies of the abyss were illuminating their palace to receive their guests from the surface of the earth.

"It's magnificent!" I cried in spite of myself. "What a sight, Uncle! Do you see these shades of lava running from red-brown to brilliant yellow by the faintest gradations? And these crystals glowing at us like luminous globes?"

"Ah, now you're coming round, Axel!" my uncle answered. "Ah, so you find it splendid, my boy! You'll see plenty more, I hope. March! March!"

He'd have done better to say "slide," for we were letting ourselves go, effortlessly, down the tilting slopes. This was Virgil's facilis descensus Averni — the easy way down into the underworld. The compass, which I consulted often, pointed southeast with unshakable steadiness. This flow of lava swerved neither one way nor the other. It held to the straightness of a drawn line.

Yet the heat wasn't rising in any noticeable way; that bore out Davy's theories, and more than once I consulted the thermometer in astonishment. Two hours after starting out, it still read only 10° — an increase of just 4°. That gave me grounds to suppose our descent was more horizontal than vertical. As for knowing exactly what depth we'd reached, nothing was easier. The professor measured precisely the angles of deviation and slope of the route — but he kept the results of his calculations to himself.

Around eight in the evening he gave the signal to halt. Hans sat down at once; the lamps were hung from a shelf of lava. We were in a kind of cavern where the air was in no way lacking. On the contrary. Certain gusts reached us. What caused them? What disturbance in the atmosphere gave rise to them? It was a question I didn't try to settle just then; hunger and exhaustion had left me incapable of reasoning. Seven straight hours of descent aren't made without a great expenditure of strength. I was worn out. So the word halt was a pleasure to hear. Hans spread a few provisions on a block of lava, and each of us ate with appetite. One thing worried me, though: our reserve of water was half gone. My uncle counted on refilling it from underground springs, but so far there'd been absolutely none. I couldn't help drawing his attention to the subject.

"This absence of springs surprises you?" he said.

"It does — it even worries me. We've only got water enough for five days."

"Set your mind at ease, Axel. I promise you we'll find water, and more than we'll want."

"When?"

"When we've left this casing of lava behind. How do you expect springs to break out through walls like these?"

"But perhaps this flow runs on to great depths. It seems to me we haven't come very far yet, not vertically at least."

"What makes you suppose that?"

"Because if we were very deep inside the earth's crust, the heat would be stronger."

"According to your system," my uncle replied. "And what does the thermometer show?"

"Barely fifteen degrees — an increase of only nine degrees since we started."

"Well then, draw your conclusion."

"Here's my conclusion. According to the most exact observations, the rise in temperature inside the globe is one degree for every hundred feet. But certain local conditions can alter that figure. At Yakutsk in Siberia, for instance, it's been noted that the increase of one degree occurred every thirty-six feet — which obviously depends on the conductivity of the rocks. I'll add, too, that in the neighborhood of an extinct volcano, and through gneiss, it's been observed that the temperature rose by only one degree for every hundred and twenty-five feet. Let's take that last figure, then, which is the most favorable, and calculate."

"Calculate away, my boy."

"Nothing could be easier," I said, laying out the figures in my notebook. "Nine times a hundred and twenty-five feet gives eleven hundred and twenty-five feet of depth."

"Exactly right."

"Well then?"

"Well, according to my observations, we've reached ten thousand feet below sea level."

"Is that possible?"

"Yes — or figures are no longer figures!"

The professor's calculations were correct. We had already passed by six thousand feet the greatest depths ever reached by man, such as the mines of Kitz-Bahl in the Tyrol and those of Wüttemberg in Bohemia.

The temperature, which should have been eighty-one degrees at that spot, was barely fifteen. It gave one singularly much to think about.
