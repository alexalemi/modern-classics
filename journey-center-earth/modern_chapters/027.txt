Chapter 28

When I came back to life, my face was wet — but wet with tears. How long that stupor lasted, I couldn't say. I no longer had any way of measuring time. Never was there a solitude like mine, never an abandonment so complete.

The fall had cost me a great deal of blood. I could feel myself drenched in it! Oh, how I wished I were dead — and that the dying were still to be done! I wanted no more thoughts. I drove every idea away, and, beaten down by the pain, I rolled myself against the far wall.

Already I could feel the faint coming over me again, and with it the last, utter blotting-out, when a violent noise struck my ear. It was like a long roll of thunder, and I heard the waves of sound go losing themselves, little by little, in the distant depths of the abyss.

Where had that noise come from? From some phenomenon, no doubt, taking place deep in the mass of the earth — the explosion of a pocket of gas, or the collapse of some mighty stratum of the globe.

I listened again. I wanted to know whether the sound would come a second time. A quarter of an hour went by. Silence reigned in the gallery. I could no longer even hear the beating of my own heart.

Suddenly my ear, pressed by chance against the wall, seemed to catch vague, elusive, faraway words. I gave a start.

"It's a hallucination!" I thought.

But no. Listening harder, I really did hear a murmur of voices. What was being said I was too weak to make out — but people were talking. Of that I was certain.

For a moment I was afraid the words might be my own, thrown back by an echo. Perhaps I had cried out without knowing it? I clamped my lips shut and pressed my ear once more to the rock.

"Yes — yes, someone's speaking! Someone's speaking!"

Even by dragging myself a few feet farther along the wall, I heard more clearly. I managed to catch words — uncertain, strange, incomprehensible. They reached me like syllables spoken under the breath, murmured, so to speak. The word "förlorad" came again and again, in a tone of grief.

What did it mean? Who was saying it? My uncle or Hans, obviously. But if I could hear them, then they could hear me.

"Here!" I shouted with all my strength. "Over here!"

I listened; I strained into the dark for an answer, a cry, a sigh. Nothing came. A few minutes passed. A whole world of ideas had bloomed in my mind. I decided my weakened voice couldn't reach my companions.

"Because it is them," I repeated. "What other men would be buried thirty leagues underground?"

I set myself to listening again. Running my ear along the wall, I found one mathematical point where the voices seemed to reach their greatest strength. The word "förlorad" came back to me, then that roll of thunder that had roused me from my torpor.

"No," I said. "No. It isn't through the solid mass that these voices come. The wall is granite; it wouldn't let the loudest blast pass through it! This sound is arriving along the gallery itself. There must be some very particular trick of acoustics at work here!"

I listened once more, and this time — yes! this time I heard my own name thrown distinctly across the space!

It was my uncle who spoke it! He was talking with the guide, and the word "förlorad" was a Danish one!

Then I understood everything. To make myself heard, I had to speak precisely along this wall, which would carry my voice the way an iron wire carries electricity.

But there was no time to lose. If my companions moved a few steps away, the acoustic effect would be destroyed. So I went up close to the wall and pronounced these words as clearly as I could:

"Uncle Lidenbrock!"

I waited in the keenest anxiety. Sound does not travel at any tremendous speed. The density of the layers of air doesn't even increase its velocity; it only increases its loudness. A few seconds — centuries — went by, and at last these words reached my ear:

"Axel! Axel! Is that you?"

.............................

"Yes! Yes!" I answered.

.............................

"My poor boy, where are you?"

.............................

"Lost, in the deepest darkness!"

.............................

"But your lamp?"

.............................

"Out."

.............................

"And the stream?"

.............................

"Gone."

.............................

"Axel, my poor Axel, take heart!"

.............................

"Wait a little — I'm exhausted. I haven't the strength to answer. But keep talking to me!"

.............................

"Courage," my uncle went on. "Don't speak — listen. We searched for you, going up the gallery and down it. Impossible to find you. Oh, how I wept for you, my boy! At last, supposing you were still somewhere on the road along the Hansbach, we came back down here firing off our guns. Now, if our voices can meet — pure trick of acoustics! — our hands still can't touch! But don't despair, Axel. It's already something, to be able to hear each other!"

.............................

While he spoke I had been thinking. A certain hope, still faint, was creeping back into my heart. Above all, there was one thing I needed to know. So I brought my lips to the wall and said:

"Uncle?"

.............................

"My boy?" came the answer after a few moments.

.............................

"First we have to find out how far apart we are."

.............................

"That's easy enough."

.............................

"You have your chronometer?"

.............................

"Yes."

.............................

"Then take it. Say my name, and note the exact second you speak. I'll repeat it back the instant it reaches me, and you note the precise moment my answer comes to you."

.............................

"Good — and half the time between my call and your reply will give the time my voice takes to travel to you."

.............................

"That's it, Uncle."

.............................

"Are you ready?"

.............................

"Yes."

.............................

"Then pay attention — I'm going to say your name."

.............................

I pressed my ear to the wall, and the moment the word "Axel" reached me I answered at once, "Axel," and then I waited.

.............................

"Forty seconds," my uncle said. "Forty seconds passed between the two words; so the sound takes twenty seconds to climb up. Now, at one thousand and twenty feet a second, that comes to twenty thousand four hundred feet — a league and a half, and an eighth over."

.............................

"A league and a half!" I murmured.

.............................

"Well — that can be crossed, Axel!"

.............................

"But do I have to go up, or down?"

.............................

"Down — and here's why. We've reached a vast open space where a great many galleries come together. The one you followed can't help but bring you here, because it seems all these cracks, all these fractures of the globe, radiate out around the immense cavern we're sitting in. So get up, take to the road again; walk, crawl if you have to, slide down the steep slopes, and you'll find our arms waiting to catch you at the end of it. On your way, my boy — on your way!"

.............................

Those words breathed life back into me.

"Goodbye, Uncle!" I cried. "I'm off. Our voices won't be able to reach each other anymore, once I've left this spot. So — goodbye!"

.............................

"Until we meet, Axel! Until we meet!"

.............................

Those were the last words I heard. That astonishing conversation, carried through the solid mass of the earth, exchanged across more than a league of distance, ended on those words of hope. I said a prayer of thanks to God, for He had led me, among all these dark immensities, to perhaps the one single point where my companions' voices could reach me.

That very surprising acoustic effect was easily explained by ordinary physical laws alone: it came from the shape of the corridor and the conductivity of the rock. There are plenty of examples of this carrying of sounds that can't be heard at all in the spaces between. I remembered that the phenomenon had been observed in many places — among others, in the inner gallery of the dome of Saint Paul's in London, and above all among the curious caverns of Sicily, those latomias near Syracuse, the most marvelous of which is known as the Ear of Dionysius.

These memories came back to me, and I saw clearly that, since my uncle's voice reached me, no obstacle stood between us. By following the path of the sound, I was bound, logically, to arrive where he was — if my strength didn't betray me on the road.

So I got up. I dragged myself rather than walked. The slope was fairly steep; I let myself slide down it.

Soon the speed of my descent grew at a frightening rate, and threatened to turn into an outright fall. I no longer had the strength to stop myself.

All at once the ground gave way beneath my feet. I felt myself rolling, bouncing off the jagged edges of a vertical gallery — a true well. My head struck a sharp rock, and I lost consciousness.
