Chapter 37

It would be impossible for me to paint the succession of feelings that shook Professor Lidenbrock—the stupefaction, the disbelief, and finally the rage. Never had I seen a man so thoroughly thrown at first, so infuriated after. The exhaustion of the crossing, the dangers we'd run, all of it to do over again! We had gone backward instead of forward!

But my uncle quickly got the upper hand of himself.

"So! Fate plays me tricks like this!" he cried. "The elements are conspiring against me! Air, fire, and water are joining forces to block my way! Well, then—they'll learn what my will can do. I will not give in, I will not fall back a single inch, and we shall see who wins out, man or nature!"

Standing on the rock, furious, menacing, Otto Lidenbrock looked like the ferocious Ajax defying the gods. But I judged it wise to step in and put some brake on this senseless zeal.

"Listen to me," I said in a firm tone. "There's a limit to every ambition here below. You mustn't fight against the impossible. We're badly equipped for a sea voyage. Five hundred leagues can't be made on a sorry heap of beams with a blanket for a sail, a stick in place of a mast, and against winds let loose. We can't steer, we're the plaything of every storm, and it's the act of madmen to attempt this impossible crossing a second time!"

I was able to unspool the whole series of these utterly irrefutable arguments for a good ten minutes without being interrupted—but that was solely due to the professor's inattention, for he didn't hear a word of my reasoning.

"To the raft!" he cried.

That was his answer. I did everything I could—begged, raged—and ran up against a will harder than granite.

Just then Hans was finishing his repairs to the raft. You'd have thought this strange creature could read my uncle's intentions. With a few pieces of surtarbrandur he had reinforced the craft. A sail already rose from it, and the wind played in its rippling folds.

The professor said a few words to the guide, and at once the man set to loading the baggage and getting everything ready for departure. The air was fairly clear, and the northwest wind held steady.

What could I do? Resist alone against two? Impossible. If only Hans would side with me. But no! It seemed the Icelander had set aside every personal will and taken a vow of self-denial. There was nothing to be got from a servant so utterly bound to his master. I had to march forward.

So I was about to take my accustomed place on the raft when my uncle stopped me with his hand.

"We won't set out until tomorrow," he said.

I made the gesture of a man resigned to anything.

"I must neglect nothing," he went on, "and since fate has driven me onto this stretch of coast, I won't leave it until I've explored it."

This remark will make sense once you know that we had come back to the northern shore, but not to the very spot of our first departure. Port Graüben had to lie farther to the west. Nothing more reasonable, then, than to examine carefully the surroundings of this new landing.

"Let's go exploring!" I said.

And, leaving Hans to his work, off we went. The space between the reach of the sea and the foot of the buttressing cliffs was very wide; you could walk half an hour before reaching the wall of rock. Our feet crushed countless shells of every shape and size, the homes of animals from the earliest ages. I also spotted enormous shells whose diameter often topped fifteen feet. They had belonged to those gigantic glyptodons of the Pliocene period, of which the modern turtle is only a small reduction. Beyond that, the ground was strewn with a great quantity of stony debris—a kind of pebble rounded by the surf and laid out in successive lines. I was thus led to the observation that the sea must once have occupied this space. On the scattered rocks, now beyond its reach, the waves had left plain traces of their passage.

This could explain, up to a point, the existence of this ocean forty leagues below the surface of the globe. But in my view this mass of water was bound to lose itself little by little in the bowels of the earth, and it clearly came from the waters of the Ocean, which had forced their way through some fissure. And yet one had to grant that this fissure was now plugged, for otherwise this whole cavern—or better, this immense reservoir—would have filled in a fairly short time. Perhaps this water, having had to struggle against subterranean fires, had partly turned to vapor. Hence the explanation of the clouds hanging over our heads and the release of that electricity which brewed storms inside the earth's mass.

This theory of the phenomena we had witnessed struck me as satisfactory; for however great the wonders of nature may be, they are always explicable by physical reasons.

So we were walking over a kind of sedimentary terrain formed by the waters, like all the terrain of that period, so widely spread across the surface of the globe. The professor examined every crevice of rock attentively. Let any opening at all exist, and it became important to him to sound its depth.

For a mile we had followed the shores of the Lidenbrock Sea when the ground suddenly changed its look. It seemed churned up, convulsed by a violent upheaval of the lower strata. In many a spot, sinkings and heavings bore witness to a powerful dislocation of the earth's mass.

We were advancing with difficulty over these fractures of granite, mixed with flint, quartz, and alluvial deposits, when a field—more than a field, a whole plain of bones—rose before our eyes. It looked like an immense cemetery, where the generations of twenty centuries mingled their eternal dust. High swells of debris rose in tiers into the distance. They rolled away to the limits of the horizon and lost themselves there in a melting haze. There, over perhaps three square miles, all the life of animal history lay heaped—the history barely written in the too-recent terrain of the inhabited world.

And yet an impatient curiosity swept us on. Our feet crushed with a dry crackle the remains of these prehistoric animals, fossils that the Museums of the great cities fight over for their rare and fascinating scraps. A thousand Cuviers would not have lived long enough to reconstruct the skeletons of the organic beings laid out in this magnificent ossuary.

I was dumbfounded. My uncle had raised his great arms toward the thick vault that served us for a sky. His mouth gaping wide, his eyes blazing behind the lenses of his glasses, his head bobbing up and down and side to side—his whole posture, in short, betrayed a boundless astonishment. He stood before a priceless collection of Leptotheriums, Mericotheriums, Mastodons, Protopithecuses, Pterodactyls, of every antediluvian monster piled up there for his personal satisfaction. Picture a passionate bibliomaniac suddenly carried into that famous library of Alexandria burned by Omar and by some miracle risen again from its ashes! Such was my uncle, Professor Lidenbrock.

But his wonder took an altogether different turn when, running across that volcanic dust, he seized a bare skull and cried out in a quivering voice:

"Axel! Axel! A human head!"

"A human head, Uncle?" I answered, no less stunned.

"Yes, my nephew! Ah, Monsieur Milne-Edwards! Ah, Monsieur de Quatrefages! Why aren't you here where I am—I, Otto Lidenbrock!"
