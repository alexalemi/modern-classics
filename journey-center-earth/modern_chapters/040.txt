Chapter 41

The next day, Thursday, August 27, was a famous date in that underground voyage. I can't call it to mind even now without dread setting my heart pounding all over again. From that moment on, our reason, our judgment, our ingenuity counted for nothing — we became the playthings of the forces of the earth.

By six we were up. The moment was near to blast our way with powder through the crust of granite.

I begged for the honor of lighting the fuse. Once it was lit, I was to rejoin my companions on the raft, which we had left fully loaded; then we would stand well off, to guard against the dangers of the explosion, whose effects might not stay confined inside the rock.

By our calculations the fuse would burn for about ten minutes before it carried the flame to the powder chamber. So I had time enough to get back to the raft.

I made ready to play my part, not without a certain nervous flutter.

After a hurried meal, my uncle and the hunter went aboard while I stayed on the shore. I was armed with a lit lantern, which would serve to set the fuse alight.

"Go, my boy," my uncle told me, "and come straight back to us."

"Don't worry, Uncle. I won't dawdle along the way."

At once I made for the mouth of the gallery, opened my lantern, and took hold of the end of the fuse.

The Professor stood with his chronometer in his hand.

"Are you ready?" he called to me.

"I'm ready."

"Then — fire, my boy!"

I plunged the fuse quickly into the flame, which sputtered at its touch, and, running the whole way, I came back to the shore.

"Aboard," said my uncle, "and let's shove off."

With one powerful shove, Hans sent us out to sea. The raft drew off some twenty fathoms.

It was a heart-stopping moment. The Professor followed the sweep of the chronometer's needle with his eye.

"Five minutes more," he said. "Four more. Three more."

My pulse was beating half-seconds.

"Two more. One!... Come crashing down, mountains of granite!"

What happened then? The noise of the detonation — I don't think I heard it. But the shape of the rocks changed all at once before my eyes; they parted like a curtain. I glimpsed a bottomless abyss opening up right there in the shore. The sea, seized with vertigo, became nothing but one enormous wave, and the raft rose straight up on its back.

All three of us were flung down. In less than a second, light gave way to the deepest darkness. Then I felt the solid support fail — not under my feet, but under the raft itself. I thought it was going straight down. It wasn't. I wanted to speak to my uncle, but the roar of the waters would have kept him from hearing me.

For all the darkness, the noise, the shock, the terror, I understood what had just happened.

Beyond the rock that had just blown apart, there was a chasm. The explosion had set off a kind of earthquake in that ground riddled with fissures; the gulf had opened, and the sea, turned to a torrent, was sweeping us down into it.

I felt myself lost.

An hour, two hours — how would I know! — passed like that. We pressed elbow to elbow, we held one another's hands to keep from being pitched off the raft; savage jolts came whenever it struck the wall. Still, these shocks were rare, from which I concluded that the gallery was widening considerably. This was Saknussemm's road, beyond a doubt — but instead of going down it alone, we had, through our own recklessness, dragged a whole sea along with us.

These thoughts, you'll understand, presented themselves to my mind in a vague, murky form. I could barely string them together during that dizzying rush, so like a fall. Judging by the wind that whipped my face, it must have outstripped the fastest trains. Lighting a torch under such conditions was impossible, and our last electric apparatus had been smashed at the moment of the explosion.

So I was greatly surprised to see a light suddenly shine near me. Hans's calm face was lit up. The deft hunter had managed to light the lantern, and though its flame flickered as if about to go out, it threw a few gleams into the frightful darkness.

The gallery was wide. I'd been right to judge it so. Our feeble light didn't let us make out both walls at once. The slope of the waters that carried us along outdid the most unclimbable rapids of America; their surface seemed made of a sheaf of liquid arrows loosed with tremendous force. I can't render my impression by any truer comparison. Caught now and then in eddies, the raft sometimes went spinning as it flew. When it neared the walls of the gallery, I cast the lantern's light on them, and I could gauge our speed by watching the jutting rocks change into continuous streaks, so that we were hemmed in by a web of moving lines. I reckoned our speed must have reached thirty leagues an hour.

My uncle and I looked around us with haggard eyes, propped against the stump of the mast, which had snapped clean off at the moment of the catastrophe. We turned our backs to the air so as not to be smothered by the speed of a motion no human power could check.

Meanwhile the hours slipped by. The situation didn't change, but one thing came to complicate it.

Trying to put a little order into the cargo, I saw that most of the things we'd stowed aboard had vanished at the moment of the explosion, when the sea assailed us so violently! I wanted to know exactly where we stood on our resources, and, lantern in hand, I began my search. Of our instruments, nothing was left but the compass and the chronometer. The ladders and ropes came down to a scrap of cable coiled around the stump of the mast. Not a pickaxe, not a mattock, not a hammer — and, an irreparable disaster, we hadn't a single day's food!

I set about ransacking the crannies of the raft, the least corners formed by the beams and the joints of the planking. Nothing! Our provisions came down to one piece of dried meat and a few biscuits.

I stared like an idiot! I refused to understand! And yet, what danger was I worrying over? Even if our provisions had been enough for months, for years, how were we to get out of the abysses into which this unstoppable torrent was dragging us? What good was it to dread the tortures of hunger, when death was already offering itself in so many other forms? Starve to death — would we even have the time?

Still, by some inexplicable quirk of the imagination, I forgot the immediate peril for the menaces of the future, which loomed before me in all their horror. Besides, perhaps we could escape the fury of the torrent and come back up to the surface of the globe. How? I had no idea. Where? What did it matter! One chance in a thousand is always a chance, whereas death by starvation left us no hope in any proportion, however small.

The thought came to me to tell my uncle everything, to show him how utterly destitute we were, and to make the exact reckoning of how much time we had left to live. But I had the courage to hold my tongue. I wanted to leave him all his composure.

At that moment, the lantern's light dimmed little by little and went out entirely. The wick had burned to the end. The darkness became absolute again. There was no longer any thought of driving off that impenetrable gloom. We still had one torch, but it could never have stayed lit. So, like a child, I shut my eyes to keep from seeing all that darkness.

After a fairly long stretch of time, the speed of our rush redoubled. I could tell by the beating of the air on my face. The slope of the waters was becoming extreme. I truly believe we were no longer sliding. We were falling. I had within me the sense of an almost vertical drop. My uncle's hand and Hans's, clamped on my arms, held me back with all their strength.

Suddenly, after an immeasurable time, I felt something like a shock; the raft hadn't struck any solid body, but it had abruptly stopped in its fall. A waterspout, an immense liquid column, crashed down on its surface. I was suffocated. I was drowning.

Yet this sudden flood didn't last. In a few seconds I found myself in open air, which I gulped down in great lungfuls. My uncle and Hans were gripping my arm hard enough to break it, and the raft still bore all three of us.
