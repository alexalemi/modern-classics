Chapter 9

The day of departure arrived. The evening before, the obliging Mr. Thomson had brought us pressing letters of recommendation for Count Trampe, the governor of Iceland; for Mr. Pietursson, the bishop's coadjutor; and for Mr. Finsen, the mayor of Reykjavik. In return, my uncle gave him the warmest of handshakes.

On the 2nd, at six in the morning, our precious baggage was safely aboard the Valkyrie. The captain led us to some rather cramped cabins set beneath a sort of deckhouse.

"Have we got a good wind?" asked my uncle.

"Excellent," answered Captain Bjarne. "A south-easterly. We'll leave the Sund on a broad reach with every sail set."

A few moments later the schooner — under her foresail, her spanker, her topsail, and her topgallant — got under way and stood out into the strait under full canvas. An hour later the capital of Denmark seemed to sink into the distant waves, and the Valkyrie was skimming the coast of Elsinore. In the jittery state I was in, I half expected to see the ghost of Hamlet wandering along that legendary terrace.

"Sublime madman!" I said to him. "You'd surely approve of us! Maybe you'd even come along, down to the center of the globe, to find a solution to your eternal doubt!"

But nothing showed itself on the ancient walls. The castle, in any case, is far younger than the heroic prince of Denmark. Today it serves as a sumptuous lodge for the gatekeeper of that strait of the Sund, through which fifteen thousand ships of every nation pass each year.

The castle of Kronborg soon vanished into the mist, and so did the tower of Helsingborg, standing on the Swedish shore, and the schooner heeled gently under the breezes of the Cattegat.

The Valkyrie was a fine sailer, but with a sailing ship you can never be too sure of anything. She was carrying coal, household utensils, pottery, woolen clothing, and a cargo of wheat to Reykjavik; five crewmen, all Danes, were enough to handle her.

"How long will the crossing take?" my uncle asked the captain.

"About ten days," he replied, "if we don't run into too many north-west squalls off the Faroes."

"But, all the same, you're not liable to suffer any serious delays?"

"No, Professor Lidenbrock; rest easy, we'll get there."

Toward evening the schooner rounded Cape Skagen at the northern tip of Denmark, crossed the Skagerrak during the night, ranged along the far end of Norway off Cape Lindesnes, and stood out into the North Sea.

Two days later we sighted the coasts of Scotland at the level of Peterhead, and the Valkyrie steered for the Faroes, passing between the Orkneys and the Shetlands.

Soon our schooner was being battered by the waves of the Atlantic; she had to tack against the north wind, and it was no easy matter to reach the Faroes. On the 3rd, the captain made out Mygganes, the easternmost of those islands, and from that moment on he ran straight for Cape Portland, on the southern coast of Iceland.

The crossing offered no incident worth remarking on. I bore the trials of the sea well enough; my uncle, to his great annoyance — and to his still greater shame — never stopped being sick.

He was therefore unable to press Captain Bjarne with questions about the Sneffels, about means of communication, about the ease of transport; he had to put off his inquiries until he arrived, and spent the whole time stretched out in his cabin, whose bulkheads groaned under the great heaves of the ship's pitching. It has to be admitted that he rather deserved his fate.

On the 11th, we raised Cape Portland. The weather, clear just then, allowed us to make out the Myrdals Yocul, which towers over it. The cape consists of a big steep-sloped hill, planted all alone on the beach.

The Valkyrie kept a reasonable distance from the coast, working westward along it, in the midst of numerous schools of whales and sharks. Soon an immense rock appeared, pierced clean through, and the foaming sea drove furiously against it. The Westman islets seemed to rise out of the ocean, like a scattering of rocks sown across the liquid plain. From that point on, the schooner stood well out to round Cape Reykjanes at a safe distance — the cape that closes off the western angle of Iceland.

The sea, running very high, kept my uncle from coming up on deck to admire those jagged coasts battered by the south-westerly winds.

Forty-eight hours later, coming out of a storm that forced the schooner to run under bare poles, we raised to the east the beacon of Skagen point, whose dangerous rocks reach out a great distance beneath the waves. An Icelandic pilot came aboard, and three hours later the Valkyrie was dropping anchor off Reykjavik, in Faxa Bay.

The professor came out of his cabin at last, a little pale, a little the worse for wear, but as enthusiastic as ever, and with a look of satisfaction in his eyes.

The townspeople, singularly interested by the arrival of a ship in which everyone had a stake, were gathering on the quay.

My uncle was in a hurry to abandon his floating prison — not to say his floating hospital. But before leaving the deck of the schooner, he pulled me forward, and there, with his finger, he pointed out to me, at the northern part of the bay, a tall two-peaked mountain, a double cone crowned with everlasting snows.

"The Sneffels!" he cried. "The Sneffels!"

Then, with a gesture, he charged me to keep absolute silence, and went down into the boat that was waiting for him. I followed him, and soon we were setting foot on the soil of Iceland.

The first man to appear was a fine-looking fellow dressed in a general's uniform. He was, however, nothing but a plain magistrate, the governor of the island, Baron Trampe himself. The professor recognized the man he was dealing with. He handed the governor his letters from Copenhagen, and a short conversation followed in Danish, one to which I remained an utter stranger — for good reason. But out of this first exchange came one result: that Baron Trampe placed himself entirely at the disposal of Professor Lidenbrock.

My uncle received a most gracious welcome from the mayor, Mr. Finsen, no less military in his dress than the governor, but just as peaceable by temperament and by office.

As for the coadjutor, Mr. Pietursson, he was at that moment making an episcopal tour of the northern Bailiwick; we had to give up, for the time being, on being presented to him. But one charming man, whose help became very precious to us, was Mr. Fridriksson, professor of natural sciences at the Reykjavik school. This modest scholar spoke only Icelandic and Latin; he came to offer me his services in the language of Horace, and I felt that we were made to understand each other. He was, in fact, the only person I was able to converse with during my stay in Iceland.

Of the three rooms that made up his house, this excellent man put two at our disposal, and we were soon installed in them with our baggage, whose quantity rather astonished the inhabitants of Reykjavik.

"Well, Axel," said my uncle, "this is going nicely, and the hardest part is done."

"The hardest part?" I exclaimed. "How so?"

"Why, of course — all that's left is to go down!"

"If you put it that way, you're right; but after going down, I imagine we'll have to come back up?"

"Oh, that hardly worries me at all! Come now — there's no time to lose. I'm going to the library. There may be some manuscript of Saknussemm's there, and I'd be very glad to consult it."

"Then, in the meantime, I'll go and see the town. Won't you do the same?"

"Oh, that interests me only moderately. What's curious in this land of Iceland isn't above ground, but below."

I went out and wandered at random.

Getting lost in the two streets of Reykjavik would not have been an easy thing to do. So I wasn't obliged to ask my way — which, in the language of gestures, exposes you to a great many misunderstandings.

The town stretches out over fairly low, marshy ground, between two hills. An immense flow of lava covers it on one side and slopes down in gentle ramps toward the sea. On the other side spreads that vast Faxa Bay, bounded to the north by the enormous glacier of the Sneffels, and in which the Valkyrie was, at that moment, the only ship at anchor. Ordinarily the English and French fishery-guard vessels lie moored out in the offing, but they were then on duty along the eastern coasts of the island.

The longer of Reykjavik's two streets runs parallel to the shore; there live the merchants and traders, in wooden cabins built of red beams laid horizontally. The other street, situated farther to the west, runs toward a small lake, between the houses of the bishop and of the other figures who have nothing to do with trade. I had soon paced out these dreary, dismal thoroughfares; here and there I caught a glimpse of a scrap of faded turf, like an old wool carpet worn threadbare by use, or some semblance of a kitchen garden, whose sparse vegetables — potatoes, cabbages, and lettuces — would have sat comfortably on a Lilliputian table; a few sickly gillyflowers were also trying to put on a little air of sunshine.

Toward the middle of the non-commercial street, I came upon the public cemetery, enclosed by an earthen wall, and in which there was no shortage of room. Then, in a few strides, I reached the governor's house — a hovel compared with the town hall of Hamburg, a palace next to the huts of the Icelandic population.

Between the little lake and the town rose the church, built in the Protestant taste and constructed of calcined stones that the volcanoes themselves go to the trouble of extracting; in the great westerly winds, its roof of red tiles must obviously scatter through the air, to the considerable detriment of the faithful.

On a nearby rise, I caught sight of the National School, where — as I learned later from our host — they taught Hebrew, English, French, and Danish, four languages of which, to my shame, I did not know a single word. I would have been the last of the forty pupils that little college could boast, and unworthy to sleep alongside them in those two-compartment cupboards where more delicate souls would suffocate the very first night.

In three hours I had visited not only the town but its surroundings. The general aspect of it was singularly bleak. No trees, no vegetation, so to speak. Everywhere the sharp ridges of volcanic rock. The Icelanders' huts are made of earth and peat, with walls sloping inward; they look like roofs laid down on the ground. Only, these roofs are relatively fertile meadows. Thanks to the heat of the dwelling, the grass grows on them well enough, and it is carefully mown at haying time — otherwise the household animals would come and graze on these verdant homes.

During my excursion I met few inhabitants; coming back along the commercial street, I saw the greater part of the population busy drying, salting, and loading cod, the principal article of export. The men looked robust but heavy — a sort of blond Germans, with pensive eyes, who feel themselves a little set apart from the rest of humanity, poor exiles banished to this land of ice, whom nature really ought to have made into Eskimos, since she condemned them to live on the edge of the polar circle! I tried in vain to surprise a smile on their faces; they laughed sometimes, by a kind of involuntary contraction of the muscles, but they never smiled.

Their costume consisted of a coarse blouse of black wool, known throughout the Scandinavian countries by the name of "vadmel," a broad-brimmed hat, trousers with a red border, and a piece of leather folded into the shape of a shoe.

The women, with sad, resigned faces of a rather pleasant cast but without expression, were dressed in a bodice and skirt of dark "vadmel"; unmarried girls wore, over their hair braided into garlands, a little cap of brown knitting; married women wrapped their heads in a colored kerchief, topped with a crest of white linen.

After a good long walk, when I came back into Mr. Fridriksson's house, my uncle was already there in the company of his host.
