Chapter 23

For a whole hour my fevered brain churned through every reason that could have made our unflappable hunter act as he had. The most absurd ideas tangled themselves together in my head. I honestly thought I was going mad.

But at last a sound of footsteps came echoing up from the depths of the gulf. Hans was climbing back. A wavering light began to slide along the walls, then broke out through the mouth of the passage. Hans appeared.

He went over to my uncle, laid a hand on his shoulder, and gently woke him. My uncle sat up.

"What is it?" he asked.

"Vatten," the hunter answered.

You have to believe that under the spur of terrible suffering, every one of us becomes a linguist. I didn't know a single word of Danish, and yet I understood our guide's meaning by pure instinct.

"Water! Water!" I cried, clapping my hands and flailing about like a lunatic.

"Water!" my uncle repeated. "Hvar?" he asked the Icelander.

"Nedat," Hans replied.

Where? Down below! I understood every word. I had seized the hunter's hands and was squeezing them hard, while he looked back at me with perfect calm.

The preparations for departure were quickly made, and soon we were heading down a corridor that dropped two feet with every fathom.

An hour later, we had gone about a thousand fathoms and descended two thousand feet.

Just then we heard, quite distinctly, an unfamiliar sound running through the flanks of the granite wall — a kind of muffled roar, like distant thunder. Through that first half hour of walking, finding no sign of the promised spring, I felt the old anguish creeping back over me. But then my uncle explained where the noise was coming from.

"Hans wasn't mistaken," he said. "What you hear there is the roar of a torrent."

"A torrent?" I exclaimed.

"There's no doubt about it. An underground river is running all around us!"

We quickened our pace, keyed up with hope. I no longer felt my exhaustion. The sound of that murmuring water was already reviving me. The torrent, after running for a long time above our heads, was now coursing through the wall on our left, roaring and leaping. Again and again I ran my hand over the rock, hoping to find some trace of seepage or damp. But in vain.

Another half hour slipped by. Another half league was covered.

And now it became clear that the hunter, during his absence, could not have carried his search any farther than this. Guided by an instinct peculiar to mountain folk and to water-diviners, he had "felt" this torrent through the rock — but he certainly hadn't laid eyes on the precious liquid, and he hadn't drunk from it.

Soon it even became obvious that if we kept walking, we would be moving away from the torrent, whose murmur was steadily fading.

We turned back. Hans stopped at the exact spot where the torrent seemed to run closest.

I sat down against the wall, while the waters raced past just two feet from me with tremendous force. But a wall of granite still stood between us.

Without thinking, without asking myself whether some way of reaching that water might exist, I gave in to a first surge of despair.

Hans looked at me, and I thought I saw a smile flicker across his lips.

He rose and took the lamp. I followed him. He walked over to the wall. I watched what he did. He pressed his ear to the dry stone and slid it slowly along, listening with the greatest care. I realized he was searching for the precise point where the torrent could be heard most loudly. He found that point in the left-hand wall, three feet above the floor.

How stirred I was! I hardly dared guess what the hunter meant to do! But I couldn't help understanding it — and applauding it, and smothering him with grateful caresses — when I saw him take up his pick to attack the rock itself.

"Saved!" I cried. "Saved!"

"Yes," my uncle repeated, beside himself. "Hans is right! Ah, the brave fellow! We'd never have thought of that!"

I should think not! A trick like that, however simple, would never have crossed our minds. Nothing could be more dangerous than to drive a pickaxe into this framework of the globe. What if some collapse were to bury us? What if the torrent, forcing its way through the rock, were to come flooding in on us? These dangers were anything but imaginary — but just then no fear of cave-in or of drowning could stop us, and our thirst was so fierce that, to slake it, we would have dug at the very bed of the ocean.

Hans set to work at a task neither my uncle nor I could have managed. Our impatience would have run away with our hands, and the rock would have flown apart under our reckless blows. The guide, on the contrary, calm and steady, wore the stone away little by little with a series of small, repeated strokes, opening a gap half a foot wide. I could hear the sound of the torrent swelling, and I already imagined I could feel the blessed water splashing against my lips.

Soon the pick had sunk two feet into the granite wall. The work had been going on for more than an hour. I was writhing with impatience! My uncle wanted to resort to drastic measures. I had trouble holding him back, and he had already snatched up his pick when suddenly a hissing sound broke out. A jet of water shot from the wall and smashed against the rock face opposite.

Hans, half thrown down by the impact, couldn't hold back a cry of pain. I understood why when, plunging my own hands into the liquid jet, I let out a violent shout of my own: the spring was boiling hot.

"Water at a hundred degrees!" I cried.

"Well, it'll cool down," my uncle answered.

The corridor filled with steam, while a stream took shape and went off to lose itself in the winding passages underground. And soon after, we were scooping up our first mouthfuls of it.

Ah, what bliss! What incomparable delight! What was this water? Where did it come from? It hardly mattered. It was water — and though it was still hot, it brought back to the heart the life that had been about to slip away. I drank without stopping, without even tasting.

It was only after a full minute of pure pleasure that I exclaimed:

"Hey — but this is iron-rich water!"

"Excellent for the stomach," my uncle replied, "and highly mineralized! Here's a trip worth more than a cure at Spa or Toeplitz!"

"Ah, it's so good!"

"I should think so — water drawn from two leagues underground. It has a taste of ink about it that's not the least bit unpleasant. A splendid resource Hans has found us here! And so I propose we give his name to this life-saving stream."

"Agreed!" I cried.

And the name "Hansbach" was adopted then and there. Hans was no prouder for it. After refreshing himself in moderation, he settled into a corner with his usual composure.

"Now," I said, "we mustn't let this water go to waste."

"What's the point?" my uncle replied. "I suspect the spring is inexhaustible."

"Never mind that! Let's fill the skin and the flasks, then we'll try to plug the opening."

My advice was followed. Using chips of granite and some tow, Hans tried to stop up the hole he'd made in the wall. It was no easy matter. We scalded our hands without managing it; the pressure was too great, and our efforts came to nothing.

"It's obvious," I said, "that the upper reaches of this watercourse lie at a great height, judging by the force of the jet."

"No doubt about it," my uncle answered. "There must be a thousand atmospheres of pressure there, if this column of water is thirty-two thousand feet high. But an idea occurs to me."

"What's that?"

"Why are we so bent on plugging this opening?"

"Well — because..."

I would have been hard put to come up with a good reason.

"When our flasks are empty, are we sure of finding water to fill them again?"

"No, obviously not."

"Well then, let's let this water run. It'll flow downhill of its own accord, and it will guide the very people it refreshes along the way!"

"Now that's clever!" I cried. "And with this stream for a companion, there's no longer any reason we shouldn't succeed in what we've set out to do."

"Ah, so you're coming around, my boy," said the professor, laughing.

"I'm doing better than coming around — I'm already there."

"One moment! Let's begin by taking a few hours' rest."

I really had forgotten it was night. The chronometer took it upon itself to remind me. Soon each of us, sufficiently restored and refreshed, fell into a deep sleep.
