Chapter 45

Here is the conclusion of a story that even the people most accustomed to being astonished by nothing will refuse to believe. But I have armored myself in advance against human incredulity.

We were received by the fishermen of Stromboli with all the consideration owed to castaways. They gave us clothes and food. After forty-eight hours' wait, on August 31, a little speronare carried us to Messina, where a few days' rest cured us of all our exhaustion.

On Friday, September 4, we boarded the Volturne, one of the mail-packets of the Imperial French steamship line, and three days later we set foot in Marseille, with only one thing left nagging at our minds: that cursed compass. This inexplicable business went on troubling me quite seriously. On the evening of September 9, we arrived in Hamburg.

What Martha's astonishment was, what Graüben's joy was, I give up trying to describe.

"Now that you're a hero," my dear fiancée said to me, "you'll never have to leave me again, Axel!"

I looked at her. She was crying and smiling at the same time.

I leave you to imagine the sensation Professor Lidenbrock's return made in Hamburg. Thanks to Martha's indiscretions, the news of his departure for the center of the earth had spread all over the world. Nobody had been willing to believe it — and, seeing him again, nobody believed it any more than before.

Still, the presence of Hans, and various reports arriving from Iceland, gradually changed public opinion.

And so my uncle became a great man, and I the nephew of a great man, which is already something. Hamburg threw a celebration in our honor. A public session was held at the Johannaeum, where the Professor recounted his expedition and left out only the facts concerning the compass. That same day he deposited Saknussemm's document in the city archives, and he expressed his keen regret that circumstances stronger than his own will had not allowed him to follow the Icelandic traveler's trail all the way to the center of the earth. He was modest in his glory, and his reputation grew all the greater for it.

So much honor was bound to stir up envy against him. It did — and since his theories, backed by hard facts, contradicted the accepted scientific systems on the question of the central fire, he waged remarkable debates by pen and by word with the learned men of every country.

For my own part, I cannot accept his cooling theory: in spite of everything I saw, I believe, and I always will believe, in central heat; but I admit that certain circumstances, still poorly defined, may modify that law under the action of natural phenomena.

At the very moment these questions were at their most heated, my uncle suffered a real grief. Hans, despite our pleading, had left Hamburg; the man to whom we owed everything refused to let us pay him our debt. He had been seized by homesickness for Iceland.

"Färval," he said one day — and with that single word of farewell, he set out for Reykjavik, where he arrived safely.

We were singularly attached to our brave eider-hunter; his absence will never make him forgotten by those whose lives he saved, and I will certainly not die without having seen him one last time.

To conclude, I must add that this Voyage to the Center of the Earth caused an enormous sensation in the world. It was printed and translated into every language; the most respected newspapers fought over its principal episodes, which were commented on, disputed, attacked, and defended with equal conviction in the camp of the believers and in that of the skeptics. A rare thing! — my uncle enjoyed, in his own lifetime, all the glory he had won, and there was even a certain Mr. Barnum who offered to "exhibit" him at a very high price throughout the United States.

But one annoyance — let us call it, rather, a torment — crept in among all this glory. One fact remained inexplicable: the business of the compass. Now, for a scientist, an unexplained phenomenon like that becomes an agony of the intellect. Well! Heaven had it in store for my uncle to be completely happy after all.

One day, while I was arranging a collection of minerals in his study, I caught sight of that famous compass and began to examine it.

For six months it had been sitting there in its corner, never suspecting the trouble it was causing.

All at once — what astonishment seized me! I let out a cry. The Professor came running.

"What is it?" he demanded.

"This compass!..."

"Well?"

"Its needle points south instead of north!"

"What are you saying?"

"Look! Its poles are switched."

"Switched!"

My uncle looked, compared, and made the whole house shake with one magnificent leap.

What a flash of light lit up his mind and mine at the very same instant!

"So then," he cried, the moment he found his voice again, "ever since our arrival at Cape Saknussemm, the needle of this blasted compass has been pointing south instead of north?"

"Obviously."

"Then our mistake explains itself. But what phenomenon could have produced this reversal of the poles?"

"Nothing could be simpler."

"Explain yourself, my boy."

"During the storm on the Lidenbrock Sea, that ball of fire, the one that magnetized all the iron on the raft, quite simply threw our compass out of true!"

"Ah!" cried the Professor, bursting out laughing, "so it was a trick of the electricity, was it?"

From that day on, my uncle was the happiest of scientists, and I the happiest of men — for my pretty Virland girl, giving up her position as ward, took her place in the house on König-strasse in the double capacity of niece and wife. Needless to add, her uncle was the illustrious Professor Otto Lidenbrock, corresponding member of every scientific, geographical, and mineralogical society in the five parts of the world.
