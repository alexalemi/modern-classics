Chapter 8

Altona, really just a suburb of Hamburg, is the terminus of the Kiel railway, the one that would carry us down to the shore of the Belt. In less than twenty minutes we had crossed into Holstein.

At half past six the carriage pulled up in front of the station. My uncle's countless parcels, his bulky travel gear, were unloaded, carried, weighed, labeled, and loaded again into the baggage car, and by seven o'clock we were seated face to face in the same compartment. The whistle screamed, the locomotive lurched into motion. We were off.

Was I resigned to it? Not yet. Still, the cool morning air and the details of the countryside, swept endlessly past by the speed of the train, drew my mind away from my great worry.

As for the Professor's thoughts, they plainly ran ahead of this train, which crawled far too slowly for his impatience. We were alone in the car, and we said nothing. My uncle kept going through his pockets and his travel bag with meticulous care. I could see that he was missing none of the items needed to carry out his plans.

Among other things, one sheet of paper, carefully folded, bore the letterhead of the Danish chancery and the signature of Mr. Christiensen, the consul at Hamburg and a friend of the Professor's. That was to smooth our way in getting recommendations at Copenhagen for the governor of Iceland.

I also caught sight of the notorious document, buried away in the most secret pocket of his wallet. I cursed it from the bottom of my heart, and went back to studying the country. It was a broad stretch of uninteresting plains, monotonous, muddy, and fairly fertile: perfect country for laying down a railway, ideal for those dead-straight lines the railroad companies love so much.

But the monotony had no time to wear me out, for three hours after we set off the train stopped at Kiel, a stone's throw from the sea.

Since our baggage was booked all the way through to Copenhagen, we didn't have to deal with it. Even so, the Professor followed it with an anxious eye as it was carried down to the steamer. There it vanished into the hold.

In his haste, my uncle had timed the railway and boat connections so cleverly that we had a whole day to kill. The steamer Ellenora didn't sail until nightfall. Hence a nine-hour fever, during which the irascible traveler consigned to the devil the entire administration of boats and railways, and every government that tolerated such abuses. I had to chime in with him when he tackled the captain of the Ellenora about it. He wanted to force the man to fire up the boilers and leave that instant. The captain told him to go take a walk.

At Kiel, as anywhere, a day somehow has to pass. By dint of strolling along the green shores of the bay, at the head of which the little town sits; wandering the thick woods that make it look like a nest bundled in branches; admiring the villas, each with its own little cold-bath house; and, in short, by walking and grumbling, we finally reached ten o'clock at night.

The clouds of smoke from the Ellenora were unfurling into the sky; the deck trembled under the shuddering of the boiler; we were aboard and the proud owners of two berths stacked one above the other in the boat's single cabin.

At a quarter past ten the moorings were cast off, and the steamer ran swiftly out onto the dark waters of the Great Belt.

The night was black; there was a fine breeze and a heavy sea; a few lights along the coast showed in the gloom; later, I couldn't say when, a flashing lighthouse blazed out above the waves. That was all that stayed with me of that first crossing.

At seven in the morning we landed at Korsör, a little town on the western coast of Zealand. There we jumped straight from the boat into another railway that swept us across a country no less flat than the plains of Holstein.

That was three more hours of travel before reaching the capital of Denmark. My uncle hadn't shut his eyes all night. In his impatience, I honestly think he was pushing the car along with his feet.

At last he spotted an opening of sea.

"The Sound!" he cried.

Off to our left stood a vast building that looked like a hospital.

"That's a madhouse," said one of our fellow travelers.

"Good," I thought. "There's an establishment where we ought to end our days! And huge as it is, that hospital would still be too small to hold all of Professor Lidenbrock's madness!"

At last, at ten in the morning, we set foot in Copenhagen; the baggage was loaded onto a cart and driven with us to the Hôtel du Phoenix on Bred-Gade. It took half an hour, since the station is out beyond the edge of the city. Then my uncle, after a hasty wash, dragged me along at his heels. The hotel porter spoke German and English; but the Professor, being the polyglot he was, questioned him in good Danish, and it was in good Danish that the man told him where to find the Museum of Northern Antiquities.

The director of that remarkable place — where wonders are heaped up that would let you reconstruct the whole history of the country from its old stone weapons, its drinking cups, and its jewelry — was a scholar, a friend of the Hamburg consul: Professor Thomson.

My uncle carried a warm letter of introduction to him. As a rule, one scholar gives another a rather cold reception. But here it was quite the opposite. Mr. Thomson, an obliging man, gave a cordial welcome to Professor Lidenbrock, and even to his nephew. It hardly needs saying that our secret was kept from the excellent director of the Museum. We simply wanted to visit Iceland as disinterested amateurs.

Mr. Thomson placed himself entirely at our service, and we combed the docks in search of a ship about to sail.

I was hoping there would be no means of transport at all; but no such luck. A little Danish schooner, the Valkyrie, was to set sail on the second of June for Reykjavik. The captain, Mr. Bjarne, happened to be aboard; his future passenger, in his joy, wrung his hands nearly to breaking. The good man was a little startled by such a grip. To him, going to Iceland was the most ordinary thing in the world, since it was his trade. To my uncle it was sublime. The worthy captain took advantage of this enthusiasm to charge us double for passage on his vessel. But we weren't looking that closely at the price.

"Be aboard on Tuesday, at seven in the morning," said Mr. Bjarne, after pocketing a respectable number of species-dollars.

We then thanked Mr. Thomson for his kind help, and made our way back to the Hôtel du Phoenix.

"It's going well! Very well indeed," my uncle kept repeating. "What luck, finding a ship all ready to leave! Now let's have lunch, and then go see the town."

We went to Kongens-Nye-Torw, an irregular square where there's a guardpost with two harmless cannons trained on nothing, frightening no one. Close by, at number 5, was a French "restauration" run by a cook named Vincent; there we lunched well enough for the modest price of four marks apiece.


Then I took a childish pleasure in roaming the city; my uncle let himself be led about, though he saw nothing of it — not the unremarkable royal palace, nor the pretty seventeenth-century bridge that spans the canal in front of the Museum, nor Thorvaldsen's huge cenotaph, decorated with dreadful murals and holding inside it the works of that sculptor, nor, in a rather handsome park, the little candy-box château of Rosenborg, nor the admirable Renaissance building of the Exchange with its steeple made from the intertwined tails of four bronze dragons, nor the great mills on the ramparts, whose vast sails swelled like the canvas of a ship in the sea wind.

What delightful walks my pretty Virland girl and I would have taken, down toward the harbor where the two-deckers and the frigates slept peacefully under their red roofing, along the green banks of the strait, through those thick shady groves where the citadel hides itself, its cannons poking their blackish muzzles out between the branches of the elders and the willows!

But alas! She was far away, my poor Graüben, and could I even hope to see her again?

Still, if my uncle noticed none of these enchanting sights, he was struck hard by the sight of a certain steeple on the island of Amak, which forms the southwest quarter of Copenhagen.

I got the order to steer our steps that way; I climbed aboard a little steam launch that ran the canal service, and in a few moments it drew up at the Dock-Yard quay.

After crossing a few narrow streets where convicts, dressed in trousers half yellow and half gray, labored under the cudgels of the prison guards, we came to Vor-Frelsers-Kirk. There was nothing remarkable about the church itself. But here is why its rather tall steeple had caught the Professor's eye: starting from the platform, an exterior staircase wound around its spire, its spirals unspooling straight up into open sky.

"Let's go up," said my uncle.

"But — my vertigo?" I objected.

"All the more reason. You need to get used to it."

"Still —"

"Come on, I tell you. Let's not waste time." I had to obey. A keeper who lived across the street handed us a key, and the climb began.

My uncle went ahead of me at a brisk pace. I followed, not without terror, for my head spun with woeful ease. I had neither the steadiness of eagles nor the steel nerves that go with it.

As long as we were shut inside the inner spiral, all went well; but after a hundred and fifty steps the open air struck me in the face; we had reached the platform of the steeple. There the airy staircase began, guarded by a flimsy railing, its steps growing narrower and narrower, seeming to climb toward infinity.

"I'll never manage it!" I cried.

"Would you be a coward, by any chance? Climb!" the Professor answered pitilessly.

I had no choice but to follow him, clinging on for dear life. The open air made my head swim; I felt the steeple sway under the gusts; my legs gave way beneath me; soon I was crawling on my knees, then on my belly; I kept my eyes shut; I was seasick with sheer space.

At last, my uncle hauling me up by the collar, I reached the ball at the top.

"Look," he said. "Look, and look hard! You need lessons in the abyss!"

I had to open my eyes. I saw the houses flattened, as though crushed by a fall, in the middle of a fog of smoke. Over my head, ragged clouds went streaming by, and by a trick of the eye they seemed to hang motionless, while the steeple, the ball, and I were all being dragged along at a fantastic speed. Far off, on one side, stretched the green countryside; on the other, the sea glittered under a shaft of sunlight. The Sound unrolled toward the point of Elsinore, dotted with a few white sails, true gull's wings, and in the eastern haze the barely sketched coasts of Sweden rose and fell. All this immensity spun before my eyes.

Nevertheless I had to stand up, hold myself straight, and look. My first lesson in vertigo lasted an hour. When at last I was allowed to go back down and set my foot on the solid pavement of the streets, I ached in every limb.

"We'll start again tomorrow," said my professor.

And in fact, for five days, I went back to this dizzying exercise, and, like it or not, I made noticeable progress in the art of "lofty contemplation."
