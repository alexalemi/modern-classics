Chapter 13

By rights it should have been dark, but at the sixty-fifth parallel the daylight of the polar regions was no surprise to me. In Iceland, through the months of June and July, the sun never sets.

Even so, the temperature had dropped. I was cold, and above all I was hungry. Welcome, then, was the "böer" that opened its doors to take us in.

It was a peasant's house, but for hospitality it might have belonged to a king. The moment we arrived, the master came out to shake our hands, and without further ceremony he beckoned us to follow him.

To follow him, mind you — walking beside him would have been out of the question. A long, narrow, gloomy passage led into the dwelling, which was built of barely squared timbers, and gave access to each of the rooms. There were four of them: the kitchen, the weaving workshop, the "badstofa" or family bedroom, and — best of all — the room for strangers. My uncle, whose height had not been considered when the house was built, cracked his head three or four times against the beams jutting from the ceiling.

We were shown into our room, a sort of large hall with a floor of packed earth, lit by a single window whose panes were made of sheep membrane, none too transparent. The bedding consisted of dry fodder tossed into two wooden frames painted red and decorated with Icelandic proverbs. I hadn't expected such comfort — only, the whole house was steeped in a powerful smell of dried fish, pickled meat, and sour milk, which my nose found hard going.

Once we had set aside our traveling gear, our host's voice called out, inviting us into the kitchen — the only room where a fire was ever lit, even in the bitterest cold.

My uncle hurried to obey this friendly summons. I followed.

The kitchen chimney was of an ancient design: a single stone in the middle of the room for a hearth, and a hole in the roof through which the smoke escaped. This kitchen served as the dining room as well.

As we came in, our host — as if he hadn't already seen us — greeted us with the word "saellvertu," which means "be happy," and came to kiss us on the cheek.

After him his wife spoke the same words, with the same ceremony; then the two of them, laying their right hands over their hearts, bowed deeply.

I should mention right away that this Icelandic woman was the mother of nineteen children, all of them — big and small — swarming together in the coils of smoke that the hearth poured into the room. Every moment I would catch sight of a little blond head, faintly melancholy, emerging from the fog. You would have taken them for a garland of angels who hadn't been quite washed.

My uncle and I gave this "brood" a warm welcome, and soon there were three or four of the little scamps on our shoulders, as many on our knees, and the rest between our legs. The ones who could talk repeated "saellvertu" in every tone imaginable. The ones who couldn't talk only shrieked the louder.

This concert was interrupted by the announcement of the meal. Just then the hunter came back in, having seen to feeding the horses — that is, he had economically turned them loose across the fields. The poor beasts would have to make do with grazing the scant moss on the rocks and a few not-very-nourishing seaweeds, and the next morning they would be sure to come back on their own to take up the work of the day before.

"Saellvertu," said Hans as he came in.

Then, calmly, automatically, without one kiss being any more pronounced than the next, he embraced the host, the hostess, and their nineteen children.

The ceremony over, we sat down at table, twenty-four strong, and therefore quite literally one on top of another. The luckiest among us had only two little ones on their knees.

Still, silence fell over this small world when the soup arrived, and the natural taciturnity — natural even to Icelandic urchins — resumed its rule. Our host served us a lichen soup that was not at all unpleasant, then an enormous portion of dried fish swimming in butter that had been souring for twenty years and was therefore, by Icelandic notions of good eating, far preferable to fresh. Along with this there was "skyr," a kind of curdled milk, served with biscuit and sharpened with juniper-berry juice; and finally, for drink, whey mixed with water, which the locals call "blanda." Whether this singular fare was good or not, I was in no state to judge. I was hungry, and for dessert I swallowed to the last mouthful a thick buckwheat porridge.

The meal finished, the children vanished; the grown-ups gathered around the hearth, where peat, heather, cow dung, and dried fish bones were burning. Then, after this "warming up," the various groups withdrew to their respective rooms. The hostess offered, as custom demanded, to pull off our stockings and trousers for us; but at our most gracious refusal she did not insist, and I was able at last to burrow into my bed of fodder.

The next day, at five in the morning, we said our farewells to the Icelandic peasant. My uncle had a great deal of trouble getting him to accept fair payment, and Hans gave the signal to set off.

A hundred paces out from Gardär, the land began to change its look; the ground turned marshy and less kind to walking. On the right, the chain of mountains ran on without end, like an immense system of natural fortifications, and we followed along its outer slope. Often streams presented themselves that had to be forded, and crossed without soaking the baggage too badly.

The wilderness grew steadily emptier. Now and then, though, a human shadow seemed to flee off in the distance; and if a bend in the road brought us unexpectedly close to one of these specters, I felt a sudden revulsion at the sight of a swollen head, its skin glistening and hairless, and the repellent sores betrayed by the rents in a set of wretched rags.

The unhappy creature did not come toward us with its deformed hand outstretched; on the contrary, it fled — though not so fast that Hans failed to hail it with the usual "saellvertu."

"Spetelsk," he would say.

"A leper!" my uncle would repeat.

And that one word was enough to work its repulsive effect. This horrible affliction of leprosy is fairly common in Iceland; it is not contagious but hereditary, and for that reason marriage is forbidden to these poor wretches.

Such apparitions were not the sort to brighten a landscape that was growing profoundly bleak; the last tufts of grass were dying away beneath our feet. Not a tree, unless you counted a few clumps of dwarf birch, no better than scrub. Not an animal, apart from a few horses — the ones their master couldn't feed — wandering across the dismal plains. Sometimes a falcon would glide through the gray clouds and then wing off swiftly toward the southern lands; I let myself drift into the melancholy of this wild country, and my memories carried me back to my native home.

Soon we had to cross several small fjords of no importance, and at last a real gulf; the tide, slack just then, let us pass without waiting and reach the hamlet of Alftanes, a mile beyond.

That evening, after fording two rivers rich in trout and pike, the Alfa and the Heta, we were forced to spend the night in an abandoned hovel, fit to be haunted by every goblin of Scandinavian mythology. The genie of cold had certainly taken up residence there, and it plagued us all night long.

The following day brought no particular incident. Always the same marshy ground, the same monotony, the same dreary aspect. By evening we had covered half the distance we had to travel, and we slept at the "annexia" of Krösolbt.

On the 19th of June, for about a mile, a field of lava stretched beneath our feet; this formation of the ground is called "hraun" in the country. The lava, wrinkled at the surface, took the shapes of cables, sometimes drawn out, sometimes coiled on themselves; an immense flow came down from the neighboring mountains — volcanoes now extinct, though these ruins bore witness to their past violence. Here and there, however, a few wisps of steam from hot springs crept along the ground.

We had no time to study these phenomena; we had to keep moving. Before long the marshy ground reappeared under our mounts' feet, broken up by little lakes. Our course now lay to the west; we had rounded the great bay of Faxa, and the twin white peaks of the Sneffels rose into the clouds less than five miles off.

The horses marched well; the difficulties of the ground did not stop them. For my part, I was beginning to grow very tired; my uncle stayed as firm and upright as on the first day, and I couldn't help admiring him as much as I admired the hunter, who looked on this whole expedition as a simple stroll.

On Saturday, the 20th of June, at six in the evening, we reached Büdir, a small town on the seashore, and the guide claimed his agreed-upon pay. My uncle settled up with him. It was Hans's own family — his uncles and first cousins, in fact — who offered us their hospitality; we were well received, and if it had been up to me, without imposing too far on the kindness of these good people, I would gladly have recovered from the fatigues of the journey among them. But my uncle, who had nothing to recover from, saw things otherwise, and the next morning we had to climb back onto our good beasts.

The ground felt the nearness of the mountain, whose granite roots pushed up out of the earth like those of an old oak. We were skirting the volcano's immense base. The professor never took his eyes off it; he gestured, he seemed to challenge it and to say: "There stands the giant I am going to tame!" At last, after twenty-four hours' march, the horses stopped of their own accord at the door of the presbytery of Stapi.
