Chapter 34

Wednesday, August 19. Luckily the wind, blowing hard, let us flee the scene of the battle in a hurry. Hans is still at the tiller. My uncle, roused out of his all-consuming thoughts by the incidents of that fight, has sunk back into his impatient contemplation of the sea.

The voyage takes up its monotonous sameness again — and I have no desire to break it at the price of yesterday's dangers.

Thursday, August 20. A north-northeast breeze, fairly gusty. Warm temperature. We're making three and a half leagues an hour.

Around noon a very distant noise reaches us.

I set the fact down here without being able to explain it. It is a steady, low roar.

"Somewhere far off," says the professor, "there must be a rock, or some little island where the sea is breaking."

Hans hauls himself to the top of the mast, but sees no reef. The ocean is smooth all the way to the horizon.

Three hours pass. The roaring seems to come from a distant waterfall.

I point this out to my uncle, who shakes his head. And yet I'm convinced I'm not mistaken. Are we, then, racing toward some cataract that will fling us down into the abyss? This way of going deeper may well please the professor, since it comes near the vertical — but for me...

In any case, there must be some thunderous phenomenon a few leagues to windward, because now the roars carry to us with tremendous violence. Do they come from the sky, or from the ocean?

I turn my eyes toward the vapors hanging in the atmosphere, and try to plumb their depths. The sky is calm; the clouds, swept up to the very top of the vault, seem motionless and lose themselves in the intense blaze of the light. So the cause of this phenomenon must be sought elsewhere.

I question, then, the horizon, pure and clear of any haze. Its look hasn't changed. But if this noise comes from a fall, from a cataract; if this whole ocean is plunging into some lower basin; if these roars are made by a mass of falling water, then the current must be picking up, and its growing speed can give me the measure of the peril that threatens us. I check the current. It's nil. An empty bottle I throw into the sea stays where it is, to leeward.

Around four o'clock Hans rises, grips the mast, and climbs to its very top. From up there his gaze sweeps the arc the ocean describes before the raft and stops at a single point. His face shows no surprise, but his eyes have fixed and gone still.

"He's seen something," says my uncle.

"I think so."

Hans comes back down, then stretches his arm toward the south and says:

"Der nere!"

"Down there?" my uncle answers.

And snatching up his spyglass, he stares hard for a full minute — a minute that feels to me like a century.

"Yes, yes!" he cries.

"What do you see?"

"An enormous spout of water rising above the waves."

"Another sea animal?"

"Perhaps."

"Then let's steer farther west, because we know now what to expect from meeting these antediluvian monsters!"

"Let it run," my uncle answers.

I turn to Hans. Hans holds his tiller with unbending firmness.

And yet — if, at the distance separating us from this animal, which must be reckoned at twelve leagues at least, we can make out the column of water driven up by its blowholes, then it must be of supernatural size. To flee would be to obey the plainest, most ordinary prudence. But we didn't come here to be prudent.

So on we go. The nearer we draw, the taller the spout grows. What monster could fill itself with such a quantity of water and expel it endlessly like that?

By eight in the evening we are less than two leagues from it. Its blackish body, huge, monstrous, stretches across the sea like a little island. Is it an illusion? Is it terror? Its length seems to me to top a thousand fathoms! What cetacean can this be, foreseen by neither the Cuviers nor the Blumenbachs? It lies motionless, as though asleep; the sea seems unable to lift it, and it is the waves that ripple along its flanks. The column of water, hurled up to a height of five hundred feet, falls back with a deafening crash. Like madmen we race toward this mighty mass that a hundred whales couldn't feed for a single day.

Terror grips me. I won't go any farther! I'll cut the halyard of the sail if I have to! I turn mutinous against the professor, who doesn't answer me.

All at once Hans stands, and pointing his finger at the menacing spot:

"Holme!" he says.

"An island!" cries my uncle.

"An island!" I say in my turn, shrugging my shoulders.

"Obviously," the professor replies, letting out a great burst of laughter.

"But that column of water!"

"Geyser," says Hans — a famous jet of spouting water at the foot of Mount Hekla in Iceland.

"Why yes, of course, a geyser," my uncle shoots back, "a geyser just like the ones in Iceland!"

At first I don't want to have been so grossly wrong. To have taken a little island for a sea-monster! But the truth becomes plain, and in the end I have to own up to my mistake. There's nothing here but a natural phenomenon.

As we draw nearer, the dimensions of the liquid sheaf grow grandiose. The island is uncannily like some immense cetacean whose head rears ten fathoms above the waves. The geyser — a word the Icelanders pronounce "geysir," meaning "fury" — rises majestically at its far end. Muffled detonations burst out now and then, and the enormous jet, seized with more violent rages, shakes its plume of vapors as it leaps up to the first layer of clouds. It stands alone. No fumaroles, no hot springs surround it, and all the volcanic power is summed up in it. The rays of the electric light come to mingle with this dazzling spout, and every drop of it takes on all the colors of the prism.

"Let's put in," says the professor.

But we have to carefully avoid this waterspout, which would sink the raft in an instant. Hans, maneuvering skillfully, brings us to the far end of the island.

I leap onto the rock; my uncle follows nimbly, while the hunter stays at his post, like a man above such astonishments.

We walk on a granite mixed with siliceous tufa; the ground shivers under our feet like the walls of a boiler where overheated steam is writhing; it's scorching hot. We come in sight of a little central basin from which the geyser rises. I plunge an overflow thermometer into the water that comes gushing and boiling out, and it reads a heat of a hundred and sixty-three degrees.

So this water issues from a burning source. That contradicts the professor's theories rather sharply. I can't help remarking on it.

"Well," he retorts, "what does that prove against my doctrine?"

"Nothing," I say curtly, seeing that I've run up against an absolute stubbornness.

Still, I'm forced to admit that we've been singularly favored so far, and that, for some reason that escapes me, this journey is unfolding under unusual conditions of temperature; but it seems to me obvious, certain, that one day or another we'll reach those regions where the central heat climbs to the highest limits and passes beyond every gradation of the thermometers.

We'll soon see. That's the professor's phrase; and after christening this volcanic island with his nephew's name, he gives the signal to re-embark.

I stay a few more minutes, contemplating the geyser. I notice that its jet is irregular in its fits, that it sometimes slackens, then picks up again with fresh vigor, which I put down to the changing pressure of the vapors piling up in its reservoir.

At last we set off, rounding the very steep rocks to the south. Hans has taken advantage of the halt to put the raft back in order.

But before shoving off I make a few observations to calculate the distance covered, and I note them in my journal. We have crossed two hundred and seventy leagues of sea since Port Graüben, and we are six hundred and twenty leagues from Iceland, under England.
