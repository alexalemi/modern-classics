Chapter 15

The Sneffels is five thousand feet high. With its double cone, it caps a band of trachyte that breaks away from the island's mountain system. From where we set out, you couldn't yet make out its two peaks against the gray backdrop of the sky. All I could see was an enormous cap of snow pulled low over the giant's brow.

We walked single file, the hunter leading the way, climbing narrow paths where two people couldn't have gone side by side. That made conversation just about impossible.

Beyond the basalt wall of the Stapi fjord, the ground was at first a grassy, fibrous peat, the residue of the ancient vegetation that once choked the marshes of the peninsula. There was enough of this fuel — still untouched — to heat the whole population of Iceland for a century. Measured from the bottom of some of the ravines, this vast peat bog stood as much as seventy feet high in places, and showed layer upon layer of charred debris, separated by thin sheets of pumice tuff.

True nephew of Professor Lidenbrock that I was, I studied the mineral curiosities laid out in this huge cabinet of natural history with real interest, in spite of my worries; and at the same time I ran back through the whole geological history of Iceland in my mind.

This strange island clearly rose up out of the depths of the sea in a relatively recent epoch. Maybe it's even still rising, by some movement too slow to feel. If that's the case, its origin can only be put down to the action of underground fires. And in that case, Humphry Davy's theory, Saknussemm's document, my uncle's whole grand claim — all of it went up in smoke. This idea led me to examine the nature of the ground closely, and I soon worked out the sequence of events that had governed the island's formation.

Iceland, entirely lacking any sedimentary terrain, is made up solely of volcanic tuff — that is, an agglomerate of stones and rocks with a porous texture. Before the volcanoes existed, it was a mass of trap rock, slowly heaved above the waves by the push of the central forces. The inner fires had not yet burst out into the open.

But later, a wide fissure opened diagonally across the island, from southwest to northwest, and through it the whole trachytic paste gradually flowed out. The thing happened without violence back then; the opening was enormous, and the molten matter, thrown up from the bowels of the globe, spread quietly out in vast sheets or in rounded, breast-shaped masses. This was the age of the feldspars, the syenites, and the porphyries.

But thanks to this outpouring, the thickness of the island grew considerably, and with it, its power to resist. You can imagine what a quantity of elastic fluids got stored up inside it once it offered no outlet at all, after the trachytic crust had cooled. So there came a moment when the mechanical force of these gases was such that they lifted the heavy shell and hollowed out tall chimneys for themselves. That's how the volcano was made, from the heaving-up of the crust — and then the crater, suddenly punched through at the volcano's summit.

Then the eruptive phenomena gave way to the volcanic ones. Through the newly formed openings came, first, the basaltic discharges, of which the plain we were crossing at that very moment offered the most marvelous specimens to our eyes. We were walking over these heavy, dark-gray rocks that the cooling had cast into prisms with hexagonal bases. Far off could be seen a great many flattened cones, each of which had once been a fire-vomiting mouth.

Then, once the basaltic eruption was spent, the volcano — its force swollen by that of the extinct craters — gave passage to the lavas and to those tuffs of ash and scoria whose long streams I could see scattered down its flanks like a rich head of hair.

Such was the sequence of events that put Iceland together; all of it came from the action of the inner fires, and to suppose that the internal mass did not remain in a permanent state of incandescent liquidity was madness. Madness above all to claim you could reach the center of the globe!

So I reassured myself about how our enterprise would turn out, even as I marched to the assault of the Sneffels.

The route grew harder and harder. The ground rose; splinters of rock shifted underfoot, and it took the most scrupulous attention to avoid a dangerous fall.

Hans went forward calmly, as though over level ground. Sometimes he vanished behind the big blocks and we lost sight of him for a moment; then a sharp whistle, escaping his lips, showed us the way to follow. Often, too, he stopped, gathered a few chips of rock, arranged them in a recognizable pattern, and so built up landmarks meant to mark the road back. A good precaution in itself — but one that later events would make useless.

Three exhausting hours of walking had brought us only to the base of the mountain. There, Hans signaled a halt, and a hasty meal was shared out among us all. My uncle ate his mouthfuls double to go faster. But this refueling stop was also a rest stop, so he had to wait on the good pleasure of the guide, who gave the signal to move on an hour later. The three Icelanders, as taciturn as their comrade the hunter, didn't say a single word and ate sparingly.

Now we began to climb the slopes of the Sneffels. Its snowy summit, by an optical illusion common in the mountains, looked very close to me — and yet, what long hours before we'd reach it! What exhaustion, above all! The stones, bound together by no cement of earth, by no grass, gave way under our feet and went tumbling down into the plain with the speed of an avalanche.

In certain spots, the flanks of the mountain made an angle with the horizon of at least thirty-six degrees; it was impossible to climb them straight on, and these stony steeps had to be worked around, not without difficulty. Then we lent one another a hand with our staffs.

I have to say that my uncle stayed as close to me as he could. He never let me out of his sight, and more than once his arm gave me a solid support. For his own part, he no doubt had an inborn sense of balance, because he never stumbled. The Icelanders, though loaded down, climbed with a mountaineer's agility.

Looking at the height of the Sneffels's peak, it seemed impossible to me that anyone could reach it from this side, unless the angle of the slopes eased off. Luckily, after an hour of hard labor and feats of strength, in the middle of the vast carpet of snow spread over the volcano's rump, a kind of staircase appeared out of nowhere and simplified our ascent. It was formed by one of those torrents of stone thrown out by the eruptions, whose Icelandic name is "stinâ." If this torrent hadn't been stopped in its fall by the lay of the mountain's flanks, it would have gone plunging into the sea and made new islands.

Just as it was, it served us well. The slopes were getting steeper, but these stone steps let us climb them easily — so quickly, in fact, that when I fell behind for a moment while my companions kept climbing, I already saw them shrunk by distance to a microscopic size.

By seven in the evening we had climbed the two thousand steps of the staircase, and we stood above a swelling of the mountain, a kind of shelf on which rested the cone proper of the crater.

The sea stretched out three thousand two hundred feet below us. We had passed the line of perpetual snow, which lies fairly low in Iceland because of the constant damp of the climate. The cold was fierce; the wind blew hard. I was worn out. The Professor saw clearly that my legs would carry me no further, and, in spite of his impatience, he decided to stop. So he signaled to the hunter, who shook his head and said:

"Ofvanför."

"It seems we have to go higher up," said my uncle.

Then he asked Hans the reason for his answer.

"Mistour," replied the guide.

"Ja, mistour," repeated one of the Icelanders in a frightened tone.

"What does that word mean?" I asked, uneasy.

"Look," said my uncle.

I turned my eyes toward the plain. An immense column of pulverized pumice, sand, and dust was rising, spinning like a waterspout; the wind was driving it against the flank of the Sneffels we clung to. This opaque curtain, spread in front of the sun, cast a great shadow over the mountain. If this whirlwind tipped our way, it would inevitably wrap us up in its swirls. The phenomenon — common enough when the wind blows off the glaciers — goes by the name "mistour" in the Icelandic tongue.

"Hastigt, hastigt!" cried our guide.

Without knowing any Danish, I understood we had to follow Hans as fast as we could. He began to circle the cone of the crater, but at a slant, to make the walking easier. Soon the whirlwind crashed down on the mountain, which shuddered under the blow; the stones caught in the eddies of the wind flew in a shower, as in an eruption. Luckily, we were on the opposite slope, sheltered from all danger. Without the guide's precaution, our bodies — shredded, ground to dust — would have come raining down far away, like the product of some unknown meteor.

Even so, Hans did not think it prudent to spend the night on the flanks of the cone. We continued our ascent in zigzags; the fifteen hundred feet that remained to climb took nearly five hours; the detours, the slants, and the countermarches measured at least three leagues. I was finished. I was going under, from cold and hunger. The air, a little thin now, wasn't enough for my lungs to work on.

At last, at eleven o'clock at night, in total darkness, the summit of the Sneffels was reached — and, before going to shelter inside the crater, I had time to catch sight of "the midnight sun" at the lowest point of its course, throwing its pale rays over the sleeping island at my feet.
