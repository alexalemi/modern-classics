Chapter 2

That study was a genuine museum. Every specimen the mineral kingdom has to offer was there, labeled and arranged in the most perfect order, according to the three great divisions of minerals: the flammable, the metallic, and the stony.

How well I knew them, those trinkets of the science of mineralogy! How many times, instead of loafing about with boys my own age, had I amused myself dusting these graphites, these anthracites, these coals, these lignites, these peats! And the bitumens, the resins, the organic salts that had to be shielded from the least speck of dust! And these metals, from iron all the way up to gold, whose relative value vanished before the absolute equality of scientific specimens! And all these stones, enough to rebuild the house on König-strasse from scratch — even with a fine extra bedroom, which I could have made very good use of!

But as I stepped into the study, none of these wonders was on my mind. My uncle alone filled my thoughts. He had buried himself in his big armchair upholstered in Utrecht velvet, and he held a book in his hands that he was contemplating with the most profound admiration.

"What a book! What a book!" he kept exclaiming.

That outburst reminded me that Professor Lidenbrock was also, in his idle moments, a book fanatic. But an old volume had no value in his eyes unless it was impossible to find, or at the very least impossible to read.

"Well!" he said to me. "Don't you see it? It's a priceless treasure that I turned up this morning, poking around in the shop of Hevelius the Jew."

"Magnificent!" I answered, with a show of enthusiasm to order.

For really, what was all this fuss about? An old quarto whose spine and boards seemed to be made of coarse calfskin, a yellowish book with a faded bookmark dangling from it.

Meanwhile the professor's admiring exclamations went on without a pause.

"Look," he was saying, putting the questions and the answers to himself. "Isn't it beautiful? Yes, it's splendid! And what a binding! Does the book open easily? Yes, because it stays open at any page you like! But does it close properly? Yes, because the cover and the pages form a single unbroken whole, without gaping or coming apart anywhere. And this spine — not a single crack in it after seven hundred years! Ah, there's a binding that Bozerian, Closs, or Purgold would have been proud of!"

And as he talked, my uncle kept opening and closing the old book. The least I could do was to ask him about its contents, though they didn't interest me in the slightest.

"And what is the title of this marvelous volume?" I asked, with an eagerness far too enthusiastic not to be put on.

"This work!" my uncle replied, warming up. "This is the Heims-Kringla of Snorre Turleson, the famous Icelandic author of the twelfth century! It's the Chronicle of the Norwegian princes who reigned in Iceland."

"Really!" I cried, as best I could. "And no doubt it's a translation into German?"

"A translation!" the professor shot back sharply. "And what would I want with your translation? Who cares about your translation? This is the original work, in the Icelandic tongue — that magnificent language, rich and simple at the same time, which allows for the most varied grammatical combinations and countless modifications of words!"

"Like German," I ventured, rather pleased with myself.

"Yes," my uncle answered, shrugging his shoulders, "with the difference that Icelandic admits the three genders, like Greek, and declines proper nouns, like Latin!"

"Ah!" I said, my indifference a little shaken. "And are the letters of this book beautiful?"

"Letters! Who's talking about letters, you wretched Axel! Letters, indeed! Ah, so you take this for a printed book! But you ignoramus, it's a manuscript — and a runic manuscript!"

"Runic?"

"Yes! Are you going to ask me now to explain that word for you?"

"I'll be careful not to," I replied, with the tone of a man wounded in his self-respect.

But my uncle carried on more energetically than ever, and instructed me, against my will, in things I had no great wish to know.

"The runes," he went on, "were characters of writing once used in Iceland, and, according to tradition, they were invented by Odin himself! But look, then — admire them, you heathen — these letters that sprang from the imagination of a god!"

Well, for lack of any reply, I was on the point of prostrating myself — the sort of answer that ought to please gods and kings alike, since it has the advantage of never putting them on the spot — when an incident came to divert the course of the conversation.

It was the appearance of a grimy parchment that slipped out of the book and fell to the floor.

My uncle pounced on this scrap with an eagerness easy to understand. An old document, sealed away perhaps for time out of mind inside an old book, could not fail to be of the highest value in his eyes.

"What's this?" he cried.

And at the same moment he was carefully spreading out on his table a piece of parchment five inches long and three wide, across which ran, in horizontal lines, characters straight out of some book of spells.

Here is the exact facsimile. I am determined to make these strange signs known, for they led Professor Lidenbrock and his nephew to undertake the most extraordinary expedition of the nineteenth century:

The professor studied this string of characters for a few moments; then he said, pushing his spectacles up onto his forehead:

"This is runic. These letters are absolutely identical to those in Snorre Turleson's manuscript! But... what on earth can it mean?"

Since runic struck me as an invention of scholars meant to mystify the poor world, I was not sorry to see that my uncle understood none of it. At least, that was how it seemed to me from the movement of his fingers, which were beginning to twitch alarmingly.

"And yet it's Old Icelandic!" he muttered between his teeth.

And Professor Lidenbrock surely ought to have known, for he passed for a real polyglot. Not that he spoke fluently the two thousand languages and four thousand dialects used across the face of the globe, but he knew his fair share of them.

He was on the verge, then, faced with this difficulty, of giving free rein to the full impetuosity of his temper, and I foresaw a violent scene — when two o'clock chimed on the little clock over the mantelpiece.

At once, good Martha opened the door of the study and announced:

"The soup is served."

"To the devil with the soup," cried my uncle, "and with the woman who made it, and with anyone who eats it!"

Martha fled. I flew after her, and, hardly knowing how, found myself seated in my usual place in the dining room.

I waited a few moments. The professor did not come. It was the first time, to my knowledge, that he had missed the solemn ritual of dinner. And what a dinner it was, too! Parsley soup, a ham omelette seasoned with sorrel and nutmeg, a loin of veal with stewed plums, and, for dessert, sugared shrimp — all of it washed down with a nice Moselle wine.

That was what an old sheet of paper was going to cost my uncle. As a devoted nephew, I felt it my duty to eat for him, and for myself as well — which I did in all good conscience.

"I have never seen anything like it!" said good Martha as she served. "Professor Lidenbrock not at the table!"

"It's beyond belief."

"It's a sign of some grave event to come!" the old servant went on, shaking her head.

In my opinion it was a sign of nothing at all — nothing except a frightful scene when my uncle found his dinner devoured.

I was down to my last shrimp when a thundering voice tore me from the delights of dessert. In a single bound I was out of the dining room and into the study.
