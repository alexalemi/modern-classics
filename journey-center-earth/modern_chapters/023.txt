Chapter 24

By the next day we had already forgotten everything we'd suffered. My first surprise was that I was no longer thirsty, and I asked why. The little stream murmuring at my feet answered for me.

We ate breakfast and drank our fill of that excellent iron-rich water. I felt thoroughly revived and ready to go a long way. Why shouldn't a man as certain of himself as my uncle succeed, with a guide as resourceful as Hans and a nephew as "determined" as I was? Those were the fine ideas slipping into my brain! If anyone had suggested climbing back up to the summit of the Sneffels, I'd have refused with indignation.

Fortunately, it was only a question of going down.

"Let's go!" I cried, waking the old echoes of the globe with my enthusiasm.

We set off again on Thursday at eight in the morning. The granite corridor twisted through winding detours, threw out sudden elbow-bends, and had all the tangle of a labyrinth about it; but on the whole its main heading was still to the southeast. My uncle never stopped consulting his compass with the greatest care, keeping track of the distance we'd covered.

The gallery ran almost dead level, with two inches of drop per fathom, no more. The stream ran on unhurried, murmuring beneath our feet. I compared it to some friendly household spirit guiding us through the earth, and with my hand I stroked the warm naiad whose songs kept time with our steps. My good mood was happy to take a mythological turn.

As for my uncle, he cursed the flatness of the road — he, "the man of verticals." His path stretched on endlessly, and instead of sliding straight down the earth's radius, as he put it, he was going by way of the hypotenuse. But we had no choice, and as long as we were gaining on the center, however little, there was no cause to complain.

Besides, every now and then the slopes dropped away; the naiad went tumbling down with a roar, and we descended deeper along with her.

All told, that day and the next we covered a great deal of horizontal ground and relatively little vertical.

By the evening of Friday, July 10th, by our reckoning, we should have been thirty leagues southeast of Reykjavik and at a depth of two and a half leagues.

At that point a rather frightening well opened up beneath our feet. My uncle couldn't help clapping his hands as he calculated the steepness of its sides.

"This will take us a long way," he cried, "and easily too, because the jutting rocks make a real staircase!"

Hans arranged the ropes so as to prevent any accident. The descent began. I don't dare call it perilous, since I was already used to this sort of exercise.

This well was a narrow crack cut into the rock mass, one of the kind called a "fault"; the contraction of the earth's framework as it cooled had clearly produced it. If it had once served as a passage for the erupted matter vomited out by the Sneffels, I couldn't understand how none of that matter had left any trace. We were descending a sort of winding screw that you'd have sworn was made by human hands.

Every quarter of an hour we had to stop for a necessary rest, to give our legs back their spring. We'd sit down on some jutting ledge, legs dangling, and talk while we ate and quenched our thirst at the stream.

It goes without saying that in this fault the Hansbach had turned itself into a waterfall, at some cost to its volume; but it was more than enough to slake our thirst; besides, once the slopes grew gentler it was bound to settle back into its peaceful course. Just now it reminded me of my worthy uncle, his impatience and his tempers, whereas on the softened slopes it was all the calm of the Icelandic hunter.

On the 6th and 7th of July we followed the spirals of this fault, sinking another two leagues into the earth's crust, which put us nearly five leagues below sea level. But on the 8th, around noon, the fault took on a much gentler slope in the southeast direction, about forty-five degrees.

The way then became easy and perfectly monotonous. It could hardly have been otherwise. The journey couldn't be varied by any incidents of scenery.

At last, on Wednesday the 15th, we were seven leagues underground and about fifty leagues from the Sneffels. Though we were a little tired, our health was holding up reassuringly, and the travel medicine chest was still untouched.

My uncle kept hour-by-hour readings of the compass, the chronometer, the pressure gauge, and the thermometer — the very ones he later published in the scientific account of his voyage. So he could easily work out our position. When he told me we were fifty leagues away horizontally, I couldn't hold back an exclamation.

"What is it?" he asked.

"Nothing — only a thought struck me."

"What thought, my boy?"

"That if your calculations are right, we're no longer under Iceland."

"You think so?"

"That's easy enough to check."

I took my measurements with the dividers on the map.

"I wasn't mistaken," I said. "We've passed Cape Portland, and these fifty leagues to the southeast put us out under the open sea."

"Under the open sea," my uncle repeated, rubbing his hands.

"So," I cried, "the ocean stretches over our heads!"

"Bah! Axel, nothing could be more natural! Aren't there coal mines at Newcastle that run out under the waves?"

The professor might find the situation perfectly simple, but the thought of strolling around beneath a mass of water didn't stop nagging at me. And yet, whether it was the plains and mountains of Iceland hanging over our heads or the waters of the Atlantic, it made little difference in the end, so long as the granite framework held solid. Besides, I got used to the idea soon enough, for the corridor — now straight, now winding, as capricious in its slopes as in its turns, but always running southeast and always sinking deeper — carried us quickly to great depths.

Four days later, on the evening of Saturday, July 18th, we reached a kind of fairly large grotto; my uncle handed Hans his three rix-dollars for the week, and it was decided that the next day would be a day of rest.
