Chapter 6

At those words a shiver ran through my whole body. Still, I kept my composure. I even resolved to put on a brave face. Only scientific arguments could stop Professor Lidenbrock — and there were some, good ones, against the very possibility of such a journey. Go to the center of the earth! What madness! I saved my debating skills for the right moment and turned my attention to dinner.

No need to repeat my uncle's curses when he found the table cleared. It all got sorted out. Good Martha was set free again. She ran to the market and did so well that an hour later my hunger was satisfied and I was coming back to a sense of the situation.

During the meal my uncle was almost cheerful; a few of those harmless scholarly jokes escaped him — the kind that never do much damage. After dessert he signaled me to follow him into his study.

I obeyed. He sat down at one end of his worktable, and I at the other.

"Axel," he said in a rather gentle voice, "you're a very clever boy. You did me a fine service just now, when I was worn out and about to abandon that combination. Where might I have wandered off to? No one can say! I'll never forget it, my boy, and of the glory we're about to win, you'll have your share."

"Right," I thought. "He's in a good mood. Now's the moment to discuss this glory."

"Above all," my uncle went on, "I ask you for absolute secrecy — you understand me? I don't lack for envious rivals in the world of science, and plenty of them would love to undertake this journey, though they won't suspect a thing until we're safely home again."

"Do you really believe," I said, "that there are so many of these bold souls?"

"Of course! Who would hesitate to win such fame? If this document were known, an entire army of geologists would go charging off on the trail of Arne Saknussemm!"

"That's exactly what I'm not convinced of, Uncle, because nothing proves this document is genuine."

"What! And the book we found it in?"

"Fine! I'll grant that this Saknussemm wrote these lines. But does it follow that he actually made the journey? Couldn't this old parchment be nothing but a hoax?"

That last word was a bit reckless, and I almost regretted saying it. The professor knit his thick brow, and I feared I had wrecked the rest of the conversation. Luckily, no. My stern companion sketched something like a smile on his lips and replied:

"That's what we'll find out."

"Ah!" I said, a little vexed. "But allow me to run through the whole series of objections regarding this document."

"Speak, my boy, don't hold back. I give you complete freedom to express your opinion. You're no longer my nephew, but my colleague. So — go ahead."

"Well then, first I'll ask you: what are this Yocul, this Sneffels, this Scartaris — names I've never heard of?"

"Nothing simpler. As it happens, I received a while ago a map from my friend Peterman, of Leipzig; it couldn't have come at a better time. Take down the third atlas in the second bay of the big bookcase — series Z, plate 4."

I got up, and thanks to these precise directions I quickly found the atlas he wanted. My uncle opened it and said:

"Here's one of the best maps of Iceland — Handerson's — and I think it's going to give us the answer to all your difficulties."

I leaned over the map.

"See this island made up of volcanoes," said the professor, "and notice that they all bear the name Yocul. That word means 'glacier' in Icelandic, and at Iceland's high latitude most eruptions break out through the layers of ice. Hence the name Yocul applied to all the fire-belching mountains of the island."

"All right," I answered, "but what is the Sneffels?"

I was hoping this question would have no answer. I was mistaken. My uncle went on:

"Follow me along the western coast of Iceland. Do you see Reykjavik, its capital? Yes. Good. Now trace up the countless fjords of these sea-gnawed shores, and stop a little below the sixty-fifth degree of latitude. What do you see there?"

"A kind of peninsula, like a picked-clean bone, ending in an enormous kneecap."

"The comparison is apt, my boy. Now — do you notice anything on that kneecap?"

"Yes, a mountain that seems to have sprouted up out of the sea."

"Good! That's the Sneffels."

"The Sneffels?"

"The very one. A mountain five thousand feet high, one of the most remarkable on the island, and beyond doubt the most famous in the entire world — if its crater leads down to the center of the globe."

"But that's impossible!" I cried, shrugging my shoulders, revolted by such a notion.

"Impossible!" the professor answered sternly. "And why is that?"

"Because this crater is obviously blocked up by lava, by scorching rock, and so —"

"And what if it's an extinct crater?"

"Extinct?"

"Yes. The number of active volcanoes on the surface of the globe is currently only about three hundred; but there exist a far greater number of extinct ones. Now the Sneffels ranks among the latter, and in all of recorded history it has had only a single eruption — that of 1219. From that time on, its rumblings gradually died away, and it is no longer counted among the active volcanoes."

To these flat assertions I had absolutely nothing to reply, so I fell back on the other obscurities the document contained.

"What does this word Scartaris mean," I asked, "and what have the calends of July got to do with anything?"

My uncle took a few moments to think. I had one instant of hope — just one — for he soon answered me in these terms:

"What you call obscurity is, to me, clarity. It proves the ingenious care with which Saknussemm meant to pin down his discovery. The Sneffels is made up of several craters; it was therefore necessary to indicate which of them leads to the center of the globe. What did the learned Icelander do? He noticed that as the calends of July approach — that is, toward the last days of June — one of the mountain's peaks, the Scartaris, casts its shadow all the way to the mouth of the crater in question, and he set the fact down in his document. Could he have devised a more exact instruction? Once we've reached the summit of the Sneffels, will there be any hesitating over which road to take?"

Decidedly, my uncle had an answer for everything. I saw plainly that he was unassailable on the words of the old parchment. So I stopped pressing him on that score, and, since convincing him was the whole point, I moved on to the scientific objections — far graver ones, in my opinion.

"All right," I said. "I'm forced to admit it: Saknussemm's sentence is clear and leaves no room for doubt. I'll even grant that the document has an air of perfect authenticity. This scholar went down into the Sneffels; he saw the shadow of the Scartaris caress the rim of the crater before the calends of July; he even heard it told, in the legends of his day, that this crater led to the center of the earth. But as for reaching it himself — as for having made the journey and come back from it, if he ever set out — no, a hundred times no!"

"And the reason?" said my uncle in a singularly mocking tone.

"The reason is that every theory in science proves such a venture is impossible!"

"Every theory says so?" replied the professor, putting on a genial air. "Ah! The wicked theories! How they're going to get in our way, these poor theories!"

I saw that he was making fun of me, but I pressed on all the same.

"Yes! It is perfectly established that heat increases by about one degree for every seventy feet of depth below the surface of the globe. Now, assuming this proportion stays constant, and the earth's radius being fifteen hundred leagues, there exists at the center a temperature of two million degrees. The matter inside the earth is therefore in the state of an incandescent gas, for the metals, gold, platinum, the hardest rocks, cannot hold up against such heat. So I have every right to ask whether it's even possible to venture into such a place!"

"So, Axel, it's the heat that troubles you?"

"Naturally. If we got down to a depth of just ten leagues, we'd have reached the limit of the earth's crust, because already the temperature is above thirteen hundred degrees."

"And you're afraid of melting?"

"I'll leave the question for you to decide," I answered testily.

"Here's what I decide," replied Professor Lidenbrock, drawing himself up grandly. "It's that neither you nor anyone knows for certain what goes on inside the globe, seeing that we scarcely know one twelve-thousandth part of its radius; it's that science is eminently perfectible, and every theory is forever being demolished by a newer one. Wasn't it believed, right up until Fourier, that the temperature of the planetary spaces went on falling forever? And don't we know today that the greatest cold of the ethereal regions doesn't drop below forty or fifty degrees under zero? Why shouldn't it be the same with the internal heat? Why, at a certain depth, shouldn't it reach an impassable limit, instead of climbing to the melting point of the most stubborn minerals?"

Since my uncle had shifted the question onto the ground of hypotheses, I had nothing to answer.

"Well, I'll tell you that genuine scholars, Poisson among them, have proved that if a heat of two million degrees existed inside the globe, the incandescent gases given off by the molten matter would take on such elasticity that the earth's crust couldn't resist and would burst like the walls of a boiler under the strain of steam."

"That's Poisson's opinion, Uncle — that's all."

"Agreed, but it's also the opinion of other distinguished geologists that the interior of the globe is formed neither of gas, nor of water, nor of the heaviest stones we know — for in that case the earth would weigh half as much as it does."

"Oh! With figures you can prove anything you like!"

"And with facts, my boy — is it the same? Isn't it an established fact that the number of volcanoes has considerably decreased since the first days of the world? And if there is a central heat, can't we conclude that it's tending to weaken?"

"Uncle, if you're going to venture into the field of suppositions, I've nothing more to argue."

"And I have to say that my opinion is joined by the opinions of highly competent men. Do you remember a visit paid me by the famous English chemist Humphry Davy in 1825?"

"Not in the least, since I only came into the world nineteen years afterward."

"Well, Humphry Davy came to see me on his way through Hamburg. We discussed at length, among other questions, the hypothesis of the liquidity of the earth's inner core. We were both agreed that such liquidity couldn't exist, for a reason science has never found an answer to."

"And what reason is that?" I said, a little surprised.

"It's that this liquid mass would be subject, like the Ocean, to the moon's attraction, and consequently, twice a day there would be internal tides that, heaving up the earth's crust, would give rise to periodic earthquakes!"

"But it's nevertheless obvious that the surface of the globe underwent combustion, and one is allowed to suppose that the outer crust cooled first, while the heat took refuge at the center."

"Wrong," replied my uncle. "The earth was heated by the combustion of its surface, and in no other way. Its surface was composed of a great quantity of metals — potassium, sodium — which have the property of catching fire at the mere contact of air and water. These metals took fire when the atmospheric vapors came pouring down as rain onto the ground, and little by little, as the waters worked their way into the fissures of the earth's crust, they set off fresh blazes with explosions and eruptions. Hence the volcanoes so numerous in the first days of the world."

"But that's an ingenious hypothesis!" I cried, almost in spite of myself.

"And one that Humphry Davy made vivid to me, right here, by a very simple experiment. He made up a metallic ball, mainly of the metals I've just mentioned, which perfectly represented our globe. When a fine dew was let fall onto its surface, that surface swelled, oxidized, and formed a little mountain; a crater opened at its summit; the eruption took place and gave off, through the whole ball, a heat so great that it became impossible to hold it in the hand."

Truly, I was beginning to be shaken by the professor's arguments; and besides, he pressed them home with his usual passion and enthusiasm.

"You see, Axel," he added, "the state of the central core has raised various hypotheses among geologists; nothing is less proved than this business of an internal heat. In my view, it doesn't exist; it can't exist; and besides, we shall see for ourselves and, like Arne Saknussemm, we'll know what to make of this great question."

"Well then! Yes," I answered, feeling myself won over by his enthusiasm. "Yes, we shall see — if one can see anything down there at all."

"And why not? Can't we count on electrical phenomena to light our way, and even on the atmosphere itself, which its own pressure may render luminous as we near the center?"

"Yes," I said, "yes! That's possible, after all."

"It's certain," my uncle answered triumphantly. "But silence, do you hear! Silence about all of this, and let no one so much as dream of discovering the center of the earth before we do."
