Chapter 12

We had set out under an overcast but settled sky. No tiring heat to dread, no disastrous rain. Perfect weather for tourists.

The sheer pleasure of riding on horseback across an unknown country made me easygoing about the start of the whole business. I gave myself up entirely to the holiday-maker's happiness, all appetite and freedom. I was beginning to make my peace with the expedition.

"Besides," I said to myself, "what am I really risking? A trip through the most curious country on earth! The climb up a truly remarkable mountain! At worst, a scramble down to the bottom of a dead crater! It's perfectly obvious that this Saknussemm did nothing more than that. As for the existence of some tunnel leading to the center of the globe — pure imagination! Pure impossibility! So whatever there is to enjoy in this expedition, let's take it, and no haggling!"

I had barely finished this line of reasoning when we left Reykjavik behind.

Hans marched at the head, at a quick, steady, tireless pace. The two horses loaded with our baggage followed him without needing to be led. My uncle and I came next, and, truly, we didn't cut too poor a figure on our little mounts — small beasts, but sturdy ones.

Iceland is one of the great islands of Europe. It measures fourteen hundred miles of surface and counts no more than sixty thousand inhabitants. Geographers have divided it into four quarters, and we had to cross, almost on the diagonal, the one that goes by the name of the Country of the Southwest Quarter — "Sudvestr Fjordùngr."

On leaving Reykjavik, Hans had immediately struck out along the seashore. We crossed meager pastures that were doing their level best to be green; the yellow was having more success. The rugged summits of the trachytic masses blurred into the eastern mists on the horizon; now and then a few patches of snow, concentrating the diffused light, blazed on the slopes of the far-off peaks; certain summits, thrusting up more boldly, pierced the gray clouds and reappeared above the drifting vapors, like reefs surfacing in the open sky.

Often these chains of barren rock ran out toward the sea and bit into the pasture; but there was always room enough left to get by. Our horses, moreover, picked out the passable spots by instinct, never slackening their pace. My uncle didn't even have the consolation of urging his mount along with voice or whip; he wasn't allowed to be impatient. And I couldn't help smiling to see him so tall on his little horse — with his long legs nearly grazing the ground, he looked like a six-legged centaur.

"Good beast! Good beast!" he kept saying. "You'll see, Axel, that no animal on earth outdoes the Icelandic horse for intelligence. Snow, storms, impassable trails, rocks, glaciers — nothing stops him. He's brave, he's frugal, he's sure-footed. Never a false step, never a shy. Let some river turn up, some fjord to cross — and one will, you'll see — and he'll throw himself into the water without a moment's hesitation, like an amphibian, and make the far bank! Only let's not rush him — let him do his work, and between the two of us we'll manage our ten leagues a day."

"We will, no doubt," I answered, "but what about the guide?"

"Oh, he doesn't worry me in the least. Men like that walk without even noticing it. This one stirs so little that he can't possibly tire. Anyway, if it comes to it, I'll give up my mount to him. I'll be seized with cramps soon enough if I don't get some exercise. The arms are doing fine, but I have to think of the legs."

Meanwhile we were pushing on at a good clip; the country was already all but deserted. Here and there a lone farm, some solitary boër — the Icelandic peasant's cottage, built of wood, of earth, of chunks of lava — appeared like a beggar at the edge of a sunken road. These tumbledown huts had the look of imploring charity from passersby, and one felt half-inclined to give them alms. In this country roads, and even footpaths, were utterly lacking, and the vegetation, slow as it was, made short work of erasing the tracks of the rare traveler.

And yet this part of the province, lying a stone's throw from its capital, ranked among the inhabited and cultivated portions of Iceland. What, then, must the more desolate regions be, beyond this desert? Half a mile covered, and we still hadn't met a single farmer at his cottage door, nor a wild shepherd grazing a flock less wild than himself — only a few cows and sheep left to fend for themselves. What, then, would the convulsed regions be like, the ones churned up by eruptions, born of volcanic explosions and subterranean upheavals?

We were fated to know them soon enough; but, consulting Olsen's map, I saw that they were avoided by following the winding fringe of the shore. In fact, the great plutonic upheaval has been concentrated mostly in the island's interior; there the horizontal layers of stacked rock — called trapps in the Scandinavian tongue — the trachytic bands, the eruptions of basalt, of tuff, and of every volcanic conglomerate, the flows of lava and molten porphyry, have made a land of unearthly horror. Little did I suspect then the spectacle awaiting us on the Sneffels peninsula, where these ravages of a fiery nature form a formidable chaos.

Two hours after leaving Reykjavik we reached the little town of Gufunes, called "Aoalkirkja," or Principal Church. It offered nothing remarkable. A few houses only. Barely enough to make a hamlet in Germany.

Hans halted there for half an hour; he shared our frugal breakfast, answered my uncle's questions about the nature of the road with yes and no, and when he was asked where he meant to spend the night, he said only:

"Gardär."

I consulted the map to find out what Gardär was. I saw a village of that name on the shores of the Hvalfjörd, four miles from Reykjavik. I showed it to my uncle.

"Only four miles!" he said. "Four miles out of twenty-two! What a pleasant little stroll."

He was about to make some remark to the guide, but Hans, without answering him, took his place at the head of the horses again and set off.

Three hours later, still treading the faded turf of the pastures, we had to skirt the Kollafjörd, a detour easier and shorter than a crossing of that gulf; and soon we were entering a "pingstaoer," a place of communal jurisdiction, named Ejulberg, whose bell tower would have struck noon — if Icelandic churches had been rich enough to own a clock. But they closely resemble their parishioners, who have no watches and manage very well without them.

There the horses were refreshed; then, taking a shoreline squeezed between a chain of hills and the sea, they carried us in a single stretch to the "aoalkirkja" of Brantar, and a mile farther on to Saurböer "annexia," an annexed church on the southern bank of the Hvalfjörd.

It was then four in the afternoon; we had covered four miles — a good eight leagues.

At that spot the fjord was at least half a mile wide; the waves broke noisily on the sharp rocks; the gulf widened out between walls of rock, a sort of sheer escarpment three thousand feet high, remarkable for its brown strata parted by beds of tuff in a reddish shade. Whatever the intelligence of our horses, I augured nothing good from the crossing of a genuine arm of the sea on the back of a four-legged animal.

"If they're intelligent," I said, "they won't so much as try to cross. In any case, I'll take it upon myself to be intelligent for them."

But my uncle wasn't willing to wait; he spurred hard for the shore. His mount came up to sniff the last ripple of the waves and stopped; my uncle, who had an instinct all his own, pressed it forward. A fresh refusal from the animal, which shook its head. Then came oaths and lashes of the whip — but the beast kicked out and began to unseat its rider; at last the little horse, buckling its hocks, slipped out from under the professor's legs and left him planted bolt upright on two stones of the shore, like the Colossus of Rhodes.

"Ah! Cursed animal!" cried the rider, suddenly turned into a pedestrian and as mortified as a cavalry officer demoted to the infantry.

"Farja," said the guide, touching him on the shoulder.

"What? A ferry?"

"Der," Hans answered, pointing to a boat.

"Yes," I exclaimed, "there's a ferry."

"You should have said so, then! Well — off we go!"

"Tidvatten," the guide went on.

"What's he saying?"

"He says tide," my uncle answered, translating the Danish word for me.

"No doubt we have to wait for the tide?"

"Förbida?" my uncle asked.

"Ja," Hans replied.

My uncle stamped his foot, while the horses made their way toward the ferry.

I understood perfectly the necessity of waiting for a certain moment of the tide to undertake the crossing of the fjord — the moment when the sea, having reached its greatest height, stands slack. Then the ebb and flow have no perceptible pull, and the ferry runs no risk of being swept off, either to the bottom of the gulf or clear out into the open ocean.

The favorable instant didn't come until six in the evening; my uncle, myself, the guide, two ferrymen, and the four horses took our places in a sort of flat, rather flimsy barge. Used as I was to the steam ferries of the Elbe, I found the boatmen's oars a sorry sort of machinery. It took more than an hour to cross the fjord; but in the end the passage came off without mishap.

Half an hour later we reached the "aoalkirkja" of Gardär.
