Chapter 32

On August 13 we woke early. The day had come to try out a new mode of travel—fast and, best of all, hardly any work.

A mast made of two poles lashed together, a yard formed from a third, a sail borrowed from our blankets: that was the whole rig of the raft. There was no shortage of rope. The whole thing was solid.

At six o'clock the Professor gave the order to embark. The provisions, the baggage, the instruments, the weapons, and a good quantity of fresh water were all stowed in place.

Hans had rigged a rudder that let him steer his floating contraption. He took the tiller. I cast off the mooring line that held us to the shore; the sail was trimmed, and we pulled away from land in a hurry.

Just as we were leaving the little harbor, my uncle—who was attached to his geographical naming rights—wanted to give the place a name. Mine, among others.

"Actually," I said, "I've got another one to suggest."

"Which is?"

"Graüben's. Port Graüben—that'll look very well on the map."

"Port Graüben it is."

And that is how the memory of my dear girl from Virland came to be bound up with our fortunate expedition.

The breeze was blowing from the northeast; we ran before the wind at tremendous speed. The very dense layers of the atmosphere gave a considerable push and worked on the sail like a powerful fan.

After an hour, my uncle had been able to gauge our speed.

"If we keep going like this," he said, "we'll make at least thirty leagues in twenty-four hours, and it won't be long before we sight the opposite shores."

I didn't answer, and I went to take up a place at the front of the raft. Already the northern coast was sinking below the horizon; the two arms of the shoreline were opening wide, as if to speed us on our way. Before my eyes stretched an immense sea. Great clouds trailed their grayish shadows swiftly over its surface, shadows that seemed to weigh down upon the dismal water. The silvery rays of the electric light, reflected here and there by some stray droplet, kindled points of brightness along the sides of our craft. Soon all land was lost from view, every landmark vanished, and but for the foaming wake of the raft, I could have believed we hung in perfect stillness.

Toward noon, enormous seaweeds came undulating up to the surface of the waves. I knew the growing power of these plants, which creep along the sea bottom at depths of more than twelve thousand feet, reproduce under a pressure of nearly four hundred atmospheres, and often form beds vast enough to snag a ship's passage. But never, I think, was seaweed more gigantic than that of the Lidenbrock Sea.

Our raft skirted lengths of kelp three and four thousand feet long, immense serpents that unrolled beyond the reach of sight. I amused myself following their endless ribbons with my eyes, always expecting to reach the end of them, and for hours on end my patience was foiled—though never my astonishment.

What force of nature could produce such plants, and what must the earth have looked like in the first ages of its formation, when, under the action of heat and moisture, the plant kingdom developed all alone across its surface?

Evening came, and, just as I'd noticed the day before, the luminous state of the air did not dim in the least. It was a constant phenomenon whose duration one could count on.

After supper I stretched out at the foot of the mast, and it wasn't long before I fell asleep amid lazy daydreams.

Hans, motionless at the rudder, let the raft run—which, pushed along before the wind, didn't even need steering.

Since our departure from Port Graüben, Professor Lidenbrock had put me in charge of keeping the "ship's log," of noting the smallest observations, recording anything interesting, the direction of the wind, the speed we were making, the distance covered—in a word, every incident of this strange voyage.

So I'll limit myself here to reproducing these daily notes, written practically to the dictation of events, in order to give a more exact account of our crossing.

Friday, August 14.—Steady breeze from the NW. The raft runs fast and in a straight line. The coast lies thirty leagues to leeward. Nothing on the horizon. The intensity of the light doesn't vary. Fine weather—that is to say, the clouds are very high, thin, and bathed in a white atmosphere, like molten silver.

Thermometer: +32° centigrade.

At noon Hans baits a hook on the end of a line; he sets it with a small piece of meat and casts it into the sea. For two hours he catches nothing. So these waters are lifeless? No. There's a tug. Hans hauls in his line and brings up a fish that struggles vigorously.

"A fish!" cries my uncle.

"It's a sturgeon!" I cry in turn. "A small sturgeon!"

The Professor studies the animal closely and doesn't share my opinion. This fish has a flat, rounded head, and the forward part of its body is covered with bony plates; its mouth has no teeth; fairly well-developed pectoral fins are set on a body without any tail. The animal does belong to an order in which naturalists have classed the sturgeon, but it differs from it in some rather essential respects.

My uncle is not mistaken, for after a fairly short examination he says:

"This fish belongs to a family extinct for centuries, one whose fossil traces we find in the Devonian strata."

"What!" I say. "We've caught one of the inhabitants of the primitive seas alive?"

"Yes," the Professor answers, going on with his observations, "and you can see that these fossil fish have no identity with present-day species. To hold one of these creatures alive is a true joy for a naturalist."

"But what family does it belong to?"

"To the order of the Ganoids, family of the Cephalaspids, genus—"

"Well?"

"Genus Pterychtis, I'd swear to it. But this one shows a peculiarity said to occur in the fish of underground waters."

"Which is?"

"It's blind!"

"Blind!"

"Not only blind, but the organ of sight is missing from it entirely."

I look. Nothing could be truer. But it may be a special case. So the line is baited again and thrown back into the sea. This ocean is certainly rich in fish, for in two hours we take a great quantity of Pterychtis, along with fish belonging to a family equally extinct, the Dipterides, though my uncle can't identify their genus. All of them lack the organ of sight. This unexpected catch is a welcome resupply of our provisions.

So it seems settled: this sea holds only fossil species, in which the fish, like the reptiles, are all the more perfect the more ancient their creation.

Perhaps we'll come across some of those saurians that science has managed to reconstruct from a scrap of bone or cartilage.

I take up the telescope and examine the sea. It's deserted. No doubt we're still too close to the coast.

I look into the air. Why shouldn't some of those birds reconstructed by the immortal Cuvier be beating these heavy layers of atmosphere with their wings? The fish would supply them ample food. I scan the emptiness, but the skies are as uninhabited as the shores.

Even so, my imagination carries me off into the marvelous hypotheses of paleontology. I dream wide awake. I seem to see, on the surface of the waters, those enormous Chersites, those antediluvian turtles, like floating islets. Along the darkened beaches I seem to see the great mammals of the earliest days go by—the Leptotherium, found in the caves of Brazil, the Mericotherium, come from the frozen regions of Siberia. Farther off, the pachyderm Lophiodon, that gigantic tapir, hides behind the rocks, ready to fight for its prey with the Anoplotherium, a strange animal, part rhinoceros, part horse, part hippopotamus, part camel, as if the Creator, pressed for time in the world's first hours, had bundled several animals into one. The giant Mastodon swings its trunk and grinds the shore-rocks beneath its tusks, while the Megatherium, braced on its enormous paws, digs at the earth and wakes the echoes of the ringing granite with its roars. Higher up, the Protopithecus, the first ape to appear on the surface of the globe, climbs the steep summits. Higher still, the Pterodactyl, with its winged hand, glides like a great bat on the compressed air. Finally, in the topmost layers, immense birds, mightier than the cassowary, larger than the ostrich, spread their vast wings and go crashing headlong into the wall of the granite vault.

This whole fossil world is reborn in my imagination. I carry myself back to the biblical ages of creation, long before the birth of man, when the unfinished earth could not yet suffice for him. Then my dream outstrips the appearance of living beings. The mammals disappear, then the birds, then the reptiles of the secondary era, and finally the fish, the crustaceans, the mollusks, the articulates. The zoophytes of the transition period return to nothingness in their turn. All the life of the earth is summed up in me, and my heart is the only one beating in this depopulated world. There are no more seasons; there are no more climates; the globe's own heat rises without cease and neutralizes that of the radiant star. The vegetation runs wild; I pass like a shadow amid the arborescent ferns, treading with my uncertain step the iridescent marls and the mottled sandstones of the ground; I lean against the trunks of immense conifers; I lie down in the shade of Sphenophylls, of Asterophylls, of Lycopods a hundred feet high.

The centuries flow past like days; I climb back up the series of the earth's transformations; the plants vanish; the granite rocks lose their hardness; the liquid state is about to replace the solid under the action of a more intense heat; the waters run across the surface of the globe; they seethe, they volatilize; the vapors wrap the earth, which little by little becomes nothing more than a gaseous mass, heated to white heat, as big as the sun and as brilliant as it!

At the center of this nebula, fourteen hundred thousand times larger than the globe it will one day form, I am swept off into planetary space; my body grows tenuous, sublimes in its turn, and mingles like a weightless atom with these immense vapors that trace their flaming orbit through infinity!

What a dream! Where is it carrying me? My feverish hand throws its strange details down onto the paper.

I've forgotten everything—the Professor, the guide, the raft! A hallucination has seized my mind—

"What's the matter with you?" says my uncle.

My wide-open eyes fix on him without seeing him.

"Careful, Axel, you're about to fall into the sea!"

At the same moment I feel myself seized firmly by Hans's hand. Without him, in the grip of my dream, I would have hurled myself into the waves.

"Has he gone mad?" cries the Professor.

"What is it?" I say at last, coming back to myself.

"Are you ill?"

"No, I had a moment of hallucination, but it's passed. Everything all right otherwise?"

"Yes! Good breeze, fine sea! We're running fast, and if my reckoning hasn't misled me, we can't be long in making land."

At these words I get up, I search the horizon; but the line of the water still blends into the line of the clouds.
