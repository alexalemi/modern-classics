Chapter 43

Yes, gone mad! The needle leaped from pole to pole in violent jerks, ran through every point on the dial, and spun as though it had been seized by vertigo.

I knew well enough that, according to the most widely accepted theories, the mineral crust of our globe is never in a state of absolute rest; the changes brought on by the decomposition of internal matter, the churning of the great liquid currents, the action of magnetism—all of these work ceaselessly to shake it, even when the creatures scattered across its surface never suspect the least tremor beneath them. So the phenomenon alone would not have frightened me especially—or at least it would not have planted a truly terrible idea in my mind.

But other facts, certain details sui generis, could not fool me for long. The detonations were multiplying with a terrifying intensity; I could compare them to nothing but the racket of a great many carts hauled at speed over paving stones. It was one continuous roll of thunder.

Then the frenzied compass, jolted by the electrical phenomena, confirmed my opinion: the mineral crust was threatening to break apart, the granite masses to close in on one another, the fissure to seal itself, the void to fill—and we, poor atoms, were about to be crushed in that formidable embrace.

"Uncle! Uncle!" I cried. "We're lost!"

"What's this new terror?" he answered me with startling calm. "What's the matter with you?"

"The matter! Look at these walls shaking, this rock coming apart, this scorching heat, this boiling water, these thickening vapors, this crazed needle—every last sign of an earthquake!"

My uncle shook his head gently.

"An earthquake?" he said.

"Yes!"

"My boy, I think you're mistaken!"

"What! You don't recognize the symptoms?"

"Of an earthquake? No! I'm expecting something better than that!"

"What do you mean?"

"An eruption, Axel."

"An eruption!" I said. "We're in the chimney of an active volcano!"

"I believe we are," said the professor, smiling, "and it's the happiest thing that could possibly happen to us!"

The happiest thing! Had my uncle gone mad? What did these words mean? Why the calm, the smile?

"What!" I cried. "We're caught in an eruption! Fate has thrown us into the path of molten lava, of blazing rock, of boiling water, of every kind of erupting matter! We're going to be driven up, expelled, flung out, vomited, hurled into the air along with chunks of rock and rains of ash and cinders, in a whirlwind of flame! And this is the happiest thing that could happen to us!"

"Yes," answered the professor, looking at me over the top of his glasses, "because it's the only chance we have of getting back to the surface of the earth!"

I'll pass quickly over the thousand thoughts that crossed one another in my brain. My uncle was right, absolutely right, and never had he struck me as more daring or more certain than at that moment, when he waited and calmly reckoned the odds of an eruption.

Meanwhile we kept rising; the night passed in that upward motion; the surrounding din redoubled; I was nearly suffocating, I thought I had reached my final hour, and yet—the imagination is such a strange thing—I gave myself over to a truly childish line of inquiry. But I was at the mercy of my thoughts, not their master!

It was clear that we were being thrust upward by an eruptive surge. Beneath the raft there were boiling waters, and beneath those waters a whole paste of lava, an aggregate of rocks that, at the summit of the crater, would scatter in every direction. So we were in the chimney of a volcano. No doubt about that.

But this time, instead of the Sneffels—an extinct volcano—we were dealing with one in full activity. So I asked myself what mountain this might be, and in what part of the world we were going to be spat out.

In the northern regions, of that there was no doubt. Before its fits of madness, the compass had never once wavered on that point. Ever since Cape Saknussemm, we had been carried due north for hundreds of leagues. So—had we come back beneath Iceland? Were we to be cast out through the crater of Hecla, or through those of the seven other fire-belching peaks of the island? Within a radius of 500 leagues to the west, along this parallel, I could see only the little-known volcanoes of the northwest coast of America. To the east there was but one below the eightieth degree of latitude: the Esk, on the island of Jan Mayen, not far from Spitzbergen! Craters there were plenty, to be sure, and spacious enough to vomit up a whole army! But which one would serve as our way out—that was what I kept trying to guess.

Toward morning, the upward motion quickened. If the heat grew instead of dropping as we neared the surface of the globe, it was because it was entirely local, the product of a volcanic influence. Our mode of travel could no longer leave any doubt in my mind: an enormous force, a force of several hundred atmospheres, produced by the vapors piled up in the bosom of the earth, was pushing us irresistibly upward. But to what countless dangers it exposed us!

Soon a tawny glare filtered into the vertical gallery, which was widening; I made out, to the right and left, deep corridors like immense tunnels, from which thick vapors escaped; tongues of flame licked and crackled along their walls.

"Look! Look, Uncle!" I cried.

"Well! Those are sulfurous flames. Nothing more natural in an eruption."

"But what if they wrap around us?"

"They won't wrap around us."

"But what if we suffocate?"

"We won't suffocate; the gallery is widening, and if we must, we'll abandon the raft and shelter in some crevice."

"And the water! The rising water?"

"There's no more water, Axel—only a kind of lava paste carrying us up with it, all the way to the mouth of the crater."

The column of liquid had indeed vanished, giving way to eruptive matter that was fairly dense, though still boiling. The temperature was becoming unbearable, and a thermometer held out in that atmosphere would have read more than seventy degrees! Sweat drenched me. Without the speed of our ascent, we would certainly have suffocated.

Still, the professor did not follow through on his idea of abandoning the raft, and he was right not to. Those few ill-joined beams offered us a solid surface, a foothold that we would have lacked anywhere else.

Around eight in the morning, a new incident occurred for the first time. The upward motion stopped short. The raft stayed absolutely still.

"What now?" I asked, jolted by that sudden halt as though by a blow.

"A pause," my uncle answered.

"Is the eruption dying down?"

"I certainly hope not."

I got to my feet. I tried to see around me. Perhaps the raft, caught on a jutting ledge of rock, was momentarily resisting the eruptive mass. In that case, we had to work it free as fast as we could.

But that was not it. The column of ash, cinders, and stony debris had itself stopped rising.

"Is the eruption going to stop?" I cried.

"Ah!" said my uncle through clenched teeth. "You're afraid of that, my boy; but don't worry, this moment of calm can't last long; it's already gone on five minutes, and before long we'll resume our climb toward the mouth of the crater."

As he spoke, the professor never stopped consulting his chronometer, and once again his forecasts proved right. Soon the raft was seized by a rapid, chaotic motion that lasted about two minutes, then stopped again.

"Good," said my uncle, checking the time. "In ten minutes it will start up again."

"Ten minutes?"

"Yes. We're dealing with a volcano whose eruption is intermittent. It lets us breathe along with it."

Nothing could have been truer. At the appointed minute, we were launched again with extreme speed; we had to cling to the beams to keep from being thrown off the raft. Then the surge stopped.

Since then, I've thought about this odd phenomenon without finding a satisfying explanation. Still, it seems clear to me that we did not occupy the volcano's main chimney, but rather a side conduit, where the effect made itself felt as a kind of recoil.

How many times this maneuver repeated itself, I couldn't say; all I can affirm is that with each renewal of the motion we were launched with growing force, as though carried off by an actual projectile. During the moments of pause, we suffocated; during the moments of propulsion, the scorching air cut off my breath. For an instant I dreamed of the bliss of finding myself suddenly in the hyperborean regions, in a cold thirty degrees below zero. My overexcited imagination wandered across the snowfields of the arctic lands, and I longed for the moment when I would roll myself over the icy carpets of the pole! Little by little, in any case, my head, battered by these repeated jolts, gave way. Without Hans's arms, more than once I would have cracked my skull against the granite wall.

I've kept, then, no clear memory of what happened during the hours that followed. I have a confused sense of continuous detonations, of the shaking of the rock mass, of a whirling motion that seized the raft. It rocked upon waves of lava, in the midst of a rain of ash. The roaring flames wrapped around it. A hurricane, as though driven from some immense fan, fed the underground fires. One last time, Hans's face appeared to me in a glow of flame, and after that I felt nothing but the grim horror of the condemned man lashed to the mouth of a cannon, at the instant the shot goes off and scatters his limbs through the air.
