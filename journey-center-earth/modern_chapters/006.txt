Chapter 7

And so that memorable session came to an end. The whole conversation had left me feverish. I stumbled out of my uncle's study half stunned, and there wasn't enough air in all the streets of Hamburg to bring me around, so I made for the banks of the Elbe, over by the steam ferry that links the town with the Harburg railway.

Was I really convinced by what I'd just heard? Hadn't I simply buckled under Professor Lidenbrock's sheer force of will? Was I actually supposed to take seriously his decision to go to the center of the earth? Had I just been listening to the crazed ravings of a lunatic, or the scientific deductions of a great genius? Where, in all of it, did the truth leave off and the delusion begin?

I drifted among a thousand contradictory ideas without being able to hold onto a single one.

And yet I remembered that I had been convinced, even as my enthusiasm began to cool. What I wanted was to leave that very instant, without giving myself time to think it over. Yes — right then and there, I'd have had the nerve to strap up my suitcase.

Still, I have to admit that an hour later the fever broke. My nerves unknotted, and from the deep chasms of the earth I climbed back up to its surface.

"It's absurd!" I cried. "There's no sense in it! It's not a serious thing to propose to any level-headed young man. None of it is real. I slept badly. I had a bad dream."

Meanwhile I'd followed the banks of the Elbe and rounded the town. Once past the harbor, I'd reached the Altona road. A hunch was guiding me — and a well-founded hunch, because soon I caught sight of my own little Graüben, striding briskly and bravely back toward Hamburg on those light, quick feet of hers.

"Graüben!" I called from a distance.

The girl stopped, a little flustered, I imagine, to hear herself hailed like that on the open road. In ten strides I was beside her.

"Axel!" she said, surprised. "Oh! You came out to meet me! Now that's very nice of you, sir."

But looking at me, Graüben couldn't mistake my anxious, shaken face.

"Whatever's the matter?" she said, holding out her hand.

"The matter, Graüben!" I burst out.

In two seconds and three sentences my pretty Virland girl was fully up to speed. For a few moments she said nothing. Was her heart pounding as hard as mine? I couldn't tell — but her hand didn't tremble in mine. We walked a hundred paces without a word.

"Axel!" she said at last.

"My dear Graüben?"

"That is going to be a wonderful journey."

I jumped at the words.

"Yes, Axel, and a journey worthy of a scholar's nephew. It's a fine thing for a man to make his name by some great undertaking!"

"What! Graüben, you're not going to talk me out of attempting an expedition like this?"

"No, dear Axel — and I'd gladly come along with you and your uncle, if a poor girl weren't bound to be a burden to you."

"Do you mean it?"

"I mean it."

Oh, women — girls — female hearts, forever past all understanding! When you're not the most timid creatures alive, you're the bravest! Reason has no hold on you at all. What! This child was egging me on to take part in the expedition! She wouldn't have been afraid to try the adventure herself. She was pushing me toward it — me, whom she loved, all the same!

I was thrown, and — why not admit it — ashamed.

"Graüben," I went on, "we'll see whether you still talk this way tomorrow."

"Tomorrow, dear Axel, I'll say exactly what I say today."

Hand in hand, but keeping a deep silence, Graüben and I walked on. I was worn out by the emotions of the day.

"After all," I thought, "the calends of July are still a long way off, and between now and then plenty could happen to cure my uncle of his mania for traveling underground."

Night had fallen by the time we reached the house on König-strasse. I expected to find the place quiet, my uncle in bed as usual, and good Martha giving the dining room its last flick of the feather duster for the evening.

But I'd reckoned without the professor's impatience. I found him shouting and dashing about in the middle of a squad of porters who were unloading crates of goods in the hallway. Our old servant didn't know which way to turn.

"Come on, Axel — hurry up, you wretched boy!" my uncle cried the instant he spotted me. "Your trunk isn't packed, my papers aren't in order, I can't find the key to my traveling bag, and my gaiters haven't come!"

I stood there thunderstruck. My voice failed me. My lips could barely form the words:

"So we're leaving?"

"Yes, you miserable boy, off gallivanting instead of being here where you belong!"

"We're leaving?" I repeated, my voice fainter still.

"Yes — the day after tomorrow, at first light."

I couldn't listen to another word, and I fled to my little room.

There was no room for doubt now: my uncle had spent the afternoon laying in a stock of the gear and supplies he'd need for the trip. The hallway was cluttered with knotted rope ladders, torches, water flasks, iron clamps, picks, iron-shod staffs, mattocks — enough to load down ten men at least.

I passed a dreadful night. The next morning I heard myself being called early. I was determined not to open my door. But how do you hold out against the sweet voice that spoke these words: "My dear Axel?"

I came out of my room. I thought that my haggard face, my pallor, my eyes red from the sleepless night, would be sure to work on Graüben and change her mind.

"Ah, my dear Axel," she said, "I see you're feeling better, and that the night has calmed you down."

"Calmed me down!" I cried.

I rushed to my mirror. Well — I looked less awful than I'd supposed. It was hard to believe.

"Axel," Graüben said, "I've had a long talk with my guardian. He's a bold scholar, a man of great courage, and you'll remember that his blood runs in your veins. He told me his plans, his hopes — why and how he expects to reach his goal. He'll get there, I have no doubt of it. Oh, dear Axel, how splendid it is to devote yourself to science like that! What glory awaits Professor Lidenbrock, and will reflect onto his companion! When you come home, Axel, you'll be a man, his equal — free to speak, free to act, free at last to —"

The girl broke off, blushing, and didn't finish. But her words were reviving me. Even so, I still didn't want to believe we were really going. I dragged Graüben off to the professor's study.

"Uncle," I said, "so it's truly settled that we're leaving?"

"What! You doubt it?"

"No," I said, so as not to cross him. "Only I'd like to ask what the hurry is."

"The time! Time flying past with a speed you can never get back!"

"But it's only the 26th of May, and up until the end of June —"

"What! Do you imagine, you ignorant boy, that it's so easy to get to Iceland? If you hadn't run off on me like a madman, I'd have taken you to the Copenhagen booking office, at Liffender and Co. There you'd have seen that there's only one sailing from Copenhagen to Reykjavik."

"And so?"

"And so — if we waited for the 22nd of June, we'd arrive too late to see the shadow of Scartaris caress the crater of the Sneffels. We have to reach Copenhagen as fast as we can and find some means of getting across. Go and pack your trunk!"

There wasn't a word to say in reply. I climbed back up to my room. Graüben followed. It was she who took charge of arranging the things I'd need in a small suitcase. She was no more upset than if it had been a matter of a day trip to Lübeck or Heligoland. Her little hands came and went without hurry; she chatted calmly; she gave me the most sensible reasons in favor of our expedition. She enchanted me — and I felt a great swell of anger against her. Now and then I wanted to lose my temper, but she took no notice and went methodically on with her quiet work.

At last the final strap of the suitcase was buckled. I went down to the ground floor.

All through that day the merchants of scientific instruments, weapons, and electrical apparatus had multiplied at our door. Good Martha was losing her mind over it.

"Has the master gone mad?" she asked me.

I nodded that he had.

"And he's taking you with him?"

Another nod.

"Where to?" she said.

I pointed a finger toward the center of the earth.

"To the cellar?" the old servant cried.

"No," I said finally, "lower!"

Evening came. I'd lost all sense of how much time had gone by.

"Until tomorrow morning," said my uncle. "We leave at six o'clock sharp."

At ten I dropped onto my bed like a dead weight.

During the night my terrors seized me again.

I spent it dreaming of chasms! I was in the grip of delirium. I felt myself clutched in the professor's powerful hand, dragged along, swallowed up, sucked under! I was plunging to the bottom of bottomless pits with that ever-quickening speed of bodies let fall through space. My life had become nothing but one endless drop.

I woke at five, wrecked with exhaustion and emotion. I went down to the dining room. My uncle was at the table, wolfing down his food. I looked at him with a feeling of horror. But Graüben was there. I said nothing. I couldn't eat.

At half past five a rumbling sounded in the street. A large carriage was arriving to carry us to the Altona railway. It was soon loaded down with my uncle's baggage.

"And your trunk?" he said to me.

"It's ready," I answered, going faint.

"Then hurry up and bring it down, or you'll make us miss the train!"

Fighting my fate struck me, just then, as impossible. I climbed back up to my room, and, letting my suitcase slide down the stairs, I flung myself after it.

At that moment my uncle was solemnly placing "the reins" of his household into Graüben's hands. My pretty Virland girl kept her usual calm. She embraced her guardian, but she couldn't hold back a tear as she brushed my cheek with her soft lips.

"Graüben!" I cried.

"Go, my dear Axel, go," she told me. "You're leaving your fiancée behind, but you'll find your wife waiting when you return."

I held Graüben tight in my arms and took my seat in the carriage. From the doorstep, Martha and the girl sent us a last farewell; then the two horses, spurred on by their driver's whistle, sprang off at a gallop down the Altona road.
