Chapter 10

Dinner was ready, and Professor Lidenbrock fell on it ravenously, for the forced fasting aboard ship had turned his stomach into a bottomless pit. The meal was more Danish than Icelandic, and unremarkable in itself; but our host, more Icelandic than Danish, reminded me of the heroes of ancient hospitality. It was plain to me that we were more at home in his house than he was himself.

The conversation ran in the native tongue, which my uncle laced with German and Mr. Fridriksson with Latin, so that I could follow it. It turned on scientific questions, as befits men of learning; but Professor Lidenbrock kept himself under the most extreme reserve, and with every sentence his eyes charged me to keep absolute silence about our plans.

First of all, Mr. Fridriksson inquired of my uncle how his research at the library had turned out.

"Your library!" my uncle exclaimed. "It's nothing but odd, unmatched volumes on nearly empty shelves."

"What!" replied Mr. Fridriksson. "We own eight thousand volumes, many of them precious and rare — works in the old Scandinavian tongue, and all the new titles that Copenhagen sends us every year."

"And where are these eight thousand volumes? As far as I could see—"

"Oh, Mr. Lidenbrock, they're out roaming the country! We have a taste for study on our old island of ice. There isn't a farmer, isn't a fisherman, who can't read and doesn't. We believe that books are meant to be worn out under the eyes of readers, not to molder behind an iron grille, far from curious gazes. And so these volumes pass from hand to hand — leafed through, read and reread — and often they don't come back to their shelf until a year or two have gone by."

"Meanwhile," my uncle answered with a touch of pique, "visiting scholars—"

"What do you expect! Foreigners have their own libraries at home, and above all our country folk must educate themselves. I tell you again, the love of study is in the Icelandic blood. Why, back in 1816 we founded a Literary Society that is thriving; foreign scholars are proud to belong to it; it publishes books for the education of our countrymen and does the country real service. If you'd care to be one of our corresponding members, Mr. Lidenbrock, you would give us the greatest pleasure."

My uncle, who already belonged to a hundred or so scientific societies, accepted with a good grace that touched Mr. Fridriksson.

"Now then," our host went on, "be so kind as to tell me which books you'd hoped to find in our library, and perhaps I can give you some word of them."

I glanced at my uncle. He hesitated to answer. This touched directly on his plans. Still, after a moment's thought, he made up his mind to speak.

"Mr. Fridriksson," he said, "I wanted to know whether, among your old works, you happened to have any by Arne Saknussemm?"

"Arne Saknussemm!" replied the professor from Reykjavik. "You mean that sixteenth-century scholar — the great naturalist, great alchemist, and great traveler all at once?"

"Precisely."

"One of the glories of Icelandic literature and science?"

"The very man."

"A figure illustrious above all others?"

"I grant you that."

"And whose daring matched his genius?"

"I see you know him well." My uncle was fairly swimming in joy to hear his hero spoken of this way. He devoured Mr. Fridriksson with his eyes.

"Well then," he demanded, "his works?"

"Ah! His works — we don't have them."

"What! Not in Iceland?"

"They exist neither in Iceland nor anywhere else."

"And why is that?"

"Because Arne Saknussemm was persecuted for heresy, and in 1573 his works were burned at Copenhagen by the hand of the executioner."

"Excellent! Perfect!" cried my uncle, to the great scandal of the professor of natural sciences.

"I beg your pardon?" said the latter.

"Yes! It all makes sense, it all links up, it's all clear — and I understand now why Saknussemm, blacklisted and forced to hide the discoveries of his genius, had to bury the secret in an incomprehensible cryptogram—"

"What secret?" asked Mr. Fridriksson sharply.

"A secret that... whose..." my uncle stammered.

"Might you have some particular document?" our host pressed.

"No. I was making a pure supposition."

"Very well," said Mr. Fridriksson, who had the kindness not to insist when he saw how flustered his companion had become. "I hope," he added, "that you won't leave our island without drawing on its mineralogical riches?"

"Certainly," my uncle answered. "But I arrive a little late — scholars have already passed this way, haven't they?"

"Yes, Mr. Lidenbrock. The labors of Messrs. Olafsen and Povelsen, carried out by order of the king; the studies of Troïl; the scientific mission of Messrs. Gaimard and Robert aboard the French corvette the Recherche;* and, more recently, the observations of the scholars aboard the frigate the Reine-Hortense — all have contributed powerfully to the survey of Iceland. But believe me, there is still work to be done."

(The Recherche was sent out in 1835 by Admiral Duperré to find some trace of a lost expedition — that of Monsieur de Blosseville and the Lilloise, of which no news was ever heard.)

"You think so?" asked my uncle, with a good-natured air, trying to dampen the flash in his eyes.

"Yes. So many mountains, glaciers, volcanoes to study, and so little known about them! Look — without going any farther, see that peak rising on the horizon. That's the Sneffels."

"Ah!" said my uncle. "The Sneffels."

"Yes, one of the most curious volcanoes there is, and one whose crater is rarely visited."

"Extinct?"

"Oh! Extinct these five hundred years."

"Well then," replied my uncle, who was frantically crossing his legs to keep from leaping into the air, "I have a mind to begin my geological studies with this Seffel... Fessel... how do you say it?"

"Sneffels," said the excellent Mr. Fridriksson.

This part of the conversation had taken place in Latin; I had understood every word, and I could barely keep a straight face watching my uncle rein in a satisfaction that was spilling out of him on every side. He put on a little air of innocence that looked exactly like the grimace of an old devil.

"Yes," he said, "your words decide me; we'll try to climb this Sneffels — perhaps even to study its crater!"

"I only regret," replied Mr. Fridriksson, "that my duties won't allow me to get away; I would have gone with you gladly, and to good purpose."

"Oh, no! Oh, no!" my uncle answered quickly. "We don't want to trouble anyone, Mr. Fridriksson; I thank you with all my heart. The presence of a scholar like yourself would have been most useful, but the duties of your profession..."

I like to think that our host, in the innocence of his Icelandic soul, never grasped my uncle's broad cunning.

"I entirely approve, Mr. Lidenbrock," he said, "of your beginning with that volcano; you'll gather a rich harvest of curious observations there. But tell me — how do you mean to reach the Sneffels peninsula?"

"By sea, crossing the bay. That's the quickest route."

"No doubt; but it's impossible to take."

"Why?"

"Because we don't have a single boat in Reykjavik."

"The devil!"

"You'll have to go overland, following the coast. It'll be longer, but more interesting."

"Good. I'll see about finding a guide."

"I have precisely the man to offer you."

"Someone dependable, intelligent?"

"Yes, a native of the peninsula. He's an eider-down hunter, very skilled, and you'll be pleased with him. He speaks Danish perfectly."

"And when can I see him?"

"Tomorrow, if that suits you."

"Why not today?"

"Because he doesn't arrive until tomorrow."

"Tomorrow it is, then," my uncle answered with a sigh.

This momentous conversation ended a few moments later with warm thanks from the German professor to the Icelandic one. Over the course of that dinner my uncle had learned some important things — among them the story of Saknussemm, the reason behind his mysterious document, the fact that our host would not be joining his expedition, and that as early as the next day a guide would be at his command.
