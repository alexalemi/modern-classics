Chapter 27

I cannot paint my despair. No word in any human language could capture what I felt. I had been buried alive, with the prospect of dying in the tortures of hunger and thirst.

Without thinking, I dragged my burning hands across the ground. How parched that rock felt to me!

But how had I lost the course of the stream? Because it was gone — utterly gone! I understood then the reason for that strange silence, back when I had listened one last time for some call from my companions to reach my ears. So it must have been that the moment my first step carried me onto that reckless path, I never noticed the stream had fallen silent. It was obvious now: at that instant a fork in the gallery had opened before me, while the Hansbach — obeying the whim of some other slope — had gone off with my companions toward depths unknown.

How was I to get back? There were no tracks. My foot left no print on that granite. I battered my brain trying to solve the unsolvable. My whole situation came down to a single word: lost!

Yes — lost, at a depth that seemed to me beyond all measure. Those thirty leagues of the earth's crust bore down on my shoulders with a horrifying weight. I felt myself being crushed.

I tried to turn my thoughts back to the things of the surface. I could barely manage it. Hamburg, the house on König-strasse, my poor Graüben — that whole world beneath which I was now lost — flashed in terror through my memory. In one vivid hallucination I lived over again the incidents of the journey: the crossing, Iceland, Mr. Fridriksson, the Sneffels! I told myself that if, in my position, I still clung to the faintest shadow of a hope, it would be a sign of madness — that it was better simply to despair.

For truly, what human power could carry me back to the surface of the globe and prise apart these enormous vaults that arched above my head? Who could set me back on the road home and reunite me with my companions?

"Oh, Uncle!" I cried, in a voice of pure despair.

It was the only word of reproach that came to my lips, for I understood how the poor man must be suffering as he, in his turn, went searching for me.

When I saw myself thus cut off from all human help, unable to attempt anything at all to save myself, I thought of the help of Heaven. Memories of my childhood came back to me — memories of my mother, whom I had known only in the days of a small child's kisses. I turned to prayer, however little right I had to be heard by the God I was appealing to so late, and I implored Him with all my heart.

This return toward Providence gave me back a little calm, and I was able to bring the whole force of my mind to bear on my situation.

I had three days' worth of food, and my flask was full. Even so, I could not stay alone any longer. But should I climb, or descend?

Climb, obviously! Always climb!

That way I was bound to arrive back at the point where I had left the spring — at that fatal fork. Once I had the stream under my feet again, I could always make my way back up to the summit of the Sneffels.

Why hadn't I thought of it sooner? Here, clearly, was a chance of salvation. The most urgent thing, then, was to find the course of the Hansbach again.

I stood, and, leaning on my iron-tipped staff, I started back up the gallery. The slope was fairly steep. I walked full of hope and without hesitation, like a man who has no choice of which road to take.

For half an hour, no obstacle stopped my steps. I tried to recognize my route by the shape of the tunnel, by the jut of certain rocks, by the arrangement of the crannies in the walls. But no particular sign struck me, and I soon realized that this gallery could not lead me back to the fork. It was a dead end. I ran up against an impenetrable wall, and I fell onto the rock.

What terror, what despair seized me then, I could not begin to say. I lay there, annihilated. My last hope had just shattered against that wall of granite.

Lost in this labyrinth, whose windings crossed and recrossed in every direction, I no longer had even an impossible escape to attempt. I would have to die the most dreadful of deaths! And — strange thing — the thought came to me that if my fossilized body were ever found one day, its discovery thirty leagues down in the bowels of the earth would raise grave scientific questions!

I tried to speak aloud, but only hoarse sounds passed between my parched lips. I was gasping for breath.

In the midst of this anguish, a new terror took hold of my mind. My lamp had been damaged in the fall. I had no way to repair it. Its light was going pale — it was about to fail me!

I watched the luminous current shrink within the coil of the instrument. A procession of moving shadows unrolled across the darkening walls. I no longer dared lower my eyelids, afraid of losing the least atom of that fleeting brightness. At every instant it seemed to me that it was about to die out, and that "the black" was closing in on me.

At last a final gleam trembled in the lamp. I followed it, I drank it in with my eyes, I concentrated on it all the power of my sight, as on the last sensation of light my eyes would ever be given to know — and then I was plunged into a vast and total darkness.

What a terrible cry escaped me! On the surface of the earth, in the depths of the blackest night, light never entirely surrenders its rights; it is diffuse, it is subtle, but however little of it remains, the retina of the eye ends by perceiving it. Here, nothing. The absolute shadow made of me a blind man in the fullest sense of the word.

Then my mind gave way. I got to my feet, arms thrown out before me, groping in the most agonizing way; I broke into a run, hurling my steps at random through that inextricable labyrinth, forever descending, racing through the earth's crust like some creature of the underground faults — calling, shouting, howling — soon bruised on the jutting rocks, falling and getting up again bloodied, trying to drink the blood that streamed over my face, and forever waiting for some unforeseen wall to offer my head an obstacle to smash itself against!

Where did that mad race take me? I shall never know. After several hours — no doubt at the very end of my strength — I fell like a lifeless mass along the wall, and I lost all sense of my own existence.
