Chapter 16

Supper was wolfed down in a hurry, and our little troop bedded down as best it could. The mattress was hard, the shelter flimsy, the whole situation thoroughly miserable — five thousand feet above sea level. And yet my sleep that night was uncommonly peaceful, one of the best I'd had in a long while. I didn't even dream.

The next morning we woke half frozen in the sharp, biting air, under the rays of a glorious sun. I left my granite bed and went to drink in the magnificent scene unrolling before my eyes.

I was standing on the summit of one of Sneffels' two peaks — the southern one. From up there my gaze swept over the greater part of the island. That trick of perspective, common to all great heights, lifted up the coastlines while the central regions seemed to sink away. It was as though one of Helbesmer's relief maps lay spread out beneath my feet. I could see the deep valleys crisscrossing in every direction, the precipices sunk like wells, the lakes shrunk to ponds, the rivers dwindled to streams. On my right marched glaciers without number and peak upon peak, a few of them plumed with faint wisps of smoke. The undulations of those endless mountains, their sheets of snow seeming to make them foam, called to my mind the surface of a heaving sea. And when I turned toward the west, the ocean stretched out there in all its majesty, like a continuation of those rolling, fleece-white summits. Where the land ended and the waves began, my eye could scarcely tell.

I lost myself like that in the marvelous rapture the high peaks give — and this time without vertigo, for I was growing used at last to these sublime contemplations. My dazzled eyes bathed in the transparent flood of sunlight; I forgot who I was, where I was, and lived instead the life of the elves and sylphs, those imaginary creatures of Scandinavian myth. I drank myself drunk on the sheer delight of the heights, without a thought for the abysses into which my fate would soon be plunging me. But I was called back to the world by the arrival of the professor and Hans, who joined me on the summit of the peak.

My uncle turned toward the west and pointed to a faint vapor, a haze, a suggestion of land riding above the line of the waves.

"Greenland," he said.

"Greenland?" I cried.

"Yes — we're not thirty-five leagues from it, and during the thaws the polar bears drift all the way down to Iceland, carried along on the ice floes from the north. But no matter. We're standing on the summit of Sneffels. Here are its two peaks, one to the south, one to the north. Hans will tell us what the Icelanders call the one holding us up at this moment."

The question put to him, the hunter answered: "Scartaris."

My uncle shot me a triumphant look.

"To the crater!" he said.

The crater of Sneffels was an inverted cone, its mouth perhaps half a league across. Its depth I put at around two thousand feet. Imagine the state of a vessel like that when it filled up with thunder and flame. The bottom of the funnel couldn't have measured more than five hundred feet around, so that its fairly gentle slopes made it easy enough to reach the lower part. In spite of myself I kept comparing this crater to an enormous flared blunderbuss — and the comparison frightened me.

"To climb down into a blunderbuss," I thought, "when it's maybe loaded and might go off at the slightest jolt — that's the work of madmen."

But there was no backing out now. With an indifferent air, Hans took the lead again. I followed him without a word.

To make the descent easier, Hans traced long, drawn-out ellipses inside the cone. We had to walk through the volcanic rock, some of it so loosely lodged in its sockets that it broke free and went bounding all the way to the bottom of the pit. Each fall set off strange, ringing echoes.

Certain stretches of the cone were sheeted in interior glaciers. There Hans went forward only with extreme caution, sounding the ground with his iron-tipped staff to find the crevasses. At the more doubtful passages it became necessary to rope ourselves together with a long line, so that anyone whose foot suddenly went out from under him would be held up by his companions. Prudent enough, that solidarity — but it didn't rule out all danger.

Still, despite the difficulties of descending slopes the guide didn't know, the route was made without mishap, apart from one bundle of ropes that slipped from an Icelander's hands and took the shortest way straight to the bottom of the pit.

By noon we had arrived. I raised my head and saw the upper mouth of the cone, framing a scrap of sky of a strangely reduced circumference — but almost perfectly round. At a single point the peak of Scartaris broke the ring, thrusting up into the vast beyond.

At the bottom of the crater three chimneys opened up, the shafts through which, in the days of Sneffels' eruptions, the central furnace had driven out its lava and its vapors. Each of these chimneys was about a hundred feet across. They gaped there beneath our feet. I hadn't the courage to look down into them. Professor Lidenbrock, for his part, had made a quick study of how they lay; he was breathless, running from one to the next, gesturing and flinging out unintelligible words. Hans and his companions, seated on chunks of lava, watched him at it; they plainly took him for a lunatic.

All at once my uncle let out a shout. I thought he'd lost his footing and gone tumbling into one of the three gulfs. But no. There he was, arms spread, legs planted apart, standing before a block of granite set at the center of the crater like an enormous pedestal made to hold a statue of Pluto. He had the look of a man dumbfounded — but the dumbfoundedness soon gave way to a wild, delirious joy.

"Axel! Axel!" he cried. "Come here! Come!"

I ran to him. Neither Hans nor the Icelanders stirred.

"Look," said the professor.

And, sharing his astonishment if not his joy, I read on the western face of the block, in runic characters half eaten away by time, this thousand-times-accursed name:

ᛐ ᛦ ᚳ ᛅ ᚼ ᛐ ᚴ ᚳ ᚢ ᚼ ᚼ ᛅ ᛯ

"Arne Saknussemm!" cried my uncle. "Will you doubt now?"

I made no answer, and I went back, dismayed, to my bench of lava. The proof of it crushed me.

How long I sat there sunk in my thoughts, I have no idea. All I know is that when I lifted my head I saw only my uncle and Hans at the bottom of the crater. The Icelanders had been dismissed, and were now heading back down the outer slopes of Sneffels to return to Stapi.

Hans slept peacefully at the foot of a rock, in a channel of hardened lava where he'd rigged himself a makeshift bed. My uncle prowled the bottom of the crater like a wild beast in a trapper's pit. I had neither the wish nor the strength to get up, and, following the guide's example, I let myself sink into an uneasy, aching doze, fancying I could hear noises or feel shudders running through the flanks of the mountain.

So passed that first night at the bottom of the crater.

The next day a gray, cloudy, heavy sky settled down over the summit of the cone. I didn't notice it so much from the gloom of the pit as from the fury that seized my uncle.

I understood the reason for it, and a last remnant of hope crept back into my heart. Here is why.

Of the three routes open beneath our feet, only one had been followed by Saknussemm. According to the Icelandic scholar, it was to be recognized by that peculiarity singled out in the cryptogram — that the shadow of Scartaris came to caress its edges during the last days of June.

You could, in fact, think of that sharp peak as the gnomon of a huge sundial, its shadow on one given day pointing out the road to the center of the globe.

Now, if the sun failed to appear — no shadow. And consequently, no signpost. It was the 25th of June. Let the sky stay overcast for six days, and we would have to put the observation off to another year.

I won't try to paint Professor Lidenbrock's helpless rage. The day dragged by, and no shadow came to stretch across the floor of the crater. Hans never budged from his place; he must nonetheless have wondered what we were waiting for — if he wondered anything at all! My uncle didn't address a single word to me. His eyes, forever turned toward the sky, lost themselves in its gray and misty tint.

On the 26th, still nothing; rain mixed with snow fell all day long. Hans built a hut out of pieces of lava. I took a certain pleasure in following with my eye the thousands of makeshift waterfalls streaming down the sides of the cone, each stone swelling their deafening murmur.

My uncle could no longer contain himself. It was enough to try a far more patient man, for this was truly to founder within sight of port.

But heaven never fails to mingle great joys with great sorrows, and it was holding in reserve for Professor Lidenbrock a satisfaction to match his maddening frustration.

The next day the sky was overcast once more. But on Sunday, the 28th of June, the antepenultimate day of the month, with the change of the moon came a change in the weather. The sun poured its rays in floods into the crater. Every hillock, every rock, every stone, every jutting edge took its share of that beneficent radiance and instantly cast its shadow on the ground. Among them all, Scartaris' shadow stood out like a keen blade and began to swing imperceptibly toward the blazing star.

My uncle turned with it.

At noon, in its shortest phase, it came to lick softly at the rim of the central chimney.

"There it is!" cried the professor. "There it is! To the center of the globe!" he added — in Danish.

I looked at Hans.

"Forüt!" said the guide calmly.

"Forward!" answered my uncle.

It was thirteen minutes past one in the afternoon.
