Chapter 4

"He's gone out?" cried Martha, hurrying up at the sound of the street door, which had slammed shut hard enough to shake the whole house.

"Yes," I answered, "gone right out!"

"But what about his dinner?" the old servant asked.

"He won't be having dinner!"

"And his supper?"

"He won't be having supper either!"

"What?" said Martha, clasping her hands.

"No, my good Martha. He won't be eating at all — and neither will anyone else in this house! My uncle Lidenbrock is putting us all on a starvation diet until he cracks an old scribble that is absolutely uncrackable!"

"Heaven help us! So we're all to starve to death, then!"

I didn't dare admit that, with a man as absolute as my uncle, starving was exactly the fate in store for us.

Seriously alarmed, the old servant went back to her kitchen, groaning.

Once I was alone, the thought came to me of going to tell Graüben all about it. But how could I leave the house? What if he called for me? What if he wanted to take up that word-puzzle again — the sort of riddle that would have stumped old Oedipus himself? And if I failed to answer his summons, what then?

The wisest course was to stay put. As it happened, a mineralogist from Besançon had just sent us a collection of siliceous geodes that needed sorting. So I set to work. I sorted, I labeled, I arranged in their display case all those hollow stones with the little crystals stirring inside them.

But the task didn't absorb me. The business of the old document kept nagging at me in the strangest way. My head was seething, and I felt seized by a vague uneasiness. I had a premonition of some imminent disaster.

Within an hour my geodes were neatly shelved. I let myself sink back into the big Utrecht-velvet armchair, arms dangling, head thrown back. I lit my long pipe with the curved stem, whose carved bowl showed a naiad lounging lazily; then I amused myself watching the charring creep along, turning my naiad little by little into a thoroughly blackened figure. Every so often I listened for a footstep on the stairs. But no. Where could my uncle be just now? I pictured him striding beneath the handsome trees along the Altona road, gesticulating, thwacking the wall with his cane, whipping the tall grass with a furious arm, beheading thistles, and rousting the solitary storks out of their peace and quiet.

Would he come home triumphant or defeated? Which would win out over the other — the secret, or the man? I asked myself these questions and, without thinking, picked up between my fingers the sheet of paper on which I had copied out that unintelligible string of letters. I kept repeating to myself:

"What on earth does it mean?"

I tried grouping the letters so as to form words. Impossible. Whether I joined them in twos, threes, fives, or sixes, it came to absolutely nothing intelligible. True, the fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth letters made the English word "ice," and the eighty-fourth, eighty-fifth, and eighty-sixth spelled out "sir." And then, in the body of the document, on the second and third lines, I also spotted the Latin words "rota," "mutabile," "ira," "neo," "atra."

"The devil," I thought. "These last words would seem to prove my uncle right about the language of the document! And look — on the fourth line I can even make out the word 'luco,' which means 'sacred grove.' Yet on the third line you read 'tabiled,' with a perfectly Hebrew look to it, and on the last line the words 'mer,' 'arc,' 'mère,' which are pure French."

It was enough to drive you out of your mind! Four different languages in one absurd sentence! What possible connection could there be among the words ice, sir, anger, cruel, sacred grove, changing, mother, bow, and sea? The first and the last did fit together easily enough — no surprise that a document written in Iceland should mention a "sea of ice." But going from that to understanding the rest of the cryptogram was another matter entirely.

So there I was, wrestling with an unsolvable problem. My brain was overheating; my eyes were blinking at the sheet of paper; the hundred and thirty-two letters seemed to flutter around me like those silvery flecks that swim in the air about your head when the blood has rushed to it.

I was in the grip of a kind of hallucination. I was suffocating; I needed air. Absently, I fanned myself with the sheet of paper, and its back and front passed before my eyes one after another.

Imagine my surprise when, in one of those rapid flips — just as the back turned toward me — I thought I saw perfectly legible words appear, Latin words, among them "craterem" and "terrestre."

Suddenly a flash of light broke in my mind. Those few clues let me glimpse the truth; I had discovered the law of the cipher. To read this document, you didn't even need to read it through the reversed sheet! No. Just as it stood, just as it had been dictated to me, it could be spelled out fluently. All the professor's ingenious calculations came right; he had been correct about the arrangement of the letters, correct about the language of the document! It had come within a hair's breadth of letting him read this whole Latin sentence straight through — and that hair's breadth chance had just fallen into my lap!

You can imagine how shaken I was! My eyes clouded over; I couldn't use them. I had spread the sheet of paper flat on the table. A single glance was all it would take to make me master of the secret.

At last I managed to calm my agitation. I laid down the rule that I would walk twice around the room to settle my nerves, and then I came back and buried myself in the vast armchair.

"Let's read it," I cried, after laying in a full lungful of air.

I bent over the table; I set my finger on each letter in turn and, without stopping, without hesitating for an instant, I read the whole sentence aloud.

But what astonishment, what terror flooded through me! At first I sat as though struck by a sudden blow. What! The thing I had just learned had actually been done! A man had had the daring to venture down there! . . .

"No!" I cried, leaping to my feet. "No, no! My uncle must not know! It would be the last straw for him to learn of such a journey! He'd want a taste of it himself! Nothing could stop him! A geologist that determined — he'd go anyway, no matter what, in spite of everything! And he'd drag me along with him, and we'd never come back! Never! Never!"

I was in a state of excitement almost impossible to describe.

"No! No! It shall not be," I said forcefully. "And since I can keep such an idea from ever entering my tyrant's head, I will. Turning this document over and over, he might just stumble on the key by chance. Let's destroy it!"

There was still a bit of fire glowing in the hearth. I snatched up not only the sheet of paper but Saknussemm's parchment as well; with a feverish hand I was about to fling the whole lot onto the coals and wipe out this dangerous secret — when the study door opened. My uncle appeared.
