Chapter 25

So I woke on Sunday morning without the usual nagging worry of an immediate departure. And even though we were down in the deepest of abysses, that wasn't such an unpleasant feeling. Besides, we'd grown used to this troglodyte existence. I barely gave a thought to the sun, the stars, the moon, the trees, the houses, the towns — all those terrestrial luxuries that creatures under the sky have convinced themselves they can't live without. As fossils now, we snapped our fingers at those useless marvels.

The grotto formed a vast hall. Across its granite floor the faithful little stream ran quietly along. At such a distance from its source, its water had cooled to the surrounding temperature and could be drunk without any trouble.

After breakfast, the Professor decided to devote a few hours to putting his daily notes in order.

"First," he said, "I'm going to work out some figures, so I can fix our position exactly. When we get back, I want to be able to draw a map of our journey — a kind of vertical cross-section of the globe, giving the profile of the expedition."

"That would be fascinating, Uncle. But will your observations be accurate enough?"

"Yes. I've carefully recorded the angles and the gradients; I'm certain I haven't made a mistake. Let's see first where we are. Take the compass and tell me what direction it points."

I looked at the instrument, and after examining it closely, I answered:

"East-quarter-southeast."

"Good!" said the Professor, jotting down the observation and running through some quick calculations. "I conclude that we've come eighty-five leagues from our starting point."

"So we're traveling under the Atlantic?"

"Precisely."

"And at this very moment there might be a storm raging up there, ships being tossed on our heads by the waves and the gale?"

"That's possible."

"And whales beating their tails against the walls of our prison?"

"Don't worry, Axel — they won't manage to shake it. But let's get back to our calculations. We're to the southeast, eighty-five leagues from the base of the Sneffels, and from my earlier notes I estimate we've reached a depth of sixteen leagues."

"Sixteen leagues!" I cried.

"No doubt about it."

"But that's the absolute limit science sets for the thickness of the Earth's crust."

"I won't say otherwise."

"And here, following the law of rising temperature, there ought to be a heat of fifteen hundred degrees."

"Ought to be, my boy."

"And all this granite couldn't stay solid — it would be in full meltdown."

"As you can see, it isn't, and the facts, as usual, come along to give the theories the lie."

"I'm forced to admit it, but still, it amazes me."

"What does the thermometer read?"

"Twenty-seven and six-tenths degrees."

"So the scientists are off by fourteen hundred and seventy-four degrees and four-tenths. Which means the proportional increase of temperature is a mistake. Which means Humphry Davy wasn't wrong. Which means I was right to listen to him. What do you have to say to that?"

"Nothing."

To tell the truth, I'd have had plenty to say. I didn't accept Davy's theory in any way; I still held with the idea of central heat, even though I felt none of its effects. I honestly preferred to believe that this chimney of an extinct volcano, coated over by lava with a fireproof lining, simply wouldn't let the temperature spread through its walls.

But rather than stopping to hunt for new arguments, I just took the situation as it stood.

"Uncle," I went on, "I accept all your figures as accurate, but allow me to draw one strict conclusion from them."

"Go ahead, my boy, at your leisure."

"At the point where we are, at the latitude of Iceland, the Earth's radius is about fifteen hundred and eighty-three leagues, correct?"

"Fifteen hundred and eighty-three leagues and a third."

"Let's call it sixteen hundred leagues in round numbers. Out of a journey of sixteen hundred leagues, we've done twelve?"

"Just as you say."

"And that at the cost of eighty-five leagues traveled on the diagonal?"

"Precisely."

"In about twenty days?"

"In twenty days."

"Now, sixteen leagues make up one-hundredth of the Earth's radius. At this rate, then, it will take us two thousand days, or nearly five and a half years, to reach the bottom!"

The Professor didn't answer.

"Not to mention that if a vertical drop of sixteen leagues costs us a horizontal run of eighty, that comes to eight thousand leagues to the southeast, and we'll have come out through some point on the circumference long before we ever reach the center!"

"To the devil with your calculations!" my uncle snapped, with a flash of anger. "To the devil with your hypotheses! What are they even based on? Who says this passage doesn't go straight to our goal? Besides, I have a precedent on my side. What I'm doing here, another man has done, and where he succeeded, I'll succeed too."

"I hope so. But surely I'm allowed —"

"You're allowed to hold your tongue, Axel, whenever you feel like talking such nonsense."

I saw clearly that the terrible Professor was threatening to break out again from under the skin of the uncle, and I took the hint.

"Now then," he resumed, "check the pressure gauge. What does it show?"

"A considerable pressure."

"Good. You see that by descending slowly, gradually getting used to the density of this atmosphere, we suffer no ill effects at all."

"None — except a bit of pain in the ears."

"That's nothing, and you'll make that discomfort disappear by putting the outside air quickly in touch with the air held in your lungs."

"Absolutely," I answered, quite determined not to cross my uncle any further. "There's even a real pleasure in feeling oneself plunged into this denser air. Have you noticed how intensely sound carries through it?"

"No doubt; a deaf man would end up hearing perfectly down here."

"But surely this density will keep increasing?"

"Yes, according to a rather poorly defined law. It's true that the force of gravity will diminish as we go down. You know that it's at the very surface of the Earth that its pull is felt most strongly, and that at the center of the globe objects weigh nothing at all."

"I know that. But tell me — won't this air eventually take on the density of water?"

"No doubt, under a pressure of seven hundred and ten atmospheres."

"And lower down?"

"Lower down, that density will increase still more."

"Then how will we go down?"

"Well, we'll put stones in our pockets."

"My word, Uncle, you have an answer for everything."

I didn't dare venture any further into the field of hypotheses, for I'd have collided again with some impossibility that would have set the Professor off.

It was obvious, all the same, that air under a pressure that might reach thousands of atmospheres would eventually turn solid, and then — assuming our bodies had held up — we'd have to stop, in spite of every argument in the world.

But I didn't press the point. My uncle would only have countered again with his eternal Saknussemm, a worthless precedent — for even granting that the learned Icelander's journey had really happened, there was one very simple thing to say in reply:

In the sixteenth century, neither the barometer nor the pressure gauge had been invented; so how could Saknussemm ever have determined his arrival at the center of the globe?

But I kept that objection to myself and waited to see what would happen.

The rest of the day passed in calculations and conversation. I agreed with Professor Lidenbrock on everything, and I envied the perfect indifference of Hans, who, without hunting for effects and causes, went blindly wherever destiny led him.
