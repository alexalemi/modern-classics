Chapter 38

To understand this speech my uncle delivered to those illustrious French scientists, you have to know that a fact of the highest importance in paleontology had come to light shortly before we left.

On March 28, 1863, laborers digging under the direction of Monsieur Boucher de Perthes in the quarries of Moulin-Quignon, near Abbeville in the department of the Somme, in France, found a human jawbone fourteen feet below the surface of the ground. It was the first fossil of its kind ever brought up into broad daylight. Beside it lay stone axes and worked flints, colored and coated by time with a uniform patina.

The noise this discovery made was enormous, not only in France but in England and Germany. Several scientists of the French Institute — among them Milne-Edwards and de Quatrefages — took the matter to heart, proved the unquestionable authenticity of the bone in question, and made themselves the most ardent champions of this "jawbone trial," as the English put it.

To the geologists of the United Kingdom who held the fact to be certain — Falconer, Busk, Carpenter, and others — were joined the scientists of Germany, and among them, in the front rank, the most fiery, the most enthusiastic, my uncle Lidenbrock.

The authenticity of a human fossil from the Quaternary epoch thus seemed incontestably proven and accepted.

This theory, it's true, had a fierce opponent in Monsieur Élie de Beaumont. That scientist, a man of such high authority, maintained that the ground at Moulin-Quignon did not belong to the "diluvium" but to a more recent layer, and — agreeing on this point with Cuvier — he refused to admit that the human species had been contemporary with the animals of the Quaternary epoch. My uncle Lidenbrock, in concert with the great majority of geologists, had held firm, argued, disputed, and Monsieur Élie de Beaumont had been left almost alone in his camp.

We knew all these details of the affair, but we had no idea that, since our departure, the question had made fresh progress. Other identical jawbones — though belonging to individuals of different types and different nations — had been found in the loose gray earth of certain caves, in France, in Switzerland, in Belgium, along with weapons, utensils, tools, the bones of children, of adolescents, of grown men, of old men. The existence of Quaternary man was thus affirming itself more strongly every day.

And that wasn't all. New remains dug out of the Tertiary Pliocene ground had allowed even bolder scientists to assign a great antiquity to the human race. These remains, it's true, were not human bones, but only objects of human industry — tibias, femurs of fossil animals, regularly grooved, carved, so to speak, and bearing the mark of human workmanship.

And so, at a single bound, man leaped up the ladder of the ages by a great many centuries; he came before the Mastodon; he became the contemporary of the Elephas meridionalis; he had a hundred thousand years of existence behind him, since that is the date the most renowned geologists assign to the formation of the Pliocene ground!

Such was the state of the science of paleontology at that time, and what we knew of it was enough to explain our behavior before this boneyard of the Lidenbrock Sea. So it will be easy to imagine my uncle's astonishment and his joy, especially when, twenty paces further on, he found himself in the presence of — you might even say face to face with — one of the specimens of Quaternary man.

It was an absolutely recognizable human body. Had some soil of a peculiar nature, like that of the Saint-Michel cemetery at Bordeaux, preserved it this way for centuries? I couldn't say. But there it was, a corpse with skin drawn tight and turned to parchment, limbs still supple — to the eye at least — teeth intact, hair abundant, the nails on its fingers and toes of a frightful length, showing itself to our eyes just as it had lived.

I stood mute before this apparition out of another age. My uncle, so talkative, so impetuously long-winded as a rule, fell silent too. We had lifted the body. We had propped it upright. It looked back at us with its hollow sockets. We felt its resonant chest.

After a few moments of silence, the uncle was overcome by the professor. Otto Lidenbrock, swept away by his temperament, forgot the circumstances of our journey, the place we were in, the immense cavern that held us. No doubt he thought himself back at the Johannaeum, lecturing to his students, for he adopted a professorial tone, and addressing an imaginary audience:

"Gentlemen," he said, "I have the honor of presenting to you a man of the Quaternary epoch. Great scientists have denied his existence; others, no less great, have affirmed it. The doubting Thomases of paleontology, if they were here, could touch him with a finger and would be forced to admit their error. I know very well that science must be on guard against discoveries of this kind! I'm well aware of the use that Barnum and other charlatans of the same stripe have made of fossil men. I know the story of the kneecap of Ajax, of the supposed body of Orestes recovered by the Spartans, and of the body of Asterius, ten cubits long, that Pausanias tells of. I have read the reports on the skeleton of Trapani discovered in the fourteenth century, in which some wished to recognize Polyphemus, and the story of the giant dug up during the sixteenth century in the neighborhood of Palermo. You know as well as I do, gentlemen, of the analysis carried out near Lucerne in 1577 of those great bones that the famous physician Félix Plater declared belonged to a giant nineteen feet tall! I have devoured the treatises of Cassanion, and all those memoirs, pamphlets, speeches, and counter-speeches published on the subject of the skeleton of Teutobochus, king of the Cimbri, invader of Gaul, exhumed from a sand-pit in Dauphiné in 1613! In the eighteenth century I would have fought alongside Pierre Campet against the existence of Scheuchzer's pre-Adamites! I have held in my hands the writing entitled Gigans—"

Here reappeared my uncle's natural infirmity, which in public kept him from pronouncing difficult words.

"The writing entitled Gigans—" he began again.

He could go no further.

"Giganteo—"

Impossible! The unlucky word refused to come out! There would have been fine laughter at the Johannaeum!

"Gigantosteology," the professor Lidenbrock finally managed, between two oaths.

Then, resuming with even greater vigor, and warming to his theme:

"Yes, gentlemen, I know all these things! I also know that Cuvier and Blumenbach recognized in these bones nothing but simple Mammoth bones and those of other animals of the Quaternary epoch. But here, the merest doubt would be an insult to science! The corpse is right here! You can see it, touch it! It is not a skeleton, it is an intact body, preserved for purely anthropological purposes!"

I was quite willing not to contradict this assertion.

"If I could wash it in a solution of sulfuric acid," my uncle went on, "I would make all the earthy parts vanish, along with these gleaming shells encrusted on it. But I lack that precious solvent. Even so, just as it is, this body will tell us its own story."

Here the professor took hold of the fossil corpse and handled it with the dexterity of a showman displaying curiosities.

"You see," he continued, "it is not six feet long, and we are a long way from the so-called giants. As for the race it belongs to, it is unquestionably Caucasian. It is the white race, our own! The skull of this fossil is regularly ovoid, with no development of the cheekbones, no projection of the jaw. It shows none of that prognathism which alters the facial angle. (The facial angle is formed by two planes — one more or less vertical, tangent to the forehead and the incisors, the other horizontal, passing through the opening of the ear canals and the base of the nasal spine; prognathism, in anthropological language, is that projection of the jaw which alters the angle.) Measure this angle: it is nearly ninety degrees. But I will go still further down the road of deduction, and I will dare to say that this human specimen belongs to the Japhetic family, spread from the Indies to the limits of western Europe. Do not smile, gentlemen!"

No one was smiling, but the professor was so used to seeing faces break into grins during his learned lectures!

"Yes," he went on with fresh animation, "this is a fossil man, contemporary with the Mastodons whose bones fill this amphitheater. But as for telling you by what route he arrived here, how the layers in which he was buried slid all the way down into this enormous cavity of the globe — that I will not permit myself to do. No doubt, in the Quaternary epoch, considerable upheavals were still at work in the earth's crust: the continual cooling of the globe produced cracks, fissures, faults, down which some part of the upper ground very likely slid. I make no pronouncement, but at the end of the day the man is here, surrounded by the works of his own hands, by these axes, these worked flints that make up the Stone Age; and unless he came here as I did, as a tourist, as a pioneer of science, I cannot doubt the authenticity of his ancient origin."

The professor fell silent, and I burst into unanimous applause. Besides, my uncle was right, and men more learned than his nephew would have been hard put to argue against him.

Another sign. This fossilized body was not the only one in the immense boneyard. Other bodies turned up at every step we took through that dust, and my uncle could choose the most marvelous of these specimens to convince the skeptics.

In truth, it was an astonishing spectacle — these generations of men and animals mingled together in that cemetery. But a grave question presented itself, one we did not dare to resolve. Had these living beings slid down toward the shores of the Lidenbrock Sea by some convulsion of the ground, at a time when they were already reduced to dust? Or rather, had they lived here, in this underground world, beneath this artificial sky, born and dying like the inhabitants of the earth above? Until now, only the sea monsters, only the fish, had appeared to us alive! Might some man of the abyss still be wandering across these deserted shores?
