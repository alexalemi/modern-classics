TEXT ANALYSIS — A Journey to the Center of the Earth
(Jules Verne, 1864; Voyage au centre de la Terre)
Source: the ORIGINAL FRENCH from Project Gutenberg (#4791). TRANSLATED
FROM THE FRENCH. Third volume in the Verne recovery project (after
Twenty Thousand Leagues and Around the World in Eighty Days); the
from-the-French novel pipeline is proven — see those directories.

WHY THIS BOOK

The purest wonder-and-descent adventure Verne wrote: a coded parchment,
a dead volcano in Iceland, and a plunge miles down into a lost world of
underground oceans and prehistoric monsters. Victorian English readers
got heavily altered translations (the worst even renamed the characters
and rewrote passages). Retold complete and vivid from Verne's French, it
should read like the thrilling, funny, awe-struck page-turner it is.

WHAT THIS IS

Hamburg, 1863. The formidable mineralogist Professor Lidenbrock finds a
runic cryptogram hidden in an old book — a message from the 16th-century
alchemist Arne Saknussemm claiming a route to the Earth's center through
the crater of the Sneffels volcano in Iceland. Lidenbrock drags his
terrified nephew Axel and hires the imperturbable Icelandic guide Hans;
together they descend into a subterranean world of caverns, a vast inner
sea, giant fungi, battling sea-monsters, and a prehistoric wilderness —
before a volcanic eruption hurls them back to the surface in Italy.

THE VOICES — a three-hander held together by the narrator

- PROFESSOR OTTO LIDENBROCK — the uncle: brilliant, learned, and
  gloriously irascible; impatient, absolute, driven by scientific
  obsession; blunt and explosive but not a cartoon — his passion is real
  and, at the depths, so is his tenderness for Axel. Give him crackling,
  imperious speech.
- AXEL — the narrator: young, bookish, timid, in love with Graüben;
  skeptical of the whole mad scheme and often frightened — the reader's
  surrogate, who grows braver as they go deeper. Warm, wry, vivid
  first-person voice carrying the science and the wonder; keep it lucid
  and human, never dry.
- HANS BJELKE — the Icelandic eider-down hunter hired as guide: utterly
  calm, silent, faithful, resourceful; speaks rarely and in few words;
  saves the party again and again without fuss. His phlegmatic
  steadiness is the counterweight to Lidenbrock's fire. Keep his few
  utterances spare (e.g. his Danish/Icelandic tags, kept then glossed
  lightly in-line only where Verne does).
- GRAÜBEN — Axel's fiancée, Lidenbrock's young Virland ward: gentle,
  brave-hearted, encouraging. Minor but keep her dignity and warmth.
  Keep Verne's spelling "Graüben".

STYLE AND TONE

- ADVENTURE + WONDER, first-person. The modern voice is vivid, propulsive,
  and often funny (Axel's dread played for wry comedy against Lidenbrock's
  zeal). Verne's long descriptive/geological passages STAY complete — but
  render them with awe and clarity so they read as the texture of the
  wonder, not a textbook. Contractions welcome.
- THE SCIENCE stays as Verne wrote it (1864 geology, paleontology, the
  hollow depths, the prehistoric creatures) — no silent corrections; keep
  his figures, his Latin/Greek tags, and his mineralogical vocabulary.
- Keep the SET-PIECES electric: the cryptogram puzzle; the descent; the
  loss of Axel in the tunnels; the discovery of the inner "Lidenbrock
  Sea"; the raft voyage and the storm; the ichthyosaurus-vs-plesiosaurus
  battle; the giant prehistoric man/herd; the eruption ejection.

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY

- Names kept as Verne has them: Professor (Otto) Lidenbrock; Axel; Hans
  (Hans Bjelke); Graüben; Arne Saknussemm; the housekeeper Martha
  (Marthe). Hamburg; König-strasse; the Johannaeum. Iceland: Reykjavik,
  the Sneffels (Snæfells) volcano and its craters (Scartaris the peak
  whose shadow marks the way), Stapi, the Snæfellsjökull.
- "the Lidenbrock Sea" (the inner sea Lidenbrock names). The raft.
- THE CRYPTOGRAM: the runic message and its Latin solution must survive
  intact. Keep Saknussemm's Latin verbatim: "In Sneffels Yoculis
  craterem kem delibat umbra Scartaris Julii intra calendas descende,
  audas viator, et terrestre centrum attinges. Kod feci. Arne
  Saknussemm." — with Verne's own translation rendered in English
  ("Descend into the crater of Sneffels Yocul, which the shadow of
  Scartaris caresses before the calends of July, bold traveler, and you
  will reach the center of the earth. I have done it. Arne Saknussemm.").
  Do not modernize away the puzzle.
- Units: metric (meters, kilometers, degrees Celsius, atmospheres) and
  Verne's leagues kept as written; depths in the descent kept exact.
- Hans's recurring word for water/spring "Förlorad" and his tags kept as
  Verne gives them.

SENSITIVE / EDITORIAL

- Little period-sensitive content here; a few dated ethnographic notes on
  Icelanders/Danes — render faithfully in a measured, neutral register.

FAMOUS PASSAGES

- The decipherment of Saknussemm's cryptogram (and the Latin solution).
- "Descend, bold traveler..." — Saknussemm's message.
- The revelation of the Lidenbrock Sea, and the prehistoric-monster battle.
- The final eruption that flings them out of Stromboli.

FORMAT

45 files, one per chapter (I–XLV), single part, no dividers; chapters are
short (~1,500 French words average), none needed splitting. Verne titles
them only by number — the modern edition heads them "Chapter 1" … "Chapter
45". Ratio: French -> modern English narrative ≈ 0.95–1.30 (near 1:1; do
NOT pad). Verify with --min-ratio 0.9 --max-ratio 1.5.
