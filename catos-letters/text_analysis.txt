TEXT ANALYSIS — Cato's Letters, selected (Trenchard & Gordon, 1720–23)
Source: Wikisource's transcription of the original London Journal /
British Journal letters (prep.py strips wiki apparatus; original
spelling like "publick" survives in the source and is modernized in
translation).

THE SELECTION (documented per Alex's decision for a selected volume)

138 letters exist; this volume takes 18 — the ones revolutionary-era
American printers actually reprinted and founders quoted (Bailyn's
Ideological Origins documents the influence): the free-speech and
arbitrary-power letters (15, 25, 31, 33, 35, 38, 42, 45), the
natural-rights core that flows into the Declaration (59, 60, 61, 62,
63), property and power (68, 84), the American colonies letter (106),
the eternal-vigilance letter (115), and Cato's Farewell (138).
Letter 15 is the most famous: Franklin, age sixteen, reprinted its
argument in the New England Courant.

WHAT THIS IS

Weekly newspaper columns, published under the name "Cato" (the
Younger — the martyr of the Roman Lives volume, which is why the
pseudonym carried authority). Trenchard and Gordon wrote in the wake
of the South Sea Bubble: furious at corruption, suspicious of all
power, eloquent about liberty as every man's birthright. This is
18th-century op-ed writing at its peak — long periodic sentences,
righteous irony, classical examples.

STYLE AND TONE

- Newspaper polemic, addressed to the public through the fiction of a
  letter to the printer: keep the "Sir," salutation and the closing
  signature initial ("G" or "T") exactly as the source has them.
- The modern voice: a fearless columnist — direct, urgent, witty,
  occasionally savage. Modernize the periodic sentences decisively;
  the arguments should hit like modern op-eds because that is what
  they were.
- The ironies and thunder are the payload ("men... are afraid to
  think ill of a great man, though they see him doing ill") — keep
  every aphorism sharp.
- Classical and biblical examples stay (Brutus, Cato, Nero's crew) —
  they cross-reference our own Roman Lives volume nicely.

LOCKED RENDERINGS (checked by must_contain.txt)

- Letter 15 opening, near-verbatim modern form: "Without freedom of
  thought there can be no such thing as wisdom, and no such thing as
  public liberty without freedom of speech." Also: "the security of
  property and the freedom of speech always go together."
- Letter 33: the maxim of watchfulness — keep the shape "all history
  affords but few instances of men trusted with great power without
  abusing it."
- Letter 62: "Liberty is, to live upon one's own terms; slavery is,
  to live at the mere mercy of another." (modernized lightly, the
  antithesis intact).
- "publick" → "public" and all archaic spellings modernized;
  capitalized abstractions (Liberty, Power) lowercased except where
  personified.

SENSITIVE CONTENT

- 18th-century invective against standing armies, courtiers, South
  Sea directors, and "Turkish slavery" comparisons: render plainly as
  period polemic; the anti-tyranny argument is the content.
- Letter 106 discusses colonies as economic instruments — render
  faithfully; its cold mercantile logic is historically illuminating.

FORMAT

18 files, one letter each, no part markers. First line: "Letter N:
Title" exactly as in the source file. Then "Sir," then the modern
text, ending with the signature initial as the source has it. Word
ratio target 0.85–1.15.
