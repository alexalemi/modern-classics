Letter 115: The Encroaching Nature of Power

Sir,

The only thing that makes a nation free is the checks it puts on its magistrates; the only thing that makes a nation a nation of slaves is the lack of them. A people is free where its officials are held within limits the people set, and act by rules the people prescribe. A people is enslaved where its officials pick their own rules and follow their own appetites and moods — and no more dreadful curse can fall on any people than that. No magistrate ever did whatever he pleased without the people being ruined by his pleasure. That is why most nations in the world are ruined, and why only the nations that bridle their governors do not wear chains.

Unlimited power is so wild and monstrous a thing that, however natural it is to want it, it is just as natural to resist it. It should never be trusted to any mortal man, no matter how upright his intentions. For one thing, he will never want to give it up; for another, he will rarely dare to. Whether he likes it or not, he will make enemies, and his power will be his only protection against them — or at least he will believe it is. The constant, unforeseen emergencies of his position, the constant difficulties and opposition, will drive him — for his own survival, or for his power's survival — to try desperate expedients, to run risks, and to do things he never foresaw, never intended, and perhaps, at the start, genuinely abhorred.

We know from endless examples and endless experience that men who hold power will do anything rather than let go of it — anything, including the worst and blackest things — to keep it. Hardly any man on earth ever walked away from power while he could still get his own way inside it; when he could not, he resigned. I doubt there is a single exception to this rule anywhere in the world. Diocletian, Charles V, even Sulla — they laid down their power out of pique and discontent, out of opposition and disappointment. One thing seems certain: the good of the world, or of their own people, was not among their motives, either for staying in power or for quitting it.

It is the nature of power to be forever encroaching — to convert every extraordinary power, granted at a particular time for a particular occasion, into an ordinary power, to be used at all times and when there is no occasion at all. Power never willingly gives back an inch of ground. This is the spirit that has sometimes turned temporary commissions into permanent ones; that has stretched three years into seven, and one year into twenty; and that ensures that when the people are done with their magistrates, their magistrates are not done with the people.

The Romans knew this evil, having suffered from it, and built wise remedies against it. When one ordinary power grew too great, they checked it with another. So the office of the tribunes — the people's elected protectors — was set up to balance the power of the consuls, and to defend ordinary citizens against the arrogance, pride, and land-grabbing of the nobility. And when the tribunes' own authority grew too formidable, a good device was found to restrain it: in any turbulent or factious scheme of the tribunes, the protest or dissent of any single one of them voided the plans and proceedings of all the rest. And both consuls and tribunes were elected for one year only.

This is how the Romans preserved their liberty: by limiting the time and the power of their magistrates, and by making them answerable afterwards for how they had behaved in office. On top of all this, there lay an appeal from the magistrates to the people — a power which, great as it was, the people generally used with remarkable restraint and mercy. Like the people of other nations, they sinned far less often than their governors did. In fact, in any public disorder or disaster, the people are almost never the ones at fault. Far more often they suffer, with a patience that is itself almost criminal, the terrible evils brought down on them — wantonly or foolishly — by others, others whom they pay handsomely to prevent exactly those evils.

This sacred right of appealing to the people was secured by a very good and very severe law, which Livy records in these words:

Aliam deinde consularem legem de provocatione, unicum praesidium libertatis, decemvirali potestate eversam, non restituunt modo, sed etiam muniunt, sanciendo novam legem, ne quis ullum magistratum sine provocatione crearet: Qui creasset, eum jus fasque esset occidi: Neve caedes capitalis noxae haberetur.

The old consular law of appeal to the people — the first and only great safeguard of liberty — having been overturned by the usurping Decemviri, was now not merely restored but fortified with a new law: no magistrate could be created from whom there was no appeal, and it was lawful to kill any man who created one, without the killer facing a capital charge.

The Romans had all too good a reason for these laws; for the Decemviri — the ten men from whom there was no appeal — had enslaved them.

And because being chosen into power too often could be as dangerous as holding it too long, Cicero tells us in his book De Legibus that there was an express law — Eundem magistratum, ni interfuerint decem anni, ne quis capito — "that no man should hold the same magistracy he had held before, except after an interval of ten years." This law was later reinforced with severe penalties. So Rutilius Censorius publicly scolded the people for electing him censor twice. And Fabius Maximus tried to stop them from electing his own son consul — a man with every virtue the office required — because the chief magistracies had been held too long and too often by the Fabian family. Roman history is full of instances of magistrates, even chief magistrates, being stripped of office for pride, greed, and misgovernment; and those so degraded were barred by law, like our own late South Sea directors, from ever holding any post or power again. Nor were the Romans any less careful to make their magistrates, the moment they came out of their offices and governorships, open their books and give a strict account of their conduct. For a bad account they were often condemned and their estates confiscated. Beyond all this, to be a senator or a magistrate a man had to meet a property qualification; and those who had run through their fortunes were stripped of senatorial rank. A reasonable precaution: the men entrusted with their country's interest should have some interest of their own in it.

This is how the Roman people checked power and the men who held it; and when any power had grown completely ungovernable, they abolished it. So they expelled Tarquin, and monarchy with him, having first suffered a great deal under it; and they prospered spectacularly without it. Even that royal government had been tightly limited. The first Roman kings were little more than generals for life. They had no veto in the senate; they could make neither war nor peace; and even in the administration of justice an appeal lay from them to the people — as is clear in the case of the surviving Horatius, who killed his own sister. Servius Tullius made laws, Tacitus tells us, which even the kings had to obey. By confining the power of the crown within proper bounds, he gained power without bounds in the affections of the people. But the insolent Tarquin smashed through every bound, and acted so openly against the law and against the people of Rome that they had no remedy left but to expel him and his whole line — which they did, with glorious success.

Afterwards the power of a dictator was granted from time to time, and proved very useful; but it was always limited to a fixed number of months, and there are cases where even the dictator could not do as he pleased, but was overruled by the judgment of the people. And when Rome came to have great and distant territories, and great armies, the Romans decided the dictatorial power was too great and too dangerous to trust to any subject, and set it aside entirely. It was never used again — until it was violently usurped, first by Sulla, then by Caesar. And then Rome lost its liberty.
