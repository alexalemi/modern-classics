Letter 35: Of Public Spirit

Sir,

The love of one's country — public spirit, as we call it — is a phrase in everybody's mouth, but it rarely goes any deeper. It is talked about without being felt. Some people mention it without attaching any idea to it at all, except that it is a fine thing which everybody likes, and a good quality one would not want to seem to be without.

Others, when they name it, mean nothing but some poor, selfish gratification of their own. With great men it means wealth and empire — doing whatever they please and getting whatever they can. That is plain faction: promoting, under cover of the public, schemes that are inconsistent with it. With the trader and the craftsman it means encouraging only the particular trade or product he himself deals in; and that is monopoly and market-cornering, always harmful to the public.

In popish countries, it is public spirit to build and beautify a great many churches at the expense of the poor — who must then pay, at further expense, to maintain a long train of luxurious clergymen to perform tricks in them; or, in plainer words, to keep the heads and the pockets of their deluded hearers as empty as possible. It is likewise great public spirit there to deck an old skull with pearls and diamonds, and to enrich one venerable rotten tooth with gold and emeralds worth enough to maintain a city and all its inhabitants — who are perhaps starving to pay for it. It is very public-spirited there for a man to starve his family and his descendants in order to endow a monastery, and to feed — or rather gorge — a brotherhood of reverend gluttons, professed enemies of truth, of peace, and of the prosperity of the world; idlers maintained to stuff themselves and deceive. This, if you please, is public spirit: to rob a country of its working hands, to raise up a pernicious and turbulent mob of drones schooled in principles destructive of liberty, and to breed up a nation's enemies at the nation's own expense.

In arbitrary countries, it is public spirit to be a blind slave to the blind will of the prince, and to slaughter or be slaughtered for him at his pleasure. But in Protestant free countries, public spirit is another thing entirely. It is to fight force and delusion; it is to reconcile the true interests of the governed and the governors; it is to expose impostors and to resist oppressors; it is to keep the people in liberty, plenty, ease, and security.

This is public spirit. It contains every praiseworthy passion, and it takes in parents, family, friends, neighbors, and everything dear to mankind. It is the highest virtue, and holds almost all the others within it: steadiness to good purposes, faithfulness to one's trust, resolution in difficulties, defiance of danger, contempt of death, and impartial goodwill toward all mankind. It is a passion to promote the universal good at personal pain, loss, and peril. It is one man's care for many, and the concern of every man for all.

Look at this picture, you great patriots and guardians of the earth, and see whether you resemble it! Whom have you raised up for his own merit? Whom have you pulled down for your country's sake? What advantages have you won for your nation at a loss to yourselves? And have your people's losses never been your gains?

Outside England, these questions cannot easily be answered. Nor, in former days, could they be answered in England.

If my portrait of public spirit is thought too heroic — at least for the living generation, who are frankly babes in that virtue — I will readily grant that every man has a right, and a call, to provide for himself, to attend to his own affairs, and to pursue his own happiness. All I insist on is that a man's duty to himself come after the general welfare, and stay consistent with it. The business of all should be minded before the business of any one — as every man is quick to admit the moment his own stake is embarked with the whole; and every man's stake will prove to be embarked with the whole sooner or later. Some may thrive for a while on the public ruins; but their fate seldom fails to find them in the end — them, or their children.

It is a favorable sign of public spirit, though not a certain one, when a man's interest and his reputation rise together; and there is policy and wisdom in that. He who piles up money at the cost of his good name pays dearly for his avarice: it returns him hatred and curses along with the gold; and to be rich and detested is, to my mind, no pleasant character. The same holds for ambition, and for every other passion that breaks its bounds and makes a captive of its owner. It is scarcely possible to be a rogue and be beloved. And when men have grown numb to public censure — indifferent to what the community thinks of their honesty or dishonesty — it is a sign that they are at open defiance with the society they live in, and that everyone else ought to be on guard against them. They have, in effect, cut themselves off from society, and taught the people what to call them.

It is true that great bad men never lack for court paid to their fortunes — court which their self-love always construes as paid to their persons. But there is a way to undeceive them, and it often happens: let them once sink into meanness, and they will soon find themselves sunk into contempt; which is what hatred becomes when the thing hated shrinks.

There is a sort of man found almost everywhere who, having got hold of a set of profitable pet theories, is always ready to spread and enforce them, and calls the doing of it public spirit — though it often turns the world upside down. He is like the mad monk of Heidelberg, who wanted to knock every man on the head who did not care for Rhenish wine, which it seems was his beloved drink. Perhaps he thought it as reasonable to make all the world swallow Rhenish as to make them swallow transubstantiation.

Opinions — bare opinions — signify no more to the world than men's differing tastes; and all mankind would have to be made of one complexion, one size, and one age before they could all be made of one mind. Those patrons of dry dreams, who do harm to the world in order to improve it, are the pests and tormentors of mankind, and shut themselves out of any claim to the love of their country. Strange men! They would force everyone into absolute certainty about absolute uncertainties and contradictions; they would settle ambiguities without removing them; and they would plague and punish men for having only five senses.

I will assert another proposition, as true as the last, though it may sound stranger: that taking a thousand or ten thousand pounds a year for the merit of helping to drain a hundred times as much from the people is not public spirit, whatever custom may call it; and that grasping at everything, and stuffing a whole country into two or three pockets, is a sort of public spirit which I hope to God never to see — though there have been nations that sorrowfully felt it.

Liberty can never survive without equality, and equality cannot long be preserved without an agrarian law — a cap on landholdings — or something like it. So when men's riches grow immeasurably or startlingly great, a people who care about their own security ought to inquire strictly into how they got them, and oblige them to come back down to size, for fear of their terrifying the community — or mastering it. In every country, and under every government, particular men may be too rich.

If the Romans had kept their agrarian law well observed — the law fixing the extent of every citizen's estate — some citizens could never have risen so high above the others as they did; and consequently no one man could ever have been set above the rest, and established, as Caesar finally did, a tyranny in that great and glorious state. I have always thought that an inquiry into men's fortunes — especially monstrous fortunes raised out of the public, like Milton's infernal palace, as it were in an instant — matters more to a nation than certain other inquiries I have heard of.

But, some will say, is it a crime to be rich? Yes, certainly — at the public expense, or to the public danger. A man may be too rich for a subject; even the revenues of kings may be too large. It is one of the effects of arbitrary power that the prince has too much and the people too little; and that same inequality can, in its turn, be the cause of arbitrary power. It is as astonishing as it is melancholy to travel through a whole country — as one may through many in Europe — gasping under endless taxes, groaning under soldiers and poverty, and all of it to keep up a wanton and luxurious court filled, for the most part, with the worst and vilest of men. Good God! What hard-heartedness, what barbarity, to starve half a province to make a pretty garden! And yet even this gross wickedness is sometimes called public spirit, because — if you please — a few workmen and laborers are maintained out of the bread and the blood of half a million.

In those countries, if the judgment of the people were consulted, things would go better. But the people are despised, and their governors count them happy enough so long as they do not eat grass; and having no representatives and no share in the government, they have no remedy. Their misery, indeed, is such that their condition would be greatly improved if they could trade places with the beasts of the field: for then, being destined to be eaten, they would be better fed. What a misfortune for them that their governors are not cannibals! O happy Britain — may you remain so forever!

For a conclusion: since the preservation of property is the source of national happiness, whoever violates property, or lessens or endangers it, is — common sense says — an enemy to his country; and public spirit says that he should feel its vengeance. As yet in England, we can speak such bold truths; and we never dread to see the day when it will be safer for one man to be a traitor than for another man, or for a whole people, to call him one. Wherever public spirit is found dangerous, she will soon be seen dead.

I am, etc.

G
