Letter 33: Cautions Against the Encroachments of Power

Sir,

Considering what sort of creature man is, it is scarcely possible to put him under too many restraints when he holds great power. He may possibly use it well; but the wise course is taken by those who assume he will use it badly — who fence him within fixed bounds, and make it terrible to him to step beyond them.

Men who are above all fear soon grow above all shame. Tacitus says of Tiberius that once shame and fear were gone, he simply followed his own nature — straight down into crime and dishonor. Even Nero lived a long while inoffensively, and reigned virtuously. But when he found at last that he could do whatever he pleased, he let loose his appetite for blood, and committed slaughters so mighty, so monstrous, so unnatural, that only a heart devoted to the study of cruelty could have devised them. The good counsels of Seneca and Burrhus were, for a time, checks on his wolfish nature; and no doubt he suspected that if he made direct, outright war on his own people, they would resist and strike back. But discovering, degree by degree, that they would bear anything, and that his soldiers would execute everything, he moved into open defiance of mankind, and daily, wantonly, wallowed in their blood. Having no other rival left, he seemed to rival himself: every day's wickedness was blacker than the last.

Yet Nero was not the worst of all men. There have been thousands as bad as he, who only lacked the same opportunity to show it. And there have actually been many princes in the world who shed more blood, and did more damage to mankind, than Nero ever did. I could name a recent one who destroyed more lives than Nero destroyed — perhaps a hundred to one. It makes no difference that Nero committed his butcheries out of cruelty, and the other only for his glory. The world may be deceived by the change of names into abhorring the one and admiring the other; but to a nation about to be slaughtered it is all one whether it is slaughtered by the hangman or by dragoons, in prison or in the field. Ambition is no better than cruelty when it breeds mischief just as great.

It is nothing strange that men who think themselves unaccountable should act unaccountably — or that all men would be unaccountable if they could. Even those who have done nothing to displease their master do not know but that some day they may; and no man cares to be entirely at another's mercy. That is why, if every man had his will, every man would exercise dominion, and no man would submit to it. So it is owing far more to men's necessities than to their inclinations that they have put themselves under the restraint of laws, and appointed certain persons — called magistrates — to enforce them. Otherwise the laws would never be enforced at all; scarcely any man has virtue enough to enforce the laws against himself. On the contrary, most men think the law a grievance the moment it comes meddling with themselves and their property. "The author and the breaker of his own laws" was the character given of Pompey: he made laws when they suited his purposes, and broke them when they crossed his will. And it is the character of almost every man possessed of Pompey's kind of power. Such men intend the laws as a security for themselves, and a terror for everyone else. This shows the distrust that men have of men; and this is what made a great philosopher — Hobbes — call the state of nature a state of war. In a limited sense the definition is true, since human societies and human laws are the product of necessity and experience. For if all men were left to the boundless liberty they claim from nature, every man would be interfering and quarreling with another; every man would be plundering what another had acquired; the labor of one man would become the property of another; weakness would be the prey of force; and one man's industry would be the cause of another man's idleness.

Hence grew the necessity of government: a mutual contract among a number of men, agreeing on certain terms of union and society, and binding themselves under penalties — called laws — if they violated those terms, with one or more men appointed to enforce them. And so men gave up part of their natural liberty to acquire civil security. But the remedy has frequently proved worse than the disease; and human society has often had no enemies so great as its own magistrates — for all history affords but few instances of men trusted with great power without abusing it, and growing dangerous to the very people who made them what they were. Rome, while she was free — that is, while she kept her magistrates within due bounds — could defend herself against all the world, and conquer it. But once enslaved — that is, once her magistrates had broken their bounds — she could not defend herself against her own single tyrants, nor could they defend her against her foreign foes and invaders; for by their madness and cruelties they had destroyed her virtue and spirit, and exhausted her strength. This shows that magistrates who stand in absolute defiance of a nation either cannot last long themselves, or will not let the nation last long; and that mighty traitors, rather than fall alone, will pull their country down with them.

What a dreadful spirit that man must possess, who can weigh a private appetite against the universal good of his country and of mankind — and choose the appetite! Alexander and Caesar were that sort of men: they would set the world on fire and spill its blood rather than not govern it. Caligula knew that he was hated, and knew that he deserved to be hated; it did not mend him. Oderint dum metuant — "let them hate, so long as they fear" — was his byword. All that the monster aimed at was to be great and terrible. Most of these tyrants died as they deserved, and as they had reigned: by violence. But that did not mend their successors, who generally earned the fate of the men who went before them almost before they were warm in their place. "Even wickedness that fails finds imitators," runs the old line; "what then, when it flourishes and prospers?" If unlucky villainy finds rivals, what shall we say when it lifts up its head and wins?

There is no evil under the sun that is not to be dreaded from men who may do what they please with impunity. Such men seldom or never stop at some fixed degree of mischief when they have the power to go farther; they hurry on from wickedness to wickedness, as far and as fast as human malice can drive human power. "Once a man strays from the right path," says a Roman historian, "he plunges headlong — from propriety to depravity, from depravity to crime, from crime to the abyss." He speaks the truth here, though in other places he tells many lies — I mean that base flatterer of power, Velleius Paterculus. So when we see any great mischief committed with safety, we may justly expect mischiefs greater still.

The world is governed by men, and men by their passions; and passions, being boundless and insatiable, are always terrible when they are not controlled. Who was ever satisfied with riches, or stuffed full of power, or tired of honors? There is a tradition about Alexander: that having pushed through to the Eastern Ocean, and ravaged as much of this world as he knew of, he wept that there was not another world for him to conquer. True or not, it shows the spirit of the man — and indeed of human nature, whose appetites are infinite.

People are ruined by their ignorance of human nature; and that ignorance leads them to credulity, and to too much confidence in particular men. They fondly imagine that a man who possesses a great deal by their favor, and owes them great gratitude and every good office in return, will therefore repay their kindness. But alas — how often are they mistaken in their favorites and trustees, who, the more they have been given, are often the more incited to take everything, and to return destruction for generous usage. The common people generally think that great men have great minds, and scorn base actions. That judgment is so false that the basest and worst of all actions have been done by great men. Perhaps they have not picked private pockets, but they have done worse: they have disturbed, deceived, and pillaged the world. And he who is capable of the highest mischief is capable of the meanest. The man who plunders a country of a million would, in suitable circumstances, steal a silver spoon; and the conqueror who steals and pillages a kingdom would, in humbler fortune, rifle a traveler's bag, or rob an orchard.

Political jealousy in the people, therefore, is a necessary and praiseworthy passion. But in a chief magistrate, jealousy of his people is not so easy to justify — their only ambition is to preserve themselves, while it is natural for power to keep striving to enlarge itself, and to encroach on those who have none. The most praiseworthy jealousy in a magistrate is to be jealous for his people: that shows he loves them, and has used them well. To be jealous of them would show that he has evil designs against them, and has used them ill. The people's jealousy tends to preserve liberty; the prince's, to destroy it. Venice is a glorious example of the former, and so is England; and all the nations that have lost their liberty are melancholy proofs of the latter.

Power is naturally active, vigilant, and distrustful. These qualities push it toward every means and expedient of fortifying itself, toward destroying all opposition and even all seeds of opposition, and they keep it restless as long as anything stands in its way. Power would do what it pleases, and suffer no check. And because liberty chastises power and cuts it short, power would extinguish liberty; so liberty has every cause to be exceedingly jealous, and always on her guard. Power holds many advantages over her: it generally has numerous guards, many creatures, and much treasure — besides more craft and experience, and less honesty and innocence. And whereas power can subsist, and for the most part does, where liberty is not, liberty cannot subsist without power; so that she has the enemy, as it were, always at her gates.

Some have said that magistrates, being accountable to none but God, ought to know no other restraint. But this reasoning is as frivolous as it is wicked. No good man cares how many punishments and penalties stand in the way of an offense he never intends to commit: a man who does not mean to commit murder is not sorry that murder is punished with death. And as for wicked men, their being accountable to a God whom they do not fear is no security to us against their folly and malice; and to say that we ought to have no security against them is to insult common sense, and to give the lie to the first law of nature — the law of self-preservation. Human reason says that no obedience and no regard are due to rulers who govern by no rule but their appetite. Such men are not rulers; they are outlaws, who, being at defiance with God and man, are protected by no law of God or of reason. By what precept, moral or divine, are we forbidden to kill a wolf, or burn an infected ship? Is it unlawful to prevent wickedness and misery, and to resist the authors of them? Are crimes sanctified by their size? Is the man who robs a country and murders ten thousand less a criminal than the man who steals single guineas and takes single lives? Is there any sin in preventing, restraining, or resisting the greatest sin that can be committed — the oppression and destruction of mankind wholesale? Surely there never were such open, such shameless, such self-serving impostors as the advocates for lawless power! It is a damnable sin to oppress them; yet it is a damnable sin to oppose them when they oppress others, or grow rich by the oppression! Let them be hurt ever so little themselves — let them merely imagine they have been hurt — and they are the loudest of all men in their complaints, and the most outrageous in their behavior. But when others are plundered, oppressed, and butchered, complaints become sedition, and seeking redress becomes damnation. Is this not to be the authors of all wickedness and all falsehood?

To conclude: power without control belongs to God alone, and no man ought to be trusted with what no man is equal to. In truth, there are so many passions, so many inconsistencies, and so much selfishness in human nature that we can scarcely be too much on our guard against each other. The only security we can have that men will be honest is to make it their interest to be honest; and the best defense we can have against their being knaves is to make it terrible to them to be knaves. And since there are many men who are wicked in some stations who would be innocent in others, the best way is to make wickedness unsafe in any station.

I am, etc.
