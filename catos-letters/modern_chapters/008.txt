Letter 59: Liberty Proved to Be the Unalienable Right of All Mankind

Sir,

I intend to entertain my readers with a series of essays on liberty in some of my coming letters; and as a preface to that project, I will try to prove in this one that liberty is the unalienable right of all mankind.

All governments, under whatever form they are administered, ought to be administered for the good of the society; when they are administered otherwise, they cease to be government and become usurpation. Since this is the purpose of all government, even the most despotic has this limit on its authority. In this respect, the only difference between the most absolute prince and a limited magistrate is this: in free governments, the checks and restraints are appointed and spelled out in the constitution itself; in despotic governments, the people submit themselves to the prudence and discretion of the prince alone. But even there, a tacit condition is attached to his power: he must act by the unwritten laws of discretion and prudence, and use his power for the sole interest of the people — the people who gave it to him, or who allow him to enjoy it, and who do so only for their own sakes.

Even in the freest governments, single men are often trusted with discretionary power; but they must answer for that discretion to those who trust them. Generals of armies and admirals of fleets often carry unlimited commissions — and are they not answerable for executing those commissions prudently? The Council of Ten in Venice holds absolute power over the liberty and life of every man in that state. But suppose they used that power to slaughter, abolish, or enslave the senate, and to set themselves up in its place, as the Decemviri — Rome's ten commissioners turned tyrants — once did. Would it not be lawful for those who gave them that authority for other ends to put those ten unlimited traitors to death, any way they could? The crown of England has generally been trusted with the sole disposal of the money voted for the civil list, and often with the spending of great sums raised for other public uses. Yet if the lord treasurer had applied that money to the dishonor of the king and the ruin of the people — even on the private direction of the crown itself — will any man say that he should not have paid for his crime with his head and his estate?

I have said all this to show that no government can be absolute in the sense — or rather the nonsense — of our modern dogmatizers, and indeed in the sense too commonly practiced. No barbarous conquest; no consent extorted from miserable people submitting to the chain to escape the sword; no repeated and inherited acts of cruelty, however dignified with the name of succession; no continuation of violence, however dressed up as prescription — none of these can alter, much less abolish, the fundamental principles of government itself. None of them can turn the means of preservation into the means of destruction, or make the condition of mankind infinitely more miserable than that of the beasts of the field — and all through the sole privilege of that reason which sets men above the brutes.

Force can give no title but to revenge, and to the use of force again. Nor could it ever enter the heart of any man to give another power over him for any end but to have that power exercised for his own advantage. If there are men mad or foolish enough to claim they have done otherwise, they ought to be treated as idiots or lunatics, and the reason for their conduct traced to their folly and frenzy.

All men are born free. Liberty is a gift they receive from God himself; and they cannot alienate it by consent, though they may possibly forfeit it by crimes. No man has power over his own life, or the right to dispose of his own religion; and so he cannot transfer the power over either to anyone else. Much less can he give away the lives and liberties, the religion or the earned property, of his posterity — who will be born as free as he himself was born, and can never be bound by his wicked and ridiculous bargain.

The right of the magistrate arises only from the right of private men to defend themselves, to repel injuries, and to punish those who commit them. That right is conveyed by the society to its public representative, and he can exercise it no further than the benefit and security of that society requires. When he exceeds his commission, his acts are as void as those of any private officer usurping an unlawful authority; and he is answerable, like every other man, for the wrong that he does. A power to do good can never become a warrant for doing evil.

But here arises a grand question, one that has perplexed and puzzled the greatest part of mankind — though I think the answer to it is easy and obvious. The question is this: who shall judge whether the magistrate acts justly and keeps to his trust? To this it is fairly answered that if those who complain of him are to judge him, then there is a settled authority above the chief magistrate, and that authority must itself be the chief magistrate — which contradicts the premise; and the same question and the same difficulty will simply recur with this new magistracy. All this I grant is absurd. But I insist that it is at least as absurd to say that the person accused is to be the final judge of his own actions, when it is certain that he will always decide in his own favor — and thus the whole race of mankind will be left helpless under the heaviest injustice, oppression, and misery that can afflict human nature.

But if neither magistrates, nor the people who complain of magistrates and are wronged by them, have a right to decide finally, the one for the other; and if there is no common established power to which both are subject; then every man with a stake in the outcome must act by the light and dictates of his own conscience, and inform it as well as he can. Where no judge exists, and none can be appointed, every man must be his own. That is: when there is no settled judge upon earth, we must have recourse to heaven, and obey the will of heaven by declaring ourselves on the side we think the juster.

If the senate and people of Rome had quarreled beyond reconciliation, there could have been no common judge in the world between them, and consequently no remedy but the last one. Their government consisted in the union of the nobles and the people; when those two differed, no man could decide between them; and so every man must have been at liberty to provide for his own security, and for the general good, in the best manner he was able. With the common judge gone, every man became his own. The government, unable to act, suffered a political death. The constitution was dissolved; and there being no government in existence, the people were back in the state of nature.

The same must be true where two absolute princes governing one country come to quarrel — as the partnered Caesars sometimes did, especially toward the end of the Roman empire — or where a sovereign council governs a country and its votes split exactly down the middle. In such a circumstance every man must take the side he thinks best for the public good, or choose whatever measures he judges proper for his own security. For if I owe my allegiance to two princes agreeing, or to the majority of a council, then when there is no longer any union between those princes, and no longer any majority in that council, no submission can be due to a thing that does not exist; and the laws of nature and self-preservation must take over where there are no other laws.

The case is still the same when there is a dispute about the titles of absolute princes who govern without the estates — the representative assemblies — of a country, and never call them. Here too every man must judge for himself which party he will take and which of the titles he will adhere to; and the same private judgment must guide him whenever the question arises whether the prince is an idiot or a lunatic, and consequently whether he is capable or incapable of governing. Where there are no estates, there can be no other way of judging: the capacity of the prince must be judged by the judgment of private men, and his fate determined by it. Lunacy and idiocy are, I think, admitted by everyone to be certain disqualifications for government — as much so as if the prince were deaf, blind, and dumb, or even dead. A man who can neither execute an office nor appoint a deputy is not fit to hold one.

Now I would dearly like to know why private men may not use their judgment just as well in a matter that concerns them far more — I mean a tyrannical government, whose sad effects and sorrowful proofs they feel every hour of the day — when they have nowhere near equal means of reaching certainty about their governor's natural incapacity. The persons of great princes are known to only a few of their subjects, and their abilities to far fewer; and several princes have, through the management of their wives, or their ministers, or their murderers, gone on reigning a good while after they were dead. In truth, I think it is as much the business and the right of the people to judge whether their prince is good or bad, whether he is a father or an enemy, as to judge whether he is dead or alive. Unless we are to say — as many equally wise things have been said — that they may judge whether he can govern them, but not whether he does; that it is their duty to put the administration into wiser hands if he is a harmless fool, but impious to do it if he is only a destructive tyrant; that lack of speech is a disqualification, but lack of humanity none at all.

That subjects were not to judge of their governors — or rather, judge for themselves in the business of government, which of all human things concerns them most — was an absurdity that never entered the imagination of the wise and honest ancients. Following as their guide that everlasting reason which is the best and only guide in human affairs, they carried liberty, and human happiness — the legitimate offspring and work of liberty — to the highest pitch they were capable of reaching. No: that absurdity, along with many others as monstrous and mischievous, was reserved for the discovery of a few wretched, dreaming Muslim and Christian monks, who, ignorant of all things, were made — or made themselves — the directors of all things; who bewitched the world with holy lies and unaccountable ravings dressed up in barbarous words and uncouth phrases; who bent all their fairy force against common sense and common liberty and truth; and who founded a pernicious, absurd, and visionary empire upon their ruins. Systems without sense, propositions without truth, religion without reason, a rampant church without charity, severity without justice, and government without liberty or mercy — these were the blessed handiworks of these religious madmen and godly pedants, who by pretending to know the other world cheated and confounded this one. Their enmity to common sense, and their want of it, became their warrant for governing the sense of all mankind. By lying, they were thought the champions of truth; and by their fooleries, impieties, and cruelty, they were esteemed the favorites and confidants of the God of wisdom, mercy, and peace.

These were the men who, having demolished all sense and human judgment, first made it a principle that people were not to judge of their governor and government, nor meddle with it, nor preserve themselves from public destroyers falsely calling themselves governors. Yet these same men, who set up as the supporters and defenders of government — without the common honesty of distinguishing good government from bad, or protection from murder and plunder — were all the while themselves the constant and open troublers of every government they could not direct and command. Every government, however excellent, that did not make their reveries its own rules and make them alone its special care, has been honored with their professed hatred; while tyrants and public butchers who flattered them have been deified. This was the poor state of Christendom before the Reformation; and I wish I could say of no parts of it since.

This barbarous anarchy in reasoning and politics has made it necessary to prove propositions that the light of nature had already demonstrated. And as the apostles were forced to prove to the misled gentiles that idols made with hands were no gods, so I am put to proving that the people have a right to judge whether their governors were made for them, or they for their governors; whether their governors have the necessary and natural qualifications; whether they have any governors at all; and whether, when they have none, every man must not be his own. I therefore return to instances and illustrations drawn from facts that cannot be denied — though propositions as true as facts may be denied, especially by those who are lacking in modesty or discernment.

In Poland, we are told, the constitution requires that in their diets the consent of every single member present must be had before a resolution can take effect. And so, to prevent the cutting of throats, they have no remedy but to cut one another's throats: they must pull out their sabres and force the refusing members — who are always the minority — to submit. And among us in England, where a jury cannot agree, there can be no verdict; so the jurors must fast until they do agree, or until one of them is dead — and then the jury is dissolved.

This, from the nature of things themselves, must always be the case in disputes between dominion and property. Where the interest of the governors and the interest of the governed clash, there can be no settled judge between them. To appeal to a foreign power is to give up the sovereignty; for either side to submit is to give up the question. And therefore, if they do not settle the dispute amicably between themselves, heaven alone must settle it. In such a case, we must go back to the first principles of government itself. Government is a departure from the state of nature — a union of many families forming themselves into a political machine for mutual protection and defense. It is evident that this relation can last no longer than the machine itself subsists and can act; and when it cannot, the individuals must return to their former state again. No constitution can provide against what will happen when that constitution is dissolved. Government is only an appointment of one or more persons to do certain things for the good and benefit of the society; and if the persons so trusted will not act at all, or act contrary to their trust, their power must of course return to those who gave it.

Suppose, for example, that the Grand Monarch — Louis XIV of France — had bought a neighboring kingdom, and all the lands in it, from the courtiers and the majority of the people's deputies, with the church lands thrown into the bargain by consent of their convocation or synod or whatever that assembly was called. Would the people and clergy have thought themselves obliged to make good this bargain, if they could have helped it? I dare say neither would. On the contrary, the people would have had the blessing of these reverend patriots in telling their representatives, in round terms, that they were chosen to act for the interest of those who sent them, and not for their own; that their power was given them to protect and defend their country, not to sell and enslave it.

This supposition, wild as it seems, is not absolutely and universally impossible. King John actually sold the kingdom of England to the Pope. And there are people in every nation ready to sell their country at home; and such people can never have any principles to keep them from selling it abroad.

It is foolish to say that this doctrine can be mischievous to society — at least in any proportion to the wild ruin and fatal calamities that must befall, and do befall, the world where the contrary doctrine is maintained. For all bodies of men who live on their own substance, or on the profits of their trade and industry, find their advantage so much in ease and peace, and have such justly terrible apprehensions of civil disorders, which destroy everything they enjoy, that they always bear a thousand injuries before they return one, and stand under their burdens as long as they can bear them — as I have observed in another letter.

Consider the force of education, and the reverence which people are taught, and have always been used, to pay to princes. Consider the perpetual harangues of flatterers; the gaudy pageantry and outward show of power, its gilded ensigns always glittering in the people's eyes. Consider that the execution of the laws rests in the sole power of the prince; that all the regular magistrates, the pompous guards and standing troops, the fortified towns, the artillery, and all the magazines of war are at his disposal — besides large revenues, and multitudes of followers and dependents to support and abet everything he does. Obedience to authority is so well secured by all this that it is wild to imagine that any number of men formidable enough to disturb a settled state could unite together and hope to overturn it — until the public grievances are so enormous, the oppression so great, and the disaffection so universal, that no question can remain whether their calamities are real or imaginary, and whether the magistrate has protected his people or tried to destroy them.

This was the case with Richard II, Edward II, and James II; and it will ever be the case under the same circumstances. No society of men will groan under oppressions any longer than they know how to throw them off — whatever unnatural whimsies and fairy notions idle, sedentary babblers may utter from colleges and cloisters, teaching others, for vile self-serving ends, doctrines which they themselves are famous for not practicing.

Upon this principle — the people judging for themselves and resisting lawless force — stands our late happy Revolution, and with it the just and rightful title of our most excellent sovereign King George to the scepter of these realms: a scepter which he has swayed, and I do not doubt will ever sway, to his own honor, and to the honor, protection, and prosperity of us his people.

I am, etc.
