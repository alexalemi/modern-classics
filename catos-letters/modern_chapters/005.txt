Letter 38: The Right of the People to Judge of Government

Sir,

The world has been marched, age after age, through a long maze of errors — led there by men who made their living from the deceiving. So anyone who wants to teach humanity anything must start by clearing away the lies. If people everywhere were honestly told the truth and restored to their senses, there would not remain one nation of bigots or slaves under the sun. A happy outcome — always to be wished for, never to be expected.

In most of the world there is neither light nor liberty; and even in the best parts of it, both get faint encouragement and cold support, because in every place there are many people whose interests enlist them in a permanent conspiracy against both. Light and liberty are the two greatest civil blessings we have. Their interests are inseparable; each is the other's support; and whoever would destroy one must destroy both. That is why, everywhere you look, tyranny and fraud go together, ignorance and slavery go together, and oppressors and deceivers work hand in hand, forever paying court to each other. Wherever truth is dangerous, liberty is precarious.

Of all the sciences in the world, the science of government concerns us most, is the easiest to learn — and is the least understood. Most of the men who run governments would have the rest of us believe there is some deep difficulty and mystery in it, far above ordinary understanding. That claim is plain craft and imposture. Every plowman knows a good government from a bad one by its effects: he knows whether the fruits of his labor are his own, and whether he enjoys them in peace and security. If he does not know the principles of government, it is only for lack of thinking and inquiry, because those principles lie open to common sense. But people are generally taught not to think about them at all — or to think about them wrongly.

What is government but a trust, committed by all or most of the people to one or a few, who are to look after the affairs of everyone so that each person can look after his own with greater security? A great and honorable trust — but too seldom honorably executed. The men who hold it too often care more about increasing their power than about making it useful; more about being feared than about doing good. It is therefore a trust that ought to be bound with many strong restraints, because power makes men reckless, insolent to others, and in love with themselves. Every serious violation of this trust ought to meet with punishment in proportion; and even the smallest violation ought to meet with some, because indulging the least faults of officials can be cruelty to a whole people.

Honesty, diligence, and plain sense are the only talents needed to execute this trust, and the public good is its only purpose. As for refinements and finesse — those are usually just the false glitter of wisdom and talent, and more often tricks to hide guilt and emptiness; and they are generally mean and dishonest. They are the arts of political operators who play their own game under public cover and live on cheap shifts and expedients: starved politicians who scrape along hand to mouth, day to day, chasing the small aims of ambition, greed, revenge, and the other personal passions — too ashamed to admit them, too small-souled to give them up. Petty, wicked statesmen, who make a private market of the public, and deceive it in order to sell it.

These are the shabby parts that great and good governors scorn to play, and could not play if they tried. Their designs, like their offices, are purely public: open and undisguised. They do not treat their people as prey. They do not lie in ambush for their own citizens, or dread them, or handle them and spring on them like enemies — which is exactly what all bad officials do. Bad officials are not governors at all; they are jailers and sponges. They chain the people and squeeze them, and then take great offense if the people so much as murmur — which is far less than a people so abused ought to do. There have been times and countries in which "public minister" and "public enemy" were names for the same men. What a dismal thought, that a nation's most terrible and destructive foes should be its own officials! Yet in every enslaved country — which is almost every country — that is exactly the people's miserable condition.

Honesty and plainness always travel together; and the makers and multipliers of political mysteries deserve to be sharply suspected of dark designs. Cincinnatus, the Roman farmer-general, was taken from his plow to save and defend the Roman state — an office he executed honestly and successfully, without a statesman's posturing or a statesman's profits. Nor did he cling stubbornly to power afterward to build a faction, raise a fortune, and settle himself at the top. He came into office with universal consent, and he resigned it with universal applause.

Government, it seems, had not yet become a trade in those days — at least not a profitable one. Honest Cincinnatus was only a farmer. And it would have been a happy thing for the Romans if, once they were enslaved, they could have taken the administration out of the hands of the emperors and their refined politicians and handed it to such farmers — or to any farmers. It is certain that many of their imperial governors acted more ridiculously than a board of plowmen would have done, and more barbarously than a club of butchers could have done.

But some have said: it is not the business of private men to meddle with government. A bold, false, and dishonest saying. Whoever repeats it either does not know what he is saying, or does not care, or is slavishly mouthing the opinions of others. It is a piece of cant now almost forgotten in England — a slogan that never caught on except when liberty and the constitution were under attack, and never can catch on except on the same occasion.

It is a vexation to have to answer nonsense and refute absurdities. But since the great design of this paper is, and has always been, to maintain and explain the glorious principles of liberty, and to expose the arts of those who would darken or destroy them, I will show here, in particular, the wickedness and the stupidity of that saying — a saying fit for no mouth but a tyrant's or a slave's, and one that no man with an honest and free soul can hear without horror and indignation. It is, in short, a saying that ought to make the man who utters it forever unfit for office or trust in a free country. It exposes the malignity of his heart and the baseness of his nature, and it pronounces a doom upon our constitution. A crime — or rather a bundle of crimes — for which lasting infamy ought to be only part of the punishment.

Now to the falsehood of the thing itself. Public truths should never be kept secret; those who hide them are guilty of a contradiction in terms. Every man ought to know what it concerns all men to know. And nothing on earth is more universal in its nature than government. Every private man on earth has a stake in it, because his virtue, his property, and the safety of his person are bound up in it — nearly and immediately bound up in it. Where those things are best preserved and advanced, the government is best administered; and where they are not, the government is impotent, wicked, or unlucky. And as the government goes, so go the people; for there is always and everywhere a certain sympathy between the nature of a government and the nature of its people. This holds in every case. Public men set the pattern for private ones, and the virtues and vices of the governors quickly become the virtues and vices of the governed.

As the old Latin line puts it: the whole world arranges itself after the example of its king.

Nor is example the only mechanism. Bad governments, living by vice and plunder, are jealous of private virtue and enemies of private property. As Tacitus said of such a reign: wealth was treated as a crime, and virtue brought certain destruction. Such governments must be wicked and destructive to be what they are; and they are never secure while anything good or valuable is secure. That is why draining, harassing, and debauching their subjects are the standing maxims of their politics — their favorite arts of ruling. In that wretched situation the people, to be safe, must be poor and degraded. There will be little industry where property is precarious, and small honesty where virtue is dangerous.

Extravagance or frugality, and the other virtues and vices that touch the public, will be practiced in the city if they are practiced at court, and in the country if they are practiced in the city. Even Nero — that royal monster in a man's shape — was adored by the common crowd at Rome as much as he was flattered by the great; and little and great alike admired his manners, or pretended to admire them, and many imitated them. Tacitus tells us that those sorts of people mourned him for a long time, and rejoiced when the succession fell to a man who resembled him — the dissolute Otho.

Good government, by contrast, produces great virtue, much happiness, and many people. Greece and Italy, while they remained free, were each so thickly populated that they seemed one continuous city; and for virtue, knowledge, and great men, they set the standard for the world. Ever since, whatever age and country could come nearest them has been counted the happiest. Their government — their free government — was the root of all these advantages, all this happiness and renown. In those great and fortunate states the people were the principals in the government: laws were made by their judgment and authority, and by their voice and command magistrates were created and condemned. The city of Rome could conquer the world; and the great Persian monarch, the greatest ruler then on earth, could not stand before the face of one Greek city.

And what are Greece and Italy now? Rome holds a herd of pampered monks and a few starving lay inhabitants. The Campagna of Rome, the finest stretch of ground in Europe, is a desert. As for the modern Greeks, they are a few abject, contemptible slaves, held down in ignorance, chains, and squalor by the Turkish monarch, who keeps a great part of the globe intensely miserable so that he may seem great without being great.

Such is the difference between one government and another; and of such enormous consequence to a people is the nature and administration of its government. To say that private men have nothing to do with government is to say that private men have nothing to do with their own happiness and misery.

What is the public, after all, but the collective body of private men, just as every private man is a member of the public? And as the whole ought to be concerned for the preservation of every private individual, so it is the duty of every individual to be concerned for the whole — in which he himself is included.

One man, or a few men, have often invoked "the public" while meaning themselves, and pursued their own personal interest in matters essential to the public's well-being. But the whole people, by consulting their own interest, consult the public's; they act for the public by acting for themselves. That is precisely the spirit of our constitution, in which the whole nation is represented; and our records show instances in which the House of Commons declined to take up an important question until they had gone home to the country and consulted their principals — the people. So far were they from thinking that private men had no right to meddle with government. In truth, our whole worldly happiness and misery — setting aside accidents and disease — comes from the good order or the mismanagement of government. So the man who says that private men have no concern with government is wisely and modestly telling us that men have no concern in the thing that concerns them most. He is saying that people ought not to care whether they are naked or clothed, fed or starved, deceived or instructed, protected or destroyed. What nonsense — and what servility — in a free and wise nation!

For my own part, having thought a good deal about these matters, I am of the opinion that a whole nation is at least as likely to stay attached to its own interest as one man or a few men are — men who can be detached from the nation's interest by any number of means. It is certain that one man, or several men, can be bribed into an interest opposite to the public's. But it is just as certain that a whole country can never find an equivalent for itself, and consequently a whole country can never be bribed. It is the eternal interest of every nation that its government should be good; but the men who run it frequently reason the other way, and find their private account in plunder and oppression; and while the public voice is pretended — declared by one man or a few, for vile and private ends — the public itself knows nothing of what is done until it feels the terrible effects.

By the Bill of Rights and the Act of Settlement, at the Revolution, a right is asserted to the people: the right of applying to the King and to Parliament, by petition and address, for the redress of public grievances and mismanagements — when such there are, and of that the people are left to judge. Here, principally, lies the difference between free countries and enslaved ones: in the free, the magistrates must consult the voice and interest of the people; in the enslaved, the private will, interest, and pleasure of the governors are the sole ends and motives of their administration.

Such is the difference between England and Turkey — a difference that the men who say private citizens have no right to concern themselves with government would flatly abolish. They would convert magistrates into pashas, and bring the spirit of popery into politics. The late Revolution stands upon the very opposite maxim; and that any man dares contradict that maxim since the Revolution would be astonishing, did we not know that in every country there are hirelings who would betray it for a sop.

I am, etc.

G
