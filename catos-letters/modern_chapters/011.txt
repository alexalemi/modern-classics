Letter 62: The Nature and Extent of Liberty

Sir,

In a recent paper I showed what separates free governments from arbitrary ones in their frame and constitution. In this letter and the next, I will show how they differ in spirit and in effects. But first I need to establish what liberty itself actually is.

By liberty, I mean the power every man has over his own actions, and his right to enjoy the fruits of his own labor, skill, and industry — so far as he does not harm society or any member of it, either by taking what belongs to another or by blocking another man from enjoying what he himself enjoys. The fruits of a man's honest work are the just rewards of that work, secured to him by natural and eternal equity, and so is his title to use them however he thinks best. Within those limits, every man is the sole lord and judge of his own private actions and his own property. That is a standing no man alive can strip from him — except by usurpation, or by his own consent.

Entering into political society is so far from being a surrender of a man's natural rights that preserving those rights was the only reason men ever did it. Mutual protection and mutual help are the only reasonable purposes of any reasonable society. To make that protection real, the office of the magistrate was created, with power to defend the innocent from violence and to punish those who commit it — and there is no other justification for magistracy anywhere in the world. Toward that good end, the magistrate is entrusted with directing the united force of the community, and with collecting from every man the share of his property needed to preserve the whole and to defend every man and his property against injuries foreign and domestic. Those are the boundaries of the magistrate's power, and he abandons his office the moment he crosses them. By the laws of society, he is more limited and restrained than any man in it. Private citizens are absolutely free in every action that concerns only themselves; but every action of his, as a public person, is done for the sake of society, and must refer to society, and answer society's ends.

It is a mistaken idea of government that only the interest of the majority needs consulting. In society, every man has a right to every man's help in enjoying and defending his own private property. Otherwise the greater number could simply sell the smaller number and divide their estates among themselves — and then, instead of a society where all peaceable men are protected, you would have a conspiracy of the many against the minority. By the same logic one man might wantonly dispose of everything and everyone, and violence would be sanctified by mere power.

It is just as foolish to say that government has any business meddling with the private thoughts and actions of men, so long as they injure neither society nor any of its members. Every man is, by nature and by reason, the judge and manager of his own domestic affairs; and by the rules of religion and equity, every man must carry his own conscience. So the magistrate has no right to direct men's private behavior; and neither the magistrate nor anybody else has any power whatsoever to shape people's speculations — any more than their dreams. Government exists to protect men from injuring one another, not to direct them in their own affairs, which concern no one but themselves. Plainly, then, men's thoughts and domestic concerns are entirely outside its jurisdiction. In truth, men's thoughts are not even subject to their own jurisdiction.

Those who genuinely cannot take care of themselves must, of course, be taken care of by others. But while men have their five senses, I cannot see what the magistrate has to do with actions that cannot affect society; and where he meddles with such actions, he meddles either impertinently or tyrannically. Must the magistrate tie up every man's legs because some men fall into ditches? Must he put out men's eyes because eyes sometimes look at lying vanities? Would it suit the wisdom and care of our governors to establish an official escort service to keep people, by proper confinement, from throwing themselves into wells or over cliffs? Or to endow a national brotherhood of physicians and surgeons to manage their subjects' health without being consulted — free to vomit, bleed, purge, and scarify them at pleasure, willing or not, exactly as these established judges of health think fit? If that were the arrangement, what an uproar we would soon see over the established potions and lancets! Every man, woman, and child, however healthy, must be a patient — or else. The best diet and the best medicine would instantly become poison from any other hand; and their pills alone, however ridiculous, useless, or foul, would come with a blessing attached.

Let people alone, and they will take care of themselves, and do it best. And if they do not, a sufficient punishment follows their neglect on its own, without the magistrate's interference and penalties. It is plain that this busy care, this officious intrusion into the personal affairs, private actions, thoughts, and imaginations of men, has more craft in it than kindness. It is only a device to mislead people and pick their pockets, under the false pretense of the public good and their own. To quarrel with a man over his opinions, his moods, or the fashion of his clothes is an offense taken, not given. What is it to a magistrate how I wash my hands or cut my corns; what fashions or colors I wear; what notions I entertain, what gestures I use, what words I pronounce — when they please me, and do him and my neighbor no harm? He might as well determine the color of my hair and regulate my shape and features.

True and impartial liberty, then, is the right of every man to follow the natural, reasonable, and religious dictates of his own mind; to think what he will, and act as he thinks, provided he does not act to the injury of another; to spend his own money himself, and lay out the product of his labor his own way; and to work for his own pleasure and profit — not for the idle, who would live and riot by plundering and oppressing him and everyone like him.

So civil government is only a partial restraint, placed by the laws of agreement and society upon natural and absolute liberty, which might otherwise run wild. Tyranny is an unlimited restraint placed upon natural liberty by the will of one man or a few. Magistracy, among a free people, is the exercise of power for the sake of the people; tyrants abuse the people for the sake of power. Free government is the protection of the people in their liberties by fixed rules; tyranny is a brutish scramble for unlimited liberty for one man or a few — men who would rob everyone else of liberty, and act by no rule but lawless appetite.

So much for an idea of civil liberty. Now a word or two to show how much liberty is the delight and passion of mankind; and then I will show its advantages.

The love of liberty is an appetite planted so deep in the nature of all living creatures that even the appetite for self-preservation — allowed to be the strongest of all — seems to be contained inside it; for it is by means of liberty that creatures secure the means of preserving themselves, and of satisfying their desires in the way they themselves choose and like best. Many animals can never be tamed. They feel the bitterness of restraint even in the midst of the kindest treatment, and rather than bear it, they grieve and starve themselves to death; some beat out their brains against their prisons.

Where liberty is lost, life grows precarious — always miserable, often intolerable. Liberty is, to live upon one's own terms; slavery is, to live at the mere mercy of another. And a life of slavery is, to those who can bear it, a continual state of uncertainty and wretchedness: often the apprehension of violence, often the lingering dread of a violent death. Others, when no other remedy is left, reckon death itself a good one. And so, to many men — and to many other creatures besides men — the love of liberty runs beyond the love of life.

This passion for liberty, and the possession of it, is of such force and consequence that it seems to be the parent of all the virtues. That is why free countries seem to contain another species of mankind than what is found under tyrants. Small armies of Greeks and Romans held the vastest hosts of slaves in contempt; a million slaves have sometimes been beaten and conquered by a few thousand free men — so that the difference seemed greater between them than between men and sheep. Lucullus put it well. About to engage the army of the great King Tigranes, he was told by some of his officers how enormous that army was — somewhere between three and four hundred thousand men. "No matter," said that brave Roman, drawing up his own little army of fourteen thousand — but fourteen thousand Romans. "No matter; the lion never inquires into the number of the sheep." And these royal troops proved no better than sheep; the Romans had little to do but kill and pursue, and could barely manage even that for laughing — so much more were they entertained than challenged by the ridiculous panic and instant flight of these imperial slaves and royal cowards.

Men perpetually cowed and crushed by haughty, insolent governors are made base by the baseness of that kind of government; they become slaves by ruling over slaves; and they lack the spirit and the soul to meet, on the battlefield, free men — men who scorn oppressors, who are their own governors, or who at least measure and direct the power of the governors they have.

Habit alters nature, and becomes stronger than nature. Slavery, while it lasts, is a perpetual dread pressing on the spirit; it depresses the spirit and sinks natural courage; and want and fear, the constant companions of bondage, always produce despondency and baseness. Men in chains will never fight bravely — except to be free. And what else should they fight for? Every victory they win for a tyrant makes them poorer and fewer; and by feeding his pride it feeds his cruelty, along with their own misery and their own chains.

Those who, through terror and delusion — the frequent causes and the certain effects of servitude — come to think their governors greater than men, even as they find them worse than men, will be just as ready to think themselves less than men. And when the head and the heart are both gone, the hands will not count for much. Men who are used like beasts will tend to degenerate into beasts. But those who, by the freedom of their government and their upbringing, are taught and accustomed to think freely about men and things, discover by comparing one man with another that all men are naturally alike. They see that their governors have the same faces, constitutions, and shapes as themselves; that they are subject to the same sickness, the same accidents, and the same death as the lowest of their people; and that they possess the same passions and faculties of mind as their subjects — and no better. Such men scorn to degrade and prostrate themselves in worship of members of their own species, however loaded with titles and disguised by power. They regard their governors as their own creatures, the work of their own hands as far as they rise above other men, and merely the chief servants of the state — with no more right to do evil than any one of themselves, and holding no privilege and no superiority except that of serving them and the state. They know it is a contradiction in religion and reason for any man to have a right to do evil; that failing to resist a man's wickedness is encouraging it; and that they have the least reason of all to bear evil and oppression from their governors — the men most obliged, of all men, to do them good. So they detest slavery, and despise or pity slaves; and adoring liberty alone, as everyone who sees its beauty and feels its advantages always will, it is no wonder they are brave in its defense.

Indeed, liberty is the divine source of all human happiness. To possess, in security, the fruits of our industry is the most powerful and reasonable incentive to be industrious; and to be able to provide for our children, and leave them everything we have, is the best motive for having them. But where property is precarious, labor will languish. The privileges of thinking, saying, and doing what we please, and of growing as rich as we can — with no restriction beyond hurting neither the public nor one another — are the glorious privileges of liberty; and its effects are to live in freedom, plenty, and safety.

These are privileges that multiply mankind, and multiply the happiness of mankind. Countries are generally peopled in proportion as they are free, and are certainly happy in that proportion; and on the same tract of land that would maintain a hundred thousand free men in plenty, five thousand slaves would starve. In Italy — fertile Italy — men sometimes die of hunger among the sheaves, in the middle of a plentiful harvest; for what they sow and reap is none of their own, and their cruel, greedy governors, who live off the labor of their wretched vassals, will not allow them to eat the bread they have earned, or to sustain their lives with their own hands.

Liberty naturally draws new people to it, even as it increases the old stock; and men just as naturally run from slavery and wretchedness whenever they dare, wherever they can better themselves. That is why great cities that lose their liberty become deserts, while little towns grow into great cities by liberty — as I will fully prove before I am through with this argument. I will not deny that there are some great cities of slaves; but these are only imperial cities, the seats of great princes, who drain the wealth of a continent into their capital, the center of their treasure and their luxury. Babylon, Antioch, Seleucia, and Alexandria were great cities peopled by tyrants — but peopled partly by force, partly for the reason above, and partly by grants and indulgences. Their power, vast and boundless as it was, could not by itself people their cities. They were forced to soften authority with kindness; and having herded the inhabitants together by force, driving them captive like cattle, they could not keep them together without granting them many privileges — to persuade the first inhabitants to stay, and to invite more to come.

This was a confession by those tyrants that their own power was destructive and unjust: they could not build one great city and make it flourish without renouncing, in large measure, their power over it — which, by granting those privileges, is exactly what they did. The privileges were fixed laws, under which the trade and industry of the citizens were encouraged, and their lives and properties secured and protected, no longer subject to the law of mere will and pleasure. And so, while these free cities flourished under their own liberties and laws, the provinces were miserably harassed, pillaged, depopulated, and impoverished, and their inhabitants exhausted, starved, butchered, and carried away captive.

This shows that all civil happiness and prosperity is inseparable from liberty, and that tyranny cannot make men, or societies of men, happy without departing from its own nature and granting privileges inconsistent with tyranny. And here — among a thousand others — is an unanswerable argument against absolute power in a single man. There is no way in the world to give happiness to communities except by sheltering them under certain and express laws, revocable at no man's pleasure.

There is not, and cannot be, any security for a people who trust to the mere will of one man — a man who, while his will is law, could not protect them even if he wanted to. The crowd of sycophants and wicked counselors he will always and necessarily have around him will defeat every good intention he has: representing things falsely and persons maliciously; conjuring danger where there is none and urging necessity where none exists; filling their own coffers under color of filling his; raising money for themselves while pleading the public needs of the state; sacrificing particular men to their private revenge under the pretense of public security; dragging him and his people into dangerous, destructive wars for their own profit or fame; throwing public affairs into perpetual confusion to prevent any inquiry into their own conduct; and making him jealous of his people, and his people jealous of him, on purpose — the better to manage and mislead both sides.

By all these arts, and many more just as wicked, they will steadily lead him into cruel and oppressive measures — destructive to his people, scandalous and dangerous to himself, and entirely agreeable to their own spirit and designs. Thus they will commit every wickedness by their master's authority, against his inclinations, and grow rich on the people's poverty without his knowledge; and the royal authority will serve first as a warrant for oppression, and afterwards as protection from the punishment due for it. In short, the power of princes is often little more than a stalking-horse for the intrigues and ambition of their ministers.

But if such a prince's own disposition is evil, what must be the forlorn condition of his people, and what door of hope can remain for common protection? The best princes often have evil counselors; the bad will have no other. And in that case, what bounds can be set to their fury, and to the havoc they will make? The instruments and advisers of tyranny and plunder always thrive best, and come nearest their goals, when plunder and tyranny run highest: the more that is stripped from the people, the greater their share. We may therefore expect every evil to befall such a people, without expecting extravagantly. No happiness, no security — only certain misery, and a vile and precarious life: these are the blessed terms of such a government. A government of this kind necessarily introduces every evil, and by the same necessity neither must nor can redress any of them.

The nature of such a prince's education — bred up, as he always is, in perpetual flattery — makes him haughty and ignorant; and the nature of his government, which subsists by brutish severity and oppression, makes him cruel. He is inaccessible except through his ministers, whose study and interest is to keep him from knowing, or helping, the condition of his miserable people. Their master's knowledge of his own affairs would break in upon their scheme and their power. They are not likely to lay before him accounts of grievances they themselves have caused; and if the grievances are the effects of his own barbarity and his own commands, he will not hear them.

Even where absolute princes are not tyrants, their ministers will be tyrants. But in truth it is impossible for an arbitrary prince to be anything else, since oppression is absolutely necessary to his remaining arbitrary. Without giving his people liberty, he cannot make them happy; and by giving them liberty, he gives up his own power. So, to be and remain arbitrary, he is doomed to be a tyrant in his own defense. The oppression of the people; corruption, wicked counselors, and pernicious maxims in the court; and everywhere baseness, ignorance, and chains — these must support tyranny, or it cannot be supported. In such governments, therefore, there are inevitable grievances without possible redress, and misery without mitigation or remedy. Whatever is good for the people is bad for their governors; and what is good for the governors is destructive to the people.
