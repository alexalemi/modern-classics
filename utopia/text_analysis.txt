TEXT ANALYSIS AND TRANSLATION STRATEGY
Utopia — Thomas More, 1516 (Latin); Gilbert Burnet's English translation, 1684
(Gutenberg #2130, editorial introduction dropped)

================================================================================
1. WHAT THIS BOOK IS
================================================================================

The book that named a genre. More — lawyer, scholar, future Lord
Chancellor and martyr — frames a traveler's report of a perfect island
commonwealth inside a sly fictional apparatus: the narrator is "Thomas
More" himself, the traveler Raphael Hythloday ("speaker of nonsense" in
Greek) is vouched for with deadpan seriousness, and the island's names
are jokes (Utopia = "no-place"; its capital Amaurot = "dim city"; its
river Anider = "no-water").

Book I: a dialogue in a garden in Antwerp. Raphael's critique of
European statecraft — counselors who flatter kings, wars of conquest,
enclosure ("sheep devouring men"), the death penalty for theft, and the
root diagnosis: private property itself. Ends with More begging for the
description of Utopia.

Book II: the description — geography, towns, magistrates, working hours
(six!), communal meals, contempt for gold (chamber pots of it), no
private property, marriage customs (including premarital inspection),
euthanasia, slavery, just-war doctrine, religious toleration (with
limits), and the famous ambivalent closing: "many things in the
commonwealth of Utopia that I rather wish, than hope, to see followed in
our governments" — keep that closing sentence near-verbatim.

THE INTERPRETIVE TIGHTROPE: scholars still argue what More meant
seriously and what is satire. The translation must NOT resolve this —
preserve the deadpan, the jokes-in-names (gloss each Greek name's
meaning once, lightly, since More's readers got the joke and modern
readers deserve it), and the layered irony of "More"-the-character
disagreeing with Raphael.

================================================================================
2. STYLE
================================================================================

- Burnet's 1684 English: long balanced periods, semicolon chains —
  break into modern sentences but keep the courteous, reasonable,
  faintly amused register. More is urbane; never let him sound earnest
  or preachy.
- Book I is live dialogue — keep the conversational back-and-forth
  vivid, with speakers clearly tracked ("said I", "answered Raphael" →
  modern dialogue attribution).
- Book II is Raphael's monologue in quotation marks in the source —
  drop the enclosing quotation marks (note this once in running notes)
  and render it as his first-person report; keep his enthusiasm and the
  occasional too-good-to-be-true earnestness that IS the satire.
- Numbers and institutional details (54 cities, 6-hour workday, 30
  families per Syphogrant) are load-bearing — keep exact.

================================================================================
3. FORMAT CONVENTIONS
================================================================================

- Files: Book I in 3 parts; Book II split by the source's section
  headings (Of Their Towns, Of Their Magistrates, ...). Render section
  headings as chapter-style headings, modernized: "Their Towns, and
  Especially Amaurot", "Their Magistrates", "Their Trades and Way of
  Life", etc. First line of each file = its heading.
- Book I parts: heading "Book I: The Dialogue" + part marker; Book II
  opener (file 003): heading "Book II: The Island of Utopia".
- No invented subheadings within sections.

================================================================================
4. VOCABULARY MAP
================================================================================

  Hythloday                 -> Raphael Hythloday (gloss once: Greek for
                               "speaker of nonsense" — More's joke)
  Utopia                    -> Utopia (gloss once: "no-place")
  Amaurot                   -> Amaurot (gloss once: "dim city")
  Anider                    -> Anider (gloss once: "no-water")
  Syphogrant / Tranibor     -> keep, gloss once as Utopian magistrate
                               ranks (a Syphogrant oversees thirty
                               families; a Tranibor, ten Syphogrants)
  Prince (of a city)        -> governor (Burnet's "Prince" is a city's
                               elected head — never a monarch; gloss once)
  close-stools              -> chamber pots (the gold passage: "chamber
                               pots and close-stools of gold and silver"
                               → "chamber pots and toilet seats of gold
                               and silver" — keep the joke physical)
  Zapolets                  -> Zapolets (mercenaries; gloss once)
  bondmen / slaves          -> slaves (their slavery = penal servitude;
                               render precisely as the source has it)
  Cardinal Morton           -> Cardinal Morton (historical, Book I)
  the French king's council -> keep the satirical council scene intact

================================================================================
5. FAMOUS PASSAGES — NEARLY VERBATIM
================================================================================

- The sheep passage (Book I): Burnet has sheep that "devour men" —
  render with the bite intact: sheep, once so gentle, that now "devour
  men, and unpeople not only villages, but towns."
- The gold deflation (Book II): chamber pots of gold and silver; gold
  chains for slaves; criminals' gold earrings — the inversion must stay
  vivid and funny.
- The closing sentence: "...there are many things in the commonwealth
  of Utopia that I rather wish, than hope, to see followed in our
  governments." Near-verbatim; it is the book's final tightrope wobble.
- "Utopus, that conquered it" — the channel-digging origin story kept
  complete.

================================================================================
6. CONTENT NEEDING CAREFUL HANDLING
================================================================================

- PREMARITAL INSPECTION (Of Their Slaves and Marriages): the naked
  viewing of prospective spouses — translate completely and
  matter-of-factly; Raphael reports it as sensible policy, More's
  readers giggled; the deadpan IS the joke.
- EUTHANASIA AND SUICIDE: Utopian priests counsel the incurably ill
  toward voluntary death — render precisely as described, in the
  measured reportorial register; it is 16th-century speculative ethics.
- SLAVERY AND PENAL POLICY: penal servitude described approvingly by
  Raphael — translate faithfully as his report.
- RELIGIOUS TOLERATION AND ITS LIMITS: tolerance is broad but atheists
  are barred from office and dying-of-the-soul deniers despised —
  render the limits as precisely as the tolerance.
- WAR DOCTRINE (Of Their Military Discipline): assassination bounties,
  mercenary spending, colonization of "waste" land — faithful and
  complete; this is More holding a mirror to European practice.

================================================================================
7. VOICE TARGET — SAMPLE
================================================================================

Burnet:
  "your sheep, which are naturally mild, and easily kept in order, may
  be said now to devour men and unpeople, not only villages, but towns"

Modern target:
  "Your sheep. Normally so meek, so cheap to keep — they have now, I
  hear, become so greedy and so wild that they devour men themselves.
  They empty out not just villages but whole towns."

Register: 8th grade–high school. Urbane, amused, precise. Dialogue in
Book I crackles; Book II reads like the world's most fascinating
field report delivered by a man who believes every word of it.
