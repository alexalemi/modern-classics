Their Slaves and Their Marriages

The Utopians do not make slaves of prisoners of war, unless those prisoners were captured in a battle fought against Utopia itself. They do not enslave the children of slaves, and they do not buy people who were enslaved in other countries. Their slaves are of one kind only: people condemned to that condition for committing some crime — or, more commonly, foreigners whom Utopian merchants find under sentence of death in the countries where they trade. These they sometimes buy at a low price, and in some places they get them for nothing. Such slaves are kept at constant labor and are always in chains, with one distinction: native Utopians who fall into slavery are treated worse than the rest. They are considered the more hopeless cases — people who had every advantage of an excellent upbringing and still could not be kept from crime — and so they are judged to deserve harsher treatment.

There is one other class of slaves: poor laborers from neighboring countries who volunteer, of their own free will, to come and serve in Utopia. These are treated well — in almost every respect like citizens — except that a heavier load of work is placed on them, which is no hardship to people who are used to it. If any of them wish to return home, which seldom happens, they are not forced to stay, and they are not sent away empty-handed.

I have already told you how carefully they look after their sick, so that nothing is left undone that could contribute to their comfort or recovery. Those with fixed and incurable diseases they tend with every possible kindness, making their lives as comfortable as they can — visiting them often, taking great pains to help the time pass easily. But when a patient is gripped by a torturing, lingering pain, with no hope of either recovery or relief, the priests and magistrates come to him with a proposal. He is no longer able, they point out, to carry on the business of living. He has become a burden to himself and to everyone around him. He has, in effect, outlived himself. Why keep feeding such a rooted disease? Since living can mean nothing for him now but misery, he should choose to die instead — confident that if he frees himself from his torture, or allows others to free him, he will be happy after death. Since he gives up none of life's pleasures, only its pains, they consider such a choice not only reasonable but an act of religion and piety, for it follows the advice of the priests, who are the interpreters of God's will.

Those who are persuaded by these arguments either starve themselves voluntarily or take opium, and so die without pain. But no one is forced to end his life this way, and if a patient cannot be persuaded, they do not slacken in the least in their attendance and care of him. They believe that a voluntary death, chosen on such authority, is highly honorable. But if a man takes his own life without the approval of the priests and the Senate, they grant him none of the honors of a decent funeral. They throw his body into a ditch.

Their women do not marry before eighteen, their men not before twenty-two. Anyone caught in forbidden embraces before marriage is severely punished, and the right to marry at all is denied them unless they obtain a special pardon from the governor. Such a scandal also falls heavily on the master and mistress of the household where it happened, since they are presumed to have failed in their duty. The reason they punish this so harshly is their belief that unless people were strictly held back from every roving appetite, very few would ever commit to marriage — a state in which you stake the peace of your whole life on a single person, and must put up with all the inconveniences that come with the arrangement.

In choosing their wives they follow a custom that struck us as completely absurd and ridiculous, but which they observe without fail and consider perfectly wise. Before the marriage, a respectable older woman presents the bride — naked, whether she is a virgin or a widow — to the groom. Then a respectable older man presents the groom, naked, to the bride. We laughed at this and condemned it as indecent. But they, for their part, marveled at the folly of every other nation on earth. When a man buys a horse, they pointed out — a purchase of small value — he is so cautious that he inspects every part of the animal, stripping off the saddle and all the tack, in case some hidden sore lies underneath. Yet in choosing a wife, a decision on which the happiness or misery of the rest of his life depends, he proceeds entirely on trust, seeing only a hand's-breadth of her face while all the rest of her body stays covered — under which may lurk something contagious as well as repulsive. Not all men are wise enough to choose a woman for her good qualities alone, and even wise men consider the body no small addition to the mind. It is certain that clothes can hide a deformity bad enough to turn a man completely against his wife when it is too late to part with her. If such a thing is discovered after the wedding, a husband has no remedy but patience. So the Utopians think it only reasonable to take solid precautions against fraud of this damaging kind.

They had all the more reason to regulate this carefully, because they are the only people in those parts of the world who allow neither polygamy nor divorce — except in cases of adultery or insufferable perverseness. In those cases the Senate dissolves the marriage and grants the injured party leave to marry again; but the guilty party is publicly disgraced and never permitted a second marriage. No one is allowed to put away a wife against her will because of some calamity that has struck her body, for they consider it the height of cruelty and treachery to abandon a spouse exactly when she most needs her partner's tender care — above all in old age, which brings so many diseases with it and is a kind of disease in itself.

It does happen, though, that a married couple simply cannot get along, and then, by mutual consent, they separate and find other partners with whom they hope to live more happily. But even this is not done without the Senate's permission, and the Senate never grants a divorce until the senators and their wives have made a strict inquiry into the grounds for it. Even when they are satisfied that the reasons are good, they proceed slowly, for they believe that making it too easy to move on to a new marriage would badly shake the affection of married people everywhere.

Those who defile the marriage bed are punished severely. If both offenders are married, both marriages are dissolved; the injured parties may marry each other, or anyone else they please, while the adulterer and the adulteress are condemned to slavery. Yet if either of the injured spouses cannot shake off their love for the guilty one, they may continue to live with them in that condition — but they must follow them into the labor the slaves are sentenced to. And sometimes the repentance of the condemned, together with the unshaken devotion of the innocent and injured partner, has so moved the governor that he has lifted the sentence. But those who relapse after being once pardoned are punished with death.

For other crimes, their law sets no fixed penalties; the Senate weighs each case and fits the punishment to the circumstances. Husbands have authority to correct their wives, and parents to discipline their children, unless the offense is so serious that a public punishment is thought necessary to strike terror into others. For the most part, slavery is the punishment even for the worst crimes. They reason that slavery is just as terrible to the criminal as death would be, while keeping him alive in servitude is far more useful to the commonwealth than killing him — his labor benefits the public more than his death ever could, and the long sight of his misery is a more lasting warning to others than any execution. If slaves rebel and refuse to bear their yoke and do the work assigned to them, they are treated like wild beasts that neither prison nor chains can hold, and are finally put to death. But those who bear their punishment patiently — who are so worked on by the weight pressing on them that they show themselves more troubled by the crimes they committed than by the miseries they suffer — are not without hope. In the end the governor, by his own authority, or the people, by their intercession, may restore them to liberty, or at least greatly lighten their slavery.

A man who tempts a married woman to adultery is punished just as severely as one who commits it, for they hold that a deliberate intention to commit a crime is equal to the deed itself. The fact that the attempt failed does not make the man who failed any less guilty.

They take great pleasure in fools. While it is considered base and shameful to treat them badly, there is nothing wrong with being amused by their folly — and in their view, this actually works to the fools' advantage. For if people were so grim and humorless that they took no delight at all in a fool's ridiculous behavior and silly sayings — which is the only way a fool can recommend himself to anyone — fools could hardly expect to be so well provided for, or handled so tenderly, as they are.

If a man mocks another for being misshapen or imperfect in some part of his body, the disgrace falls not on the person mocked but on the mocker, who is held in contempt for ridiculing what no one can help. They consider it a sign of a lazy and sluggish nature not to take care of one's natural beauty — but it is equally a disgrace among them to use makeup. They all know from experience that no beauty recommends a wife to her husband so much as honest character and an obliging temper; a pretty face may catch a few men, but only those other excellences hold everyone.

Just as they frighten people away from crime with punishments, they draw them toward virtue with public honors. They put up statues in their marketplaces to the memory of men who have served their country well — both to preserve the memory of their deeds and to spur their descendants to follow the example.

Any man who campaigns for public office is guaranteed never to get it. They all live easily together, for none of their magistrates are arrogant or cruel to the people. They prefer to be called fathers, and because they actually behave like fathers, they deserve the name. The people pay them every mark of honor all the more freely because none is demanded. Even the governor has no special distinction — no robes, no crown. He is known only by a sheaf of grain carried before him, just as the High Priest is known by the wax candle carried ahead of him.

They have very few laws, and their constitution is such that they need few. They strongly condemn other nations, whose laws and legal commentaries swell into endless volumes. They think it outrageous to bind men to obey a body of law that is both so enormous and so obscure that no ordinary subject can read and understand it.

They have no lawyers at all. They consider lawyers a class of people whose profession is to disguise the facts and twist the laws. Far better, they think, for every man to plead his own case and trust it directly to the judge, the way people elsewhere trust it to an attorney. This cuts out endless delays and gets at the truth more reliably: each party lays out the merits of his case without the tricks lawyers love to whisper, and the judge weighs the whole matter himself, taking care to support plain, well-meaning people against the clever men who would otherwise run them down. In this way they escape the evils so glaring in every nation that staggers under a vast load of laws.

Every Utopian is skilled in their law — it is a very short course of study, and the plainest meaning the words can carry is always taken as the law's true sense. Their reasoning runs like this: laws are published for one purpose only, so that every man may know his duty. Therefore the plainest, most obvious reading is the one that should stand, since a subtler interpretation can be grasped by only a few, and would just make the laws useless to most of mankind — and especially to the people who need their guidance most. It comes to the same thing whether you make no law at all, or write one in terms so dark that only a quick mind and long study can dig out its meaning; for most people are too slow, and too busy with their trades, to have either the leisure or the capacity for that kind of inquiry.

Some of their neighbors — free peoples who long ago, with Utopian help, threw off the yoke of tyranny, and who admire the virtues they see in the Utopians — have taken to asking Utopia to send out magistrates to govern them, some replacing them every year, others every five years. When a magistrate's term ends, they escort him back to Utopia with great honor and esteem, and bring home another to govern in his place. In this, it seems to me, these nations have hit on an excellent recipe for their own happiness and safety. Since the good or ill condition of a nation depends so heavily on its magistrates, they could not have chosen better than men whom no advantage can sway. Wealth is useless to a Utopian governor, since he must soon go home where money buys nothing; and being a stranger in the country he rules, he is tangled in none of its feuds and factions. Wherever courts of justice are bent by greed or partiality, the result is the dissolution of justice itself — the chief sinew of society.

The Utopians call the nations that come asking for magistrates their Neighbors; those they have served in some more particular way they call Friends. But while every other nation is forever making alliances and breaking them, the Utopians make no treaties with any state at all. What use is a treaty, they ask? If the common ties of humanity will not bind men together, no faith in promises will. They are confirmed in this view by what they see among the nations around them, who are not exactly strict observers of leagues and treaties.

We all know, of course, how religiously treaties are kept in Europe — above all in the lands where the Christian faith is received, where they are sacred and inviolable! This is owing partly to the justice and goodness of the princes themselves, and partly to the reverence they pay to the popes, who, being the most scrupulous keepers of their own promises, command all other princes to keep theirs — and when gentler methods fail, compel them by the severity of pastoral censure. Nothing, after all, could be more indecent than for men who bear the very title of "the Faithful" to break faith in their treaties.

But in that new-found world — as far from us in manners and way of life as it is in distance — no one trusts a treaty, however much pomp and sacred ceremony attended the signing. On the contrary, the ceremony only speeds the breaking, for some loophole is always found in the wording. Indeed, the terms are deliberately drafted in language so ambiguous that no party can ever be bound tight enough; there is always some gap to slip through, and so they break both the treaty and their word. And here is the impudence of it: the very statesmen who pride themselves on suggesting these tricks to their princes would, with lofty scorn, denounce such craft — or to speak plainly, such fraud and cheating — if they caught private men using it in their bargains, and would readily declare that such men deserved to be hanged.

By this means justice has come to be regarded as a low-spirited, vulgar virtue, far beneath the dignity of royal greatness. Or rather, two kinds of justice have been set up. One is mean and creeps along the ground; it suits only the lower sort of people, and must be fenced in by many restraints so it never wanders past the bounds assigned to it. The other is the special virtue of princes — more majestic than the kind that fits the common crowd, and therefore far freer in its range, so that for princes the only measure of lawful and unlawful is pleasure and interest.

It is this behavior among the princes who live around Utopia — men who set so little store by their own word — that seems to have settled the Utopians against entering any confederacy. Perhaps they would change their minds if they lived among us. And yet, even if treaties were faithfully kept, they would still dislike the whole custom of making them. For the custom rests on a false assumption the world has adopted: that no natural tie unites one nation to another — as if a mountain or a river between them dissolved the partnership of nature — and that all peoples are born enemies, free to do their neighbors any harm not expressly forbidden by treaty. And on that assumption, even a signed treaty does not end the hostility or restrain the plundering, wherever clumsy drafting has failed to nail down an effective clause against it.

The Utopians judge otherwise. No man is to be counted an enemy who has done us no injury. The partnership of human nature stands in place of any treaty. And kindness and good nature bind men together more effectually, and with greater strength, than any formal agreement ever could — for the engagement of the heart is stronger than the bond and obligation of words.
