Book I: The Dialogue
(Part 1 of 3)

Henry VIII, the unconquered King of England — a prince graced with every virtue a great monarch could ask for — recently had a dispute of no small weight with Charles, the most serene Prince of Castile. So he sent me to Flanders as his ambassador to negotiate and settle the matter. I went as colleague and companion to that incomparable man Cuthbert Tonstal, whom the King had just made Master of the Rolls to everyone's applause. About him I'll say nothing — not because I worry a friend's testimony would be suspect, but because his learning and character are too great for me to do them justice, and too well known to need my advertising. Praising him would be, as the proverb says, showing the sun with a lantern.

The men the Prince appointed to negotiate with us met us at Bruges, as agreed — all of them distinguished. The Margrave of Bruges headed their delegation, but their wisest member, and the one who spoke for the rest, was George Temse, the Provost of Casselsee. Art and nature had teamed up to make him eloquent: he was deeply learned in the law, and his natural talent, sharpened by long practice in public business, made him very good at untangling difficult matters. After several meetings without an agreement, they went off to Brussels for a few days to learn their Prince's pleasure; and since our business allowed it, I went to Antwerp.

While I was there, of the many people who came to visit me, none was more welcome than Peter Giles. He was born in Antwerp, where he is a man of great honor and good standing — though less than he deserves, for I doubt you could find anywhere a young man more learned or better bred. He is as worthy as he is knowledgeable, courteous to everyone, especially warm toward his friends, and so full of openness and affection that there is hardly one friend in a thousand so complete in every respect. He is unusually modest; there is nothing artificial about him, and yet no one carries plain good sense more gracefully. His conversation was so pleasant, and so innocently cheerful, that his company went a long way toward easing my longing for home — for my country, my wife, and my children, whom four months of absence had made me miss very badly.

One day, as I was walking home from mass at St. Mary's — the chief church in Antwerp, and the busiest — I happened to see Peter talking with a stranger. The man looked past the prime of life: his face was tanned, his beard was long, and his cloak hung carelessly off his shoulder. From his look and his dress, I took him for a ship's captain.

The moment Peter spotted me, he came over and greeted me, and as I was returning the courtesy he drew me aside and pointed to the man he'd been talking with. "Do you see that man?" he said. "I was just about to bring him to you."

"He would have been very welcome," I said, "for your sake."

"And for his own, if you knew him," Peter replied. "There is no man alive who can tell you as much about unknown nations and countries as he can — and I know how hungry you are for that kind of thing."

"Then I guessed right," I said. "At first glance I took him for a sailor."

"Then you guessed very wrong," he said. "He has sailed, yes — but not as a sailor. As a traveler. Or really, as a philosopher. This is Raphael; his family name is Hythloday — Greek, as it happens, for 'speaker of nonsense.' He knows Latin well enough, but he is brilliant in Greek, which he studied much harder, because his real passion is philosophy — and in philosophy, he says, the Romans left us nothing worth having except some Seneca and Cicero. He's Portuguese by birth, and he was so eager to see the world that he divided his whole estate among his brothers and threw in his lot with Amerigo Vespucci. He shared in three of the four voyages that everyone is now reading about; only on the last one he didn't come home. Instead he begged Vespucci — practically forced him — for permission to be one of the twenty-four men left behind at the farthest point they reached on that final voyage to New Castile. Being left behind suited him perfectly. He has always cared more about traveling than about going home to be buried in his own country. He likes to say that the road to heaven is the same length from everywhere, and that a man with no grave still has the whole sky to cover him. Though that attitude would have cost him dearly if God hadn't been gracious to him: after he and five Castilian companions had traveled through many countries, by an astonishing stroke of luck he reached Ceylon, and from there Calicut, where he conveniently found some Portuguese ships — and so, against everyone's expectations, he made it back to his native country."

When Peter had finished, I thanked him for his kindness in going to such trouble to introduce me to a man whose conversation he knew I would enjoy. Then Raphael and I greeted each other warmly, and after the usual courtesies that strangers exchange on first meeting, we all went to my house, sat down in the garden on a bench of green turf, and fell into conversation.

Raphael told us that after Vespucci sailed away, he and the companions who had stayed behind in New Castile gradually won the affection of the local people by meeting with them often and treating them gently. Before long they were not only living among them in safety but talking with them like old friends, and they became such favorites of a certain prince — whose name and country I've forgotten — that he generously supplied them with everything they needed, including the means to travel: boats when they went by water, wagons when they went by land. He sent along a thoroughly reliable guide with instructions to introduce and recommend them to whatever other princes they wished to visit. And after many days' journey, they came to towns, and cities, and commonwealths that were both well governed and full of people.

Under the equator, he said, and for as far on either side of it as the sun ranges, lie vast deserts scorched by perpetual heat. The soil is withered, everything looks bleak, and the land is either empty or overrun with wild beasts and serpents — along with a few men no less wild and cruel than the beasts themselves. But the farther they traveled, the milder everything became: the air less burning, the soil greener, even the animals tamer. At last they reached nations, towns, and cities that traded not only among themselves and with their neighbors but by sea and land with very distant countries. From then on, they had the chance to see lands in every direction, because no ship set out on any voyage that didn't gladly take him and his companions aboard.

The first vessels they saw were flat-bottomed, with sails of reeds and wicker woven tightly together, though some were of leather. Farther on, they found ships with rounded keels and canvas sails — like our own ships in every way — and sailors who understood astronomy and navigation. He won enormous goodwill among them by teaching them the use of the magnetic needle, which until then they had never heard of. They had always sailed cautiously, and only in summer; now they trust the magnet completely and sail in every season — more confident, perhaps, than safe. There is real reason to fear that a discovery they expected to be such a blessing may, through their own recklessness, end up doing them great harm.

It would take too long to repeat everything he told us he had observed in each place, and it would carry us too far from our purpose. Whatever is worth telling about the wise and prudent institutions he found among civilized nations may perhaps be told another time, on a better occasion. We asked him question after question about all of it, and he answered very willingly. We made no inquiries about monsters, since nothing is less of a novelty: you can hear about ravening dogs and wolves and cruel man-eaters almost anywhere. What is genuinely hard to find is a state that is well and wisely governed.

Now, while he noted plenty that was wrong in those newly discovered countries, he also counted up quite a few things that could serve as models for correcting the mistakes of our own nations. Those, as I've promised, I will report some other time. For now I intend to relate only what he told us about the manners and laws of the Utopians — the people of the island of Utopia, a name that is Greek for "no-place." But first let me describe the conversation that led us around to that commonwealth.

Raphael had been talking with great judgment about the errors found both here and over there, and the wise institutions of both, and he discussed the customs and government of every nation he'd passed through as precisely as if he'd lived his whole life in each one. Peter, amazed, said, "Honestly, Raphael, I can't understand why you don't enter some king's service. There isn't a king alive who wouldn't be delighted to have you. With your learning, and your knowledge of people and places, you wouldn't just keep a king entertained — you'd be genuinely useful to him, with all the examples you could lay before him and the advice you could give. And in the process you'd advance your own interests and be a great help to all your friends."

"As for my friends," Raphael answered, "I don't lose much sleep over them. I've already done my duty by them. Most people cling to their possessions until they're old and sick, and even then they give them up grudgingly, only when they can't enjoy them anymore. I handed mine over to my relatives and friends while I was still healthy, fresh, and young. They ought to be satisfied with that, and not expect me to go enslave myself to some king for their sake on top of it."

"Easy now!" said Peter. "I didn't say be a slave to a king — just be of service to one."

"Change the word," said Raphael, "and the thing remains the same."

"Call it whatever you like," Peter replied. "I still don't see any other way you could be so useful — privately to your friends and publicly to everyone — or make your own condition any happier."

"Happier?" answered Raphael. "By a way of life my whole soul recoils from? Right now I live exactly as I please, which I suspect very few courtiers can say. Besides, there are plenty of men scrambling after the favor of the great; the great will hardly notice if they have to do without me, and one or two others of my temperament."

At that point I spoke up. "It's clear, Raphael, that you want neither wealth nor power — and frankly, I admire a man like that more than I admire any of the great men of the world. But it seems to me it would be worthy of a soul as generous and philosophical as yours to apply your time and your thinking to public affairs, even if it costs you some personal comfort. And you could never do that to greater effect than by joining the council of some great prince and urging him — as I know you would — toward what is noble and right. For good and evil flow out from the prince over the whole nation, as from an unfailing spring. Your learning is so deep that even without practical experience, and your experience so wide that even without the learning, you would make an excellent counselor to any king on earth."

"You are doubly mistaken, my dear More," he said, "both about me and about the job itself. I don't have the ability you imagine I have — and even if I did, the public wouldn't be one jot better off for my sacrificing my peace and quiet to it. Start with the princes themselves. Most of them are far more devoted to the arts of war, which I neither know nor want to know, than to the useful arts of peace. They are far more interested in acquiring new kingdoms — by fair means or foul — than in governing well the ones they already have. Then consider their ministers. Every one of them is either genuinely so wise that he needs no one's advice, or at the very least so convinced of his own wisdom that he won't take any — unless it comes from whichever men happen to be the prince's favorites, and those they flatter and fawn over in hopes of binding them to their own interests. Nature has built us all this way: we love to be flattered, and we are charmed by our own ideas. The old crow loves his chick, and the ape adores her cub.

"Now picture a court like that — a roomful of men who envy everyone else and admire only themselves — and imagine someone proposing something he had read in history or seen in his travels. The rest of them will act as though their entire reputation for wisdom were in danger, as though they'd be written off as fools forever unless they could shoot the idea down. And if everything else fails, they fall back on this: 'Such-and-such was good enough for our ancestors, and we should only wish we could match their wisdom.' Then they settle back, very pleased with themselves, as if that answer demolished everything — as if it would be some terrible catastrophe for anyone to be caught being wiser than his ancestors. Mind you, we cheerfully let the genuinely good things from past ages slip away; but the moment something better is proposed, we grab the excuse of 'reverence for the past' and cling to it for dear life. I have run into these proud, stubborn, ridiculous judgments in many places — once, memorably, in England."

"You've been to England?" I asked.

"I have," he answered. "I spent several months there, not long after the rebellion in the West Country had been crushed, with a terrible slaughter of the poor people caught up in it.

"During that stay I owed a great deal to the reverend prelate John Morton — Archbishop of Canterbury, Cardinal, and Chancellor of England. He was a man, Peter — Mr. More can vouch for this, since he spent part of his boyhood in the Cardinal's own household — as venerable for his wisdom and virtue as for his high office. He was of middling height and not stooped by age; his face commanded respect rather than fear. He was easy company, but serious and dignified. He sometimes enjoyed testing the people who came to him on business by speaking sharply to them — never indecently — to see what spirit and presence of mind they had. When it stopped short of insolence, that kind of nerve delighted him, since it resembled his own temperament, and he considered such people the fittest for public office. He spoke gracefully and with weight; he was eminently skilled in the law; he had a vast understanding and a prodigious memory; and these natural gifts had been polished by study and experience. While I was in England, the King relied heavily on his advice, and the government seemed to rest chiefly on his shoulders. He had been schooled in public affairs from his youth, and having been tossed through many turns of fortune, he had acquired — at great cost — an immense stock of wisdom. Wisdom bought that dearly is not soon lost.

"One day, when I was dining with him, there happened to be an English lawyer at the table, who seized the chance to deliver a glowing tribute to the strict justice then being dealt out to thieves. 'They're being hanged so fast,' he said, 'that sometimes twenty swing from a single gallows!' And then he declared he couldn't understand for the life of him how, since so few of them escaped the rope, so many thieves were still out robbing everywhere.

"At this I took the liberty of speaking freely in front of the Cardinal. 'There's no mystery in it,' I said. 'This way of punishing thieves is neither just in itself nor good for the public. The punishment is too severe, and as a deterrent it doesn't work. Simple theft is not so monstrous a crime that it should cost a man his life — and no punishment on earth, however savage, will keep people from stealing when stealing is the only way they can eat. In this matter, you English — along with a good part of the rest of the world — behave like bad schoolmasters, who would rather whip their pupils than teach them. You enact dreadful punishments for thieves, when it would be far better to make sure that everyone has some way to earn a living — so that nobody is driven to the terrible necessity of stealing first and dying for it afterward.'

"'Society has provided for that already,' said the lawyer. 'There are trades, and there is farming. A man can always make a living by those — unless he simply prefers a life of crime.'

"'That dodge won't save you,' I said. 'Let's set aside the men who come home maimed from wars, foreign or civil — from the recent rebellion in Cornwall, say, or your wars with France before that — men who lose their limbs in the service of king and country and come back too broken to follow their old trades and too old to learn new ones. Set them aside, since wars come only now and then. Let's look instead at what happens every single day.

"'First, there is the great crowd of noblemen who live as idle as drones off the labor of other men — off their tenants, whom they skin to the quick to inflate their rents. That, by the way, is the one place they practice thrift; everywhere else they spend so freely they beggar themselves. And each of them drags around a swarm of idle attendants who have never learned any trade they could live by. The moment the lord dies, or the attendants fall sick, out they go — for your lords would sooner feed idlers than care for the sick, and the heir often can't afford to keep up the household his father kept. Now, a man turned out of doors like that finds his stomach growing sharp very quickly — and he turns to robbery just as sharply, because what else is there? He wanders until his health and his clothes are both worn through, and once he's ragged and hollow-faced, no gentleman will take him in, and no poor man dares to. The poor man knows perfectly well that a fellow raised in idleness and pleasure — a fellow used to strutting around with sword and buckler, sneering at the whole neighborhood as beneath him — is never going to take up a spade and mattock, or serve a poor master faithfully for thin wages and a thinner diet.'

"'But those are exactly the men we ought to cherish,' the lawyer answered. 'When war comes, the strength of our armies lies in them. Their noble birth gives them a loftier sense of honor than you'll find among tradesmen and plowmen.'

"'You might as well say,' I replied, 'that for the sake of war we ought to cherish thieves — and you'll certainly never run out of the one while you keep the other. Robbers make passable soldiers, after all, and soldiers make very enterprising robbers; the two professions are closely related. But this bad habit of keeping crowds of idle retainers isn't unique to England. France suffers from an even more pestilent version of it: the whole country is packed with soldiers kept on the payroll in peacetime — if you can call such a state of affairs peace — and kept there on the very logic you've just used to defend your noblemen's hangers-on. The self-styled statesmen there have a maxim that public safety requires a strong body of veteran soldiers standing ready at all times, since raw recruits, they think, can't be trusted. So they sometimes go hunting for a war simply to keep their men in training — to keep their hands from "growing dull," as Sallust put it, "through too long an intermission" of throat-cutting. France has learned at her own expense how dangerous it is to feed beasts like these. And the fates of the Romans, the Carthaginians, the Syrians, and many other nations and cities — all of them overturned and utterly ruined by their own standing armies — ought to make everyone else wiser. Even on its own terms the French maxim is folly, as their experience shows: their hardened veterans can't claim any great record of success against your raw recruits — though I'll say no more on that point, in case you think I'm flattering the English present. In any case, everyday experience shows that your town craftsmen and your country laborers, unless they're disabled in body or crushed in spirit by extreme want, aren't the least bit afraid of the noblemen's idle bodyguards. And those tall, strong men — for the nobility only bother to keep handsome specimens, whom they then proceed to spoil — those men, who now grow feeble with ease and soft with their pampered way of life, would hardly be less fit for action if they were taught honest work and made to earn their bread like men. However you look at it, it seems completely unreasonable to maintain a horde of idlers — who are a standing menace to the peace — for the sake of a war you need never have unless you choose to; and peace deserves far more of your attention than war ever will. But even all this isn't the whole reason men are driven to steal. There is another cause, one more peculiar to England.'

"'And what is that?' asked the Cardinal.

"'Your sheep,' I said. 'Normally so meek, so easily kept in order — they have now begun, I hear, to devour men. They unpeople not just villages but whole towns. For wherever the sheep of a district yield wool that is finer and richer than usual, there the nobles and the gentry — and even those holy men, the abbots! — are no longer satisfied with the old rents their estates produced. It isn't enough, apparently, to live at their ease and do the public no good; they have resolved to do it positive harm instead. They put a stop to all farming. They level houses and wipe out whole towns, leaving only the church standing, and they fence in the land as pasture for their sheep. As if your forests and game parks hadn't already swallowed enough ground, these fine gentlemen turn the best-inhabited country into a wilderness. One insatiable glutton — a plague upon his own nation — decides to enclose several thousand acres inside a single hedge, and the owners as well as the tenants are forced off their land: cheated out of it, or driven off by brute force, or so worn down by abuse that they finally sell. However it's done, out they go — these poor wretched people: men and women, husbands and wives, the old and the young, whole families, penniless but numerous, since farm work takes many hands. Out they trudge from the only homes they've ever known, with nowhere to go, and their household goods — worth little enough even if they could wait for a buyer — fetch almost nothing when they can't. That little money is soon spent. And then what is left for them but to steal — and be hanged for it, with what justice God only knows! — or to take to the roads and beg? Though if they beg, they're thrown into prison as idle vagabonds — these people who would gladly work, if anyone would hire them. But no one will, because there is no farm work to be had, and farm work is all they were ever bred to, now that there's no plowland left. One single shepherd can graze, on land turned over to pasture, a flock covering ground that would take many hands to plow and reap.

"'This, in many places, has driven up the price of grain. The price of wool has risen too — so high that the poor people who used to weave it into cloth can no longer afford to buy it, which throws still more of them out of work. It's true that since pasture began swallowing everything, God has punished the owners' greed with a rot that has destroyed sheep in enormous numbers — though to our eyes it might have seemed more just had it fallen on the owners themselves. But let the sheep multiply as fast as they like; the price will never fall. The wool trade may not technically be a monopoly, since it isn't cornered by a single man, but it lies in so few hands — and such rich ones — that the owners are never pressed to sell until they feel like it, and they never feel like it until the price is driven as high as it can go. The other livestock are expensive for the same reason, and more so: with the villages pulled down and farming everywhere in decay, no one is left whose business it is to breed the animals at all. The rich don't breed cattle the way they breed sheep; they buy them lean and cheap from elsewhere, fatten them on their own pastures, and sell them again at steep prices. And I don't believe the full damage of this system has been felt yet. So far the dealers raise prices only where they sell; but once they've been buying beasts out of the breeding districts faster than those districts can produce them, the supply must shrink, and the whole thing has to end in genuine scarcity. And so this island of yours, which seemed in this respect the happiest in the world, will be brought to misery by the cursed greed of a handful of men.

"'On top of this, the high price of grain leads everyone who can to cut down their households — and where do the dismissed servants go, except to begging, or to robbery, which a man of any spirit takes to sooner? And as if to speed your poverty and misery along, luxury comes flooding in. There is extravagant vanity in dress and reckless spending on food — and not just in noble households, but among tradesmen, among the farmers themselves, among people of every rank. Then you have your many houses of ill fame — and besides the notorious ones, your taverns and ale-houses are no better — plus dice, cards, backgammon, football, tennis, and quoits, all of which empty a man's purse at speed and send their devotees straight off to robbery for fresh funds.

"'Banish these plagues. Order the men who have unpeopled so much soil either to rebuild the villages they have pulled down or to hand the land over to those who will. Rein in the rich men's cornering of markets, which is nearly as bad as open monopoly. Feed fewer idlers. Restore agriculture, and regulate the wool trade, so that honest work can be found for all that crowd of idle men — the ones whom want has already turned into thieves, and the drifting vagabonds and useless serving-men who will certainly turn into thieves before long. Until you remedy these evils, it is pointless to boast about your strict justice against theft. That justice may look impressive, but it is neither truly just nor practical. You allow your people to be raised miserably, their characters corrupted from earliest childhood — and then, when they're grown, you punish them for the very crimes their whole upbringing promised from the start. What else can one conclude, I ask you, but that you first make the thieves and then punish them for it?'
