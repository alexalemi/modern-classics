Their Magistrates

Every year, each group of thirty families elects a magistrate to oversee them. In the old language he was called a Syphogrant, though nowadays the title is Philarch. Over every ten Syphogrants — and all the families under them — stands a higher magistrate, anciently called a Tranibor, though lately the title has become Archphilarch. (So a Syphogrant oversees thirty families, and a Tranibor oversees ten Syphogrants; those are the two ranks of Utopian magistrate, and I'll stick with the old names.)

The Syphogrants, two hundred in all, elect the city's governor — the office Europeans would call a prince, though he is no monarch — choosing from a list of four candidates named by the people of the city's four districts. Before voting, every Syphogrant swears an oath to choose the man he honestly believes best suited for the job. The ballot is secret, so no one knows how anyone else voted. The governor serves for life — unless he falls under suspicion of plotting to make himself a tyrant and enslave the people, in which case he is removed.

The Tranibors are elected fresh every year, though in practice they are usually re-elected. Every other office in Utopia lasts just one year.

The Tranibors meet with the governor every third day — more often if something urgent comes up — to discuss public business, or, on the rare occasion it happens, to settle private disputes among the people. Two Syphogrants always sit in on the council chamber, and they are different ones each day. There is a fundamental rule of their government: no decision touching public affairs can be finalized until it has been debated in the council on three separate days. And it is a capital crime to meet and discuss state business anywhere outside the regular council or the general assembly of the people.

All these arrangements exist for one reason: to make sure the governor and the Tranibors can never conspire together to overturn the government and enslave the people. That is why, whenever anything of real importance comes up, it is sent down to the Syphogrants, who lay it before the families in their districts, talk it over among themselves, and then report back to the senate. On the greatest questions of all, the matter goes before the council of the whole island.

The council follows one more rule: never debate a proposal on the same day it is first introduced. It always waits until the next meeting. The reason is that men who speak up too quickly, in the heat of the moment, can lock themselves into a position — and then, instead of weighing what is best for the public, they spend their energy defending their first opinions. Out of a perverse, backwards sort of pride, a man like that would sooner put his country at risk than his reputation, rather than let anyone suspect he hadn't thought things through the first time. So to head all that off, they make sure their deliberations are deliberate, not sudden.
