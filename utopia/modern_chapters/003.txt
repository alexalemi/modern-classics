Book II: The Island of Utopia

The island of Utopia is two hundred miles across at its widest point, and it holds nearly that width for most of its length, narrowing only toward the two ends. Its shape is something like a crescent moon. Between the two horns of the crescent, the sea flows in through a strait eleven miles wide and opens into a great bay, ringed by about five hundred miles of coastline and sheltered from the wind. There is hardly any current in this bay, so the whole coast works, in effect, as one enormous harbor — which makes it wonderfully easy for the islanders to trade with one another by ship.

But getting into that bay is another matter. The entrance is treacherous: rocks on one side, sandbars on the other. In the middle of the channel stands a single rock that rises above the water — easy enough to steer around, and on top of it they have built a tower with a permanent garrison. The real danger is the other rocks, the ones hidden below the surface. Only the natives know the safe passage, so any stranger who tries to enter the bay without one of their pilots is courting shipwreck. Even the Utopians themselves could not get through safely without certain landmarks on the coast to steer by. And if those markers were ever quietly moved, an enemy fleet — no matter how large — would be lured onto the rocks and destroyed, every ship of it.

The outer side of the island has plenty of harbors too, but the coast there is so well defended, by nature and by engineering alike, that a handful of men could hold off a great army trying to land.

Now, here is something remarkable — and they say the evidence is still visible, which makes it believable. Utopia was not originally an island at all. It was part of the mainland. The man who conquered it was named Utopus, and the island still carries his name (before that it was called Abraxa). Utopus took a rough, uncivilized people and brought them under such good government, and raised them to such a level of culture, that they now outshine practically everyone else on earth.

Once he had subdued the place, Utopus decided to cut it off from the continent entirely and bring the sea all the way around it. So he ordered a channel dug — fifteen miles long. And so that the natives would not feel he was treating them like slaves, he put not only the local inhabitants to work on it but his own soldiers as well, right alongside them. With that enormous workforce, he finished the job faster than anyone thought possible. His neighbors on the mainland had laughed at the whole project as a piece of folly. When they saw it completed, the laughter stopped. What replaced it was admiration — and fear.

There are fifty-four cities on the island, all of them large and well built, and all sharing the same language, manners, customs, and laws. They are even laid out on the same plan, as nearly as the ground each one stands on will allow. The closest cities are at least twenty-four miles apart; the farthest are still near enough that a person can walk from one to the next in a single day.

Once a year, every city sends three of its wisest senators to Amaurot to confer about the affairs of the whole island. (The name Amaurot, in Greek, means something like "dim city" — and the river it stands on is called the Anider, "no-water.") Amaurot is the chief town of the island, and since it lies near the center, it is the most convenient meeting place for everyone.

Each city's territory extends at least twenty miles in every direction, and more where the cities are spaced farther apart. No town has any desire to push its boundaries outward, because the people think of themselves as tenants of the land rather than its owners.

All across the countryside they have built farmhouses, well designed and stocked with everything needed for farm work. People are sent out from the cities, by turns, to live in them. No country household has fewer than forty men and women in it, plus two slaves. Each household is run by a master and a mistress, and every thirty households are overseen by a magistrate.

Every year, twenty members of each farm household move back to town, having finished a two-year stay in the country, and twenty fresh replacements come out from town to take their places. The newcomers learn farming from the people who have already been there a year — and a year later, they teach the next batch in turn. This way, the farms are never run entirely by beginners, and no one makes the kind of rookie mistakes that could ruin a harvest and leave everyone short of grain. And though this annual rotation exists so that nobody is forced to spend too long at hard country labor against his will, plenty of people genuinely enjoy the life and ask permission to stay on for years.

These farmers work the land, raise cattle, and cut timber, hauling it to the towns by road or by water, whichever is more convenient. They also raise chickens in astonishing numbers, and by a very clever method: the hens do not sit on the eggs. Instead, huge batches of eggs are kept at a gentle, even warmth until they hatch — and the moment the chicks are out of the shell and able to walk, they treat the people who feed them as their mothers, following them around the way ordinary chicks follow a hen.

They breed very few horses, and the ones they keep are high-spirited animals used only to teach the young people horsemanship. All the actual work — plowing, hauling — is done by oxen. Horses are stronger, they admit, but oxen last longer, get sick less often, and cost less to feed and care for. And when an ox is finally too worn out to work, it still makes a good dinner.

They plant grain only for bread, because their drink is wine, cider, or perry — and often plain water, sometimes boiled with honey or licorice, which they have in abundance. They know exactly how much grain each town and its surrounding district will consume, yet they deliberately plant far more, and raise far more cattle, than they need — and the surplus they simply give away to their neighbors. When country people need something the land doesn't produce, they go fetch it from town without bringing anything to trade for it, and the town magistrates see that they get it, no questions asked. Most of them go into town anyway once a month, on a festival day.

When harvest time approaches, the country magistrates send word to the towns of how many extra hands they'll need for the reaping. The requested workers are sent out — and the whole harvest is usually brought in within a single day.
