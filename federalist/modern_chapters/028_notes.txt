Major changes in this translation of Federalist No. 28:

1. Vocabulary modernization: "necessitated" → "forced," "maladies" → "inseparable [problems]," "reveries" → "fantasies," "sagacity" → "supposed wisdom," "felicity" → "well-being," "tumultuously" → "in a disorganized panic," "celerity" → "speed," "preponderate" → "tip the balance," "penetration" → ability to fool/spot.

2. The powerful medical metaphor in the opening ("seditions and insurrections are... maladies as inseparable from the body politic as tumors and eruptions from the natural body") was preserved and made slightly more accessible ("tumors and infections").

3. Hamilton's key argument about the self-defense right of the people (paragraph 6) — a passage with significant constitutional implications — was translated carefully to preserve its force while improving clarity. The vivid imagery of citizens "rushing tumultuously to arms, without concert, without system, without resource; except in their courage and despair" was maintained in spirit.

4. The central federalism argument — that state and national governments check each other, with the people as the ultimate arbiter — was kept prominent. This is one of Hamilton's most important contributions to constitutional theory.

5. The concluding metaphor comparing fear of federal tyranny to a "disease" was preserved, maintaining Hamilton's dismissive rhetorical force.

6. Long sentences with multiple subordinate clauses were broken into shorter units, especially in the paragraphs about resistance to tyranny.

7. Footnote [1] modernized from "Its full efficacy will be examined hereafter."
