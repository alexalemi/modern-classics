1. HAMILTON'S VOICE: This is Hamilton at his most intellectually provocative. His argument against a Bill of Rights — that it's not only unnecessary but actively dangerous — is historically one of his most important contributions. Preserved his boldness and confidence: "Why declare that things shall not be done when there's no power to do them?" This rhetorical question is one of his most famous, and it's been kept close to the original.

   - "depositary" → "place to deposit"

3. KEY ARGUMENTS PRESERVED:
   - Bills of rights as agreements between kings and subjects, not for popular governments
   - The "We, the People" Preamble as itself a declaration of rights
   - The dangerous-if-adopted argument (listing exceptions to non-existent powers implies the powers exist)
   - The press freedom argument (impossible to define, so declarations are meaningless)
   - The Constitution itself IS a bill of rights
   - The distance/monitoring objection and its refutation
   - The expense objection and detailed cost analysis
