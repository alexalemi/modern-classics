Major changes in this translation of Federalist No. 30:

1. Vocabulary modernization: "pecuniary" → "financial," "civil list" → "operating costs," "bashaws" → "provincial governors," "annihilation" → "near collapse," "ignis fatuus" → explained as "will-o'-the-wisp — this false light leading us astray," "tutelage" → "dependent on," "necessitous" → "strapped for cash," "engagements" → "commitments," "halcyon scenes" → "golden age utopia," "solicitude" → "concern," "deprecate" → "dread."

2. The Ottoman Empire example was preserved as it's an effective illustration, with "bashaws" modernized to "provincial governors" for clarity.

3. Hamilton's dramatic escalating series of rhetorical questions ("How is it possible...? How can it ever...? How can its administration...?") was preserved in full — this is quintessential Hamilton at his most forceful.

4. The war scenario argument (paragraphs 8-9) was kept as a concrete, vivid illustration of why limited taxing power fails. The loan shark analogy ("usurers commonly lend to bankrupt and fraudulent debtors") was preserved and made slightly more accessible.

5. "Ignis fatuus" — a Latin term meaning "foolish fire" or will-o'-the-wisp — was given an inline explanation since modern readers won't know the term, but the metaphor (a misleading light) was preserved.

6. The famous maxim about nations' needs always equaling their resources was preserved as a highlighted statement, as Hamilton clearly intended it as a memorable principle.

7. The final paragraph's contrast between utopian dreamers and realistic thinkers was kept as Hamilton's characteristic rhetorical closing.
