Major changes and rationale for Federalist No. 20:

1. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATION:
   - "imbecility in the government" -> "weakness in the government"
   - "jealousy in each province" -> "suspicion of the others"
   - "contumacious" -> "refused to comply" (context-dependent)
   - "ad referendum" -> "referred back to provincial capitals" (Latin made plain)
   - "notwithstanding" -> "despite" (per conventions)
   - "laudable zeal" -> "admirable determination"
   - "monitory" -> "cautionary"
   - "ejaculation to Heaven" -> "prayer to Heaven" (18th-c. "ejaculation" meant a short, fervent prayer)
   - "propitious concord" -> "fortunate harmony"
   - "solecism in theory" -> "absurd in theory"
   - "salutary coercion of the magistracy" -> "gentle authority of the courts"
   - "convulsions" -> "upheaval"
   - "tardiness" -> "slowness"

2. HISTORICAL REFERENCES:
   - "house of Austria" -> "Spanish Habsburgs" (clearer for modern readers)
   - "Union of Utrecht" -> kept (important historical document)
   - "Grotius" -> "the great legal scholar Grotius"
   - "Sir William Temple" -> kept with context ("who was himself a foreign diplomat")
   - "Abbe Mably" -> kept as is (established in earlier papers)
   - "stadtholder" -> kept as is (distinctive enough to be worth preserving)
   - "States-General" -> kept as is (proper name of the institution)
   - "florins" -> kept (evocative and clear enough)

3. MADISON'S VOICE (with Hamilton):
   - Maintained the systematic survey structure
   - Preserved the dramatic emotional pivot: "Let us pause, my fellow citizens..."
   - This passage is one of the most personal in the Papers 18-20 trilogy -- kept its full emotional force
   - The final paragraph builds to a powerful rhetorical climax, echoing Hamilton's energy
   - The scholarly tone gives way to passionate conviction in the conclusion

4. SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
   - Constitutional descriptions broken into clearer, more digestible segments
   - The stadtholder's powers organized more cleanly (political/military/naval)
   - Long quotations from various writers simplified and integrated
   - The final sentence preserved as a single powerful statement

5. FAMOUS PASSAGES:
   - "Experience is the oracle of truth" -- preserved nearly verbatim as it's a quotable formulation
   - "a sovereignty over sovereigns, a government over governments" -- preserved as the thesis statement of the entire 18-20 trilogy
   - "Let us pause, my fellow citizens" -- preserved with full emotional weight

6. THE CONCLUDING PRINCIPLE:
   - The paper's final statement crystallizes the argument of Papers 15-20: legislating for states rather than individuals replaces law with violence. This is rendered clearly and forcefully.
