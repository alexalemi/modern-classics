Key translation decisions for Paper 54:

1. THREE-FIFTHS COMPROMISE: This paper discusses the Constitution's three-fifths clause, which counted enslaved people as three-fifths of a person for both representation and taxation purposes. Translated faithfully without editorial commentary, but made the subject matter unmistakably clear to modern readers. Where the original uses "slaves," the translation uses "enslaved people" or "slaves" depending on context. "Negroes" in the original is rendered as "enslaved Black people" or "enslaved people" to maintain clarity.

2. RHETORICAL DEVICE PRESERVED: Madison's unusual technique of putting the Southern argument in the mouth of a hypothetical "Southern brethren" speaker, using extended quotation, is faithfully preserved. This distancing device was deliberate — Madison (himself a slaveholder) chose to present these arguments at arm's length rather than in his own voice.

3. "The SLAVE as divested of two fifths of the MAN" — the original's ALL CAPS emphasis and archaic phrasing is translated clearly: "treating each slave as three-fifths of a person." This makes the formula's meaning explicit for modern readers.

4. "Vendible" (meaning able to be sold) expanded to "sold by one master to another" for clarity.

5. "The United Netherlands" modernized to "the Dutch Republic" for accessibility.

6. The complex final section about how a shared formula for representation and taxation creates opposing incentives that cancel out is preserved — it's a clever argument that's easy to follow once the sentence structure is simplified.

7. "Candid" in its 18th-century sense of "honest/frank" rendered as "honest."

8. Maintained Madison's systematic, logical voice throughout — his careful structuring of the argument through claim, objection, and response.
