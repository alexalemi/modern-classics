Major changes and rationale for Federalist No. 60:

1. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATION:
   - "chimerical" -> "far-fetched" (used twice for emphasis, mirroring the original's repetition)
   - "partiality" -> "favoritism" or "bias"
   - "preponderancy" -> "dominance"
   - "propensities and inclinations" -> "tendencies"
   - "predilection" -> "preference"
   - "extraneous circumstances" -> "external factors"
   - "credulous votaries of State power" -> "those who zealously defend state power"
   - "obsequious" -> removed (rephrased for clarity)
   - "importuned" -> "reminded... by the pressing demands"
   - "capricious" -> "arbitrary"
   - "actuated by" -> "driven by" / "motivated by"
   - "usurpation" -> "seizure"
   - "espouse" -> "strongly favor"
   - "addicted to agriculture" -> "devoted to agriculture"

2. SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
   - Broke Hamilton's characteristically long, building sentences into shorter, more powerful constructions
   - Preserved his series of rhetorical questions (especially the devastating series at the end about what a government with military force would actually do)
   - Maintained the logical progression from general improbability to specific impossibility
   - Kept the industry-by-industry analysis but made it cleaner

3. TONE PRESERVATION:
   - Maintained Hamilton's energetic, combative, sometimes dismissive voice
   - Preserved his willingness to call opponents' arguments absurd ("of all the far-fetched scenarios, this is the most far-fetched")
   - Kept his aggressive confidence and rhetorical force
   - Preserved the brilliant escalation in the final paragraph: even granting every assumption, the scheme still fails because anyone with that much power would just seize power directly
   - Maintained his characteristic use of hypotheticals to reduce arguments to absurdity

4. STRUCTURAL DECISIONS:
   - Preserved the progression: general improbability -> structural obstacles -> branch-by-branch analysis -> industry analysis -> "wealthy and well-born" argument -> reductio ad absurdum finale
   - Kept the constitutional limitation argument (Congress can only regulate time, place, manner -- not qualifications)
   - Updated the footnote to be clearer about its reference
   - Preserved Hamilton's quotation marks around "the wealthy and the well-born" (the opponents' catchphrase)

5. HAMILTON'S RHETORICAL TECHNIQUE:
   - The final paragraph is Hamilton at his most brilliant: he concedes every premise his opponents need, then shows that even with all those concessions, the conclusion doesn't follow. This "even if" argument is preserved in full force.
   - His series of rhetorical questions builds inexorably to the climactic image of citizens flocking to overthrow tyrants
