   - "sceptre" -> "reach" (modernized metaphor)
   - "conceited projector" -> "self-assured reformer"
   - "pernicious" -> "dangerous"

2. SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
   - Hamilton's long periodic sentences broken into more forceful shorter constructions
   - Preserved his characteristic rhetorical questions -- they're central to his argumentative style
   - The double jeopardy argument carefully reorganized for clarity while maintaining logical force
   - Hamilton's building chains of questions preserved as they create his signature dramatic momentum

3. TONE PRESERVATION:
   - Maintained Hamilton's energetic, combative voice throughout
   - Kept his aggressive confidence and dramatic flair
   - Preserved his characteristic move of considering and dismissing alternatives one by one
   - The closing passage about perfection captures Hamilton's pragmatic impatience with idealists
   - "The demon of faction" preserved as quintessential Hamilton -- vivid, dramatic, slightly theatrical
   - His rhetorical questions are sharper and more confrontational than Madison's or Jay's

4. KEY ARGUMENTS PRESERVED:
   - Impeachment offenses are political in nature
   - Impeachment trials will inevitably be partisan
   - The Supreme Court lacks the courage and credibility for this role
   - Double jeopardy problem if the same judges serve in both impeachment and criminal trials
   - The Chief Justice as presiding officer is a wise compromise
   - A separate tribunal would be either too expensive or too cumbersome
   - The Constitution needn't be perfect in every detail to be accepted

5. STRUCTURAL DECISIONS:
   - ALL CAPS (POLITICAL, NATIONAL INQUEST, CONFIDENCE ENOUGH IN ITS OWN SITUATION, etc.) converted to plain text
   - Retained paper header, author, salutation, and PUBLIUS signature
   - Hamilton's systematic elimination of alternatives preserved as organizational structure
   - The progression from "why the Senate?" through "why not the Court?" to "why not a separate body?" maintained clearly
