Major changes in this translation:

1. HAMILTON'S VOICE AND FAMOUS PASSAGES: This is one of Hamilton's most important papers. I preserved the key passages as closely as possible while making them accessible:
   - "will always be the least dangerous to the political rights of the Constitution" — preserved nearly verbatim
   - "has no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society" — preserved with slight modernization ("command" for "direction")
   - "neither force nor will, but merely judgment" — preserved exactly (converted from ALL CAPS)
   - The entire judicial review argument — the logical chain from "delegated authority" through "the deputy is greater than his principal" to "the Constitution ought to be preferred to the statute" — preserved in full with only sentence structure and vocabulary modernized
   - "the fortress of public justice and public security" — preserved ("citadel" → "fortress")

2. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATIONS:
   - "judicature" → "court system"
   - "magistracy" → "judges" / contextual
   - "expedient" → "tool"
   - "encroachments and oppressions" → "overreach and oppression"
   - "co-ordinate branches" → "other branches"
   - "bills of attainder" → kept with gloss "(laws that punish specific people without a trial)"
   - "ex post facto laws" → kept with gloss "(laws that criminalize actions after the fact)"
   - "repugnancy" → "constitutional conflict"
   - "ill humors" → "bad moods"
   - "arts of designing men" → "schemes of manipulative people"
   - "conjunctures" → "crises"
   - "cabals" → "scheming"
   - "complaisance" → "deference"
   - "sinister expectations" → "underhanded plans"
   - "voluminous code" → "large and complex body"

3. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: The original contains some extraordinarily long sentences — particularly the climactic passage about permanency in office, which is one sentence with multiple "that as" clauses. I broke this into a series of clauses connected by dashes to preserve the building, cumulative force of the argument while making it readable.

4. THE JUDICIAL REVIEW ARGUMENT: This is the heart of the paper. Hamilton builds through precise logical steps: (1) delegated authority can't exceed its commission; (2) unconstitutional laws are therefore void; (3) the legislature can't be the judge of its own powers; (4) courts are the natural intermediary between the people and the legislature; (5) the Constitution must prevail over ordinary statutes; (6) this doesn't make judges superior to legislators — it means the people are superior to both. Every step of this chain is preserved.

5. ALL CAPS: Converted "FORCE," "WILL," "EQUAL" to regular case. The emphasis carries through the argument itself.

6. FOOTNOTES: Expanded with brief context — identified Montesquieu as "the French political philosopher" and italicized the work title.

7. The paper's structure is preserved: appointment of judges (brief, cross-referencing Papers 76-77) → tenure during good behavior → the "least dangerous branch" → judicial review → independence protects against bad laws and mob sentiment → practical need for experienced judges.
