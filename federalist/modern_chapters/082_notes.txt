Major changes in this translation:

1. HAMILTON'S VOICE: This is Hamilton in professorial-legal mode rather than combative mode. He's carefully explaining how concurrent jurisdiction works, laying out interpretive principles, and reasoning through constitutional text. Preserved his confidence and directness while making his dense legal reasoning accessible.

2. VOCABULARY MODERNIZATIONS:
   - "intricacy and nicety" → "complicated and delicate questions"
   - "liquidate the meaning" → "clarify the meaning"
   - "cognizance" → "jurisdiction" / "cases" / "handle"
   - "construction" → "reading" / "interpretation"
   - "alienation of State power" → "strip the states of power"
   - "primitive jurisdiction" → "existing jurisdiction"
   - "genius of the system" → "overall design of the system"
   - "auxiliaries to the execution" → "supporting institutions for enforcing"
   - "subversion of the intent" → "undermine its intent"
   - "countenance the affirmative" → "support saying yes"
   - "embarrass its measures" → "obstruct its operations"

3. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Broke Hamilton's very long, clause-heavy legal sentences into shorter modern constructions while preserving the logical chain. His three-part test for exclusive delegation was reformatted for clarity.

4. LEGAL CONCEPTS: Kept key legal terms (concurrent jurisdiction, appellate jurisdiction, original jurisdiction) since they're central to the argument and still used today. Provided enough contextual framing so readers can follow the analysis.

5. THE "ONE WHOLE" EMPHASIS: Hamilton twice stresses that state and national systems are parts of "one whole" — preserved this emphasis, which is central to his federalism theory.

6. FORMATTING: Retained paper number, title, author, salutation, PUBLIUS signature. Footnotes use [N] notation.
