STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Translated neutrally per instructions -- Tocqueville's views on women's roles reflect his era; let readers judge
- The chapter's structure is clear: 1) the sharp contrast between unmarried freedom and married constraint, 2) the logic behind it, 3) American women's resilience in hardship. Maintained this arc.
- The "convent" comparison is Tocqueville's own image; kept it for its striking quality
- The passage about women choosing their constraint knowingly is one of the most thought-provoking in the chapter -- preserved its full complexity without editorializing
- The frontier women passage is one of Tocqueville's most vivid and admiring first-person observations. "Their faces were worn and faded, but their gaze was steady" -- preserved the stark, visual quality
- "At once sad and resolute" -- kept this powerful, compressed observation
- The closing observation (the girl's education persists beneath the surface of married life) ties back to the previous chapter beautifully; preserved the connection

TONE CALIBRATION

- Opening: stark, almost clinical -- the contrast between freedom and constraint stated bluntly
- The public opinion passages: analytical, showing the mechanism of social pressure
- The "she chose it" passages: Tocqueville's attempt to frame constraint as agency -- translated neutrally
- The frontier women passage: admiring, vivid, first-person -- Tocqueville the fieldworker at his best
- Closing: reflective, tying the themes together

RECURRING TERMS

- "public opinion" as the enforcement mechanism -- consistent with earlier chapters
- "equality of conditions" -- present implicitly throughout but the chapter focuses more on gender-specific dynamics
- "virtue" -- used sparingly here; the chapter focuses more on strength of character than sexual morality
