- The theoretical opening (imagine a perfect merger of equality and freedom) — kept but tightened; it sets up the crucial distinction
- "A thousand other combinations" passage about equality without freedom — this is prophetic (think modern China); preserved the full argument

- "The pleasures of equality are self-serve" — deliberate modern colloquialism for Tocqueville's "self-proffered" — captures the effortless, automatic quality he's describing
- The France-specific passage: clearly signaled the shift ("What I'm about to say concerns France specifically")
- Final paragraph: "They demand equality in freedom, and if they can't have that, they still demand equality in slavery" — this is one of Tocqueville's most devastating lines; preserved it with full force
- Closing: "even despotism cannot rule without it" — kept this chilling final note exactly
