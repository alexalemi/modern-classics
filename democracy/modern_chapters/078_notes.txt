STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This chapter is the capstone of the materialism sequence (chapters 073-078). It ties together the themes of material comfort, freedom, and civic engagement. Structured the translation to make the argument's logic as clear as possible.
- The dangerous-passage section is the emotional heart: people so focused on getting rich that they voluntarily surrender their rights. Kept the urgency and made the logic of self-defeat vivid.
- "They loosen their own grip willingly" -- this is the chapter's key insight, stated plainly.
- The "slave in its heart" passage is one of Tocqueville's most quotable lines. Preserved it with minimal alteration: "A nation that asks nothing of its government except the maintenance of order is already a slave in its heart -- the slave of its own comfort, just waiting for the hand that will bind it."
- The theater metaphor at the end ("a multitude represented by a handful of actors") -- kept this vivid; it's a brilliant image for political apathy.
- The closing section on American exceptionalism (they've avoided these dangers so far) maintains Tocqueville's characteristic mix of admiration and implicit warning.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The chapter oscillates between analytical argument and urgent warning. The warning passages are kept sharp and direct -- this is Tocqueville the prophet, not Tocqueville the detached observer.
- The final paragraphs about Americans successfully combining material self-interest with civic engagement are warmer and more admiring -- matching Tocqueville's genuine respect.
- "The human heart can't really be split like that" -- a characteristic Tocquevillean aside, informal and psychologically acute.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER

- This concludes the materialism/restlessness/well-being sequence (Volume 2, Section 2, Chapters IX-XIV). The next chapters will likely shift to a new thematic cluster.
- Key themes established across these six chapters that should carry forward:
  1. Self-interest properly understood as a moral philosophy compatible with (and dependent on) religion
  2. The universal but moderate nature of democratic materialism vs. aristocratic excess
  3. The paradox of restlessness amid prosperity -- equality's insatiable promise
  4. The danger that material comfort will make people surrender their freedom
  5. America's (so far) successful fusion of material ambition and civic engagement
- Vocabulary to maintain: "material comfort," "self-interest properly understood," "respectable materialism," "restlessness," "freedom" (not "liberty")
