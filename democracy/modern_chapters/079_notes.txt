Next Chapter Notes -- Chapter 079 (Chapter XV: Religion and Immaterial Pleasures)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "immaterial pleasures" -> "the spiritual" in the chapter title; "spiritual" or "beyond the material" in text
- "the marts of traffic" -> "the markets"
- "sublimest faculties" -> "highest faculties"
- "metempsychosis" -> "reincarnation" (modern equivalent for the Greek philosophical term)
- "supersensual" -> "spiritual" / "beyond the physical"
- "materialists" -> kept as "materialists" (the philosophical school; still current)
- "manners" -> not used in this chapter
- "panegyric" -> not used
- "the legislator" -> "governing" / "those who govern" / "democratic leaders"
- "enervates" -> "weakens" (used in ch 080)
- "pernicious" -> "destructive"
- "amalgamates" -> "blends"
- "inculcate" -> "teaches"
- "bequest" -> "legacies"
- "momentary service" -> "temporarily serve"
- "constituted authorities" -> "governments"
- "effectual" -> "effective"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The opening paragraph is a single vivid scene -- kept it as one flowing paragraph with the same Sunday-morning rhythm, just modernized the vocabulary.
- Tocqueville's hypothetical about being born in an aristocratic age -- preserved the rhetorical structure but broke the mega-sentence into shorter units.
- The materialists passage has genuine emotion (disgust, moral urgency) -- preserved the heat.
- The Socrates/Plato passage -- kept the philosophical argument clear without dumbing it down.
- The final paragraph about governments acting as if they believe -- preserved the rhetorical punch of the closing line.
- "the angel teaches the brute" metaphor deferred to chapter 080 where it actually appears.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter has Tocqueville at his most philosophical and religious. The register shifts between practical observation (the Sunday scene) and moral argument (materialism as a danger to democracy).
- Preserved his genuine conviction without making him sound preachy -- he's making a political argument about why religion matters for democracy, not a theological argument about God.
- The closing about governments acting "as if they believed" has a wonderful pragmatic cynicism -- kept that intact.

RECURRING TERMS ESTABLISHED

- "physical pleasure" / "physical gratification" -> "physical pleasure" / "material pleasure" consistently
- "immortality of the soul" -> kept as is (central concept in this chapter)
- "materialism" / "materialists" -> kept as is
- "the infinite" -> kept (Tocqueville's term for transcendent spiritual longing)

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 080

- Chapter 080 is very short -- a brief philosophical argument that the soul and the body are linked, and that neglecting the soul will eventually impair material progress too.
- Uses the "angel teaches the brute" metaphor -- render clearly.
- Continues the themes of 079 (spiritual vs. material) but shifts to the pragmatic argument: you need the soul to be strong even for material success.
