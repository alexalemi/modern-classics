STRUCTURAL DECISIONS

- Used "***" section breaks between the major subsections (History, Summary, Powers, Legislative, Executive, President vs. King) rather than explicit "Part I / Part II" labels
- Chapter summaries: modernized and kept, placed after each section break as brief italic-style overview text
- The chapter transitions from being called "Part I" in the source -- ended at the natural break point where Tocqueville compares the President to the King of France
- Footnotes: integrated essential ones into the text (e.g., "Congress made this declaration on February 21, 1787" woven into the narrative; the Constitutional Convention's fifty-five members including Washington, Madison, Hamilton, and the two Morrises; the ratification process by special conventions rather than legislatures)
- Dropped purely bibliographic footnotes (Federalist numbers, Story page numbers, Kent's Commentaries references)
- The Federalist Papers description: kept Tocqueville's own introduction of the work but modernized it, using "Alexander Hamilton" and "James Madison" with first names for modern readers

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The historical narrative section (colonies to Constitution) is told as a story -- maintained narrative momentum
- The "exaggerated" passage about American independence is vintage Tocqueville provocation -- kept it sharp and direct, preserved the unflattering comparison to France's revolution
- The Great Compromise section (Senate vs. House) is inherently structural/expository -- kept it clear and brisk, using concrete examples (New York vs. Delaware) as Tocqueville does
- The executive power sections build a careful argument -- preserved the logical chain while breaking long sentences
- The comparison passage about monarchies and republics (beheading kings, Roman emperors dining casually) -- this is one of Tocqueville's great rhetorical set pieces, kept the vivid examples
- "There is no country in the world where the laws can provide for everything" -- preserved as a standalone concluding thought, it's a great line

TONE CALIBRATION

- The opening transition ("So far, I've been looking at each state as a separate entity") is direct and conversational
- The Constitutional Convention narrative has a storytelling quality -- "America had the double advantage of..."
- The structural/legal sections maintain clarity without becoming dry
- The rhetorical passages (the beheading of kings, etc.) keep their wit and vividness
