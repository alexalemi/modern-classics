STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is a short, punchy chapter. Kept it tight. Broke the two long paragraphs into five shorter ones for better readability.
- The final sentence is one of Tocqueville's most famous aphorisms: "born equal, instead of becoming so." Preserved it exactly -- it's perfect as-is and widely quoted.
- The psychological portraits (former aristocrats looking down with bitterness, former commoners looking up with a mix of triumph and fear) -- kept these vivid and concrete. They're excellent social observation.

TONE CALIBRATION

- More analytical than 066, less elegiac. This is Tocqueville the political sociologist, making a sharp observation about revolutionary dynamics.
- The France-America comparison is implicit throughout (France endured a revolution; America didn't). Kept it implicit as Tocqueville does -- he makes it explicit only in the final line.

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "individualism" / "selfishness" distinction continues
- "born equal, instead of becoming so" -- this may be referenced later
- "democratic revolution" -- Tocqueville's term for the violent transition from aristocracy to democracy (as opposed to the American experience)

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 068

- Chapter 068 shifts to the remedy: free institutions as the antidote to individualism. Watch for the argument about local government, the road-building example, and the contrast between despotism and freedom.
- The despotism/equality parallel at the start is crucial -- Tocqueville argues they reinforce each other.
- The chapter on French attitudes at the very end (equality as one evil, freedom as a second) is Tocqueville's direct plea to his French readers.
