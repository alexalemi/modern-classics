STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The long slippery-slope argument (censorship leads inevitably to despotism) is one continuous chain of logic in the original. Preserved its cumulative force but broke the single mega-paragraph into clearer sentences. The progression -- jury -> judges -> censorship -> silencing speech -> full tyranny -- is one of Tocqueville's best arguments and still resonates.
- The Jackson newspaper excerpt: kept it as a block quote, modernized only lightly. It's supposed to sound inflammatory -- that's the whole point.
- The comparison between French and American newspapers: Tocqueville is essentially describing the difference between opinion journalism and advertising-driven local news. This still feels remarkably current.
- Tocqueville's argument about press decentralization (multiplying newspapers weakens each one but strengthens the institution) is genuinely brilliant and still relevant to debates about media fragmentation. Preserved the full argument.
- The three-stage epistemology section (belief -> doubt -> informed belief) is more philosophical than the rest of the chapter. Kept it clear but didn't oversimplify.
- Footnote *a (about prominent figures only writing to refute slander): integrated as parenthetical.
- Footnote *b (See Appendix, P.): dropped as purely bibliographic.
- Footnote *c (about rational conviction vs. dogmatic belief): integrated as parenthetical.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The opening confession ("I don't have an absolute love for press freedom") sets a characteristically honest, contrarian Tocqueville tone. He supports press freedom but refuses to be sentimental about it.
- The slippery-slope argument has a building, almost breathless quality -- preserved this.
- The comparison sections (France vs. America) are more measured and analytical.
- The closing section on opinion stability is philosophical but accessible.
