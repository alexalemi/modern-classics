STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The "political energy" passage: This is Tocqueville at his most vivid -- almost like travel journalism. Made it feel immediate and breathless. The list of activities (church meetings, elections, delegates, farmers, protest meetings, temperance societies) preserved as a rapid-fire catalogue to convey the feeling of constant motion.
- The "Gentlemen" anecdote: Preserved Tocqueville's dry humor about Americans who can't have a casual conversation without turning it into a speech.
- The Roman footnote (Montesquieu on citizens under the Caesars): Dropped -- it's a scholarly aside that doesn't add much for modern readers, and the point about Americans needing political engagement is clear enough without it.
- The closing passage (aristocracy vs. democracy choice): This is one of the most famous passages in the book. Preserved the parallel structure (if X... then aristocracy / if Y... then democracy) while breaking the mega-sentences into more manageable chunks. The rhetorical force depends on the accumulation, so kept the lists long but made each item clearer.
- The final paragraph about making the best of democracy: Preserved as a quiet, pragmatic close -- very characteristic of Tocqueville's mature wisdom.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Law section: Matter-of-fact, almost sociological -- explaining the mechanism by which democratic law commands respect
- Political energy section: Vivid, almost breathless -- capturing the chaos and vitality of American public life
- "True advantages" passage: Builds to an important analytical conclusion -- democracy's real strength is indirect, not direct
- Closing rhetorical passage: Grand, sweeping, genuinely moving -- the culmination of the entire chapter on democracy's advantages

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "enlightened self-interest" -> continued from 023 (Americans obey law because it's in their interest)
- "energy" / "vitality" / "restless activity" -> democracy's defining characteristic (opposed to aristocratic calm)
- "the true advantages of democracy" -> the key takeaway: not good governance but civic energy
- "habits of freedom" -> Americans' ingrained political habits (will recur throughout)
- "indirect cause" -> Tocqueville's nuanced claim: democracy causes prosperity indirectly, through civic engagement
