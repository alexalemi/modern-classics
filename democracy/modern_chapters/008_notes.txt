STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Voice: First person maintained and made vivid where Tocqueville uses it. "During my time in the United States, I witnessed..." -- kept immediate and journalistic.
- The centralization argument is one of Tocqueville's signature pieces. Preserved the rhetorical arc: distinction between types -> analysis -> devastating critique of administrative centralization -> defense of American energy. The China comparison from a footnote was woven in as a parenthetical illustration.
- The passage about European citizens as "tenants" indifferent to their communities is one of Tocqueville's most powerful set pieces -- kept the imagery vivid and the emotional weight intact.
- The criminal justice anecdote ("I witnessed the spontaneous formation of citizen committees") is classic Tocqueville fieldwork -- kept direct and punchy.
- Footnotes: The Civil War translator's note was integrated into the text as a parenthetical. The China note was woven in. Bibliographic footnotes dropped. The footnote about French vs. American budget accounting was woven into the main text about surface disorder vs. underlying vitality.
- Benjamin Franklin reference: kept name, explained his reasoning briefly.
- French Convention: kept reference with brief context ("during the Revolution").
- Montesquieu: kept as name-drop, his argument is clear from context.

- The legislative section is brisk and informative -- kept it crisp
- The centralization argument builds from analytical to passionate -- this is Tocqueville at his most persuasive, and the translation preserves the crescendo
- The European "tenant" passage is bitter and worried -- kept the emotional intensity
- The crime-fighting anecdote is vivid and almost delighted -- Tocqueville clearly admires what he saw

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "centralized government" vs. "centralized administration" -- this is THE key distinction of the chapter; maintain precisely
- "local self-government" / "local institutions" / "local independence" -- used as equivalents for Reeve's "provincial institutions" / "provincial liberties"
- "township" / "county" / "state" -- levels of government, consistently used
- "the majority" -- Tocqueville's loaded term; the legislature "claims to be the sole voice of reason"
- "freedom" preferred over "liberty" -- maintained throughout
- "public spirit" -- kept as Tocqueville's key concept about civic engagement
