STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Book Three heading: "Influence Of Democracy On Manners, Properly So Called" -> "How Democracy Shapes Social Behavior and Interpersonal Relations." Used *** section break after the book heading.
- The French passages from Madame de Sevigne: Paraphrased into English rather than leaving the original French, which would be inaccessible to most modern readers. Preserved the key details (the hundred thousand crowns, the breaking on the wheel, the quartering, the expelled families) and especially the horrifying casualness of her tone. Kept one direct quote ("This province is a fine example for the others") translated into English. The second letter's dark humor ("hanging now seems like a relief") is translated and presented as near-direct quotation.
- The footnote about Madame de Grignan being Gouvernante de Provence was dropped -- it explains a pun in the French that doesn't survive translation.
- The slavery passage: Handled carefully. "Their masters" -> "their enslavers." Preserved Tocqueville's argument intact -- that equality breeds empathy and inequality breeds indifference, even within the same person. This is his point, and it's devastating.
- The Cicero/Roman passage at the end: kept vivid and specific. "Circus" -> "arena" (more immediately clear to modern readers).

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter shifts between historical analysis (medieval chronicles, Madame de Sevigne) and philosophical argument (equality breeds empathy). The historical examples should feel concrete and vivid; the philosophy should feel like clear argumentation.
- The Madame de Sevigne section is the chapter's emotional centerpiece. The horror comes not from the violence itself but from her casual tone about it. Made sure that casualness comes through clearly in the paraphrase.
- The slavery passage is blunt and should feel that way. Tocqueville isn't moralizing -- he's making a clinical observation about the limits of democratic empathy.
- The closing lines about nations becoming more compassionate as they become more alike -- clean, hopeful, but measured.

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 086

- Chapter 086: Democracy makes social interaction simpler and more natural.
- Central comparison: English social stiffness (driven by anxiety about status in a fluid class system) vs. American ease (driven by genuine equality).
- The key insight: aristocracy of birth produces awkward but stable social relations; aristocracy of money produces the most anxious and hostile social dynamics; democracy produces the most relaxed.
- Light, observational chapter -- more travelogue than philosophy. Keep the anecdotal quality.
- The Englishmen meeting at the Antipodes is a wonderful opening image -- preserve it.
