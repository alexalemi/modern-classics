STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Hamilton quotation from Federalist No. 71: kept it substantially intact but trimmed some of the more repetitive phrases. The quotation is powerful enough to stand mostly as-is.
- The "New York versus Ohio" courtroom scene: gave it the dramatic weight it deserves. This is one of Tocqueville's most vivid moments -- the clerk simply announcing the case and the reader being struck by its gravity.
- Seven judges passage: preserved the full rhetorical force. The list of what the Court defends (Union from states, states from Union, public from private, order from innovation) is one of Tocqueville's best parallel constructions.
- The theoretical section on what makes the American system unique: kept it grounded by preserving the concrete historical examples (Philip of Macedon, Holland, Swiss Confederation, Articles of Confederation).
- The passage about "the new word which will one day designate this novel invention does not yet exist" -- preserved this lovely meta-observation about language lagging behind political innovation.
- Footnotes: integrated the Greece/Holland/Swiss examples from footnotes directly into the text. Dropped purely bibliographic references.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Federal courts procedure section: matter-of-fact, analytical, explaining a clever legal mechanism
- Supreme Court passage: builds to genuine rhetorical power -- Tocqueville is clearly awed by what the Americans created
- Comparison of federal vs. state constitutions: structured as a clear argument, like an editorial making a case
- The "unique among confederations" section: the most theoretical/abstract passage; kept it grounded and readable by emphasizing the practical difference (governing individuals vs. governing states)
- Final paragraph about the framers: warm, admiring, almost elegiac

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "the framers" for the constitutional convention delegates (consistent with ch. 013)
- "the Union" for the federal entity
- "sovereignty" as a key structural term
- "the Supreme Court" -- capitalized consistently
- "the Constitution" -- capitalized when referring to the U.S. Constitution
- "federal" / "state" -- lowercase as adjectives per modern convention
- "the Articles of Confederation" -- used once to identify "the first American Confederation"
- "incomplete national government" -- Tocqueville's key formulation for what the American system actually is

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 015

- Chapter 015 covers the advantages of the federal system -- small nations vs. large nations, war as a threat to confederations, and why the system works specifically for Americans
- The small-nations-vs-large-nations argument is one of Tocqueville's most sweeping passages; keep the scope and grandeur while breaking up the longer sentences
- The War of 1812 example with Connecticut and Massachusetts refusing to send militia is a vivid concrete story -- tell it clearly
- The passage about Mexico copying the American Constitution verbatim but failing is a pointed observation -- preserve the sharpness
- The section about state sovereignty being stronger than federal sovereignty in practice is a crucial insight that reverberates through the rest of the book
- The final paragraph about America's geographic advantage is almost wistful -- Tocqueville envies the position
- Watch for the passage about small nations being "the cradle of political liberty" -- a famous formulation
