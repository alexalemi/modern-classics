STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is one of Tocqueville's most important chapters. Preserved the full argument carefully.
- Structure: Used section breaks (***) to separate the main argument about revolutions from the section about opinion change, and to set off the magnificent closing passage.
- The middle class as stabilizing force -- one of Tocqueville's most prescient observations. Kept it vivid and concrete.
- The commerce-as-anti-revolutionary passage is tight and beautiful in the original; kept it flowing.
- The Luther example -- kept; it's striking and illustrative.
- The "empty phantom of public opinion" passage -- preserved; it's prophetic and eerily modern.
- The closing passage about stagnation being worse than revolution -- this is one of the great rhetorical passages of the entire book. Preserved its full power and urgency. The final image (humanity ceasing to advance despite constant motion) is devastating.
- The long footnote about intermediate periods favorable to intellectual revolution -- integrated as a parenthetical section, since it contains an important idea.
- The passage about the revolutionary leader who lifts people for a moment but finds them falling back by their own weight -- kept the metaphor; it's vivid and effective.
- "Against his poetry, their prose" -- preserved this brilliant antithesis.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The first half is analytical and precise -- laying out the argument methodically.
- The middle section on opinion change is more observational, drawing on Tocqueville's fieldwork.
- The closing is passionate, almost prophetic -- Tocqueville drops the analytical mask and speaks from deep personal conviction. Preserved the emotional intensity.
- The line "I dread -- and I'll admit it" captures Tocqueville at his most personal and honest.
