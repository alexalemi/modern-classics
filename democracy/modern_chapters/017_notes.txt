STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Tocqueville's distinction between "great parties" and "small parties" is his own analytical framework. Kept these exact terms since they're clear and powerful.
- The party name reversal footnote (1861 translator's note): integrated as a parenthetical explaining that the names eventually switched meanings. Phrased to be useful without being distracting.
- Jackson and the Bank: kept Tocqueville's framing -- the people attacked the Bank not out of economic understanding but because it was an immovable object in a society that insists on being able to move everything. This is a great Tocqueville insight.
- The wealthy-citizen vignette (crossing town, meeting the shoemaker): preserved the specificity and irony. This is Tocqueville at his most observational and witty.
- "as desperate to hide his fortune as a Jewish merchant in the Middle Ages" -- preserved Tocqueville's historical comparison as-is; it's a historical reference to medieval persecution, not an antisemitic remark. The comparison highlights the absurdity of wealth-hiding in a supposedly free society.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The opening theoretical section (great parties vs. small parties) is more abstract -- kept it analytical but punchy.
- The historical section (Federalists vs. Republicans) shifts to narrative mode -- maintained storytelling pace.
- The wealthy-citizen section at the end is vintage Tocqueville fieldwork -- kept it vivid, specific, ironic.
- The final one-line transition ("The two main weapons parties use...") sets up the next two chapters perfectly.

RECURRING TERMS

- "great parties" / "small parties" -- Tocqueville's own framework, maintain consistently
- "the democratic party" -- lowercase, referring to the democratic movement/tendency, not the modern Democratic Party
- "the Bank" -- the Bank of the United States (Second Bank), capitalized as Tocqueville uses it
- "equality" / "independence" -- the two moral principles at stake in the Federalist-Republican divide
- "the Union" -- maintained for the federal entity
