STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Voice: Maintained first person, kept Tocqueville's direct and observational tone. "I happened to be staying..." becomes vivid scene-setting.
- The prayer passage: Kept it substantially intact with only light modernization. It's a primary source document and its rhetorical force works as-is. Changed "Man" to "humanity" where generic, kept the biblical cadence.
- The Poland meeting scene: Preserved as a concrete anecdote -- one of Tocqueville's great strengths as a writer.
- Chester County witness anecdote: Kept as a short, punchy illustration. Dropped the full newspaper quotation footnote as purely documentary.
- Catholicism discussion: Preserved Tocqueville's counterintuitive argument (Catholicism = more democratic than Protestantism) clearly. This is one of his best analytical moves.
- The closing rhetorical flourish ("Despotism may govern without faith, but liberty cannot") -- modernized to "Authoritarianism can rule without faith, but freedom cannot." Strong, punchy ending preserved.
- Broke long paragraphs into clearer modern structures while preserving the argumentative flow.
- Footnotes: Integrated the note about Catholic population growth being driven by immigration by referencing "more than a million" (Tocqueville's own figure). Dropped the 1868 Edinburgh Review statistical footnote. Dropped the constitutional quotation footnotes about clergy exclusion from office as purely documentary. Kept the education footnote context by noting clergy's role in education was dropped as tangential.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The religion section walks a fine line -- Tocqueville genuinely admires American religiosity but he's analyzing it as a political phenomenon, not endorsing it theologically. Preserved this analytical distance.
- The Cabanis passage: Kept the sarcastic edge. This is Tocqueville at his most pointed -- dismissing French materialist philosophers. "When they return from that trip, we can hear what they have to say" preserves his dry wit.
- The closing passage about French republicans attacking religion is one of Tocqueville's most passionate political arguments. Kept the urgency and directness.
