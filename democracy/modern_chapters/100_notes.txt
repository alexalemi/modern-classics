STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Chapter title: "captious" modernized to "touchy" -- captures the meaning without the archaic feel
- The comic dialogue scene with an American demanding praise -- preserved the back-and-forth rhythm exactly, it's one of Tocqueville's funniest passages
- Dropped the footnote reference to Appendix U (purely scholarly)
- The English/American contrast is drawn with wonderful sharpness -- preserved the parallel structure
- "His pride needs no fuel -- it feeds on itself" -- compressed Tocqueville's longer formulation for punch
- The final paragraph about aristocratic courtiers displaying the same vanity as democratic citizens -- this is Tocqueville's signature move of showing that supposedly "democratic" traits appear wherever conditions are similar, regardless of the political system

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter is comic-observational -- Tocqueville is genuinely amused by American vanity
- But his analysis is serious: vanity comes from insecurity, which comes from equality's instability
- The English comparison is drawn with a Frenchman's dry appreciation for both nations' absurdities
- Preserved the shift from personal anecdote to structural analysis
