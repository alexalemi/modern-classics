Next Chapter Notes — Chapter 004 (Social Conditions of the Anglo-Americans)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "social condition" — kept as Tocqueville's term; used consistently throughout
- "equality of conditions" — maintained from ch. 000; used when Tocqueville invokes the concept broadly
- "the law of descent" → "inheritance law" throughout; clearer for modern readers
- "law of partible inheritance" → "equal inheritance" or "equal division"
- "law of entail" → "entail" with brief contextual explanation on first use
- "primogeniture" → "eldest son inherits everything" (explained inline)
- "right of primogeniture" → "the eldest son's right to inherit"
- "landed proprietors" → "landowners" or "plantation owners" (South-specific context)
- "pecuniary" → "financial"
- "esprit de famille" → "family spirit" (translated the French naturally)
- "commingled with the general mass" → "blended into the general population"
- "opulent" → "wealthy"
- "preternatural" → "supernatural"
- "uninstructed" → "uneducated"
- "scarcely" → "barely," "hardly," or "almost no one" (context-dependent)
- "calling" → "career"
- "eminently" → "fundamentally"
- Slaves / slavery — kept "enslaved people" when referring to individuals; "slavery" for the institution
- "tenants depending on them" → "tenant farmers" / "dependents" / "patronage network"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Footnotes: Integrated footnote *a (definition of inheritance law) as a parenthetical paragraph. Integrated footnote *b (small vs. large landowners) into the main text. Footnote *c (long note about land preferences) kept as a parenthetical paragraph since it contains substantive analysis. Footnotes *d and *e: *d was purely bibliographic (dropped); *e was Reeve's 1874 translator's note about western cities — dropped as dated editorial comment.
- Section break between main body and "Political Consequences" subsection maintained as a heading
- Long paragraph on inheritance law's mechanical effects: preserved the building-to-a-climax structure ("ground down to the fine, shifting sand") — this is one of Tocqueville's best metaphors
- The two-types-of-equality passage near the end is famous; preserved its rhetorical force while modernizing vocabulary
- "Men" → "people" or "citizens" throughout except where context is clearly gendered

TONE CALIBRATION

- Chapter is more analytical than the introduction — maintained the "brilliant political analyst" register
- The inheritance law passages required careful handling: Tocqueville is making a technical legal argument accessible through vivid metaphor. Kept the metaphors vivid, simplified the legal logic.
- The western states passage has a sense of wonder — preserved that energy
- The education section has Tocqueville's characteristic dryness — "their education ends at the age when ours begins" — preserved these sharp observations
- The closing passage on two kinds of equality is rhetorically powerful — preserved the antithetical structure

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "social conditions" — Tocqueville's framework for this chapter; keep using consistently
- "inheritance law" / "equal inheritance" — established as modern equivalent of "law of descent" / "partible inheritance"
- "equality" vs. "freedom" — the tension Tocqueville identifies in the closing section; these are his key opposing terms going forward
- "sovereignty of the people" — introduced at chapter's end; will be central in next chapter
- "the democratic principle" — Tocqueville's shorthand for the force of equality; keep this phrase
- Anglo-Americans — kept Tocqueville's term in chapter title; used "Americans" in body text where natural

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 005

- Chapter 005 (Sovereignty of the People) follows directly from the closing of this chapter. The concept of popular sovereignty has been introduced; next chapter will develop it fully.
- Maintain the established tension between equality and freedom.
- "Sovereignty of the people" = "popular sovereignty" or "the power of the people" — use interchangeably for variety.
- Continue using "freedom" over "liberty" in most contexts.
- Watch for Tocqueville's comparisons to European systems — make them accessible.
- The register should stay analytical but may become more concrete as Tocqueville describes how popular sovereignty actually works in practice.
