KEY ARGUMENTS PRESERVED

- The Ohio River comparison: Kentucky (slave state) vs. Ohio (free state) -- This is one of Tocqueville's most famous passages and one of the most effective rhetorical devices in the book. The left bank/right bank contrast works like a controlled experiment. Preserved with full visual power.
- The economic argument against slavery: enslaved labor appears free but is actually more expensive than free labor when you account for maintenance costs across the entire lifespan. A sophisticated economic analysis that presages modern arguments.
- The cultural argument: slavery doesn't just impoverish the economy; it corrupts the slaveholder's character, creating a culture that scorns labor itself. The Kentuckian portrait is vivid and damning.
- The inheritance/primogeniture argument: The law of primogeniture created an aristocratic idle class that depended on slave labor because no white person would work. Abolishing primogeniture created the need for free labor, which exposed slavery's inefficiency.
- The mechanics of gradual abolition: Tocqueville's brilliant analysis of how Northern abolition actually worked -- not by freeing enslaved people but by making them financially burdensome and incentivizing their sale southward. "Abolition didn't free the enslaved person so much as transfer him from one master to another, from the North to the South."
- The climate and crop argument: Why the South is structurally more dependent on slave labor (cotton, sugar, tobacco demand year-round labor; grain only needs seasonal workers).
- The population trap: The Black population is so large in the South that the Northern model of gradual emancipation is explosive rather than manageable.

SENSITIVE CONTENT HANDLING

- "the negro is free, but he can share neither the rights, nor the pleasures..." -> This devastating rhetorical accumulation from Part III carries its force into Part IV's analysis. The modern language makes the injustice even clearer than the Victorian original.
- The Ohio comparison: Tocqueville's description of the enslaved people "loitering in the half-desert fields" -> "working listlessly in half-empty fields" -- slightly less condescending while preserving the visual contrast.
- "many of them perish miserably" (about free Black people in the North) -> kept. The Philadelphia mortality statistics are devastating evidence.
- The argument that Southern whites "might" need Black labor because of climate -> Tocqueville explicitly dismisses this as a self-serving excuse, which is preserved and even slightly sharpened ("so conveniently flattering to the laziness of Southern whites").

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The Ohio River passage opens with cinematic scene-setting and maintains the split-screen visual throughout. Treated as Tocqueville at his most journalistic -- punchy, vivid, observational.
- The economic analysis (free vs. slave labor costs) -- broken into clearer logical steps while maintaining the argument's force. The original has several 80+ word sentences that obscure the logic.
- The Kentucky/Ohio character contrast -- presented as a direct portrait comparison. The Kentuckian passage in particular has great energy and reads like a character sketch.
- The New York abolition example -- presented as a clear narrative. Tocqueville's step-by-step explanation of how gradual abolition actually transferred enslaved people southward is one of his most analytically brilliant passages.
- Footnotes: The sugar economy footnote condensed into a bracketed aside. The Ohio canal footnote similarly condensed. The population statistics kept as a clean bracketed summary.
