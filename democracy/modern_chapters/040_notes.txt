STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is a long, dense chapter covering (1) uneven state growth threatening the Union, (2) the trend toward federal weakness, (3) internal improvements controversy, (4) Native American treaty betrayal, (5) western land disputes, (6) the Bank war, (7) the tariff crisis and Nullification. Maintained analytical register throughout.
- Broke many mega-sentences, especially the long semicolon chains about the South's grievances and the bank controversy.
- Preserved Tocqueville's direct quotation from Calhoun and the South Carolina petition verbatim -- these are historical primary sources.
- Kept the sharp observation about war: "If kings and peoples throughout history had always acted on their true interests alone, the word 'war' would barely be known to humanity."
- The passage about Americans rushing toward their destiny is one of Tocqueville's best -- kept the rhetorical punch intact.
- Integrated footnote content where useful (e.g., noting the bank's charter details, Virginia's mediating role) but dropped purely bibliographic references.
- The Native American passage required careful modernization: "savages" removed, "Indian tribes" -> "Native American tribes," but preserved Tocqueville's sharp point about federal betrayal -- treating tribes as sovereign nations for treaties then abandoning them as subjects to state tyranny.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Analytical and journalistic throughout. The chapter reads as political analysis with occasional editorial sharpness.
- The bank section captures Tocqueville's characteristic move: taking a specific contemporary controversy and showing how it illustrates a deeper structural tendency.
- The tariff/Nullification section maintains the legal-analytical tone while making the constitutional arguments accessible.
