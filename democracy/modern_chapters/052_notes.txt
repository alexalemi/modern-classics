- "beacon-lights grow dim" -> "brightest lights grow dim" (kept the metaphor, simplified)
- "bequeathed by them to their descendants" -> "passed on to their descendants"
- "ceremonial pomp" -> "ceremonial display"
- "reluctant sufferance to the pleasures of literature" -> "grudgingly tolerated the pleasures of literature"
- "the spirit of gain is always on the stretch" -> "the pursuit of profit never let up"
- "fertilize without difficulty" -> "prosper without great difficulty"
- "the labors of the intellect" -> "intellectual labor"
- "kindred habits" -> "shared habits"
- "enlarge in all directions the empire of the mind" -- kept this striking phrase nearly intact
- "singularly concurred" -> "converged"
- "a sect...celebrated for its talents and its extravagance" -> "a movement...celebrated for its brilliance and its extremism" (this is the Saint-Simonians; kept implicit rather than naming them, matching Tocqueville's own approach)
- "opulence or competency" -> "well-off and prosperous"
- "propensities" -> "tastes"
- "the humblest artisan" -> "the humblest worker"
- "prodigious activity" -> "frantic activity"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is a long chapter with a complex argument: (1) America's intellectual poverty is American, not democratic; (2) a thought experiment about democracy without freedom; (3) democracy with freedom produces broad intellectual culture. Gave each section clear structural breaks.
- The dark passage about "democracy without freedom" is one of Tocqueville's most vivid metaphors -- the suffocating dark room. Preserved it fully.
- "So let's stop looking at all democratic nations through the lens of the American people." -- Made this pivot sentence punchy and direct, matching Tocqueville's rhetorical shift.
- The Saint-Simonian reference kept as "a movement" -- adding the name would have required a distracting parenthetical.
- "Natural inequality will quickly assert itself" -- Tocqueville is being remarkably blunt here about meritocracy's limits; kept it direct.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The opening is analytical and somewhat critical of America -- maintained the candor.
- The dark passage about democracy-without-freedom shifts to something almost Gothic -- preserved the mood.
- The final sections are more optimistic, arguing that free democracy produces broad intellectual culture even if not elite culture -- kept this hopeful but measured.
