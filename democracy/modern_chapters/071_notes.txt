VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "civil associations" / "political associations" -- kept both terms, with "civil" generally replaced by "everyday" or "business" in running text where appropriate, but preserved when used in the formal contrast
- "chimerical" -> "naive"
- "prosecuting great undertakings" -> "carrying out large ones"
- "joint-stock account" -> "business partnerships"
- "the mother of action" -> "the foundation of all action" (consistent with 069's "foundation of all progress")
- "the great vassals of the Crown" -> "their great vassals" (kept the medieval comparison -- it's vivid)

- "perturb the State" -> "disrupting the state"
- "repugnance" -> "reluctance"
- "dexterous" -> "capable"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The chapter's central argument (political association teaches civil association) is rendered as a clear logical chain. Used "Here's how it works" to introduce the mechanism, giving it a journalistic feel.
- The "large free schools" metaphor -- kept. It's perfect.
- The long footnote about government discretion in banning associations -- integrated as a parenthetical paragraph. It's substantive and important: Tocqueville distinguishes between laws that ban specific associations (acceptable) and discretionary executive power to ban any association at will (unacceptable). This distinction remains extremely relevant.
- The self-quotation from Volume 1 near the end -- preserved. It's important that readers see Tocqueville engaging with his own earlier argument and nuancing it.
- The amputation metaphor at the end ("cut off a person's arm to save their life") -- preserved with only pronoun modernization. It's a perfect closing image.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This is one of the more complex argumentative chapters. The chain of reasoning is: political association -> skill in association -> civil association -> prosperity -> stability. Made sure each link in the chain is clear.
- The passage about governments preferring civil associations over political ones (because civil ones distract from politics) has a sharp, critical edge. Tocqueville is saying governments are being short-sighted. Preserved that sharpness.
- The closing paragraphs are carefully balanced -- Tocqueville acknowledges real dangers of unlimited political association while insisting the alternative is worse. Kept that balance.

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "the art of association" / "the principle of association" / "the spirit of association"
- "civil associations" vs. "political associations" -- the chapter's central distinction
- "large free schools" -- memorable metaphor
- The arm amputation metaphor -- may be referenced
