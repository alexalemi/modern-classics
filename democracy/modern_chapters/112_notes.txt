FOOTNOTE HANDLING
- No footnotes in this chapter

STYLISTIC DECISIONS
- This chapter builds a sustained argument: democratic minds → centralized power → uniform laws → individual disappears into the mass → everyone agrees on centralization even while disagreeing on everything else
- Kept the logical progression clear with strong paragraph breaks
- The passage about France is kept specific — Tocqueville is writing as a Frenchman for a French audience here; the directness is important
- The closing paragraph about this idea being "innate" and "a necessary product of the current state of humanity" — this is Tocqueville at his most deterministic; preserved the force of it
- The observation that people "argue endlessly about who should hold power but readily agree on what that power should look like" — sharpened this for maximum punch; it's one of the chapter's best insights

TONE CALIBRATION
- This chapter is more purely analytical than the military chapters; kept the tone like a political science essay
- Tocqueville's frustration with French political parties (they all want a powerful central state, they just disagree on who runs it) comes through clearly; let that edge show
- The passage about monarchs learning new ideas about their own power — Tocqueville is being subtly devastating here; preserved the irony

RECURRING TERMS
- "central power" / "centralized government" — primary terms for this section
- "intermediate powers" — the thing democracy destroys
- "uniformity" — key concept; democracy demands uniform rules
- "equality" — maintained as the driving force behind all these tendencies
- "the state" / "society" — used consistently to represent collective power vs. individual weakness
