Next Chapter Notes -- Chapter 084 (Chapter XX: Aristocracy from Manufactures)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "manufactures" -> "manufacturing" throughout (as noun)
- "fabrication" -> "making"
- "adroit" -> "dexterous" / "skilled"
- "industrious" -> "versatile" (in context, Tocqueville means broadly capable, not hard-working)
- "manners" -> "social norms" (in the phrase "laws and manners")
- "opulence" -> "wealth" / "riches"
- "serving-men" -> "dependents"
- "succor their distresses" -> "relieve their hardships"
- "debases" -> "degrades"
- "monstrous exception" -> kept "monstrous exception" (Tocqueville's own emphasis; still vivid)
- "in the bosom of democracy" -> "from the very heart of democracy"
- "the channel by which they will enter" -> "the gate through which they will enter"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is one of Tocqueville's most prescient chapters. The argument (division of labor creates a new industrial class system) anticipates Marx by a decade. Kept the analytical clarity sharp.
- The pin-factory passage (echoing Adam Smith, though uncited) is the chapter's central set piece. Preserved the rhetorical escalation: "What can you expect from someone who has spent twenty years making heads for pins?"
- "As the worker improves, the person degrades" -- kept this aphorism clean and punchy. It's one of Tocqueville's best lines.
- "One increasingly resembles the administrator of a vast empire; the other, a machine" -- modernized "brute" to "machine" since Tocqueville's point is about mechanization of the human, not animal comparison.
- The closing warning is the chapter's climax. Preserved the two-part structure: (1) this new aristocracy is the harshest ever but also the most limited; (2) but watch out -- this is how inequality gets back in.
- "There are members, but no body" -- kept this brilliant formulation exactly.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Tocqueville is at his most analytical and prophetic here. The tone combines detached sociological observation with mounting moral concern.
- The passage about workers being rendered "stationary" amid "universal movement" is devastating -- preserved the contrast.
- The final paragraph shifts from analysis to warning. The last sentence should feel like an alarm bell.

RECURRING TERMS

- "division of labor" -- kept as is (standard economics term, still current)
- "manufacturing aristocracy" -- Tocqueville's coinage for this chapter; kept consistently
- "territorial aristocracy" -> "old territorial aristocracy" (to clarify the contrast with the new industrial kind)

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 085

- Chapter 085 opens Book Three (Influence of Democracy on Manners/Customs). Needs a book heading.
- "Manners" in the book title means customs, social norms, interpersonal behavior -- NOT etiquette. Modernize the title.
- Chapter I argues that equality makes people more compassionate because they can imagine themselves in others' shoes. Aristocratic societies had rigid duty but no empathy across classes.
- The Madame de Sevigne passage (in French) is key evidence -- need to handle the untranslated French. Strategy: provide English paraphrase/summary rather than leaving it in French.
- The slavery passage at the end is important and nuanced -- slaveholders can be kind to equals but cruel to those they don't see as equals. Handle with care.
