- "a constrained and absent air" -> "a stiff, distracted manner"
- "moneyed aristocracy" -> "aristocracy of money"
- "ostensibly" -> "permanently" (in context)

- "the constitution of their country" -> "the structure of their society"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Chapter title: "Habitual Intercourse" -> "Everyday Interactions." The original sounds unintentionally comical to modern ears.
- The opening image (two Englishmen meeting at the ends of the earth and still being awkward) is wonderful -- preserved it vividly. Tocqueville is being gently funny here.
- The three-stage social model (birth aristocracy -> money aristocracy -> democracy) is the chapter's analytical spine. Made the transitions between stages clear.
- The passage about England's money aristocracy creating universal anxiety is sharp social observation -- people simultaneously climbing and defending. Kept the energy.
- The American contrast is simple and clean: "natural, frank, and open." Preserved the list without embellishing.
- "Two Americans are instant friends, simply because they're Americans" -- kept this punchy observation. It still rings true.
- The closing argument (Americans and English differ only in social structure, proving that society -- not biology -- determines behavior) is crisp and modern-sounding. Preserved it as the chapter's payoff.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Light, observational, slightly amused. This is Tocqueville the travel writer, comparing national characters with a sharp but affectionate eye.
- The England sections have gentle satire -- people terrified of accidental friendliness. Kept the humor.
- The America sections are admiring but not uncritical -- Americans are easy and natural, but sometimes "cool and serious." Preserved the balance.
