STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Chapter title: modernized for clarity -- "Why Americans Are So Serious -- and Why That Doesn't Stop Them from Acting Rashly"
- The drinking-at-home anecdote is one of Tocqueville's driest, funniest moments: "He can keep thinking about his business, and he can get respectably drunk by his own fireside" -- preserved the humor with minimal alteration
- The contrast between despotism's ignorance (told nothing) and democracy's ignorance (told everything) is brilliantly prescient -- kept the parallel structure sharp
- "Good enough" for "pretty well" -- slightly more modern and punchy
- The final line about "the habit of inattention" is Tocqueville at his most aphoristic -- preserved as-is

TONE CALIBRATION

- Lighter and more observational than the previous chapters -- more anecdotal, more wry
- The opening section about American entertainment preferences has an almost anthropological quality
- Tocqueville's genuine puzzlement about American seriousness comes through -- he finds it fascinating
- The pivot to rashness in the second half gives the chapter a satisfying dialectical structure
