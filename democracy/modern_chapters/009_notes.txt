STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Voice: The chapter opens with a strong authorial "I" -- kept direct and immediate. "I've set aside a separate chapter for the judicial branch because..."
- The three characteristics of judicial power (arbitration, specific cases, passivity) were kept as a clean numbered list. Tocqueville's systematic approach here is one of his strengths -- clarity preserved.
- The explanation of judicial review was made maximally clear for modern readers. This is arguably the most important constitutional concept in the chapter, and Tocqueville was explaining something genuinely novel in 1835. Modern readers take it for granted; the translation should help them see it fresh.
- The France/England/America three-way comparison is the chapter's analytical backbone. Kept each country's logic distinct and clear.
- The Article 75 section is one of Tocqueville's most indignant passages -- preserved the escalating frustration and the punchline about Americans and English refusing to believe him. This is prime Tocqueville: witty, outraged, and making a devastating point through personal anecdote.
- Footnotes: The translator's note about the amendment process was integrated into the text as a parenthetical. The purely bibliographic/historical footnote about specific amendments was condensed to its essential information. The Parliament of Paris references were kept for clarity.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The chapter overview is professional and direct -- sets up the stakes clearly
- The three-characteristics section is analytical and precise -- Tocqueville the political scientist
- The judicial review explanation builds steadily to the key insight -- maintained the logical momentum
