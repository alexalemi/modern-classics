VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "egotism" -> "selfishness" (consistent)
- "electioneering intrigues" -> "scheming"
- "calumnies" -> "mudslinging"
- "meannesses of candidates" -> "pandering of candidates"
- "the legislators of America" -> "America's founders" (context makes clear this refers to the constitutional framers)
- "magistrates" -> "elected officials" (Reeve's "magistrate" means any government official here)
- "opulent citizens" -> "wealthier citizens"
- "the lower classes" -> "ordinary people"
- "affability" -> "friendliness"
- "disinterestedness" -> "putting others first"
- "propensities which sever them" -> "instincts that pull them apart"
- "manners" -> "manner" (kept singular where it means personal demeanor, not customs)
- "effectual remedy" -> "effective remedy"
- "political freedom" / "political liberty" -> "political freedom" consistently

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The opening paragraph on despotism is one of Tocqueville's sharpest passages. The line "a despot easily forgives his subjects for not loving him, as long as they don't love each other" is devastating -- preserved nearly verbatim.
- "perverting the natural meaning of words" -> "twisting the natural meaning of words" -- kept this because it's a brilliantly modern-sounding observation about political language
- The road-building example is classic Tocqueville -- a concrete, homely illustration that makes an abstract point vivid. Kept it punchy.
- "Elected magistrates do not make the American democracy flourish; it flourishes because the magistrates are elective" -> rephrased to "Elected officials don't make American democracy work. American democracy works because its officials are elected." Preserved the chiasmus.
- The final one-line paragraph about France is a direct plea to his French audience. Kept it short and emphatic.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter is more energetic and argumentative than 066-067. Tocqueville is making his case for freedom as medicine. Matched that urgency.
- The section on wealthy citizens and pride has a quietly cutting quality -- Tocqueville is telling the rich they're doing it wrong. Preserved that edge.

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "free institutions" -- Tocqueville's umbrella term for elected local government, political rights, civic participation
- "self-interest" -- introduced here as a neutral/positive force when channeled correctly (sets up chapter 072)
- "political freedom" as the remedy for equality's problems
- The despotism/equality feedback loop -- may recur in later chapters

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 069

- Chapter 069 turns to civil associations (as opposed to political ones). The famous "Americans of all ages, all conditions" passage is here.
- Watch for the temperance society anecdote -- Tocqueville's wry comparison with France.
- The chapter builds to the declaration that "the science of association is the mother of science" -- preserve this aphorism.
- Government vs. associations is a key tension -- Tocqueville warns against government filling the role of private associations.
