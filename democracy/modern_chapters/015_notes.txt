STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The small-nations-vs-large-nations argument: preserved the sweeping philosophical scope while breaking up the Victorian mega-sentences. This is one of Tocqueville's most ambitious theoretical passages -- kept the grandeur while making it readable.
- "Small nations have therefore always been the cradle of political freedom" -- preserved this famous formulation nearly verbatim; it's too good to rewrite.
- The passage about passions intensifying in crowds: modernized but kept the insight vivid. Tocqueville anticipates crowd psychology decades before Gustave Le Bon.
- The War of 1812 militia example: told it as a clear narrative with the specific states (Connecticut, Massachusetts) and their specific arguments. This is Tocqueville at his most concrete.
- Mexico example: kept it sharp and pointed. Tocqueville's observation that Mexico copied the letter but not the spirit of the Constitution remains devastatingly apt.
- Civil War editorial note: integrated naturally into the text since it's historically relevant and confirms Tocqueville's prediction.
- The passage about state sovereignty being "natural" vs. federal sovereignty being "artificial" -- this is one of Tocqueville's most important structural insights, and it reads prophetically in light of the Civil War. Gave it appropriate weight.
- The closing paragraph: preserved the wistful, envious tone. Tocqueville admires America's position but knows Europe can't replicate it. The final sentence is beautifully simple -- kept it close to the original.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Philosophical sections (small vs. large nations): sweeping, confident, slightly melancholic -- Tocqueville the political theorist at full stretch
- Practical sections (War of 1812, Mexico): crisp, journalistic, almost amused by the absurdity of the militia standoff
- The passage about state sovereignty vs. federal sovereignty: Tocqueville at his most prophetic and penetrating -- the language of "natural" vs. "artificial" carries real analytical force
- Final paragraphs: personal, wistful, generous. Tocqueville genuinely admires what the Americans have built while recognizing it can't be exported
