KEY ARGUMENTS PRESERVED

- Tocqueville's comparison of ancient and modern slavery: In the ancient world, slaves and masters were the same race, so emancipation could fully integrate freed people. In the modern world, slavery is permanently linked to race, making its effects far harder to undo. This is one of his most important analytical distinctions -- preserved in full.
- The paradox of Northern prejudice: Racial prejudice is *worse* in states that have abolished slavery than in slave states, and worst of all in states that never had slavery. Tocqueville's explanation (proximity breeds anxiety; distance permits condescension) is brilliantly counterintuitive and still resonant.
- The "three prejudices" framework: prejudice of the master, prejudice of race, prejudice of color. Each layer makes the problem harder than the one before.
- The vicious circle: whites won't change their view of Black people until Black people change; Black people can't change as long as white views persist. Preserved from the footnote.
- The economic argument against slavery begins at the end of this section: slavery is bad not just for the enslaved but for the enslaver. This sets up the Ohio River comparison in Part IV.

SENSITIVE CONTENT HANDLING

- "His physiognomy is to our eyes hideous, his understanding weak, his tastes low" -- This is one of Tocqueville's most uncomfortable passages. He is describing white European perceptions, not endorsing them (the "to our eyes" framing), but it's still painful to read. Translated faithfully as "His appearance strikes our eyes as alien, his understanding we judge to be weak, his tastes we consider crude" -- making the subjectivity of the white gaze even more explicit while preserving his point about the depth of prejudice.
- "a being intermediate between man and the brutes" -> "a being somewhere between human and animal" -- kept because Tocqueville is describing the prejudice, not endorsing it, and softening it would weaken his argument about how extreme racial prejudice actually is.
- The passage about intermarriage and sexual relations in the North vs. South -- translated directly. Tocqueville's observation that Northern white men would sleep with Black women but refuse to marry them is a devastating insight into the psychology of racial prejudice.
- "the gates of Heaven are not closed against these unhappy beings" through the cemetery passage -- one of Tocqueville's most powerful rhetorical constructions. The accumulation (schools, theaters, hospitals, churches, cemeteries) builds to the devastating conclusion that inequality persists even in death. Preserved in full with the rhetorical escalation intact.

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 036

- Part IV opens with the famous Ohio River comparison (Kentucky vs. Ohio). This is one of Tocqueville's most vivid and influential passages -- essentially the same technique as a modern split-screen documentary. Preserve the visual contrast.
- The economic analysis of slavery continues: free labor vs. slave labor, the effect of slavery on the master's character, the influence on Southern vs. Northern commercial development.
- The mechanics of how abolition works state by state (New York example) -- Tocqueville explains how gradual emancipation actually transferred enslaved people southward rather than freeing them. This is a subtle and important argument.
- The discussion of why the South cannot easily abolish slavery: climate, crop types (cotton, sugar, tobacco vs. wheat), and above all the sheer size of the Black population in the South.
- Watch for the inheritance law argument (primogeniture -> aristocratic idleness -> dependence on slave labor).
