STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is the longest chapter in the batch; maintained narrative momentum by keeping paragraphs focused and transitions clear
- The Muhammad/Gospels comparison: presented straightforwardly as Tocqueville's argument without editorial hedging; the point (religions that mix spiritual and temporal authority struggle in democratic ages) is analytical, not polemical
- Roman Empire passage: streamlined the historical sweep while keeping the essential argument (universal political equality under the Caesars prepared people for Christianity's universal message)
- The post-Roman fragmentation passage: kept the vivid detail about saints and angels becoming quasi-idolatrous; Tocqueville is making a sociological observation about how social structure shapes religious practice
- Footnote about Roman Catholicism: integrated as a parenthetical in the text rather than a separate note; it's a qualifying observation, not a major argument
- American clergy section at the end: this is Tocqueville at his most admiring; kept the tone warm and specific
- "Unable to subdivide God himself, they multiplied the divine agents" -- preserved this sharp formulation exactly

TONE CALIBRATION

- Opening sections are serious and analytical -- Tocqueville is making his strongest case for why religion matters in democracies
- The "if people lack faith, they must serve" line -- one of the chapter's most provocative claims; kept it direct and unhedged
- The practical advice sections (religions should simplify forms, stay within spiritual sphere) have an almost consultative tone -- like a strategist advising religious leaders
- The closing section on American clergy is observational and respectful -- Tocqueville genuinely admires how American religion works
- Final sentence -- reframed from passive/abstract to active: "religion sustains a winning struggle against its most dangerous adversary"
