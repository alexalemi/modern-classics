STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Preserved Tocqueville's first-person observations throughout: "I met an old man in the South," "I have to be honest," "When I see the order of nature overturned"
- The old man anecdote (father who enslaved his own children) -- kept the emotional power intact, modernized language while preserving the gut-punch impact
- Tocqueville's prediction about racial conflict -- translated faithfully without softening. His view that abolition would increase white hostility is stated plainly.
- The Liberia passage -- modernized while keeping Tocqueville's ironic observations about the "strange twist of fortune"
- The passage about ancient vs. modern slavery -- this is one of Tocqueville's most devastating arguments; preserved the logical structure and rhetorical force
- The final paragraph's editorial note from the Reeve translation -- integrated as a brief summary rather than a formal footnote, since it provides important historical context (Civil War, 13th Amendment) that helps modern readers

SENSITIVE CONTENT HANDLING

- Tocqueville's predictions about racial war and potential extermination -- translated faithfully. These were his honest (and in some ways prescient) observations.
- His argument that the South is "trapped" by slavery -- presented as his analysis, not endorsed. The modern language makes it clear he's describing a system's internal logic, not justifying it.
- His comment that freed Black Americans would "abuse" their freedom -- kept as-is in the final line; this is one of Tocqueville's blind spots that modern language makes more visible.
- The paternalistic framing ("can they be condemned for using whatever means...") -- translated faithfully. The modern English makes the moral weight of this argument more apparent to contemporary readers.
