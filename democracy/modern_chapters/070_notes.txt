STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The opening paragraph about newspapers as advisors is elegant and prescient. Kept the personification ("an advisor that doesn't need to be sought out") -- it's vivid and translates perfectly to modern media.
- The beacon metaphor ("scattered minds, which had long searched for each other in the dark, finally meet and unite") -- preserved. This is beautiful writing and remarkably prophetic of internet-era dynamics.
- The footnote about aristocratic societies and decentralized administration -- integrated as a parenthetical paragraph. It's a useful clarification that doesn't deserve to be lost.
- The structural argument (more local government = more newspapers) required careful rendering to keep it clear. Broke it into digestible steps.
- The final reflection about newspapers as associations representing their readers -- remarkably modern. Preserved its power.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter is more analytical and structural than the previous ones. Tocqueville is building a chain of logical connections (associations need newspapers, newspapers need local government, local government needs democracy). Kept the logical progression clear and readable.
- The prescience of this chapter is striking -- Tocqueville is essentially describing the media ecosystem as a function of democratic social structure. Let the ideas speak for themselves without calling attention to how prophetic they are.
