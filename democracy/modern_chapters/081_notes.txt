STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Chapter title heavily modernized: the original is unwieldy ("That In Times Marked By Equality Of Conditions And Sceptical Opinions, It Is Important To Remove To A Distance The Objects Of Human Actions"). New title captures the core argument in plain language.
- The opening paragraph's argument (religious ages -> long-term thinking -> worldly success) is clean and powerful -- preserved its logical structure but made each step crisper.
- The paragraph about democracy intensifying the danger uses vivid imagery (visions of sudden fortunes, luck haunting the mind) -- kept this energy.
- The closing argument (teaching long-term thinking as a backdoor to faith) is one of Tocqueville's most interesting observations. Preserved its subtlety -- it's pragmatic, almost Machiavellian.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This is Tocqueville as strategic advisor. The tone is prescriptive: "here's what leaders should do." Kept it direct and urgent.
- The passage about skeptical ages encouraging short-term thinking feels remarkably contemporary. Let the modern relevance speak for itself without editorializing.
- The final sentence about the "long and roundabout path to faith" has a wonderful intellectual elegance -- preserved it as the chapter's payoff.

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 082

- Chapter 082 shifts to a completely different topic: the dignity of labor in democratic society.
- Key argument: in democracies, all honest work is honorable because everyone works (even the president). In aristocracies, work for money is quietly shameful.
- Short chapter -- keep it tight and punchy.
- Watch for the observation about rich Americans fleeing to Europe to escape the obligation to work -- vintage Tocqueville social observation.
