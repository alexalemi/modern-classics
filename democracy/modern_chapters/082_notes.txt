STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Short, punchy chapter. Tocqueville makes one clean argument: in democracies everyone works, including the president, so all work is dignified. Kept the structure tight.
- The observation about rich Americans fleeing to Europe is a gem -- placed it prominently at the end of paragraph one.
- The paragraph about aristocratic officials hiding their profit motive is delightfully satirical -- preserved the dry humor.
- The closing line ("Every honest profession is honorable") echoes the chapter title -- preserved this as a clean payoff.
- "He is paid for commanding, other men for obeying orders" -- kept the parallel structure, just modernized slightly.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Light, observational, almost amused. Tocqueville is noting something he finds interesting and admirable about American culture.
- The aristocratic comparison is gently mocking -- people pretending they don't care about money. Kept the irony.

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 083

- Chapter 083 is longer and more analytical: why Americans gravitate toward commerce and industry rather than agriculture.
- Contains two substantial footnotes that should be integrated.
- Key observations: farming is too slow for democratic temperaments; even the rich in democracies go into business; Americans treat farming itself as a business; the country is prone to economic panics because everyone is tied to commerce.
- The passage about American infrastructure (Hudson to Mississippi, railroads) is vivid and specific -- preserve it.
- The "endemic disease" of commercial panics is a prophetic observation -- keep it sharp.
