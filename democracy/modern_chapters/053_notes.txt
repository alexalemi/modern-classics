  1. Democratic science favors facts over theory
  2. Three levels of science
  3. America excels at practical science, ignores pure theory
  4. Democracy's restlessness vs. contemplation
  5. Revolution vs. democratic agitation (French example)
  6. Active life vs. contemplative life
  7. Two kinds of scientific passion (utilitarian vs. pure love of truth)
  8. The Pascal passage -- emotional peak
  9. Aristocratic vs. democratic ideals of science (Archimedes quote)
  10. Democratic science serves the body; aristocratic science serves the mind
  11. The steamship as proof
  12. Policy recommendation: governments should support pure science
  13. The China cautionary tale
  14. Closing warning

- The Pascal passage is treated with full emotional weight -- "I stand amazed" is kept, and the structure of watching Pascal destroy himself for truth is preserved.
- The Archimedes/Plutarch quote is kept intact -- it's too good to paraphrase.
- The China passage is turned into a clear narrative: arrived -> impressed -> discovered the loss -> explained the stagnation -> drew the lesson.
- The final line is devastating: "while some nations have their civilization torn from them by force, others trample it underfoot all by themselves." Kept it punchy and final.
- "Here's my bottom line." -- Used as a pivot to the concluding summary, matching the Atlantic-essay register.
