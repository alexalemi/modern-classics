STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Register shift: This chapter moves from the geographical/landscape voice of Ch. 001 into historical narrative and political argument. Used a more essayistic, analytical register while keeping the same journalistic energy.
- The opening analogy (infant/nation): Preserved the structure of the argument -- it's a strong rhetorical move. Modernized the language but kept the building-block logic: person -> nation -> America is the unique case.
- "The entire man is, so to speak, to be seen in the cradle of the child" -> "In a sense, the entire person is already visible in the cradle." Gender-neutralized per text analysis guidelines.
- "Readers of this book will find the germ of all that is to follow": Kept this direct address -- Tocqueville is telling you this is the most important chapter. Worth preserving that confidence.
- Virginia section: Kept it brisk and pointed. Tocqueville is making a sharp contrast (Virginia = gold seekers and slavery, New England = ideas and democracy). Let the structure do the work.
- Stith quotation: Integrated the substance of footnote b (describing the dissolute early settlers) directly into the text as a quoted excerpt, attributed to "the historian Stith." This adds color and supports Tocqueville's argument without interrupting flow.
- Slavery introduction: Combined footnote d (date and Dutch ship detail) into the main text. Used "enslaved Africans" per text analysis guidelines. Kept Tocqueville's powerful indictment of slavery's effects intact -- it's one of the strongest passages in the chapter.
- New England section: This is the emotional heart of the chapter. Gave it room to breathe. Tocqueville is genuinely moved by the Pilgrims' story, and the prose should convey that.
- Morton quotations (three long passages): Kept these in their original early-modern English, lightly cleaned up for readability (e.g., "onely" -> "only," minor punctuation fixes) but preserved the period flavor. These are primary documents -- Tocqueville is using them for rhetorical effect, and they work. The archaic language IS the point.
- Plymouth Rock footnote (h): This is Tocqueville's own wonderful aside about the rock as relic. Integrated it into the main text as "Tocqueville himself noted..." since it reveals his sensibility and is genuinely interesting. Different approach from Ch. 001 where it was dropped -- reconsidered per the note.
- Mayflower Compact: Lightly modernized the language for clarity while preserving the formal, covenantal tone. Named it explicitly ("This was the Mayflower Compact") since modern readers should know what they're reading.
- Footnote i (other colonies' social contracts): Integrated as a brief follow-up sentence after the Compact.
- Bibliographic footnotes (a, c, f, g): Dropped -- purely scholarly references. Substantive content from a (crown's fifth of gold/silver) was too minor to integrate. Content from c (rich capitalists came later) was too minor.
- Footnote e (list of New England states): Integrated directly into the text as a parenthetical.
- Footnote g (translator's note about Leiden): Background about the Pilgrims in Leiden is already clear from Morton's narrative; dropped.
- The closing line: "A democracy more perfect than any which antiquity had dreamt of, started in full size and panoply from the midst of an ancient feudal society." This is a showstopper. Kept it as the final sentence, modernizing "dreamt" -> "dreamed" and "panoply" -> "fully formed" but preserving the dramatic contrast. This is Tocqueville at his most powerful -- the payoff of the whole chapter.
