Next Chapter Notes — Chapter 005 (Sovereignty of the People)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "sovereignty of the people" → "popular sovereignty" in chapter title and most uses; alternated with "the power of the people" and "the people are the ultimate source of power" for variety
- "the doctrine of the sovereignty of the people" → "the doctrine of popular sovereignty" — kept "doctrine" as it's appropriate here
- "the elective qualification" → "voting rights" / "the right to vote"
- "universal suffrage" — kept as-is; still the standard term
- "venal suffrages" → "bought-and-paid-for votes"
- "satellites of power" → "a dictator's cronies"
- "exigency of the occasion" → "the situation demands"
- "ostensibly" → "openly"
- "the higher orders" → "the upper classes" / "the old elite"
- "without a murmur" → "without protest"
- "thenceforth" → "had become"
- "emanate" → "derive"
- "the body of electors" → not needed (chapter 006 may use this)
- Footnote *a: purely bibliographic (Appendix H reference) — dropped

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is a shorter, more focused chapter — kept the translation tight and punchy
- The opening paragraph about "the will of the nation" being abused is powerful rhetoric; preserved its force while making the examples concrete and vivid
- "the law of laws" — kept this phrase; it's a memorable Tocquevillian formulation
- The passage about voting rights expanding inevitably is one of Tocqueville's sharpest observations; preserved the logical chain and the sense of inevitability
- The Maryland example — kept without additional context since the point (aristocratic founders → first to go democratic) is clear from the text
- The closing paragraph's rhythm — "society governs itself, for itself" — preserved this powerful formulation
- Maintained contrast between American openness about popular sovereignty vs. other nations' fictions/concealment

TONE CALIBRATION

- More argumentative and direct than the previous chapter — Tocqueville is making a forceful case about popular sovereignty as the foundation of everything
- The passage about falling elites pursuing self-interest reads almost like modern political commentary — let that contemporary feel come through
- The "ironclad rule" about voting expansion is Tocqueville at his most prophetic — kept the language confident and declarative

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "popular sovereignty" — now established as the primary modern term
- "universal suffrage" — kept as-is
- "the democratic cause" / "the democratic impulse" — Tocqueville's terms for the movement toward equality
- "townships" — will be central in the next chapter; keep consistent
- "the upper classes" for Tocqueville's "higher orders"
- "the mother country" — kept for England; clear and natural

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 006

- Chapter 006 begins Tocqueville's detailed examination of state and local government, starting with townships. This is where his analysis gets most granular and institutional.
- "Township" — keep this term consistently; Tocqueville will compare it to French communes and English tithings
- "Selectmen" — introduce with context on first use (elected town officials), then use the term naturally
- "Town meeting" — keep as-is; this is the standard American term
- The chapter will compare American and French/European local government structures extensively. Make these comparisons accessible to readers who don't know French administrative divisions.
- "Commune" → "local community" or "town" when Tocqueville uses it for the French equivalent
- "Arrondissement" → may need brief gloss (French administrative district)
- "Magistrate" in this context often means any local official, not just a judge — translate accordingly
- The register should shift toward more concrete, institutional description while maintaining the analytical voice
- Tocqueville's key argument: townships are the school of democracy — make this point land clearly
- "Manners" → "customs" / "social norms" / "habits" — never etiquette
