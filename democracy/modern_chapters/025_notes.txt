Next Chapter Notes -- Chapter 025 (Chapter XV: Power of the Majority, Part I)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "preponderant" -> "dominant"
- "irresistible" -> kept in most places (Tocqueville uses it constantly and deliberately to describe majority power)
- "celerity" -> "speed" / "immediately"
- "caprices" -> "whims" / "every passing impulse"
- "propensities" -> "impulses"
- "amelioration" -> "improvement" / "reform" / "progress"
- "sagacious" -> "deliberate"
- "penitentiaries" -> "prisons"
- "delinquent" -> "inmates"
- "salutary" -> "promising" (context-dependent)
- "mutability" -> "instability" / "constant changing"
- "pledged delegates" -- kept and explained clearly; this is Tocqueville describing the practice of binding mandates/instructions given to elected representatives
- "omnipotence of the majority" -> "near-total power" / "unlimited power"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Tone shift: This chapter begins the pivot from "advantages of democracy" to "dangers of democracy." The opening is analytical but the undertone grows increasingly ominous. Made the final line of the first section -- "This situation is dangerous in itself and ominous for the future" -- land with weight.
- The footnote about the federal vs. state government distinction: Integrated as a paragraph after the opening statement, since it's an important clarification (Tocqueville is talking about state governments, not the federal system).
- The Massachusetts footnote: Integrated parenthetically into the text.
- The "king can do no wrong" parallel: Preserved this comparison between the old French/English doctrine and American attitudes toward the majority -- it's a pointed analogy.
- Prison reform anecdote: Made it vivid and concrete -- a perfect case study of majority-driven enthusiasm that burns hot then fades. The contrast between the new and old prisons is Tocqueville at his most effective as a reporter.
- The passage about minorities that can never become majorities (aristocracies): Kept this tight and clear -- it's an important structural observation about why American politics works differently from European politics.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Opening: Measured, analytical, setting up the structural argument
- Middle (moral authority of majority): Intellectual, slightly provocative -- the idea that equality is being applied to intelligence itself
- Final section (instability): Increasingly alarmed, concrete -- the prison example makes the abstract argument tangible
- Overall: The chapter reads as a careful indictment building its case piece by piece

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "irresistible" -- Tocqueville's favorite word for majority power; keep using it
- "moral authority" vs. "actual power" of the majority -- his key distinction (the majority rules not just by force but by persuasion)
- "instability" -- the built-in flaw of democratic legislation
- "pledged delegates" -- the mechanism by which representative democracy slides toward direct democracy
- "the majority's unlimited power" -- the chapter's central concept; use consistently

  4. The Pennsylvania polling booth anecdote -- integrate from the footnote, it's one of the most powerful concrete examples in the book
  5. The "fetters and headsmen" passage about democratic despotism controlling minds, not bodies -- the single most famous passage
  6. The "sovereign says" passage -- preserve its chilling power
  7. The section on American sycophancy and the death of honest criticism
  8. The Hamilton and Jefferson quotes -- keep substantially intact
