STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Register shift: This chapter moves from historical narrative into legal/institutional analysis. Kept the journalistic voice but leaned into a more analytical, argumentative register -- like a feature piece on constitutional history.
- The Connecticut Code passages: These are detailed and legalistic. Preserved the accumulation of specific laws (tobacco, kissing, long hair) because the absurdity is part of Tocqueville's rhetorical strategy -- he's building to the punchline that these same theocratic busybodies created the most advanced democratic institutions in the world.
- The "long hair" detail: Kept it as the kicker to the paragraph, as Tocqueville intended. It's a perfect deadpan moment.
- Winthrop's speech: Preserved in original language. This is a primary historical document that Tocqueville presents as a set piece. The archaic English is the point -- it shows what these "obscure" colonists were capable of. Added the contextual information from the footnote (Winthrop's identity, the accusation, the acquittal) as a lead-in rather than a footnote.
- The religion/liberty passage: This is one of the chapter's big rhetorical moments. Preserved the parallel structure and the building rhythm. Kept sentences longer here because the accumulative effect IS the argument.
- "sumus omnes deteriores" -- left the Latin in Winthrop's speech since it's a direct quotation. Means "we are all the worse for it."

TONE CALIBRATION

- The first half of this chapter requires a specific tonal balancing act: Tocqueville is simultaneously appalled by Puritan theocratic excess and deeply admiring of their democratic institutions. The translation needs to let both reactions coexist -- the reader should feel the whiplash of going from "they banned kissing and tobacco" to "they invented modern democracy." That's the whole point.
- The Europe-vs-America comparison passage near the end is one of the chapter's rhetorical peaks. Kept the momentum building and the sentences getting more expansive as the argument crescendos.
- The bail/legal system passage at the end is dry, analytical, almost conversational -- a come-down from the rhetorical heights. This is intentional; Tocqueville is being a careful social scientist after the grand sweep.

KEY THEMES FOR CONTINUITY

- The religion/freedom synthesis: Tocqueville's argument that Puritan religion and democratic politics reinforced each other is central. This needs to echo throughout the book whenever religion and democracy come up.
- Township democracy as the foundation: The idea that American democracy was built from the bottom up (township -> county -> state -> Union) is introduced here as a key structural argument. Will recur in the chapters on local government.
- English legal inheritance: The bail system example introduces the theme that American law has aristocratic English elements hiding under its democratic surface. This will come back in discussions of the judiciary and legal profession.
- The "obscure democracy" producing world-historical ideas: This paradox -- that the most advanced political thinking came from the most humble communities -- is a theme Tocqueville will return to.
