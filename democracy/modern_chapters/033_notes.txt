SENSITIVE CONTENT HANDLING

- Added a brief bracketed note after Tocqueville's passage on the psychological effects of slavery on Black Americans. His observations about what oppression does to people are genuinely insightful, but his framing sometimes blurs the line between describing systemic damage and attributing inherent qualities. The modern language makes this more visible without editorializing.
- The Green Bay scalping anecdote: converted from nested quotation to a bracketed summary. The point (that cultural identity persists despite education) is preserved without sensationalizing the violence.
- "the European is to other races what man is to the lower animals" -- kept faithfully; Tocqueville is describing the empirical reality of European behavior, not endorsing it. The "if we judge by what we see" framing makes clear this is observational.
- Tocqueville's searing irony in the forced migration section ("a convenient distinction that escaped earlier commentators") -- preserved and slightly sharpened. His dark humor here is one of his most powerful rhetorical tools.

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The Alabama vignette (Native woman, Black woman, white child by the spring): kept the vivid scene-setting and emotional impact. This is Tocqueville at his most literary and it should read like great reportage.
- The Memphis/Choctaw passage: preserved nearly intact -- this is one of the most famous eyewitness accounts of the Trail of Tears and it needs to retain its full devastating power.
- The government speech to Native Americans ("What do you have left in the land of your fathers?"): kept in direct quotation, modernized slightly for flow. Tocqueville's rendering of this manipulative rhetoric is extraordinary.
- Footnotes: bibliographic references dropped. Tocqueville's own observations (Green Bay anecdote, Volney's buffalo account, Clarke and Cass quotes) woven in as bracketed asides or integrated into the text. The price statistics from Mr. Bell's report condensed into a powerful bracketed summary.
