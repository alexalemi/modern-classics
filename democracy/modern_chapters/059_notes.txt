STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is one of the longest and most analytically dense chapters in this cluster -- broke it into shorter, clearer argument blocks
- The section on aristocratic language stability vs. democratic language flux is a core distinction; kept the contrast sharp
- The "box with a false bottom" metaphor is one of Tocqueville's best in the book -- preserved it exactly
- Milton introducing 600+ words -- this is a vivid factual detail, kept it prominently
- The French examples (capacites, etc.) needed careful handling -- used parenthetical translations so modern readers get both the French and the point
- The caste/India comparison is striking; preserved it
- "Without clear language, there is no good language at all" -- preserved this as Tocqueville's strongest declarative statement in the chapter
- Broke the massive paragraph on abstract terms into several paragraphs for readability

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter alternates between linguistic analysis and Tocqueville's personal opinions
- His frustration with linguistic ambiguity is genuine and sharp ("I'd honestly rather see the language made ugly with foreign words...")
- The self-deprecating moment (admitting he personifies "equality") is classic Tocqueville -- honest and witty
- The "box with a false bottom" passage is almost playful

RECURRING TERMS MAINTAINED

- "equality" / "the principle of equality" -- consistent with established usage
- "democracy" / "aristocracy" as social conditions
- "customs" for "manners" (consistent with mapping)

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 060
