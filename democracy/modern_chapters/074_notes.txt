STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This chapter establishes Tocqueville's key insight about the middle-class nature of material desire. Structured the translation to build to that revelation clearly.
- The aristocracy-vs-democracy comparison is the spine of this chapter. Kept the parallel structure but made each side of the comparison sharper and more immediate.
- "The human heart isn't captured so much by the undisturbed possession of something valuable as by the desire -- still imperfectly satisfied -- to possess it" -- this is a key Tocquevillean insight; preserved the structure while modernizing the language.
- The passage about the poor whose imagination "escapes and flies off" -- kept the poetic quality; this is one of Tocqueville's more lyrical moments.
- The final paragraph about wealthy Americans who were once poor is vintage Tocqueville field observation -- "intoxicated by the small pleasures they chased for forty years" captures his wry sympathy perfectly.

- The chapter builds to the powerful final image of the "great current" sweeping everything along -- preserved as the climactic closing.
