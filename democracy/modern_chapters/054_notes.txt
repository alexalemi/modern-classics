STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This chapter is vintage Tocqueville sociology -- analyzing the economics and psychology of production and consumption in democracy. Kept the analytical structure but made it feel like sharp cultural criticism rather than dusty theory.
- The watches example is kept tight and punchy: "When only the rich owned watches, nearly all watches were very good. Few watches worth much are made today, but everyone has one in their pocket."
- The New York anecdote is one of the best moments in the chapter -- made it read like a scene from a travel essay. "I discovered that its walls were whitewashed brick and its columns were painted wood."
- The fake diamonds passage preserved with its dry wit intact.
- The "hypocrisy of luxury" line is given its own emphasis as a standalone observation.
- The Raphael vs. David comparison is kept clear but not over-explained -- the reader can grasp the aristocratic/ideal vs. democratic/real distinction from context.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The chapter moves from economic analysis (guild systems, mass production) to cultural criticism (fake luxury, mediocrity) to art criticism (Raphael vs. David). Each section gets its own register: the economic parts are brisk and precise, the cultural criticism is wry and sharp, the art criticism is more elevated.
- The closing paragraph about medieval painters vs. modern painters has a note of wistfulness -- Tocqueville genuinely mourns something lost. Preserved that tone without sentimentalizing.
