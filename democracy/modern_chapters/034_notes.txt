KEY ARGUMENTS PRESERVED

- Tocqueville's central historical thesis: conquered peoples who are less advanced than their conquerors rarely civilize -- they retreat or are destroyed. Only when the less advanced people is the conqueror (Rome, China) does cultural exchange succeed. This is a sophisticated argument and it's preserved with full intellectual architecture.
- The "feudal parallels" passage -- Tocqueville compares Native American warrior culture to medieval European nobility. This is genuinely illuminating and kept intact.
- His devastating analysis of the "competition trap" -- Native Americans who adopt farming face immediate economic competition with vastly more experienced white farmers, making the transition nearly impossible. This argument is brilliantly structured and preserved in full.
- The Texas prophecy (footnote) -- kept as a bracketed aside because it's remarkably prescient.

SENSITIVE CONTENT HANDLING

- "the Indian is a rough beginner in an art with which he is unacquainted" -> Kept the economic argument faithfully; the point is about structural disadvantage, not inherent capacity, and the modern language makes that clear.
- "men of penetration" (Native leaders who foresee doom) -> "leaders of real vision" -- preserves Tocqueville's genuine respect for these figures.
- "childish carelessness of the morrow which characterizes savage life" -> "careless disregard for the future that characterizes traditional societies" -- softened the condescension slightly while keeping the observation (which is Tocqueville's explanation for why tribes don't unite preemptively).
- The Tanner anecdote: condensed from the full footnote into a bracketed summary. The point (the addictive pull of wilderness life) is preserved.
