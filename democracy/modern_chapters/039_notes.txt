STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The opening passage on patriotism and social bonds -- this is a genuinely important sociological insight (society requires shared beliefs, not just shared laws). Made it punchy and clear.
- The shared American beliefs passage -- Tocqueville's list of core American convictions is remarkably modern. Translated as a flowing catalog without breaking the momentum.
- American exceptionalism passage ("they are not far from believing they belong to a distinct race of mankind") -- kept the edge. Tocqueville is describing this with dry amusement, not endorsement.
- North vs. South character sketch -- this is vivid sociological portraiture. Tocqueville draws the Southerner as an aristocratic type (impulsive, proud, idle, brilliant) and the Northerner as a bourgeois type (practical, patient, energetic, narrow). Preserved the sharpness of both portraits.
- The western frontier passage ("This gradual, relentless westward march... has the solemnity of a providential event. It's like a flood of humanity...") -- this is one of Tocqueville's most powerful images. Kept the grandeur.
- The Virginia/New York/Ohio congressional representation shift -- good concrete example that makes the abstract argument tangible. Preserved the numbers.
- The mathematical footnote about proportional representation (footnote p) -- condensed into a single clear paragraph explaining why a growing state can lose representatives.

- The North/South character comparison driven by slavery -- this is sharp but fair observation. Tocqueville isn't condemning individual Southerners; he's analyzing how an institution shapes character. Translated faithfully.
