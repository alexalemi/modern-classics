STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is one of Tocqueville's most famous and powerful chapters. The translation preserves the emotional arc: vivid opening contrast -> the unforgettable litany of American restlessness -> philosophical explanation -> the devastating closing about melancholy in abundance.
- The opening contrast (poor European villagers vs. prosperous Americans) is kept clean and sharp. The "cloud permanently hung over their faces" image is too good to lose.
- The "builds a house and sells it before the roof is on" passage is one of the most quoted in the entire book. Kept the rapid-fire structure with its accumulating examples -- this is a passage where the rhythm IS the argument. Modernized vocabulary without disrupting the pace.
- "Death finally catches up with him, but only before he's grown tired of his endless chase after a complete happiness that's always just out of reach" -- restructured the famous closing line for clarity while preserving its melancholy force.
- The passage about equality's insatiability ("it retreats before them constantly, yet never quite disappears from view") is kept lyrical -- this is Tocqueville at his most poetic and it earns the register.
- The suicide/mental illness comparison at the end is presented as Tocqueville's observation without editorial framing. "Mental illness" replaces "insanity" as a modern term.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter requires the full range: vivid reportage, rapid-fire observation, philosophical argument, and lyrical melancholy. Each section gets its appropriate register.
- The famous restlessness litany is conversational and punchy.
- The philosophical passages about equality's paradox are measured and analytical.
- The closing is deliberately somber -- matching Tocqueville's own mood.
