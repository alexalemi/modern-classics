Next Chapter Notes -- Chapter 080 (Chapter XVI: Worldly Welfare May Impair Itself)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "amelioration" -> "improving"
- "the beasts" -> "animals" throughout
- "the angel teaches the brute" -> kept this metaphor exactly; it's vivid and clear
- "contemning life" -> "holding life itself in contempt"
- "enervates" -> "weakens"
- "chiefest" -> "the highest"
- "discernment" -> "thought"
- "inferior races" -> "lesser creatures" (Tocqueville means animals here, not human races; "races" in the archaic sense of "species/kinds")

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is a very short chapter -- essentially a single philosophical argument in two paragraphs. Kept it tight and direct.
- The chapter title was modernized from the passive/archaic phrasing to something clearer: an obsession with comfort can undermine itself.
- The rhetorical question at the end of paragraph one ("So why is it that...") is preserved -- it's the hinge of the argument.
- The angel/brute metaphor is the chapter's most memorable image -- kept it prominent.
- The closing warning (people would "enjoy them like animals -- without thought and without improvement") lands the argument with a punch.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Philosophical but accessible. This reads like a sharp opinion column -- one clear argument, well made.
- No first-person "I" in this chapter (Tocqueville doesn't use it here either). The voice is more essayistic and universal.

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 081

- Chapter 081 deals with skepticism, long-term thinking, and human perfectibility in democratic ages.
- Key theme: when people lose religious faith, they become dangerously short-term in their thinking. Governments should encourage long-term thinking as a backdoor to restoring some form of faith.
- Watch for the connection to the previous two chapters (079-080): this completes a three-chapter arc about religion, materialism, and the soul in democratic society.
