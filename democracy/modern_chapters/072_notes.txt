Next Chapter Notes -- Chapter 072 (Self-Interest Rightly Understood)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "interest rightly understood" -> "self-interest rightly understood" -- added "self-" for clarity in modern English. This is Tocqueville's key concept for this chapter. The phrase "rightly understood" is kept because it carries the essential nuance: not raw self-interest, but self-interest that has been thought through to its logical conclusion (i.e., that helping others helps you).
- "egotism" -> "selfishness" (consistent throughout)
- "abnegation" -> "self-sacrifice" / "spirit of self-sacrifice"
- "with complacency" -> "cheerfully" (Reeve's "complacency" means satisfaction/pleasure, not the modern sense of smugness)
- "lofty" -> kept in some places ("not a lofty one") where it works; replaced elsewhere
- "the Deity himself" -> "God" (simpler)
- "moralists" -> "moralists" (kept -- it's the right word)
- "gross depravity" -> "gross depravity" (kept -- it's clear and strong)
- "implicit self-sacrifice" -> "instinctive self-sacrifice"
- "fain" -> "try to" (archaic word eliminated)

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- The Montaigne quotation -- preserved exactly as Reeve rendered it. It's a famous passage and reads well enough in this form.
- The balanced assessment paragraph ("not a lofty one, but it is clear and sure") is one of Tocqueville's finest passages of philosophical evaluation. Preserved the parallel structure and measured tone almost exactly. This is Tocqueville at his most precise.
- "Look at a few individuals, and they're diminished by it. Survey humanity as a whole, and it's elevated." -- tightened from Reeve's version but preserved the antithesis. This is a key formulation.
- The closing exhortation about education -- kept its urgency. "Educate, then, at all costs" is a powerful modern rendering of "Educate, then, at any rate." The final sentence about freedom, peace, and social order is preserved nearly verbatim as the chapter's and section's climactic statement.
- The ironic observation that Americans sell themselves short (claiming self-interest when they're actually being generous) -- preserved Tocqueville's wry affection for this American quirk.

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter is more philosophical and reflective than the association chapters. Tocqueville is doing moral philosophy here, not political sociology. Matched that register -- more measured, more careful, more willing to pause and evaluate.
- The passage where Tocqueville declares his own position ("I'm not afraid to say it") is one of the few moments where he drops his analytical distance and speaks as a moralist himself. Preserved that directness.
- The closing paragraphs build to genuine urgency. The penultimate paragraph about ignorant selfishness leading to "stupid excess" and "disgrace and misery" is dark. The final line about education is Tocqueville's answer -- and it's both hopeful and desperate. Preserved both emotions.

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "self-interest rightly understood" -- the chapter's central concept, will likely recur
- "selfishness" (negative) vs. "self-interest" (neutral/positive when "rightly understood")
- "education" as the essential requirement for democratic society
- The distinction between aristocratic morality (self-sacrifice as ideal) and democratic morality (enlightened self-interest as practical reality)

NOTES FOR NEXT SECTION

- This chapter effectively concludes the section on individualism and its remedies (associations, newspapers, self-interest rightly understood). The next chapters likely move to new topics.
- Key concepts established in this section that may recur: individualism vs. selfishness, the art of association, self-interest rightly understood, the necessity of political freedom and education as democratic safeguards.
- The voice has been consistent across all seven chapters: analytical, direct, journalistic but philosophically engaged. Maintain this register going forward.
