STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Voice: Maintained first person throughout. Made Tocqueville's "I" voice direct and conversational -- "Let me remind you," "I'll say again what I've said before," "my answer is"
- Sentence length: Broke most mega-sentences (80+ words) into 2-3 clearer sentences. Kept some longer sentences in rhetorical passages (the patriotism-in-crisis passage, the closing on freedom's apprenticeship) where accumulation is the point.
- The aristocracy vs. democracy comparison: Made it feel like a genuine, fair-minded weighing of pros and cons. Tocqueville respects aristocratic governance even while arguing democracy is better on balance.
- The English aristocracy passage: Integrated the footnote (about the Reform Bill of 1832) -- dropped it since it's a later editorial addition that doesn't add much for modern readers.
- The patriotism section: Two kinds of patriotism (instinctive vs. rational) -- made this distinction very clear and vivid. The "crisis of patriotism" passage (when old customs break and new ones haven't formed) is one of Tocqueville's best -- preserved its rhetorical force with the anaphora "They can't find it in..."
- The touchy patriotism passage: This is classic Tocqueville wit -- preserved the humor and sharpness of his observation about Americans defending even the weather.
- The rights section: Made the analogy between children learning about property and citizens learning about rights very clear and concrete.
- "Homo puer robustus" -- kept the Latin phrase with an English gloss, as Tocqueville uses it deliberately.

TONE CALIBRATION

- Opening sections: Analytical, fair-minded, almost like a debate moderator laying out both sides
- Patriotism section: Shifts between historical sweep, dark analysis (the crisis passage), and dry humor (the touchy patriotism passage)
- Rights section: More philosophical and earnest, building to an impassioned argument about why political rights matter now more than ever
- Closing: The "freedom's apprenticeship" passage is powerful and compressed -- kept it tight

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "the welfare of the greatest number" -- Tocqueville's utilitarian-adjacent framing of democracy's advantage
- "instinctive patriotism" vs. "considered/rational patriotism" -- his key distinction
- "enlightened self-interest" -- the American motivation (will recur heavily in Volume 2)
- "the concept of rights" -- his philosophical anchor in this section
- "freedom's apprenticeship" -- preserved this metaphor
- "class interests" -- how aristocracies naturally work against the common good
