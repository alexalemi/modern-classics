STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This chapter has two major sections: (1) the end of the Nullification crisis and the Jackson analysis, and (2) the durability of republican institutions. Used *** section breaks and italicized headers (per text_analysis.txt guidance on part divisions within chapters).
- The Jackson portrait is one of Tocqueville's best character studies. Preserved his key paradox intact: Jackson is both the slave of the majority and a personal strongman. "He's a favorite who sometimes treats his master roughly" -- kept this vivid formulation.
- "A Federalist by taste and a Republican by calculation" -- preserved this perfectly balanced formulation.
- The chapter summary for "Republican Institutions" was modernized from the original italic overview format.
- The passage about European demagogues redefining republicanism is Tocqueville at his most acidic. Kept the bite: "legitimate tyranny and holy injustice."
- The comparison of republican government in America to monarchy under Louis XIV is a wonderful analogy -- preserved it fully.
- Montesquieu reference kept with brief context ("Montesquieu observed that...").
- The aristocracy discussion at the end is an important theoretical passage. Preserved the argument's logical structure: no people has ever voluntarily created an aristocracy; they've all been imposed by conquest.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The Jackson section has an edge -- Tocqueville clearly finds Jackson fascinating and somewhat alarming. Preserved the mixture of grudging admiration and analytical concern.
- The republican institutions section is more measured and theoretical, with occasional sharp observations (the European demagogues passage).
- The aristocracy section is confident and declarative -- Tocqueville is making a strong historical argument.
