VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "equality of conditions" — kept as Tocqueville's signature phrase, introduced with "sheer equality of social conditions" on first use, then alternating between "equality of conditions" and "equality of social conditions" for natural flow
- "amongst" → "among" throughout
- "whilst" → "while" throughout
- "henceforward" → "from that point on" / "going forward"
- "thence" → "this created" / "as a result"
- "roturier" — translated as "commoner" (French term for non-noble)
- "villein" → "peasant" / "serf" (context-dependent)
- "panegyric" → "celebration"
- "amelioration" → "improvement"
- "concomitant" → "accompanying"
- "exigencies" → "demands"
- "propensities" → "instincts" / "impulses"
- "liberty" → "freedom" in most cases; kept "liberty" only in the phrase "human liberty" where it appears in a rhetorical passage about democracy's champions
- "men" → "people" / "human beings" where clearly generic; kept gendered where Tocqueville specifically references men
- "manners" → "customs" / "social norms" / "customs and habits" — never in the etiquette sense
- "the lower orders" → "the common people" / "ordinary people"
- "egotism" → "selfishness"
- "the legislator" → "the lawmakers" / "lawmakers"
- "the body of a State" → "a society"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Voice: Maintained first person throughout. Made Tocqueville's "I" voice more direct and immediate — "I'll be honest," "I'll admit it," "Here's the question"
- Sentence length: Broke most mega-sentences (80+ words) into 2-3 clearer sentences. Preserved some longer sentences in the grand rhetorical passages (the "seven hundred years" survey, the final crescendo about confusion) where the accumulation is part of the effect.
- Dashes: Used em dashes (rendered as --) freely as a modern equivalent of Tocqueville's semicolon chains. This gives the prose a more journalistic, Atlantic-essay feel.
- Passive → active: Converted most passive constructions. "The consequence of this has been that the democratic revolution has been effected only in the material parts of society" → "The result is that the democratic revolution transformed only the material structure of society"
- Throat-clearing: Cut "it is evident to all alike that" → opened directly. Cut "I readily discovered" → "I quickly saw." Cut "it is worthy of remark" type constructions throughout.
- Semicolons: Mostly replaced with periods or dashes. Kept a few where the parallel structure benefits from them.
- Historical context: Light-touch — "Louis XI and Louis XIV" kept without lengthy gloss since Tocqueville's point (kings as levelers) is clear from context. "The Crusades and the wars with England" kept the "English" wars reference but didn't add a parenthetical about the Hundred Years' War.
- The famous shepherd/flock metaphor for aristocratic paternalism — preserved as-is, it still reads clearly.
- The providential/religious language — preserved Tocqueville's framing of equality as God's will; this is central to his argument and shouldn't be softened.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The opening paragraph sets the "brilliant journalist" register: direct, punchy, but authoritative
- The historical survey (700 years of equality) maintains a storytelling pace — each new development gets its own beat
- The dark passages about France's failed revolution are the chapter's emotional core — kept the language sharp and slightly bitter, matching Tocqueville's tone
- The famous confusion passage ("where virtue exists without talent, and talent without honor") — preserved the rhetorical accumulation, just modernized the vocabulary
- The closing methodological section — made it feel honest and personal rather than formulaic; Tocqueville's modesty here is genuine

RECURRING TERMS TO MAINTAIN

- "equality of conditions" (or "social equality") — central concept, use consistently
- "democracy" — following Tocqueville, this means the social condition of equality, not just a voting system
- "the democratic revolution" — his term for the multi-century trend
- "Providence" / "the will of God" — keep this framing; it's not decorative, it's structural to his argument
- "freedom" preferred over "liberty" in most contexts
- "customs and habits" or "social norms" for Reeve's "manners"
- "self-interest" — key concept that recurs throughout both volumes; established here

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 001

- The next chapter likely begins the geographical/historical description of America. Maintain the same journalistic register but shift toward more concrete, descriptive prose.
