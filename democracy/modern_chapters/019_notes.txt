STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Tocqueville's three-stage framework for associations (intellectual bond -> physical meetings -> representative conventions) is a beautifully clear analytical structure. Kept the numbered stages implicit but clear.
- The 1831 tariff Convention in Philadelphia: maintained narrative detail and pacing. This is Tocqueville doing historical journalism -- vivid, specific, building to a point.
- The section break between the main chapter and the Europe vs. America comparison: used "***" as a section divider per the project style guidelines, since this is a distinct sub-section with its own heading in the original.
- The military metaphor for European associations vs. the democratic metaphor for American ones: this is a powerful comparison and still resonates. Preserved the contrast sharply.
- "In America, there are countless factions, but no conspiracies" -- preserved this as a standalone sentence; it's one of Tocqueville's memorable one-liners.
- The closing paragraph about American associations borrowing from democratic government forms: a clean, concise ending that captures the core thesis.

TONE CALIBRATION

- The opening examples (children's games, road obstructions, public celebrations) are vintage Tocqueville -- concrete, slightly amused observations of American life. Kept the warmth and specificity.
- The theoretical sections (three stages of association, danger of conventions vs. legislatures) are more analytical -- kept them clear and purposeful.
- The Europe vs. America comparison is argumentative and passionate -- Tocqueville is making a case for the American model. Preserved the advocacy tone.
- The military metaphor section about European associations has real bite -- these organizations demand "blind obedience" and are more tyrannical than the governments they oppose. Let the irony land.
