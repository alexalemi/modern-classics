Next Chapter Notes -- Chapter 057 (The Business of Literature)

VOCABULARY DECISIONS

- "the trading classes" -> "the commercial classes"
- "a trading spirit" -> "a commercial mentality"
- "fastidious" -> "demanding"
- "a meagre reputation" -> "a modest reputation"
- "venal souls" -> "mercenary souls"
- "idea-mongers" -> "idea-peddlers" (preserves the contemptuous tone with a modern equivalent)
- "infested with a tribe of writers" -> "overrun with writers" (toned down the dehumanizing metaphor slightly while keeping the critical force)
- "letters as a mere trade" -> "books as nothing more than a business"

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- This is one of the shortest chapters in the book -- kept it punchy and compact
- The courtier metaphor is one of Tocqueville's sharpest lines; preserved its sting
- "He doesn't need to be admired; it's enough to be liked" -- maintained Tocqueville's epigrammatic quality
- The final line ("thousands of idea-peddlers") lands like a punchline -- kept the structure

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter is dripping with aristocratic disdain -- Tocqueville at his most sardonic
- The contempt is directed at both the public (who "despise" the authors they pay) and the hack writers who treat literature as commerce
- Preserved the biting quality without making it sound merely snobbish

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 058

- Next chapter discusses Greek and Latin literature in democracies
- Continue the aristocracy-vs-democracy framework
- The chapter argues that classical education is the antidote to democratic literary defects
- Watch for the distinction between practical education (for most) and literary education (for a few)
