- "industrial callings" -> "business" (chapter title and throughout)
- "a competency" -> "a decent income"
- "penury" -> "poverty" / "so poor"
- "manufactures" -> "manufacturing" (noun form modernized throughout)
- "the readiest and most powerful means" -> "the quickest and most powerful path"
- "imperious" -> "urgent"
- "opulent" -> "wealthy" / "wealthiest"
- "inquietude" -> "restlessness"
- "obtrusive" -> not present
- "leagues" -> kept "leagues" (specific historical measurement; adds period flavor)
- "the commonweath" -> "the nation" / "citizens"
- "embarrassments" -> "setbacks" / "devastating setbacks"
- "endemic disease" -> kept as is (still perfectly current and vivid)

STYLISTIC DECISIONS

- Two footnotes integrated as parenthetical paragraphs. Footnote (a) about causation of commercial taste -- kept as a parenthetical aside, preserving Tocqueville's "I think that gets the causation backward" voice. Footnote (b) about money-based aristocracies and England -- kept as a longer parenthetical, as it contains an important extended argument.
- The hypothetical person at the start ("Picture an active, educated, free person...") -- modernized from third-person male to second-person-inclusive; this makes the thought experiment more immediate.
- The passage about American infrastructure -- shifted to past tense ("when Tocqueville was writing") to help modern readers understand the temporal context. The specific details (Hudson to Mississippi, Atlantic to Gulf of Mexico) are kept; they're vivid.
- Changed "[in 1840]" bracketed editorial note to a natural contextual sentence.
- "commercial panics" -> "financial panics" (more recognizable to modern readers; same concept).

TONE CALIBRATION

- This chapter has a lot of energy -- Tocqueville is genuinely impressed by American commercial dynamism. Kept the pace brisk and the admiration palpable.
- The passage about farmers speculating on land reads like great reportage -- preserved the observational detail.
- The closing warning about financial panics is prophetic and should feel urgent. "Endemic disease" is a perfect metaphor -- left it exactly as is.
- The England footnote has a nice analytical detachment -- preserved the comparative sociological tone.

NOTES FOR CHAPTER 084

- Chapter 084 is one of Tocqueville's most famous and prescient: how manufacturing creates a new kind of aristocracy within democracy.
- Key concepts: division of labor degrading workers, the widening gap between factory owners and workers, the new industrial aristocracy as potentially the "harshest which ever existed."
- The Adam Smith pin factory reference is here (though not cited by name). Keep the argument crisp.
- The closing warning about industrial aristocracy is the chapter's climax -- make it land.
- "Aristocracy" in this chapter means a class hierarchy emerging from manufacturing, not traditional birthright aristocracy. Keep the distinction clear.
