Book X

(Part 2 of 2)

Of him was born that Cinyras who, had he been childless, might have been counted among the happy. I will sing of dreadful things. Daughters, be far from here; parents, be far — or, if my songs charm your minds, do not believe me in this part, do not believe it happened; or, if you will believe it, believe as well in the punishment of the deed. If nature does let such a crime be seen to have been done, then I count this land lucky that it lies far from the regions that bred so great an outrage. Let the land of Panchaia be rich in balsam, and bear its cinnamon and its costmary and its incense sweated from the wood and its other flowers — so long as it bears myrrh too: the new tree was not worth so much. Cupid himself denies, Myrrha, that his arrows hurt you, and clears his torches of that charge; one of the three Sisters breathed on you with a Stygian brand and swollen vipers. To hate a parent is a crime; this love is a crime greater than hate.

On every side chosen nobles want you; from all the East the young men come to the contest for your bed. Choose one man out of them all, Myrrha — only let that one, out of them all, not be your father. She indeed feels her guilt and fights against her foul passion, and says to herself: "Where is my mind carrying me? What am I attempting? I beg you, gods, and natural love, and the sacred bonds of parents — forbid this crime, stop my wickedness, if indeed it is wicked. But they say natural affection does not condemn this love: the other animals couple without distinction; it is no shame for a heifer to bear her sire, a horse takes his own daughter to mate, the he-goat goes with the flocks he fathered, and the bird conceives by the one whose seed conceived her. Happy those to whom such things are allowed! Human care has made spiteful laws, and what nature permits, envious ordinances forbid. Yet there are said to be nations where mother is joined to son and daughter to father, and love grows greater by the doubled bond. Wretched me, that it was not my luck to be born there, and that I am wronged by the accident of place! But why do I dwell on this? Away, forbidden hopes! He is worthy to be loved — but as a father. So then, were I not the daughter of great Cinyras, I could lie with Cinyras. Now, because he is already mine, he is not mine, and my very nearness is my ruin: a stranger, I would be stronger. I would gladly go far from here and leave my country's borders, if only I might escape the crime; but a wicked fire holds me back as I go, so that I may look at Cinyras in the flesh, and touch him, and speak to him, and give him kisses, if nothing more is allowed. But can you hope for anything more, unnatural girl? Do you feel how many ties and names you are confusing? Will you be your mother's rival and your father's mistress? Will you be called your son's sister and your brother's mother? Will you not dread the Sisters with black snakes for hair, whom guilty minds see aiming their cruel torches at their eyes and faces? But you — while you have suffered no crime in body — do not conceive it in mind, and do not defile all-powerful nature's law with a forbidden bed. Suppose you wished it: the thing itself forbids you; he is good, and mindful of what is right — and oh, that the same madness were in him!"

She had spoken; but Cinyras, whom a worthy crowd of suitors puts in doubt what to do, asks the girl herself, running through their names, whose wife she would be. At first she is silent, and, fixed on her father's face, she burns and fills her eyes with warm dew. Cinyras, taking this for a virgin's fear, forbids her to weep, dries her cheeks, and gives her kisses. Myrrha is too glad of the kisses; and, asked what kind of husband she would have, she said, "One like you." He praised the answer he did not understand, and said, "Always be so loving." At the word "loving" the girl, conscious of her guilt, cast down her face.

It was midnight, and sleep had loosed the cares and bodies of mortals; but the daughter of Cinyras, wide awake, is devoured by an unquenched fire and turns over her mad longings. Now she despairs, now she wants to try; she is ashamed, and desires, and cannot find what to do. As a great tree, wounded by the axe, when the last stroke is left, is doubtful which way to fall and is feared on every side, so her mind, shaken by wound after wound, wavers this way and that and takes an impulse both ways; and no bound or rest is found for her love but death. Death pleases her. She rises, resolves to fit her neck in a noose; and, tying her girdle to the top of the doorpost, she said, "Farewell, dear Cinyras, and understand the cause of my death" — and set the band to her pale neck.

The murmur of her words, they say, reached the faithful ears of her nurse, keeping the threshold of her charge. The old woman rises, opens the door, and, seeing the tools of the death she had planned, cries out at the same moment, and beats herself, and tears her breast, and, snatching the band from her neck, rips it apart. Only then had she leisure to weep, to embrace her, to ask the cause of the noose. The girl is silent, as if dumb, and, unmoving, stares at the ground, and grieves that her slow attempt at death is caught. The old woman presses her, and, baring her gray hair and her empty breasts, begs her by her cradle and her first nourishment to trust her with whatever grieves her. The girl turns away from the asking and groans. The nurse is set on finding it out, and pledges not only faith: "Tell me," she says, "and let me help you: my old age is not idle. If it is a frenzy, I have one who can heal with charm and herbs; if someone has hurt you by a spell, you shall be cleansed by magic rite; if it is the gods' anger, that anger can be soothed by sacrifice. What else can I think? Surely your fortune and your house are safe and thriving: your mother and father are alive." At the word "father," Myrrha drew a sigh from the depth of her breast. Even now the nurse conceives no crime in her mind, yet senses it is some love; and, holding to her purpose, she begs her to tell, whatever it is, and lifts the weeping girl into her aged lap, and, so clasping her limbs in her weak arms, says, "I have understood — you are in love! And in this my care will serve you; your father shall never know." The girl sprang up in a frenzy from her lap, and, pressing her face into the bed, said, "Go, I beg, and spare my wretched shame!" And when the nurse pressed on, she said, "Go, or stop asking why I grieve. What you are laboring to know is a crime." The old woman shudders, and stretches out her hands, trembling with years and fear, and falls a suppliant before her nursling's feet; and now she coaxes, now, if she is not told, she frightens her, threatening to reveal the noose and the attempted death, and pledges her service if the love is confided. The girl lifted her head and filled the nurse's breast with bursting tears, and, often trying to confess, as often checked her voice, and hid her blushing face in her robe, and said, "O mother, happy in your husband!" — this much, and groaned. A cold shudder pierced the nurse's limbs and bones — for she understood — and the white hoariness stood bristling all over her head; and she added many words to drive off, if she could, so dreadful a love. But the girl knows she is not warned falsely; still she is resolved to die if she cannot have what she loves. "Live, then," the nurse says, "you shall have your—" and, not daring to say "father," fell silent, and confirmed her promise by a god.

The pious matrons were keeping the yearly festival of Ceres, when, their bodies clothed in snow-white robes, they offer garlands of corn-ears as the first fruits of the harvest, and for nine nights count the touch of a husband among forbidden things. Cenchreis, the king's wife, is in that company, at the secret rites. So, while his lawful wife's bed is empty, the nurse, wickedly diligent, finding Cinyras heavy with wine, tells him of a real love under a false name, and praises the girl's beauty. When he asks the maiden's age, she says, "The same as Myrrha's." Bidden to bring her, and back home, she says, "Rejoice, my child: we have won." The unhappy girl does not feel the joy through her whole heart; her foreboding breast is sad — and yet she is glad too: so great is the discord of her mind.

It was the hour when all things are silent, and Boötes had turned his wain with the pole slanting among the stars. She comes to her crime. The golden moon flees the sky; black clouds hide the lurking stars; the night is without its fires. You first, Icarus, cover your rising face, and you, Erigone, hallowed by holy love of your father. Three times she was called back by the omen of a stumbling foot; three times the funeral owl gave a warning with its deadly cry; yet she goes on, and the darkness and the black night lessen her shame. With her left hand she holds her nurse's, with the other she gropes the blind way. Now she touches the chamber's threshold, now opens the door, now is led inside — but her knees shook and gave beneath her, her color and her blood fled, and her courage left her as she went. The nearer she is to her crime, the more she dreads it; she repents her daring, and would wish she could turn back unknown. As she hesitates, the aged woman leads her by the hand and, bringing her to the high bed, delivers her up: "Take her, Cinyras," she said, "she is your own" — and joined the doomed bodies. The father takes his own flesh into the unclean bed, calms her virgin fears, and encourages her as she trembles. Perhaps, too, by reason of her age, he called her "daughter," and she called him "father," so that the fitting names should not be missing from the horror.

Full of her father, she left his chamber, carrying his unholy seed in her womb, bearing her conceived crime. The next night doubles the deed, nor is there an end in that; until at last Cinyras, eager after so many nights to know his lover, brought in a light and saw both the crime and his daughter. His words checked by grief, he tore his gleaming sword from its hanging sheath. Myrrha fled: saved from death by the dark and the gift of blind night, she wandered over the wide fields, left the palm-bearing Arabs and the Panchaean lands, and roamed through nine horns of the returning moon, until at last, weary, she rested in the Sabaean land; and scarcely could she carry the burden of her womb. Then, not knowing what to wish, between the fear of death and weariness of life, she framed this prayer: "O powers, if any of you are open to those who confess — I have deserved, and I do not refuse, a grim punishment; but, so that I may not defile the living by surviving, nor the dead by dying, drive me from both realms, and, changing me, deny me both life and death." Some power is open to those who confess: her last prayers at least found their gods. For the earth came over her legs as she spoke, and a root spread out slantwise through her bursting nails, a firm base for the tall trunk; her bones turn to hard wood, and, the marrow staying in the middle, her blood runs into sap, her arms into great branches, her fingers into small ones, her skin hardens into bark. And now the growing tree had bound tight her heavy womb, had covered her breast, and was making to close over her neck. She could not bear the delay, and, meeting the rising wood, she sank down and sank her face into the bark. Though she lost her old senses with her body, still she weeps, and warm drops run from the tree. There is honor even in her tears, and the myrrh that drips from the bark keeps its mistress's name, and will be unspoken of in no age.

But the child conceived in guilt had grown beneath the wood and was seeking a way to push out, having left his mother; the swollen belly bulges in the middle of the tree. The burden strains the mother, but the pains have no words of their own, and Lucina cannot be called by the voice of the one giving birth. Yet the tree is like a woman in labor, and, bending, gives frequent groans, and is wet with falling tears. Gentle Lucina stood by the aching boughs, laid on her hands, and spoke the words of childbirth: the tree splits into cracks and, through the cloven bark, gives back its living burden, and the boy cries; the Naiads laid him on soft grass and anointed him with his mother's tears. Even Envy would have praised his face; for such as the bodies of naked Cupids are painted in a picture, such was he — but, that their dress may make no difference, either give him, or take from them, the light quivers.

Winged time slips by unseen and deceives us, and nothing is swifter than the years. He, born of his own sister and his grandfather, so lately shut in a tree, so lately born, just now a most beautiful infant, is now a youth, now a man, now more beautiful than himself; now he pleases even Venus, and avenges his mother's fire. For, while the quiver-bearing boy was kissing his mother, he grazed her breast, unknowing, with a jutting arrow; the hurt goddess pushed her son away with her hand — the wound was driven deeper than it seemed, and had at first deceived even her. Taken with the beauty of the youth, she no longer cares for the shores of Cythera, nor seeks again sea-girt Paphos, or fishy Cnidos, or Amathus rich in ores; she stays away even from the sky — Adonis is preferred to the sky. Him she holds, him she keeps company; and she who was always used to indulge herself in the shade, and to improve her beauty by care of it, now wanders over ridges, through woods, over bushy rocks, her dress girt to the knee in Diana's fashion, and cheers on the dogs and hunts harmless quarry — the fleeing hares, or the high-antlered stag, or the does; she keeps away from the strong boars, and avoids the ravening wolves, the claw-armed bears, and the lions gorged with the slaughter of herds. You too she warns to fear these, Adonis, if warning could do any good, and says: "Be brave against those that flee; against the bold, boldness is not safe. Do not be rash at my peril, young man, and do not provoke the beasts nature has armed, lest your glory cost me dear. Neither your age nor your beauty, nor the things that have moved Venus, move lions and bristly boars and the eyes and hearts of wild beasts. Fierce boars carry lightning in their curved tusks; there is fury in the tawny lions and a boundless rage; and the whole race is hateful to me." When he asks the reason, she says, "I will tell you, and you will marvel at the strange result of an old fault. But this unaccustomed toil has tired me now, and look — a poplar invites us with its welcome shade, and the turf gives a couch: I should like to rest here with you" — and she rested — "on the ground," and pressed both the grass and himself, and, her neck reclined on the young man's breast, she spoke thus, mingling kisses among her words:

Perhaps you have heard how a certain girl outran the swiftest men in the contest of speed. That report was no idle tale — she did outrun them; nor could you have said whether she was more distinguished by the merit of her feet or the gift of her beauty. When she asked the oracle about a husband, the god said: "You have no need of a husband, Atalanta; flee the use of a husband. Yet you will not escape it, and, still living, you will lose yourself." Frightened by the god's answer, she lives unwed in the shady woods, and drives off the pressing crowd of suitors with a fierce condition: "I am not to be won unless first beaten in running. Race with me on foot: a wife and a wedding will be given to the swift; death is the price of the slow. Let that be the law of the contest." She was cruel indeed; but — so great is the power of beauty — a rash crowd of suitors came to that condition.

Hippomenes had sat as a spectator of the unfair race, and had said, "Is a wife sought by anyone through such perils?" — and had condemned the young men's excessive love. But when he saw her face, and her body with the clothing laid aside — such as mine is, or such as yours would be, Adonis, if you became a woman — he was stunned, and, raising his hands, said, "Forgive me, you whom I just now blamed! I did not yet know the prize you contended for." In praising her, he catches fire, and hopes that none of the young men may run faster than she, and fears it in his envy. "But why is the chance of this contest left untried by me?" he says. "The god himself helps the daring." While Hippomenes weighs this within himself, the girl flies on winged foot. Though she seemed to the Aonian youth to go no less swiftly than a Scythian arrow, still he marvels the more at her grace, and the very running makes her graceful. The breeze bears back the wings on her swift feet; her hair is tossed over her ivory shoulders, and the ribboned bands below her knees with their bright border; and over her girlish whiteness her body had drawn a blush, just as when a purple awning over a white hall tints it with a matching shade. While the stranger marks this, the last lap is run, and victorious Atalanta is crowned with the festival wreath. The beaten men groan and pay the penalty by the agreement.

Yet, not daunted by the young men's end, Hippomenes stood up in the midst and, fixing his eyes on the girl, said: "Why seek an easy fame by beating the sluggish? Match yourself with me! If fortune makes me the winner, you will not think it shame to be beaten by so great a man; for my father was Megareus of Onchestus, his grandfather Neptune, and I am the great-grandson of the king of the waters — nor is my worth less than my birth. Or if I am beaten, in beating Hippomenes you will have a great and memorable name." As he spoke, the daughter of Schoeneus looked on him with a soft face, and wavered whether she would rather be beaten or win, and said: "What god, an enemy to the beautiful, wants to destroy this youth, and bids him seek this marriage at the risk of his dear life? In my own judgment I am not worth so much. Nor am I moved by his beauty — though I could be moved by that too — but because he is still a boy; it is not himself that moves me, but his age. And is it not that he has courage, and a mind undismayed by death? And that he is counted fourth from the sea's own line? And that he loves me, and thinks marriage with me worth so much that he would die if hard fortune denied me to him? While you may, stranger, go, and leave this bloodstained wedding. A match with me is cruelly dangerous. No woman will be unwilling to marry you, and a wise girl might well desire you. But why do I care for you, when so many have already died? Let him look to it! Let him die, since he was not warned by the slaughter of so many suitors, and is driven to weariness of life. So he will die, then, because he wished to live with me, and suffer an undeserved death, the reward of his love? My victory will not bear the hatred of it. But it is no fault of mine! I wish you would give up — or, since you are mad, I wish you were swifter! But what a girlish look there is in his boyish face! Ah, poor Hippomenes, I wish you had never seen me! You were worthy to live. And if I had been luckier, and the grudging Fates had not denied me marriage, you are the one man I would have shared my bed with." So she said; and, untried and touched by love for the first time, not knowing what she does, she loves, and does not feel that she loves.

And now the people and her father demand the usual race, when Hippomenes, the descendant of Neptune, called on me with anxious voice: "I pray that Cytherea may favor my daring, and aid the love she has given me." A breeze, not unfriendly, carried the tender prayer to me; I was moved, I confess, and there was no long delay in giving aid. There is a field the natives call the Tamasenian, the choicest part of the Cyprian land, which the elders of old made holy to me and ordered added as an endowment to my temples. In the middle of it a tree shines, tawny of leaf, its branches rattling with tawny gold. Coming from there, by chance, I was carrying three golden apples I had picked in my hand; and, seen by none but him, I came to Hippomenes and showed him what use to make of them. The trumpets had given the signal, when each darted forward from the barrier and skimmed the surface of the sand with quick feet: you would think they could graze the sea with dry step, and run over the standing ears of white grain. Shouts and cheers give the youth heart, and the cries of those who call, "Now, now is the time to press on, Hippomenes! Hurry! Now use all your strength! Away with delay — you will win!" It is doubtful whether the hero of Megara or the daughter of Schoeneus was the gladder of these words. O how often, when she could have passed him, she slowed, and unwillingly left behind the face she had long gazed on! A dry, panting breath came from his tired mouth, and the goal was far off; then, at last, the son of Neptune threw one of the three fruits of the tree. The girl was amazed and, in her longing for the shining apple, turned aside from her course and picked up the rolling gold. Hippomenes passes her; the stands ring with applause. She makes up for her delay and the time lost with a swift run, and again leaves the youth behind. Slowed by the throw of a second apple, again she overtakes and passes the young man. The last stretch of the race remained. "Now," he said, "be with me, goddess who gave the gift!" — and, with a young man's throw, he flung the shining gold slantwise to the side of the field, where she would come back the slower. The girl seemed to doubt whether to fetch it; I made her take it up, and, when she had taken it, added weight to the apple, and hindered her both by the burden's heaviness and by the delay of reaching it. And, that my story may not be slower than the race itself — the girl was outrun; the winner led away his prize.

Was I not worthy, Adonis, that he should thank me and bring me the honor of incense? But, ungrateful, he gave me neither thanks nor incense. I was thrown into sudden anger; and, stung at being slighted, and lest I be despised in time to come, I guarded against it by an example, and roused myself against them both. They were passing a temple hidden in shady woods, which famous Echion had once built by vow to the Mother of the Gods; and the long journey urged them to rest there. There an untimely desire to embrace his wife seized Hippomenes, stirred by my power. Near the temple was a dim recess, like a cave, roofed with native pumice, holy by an ancient rite, where the priest had gathered many wooden images of the old gods. This he entered, and defiled the shrine with a forbidden shame. The sacred images turned away their eyes, and the tower-crowned Mother wondered whether to plunge the guilty in the Stygian stream. That seemed too light a penalty; so tawny manes cover their necks, so lately smooth; their fingers curve into claws; their shoulders become forelegs; all their weight passes into their chests; their tails sweep the surface of the sand. Their look has anger in it; instead of words they give growls; instead of chambers they haunt the woods; and, lions dreaded by others, they champ Cybele's bit with tamed jaws. These, my beloved, and with them every kind of beast that turns not its back in flight but its breast to the fight, avoid — lest your courage be the ruin of us both.

She warned him thus, and, yoking her swans, took her way through the air; but his courage stood against her warning. By chance his dogs, following a sure track, roused a boar, and, as it made to leave the wood, the son of Cinyras pierced it with a glancing stroke in the side. At once the fierce boar struck out the blood-stained hunting-spear with its curved snout, and pursued him as he trembled and sought a safe retreat, and buried its whole tusks in his groin, and stretched him dying on the tawny sand. Cytherea, borne in her light chariot through the middle air, had not yet come to Cyprus on her swans' wings; she knew the groan of the dying from afar and turned her white birds that way; and when, from the high sky, she saw him half dead, his body thrashing in its own blood, she leaped down, and tore both her robe and her hair, and beat her breast with unworthy hands, and, reproaching the Fates, said: "Yet all shall not be in your power. The memorial of my grief, Adonis, will last forever; and the yearly re-enactment of your death shall carry out an image of my mourning. But your blood shall be changed into a flower. Was it once allowed you, Persephone, to change a woman's limbs into fragrant mint — and shall the hero, the son of Cinyras, be a grudge against me if he is changed?" So saying, she sprinkled his blood with sweet-smelling nectar; and, touched by it, the blood swelled up, as a clear bubble is wont to rise in tawny mud; and no longer than a full hour's delay had passed when a flower sprang from the blood, its own color, such as the pomegranates bear that hide their seeds beneath a tough rind. Yet the enjoyment of it is short-lived; for the very winds that give it its name beat it down, since it clings poorly and falls by its own lightness.
