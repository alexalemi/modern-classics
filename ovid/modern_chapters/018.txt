Book X

(Part 1 of 2)

From there, wrapped in his saffron cloak, Hymenaeus passed through the measureless air and made for the land of the Ciconians, called — in vain — by the voice of Orpheus. He came, indeed, but he brought no solemn words, no glad face, no lucky omen. The very torch he held kept hissing with a tearful smoke, and, for all his shaking, found no flame. The outcome was heavier than the omen: for the new bride, wandering through the grass with her train of Naiads, fell dead, a serpent's fang in her ankle.

When the bard of Rhodope had wept for her enough in the upper air, he dared, so as to try the shades below as well, to go down to the Styx by the gate of Taenarus; and through the weightless peoples and the ghosts that had had their burial he came to Persephone and to the lord of the shades who holds that joyless realm, and, striking his strings in tune with his words, he sang:

"O powers of the world set beneath the earth, to which we all fall back, all of us who are born to die — if I may, and if you let me speak the truth, laying aside the winding tricks of a false tongue: I have not come down here to see dark Tartarus, nor to bind the triple throat of the Medusan monster, shaggy with snakes. My wife is the reason of my journey — a viper, trodden underfoot, poured its poison into her and cut short her growing years. I wished to be able to bear it, and I will not deny that I tried; but Love has won. That god is well known in the world above; whether he is known here too, I doubt — and yet I think that even here he is, and, if the tale of the old rape is not a lie, Love joined you two as well. By these places full of dread, by this vast Chaos, by the silence of these boundless realms, I beg you: unravel the too-swift fate of Eurydice. To you we all belong; we linger a little while above, then, sooner or later, hurry to this one home. This way we all are bound; this is our last house; you hold the longest reign over the human race. She too, when in due time she has lived out her rightful years, will fall under your law. I ask her use as a gift; and if the Fates deny this favor for my wife, I am resolved not to return — take your joy in the death of us both."

As he spoke this, striking the strings to his words, the bloodless spirits wept. Tantalus did not snatch at the fleeing water; Ixion's wheel stood still in wonder; the birds did not tear at the liver of Tityus; the daughters of Belus paused at their urns; and you, Sisyphus, sat down on your stone. Then, for the first time — so the story goes — the cheeks of the Furies, mastered by the song, were wet with tears; and neither the royal wife nor the ruler of the depths could bear to refuse him, and they called Eurydice. She was among the newly come shades, and came forward with a slow step from her wound. The Rhodopeian hero took her, and with her this condition: that he not turn his eyes back until he had left the vales of Avernus — or the gift would be void.

Up the sloping path they climb through the mute silence, steep, dark, thick with black fog. And now they were not far from the edge of the upper earth, when he, in love, afraid she might fail and hungry to see her, turned his eyes — and at once she slipped back. Reaching out her arms, straining to be held and to hold him, the poor woman caught nothing but the yielding air. And now, dying a second time, she made no complaint of her husband — for what could she complain of but that she was loved? She spoke one last "Farewell," which he could barely catch, and was swept back again to the same place.

No otherwise was Orpheus stunned at his wife's double death than the man who, trembling, saw the three necks of the dog, the middle one in chains, and whom fear did not leave before his old nature did, as stone spread over his body; or than Olenus, who took another's guilt upon himself and chose to seem the guilty one, and than you, unhappy Lethaea, so sure of your beauty — hearts once most closely joined, now stones that watery Ida holds. He begged, and longed in vain to cross again, but the ferryman drove him off. Still, for seven days he sat squalid on the bank, without the gifts of Ceres; care and grief of mind and tears were his food. Crying that the gods of Erebus were cruel, he withdrew to high Rhodope and to Haemus, battered by the north winds.

Three times the sun had ended the year bounded by the ocean's Fishes, and Orpheus had shunned all love of women, whether because it had gone so badly for him, or because he had given his word; yet many women burned to join themselves to the bard, and many grieved to be refused. He was even the first to teach the people of Thrace to turn their love to tender boys, and to pluck the brief spring and the first flowers of their youth this side of manhood.

There was a hill, and on the hill a level stretch of open ground, which the blades of grass made green; the place lacked shade. When the god-born bard sat down there and struck his sounding strings, a shade came over the spot. The tree of Chaonia was not missing, nor the grove of the Heliades, nor the tall-branched winter-oak, nor the soft lindens, nor the beech, nor the unwed laurel, nor the brittle hazels, nor the ash good for spears, nor the knotless fir, nor the holm bent under its acorns, nor the friendly plane, nor the maple of many colors, nor the willows that grow by rivers, and the water-lotus, and the evergreen box, and the slender tamarisks, and the two-colored myrtle, and the tine with its dark-blue berries. You too, ivy of the winding foot, came, and with you the tendriled vines and the elms clothed in vines, the mountain-ash and the pitch-pines, the arbutus loaded with its blushing fruit, the pliant palm, the victor's prize, and the pine with its tufted top bristling — dear to the Mother of the Gods, since for her sake Cybele's Attis put off the man and hardened into that trunk.

Among this throng was the cypress, shaped like a cone — now a tree, but once a boy, beloved of the god who fits the lyre and the bow alike with strings. For there was a great stag, sacred to the nymphs who hold the fields of Carthaea, that gave a wide shade to its own head with its far-spreading antlers. His horns shone with gold; a jeweled collar hung from his smooth round neck down to his shoulders; a silver disc, tied with little straps, played on his brow; and matching pendants of bronze gleamed on his two ears about his hollow temples. Free of fear, his natural shyness laid aside, he used to haunt the houses and offer his neck to be stroked by any hands, even strangers'. But above all others he was dear to you, Cyparissus, most beautiful of the people of Cea. You led the stag to fresh pasture and to the water of the clear spring; now you wove flowers of many colors through his horns, now, seated on his back like a rider, you steered his gentle mouth this way and that with a purple bridle.

It was summer, the middle of the day, and the curving arms of the shore-loving Crab glowed with the sun's heat. The stag, weary, had laid his body on the grassy ground and was drinking the cool of a tree's shade. Without meaning to, the boy Cyparissus pierced him with his sharp javelin; and when he saw him dying of the cruel wound, he resolved to die himself. What comfort did Phoebus not offer, urging him to grieve in measure, and no more than the loss deserved! Still the boy groaned, and asked this last gift of the gods: to mourn forever. And now, his blood drained by endless weeping, his limbs began to turn green, and the hair that lately hung over his snow-white brow became a bristling bush, and, stiffening, pointed to the starry sky with a tapering top. The god groaned and, grieving, said: "You will be mourned by me, and you will mourn for others, and stand by those who sorrow."

Such a wood the bard had drawn about him, and he sat in the midst of a gathering of wild beasts and a crowd of birds. When he had tried the strings enough with his thumb and heard that the different tones, though they sounded apart, still chimed together, he lifted his voice in this song:

From Jove, my mother Muse — all things bow to the reign of Jove — set my song moving! Often before I have sung the power of Jove; in a heavier strain I sang the Giants and the victorious bolts scattered over the Phlegraean plains. Now I need a lighter lyre: let me sing of boys the gods have loved, and of girls struck by forbidden fires who earned punishment for their lust.

The king of the gods once burned with love for Phrygian Ganymede, and something was found that Jupiter would rather be than what he was. Yet he stooped to turn himself into no bird but the one that could carry his bolts. Without delay, beating the air on borrowed wings, he snatched away the boy of Ilium — who even now mixes his cups and, though Juno is unwilling, serves Jove his nectar.

You too, descendant of Amyclas, Phoebus would have set in the sky, if the grim Fates had given him time to set you there. Still, so far as may be, you are immortal; and as often as spring drives off winter, and the Ram follows the watery Fish, so often you rise and bloom on the green turf. My father loved you beyond all others, and Delphi at the center of the earth went without its guardian god while he haunted the Eurotas and unwalled Sparta; his lyre and his arrows were held in no honor. Forgetting himself, he did not refuse to carry the nets, to hold the dogs, to go as a companion over the ridges of the harsh mountains; and by long habit he fed his flame.

Now Titan stood almost midway between the coming and the gone night, an equal distance from both, when they stripped their bodies and shone with the juice of the rich olive and began the game of the broad discus. Phoebus first sent it, well poised, into the airy breeze, and split the facing clouds with its weight. After a long while the heavy mass fell to the solid ground, showing skill joined with strength. At once, thoughtless and driven by eagerness for the sport, the boy of Taenarus hurried to lift the disc — but the hard ground bounced it back with a rebounding stroke into your face, Hyacinthus. The god went as pale as the boy, and caught up the sinking limbs, and now warms you, now stanches your sad wound, now checks the fleeing life with herbs pressed to it. His arts are no use; the wound was past healing. As when, in a well-watered garden, someone breaks off violets, or a stiff poppy, or lilies bristling with their yellow tongues, and, withered, they suddenly droop their heavy heads, and cannot hold themselves up, and look at the ground with their tops — so the dying face sinks, and, its strength gone, the neck is a burden to itself and leans on the shoulder.

"You are falling, son of Oebalus, cheated of your first youth," said Phoebus, "and I see your wound, my own crime. You are my grief and my guilt: my right hand must be charged with your death; I am the author of your dying. Yet what is my fault — unless to have played can be called a fault, unless to have loved can be called a fault? And oh, that I might die with you, or give my life for yours! But since we are held by the law of fate, you shall always be with me, and dwell on my remembering lips. My songs, my lyre struck by my hand, shall sound of you; and, a new flower, you shall copy my laments in your markings. The time will come, too, when a most valiant hero shall join his name to this flower, and it shall be read on the same petals."

While such things were told by Apollo's truthful lips, look — the blood that, spilled on the ground, had stained the grass, ceases to be blood, and a flower springs up, brighter than Tyrian purple, taking the shape lilies have, but for the color: theirs is silver, this is purple. This was not enough for Phoebus — for he was the author of the honor: he himself wrote his laments on the petals, and the flower bears the letters AI, AI inscribed, the mournful marks traced there. Nor is Sparta ashamed to have borne Hyacinthus; his honor lasts to this day, and the Hyacinthia comes round each year to be kept, with its solemn procession, in the manner of old.

But if you should ask Amathus, rich in ores, whether she would wish to have borne the Propoetides, she would say no — as she would to those whose brows were once rough with two horns, from which they took their name, the Cerastae. Before their doors stood an altar of Jupiter the Guest — a scene of horror: if some stranger had seen it stained with blood, he would have thought suckling calves had been killed there, and Amathusian sheep. It was guests who were slaughtered! Kindly Venus, offended at the wicked sacrifices, was making ready to abandon her own cities and the Ophiusian fields. "But how have my pleasant places, how have my cities sinned?" she said. "What crime is theirs? Let this impious race pay instead by exile, or death, or something between death and flight — and what can that be, but the punishment of a changed shape?" While she hesitates what to change them into, she turns her eyes to their horns, and is reminded that these may be left them; and she transforms their huge limbs into fierce bulls.

Yet the vile Propoetides dared to deny that Venus was a goddess. For that, through the goddess's anger, they are said to have been the first to sell their bodies and their good name; and when shame was gone and the blood of their faces had hardened, they were turned, by a small change, into flint.

Because Pygmalion had seen these women living out their lives in crime, offended by the many faults nature had given the female mind, he lived unmarried, without a wife, and long had no partner of his bed. Meanwhile, with wonderful skill he carved snow-white ivory and gave it a beauty no woman could be born with, and fell in love with his own work. The face was that of a real girl, one you would think alive, and, if modesty did not forbid, wishing to move: so well did the art hide its own art. Pygmalion marvels, and draws into his breast the fire of that pretended body. Often he lays his hands on the work to test whether it is flesh or ivory, and will not yet admit it is ivory. He gives it kisses, and thinks they are returned; he speaks to it, holds it, and believes his fingers sink into the limbs he touches, and fears a bruise may come on the pressed flesh. Now he uses soft words, now brings it gifts that please girls: shells and smooth pebbles, little birds and thousand-colored flowers, lilies, painted balls, and the amber tears of the Heliades fallen from the trees. He dresses its limbs too, puts gems on its fingers, a long necklace on its neck; light pearls hang from its ears, ribbons on its breast — all become it, and it seems no less lovely bare. He lays it on a couch dyed with Sidonian purple, calls it the companion of his bed, and rests its reclining neck on soft down, as if it could feel.

The festival day of Venus, kept most solemnly through all Cyprus, had come; heifers with snow-white necks, their curved horns tipped with gold, had fallen at the stroke; the incense smoked; when, his offering made, Pygmalion stood at the altar and shyly said, "If you gods can give all things, let my wife be, I pray" — not daring to say "the ivory maid" — "one like my ivory." Golden Venus, present at her own festival, understood what that prayer meant; and as a sign that the god was kind, three times the flame flared and drew a tongue of fire up through the air. When he came home, he sought the image of his girl, and, leaning on the couch, gave it kisses: it seemed warm. He puts his mouth to it again, feels its breast with his hands — the ivory softens at the touch, and, its hardness gone, yields to the fingers, gives way as Hymettian wax grows soft in the sun and, worked by the thumb, is bent into many shapes and made usable by the very handling. While he stands amazed, and doubtfully rejoices, and fears he is deceived, again and again the lover touches with his hand the object of his prayer. It was a body! The veins throb, tested by the thumb. Then indeed the Paphian hero pours out the fullest words to thank Venus, and at last presses his mouth on a mouth no longer false; and the girl felt the kisses given her, and blushed, and, lifting her timid eyes to the light, saw at once her lover and the sky. The goddess was present at the marriage she had made; and now, when the moon's horns had nine times been gathered to a full circle, the girl bore Paphos, from whom the island keeps its name.
