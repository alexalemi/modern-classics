Book IV

(Part 2 of 2)

But now the power of Bacchus was the great story in every corner of Thebes, and his aunt Ino went about telling everyone of the mighty new god's deeds — she alone, of so many sisters, free of grief, except for the grief her sisters had brought on her. Juno watched her: watched her carry her head high because of her children, her marriage to Athamas, and above all the god she had reared. She could not stomach it. "The son of my rival," she said to herself, "was able to change the sailors of Lydia into fish and drown them, was able to hand a son over to his own mother to be torn to pieces, was able to fold three daughters of Minyas into new-grown wings — and Juno can do nothing but weep over wrongs no one has answered for? And that is to be enough for me? That is the whole of my power? He himself shows me what to do. There's no shame in learning even from an enemy — and what madness can do, he's proved past all doubt in the butchering of Pentheus. So why shouldn't Ino be goaded by madness too, and driven down the same road her sisters took?"

There is a downward path, shaded with dismal yew, that leads through the depths of silence to the halls of the dead. Here the sluggish Styx breathes up its fogs, and this is the way the newly made ghosts come down, and the shades of those who have had their burial rites. A deathly pallor and an unending winter hold those bleak wastes far and wide, and the newcomer dead do not know the road that leads to the Stygian city, or where the grim palace of black Pluto stands. The vast city has a thousand entrances and gates thrown open on every side; and as the sea takes in the rivers of the whole earth, so that place takes in every soul, and is never too small for any tide of people, and never feels the crowd grow. The shades wander bloodless, without body or bones; some throng the place of judgment, some the hall of the king below; some ply the trades they knew, in imitation of the life they had before, while their own punishment holds the rest fast.

Juno, daughter of Saturn, so far gave in to her hatred and her rage that she left her home in heaven and went down there. The moment she stepped in, and the threshold groaned under the weight of her sacred body, Cerberus lifted his three heads and let out three barks at once. She called on the Sisters born of Night, terrible and unappeasable goddesses. They sat before the prison door, shut close with adamant, combing black vipers out of their hair. As soon as they knew her, there among the shadows of the dark, the goddesses rose. This is the place they call Accursed. Here Tityos gave up his innards to be mangled, stretched across nine acres of ground. You, Tantalus, could reach no water, and the tree that leaned over you kept snatching itself away. Sisyphus, you were forever chasing or shoving that stone that must roll back again. Ixion spun on his wheel, fleeing himself and following himself at once. And the granddaughters of Belus, who had dared to plot the murder of their cousins, kept fetching over and over the water they kept losing.

When Juno had looked over all of them with a hard eye — Ixion first of all, and then, turning from him, Sisyphus — she said, "Why should this one, alone of all the brothers, suffer punishment without end, while a rich palace keeps proud Athamas, who along with his wife has always held me in contempt?" And she laid out the reason for her hatred and her coming, and what she wanted. What she wanted was this: that the house of Cadmus should not stand, and that the Furies should drag Athamas into some horror. Command, promise, and plea she rolled into one, and pressed the goddesses hard. When Juno had finished, Tisiphone shook her tangled white hair as it was, tossed the snakes back from across her face, and said: "No need for a long speech. Whatever you order, count it done. Leave this loveless kingdom and take yourself back to the better air of heaven." Juno went back overjoyed; and as she made ready to enter the sky, Iris, the rainbow's goddess, purified her with a sprinkle of dewy water.

There was no delay. Grim Tisiphone caught up a torch soaked in gore, pulled on a cloak red with dripping blood, wound a writhing snake about her waist for a belt, and went out from her house. Mourning went with her as she walked, and Fright, and Terror, and Madness with its trembling face. She stopped on the threshold. The doorposts of Athamas, the son of Aeolus, are said to have shuddered; the maple doors went pale, and the sun fled the spot. His wife was terrified by these signs; Athamas was terrified too, and the two of them were getting ready to leave the house — but the deadly Fury planted herself in the doorway and blocked the way out. She spread wide her arms knotted with vipers and shook her hair. The snakes she shook cried out; some lay along her shoulders, some slid around her breast, hissing and spewing corruption and flickering their tongues. Then she wrenched two snakes from the middle of her hair and threw them, in her plague-bringing hand, at the pair of them; and the snakes went crawling over the breast of Ino and the breast of Athamas, breathing their foul breath into them. They left no wound on the body — it was the mind that felt the dreadful blows.

She had brought with her, too, a monstrous brew of liquid poison: foam from the mouths of Cerberus, and the venom of Echidna, and blind wanderings, and the forgetfulness of a darkened mind, and crime, and tears, and rabid fury, and the love of killing — all of it ground together, mixed with fresh blood, and boiled in a hollow bronze pot, stirred with a stalk of green hemlock. And while the two of them stood shaking, she poured the maddening poison over both their hearts and stirred it down into their inmost being. Then, whirling her torch round and round the same circle, she chased fire with fire until the flames blurred into one ring. So, triumphant, her orders carried out, she went back to the empty kingdom of great Pluto and unwound the snake she had worn as a belt.

At once the son of Aeolus, out of his mind, cried out in the middle of his hall: "Ho, men! Spread your nets here in these woods! I just saw a lioness with a pair of cubs!" And, insane, he chased the tracks of his wife as if she were a wild beast, and snatched Learchus — the boy smiling and stretching out his little arms from his mother's breast — and swung him two or three times through the air like a stone in a sling, and, savage, smashed the baby's small face against the hard rock. Then at last the mother, roused — whether by grief, or by the poison spread through her — screamed aloud and ran, out of her senses, with her hair streaming, carrying you, little Melicerta, in her bare arms, and shouting "Euhoe, Bacchus!" At the name of Bacchus, Juno laughed. "May your foster-child do you many such favors," she said.

A cliff hangs out over the sea. Its base is hollowed by the waves and shelters the water beneath it from the rain; its top is jagged and stretches its brow out over the open water. Ino climbed it — madness had given her the strength — and, held back by no fear, threw herself and her burden into the deep; the water, struck by her fall, turned white with foam. But Venus, pitying the undeserved sufferings of her granddaughter, coaxed her uncle with soft words: "God of the waters, Neptune, to whom fell a power second only to heaven's own — I ask a great thing, but take pity on my own blood, whom you see tossed on the boundless Ionian sea, and add them to your gods. I have some claim on the sea myself, if it's true that once, in the middle of the deep, I formed out of the foam — and from that foam my Greek name still comes." Neptune granted her prayer. He took from the two of them all that was mortal, laid on them a majesty to be revered, and made both their names and their shapes new: he called the son Palaemon, and his mother Leucothoë.

Her Sidonian companions had followed the prints of Ino's feet as far as they could, and saw the last of her at the edge of the rock. Sure that she was dead, they beat their breasts and tore their hair and their clothes for the house of Cadmus, and heaped the blame on the goddess as unjust and far too cruel to her rival. Juno would not bear their reproaches. "I'll make you the greatest monuments of my cruelty," she said — and the deed came hard on the word. The one who had loved Ino most cried, "I'll follow my queen into the sea!" and was about to leap, but could not stir a muscle: she stuck fast, fixed to the rock. Another, as she tried to strike her breast with the old blow, felt her arms stiffen mid-motion. One, who had chanced to stretch her hands out over the waves of the sea, turned to stone with her hands still reaching over those same waves. Another, tearing at the hair she had caught at the crown of her head, you would have seen — her fingers suddenly hardening in the hair. Whatever posture each was caught in, she froze in it. And some turned into birds — the Ismenides, who to this day skim the surface of that same water with the tips of their wings.

The son of Agenor does not know that his daughter and his little grandson are now gods of the sea. Broken by grief, by one calamity heaped on the next, and by all the omens he had seen, Cadmus the founder fled his own city, as though the bad luck of the place, and not his own, were bearing down on him. Driven by long wandering, he came at last, an exile with his exiled wife, to the borders of Illyria. And now, weighed down with woes and years, while they were dwelling again on the first disasters of their house and going back over their sufferings in talk, Cadmus said: "That serpent I ran through with my spear — was it a sacred one, back when I set out from Sidon and sowed the viper's teeth across the ground, a strange new seed? If the gods are avenging it with such unerring rage, then I pray that I myself may be stretched out as a serpent into a long belly." He had barely spoken when, as a serpent, he was stretched into a long belly, and felt scales rising over his toughening skin, and his darkening body flecked with spots of blue-green. He fell forward onto his breast; his legs, fused into one, tapered slowly to a smooth, rounded point. His arms were all that was left. He stretched out the arms that were left, and, with the tears still running down his face — a face still human — he said, "Come here, my wife, come here, most wretched of women, and while some part of me is still here, touch me, take my hand while it is still a hand, while the snake has not taken all of me." He wanted to say more, but his tongue split suddenly into two, and the words would not come to him though he willed them, and every time he tried to force out some lament, he hissed. That was the voice Nature left him.

His wife struck her bare breast with her hand and cried out: "Cadmus, stay! Unhappy man, free yourself from this monstrous shape! Cadmus, what is this? Where are your feet, where your shoulders and your hands, your color, your face — where, while I am still speaking, is all the rest of you? Why, gods of heaven, do you not turn me too into the same serpent?" So she cried; and he licked his wife's face, and slid into her dear breast as though he knew her, and gave her the old embraces and reached for the neck he knew so well. Everyone there — for companions were present — was horrified; but she stroked the smooth, crested neck of the dragon, and all at once there were two of them, gliding along in joined coils, until they slipped into the cover of a nearby grove. Even now they neither flee from a man nor strike him with a wound: gentle serpents, they remember what they once were.

Yet, changed as they were, the two of them found great comfort in their grandson — Bacchus, whom a conquered India worshipped as a god, and whom Achaia honored with temples raised in his name. Only Acrisius, son of Abas, sprung from the same line, was left to bar the god from the walls of Argos and take up arms against him and refuse to believe that he was any son of Jupiter. He did not believe Perseus a son of Jupiter either — Perseus, whom Danae had conceived in a shower of gold. But soon (such is the force of truth) Acrisius was sorry both that he had wronged the god and that he had failed to recognize his grandson: for the one was already set in heaven, while the other, carrying the memorable spoils of the viper-haired monster, was slicing through the yielding air on whirring wings. And as the conqueror hung over the Libyan sands, drops of blood fell from the Gorgon's head; the earth caught them and quickened them into snakes of every kind — which is why that land, to this day, swarms and seethes with serpents.

Carried on from there through the vast sky by warring winds, borne now here, now there, like a rain-heavy cloud, he looked down from the heights on the far-off earth below and flew over the whole round world. Three times he saw the cold Bears, three times the claws of the Crab; often he was swept off toward the west, often toward the east. And now, as the day was failing, afraid to trust himself to the night, he came to rest in the western part of the world, in the kingdom of Atlas, and looked for a little rest until the Morning Star should call up the fires of Dawn, and Dawn the chariot of the day.

Here lived Atlas, the son of Iapetus, greater than all other men in the sheer bulk of his body. Under this king lay the world's farthest edge, and the sea that takes in the panting horses of the Sun and receives his weary chariot. A thousand flocks and a thousand herds roamed his pastures, and no neighbor crowded his land. And here leaves of gleaming, radiant gold hid golden branches and golden fruit. "Friend," Perseus said to him, "if the glory of a great line means anything to you, Jupiter is the father of my blood; and if you admire great deeds, you'll admire mine. I ask for shelter and rest." But Atlas remembered an old prophecy — the one Themis of Parnassus had given him: "A time will come, Atlas, when your tree will be stripped of its gold, and a son of Jove will carry off the glory of the prize." Dreading it, Atlas had walled his orchard round with solid stone and given it over to a huge dragon to guard, and kept all strangers out of his land. To Perseus, too, he said, "Get far away from here, or the glory of the deeds you lie about — and Jupiter with it — will be far off when you need them!" And he added force to his threats, and tried to shove him from the door as Perseus held back and mixed brave words with gentle ones. Weaker in strength — for who could match Atlas for strength? — Perseus said, "Well, since my friendship means so little to you, then take this gift!" And, turning his own face away, he thrust out, on his left, the ghastly head of Medusa.

Huge as he was, Atlas became a mountain. His beard and his hair passed into forests; his shoulders and his arms became ridges; what had been his head was now the peak at the mountain's top; his bones turned to stone. Then, swelling out on every side, he grew to an immense height — so you ordained it, gods — and the whole sky, with all its stars, came to rest on him.

The grandson of Hippotas — Aeolus, keeper of the winds — had shut the winds up in their eternal prison, and the Morning Star, that rouses men to their work, had risen bright in the high heavens. Perseus took up his wings again, strapped them to his feet on either side, buckled on his hooked sword, and cut through the clear air on his beating ankle-wings. Leaving countless nations behind him, around and below, he came in sight of the Ethiopians and the fields of Cepheus. There the unjust judgment of Ammon had condemned the innocent Andromeda to pay the penalty for her mother's boasting tongue.

The moment Perseus saw her, her arms bound to the hard rock — but for the light breeze stirring her hair and the warm tears running from her eyes, he would have taken her for a statue of marble — he caught fire without knowing it, and stood amazed; seized by the vision of the beauty before him, he all but forgot to keep beating his wings in the air. When he had landed, he said, "You do not deserve these chains — only the chains that bind eager lovers to each other. Tell me, since I ask it, the name of this land and your own name, and why you are in chains." At first she was silent — a girl, she dared not speak to a man — and she would have hidden her modest face in her hands, if she had not been bound. Her eyes, all she could do, she filled with welling tears. When he pressed her again and again, she told him — so that she would not seem unwilling to confess some fault of her own — the name of her country and her own name, and how great her mother's confidence in her own beauty had been. Not everything was yet told when the water roared, and a monster came looming over the boundless sea, its head raised high, its breast covering the wide expanse of water. The girl shrieked. Her grieving father and mother were there, both of them wretched — though the mother more justly so — and they brought no help with them, only tears fit for the moment and loud lamentation, and they clung to her bound body.

Then the stranger spoke: "There will be a long time left for tears; the hour for bringing help is short. If I were to ask for her hand — I, Perseus, son of Jove and of the woman whom Jove filled, shut in her tower, with his fertile gold; Perseus, who overcame the snake-haired Gorgon, and dared to travel the winds of the air on beating wings — surely I would be preferred above all others as your son-in-law. But I'll try to add merit of my own to gifts like these, if only the gods are with me: I bargain only that she be mine, once she is saved by my courage." Her parents accepted the terms — who could hesitate? — and begged for his help, and promised the kingdom too, as a dowry on top.

Look now: as a ship with its beak fixed at the prow plows the water, driven on by the sweating arms of young rowers, so the monster came, shoving the waves aside with the thrust of its breast. It was as far from the rocks as a Balearic sling can send its whirled lead across the middle of the sky, when suddenly the youth, kicking off from the ground, shot high into the clouds. As the hero's shadow fell on the surface of the sea, the monster hurled all its fury at the shadow it saw. And just as the bird of Jupiter, when it spots in an open field a serpent baring its blue-black back to the sun, takes it from behind, and, to keep it from twisting its savage mouth around, sinks its greedy talons into the scaly neck — so the winged hero, plunging headlong through the empty air in his swift flight, pressed down on the monster's back, and, as it roared, drove his sword up to the curved hook into its right shoulder.

Torn by the deep wound, the beast now reared high into the air, now dove beneath the water, now wheeled about like a wild boar hemmed in and terrified by a baying pack of hounds. Perseus dodged the greedy jaws on his swift wings, and wherever the beast lay open he struck with his curved blade — now its back, crusted with hollow shells, now the ribs of its flanks, now where the tail thinned away and ended in a fish. The beast vomited from its mouth a flood of seawater mixed with red blood, and the spray soaked the hero's wings and weighed them down. Perseus no longer dared trust his dripping ankle-wings; he spotted a rock that stood clear of the water at its highest point when the sea lay calm, though now it was hidden by the heaving waves. Bracing himself on it, gripping the top of the ridge with his left hand, three or four times he drove the iron through the monster's guts. A shout of applause filled the shores and the high halls of the gods; Cassiope and Cepheus the father rejoiced, and hailed him as their son-in-law, and confessed that he was the prop and the savior of their house. Freed from her chains, the girl came walking forward — at once the reward of his labor and its cause.

Perseus washed his victorious hands in water dipped from the sea; and so that the harsh sand would not bruise the snake-bearing head, he softened the ground with leaves, spread over it fronds grown beneath the water, and laid Medusa's face, the daughter of Phorcys, on them. The fresh fronds, still alive with their thirsty pith, drank in the monster's power and hardened at its touch, taking a strange new stiffness in branch and leaf. And the sea-nymphs tried the wondrous thing on more fronds, and, delighted by the same result, scattered the seeds of them across the waves. Even now that same nature stays in the coral: it takes its hardness from the touch of the air, and what was a soft plant under the sea becomes stone above it.

To three gods he built three altars of turf: the left one to Mercury, the right one to you, warlike maiden — Minerva — and Jove's altar in the middle. A cow was sacrificed to Minerva, a calf to the wing-footed god, and a bull to you, greatest of the gods. And straightaway he took Andromeda, the prize of so great a deed, with no dowry asked. Hymen and Cupid waved the wedding torches before them; the fires were heaped high with rich perfumes; garlands hung from the houses; and everywhere lyres and flutes and pipes and singing rang out, the glad tokens of a joyful heart. The doors were thrown open, and the golden halls stood wide and displayed, and the nobles of King Cepheus came in to a banquet laid out in splendor.

When the feast was done and their spirits opened with the gift of generous Bacchus, Perseus asked about the ways and the character of the country and of its people. And when one of them had told him, he said, "Now, bravest Perseus, tell us, I beg you, by what courage and by what arts you carried off that head bristling with serpents." Perseus told them of a place lying beneath cold Atlas, safe behind a bulwark of solid rock; how at its entrance lived two sisters, the daughters of Phorcys, who shared the use of a single eye between them; how, by sly craft, he slipped his hand in the way as the eye was being passed from one to the other, and stole it; and how, through hidden, pathless country, over crags bristling with rough woods, he reached the home of the Gorgons; and how, along the fields and the roads, he saw the shapes of men and beasts turned from their own forms into stone at the sight of Medusa. Yet he himself had looked at Medusa's face only in the bronze of the shield on his left arm, in the reflection thrown back from it; and while a deep sleep held her and her snakes, he had struck the head from her neck — and Pegasus, the swift-winged, and his brother had been born from their mother's blood. He added, too, the dangers of his long journey — no invention, those — what seas and what lands he had seen far below him from on high, and what stars he had brushed with his beating wings.

Yet he fell silent sooner than they expected. Then one of the nobles took it up, asking why she alone of the sisters wore snakes braided in among her hair. And the stranger said: "Since you ask a thing worth the telling, hear the reason. She was once famous for her beauty, the envied hope of many suitors; and in her whole person there was no part more striking than her hair — I've met a man who said he'd seen it. The ruler of the sea, they say, forced himself on her in the temple of Minerva. Jove's daughter turned away and covered her chaste eyes with her shield; and, so that it would not go unpunished, she changed the Gorgon's hair into hideous water-snakes. And now, to strike terror into the enemies she stuns with dread, she still wears on her breast the very snakes that she made."
