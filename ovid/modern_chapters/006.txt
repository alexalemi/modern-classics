Book IV

(Part 1 of 2)

But Alcithoë, daughter of Minyas, will have none of it. She refuses to accept the god's wild rites, and in her recklessness she still denies that Bacchus is any son of Jupiter — and she has her sisters for partners in that impiety.

The priest had commanded the whole city to keep the festival: mistresses and serving-women alike were to lay their work aside, to cover their breasts with skins, to loosen the ribbons that bound their hair and wind garlands through it, and to take up the leaf-wreathed thyrsus in their hands; and he had prophesied that the anger of the slighted god would be savage if he were offended. The matrons obeyed, and the young brides too. They put away their weaving and their work-baskets and their half-finished wool, and they offered incense and called on the god by all his names — Bacchus, and Bromius, and Lyaeus; the Fire-born, the Twice-born, the only god ever given two mothers; and they added Nyseus, and unshorn Thyoneus, and Lenaeus lord of the winepress who first planted the joy-giving vine, and Nyctelius of the night-rites, and father Eleleus, and Iacchus, and Euhan — and all the other names besides that you bear, Liber, up and down the peoples of Greece.

For your youth is never used up. You are the boy who never ages; you are seen, most beautiful of all, high in the sky; and when you stand without your horns you have the face of a girl. The East was conquered by you, all the way to where dark-skinned India is washed by the far Ganges. It was you, god worthy of our worship, who struck down Pentheus and axe-bearing Lycurgus for their sacrilege, you who flung the Etruscan sailors into the sea, you who press the necks of the yoked lynxes under their painted reins. Bacchant women and satyrs come streaming after you — and that drunken old man, Silenus, propping his tottering limbs on a staff and clinging none too steadily to the swayback of his donkey. Wherever you go, the shouting of young men and the cries of women rise together, and hand-beaten drums, and the hollow clash of bronze, and the boxwood pipe with its long throat all sing out. "Be with us," the women of Thebes pray, "calm and kind" — and they keep the sacred rites as they were told.

Only the daughters of Minyas stay indoors, spoiling the festival with work no one should be doing on such a day. They card their wool, or twist the thread between their fingers, or bend to the loom, and keep their maids hard at it. At last one of them, drawing out her thread with a light thumb, said: "While the others are idling, crowding to these made-up rites, let us, whom Minerva keeps at her better craft, lighten our useful work with a bit of talk. Let's take turns telling a story, each to the others' unoccupied ears, so the long hours won't drag." The sisters liked the idea, and told her to be first.

She turned over in her mind which of the many tales she knew to tell — for she knew a great many — and could not decide. Should it be your story, Dercetis of Babylon, whom the Palestinians believe took on a new shape, scales sheathing your limbs, and stirred their pools? Or how your daughter grew wings and lived out her last years in whitewashed towers? Or how a water-nymph, with her spells and her too-powerful herbs, turned the bodies of young men into silent fish, until at last the same fate came for her? Or how the tree that once bore white fruit now bears it dark, stained by the touch of blood? That last one pleased her — it was no everyday tale — and so, while the wool ran out along her thread, she began.

"Pyramus and Thisbe — he the handsomest of young men, she the loveliest girl the East could show — lived in houses that shared a wall, in the city where Semiramis is said to have raised her high walls of baked brick. Being neighbors brought them together; it was where they first met, and where love first took its first steps. In time it grew. They would have joined hands in marriage, too, but their fathers forbade it. One thing, though, no father could forbid: they burned, both of them, their captured hearts on equal fire. There was no one they could confide in; they spoke by nods and signs. And the more the fire was smothered, the hotter it burned under the smothering.

"In the wall the two houses shared there was a thin split, a crack it had taken on long ago, when it was built. No one in all those years had ever noticed the flaw — but what does love not notice? You lovers were the first to find it, and you made it the road for your voices. Through it, safely, in the softest whisper, your endearments used to cross. Often, when they had taken their places — Thisbe here, Pyramus there — and each had caught the other's breath as it came, they would say, 'Jealous wall, why do you stand between two lovers? How hard would it be to let us join, our whole bodies together? Or if that's too much, at least open enough for a kiss. And don't think us ungrateful — we know we owe it to you that our words are given this crossing to loving ears.' So they would talk, apart and hopeless on their two sides, until, as night came on, they said 'Goodbye,' and each pressed kisses to their own face of the wall that could never reach across.

"The next morning had swept away the fires of the stars, and the sun had dried the frost from the grass, when they met again at the usual spot. First, in low murmurs, they poured out their long complaints; then they made a plan. That night, in the silence, they would try to slip past their guards and out their doors; and once out of the house, they would leave the city behind as well. But so as not to lose each other, wandering across the open country, they would meet at the tomb of Ninus and hide in the shadow of a tree — a tall mulberry, thick with snow-white fruit, standing close beside a cold spring. The plan pleased them, and the daylight, which seemed so slow to leave, at last sank into the sea, and out of that same sea the night came up.

"In the dark, clever Thisbe worked the door on its hinge and slipped out, unseen by her household. With her face veiled she reached the tomb and sat down under the appointed tree; love made her brave. And now — look — a lioness came, her foaming jaws smeared with the fresh kill of cattle, to quench her thirst at the neighboring spring. By the light of the moon Thisbe of Babylon saw her, still far off, and on trembling feet fled into a dark cave — and as she fled, the veil slipped from her shoulders and she left it lying behind. When the fierce lioness had drunk her fill of water and was turning back into the woods, she happened on the thin veil, without the girl in it, and shredded it in her bloodied mouth.

"Pyramus, who came out later, saw the unmistakable tracks of a wild beast in the deep dust, and the color drained from his whole face. But when he found the veil as well, soaked in blood, he said, 'One night will destroy two lovers. She was the one who deserved a long life; mine is the guilty soul. I killed you, my poor love — I, who told you to come by night to a place full of terror, and did not come here first myself. Tear my body apart, rip out my guilty guts with your savage jaws, all you lions who lurk beneath this rock! — But wishing for death is the coward's part.' He gathered up Thisbe's veil and carried it with him into the shade of the tree they had chosen, and when he had wept over the well-known cloth and covered it with kisses, he said, 'Now take a drink of my blood too.' And he drove the sword he wore into his side, and at once, dying, drew it back out of the hot wound. He fell on his back to the ground, and the blood leapt up high — the way water bursts from a lead pipe when the metal splits, hissing out through the narrow crack in a long jet, cutting the air. The fruit of the tree, spattered by that spray of death, turned dark, and the roots, drenched with his blood, dyed the hanging mulberries a deep purple.

"And now — look — with her fear not yet put aside, but afraid she might fail her lover, Thisbe came back, searching for the young man with her eyes and with all her heart, longing to tell him what great dangers she had escaped. She knew the place, she knew the shape of the tree — but the color of the fruit made her doubt: was this really it? While she wavered, she saw limbs on the bloody ground, quivering, twitching. She drew her foot back. Her face went paler than boxwood, and she shuddered like the surface of the sea when a light breeze skims across it and it trembles. But when she paused, and recognized her own love, she beat her innocent arms with loud blows of grief, and tore her hair, and threw her arms around the body she loved, filling his wounds with her tears, mixing her weeping into his blood; and pressing kisses on his cold face she cried out, 'Pyramus! What accident has torn you from me? Pyramus — answer me! It's your own Thisbe, your dearest, calling your name. Hear me — lift your fallen face!'

"At the name of Thisbe, Pyramus raised his eyes, already heavy with death, and once he had seen her, closed them again. When she saw her own veil, and saw the ivory sheath empty of its sword, she said, 'It was your own hand, and your love, that destroyed you, poor one. I too have a hand brave enough for this one thing, and I too have love — it will give me the strength to strike. I will follow you into death, and they will call me the most wretched cause and companion of your fate. You who could be torn from me by death alone — not even death will tear you from me now. And this one thing we ask of you both, with the words of us two: O our most wretched parents, mine and his, do not begrudge us this — that those whom a sure love and a last hour have joined be laid together in one tomb. And you, tree, whose branches now shade one poor body, and soon will shade two: keep the marks of this death. Bear fruit forever dark and fit for mourning, in memory of two lovers' blood.' She spoke, and set the point beneath her breast, and leaned down onto the blade still warm with his blood.

"Her prayers reached the gods; they reached the parents. For the color of the ripened fruit is black, and what the fire left of the two of them rests in a single urn."

She finished, and after a short pause Leuconoë began to speak, and her sisters fell silent.

"Even the Sun — who governs all things with his heavenly light — even he was caught by love. Let me tell you the loves of the Sun. This god is thought to have been the first to see the affair between Venus and Mars; this god sees everything first. Grieved at what he saw, he went to Venus's husband — Vulcan, the son of Juno — and showed him the wrong done to his marriage bed, and the place where it was done. Vulcan's mind reeled, and the work he was holding in his craftsman's hand dropped from it. At once he began to file out fine chains of bronze, and nets, and snares too delicate for the eye to catch — thread finer than the finest weaving, finer than the cobweb that hangs from the top of a rafter. He made them to yield to the lightest touch, the smallest movement, and arranged them cunningly around the bed. When his wife and her lover came together into that one bed, both were caught by the husband's craft, snared in the fetters he had rigged by this new trick, and pinned fast in the very middle of their embrace. The Lemnian god threw open the ivory doors and let the other gods in. There the two lay, bound in their shame — and more than one of the gods, and not the solemn ones, wished he could be shamed just so. The gods of heaven laughed, and for a long time this was the best-known story in all the sky.

"But the goddess of Cythera — Venus — did not forget the one who had exposed her. She took her revenge, and made the god who had wounded her secret love feel a wound of the same kind. What good now, son of Hyperion, is your beauty, your heat, your radiant light? You who scorch every land with your fire are burning with a strange new fire yourself. You who ought to be watching everything are watching Leucothoë, and on one girl you fix the eyes you owe to the whole world. Sometimes you rise too early in the eastern sky; sometimes you sink too late into the waves, and by lingering to look at her you stretch out the short hours of midwinter. Sometimes you fail altogether, and the trouble of your mind passes into your light, so that, darkened, you frighten the hearts of mortals. And it is not the moon, standing closer to earth, that blocks your face and makes you pale: it is this love that gives you that color. You love her alone. Clymene cannot hold you now, nor Rhodos, nor Perse, the lovely mother of Circe of Aeaea, nor Clytie, who longed for your embrace though you scorned her, and who at that very time was nursing a deep wound of love. Leucothoë made you forget them all — Leucothoë, whom Eurynome, loveliest woman of the perfume-bearing lands, brought into the world; but once the daughter grew up, she outshone her mother as far as her mother had outshone all others. Her father, Orchamus, ruled the Persian cities, seventh in line from old Belus at the root of the family.

"Under the western sky lie the pastures of the Sun's horses. Instead of grass they feed on ambrosia; it restores their limbs, worn out by the day's labor, and refits them for their work. And while the horses cropped their heavenly fodder there, and night was taking her turn at the sky, the god slipped into the chamber he longed for, changed into the shape of Eurynome, the girl's mother. There, among twice six handmaids by the lamplight, he saw Leucothoë drawing out smooth threads on her turning spindle. And so, like a mother kissing her dear daughter, he kissed her, and said, 'There is a private matter — maids, leave us; don't rob a mother of the right to speak with her own daughter alone.' They obeyed, and with no witness left in the room the god said, 'I am the one who marks out the long year, who sees everything, through whom the earth sees everything — the eye of the world. Believe me: you please me.' She was terrified; in her fear the distaff and spindle fell from her loosened fingers. Even her fear became her. He waited no longer, but returned to his true form and his familiar brightness. And the girl, though shaken by the sudden sight, was overcome by the god's radiance, and, all her protests set aside, she let his violence be done to her.

"Clytie was jealous — for her own love of the Sun had been anything but moderate — and, stung by rage at a rival, she spread the affair abroad and, once it was talked of, carried it to Leucothoë's father. He was fierce and unforgiving. As his daughter reached out her hands toward the Sun's light and pleaded, crying, 'It was he who forced me against my will,' he buried her alive, deep in the ground, and heaped a mound of heavy sand on top of her. The son of Hyperion scattered it with his rays and opened a passage for her to lift her buried face through — but by now, nymph, you could not raise your head, crushed under the weight of earth, and you lay there, a bloodless body. The driver of the winged horses is said to have seen nothing more heartbreaking since the fires that killed Phaethon. He tried, if he could, to call the cold limbs back to living warmth with the force of his rays; but since fate stood in the way of so great an effort, he sprinkled her body and the place with fragrant nectar, and, after a long lament, said, 'Still, you will reach the sky.' At once the body, steeped in that heavenly nectar, dissolved and soaked the earth with its perfume, and a shoot of frankincense, sending its roots down slowly through the soil, rose up and split the mound with its tip.

"But to Clytie the giver of light came no more — though her love might have excused her grief, and her grief her betrayal — and he ended everything between them. From that time she wasted away, having used her passion so madly. She could not bear the company of the other nymphs, and day and night, under the open sky, she sat on the bare ground, her hair loose and uncared for. For nine days she went without water, without food, feeding her fast on nothing but dew and her own tears, and never stirred from the ground. She only gazed at the face of the god as he moved across the sky, and turned her own face to follow him. They say her limbs took root in the soil; a sickly paleness turned part of her color into that of a bloodless plant, but part of her is red, and a flower much like a violet hides her face. And though a root holds her fast, she turns toward the Sun, and, changed as she is, she keeps her love."

She had finished, and the wondrous tale had caught their ears. Some of the sisters said it could never have happened; others insisted that true gods can do anything — but Bacchus is not among them. When they had fallen silent, Alcithoë was called on next. Running her shuttle through the threads of the loom strung before her, she said, "I'll pass over the well-known love of Daphnis, the shepherd of Ida, whom a nymph's jealous rage — she was his lover, and he wronged her — turned into a stone: such is the pain that burns those in love. Nor will I tell how Sithon, when nature's law was overturned, was of both sexes, now a man, now a woman. You too I skip over, Celmis — now hard adamant, but once the most faithful playmate of the infant Jupiter — and the Curetes, born from a heavy shower of rain, and Crocus, changed together with Smilax into little flowers. Instead I'll hold your minds with a sweet novelty.

"Learn how the spring of Salmacis got its evil name, and why it saps a man's strength and softens the limbs of whoever bathes in it. The reason is hidden, but the power of the fountain is famous. To Mercury the goddess of Cythera — Venus — bore a boy, and the water-nymphs raised him in the caves of Mount Ida. In his face you could make out both his mother and his father, and from them he took his name too. As soon as he had turned fifteen, he left his native mountains, left Ida where he had been reared, and delighted in wandering strange places and seeing unknown rivers, his eagerness making the hardship light. He came at last to the cities of Lycia, and to the Carians who border on them. There he found a pool of water, clear all the way down to the bottom — no marsh-reeds around it, no barren sedge, no sharp-tipped rushes; the water was pure and see-through, and the edges of the pool were ringed with fresh turf and grass forever green.

"A nymph lived there — but one no good at hunting, who never learned to bend a bow or race for speed; the only water-nymph swift Diana never knew. Her sisters, it's said, often told her, 'Salmacis, take up a javelin, or a painted quiver, and mix some hard hunting into your idleness.' But she took up no javelin and no painted quiver, and mixed no hunting into her idleness. Instead she would bathe her lovely limbs in her own spring, or comb her hair smooth with a boxwood comb from Mount Cytorus, and, looking into the water, ask its reflection what suited her best. Or, wrapping her body in a sheer robe, she would lie on the soft leaves or the soft grass, and often she gathered flowers. And she happened to be gathering them just then, when she saw the boy, and, seeing him, longed to have him.

"Still, though she was eager to go to him, she did not go before she had set herself in order, arranged her robe, composed her face, and made sure she looked beautiful. Then she began: 'You boy, worthy to be taken for a god — if you are a god, you could be Cupid; but if you are mortal, then blessed are the parents who made you, lucky your brother, and fortunate indeed any sister you have, and the nurse who gave you her breast. But far, far happier than all of them is she — if you have any bride, if you would ever grant a girl the honor of your marriage bed. If there is such a girl, let my pleasure at least be stolen; and if there is none, then let it be me — let us go into one bed together.' The nymph said no more. A blush colored the boy's face — he did not know what love was — but even the blush became him: it was the color of apples hanging on a sunlit tree, or of stained ivory, or of the moon reddening under her own brightness when the bronze cymbals ring out to rescue her, in vain. When the nymph would not stop begging for kisses — at least a sister's kisses — and was already laying her hands on his ivory-white neck, he said, 'Will you stop, or must I run for it and leave this place, and you with it?'

"Salmacis took fright. 'I give the place up to you freely, stranger,' she said, and pretended to turn and go. But she kept looking back, and hid herself in a thicket of shrubs, sinking down on one bent knee. The boy, thinking himself alone and unwatched on the empty grass, wandered here and there, and dipped the tips of his feet, then his feet up to the ankles, in the playful water. And with no delay — charmed by the gentle warmth of it — he slipped the soft clothes from his tender body. Then indeed Salmacis was lost, on fire with desire for his naked beauty. Her eyes blazed, no differently than when the sun, at its brightest, is caught and flung back from the face of a mirror. She could hardly bear to wait, hardly hold off her joy now, longing to hold him, out of her mind and barely in command of herself. He slapped his body with his cupped palms and dove quickly into the pool, and, throwing one arm after the other, shone through the clear water like an ivory statue, or white lilies, set behind clear glass.

"'I've won — he's mine!' the nymph cried, and, flinging every stitch of clothing far off, she plunged into the middle of the water. She seized him as he fought her, tore reluctant kisses from him, forced her hands beneath him, touched his unwilling body, and wound herself around the boy now one way, now another. At last, as he strained against her and tried to slip free, she twisted around him like a serpent that the eagle, the king's own bird, snatches up and carries aloft — the snake, hanging, binds its head and feet and wraps its coiling tail through the spread wings. Or as ivy weaves itself along tall tree-trunks; or as the octopus holds an enemy it has caught underwater, letting down its suckers from every side — so the boy held out and refused the nymph the joy she hoped for. She pressed harder, and clinging to him with her whole body, gripping fast, she said, 'Fight all you like, cruel one — you still won't escape. So ordain it, you gods: let no day part him from me, or me from him.' The gods heard her prayer and granted it. The two bodies, mingled, were joined together, and a single face was drawn over them both. Just as, when someone grafts branches under a single bark, he sees them join as they grow and mature together, so, when their limbs met in that stubborn embrace, they were no longer two, and their form was double: it could be called neither woman nor boy — it seemed neither, and both.

"So when Hermaphroditus saw that the clear water he had gone down into as a man had made him only half a man, and that his limbs had gone soft in it, he lifted his hands — but no longer with a man's voice — and said, 'Grant your son this gift, father and mother, since I bear the name of you both: whoever comes into these waters a man, let him go out half a man, and let him weaken the instant he touches the pool.' Both his parents were moved, and made the words of their two-shaped son come true, and tainted the fountain with a strange, unnatural drug."

That was the end of the stories — and still the daughters of Minyas pressed on with their work, still scorned the god and profaned his festival, when all at once drums no one could see boomed out with their harsh, jarring beat, and the pipe with its curved horn and the ringing bronze sounded, and the air went sweet with myrrh and saffron. And then, past all belief, the weaving began to grow green: the cloth hanging in the loom put out leaves like ivy; part of it turned into grapevine; what had been threads a moment before became tendrils; vine-shoots sprang from the warp; and the purple dye lent its glow to the painted grapes.

And now the day was spent, and there came that hour you could call neither dark nor light, but the very border of doubtful night mixed with the last of day — when suddenly the house seemed to shake, and torches thick with oil seemed to blaze, and the whole place to glow with red fires, and phantom shapes of savage beasts to howl through it. The sisters hid, scattering through the smoke-filled house, ducking into different corners to escape the fires and the light. And while they groped for the dark, a thin membrane stretched over their shrinking limbs and sheathed their arms in delicate wings. The darkness would not let them know how they had lost their old shape. No feathers lifted them, yet they held themselves up on see-through wings, and when they tried to speak they gave off a voice as tiny as their bodies, and wrung out their little complaints in a faint, thin squeak. They keep to houses, not woods; they hate the light, and fly by night — creatures that take their name from the fallen dusk of evening: bats.
