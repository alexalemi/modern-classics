Book VII

(Part 2 of 2)

But Oliaros and Didyme, Tenos and Andros, and Gyaros, and Peparethos rich in its smooth olives — none of these would lend their ships to the king of Knossos. So Minos bore away to the left, to Oenopia, the kingdom of Aeacus. The old name of the island was Oenopia, but Aeacus himself had renamed it Aegina, after his mother. The crowd came pouring out, eager to lay eyes on a man of such fame, and Telamon went to meet him, and Peleus, younger than Telamon, and Phocus, the third of the sons. Aeacus came too, slow with the weight of his years, and asked what had brought the king. Reminded of his own father's grief, the ruler of a hundred cities sighed, and gave his answer:

"I ask you to help the arms I have taken up for my son's sake — to join a war fought out of love. It is comfort for a grave that I am asking."

"You ask in vain," said the grandson of Asopus, "and you ask my city for what it cannot do. No land is bound more closely to Athens than this one. That is the treaty between us."

Minos went away heavy-hearted. "Your treaty will cost you dear," he said — but he thought it wiser to threaten war than to wage it, and to spend his strength here too soon.

The fleet from Knossos could still be seen from the walls of Oenopia when an Athenian ship came on under full sail and glided into the friendly harbor. It carried Cephalus, and with him the request of his homeland. The young sons of Aeacus knew Cephalus at once, though they had not seen him in years, and gave him their hands and led him into their father's house. Still handsome, still keeping some trace of the beauty that had been his, the hero came in holding a branch of his country's olive; being the elder, he had on either side of him two men younger than himself, Clytus and Butes, the sons of Pallas. After the first greetings had passed, Cephalus delivered the plea of the people of Athens: he asked for help, he recalled the old treaties and alliances of their fathers, and he added that Minos was reaching for the mastery of all Greece. When his eloquence had done its work for the cause he carried, Aeacus, his left hand resting on the bright pommel of his scepter, said:

"Do not ask Athens for our help — take it. Count every resource this island holds as your own, and let all the forces of my kingdom march with you. Strength I have to spare — soldiers enough to defend myself and to face the enemy. Thank the gods, this is a fortunate hour, and one that leaves me no excuse to refuse."

"May it stay so," said Cephalus, "and may your city grow with fine citizens. Truly, as I came just now, it gladdened me to see so many young men come to meet me, all handsome and all of an age. And yet I look for many I once saw here, when this city welcomed me long ago."

Aeacus groaned, and answered in a mournful voice.

A happier fortune had followed a grievous beginning — how he wished he could tell them the one without the other. But he would tell it now, plainly, in no set order, so as not to keep them with a long preamble. Those they were asking after, remembering them so well, were bones and ashes now; and how great a part of all he had were they, who had perished!

A dreadful plague had fallen on his people, sent by the anger of unjust Juno, who hated a land named for her rival. As long as it seemed a natural sickness, and the true, poisonous cause of so great a ruin stayed hidden, they fought it with the art of medicine; but the destruction overran all help, and help lay beaten. In the beginning the sky pressed down on the earth with a thick fog and shut a sluggish heat inside its clouds. And while the Moon four times filled her horns to a full circle and four times, thinning, unspun it again, the hot south winds blew with their deadly breath. It is known for a fact that the sickness reached even the springs and the pools; that thousands upon thousands of serpents crawled across the untilled fields and fouled the rivers with their venom. The strength of the sudden disease first showed itself in the dying of dogs and birds and sheep and cattle and the wild beasts. The luckless ploughman stares to see his strong oxen drop at their work and lie down in the middle of the furrow. The wool-bearing flocks bleat weakly; their wool falls off of itself, and their bodies waste away. The horse, once fiery, once famous on the racecourse, forgets his old honors and his victories and stands groaning at the manger, doomed to die a slow, inglorious death. The boar forgets to be angry, the hind no longer trusts her speed, the bears no longer charge the strong herds. A dull weakness holds everything. In the woods, in the fields, along the roads lie foul carcasses, and the air is spoiled with the stench of them.

And here is a strange thing: the dogs would not touch them, nor the greedy birds, nor the gray wolves. The bodies rotted where they fell, and by their reek did their harm, spreading the contagion far and wide.

Then, with heavier ruin, the plague reached the wretched farmers, and took its throne inside the walls of the great city. First the vitals are scorched; a flush of the skin and a labored breath are the sign of the hidden flame. The tongue swells, rough with fever; the parched mouth hangs open, and the sick gulp at the heavy air. They can bear no bed, no covering; they lay their burning chests on the bare ground — and the body is not cooled by the earth, but the earth grows hot from the body. No one can hold the sickness in check. The cruel plague breaks out upon the very healers, and their arts turn against their owners. The nearer a man is, and the more faithfully he tends the sick, the sooner he takes his own share of death. And once the hope of getting well is gone, and they see the only end of the illness in death, they give themselves up to their whims, and there is no care for what might help — for nothing helps. Everywhere, all shame thrown aside, they cling to fountains and rivers and deep wells, and the thirst is not put out until the life is. Many, too heavy to rise, die in the very water — and still another drinks it. So sick are these wretched people of their hated beds that they spring up, or, if their strength will not let them stand, roll their bodies onto the ground; and every man flees his own house, for each one's home seems deadly to him, and since the cause is hidden, the known place gets the blame. You might see them half dead, wandering the roads as long as they could stand; others weeping and sprawled on the earth, rolling their weary eyes in one last movement. They reach their limbs up toward the stars of the overhanging sky, and here and there, wherever death has caught them, breathe out their lives.

What were his feelings then? What should they have been — to hate his own life and long to be gone with his people? Wherever he turned his eyes, there lay the crowd strewn on the ground, like rotten apples falling from a shaken branch, like acorns from a storm-tossed oak. Across from him stood a high temple, raised on its long flight of steps: the temple of Jupiter. Who did not burn incense at those altars, and burn it for nothing? How often, while a husband spoke words of prayer for his wife, or a father for his son, they died at the unmoved altars, the incense still unburned in their hands! How often the bulls, led to the temple while the priest was pronouncing the vows and pouring pure wine between their horns, fell before the stroke could come! When Aeacus himself was sacrificing to Jupiter for himself, his country, and his three sons, the victim let out a dreadful lowing and dropped all at once, with no blow struck, staining the knife thrust into it with its thin blood. The diseased entrails had lost every true mark and every warning of the gods; the sickness struck inward, to the very organs. He had seen the corpses flung down before the sacred doors, before the very altars — as if to make death more hateful to the gods. Some ended their lives with a noose, driving off the fear of death by death, calling on the fate that was already coming. The dead were carried out with no funeral rites, in the old way — for the city gates could not hold the crowd of processions. Either they lay unburied on the ground, or they were laid on the high pyres without the customary honors. By now there was no more reverence: men fought over the pyres and burned their own dead on fires meant for others. There was no one left to weep; unmourned, the souls of sons and fathers, of young men and old, drifted away — and there was no room for the graves, and no trees enough for the fires.

Stunned by so great a whirlwind of misery, he cried out: "O Jupiter! If the stories do not lie, if you truly came into the arms of Aegina, the daughter of Asopus, and if you are not ashamed, great Father, to be my father — then either give me back my people, or lay me too in the tomb." Jupiter gave his sign, with lightning and a favorable roll of thunder. "I take it," he said, "and I pray these are happy tokens of your will. The omen you give me, I hold as a pledge."

By chance, close by, there stood an oak sacred to Jupiter, grown from Dodona's own seed, with wide-spreading but thin-set boughs. There they saw a long column of ants, the harvesters of grain, carrying their heavy loads in their little mouths and keeping to their track along the wrinkled bark. Marveling at their number, he prayed: "Most gracious Father, give me citizens as many as these, and fill my empty walls again." The tall oak trembled and made a sound in its branches, though no wind stirred them. His limbs shuddered with fear, his hair stood on end — yet he kissed the ground and the oak, and would not confess that he hoped; and hope he did, cherishing his wishes in his heart.

Night came on, and sleep took hold of his body, worn out with worry. Before his eyes the same oak seemed to stand, bearing the same boughs and the same creatures on its boughs, quivering with the same motion, and scattering its grain-gathering troop over the fields below; and these, all at once, seemed to grow, to look bigger and bigger, to lift themselves from the earth and stand upright on their own, and to shed their thinness and their many feet and their black color, and to take on human form in their limbs.

Then sleep left him. Awake, he scorned the vision and complained that there was no help from the gods above. But inside the palace there was a great murmur, and he seemed to hear the voices of men — a sound he had grown unused to. While he was supposing that this too was part of his dream, Telamon rushed in, threw open the door, and said: "Father, come out — you will see more than you dared hope or believe!" Out he went, and there, one after another, he saw and knew the very men he had seemed to see in his sleep. They came forward and hailed him as their king. He paid his vows to Jupiter, and shared out the city and the fields left empty of their old tillers among this new-made people; and he called them Myrmidons, and did not rob the name of the mark of its origin. Their bodies Cephalus had seen. The character they had before, they keep still: a thrifty race, patient in toil, holding tight to what they win and laying by what they hold. These, matched to Cephalus in years and courage, would follow him to the war — as soon as the east wind that had carried him so kindly here (for it was the east wind that had brought him) should shift into the south.

With such talk and much else they filled out the long day. The last of the light was given to the feast, the night to sleep. The golden Sun had raised his beams, but the east wind still blew and held the sails from returning. The sons of Pallas came to Cephalus, who was the elder; and Cephalus, with the sons of Pallas beside him, came to the king — but a deep sleep still held the monarch, and it was Phocus, the son of Aeacus, who received them at the threshold, for Telamon and his brother were levying men for the war. Phocus led the Athenians into an inner room, a handsome chamber, and sat down there with them. As they talked he noticed that Cephalus, grandson of Aeolus, held in his hand a javelin of some unknown wood, its point of gold.

After a few words of idle conversation, he said: "I love the woods and the hunting of wild game; and yet I have been wondering some while now from what tree the shaft of that javelin was cut. If it were ash, it would be tawny; if cornel, it would have knots. Where it comes from I cannot tell — but my eyes have never looked on a lovelier throwing-spear." One of the Athenian brothers answered: "You will admire its use even more than its beauty. Whatever it is aimed at, it strikes; chance does not steer it once it is thrown, and it flies back bloodied, with no one to fetch it." At that the son of Nereus's line asked everything — why it was given, and from where, and who had made him a gift of such worth. What he could tell, Cephalus told; but of the price he had paid for it he was ashamed to speak, and stayed silent. Then, touched with grief for the wife he had lost, he began, with the tears starting from his eyes:

"Son of a goddess, this weapon — who could believe it? — makes me weep, and will do for a long time, if the Fates grant me a long life. It was the ruin of me and my dear wife. Would that I had never had this gift!"

Procris — if by chance the name of Orithyia has more likely reached your ears — was the sister of Orithyia, the girl who was carried off; and if you cared to set the face and the character of the two side by side, Procris was the worthier to be seized. Her father Erechtheus joined her to me; love joined her to me. I was called happy — and happy I was. The gods did not think it should last so; or perhaps, even now, I would be happy still.

The second month was passing since the marriage rites, when the saffron-robed Dawn, driving off the dark, saw me one morning from the top of ever-blooming Hymettus as I was spreading nets for the antlered deer, and carried me off against my will. Let me tell the truth, by the goddess's leave: though she is lovely with her rosy face, though she holds the borders of light and the borders of night, though she is fed on the waters of nectar — still I loved Procris. Procris was in my heart, Procris always on my lips. I kept pleading the sacred bond of marriage, our new-made union, the fresh vows of the bed I had left. The goddess grew angry: "Stop your complaining, you ingrate — keep your Procris. But if my mind sees true, you will wish you never had." And in her anger she sent me back.

On the way home, turning the goddess's words over in my mind, I began to be afraid that my wife had not kept the marriage faith. Her beauty and her youth told me to fear the worst; her character forbade me to believe it. Yet I had been away — and besides, the very one I was coming home from was proof that such a thing could happen — and we who are in love fear everything. So I resolved to search out the very thing that would wound me: to make trial of her chaste faith with gifts. Dawn favored this dread of mine, and altered my shape — so I seemed to feel. Unrecognizable now, I came to Athens, the city of Pallas, and went into my own house. The house itself was blameless: it gave every sign of chastity, and grieved for the master who had been carried off. Only by a thousand tricks did I at last win my way to the daughter of Erechtheus. When I saw her, I was stunned, and nearly gave up the trial of her faith I had planned. It was all I could do not to tell her the truth, all I could do not to give her the kisses I owed her. She was sorrowful — and yet no one could be lovelier than she was in her sorrow — pining with longing for the husband torn from her. Only think, Phocus, how great her beauty must have been, that even grief so became her!

Why should I tell how often her chaste ways threw back my attempts? How often she said, "I am kept for one man only; wherever he is, for him I keep my joys"? For any man in his right mind, what greater proof of faith could there be? I was not content. I fought on to wound myself, promising vast sums for a single night, piling reward on reward until at last I forced her to waver. Then I cried out, in my wretched triumph: "Look — a sham lover, and it is your own husband! I am your witness, you faithless woman: you are caught." She said nothing. Only, overwhelmed with silent shame, she fled that house of treachery and its wicked husband; and in her hatred of me she came to loathe the whole race of men, and wandered the mountains, given over to the pursuits of Diana.

Then, left alone, a fiercer fire reached my bones. I begged forgiveness, confessed my fault, owned that I too might have fallen the same way if gifts so great had been offered me. When I had confessed it — her wounded honor first avenged — she came back to me, and we passed sweet years in harmony. And she gave me besides, as if in giving herself she had given too small a thing, a dog for a present: her own Diana, when she handed it to her, had said, "It will outrun them all." She gave me a javelin too — this one you see in my hand.

You ask what became of the first gift, the dog? Then hear a marvel — the strangeness of it will astonish you. The son of Laius had solved the riddle that no wit before him had understood, and the dark prophetess had flung herself down, forgetting her own riddles. But kindly Themis, of course, does not leave such things unavenged: at once another scourge was loosed on Aonian Thebes, and many country folk fed a savage beast with the ruin of their cattle and of themselves. We of the neighborhood came and ringed the broad fields with our nets. With a light leap the creature cleared them and bounded over the highest of the toils we had set. The dogs were slipped from their leashes, but it fled them as they gave chase and made sport of the pack, no slower than a bird. With one loud voice they all called for my Laelaps — Tempest, that was the name of my wife's gift. He had long been fighting to be free of the leash, straining it against his neck. He was scarcely well let go before we could no longer tell where he was: the warm dust held the prints of his feet, but he himself was snatched from our eyes. No spear flies faster, nor pellets whirled from a twisted sling, nor the light arrow from a Cretan bow. A hilltop rose in the middle, looking down on the plains below. I climbed it and watched that strange chase, in which the beast one moment seemed caught, the next to slip out from under the very bite. Crafty, it would not run straight and clear away, but tricked the mouth that pursued it and wheeled in circles, so its enemy could get no full charge at it. The dog hung close, matched it stride for stride, and — for all the world like a dog with its prey in its teeth — closed on nothing, and snapped again and again at the empty air. I was turning to my javelin for help; while my right hand was balancing it and I was working my fingers into the throwing-loop, I glanced away — and when I looked back to the same spot, there in the middle of the plain, incredibly, I saw two marble shapes: you would swear the one was fleeing, the other barking in pursuit. Some god, surely — if any god was watching them — willed that neither should be beaten in that contest of speed."

He said this much, and fell silent. "But what is the fault in the javelin itself?" said Phocus. And Cephalus told him the javelin's offense:

"Let my joys be the first part of my sorrow, Phocus; those I will tell first. It gladdens me even to remember that blessed time, son of Aeacus, when through the first years of our marriage I was happy in my wife, and she was happy in her husband. A shared devotion, a wedded love, held us both. She would not have set the bed of Jupiter above my love, nor was there any woman who could have taken me — not if Venus herself had come. Equal flames burned in the breasts of us both.

When the Sun was just striking the mountaintops with his early rays, I used to go, with a young man's eagerness, hunting in the woods. I took no servants with me, no horses, no keen-nosed hounds, no knotted nets: I was safe with my javelin. And when my hand had had its fill of the slaughter of wild game, I would seek out the cool and the shade, and the breeze that came breathing from the cold valleys. That gentle breeze I looked for in the heat of the day; for that breeze I waited — it was my rest after my labor. 'Come, breeze,' I would sing, for I remember it well, 'come, most welcome, and enter my breast; be willing, as you always are, to ease the heat that burns me.' Perhaps I added — my fate was drawing me on — still more endearments; perhaps I used to say, 'You are my great delight; you refresh me and cherish me; you make me love the woods and the lonely places, and my mouth is forever reaching for your breath.'

Someone — I do not know who — gave a credulous ear to these double-meaning words, and took the name of the breeze I called on so often to be the name of a nymph: he believed I was in love with some nymph named Aura. And, rash informer of a crime that never was, he went straight to Procris and whispered to her what he had heard. Love is a credulous thing. Struck down by sudden grief, she fainted, so I am told; and recovering after a long while, she called herself wretched, born to a cruel fate, and grieved over my faithfulness. Stirred up by a groundless charge, she dreads a thing that is nothing, fears a name without a body, and in her misery mourns as though over a real rival. Yet often she doubts, and in her wretchedness hopes she is deceived, and refuses to believe the tale, and will not condemn her husband's guilt unless she sees it with her own eyes.

The next morning's light had driven off the night. I went out and made for the woods; and, victorious among the fields, I said, 'Come, breeze, and ease my pain.' And suddenly, in the middle of my words, I thought I heard some sort of groan; but still I said, 'Come, most welcome one.' When the falling leaves made a soft rustle again, I took it for a wild beast and threw my flying weapon. It was Procris. Clutching the wound in the middle of her breast, she cried, 'Ah, wretched me!' When I knew the voice of my faithful wife, headlong and out of my mind I ran toward it. I found her dying, her scattered garments fouled with blood, drawing her own gift — wretched that I am! — out of the wound. In my arms, gentler than my own, I lifted the body dearer to me than my own; I tore the clothing from my breast and bound her cruel wound, and tried to stanch the blood, and begged her not to leave me guilty of her death. She, her strength gone, already dying, forced herself to speak these few words:

'By the bond of our marriage, by the gods above and my own gods, by whatever good I have done you, and by the love that even now, as I die, is the cause of my death — I beg you, do not let Aura take my place in our marriage bed.' She spoke; and only then did I understand — and tell her — the mistake in the name. But what use was the telling? She sinks; her little strength flies out with her blood. And as long as she can look at anything, she looks at me, and breathes out her unhappy life against my face — yet she seems to die at peace, with a more contented look."

In tears, the hero told them this, and they wept to hear it. And now Aeacus came in, with his two sons and his newly levied soldiers; and Cephalus received them, well armed and brave.
