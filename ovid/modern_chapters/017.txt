Book IX

(Part 2 of 2)

Every god has some favorite, and the unruly quarrel grew with their partiality, until Jupiter opened his lips and said: "O, if you have any respect for me, where are you rushing? Does any one of you think himself so powerful as to overcome even the Fates? By the Fates has Iolaüs come back to the years he has spent; by the Fates must the sons of Callirhoë grow young — not by ambition or by force of arms. And you too — that you may bear this with a better mind — even me the Fates govern. Could I change them, no late years would bend my Aeacus; Rhadamanthus would keep the everlasting flower of youth, and my Minos with him, who now is despised for the grievous weight of old age and no longer reigns with the dignity he once had."

The words of Jupiter moved the gods; and no one kept complaining when they saw Rhadamanthus and Aeacus and Minos worn with years — Minos, who in his prime had terrified great nations with his very name, but was now feeble, and feared Miletus, the son of Deione, proud in the strength of youth and in Apollo his father. And though Minos believed Miletus was reaching for his throne, still he did not dare drive him from his native home. Of your own accord, Miletus, you fled, and in a swift ship measured the Aegean waters, and in the land of Asia founded a city that bears its founder's name. Here, as she followed the windings of her father's bank, Cyanee, the daughter of Maeander that so often returns to the same place, a woman of surpassing beauty, came to know you, and bore twin children, Byblis and her brother Caunus.

Byblis is a warning that girls should love only what the law allows: Byblis, seized with passion for her brother, the descendant of Apollo, loved him not as a sister loves a brother, and not as she ought. At first she understands nothing of the flame, and thinks it no fault to give him so many kisses, to throw her arms round her brother's neck; and long she is fooled by this false likeness of natural love. Little by little the affection slips awry; she comes dressed up to see her brother, is too anxious to look beautiful, and, if any woman there is lovelier, envies her. But she is not yet clear to herself, and under that fire she forms no wish — yet inwardly she burns. Already she calls him her darling; already she hates the words of kinship; already she would rather he call her Byblis than sister.

Still, awake, she does not dare let shameful hopes into her mind; but loosened in soft sleep she often sees what she loves — she even seems to embrace her brother, and she blushes, though she lies buried in sleep. Sleep goes; long she is silent, calling back the vision of her rest, and speaks with a wavering mind:

"Wretched me! What does this vision of the silent night mean? How I would not have it come true! Why have I dreamed this dream? He is beautiful, even to unfriendly eyes; he pleases me, and, were he not my brother, I could love him, and he would be worthy of me. But it is my ruin that I am his sister. Only let me try, awake, to do no such thing — but let sleep return often with the same shape! There is no witness in sleep, and the pleasure it copies is not withheld. O Venus and winged Cupid with your tender mother, what joys I have known! What plain, palpable delight has touched me! How I lay dissolved to the marrow! How sweet to remember it — though that pleasure was brief, and the night sped on, jealous of what I had begun. Oh, if I could be joined to you under a changed name, how good a daughter-in-law I could be, Caunus, to your father; how good a son-in-law you could be, Caunus, to mine! Would the gods grant that all were shared between us — all but our ancestors! I could wish you were the nobler-born. So then, most beautiful one, you will make some woman I do not know a mother; but to me, who have wretchedly drawn the same parents as you, you will be nothing but a brother. The one tie we have is the one that bars all the rest.

"What then do my visions mean? What weight have dreams — do dreams have any weight? The gods fare better: the gods have married their own sisters. So Saturn wed Ops, his kin by blood; Ocean wed Tethys, and the ruler of Olympus, Juno. The gods above have their own laws! Why do I try to measure the ways of heaven by human rule, when the two are so different? Either this forbidden fire will be driven from my heart — or, if I cannot do that, I pray I may die first, and be laid out dead on my bed, and my brother kiss me as I lie. And yet the thing needs the will of two. Suppose it pleases me: to him it will seem a crime. And yet the sons of Aeolus did not shrink from their sisters' beds — but how do I know of them? Why have I furnished myself with such precedents? Where am I carried? Away, far from here, you unclean flames! Let my brother be loved by me only as a sister may. And yet — if he had first been seized with love of me, perhaps I could yield to his madness. So then, I, who would not have refused him had he asked, shall I ask myself? Can you speak it? Can you confess it? Love will force me — I can. Or if shame holds my lips, a secret letter shall confess the hidden fire."

This thought pleases her; this settles her wavering mind. She raises herself on her side, leaning on her left elbow, and says, "Let him see it — let me confess my mad love! Wretched me, where am I sliding? What flame is my mind kindling?" And, with trembling hand, she sets down her well-weighed words. Her right hand holds the iron pen, the other the blank wax. She begins, and hesitates; she writes and blames what she has written; she marks and deletes, alters, condemns, approves; and by turns she throws the tablet down and takes it up again. She does not know what she wants; whatever she seems about to do displeases her. In her face is boldness mixed with shame. She had written "sister"; then she chose to erase "sister" and cut such words as these into the smoothed wax:

"The health that she herself will never have unless you give it, your lover sends you. I am ashamed — ah, ashamed to speak my name; and if you ask what I desire, I could wish my case might be pleaded without a name, and that I not be known as Byblis until my hopes were sure. You might have had, as a sign of my wounded heart, my pallor, my wasting, my downcast looks, my eyes often wet, sighs with no plain cause, frequent embraces, and kisses that — if you happened to notice — could not be felt to be a sister's. Yet, though I bore a heavy wound in my soul, though a fiery madness was within, I did everything — the gods are my witnesses — to be cured at last; long, in my misery, I fought to escape the cruel weapon of Cupid, and endured more than you would think a girl could bear. Beaten, I am forced to confess my passion, and with timid prayers to beg your help. You alone can save, you alone destroy the one who loves you: choose which you will do. It is no enemy who begs this, but one who, though already most closely bound to you, longs to be bound closer still, tied by a nearer knot. Let old men know the ordinances, and ask what is lawful and wicked and right, and keep the balance of the laws. A love that dares all suits our years. What is allowed, we do not yet know, and we believe all things allowed, and follow the example of the great gods. No stern father, no regard for reputation, no fear will stop us — only let the cause of fear be gone. Under a brother's name we will hide our sweet stolen joys. I have the freedom to talk with you in private, and we give embraces and exchange kisses in the open. How little is it that is lacking! Pity one who confesses her love — who would not confess it, did not utmost passion compel — and do not earn a place as the cause written on my tomb."

The full wax fell short of her hand as it vainly traced such words, and the last line clung to the margin. At once she seals her own crime with the stamp of a signet, which she wets with tears, for the moisture had fled her tongue. In shame she called one of her servants and, coaxing the frightened man in timid tones, said, "Carry these, most faithful one, to my—" and, after a long pause, added, "brother." As she gave them, the tablets slipped from her hands and fell. She was shaken by the omen, but sent them still. The servant, finding a fit moment, went and delivered the hidden words. The young son of Maeander's line, seized with sudden anger, threw down the tablets he had taken when he had read part; and, barely holding his hands from the trembling servant's face, he said, "Flee while you can, you cursed pander to forbidden lust! — who, if your ruin would not drag my honor with it, would have paid me with your death." The man fled in terror and reported to his mistress the fierce words of Caunus.

You turn pale, Byblis, hearing of the refusal, and your body, gripped by an icy chill, is struck with dread; yet when your senses return, so does your frenzy, and your tongue barely gives such words as these to the beaten air: "And I deserve it! For why, rashly, did I make this wound known? Why did I so quickly trust to a hasty letter words that should have been hidden? I should first have felt out the leanings of his mind with ambiguous speech. To be sure the wind would follow as I went, I should have watched, with only part of my sail, what breeze it was, and run over a safe sea; and now I have filled my canvas with untried winds. So I am driven upon the rocks, and, capsized, buried under the whole ocean, and my sails have no way back. And was I not forbidden by sure omens to indulge my love, when the wax fell as I bade him carry it, and made my hopes drop to the ground? Should not the day have been changed, or my whole intent — rather, the day? The god himself was warning me, giving sure signs, had I not been out of my mind. And I should have spoken myself, not trusted to wax, and shown my madness in person. He would have seen my tears, seen the face of the woman who loved him; I could have said more than the letter held. I could have thrown my arms round his unwilling neck; and, if refused, could have seemed at the point of death, and clasped his feet, and, flung down, begged for my life. I would have done all this; and if each thing alone could not bend his hard heart, all together might have. Perhaps, too, there is some fault in the servant I sent: he did not approach him well, or choose, I think, a fit time, or seek an idle hour and a free mind. This has undone me. For he was not born of a tigress, nor does he carry hard flint or solid iron or adamant in his breast, nor did he suck a lioness's milk. He will be won! He must be tried again, and I will take no weariness of what I have begun, so long as this breath stays in me. For the best thing — could I recall what is done — was not to have begun; the next best is to press on with what is begun. Even if I gave up my desire, he cannot ever forget that I dared it; and, because I stopped, I will seem to have wanted it lightly, or even to have been testing him, to have solicited him meaning to betray — or at least be thought conquered not by this god who burns me so, but by mere lust. In any case I can no longer be free of a shameful deed: I have written, I have solicited; my will is defiled. Though I add nothing more, I cannot be called innocent. What remains adds much to my desire, little to my guilt."

So she says; and — so great is the discord of her wavering mind — though she is loath to try him, she longs to try him, and, past all bounds, poor woman, lays herself open to be refused again and again. At last, when there is no end to it, he flees his country and the crime, and founds a new city in a foreign land.

Then indeed, they say, the daughter of Miletus wholly lost her mind in grief; then she tore the robe from her breast and beat her arms in frenzy. Now she raves openly, confessing her hope of an unlawful union, and, leaving her country and her hated home, follows the tracks of her fleeing brother. As the Thracian Bacchants, roused by your thyrsus, son of Semele, keep your festivals each third year, so the women of Bubasus saw Byblis howling across the wide fields. Leaving them, she wandered through the land of the Carians, the warlike Leleges, and Lycia; and now she had left behind Cragos and Limyre and the waters of the Xanthus, and the ridge where the Chimaera had fire in its middle, the breast and face of a lioness, the tail of a serpent. The woods gave out at last, when you, Byblis, worn with the chase, fall down, and, your hair on the hard ground, lie silent and press the fallen leaves with your face. Often the Leleges' nymphs try to lift her in their tender arms; often they urge her to master her passion, and bring comfort to a mind that cannot hear. Silent Byblis lies, tearing the green herbs with her nails and watering the grass with her stream of tears. Beneath these tears, they say, the Naiads set a channel that could never run dry — for what greater gift had they to give? At once, as drops ooze from the cut bark of the pitch-pine, or sticky bitumen wells from the heavy earth, or as water frozen with cold melts in the sun at the coming of the gentle west wind, so Byblis, the descendant of Apollo, consumed by her own tears, turns into a fountain, which even now in those valleys bears its mistress's name, and flows beneath a dark oak.

The fame of this new marvel might have filled the hundred cities of Crete, had Crete not lately borne a nearer wonder of her own, in the change of Iphis.

For once the land of Phaestus, next to the kingdom of Knossos, produced a man of no famous name, Ligdus, of the free-born common folk; his means no greater than his rank, but his life and honor untainted. When his wife's delivery was near, he warned her ears with these words: "Two things I pray for: that you be eased with the least pain, and that you bear a boy. The other lot is a heavier trouble, and fortune has denied us the means. What I abhor — if by chance a girl should be born at your delivery — I command it against my will; forgive me, natural love — let it be put to death." He spoke, and both bathed their faces with streaming tears — he who commanded, and she to whom the command was given. But still Telethusa pressed her husband with fruitless prayers not to shut his hope in so narrow a bound. Ligdus's mind was fixed.

And now she could scarcely carry her womb, heavy with its ripe burden, when in the middle of the night, in a vision of sleep, the daughter of Inachus, attended by her train of holy rites, stood, or seemed to stand, before her bed. On her brow were the horns of the moon, and ears of corn bright with shining gold, and the royal splendor of the diadem; with her were barking Anubis, and holy Bubastis, and the dappled Apis, and the god who hushes his voice and bids silence with a finger; there were the sistrums, and Osiris never sought enough, and the foreign serpent full of sleep-bringing venom. Then, as though Telethusa were roused from sleep and seeing all clearly, the goddess spoke: "O Telethusa, one of my worshipers, lay down your grievous cares, and cheat your husband's command. Do not hesitate, when Lucina has eased you with the birth, to raise whatever it shall be. I am a helping goddess, and when called on I bring aid; you shall not complain that you worshiped an ungrateful power." She counseled her, and withdrew from the chamber. The Cretan woman rose from her bed rejoicing, and, lifting her pure hands to the stars as a suppliant, prayed that her vision might prove true.

When her pains grew and the burden forced itself into the light, and a girl was born to the father who did not know, the mother ordered the child raised, pretending it a boy; and the thing was believed — no one but the nurse knew the truth. The father paid his vows and gave the child its grandfather's name: the grandfather had been Iphis. The mother was glad of the name, for it belonged to both sexes, and by it she would deceive no one. From then the pious lie lay hidden. The child was dressed as a boy; and its face was such that, whether you gave it to a girl or a boy, either would be lovely.

Meanwhile the thirteenth year had come, when your father, Iphis, betrothed to you golden-haired Ianthe, most praised of the girls of Phaestus for her beauty's dower, the daughter of Dictaean Telestes. Their age was equal, their beauty equal; and they had their first lessons, the rudiments of their years, from the same teachers. From this, love touched the untried hearts of both and gave each an equal wound — but how unequal their hopes! Ianthe awaits the wedding and the promised time of the torches, and believes that the one she thinks a man will be her husband. Iphis loves one she despairs of ever enjoying, and this very thing feeds her flame; a maid, she burns for a maid. Scarcely holding back her tears, she says: "What end awaits me, held by a passion no one has known, monstrous and strange? If the gods wished to spare me, they should have spared me; if not, and they wished to destroy me, they should at least have given me a natural affliction, one common to my kind. Cow does not burn for cow, nor mare for mares; the ram burns for the ewe, the doe follows her stag. So too the birds mate; and among all the animals no female is seized with desire for a female. Would that I did not exist! Yet, so that Crete might breed every kind of monster, the daughter of the Sun loved a bull — a female, yes, a male. My love, if I confess the truth, is madder than hers. Still she followed the hope of union; still, by a trick, in the shape of a cow, she gave herself to the bull, and there was a lover to be deceived. Though the world's whole ingenuity gathered here, though Daedalus flew back on his waxen wings, what could he do? Could he, by his clever arts, make me a boy from a girl? Could he change you, Ianthe?

"Why not steady your mind, Iphis, and gather yourself, and shake off this foolish, senseless fire? See what you were born — unless you fool yourself as well — and seek what is allowed, and love what, as a woman, you should. It is hope that makes, hope that feeds love; this the very case takes from you. No guard keeps you from her dear embrace, no watchful husband's care, no father's harshness; she herself does not refuse you when you ask — and yet she cannot be yours; and, though all were done, you could not be happy, though gods and men should labor for you. Even now no part of my prayers is empty; the kind gods have given what they could, and what I wish, my father wishes, and she herself, and my father-in-law to be. But nature, more powerful than all these, wills it not — she alone harms me. Look, the longed-for time comes; the wedding-day is here, and Ianthe will soon be mine — and never come to me. In the midst of water I shall thirst. Why, Juno, guardian of weddings, and why, Hymenaeus, do you come to these rites, where there is no man to marry the bride, where we are two women wed?" With that she closed her lips. And no less the other girl burns, and prays you, Hymenaeus, to come quickly.

Telethusa, dreading the very thing Ianthe desires, now puts off the day, now draws out delay with a feigned illness, often pleads omens and visions for excuse. But now she had used up all her store of fictions; the long-delayed wedding-day was upon them, and only one day remained. Then she takes the fillets from her own head and her daughter's, and, embracing the altar with loosened hair, cries: "O Isis, who dwell in Paraetonium and the Mareotic fields and Pharos, and the Nile parted into its seven horns — bring help, I beg, and heal our fear! You, goddess, you and these your emblems I once saw, and knew them all — your attendants, your torches, the sound of the sistrums — and I marked your commands in a mindful heart. That this girl sees the light, that I am not punished — this is your counsel and your gift. Pity us both, and aid us with your help." Tears followed her words. The goddess seemed to move — and did move — her altar; the temple doors shook; her moon-like horns shone, and the ringing sistrum sounded. Not free of care, but glad of the good omen, the mother left the temple; and Iphis followed her as she went, with a longer stride than before. The fairness does not stay in her face; her strength grows, her features sharpen, her loose hair is shorter, and there is more vigor in her than she had as a girl. For you who were a girl just now are a boy! Bring gifts to the temple, and rejoice with no timid faith! They bring their gifts to the temple, and add an inscription too, a short line that ran:

Iphis, a boy, pays the gifts that as a girl he vowed.

The next dawn had opened the wide world with its rays, when Venus and Juno and Hymenaeus came to the wedding fires, and the boy Iphis won his own Ianthe.
