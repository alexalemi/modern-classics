Book XI

(Part 2 of 2)

Meanwhile Ceyx, his heart troubled by his brother's fate and by the wonders that had followed it, prepared to go to the god of Claros, to consult the sacred oracle, that comfort of mortals — for the profane Phorbas and his Phlegyans had made the oracle at Delphi impossible to reach. But first, most faithful Alcyone, he told you his plan; and at once a chill went to the marrow of your bones, a pallor much like boxwood came over your face, and your cheeks were wet with gushing tears. Three times she tried to speak, three times she drenched her face with tears; and, sobs breaking her loving complaints, she said:

"What fault of mine, dearest, has changed your mind? Where is the care for me that used to come first? Can you now be away without worry, your Alcyone left behind? Now a long journey pleases you? Now am I dearer to you at a distance? But I suppose your road is by land, and I shall only grieve, not fear as well, and my worry will be free of dread. It is the sea that frightens me, the grim face of the ocean. Lately I saw broken planks on the shore, and often I have read names on tombs with no body buried there. And do not let a false confidence touch your mind because the grandson of Hippotas is your father-in-law, who shuts the strong winds in prison and calms the seas when he wishes. Once the winds are let loose and have taken the deep, nothing is forbidden them; every land and every sea is disregarded; they harass even the clouds of heaven and strike out red fires by their wild collisions. The more I know them — and I do know them, for as a child I often saw them in my father's house — the more I think they must be feared. But if your resolve can be bent by no prayers of mine, dear husband, and you are all too set on going, then take me too! Surely we will be tossed together, and I shall fear nothing but what I suffer; together we will bear whatever comes, together be carried over the wide seas."

The starry husband is moved by such words and tears of the daughter of Aeolus — for the fire of love is no less in him. But he will neither give up the voyage he has proposed nor let Alcyone share the danger, and he answers many things to soothe her fearful heart. Yet she does not for that approve his reasons. He added this one comfort, which alone bent his loving wife: "Every delay will be long to me; but I swear to you by my father's fire, if only the Fates let me back, that I will return before the moon fills her circle twice." When by these promises a hope of his return had been raised, at once he ordered a ship drawn from the docks, launched, and fitted with its gear. Seeing it, Alcyone shuddered again, as if foreseeing the future, and shed flowing tears, and gave him embraces, and at last, in her misery, said "Farewell" with a sad voice, and sank down with her whole body.

But the young men, while Ceyx still sought excuses for delay, draw the oars in double rows to their hardy chests and cleave the sea with even strokes. She lifted her wet eyes and saw her husband standing on the curved stern, waving his hand to give her the first signals, and she returned them. When the land had drawn far off, and her eyes could no longer make out his face, still, while she could, she followed the fleeing ship with her gaze; and when that too, borne away, could not be seen for distance, still she watched the sails floating at the masthead; and when she saw the sails no more, she sought her empty bed and laid herself on the couch. The bed and the place renew Alcyone's tears, and remind her what part of herself is gone.

They had left the harbor, and the breeze stirred the ropes; the sailor turns the hanging oars to the sides, sets the yards at the masthead, and lets down the whole canvas from the mast, catching the coming winds. Less than half, or at most half, the sea had been cut by the ship, and each land was far off, when, toward night, the sea began to whiten with swelling waves and the headlong east wind to blow more strongly. "Lower the high yards at once," the master cries, "and reef the whole sail to the spars." He orders, but the head-on storm blocks the orders, and the roar of the sea lets no voice be heard. Yet of their own accord some hurry to draw in the oars, some to brace the sides, some to deny the sails to the winds; this one bails the flood and pours the sea back into the sea, that one hauls the yards down. While these things are done without order, the harsh storm grows, and on every side the fierce winds wage war and churn the raging waters. The master himself is afraid, and himself confesses he does not know how they stand, nor what to order or forbid: so great is the mass of the disaster, so much stronger than any skill. The men roar with shouts, the ropes with creaking, wave crashes on heavy wave, the sky with thunder. The sea rises in billows and seems to reach the heavens and to sprinkle the low-hung clouds with spray; now, when it stirs the yellow sand from the bottom, it is the color of it; now it is blacker than the Stygian wave; sometimes it lies flat, white with hissing foam. The Trachinian ship, too, is driven by these turns: now aloft as if from a mountaintop it seems to look down into the valleys and the depths of Acheron; now, when the curving sea has hemmed it low, it seems to look up at the sky above from an infernal gulf. Struck on its side by the wave, it often gives a huge crash, and sounds no lighter beaten than an iron ram or a catapult shaking a battered tower. And as fierce lions, gathering strength for the charge, rush chest-on against the weapons and the leveled spears, so the water, when it had risen with the risen winds, came on against the ship's rigging and towered far above it. And now the joints give way; stripped of their wax coating, the seams gape and offer a way to the deadly waters.

Look — heavy showers fall from the loosened clouds; you would think the whole sky was coming down into the sea, and the swollen sea climbing up into the tracts of heaven. The sails are soaked with rain, and the sea's waters mix with the sky's; the firmament is without its fires, and blind night is crushed by its own darkness and the storm's. Yet the lightnings scatter this and give a flashing light: the rains catch fire with the flames of the thunderbolt. Now the flood leaps inside the hollow frame of the ship; and as a soldier, above all the rest of the number, when he has often sprung at the walls of a defended city, at last wins his hope and, fired with love of glory, though one among a thousand, mounts the wall alone — so, when the waves had struck the high sides nine times, the onset of a tenth wave, rising vaster, rushes down and does not leave off battering the weary ship until it drops, as it were, within the walls of a captured vessel. So part of the sea was still trying to break into the ship, part was inside it; and all tremble no less than a city trembles when some are undermining the wall outside and others hold it within. Skill fails, courage sinks, and as many deaths seem to rush and break in as there are waves that come. One cannot hold his tears; one is stunned; one calls those happy whom burial awaits; one worships the gods with prayers, and, lifting his hands in vain to the sky he cannot see, begs for help. To one his brother and parent come to mind; to another his home and his children and whatever he has left behind.

The thought of Alcyone moves Ceyx; on Ceyx's lips is nothing but Alcyone, and, though he longs for her alone, he is glad she is away. Gladly, too, he would look back to his native shore and turn his last gaze toward his home — but he does not know where it is: the sea boils in so great a whirl, and, under the shadow of the pitch-black clouds, all the sky is hidden, and the face of night is doubled. The mast is broken by the storm's assault; the helm too is broken; and the wave, proud over its spoils and standing like a conqueror, looks down on the waves below. Then, no less heavily than if a god should hurl Athos or Pindus, torn from their roots, into the open sea, it plunges down, and with weight and blow together sinks the ship to the bottom. With her a great part of the crew, pressed down in the heavy gulf, never rising again to the air, met their fate; others cling to fragments and broken pieces of the ship. Ceyx himself grips a plank of the wreck with the hand that used to hold the scepter, and in vain, alas, calls on his father and his father-in-law. But most on the swimmer's lips is his wife Alcyone: her he remembers and names; he prays the waves to carry his body before her eyes, that dead he may be buried by the hands of friends. As he swims, whenever the billows let him open his mouth, he names the absent Alcyone and murmurs it into the very waves. And now a black arch of water broke over the middle of the waves and buried his sinking head under the bursting wave. The Morning Star was dark that night, past knowing; and, since he could not leave the sky, he veiled his face in thick clouds.

Meanwhile the daughter of Aeolus, knowing nothing of such great ills, counts the nights, and now hurries to ready the clothes he will put on, now those she herself will wear when he comes, and promises herself a return that will never be. She was piously offering incense to all the gods, but before all she tended the temple of Juno and came to the altars for a husband who no longer was, praying that he be safe and return and set no woman above her — and of all her prayers, this one alone could be granted her. But the goddess could no longer bear to be entreated for a dead man; and, to keep the death-polluted hands from her altars, she said: "Iris, most faithful messenger of my voice, go quickly to the sleep-bringing hall of Sleep, and bid him send Alcyone a dream in the shape of dead Ceyx, telling her true disaster." She spoke. Iris puts on her thousand-colored robe and, marking the sky with her arched curve, seeks the house of the king hidden beneath its cloud.

Near the Cimmerians there is a cave in a long recess, a hollow mountain, the home and inmost chamber of lazy Sleep, where the sun can never come, rising or in mid-course or setting; fogs mixed with darkness breathe up from the ground, and a twilight of doubtful light. No watchful cock there calls forth the dawn with the crowing of his crested head; no anxious dogs break the silence, nor geese keener than dogs; no wild beast, no cattle, no boughs stirred by a breeze, no outbursts of the human tongue make a sound. Mute Quiet dwells there; yet from the bottom of the rock runs a stream, the water of Lethe, whose wave, gliding with a murmur over the rattling pebbles, invites sleep. Before the doors of the cave poppies bloom in plenty, and countless herbs, from whose milk dewy Night gathers sleep and scatters it over the darkened earth. There is no door in the whole house, to grind on a turning hinge; no porter at the threshold. But in the middle is a couch, raised high on black ebony, feathered, dark-colored, hidden by a dark coverlet, where the god himself lies, his limbs loosed in languor. Around him, everywhere, lie empty Dreams mimicking many shapes, as many as the harvest bears ears of grain, the wood leaves, the shore cast-up sands.

As soon as the maiden had entered there and pushed aside with her hands the dreams that blocked her way, the sacred house shone with the splendor of her robe; and the god, heavy with slow drowsiness, barely lifting his eyes and again and again sinking back, his nodding chin striking the top of his breast, at last shook himself free of himself, and, raised on his elbow, asked why she had come — for he knew her. And she said: "Sleep, rest of all things, gentlest of the gods, peace of the mind, from whom care flees, who soothe the bodies worn with hard service and refit them for toil — bid a Dream that matches real shapes in its likeness go to Alcyone in Herculean Trachis, in the form of the king, and take on the look of a shipwrecked man. Juno commands it." Her task done, Iris left; she could no longer bear the force of the drowsiness, and, feeling sleep creep into her limbs, fled and went back along the arch by which she had just come.

But the father, out of the crowd of his thousand sons, wakes Morpheus, a craftsman and mimic of shapes: no one more cleverly renders the walk, the face, and the sound of speech; he adds the dress too, and the words each most often uses. But he imitates men only; another becomes a wild beast, a bird, a long-bodied serpent — him the gods call Icelos, the crowd of mortals Phobetor. There is a third of a different art, Phantasos: he changes himself, deceiving, into earth and stone and water and a tree and all things that lack breath. These show their faces by night to kings and generals; others roam among the peoples and the commons. These the old god passes by, and chooses from all his brothers Morpheus alone to carry out the commands of the daughter of Thaumas; and again, loosed in soft drowsiness, he dropped his head and sank back into the high couch.

Morpheus flies through the dark on noiseless wings and, in a short space of time, reaches the Thessalian city; and, laying the wings from his body, he takes the shape of Ceyx, and in that shape, wan, like a bloodless man, without any clothing, he stood before the bed of the wretched wife. The hero's beard seems wet, and heavy water to run from his dripping hair. Then, leaning on the bed, tears pouring down his face, he said: "Do you know your Ceyx, most wretched wife — or is my face so changed by death? Look: you will know me, and instead of your husband you will find your husband's ghost. Your prayers, Alcyone, brought me no help; I am dead. Do not, deceived, promise yourself my return. The cloudy south wind caught my ship in the Aegean sea and, tossing it with its mighty blast, broke it apart; and the waves filled my mouth as it cried your name in vain. It is no doubtful messenger who tells you this, no vague rumor you hear it by: I myself, shipwrecked, in person, tell you my fate. Rise, come, weep, put on mourning, and do not send me unlamented down to empty Tartarus." To these words Morpheus added a voice she would believe her husband's — he even seemed to shed real tears — and his hands had the gestures of Ceyx.

Alcyone groaned, weeping, and moved her arms in her sleep, and, reaching for his body, grasped the air, and cried out, "Stay! Where do you rush? We will go together." Shaken by her own voice and the sight of her husband, she threw off sleep, and first looked around to see if he who had just been seen was there; for the servants, roused by her voice, had brought in a light. When she found him nowhere, she struck her face with her hands, tore the clothes from her breast and beat the breast itself, and did not care to loose her hair — she tore it; and to her nurse, asking the cause of her grief, she said: "There is no Alcyone, none! She is dead, with her Ceyx. Away with words of comfort! He has died by shipwreck. I saw him, I knew him, and, as he left, I stretched out my hands, longing to hold him back. It was a ghost — but even so a clear ghost, the true ghost of my husband. He did not, if you ask, have his wonted look, nor shine with the face he had before; pale and naked and with dripping hair I saw him, poor man. Here, on this very spot, the wretched one stood" — and she looks for any footprints left. "This, this is what I feared in my foreboding mind, and I begged you not to flee me and follow the winds. But surely I could wish, since you went to die, that you had taken me too: it would have been much better for me to go with you; for then I would have spent no part of my life apart from you, nor would death have divided us. Now, absent, I have died; absent, I too am tossed on the waves, and the sea holds me without me. My heart would be crueler than the sea itself if I strove to draw out my life longer, and fought to outlive so great a grief. But I will not fight, nor leave you, poor one; at least I will come now as your companion; and if not an urn, then an inscription in the tomb shall join us; if I do not touch your bones with my bones, still I will touch your name with my name." Grief forbids more, and wailing breaks in on every word, and groans are dragged from her stricken heart.

It was morning: she goes out from the house to the shore and, in her grief, seeks the place from which she had watched him go, and says, "Here he loosed the cables; on this shore he kissed me as he left" — and while she recalls the acts marked by places, and looks out on the sea, she sees, far off on the flowing wave, something like a body; and at first it was doubtful what it was. When the water had brought it a little nearer, and, though still far, it was plainly a corpse, not knowing who it might be — because it was shipwrecked, she was moved by the omen, and, as if giving a tear to a stranger, said, "Alas, poor man, whoever you are, and if you have a wife!" Driven by the waves, the body comes nearer; and the more she looks at it, the less and less she is mistress of her mind; and now, brought close to the land, now that she can make it out, she sees: it is her husband. "It is he!" she cries, and at once tears her face, her hair, her robe, and, stretching her trembling hands to Ceyx, says, "Is it so, dearest husband, is it so, poor man, that you come back to me?"

There is a breakwater, made by human hands, next to the waves, which breaks the sea's first fury and blunts the onset of the waters. Onto this she leaped — and it was a marvel she could: she was flying, and, beating the light air with newly-formed wings, a wretched bird, she skimmed the surface of the water; and, as she flew, her mouth, chattering with its slender bill, gave a sound like one in sorrow, full of complaint. But when she touched the mute, bloodless body, she folded the beloved limbs in her new wings and gave him cold kisses in vain with her hard bill. Whether Ceyx felt this, or seemed to lift his face by the motion of the wave, the people were in doubt; but he had felt it. And at last, by the pity of the gods, both are changed into birds; subject to the same fate, their love stayed even then, and the marriage bond was not dissolved in the birds: they mate and become parents, and for seven calm days in the winter Alcyone broods on her nest floating on the sea. Then the passage of the deep is safe; Aeolus keeps the winds in and holds them from going out, and grants a smooth sea to his grandchildren.

Some old man watched them flying together over the wide sea and praised their love kept to the end of their lives. One near him — or the same, if chance had it so — said, "This one too, which you see cutting the sea with its legs drawn up," pointing at a diver-bird with its broad throat, "was of royal birth. And if you want to come down to him in an unbroken line, his ancestors are Ilus and Assaracus and Ganymede snatched by Jupiter, and old Laomedon, and Priam, whose lot was Troy's last days; this one was Hector's brother, and, had he not met a strange fate in his early youth, perhaps he would have a name no less than Hector's — though the daughter of Dymas bore that one, while Aesacus, they say, was born in secret to Alexirhoë, the daughter of two-horned Granicus, under shady Ida. He hated cities, and, far from the shining court, kept to the lonely mountains and the humble fields, and went but rarely among the crowds of Ilium. Yet his heart was not rustic, nor proof against love: often chased through all the woods, he sees Hesperia, the daughter of Cebren, on her father's bank, drying her hair, tossed over her shoulders, in the sun. The nymph, seen, takes flight, like a frightened hind from a tawny wolf, or a river-duck caught far from her wonted pool by a hawk. The Trojan hero pursues her, and, swift with love, presses the swift girl swift with fear. Look — a snake, hidden in the grass, grazed her fleeing foot with its barbed tooth and left its venom in her body; with her flight her life was cut short. Frantic, he embraces her lifeless, and cries, 'I grieve, I grieve that I ever chased you! But I did not fear this, nor was winning worth so much. We two have destroyed you, poor girl: the wound came from the snake, but the cause from me. I would be more guilty than it, unless I send you, by my death, a comfort for your death.' He spoke, and from a rock the hoarse waves had eaten away he flung himself into the sea. Tethys, pitying him as he fell, caught him softly, and covered the swimmer with feathers, and the power to gain the death he sought was not given. The lover is angry, forced to live against his will, and thwarted when his spirit longed to leave its wretched home; and, as he had taken new wings on his shoulders, he flies up and again throws his body over the sea: the feathers break the fall. Aesacus rages, and headlong plunges into the deep, and endlessly tries the way to death. Love made him lean: the spaces between the joints of his legs are long, his neck stays long, his head is far from his body. He loves the sea, and keeps his name because he plunges into it."
