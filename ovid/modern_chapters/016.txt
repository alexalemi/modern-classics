Book IX

(Part 1 of 2)

The Neptunian hero asked the cause of the god's groan and of his broken brow; and the Calydonian river, his unkempt hair bound with reeds, began:

"You ask a mournful thing. For what beaten man wants to recount his own battles? Yet I will tell it in order; it was not so shameful to be beaten as it is glorious to have fought, and so great a conqueror is a great comfort to me.

"If the name of Deianira has at last reached your ears — once she was a most beautiful girl, the envied hope of many suitors. When, with the rest, I entered the house of the man I wished for a father-in-law, I said, 'Take me, son of Parthaon, for your son-in-law.' Hercules said the same; the others gave way to us two. He offered Jupiter for a father-in-law, and the fame of his labors, and the tasks his stepmother had set and he had overcome. Against this I said — for I thought it a disgrace for a god to yield to a mortal, and he was not yet a god — 'You see before you a king of the waters, flowing in winding channels through your realm; I am no stranger sent you as son-in-law from foreign shores, but one of your own people, a part of your state. Only let it not count against me that royal Juno does not hate me, and that no punishment of ordered labors comes near me. For you, son of Alcmena, who boast your birth — either Jupiter is your pretended father, or your real one through a crime. By your mother's adultery you claim a father: choose, then, whether you had rather Jupiter were falsely your father, or that you were born of disgrace.'

"As I said this, for a while he glared at me with a grim eye, and did not very well master his kindled anger, and gave me back just these words: 'My right hand is better than my tongue. Only let me win at fighting, and you may win at talking' — and he came at me fiercely. I was ashamed, after such big words, to give way. I threw off the green robe from my body, put up my arms against his, held my hands on guard, curved before my breast, and set my limbs for the fight. He scooped up dust in his hollow hands and threw it over me, and in his turn went yellow with the tawny sand cast on himself. He grabbed now at my neck, now at my legs as they shifted, or you would think he did, and pressed me from every side. My bulk defended me, and he attacked in vain — no differently than a breakwater the waves batter with a great roar: it stands, safe in its own weight. We drew apart a little, then closed again for battle, and stood our ground, resolved not to yield; foot was locked to foot, and I, leaning in full with my chest, pressed finger on finger and forehead on forehead. Just so I have seen strong bulls charge, when the loveliest cow in all the pasture is the prize of the fight, and the herds look on and tremble, not knowing which the mastery of so great a realm awaits. Three times, without success, Hercules tried to fling my chest off him as it bore hard against him; the fourth time he shook off my hold, loosed my clinched arms, and, striking me a blow — I am resolved to tell the truth — spun me right round and clung, a mighty weight, to my back. If you will believe me — and I seek no glory by a false tale — I seemed to have a mountain laid on me. Barely could I work my arms, streaming with sweat, back in; barely loosen his hard grip from my body. He pressed me, panting, gave me no chance to gather strength, and got hold of my neck. Then at last the earth was pressed by my knee, and I bit the sand.

"Weaker in strength, I turned to my arts, and slipped from the hero, changed into a long snake. When I had wound my body into coils and darted my forked tongue with a dreadful hiss, the Tirynthian laughed, and mocking my arts said, 'It was my cradle's work to master snakes; and though you outdo all other serpents, Acheloüs, how small a part of the Lernaean Hydra will you be, a single snake? She grew by her own wounds, and not one head of her hundred could be cut off without new danger — her neck came back stronger, with two heirs for one. Her, branching with serpents sprung from each cut and growing by her losses, I mastered and laid open. What do you think will become of you, who move borrowed weapons in a false snake's shape, hidden under a form you only borrowed?' He spoke, and clamped his fingers on the top of my neck. I was choked, as if my throat were gripped in pincers, and I fought to tear my jaws free of his thumbs.

"Even so beaten, I still had a third shape left, the fierce bull; changed to a bull, I renewed the fight. He threw his arms over my brawny neck on the left, and, dragging as I ran, followed me; and, seizing my horns, he drove them into the hard ground and flattened me on the deep sand. That was not enough: his savage right hand, gripping the stiff horn, broke it and tore it from my mutilated brow. This the Naiads filled with fruit and fragrant flowers and made holy; and now bounteous Plenty is rich by my horn." He spoke; and a nymph, girt like Diana, one of his attendants, her hair loose on either side, came in, and brought all the wealth of autumn in that overflowing horn, and choice fruit for the second course.

Day comes on; and with the first sun striking the peaks the young men leave, for they will not wait until the streams win back their peace and gentle flow and the whole flood sinks. Acheloüs hid his rustic face and his broken-horned head in the middle of the waves. He grieves at the loss of that one stolen honor; in all else he is whole, and the hurt to his head is hidden by a willow branch, or reeds laid over it.

But love of that same girl had been the ruin of you, fierce Nessus, shot through the back by a swift arrow. For the son of Jupiter, returning to his native city with his new bride, had come to the rushing waters of the Evenus. The river was swollen higher than usual with winter rains, full of whirlpools and impassable. Nessus came up — bold for himself but anxious for the wife — strong of limb and knowing the fords: "By my service she shall be set on that far bank, Hercules; use your strength to swim." So the Aonian hero handed the frightened Calydonian woman, pale with fear and dreading both the river and Nessus himself, to Nessus. Then, just as he was, weighed down by his quiver and the lion's skin — for he had thrown his club and curved bow to the far side — he said, "Since I have begun, the river must be crossed," and did not hesitate, or look for where the stream was gentlest, or stoop to be ferried by the river's favor. And now, having gained the bank and lifting the bow he had thrown across, he heard his wife's voice, and, as Nessus made to steal what had been entrusted to him, he shouted: "Where does vain confidence in your feet carry you, you ravisher? To you I speak, two-shaped Nessus! Listen, and do not seize what is mine. If no respect for me moves you, still your father's turning wheel might have kept you from forbidden embraces. You will not escape, for all your trust in your horse's speed: with a wound, not with my feet, I will overtake you." His last words he made good by the deed, and shot the fleeing back through with an arrow. The barbed steel stood out from the breast; and when it was wrenched free, the blood spurted from both wounds, mixed with the Lernaean poison's venom. Nessus caught it, and said to himself, "I shall not die unavenged" — and gave his robe, dyed in the warm blood, as a gift to the woman he was carrying off, as if a charm for love.

Long was the stretch of time between; and the deeds of great Hercules, and his stepmother's hatred, had filled the earth. Victorious, home from Oechalia, he was preparing a sacrifice vowed to Cenaean Jupiter, when chattering Rumor — who loves to add lies to truth, and swells from a tiny start by her falsehoods — ran ahead to your ears, Deianira: that the son of Amphitryon was gripped by love of Iole. In love, she believes it; terrified by the report of this new passion, at first she gives way to tears, and pours out her grief, poor woman, in weeping. Then she says, "But why do I weep? My rival will delight in these tears. Since she is coming, I must hurry, must try something new while I still can, while another has not yet taken my bed. Shall I complain, or keep silent? Go back to Calydon, or stay? Leave the house — or, if nothing else, block her way? What if, Meleager, remembering I am your sister, I steel myself to some desperate deed, and, by cutting my rival's throat, show what injury and a woman's grief can do?" Her mind runs through many courses. Above them all she chooses to send the robe steeped in Nessus's blood, to give strength back to her fading love. Not knowing herself what she gives, she hands the cause of her own sorrows to the unsuspecting Lichas, and, poor wretch, with coaxing words bids him carry this most deadly gift to her husband. Ignorant, the hero takes it, and puts on his shoulders the venom of the Lernaean serpent.

He was laying incense on the first flames, saying the words of prayer, pouring wine from the bowl on the marble altars, when the power of the poison grew warm and, loosed by the flames, ran melting far and wide through the limbs of Hercules. As long as he could, he held back his groans with his old courage. When his endurance was beaten by the pain, he shoved the altars over and filled woody Oeta with his cries. At once he tries to tear off the deadly robe: where it is pulled, it pulls the skin, and — a thing dreadful to tell — either it clings to the limbs when he tries in vain to strip it, or it lays bare the mangled flesh and the huge bones. The blood itself hisses, as a red-hot blade hisses when dipped in cold water, and boils with the burning venom. There is no limit to it; greedy flames drink up his vitals, a blue-black sweat pours from his whole body, his scorched sinews crackle, and, his marrow melted by the hidden decay, he lifts his hands to the stars and cries: "Daughter of Saturn, feed on my ruin — feed, and look down from on high, cruel one, on this destruction of mine, and glut your savage heart! Or, if I am to be pitied even by an enemy — for an enemy I am to you — take away a life racked and hateful to me, born only for toil. Death will be a gift to me; it fits a stepmother to grant such a gift.

"Was it for this I subdued Busiris, who fouled the gods' temples with strangers' blood? For this I tore from savage Antaeus the strength his mother gave him? Did the Iberian herdsman's triple shape not shake me, nor your triple shape, Cerberus? Did you, my hands, grip the horns of the mighty bull? Elis holds your work, and the waters of Stymphalus, and the Parthenian grove. By your valor was won the belt inlaid with the gold of Thermodon, and the apples guarded in vain by the sleepless dragon. Could the Centaurs stand against me, or the boar that ravaged Arcadia? Did it help the Hydra to grow by its own loss and take back double strength? And when I saw the Thracian horses fat with human blood and their mangers full of torn bodies, did I not throw them down and kill both the master and the horses? By these arms the Nemean lion lies crushed; on this neck I carried the sky. Jupiter's cruel wife grew weary of commanding — but I was tireless in doing. Now a new scourge is on me, one no valor, no weapons, no arms can resist. A devouring fire wanders in the depths of my lungs and feeds on all my limbs. And Eurystheus lives and thrives! And are there still men who can believe the gods exist?"

And then, racked with pain, he ranges over high Oeta, no differently than a bull that carries the hunting-spears fixed in its body while the man who threw them flees. Often you might have seen him groaning, often bellowing, often trying to tear off the whole robe, and leveling trees, and raging at the mountains, or stretching his arms toward his father's sky. Look — he spies Lichas, trembling, hidden in a hollow rock; and as his pain gathered all its fury, he cried, "Was it you, Lichas, who brought this deadly gift? Will you be the cause of my death?" The man trembles, pales in terror, and timidly stammers out excuses. As he speaks, reaching to clasp the hero's knees, Hercules seizes him, and, whirling him three and four times, hurls him into the Euboean waves harder than a siege-engine could. Hanging in the air, he hardened; as they say showers freeze in cold winds and turn to snow, and the whirling snow packs soft and rounds into thick hail, so, thrown through the void by those strong arms, drained of blood by fear and with no moisture left in him, he was turned — the old ages tell — into hard flint. Even now a low rock juts from the deep Euboean sea and keeps the traces of a human shape, which sailors fear to tread, as if it could feel, and they call it Lichas.

But you, famous child of Jupiter, cut down the trees that lofty Oeta bore, built them into a pyre, and bade the son of Poeas take the bow and the roomy quiver and the arrows that would visit the Trojan realm again; by his hand the flame was set beneath. And while the heaped wood is caught by the greedy fires, you spread the Nemean lion's skin over the top of the pile and lay down with your neck on your club, no other look on your face than a banqueter's reclining among full cups, wreathed in garlands.

And now the flames, gathering and spreading on every side, roared, and reached the untroubled limbs of the man who scorned them. The gods feared for the earth's champion; and Jupiter, son of Saturn — for he saw it — addressed them with joyful face: "This fear of yours is my delight, gods of heaven, and with all my heart I gladly congratulate myself that I am called the ruler and father of a grateful people, and that my offspring too is safe in your goodwill. For though this is owed to his own mighty deeds, still I am bound by it as well. But do not let your loyal hearts quail with empty fear: scorn the flames of Oeta! He who has conquered all will conquer the fires you see; he will feel the flame's power only in the part he got from his mother. What he drew from me is eternal, exempt and immune from death, to be tamed by no fire. That part, done with earth, I will receive into the heavenly regions, and I trust this act of mine will please all the gods. Yet if anyone — anyone — should grieve that Hercules is a god and grudge him the honor, still he shall know Hercules deserves it, and, unwilling, shall approve." The gods agreed; and even the royal wife seemed to bear the rest of his words with no hard look — only the last with a hard look, taking it ill that she was so plainly named.

Meanwhile whatever the flame could destroy, Vulcan had consumed; and no recognizable shape of Hercules remained, nothing drawn from his mother's likeness — only the marks of Jupiter were kept. And as a fresh serpent, sloughing old age with its skin, grows lush and gleams in new scales, so, when the Tirynthian shed his mortal limbs, he thrived in his better part, and began to look greater and to become awful in august dignity. Him the all-powerful Father, caught up among the hollow clouds, bore in his four-horse chariot up into the shining stars. Atlas felt the weight.

Even now Eurystheus, son of Sthenelus, had not laid aside his wrath, and, fierce, worked out his father-hatred on the offspring. But the Argive Alcmena, worn with long cares for her son, had Iole to pour out her old woman's laments to, to tell of her son's deeds attested by the whole world, and of her own misfortunes. At Hercules' command Hyllus had taken Iole into his bed and heart, and filled her womb with noble seed. To her Alcmena thus began:

"May the gods at least be kind to you, and cut short the long hours when, your time come, you call on Ilithyia, who watches over women in the fear of labor — the goddess whom Juno's favor made pitiless to me. For when the birth-hour of Hercules, born for so many toils, was near, and the tenth sign of the Zodiac was weighed down with the great star, the burden stretched my womb; and what I carried was so great you could easily call Jupiter the father of the hidden weight. I could bear the pains no longer. Even now, as I speak, a cold shudder takes my limbs, and part of the pain is remembering it. Tortured seven nights and as many days, worn out with agony, arms stretched to heaven, I called with loud cries on Lucina and the two Nixi. She came, indeed — but bought beforehand, ready to give my life to vengeful Juno. When she heard my groans, she sat on that altar before the door, and, her right knee pressed on her left, her fingers laced like a comb, she held back the birth; she said charms too, in a low voice, and the charms held back the birth already begun. I strained, and in my frenzy hurled vain reproaches at ungrateful Jupiter, and longed to die, and complained in words that would have moved hard stones. The women of Thebes were with me, made their vows, and cheered me in my pain.

"There was one of my maids, of the common people, Galanthis, golden-haired, quick to do my bidding, dear for her good service. She sensed that something was being worked by hostile Juno; and, going often in and out the door, she saw the goddess sitting on the altar, arms locked on her knees by the fingers, and she said, 'Whoever you are, wish my mistress joy: the Argive Alcmena is delivered; her prayer is won, the child is born.' The goddess of the womb leaped up, and, startled, loosed her joined hands — and I, on the loosing of those bonds, was delivered. They say Galanthis laughed at fooling the goddess. As she laughed, the cruel goddess dragged her by the very hair, and, when she tried to lift her body from the earth, stopped her, and changed her arms to forefeet. Her old quickness stays, and her back has not lost its color; but her shape is not what it was. Because she helped me in labor with a lying mouth, she gives birth from the mouth, and, as before, she haunts my house."

She spoke, and, moved by the memory of her old servant, she sighed. Her daughter-in-law spoke to her as she grieved: "Yet the stolen shape of one no kin to your blood moves you, mother. What if I told you the strange fate of my own sister? — though tears and grief hinder me and forbid me to speak. Dryope, the most beautiful of the Oechalian girls, was her mother's only child (my father had me by another wife). Robbed of her virginity, having suffered force from the god who owns Delphi and Delos, she was married by Andraemon, who was thought lucky in his wife.

"There is a lake whose shelving edge makes the shape of a sloping shore, with myrtle-groves crowning the top. Here Dryope came, knowing nothing of her fate, and — that you may be angrier at her lot — bringing garlands for the nymphs. On her breast she carried her son, not yet a year old, a sweet burden, feeding him with the help of her warm milk. Not far from the lake a water-lotus was blooming, mimicking the Tyrian dye, in hope of berries to come. Dryope had picked some of its flowers to give her child as toys, and I was about to do the same — for I was there — when I saw drops of blood fall from the blossom and the boughs shiver with a trembling dread. For, as the country folk now say too late, the nymph Lotis, fleeing the lust of Priapus, had passed her changed features into this plant, her name still kept.

"My sister did not know it. When, in her alarm, she tried to draw back and leave, having prayed to the nymphs, her feet were held fast by a root. She fights to tear them up, but moves nothing except her upper parts. From below the bark climbs slowly and by degrees covers all her groin. When she saw it, and made to tear her hair, she filled her hand with leaves — leaves were covering all her head. But the boy Amphissos — for his grandfather Eurytus gave him that name — felt his mother's breast go hard, and the milky stream no longer followed his sucking. I watched your cruel fate, sister, and could give you no help; and, as long as I could, I hugged the rising trunk and branches and held them back, and — I confess it — I longed to be hidden under the same bark. Look — her husband Andraemon and her most wretched father come, asking for Dryope; and when they ask for Dryope I show them the lotus. They kiss the wood still warm with life, and, flung down, cling to the roots of their own tree. Now my dear sister had nothing but her face that was not tree. Tears drop on the leaves made from her changed body; and, while she can, while her mouth still gives passage to her voice, she pours out into the air such laments as these:

"'If any faith is owed the wretched, I swear by the gods I did not deserve this cruelty. I am punished without a crime. I have lived in innocence; if I lie, may I dry up and lose the leaves I bear, and be cut down with axes and burned. But take this infant from his mother's branches and give him to a nurse; and often, beneath my tree, let him drink milk, and beneath my tree let him play; and when he can speak, let him greet his mother, and say in sorrow, "My mother is hidden in this trunk." Yet let him fear the ponds, and pick no flowers from the trees, and think all shrubs are the bodies of goddesses. Farewell, dear husband; and you, sister; and you, father — if there is any love in you, guard my branches from the sharp pruning-knife and the bite of the flocks. And since I may not bend down to you, reach your limbs up here and come for my kisses while I can still be touched, and lift up my little son. I can say no more. Already the soft bark creeps up my white neck, and I am buried at the top of my head. Take your hands from my eyes: without your help, let the closing bark cover my dying sight.' At the same moment her mouth ceased to speak and to be; and long after the body was changed, the new-made branches stayed warm."

And while Iole was telling her sister's wretched fate, and while Alcmena was drying the daughter of Eurytus's tears with her fingers — herself weeping too — a new event hushed all their sorrow: for Iolaüs stood at the high threshold, almost a boy again, his cheeks veiled in faint down, his face changed back to the first years of manhood. Hebe, the daughter of Juno, had granted him this favor, won over by her husband's pleas. As she was about to swear she would grant such gifts to no one after him, Themis stopped her: "For now," she said, "Thebes stirs up civil war, and Capaneus cannot be beaten except by Jupiter, and the two brothers will fall equal in their wounds, and the seer Amphiaraüs, the earth opening, will see his own ghosts while still alive; and a son, avenging one parent by killing the other, will be dutiful and wicked in the same act, and, driven mad by his miseries, robbed of reason and of home, will be hounded by the faces of the Furies and his mother's ghost, until his wife demands of him the fatal gold, and the Phegeian sword drains his kinsman's side. Then at last Callirhoë, the daughter of Acheloüs, will beg mighty Jupiter as a suppliant for these years of youth for her infant sons, and not let the avenger's death go unavenged. Jupiter, moved for them, will bestow the gift of his own stepdaughter and daughter-in-law, and make them men in their childhood years."

When Themis, foreknowing the future, had spoken this with prophetic voice, the gods above murmured in mixed talk, and the complaint was, why the same gifts might not be granted by others. Aurora, the daughter of Pallas, complained of her husband's aged years; gentle Ceres complained that Iasion was going gray; Vulcan asked a second life for Erichthonius; concern for the future touched Venus too, and she bargained to renew the years of Anchises.
