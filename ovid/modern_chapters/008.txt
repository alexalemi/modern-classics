Book V

(Part 1 of 2)

And while Perseus — the son of Danaë — was still in the middle of telling these tales to the gathered lords of Cepheus's people, the royal halls filled with the roar of a raging crowd. This was no clamor that sings a wedding; it was the din that cries out war. You could have compared that banquet, turned in an instant to uproar, to a calm sea that the wild fury of the winds churns up, lashing its waters into waves.

At the head of it all came Phineus, the reckless author of this attack, brandishing an ashwood spear tipped with bronze. "Look — here I am," he shouted, "here I stand, come to avenge the bride stolen from me! Now let your wings save you, if they can; let Jupiter save you, turned to his lying shower of gold — neither will tear you out of my hands!" And he drew back to hurl the lance.

But Cepheus cried out, "What are you doing? What madness, brother, drives you to this crime? Is this the thanks you pay for a service so great? Is this the bride-price you give for the life that was saved? It was not Perseus who took her from you — if you want the truth of it — but the terrible power of the Nereids, and horned Ammon, and the sea-beast that came to gorge itself on flesh torn from my own child. She was lost to you at the very hour she was doomed to die — unless, cruel as you are, that is exactly what you want: that she should die, so my grief can be your comfort. Was it not enough that you stood and watched her chained, and lifted not a finger to help — you, her uncle and her betrothed both? Now will you grieve on top of it that another man saved her, and rob him of his reward? If that reward looks so precious to you, you should have gone to fetch it off the rock where she hung bound. Let the man who did go — the man through whom my old age is not left childless — keep what he earned, by his deeds and by our given word. And understand this: he was not chosen over you, but over certain death."

Phineus said nothing back. He only looked from Cepheus to Perseus and back again, unsure which of them to strike first. Then, after a short pause, he threw his spear — hurled it with all the force his rage could lend — and threw it for nothing: it stuck fast in the couch. At that Perseus sprang up from the cushions at last, and in his fury he would have driven the weapon back through his enemy's chest — but Phineus had ducked behind an altar, and so (the shame of it) an altar sheltered a scoundrel. Still, the spear did not fly wasted: it lodged in the forehead of Rhoetus, who dropped, and when the iron was wrenched from the bone he thrashed on the ground and spattered the laid tables with his blood.

Then indeed the mob blazed up into ungovernable rage and flung their weapons. There were some who said that Cepheus deserved to die along with his son-in-law — but Cepheus had already gone out through the doorway, calling on right and good faith and the gods of hospitality to witness that this violence was done against his will. Warlike Minerva was there: she covered her brother Perseus with her aegis and put heart into him.

There was an Indian named Athis, born — so it is believed — beneath the glassy waters of the Ganges, to Limnaee the river's daughter. He was strikingly beautiful, and his rich dress only heightened it. He was in his prime, just sixteen, wrapped in a Tyrian purple cloak edged with a golden hem; a chain of gold set off his throat, and a curved clasp held back his hair, glossy with myrrh. He had been trained to hit a target however far off with a thrown javelin, but he was better still at drawing the bow. Even now, as he was bending the pliant horns of that bow with his hands, Perseus caught up a smoldering brand from the middle of the altar and struck him down, smashing his face into the splinters of his own skull.

When Lycabas the Assyrian — Athis's closest companion, and no man to hide the true love he bore him — saw those features, so often praised, rolling now in blood, he wept for Athis as the youth breathed his life out through that bitter wound. Then he snatched up the bow Athis had been bending. "Now the fight is with me," he said. "You will not gloat long over a boy's death — a thing that wins you more hatred than glory." He had not yet finished speaking when the piercing arrow leapt from the string. Perseus dodged it, and it hung caught in the folds of his robe. He turned on Lycabas the curved falchion that had already proved itself in the killing of Medusa, and drove it into his chest. Even as he died, his eyes swimming in a black night, Lycabas looked round for Athis, sank down against him, and carried to the shades below the comfort of a death shared with the one he loved.

Then Phorbas of Syene, Metion's son, and Amphimedon the Libyan, both hungry to join the fight, went down as they slipped in the blood that lay warm and wide across the floor; as they struggled up, the sword met them — driven into the ribs of the one, into the throat of Phorbas. But against Erytus, Actor's son, whose weapon was a broad double-axe, Perseus did not use his hooked sword. Instead he lifted in both hands a huge mixing-bowl, all deep-carved figures and massive weight, and smashed it down on the man. Erytus vomited red blood, toppled backward, and beat the ground with his dying head. Then Perseus struck down Polydegmon, of Semiramis's line, and Abaris from the Caucasus, and Lycetus the son of Sperchius, and long-haired Helix, and Phlegyas, and Clytus — and trampled the mounded heaps of the dying he had piled up.

Phineus, not daring to close with his enemy hand to hand, hurled a javelin — but a slip sent it into Idas instead, a man who had held back from the war in vain and taken up arms for neither side. Idas glared at cruel Phineus with hard eyes. "Since you drag me into a side after all," he said, "then take the enemy you have made, Phineus — and pay for this wound with a wound." He was just drawing the dart from his body to throw it back when he crumpled, collapsing on limbs emptied of blood.

Here too Hodites — next in rank among Cepheus's men after the king himself — fell beneath the sword of Clymenus. Hypseus cut down Prothoenor, and Perseus cut down Hypseus. Among them was old Emathion as well, a man who loved justice and feared the gods; since his years forbade him the sword, he fought with his voice instead, condemning and cursing those wicked weapons. As he clung to the altars with trembling hands, Chromis struck his head off; it fell straight onto the altar, and there, on the half-living tongue, it still spat out its curses before he breathed his last soul into the flames.

After them the twin brothers Broteas and Ammon — unbeaten with the boxing-glove, if only swords could be beaten by boxing — died by Phineus's hand; and Ampycus too, the priest of Ceres, his temples bound with a white fillet. And you, Lampetides — you were never meant for work like this, but for tuning the lyre to your voice, the labor of peacetime; you had been asked to grace the feast and the festival with your song. As you stood off to one side, holding your harmless plectrum, Pedasus jeered at you: "Go sing the rest to the ghosts down by the Styx!" and fixed his sword-point in your left temple. You fell, and your dying fingers plucked once more at the lyre's strings, so that as you dropped you struck out, by chance, one last mournful song.

Fierce Lycormas would not let him die unavenged: he tore a stout bar from the right-hand doorpost and slammed it against the bones at the center of Pedasus's neck, and Pedasus dropped to the ground like a felled bullock.

Pelates of the Cinyps was trying to wrench the oak bar from the left-hand post — and as he tried, his right hand was pinned to it by the spear of Corythus the Marmarid, nailed fast to the wood. While he hung there caught, Abas ran him through the side; but he did not fall — dying, he hung from the post that still gripped his hand.

Melaneus went down too, a man of Perseus's own side, and Dorylas, the richest lord of Nasamonian land — Dorylas, rich in acres, whom no one matched for the breadth of his fields or the heaps of incense they yielded him. The hurled steel stuck slantwise in his groin, a killing spot. When Halcyoneus of Bactria, who had dealt the wound, saw him sobbing out his soul and rolling his eyes, he said, "Out of all your fields, keep this one patch of earth you lie on," and left the lifeless body.

But Perseus, as his avenger, wrenched the still-warm spear from the wound and flung it back at Halcyoneus; it took him full in the nostrils, drove clean through his neck, and jutted out on either side. And while Fortune was steadying his hand, he laid low Clytius and Clanis, sons of one mother, each by a different wound: through both of Clytius's thighs an ash spear was driven by a strong arm, while Clanis bit down on the javelin in his mouth. Down went Celadon of Mendes; down went Astreus, born of a Palestinian mother and no certain father; and Aethion, once sharp-eyed to read the things to come, now fooled by a false omen; and Thoactes, the king's armor-bearer; and Agyrtes, infamous for the murder of his own father.

Still more work remained than was already done, for the whole mob had set its mind on crushing one man. From every side the sworn ranks pressed the fight, in a cause that made war on merit and good faith alike. On Perseus's side his father-in-law stood loyal — and helpless — with the new bride Andromeda and her mother, who filled the halls with their shrieks of encouragement; but the clash of weapons and the groans of the dying drowned them out. Bellona, the goddess of war, was flooding the household gods, defiled now, with a tide of blood, and stirring the battle to fresh life.

Phineus and the thousand who followed Phineus ringed Perseus in, alone. Their darts flew past him thicker than winter hail — past both his flanks, past his eyes, past his ears. He set his shoulders against the stone of a great pillar, and with his back thus guarded he turned to face the crowd bearing down on him and held them off. On his left pressed Molpeus of Chaonia, on his right Ethemon of Nabataea. Like a tiger that hears, from two different valleys, the lowing of two herds, and, goaded by hunger, cannot choose which to charge though it burns to charge at both — so Perseus, torn between right and left, drove off Molpeus with a wound clean through the leg and was content to let him flee. Ethemon gave him no such time, but raged in, mad to sink a wound deep in Perseus's neck; he swung with reckless force, and his sword shattered against the column's edge; the blade sprang off and lodged in its own owner's throat — though the blow had not force enough to kill him. Perseus stabbed the trembling man, who stretched out his unarmed hands in vain, with the falchion Mercury had given him.

But when Perseus saw his courage giving way at last to sheer numbers, he called out, "Since you force my hand yourselves, I will fetch help from an enemy. Turn your faces away, every friend of mine here!" — and he raised the Gorgon's head. "Find someone else to work your marvels on," said Thescelus — and even as his hand made ready to hurl its deadly javelin, he froze in that very pose, a statue of marble. Next to him, Ampyx thrust his sword at the breast of Perseus, that man of great and daring heart — and in the thrusting his right hand stiffened, unable to move forward or back. But Nileus, who had falsely bragged that the seven-mouthed Nile had fathered him, and had engraved its seven channels on his shield, half in silver, half in gold, cried, "Look, Perseus, at the source of my line! You will carry a great comfort down to the silent shades — that you fell to a man as great as I am." The last of his words was choked off in the middle of the sound; you would think his half-open mouth was still trying to speak, but it gave his words no way through. Eryx rounded on them: "It is your own cowardice that has frozen you, not the Gorgon's locks! Charge in with me and throw down this boy and his sorcerer's weapons!" He was about to charge when the ground caught his feet, and he stood there an immovable stone, an armed statue.

These men earned their punishment. But there was one, Aconteus, a soldier of Perseus's own: fighting for his lord, he caught sight of the Gorgon and hardened, stone rising over him. Astyages, thinking he was still alive, struck him with a long sword — and the sword rang out with a shrill, clear note. While Astyages stood there stunned, he took on the same nature himself, and the look of a man caught in astonishment stayed fixed on his marble face. It would take too long to name the men of lower rank. Two hundred bodies were still left for the fight; two hundred bodies stiffened at the sight of the Gorgon.

Now at last Phineus was sorry for his unjust war. But what could he do? He saw the statues standing in their scattered poses, recognized his own men, and, calling each by name, begged them for help. Not yet believing it, he touched the bodies nearest him: marble. He turned his face away, and — a suppliant now — stretched out his hands and held his arms aside in confession of his guilt. "You win, Perseus!" he said. "Take away your monster, take that stone-making face of your Medusa, whatever she is — take it away, I beg you! It was not hatred, not the lust for a throne, that drove me to war — it was for a wife that I took up arms. Your claim was the better one in merit, mine only in time. I am not ashamed to yield. Grant me nothing, bravest of men, but this one life; let all the rest be yours."

As he said all this, not daring even to look at the man he was pleading with, Perseus answered: "What I can give you, most craven Phineus — and it is a great gift for a coward — that I will give. Put away your fear: no weapon shall touch you. Better still, I will make you a monument to last forever; in my father-in-law's house you will always be on show, so that my wife can console herself with the likeness of the man she was promised to." And with that he swung Medusa, the daughter of Phorcys, round to the side toward which Phineus had turned his frightened face. Then, even as Phineus struggled to twist his eyes away, his neck went rigid, and the very moisture of his eyes hardened into stone. But still the fearful face, the pleading look, the hands hanging down, the guilty crouch — all of it stayed, exactly as it was.

Perseus came home victorious, his wife beside him, and entered the walls of his fathers' city. There, as the champion and avenger of his wronged grandfather Acrisius, he moved against Proetus — for Proetus had driven his brother out by force of arms and seized the citadel of Acrisius. But neither his weapons nor the stronghold he had so wrongly taken could save him against the grim eyes of the snake-haired monster.

And yet you, Polydectes, lord of little Seriphus — the young man's courage, proven through so many trials, had not softened you, nor had his sufferings; you nursed instead a hard and unbending hatred, and there was no end to your unjust spite. You even belittled his fame, and claimed that the killing of Medusa was a lie. "Then we will give you proof of the truth," said Perseus. "Shield your eyes, the rest of you!" — and with Medusa's face he turned the king's own face to bloodless stone.

All this while, Minerva had stayed at the side of her brother — Perseus, the child of the golden shower. Now, wrapped in a hollow cloud, she left Seriphus behind, with Cythnus and Gyarus on her right, and, taking the shortest way across the sea, made for Thebes and Helicon, the mountain of the virgin Muses. Reaching it, she came to rest and spoke to the learned sisters: "The fame of a new spring has come to my ears — one that the hard hoof of the winged horse, sprung from Medusa's blood, struck open. That is what brings me here. I wanted to see the marvel for myself; I saw the horse himself born out of his mother's blood." Urania answered, "Whatever brings you to our home, goddess, you are most welcome to us. The story is true: Pegasus is indeed the maker of this spring" — and she led Minerva to the sacred waters.

Minerva marveled a long while at the pool struck open by a hoof, then looked round at the groves of the ancient woods, the caves, the grass picked out with countless flowers, and called the daughters of Mnemosyne happy alike in their art and in their home. One of the sisters answered her: "O Minerva — you who would have come to be one of our number, had your valor not carried you off to greater deeds — you speak truly, and you are right to praise both our art and our home; ours is a happy lot, if only we can be safe. But nothing is off-limits to wickedness any longer, and so everything frightens our virgin minds. Cruel Pyreneus still turns before our eyes, and I have not yet fully come back to myself. That savage man had seized the lands of Daulis and Phocis with his Thracian troops and ruled them by force. We were on our way to the temple on Parnassus; he saw us going, and, pretending to honor our godhead with a false face — for he knew who we were — he said, 'Daughters of Mnemosyne, stop here. Do not scruple, I beg you, to shelter under my roof from the foul weather and the rain' — for it was raining — 'the gods above have often gone into humbler cottages than mine.' Moved by his words and by the weather, we agreed and stepped into the front rooms of his house. The rain soon stopped; the North Wind mastered the South, and the dark clouds fled from a clearing sky. We meant to leave — but Pyreneus shut up his house and made ready to force us, and we escaped him only by taking wing. He stood high on his rooftop as though to follow: 'Whatever way is open to you,' he said, 'will be open to me as well' — and in his madness he flung himself from the top of the tower, and fell on his face, and shattered the bones of his skull, and dying beat the ground he had stained with his wicked blood."

So the Muse was speaking — when wings whirred through the air, and a voice, as if in greeting, came down from the high branches. Jupiter's daughter looked up, searching for where such clear-speaking tongues could be sounding from, and thought a human being had spoken. But it was birds — nine magpies, perched on the boughs, bewailing their fate, mimicking every sound. As the goddess wondered at them, Urania began: "These too, not long ago, were beaten in a contest and added to the flock of birds. Pierus, rich in the fields of Pella, was their father; their mother was Euippe of Paeonia, who called nine times on Lucina, the goddess of childbirth, and nine times gave birth. Puffed up with their own number, the foolish sisters came marching through all the cities of Thessaly and Achaea, and challenged us to a contest with words like these: 'Stop cheating the ignorant crowd with your empty sweetness. If you have any faith in yourselves, contend with us, you goddesses of Thespiae — we will not be beaten in voice or in skill, and there are just as many of us. Either yield, if you lose, the spring that Medusa's steed opened and the fountain of Aganippe as well, or we will give up the plains of Emathia as far as the snowy Paeonians. Let the nymphs be the judges between us.' It was shameful to compete with them — but to back down seemed more shameful still. The chosen nymphs swore their oath by the rivers and took their seats on thrones of living rock. Then, without even drawing lots, the one who had first proposed the contest began to sing: the wars of the gods, and false glory heaped on the Giants, and the great gods belittled. She sang how Typhoeus, sent up from the deepest realm of the earth, had struck terror into the dwellers in heaven, so that they all turned their backs and fled, until the land of Egypt took them in, exhausted, and the Nile that splits into seven mouths. She told how earth-born Typhoeus came even there, and the gods above hid themselves in borrowed shapes. 'Jupiter,' she said, 'became the leader of a flock — which is why, even now, Libyan Ammon is shown with curling horns. Apollo hid as a crow, Bacchus, Semele's son, as a goat, Diana, Apollo's sister, as a cat, Juno, the daughter of Saturn, as a snow-white cow, Venus as a fish, and Mercury under the wings of an ibis.'

"So far she had raised her singing voice to the lyre. Then it was our turn — we of Aonia were called on to sing. But perhaps you have no leisure for this, no time to spare your ears to our songs?" "Have no doubt of it — tell me your song, in its proper order," said Minerva, and she settled down in the light shade of the grove. So the Muse went on. The whole conduct of the contest, she told Minerva, had been handed to one of their number; and that one rose now, and bound up her loose-flowing hair with ivy.
