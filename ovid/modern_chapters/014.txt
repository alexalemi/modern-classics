Book VIII

(Part 1 of 2)

Now the Morning Star unveiled the bright day and drove off the hours of night; the east wind dropped, and the moist clouds rose. A gentle south wind gave the sons of Aeacus and Cephalus their passage home, and, carried on it prosperously, they reached the harbor they were bound for sooner than they had hoped.

Meanwhile Minos was laying waste the coast of the Lelegians, trying out the strength of his arms against the city of Alcathoüs, ruled by Nisus — Nisus, among whose honored gray hairs a single lock, bright with purple, grew from the middle of his crown, and on it hung the safety of his great kingdom.

The horns of the rising moon were coming round for the sixth time, and still the fortune of the war hung in the balance, and long did Victory hover between the two on wavering wings. There was a royal tower set against the city's singing walls, on which — so the story goes — the son of Latona once laid his golden lyre; the sound of it sank into the stone. Often, in time of peace, the daughter of Nisus used to climb up there and strike the ringing stones with a little pebble. In wartime, too, she often watched from that tower the struggles of hard warfare; and by now, as the siege dragged on, she knew the names of the chieftains, their weapons and horses, their dress and their Cretan quivers. Above all she had learned the face of the leader, the son of Europa — learned it better than mere knowing needed.

In her eyes, whether Minos had shut his head in a plumed and crested helmet, he was beautiful in the helmet; or whether he had taken up his gold-gleaming shield, taking up the shield became him. When he drew back his arm and hurled the whipping javelin, the girl praised the skill that went with the strength; when he laid an arrow on the wide bow and bent it, she would swear that Apollo stood just so when he took up his arrows. But when he bared his face by lifting off the bronze, and, robed in purple, pressed the back of a white horse gay with embroidered trappings, and reined in its foaming mouth — then the daughter of Nisus was hardly her own mistress, hardly in her right mind. Happy, she called the javelin he touched; happy the reins he pressed with his hand. Her whole impulse was to carry her maiden steps through the enemy ranks, if only she could; her impulse was to fling her body from the top of the towers into the Cretan camp, or to open the bronze-bound gates to the enemy — or anything else that Minos might want. And as she sat gazing at the white tents of the Cretan king, she said:

"I do not know whether to be glad or sorry that this grievous war is fought. I grieve that Minos is the enemy of the woman who loves him; but if there had been no war, he would never have been known to me! And yet — he could lay down the war and take me as a hostage: me for his companion, me as the pledge of peace. If she who bore you, most beautiful of kings, was such as you are yourself, then with reason a god burned for her. Oh, three times happy would I be, if I could rise on wings through the air and light on the camp of the Cretan king, and, confessing myself and my love, ask him at what price he would be bought — only let him not ask for my father's citadel! For let the marriage I long for perish, rather than that I win it by treason! — and yet, the mercy of a gentle conqueror has often made it a blessing to many to be beaten. He wages a just war, surely, for his murdered son, and he is strong both in his cause and in the arms that guard his cause. We shall be beaten, I think; and if that is to be this city's end, why should his own warfare open these walls to him, and not my love? Better for him to win without slaughter and delay and the spending of his own blood. At least I need not fear, Minos, that someone may wound your breast without knowing you — for who is so hard-hearted that he would dare, unless in ignorance, to aim a cruel spear at you? The plan pleases me, and I am resolved: to hand over my country as my dowry and put an end to the war. But to be willing is not enough! A guard watches the gates, and my father keeps the keys. Him alone, in my misery, do I dread; he alone stands in the way of my desire. Would the gods might make me fatherless! But surely everyone is a god to themselves: Fortune has no patience with idle prayers. Another woman, fired by a passion this great, would long since have taken joy in destroying whatever blocked her love. And why should any woman be braver than I am? I would dare to go through fire and swords — and yet here there is no need of fire or swords; I need only my father's lock of hair. That purple lock is more precious to me than gold: it will make me happy, will make me mistress of my own wish."

As she said such things, night came on — the greatest nurse of cares — and with the darkness her boldness grew. The first sleep had come, when sleep takes hold of hearts worn out with the day's worries. Silently she entered her father's chamber and — the horror of it! — the daughter robbed the father of his fatal lock. Having won her unspeakable prize, she carried the plunder out through the very midst of the enemy — so great was her confidence in the service she had done — and came to the king; whom, as he stood aghast, she thus addressed:

"Love urged the crime. I am Scylla, the royal daughter of Nisus, and I hand over to you the fortunes of my country and my own. I ask no reward but you. Take this purple lock as the pledge of my love — and believe that I am handing you not a lock of hair, but my father's life." And in her guilty hand she held out the gift. Minos shrank back from what she offered, and, shaken at the thought of so monstrous a deed, he answered: "May the gods banish you, you disgrace of our age, from their whole world; may earth and sea alike be denied you! I at least will not allow so great a monster to touch Crete, the cradle of Jupiter, which is my realm."

He spoke; and when, most just of lawgivers, he had set his terms on the conquered, he ordered the fleet's cables loosed and the bronze-beaked ships driven on by the oars. When Scylla saw the launched keels cutting the water, and saw that the prince gave her no reward for her wickedness, her pleading spent, she turned to violent rage, and, stretching out her hands, hair streaming, cried in her frenzy:

"Where do you flee, leaving behind the maker of your victory — O you whom I set above my country, above my father? Where do you flee, heartless man, whose triumph is both my crime and my service? Did the gift I gave not move you, nor my love, nor that all my hope was heaped on you alone? For where shall I go, deserted? Home? It lies conquered. But suppose it stood — my treason has closed it to me. To my father's face? The father I handed to you? My people rightly hate me; my neighbors fear my example. I have shut myself out of the whole world, that Crete alone might be open to me — and you forbid me even that, and leave me, ingrate? Then Europa was not your mother, but the pitiless Syrtis, some Armenian tigress, or Charybdis churned by the south wind! Nor are you Jupiter's son, nor was your mother tricked by the shape of a bull. That tale of your birth is a lie: it was a real bull that fathered you, wild, and charmed by love of no heifer. Take your vengeance on me, Nisus my father! Rejoice at my ruin, walls I have just betrayed! For I confess I have deserved it, I am worthy to die. Yet let one of those I so wickedly wronged destroy me. Why should you, who have conquered by my crime, punish that crime? What was treason to my country and my father, let it be a kindness to you! She is a fit wife for you, the one who tricked the savage bull with the wooden cow and carried in her womb that mismatched thing. Do my words reach your ears at all — or do the winds carry off my empty words and your ships together, ungrateful man? Ah, now, now it is no wonder Pasiphaë preferred a bull to you: you were the more savage beast! Wretch that I am — he bids them hurry! The water roars, cleft by the oars, and my own land, and I with it, dwindle from his sight. It is no use — no use your forgetting all I have earned. In spite of you I will follow, and, gripping the curved stern, be dragged across the long seas."

She had scarcely spoken when she leaped into the waves and, with love lending her strength, caught up to the ships and clung, a hateful passenger, to the ship of Knossos. When her father saw her — for now he hung in the air, just made a sea eagle with tawny wings — he swooped to tear her with his hooked beak. In terror she let go the stern; but the light air seemed to hold her as she fell, so she would not touch the sea. It was feathers holding her: changed into a bird, she is called Ciris, the cutter, and she takes that name from the lock she cut away.

Minos, once he stepped from his ships and touched the land of Crete, paid his vow to Jupiter — a hundred bulls' bodies — and his palace was decked with the spoils he hung there. But the reproach of his house had grown, and his wife's shameful adultery was laid bare by the monstrous, two-shaped creature she had borne. Minos resolves to remove the disgrace from his home, to shut it away in a house of many divisions, a dwelling full of blind mazes. Daedalus, most famous of men for his craftsman's skill, laid out the work and confused its marks, leading the eye astray through the tangle of its winding ways. Just as the clear Maeander plays in the fields of Phrygia and flows now forward, now back, in its shifting course, and, meeting itself, looks on its own oncoming waters, and turns now toward its source, now toward the open sea, keeping its uncertain waters at work — so Daedalus filled his countless paths with turnings, and could scarcely find his own way back to the threshold, so great was the trickery of the place.

Here he shut up the double form of the bull and the young man; and when the monster, twice gorged before on Athenian blood, was subdued at the third tribute drawn by lot every ninth year — and when the difficult door, retraced by none who had entered before, was found by the help of the maiden and her gathered-up thread — at once the son of Aegeus, carrying off the daughter of Minos, set sail for Dia, and there on that shore cruelly abandoned his companion. To her, deserted and bitterly weeping, Bacchus brought his embrace and his help; and so that she might shine in a lasting star, he took the crown from her brow and flung it into the sky. Through the yielding air it flew, and as it flew its jewels turned to gleaming fires and settled in their place, the shape of the crown still kept, midway between the Kneeling One and the Serpent-holder.

Meanwhile Daedalus, sick of Crete and of his long exile, and touched by love of his native land, was shut in by the sea. "Though Minos block the land and the water," he said, "still the sky at least is open; we will go that way. Let Minos own all else — he does not own the air." So he spoke, and turned his mind to arts unknown, and made nature new. For he lays feathers in order, beginning with the smallest, a shorter following a longer, so you would think they had grown on a slope — just as a rustic pan-pipe rises little by little on its uneven reeds. Then he binds the middle ones with thread, the lowest with wax, and, so arranged, bends them into a gentle curve, to imitate real birds. His son Icarus stood beside him, and, not knowing that he was handling his own peril, kept catching with a bright face at the feathers the wandering breeze had ruffled, or working the yellow wax soft with his thumb, and by his play hindered his father's marvelous work. When the last touch had been put to the undertaking, the craftsman balanced his own body on the twin wings and hung poised in the beaten air. He fitted his son with wings too, and told him: "Icarus, I warn you, keep to the middle way. If you fly too low, the sea will weight your feathers; too high, and the fire of the sun will scorch them. Fly between the two. And do not watch Boötes, or the Great Bear, or the drawn sword of Orion: take your course with me for guide." At once he gives him the rules of flight and fits the untried wings to his shoulders. As he worked and warned, the old man's cheeks grew wet, and his father's hands trembled. He gave his son kisses never to be given again; and, lifted on his wings, he flew ahead, afraid for his companion, like a bird that has led her tender young out of the high nest into the air. He urges the boy to follow, teaches him the deadly art, moves his own wings, and looks back at his son's.

Some angler, plying his quivering rod, or a shepherd leaning on his crook, or a ploughman on his plow-handle, saw them and stood amazed, and believed that those who could cut the sky must be gods. And now Samos, sacred to Juno, was on the left (Delos and Paros were behind them), and on the right lay Lebynthos and Calymne rich in honey — when the boy began to delight in his bold flight, and left his guide, and, drawn by longing for the sky, drove his course higher. The nearness of the blazing Sun softened the fragrant wax that bound his feathers; the wax melted. He beat his bare arms, and, having lost his oarage, caught no air, and his mouth, crying out his father's name, was taken into the blue water, which drew its name from him.

But the unhappy father — a father no longer — cried, "Icarus, where are you? In what quarter shall I look for you? Icarus!" he cried — and then he saw the feathers on the waves, and cursed his own arts, and buried the body in a tomb; and the land was called by the name of the boy laid there. As he was laying his poor son's body in the ground, a chattering partridge watched him from a leafy holm-oak, and clapped its wings, and sang out its delight. It was then the only bird of its kind, never seen in earlier years, and, newly made a bird, a lasting reproach to you, Daedalus. For his sister, not knowing what fate held, had once handed her son to him to be taught — a boy of twelve, with a mind quick to learn. The boy it was who studied the backbone he noticed in the middle of a fish and, taking it for a model, cut a row of teeth in sharp iron and so invented the saw; he was the first, too, to bind two iron arms at a single joint, so that, held equally apart, one might stand fixed while the other traced a circle. Daedalus envied him, and threw him headlong from the sacred citadel of Minerva, lying that he had slipped. But Pallas, who favors the clever, caught him and made him a bird and clothed him with feathers in mid-air; yet the force of that quick genius passed into his wings and feet, while his name stayed as before, Perdix. This bird does not raise its body high, or build its nest in branches and treetops; it flutters near the ground and lays its eggs in hedgerows, and, remembering that old fall, it dreads the heights.

And now the land of Etna held Daedalus in his weariness, and Cocalus, who took up arms at his plea, was praised for his kindness. By now, too, Athens had ceased to pay its mournful tribute, thanks to the deeds of Theseus. The temples are hung with garlands; they call on warlike Minerva, with Jupiter and the other gods, honoring them with the blood of vowed victims, with gifts laid down, with censers of incense. Wandering Fame had spread the name of Theseus through the Argive cities, and the peoples of rich Achaia begged his help in their great dangers. Calydon too, though it had Meleager, sought him with anxious, suppliant prayer. The cause of the plea was a boar, the servant and avenger of Diana's wrath.

For they say that Oeneus, for a year full of blessings, had offered the first fruits of the corn to Ceres, his wine to Bacchus, and the oil of Pallas to golden-haired Minerva. Begun with the country gods, that coveted honor went round to all the powers above — all but the altars of Latona's daughter, which alone, they say, were passed over and left without incense. Anger touches even the gods. "But I will not take this quietly," she said; "and I who am dishonored will not be called unavenged as well." And, slighted, she sent an avenging boar through the fields of Oeneus, larger than the bulls that grassy Epirus rears, and bigger than the fields of Sicily have. His eyes glare with blood and fire; his rough neck stands stiff; his bristles stand up like spikes, like a palisade of tall stakes. Boiling foam runs down his broad shoulders with a harsh hiss; his tusks match the tusks of India; lightning comes from his mouth, and the leaves burn at his breath. One while he tramples the growing crops in the blade; another he reaps the ripe hopes of the farmer doomed to weep, and cuts down the grain in the ear. In vain the threshing-floor, in vain the barns wait for the promised harvest. Down go the heavy clusters with their long vine-shoots, down the berries with the boughs of the ever-green olive. He rages against the flocks too: neither shepherd nor dog can guard them, nor the fierce bulls defend the herds. The people scatter and think themselves safe only within a city's walls — until Meleager and a chosen band of young men band together, hungry for glory:

the twin sons of Tyndareus, one famous with the boxing-glove, the other on horseback; and Jason, builder of the first ship; and Theseus with Pirithoüs, a happy pairing; and the two sons of Thestius; and the sons of Aphareus, Lynceus and swift Idas; and Caeneus, now no longer a woman; and fierce Leucippus, and Acastus famed for the javelin, and Hippothoüs, and Dryas, and Phoenix the son of Amyntor, and the matched sons of Actor, and Phyleus, sent from Elis. Nor was Telamon missing, nor the father of great Achilles; and with the son of Pheres and the Boeotian Iolaüs came tireless Eurytion and Echion, unbeaten in the race, and Lelex of Naryx, and Panopeus, and Hyleus, and bold Hippasus, and Nestor, still in his early years; and those Hippocoön sent from ancient Amyclae, and the father-in-law of Penelope with Ancaeus of Parrhasia, and the far-seeing son of Ampycus, and the son of Oecleus, still safe from his wife — and Atalanta of Tegea, the glory of the Lycaean woods. A polished clasp fastened the top of her robe; her plain hair was gathered in a single knot; the ivory guard of her arrows rattled, slung from her left shoulder, and her left hand held the bow. Such was her dress; and her face was one you might truly call a girl's in a boy, a boy's in a girl. The Calydonian hero saw her and, at the same moment, longed for her — though the god was unwilling — and drank in the hidden flame, and said, "Happy the man she thinks worthy of her!" But the moment and his sense of shame let him say no more: the greater work of the great contest presses on.

A wood thick with timber, which no age had felled, rose from the level ground and looked out over the fields below. When the men came there, some spread the nets, some slipped the dogs from their leashes, some followed the pressed prints of the tracks, each eager to find his own danger. There was a hollow gully where rainwater used to drain; at the bottom of the pool grew pliant willows, smooth sedge, marsh rushes, osiers, and thin reeds on their long stalks. Roused from here, the boar hurls himself violently into the midst of his enemies, like lightning struck from bursting clouds. The grove is flattened by his charge; the shoved trees give a crash. The young men shout, and hold their weapons out in strong right hands, the broad iron quivering. On he rushes and scatters the dogs, cutting down with sidelong strokes whichever bays at him in his fury. The spear first hurled by Echion's arm did no good, and only nicked a maple trunk. The next, had it not used too much of the thrower's strength, seemed sure to lodge in the back it was aimed at; but it flew too far — Jason of Pagasae had thrown it. "Phoebus," said the son of Ampycus, "if I have worshiped you and worship you still, grant me to hit my mark with an unerring shot!" The god granted his prayer as far as he could: the boar was struck by him, but without a wound — Diana took the iron head from the flying shaft, and the wood came without its point. The beast's fury was roused, and it blazed no gentler than the lightning: light flashed from its eyes, flame breathed from its chest. As a boulder flies, launched by a taut rope, when it is aimed at walls or towers packed with soldiers, so with sure onset the wound-dealing boar is carried among the young men, and lays low Eupalamus and Pelagon, who guarded the right wing; their comrades snatched them up as they fell. But Enaesimus, son of Hippocoön, did not escape the deadly stroke: as he trembled and made to turn his back, the cut sinews of his knee gave way. Perhaps the Pylian Nestor too would have perished before the time of Troy, but, taking a spring from his planted spear, he leaped into the branches of a tree standing close by and, safe in his perch, looked down on the enemy he had fled. The boar, whetting his tusks on an oak trunk, stood fierce and ready for their ruin, and, trusting his newly sharpened weapons, gored the thigh of great Othriades with his hooked tusk. But the twin brothers — not yet stars of the sky — both conspicuous, both riding horses whiter than snow, both shaking the poised points of their lances with a trembling motion, would have dealt wounds, had the bristly beast not gone into the shadowy woods, a place no javelin or horse could reach. Telamon pursues him and, careless in the heat of the chase, falls headlong, tripped by a tree root. While Peleus lifts him, the girl of Tegea set a swift arrow to the string and let it fly from the bent bow: fixed under the beast's ear, it grazed the top of his body and reddened the bristles with a little blood. Yet she was no more glad at the success of her shot than Meleager was. He is thought to have seen the blood first and first to have shown it to his comrades, and to have said, "You will win the honor your courage has earned." The men flushed with rivalry and cheered one another on, raising their spirits with shouts, and threw their weapons without order; the very crowd of them fouled the throws and baffled the strokes they aimed.

Look — the Arcadian, wielding his double axe and rushing against his fate, cried, "Learn, young men, how much a man's weapons beat a woman's, and give way to my work! Though Latona's daughter herself should shield him with her own arms, still, in spite of Diana, my right hand will destroy him." So he boasted with his swelling mouth; and, lifting the double-edged axe in both hands, he stood on tiptoe, poised for the downward blow. The beast forestalled the bold man, and where the way to death is quickest drove both tusks into the top of his groin. Ancaeus fell, and his coiled bowels slid out in a rush of blood, and the earth was soaked with gore. Pirithoüs, the son of Ixion, was advancing straight at the enemy, shaking his hunting-spear in his strong hand, when the son of Aegeus called to him from a distance: "Stop, O dearer to me than myself, better part of my soul! There is no shame in valor at long range: rash courage was the death of Ancaeus." He spoke, and hurled his heavy cornel spear with its bronze point; well aimed, and about to grant his wish, it was stopped by a leafy bough of a beech. The son of Aeson threw his javelin too, which chance turned aside from the boar to the death of an innocent hound: flung through its belly, it pinned the belly to the ground. But the hand of Oeneus's son had different luck. Of two he threw, the first spear stood fixed in the earth, the second in the middle of the boar's back. There is no delay: while the beast rages, wheeling its body round and spewing hissing foam with fresh blood, the giver of the wound comes up, provokes his enemy to fury, and buries his bright hunting-spear deep in the shoulder facing him. His comrades bear witness to their joy with a glad shout, and reach to clasp the victor's right hand in theirs, and gaze in wonder at the huge beast sprawled over so much ground; and they do not yet think it safe to touch him — but each, all the same, stains his own weapon in the blood.

Then Meleager himself, setting his foot on the deadly head, pressed it down and said, "Take, girl of Nonacris, the spoil that is mine by right, and let my glory be shared with you." At once he gives her the hide, rough with its stiff bristles, and the head marked by its great tusks. The gift, and the giver of the gift, delighted her; but the others envied it, and a murmur ran through the whole company. Of these the sons of Thestius, stretching out their arms, cried in a great voice: "Come, put them down, woman, and don't take our honors from us; and don't let confidence in your beauty fool you — your lovesick giver may be far off when you need him." And they took the gift from her, and from him the right to give it. The war-god's son could not bear it: gnashing in swelling rage, he said, "Learn, you robbers of another's honor, how far deeds stand from threats" — and he ran his cruel sword through the breast of Plexippus, who feared no such thing. Toxeus, still in doubt what to do, at once wanting to avenge his brother and afraid to share his brother's fate, Meleager did not let waver long: he warmed the weapon, still hot from the first killing, again in a brother's blood.

Althaea was carrying gifts to the temples of the gods, her son the victor, when she saw her dead brothers being carried home. She beat her breast and filled the city with mournful cries, and changed her gold-embroidered robes for black. But the moment the author of the deaths was named, all her grief fell away, and turned from tears to a lust for punishment.

There was a billet of wood which, when the daughter of Thestius lay in childbed, the three Sisters, the Fates, had laid on the fire, and, spinning the threads of destiny under their pressing thumbs, had said: "To you, newborn child, and to this wood, we give the same span of life." When they had spoken the charm and gone, the mother snatched the burning branch from the fire and doused it with water. Long had it lain hidden in an inmost room, and, kept safe, had kept safe, O youth, your years. Now the mother brings it out and orders torches and kindling laid, and, when they are laid, sets the hostile fire to them. Then, four times trying to put the branch on the flames, four times she stops. Mother and sister fight in her, and the two names pull one heart two ways. Often her face went pale with dread of the coming crime; often the boiling anger in her eyes gave it a flush; and now her look was like someone's who threatens something cruel, now one you would think full of pity. And when the fierce heat of her rage had dried her tears, tears were found even so. As a ship that the wind seizes, and the tide running against the wind, feels the double force and unsteadily obeys them both, so the daughter of Thestius wavers between two warring loves, laying down her anger and rousing it again. Still, the sister begins to win out over the mother; and, to soothe with blood the kindred shades, she is dutiful in an unnatural way. For when the deadly fire had gained strength, she said, "Let that pyre burn my own flesh." And, holding the fatal wood in her ruthless hand, she stood, poor woman, before the sepulchral altars and said: "You three goddesses of punishment, you Furies, turn your faces to these dread rites. I am avenging, and I am doing wrong. Death must be atoned by death, crime piled on crime, funeral on funeral; through heaped-up grief let this unnatural house perish. Shall Oeneus rejoice in his victorious son, and Thestius be left childless? Better that you both should mourn. Only you, ghosts of my brothers, newly made spirits, feel this service of mine, and take the offering bought at so great a cost — the guilty pledge of my own womb. Ah, wretched me! Where am I carried? Forgive me, brothers, the feelings of a mother! My hands fail at what I began. I own he has deserved to die; but that I should be the cause of his death — that I cannot bear. So shall he go unpunished? Alive, victorious, swollen with his very success, shall he hold the throne of Calydon, while you lie a little cold heap of ashes and lifeless shades? No — I will not endure it. Let the guilty man perish, and drag down with him his father's hope, his kingdom, and his country's ruin. But where is a mother's heart? Where the loving bonds of a parent, and the pangs I bore for twice five months? Oh, that you had burned as an infant in that first fire, and that I had let it be! You lived by my gift; now by your own deserts you shall die. Take the reward of your deeds, and give me back the life twice given you — first at your birth, then when I saved the brand — or lay me too in my brothers' tomb. I long to do it, and I cannot. What shall I do? One moment my brothers' wounds are before my eyes, the image of that dreadful slaughter; the next, love and a mother's name break me. Wretch that I am! To my sorrow you will win, brothers — but win; only let me follow the comfort I give you, and you yourselves." She spoke, and, turning away, with trembling right hand threw the funeral brand into the middle of the flames. The wood gave, or seemed to give, a groan, and, caught by the unwilling fire, it burned.

Far off and unaware, Meleager is scorched by that flame, and feels his vitals seared by hidden fires, and by his courage masters the great pain. Yet he grieves that he dies a bloodless, inglorious death, and calls the wounds of Ancaeus a happy lot; and, with a groan, he calls on his aged father, his brothers, his loving sisters, and, with his last words, the companion of his bed, and perhaps his mother too. The fire and the pain swell, then sink again; both are put out together, and little by little his breath slips away into the light air, as little by little the white ash veils the embers.

Lofty Calydon lies low. Young and old mourn; commons and nobles groan; the mothers of Calydon by the Evenus tear their hair and wail. His father sprawls on the ground, fouling his white hair and aged face with dust, and rails at his long life. As for the mother — her own hand, conscious of the dreadful deed, exacted the penalty, the sword driven through her body. Not if a god had given me a hundred mouths ringing with tongues, and a great genius, and the whole of Helicon besides, could I tell out the sorrowful laments of his unhappy sisters. Careless of dignity, they beat their bruised breasts; and while the body lasts, they warm it and warm it again, they kiss it, they kiss the bier where it lies. After he is ashes, they press the gathered ashes to their breasts and lie sprawled about the tomb, and, clasping his name carved in stone, pour their tears upon the name. Them at last the daughter of Latona, sated with the ruin of the house of Parthaon, lifts up on wings springing from their bodies — all but Gorge and the daughter-in-law of noble Alcmena — and stretches long wings along their arms, makes their mouths horny, and sends them, so changed, through the air.

Meanwhile Theseus, his part in the shared labor done, was making his way to the citadel of Erechtheus, the city of Minerva.
