Book XIV

(Part 1 of 2)

And now Glaucus, the Euboean plougher of the swelling waves, had left behind Etna, laid on the Giant's jaws, and the fields of the Cyclops, that knew no harrow, no plow, that owed nothing to the yoked oxen; had left Zancle too, and the facing walls of Rhegium, and the ship-wrecking strait which, pressed between the two shores, holds the border of the Ausonian and Sicilian lands. Thence, swimming with his huge hands through the Etruscan seas, Glaucus came to the grassy hills and the halls of Circe, daughter of the Sun, full of many wild beasts. When he saw her, greetings given and received, he said: "Have pity, goddess, on a god, I pray! For you alone — if only I seem worthy — can ease this love. How great the power of herbs is, daughter of Titan, none knows better than I, who was changed by them. And that the cause of my madness may not be unknown to you: on the Italian shore, opposite the walls of Messina, I saw Scylla. I am ashamed to tell my promises, prayers, caresses, and scorned words; but you, if there is any power in incantation, move the charm with your holy lips, or, if some herb is stronger, use the proven force of a potent herb. I do not ask you to cure me and heal these wounds — there is no need of an end: let her share in the flame." But Circe — for no one has a nature more open to such fires, whether the cause is in herself or Venus, offended by her father's disclosure, makes it so — answered thus: "You would do better to court one who is willing, who wants the same, caught by an equal desire. You are worthy — surely you could be — to be courted of your own accord; and if you give hope, believe me, you will be courted. That you may not doubt it, and that confidence in your beauty not fail you — see, I, though a goddess, and daughter of the shining Sun, though I can do so much with charms and so much with herbs, vow to be yours. Scorn her who scorns you, requite her who follows you, and by one act take vengeance on two." To her attempt Glaucus said: "Sooner will leaves grow in the sea and seaweed spring on the mountaintops than my love change, while Scylla lives." The goddess was outraged; and, since she could not hurt him — nor, loving him, would — she turned her anger on the one preferred to herself; and, stung by the rebuff of her love, at once she grinds herbs infamous for their horrid juices, and mixes with the grinding the charms of Hecate, and puts on sea-blue robes, and comes out through the throng of fawning beasts from the middle of her hall; and, making for Rhegium, opposite the rocks of Zancle, she enters the waves boiling with the tides, and sets her steps on them as on solid ground, and skims dry-footed over the surface of the sea.

There was a little bay, curved into a bent bow, a favorite retreat of Scylla, where she used to withdraw from the heat of sea and sky, when the sun was highest and made the shortest shadows. This the goddess taints beforehand and fouls with monster-breeding drugs; on it she sprinkles the juices pressed from a noxious root, and thrice nine times, with her magic lips, she mutters over the dark charm of strange words. Scylla comes, and had gone in up to the waist, when she sees her loins made hideous with barking monsters; and, at first not believing they are parts of her own body, she flees and drives them off and dreads the rude mouths of the dogs — but those she flees she draws along with her; and, feeling for her thighs and legs and feet, she finds Cerberus-jaws in place of those parts. She stands on the rage of the dogs, and holds the backs of savage beasts beneath her cut-off loins and her jutting belly.

Glaucus, still in love, wept for her, and fled a marriage with Circe, who had used the power of herbs too cruelly. Scylla stayed in that place; and, when the chance was given, in her hatred of Circe she robbed Ulysses of his companions. Soon she would have sunk the Trojan ships too, had she not first been turned into a rock, which even now stands out with its crags — a rock the sailor avoids.

When the Trojan ships had passed her and greedy Charybdis with their oars, and were near the Ausonian shore, they were carried back by the winds to the Libyan coast. There the Sidonian Dido, doomed not to bear easily the loss of her Phrygian husband, received Aeneas into her home and her heart; and on a pyre built under the pretense of sacred rites she fell on the sword, and, herself deceived, deceived all. Fleeing again the new-built walls of the sandy land, and carried back to the seat of Eryx and faithful Acestes, he sacrifices and honors his father's tomb. He looses the ships that Juno's Iris had nearly burned, and passes the realm of the son of Hippotas, the lands smoking with hot sulphur, and leaves behind the rocks of the Sirens, daughters of Acheloüs; and, its pilot lost, the ship coasts Inarime and Prochyte and Pithecusae, set on a barren hill, named for its inhabitants. For the father of the gods, once loathing the frauds and perjuries of the Cercopes and the crimes of that cheating race, changed the men into ugly animals, so that the same beings could look both unlike and like men: he shrank their limbs, flattened and bent back their noses from the brow, furrowed their faces with an old man's wrinkles, and, their whole bodies clothed in yellow hair, sent them to these homes — and first he took away the use of words, and of the tongue made for dreadful perjury, and left them only the power to complain with a harsh screech.

When he had passed these and left the walls of Parthenope on the right, on the left he comes to the tomb of the tuneful son of Aeolus, and enters the shores of Cumae, a region of marsh-sedge, and the cave of the long-lived Sibyl, and begs that through Avernus he may visit his father's shade. She raised her face, long held on the ground, and at last, frenzied with the god within her, said: "You ask a great thing, hero greatest in deeds, whose hand was proved by the sword, whose devotion by the flames. Yet lay aside fear, Trojan: you will win your wish, and with me for guide you will know the halls of Elysium and the last realm of the world, and the dear image of your parent. To valor no way is closed." She spoke, and showed a branch gleaming with gold in the wood of Avernus's Juno, and bade him tear it from its stem. Aeneas obeyed, and saw the powers of dread Orcus, and his own ancestors, and the aged ghost of great-hearted Anchises; he learned, too, the laws of those regions, and what dangers he must face in new wars. Retracing his weary steps by the backward path, he eased his labor in talk with his Cumaean guide. And while he made the fearful journey through the dim twilight, he said: "Whether you are a goddess in person, or dearest to the gods, you will always be to me like a god, and I will confess I live by your gift, who willed that I visit the places of death and escape the death I saw. For these kindnesses, brought up to the airy world, I will build you a temple and give you the honors of incense." The seer looked at him and, drawing sighs, said: "I am no goddess, nor honor a human head with holy incense; and, that you may not err in ignorance — eternal light, and without end, was offered me, had my virginity yielded to Phoebus in love. While he hoped for it, while he wished to bribe me first with gifts, he said, 'Maiden of Cumae, choose what you wish: you will win your wish.' I, pointing to a heap of gathered dust, foolishly asked that as many birthdays might be mine as the dust held grains; it slipped me to ask as well for years still young. Yet he offered me these, and eternal youth, if I would yield to his love: scorning Phoebus's gift, I stay unwed. But now my happier years have turned their back, and sick old age comes on trembling feet, to be long endured. For now I have lived seven ages; still, to equal the number of the dust, three hundred harvests and three hundred vintages remain to be seen. The time will come when length of days will make me tiny from so large a frame, and my limbs, wasted by age, shrink to the least weight; I will seem never to have been loved or pleased a god; and Phoebus himself, perhaps, will not know me, or deny he loved me. So changed I will be, seen by none — yet known by my voice; the Fates will leave me my voice."

While the Sibyl told such things on the upward road, the Trojan Aeneas emerged from the Stygian realms into the Euboean city, and, the sacrifice duly made, came to the shore that did not yet bear his nurse's name. Here too, after long weariness of toil, Macareus of Neritos, a companion of much-tried Ulysses, had rested; and he recognized Achaemenides, once left among the crags of Etna, and, amazed to find him alive so unexpectedly, said, "What chance or god saves you, Achaemenides? Why does a foreign ship carry a Greek? What land does your bark seek?" To his questions Achaemenides — no longer shaggy in his dress, now his own man, his covering pinned with no thorns — replied: "May I see Polyphemus again, and those jaws dripping with human blood, if home and Ithaca are dearer to me than this ship, if I honor Aeneas less than my own father; and, though I did everything, I could never be grateful enough. That I speak and breathe and see the sky and the sun's light — can I be ungrateful and forget it? Through him this life of mine did not go into the Cyclops's mouth; and though even now I left the light of life, I would be buried in a tomb, or at least not in that belly. What were my feelings then — unless terror took all sense — when, left behind, I saw you making for the deep? I longed to shout, but feared to betray myself to the enemy: even Ulysses' shout nearly ruined your ship. I saw him, when he tore a huge rock from the mountain and hurled it into the middle of the waves; I saw him again throwing vast stones with his giant arm, as if driven by a siege-engine, and I dreaded, forgetting I was no longer aboard, that the wave or the wind might sink the ship. And when your flight had saved you from a sure death, he paced all Etna, groaning, groped the woods with his hands, and, robbed of his eye, stumbled on the rocks, and, stretching his gore-stained arms to the sea, cursed the Greek race, and said, 'Oh, if some chance would bring me back Ulysses, or one of his men, on whom my rage might feed — whose guts I might eat, whose living limbs I might tear with my hand, whose blood might flood my throat, whose crushed limbs might quiver under my teeth: how slight the loss of my stolen sight would be!' This, and more, in his fury. A ghastly horror seized me as I watched his face still wet with slaughter, his cruel hands, the empty ring where the eye had been, his limbs, his beard clotted with human blood. Death was before my eyes — yet that was the least of my woes; and I thought he would seize me now, now sink my guts in his; and there stuck in my mind the picture of that time when I saw two bodies of my companions dashed three and four times against the ground, while he, lying over them like a shaggy lion, stowed their guts and flesh and white-marrowed bones and half-living limbs in his greedy belly. A trembling took me; I stood pale and bloodless, watching him chew and belch his bloody feast and vomit lumps mixed with wine; and I fancied such a fate readied for wretched me. Hiding many days, trembling at every sound, both fearing death and longing to die, staving off hunger with acorns and grass mixed with leaves, alone, helpless, hopeless, given over to death, after a long time I saw this ship far off, and by signs I begged for rescue, and ran to the shore, and moved them: a Trojan ship took in a Greek! Now do you tell your adventures, dearest of comrades, and your leader's, and the crew's that trusted the sea with you."

Macareus tells how Aeolus, grandson of Hippotas, rules the Etruscan deep and keeps the winds in prison; how the Ithacan chief received them shut in an ox-hide, a wondrous gift, and for nine days sailed with a fair breeze and saw the land he sought; how, when the dawn after the ninth arose, his companions, beaten by envy and desire for booty, thinking it gold, cut the fastenings of the winds; how with these the ship went back through the very waves it had come, and returned to the harbor of the Aeolian king. "From there," he said, "we came to the ancient city of Lamus the Laestrygonian; Antiphates reigned in that land. I was sent to him, two others with me, and safety was barely won by flight for me and one companion; the third of us stained the Laestrygonian's wicked mouth with his own blood. Antiphates pressed us as we fled and roused his band; they gathered and hurled stones and beams, and sank men and sank ships; only one, that carried us and Ulysses himself, escaped. Grieving for the lost part of our companions, and lamenting much, we came to the land you see far off — far off, believe me, be that island I saw! And you, most righteous of the Trojans, son of a goddess — for, the war ended, you are not to be called an enemy, Aeneas — I warn you: flee the shores of Circe!

"We too, our ship moored on Circe's shore, remembering Antiphates and the savage Cyclops, refused to go and enter her unknown house; but we were chosen by lot to go: the lot sent me and faithful Polites, and Eurylochus, and Elpenor, too fond of wine, and twice nine companions to Circe's walls. As soon as we reached them and stood at the threshold, a thousand wolves, and bears and lionesses mixed with wolves, made us fear at the meeting — but none was to be feared, none would wound our bodies; rather, they wagged coaxing tails in the air and, fawning, followed our steps, until the maidservants received us and led us through marble halls to their mistress. She sits in a lovely alcove on her wonted throne, clad in a shining robe, and over it wrapped in a garment of gold. The Nereids and nymphs, who card no fleece with moving fingers nor draw out threads, arrange herbs and sort the scattered flowers into baskets, and the plants of varied hue; she herself sets the tasks they do, herself knows the use of each leaf, and what harmony there is in the mixture, and, attentive, weighs the herbs she has portioned. When she saw us, greetings given and received, she brightened her face and answered our wishes with good omens; and without delay she orders roasted barley grains mixed, and honey, and the strength of wine, and curds with pressed milk, and secretly adds drugs to lurk beneath the sweetness. We took the cups given by her sacred hand; and as soon as, thirsty, we drained them with parched mouths, and the cruel goddess touched the tops of our hair with her wand, I began — I am ashamed, but I will tell it — to bristle with hair, and could no longer speak, but gave a harsh growl for words, and fell face-down to the ground, and felt my mouth harden into a curved snout, my neck swell with muscle, and, with the part that had just now taken the cup, I made my footprints. With the rest who had suffered the same — so powerful are the drugs — I was shut in a sty; and we saw that Eurylochus alone kept his shape: he alone fled the offered cup. Had he not dodged it, I would be one of the bristle-clad beasts even now, nor would Ulysses, told of the disaster by him, have come as our avenger to Circe. The peace-bringing Cyllenian had given him a white flower — the gods call it moly, held by a black root; safe with it and with heaven's counsel, he entered Circe's house, and, called to the treacherous cups, he pushed back her hand as she tried to stroke his hair with the wand, and, drawing his sword, frightened her off. Then faith was pledged and hands given, and, received into her bed, he asked his companions' bodies as the marriage-gift. We are sprinkled with the kinder juice of a strange herb, struck on the head with the reversed wand, and words are said, the reverse of the words before; the more she chants, the more we rise up from the ground, the bristles fall, the cleft leaves our split feet, our shoulders return, our arms are set below the upper arms; weeping, we embrace our weeping chief, and cling to his neck, and speak no words before those that prove us grateful.

"A year's stay held us there, and in so long a time I saw much and heard much — this too among many, which one of the four maids appointed for such rites privately told me. For while Circe lingered apart with my chief, she showed me a statue of snow-white marble, bearing a woodpecker on its head, set in a sacred shrine and marked with many wreaths. When I asked and wished to know who he was, and why he was honored in the holy shrine, and why he bore that bird, she said: 'Hear, Macareus, and learn from this too what my mistress's power is; give your mind to my words.

"'Picus, the son of Saturn, was a king in Ausonia, keen on horses good for war. His form was such as you see; you may see his beauty here, and judge the true from the copied image. His spirit matched his beauty; and he had not yet, in his years, seen the Greek games at Elis held four times. He had turned to himself the Dryads born on the Latian hills; the fountain-powers wooed him, the Naiads that Albula and Numicus, that Anio's waters and the too-short Almo, and headlong Nar and shady Farfar bore, and those that haunt the woodland pool of Scythian Diana and the neighboring lakes; yet, slighting all, he loved one nymph, whom on the Palatine hill Venilia is said to have borne to two-faced Janus. When she first ripened to marriageable years, she was given to Laurentine Picus, preferred to all; rare indeed in beauty, but rarer in the art of singing, whence she was called Canens. She used to move woods and rocks with her voice, to tame wild beasts, to stay the long rivers, to hold the wandering birds.

"'While she sang her songs in a woman's voice, Picus had gone from his house into the Laurentine fields to spear the native boars, pressing the back of a spirited horse, carrying two javelins in his left hand, in a purple cloak clasped with yellow gold. The daughter of the Sun had come into the same wood; and, to pluck fresh herbs on the fruitful hills, she had left the Circaean fields named for her. As soon as, hidden in the thickets, she saw the youth, she was stunned; the herbs she had gathered fell from her hand, and a flame seemed to wander through all her marrow. When she gathered her mind from that strong heat, she was ready to confess her wish; but the speed of his horse and the ringing guards kept her from approaching. 'You will not escape me,' she said, 'though you be caught up by the wind, if I know myself, if all power in herbs has not vanished, and my charms do not fail me.' She spoke, and made a phantom of a false boar, with no body, and bade it run past the king's eyes and seem to go into a dense-timbered wood, where the forest is thickest and no horse can enter. Without delay Picus, unknowing, follows the shadow of the prey, and, quick, leaves the steaming back of his steed and, chasing a vain hope, wanders on foot in the deep wood. She frames prayers and says magic words, and worships strange gods with a strange charm, by which she is wont to blot the face of the snow-white Moon and draw thirsty clouds over her father's head. Then too, at the chanting, the sky thickens and the ground breathes fog, and his companions wander on the blind paths, and the king's guard is gone. Finding place and time, she said, 'By your eyes, which have caught mine, and by this beautiful form, most handsome one, which makes me, a goddess, your suppliant, favor my love, and take the all-seeing Sun for your father-in-law, and do not, in your cruelty, scorn Circe, daughter of Titan.' She spoke; he fiercely rejects her and her prayers, and says, 'Whoever you are, I am not yours; another holds me captive, and long, I pray, may she hold me, nor will I harm the marriage bond with a stranger's love, while the Fates keep Canens, daughter of Janus, for me.' The daughter of Titan, having tried her prayers in vain, said, 'You will not do it unpunished, nor go back to Canens; and by the event you will learn what one slighted, what one in love, what a woman can do — and Circe is in love, and slighted, and a woman!' Then twice she turned to the west, twice to the east, three times touched the youth with her wand, spoke three charms. He fled — and, marveling that he ran faster than usual, saw wings on his body; and, angry that he was added, a new bird, to the Latian woods, he strikes the hard oaks with his hard beak and, enraged, wounds the long branches; his wings took the purple of his cloak; the gold that had clasped his garment became feathers, and his neck was ringed with tawny gold, and nothing of the old Picus remained but the name.

"'Meanwhile his companions, having called on Picus in vain through the fields and found him nowhere, meet Circe — for now she had thinned the air and let the clouds be scattered by wind and sun — and charge her with true crimes, and demand back their king, and threaten force, and make to attack her with cruel weapons. She scatters harmful venom and poisonous juices, and summons Night and the gods of Night from Erebus and Chaos, and calls on Hecate with long howls. Wondrous to tell, the woods leaped from their place, the ground groaned, the nearby tree paled, the grass grew wet with sprinkled drops of blood, the stones seemed to give hoarse lowings, the dogs to bark, the earth to crawl with black snakes, and the thin ghosts of the silent dead to flit. The crowd, stunned by the marvels, is afraid; she touched their frightened, wondering faces with her poisoned wand, and at its touch the monstrous shapes of various beasts came upon the young men: none kept his own form.

"'The setting sun had now come down on the Tartessian shores, and in vain, with eyes and heart, Canens awaited her husband; her servants and people run through all the woods and carry lights to meet him. Nor is it enough for the nymph to weep and tear her hair and beat her breast — though she does all this — she rushes out and, wild, wanders the Latian fields. Six nights, and as many returning lights of the sun, saw her, without sleep or food, going over ridges and valleys wherever chance led. Last of all Tiber saw her, worn out with grief and wandering, laying her body on his long bank; there, weeping, she poured out words tuned to her very grief in a thin voice, as the dying swan sings its funeral song. At last, melting to her thin marrow with grief, she pined away and by degrees vanished into thin air; yet fame marked the spot, which the ancient Muses rightly called Canens, after the nymph's name.'

"Many such things were told me and seen by me in that long year. Idle and slow from disuse, we were bidden again to take to the sea, again to set sail; the daughter of Titan had said that doubtful ways, and a vast voyage, and the perils of the cruel sea awaited us. I was afraid, I confess, and, reaching this shore, here I stayed."

Macareus had finished; and the nurse of Aeneas, buried in a marble urn, had this short verse on her tomb:

Here Caieta was burned by her foster-child of proven love, snatched from the Argive fire, with the fire that was her due.

The moored cable is loosed from the grassy bank, and they leave far behind the wiles and the halls of the ill-famed goddess, and seek the groves where the Tiber, dark with the shade of trees, breaks into the sea with its yellow sands. Aeneas gains the house and the daughter of Latinus, son of Faunus — but not without war. War is waged with a fierce nation, and Turnus rages for his betrothed wife. All Etruria meets Latium in battle, and long is victory sought with anxious arms. Each side swells its strength with foreign force, and many defend the Rutulians, many the Trojan camp; nor had Aeneas come in vain to the walls of Evander, but Venulus came in vain to the city of the exiled Diomedes. He had founded a great city under Iapygian Daunus, and held the lands given in dower; but when Venulus had done Turnus's bidding and asked for aid, the Aetolian hero pleaded his want of resources: he would not commit his father-in-law's people to war, nor had he men of his own nation to arm. "And, that you may not think this a pretext — though my grief be renewed at the bitter memory — I will bear to tell it. After lofty Ilium burned, and Pergamum had fed the Greek flames, and the Narycian hero, for a maiden ravished, brought on all the vengeance he alone deserved for the maiden, we were scattered and driven by winds over hostile seas; we Greeks endured lightning, darkness, rain, and the wrath of sky and sea, and Caphereus, the crown of our disaster. Not to hold you with these sad events in order — Greece then might have seemed worthy even of Priam's tear. Yet the care of armed Minerva saved me, snatched from the waves; but again I was driven from my fathers' fields, and kindly Venus exacted, in revenge, the penalty for her old wound, and I bore such toils on the deep sea, such amid arms on land, that often I called those happy whom the common storm and grim Caphereus swallowed in the threatening waves — and I wished I had been one of them. My companions, having endured the utmost in war and on the sea, lost heart and asked an end of their wandering; but Acmon, hot of temper and now made bitter by disaster, said, 'What is left, men, that your patience should refuse to bear? What has Cytherea, suppose she wished, that she can do beyond this? For while worse is feared, there is room for prayers; but where one's lot is the worst, fear is trodden underfoot, and the height of misfortune is free of care. Let her hear it herself, if she likes; let her hate, as she does, all the men under Diomedes; still we all scorn her hatred — our great power is bought at no great price.' With such words the Pleuronian Acmon provokes Venus against her will and revives her old anger. His words please few; the greater part of us, his friends, rebuke Acmon; and as he made to answer, his voice and its passage grew thin, his hair turned to feathers, his new-formed neck and breast and back were covered with down, his arms took longer plumes, his elbows curved into light wings; a great part of his foot took toes, his mouth stiffened and hardened with horn and ended in a point. Lycus and Idas, and Nycteus with Rhexenor, and Abas marveled at him; and while they marveled, they took the same shape, and the greater part of the company flew up and wheeled around the oars with beating wings. If you ask the form of these sudden birds — not swans', but nearest to white swans'. With difficulty I, son-in-law of Iapygian Daunus, hold these homes and the parched fields, with a very small remnant of my men."

So far the grandson of Oeneus. Venulus leaves the Calydonian realm, and the Peucetian bays, and the Messapian fields, in which he sees a cave that, shaded by a dense grove and trickling with a smooth stream, half-goat Pan now holds, but once the nymphs held. An Apulian shepherd scared them from that spot and first moved them with a sudden fright; then, when their minds returned and they scorned him as he followed, they danced in time, moving their feet; the shepherd mocked them, and, aping them with rustic leaps, added coarse country abuse in filthy words, nor was his mouth silent until a tree closed his throat — for he is a tree, and by its sap you may know his manners: the wild olive, by its bitter berries, marks the vileness of his tongue; the roughness of his words passed into them.
