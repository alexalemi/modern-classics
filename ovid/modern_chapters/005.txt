Book III

(Part 2 of 2)

So he had toyed with her, and with other nymphs born of the water or the mountains, and before them with whole crowds of admiring young men. At last someone he had scorned lifted his hands to the sky and prayed: "Then let him love the same way — and never have the thing he loves!" It was a fair prayer, and Nemesis, the goddess who punishes such pride from her shrine at Rhamnus, granted it.

There was a spring, clear and unclouded, its waters bright as silver, that no shepherd had ever muddied, no she-goat grazing down from the hills, no cattle of any kind. No bird had troubled it, no wild beast, no branch fallen from a tree. Grass grew all around it, fed by the nearby water, and a ring of trees that let no sun in to warm the place. Here the boy came, worn out by the heat and the work of hunting, drawn by the beauty of the spot and its cool spring. He lay down, and while he bent to quench his thirst, another thirst began to grow in him. As he drank, he was captivated by the reflection of the face he saw in the water, and fell in love with a hope that had no body — mistaking for a real thing what was only a shadow.

He stared at himself, stunned, and hung there motionless with the same fixed expression, like a statue carved from Parian marble. Stretched on the ground, he gazed at the twin stars that were his own eyes, at hair worthy of Bacchus, worthy of Apollo, at his smooth boyish cheeks, his ivory neck, the loveliness of his mouth, the rosy flush blended into snow-white skin. Everything he admired was exactly what made him admirable. Without understanding, he wanted himself. The one who praised was the thing being praised; as he chased, he was chased; he lit the fire and he burned in it. How many times did he lean down to give useless kisses to that lying spring! How many times did he plunge his arms into the middle of the water to catch the neck he saw, and never catch himself! He did not know what he was looking at, but what he looked at set him alight; the same illusion that fooled his eyes drove them wild.

Poor gullible boy, why do you keep grabbing at a fleeting image? What you want is nowhere. What you love — only turn away, and you will lose it. The thing you see is the shadow of a reflection; it has nothing of its own. It comes with you and stays with you; it will leave with you — if only you could leave.

No thought of food could pull him away, no thought of rest. Sprawled in the shaded grass, he stared with eyes that never had enough at the lie of that beautiful shape, and through his own eyes he was destroyed. Raising himself a little, he stretched his arms toward the surrounding woods and cried, "Oh, you woods — has anyone ever loved more cruelly than this? You would know; you have been a good hiding place for so many lovers. In all the long ages you have lived, do you remember anyone who wasted away like this? He delights me, and I see him — but the thing I see and love, I still cannot reach. That's how completely a lover can be deceived. And this makes my grief worse: no vast sea keeps us apart, no long road, no mountains, no city walls with the gates shut. Only a little water stands between us. He wants to be held himself — for every time I stretch down my lips to the clear pool, every time he strains up toward me, face lifted. You'd think I could touch him. It is the smallest thing that keeps lovers apart.

"Whoever you are, come out to me! Why do you fool me, one boy in all the world? Where do you go when I reach for you? It can't be my looks or my age that drive you off — nymphs have loved me too. That friendly face of yours promises me some kind of hope. When I reach my arms out to you, you reach yours back. When I smile, you smile. And often I have watched your tears while I was weeping. You answer my nods with nods, and — as far as I can guess from the movement of your lovely mouth — you send back words that never reach my ears. That boy is me! I understand it now; my own reflection does not fool me anymore. I am on fire with love for myself. I light the flames and I suffer them. What do I do? Ask, or be asked? And then, ask for what? What I want is already mine. My riches have made me a beggar. Oh, if only I could step out of my own body! It's a strange wish for a lover, but I want the thing I love to be gone from me. And now grief is stealing my strength; not much of my life is left. I am dying young. But death is no hardship — it will lay my sorrows down. I only wish the one I love could live longer. As it is, the two of us, one soul, will die together in a single life."

He spoke, and, out of his mind, went back to the same face, and blurred the water with his tears until the shape rippled and dimmed. Seeing it start to vanish, he cried out, "Where are you going? Stay! Don't abandon me, cruel one — don't leave the one who loves you! Let me at least look at what I'm not allowed to touch, and feed my wretched madness that way." Grieving, he tore his tunic down from the top and beat his bare chest with hands white as marble. The struck chest took on a soft red glow — the way apples do, pale on one side, red on the other, or the way grapes in their dappled clusters, still unripe, begin to blush with purple. As soon as he saw this in the water, now grown clear again, he could bear no more. As yellow wax melts before a small flame, as morning frost melts under a warming sun, so he wasted away, thinned by love, slowly consumed by the fire hidden inside him. The white blended with red was gone; gone was the strength, the vigor, all the things that had been so lovely to see a moment ago. Nothing was left of the body that Echo had once loved.

And when she saw it — still angry, still remembering how he had used her — she grieved anyway. Every time the pitiful boy said "Alas," she gave back "Alas" in her echoing voice; and when he struck his arms with his hands, she gave back the same sound of the blow. His last words, as he looked into the familiar pool, were these: "Oh boy I loved in vain!" — and the place gave back just as many words. When he said "Farewell," Echo said "Farewell" too. He let his weary head sink into the green grass, and death closed the eyes that still marveled at their master's beauty. Even then, taken down into the world below, he kept gazing at himself in the waters of the Styx. His sisters the Naiads wept for him and cut off their hair to lay upon their brother; the Dryads wept too, and Echo echoed their grieving back. They were already making ready the pyre, the shaken torches, the bier — but the body was nowhere to be found. In its place they found a flower, saffron at its heart, with white petals ringed around it.

When the story got out, it brought the prophet the fame he deserved through the cities of Greece, and the name of Tiresias the seer stood high. Yet one man alone despised him — Pentheus, son of Echion, a scorner of the gods. He laughed at the old man's warnings and threw his blindness in his face, the ruin of his lost sight. Tiresias only shook his temples, white with age, and said: "How lucky you would be if you too were robbed of this light, and could not watch the rites of Bacchus. For the day is coming — and I foretell it is not far off — when a new god will arrive here, Liber, the son of Semele. Unless you honor him with a temple, you will be torn to a thousand pieces and scattered, and with your blood you will defile the woods, your own mother, and your mother's sisters. It will happen. You will refuse the god his honor, and then you'll be sorry that in this darkness I saw all too clearly." As he said it, Echion's son shoved him away — but truth came behind the words, and the seer's prophecy was carried out.

Liber arrived, and the fields roared with festive shrieking. The people rushed out — mothers, brides, and men all mixed together, common folk and nobles alike, swept along to rites they had never known. "What madness," Pentheus shouted, "has stunned your minds, you sons of the Dragon, you children of Mars? Can bronze clashed against bronze do so much? A pipe with its curved horn, and cheap magic tricks? Are you men whom no war-sword could frighten, no trumpet, no ranks with drawn weapons — and now women's voices, and drunken raving, and packs of degenerates, and empty tambourines defeat you? Should I be amazed at you old men, who sailed across the wide seas and set up a new Tyre here, your household gods carried into exile — and now let them be taken without a fight? Or at you, the younger and stronger, closer to my own age — you should be gripping weapons, not fennel-wands, your heads covered by helmets, not leaves! Remember, I beg you, the stock you sprang from, and take on the spirit of that serpent who, one against many, destroyed so many. He died for his spring and his pool — but you, conquer for your own good name! He put brave men to death; you can drive off these soft ones and hold on to the honor of your fathers. If fate forbids Thebes to stand for long, I'd rather it were war engines and soldiers tearing down her walls, iron and fire roaring. Then we'd be wretched without disgrace; our fate would be worth grieving, but nothing to hide, and our tears would carry no shame. But now Thebes will fall to an unarmed boy, one who cares nothing for battles or weapons or horses, but only for hair soaked in myrrh, soft garlands, purple robes, and gold woven into embroidered cloth. I'll force him soon enough — just stand aside — to confess his father is a lie and his rites a fraud. Acrisius had spirit enough to scorn this hollow god and shut the gates of Argos in his face; and shall this foreigner terrify Pentheus and all of Thebes? Go quickly" — this to his servants — "go, and drag their leader here in chains. No sluggish delay in carrying out my orders!"

His grandfather Cadmus, and Athamas, and the rest of his household rebuked him and tried in vain to hold him back. Their warnings only made him fiercer. Curbed, his fury was goaded and grew, and the very restraint did harm. I have watched a torrent do the same — where nothing blocked its path, it ran gently, with a modest murmur; but wherever fallen logs and rocks stood in its way, it went foaming and boiling, wilder for the barrier.

Then — look — the servants came back covered in blood, and when their master demanded where Bacchus was, they said they had not seen him. "But we captured this one," they said, "his companion, a servant of the rites" — and they handed over a man, his hands bound behind his back, someone of Etruscan stock who had followed the god's worship. Pentheus fixed him with eyes that anger had made terrible, and though he could barely put off the moment of punishment, he said: "You there — soon to die, and by your death to be a lesson to the rest — tell me your name, your parents' names, your country, and why you take part in these newfangled rites." Without fear, the man answered: "My name is Acoetes; my homeland is Maeonia, my parents were common folk. My father left me no fields for hardy oxen to plow, no woolly flocks, no herds. He himself was poor, and made his living with line and hooks and rod, tricking the leaping fish and reeling them in. His skill was his whole fortune. When he handed the trade down to me, he said, 'Take what I have, heir and successor to my craft,' and dying, he left me nothing but the waters — the one thing I can call my inheritance from him. Soon, so I wouldn't be stuck forever among the same rocks, I learned to steer a ship, my right hand guiding the keel. I marked with my eyes the rainy star of the Olenian she-goat, and Taygete, and the Hyades, and the Bear, and the homes of the winds, and the harbors fit for ships.

"Once, making for Delos, I happened to touch the coast of the island of Chios. I brought us to shore with a pull of the right-hand oars, sprang lightly out, and landed on the wet sand. We spent the night there. As soon as dawn began to redden, I got up and told my men to bring fresh water, pointing out the path that led to a stream. Then I climbed a high hill myself to see what the breeze promised, called my crew, and headed back to the ship. 'Here we are!' said Opheltes, first of my mates, and — thinking he'd found a prize in that lonely field — he led along the shore a boy with the beauty of a girl. The boy seemed heavy with wine and sleep, staggering, barely able to keep up. I looked at his clothes, his face, his walk — I could see nothing about him that seemed mortal. I sensed it, and told my crew: 'What god is in that body I can't say — but there is a god in that body! Whoever you are, be kind to us, help our labors, and forgive these men.' 'Stop praying for us!' said Dictys — no one quicker at scrambling up to the top yardarms or sliding back down a rope. Libys agreed, and blond Melanthus, watchman of the prow, and Alcimedon, and Epopeus, who called out the rhythm to the oars and cheered their spirits — all of them agreed. So blind is the greed for plunder.

"'I will not let this ship be defiled by carrying off something sacred,' I said. 'Here I have the greatest authority.' And I blocked the gangway. Lycabas, the boldest of the whole crew, went into a rage. He had been driven out of an Etruscan town, paying for a brutal murder with exile. As I held my ground, he punched me in the throat with his young fist and would have thrown me overboard into the sea, if I hadn't caught a rope and clung to it, dazed as I was. The godless crew cheered him on. And then Bacchus — for it was Bacchus — as if the shouting had shaken off his sleep and the wine were draining from his mind and his wits returning, said, 'What are you doing? What's this shouting? Tell me, sailors, how did I get here? Where do you mean to take me?' 'Have no fear,' said Proreus. 'Just name the harbor you want to reach. We'll set you down on whatever land you ask.' 'Steer your course for Naxos,' said Liber. 'That is my home; it will be a friendly shore to you.'

"By the sea and by all the gods those liars swore it would be so, and told me to give sail to the painted ship. Naxos lay to our right. As I set the canvas for the right, each man in his own way hissed at me: 'What are you doing, you fool? What madness has hold of you, Acoetes? Steer left!' Most signaled their meaning with a nod; some whispered it in my ear. I was stunned. 'Let someone else take the helm,' I said, and pulled myself out of their crime and my own craft. The whole crew reviled me, muttering. One of them, Aethalion, said, 'As if all our safety depended on you alone!' — and he took over my job himself, and, leaving Naxos behind, steered the other way. Then the god, toying with them, as though only now he'd caught on to the trick, gazed out at the sea from the curved stern and, as if weeping, said: 'These are not the shores you promised me, sailors. This is not the land I asked for. What have I done to deserve this? What glory is it for you — grown men — to cheat a boy? For so many to deceive one?' I had been weeping already. The impious gang laughed at my tears and drove the sea faster with hurrying oars.

"Now I swear to you by the god himself — for no god is nearer than he is — that what I'm telling you is as true as it is beyond belief. The ship stood still on the water, as if it were held fast in a dry dock. Bewildered, the men kept working the oars, unfurled the sails, and tried to run with both together. Ivy tangled the oars, twisting up them in curling coils and spreading over the sails in heavy clusters of berries. And the god himself, his brow wreathed in bunches of grapes, brandished a lance draped in vine-leaves. Around him lay tigers, and the phantom shapes of lynxes, and the fierce spotted bodies of panthers.

"The men leaped overboard — whether from madness or from terror — and first Medon began to blacken all over, his body flattening, his spine bending into a curve. 'What monstrous thing are you turning into?' Lycabas said to him — and as he spoke, his own mouth gaped wide, his nose hooked, and his hardening skin drew scales across it. Libys, trying to pull back the resisting oars, saw his hands shrink into a small space — no longer hands, more like fins now. Another, reaching his arms toward the twisted ropes, found he had no arms, and, arching his limbless body, plunged into the waves; the tip of his tail was hooked, curved like the horns of a half moon. They flung themselves everywhere, spattering the ship with spray, surfacing and diving back under, playing like a dancing troupe, tossing their frolicking bodies and blowing the seawater out through their wide nostrils. Of the twenty who had been aboard just before — for the ship carried that many — I was the only one left. As I shook, frozen with fear, barely master of myself, the god steadied me. 'Cast the fear from your heart,' he said, 'and steer for Dia.' Landing there, I joined his worship, and I keep the rites of Bacchus to this day."

"We've lent our ears to a long rambling story," said Pentheus, "so that time might wear down our anger. Servants — drag this man off headlong, rack his body with hideous tortures, and send it down to the night of the Styx!" At once the Etruscan Acoetes was hauled away and locked in a solid cell. But while the cruel instruments of the death he'd ordered were being prepared — the iron, the fire — the story goes that the doors swung open by themselves and the chains slipped from his arms by themselves, with no one loosening them.

Echion's son held firm. Now he did not send others; he went himself, to where Mount Cithaeron, chosen for the sacred rites, rang with singing and the shrill cries of the Bacchantes. As a spirited warhorse whinnies when the trumpeter signals battle on the ringing bronze and it seizes on the lust for the fight, so the air, struck by those long wailing cries, stirred Pentheus, and hearing the clamor his rage flared white-hot again. About halfway up the mountain, ringed at its edges by woods, there was a clearing, free of trees, open to view on every side. Here, as he watched the sacred rites with unhallowed eyes, the first to see him was his own mother; the first to charge at him in a frenzy; the first to wound her son Pentheus, hurling her wand. "Come, my two sisters!" she cried. "That boar, the huge one roaming our fields — that boar is mine to strike!" The whole raving crowd rushed at him alone; they closed in together and chased him, trembling now, now speaking with less fury, now blaming himself, now confessing his sin.

Wounded, he still managed to say, "Help me, my aunt Autonoe! Let the ghost of Actaeon move your heart!" But she did not know who Actaeon was; she tore off his right hand as he pleaded, and his other arm was ripped away by Ino. The wretched man had no arms left to stretch toward his mother; showing her the raw wounds where his limbs had been torn away, he said, "Look at this, Mother!" At the sight, Agave howled, threw back her neck, tossed her hair through the air, and, gripping his severed head in her bloody fingers, cried out, "Look, my companions — this victory is our doing!" No faster does the wind strip from a tall tree the leaves touched by autumn's cold, barely still clinging, than the man's limbs were torn apart by those unspeakable hands. Warned by such an example, the women of Thebes flocked to the new rites, burned their incense, and worshiped at the holy altars.
