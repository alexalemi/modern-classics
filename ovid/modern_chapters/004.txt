Book III

(Part 1 of 2)

And now the god had set aside the shape of the lying bull, confessed himself for who he was, and reached the fields of Crete. But her father knew nothing of any of this. He ordered Cadmus to go and search for his stolen sister, and — loving and cruel in the very same command — added exile as the penalty if he failed to find her. So Cadmus wandered the whole world over. For who could ever track down the thefts of Jupiter? A fugitive from his own country and his father's anger, the son of Agenor went as a suppliant to the oracle of Apollo and asked what land he was meant to live in.

"A heifer will meet you," Apollo said, "out in the lonely fields — a cow that has never worn a yoke, never known the curved plow. Follow where she leads. Where she lies down in the grass, there build your city walls, and call that country Boeotia."

Cadmus had barely come down from the Castalian cave at Delphi when he saw a heifer, unguarded, moving slowly along with no mark of servitude on her neck. He fell in behind her and tracked her footsteps at a measured pace, silently thanking Apollo for showing him the road. He had already crossed the fords of the Cephisus and the fields of Panope when the cow stopped, lifted toward the sky a forehead crowned with tall handsome horns, and set the air ringing with her lowing. Then she looked back at his men following behind, sank down, and laid her flank in the soft grass. Cadmus gave thanks. He pressed his lips to the foreign ground and greeted the unfamiliar mountains and fields as his own.

He meant to make a sacrifice to Jupiter. He sent his servants to go and fetch running spring water for the offering. An ancient forest stood nearby, never once violated by an axe. In the middle of it was a cave thick with brush and twisted branches, its low arch built up out of joined stones, rich with a plentiful spring. Hidden away in that cave lay a serpent sacred to Mars, splendid with a golden crest. Its eyes flashed with fire; its whole body was swollen with venom; three tongues flickered in its mouth, and its teeth stood in a triple row.

When the men who had set out from the Tyrian people reached that grove with their ill-fated steps, and the urn plunged into the water gave its splash, the sea-blue serpent lifted its head from deep in the cave and hissed a horrifying hiss. The urns dropped from their hands. The blood drained out of their bodies, and a sudden shudder seized their stunned limbs. The serpent twisted its scaly coils into rolling spirals, sprang, and bent itself into enormous arcs. Rearing up more than half its length into the light air, it looked down on the whole forest — a thing as huge, if you could see it all at once, as the Dragon in the sky that separates the two Bears. There was no delay. Whether the Phoenicians were reaching for their weapons or for flight, or whether sheer terror froze them from doing either, it was on them: some it killed with its bite, some crushed in its long coils, some it poisoned with the deadly breath of its venom.

The sun stood at its peak now and shrank the shadows to almost nothing. The son of Agenor began to wonder what was keeping his men, and went to look for them. His covering was a lion's hide stripped from the beast; his weapon a lance of shining steel, and a javelin — and a courage greater than any weapon. When he entered the grove and saw the lifeless bodies, and above them the victorious enemy, immense, licking the horrible wounds with its bloody tongue, he cried, "Either I will avenge your deaths, my faithful friends, or I will share them." With that he lifted a boulder in his right hand and hurled the vast weight with a tremendous heave. Walls and high towers would have shaken at that blow, but the serpent took it without a wound. Its scales shielded it like a coat of mail, and the toughness of its black hide turned the mighty stroke aside. But that same toughness did not beat the javelin. It stuck fast in the middle of the pliant spine and drove its whole iron point down into the guts. Wild with pain, the serpent wrenched its head back over its own body, looked at its wound, bit at the shaft lodged there, and worked it loose on every side with all its strength — barely tearing it free at last, though the iron stayed buried in the bone.

Then, when this fresh cause was added to its usual rage, the throat swelled with gorged veins, white foam frothed around its pestilent jaws, the ground scraped by its scales rasped and hissed, and the black breath pouring from its Stygian mouth fouled the tainted air. Now it wound itself into vast circling coils; now it stood up straighter than a long beam; now, driven forward with enormous force like a river swollen with rain, it swept along and battered down the trees in its path with its chest. The son of Agenor gave a little ground, taking the charge on his lion's hide, holding off the lunging mouth with his spear-point thrust out before him. The serpent raged and drove its teeth uselessly against the hard steel, biting at the point. Now blood began to run from its venomous palate and stained the green grass with its spray — but the wound was slight, because the serpent kept pulling back from the blow, drawing its hurt neck away, and by retreating kept the stroke from sinking and biting deep. At last the son of Agenor drove the iron into its throat and kept coming, following it, pressing after it, until an oak stood in the serpent's way as it backed off and its neck was pinned through, nailed to the wood. The tree bent under the serpent's weight and groaned as the tip of that lashing tail whipped against its trunk.

While the victor stood measuring the size of his beaten enemy, a voice was suddenly heard. Where it came from was impossible to say — but heard it was. "Why, son of Agenor, do you stand staring at the serpent you have killed? You too will be gazed upon as a serpent." For a long time he stood there terrified, his color and his wits drained away together, his hair stiff with a cold horror. Then, look — Pallas, the hero's champion, came gliding down through the upper air and stood beside him. She told him to plow the ground and sow the serpent's teeth in it, the seeds of a people yet to come. He obeyed. He opened a furrow with the pressing plow and scattered the teeth as ordered across the earth — mortal seed. Then, past all belief, the clods began to stir. First the point of a spear rose out of the furrows; next helmets, their painted crests nodding; then shoulders, chests, arms already loaded with weapons came up; a whole crop of shielded men sprang into being. It was like a theater on a festival day, when the curtain is drawn up and the painted figures rise into view, showing their faces first, then the rest little by little, drawn up in one smooth motion until they stand there entire, feet planted on the lower edge of the stage.

Frightened by these new enemies, Cadmus reached for his weapons. "Don't!" shouted one of the men the earth had made. "Keep out of our civil war!" And with that he struck one of his earth-born brothers with a stroke of his sword, hand to hand — then fell himself to a javelin thrown from a distance. The one who had killed him lived no longer than his victim, and breathed out the very air he had so lately breathed in. The whole crowd went mad in the same way, and these brothers, sprung up together only moments before, fell in battle against each other, wound trading for wound. So the young men, whose share of life had been so brief, beat their bloodied mother earth with dying chests — until only five were left. One of them was Echion. At the warning of Minerva he threw his weapons down and both offered and received a pledge of brotherly peace. These were the companions the wanderer from Sidon had when he built the city Apollo's oracle had commanded.

And now Thebes stood. Now, Cadmus, you might have seemed happy even in your exile. Mars and Venus themselves were your father-in-law and mother-in-law; add to that children by so glorious a wife, so many sons and daughters, and grandchildren too, those dear pledges of love, already grown to young men and women. But of course — a man's last day must always be waited for, and no one should be called happy before his death and his final funeral rites.

Among so much good fortune, Cadmus, it was your grandson who first became your cause of grief — the stranger's horns set on his forehead, and you, his hounds, glutted on your own master's blood. And yet, if you look at it honestly, you will find in him the fault of bad luck, not of crime. For what crime is there in an honest mistake?

There was a mountain stained with the killing of every kind of wild beast. The day had shortened the shadows to their noon length, and the sun stood equally far from both ends of its course, when the young Boeotian called out in a gentle voice to the companions who shared his work as they roamed the pathless wilds: "Our nets are wet, my friends, and our spears too, with the blood of the beasts; the day has given us sport enough. When Dawn returns tomorrow, riding her saffron chariot to bring back the light, we'll take up our task again. Right now the sun stands halfway between east and west and splits the fields open with its heat. Break off the work you're at, and gather up the knotted nets." The men did as he asked and set their labor aside.

There was a valley dense with pine and sharp-pointed cypress, called Gargaphie, sacred to Diana, who tucks up her robe to hunt. Deep in its farthest recess was a woodland grotto shaped by no art at all — nature, with her own genius, had counterfeited art. She had drawn a natural arch out of the living pumice and the light tufa stone. A spring ran murmuring on the right, clear and shallow, its wide pool fringed with a border of grass. Here the goddess of the woods, when tired with the hunt, used to bathe her virgin limbs in the clear water.

She came to it now. She handed her javelin, her quiver, and her unstrung bow to one of the nymphs, her armor-bearer; another slipped her arms under the robe as it came off; two more unlaced the sandals from her feet. Crocale, daughter of Ismenus, more skilled than the rest, gathered up the goddess's hair, scattered loose over her neck, and bound it into a knot — though her own hair hung free. Nephele, Hyale, Rhanis, Psecas, and Phyale scooped up water and poured it from their broad urns. And while Diana was there, washing in the water where she always washed, look — the grandson of Cadmus, his day's hunting cut short, came wandering with uncertain steps through unfamiliar woods and into that grove. So his fate led him.

The moment he entered the cave dripping with spring water, the nymphs, naked, seeing a man, beat their breasts and filled the whole forest with sudden shrieks, and crowded around Diana to shield her with their own bodies. But the goddess stood taller than they were, head and shoulders above them all. The color that fills the clouds when the sun strikes them from across the sky, or the color of rosy Dawn, rose in the face of Diana caught without her robes. Hemmed in as she was by the packed ring of her attendants, she still turned herself sideways and twisted her face back — and how she wished she had her arrows within reach! What she did have within reach was the water, and she scooped it up and flung it into the man's face. Drenching his hair with that avenging stream, she added these words, the forewarning of the doom to come: "Now you may tell how you saw me undressed — if you can tell it at all!" No further threat. She set on his dripping head the antlers of a full-grown stag, drew his neck out long, tapered the tips of his ears to points, changed his hands into feet and his arms into long forelegs, and wrapped his body in a dappled hide. And she added fear. The son of Autonoe fled — and marveled, even as he ran, at how fast he was. But when he saw his face and the antlers in the water, he tried to say, "Oh, this is misery" — and no voice came. He groaned; the groan was all his voice now, and tears ran down a face that was no longer his. Only his old mind stayed behind.

What was he to do? Go home, back to the royal halls? Or hide in the woods? Shame blocked the one, terror the other. While he wavered, the hounds caught sight of him. Melampus and the keen-nosed Ichnobates were the first to bay the signal — Ichnobates of Knossos, Melampus of Spartan stock. Then the rest came rushing, faster than the racing wind: Pamphagus, Dorceus, and Oribasus, all bred in Arcadia; powerful Nebrophonus, and savage Theron running with Laelaps; Pterelas, best of them for speed, and Agre, best of them for scent; fierce Hylaeus, gored by a wild boar not long ago; Nape, sired by a wolf; Poemenis, who used to herd the flocks; Harpyia with her two pups at her side; Ladon of Sicyon, lean in the flank; Dromas and Canache and Sticte and Tigris and Alce; Leucon with his snow-white coat and Asbolus with his black one; brawny Lacon and Aello, strong at the run; Thous, and swift Lycisca beside her Cyprian brother; Harpalus, his black face marked with a white blaze down the middle; Melaneus, and Lachne with her shaggy body; Labros and Agriodus, sons of a Cretan father and a Spartan mother; and shrill-voiced Hylactor — and more it would take too long to name. The whole pack, wild for the kill, poured after him over rocks and cliffs and crags no path could reach, wherever the way was hard and wherever there was no way at all.

He ran along the very trails he had so often hunted down — running, God help him, from his own servants. He longed to cry out, "I am Actaeon! Recognize your own master!" but the words would not come. The air rang with barking. Melanchaetes was the first to tear a wound in his back, Blackfoot the next; Oresitrophus sank into his shoulder. They had set out late, but a shortcut over the hill had brought them up in time. While those three held their master down, the rest of the pack closed in and buried their teeth in his body. Soon there was no room left for more wounds. He groaned and made a sound that was not a man's, though no stag could make it either, and filled the ridges he knew so well with his anguished cries. Down on his knees, like a suppliant begging for mercy, he turned his silent face this way and that, as if it were his pleading arms. His companions, knowing nothing, egged the ravening pack on with their usual shouts, hunting the grove with their eyes for Actaeon and calling out "Actaeon!" over and over, as though he were somewhere else — he lifted his head at the name — complaining that he wasn't there, that the lazy man was missing the fine spectacle their prey was giving them. He wished with all his heart he were somewhere else, but he was here. He wished he could watch, and not feel, the savage work of his own dogs. They stood around him on every side, sank their muzzles into his flesh, and tore their master to pieces under the false shape of a stag. Not until his life had run out through wound after wound, they say, was the rage of the quiver-bearing goddess satisfied.

People took it different ways. To some, the goddess seemed harsher than she had any right to be; others praised her and called the punishment worthy of her strict virginity. Both sides found their reasons. Only Jupiter's wife did not so much say whether she condemned it or approved as take open joy in the ruin of the house sprung from Agenor — carrying over onto the whole line the hatred she had first conceived for her Tyrian rival, Europa. And now, look, a fresh grievance was heaped on the old one: she was galled that Semele was pregnant with the seed of great Jupiter. She loosed her tongue to rage — then checked herself. "And what," she said, "have I ever gained by all this railing? It's the woman herself I have to go after. Her I will destroy — if I am rightly called almighty Juno, if it becomes me to hold the jeweled scepter in my hand, if I am the queen, and the sister and wife of Jupiter — well, the sister, at least, beyond dispute. But I suppose she's content with a stolen tumble, and the insult to my bed is a small thing. She's pregnant — that was all that was missing! — carrying the plain proof of his guilt in her swollen belly, wanting to be made a mother by Jupiter, which is more than I ever managed for myself. That's how sure she is of her beauty. I'll see to it her beauty fails her. Let me be no daughter of Saturn if I don't send her down to the waters of the Styx, sunk there by her own dear Jupiter."

At that she rose from her throne and, wrapped in a tawny cloud, went to Semele's door. She did not shed the cloud until she had disguised herself as an old woman — set gray hair at her temples, plowed her skin into wrinkles, hunched her body and moved it with a trembling, palsied step. She made her voice an old woman's voice too. She became Beroe herself, Semele's nurse from Epidaurus. So, drawing the girl into conversation and talking on and on, they came around to the name of Jupiter, and the old woman sighed and said, "I do hope it's Jupiter — but I'm afraid of everything. Plenty of men have talked their way into a decent girl's bed under a god's name. And it's not enough that he's Jupiter. If he really is the true one, let him give you some proof of his love: ask him to hold you exactly as great and glorious as he is when he's welcomed by lofty Juno, and to put on all his royal splendor first." That was how Juno primed the unsuspecting daughter of Cadmus. And Semele asked Jupiter for a gift without naming it. "Choose," the god said, "you'll be refused nothing. And so you'll believe me all the more, let the divine power of the Stygian torrent be my witness too — the terror and the god even of the gods."

Delighted with what would destroy her, too successful, and about to die of her lover's compliance, Semele said, "Show yourself to me just as the daughter of Saturn holds you when the two of you seal the bond of love — give yourself to me like that." The god wanted to clap a hand over her mouth as she spoke, but the words had already rushed out into the air. He groaned. She could not now unwish it, and he could not now unswear it. So in the deepest grief he climbed the high sky and drew the trailing clouds along after him with a nod, and added rainstorms and lightning mixed with wind, and thunder, and the bolt there is no escaping. Yet as much as he could, he tried to strip himself of his own power. He did not arm himself with the fire that had brought down hundred-handed Typhoeus — there was too much savagery in that one. There is a lighter bolt, into which the hands of the Cyclopes had forged less cruelty, less flame, less rage; the gods call it the second-rank weapon. That one he took up, and entered the house of Agenor's line. Her mortal body could not bear the storm of heaven, and she burned to ash in the fire of her wedding gift.

The infant, still unformed, was pulled from his mother's womb and — if this is worthy of belief — sewn, tender as he was, into his father's thigh, to finish out the months that belonged to the mother. His aunt Ino secretly nursed him in his first cradle; then the nymphs of Nysa took him, hid him in their caves, and fed him milk.

And while all this was going on down on earth by the law of fate, and the cradle of twice-born Bacchus lay safe, they say that Jupiter, well soaked in nectar and off his guard, set his heavy cares aside and fell to trading idle jokes with Juno in a slack hour. "There's no question," he said, "the pleasure you women get out of love is greater than what falls to us men." She said no. They agreed to ask the learned Tiresias for a ruling, since he had known love as both. For once, in a green wood, he had struck with his staff two great serpents coupling, and — the marvel of it — had been changed from a man into a woman and lived seven autumns so. In the eighth he came upon the same pair again and said, "If there's such power in a blow struck against you that it turns the striker into his opposite, I'll strike you now once more." He struck those same snakes, and his earlier form came back, the shape he was born with. So he was chosen as judge in this playful quarrel and backed Jupiter's word. Saturn's daughter, they say, took it harder than the matter warranted, out of all proportion, and condemned the eyes of her judge to everlasting night. But the almighty father — since no god is permitted to undo what another god has done — gave him, in return for his lost sight, the knowledge of things to come, and eased the punishment with that honor.

Famous now throughout the cities of Boeotia, Tiresias gave flawless answers to all who came to consult him. The first to test his voice and prove it true was sea-blue Liriope, a water nymph, whom the river Cephisus once wound in his curving current and, trapping her inside his waters, forced. In the fullness of time the loveliest of nymphs bore a child, one who even then could stir love, and she named him Narcissus. Asked about the boy — whether he would live to see the long years of a ripe old age — the prophet who spoke destiny said, "If he never comes to know himself." For a long time the seer's answer seemed empty. Then the outcome proved it — the way it all turned out, the manner of his death, the strange new kind of madness.

For the son of Cephisus had added one year to three times five, and could pass for either a boy or a young man. Many a youth desired him, many a girl — but there was so hard a pride in that tender beauty that no youth, no girl could touch him. Once, as he was driving frightened deer into his nets, a nymph with a voice caught sight of him — resounding Echo, who had never learned to keep quiet when another spoke, nor to speak first herself. Echo still had a body then, she was not just a voice; and yet, chatterer that she was, she could do no more with her mouth than she can now — give back, out of many words, only the last. Juno had done this to her. Time and again, when the goddess might have caught the nymphs lying with her Jupiter in the mountains, Echo would cunningly hold Juno in long conversation while the nymphs slipped away. When the daughter of Saturn saw through it, she said, "That tongue of yours, that made a fool of me — I'll give you only the smallest power over it, the briefest use of your voice." And she made good on the threat. So now Echo only doubles back the end of what is said and returns the words she hears.

So when she saw Narcissus wandering through the trackless country and caught fire, she followed his tracks in secret, and the more she followed, the closer the flame, the hotter she burned — the way quick sulphur smeared around the top of a torch snatches up a flame brought near it. Oh, how often she longed to come up to him with coaxing words and soft pleas! Her nature fought her and would not let her begin. But what her nature did allow, she was ready for — to wait for his sounds and send her own words back to them. By chance the boy, having lost the trusty band of his companions, called out, "Is anyone here?" and "Here," Echo answered. He stood amazed, and casting his eyes in every direction, shouted, "Come!" — and she called back the one who called. He looked around, and when no one came, said, "Why do you run from me?" — and got back exactly the words he had spoken. He stood his ground, fooled by the answering voice that seemed another's, and said, "Here, let us meet" — and Echo, who would never answer any sound more gladly, called back, "Let us meet," and made good on her own words, and came out of the woods and started toward him, to throw her arms around the neck she had longed for. He ran. And as he ran he cried, "Keep your hands off me! I'll die before I'll give myself to you!" She answered nothing but "I'll give myself to you." Spurned, she hid in the woods, covering her shamed face with leaves, and from that day lived alone in caves. But her love clung on and grew with the pain of being refused. Sleepless heartache wasted her wretched body away; her skin shriveled with a wasting thinness, and all the sap of her body drained off into the air. Only her voice and her bones were left. Then only her voice — her bones, they say, took on the shape of stone. Ever since, she hides in the woods and is seen on no mountain, but is heard by all: it is sound, and sound alone, that lives on in her.
