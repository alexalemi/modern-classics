Book II

(Part 1 of 2)

The palace of the Sun rose high on soaring columns, ablaze with glittering gold and with bronze-red pyrope that mimics living flame. Polished ivory sheathed its topmost gables, and its double doors threw back the light from panels of silver. But the craftsmanship outshone even the costly stuff it was made of. For there Vulcan, the fire-god and smith of the sky, had chased into the metal the seas that ring the enclosing lands, and the round world, and the heavens that arch over the world. The waves hold their sea-blue gods: Triton blowing his echoing shell; shifting Proteus, who is never twice the same; Aegaeon, his arms flung across the vast backs of whales; and Doris and her daughters, the sea-nymphs — some of them shown swimming, some sitting on a rock to dry their green hair, some riding on the backs of fish. Their faces are not all one face, and yet not wholly unlike — just as sisters' faces should be. The land bears men and cities and forests, wild beasts and rivers, nymphs and all the other spirits of the countryside. And over them all is set the image of the shining sky, with six signs of the zodiac on the right-hand door and six on the left.

By a steep, climbing path the son of Clymene came at last to that place and stepped inside the house of the father he had only been promised. At once he turned his steps toward his father's face — and stopped a long way off, for he could not bear that light any nearer. Wrapped in a purple robe, Phoebus the Sun sat on a throne bright with clear-cut emeralds. To his right and his left stood the Day, the Month, the Year, the Ages, and the Hours, ranged at even spaces; young Spring stood there, wreathed in a crown of flowers; Summer stood naked, wearing garlands of grain; Autumn stood stained with the trodden juice of grapes; and icy Winter, his white hair stiff and shaggy.

From his place at the center, the Sun — with those eyes that see all things — saw the young man flinching at the strangeness of it all, and said, "What has brought you all this way? What do you come seeking in this high house, Phaethon — a son no father would deny?"

He answered, "Light shared by the whole measureless world, Phoebus, my father — if you let me use that name, if Clymene is not hiding some fault behind a lie — give me a token, father, some proof that lets the world believe I am truly your child; take this doubt out of my heart." So he spoke; and his father laid aside the blaze of rays that ringed his head and told the boy to come closer. He folded him in his arms and said, "You do not deserve to be denied as mine, and Clymene has told you your true birth. And so that you may doubt no longer — ask any gift you like, and you shall have it from my hand. Let the marsh the gods swear by be witness to my promise, the pool my own eyes have never seen."

Scarcely had he finished when the boy asked for his father's chariot, and for the right to drive the wing-footed horses for a single day.

His father repented the oath. Three times, four times he shook his shining head, and said, "Your words have made mine reckless. If only I were free not to grant what I promised! I confess it, son — this one thing, and only this, I would refuse you. But I am still allowed to argue you out of it. What you want is not safe. You ask for something too great, Phaethon, a gift that suits neither your strength nor your boyish years. Your lot is a mortal's; what you long for is not for mortals. In your ignorance you reach even beyond what the gods above are allowed. Let each of them please himself as he likes — still, not one of them but me can stand on the fire-bearing axle. Even the ruler of vast Olympus, who hurls the savage thunderbolts with his terrible right hand, could not drive this chariot; and what have we greater than Jupiter?

"The first stretch of road is steep, so steep that the horses, fresh in the morning, can barely struggle up it. At the very top of the sky it runs highest of all, and even I am often afraid to look down from there on the sea and the land, and my heart pounds with trembling dread. The last stretch drops sheer, and needs a sure hand on the reins; then even Tethys, the sea that takes me in below, is afraid I will come plunging headlong down. And the whole sky is forever wheeling round, dragging the high stars with it and spinning them in a rushing whirl. I drive against it — that force which overpowers all else does not overpower me — and I ride the opposite way to the racing world. Suppose the chariot were yours: what would you do? Could you go against the spinning poles and keep the swift axle from sweeping you away? Perhaps you picture groves up there, and cities of the gods, and temples rich with offerings? The way runs through ambushes, and the shapes of savage beasts! Even if you hold your course and are led astray by no wandering, still you must pass through the horns of the Bull that fronts you, past the Thessalian archer's bow and the jaws of the raging Lion, past the Scorpion curving his cruel arms in one long sweep and the Crab that curves his arms the other way. And the horses, hot with the fire they carry in their chests and breathe out from mouth and nostrils — those you will not easily rule. Even I can barely hold them when their fierce spirit heats up and their necks fight the reins.

"But you, my son — take care I am not the giver of a deadly gift; while there is still time, mend what you are asking for. You want a sure proof that you are sprung from my blood? I give you a sure proof by my fear for you: by a father's dread I am shown to be your father. Look — look at my face! I wish you could sink your eyes into my breast and catch the father's cares inside it. And, last of all — look round you at everything the rich world holds, and out of all the blessings of heaven and earth and sea, so many and so great, ask for anything; you shall meet no refusal. This one thing only I beg to be let off, which by its true name is a punishment, not an honor. You are asking, Phaethon, for a punishment dressed up as a gift. Why do you throw your coaxing arms around my neck, you foolish boy? Have no doubt — whatever you choose will be given; I have sworn it by the waters of the Styx. But choose more wisely!"

He had finished his warnings; but the boy fought them off all the same, held to his purpose, and burned with longing for the chariot. So the father, having stalled as long as he could, led the young man out to the tall chariot, Vulcan's handiwork. The axle was gold, the pole was gold, gold ran round the rims of the wheels, and the spokes were ranged in silver. Along the yoke, chrysolites and gems set in rows threw the bright light back to Phoebus above. And while the high-hearted Phaethon marveled at the work and studied it, look — wakeful Dawn threw open her purple doors in the reddening east and her halls full of roses. The stars fled; the Morning Star herded the last of their ranks and was himself the last to quit his post in the sky.

When the Sun saw him make for the earth, and the world turning red, and the horns of the waning Moon seem to melt away, he ordered the swift Hours to yoke the horses. The goddesses did his bidding quickly, leading the beasts from their lofty stalls — snorting flame, gorged on the juice of ambrosia — and buckling on the ringing bits. Then the father touched his son's face with a holy salve and made it able to bear the scorching flame; he set the rays on the boy's hair, and, drawing sigh after sigh from a heart that already foreknew grief, he said:

"If you can obey at least this much of your father's advice — go easy on the whip, boy, and pull hard on the reins. They race of their own accord; the work is to hold them back when they want to run. And do not let the road tempt you straight across the five circles of the sky. There is a track cut slantwise in a broad curve, kept within the bounds of three zones, that shuns the far southern pole and the Bear that keeps to the north wind — go that way; you will see the clear ruts of my wheels. And so that heaven and earth may share the heat alike, do not press too low, and do not force the chariot up through the highest air. Go too high, and you will scorch the roofs of heaven; too low, and you will burn the earth. The middle way is safest. Do not let the wheel veer right toward the coiled Serpent, or the left carry you down to the low Altar. Hold between the two. The rest I leave to Fortune, and I pray she helps you and looks after you better than you look after yourself. While I have been speaking, damp Night has touched her turning-post on the western shore. We are not free to wait; I am called for. Dawn is blazing out, the darkness put to flight. Take the reins in your hand — or, if your heart can still be changed, use my advice and not my chariot, while you still can, while you still stand on solid ground, before you have climbed, in your ignorance, into the axle you so wrongly wanted. Let me give the light to the earth — a light you can watch from a safe place!"

But the boy had already sprung into the light chariot with his young body, and stood tall, and rejoiced to take the light reins in his hands, and thanked his unwilling father for them.

Meanwhile the Sun's winged horses — Pyroeis and Eous and Aethon, and Phlegon the fourth — filled the air with fiery neighing and beat their hooves against the barriers. When Tethys, knowing nothing of her grandson's fate, pushed the barriers back and gave them the whole open sky, they leapt to the road, and, working their feet through the air, tore through the clouds that blocked them, and, lifted on their wings, outran the east winds that had risen from the same quarter. But the load was light — too light for the Sun's horses to feel — and the yoke lacked its usual weight. As curved ships without their proper ballast reel and are carried unsteady over the sea for their very lightness, so the chariot, empty of its accustomed burden, bounded up into the air, tossed high and pitching like a thing with nothing in it.

The moment the team felt it, they bolted, left the beaten track, and no longer ran in their old order. The boy himself was in a panic. He did not know which way to turn the reins put into his hands, did not know where the road lay, and, if he had known, could not have made them obey. Then, for the first time, the cold Wain-stars — the northern Bears — grew warm under the sunbeams, and tried in vain to dip themselves in a sea forbidden to them. The Serpent that lies coiled nearest the frozen pole, sluggish before with cold and a threat to no one, grew hot and took a new fury from the heat. And you too, Boötes, they say, fled in confusion, slow as you were and held back by your wagon.

But when the luckless Phaethon looked down from the roof of the sky at the lands lying far, so far below, he went white, and his knees shook with sudden fear, and in all that light darkness swam across his eyes. Now he wished he had never touched his father's horses; now he was sorry he had learned his birth and had won his own request; now, longing to be called the son of Merops, he was swept along like a ship driven by a screaming north wind, whose pilot has thrown down the helm he can no longer master and left her to the gods and his prayers. What could he do? Much of the sky lay behind him now, but more of it stretched ahead. He measured both in his mind. Now he looked forward to the west, which fate would never let him reach; now he looked back to the east. Numb, not knowing what to do, he neither let the reins go nor had the strength to hold them, and he did not even know the horses' names. And scattered here and there across the strange sky he saw, shaking with terror, monstrous shapes of enormous beasts. There is a place where the Scorpion bends his arms into two great bows, and, with his tail and his claws crooked on either side, stretches his limbs across the space of two signs. When the boy saw him, wet with the sweat of black poison and threatening a wound with the curved barb of his sting, his wits left him and, in a chill of horror, he let the reins fall.

When the reins dropped and settled on the horses' backs, the team broke loose. With no one to hold them they went careening through the air of an unknown country, and wherever their frenzy drove them, there they rushed, lawless: they crashed against the stars fixed high in the heavens and dragged the chariot through trackless wastes. Now they made for the very top of the sky, now they plunged down headlong ways to a strip of earth closer below. The Moon watched in amazement as her brother's horses ran lower than her own, and the singed clouds smoked.

The earth caught fire, each place as it stood highest first; it split and cracked open in gaping chasms and, its sap drawn off, went dry. The pastures whitened; the trees blazed with their leaves; and the ripe standing grain gave fuel to its own ruin. But these are small griefs to mourn. Great cities perished with their walls, and the flames turned whole nations, peoples and all, to ash; woods burned together with the mountains. Athos burned, and Cilician Taurus, and Tmolus, and Oeta, and Ida — dry now, once thick with springs — and Helicon, home of the virgin Muses, and Haemus, not yet called Oeagrian. Etna burned past all measure with doubled fire, and two-peaked Parnassus, and Eryx, and Cynthus, and Othrys, and Rhodope — this time to lose its snows at last — and Mimas, and Dindyma, and Mycale, and Cithaeron, born for holy rites. Its own cold could not save Scythia: Caucasus burned, and Ossa with Pindus, and Olympus taller than both, and the airy Alps, and the cloud-carrying Apennines.

Then indeed Phaethon saw the whole world ablaze on every side, and could not endure such heat; he breathed in scalding air as if from the bottom of a furnace, and felt his own chariot going white-hot beneath him. He could not bear the ashes and flung-up cinders any longer; on every side he was wrapped in scorching smoke, and, buried in a pitch-black murk, he no longer knew where he was going or where he was, and was carried off at the whim of the winged horses.

It was then, men believe, that the peoples of Ethiopia took on their dark color, as the blood was drawn to the surface of their bodies. It was then that Libya was made a desert, its moisture seared away; then that the nymphs, hair loose, wept for their springs and lakes. Boeotia looked for Dirce, Argos for Amymone, Ephyre for the waters of Pirene. Not even rivers with wide-set banks stayed safe: Tanais steamed in the middle of its waters, and old Peneus, and Teuthrantian Caicus, and quick Ismenus, and Phocian Erymanthus, and Xanthus — doomed to burn a second time — and yellow Lycormas, and Maeander playing in its winding streams, and Mygdonian Melas, and Taenarian Eurotas. Babylonian Euphrates burned; Orontes burned, and swift Thermodon, and Ganges, and Phasis, and Ister. Alpheus boiled; the banks of Spercheus blazed; the gold that Tagus carries in its current ran molten in the flames. And the river birds, the swans that had made the Maeonian banks famous with their song, scorched in the middle of the Cayster. The Nile fled in terror to the ends of the earth and hid its head, which is hidden still; its seven mouths lay empty and dusty, seven valleys with no river in them. The same fate dried the Ismarian streams, Hebrus with Strymon, and the western rivers — the Rhine, the Rhone, the Po, and the Tiber, to whom the mastery of the world was promised.

All the ground burst apart; light drove down through the cracks into Tartarus and terrified the king of the underworld and his queen. The sea shrank in, and where lately there had been open water was now a plain of dry sand; mountains the deep had covered rose up and swelled the number of the scattered Cyclades. The fish sank to the bottom; the arched dolphins no longer dared to lift themselves into the air above the water as they used to; the bodies of seals floated lifeless on their backs on the surface of the deep. The story goes that even Nereus himself, and Doris and their daughters, hid in their warm caves. Three times Neptune, with a grim face, dared to thrust his arms up out of the water; three times he could not endure the fiery air.

Yet kindly Earth, the mother, ringed round as she was by sea, among the waters of the deep and the shrunken springs that had buried themselves in her dark body — Earth, parched, lifted her smothered face as far as her neck, put a hand to her brow, and, shaking everything with a great tremor, sank a little lower than she is wont to be, and spoke in a broken voice:

"If this is your will and I have earned it, why do your lightnings hold back, highest of the gods? If I must perish by the power of fire, let me perish by your fire, and lighten my ruin by knowing whose hand it was. I can barely open my throat for these very words" — the heat had choked her mouth — "Look at my scorched hair! Look, so many cinders over my eyes, so many over my face! Is this the return you give me, this the reward of my fruitfulness and my service — that I bear the wounds of the curved plough and the harrows and am worked hard all the year long; that I furnish leaves for the cattle, and gentle food, the grain, for the human race, and incense for you gods? But even suppose I have earned destruction — what have the waters done, what has your brother done? Why do the seas handed to him by lot shrink away and pull back farther from the sky? And if regard for your brother and for me does not move you, then take pity at least on your own heavens! Look around: both poles are smoking. If the fire ruins them, your own halls will come crashing down. See — Atlas himself is struggling, and can barely hold the white-hot sky on his shoulders! If the seas, if the lands, if the palace of heaven are lost, we are hurled back into the chaos of old. Snatch from the flames whatever is still left, and take thought for the sum of things!"

So spoke Earth; for she could bear the vapor no longer, and could say no more, and drew her face back into herself, into the caverns nearest the ghosts of the dead.

But the almighty father, having called the gods above to witness — and the one, too, who had given the chariot — that unless he acted all things would be lost to a grim fate, climbed to the topmost height from which he is used to spreading clouds across the broad lands, from which he rouses his thunder and hurls his brandished bolts. But this time he had no clouds to draw over the earth, no rain to send down from the sky. He thundered, and, poising a bolt level with his right ear, flung it at the charioteer and struck him out of his life and his seat at once, and with his own ruthless fire he curbed the fire below. The horses panicked, leapt apart, wrenched their necks from the yoke, and left the broken harness behind. Here lie the reins; there the axle, torn from the pole; there the spokes of the shattered wheels; and the wreck of the smashed chariot was scattered far and wide.

But Phaethon, the flames devouring his red-gold hair, was flung headlong and fell in a long trail through the air, as sometimes a star from a clear sky seems to fall, though it has not really fallen. Far from his homeland, in another quarter of the world, the great river Eridanus, the Po, took him in and washed his smoking face.

The nymphs of the west laid his body, still smoking from the three-forked flame, in a tomb, and carved these lines on the stone:

Here lies Phaethon, who drove his father's chariot. If he could not hold it, still he fell in a great daring.

His wretched father, sick with bitter grief, had covered his face and hidden it; and — if only we can believe it — they say one whole day went by without a sun. The burning world gave light; so even that disaster was good for something.

But Clymene, when she had said whatever there was to say in so great a sorrow, wild and out of her mind and tearing at her breast, went over the whole earth, hunting first for his lifeless limbs, and then for his bones. She found the bones at last, buried on a foreign bank. She threw herself down on the spot, bathed with her tears the name she read on the marble, and warmed it against her open breast.

The daughters of the Sun mourned no less, and gave their tears, a gift useless to the dead, and beat their breasts with their hands, and called on Phaethon night and day — Phaethon, who would never hear their sad laments — and lay stretched about the tomb. Four times the Moon had filled her disk, joining her horns; and the sisters, in their custom (for grief had made a custom of it), kept up their wailing. Then Phaethusa, the eldest of them, when she tried to throw herself on the ground, cried out that her feet had gone stiff. Fair Lampetie, trying to come to her, was held fast by a root that sprang up out of nowhere. A third, when she went to tear her hair with her hands, tore off leaves instead. One wailed that her legs were held in a trunk of wood; another that her arms had become long branches. And while they stared at this, bark closed over their loins, and by degrees wrapped their bellies, their breasts, their shoulders, their hands, until only their mouths were left, crying out for their mother. What could their mother do but run this way and that, wherever her frenzy took her, and press her lips to theirs while she still could? It was not enough. She tried to pull their bodies out of the trunks and to snap the tender branches off with her hands — and out of them dripped blood as from a wound. "Spare me, mother, I beg you," each one cried as she was hurt, "spare me! It is my own body being torn in the tree. And now — goodbye." The bark came over their last words. From the branches the tears still flow, and the amber that drips from the new-formed boughs hardens in the sun; the clear river catches it and sends it on to be worn by the brides of Latium.

Cycnus, the son of Sthenelus, was there for this strange event — Cycnus, who, though he was kin to you, Phaethon, on his mother's side, was closer still to you in his heart. He had left his kingdom (for he had ruled the peoples and the great cities of Liguria) and was filling the green banks and the river Eridanus, and the wood the sisters had thickened, with his laments, when the man's voice thinned to a shrill note, gray feathers hid his hair, a long neck reached out from his breast, a web bound his reddening toes, plumage clothed his sides, and a blunt bill closed over his mouth. Cycnus became a new bird, the swan; but he would not trust himself to the sky or to Jupiter, remembering the fire hurled so unjustly from up there. He kept to the ponds and the wide lakes, and, hating fire, chose the streams, the very opposite of flame.

Meanwhile Phaethon's father, unkempt and stripped of his own splendor — just as he is when he goes into eclipse — hated the light, hated himself and the day, gave his mind over to grief, and to his grief added anger, and refused his service to the world. "From the very beginning of time," he said, "my lot has been restless enough, and I am sick of labors without end and without honor. Let anyone else drive the chariot that carries the light! And if there is no one, and all the gods admit they cannot do it, let Jupiter himself drive it — so that, while he is trying my reins, he may at least, for a while, lay down the bolts that leave fathers childless. Then, when he has felt for himself the strength of the fire-footed horses, he will know that the one who could not steer them well did not deserve to die."

As the Sun said such things, all the gods stood around him and begged him with pleading voices not to plunge the world into darkness. Jupiter, for his part, made excuses for hurling his lightning, and, like a king, added threats to his pleas. So Phoebus gathered his horses, still crazed and trembling with terror, and in his pain lashed out at them with goad and whip — lashed at them savagely, for savage he was — and threw his son in their faces and blamed his death on them.

But the almighty father went the round of the great walls of heaven, checking that no part, weakened by the violence of the fire, was about to fall. When he saw them firm and sound in their own strength, he looked over the earth and the works of men. Yet his own Arcadia was the deeper care to him. He restored its springs and the rivers that had not yet dared to run again; he gave grass back to the ground and leaves to the trees, and bade the wounded forests grow green once more. And as he went to and fro, again and again, he stopped short at a girl of Nonacris in Arcadia, and the fire caught and burned in his bones.

She was no girl for softening wool by teasing it out, or for varying the style of her hair. A clasp held her dress, a plain white band held her carelessly falling hair; and now she carried a light javelin in her hand, now a bow. She was one of Diana's soldiers, and no nymph who ranged Maenalus was dearer to the goddess than she. But no favor lasts long.

The high sun had passed the middle of its course when she came into a grove that no age had ever cut. Here she slipped the quiver from her shoulder, unstrung her supple bow, and lay down on the ground the grass had covered, her painted quiver a pillow under her neck. When Jupiter saw her there, tired and unguarded, he said, "Here at least is one stolen love my wife will know nothing of — or if she does find out, oh, her scolding is well worth it!" At once he put on the face and dress of Diana, and said, "O girl, one of my own company — on what ridges have you been hunting?" The girl raised herself from the turf and said, "Hail, goddess — greater in my eyes than Jupiter himself, though he hear me say it." He smiled to hear it, glad to be set above himself, and kissed her — kisses too long, and not such as a virgin goddess would give. As she began to tell him in what wood she had been hunting, he cut her off with an embrace, and gave himself away by the outrage he did her. She fought back, as hard as a woman could — if only you had seen it, Juno, you would have been kinder to her — she fought, but what girl could ever have overcome him, and who could overcome Jupiter? He won, and made for the upper sky; and now the grove and the wood that had witnessed it were hateful to her. Turning back from that place, she almost forgot to take up her quiver and its arrows and the bow she had hung on a branch.

Look now — Diana the huntress, coming along the heights of Maenalus with her band around her, proud of the beasts she had killed, caught sight of the girl and called to her. At the call she shrank back, afraid at first that this too might be Jupiter in disguise; but when she saw the nymphs walking with the goddess, she knew there was no trick, and joined their number. Alas, how hard it is not to betray a fault in the face! She could barely lift her eyes from the ground; she no longer walked, as she used to, at the goddess's side or at the head of the whole company, but was silent, and by her blushes gave the sign of her wounded honor. And Diana — but that she is a virgin, and could not guess it — might have read the girl's fault by a thousand signs. The nymphs, they say, did read it.

The horns of the Moon were rising for the ninth time when the goddess, faint from her brother the Sun's flames, came upon a cool grove out of which ran a stream, murmuring as it went and turning the fine-worn sand. She praised the place, and touched the surface of the water with her foot; and, praising the water too, she said, "No one is watching, far or near — let us bathe our bodies with the stream poured over them." The Parrhasian girl blushed. They all took off their clothes; she alone looked for reasons to delay. As she hesitated, her dress was taken from her, and with it off, her fault stood plain along with her naked body. She stood stunned, and tried to hide her belly with her hands. "Away from here, far away," said Diana, "and do not defile these sacred springs" — and the goddess ordered her out of her company.

The wife of the great Thunderer had long since noticed all this, and had put off her heavy punishment to a fitting time. Now there was no reason to wait: the boy Arcas — the very thing that galled Juno — had already been born to her rival. The moment she turned her cruel thoughts and her cruel eyes that way, she said, "So this was all that was still left, you adulteress — that you should be fruitful, and your childbearing make the wrong known, and stand as proof of my Jupiter's disgrace! You will not go unpunished. I will strip you of that shape you please yourself with, you troublemaker — the shape that pleases my husband." She spoke, and, seizing the girl by the hair over her forehead, threw her face-down on the ground. Callisto stretched out her arms to beg — and the arms began to bristle with coarse black hair, and her hands to curve and grow into hooked claws and serve her for feet, and the face that Jupiter had once praised was ruined into a wide, gaping jaw. And so that no prayers, no pleading words might sway anyone, the power of speech was torn away from her: only an angry, threatening growl, thick with terror, came from her hoarse throat. Yet her old mind stayed with her, even made a bear as she was, and with unbroken groaning she bore witness to her grief, and lifted such hands as she had to the sky and the stars, and, though she could not say it, felt that Jupiter was cruel. Ah, how many times, not daring to sleep alone in the wild wood,
