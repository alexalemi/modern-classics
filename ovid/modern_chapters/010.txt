Book VI

(Part 1 of 2)

Minerva had been listening to all of this — the songs of the Muses, the daughters of Aonia — and she approved both their artistry and the justice of their anger. Then she said to herself: "To praise is not enough. Let me be praised as well. Let no one scorn my own divine power and go unpunished." And she turned her mind to the fate of Arachne, a girl of Lydia who, she had heard, refused to grant that anyone outdid her in the craft of working wool.

Arachne owed her fame to no famous birthplace and no famous family — only to her skill. Her father, Idmon of Colophon, dyed thirsty wool in Phocaean purple. Her mother was dead, but she too had been a woman of the common people, no higher than her husband. Even so, in the towns of Lydia the girl had won a name worth remembering for her art, though she came from a humble house and lived in humble Hypaepa. To watch her marvelous work, the nymphs would often leave the vineyards of their own Mount Tmolus, and the nymphs of the river Pactolus would leave their golden streams. And it was not only the finished cloth that gave them pleasure — it was watching her make it, there was such grace in the working. Whether she was winding the raw wool into its first soft balls, or teasing the fleece out again and again between her fingers, drawing it long and fine as mist; whether she was twirling the smooth round spindle with a light thumb, or picking out a picture with her needle — you could tell she had been taught by Minerva herself.

But Arachne denied it. Offended at the very idea of so grand a teacher, she said, "Let her compete with me. There is nothing I would refuse to suffer, if she beats me."

So Minerva took the shape of an old woman. She set false gray hair at her temples, and leaned her weak old limbs on a staff, and began: "Not everything old age brings is to be run from. Long years bring experience. Don't scorn my advice. By all means, seek the greatest fame among mortals for your weaving — but yield to the goddess, you reckless girl, and beg pardon for your words in a humble voice. She will forgive you, if you ask."

Arachne shot her a black look and let the threads drop. She could barely keep her hand still; her fury showed in her face; and she snapped back at the disguised goddess: "You come here out of your wits, worn down by too many years, and living too long is your curse. Save this lecture for a daughter-in-law, if you have one, or a daughter of your own. I know enough for myself, all by myself. Don't imagine your advice has done any good — my mind is unchanged. Why doesn't she come herself? Why does she dodge this contest?"

"She has come," said the goddess — and she threw off the old woman's shape and showed herself as Minerva. The nymphs bowed down, and the Phrygian women bowed; only the girl was not afraid. And yet she blushed. A sudden flush marked her face against her will, then faded again — the way the sky turns crimson when Aurora first stirs at daybreak, and a short while later goes white as the sun climbs. Arachne held to her purpose. Hungry for a foolish victory, she rushed headlong toward her own ruin. And the daughter of Jupiter did not refuse. She gave no more warnings; she put the contest off no longer.

Without delay they took their places, one here, one there, and stretched two webs on the looms with a fine warp. The warp was bound to the beam, a rod kept the threads apart, and the pointed shuttles drove the weft down the middle; the fingers worked it through, and once it was drawn between the warp-threads the notched teeth of the beater struck it home. Both women hurried, their robes hitched up to their breasts, moving their skilled arms, their eagerness making the labor no labor at all. Into the cloth went purple that had drunk the dye of the Tyrian vat, and shade after shade so close you could hardly tell them apart — the way a rainbow, when sunlight strikes the falling rain, arches its long curve across the whole sky: a thousand different colors shine in it, yet the eye that looks cannot catch the moment one becomes the next, so alike is each stripe to the stripe beside it, and only the ends stand far apart. Into the cloth, too, went threads of supple gold, and each loom told an old, old story.

Minerva wove the hill of Mars in Athens, the citadel of Cecrops, and the ancient quarrel over what the land should be named. Twelve gods of heaven sat on high thrones in solemn majesty, with Jupiter in the middle, each rendered so that his own face gave him away: Jupiter's image was every inch a king. She made the lord of the sea stand and strike the rough rock with his long trident, and out of the wound in the stone a rush of seawater leaped — the pledge by which he laid claim to the city. To herself she gave a shield, a spear with a sharp point, a helmet on her head, and the aegis to guard her breast; and she showed the earth, struck by her own spear, bringing forth a pale-green olive shoot heavy with berries, and the gods marveling at it. Victory closed the scene. But so that her rival might learn from examples what reward to expect for such mad daring, she added four contests in the four corners, each bright in its own colors, each picked out in small figures. One corner held Thracian Rhodope and Haemus — cold mountains now, once human beings, who took to themselves the names of the highest gods. Another corner held the pitiful fate of the Pygmy queen: Juno, having beaten her in a contest, ordered her to become a crane and make war on her own people. She wove Antigone as well, who once dared to match herself against the wife of mighty Jupiter, and whom royal Juno turned into a bird; neither Troy nor her father Laomedon could save her from sprouting feathers and, as a white stork, applauding herself with a clattering beak. The one corner left held Cinyras, robbed of his children: he lay clasping the temple steps that had once been his daughters' living limbs, and seemed to be weeping on the stone. Minerva bordered the outer edges with peaceful olive, and there she stopped, closing her work with her own tree.

Arachne, the girl of Lydia, drew Europa fooled by the shape of a bull — you would have thought the bull real and the sea real. Europa herself seemed to look back at the land she was leaving, to cry out to her companions, to shrink from the touch of the leaping water and pull up her frightened feet. She drew Asterie gripped by the struggling eagle; she drew Leda lying back beneath the swan's wings. She added how Jupiter, hidden in a satyr's shape, filled beautiful Antiope, the daughter of Nycteus, with twin children; how he was Amphitryon when he took Alcmene, the Tirynthian bride; how he cheated Danaë as a shower of gold, and the daughter of Asopus as fire, and Mnemosyne as a shepherd, and Proserpine, Ceres' daughter, as a speckled serpent. She set down Neptune too, changed into a fierce bull for the sake of the Aeolian girl; how, in the likeness of the river Enipeus, he fathered the giant Aloidae; how as a ram he deceived Theophane, the daughter of Bisaltes; how the golden-haired mother of the grain, Ceres, knew him as a stallion, and the snake-haired mother of the winged horse — Medusa — knew him as a bird, and Melantho knew him as a dolphin. To every one of these she gave its true face and the true look of the place. There was Apollo dressed as a countryman, and how he wore the feathers of a hawk one time and a lion's skin another, and how, as a shepherd, he deceived Isse, the daughter of Macareus. There was Bacchus tricking Erigone with false grapes, and Saturn as a horse fathering the double-natured centaur Chiron. The last strip of the web, framed in a fine border, had flowers woven through with twining ivy.

Neither Minerva nor Envy herself could fault that work. The golden-haired warrior-goddess was cut to the heart by her rival's success, and she tore the woven cloth with its record of the crimes of heaven; then, with the boxwood shuttle still in her hand, three and four times she struck the forehead of Arachne, daughter of Idmon. The wretched girl could not bear it. Full of spirit, she knotted a noose around her throat. As she hung there, Minerva took pity and lifted her up, and said: "Live, then — but hang, you insolent thing. And so that you can never rest easy about the future, let this same sentence fall on your whole line, down to your last descendants." Then, turning to go, she sprinkled her with the juices of an herb of Hecate — and at the touch of that grim drug, Arachne's hair fell away, and with it her nose and her ears. Her head shrank to almost nothing; her whole body turned tiny. Slender fingers clung to her sides in place of legs; all the rest was belly — and from it, even so, she still sends out her thread, and works her old craft, a spider at her web.

All Lydia was in an uproar. The story ran through the towns of Phrygia and filled the wide world with talk. Now, Niobe had known Arachne before her own marriage, back when she was a girl still living in Maeonia, under Mount Sipylus. And yet the punishment of her countrywoman did not teach her to yield to the powers of heaven and use humbler words. She had many things to feed her pride, but neither her husband's artistry, nor the high birth of them both, nor the might of a great kingdom pleased her — much as all of these did please her — as much as her own children. Niobe might have been called the happiest of mothers, if only she had not thought herself so.

For Manto, the daughter of Tiresias, who could read the future, had passed through the streets in the grip of a divine urging, crying her prophecy aloud: "Women of Thebes, go, all of you, and offer Latona and Latona's two children your pious incense and your prayers, and bind your hair with laurel. Latona commands it through my mouth." They obeyed. All the women of Thebes crowned their brows with the leaves the goddess had named, and offered incense and words of prayer to the sacred flames.

And then Niobe came, hemmed in by a crowd of attendants, dazzling in Phrygian robes shot through with gold, and beautiful — as beautiful as anger would let her be. Tossing the hair that fell across both shoulders, holding her lovely head high, she stopped and swept her proud eyes around, and said: "What madness is this, to set gods you have only heard of above gods you can see? Why is Latona honored at altars, while my own godhead still goes without its incense? Tantalus was my father — the only man ever allowed to sit at the tables of the gods. My mother is the sister of the Pleiades. Mighty Atlas, who carries the vault of heaven on his neck, is one of my grandfathers; Jupiter is the other — and I boast of him as my father-in-law too. The peoples of Phrygia fear me. The palace of Cadmus obeys me as its mistress. The walls my husband raised with the music of his lyre, and the people within them, are ruled by me and by him. Whichever way I turn my eyes in this house, I see measureless wealth — and add to it a face fit for a goddess. Add my seven daughters and my seven sons, and soon their husbands and their wives! Now ask yourselves what grounds my pride has. And then dare to prefer to me this Latona, daughter of some nobody Titan named Coeus — Latona, whom the whole broad earth once refused even a scrap of ground to give birth on! Your goddess was taken in by neither sky nor land nor sea; she was an outcast from the world, until Delos took pity on her wandering and said, 'You roam a stranger on land; I am a wanderer on the waves' — and gave her an unsteady resting place. There she became the mother of two. That is one seventh of what I have borne. I am fortunate — who could deny it? — and fortunate I shall stay: who could doubt that? My abundance keeps me safe. I am too great for Fortune to harm; strip away much as she may, far more she must leave me. My blessings have long outgrown my fears. Suppose it were possible for some of this crowd of my children to be taken from me: even robbed like that, I could not be brought down to Latona's two — and with her two, how far is she, I ask you, from being childless? Go — that's enough sacrifice for the occasion — and take the laurel from your hair!" They took it off, they left the rites unfinished, and they did the one thing still allowed them: they worshiped the goddess in silent murmurs.

Latona was outraged. On the highest peak of Mount Cynthus she spoke to her twin children: "Look at me — your mother, so proud to have borne you, ready to yield to no goddess but Juno alone, and now they doubt whether I am a goddess at all. For all the ages to come I am shut out from the altars that were mine, unless you help me, my children. And that is not my only grief. The daughter of Tantalus has piled insults on top of outrage; she has dared to rank her own children above the two of you, and she has called me childless — may that word fall back on her own head! — showing, the wicked woman, a tongue as vile as her father's." Latona was about to add her pleas to the report, but Apollo said, "Stop — your complaint only puts off her punishment." Diana said the same, and in a swift glide down the air the two of them came, wrapped in cloud, to the citadel of Cadmus.

Near the walls there was a level plain, wide open, forever pounded by horses, where the crowd of wheels and hard hooves had softened the clods beneath them. There, some of Amphion's seven sons were up on their spirited horses, pressing the saddlecloths red with Tyrian dye, wielding reins heavy with gold. Ismenus, who had once been the first burden his mother carried, was wheeling his mount's course into a perfect ring, reining in its foaming mouth, when he cried, "Oh, I'm hit!" — an arrow fixed square in the middle of his chest. The reins slid from his dying hand, and little by little he tipped sideways over the horse's right shoulder to the ground. The next in line, Sipylus, heard the whir of the quiver through the empty air and gave his horse its head — the way a helmsman who senses a coming storm flees at the sight of a cloud and lets out every stretch of sail on all sides, so that not the lightest breeze can slip past unused. He gave the horse its head; and even so the unerring shaft ran him down, an arrow quivering where it lodged at the top of his neck, the bare iron jutting out through his throat. Bent forward as he was, he rolled over the loosened neck and the mane, and fouled the ground with his hot blood. Luckless Phaedimus, and Tantalus, heir to his grandfather's name, had finished their usual riding and moved on to the young men's sport in the gleaming wrestling-ground, slick with oil. They had already locked breast to breast in a tight grapple when a single arrow, sped from the taut string, pierced them both just as they were joined together. They groaned in the same instant, in the same instant laid their pain-twisted bodies on the ground, in the same instant rolled their eyes one last time and breathed out their lives. Alphenor saw it and, beating his torn breast, ran to lift their cold limbs in his arms — and fell in the act of that loving duty, for Apollo tore through the depths of his chest with the deadly steel. When the arrow was pulled free, part of a lung came out on the barbs, and his blood, and his life with it, poured into the air. But it was no single wound that struck long-haired Damasicthon: he was hit where the leg begins, where the muscled hollow of the knee makes the joint soft; and while his hand was still trying to draw out the killing weapon, a second arrow drove through his throat up to the feathers. His own blood forced it out — it sprang up on its own and shot high, spurting far through the air. Last of them, Ilioneus lifted his arms in a prayer that would do him no good, and cried, "O all you gods together — spare me!" — not knowing that he need not beg them all. The god who bears the bow was moved, but by now the arrow could not be called back. Still, Ilioneus died of the lightest wound of any: the shaft did not strike deep into his heart.

The rumor of the disaster, the grief of the people, the tears of her own household made the mother certain of a ruin so sudden — that the gods above had dared it, that they held such power, left her stunned and enraged. Their father Amphion had already driven a sword through his chest and, dying, put an end to his grief and to the light together. Oh, how different was this Niobe from the Niobe who not long before had driven the people back from Latona's altars and paraded head-high through the middle of the city, the envy of her own — a Niobe now to be pitied even by an enemy! She flung herself on the cold bodies and scattered her last kisses over all her sons, sparing none. From them she raised her bruised arms to the sky and said: "Feed on my grief, cruel Latona — feed! Gorge your breast on my mourning, glut your savage heart with seven deaths. Through seven funerals I am carried to my grave. Gloat, triumph, my conquering enemy! But why conquering? In all my misery I still have more than you have in all your happiness. After so many deaths, I win." She had barely spoken when the string twanged from the bent bow — a sound that terrified everyone but Niobe alone. Disaster had made her bold.

The sisters were standing in black, hair loose, before their brothers' biers. One of them, drawing out the arrow that clung in her flesh, sank fainting toward death, her face falling across a brother's body. Another, trying to comfort her wretched mother, fell suddenly silent, doubled over by a wound no one saw, and did not close her lips until her breath had gone. Another dropped in a hopeless attempt to flee; another died across her sister; another hid herself; and another you could see shaking. Six had been given to death, wounded each in a different way, and only the last was left. Her mother, covering her with her whole body, her whole spread robe, cried out, "Leave me one — the youngest! Out of so many I beg for the smallest, only the one." And even as she begged, the girl she begged for was struck down.

Childless, Niobe sat among her dead sons and dead daughters and her husband, and hardened in her grief. No breeze stirred a hair of hers. The color in her face was bloodless. Her eyes stood fixed in her mournful cheeks; there was nothing living in the image. Her very tongue froze against the roof of her hard mouth; her veins stopped being able to move; her neck could not bend, her arms could not gesture, her feet could not walk. Inside her too, deep in her body, was stone. And still she wept; and, caught up in a whirlwind of mighty wind, she was carried off to her native land. There, fixed on a mountaintop, she melts away — and even now the marble weeps its tears.

Then indeed every man and woman feared the plainly shown anger of the goddess, and one and all worshiped with fresh zeal the great power of the goddess who bore the twins. And, as tends to happen, one recent event set them telling older ones. One of them said: "Once, in the rich farmland of Lycia, some country people insulted the goddess — and not for nothing. It is little known, because the men were nobodies, but it is a marvel all the same. I have seen the spot myself, the pool and the lake made famous by the miracle. My father, too old and worn for the road by then, had sent me to bring back some choice oxen from that country, and gave me a native guide for the journey. As we crossed the pastures together, look — there in the middle of a lake stood an ancient altar, black with the ash of old sacrifices, ringed round with trembling reeds. My guide halted and whispered, frightened, 'Be good to me,' and in the same whisper I said, 'Be good to me' as well. But I asked him whether the altar belonged to the Naiads, or to Faunus, or to some local god, and the man answered:

"'No mountain spirit owns this altar, young man. She claims it for her own whom royal Juno once banished from the world — she whom drifting Delos, when it was still a light island swimming on the sea, barely took in at her pleading. There, leaning against a palm and against Minerva's tree, the olive, Latona gave birth to twins in spite of their stepmother Juno. From there, too, the story goes, the new mother fled from Juno, carrying her two children, two divine powers, against her breast. And by now the goddess had come to the land of Lycia, the country that bred the Chimaera, worn out with her long journey and parched by the blazing summer sun; her hungry babies had drained her milk dry. By chance she caught sight of a lake of good water down in the bottom of a valley. Country folk were there, gathering bushy willow-shoots and rushes and the sedge that loves the marsh. Latona came up, knelt, and pressed the ground to scoop up the cool water and drink — and the crowd of peasants told her no. So she spoke to them: "Why do you keep me from the water? Water belongs to everyone. Nature made neither the sun nor the air nor the flowing stream the property of any one person. I have come to a gift meant for all — and yet I beg it of you humbly. I was not going to wash my body or my weary limbs here; I only want to ease my thirst. My mouth is dry even as I speak; my throat is parched; my voice can barely find a way through. A drink of water will be nectar to me, and I will admit that with it I have received my life from you: in that water you will be giving me life. And let these move you too, who stretch their little arms toward you from my breast" — and by chance the children were stretching out their arms.

"'Who would not have been softened by the goddess's gentle words? Yet they went on refusing her, and added threats on top of it, and abuse, telling her to clear off — and that was not enough. They churned up the lake itself with their hands and feet, and stirred the soft mud from the very bottom, leaping this way and that out of pure spite. Anger drove out her thirst. The daughter of Coeus would beg such worthless men no longer, could no longer stand to speak words beneath a goddess. She lifted her hands to the stars and said, "Then live forever in that pool of yours!" The goddess got her wish. It delights them to be under the water, now to plunge their whole bodies in the deep pool, now to lift their heads, now to swim along the surface; often to perch on the bank, often to leap back into the cold lake. But even now they keep working their filthy tongues in quarrels, and shameless — though they are under the water — under the water they still try to hurl their insults. Their voices are hoarse now, their bloated necks swell up, and their very cursing stretches their gaping mouths wide. Their backs run into their heads; their necks look cut away; their spines are green, and their bellies, the largest part of them, are white — and as brand-new frogs they go leaping about in the muddy pond.'"

When this man — whoever he was — had told how those people of Lycia were destroyed, another remembered the satyr Marsyas, whom Apollo, the son of Latona, had beaten in a contest on the reed pipe Minerva invented, and then had punished. "Why are you tearing me out of myself?" the satyr had screamed. "Oh, I'm sorry — oh, a flute isn't worth this much!" And as he screamed, the skin was stripped clean off the surface of his body, until he was nothing but one open wound. Blood ran everywhere; his exposed sinews lay bare; his veins throbbed and flickered with no skin to cover them. You could have counted the organs quivering inside him, and the bright fibers of his lungs behind his ribs. The country-dwellers wept for him — the fauns, the spirits of the woods, his brother satyrs, and Olympus, dear to him even then, and the nymphs, and everyone who pastured woolly flocks and horned herds on those mountains. The rich earth was soaked, and soaking, drank down the falling tears into her deepest veins; and when she had turned them to water, she sent them out into the open air. From there, running to the sea in a rush between steep banks, it bears the name of Marsyas — the clearest river in all of Phrygia.

From tales like these the people came back at once to the here and now, and mourned Amphion cut off with all his line. The mother was the object of everyone's hatred. Yet even her, they say, one man — her brother Pelops — did mourn; and when he pulled his robe down from his shoulder toward his chest, he laid bare the ivory on his left shoulder. At his birth this shoulder had matched the right one in color and had been flesh. Long ago, the story goes, his father cut him limb from limb, and afterward the gods pieced him back together; when they had found the other parts, only the piece between the throat and the top of the arm was missing. Ivory was set in to stand for the part that could not be found — and by that means Pelops was made whole again.
