Book XII

(Part 2 of 2)

This labor, this battle, brought a rest of many days, and both sides paused and laid down their arms. And while a watchful guard kept the Phrygian walls, and a watchful guard kept the Greek trenches, a festival day had come, on which Achilles, the conqueror of Cycnus, was appeasing Pallas with the blood of a fillet-decked heifer. When he had laid its cut portions on the glowing altars, and the smell, welcome to the gods, rose into the sky, the sacred rite had its share, and the rest was served at the table. The chiefs reclined on couches and filled themselves with roasted meat, and eased their cares and thirst with wine. No harps, no songs, no long boxwood pipe of many holes delights them; but they draw out the night in talk, and valor is the matter of their words. They tell of the battles of the enemy and their own, and often gladly recount, in turn, the dangers they have met and come through. For of what else should Achilles speak, or what should be spoken before great Achilles? But most of all the recent victory over Cycnus was the theme: it seemed a marvel to all that the youth's body was pierced by no weapon, took no wound, and blunted the steel itself. Both the grandson of Aeacus and the Greeks were wondering at it, when Nestor said:

"Cycnus was the only despiser of weapons in your time, and pierced by no blow. But I myself once saw Caeneus of Perrhaebia bear a thousand strokes with his body unhurt — Caeneus of Perrhaebia, who, famous for his deeds, lived on Othrys; and, that this might be the more wondrous in him, he had been born a woman." Whoever is there is moved by the strange marvel, and they beg him to tell it; and Achilles among them: "Tell it, come — for we all have the same wish to hear, eloquent old man, wisdom of our age: who was this Caeneus, and why changed to the opposite sex? In what war, in what contest of battle did you know him? By whom was he conquered, if by anyone?"

Then the old man said: Though slow age is against me, and many things I saw in my early years escape me now, still I remember most; and among so many deeds of war and peace, nothing clings more firmly to my mind than that. And if a long life could make anyone a witness of many deeds, I have lived two hundred years; now my third age is being spent.

Caenis, the daughter of Elatus, was famous for her beauty — the loveliest maiden of Thessaly, longed for in vain by the prayers of many suitors through the neighboring cities and through your cities, Achilles, for she was your countrywoman. Perhaps Peleus too would have tried that marriage; but by then either your mother's marriage had fallen to him, or been promised. Caenis married no one; and, walking a lonely shore, she suffered force from the god of the sea — so report told. And when Neptune had taken the joys of this new love, he said, "Let your wishes be safe from all refusal: choose what you desire." (The same report told this too.) Caenis said, "This wrong makes my wish a great one — that I may be able to suffer no such thing again. Grant that I be no longer a woman: you will have granted me everything." She spoke the last words in a deeper tone, and the voice could seem a man's, as it was; for already the god of the deep sea had granted her wish, and had given besides that he could be wounded by no blows and fall to no steel. Glad of the gift, the son of Atrax went off, and spent his life in a man's pursuits, roaming the Peneian fields.

Pirithous, born of bold Ixion, had married Hippodame, and had bidden the cloud-born monsters take their places at the tables set in a tree-shaded cave. The Thessalian nobles were there; I too was there; and the festive palace rang with the mingled crowd. Look — they sing the wedding song, the halls smoke with fires, and the bride comes in, ringed by a company of matrons and young wives, outstanding in beauty. We called Pirithous lucky in his wife — an omen we almost proved false. For your breast, Eurytus, most savage of the savage Centaurs, is fired as much by the wine as by the sight of the girl, and drunkenness, doubled by lust, reigns. At once the overturned tables throw the feast into uproar, and the new bride is dragged off by force, seized by the hair. Eurytus snatches Hippodame; the others each seize the one they fancied or could; and it was the very look of a captured city. The house rings with women's cries. Quickly we all rise, and Theseus first cries, "What madness drives you, Eurytus, that while I live you provoke Pirithous, and, blind, wrong two men in one?" And, that the great-hearted hero might not say it in vain, he pushes off the pressing crowd and takes the seized girl from them in their fury.

Eurytus says nothing in reply — for he cannot defend such deeds with words — but attacks her champion's face with insolent hands and strikes his noble breast. By chance there was near an ancient mixing-bowl, rough with jutting figures, which — huge as it was — the still huger son of Aegeus lifted and hurled full in Eurytus's face; and he, vomiting from wound and mouth together clots of blood and brains and wine, kicked on his back on the soaking sand. His twin-formed brothers blaze at the killing, and all, vying, with one voice cry "Arms! arms!" The wine gave them spirit, and in the first fighting flung cups fly, and shattered jars and curved cauldrons — things once for feasting, now fit for war and slaughter.

First Amycus, son of Ophion, did not fear to strip the inner house of its gifts, and first tore from the shrine a chandelier thick with blazing lamps, and, lifting it high like one who makes to break the white neck of a bull with the sacrificial axe, dashed it against the forehead of Celadon the Lapith and left the bones a shapeless mash in his face. The eyes leaped out, and, the face-bones scattered, the nose was driven back and fixed in the middle of the palate. Pelates of Pella, tearing off the foot of a maple table, laid him low on the ground, his chin sunk on his chest, and, as he spat out teeth mixed with black blood, sent him by a second wound to the shades of Tartarus.

Gryneus, standing next and glaring at the smoking altar, said, "Why not use this?" and lifted the huge altar, fire and all, and threw it into the midst of the Lapiths, and crushed two, Broteas and Orios — Orios, whose mother was Mycale, known to have often drawn down the horns of the struggling moon by her singing. "You will not do it unpunished, if only I get a weapon!" cried Exadius, and, for a weapon, seizes the antlers of a votive stag that hung on a tall pine. With the double branches Gryneus is pierced through the eyes and has them scooped out — part clinging to the antlers, part running down his beard and hanging clotted with gore.

Look — Rhoetus snatches a blazing brand of plum-wood from the middle of the altar and, on the right side, breaks through the temples of Charaxus, covered with yellow hair. His locks, caught by the swift flame, burned like dry corn, and the blood seared in the wound gave a dreadful hissing sound, as red-hot iron often does when the smith draws it out with the curved tongs and plunges it in the trough: it hisses and sinks bubbling in the water. Wounded, Charaxus shakes the greedy fire from his shaggy hair, and lifts to his shoulders a threshold torn from the ground, a wagon's load — whose very weight keeps him from throwing it at the enemy; the stone mass crushed his own comrade Cometes, standing too near. Rhoetus cannot hold his joy: "So, I pray, may the rest of your camp's crowd be brave!" he cries, and renews the wound with the half-burnt stake, and three and four times breaks the sutures of the skull with heavy blows, and the bones sank into the running brain.

Victorious, he passes to Evagrus, Corythus, and Dryas. Of these, when Corythus, his cheeks covered with their first down, had fallen, Evagrus said, "What glory have you won by killing a boy?" Rhoetus lets him say no more, and fiercely buried the red flames in the open mouth of the man as he spoke, and through the mouth into the breast. You too, cruel Dryas, he pursues, whirling fire around his head — but the same end did not wait for you: as he exulted in his run of unbroken slaughter, you pierced him with a fire-hardened stake where the neck joins the shoulder. Rhoetus groaned and barely wrenched the stake from the hard bone, and, drenched in his own blood, fled. Orneus fled too, and Lycabas, and Medon, wounded in the right shoulder, and Thaumas with Pisenor, and Mermeros, who had lately beaten all in the footrace but now went slower for his wound, and Pholus and Melaneus and Abas the boar-hunter, and Astylos the augur, who had vainly warned his own side against this war — he even said to Nessus, dreading his wounds, "Do not flee! You are saved for the bow of Hercules." But Eurynomus and Lycidas and Areos and Imbreus did not escape death; the right hand of Dryas struck them all as they faced him. You too, Crenaeus, took your wound facing him, though you had turned your back in flight: looking back, you took the heavy steel between the two eyes, where the nose joins the brow.

Amid all that din, Aphidas lay dead asleep from the wine he had drunk without stop, unwaking, holding the mixed cup in his slack hand, sprawled on the shaggy skin of an Ossaean bear. When Phorbas saw him from afar, wielding no weapon, he fitted his fingers into the strap and said, "You will drink your wine mixed with the Styx," and, without more delay, hurled the javelin at the youth; and the steel-tipped ash was driven into his neck as he lay by chance on his back. Death came without his feeling it, and the black blood flowed from his full throat onto the couch and into the very cup.

I saw Petraeus trying to tear an acorn-bearing oak from the earth; and while he grips it in his arms and shakes it this way and that and works the loosened trunk, the lance of Pirithous, thrown at Petraeus's ribs, pinned his straining breast to the tough oak. By the valor of Pirithous, they said, Lycus fell, and by the valor of Pirithous Chromis — but each gave less glory to the victor than Dictys and Helops. Helops was run through by a javelin that pierced his temples and, thrown from the right, reached his left ear. Dictys, slipping from the sheer point of a rock as he fled the pursuing son of Ixion in terror, fell headlong and, by the weight of his body, broke a great mountain-ash and impaled his own guts on it. Aphareus comes as avenger, and tries to hurl a rock torn from the mountain; but, as he tries, the son of Aegeus forestalls him with an oaken club and breaks the huge bones of his arm; and, with no leisure or care to give the useless body to death, he leaps onto the back of tall Bianor, not used to carry anyone but himself, and, planting his knee in his ribs and gripping the hair in his left hand, shatters the man's face and threatening features and hard temples with the knotty oak. With that oak he lays low Nedymnus, and Lycopes the javelin-thrower, and Hippasus, his breast covered by his flowing beard, and Ripheus, who towered above the treetops, and Thereus, who used to carry home alive the raging bears he caught in the Thessalian mountains.

Demoleon could bear no longer to see Theseus enjoying this success in the fight, and with vast effort tried to tear an aged pine from the thick wood; and, since he could not, he hurled it, broken short, at his enemy. But Theseus drew far back from the coming missile, warned by Pallas — so at least he wished it thought. Yet the tree did not fall for nothing: it sheared from the throat of tall Crantor both his breast and his left shoulder. He had been your father's armor-bearer, Achilles — one whom Amyntor, king of the Dolopians, beaten in war, had given the son of Aeacus as a pledge of peace. When Peleus saw him from afar, mangled with the foul wound, he cried, "Take this offering, Crantor, dearest of young men," and with a strong arm and all his force hurled his ash spear at Demoleon, which broke through the cage of his ribs and quivered, sticking in the bones. He drew out the shaft without the point by hand — that too barely followed; the point stayed in his lung. The very pain gave his spirit strength: sick, he reared against the enemy and trampled the man with his horse's feet. Peleus takes the ringing blows on helmet and shield, guards his shoulders, holds his arms out before him, and through the shoulders pierces two breasts at one stroke. But first, from afar, he had killed Phlegraeus and Hyles, and, in close combat, Iphinoüs and Clanis; to these is added Dorylas, who wore a wolf's skin on his temples, and, for a cruel weapon, the crooked horns of oxen reddened with much blood.

To him I said — for courage gave me strength — "See how far your horns fall short of my steel," and threw my javelin. When he could not dodge it, he held up his right hand before the brow that would take the wound; the hand was pinned to the brow. A shout went up; and Peleus — for he stood nearer — struck him as he stuck fast, overcome by the cruel wound, with his sword beneath the middle of the belly. He leaped forward, fiercely dragged his own bowels on the ground, trod on them as he dragged, burst them as he trod, tangled his legs in them too, and fell with an emptied belly.

Nor did your beauty save you in battle, Cyllarus — if we grant beauty to that monstrous nature. His beard was starting, the color of his beard gold, and golden hair hung from his shoulders to the middle of his flanks. There was a pleasing spirit in his face; his neck and shoulders and hands and breast were near the praised statues of the artists, in every part where he was a man; nor was the horse-shape beneath faulty or worse than the man's. Give him a horse's neck and head, and he would be worthy of Castor: so fit for a rider was his back, so raised with muscle his chest. He was all blacker than black pitch, but his tail was white, and the color of his legs was white too. Many of his own kind sought him, but Hylonome alone won him, than whom no lovelier female lived among the half-beasts in the high woods. She alone holds Cyllarus by coaxing and by loving and by confessing her love, and by her grooming too, as far as those limbs allow: so that her hair is smoothed with a comb, that she twines herself now with rosemary, now with violet or rose, sometimes wears white lilies, and twice a day washes her face in the springs that fall from the height of the Pagasaean wood, and twice dips her body in the stream, and throws over her shoulder or her left side no skins but such as suit her, of chosen beasts. Their love was equal: they wandered the mountains together, went into the caves together; and now they had entered the Lapith house together, together were waging the fierce war. The thrower is unknown, but a javelin came from the left and pierced you, Cyllarus, just below where the breast joins the neck. The heart, hurt by that small wound, grew cold with the whole body once the weapon was drawn out. At once Hylonome takes his dying limbs, warms the wound with her hand laid on it, sets her mouth to his, and strives to stop the fleeing life; and when she sees him dead, having said something the din kept from my ears, she fell on the weapon that had pierced him, and, dying, embraced her husband.

He stands before my eyes too — Phaeocomes, who had bound six lions' skins together with knots, and covered both man and horse: he, hurling the trunk of a tree two yoke of oxen could hardly move, broke Tectaphon, son of Olenus, on the top of his head. The very broad round of his skull was crushed, and through his mouth and hollow nostrils and eyes and ears his soft brains poured, as curdled milk drips through oaken wickerwork, or as liquid oozes under a well-pierced sieve and is squeezed out thick through the many holes. But I, while he was making to strip the fallen man of his arms — your father knows this — plunged my sword into the base of his belly as he plundered. Chthonius too, and Teleboas, lie slain by my sword; the first had borne a two-forked bough for a weapon, the other a javelin, and with his javelin he wounded me — you see the marks; the old scar still shows. Then I should have been sent to the taking of Troy; then I could have stayed, if not overcome, the arms of great Hector with mine! But at that time there was no Hector, or only a boy — and now my age fails me. Why tell you of Periphas, conqueror of two-formed Pyraethus, or of Ampyx, who fixed his pointless cornel-spear full in the face of four-footed Echeclus? Macareus laid low the Pelethronian Erigdupus by driving a crowbar into his breast; and I remember a hunting-spear thrown by Nessus's hand buried in the groin of Cymelus. And do not think Mopsus, son of Ampycus, only foretold the future: at Mopsus's throw a two-formed monster fell, and Hodites tried in vain to speak, his tongue nailed to his chin and his chin to his throat.

Caeneus had put five to death — Styphelus and Bromus and Antimachus and Elymus and axe-bearing Pyracmon: their wounds I do not remember, but I marked their number and names. Latreus, huge in limbs and body, sallied out, armed with the spoils of Emathian Halesus, whom he had slain. His age was between youth and old man; his force was a youth's; gray flecked his temples. Conspicuous with shield and helmet and Macedonian pike, turning his face to both ranks, he shook his arms, rode in one same circle, and poured out these many words boldly into the empty air: "And shall I put up with you too, Caenis? For to me you will always be a woman, to me always Caenis. Does your birth-origin not shame your spirit? Does it not come to mind by what deed you got your reward, and at what price the false shape of a man? See what you were born, or what you submitted to; go, take up a distaff with your work-baskets and twist the thread with your thumb — leave war to men." As he boasted such things, Caeneus, with a hurled lance, tore open his stretched-out side as he ran, where man was joined to horse. He raves with pain and strikes at the bare face of the Phyllean youth with his pike: it bounces back no otherwise than hail from a housetop, or as if one struck a hollow drum with a little pebble. He comes to close quarters and struggles to bury his sword in the tough side — but the parts are impervious to the sword. "Yet you will not escape me! You will be killed by the middle of the blade, since the point is blunt," he says, and slants the sword against his side, and clasps his belly with his long right arm. The blow gives a groan, as a body of marble struck; and the shattered blade flew apart on striking the neck. When Caeneus had shown his unhurt limbs enough to his amazement, "Come now," he said, "let us try your body with my steel!" and up to the hilt he drove his deadly sword into the shoulder, and worked his hand, unseen, into the entrails, and made a wound within the wound.

Look — the double-limbed monsters, enraged, rush in with a vast roar, and all hurl and thrust their weapons at him alone. The weapons fall blunted; unstabbed and bloodless the Elatean Caeneus stays from every blow. The strange thing left them stunned. "O huge disgrace!" cries Monychus. "A whole people, we are beaten by one, and scarcely a man — though he is a man, and we, by our craven deeds, are what he once was. What use are our huge limbs? Our double strength? That our twofold nature has joined the strongest creatures in us? I do not believe we were born of a goddess, nor of Ixion, who was so great he could hope for lofty Juno. We are beaten by a half-male foe! Heap on him stones and beams and whole mountains, and crush out his stubborn breath with cast-down woods! Let a whole wood choke his jaws, and weight will stand for wounds." He spoke, and, chancing on a tree thrown down by the mad force of the south wind, hurled it at the strong foe; and he was an example to the rest, and in a short time Othrys was bare of trees and Pelion had no shade. Buried under the huge heap, Caeneus struggles beneath the weight of the wood and bears the piled oaks on his hard shoulders; but when the load grew over his face and head and his breath had no air to draw, at times he faints, at times he tries in vain to lift himself into the open and to roll off the cast woods — and sometimes he moves it, as, look, if lofty Ida were shaken by earthquakes. The end is doubtful: some said his body was thrust down to roomy Tartarus by the mass of wood; the son of Ampycus denied it, and saw, from the middle of the heap, a bird with tawny wings go out into the clear air — which then for the first time, and then for the last, was seen by me. When Mopsus saw it surveying his camp in gentle flight and clanging around it with a great cry, following it with eyes and heart, he said, "Hail, glory of the Lapith race, once greatest of men, but now the only bird of your kind, Caeneus!" The thing was believed on his word. Grief added anger, and we bore it hard that one had been overwhelmed by so many foes; nor did we stop working our weapons in blood before part were killed and flight and night took the rest away.

As the Pylian told this fight between the Lapiths and the half-human Centaurs, Tlepolemus could not bear his grief that Hercules had been passed over in silence, and said, "It is strange, old man, that you should forget the deeds of Hercules; my father himself often used to tell me that these cloud-born monsters were tamed by him." The Pylian, sad at this, said, "Why do you force me to remember my sorrows, to tear open grief hidden by the years, and to confess my hatred and offense against your father? He did indeed do things beyond belief, and filled the world with his services — which I could rather wish could be denied; but we praise neither Deiphobus nor Polydamas nor Hector himself: for who would praise an enemy? That father of yours once leveled the walls of Messene, and destroyed guiltless cities, Elis and Pylos, and drove sword and fire into my home. And, to say nothing of the others he killed, we were twice six sons of Neleus, a fine band; twice six fell to the might of Hercules, all but me alone. That the others could be beaten, one might bear; but the death of Periclymenus is a marvel, to whom Neptune, founder of the Neleian line, had given the power to take whatever shapes he chose and, once taken, to lay them aside. When he had in vain turned into every other shape, he turned into the bird that carries the lightnings in its curved talons, dearest to the king of the gods; using the bird's strength — its wings, its hooked beak, its clawed talons — he tore the hero's face. The Tirynthian aims at him his too-unerring bow and hits him as he carries his limbs aloft among the clouds and hangs in the air, where the wing joins the side. The wound was not great, but the sinews, cut by it, fail him and deny him motion and the strength to fly. He fell to the earth, his weakened wings not catching the air; and where the light arrow had stuck in the wing, it was pressed farther by the weight of the falling body, and driven up through the top of the side into the left of the neck. Do I seem, then, to owe praises to the deeds of your Hercules, most handsome leader of the Rhodian fleet? Yet I will avenge my brothers no further than by keeping silent on his brave deeds; my friendship with you is firm."

When the son of Neleus had said this with his sweet tongue, and the gift of Bacchus was taken up again after the old man's tale, they rose from the couches; the rest of the night was given to sleep.

But the god who rules the sea's waters with his trident grieves, with a father's love, for the body of his son turned into the bird named for Sthenelus's son, and, hating cruel Achilles, presses his remembering wrath more than in ordinary measure. And now, the war dragged out nearly twice five years, he speaks thus to the unshorn Smintheus: "O dearest by far to me of my brother's sons, who built with me the walls of Troy in vain — do you not groan when you see these towers so soon to fall? Do you not grieve that so many thousands are slain defending these walls? Does not — to name no others — the ghost of Hector, dragged around his own Pergamum, come to you? And still fierce Achilles, bloodier than war itself, lives on, the ravager of our work. Let him but give himself to me, and I will make him feel what I can do with my triple spear; but since we may not meet the enemy hand to hand, destroy him unawares with a hidden arrow." Apollo nodded, and, indulging both his uncle's wrath and his own, veiled in a cloud, came to the Trojan army, and, in the midst of the slaughter, saw Paris scattering his darts, at intervals, among the common Greeks; and, revealing himself as a god, said, "Why waste your arrows on the blood of the rabble? If you have any care for your own, turn on the grandson of Aeacus, and avenge your slaughtered brothers." He spoke, and, pointing at the son of Peleus mowing down Trojan bodies with the sword, turned the bow at him and guided the sure arrow with a deadly hand. This was the one thing that old Priam could rejoice at after Hector. So you, Achilles, conqueror of such great men, are conquered by the cowardly stealer of a Greek wife! But if you had to fall by a woman's war, you would rather have fallen by the Amazon's axe.

Now that terror of the Phrygians, the glory and shield of the Greek name, the grandson of Aeacus, a head unconquerable in war, had burned: the same god had armed him and had burned him. Now he is ash, and of Achilles, so great, there remains some little thing that would not well fill an urn — but his glory lives, to fill the whole world. This measure suits that man, and in this the son of Peleus is equal to himself, and knows no empty Tartarus. Even his shield stirs a war, so you may know whose it was; and arms are wielded for arms. The son of Tydeus does not dare claim them, nor Ajax son of Oïleus, nor the younger son of Atreus, nor the elder, greater in war and years, nor any others: only the sons of Telamon and of Laertes had confidence for so great a prize. To spare himself the burden and the odium of a decision, the descendant of Tantalus bade the Greek leaders sit in the midst of the camp, and passed the judgment of the dispute to them all.
