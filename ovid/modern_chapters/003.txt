Book II

(Part 2 of 2)

How often she wandered before her own old house, through the fields that had once been hers! How often the baying of hounds drove her over the crags, and she — a huntress in her day — fled in terror from the hunters! Often she cowered at the sight of wild beasts, forgetting what she now was, and, a bear herself, shuddered at the bears she saw on the mountains, and went in dread of the wolves, though her own father ran among them as one of them.

Then look — Arcas comes, the child of Lycaon's daughter, knowing nothing of the mother who bore him. Some fifteen birthdays had passed over him. He was hunting the wild game, picking out the likeliest glades, ringing the Erymanthian woods with his woven nets, when he stumbled on his mother. She stopped dead at the sight of Arcas, and looked for all the world like something that knew him. He drew back, unnerved, not understanding why this bear kept her eyes fixed on him and would not look away; and when she pressed closer, eager to come to him, he was on the point of driving his hunting-spear straight through her breast. But the Almighty stayed his hand. In one stroke he swept away both of them and the horror about to be done, carried them off together through the empty air on a rushing wind, and set them in the sky, and made them neighboring constellations — the Great Bear and the Little.

Juno swelled with fury when her rival blazed among the stars. Down she went to the sea, to gray old Tethys and ancient Ocean, whom even the gods so often hold in awe; and when they asked the reason for her coming, she began: "You want to know why I, the queen of the gods, have left my home in heaven and come down to you? Because another woman holds the sky in my place! Call me a liar if, when night has darkened the world, you do not see stars newly honored in the very height of heaven — my open wound — set there where the last and smallest circle wheels around the topmost point of the axis. So now there is every reason to think twice before wronging Juno, every reason to tremble at offending me — me, whose only way of helping is to harm! Oh, what a great thing I have done! How vast my power is! I forbade her to be human — and she has been made a goddess! This is how I punish the guilty; this is my mighty authority! Let him give her back her old face and strip off this beast-shape, the way he did before for that Argive girl, Io. Why not divorce Juno outright, marry the creature, move her into my bed, and take Lycaon for a father-in-law? But you — if the insult done to your foster-child touches you at all — bar the seven stars of the Wain from your blue depths, drive off from the sky those constellations taken up as the wages of adultery, and let no mistress bathe in your clean waters!"

The gods of the sea gave their consent. Juno stepped up into her nimble chariot through the clear air, drawn by her painted peacocks — peacocks so lately given their colors, when Argus was killed, just as lately, chattering raven, as you were suddenly turned to black-winged, you who had been white before. For this bird was once silver, with snowy feathers, the equal of any spotless dove; he yielded nothing to the geese whose watchful cries would one day save the Capitol, nor to the swan that haunts the rivers. His tongue was his ruin: because of that gossiping tongue, the color that was white is now the very opposite of white.

There was no one lovelier in all of Thessaly than Coronis of Larissa. She certainly pleased you, god of Delphi — Apollo — as long as she stayed faithful, or went unwatched. But Apollo's bird, the raven, caught her out in her betrayal, and the pitiless informer set his course straight for his master, to lay her hidden guilt bare. After him, flapping her wings, came the chattering crow, wanting to hear the whole story; and when she learned the reason for his errand, she said, "You're flying an unlucky road. Don't scorn what my voice foretells. Think what I once was and what I am now, and ask what I did to deserve it — you'll find that loyalty was my downfall.

"There was a time when Pallas Minerva had shut up the infant Erichthonius, a child born of no mother, in a basket woven of Athenian osier, and handed it to the three daughters of two-formed Cecrops to guard, with strict orders: they were not to pry into her secret. Hidden in the light leaves of a thick elm, I watched to see what they would do. Two of them — Pandrosos and Herse — kept the trust without treachery. Only Aglauros called her sisters cowards, and undid the knots with her own hand; and inside they saw a baby, and a snake stretched out beside it. I carried the news to the goddess — and this is the thanks I get for it: I am cast out from Minerva's protection, and ranked below the bird of night, the owl! Let my punishment be a warning to birds not to go courting danger with their tongues.

"And mind — it wasn't as if she came looking for me, when I never asked for such a favor. Ask Pallas herself, if you doubt it; angry as she is, not even her anger will deny me this. I am the daughter of Coroneus, a man famous in the land of Phocis — this is common knowledge — and I was a princess, a virgin of royal blood, courted by wealthy suitors; so don't look down on me. My beauty was my misfortune. For as I was pacing the shore one day with my slow, usual step, along the top of the sand, the god of the sea, Neptune, saw me and caught fire. When he had wasted his time in vain, coaxing me with soft words, he turned to force and gave chase. I ran, I left the packed shore behind and wore myself out for nothing in the soft sand. Then I called on gods and men — but no mortal ear caught my voice. A virgin goddess was moved for a virgin, and came to my aid. I was stretching my arms toward the sky when those arms began to darken with soft feathers. I fought to throw the clothing off my shoulders — but it was already plumage, its roots sunk deep into my skin. I tried to beat my bare breast with my palms, but I no longer had either palms or bare breast. I ran, and the sand no longer dragged at my feet as before; I was lifted clear of the ground, and soon I was carried high through the air and given to Minerva as her blameless companion.

"And what good has it done me? — when Nyctimene, made a bird for a monstrous crime, has taken my place of honor. Or haven't you heard the thing they all know throughout Lesbos, how Nyctimene defiled her own father's bed? She is a bird, true enough — but knowing her guilt, she flees the sight of men and the daylight, and hides her shame in the dark, and every bird in the whole sky drives her out."

To all this the raven only said, "Choke on your warnings, I pray — I've no use for an empty omen." And he did not give up the road he had begun, but told his master he had seen Coronis lying with a young Thessalian man. At the word of his beloved's guilt, the laurel slipped from Apollo's brow; in the same instant the god's face changed, his color drained, the plectrum dropped from his hand. And as his heart boiled up with swelling rage, he snatched his familiar weapons, bent the bow from horn to horn, and drove an arrow no one could dodge through that breast — the very breast so often pressed against his own. She cried out at the blow, and drawing the steel from the wound she drenched her white limbs with crimson blood, and said, "I could have paid you what I owed, Phoebus — but I should have given birth first. Now two of us die in one." That was all; her life poured out with her blood, and the deadly chill of death crept over the body that no longer held a soul.

Too late — too late! — the lover repents of his cruel revenge, and hates himself for listening, for flaring up so fast. He hates the bird that forced him to learn the crime and the cause of his grief; he hates the bowstring, the bow, his own hand, and, with the hand, those reckless weapons, the arrows. He gathers her fallen body up and tries, too late, to beat back fate with his skill, and works his healing arts on her for nothing. And when he saw that all his efforts failed, that the pyre was being built and her limbs were about to burn in the last flames, then he groaned from the very bottom of his heart — for a god's face may not be wet with tears — groaning like a heifer who stands watching while the hammer, swung from beside the butcher's right ear, crashes down and shatters the hollow temples of her suckling calf. Yet after he had poured perfumes she could not feel over her breast, held her in a last embrace, and paid the funeral rites she should never have needed so soon, Apollo would not let his own seed sink into those same ashes. He tore the child from the flames and from his mother's womb and carried him to the cave of two-formed Chiron. And the raven, who had counted on a reward for telling no lie — him he forbade forever to take his place among the white birds.

Meanwhile Chiron, the half-beast, was delighted with a pupil of divine blood, glad of the honor that came with the charge. And look — the Centaur's daughter arrives, her shoulders wrapped in her red-gold hair. The nymph Chariclo had once borne her on the banks of a rushing stream and named her Ocyrhoe. This girl was not content to master her father's arts; she sang the secrets of the Fates. So when she had caught the prophetic frenzy in her mind and glowed with the god shut up inside her breast, she looked at the infant and said: "Grow, child — you who will bring the whole world healing! Again and again mortal bodies will owe their lives to you. You will be allowed to call back the dead — and once, having dared that against the will of the gods, you will be stopped from doing it again by your grandfather's fire. From a god you will become a bloodless corpse, and from that corpse a god once more, and twice you will make your own destiny new.

"And you too, dear father — immortal now, born under the law of living forever — you will long to be able to die, that day when you writhe in agony, the blood of the deadly serpent seeping through your torn limbs. The gods will make you mortal, you who cannot die, and the three goddesses of fate will loose your thread at last." Something more remained to be said. She sighed from the depths of her breast, tears welled and slid down her cheeks, and she said: "The Fates cut me short; I am forbidden to say more, and the use of my voice is being taken from me. My arts were not worth the price — not worth drawing a god's anger down on me. I wish I had never known the future! Already the human shape seems to be draining out of me; already grass is a delight to eat; already I long to gallop over the wide plains. I am turning into a mare, into a body like my father's. And yet — why wholly a mare? My father, after all, is of two shapes."

As she spoke, the last part of her lament grew hard to follow; her words ran together. Soon they were not words at all, nor quite the sound of a mare, but of someone imitating one, and in a little while she was neighing outright and had lowered her arms into the grass. Then her fingers drew together, and a single smooth hoof of unbroken horn bound her five nails into one; her face and her neck stretched out long; the greater part of her sweeping robe became a tail, and the loose hair that streamed down her neck fell over to the right and became a mane. Voice and shape were made new together — and the marvel gave her a new name as well.

Chiron, son of Philyra, wept, and begged your help, god of Delphi, but in vain. For you could not undo great Jupiter's decrees, and even if you could have, you were not there to do it — you were away in Elis and the fields of Messenia. This was the season when a shepherd's hide clothed you, when a rough cudgel filled your left hand and, in the other, a pipe of seven unequal reeds. And while love was your whole concern, while your own pipe was soothing you, some cattle — so they say — wandered untended into the plains of Pylos. The son of Maia, the daughter of Atlas — Mercury — spotted them and, with his usual cunning, hid the stolen beasts away in the woods. No one had noticed the theft except one old man, well known in that country; the whole neighborhood called him Battus. He served as watchman over the glades and grassy pastures of rich Neleus and his herds of fine mares.

Mercury was wary of him, and drew him gently aside by the hand. "Whoever you are, stranger," he said, "if anyone comes asking after these cattle, say you never saw them — and so you don't go unrewarded for it, take this fine cow." And he gave him one. The old man took it and answered, "Go on, you're safe with me. That stone there will tell of your theft before I ever do" — and he pointed to a stone. The son of Jupiter pretended to leave. But soon he came back, changing his shape and his voice, and said, "Countryman, if you've seen any cattle come along this path, help me out and don't keep the theft a secret — you'll have a cow and her bull together as your reward." And when the price was thus doubled, the old man said, "They'll be under those hills over there" — and under those hills they were. Mercury laughed and said, "You'd betray me to my own face, would you, you cheat? Betray me to myself?" And he turned that perjured heart into hard flint, the stone that even now is called the Informer — an old disgrace clinging to a rock that never earned it.

From there the god of the winged sandals lifted himself on level wings and, as he flew, looked down on the fields of Munychia, on the land dear to Minerva, and on the tended groves of the Lyceum. It happened that on that day the chaste young women were carrying, in their purity, the sacred offerings on their heads in flower-crowned baskets, up to Pallas Minerva's festal citadel. The winged god watched them coming back down, and did not hold his course straight on but wheeled around in a circle — the way the kite, quickest of birds, when it spots the entrails of a sacrifice but is nervous, with the priests crowded thick around it, banks in tight rings and dares not stray far off, and hovers, greedy, on beating wings above the thing it wants. Just so the swift Cyllenian god leaned his flight over the towers of Athens and circled the same stretch of air. As much as the Morning Star outshines the other stars, and as much as the golden Moon outshines the Morning Star, so much did Herse outshine every other girl as she walked — the crown and glory of the whole procession and of her companions. The son of Jupiter was struck dumb by her beauty; hanging there in the air, he caught fire — the way a leaden pellet slung from a Balearic sling flies and grows red-hot as it goes, finding under the clouds a fire it never had before.

He turned aside, left heaven, and took a new road, and made no attempt to hide who he was — such was his confidence in his own good looks. And though that confidence was justified, still he took pains to help it along: he smoothed his hair, arranged his cloak to hang just so, letting the border and all its gold show, made sure the polished wand — the one that summons and banishes sleep — was in his right hand, and that his winged sandals gleamed on his spotless feet.

Deep in the house there were three bedchambers finished in ivory and tortoiseshell. Yours, Pandrosos, was on the right, Aglauros had the left, and Herse the one between. Aglauros, in the left-hand room, was the first to notice Mercury coming, and dared to ask the god his name and the reason for his visit. And the grandson of Atlas and Pleione answered her: "I am the one who carries my father's commands through the air — and my father is Jupiter himself. I won't make up excuses. Just be loyal to your sister, be willing to be called aunt to my child. Herse is the reason I've come; help a lover, I beg you." Aglauros eyed him with the very same look she had turned on the hidden secrets of golden-haired Minerva, and demanded, as the price of her help, a great weight of gold — and meanwhile made him wait outside.

The warrior goddess turned the orbs of her stern eyes upon her and heaved a sigh so deep it shook her breast and the aegis that lay against her mighty chest. It came back to her: that this was the hand that had laid bare her secret, when Aglauros had looked, against Minerva's own command, on the motherless child of Vulcan, the god of Lemnos — and that now she would be dear both to the god and to her sister, and rich besides on the gold her greed had demanded. At once Minerva went to the house of Envy, filthy with black corruption. Her home lies hidden in the depths of a valley, starved of sun, closed to every wind, dismal, brimming with a numbing cold, forever without a fire and forever choked with fog.

When the goddess who is a terror in war reached the place, she stopped before the door — for she thought it wrong to go under that roof — and struck the doorposts with the tip of her spear. The doors shuddered open. Inside she saw Envy eating the flesh of vipers, the food that feeds her own vices, and at the sight she turned her eyes away. But Envy dragged herself up sluggishly from the ground, left the half-gnawed bodies of the snakes, and shuffled forward with a listless step. And when she saw the goddess in her beauty and shining armor, she groaned and drew a long sigh at the very sight. Pallor sits on her face, and all her body is wasted; she never looks at you straight; her teeth are rotted black; her breast is green with gall, her tongue drips venom. She never smiles, unless the sight of another's suffering moves her to it; she gets no sleep, kept awake by gnawing cares, but watches men's successes with misery and pines away at the watching. She tears at others and is torn apart in the tearing, her own perpetual torment. Still, hateful to her as Envy was, Minerva spoke to her briefly: "Infect one of the daughters of Cecrops with your poison. It has to be done. Aglauros is the one." Saying no more, she pushed off from the ground with her planted spear and was gone.

Envy, watching the goddess flee with a sidelong glare, muttered under her breath, grudging Minerva her success, and took up her staff, wreathed all over with brambles. Wrapped in black clouds, wherever she goes she tramples the flowering fields, scorches the grass, and crops the tops off the blossoms; with her breath she fouls whole peoples, cities, and homes. At last she caught sight of the citadel of Athens, thriving in arts and wealth and glad peace — and she could barely hold back her tears, because she saw nothing there to weep at. But once she had entered the bedroom of Cecrops's daughter, she carried out her orders. She laid her hand, stained with rust, on the girl's breast, packed her heart with barbed thorns, breathed a poisonous venom into her, and spread it black as pitch through her bones and deep into the middle of her lungs. And so that the seeds of the sickness would not stray too widely, she set the girl's sister before her eyes — the fortunate marriage, the god in all his beauty — and made every detail of it enormous.

Goaded by all this, Cecrops's daughter is gnawed by a hidden grief; she groans, tormented by night and tormented by day, and wastes away, wretched, in a slow decline, like ice worn down by a fitful sun. She smolders at happy Herse's good fortune — smolders the way a fire smothered under thorny brushwood sends up no flame but burns with a low, creeping heat. Again and again she wanted to die, so as not to see such a thing; again and again to tell it all, as if it were a crime, to her stern father. In the end she sat herself down across the threshold to shut the god out when he came. And when he tried his coaxing, his pleas, his gentlest words, she said, "Stop this. I'm not moving from here until you're driven off." "Done — let's hold to that bargain," said the swift Cyllenian god, and touched the carved door open with his wand. But when she tried to rise, the parts we bend to sit down would not move for their own dead weight. She fought to lift herself straight up, but the joints of her knees were rigid; a chill slid down through her nails, and her veins went pale as the blood drained away. And as an incurable cancer spreads in every direction and adds the sound flesh to the ruined, so, little by little, the deadly cold crept into her breast and closed off the passages of life and breath. She made no attempt to speak — and if she had, there was no road left for her voice. Stone already held her neck; her face had hardened, and she sat there, a bloodless statue. And the stone was not white: her own mind had stained it dark.

When the grandson of Atlas had punished her words and her unholy heart this way, he left the land that bears Pallas's name and rose into the sky on beating wings. His father called him aside, and, not admitting the real cause — his own new love — said, "My son, faithful carrier of my orders, shake off delay, drop down swift as ever on your usual course, and make for the country that looks up on the left at the constellation your mother is part of — the natives call it Sidon. There's a royal herd you'll see grazing far off on the mountain grass; drive it down to the shore." He spoke, and already the young bulls, driven off the mountain, were making for the shore he had named, where the great king's daughter — Europa — used to play, attended by her Tyrian companions.

Majesty and love do not sit well together, nor keep house in the same place. So the father and ruler of the gods, whose right hand is armed with the three-forked lightning, who shakes the world with a nod, laid aside the weight of his rule and put on the shape of a bull. Mixing in with the cattle, he lowed and strolled, beautiful, over the tender grass. His color was the white of snow that no hard hoof has trodden and no rain-bringing south wind has melted. The muscles bunched on his neck, and a dewlap hung down from his shoulders; his horns were small, true — but you'd have sworn a craftsman made them by hand, clearer and more shining than any polished gem. There was no menace in his forehead, nothing to fear in his eye; his whole face wore peace.

Agenor's daughter marveled that he was so beautiful, and that he threatened no attack; but gentle as he was, at first she was afraid to touch him. Little by little she came closer and held out flowers to his white mouth. The lover rejoiced, and, while the pleasure he hoped for held off, kissed her hands — barely, oh barely, could he put off the rest. Now he frolicked and skipped on the green grass; now he laid his snowy flank down on the yellow sand. And as her fear ebbed away, bit by bit, he offered his chest now for her girl's hand to pat, now his horns to be tangled with fresh garlands. At last the princess, not knowing whose back she pressed, even dared to climb up and sit on the bull — and the god, easing gradually off the land and away from the dry shore, set the false hoofs of his feet first in the shallows at the water's edge. Then he went further out, and carried his prize away over the open middle of the sea. She is terrified, and, borne off, looks back at the shore she is leaving; her right hand grips a horn, the other rests on his back, and her fluttering robes billow in the wind.
