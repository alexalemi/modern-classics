Book V

(Part 2 of 2)

Calliope brushed her thumb across the plaintive strings to try them, and to the struck chords she wedded this song:

Ceres was the first to break the clod with the curved plough, the first to give the earth its grain and its gentle nourishment, the first to give it laws. Everything we have is the gift of Ceres. She must be my song — if only I could sing verses worthy of the goddess. And she is surely worthy of a song.

The vast three-cornered island of Sicily lies heaped upon the limbs of a giant, and with its enormous weight it pins down Typhoeus, who once dared to hope for a home in heaven. He strains, of course, and struggles again and again to rise — but his right hand is trapped beneath Pelorus, the cape toward Italy, his left beneath you, Pachynus, and his legs are crushed under Lilybaeum, while Etna weighs down his head. Beneath it Typhoeus lies on his back, flinging up sand and vomiting flame from his savage mouth. Often he wrestles to shove off the weight of the land, to roll the towns and the great mountains from his body — and then the earth shudders, and even the king of the silent dead trembles, afraid the ground will split, gape wide in a great chasm, and let the daylight come pouring in to terrify the cowering ghosts.

Fearing that ruin, the lord of the dead had come up out of his shadowy kingdom, and, riding in his chariot behind black horses, he went carefully round the foundations of the Sicilian land. When he had searched enough to be sure that no part of the ground was giving way, he laid his fear aside. But Venus, sitting on her own mountain of Eryx, saw him wandering there, and, drawing her winged son into her arms, she said: "My son — my weapon, my hands, my power — take up those arrows of yours that conquer everyone, Cupid, and drive your swift shafts into the heart of the god who drew the last lot when the three brothers split the world three ways, the one who got the realm of the dead. You master the gods above, and Jupiter himself; you master the beaten powers of the sea and the very one who rules them. Why should the underworld hold out? Why not spread your empire — and your mother's — over it too? A third of the whole world is at stake. And yet even in heaven, such is the patience I've been reduced to, we are treated with contempt, and the power of Love dwindles along with mine. Don't you see how Pallas has slipped away from me, and Diana with her hunting-javelin? And Ceres' daughter, too, will stay a virgin, if we let her — for she is reaching after the very same hopes. So, for the sake of the realm we would share, if that means anything to you, join the goddess to her uncle." So Venus spoke. He opened his quiver and, as his mother wished, set apart out of his thousand arrows a single one — but the sharpest of them all, the surest, the most obedient to the bow. He braced the pliant horn against his knee, bent it, and struck Pluto through the heart with the barbed reed.

Not far from the walls of Henna lies a deep lake named Pergus. Not even the Cayster, gliding along, hears more swan-song on its waters than that lake does. A wood crowns the pool, ringing it on every side, and with its leaves, like an awning, it keeps off the blows of the sun. The branches give coolness, the moist ground gives flowers of Tyrian purple. It is spring there without end. In this grove Proserpine was playing, gathering violets or white lilies, filling her baskets and the fold of her dress with a girl's eager delight and racing her friends to pick the most — when, almost in the same instant, Pluto saw her, loved her, and swept her away. So headlong was his love. Terrified, the goddess cried out in her grief for her mother and her friends — but for her mother most of all. And when she tore her dress from its upper hem, the flowers she had gathered spilled from the loosened folds. So untouched was she in her childish years that even this loss — the fallen flowers — stirred a sorrow in her. Her captor drove his chariot on, urging his horses forward, calling each by its name, shaking along their necks and manes the reins stained a dark iron-rust. He was carried past deep lakes, past the pools of the Palici that reek of sulphur and boil up where the earth has burst open, and past the place where the Bacchiadae — a people sprung from Corinth of the two seas — built their city walls between two harbors of unequal size.

Between Cyane and Pisaean Arethusa there is a stretch of water that draws together, penned in by two narrow horns of land. Here lived Cyane, most famous of the Sicilian nymphs, and the pool took its name from her. From the middle of the water she rose, bare to the waist, and knew the god. "You'll go no further!" she cried. "You cannot be Ceres' son-in-law against her will. The girl should have been asked for, not stolen. And if I may compare small things with great, Anapis loved me too — but he wooed me, and I married him won over, not frightened into it, the way this girl has been." She spoke, and, spreading her arms wide to either side, she blocked his way. The son of Saturn held his rage no longer. Spurring on his terrible horses, he swung his royal sceptre with his strong arm and drove it down into the very bottom of the pool. The struck earth opened a road down to Tartarus and took the plunging chariot into the mouth of the crater.

But Cyane, grieving for the goddess snatched away and for the slighted rights of her own spring, carried an inconsolable wound in her silent heart, and wasted entirely into tears. Into the very waters whose great presiding spirit she had just now been, she melted away. You could see her limbs soften, her bones grow supple and bend, her nails lose their hardness. The slenderest parts of her went first, dissolving — her sea-blue hair, her fingers, her legs and feet, for it is a short passage from such thin limbs into cold water. After these her shoulders, her back, her sides, her breast faded away into thin trickling streams. At the last, in place of living blood, clear water crept into her failing veins, and nothing was left that a hand could hold.

Meanwhile the frightened mother searched for her daughter through every land, over every sea — and searched in vain. Dawn, coming up with dew still in her hair, never found her resting; neither did the evening star. In both hands she kindled pine torches at the fires of Etna and carried them, never resting, through the frost-bound dark. And again, when the kindly day had dimmed the stars, she went on hunting for her daughter from the setting of the sun to its rising. Worn out with her labor, she grew thirsty, and no spring had washed her lips, when by chance she caught sight of a little cottage roofed with thatch, and knocked at its small door. Out came an old woman, who saw the goddess, and when Ceres begged for water gave her a sweet drink she had covered over with toasted barley-meal. While Ceres drank what she was given, a bold, hard-faced boy stood in front of her, laughed, and called her greedy. The goddess took offense, and with the part of the drink not yet swallowed she flung the barley-and-water full in his face as he spoke. His skin drank in the spots. Where a moment before he had arms, now he had legs; a tail was added to his altered body. He shrank into a tiny shape — so there would be no great power in him to do harm — smaller in measure than a little lizard. The old woman stared and wept and reached to touch the strange thing, but it fled from her and made for a hiding place. Its name suits its shame: the star-spotted newt, its body flecked all over with scattered spots.

Through what lands and what waters the goddess wandered would take too long to tell. The whole world ran out before her searching did. She came back to Sicily, and, as she ranged over everything on her way, she reached Cyane too. Had Cyane not been changed, she would have told her everything; but though she longed to speak, she had no mouth and no tongue, nothing left to speak with. Still, she gave a plain sign: on the surface of her sacred pool, where it happened to have fallen, she showed Proserpine's sash, which the mother knew at once. The moment she recognized it — as if only now she had learned her daughter was stolen — the goddess tore at her disheveled hair and struck her breast again and again with her open hands. She still did not know where the girl was, but she cursed every land, called them ungrateful and unworthy of her gift of grain — and Sicily above the rest, where she had found the traces of her loss. So there, with a savage hand, she smashed the ploughs that turn the clods; in the same fury she struck down the farmers and their working oxen and gave them to death; she ordered the fields to betray the seed entrusted to them, and she blighted the seed itself. Sicily's famous fertility, its fame spread across the wide world, now lay exposed as a lie: the crops died in the first green blade; now too much sun scorched them, now too much rain. Stars and winds did their damage; greedy birds picked up the scattered seed; darnel and thistle and stubborn couch-grass wore down the wheat.

Then Arethusa lifted her head from her Elean waters and swept the dripping hair back from her forehead to her ears, and said: "You who have sought your daughter over the whole world, mother of the harvest — put an end to your measureless toil, and don't turn your anger and violence on a land that has been faithful to you. The land has done nothing to deserve it; it opened against its will to the theft. And it isn't for a homeland of my own that I plead — I came here a stranger. Pisa is my homeland; my origin is from Elis. I live in Sicily as a foreigner, yet this land is dearer to me than any soil on earth. This is Arethusa's home now, these my household gods, this my dwelling — spare it, gentlest goddess. Why I was moved from my own place and carried across so great a sea to Ortygia, there will come a fitting hour to tell, when you are eased of your grief and your face is happier. The earth gives me passage: carried down through its deepest caverns, I lift my head here and look on stars I had long done without. And so, while I glide underground through the Stygian stream, there with my own eyes I saw your Proserpine. She was sad, and her face not yet free of fear — but a queen even so, the greatest lady of that shadowed world, the mighty consort of the tyrant of the dead."

The mother stood stunned at these words, as if turned to stone, and for a long while she seemed a woman thunderstruck. When at last the crushing grief gave way to a fury as crushing as itself, she rode out in her chariot into the upper air. There, her whole face clouded, her hair streaming loose, she stood before Jupiter, a figure of grievance, and said: "I have come to you as a suppliant, Jupiter, for my own blood and for yours. If a mother counts for nothing, let the daughter move the father. Don't, I beg you, let your care for her be worth less because I was the one who bore her. Look — my daughter, sought so long, is found at last — if you call it finding to lose her more surely, or if you call it finding merely to learn where she is. That she was carried off, I will bear — so long as he gives her back. A daughter of yours does not deserve a robber for a husband — if she is no longer a daughter of mine."

Jupiter answered: "She is a pledge and a charge we share, this daughter, yours and mine alike. But if we care to give things their true names, this was no wrong that was done — it was love. And that son-in-law will be no shame to us, if only you are willing, goddess. Even if all else were lacking, how much it is to be Jupiter's brother! And in fact nothing else is lacking, and he yields to me in nothing but the draw of the lots. But if your desire for the parting is so strong, Proserpine shall return to heaven — on one fixed condition: that there she has touched no food with her lips. For so it has been settled by the compact of the Fates."

He had spoken; and Ceres was resolved to bring her daughter up. But the Fates would not allow it, for the girl had already broken her fast. While she wandered, all innocence, in the tended gardens, she had picked a red pomegranate from a bending branch, and, taking seven seeds from its pale rind, she had crushed them in her mouth. And of everyone there, one alone had seen it — Ascalaphus, whom the nymph Orphne, no obscure figure among the nymphs of Avernus, is said once to have borne to her own Acheron beneath the black woods. He saw, and by his cruel tattling he robbed her of her return. The queen of Erebus groaned, and turned that unholy witness into a bird: she sprinkled his head with water from Phlegethon and changed it into a beak, into feathers, into great round eyes. Lost to his own shape, he is wrapped in tawny wings; his head swells, his nails curve back into long talons, and he can barely stir the feathers that have sprung along his sluggish arms. He becomes a loathsome bird, the herald of grief to come — the slothful screech-owl, a dire omen to mortals.

He, at least, might be thought to have earned his punishment by his tattling tongue. But you, daughters of Achelous — where did your feathers come from, and your birds' feet, when you still wear the faces of girls? Was it because, learned Sirens, you were among her companions when Proserpine gathered the flowers of spring? After you had searched the whole world for her in vain, you longed — so that the seas too might feel your care for her — to be able to hover above the waves on the oars of wings; and you found the gods willing. And you saw your limbs turn golden with sudden feathers. But so that your singing, born to charm the ear, and so rich a gift of voice, should not lose the use of a tongue, your girls' faces stayed, and your human voice remained.

Then Jupiter, standing between his brother and his grieving sister, split the rolling year into equal halves. Now the goddess, a divinity shared between two kingdoms, spends as many months with her mother as with her husband. At once both her mood and her face are transformed: the brow that only lately could look sorrowful even to Pluto is now bright with gladness. She is like the sun, hidden before behind watery clouds, that now, the clouds beaten back, breaks free of them.

Kindly Ceres, at ease now that her daughter was safely won back, asked Arethusa what had caused her flight — why she was a sacred spring. The waters fell silent, and their goddess raised her head from the deep fountain, dried her green hair with her hand, and told the old story of the river Alpheus and his love.

"I was one of the nymphs of Achaia," she said. "None of them ranged the woodland glades more eagerly than I, none set her hunting-nets more keenly. And though I never sought a name for beauty, though I was all strength and muscle, still I had the name of a beauty. My much-praised looks gave me no pleasure. The gift of body that other girls delight in — I, like some country lass, blushed at it, and thought it a fault to be pleasing.

"I was coming back tired — I remember it — from the Stymphalian wood. The day was hot, and my exertion had doubled the great heat. I came upon a stream running without an eddy, without a murmur, clear all the way to the bottom, so that you could count every pebble deep down, and would hardly believe the water moved at all. Hoary willows and poplars, fed by the water, spread of their own accord a shade along the sloping banks. I went to it, and first dipped the soles of my feet, then went in to the knee. Not content with that, I loosened my belt, laid my soft clothes on a bending willow, and plunged in naked.

"While I struck the water and drew it toward me, gliding in a thousand ways and flinging out my arms, I felt some strange murmur beneath the middle of the stream, and in terror I scrambled onto the edge of the nearer bank. 'Where are you rushing, Arethusa?' Alpheus called from his waters. 'Where are you rushing?' he said to me again, in a hoarse voice. Just as I was, I fled without my clothes — the other bank had them. So much the more he pressed on and burned; and because I was naked, I seemed to him the readier prey. So I ran, and so that wild thing bore down on me — as doves flee the hawk on trembling wings, as the hawk drives the trembling doves. On past Orchomenus and Psophis and Cyllene, past the folds of Maenalus and cold Erymanthus and Elis I held to my running, and he was no faster than I. But, no match for him in strength, I could not keep the pace for long, while he could endure the long chase. Still I ran — over the plains, over the tree-covered mountains, over rocks and crags, and where there was no path at all. The sun was at my back. I saw a long shadow stretch out before my feet — unless it was only my fear that saw it. But surely the sound of his feet terrified me, and the great gusts of his breath were stirring the ribbons in my hair.

"Worn out with the effort of flight, I cried, 'Help me, Diana — I am being caught! Help your armor-bearer, the one you so often let carry your bow and the arrows sealed in your quiver!' The goddess was moved. She took one of the thick clouds and threw it over me. The river searched all round me where I lay hidden in the mist, and, not knowing, hunted about the hollow cloud, and twice, without realizing, circled the very spot where the goddess had hidden me, and twice he called, 'Arethusa! Arethusa!' What were my feelings then, in my misery? What the lamb feels, hearing the wolves growling around the high sheepfold — or the hare that, crouched in a thornbush, sees the enemy jaws of the hounds and dares not stir a muscle. Still he would not leave; for he could find no tracks of my feet leading away. He kept his watch on the cloud and the spot. A cold sweat seized my besieged limbs, and dark-blue drops ran down my whole body. Wherever I moved my foot, a pool welled up; dew dripped from my hair; and faster than I now tell it to you, I was changed into flowing water. But the river knew the waters he loved, and, laying aside the man's shape he had put on, he turned back into his own streams to mingle with me. Diana split the earth, and I, plunging into its blind caverns, was carried to Ortygia — which, dear to me because it bears the surname of my own goddess, was the first to bring me up into the light of day."

So far Arethusa. Then the goddess of the harvest yoked a pair of serpents to her chariot, curbed their mouths with the bridle, and was borne through the air midway between heaven and earth. She guided her light chariot to the city of Athens, to Triptolemus, and gave him seed, bidding him scatter it, part on untilled ground, part on ground brought back to cultivation after long years. By now the young man had been carried high over Europe and the land of Asia, and he turned toward the coasts of Scythia. There Lyncus was king, and Triptolemus entered the king's house. Asked where he came from, and the reason for his journey, and his name and his country, he said: "My homeland is famous Athens; my name is Triptolemus. I came neither by ship over the waves nor on foot over the land: the open sky gave me a road. I bring the gifts of Ceres, which, scattered over the broad fields, will yield you fruitful harvests and gentle nourishment." The barbarian was jealous. So that he himself might be the author of so great a gift, he took his guest in, and when the young man was heavy with sleep he came at him with a sword. As he tried to pierce that breast, Ceres turned him into a lynx, and once more sent the young Athenian on to drive her sacred team through the air.

The greatest of us had by now finished her learned song, and the nymphs with one voice declared that the goddesses who dwell on Helicon had won. Then, when the losers began flinging insults, I said: "Since it is not enough for you to have earned punishment by challenging us, and you add abuse to the offense, and our patience has its limits — we will go on to punishment, and follow wherever our anger calls us." The daughters of Pierus laughed and scorned my threatening words. But as they tried to speak and to raise their insolent hands with a great outcry, they saw feathers sprouting from their nails, saw their arms clothing over with plumage. Each watched the other's face harden into a stiff beak, and new birds join the woods. And when they tried to beat their breasts in grief, the motion of their arms lifted them, and they hung poised in the air — magpies, the scolds of the woodland. Even now, as birds, their old gift for talk remains: a raucous, grating chatter, and a monstrous, unquenchable craving to speak.
