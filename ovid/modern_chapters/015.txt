Book VIII

(Part 2 of 2)

But Acheloüs, swollen with rain, blocked his way and held him up as he went. "Come under my roof, famous son of Cecrops," he said, "and do not trust yourself to the racing flood. It is used to carrying off solid beams and rolling down boulders sideways with a great roar. I have seen high sheepfolds close to the bank swept away, flocks and all; and there it did the strong cattle no good to be strong, nor the horses to be swift. Many a young man's body, too, this torrent has drowned in its whirling eddies when the mountain snows melted. Rest is safer, until the river runs in its usual channel, until its own bed holds only the slender stream." The son of Aegeus agreed. "I will use your house and your advice, Acheloüs," he answered — and he used them both.

He entered a hall built of porous pumice and rough tufa; the floor was damp with soft moss, and shells arched the roof in alternating rows of purple. And now, Hyperion having measured out two parts of the daylight, Theseus and his fellow laborers lay down on the couches — on one side the son of Ixion, on the other Lelex, the hero of Troezen, his temples now flecked with a little gray, and others whom the river of the Acarnanians, overjoyed with so great a guest, had graced with the same honor. At once barefoot nymphs set the tables with the feast, and, the dishes cleared, poured wine in jeweled bowls. Then the mighty hero, looking out over the seas below, said, "What is that place?" — and pointed with his finger — "and tell me the name that island bears, though it does not seem to be one alone."

The River answered: "It is not one thing that you see; five lands lie there — the distance blurs the boundaries between them. And so you may wonder less at what slighted Diana can do, these were once Naiads, who, having killed twice five bullocks and called the country gods to a sacrifice, danced their festal dances and forgot me. I swelled — as great as I ever run at my fullest — and, doubled in rage and in flood, I tore woods from woods and fields from fields, and, together with the ground under them, rolled the nymphs into the sea, who then, at last, remembered me. My waves and the sea's split the once-unbroken land and broke it into as many pieces as you see islands there, the Echinades, out in the waves. But look — as you can see for yourself — one island has drawn far off from the rest, an island dear to me: the sailors call it Perimele. From her, my beloved, I took the name of virgin, which her father Hippodamas took so ill that he pushed the body of his daughter, near her time, from a cliff into the sea. I caught her, and, bearing her up as she swam, I prayed: 'O bearer of the Trident, you who won by lot the realm next below heaven, the waters where we sacred rivers end and to which we run — come, Neptune, and hear me kindly. Her whom I bear I have wronged. Grant her a place, I beg you, plunged in the water by her father's cruelty, or let her become a place herself. Even so I will embrace her.' The king of the ocean nodded, and shook all the waters with his assent. The nymph was afraid, but still she swam. As she swam I touched her breast, throbbing with a trembling motion — and while I felt it, I felt her whole body harden and her breast grow covered with spreading earth. Even as I spoke, fresh soil closed round her floating limbs, and a heavy island grew upon her changed body."

At this the River fell silent. The marvel had moved them all. But the son of Ixion laughed at their belief; a scorner of the gods and fierce of spirit, he said, "You tell a fable, Acheloüs, and think the gods more powerful than they are, if they can give and take away the shapes of things." All were shocked and did not approve such words; and before the rest Lelex, ripe in mind and years, spoke thus:

"The power of heaven is measureless and has no end, and whatever the gods above have willed is done. And that you may doubt it the less — on the Phrygian hills there is an oak beside a linden tree, ringed by a low wall. I have seen the spot myself, for Pittheus once sent me into that land of Pelops, ruled long ago by his father. Not far off is a marsh, once habitable ground, now busy with divers and the coots that love a fen. To this place Jupiter came in mortal shape, and with him his son, the grandson of Atlas, Mercury, his wings laid aside and the caduceus put by. To a thousand houses they went, seeking lodging and rest; a thousand houses shut their bolts against them. Yet one took them in — a small one, thatched with straw and marsh reeds. But a devout old woman, Baucis, and Philemon of the same age had been joined in that cottage in their youth, and in it had grown old together; and by confessing their poverty they made it light, and bore it with a mind not out of tune. It is no use asking there for masters or for servants: the whole household is two, the same who obey and command.

"So when the dwellers of heaven reached this little home and, ducking their heads, went in at the humble door, the old man set out a bench and bade them rest their limbs, and busy Baucis threw a rough cloth over it. Then she raked the warm ashes on the hearth and stirred up yesterday's fire, feeding it with leaves and dry bark and coaxing it to flame with her old woman's breath; she brought down split faggots and dry branches from under the roof, broke them small, and set them under a little copper pot. The greens her husband had gathered in the well-watered garden she strips of their leaves. With a two-pronged fork he lifts down a smoky side of bacon hanging from a black beam, and cuts a small piece from the long-kept chine, and softens it, cut, in boiling water. Meanwhile they beguile the passing hours with talk, and do not let the time be felt. There was a beechwood tub hanging by its handle from a peg; they fill it with warm water to bathe and rest the guests' feet. In the middle of the room, on a couch whose feet and frame were of willow, lay a cushion of soft sedge; over this they spread cloths they were used to lay out only on festival days — and even these were cheap and old, no shame to a willow couch.

"The gods took their places. The old woman, apron on, shaking, sets the table before them — but the third leg of the table was too short, and a potsherd slipped beneath made it level; and once the tilt was gone, green mint wiped the leveled top clean. On it are set the two-colored olives of chaste Minerva, and autumn cornel-berries pickled in dregs, and endive, and radishes, and a lump of curdled cheese, and eggs turned gently in the cooling ash — all in earthenware. After this an embossed mixing-bowl of the same clay is set out, and cups of beechwood, coated where they are hollow with yellow wax. A short pause, and the hearth sent up the warm dishes; and wine of no great age is brought again, then, set a little aside, gives way to the second course: here are nuts, here dried figs mixed with wrinkled dates, plums, and fragrant apples in wide baskets, and grapes gathered from the purple vines. In the middle is a white honeycomb — and, over it all, kind faces, and a good will neither idle nor grudging.

"Meanwhile, each time they see the emptied bowl fill itself of its own accord and the wine rise up again by itself, astonished at the strange sight, Baucis and frightened Philemon lift their hands in prayer and beg pardon for the meal and its poor preparation. There was a single goose, the guardian of that tiny farm, which its owners were making ready to kill for their divine guests; but the goose, quick on the wing, wore out the pair, slow with age, and dodged them a long while, and at last seemed to flee to the gods themselves for safety. The gods forbade its killing. 'We are gods,' they said, 'and this godless neighborhood will pay the penalty it has earned; but you shall be spared this ruin. Only leave your house and follow our steps, and come with us up the high mountain.' Both obey, and, leaning on their staffs, struggle to set their feet up the long slope. They were as far from the top as a shot arrow can carry when they turned their eyes and saw all the rest sunk in a marsh, and only their own house left standing. And while they wondered at it, while they wept for the fate of their people, that old cottage, too small even for its two owners, turned into a temple: columns rose where the forked posts had been, the thatch went golden, the ground was covered in marble, the doors seemed carved, and the roof of gold. Then the son of Saturn spoke, with gentle mouth: 'Tell us, good old man, and you, wife worthy of so good a husband, what you desire.' Philemon spoke a few words with Baucis, then told the gods their shared wish: 'We ask to be your priests and to keep your shrine; and, since we have spent our years in harmony, let the same hour take us both, so that I may never see my wife's grave, nor be buried by her.' Their prayer was granted. As long as life was given, they kept the temple; and when, worn out with years and age, they chanced to be standing before the sacred steps, telling the story of the place, Baucis saw Philemon put out leaves, and old Philemon saw Baucis put out leaves. And now, with the treetops growing over their two faces, while still they could they traded words, and together said, 'Farewell, my dear one' — and at the same moment the bark closed over and hid their faces. The people of Tyana still show there the neighboring trunks made from their two bodies. Honest old men, with no reason to deceive me, told me this; and I myself saw garlands hanging on the branches, and, laying fresh ones there, I said, 'Let those the gods love be gods, and let those who worshiped be worshiped.'"

He had finished; and the story, and the teller of it, had moved them all, and Theseus most of all. As he longed to hear more of the wonderful works of the gods, the Calydonian river, leaning on his elbow, spoke to him thus:

"There are some, bravest of men, whose shape has been changed once and stayed so changed. There are others whose privilege it is to pass into many forms — like you, Proteus, dweller in the sea that clasps the earth. For men have seen you now a young man, now a lion; now you were a raging boar, now a serpent they feared to touch; now horns made you a bull; often you might be seen as a stone, often as a tree; sometimes, mimicking the look of running water, you were a river, sometimes fire, water's very opposite.

"And no less than his is the power of the wife of Autolycus, the daughter of Erysichthon. Her father was one who scorned the majesty of the gods and burned no incense on their altars. He is said, too, to have profaned a grove of Ceres with the axe and violated her ancient woods with iron. In them stood an oak with an aged trunk, a whole wood in itself; fillets and votive tablets and garlands ringed its middle, tokens of prayers that had been answered. Often beneath it the wood-nymphs led their festal dances; often, hands linked in a ring, they measured the girth of its trunk, and the oak's circumference filled fifteen ells; and the rest of the wood stood as far below it as the grass stood below the whole wood. Yet not for that did the son of Triopas hold his axe from it, but bade his servants cut the sacred tree; and when he saw them hesitate at the order, the wicked man snatched an axe from one of them and said, 'Were it not only dear to a goddess, but the goddess herself, still it should touch the ground now with its leafy top.' He spoke, and, poising his weapon for a slanting stroke, the oak of Ceres shuddered and gave a groan, and at once its green leaves and its acorns began to pale, and its long branches to take on a pallor. And when his impious hand had made a wound in the trunk, blood ran from the cut bark just as it pours from the severed neck of a great bull-victim falling before the altar. All were struck dumb, and one of them dared to check the outrage and stay the cruel axe. The Thessalian eyed him: 'Take the reward of your piety,' he said, and turned the axe from the tree onto the man, and struck off his head, and hacked at the oak again — when from within the wood came these words: 'I, a nymph most dear to Ceres, am within this wood; and, dying, I foretell to you that the punishment of your deeds is at hand — the comfort of my death.' He pressed on with his crime; and at last, weakened by countless blows and hauled down with ropes, the tree fell, and with its weight flattened a great stretch of the wood.

"Aghast at the loss — the grove's and their own — all the wood-nymphs, her sisters, went in black robes to Ceres, mourning, and begged for Erysichthon's punishment. She granted it, and, most lovely of goddesses, with a nod of her head shook the fields heavy with their harvest, and devised a kind of punishment to be pitied — if his deeds had left him deserving of any pity: to tear him with deadly Famine. And since the goddess herself may not approach Famine (for the Fates do not let Ceres and Famine come together), she called a rustic mountain-nymph, an Oread, and said: 'There is a place at the far icy edge of Scythia, a dreary soil, a land without grain and without trees; there dwell numbing Cold, and Pallor, and Trembling, and starving Famine. Bid her bury herself in the breast of this sacrilegious wretch; let no plenty of food overcome her, and let her beat my powers in the contest. And so the long road may not frighten you, take my chariot, take the dragons you may drive on high with the reins.' And she gave them.

"Carried through the air on the granted chariot, the nymph came to Scythia, and on the peak of a steep mountain — they call it Caucasus — unyoked the dragons' necks, and saw Famine, whom she sought, in a stony field, tearing at the scattered herbs with nails and teeth. Her hair was matted, her eyes hollow, her face pale, her lips white with scurf, her throat rough with rust; her skin hard, and through it the guts could be seen; her dry bones jutted under her hooked loins; instead of a belly there was only the place for a belly. You would think her chest hung loose, held up only by the frame of her spine. Leanness had swollen her joints, her kneecaps bulged, her ankles stood out in great knobs. As soon as the Oread saw her from a distance — for she did not dare go near — she gave the goddess's orders; and, staying but a short while, though she kept her distance and had only just arrived, she seemed even so to feel hunger; and, turning the reins, she drove the dragons back on high to Thessaly.

"Famine did as Ceres bade, though she is forever the goddess's enemy; carried by the winds through the air to the appointed house, at once she entered the bedroom of the sacrilegious man and, sunk as he was in deep sleep — for it was night — folded him in her two wings, and breathed herself into him, blowing on his throat and chest and mouth, and scattering starvation through his empty veins. Her task done, she left the fruitful world and went back to her famished home, her wonted fields. Gentle Sleep still soothed Erysichthon with its calming wings. In his dream he craves food, works his jaws to no purpose, grinds tooth on tooth, tires his throat with imaginary meat, and instead of a feast devours in vain the yielding air. But when sleep was driven off, his hunger to eat rages, and holds sway over his greedy jaws and his insatiable gut. Without delay he calls for whatever the sea, the earth, the air brings forth, and complains of hunger with the tables set before him, and looks for a feast in the midst of a feast; and what would be enough for whole cities, enough for a whole people, is not enough for one man, and the more he sends down into his belly, the more he wants. As the ocean takes in the rivers of the whole earth and is never sated with water and drinks up the streams of far-off lands, and as the greedy fire never refuses its fuel and burns beams beyond number, and, the more it is given, craves the more, and grows hungrier by the very heap — so the jaws of impious Erysichthon take in every dish and ask for more at once. In him all food is the ground of more food, and there is always an empty place left by eating.

"And now, through his appetite and the pit of his belly, he had worn down his father's wealth; but even then his dreadful hunger stayed undiminished, and the flame of his ravenous throat burned on. At last, his whole fortune swallowed into his guts, only his daughter was left — a daughter who deserved a better father. Her too he sells, in his need. Nobly born, she cannot bear a master, and, stretching her hands over the nearby sea, she cried, 'Free me from my master, you who hold the prize of my ravished virginity!' — that prize Neptune had taken. He did not scorn her prayer; and though the moment before her master had seen her in pursuit, the god changed her shape and gave her the look of a man and the dress of one who catches fish. Her master, seeing her, said, 'O handler of the rod, who hide the hanging bronze hook with a little bait — so may the sea be calm for you, so may the fish in the water be ever credulous and feel no hook till caught — tell me where she is who a moment ago was standing on this shore (for I saw her standing there), her hair loose and in humble dress; for her footprints go no farther.' She saw that the god's gift had turned to good, and, glad to be asked after herself by herself, answered his question: 'Whoever you are, forgive me: I have turned my eyes nowhere from this water, but, busy at my work, have kept to my task. And that you may doubt me the less — so may the god of the sea aid this craft of mine — no man has stood on this shore for some time now, myself excepted, nor any woman.' Her master believed her, and, turning on his heel, paced off across the sand, deceived; and her own shape was given back to her.

"But when her father found she had a body that could be transformed, he sold the granddaughter of Triopas again and again to different masters; and she kept escaping — now a mare, now a bird, now a cow, now a stag — and so found a dishonest living for her hungry father. Yet after the violence of his sickness had used up all provision and heaped fresh fuel on his dreadful malady, he himself began to tear his own limbs with biting, and the wretched man fed his body by making it less. But why do I dwell on the cases of others? I too, young men, have the power to change my body — though only so many times. For one moment I appear as what I now am; the next I am coiled into a snake; then, as the leader of a herd, I take strength into my horns — my horns, so long as I could. Now one side of my brow is stripped of its weapon, as you see for yourself." And a sigh followed his words.
