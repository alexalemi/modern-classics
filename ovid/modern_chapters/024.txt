Book XIII

(Part 1 of 2)

The chiefs sat down, and, with a ring of the common people standing around, Ajax, lord of the sevenfold shield, rose before them; and, impatient in his anger, he looked back with a grim face on the Sigean shores and the fleet on the beach, and, stretching out his hands, said:

"We plead our cause before the ships, O Jupiter, and Ulysses vies with me! But he did not hesitate to give way before Hector's fires, which I withstood, which I drove from this fleet. It is safer, then, to fight with invented words than with the hand. But neither is speaking easy for me, nor acting for him; and as much as I am worth in fierce war and in the line, so much is he worth in talking. Nor do I think, Greeks, that my deeds need be told to you — for you have seen them. Let Ulysses tell his, which he does without a witness, of which night alone is aware. I own the prize sought is great; but a rival cheapens its honor: it is no proud thing for Ajax to hold whatever Ulysses has hoped for, great though it be; he has already gained the reward of this contest — that, when he is beaten, he will be said to have contended with me.

"And I, if my valor were in doubt, would be strong in noble birth, the son of Telamon, who took the walls of Troy under brave Hercules and entered the Colchian shore in the ship of Pagasae. His father was Aeacus, who gives law to the silent dead there, where the heavy stone weighs down Sisyphus, son of Aeolus. Highest Jupiter owns Aeacus and confesses him his offspring: so Ajax is the third from Jove. And yet let this line not help my cause, Greeks, if it is not shared with great Achilles: he was my cousin — I ask what belonged to my cousin! Why do you, sprung from the blood of Sisyphus and most like him in thefts and fraud, thrust a stranger family's name in among the sons of Aeacus? Are the arms to be denied me because I took up arms first, with no informer, while he seems preferable who took them last and shirked service by feigned madness, until the son of Nauplius, cleverer than he but unluckier for himself, unmasked his coward's ruse and dragged him to the arms he had dodged? Shall he now take the best, because he was willing to take none — and shall I go dishonored, robbed of my cousin's gifts, because I offered myself first to danger?

"And would that his madness had been real, or believed — that this counselor of crimes had never come with us to the towers of Phrygia! Then, son of Poeas, Lemnos would not hold you cast away, by our fault; you who now, they say, hidden in the woodland caves, move the very rocks with your groans and pray for the son of Laertes what he deserves — which, if there are gods, you do not pray in vain. And now he who was sworn on the same campaign with us, one of our leaders, alas — the man whose bow, as heir, uses the arrows of Hercules — broken by disease and hunger, is clothed and fed by birds, and, hunting fowl, spends on them the shafts owed to the fate of Troy. Yet he lives, because he did not go with Ulysses. Unhappy Palamedes too would rather have been left — then he would be alive, or at least have had a death without a charge; whom Ulysses, remembering too well the exposing of his madness, framed as betraying the Greek cause, and proved his false charge, and showed the gold he had buried before. So by exile or by death he has robbed the Greeks of their best strength: that is how Ulysses fights, that is why he is to be feared.

"Though he outdo faithful Nestor himself in speech, he will never make me think Nestor's abandonment no crime — Nestor, who, when he begged Ulysses' help, slowed by his horse's wound and worn with age, was deserted by his companion. That I do not invent these charges, the son of Tydeus knows well, who, calling him by name again and again, rebuked him and reproached his trembling friend with his flight. The gods above watch the deeds of men with just eyes: see, he who gave no help now needs it, and, as he left another, so was he to be left — the law he made for himself! He shouted to his comrades: I came, and saw him trembling, pale with fear, quaking at the death to come. I set the mass of my shield before him, covered him as he lay, and saved — this is the least of my glory — his cowardly life. If you insist on the contest, let us go back to that place: give back the enemy, and your wound, and your old fear, hide behind my shield, and under it contend with me! But after I had rescued him, he whom his wounds had left no strength to stand fled, slowed by no wound at all.

"Hector comes, and brings the gods with him to battle; and where he charges, not only you, Ulysses, are afraid, but the brave too — so much terror he brings. Him, as he exulted in his bloody success, I laid flat from afar with a huge stone; him, demanding someone to face, I alone withstood; and you, Greeks, prayed the lot might fall to me — and your prayers prevailed. If you ask the issue of that fight, I was not beaten by him. Look — the Trojans bring fire and sword and Jove himself against the Greek fleet: where now is eloquent Ulysses? I, with my breast, protected a thousand ships, the hope of your return: grant me the arms for so many ships. And, if I may speak the truth, a greater honor is sought for them than for me: our glory is joined, and it is Ajax that is sought for the arms, not the arms by Ajax. Let the Ithacan set beside these deeds Rhesus, and unwarlike Dolon, and Helenus the son of Priam taken with the stolen Pallas: nothing done by day, nothing without Diomedes! If you give these arms for services so cheap, divide them, and let Diomedes' share be the greater.

"But why these for the Ithacan, who works by stealth, always unarmed, and cheats the unwary enemy by tricks? The very shine of the helmet, gleaming with bright gold, will betray his plots and show him where he hides. Nor will the Dulichian head bear so great a weight under Achilles' helm, nor the Pelian spear be anything but heavy and burdensome for unwarlike arms; nor will the shield engraved with the image of the vast world suit a timid left hand made for theft. Why ask, wretch, for a gift that will only weaken you? If the Greek people's mistake gave it you, it would be a reason to be stripped, not feared; and your flight — the one thing in which, most cowardly of men, you beat all others — will be slowed as you drag so great a load. Add that your shield, so rarely in the fight, is unhurt; mine, gaping with a thousand blows from bearing weapons, needs a new successor. In short — what need of words? Let us be tried in action! Let the arms of the brave man be thrown among the enemy: order them fetched from there, and deck out with them the one who brings them back."

The son of Telamon had ended, and a murmur of the crowd followed his closing words, until the Laertian hero stood, and, holding his eyes a little on the ground, raised them to the chiefs and loosed the voice they awaited; nor was grace missing from his eloquent words:

"If my prayers had prevailed with yours, Greeks, the heir of so great a contest would not be in doubt: you would have your arms, Achilles, and we would have you. But since unfair Fates have denied him to me and to you" — and here he wiped his eyes with his hand as if weeping — "who should better succeed great Achilles than the man through whom great Achilles joined the Greeks? Only let it not help him that he seems as dull as he is; and let my talents, which have ever served you, be no harm to me; and let this eloquence of mine, if there is any, which now pleads for its owner and has often pleaded for you, be free of envy — and let no man disown his own gifts. For as to descent, and ancestors, and the things we did not make ourselves, I scarcely call these our own; but since Ajax has boasted himself Jove's great-grandson, Jupiter is the founder of my line too, and I am just as many steps from him: for Laertes is my father, his was Arcesius, his Jupiter — and none of these was condemned or exiled. Through my mother, too, Cyllenian Mercury adds another nobility: on both parents' side there is a god. But not because I am nobler-born on my mother's side, nor because my father is innocent of a brother's blood, do I claim these arms. Weigh the case by merit — only let it be no merit in Ajax that Telamon and Peleus were brothers, and let not blood-order but the honor of valor be sought in these spoils. Or if nearness and the next heir is wanted, Peleus is Achilles' father and Pyrrhus his son: what place is there for Ajax? Let the arms go to Phthia or Scyros! And Teucer is no less Achilles' cousin than he — yet does he claim, does he expect to carry them off?

"Since, then, the contest is simply of deeds — I have done more than I can easily put in words, yet I will go in order. His mother, the Nereid, foreseeing his death, hid her son in a disguise, and the trick of the assumed dress deceived all, Ajax among them. I mixed arms, to stir a manly mind, among a woman's wares; and the hero had not yet thrown off the maiden's dress when, as he gripped a shield and spear, I said, 'Son of a goddess, Troy keeps itself to fall by you: why delay to overthrow mighty Troy?' And I laid my hand on him and sent the brave man to brave deeds. So his deeds are mine: I subdued Telephus fighting with his spear, and healed him when he begged, beaten. That Thebes fell is my doing; believe that I took Lesbos, took Tenedos, Chryse and Cilla, cities of Apollo, and Scyros; consider that the walls of Lyrnessus fell, shaken by my right hand; and, to name no others, I gave you the one who could kill fierce Hector — through me the famous Hector lies dead! By the arms through which Achilles was found, I claim these arms: to the living I gave them; after his death I ask them back.

"When the grief of one had reached all the Greeks, and a thousand ships filled Euboean Aulis, the winds long awaited were either lacking or against the fleet, and the harsh oracles bade Agamemnon slay his innocent daughter for cruel Diana. The father refuses, and rages at the gods themselves — a king, yet still a father. By my words I turned the parent's gentle nature to the public good. This cause — I confess it, and let Atrides forgive the confession — I upheld before a biased judge, a hard case; yet the good of the people, and his brother, and the height of the granted scepter, move him to balance praise against blood. I was sent, too, to the mother, who was not to be persuaded but tricked by craft; had the son of Telamon gone, our sails would even now be without wind. I was sent, a bold envoy, to the towers of Troy; the senate-house of lofty Troy was seen and entered by me, still full of its heroes; undaunted, I pleaded the common cause that all Greece had entrusted me, and accused Paris, and demanded back the plunder and Helen, and moved Priam and Antenor at Priam's side; but Paris and his brothers and the ravishers under him barely held their wicked hands — you know this, Menelaus — and that first day of danger was mine, with you.

"It would be long to tell what I did well, by counsel and by hand, in the length of that war. After the first battles the enemy kept long within the city walls, and there was no chance of open war; only in the tenth year did we fight. What were you doing meanwhile, you who know nothing but battles? What use were you? But if you ask my deeds: I lay ambushes for the enemy, ring the defenses with a trench, cheer the allies to bear the tedium of a long war with a calm mind, teach how we are to be fed and armed, and am sent where need requires. Look — by Jove's warning, the king, deceived by a shape in his sleep, orders the war's care dismissed; he can defend his word by its author. Ajax should not have allowed it, but demanded Troy be razed, and fought — the one thing he can do. Why does he not stop those about to leave? Why not take up arms and give the wavering crowd a lead? That was not too much for one who never says anything but grand. And did you yourself flee? I was witness, and ashamed to see, when you turned your back and readied the sails of disgrace. Without delay I cried, 'What are you doing? What madness stirs you, friends, to let Troy go, all but taken, and to carry home, in the tenth year, nothing but disgrace?' With such words, and others grief itself made me eloquent, I led the turned-away back from the fleeing fleet. The son of Atreus calls the allies, struck with terror; and still the son of Telamon dares not open his mouth — yet Thersites dared to attack the kings with insolent words, and not unpunished by me! I rise, and rouse the frightened citizens against the foe, and by my voice restore their lost courage. From that time, whatever this man may seem to have done bravely is mine, who drew him back as he turned his back.

"In short, who of the Greeks praises you, or seeks you out? But the son of Tydeus shares his deeds with me, approves me, and always trusts in Ulysses his comrade. It is something, out of so many thousands of Greeks, to be singled out by Diomedes! Nor did the lot order me to go; yet, scorning the peril of night and enemy, I killed Dolon of the Phrygian race, who dared the same as we — but not before I made him tell everything, and learned what treacherous Troy was planning. I had found out all, with nothing more to spy, and could have returned with praise going before me; not content with that, I sought the tent of Rhesus, and in his own camp killed him and his men, and so, a victor with my wish won, I rode home in the captured chariot like a joyful triumph. The horses whose owner the enemy had demanded as the price of one night's work — deny me the arms, and let Ajax be your greater benefactor! Why speak of the ranks of Lycian Sarpedon laid waste by my sword? With much bloodshed I felled Coeranus son of Iphitus, and Alastor, and Chromius, and Alcander, and Halius, and Noemon, and Prytanis, and put to death Thoön with Chersidamas, and Charops, and Ennomus driven by his cruel fate, and others less famous who fell by my hand beneath the walls. I too have wounds, citizens, honorable in their place; do not trust his crafty words — look!" And with his hand he pulled his garment open: "This is the breast," he said, "that has ever been busy in your service! But the son of Telamon has spent no blood on his friends in all these years, and has a body without a wound.

"Yet what of that, if he says he bore arms for the Greek fleet against the Trojans and Jove himself? I confess he did — nor is it mine to belittle others' good deeds with malice — but let him not alone seize what is common to all, and let him give you too some of the honor. The descendant of Actor, safe in Achilles' likeness, drove the Trojans and their champion from the ships about to burn. He fancies, too, that he alone dared meet Hector in combat, forgetting the king and the chiefs and me — ninth in that duty, and preferred by the favor of the lot. But what was the issue of your combat, bravest of men? Hector went off, hurt by no wound. Wretched me — with how much grief I must recall the time when Achilles, the wall of the Greeks, fell! No tears, no grief, no fear held me from lifting his body from the ground: on these shoulders — these shoulders, I say — I bore the body of Achilles, and his arms with it, which now too I am striving to bear. I have strength enough for such a weight, and surely a soul that will feel your honors. Was his sea-blue mother, then, so anxious for her son, that a rough soldier, one without a mind, should put on the heavenly gifts, the work of so great an art? For he will not understand the shield's engravings — the Ocean, the lands, the stars with the lofty sky, the Pleiades and Hyades and the Bear that shuns the sea, and the different cities, and the bright sword of Orion: he demands to receive arms he does not understand!

"And that he charges me with shirking the hard duties of war and coming late to the toil begun — does he not see that in this he slanders great Achilles? If you call feigning a crime, we both feigned; if delay is a fault, I was earlier than he. A loving wife held me back, a loving mother Achilles; the first of our time was given them, the rest to you. I do not fear a charge shared with so great a man, even if I could not defend it — yet he was found out by Ulysses' cleverness, but not Ulysses by Ajax's. And let us not wonder that he pours out the abuse of his stupid tongue on me, when he casts at you too things worthy of shame. Is it base for me to have charged Palamedes with a false crime, and honorable for you to have condemned him? But the son of Nauplius could not defend a deed so great and so plain, and you did not merely hear the charges against him — you saw them, and the bribe itself made the charge plain. Nor did I deserve blame because Vulcan's Lemnos still holds the son of Poeas — defend your own act, for you agreed to it! — nor will I deny I advised him to withdraw from the toil of war and voyage, and to try to ease his cruel pains by rest. He obeyed — and lives! The advice was not only loyal but lucky, though loyal was enough. And since the seers demand him to destroy Troy, do not send me! The son of Telamon will go better, and by his eloquence soften the man maddened by disease and rage, or cleverly bring him back by some art! Sooner will Simois flow backward and Ida stand without leaves, and Achaia promise aid to Troy, than the skill of stupid Ajax help the Greeks while my breast is idle in your cause. Though you rage at friends and king and me, hard Philoctetes, though you curse and devote my head without end, and long to have me thrown in your way and to drink my blood — still I will approach you, and strive to bring you back with me, and win your arrows as surely (may Fortune favor) as I won the Dardanian seer I took, as I laid open the answers of the gods and the fates of Troy, as I stole the inmost image of Phrygian Minerva from the midst of the enemy.

"And does Ajax match himself with me? The Fates forbade Troy to be taken without that statue: where is the brave Ajax? Where are the great man's huge words? Why do you fear here? Why does Ulysses dare to go through the watches and trust himself to the night and, through cruel swords, enter not only the walls of Troy but its highest towers, and tear the goddess from her shrine and carry her, so torn, through the enemy? Had I not done it, the son of Telamon would have borne the seven bull-hides on his left arm in vain. On that night the victory over Troy was won by me: then I conquered Pergamum, when I made it able to be conquered. Stop showing me the son of Tydeus with your looks and muttering: a share of the glory in these things is his. Nor were you alone when you held your shield for the fleet: a crowd was with you; to me fell one. Who, did he not know that a fighter is less than a wise man, and that the prize is not owed to an untamed hand, would himself be claiming these arms — the more discreet Ajax would claim them, and fierce Eurypylus, and the son of famous Andraemon, and no less Idomeneus and Meriones of the same land, and the brother of the greater son of Atreus. But these, brave in action and no second to you in war, have all yielded to my wisdom. Your right hand is useful in war; your temper needs my guidance. You have strength without a mind; mine is the care for the future. You can fight; the son of Atreus, with me, picks the time for fighting. You serve only with your body; we with the mind; and as much as the one who steers the ship surpasses the oarsman's work, as much as the general is greater than the soldier, so much do I surpass you. In our body, too, the heart is worth more than the hand: all vigor lies there.

"But you, chiefs, give the reward to your watchman; and for the care of so many years I have spent in anxiety, return this honor to weigh against my services. The toil is now at its end: I have removed the opposing Fates, and by making it possible to take lofty Pergamum, I have taken it. By our shared hopes, by the walls of Troy doomed to fall, by the gods I lately took from the enemy, by anything that remains to be done by wisdom, by anything still to be dared from a desperate place — remember me! Or, if you do not give me the arms, give them to this," and he showed the fatal image of Minerva.

The body of chiefs was moved; and then it appeared what eloquence can do, and the fluent man carried off the arms of the brave one. He who alone had so often withstood Hector, and the sword, and fire, and Jove himself, could not withstand one thing — his own grief; and grief conquered the unconquered man. He seized his sword and said, "This at least is my own — or will Ulysses claim this too for himself? This must I use against myself; and the blade often wet with Phrygian blood will now be wet with its owner's death, that no one may conquer Ajax but Ajax." He spoke, and drove the deadly sword into his breast — then for the first time taking a wound — where it lay open to the steel. His hands could not draw the fixed weapon out; the blood itself forced it out; and the earth, reddened with blood, bore from the green turf a purple flower, the same once born from the wound of Hyacinthus. Letters shared by the boy and the hero are written in the middle of the petals: these for the name, those for the lament.

The victor, Ulysses, set sail for the country of Hypsipyle and famous Thoas, the land infamous for the old slaughter of husbands, to bring back the arrows, the weapons of Hercules. When he had carried them back to the Greeks, their owner along with them, the last hand was at length put to the long war. Troy and Priam fell together; Priam's wife, poor woman, after losing everything else, lost her human shape and frightened a foreign air with new barkings, where the long Hellespont narrows.

Ilium was burning, and the fire had not yet died, and the altar of Jove had drunk the scanty blood of old Priam; the priestess of Apollo, dragged by the hair, stretched to heaven her hands that would not avail; the Dardanian mothers, embracing while they could the images of their country's gods and clinging to the burning temples, the victorious Greeks drag off, an envied spoil. Astyanax is hurled from the towers from which, shown by his mother, he used often to watch his father fighting for himself and defending his ancestors' kingdom. And now Boreas urges them to go, and the sails, stirred by a fair breeze, resound; the sailor bids them use the winds. "Troy, farewell! We are torn away," the Trojan women cry, and kiss the soil, and leave the smoking roofs of their land. Last to board the fleet — a pitiful sight — is Hecuba, found among the graves of her children; clutching the tombs and kissing the bones, Dulichian hands dragged her off — yet she took up the ashes of one, and carried Hector's ashes with her in her breast; on Hector's tomb she left the gray hair of her head, a poor offering, her hair and her tears.

There is, opposite Phrygia where Troy stood, a land inhabited by the men of Bistonia. There stood the rich palace of Polymestor, to whom your father, Polydorus, secretly entrusted you to be raised, and removed you far from the Phrygian war — a wise plan, had he not added great riches as well, the reward of crime, the spur of a greedy heart. When the fortunes of the Phrygians fell, the wicked king of Thrace took a sword and drove it into the throat of his foster-child, and, as if the crime could be removed with the body, threw him lifeless from a cliff into the waters below.
