Book VI

(Part 2 of 2)

The neighboring lords gathered, and the nearby cities begged their kings to go and comfort Pelops in his grief — Argos, and Sparta, and Mycenae, the city of his own line; Calydon, not yet hateful to stern Diana; teeming Orchomenos, and Corinth famous for its bronze; fierce Messene, and Patrae, and lowly Cleonae; Pylos of Neleus, and Troezen, not yet named for Pittheus; and all the other towns shut in by the Isthmus between its two seas, and all those that lie outside it and look back toward that same two-sea Isthmus. Who could believe it? Athens alone — you alone, Athens — sent no one. War stood in the way of the kindness. A barbarian army, ferried in across the water, was hammering at her walls with terror. Tereus of Thrace had broken that army with his allied troops and won a shining name by the victory, and so Pandion, king of Athens, bound the man to his own house — a man rich in wealth and men, and, as it happened, tracing his blood back to mighty Mars himself — by giving him his daughter Procne in marriage.

Juno, who watches over weddings, did not come to that one. Neither did Hymen, god of the marriage-song, nor the Graces. It was the Furies who held the torches that night — torches snatched from a funeral pyre. The Furies made up the marriage-bed. And a filthy owl, that bird of ill omen, settled on the roof and brooded there above the bridal chamber. Under that bird Procne and Tereus were wed; under that bird they became parents. Thrace, of course, congratulated them; they themselves gave thanks to the gods; and they declared two days holy — the day the king had taken Pandion's daughter to wife, and the day Itys, their son, was born. So completely does our real fortune hide itself from us.

By now the Sun had drawn the wheeling seasons through five autumns, when Procne, coaxing her husband, said: "If I have earned any love from you, either send me to see my sister, or let my sister come here. Promise my father she'll be back before long — you'll be giving me a gift worth as much as a god's, just to see her face again."

He ordered the ships hauled down to the water, and with sail and oar he came into the harbor of Athens and stepped onto the shores of Piraeus. The moment he could reach his father-in-law, hand was clasped in hand, and — with a black omen hanging over it — the two of them began to talk. Tereus had just begun to explain why he had come, and to lay out his wife's request, and to promise a quick return for the sister if she were allowed to go — when in walked Philomela, richly dressed in costly finery, and richer still in her own beauty; the kind of girl we imagine when we hear of naiads and dryads moving through the deep woods, if only you dressed and adorned them the same way. The sight of the girl set Tereus alight, exactly as if you touched flame to ripe white wheat, or lit dry leaves and grass stored in a barn. Her beauty was worth catching fire for, true enough — but there was also the lust bred in his bones driving him, for the men of his country run naturally to that vice; he blazed with his people's fault and his own.

His first thought was to buy off her attendants and corrupt the loyalty of her nurse; then to work on the girl herself with lavish gifts, to spend his whole kingdom if he had to — or else to seize her outright and hold what he had seized through savage war. There was nothing this unchained hunger would not dare, and his chest could no longer contain the flames shut inside it. Now delay was unbearable to him. He turned back to Procne's request with hungry eagerness, and used it as cover to plead his own desire. Passion made him eloquent, and every time he pushed further than was decent, he claimed it was Procne who wanted it. He even added tears, as if she had asked him for those too. Great gods above — how much blind night the human heart can hold! By the very effort he put into his crime, Tereus was thought to be devoted, and won praise for his wickedness.

And how does this help? Philomela wants the very same thing. Winding her arms fondly around her father's shoulders, she begs to be allowed to go and see her sister — begs it for her own good, and against her own good. Tereus watches her, and even as he watches he takes hold of her in his mind; and when he sees her kisses, and her arms circling her father's neck, he swallows it all down as goad and torch and fuel for his madness. Every time she embraces her father, he wishes he were that father — though even then his heart would have been no less foul. The old man gives in to the pleading of both of them. Philomela is overjoyed; she thanks her father; and the poor girl counts it a triumph for them both — the very thing that will bring both of them to ruin.

Now only a little of the Sun's day's work was left, his horses striking their hooves down the sloping track of the western sky. A royal feast was laid on the tables, and wine in vessels of gold; then they gave their full bodies over to gentle sleep. But the Thracian king, though he had withdrawn for the night, still burned for her. Turning her face over in his mind, her movements, her hands, he imagined what he had not yet seen exactly as he wished it, and fed his own fire, so that his restlessness drove sleep away.

Day came. Pandion clasped the right hand of his departing son-in-law, and with tears springing up he entrusted the girl to his care. "Dear son-in-law," he said, "since a loving reason has forced me to it, and since both my daughters wished it — and you wished it too, Tereus — I give her into your keeping. And I beg you, as a suppliant, by your honor, by the bond that now joins us, by the gods above: guard her with a father's love, and send her back to me as soon as you can, the sweet comfort of my anxious old age — every day she's gone will be long to me. And you too, Philomela, come home as soon as you can, if you love me at all — it's hard enough that your sister lives so far away." So he charged them, and even as he spoke he kissed his daughter, and soft tears fell between his instructions. He asked for the right hands of them both as a pledge of good faith and joined them together, and asked them to carry his greetings, from his own remembering lips, to his absent daughter and grandson. And with a mouth choked full of sobs he could barely get out the last goodbye — and he shuddered at the warnings of his own heart.

The moment Philomela was set aboard the painted ship, and the sea was churned under the oars, and the land shoved away behind them, Tereus cried out, "I've won! What I longed for is sailing away with me!" The barbarian exulted; he could scarcely hold his joy inside; he never once turned his eyes away from her. It was the look of the eagle, Jupiter's own bird, when its hooked talons have dropped a hare into some high nest: the captive has no escape, and the robber sits and watches his prize.

Now the voyage was over. Now they had stepped out of the weary ships onto his own shore — and the king dragged Pandion's daughter into a high building deep in an ancient wood, hidden among old trees, and there he shut her up. She was pale and shaking, terrified of everything, already weeping, already asking where her sister was. And then he confessed the horror in his mind and took her by force — a virgin, and alone — while she screamed again and again, uselessly, for her father, for her sister, and above all for the great gods. She trembled like a frightened lamb that has been torn, bleeding, from the jaws of a gray wolf and still cannot believe it is safe; like a dove with its feathers soaked in its own blood, still shuddering, still afraid of the greedy talons that gripped it.

Soon her mind came back to her. Tearing her loosened hair, beating her arms like a woman in mourning, she flung out her hands and cried: "You barbarian, you and your monstrous deeds! You cruel thing! Did none of it move you — not my father's charge to you, not his loving tears, not care for my sister, not my virginity, not the laws of marriage? You have thrown everything into ruin. I have been made my sister's rival, and you the husband of us both, and Procne now my enemy, who ought to be punished for what you've done! Why not take this life too, you traitor, so that no crime is left for you to commit? I wish you had killed me before your unspeakable act — then I would have gone down to the shades with a spirit clean of any stain. But if the gods above see these things, if the power of the gods is anything at all, if everything has not died along with me — then one day, whenever it comes, you will pay me for this. I will throw shame aside and tell the world what you have done. If I get the chance, I'll go out among the people; if I'm kept locked in these woods, I'll fill the woods with it and move the very rocks to bear witness. The sky will hear this — and any god there is in it!"

Such words roused the savage tyrant's fury — and his fear was no less than his fury; spurred on by both at once, he pulled from its sheath the sword he wore, seized her by the hair, wrenched her arms behind her back, and forced her into bonds. Philomela, seeing the blade, only bared her throat to it: she had begun to hope for death. But as she cried out in her rage, calling and calling her father's name, struggling to keep speaking, he caught her tongue with a pair of pincers and cut it out with his cruel sword. The last root of it still flickered where it had been torn. The tongue itself fell to the ground, trembling, murmuring against the black earth, and just as the tail of a mangled snake goes on writhing, it twitched — and dying, groped toward its mistress's feet. Even after this — I can hardly bring myself to believe it — they say he came back again and again to satisfy his lust on her ruined body.

After deeds like these he had the nerve to return to Procne. Seeing her husband, she asked at once for her sister; but he groaned false groans and told an invented story of her death, and his tears made it convincing. Procne tore from her shoulders the robes bright with their broad bands of gold, dressed herself in black, built an empty tomb, made offerings to a ghost that did not exist, and mourned the death of a sister who was not, after all, dead to be mourned.

The Sun-god had traveled through all twelve signs; a full year had turned. What could Philomela do? A guard blocked any escape; the walls of her prison stood hard and solid stone; her silenced mouth had no way to name the crime. But grief has a vast ingenuity, and cunning comes to those in misery. She set up a loom in the barbarian fashion and, working the threads with skill, wove crimson signs into the white cloth — the whole crime, spelled out. When it was finished she handed it to a single servant and asked her, with gestures, to carry it to her mistress. The servant carried it to Procne, with no idea what she was carrying. The wife of the savage king unrolled the cloth and read her sister's pitiable fate — and (a wonder she could bear it) said nothing. Grief locked her mouth; her searching tongue could not find words fierce enough; and there was no room even for tears. She rushed straight on, past all thought of right and wrong, wholly given over to the picture of revenge taking shape in her mind.

It was the season when the women of Thrace keep the rites of Bacchus that come round every other year. Night is in on the secret; by night Mount Rhodope rings with the clash of shrill bronze. By night the queen went out from her house, decked in the god's gear, carrying the wild weapons of the festival: her head wound with vine-leaves, deerskins hanging from her left side, a light spear resting on her shoulder. Terrifying, Procne swept through the woods with a crowd of her women around her, driven by the furies of her grief — and pretended, Bacchus, that the frenzy was yours. She came at last to the lonely prison in the wood, shrieked and howled the god's cry, "Euhoe!", broke down the gates, seized her sister, dressed her too in the badges of Bacchus, hid her face under ivy-leaves, and, dragging the bewildered girl along, led her inside her own walls.

When Philomela understood she had reached that accursed house, the poor woman shuddered, and the blood drained from her whole face. Procne found a safe place and stripped off the holy trappings, uncovered the shamed face of her wretched sister, and reached to hold her — but Philomela could not lift her eyes to meet her. She felt herself the woman who had stolen her sister's husband. She kept her face turned down to the ground, and when she wanted to swear, to call the gods to witness that the disgrace had been forced on her, she made her hands speak for the voice she no longer had. Procne caught fire. She could not hold her own rage; she cut short her sister's weeping and said: "This is no time for tears — it's a time for the sword, or for anything, if you have anything, that can go further than the sword. I am ready for any crime, sister. I could set the palace ablaze with torches and throw Tereus into the middle of the flames — the schemer, the artist of all this. I could take the steel to his tongue and his eyes and the parts of him that stole your honor. I could drive his guilty soul out of him through a thousand wounds. Something enormous — that much I've decided; what it will be, I don't yet know."

While Procne was still saying this, in came Itys to his mother. The sight of him showed her what she could do. Looking at him with pitiless eyes, she said, "Ah — how like your father you are." She said no more; grimly she began to plan, seething with a silent rage. And yet, when the boy reached her, and greeted his mother, and pulled her neck down with his small arms, and gave her kisses mixed with a child's coaxing — the mother was moved. Her anger broke and stalled; against her will her eyes filled with forced tears. But the moment she felt her resolve sliding under too much tenderness, she turned away from him, back to her sister's face, and looking from one to the other she said: "Why does the one come with his sweet talk while the other sits silent, her tongue torn out? He calls me mother — why can't she call me sister? Look at the kind of man you're married to, daughter of Pandion! You're betraying your blood. To feel tenderness for the wife of Tereus — that is the crime."

No more delay. She dragged Itys off, the way a tigress on the banks of the Ganges drags a hind's nursing fawn through the shadowed forest. And when they reached a far corner of the high house, as the boy stretched out his hands — for now he saw his fate coming — and cried "Mother! Mother!" and reached for her neck, Procne struck him with the sword where the breast joins the side, and never turned her face away. That one wound was enough to kill him; but Philomela opened his throat with the steel as well. And while the limbs still quivered, still held some scrap of life, the two of them tore the body apart. Part of it leaped in bubbling bronze cauldrons; part hissed on the spits; the inmost rooms ran with gore.

To this feast the wife brought Tereus, who knew nothing, and — pretending it was a sacred rite of her country at which one man alone might be present — she sent away his companions and servants. Tereus himself, sitting high on his ancestral throne, ate, and heaped his own flesh and blood into his own belly. So deep was the darkness over his mind that he said, "Send for Itys — bring him here." Procne could not hide her savage joy. Wanting now to be the one to break the news of the ruin she had made, she said, "The one you're asking for — you have him inside you." He looked around, and asked where the boy was; and while he was still asking, still calling the name, Philomela sprang forward, just as she was, her hair spattered with the frenzied slaughter, and hurled the bloody head of Itys into his father's face. Never had she longed more to have a voice, to speak, to put her joy into the words it deserved.

With a huge cry the Thracian flung the table away from him and summoned the snake-haired sisters, the Furies, up from the valley of the Styx. One moment he wanted, if only he could, to tear open his own chest and empty out the horrible meal, the half-digested flesh; the next he wept and called himself the wretched tomb of his own son; the next he was after Pandion's daughters with his drawn sword. You would have thought the two Athenian women were floating on wings — and they were floating on wings. One made for the woods; the other went up under the eaves of houses. Even now the marks of the murder had not left her breast, and the feathers there were still stained with blood. As for Tereus, made swift by his grief and his craving for revenge, he was turned into a bird with a crest of feathers standing up on its head; a huge beak juts out where the long spear used to be — the bird is called the hoopoe, and its face looks armed for war.

This grief sent Pandion down to the shades of the underworld before his time, before the last stretch of a long old age had run out. Erechtheus took up the scepter of the land and the rule of the state — a man you could not easily say was stronger in his justice or in his strong right arm. He had fathered four sons and as many daughters, and two of the daughters were equal in beauty. One of them, Procris, made Cephalus, the son of Aeolus, a happy husband. But Boreas, the North Wind, was cursed by the memory of Tereus and the Thracians, and for a long time the god went without his beloved Orithyia — because he kept asking, choosing to use prayers rather than force. But when his sweet talk got him nowhere, bristling with the rage that is second nature to him, and only too much at home in that wind, he said: "And I deserve it! Why did I put down my own weapons — my violence, my strength, my anger, my threatening temper — and take up prayers, which don't suit me at all? Force is what fits me. By force I drive off the sullen clouds; by force I shake the seas and throw down the knotted oaks and pack the snow hard and lash the earth with hail. When I meet my brother winds up in the open sky — that's my proper field — I wrestle with such effort that the air between us thunders and fires leap out, struck from the hollow clouds. When I go down into the arched hollows of the earth and set my back fiercely against its lowest caverns, I terrify the ghosts and shake the whole world with earthquakes. That is the power I should have used to win my marriage. Erechtheus should never have been begged to be my father-in-law — he should have been made one."

Having said this, or something no less high and stormy, Boreas shook out his wings, and at the beating of them the whole earth was buffeted and the wide sea shivered from end to end. Dragging his dusty cloak over the mountaintops, he swept the ground, and, wrapped in that darkness, the lover folded his tawny wings around the terrified Orithyia. As he flew his fire burned hotter for the stirring of it, and the ravisher did not rein in his airy course until he reached the people and walls of the Ciconians in Thrace. There the Athenian girl became the wife of the cold king, and a mother too, bearing twin sons who had everything of their mother but their father's wings. Yet they say the wings were not born with their bodies. As long as the red-gold beards were still missing from their cheeks, the boys Calais and Zetes went without feathers. But soon, together, feathers began to fringe both their sides in the way of birds, and together their cheeks began to grow golden with down. And so, once the season of boyhood had passed into young manhood, the two of them sailed off with the Minyae — the Argonauts — in the first ship ever built, over an uncharted sea, chasing the fleece that blazed with its shining wool of gold.
