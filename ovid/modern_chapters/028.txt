Book XV

(Part 1 of 2)

Meanwhile one is sought who can bear so great a weight, and succeed so great a king; and Fame, the herald of truth, marks out famous Numa for the throne. Not content to know the rites of the Sabine nation, in his large mind he conceives greater things, and inquires into the nature of the world. Love of this pursuit led him to leave his country and Cures and travel to the city of the stranger Hercules; and when he asked what founder had set Greek walls on the Italian shore, one of the elder natives, not ignorant of the old days, told him:

The son of Jove, enriched with the oxen of Iberia, is said to have reached the Lacinian shore from the ocean, after a lucky voyage; and, while his herd strayed over the soft pastures, to have entered the home of great Croton, no unwelcoming house, and rested from his long labor; and, at parting, to have said, "In the time of your grandsons this will be the site of a city" — and the promise came true. For there was a certain Myscelus, son of Alemon, an Argive, most dear to the gods of that age. Leaning over him as he lay heavy with sleep, the club-bearer said, "Come, leave your native home; go, seek the pebbly waters of the far-off Aesar" — and threatened many fearful things if he did not obey; then sleep and the god departed together. The son of Alemon rose and turned the fresh vision over in his silent mind, and long his purpose fought within him: the god bids him go, the laws forbid his leaving, and death is set as the penalty for one who would change his country. The bright Sun had hidden his shining head in the ocean, and thickest Night had raised her starry face, when the same god seemed to be there and give the same commands, and threaten more and heavier things if he did not obey. He was afraid, and at once made ready to move his home to a new land; a murmur rose in the city, and he was charged with despising the laws. When the case had been made, and the crime was plain, proved without a witness, the accused, in squalid garb, lifting his face and hands to the gods above, cried, "O you for whom the twice-six labors won the right of heaven, help me, I pray — for you were the cause of my crime!" It was the old custom to condemn the accused with black pebbles, to acquit them with white; and so now the grim sentence was passed, and every black pebble was dropped into the pitiless urn. But when it was turned over to pour out the pebbles for counting, the color of them all had changed from black to white; and the verdict, made favorable by the power of Hercules, freed the son of Alemon. He thanked his father, the son of Amphitryon, and with favoring winds sailed the Ionian sea, passed Spartan Tarentum and Sybaris and Salentine Neretum and the bay of Thurium and the fields of Iapyx, and, scarcely having coasted the lands that face those seas, found the fated mouth of the river Aesar, and, not far off, a mound under which the earth covered the sacred bones of Croton; and there, on the bidden ground, he founded his walls, and drew the name of the buried man onto the city. Such, by sure tradition, was the origin of that place and of the city set on the Italian borders.

There was a man here, Samian by birth, who had fled both Samos and its masters and, in hatred of tyranny, was a willing exile. In his mind he approached the gods, far off in the region of heaven, and what nature denied to human sight he drank in with the eyes of his heart; and when he had searched out all things with mind and watchful care, he gave them to the public to learn, and taught the crowds who sat in silence and marveled — the origins of the great universe, the causes of things, what nature is, what god, whence the snow, what the source of lightning, whether Jupiter or the winds thunder when the cloud is split, what shakes the earth, by what law the stars move, and whatever else lies hidden. He was the first to forbid animals to be served at table, and the first to open his lips — learned indeed, but not believed — in words like these:

Spare yourselves, mortals, from defiling your bodies with wicked food! There is corn; there are apples that bow the boughs with their weight, and swelling grapes on the vines; there are sweet herbs, and some that can be softened and made mild by fire; and the milky stream is not denied you, nor honey fragrant with the thyme-flower. The lavish earth heaps up her riches and her gentle food, and offers feasts without slaughter and blood. Beasts satisfy their hunger with flesh — and not even all of them, for the horse and the sheep and the herds live on grass; but those whose nature is wild and savage — Armenian tigers and raging lions, and bears with wolves — delight in a bloody meal. Alas, what a crime it is for flesh to be buried in flesh, for one greedy body to grow fat on another crammed into it, for one living creature to live by the death of another! In such abundance, which the earth, best of mothers, brings forth, does nothing please you but to gnaw the sad wounds with cruel teeth and bring back the ways of the Cyclops? Can you not calm the hunger of a greedy, ill-ruled belly unless you first destroy another?

But that old age we have named the Golden was blessed in the fruit of the trees and the herbs the ground rears, and did not stain the mouth with blood. Then the birds moved their wings safely through the air, the hare wandered fearless in the open fields, and no credulity had hung the fish on the hook; all was without ambush, fearing no trickery, and full of peace. But when someone — whoever he was, no good adviser — envied this simple diet and buried a meal of flesh in his greedy belly, he made a road for wickedness; and the steel, first stained with blood, may well have grown warm from the slaughter of wild beasts — and that would have been enough. We admit that creatures which seek our destruction may be killed without breach of piety; but, though they might be killed, still they were not to be eaten. From there the wrong went further: the pig is thought to have first earned death, because it grubbed up the seeds with its snout and cut off the year's hope; the goat, for gnawing the vine, is led to the altars of avenging Bacchus — their own faults ruined the two. But what have you deserved, sheep, a gentle flock, born to tend to men, who carry nectar in your full udders, who give your wool for our soft clothing, and help us more by your life than your death? What have the oxen deserved, a beast without fraud or guile, harmless, simple, born to bear labor? Ungrateful indeed, and unworthy of the harvest's gift, is the man who could slaughter his own field-tiller the moment he had lifted off the weight of the curved plow, who could strike with the axe that neck worn bare by work, through which he had so often renewed the hard field and won so many harvests. And it is not enough to commit such a crime: they have charged the gods themselves with it, and believe a power in heaven can rejoice in the killing of the toiling ox. A victim without blemish, and finest in form — for being fair brings ruin — decked with fillets and gold, is set before the altars and, unknowing, hears the prayer, and sees the grain it helped to grow laid between its horns, and, struck, stains with its blood the knives it perhaps saw before in the clear water. At once they examine the entrails torn from the living breast and search the gods' minds in them; and then — so great is man's hunger for forbidden food — you dare to eat! Do not, I beg, and turn your minds to my warning: when you give the limbs of slaughtered oxen to your palate, know and feel that you are chewing your own farmhands.

And since a god moves my lips, I will duly follow the god who moves them, and open my own Delphi and the very sky, and unlock the oracles of a lofty mind. I will sing great things, never traced by the wits of old, things long hidden. It delights me to go among the high stars, to leave the earth and this dull place and ride on the clouds and stand on the shoulders of mighty Atlas, and look down from afar on men wandering aimlessly, lacking reason, and, trembling and fearing death, to hearten them thus and unroll the order of fate.

O race stunned by the dread of icy death, why fear the Styx, the shadows, the empty names, the poets' stuff, the bogeys of a false world? Bodies, whether the pyre takes them with flame or age with decay, cannot suffer any harm. Souls have no death, and, leaving their former seat, they live in new homes and dwell there, received. I myself — for I remember — in the time of the Trojan war was Euphorbus, son of Panthous, in whose breast once stuck the heavy spear of the younger son of Atreus; lately I recognized the shield, the burden of my left arm, in the temple of Juno at Argos! All things change, nothing perishes: the spirit wanders and comes from there to here, from here to there, and takes possession of any limbs it will, and passes from beasts into human bodies and ours into beasts, and never in any time does it die; and as pliant wax is stamped with new figures and does not stay as it was or keep the same shape, but is still the same wax, so I teach that the soul is always the same, but migrates into different forms. Therefore, lest piety be beaten by the belly's craving, cease, I warn you, to drive out kindred souls by wicked slaughter, and let not blood be fed with blood.

And since I am carried over a great sea and have given full sails to the winds: there is nothing in all the world that stays fixed. All things flow, and every image is formed in passing; time itself glides on in ceaseless motion, no differently than a river — for neither the river nor the fleeting hour can stop, but, as wave is driven by wave, and the one before is pressed by the one that comes and presses the one before, so the moments flee alike and follow alike, and are always new: for what was before is left behind, and what was not comes to be, and every instant is renewed.

You see the spent nights press on toward the light, and this bright radiance follow the black night; nor is the color the same in the sky when all things lie weary in mid-rest, and when Lucifer comes out bright on his white horse, and again when the daughter of Pallas, herald of the light, tints the world she will hand to Phoebus. The god's own disk, when it rises from the earth's edge, is red in the morning, and red when it hides at the earth's edge; at its height it is bright white, because there the nature of the aether is purer and flees far the earth's infection. Nor can the shape of nocturnal Diana ever be equal or the same, but always today's is less than tomorrow's if she is waxing, greater if she draws in her circle.

And do you not see the year pass through four seasons, giving a likeness of our life? For in early spring it is tender and milky and most like the age of a child: then the fresh blade, without strength, swells and is soft and delights the farmer with hope; all is in flower, and the kindly field plays with the colors of blossoms, and there is not yet any strength in the leaves. After spring the year passes, sturdier, into summer, and becomes a strong young man: no age is more robust, more fruitful, or burns with more warmth. Then comes autumn, the ardor of youth laid by, ripe and mild, midway between young man and old, temperate, its temples flecked with a little gray. Then comes aged winter, bristling with trembling step, its hair either stripped or white.

Our own bodies too are always changing, without rest, and what we were or are, we shall not be tomorrow. There was a day when, only as seeds and the first hope of men, we lay hidden in our mother's womb; nature laid on her skillful hands and would not have our bodies cramped, shut in the stretched mother's belly, and sent us out from that home into the empty air. Brought to the light, the infant lay strengthless; soon, on all fours, it moved its limbs like the beasts, and little by little, trembling, on knees not yet firm, it stood, its sinews helped by some support. Then it was strong and swift, and passed the span of youth; and, the years of middle life served out, it slides down the sloping path of failing age. This undermines and pulls down the strength of former years: old Milo weeps when he sees the arms that once, in the mass of their solid muscle, were like Hercules', hang loose and weak; the daughter of Tyndarus weeps too when she sees the old woman's wrinkles in her mirror, and asks herself why she was twice carried off. Time, devourer of things, and you, envious Age, destroy all, and, gnawed by the teeth of the years, you consume all things little by little in a slow death.

Nor do these things we call the elements stay fixed; give me your minds, and I will teach the changes they go through. The everlasting universe holds four generative bodies; of them two are heavy and are carried downward by their weight — earth and water — and as many lack weight and, with nothing pressing them, seek the heights — air, and fire purer than air. Though they stand apart in space, still all things are made from them and fall back into them: the loosened earth thins into flowing water, the water, made fine, passes off into breeze and air, and the air, its weight taken away, shoots up, finest of all, into the fires of the aether; then they come back again, and the same order is unwoven — for fire, thickened, passes into dense air, this into water, and earth is packed from the water made dense. Nor does its own shape stay with each; and nature, the renewer, makes new shapes from old. In all this vast world, believe me, nothing perishes, but it varies and renews its face; and to be born is to begin to be something other than before, and to die is to cease to be the same — though perhaps these things are moved here, those there, yet in the whole all things endure.

I could not believe anything lasts long under one form. So, ages, you came down from gold to iron; so the fortune of places has so often turned. I have seen what was once most solid earth be sea, and lands made from the sea; far from the ocean sea-shells lay, and an old anchor was found on a mountaintop; a rush of water made a valley of what was a plain, and a flood carried a mountain down into the sea; ground that was swampy is parched with dry sand, and places that had borne drought are wet with standing pools. Here nature has opened new springs, there shut them; and rivers, roused by old earthquakes, have burst forth, or, drying, sunk down. So, when Lycus is swallowed by a chasm in the earth, it rises again far off with another mouth; so the great Erasinus is now drunk up, now, gliding with hidden stream, is given back on the Argive plains; and they say the Mysian Caicus, weary of its source and its former banks, now goes another way; and Amenanus, rolling the Sicilian sands, now flows, now is dry, its springs stopped up. Once the Anigrus was drunk; now it pours waters you would not touch, since — unless all faith is to be taken from the poets — the Centaurs there washed the wounds made by the bow of club-bearing Hercules. And does not the Hypanis too, sprung from the Scythian mountains, once sweet, grow foul with bitter salt? Antissa and Pharos and Phoenician Tyre were once ringed by waves; none of them is now an island. The old settlers had Leucas joined to the mainland; now the sea surrounds it. Zancle too, they say, was joined to Italy, until the sea took away the border and pushed back the land with a wave between. If you seek Helice and Buris, cities of Achaia, you will find them under the water, and sailors still point out the sunken towns with their drowned walls. Near Troezen of Pittheus there is a hill without any trees, once the flattest stretch of a plain, now a hill; for — dreadful to tell — the wild force of the winds, shut in dark caverns, longing to breathe out somewhere and struggling in vain for freer air, since there was no crack in all their prison and no way out for their blasts, swelled the stretched ground, as breath from the mouth inflates a bladder or the hide stripped from a two-horned goat; that swelling stayed on the spot, took the look of a high hill, and hardened in the long years.

Though many instances come to mind, heard and known, I will add a few more. Does not water too give and take new forms? At noon your water is icy, horned Ammon, and at the rising and setting of the sun it is warm; the Athamanians, they say, kindle wood by pouring on their water when the moon has shrunk to her smallest circle. The Cicones have a river that turns the drinker's inwards to stone and lays marble on things it touches; the Crathis, and the Sybaris near it, in our own country, make the hair like amber and gold. And, what is more wondrous, there are waters that can change not only bodies but minds too. Who has not heard of shameful Salmacis and the Ethiopian lake — whose water, if a man drinks it, drives him mad or sinks him in a strangely heavy sleep? Whoever eases his thirst at the Clitorian spring flees wine and, sober, delights in pure water — whether there is a power in the water opposed to heating wine, or, as the natives tell, after the son of Amythaon, by charm and herb, had freed the maddened daughters of Proetus from their frenzy, he threw the mind's cleansings into that stream, and a hatred of wine stayed in the waters. The Lyncestian river runs opposite in effect: whoever draws of it with unruly throat reels as if he had drunk unmixed wine. There is a place in Arcadia the ancients called Pheneos, suspect for its double-natured water: fear it at night — drunk at night it harms, by day it is drunk without hurt. So lakes and rivers have, some one power, some another. There was a time when Ortygia floated on the waves; now it sits still; the Argo feared the Clashing Rocks scattered by the surge of the dashing waters, which now stand unmoved and resist the winds. Nor will Etna, that burns with sulphurous furnaces, always be fiery — nor was it always fiery. For if the earth is a living thing and breathes and has vents in many places that exhale flame, it can change the ways of its breathing and, whenever it stirs, close these caverns and open those; or if light winds are shut in its lowest caves and hurl rock against rock and matter holding the seeds of flame, and it catches fire at the blows, the caves will grow cold when the winds are calmed; or if bituminous forces catch fire, or yellow sulphur burns with a faint smoke — then, when the earth no longer gives food and rich fuel to the flame, its strength spent in the long ages, and nature's nourishment fails its greedy fire, it will not bear the hunger, and, abandoned, will abandon its flames.

There are men, they say, in northern Pallene, who are wont to veil their bodies in light feathers when they have gone nine times under the Tritonian lake; I do not believe it — though the Scythian women, their limbs sprinkled with drugs, are said to practice the same arts. But if any faith is to be given to things proved by experience — do you not see that whatever bodies waste away by time or dissolving heat turn into small living things? Bury slain oxen in a pit — the thing is known by use — and from the rotting flesh, everywhere, flower-gathering bees are born, that, like their parents, work the fields and toil in hope. The warhorse buried in the ground is the source of the hornet; take the curved claws from a shore-crab and put the rest under the earth, and from the buried part a scorpion will come out and threaten with its hooked tail; and the caterpillars that weave gray threads over the leaves — a thing farmers have watched — change their form to the deadly moth. Mud holds the seeds that breed green frogs, and breeds them without feet; soon it gives them legs fit for swimming; and, that the same may be fit for long leaps, the back pair outdo the front. Nor is the cub the bear bears at birth anything but a barely-living lump of flesh: by licking, the mother shapes it into limbs and brings it to a form like her own. Do you not see that the young of the honey-bees, hidden in the six-sided wax, are born without limbs, and only later take on feet and wings? Who would think — did he not know it happens — that the bird of Juno, which carries stars on its tail, and the eagle, the armor-bearer of Jove, and the doves of Venus, and all the race of birds come to be from the middle of an egg? Some believe that human marrow, when the spine has rotted in the closed tomb, changes into a snake.

Yet these draw their origin from other things; there is one bird that renews and reseeds itself: the Assyrians call it the phoenix. It lives not on grain or grass, but on the tears of frankincense and the juice of balsam. When it has filled the five ages of its life, it builds itself, with talons and clean beak, a nest in the branches of an oak or on the top of a swaying palm; and, when it has strewn there cassia and spikenard and bruised cinnamon with tawny myrrh, it lays itself on it and ends its life amid the fragrance. From there, they say, from the parent's body, a small phoenix is reborn, to live as many years; and when age has given it strength to bear the weight, it lifts the nest's burden from the boughs of the tall tree and, dutiful, carries its own cradle and its father's tomb, and, reaching the city of Hyperion through the light air, lays it before the sacred doors in Hyperion's temple.

And if there is anything of strange novelty in these, we may marvel more that the hyena changes its sex, and that the one which just now, a female, took the male, is now itself a male; and that the creature fed on wind and air at once takes on whatever colors it touches. Conquered India gave lynxes to grape-crowned Bacchus, and, they say, whatever their bladder gives off turns to stone and hardens at the touch of the air. So coral too, at the first moment it touches the air, grows hard: it was a soft plant beneath the waves.

The day will fail me, and Phoebus will dip his panting steeds in the deep sea, before I can take in with my words all that is changed into new forms. So we see the ages turn, and these nations gain strength while those fall: so Troy was great in wealth and men and could give so much blood for ten years, but now, laid low, shows only her old ruins and, for riches, the tombs of her ancestors. Sparta was famous, great Mycenae flourished, and the citadels of Cecrops and of Amphion; now Sparta is a worthless spot, lofty Mycenae has fallen; what is Thebes of Oedipus but a name, what remains of Athens of Pandion but a name? Now, too, there is report that Dardanian Rome is rising, which, by the waters of the Apennine-born Tiber, lays the foundations of her greatness under a vast structure: she then, by growing, changes her form, and one day will be the head of the boundless world. So the seers say, and the fate-telling oracles; and, as I recall, Helenus, son of Priam, said to weeping Aeneas, doubtful of his safety, when the Trojan state was failing: "Son of a goddess, if you well grasp the presage of my mind, Troy will not wholly fall while you live! Flame and sword will give you a way: you will go, and carry off ruined Pergamum with you, until a foreign soil, kinder than your native land, falls to Troy and to you; and I see even now that Phrygian descendants are destined to build a city so great as none is, or will be, or was seen in earlier years. Other chiefs will make her mighty through long ages, but one born of the blood of Iulus will make her mistress of the world; and when earth has had the use of him, the heavenly halls will hold him, and heaven will be his end." This, I remember, Helenus prophesied to Aeneas, bearer of the household gods from Troy; and I rejoice that my kindred walls are rising, and that to such good end the Greeks conquered the Phrygians.

But, that we not stray too far with steeds forgetful of the goal — the heavens, and all beneath them, change their forms, and the earth and all in it. We too, a part of the world, since we are not only bodies but fleeting souls as well, and can go into the homes of beasts and be hidden in the breasts of cattle — let us leave safe and unharmed the bodies that may hold the souls of our parents, or brothers, or those joined to us by some tie, or at least of men, and not heap our tables as at the feast of Thyestes! How ill he schools himself, how he readies himself for human blood, the impious man who cuts the calf's throat with the knife and lends a deaf ear to its lowing, or who can kill the kid that cries like a child, or feed on the bird he himself has fed! How little is lacking there for utter crime? What a short step onward is prepared! Let the ox plow, or owe its death to old age; let the sheep give us defense against shivering Boreas; let the well-fed she-goats offer their udders to be pressed by the hand. Away with nets and snares and nooses and cunning tricks! Do not deceive the bird with the limed twig, nor pen the deer with dreaded feathers, nor hide the barbed hook in treacherous bait.
