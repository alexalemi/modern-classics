Book I

(Part 2 of 2)

Daphne, the daughter of the river-god Peneus, was Apollo's first love — and it was no blind accident that gave her to him, but the savage anger of Cupid. Apollo, still flushed with pride from killing the serpent, had lately seen the boy bending his bow, drawing the string taut, and had jeered at him. "What are you doing with a grown man's weapons, you spoiled child?" he said. "Gear like that suits my shoulders — mine, who can deal a wild beast an unerring wound, deal a wound to an enemy, who just now laid low the bloated Python with a thousand arrows, that plague sprawled over so many acres with its poisonous belly. Be content to stir up who-knows-what little love affairs with your torch, and don't go claiming glory that belongs to me."

The son of Venus answered him: "Your bow may hit everything else, Apollo, but mine will hit you. And as much as every animal is beneath a god, that much less is your glory than mine." So he spoke, and beating the air with his wings he lit, quick and eager, on the shaded height of Parnassus, and drew two arrows out of his quiver — two arrows of opposite work. One kindles love; the other drives it off. The one that kindles it is gold, and its point glints sharp; the one that drives it off is blunt, with lead set under the reed. This second arrow the god fixed in the nymph, the daughter of Peneus; with the first he wounded Apollo, driving it clean through his bones into the marrow. At once the one is in love; the other flees the very word "lover," happy in the deep hiding-places of the woods and in the pelts of the beasts she has caught, a rival of the virgin Diana. A single band held back her hair, tied up without any care for arrangement. Many men courted her; she turned every suitor away, and, wanting no part of a man, unacquainted with men altogether, she roamed the trackless forests and cared nothing for what marriage, or love, or a wedding might be. Time and again her father said to her, "Daughter, you owe me a son-in-law." Time and again her father said, "Daughter, you owe me grandchildren." But she, hating the wedding torch as though it were something shameful, would flush that lovely face with a modest blush and, hanging on her father's neck with coaxing arms, would say, "Dearest father, let me enjoy my virginity forever. Diana's father granted her exactly this." He gave in, of course. But that very beauty forbids you to be what you wish, Daphne, and your loveliness fights against your prayer.

Apollo is in love. He has seen Daphne, and he wants to marry her, and what he wants, he expects to have — his own oracles fail him. And as light stubble burns once the ears of grain are stripped away, as a hedge catches fire from a torch that some traveler has held too close, or left behind at daybreak, so the god went up in flames — so his whole heart caught fire, and he fed a hopeless love on hope. He looks at her hair falling unadorned along her neck and thinks, "And what if it were done up?" He sees her eyes, glittering like stars with their own fire. He sees her lips, and it is not enough merely to see them. He praises her fingers, her hands, her wrists, her arms bare well past the elbow — and whatever is hidden he imagines to be lovelier still. But she flees, quicker than the light wind, and does not stop when he calls after her:

"Nymph, daughter of Peneus, wait — please! I am no enemy chasing you down. Nymph, wait! This is how the lamb runs from the wolf, the deer from the lion, the dove on trembling wing from the eagle — each one fleeing its enemy. But love is why I follow you. I am wretched! Don't fall on your face, don't let the brambles scratch those legs that never deserved a hurt — don't let me be the cause of your pain. The ground you race across is rough. Slow down, I beg you, hold back your flight, and I will follow more slowly too. Only ask yourself who it is you please. I am no mountain rough, no herdsman; I am not some shaggy creature guarding cattle and flocks out here. You don't know, rash girl — you don't know who it is you're running from, and that is the only reason you run. The land of Delphi serves me, and Claros, and Tenedos, and the royal house of Patara. Jupiter is my father. Through me is revealed whatever will be, whatever has been, whatever is; through me song comes into tune with the strings. My arrow flies true — yet there is one arrow truer than mine, the one that has struck this wound in my untouched heart. Medicine is my invention; all over the world I am called the bringer of help, and the power of every healing herb is mine to command. But look — there is no herb that can cure love, and the arts that heal everyone else are no use to their own master."

He would have said more, but the daughter of Peneus fled in fear and left him there, his words unfinished, and left the god behind along with them. Even then she looked lovely. The wind bared her body; the gusts came against her and set her clothes rippling, and a light breeze blew her hair out behind her — her beauty was only heightened by the flight. But the young god will not waste his sweet talk any longer. As love itself urged him on, he ran after her at full stride. Like a Gallic hound that has spotted a hare in an open field — the one straining after its prey, the other after its life; the dog, seeming just about to close, hopes at every stride to have her now, this instant, and grazes her heels with his outstretched muzzle, while the hare, unsure whether she is already caught, is torn from the very bite and slips clear of the mouth just touching her — so ran the god and the girl, he swift with hope, she with fear. Yet the pursuer, borne up on the wings of love, is faster; he gives her no rest, looms right over her fleeing back, and breathes on the hair scattered across her neck. Her strength was spent; she went pale, and, worn out by the effort of that headlong run, she looked toward the waters of Peneus and cried, "Help me, father! If you rivers hold any divine power, destroy this shape by which I have pleased too well — change it, take it away!"

The prayer was barely finished when a heavy numbness seizes her limbs. A thin bark closes over her soft breast; her hair lengthens into leaves, her arms into branches; her feet, so swift a moment ago, stick fast in sluggish roots; a treetop takes the place of her face. Only her shining beauty is left. Even so Apollo loves her. He lays his hand against the trunk and feels her heart still fluttering under the new bark, and folding the branches in his arms as if they were limbs, he kisses the wood — and even the wood shrinks back from his kisses. "Since you cannot be my wife," the god said to her, "you will at least be my tree. My hair will always wear you, laurel, and my lyre, and my quiver. You will stand with the Roman generals when the glad voice sings out the triumph and the long processions climb to the Capitol. You will guard the gateposts of Augustus, standing most faithful before his doors, keeping watch over the oak wreath set between them. And just as my head is young, my hair uncut, so you too shall wear the everlasting honor of your leaves." Apollo had finished. The laurel nodded with its new-made branches and seemed to shake its crown just like a head.

There is a grove in Thessaly, closed off on every side by a forest set on steep rock. They call it Tempe. Through it the river Peneus, pouring down from the foot of Mount Pindus, rolls along in foaming waters, and in its heavy fall it gathers clouds that scatter a fine mist like smoke, sprinkling the tops of the woods with spray and wearying places far off with its roar. This is the home, this the seat, these the innermost chambers of the great river. Here, in a cave hewn from the cliffs, he sat and gave law to the waters and to the nymphs who dwell in them. Here first the local rivers gather, not knowing whether to congratulate the father or to console him: the poplar-lined Spercheus, the restless Enipeus, old Apidanus, gentle Amphrysus, and Aeas — and soon after, the other streams that, wherever their current has taken them, lead their wave-weary waters down to the sea. Only Inachus is absent. Hidden deep in his cavern, he swells his own waters with weeping and mourns his daughter Io as lost — most wretched of all. He does not know whether she is still alive or already among the shades; but since he cannot find her anywhere, he believes she is nowhere, and in his heart he dreads worse.

Jupiter had seen her as she came back from her father's stream, and had said, "You girl, worthy of Jove, who will make some lucky man happy in your bed — come to the shade of these deep woods" (and he pointed out the shade of the woods) "while the day is hot and the sun stands at the very peak of its course. And if you are afraid to go alone into the haunts of wild beasts, you will enter the secret places of the grove under the protection of a god — and no common god, but the one who holds heaven's great scepter in his hand, who hurls the wandering lightnings. Don't run from me!" For she was already running. She had left behind the pastures of Lerna and the tree-planted fields of Lyrcea when the god cloaked the wide land in a rolling darkness, halted her flight, and took her modesty by force.

Meanwhile Juno looked down into the middle of Argos, and, puzzled that fast-moving clouds had made the look of night in the bright of day, she saw that these were not river-mists and were not rising from any wet ground; and she looked around for her husband, knowing all too well by now the schemes of a mate she had caught so often. When she did not find him in heaven, she said, "Either I am mistaken, or I am being wronged," and gliding down from the top of the sky she came to rest on the earth and ordered the clouds to draw back. He had foreseen his wife's arrival and had already changed the daughter of Inachus into a sleek heifer. Even as a cow she is beautiful. Juno, daughter of Saturn, admires the look of the animal, however grudgingly, and asks — as if she did not know the truth — whose it is, and where it came from, and what herd it belongs to. Jupiter lies, says it was born from the earth, so she will stop asking after its owner. Then Juno asks for the cow as a gift. What can he do? To hand over his own beloved is cruel; not to give her up looks suspicious. Shame urges him one way, love argues him the other. Shame would have lost to love — but if so trifling a gift as a cow were refused to his own sister and wife, it might well seem to be no cow at all.

Even with her rival handed over, the goddess did not put aside all her fear at once. She was still wary of Jupiter, still afraid of a theft, until she gave Io into the keeping of Argus, the son of Aristor. Argus had a head ringed with a hundred eyes. Two of them took their rest in turn while the rest kept watch and stayed on duty. However he stood, he was looking toward Io; even with his back turned he had Io before his eyes. By day he lets her graze; when the sun has sunk below the deep earth, he shuts her in and ties a rope around her undeserving neck. She feeds on the leaves of trees and on bitter grass, and for a bed the poor thing lies on ground that is not always even grassy, and drinks from muddy streams. When she wanted to stretch her arms out to Argus in appeal, she had no arms to stretch; and when she tried to voice her grief, only a lowing came out of her mouth — and she was terrified at the sound, frightened by her own voice. She came, too, to the banks where she used to play, the banks of her father Inachus, and when she saw her new horns in the water she recoiled from herself in terror and ran. The Naiads do not know her; Inachus himself does not know who she is. But she follows her father, and follows her sisters, and lets herself be touched, and offers herself to their admiring hands. Old Inachus plucked some grass and held it out; she licks his hand, kisses her father's palms, and cannot hold back her tears — and if only words would come, she would beg for help, would tell her name and her disaster. Instead of words, letters that her hoof drew in the dust told the sad story of her changed body. "Oh, wretched me!" cries her father Inachus, and clinging to the horns and the snow-white neck of the moaning heifer, he says it again: "Wretched me! Are you my daughter, that I searched for across every land? Undiscovered, you were a lighter grief to me than now, found. You say nothing; you give no words back to mine; you only heave sighs from deep in your chest, and — the only thing you can do — you answer my words with a low. And I, knowing nothing, was preparing a bridal chamber and wedding torches for you. My first hope was a son-in-law, my second grandchildren. Now you must take a mate from the herd, now your children come from the herd. And I cannot even end so great a grief by dying — it is a curse to be a god, for the gate of death is shut against me, and my mourning stretches on into ages without end." As he grieved, the star-eyed Argus dragged her away, tore the daughter from her father and led her off to distant pastures. He himself took up a high mountain peak far off and sat there, keeping watch on every side.

The ruler of the gods can bear the granddaughter of Phoroneus's suffering no longer. He calls his son Mercury, whom the shining Pleiad Maia bore, and orders him to put Argus to death. There is little delay in strapping the wings to his feet, taking the sleep-bringing wand in his strong hand, and setting the cap on his hair. Once these were in order, the son of Jupiter leapt down from his father's high citadel to the earth. There he took off the cap and laid aside the wings, keeping only the wand. With this, dressed like a shepherd, he drives a flock of she-goats he has rounded up along the byways through the wild country, and plays as he goes on a set of reed pipes. The keeper Juno had set on guard was charmed by this strange new sound. "Whoever you are," Argus said, "you're welcome to sit here with me on this rock. Nowhere is the grass richer for your flock, and you can see how good the shade is for a shepherd." So the grandson of Atlas, Mercury, sat down, and passed the daylight in a long, easy stream of talk, and by playing on his joined reeds tried to overcome the watching eyes. But Argus fought to master soft sleep, and though slumber had taken part of his eyes, with the rest he still kept watch. He asked, too — since the pipe had only lately been invented — how it had come to be discovered.

Then the god said: "In the cold mountains of Arcadia, among the wood-nymphs of Nonacris, there was one water-nymph famous above the rest. The nymphs called her Syrinx. More than once she had slipped away from the satyrs who chased her, and from every god the shadowy forest and the fertile fields hold. In her devotions and in her virginity she followed the goddess of Ortygia — Diana; dressed after Diana's fashion, she could fool anyone and be taken for Latona's daughter, if only her bow were not of horn and Diana's of gold. Even so she deceived them. As she came back from the hill of Lycaeus, Pan caught sight of her, his head wreathed in sharp pine, and he spoke these words to her —" It remained to tell the words, and how the nymph, scorning his pleas, fled through pathless country until she came to the calm stream of sandy Ladon; how there, the waters blocking her run, she begged her watery sisters to change her; and how Pan, when he thought he had caught Syrinx at last, found himself holding not the nymph's body but marsh reeds; and how, while he sighed in disappointment, the breeze stirring in the reeds made a thin sound, like someone in mourning; and how the god, captivated by this new art and the sweetness of the sound, said, "This is a way I can keep talking with you, at least" — and so, with reeds of different lengths joined by wax, he kept the girl's name. But as the Cyllenian god was about to tell all this, he saw that every one of Argus's eyes had given way, his sight buried in sleep. At once he stops his voice and deepens the slumber, stroking those drowsy eyes with his enchanted wand. Without a pause he strikes the nodding head where it joins the neck with his hooked sword, and hurls the bloodied thing down from the rock, staining the sheer cliff with gore. Argus, you lie low; the light you carried in so many eyes is put out, and one night has taken your whole hundred at once.

Juno takes them up and sets them on the feathers of her own bird, filling its tail with star-bright jewels. At once she blazed with rage, and did not put off the hour of her fury. She loosed a horrifying Fury before the eyes and mind of her Argive rival, drove invisible goads deep into her breast, and hounded her, a fugitive, across the whole world. You, O Nile, were the last thing left at the end of her endless labor. The moment she reached you, she dropped to her knees at the water's edge, threw back her neck, and — lifting to the stars the only thing she could still lift, her face — with her groans and her tears and her grieving lows she seemed to plead with Jupiter and to beg an end to her miseries. He threw his arms around his wife's neck and asked her, at last, to end the punishment. "Set aside your fears for the days to come," he said. "She will never again be a cause of grief to you." And he called the Stygian marshes to hear the oath.

Once the goddess was soothed, Io takes back her earlier form and becomes what she was before. The bristles fall away from her body; her horns shrink; the round of her eye grows smaller; her gaping jaw draws in; her shoulders and her hands come back; the hoof, dissolving, is lost and becomes five nails again. Nothing of the cow is left in her but the whiteness of her skin. Content now with the use of two feet, the nymph stands upright — yet she is afraid to speak, in case she should low like a heifer, and shyly tries out the words she has gone so long without. Now, as a goddess, she is worshipped by the linen-robed crowds of Egypt.

To her at last, it is believed, Epaphus was born from the seed of great Jupiter, and throughout the cities he holds temples set beside his mother's. He had a friend, Phaethon, child of the Sun, his equal in spirit and in years. Once, when Phaethon was boasting grandly and would not yield an inch, all puffed up about his father Apollo, the grandson of Inachus could not stand it. "You believe your mother about everything, you fool," he said, "and you're swollen with pride over a father who isn't real." Phaethon blushed and swallowed his anger in shame, and carried Epaphus's taunts to his mother Clymene. "To hurt you all the more, mother," he said, "I — the bold, the free-spoken one — held my tongue. I'm ashamed that such an insult could be flung at us and that we couldn't throw it back. But you — if I really am born of heavenly stock — give me some proof of that high descent, and claim me for the sky." So he spoke, and threw his arms around his mother's neck, and begged her by his own life, and by the life of Merops her husband, and by the wedding torches of his sisters, to give him some sign of his true father. Clymene — moved, perhaps, by Phaethon's pleading, or perhaps more by anger at the slander against her — stretched both arms toward the sky and, gazing up at the light of the sun, said, "By that radiance bright with darting rays, my son, which both hears and sees us, I swear to you: you were fathered by this Sun that you are looking at, this Sun that governs the world. If I am lying, let him refuse ever to be seen by me again, and let this daylight be the last my eyes ever know. And it is no long journey for you to find your father's home. The house where he rises borders on our own land. If you have the heart for it, go, and ask him yourself." At once Phaethon springs up, overjoyed at his mother's words, and heaven is already his in his mind. He passes by his own Ethiopians and the Indians who live beneath the sun's fires, and eagerly makes his way toward his father's rising.
