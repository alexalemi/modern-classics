Book XIII

(Part 2 of 2)

On the Thracian shore the son of Atreus had moored his fleet, until the sea should be calm and the wind kinder. Here, suddenly, Achilles, as great as he used to be alive, rose from the earth that split wide, and, like one threatening, wore the face of that hour when he fiercely attacked lawless Agamemnon with the sword, and said: "Do you leave, forgetful of me, Greeks? Is all thanks for my valor buried with me? Do not! And, so that my tomb may not be without honor, let Polyxena be slain to appease the ghost of Achilles." He spoke; and his companions obeying the pitiless shade, the girl — brave and unhappy, and more than a woman — was torn from her mother's bosom, which she now cherished almost alone, and led to the tomb, and made a victim at the cruel pyre.

Mindful of herself, when she had been brought to the cruel altar and knew the savage rite was prepared for her, and saw Neoptolemus standing with sword in hand, fixing his eyes on her face, she said: "Use my noble blood at once; there is no holding back. Bury your weapon in my throat or my breast" — and bared throat and breast together. "Surely Polyxena would not want to be any man's slave; nor by such a rite will you appease any god. Only I could wish my death might be hidden from my mother. My mother is my one grief, and lessens the joy of my death — though it is not my death but her own life that should be mourned. Only, that I may not go unfree to the shades below, stand off, if what I ask is just, and keep men's hands from a maiden's touch: my blood will be the more welcome, whoever he is you make ready to appease by my killing, if it is free. Yet if the last words of my mouth move any of you — it is King Priam's daughter, not a captive, who asks — give my body back to my mother unransomed, and let her buy the sad right of a grave not with gold but with tears. When she could, she used to buy it with gold too."

She spoke; and the people did not hold back the tears she held. Even the priest, weeping and unwilling, split the offered breast with the driven steel. She, sinking to the ground on her failing knees, kept an unafraid face to her last moment; and even then it was her care, as she fell, to veil the parts that should be hidden, and to keep the honor of chaste modesty.

The Trojan women take her up and count over the mourned children of Priam, and how much blood one house has spent, and mourn you, girl, and you, so lately called a royal wife and royal mother, image of flourishing Asia, now even the wretched lot of the plunder — whom the victor Ulysses would not have wished his, but that you had borne Hector: Hector barely found his mother a master! She, embracing the body void of that brave soul, gave to it too the tears she had so often given her country, her sons, her husband; she pours her tears into the wounds, covers them with kisses from her mouth, beats the breast she is now so used to beating, and, tearing her gray hair clotted with blood, said much, and this too, tearing at her breast:

"Daughter, last grief of your mother — for what is left? — daughter, you lie dead, and I see your wound as my own. See: lest I lose any of my children without bloodshed, you too have a wound. Because you were a woman, I thought you safe from the sword — and yet, a woman, you have fallen by the sword; and the same Achilles who destroyed so many of your brothers has destroyed you, the ruin of Troy, the robber of me! But after he fell to the arrows of Paris and Phoebus, 'Now at least,' I said, 'Achilles is not to be feared' — and even now he was to be feared by me: the very ashes of the buried man rage against this house, and even in the tomb we have found him an enemy. For the son of Aeacus I was fruitful! Great Ilium lies fallen, and the public disaster is ended in a heavy end — but ended; only for me does Pergamum remain, and my grief still runs its course. So lately the greatest of all, strong in so many sons and daughters and in-laws and my husband, now I am dragged off an exile, destitute, torn from the tombs of my kin, a gift for Penelope — who, showing me to the Ithacan matrons as I spin my allotted task, will say, 'This is that famous mother of Hector, this is the wife of Priam.' And now you, who alone eased your mother's grief after so many losses, have made atonement at the enemy's tomb! An offering for the enemy I bore! Why do I hold out, hard as iron? Why do I linger? For what do you keep me, aged old age? For what, cruel gods, unless to see fresh deaths, do you reprieve an old woman so long-lived? Who would have thought Priam could be called happy after Pergamum fell? Happy in his death — he did not see you cut down, and lost his life and kingdom together. But you, I suppose, a royal maiden, will be honored with funeral rites, and your body laid in your ancestors' tombs! This is not the fortune of your house: your mother's gifts will be your lot — tears, and a handful of foreign sand. We have lost all; only one, dearest to his mother, remains, a reason to bear life a little longer — Polydorus, once the youngest of my sons, given to the Ismarian king on these coasts. Why do I delay, meanwhile, to wash her cruel wounds and her face smeared with dreadful blood?"

She spoke, and went to the shore with an old woman's step, tearing her white hair. "Give me an urn, Trojan women," the unhappy mother said, to draw the flowing water — and saw the body of Polydorus cast up on the shore, and the great wounds made by Thracian weapons. The Trojan women cried out; she was struck dumb with grief, and grief itself swallowed both her voice and the tears that rose within, and, most like a hard rock, she stiffened, now fixing her eyes on the ground before her, now lifting her grim face to the sky, now looking at her son's face, now at his wounds — the wounds above all — and she arms and readies herself with rage. As soon as it flared, she resolved to be avenged, as if she were still a queen, and was wholly in the image of punishment. And as a lioness rages, robbed of her suckling cub, and, finding the prints of his feet, follows the enemy she does not see, so Hecuba, having mixed rage with mourning, not forgetting her spirit but forgetting her years, went to Polymestor, the contriver of the dreadful murder, and asked to speak with him — for she wished, she said, to show him a hidden hoard of gold left for her son. The Thracian believed her, and, used to the love of gain, came to a secret place. Then, with coaxing lips, he cunningly said, "Away with delay, Hecuba, give the gift to your son; all you give, and all you gave before, will be his, I swear by the gods above." She eyes him grimly as he speaks and swears falsely, and boils with heaving rage, and, seizing him, calls up the throng of captive mothers and drives her fingers into his treacherous eyes and gouges them from his cheeks — rage makes her strong — and plunges her hands in, and, fouled with his guilty blood, tears out, not the eyes, for none are left, but the sockets of the eyes.

Provoked by their king's ruin, the Thracian people begin to attack the Trojan woman with thrown darts and stones; but she pursues the hurled stone with a hoarse growl and, her mouth set for words, barks as she tries to speak. The place still stands and takes its name from the event; and long remembering her old sorrows, even then she howled mournfully across the Sithonian plains. Her fate moved her own Trojans and her Greek foes, and all the gods too — so much that even the wife and sister of Jove denied that Hecuba had deserved that end.

Though she had favored those same arms, Aurora has no leisure to be moved by the disasters and fall of Troy and Hecuba: a nearer care, a grief at home, torments the goddess — her lost Memnon, whom his saffron mother saw perish by Achilles' spear on the Phrygian plains. She saw it, and that color with which the morning hours grow red went pale, and the sky hid in clouds. But the mother could not bear to watch his limbs laid on the last fire; with loose hair, just as she was, she did not scorn to fall at great Jove's knees and add these words to her tears: "Lowest of all the goddesses the golden sky holds — for my temples are the fewest in all the world — yet a goddess, I come; not that you grant me shrines and days of sacrifice and altars warm with fire, but, if you consider how much I, a woman, do for you when at each new dawn I keep the borders of night, you would think some reward due. But that is not my care, nor is Aurora in a state now to demand deserved honors: bereft of my Memnon I come, who bore brave arms in vain for his uncle, and in his early years — so you willed it — was killed by brave Achilles. Grant him, I pray, highest ruler of the gods, some honor as a comfort for his death, and ease a mother's wounds."

Jove nodded; and suddenly Memnon's high pyre sank with its towering fires, and rolls of black smoke stained the day, as when a river-nymph breathes up fogs and the sun is not let below. The black ash flies, gathers into one body, thickens, takes a shape, draws heat and life from the fire — its own lightness gave it wings — and, first like a bird, then a true bird, it whirred with its wings; and at once countless sisters whirred with it, born of the same origin. Three times they circle the pyre, and three times a joined wailing rises into the air; at the fourth flight they part their company; then two fierce tribes from opposite sides wage war, and with beaks and hooked claws work out their rage and weary their wings and clashing breasts, and fall, kindred bodies, an offering to the buried ash, remembering they were born of a brave man. Their maker gives these sudden birds a name: after him they are called the Memnonides; when the sun has run the twelve signs, they fight again, doomed to die in their parent's honor. So, while to others it seemed sad that the daughter of Dymas now barked, Aurora is intent on her own sorrow, and even now sheds loving tears and drips them in dew over all the world.

Yet the Fates do not let the hope of Troy be destroyed with its walls: the Cytherean hero bears on his shoulders the sacred relics and, another sacred relic, his father, a venerable burden. Out of such great wealth, in his devotion he chooses that prize and his own Ascanius, and with his fleeing fleet is carried over the seas from Antandros, and leaves the accursed thresholds of the Thracians and the earth streaming with Polydorus's blood, and, with helpful winds and favoring tide, enters the city of Apollo with his companions. Here Anius, by whom, as king, men were tended, and, as priest, Phoebus was duly served, received him into temple and house, and showed him the city, the famous shrines, and the two trees Latona once clutched in her labor. Incense given to the flames, wine poured over the incense, the entrails of slain oxen duly burned, they seek the palace, and, reclining on high couches, take the gifts of Ceres with flowing wine. Then devout Anchises said, "O chosen priest of Phoebus, am I wrong, or, when I first saw these walls, did you not have a son and, as I recall, twice-two daughters?" Anius answered, shaking his temples wreathed in snow-white fillets, and said sadly: "You are not wrong, greatest hero; you saw me the father of five children, whom now — such change of fortune turns among men — you see almost bereft of all. For what help is my absent son, whom Andros, a land named for him, holds, keeping that place and kingdom for his father? The Delian god gave him augury; to my daughters Liber gave gifts beyond wish and belief: for at the touch of my daughters all things turned to grain, and the stream of wine, and the olive of Minerva, and rich was the use of them. When the son of Atreus, the destroyer of Troy, learned this — that you may not think we too felt nothing of your storm — he used the force of arms and dragged them unwilling from their father's bosom, and ordered them to feed the Greek fleet with their heavenly gift. Each fled where she could; Euboea was sought by two, and by as many, Andros, their brother's island. The soldiers came and threatened war if they were not surrendered; loyalty, beaten by fear, gave up the kindred bodies to punishment — and you might forgive a timid brother: there was no Aeneas, no Hector to defend Andros, through whom you Trojans held out to the tenth year. And now chains were being readied for their captive arms; lifting to heaven their still-free arms, they cried, 'Father Bacchus, help!' and the giver of their gift did help — if to destroy them in a strange way be called helping. By what means they lost their shape I could not learn, and cannot now tell; the sum of the loss is known: they took wings and became birds of your consort, snow-white doves."

With such talk and other they filled the feast, and, the table removed, sought sleep, and rose with the day, and went to the oracle of Phoebus, who bade them seek their ancient mother and kindred shores. The king sees them off and gives gifts to the departing: a scepter to Anchises, a scarf and a quiver to his grandson, a mixing-bowl to Aeneas, which once Therses of Thebes, his guest, had sent him from the Aonian shore — Therses had sent it, but Alcon of Hyle had made it and carved it with a long design. There was a city, and you might point out its seven gates: these stood for a name and showed which city it was. Before the city, funerals and tombs and fires and pyres, and mothers with loosened hair and bared breasts, showed grief; the nymphs too seem to weep and mourn their dried-up springs; a leafless tree stands bare and stiff, and goats gnaw the parched stones. Look — in the middle of Thebes he shows the daughters of Orion, one giving her bared throat a more-than-womanly wound, the other driving a weapon through her brave breast, dying for her people, and borne through the city in honored funerals and burned in a public place; then from the virgin ashes, lest the race fail, twin youths rising, whom fame calls the Coronae, leading the procession for their mother's ashes. So far for the figures shining on the ancient bronze; the rim of the bowl was rough with gilded acanthus.

Nor do the Trojans send back gifts of less worth: they give the priest an incense-box for the frankincense, a bowl, and a crown bright with gold and gems. Then, recalling that the Teucrians drew their origin from Teucer's blood, they made for Crete, but could not long bear its air; and, leaving its hundred cities, they long to reach the Ausonian harbors. A storm rages and tosses them, and winged Aëllo frightens them, taken into the unsafe harbors of the Strophades. And now, carried on, they had passed the Dulichian harbors, and Ithaca and Same and the Neritian homes, the kingdom of the lying Ulysses; and they see Ambracia, contested in a dispute of the gods, now famous for the Actian Apollo, and the stone that was once the transformed judge, and the land of Dodona, vocal with its oaks, and the Chaonian bays, where the sons of the Molossian king escaped the wicked fires on borrowed wings. They make for the neighboring land of the Phaeacians, planted with lovely fruit; then Epirus, and Buthrotos ruled by the Phrygian seer, and a copied Troy; and, sure of the future — all that Helenus, son of Priam, had faithfully foretold — they enter Sicily. With three points it runs out into the sea: Pachynos turned to the rainy south, Lilybaeum facing the soft west winds, and Peloros looking to the north, away from the open sea. Here the Trojans come, and with oars and favoring tide, at nightfall the fleet gains the sands of Zancle.

Scylla infests the right side, restless Charybdis the left: one swallows and vomits up again the ships it takes; the other, with a maiden's face, has her dark belly girt with fierce dogs — and, if the poets have not left it all a fiction, she too was once a maiden. Many suitors sought her; refusing them, she used to go — most dear to the sea-nymphs — to the nymphs of the sea, and tell of the youths' baffled loves. As Galatea gave her hair to be combed, she addressed her thus, drawing sighs: "Yet you, girl, no ungentle race of men courts, and you can refuse them, as you do, unharmed; but I, whose father is Nereus, whom sea-blue Doris bore, guarded too by a crowd of sisters, could not escape the Cyclops' love except through sorrow" — and tears choked her voice. When the girl had wiped them with her marble thumb and comforted the goddess, she said, "Tell me, dearest, and do not hide from me — I am true to you — the cause of your grief." The Nereid answered the daughter of Crataeis:

Acis was the son of Faunus and the nymph Symaethis, a great delight to his father and mother, but a greater to me; for he had joined himself to me alone. Beautiful, and having passed twice eight birthdays, he had marked his soft cheeks with faint down. Him I pursued; me the Cyclops pursued without end. Nor, should you ask, could I say whether the hatred of the Cyclops or the love of Acis was stronger in me: they were equal. O kindly Venus, how great is the power of your reign! For that savage, dreaded even by the very woods, seen by no stranger and unharmed, the scorner of great Olympus and the gods themselves, now feels what love is, and, caught by a strong desire, burns, forgetting his flocks and his caves. And now you care for your looks, now for pleasing, Polyphemus; now you comb your stiff hair with rakes, now it pleases you to cut your shaggy beard with a sickle, and to look at your fierce face in the water and compose it. Your love of slaughter, your fierceness, your endless thirst for blood cease, and the ships come and go in safety.

Meanwhile Telemus, come to Sicilian Etna — Telemus, son of Eurymus, whom no omen had ever deceived — approached the dreadful Polyphemus and said, "The single eye you carry in the middle of your brow, Ulysses will take from you." He laughed and said, "O most foolish of seers, you are wrong: another has already taken it." So he scorns the one who warns him truly, in vain, and either burdens the shore, striding with huge steps, or, tired, goes back to his shady cave. A hill, wedge-shaped, runs out in a long point into the sea, the ocean's wave flowing round both sides. Here the fierce Cyclops climbed and sat in the middle; his woolly flocks followed with no one leading. When the pine that served him for a staff — fit for a mast — was laid at his feet, and he had taken up his pipe of a hundred reeds, all the mountains felt the shepherd's piping, and the waves felt it. Hidden in a rock and resting in my Acis's lap, I caught from afar with my ears such words as these, and marked them in my mind:

"O Galatea, whiter than the leaf of the snow-white privet, more flowery than the meadows, taller than the tall alder, brighter than glass, friskier than a tender kid, smoother than shells worn by the ceaseless sea, more welcome than the winter sun or the summer shade, more graceful than the tall plane, clearer than ice, sweeter than the ripe grape, softer than swan's down and than curdled milk — and, if you would not flee, lovelier than a watered garden. Yet you, the same Galatea, are wilder than untamed bullocks, harder than aged oak, more treacherous than the waters, tougher than willow twigs and white vines, more immovable than these rocks, more violent than a torrent, prouder than the praised peacock, fiercer than fire, rougher than thistles, more savage than a she-bear with young, deafer than the sea, crueler than a trodden snake — and, what most I would take from you, fleeter not only than the deer driven by loud barkings, but even than the winds and the fleeting air! But if you knew me well, you would repent your flight, and blame your own delay, and strive to keep me. I have, part of the mountain, caves hung with living rock, where neither the sun is felt at noon nor the winter; there are apples that weigh down the boughs, gold-like grapes on the long vines, and purple ones too: both these and those I keep for you. With your own hands you will gather the soft strawberries born under the woodland shade, the autumn cornels, and plums, not only darkened with black juice but of the choicest kind, like new wax. With me for husband you will lack no chestnuts, no arbute fruit: every tree will serve you.

"All this flock is mine; many too wander in the valleys, many the wood hides, many are stalled in my caves; nor, should you ask, could I tell you how many — it is a poor man who counts his cattle. For their praises trust me not at all; you can see for yourself how they can barely carry their swollen udders on their legs. There are lambs, the younger breed, in the warm folds, and kids of equal age in other folds. Snow-white milk I always have: part is kept for drinking, part the curdled rennet hardens. Nor will only common delights and ordinary gifts be yours — does and hares and a she-goat, a pair of doves, or a nest taken from a treetop: I have found on the mountaintop twin cubs of a shaggy she-bear that can play with you, so alike you could scarcely tell them apart; I found them and said, 'These I will keep for my mistress.' Now raise your bright head from the blue sea, now, Galatea, come, and do not scorn my gifts! Surely I know myself, for I lately saw myself in the reflection of clear water, and my form pleased me as I looked. See how huge I am: not Jove in heaven is bigger than this body — for you are wont to tell of some Jupiter reigning; my thick hair hangs over my grim face and shades my shoulders like a wood. And do not think it ugly that my body bristles with stiff hair: a tree is ugly without leaves, a horse ugly unless a mane veils its tawny neck; feathers cover birds, their wool becomes the sheep, a beard and rough hair suit men. I have one eye in the middle of my brow, but it is like a great shield. What of it? Does not the great Sun see all this from heaven — and the Sun has but one eye? Besides, my father reigns in your sea: him I give you for a father-in-law; only pity me and hear a suppliant's prayer! For to you alone I yield, I who scorn Jove and heaven and the piercing bolt; you, daughter of Nereus, I dread — your anger is fiercer to me than the lightning. And I could bear these slights more patiently if you fled all men; but why, rejecting the Cyclops, do you love Acis, and prefer his embrace to mine? Let him please himself and please you too — though I would not have it, Galatea — only let me get the chance: he will feel that my strength matches my great body! I will drag out his living guts and scatter his torn limbs through the fields and through your waves — so let him mingle with you! For I burn, and, slighted, the fire rages fiercer, and I seem to carry Etna, moved with all its flames, in my breast — and you, Galatea, are unmoved."

Having in vain made such complaints — for I saw it all — he rises, and, like a bull raging when the cow is taken from him, he cannot stand still, but wanders the wood and the known glades; when the savage saw me and Acis, unsuspecting and fearing nothing, he cried, "I see you — and I will make that the last union of your love!" And that voice was as loud as an angry Cyclops should have: Etna shuddered at the cry. I, terror-struck, plunged into the near sea; the hero, son of Symaethis, turned and fled, crying, "Help me, Galatea, I beg! Help me, her parents, and take me, about to die, into your realms!" The Cyclops pursued, and threw a piece torn from the mountain; and though only the farthest edge of the rock reached him, it buried all of Acis. But I did the one thing the Fates allowed: I made Acis take on his grandfather's power. Red blood ran from beneath the mass, and in a little the redness began to fade, and became the color of a stream muddied by rain, and cleared with time; then the thrown mass gaped, and through the cracks a living, tall reed sprang up, and the hollow mouth of the rock rang with leaping waters; and — a marvel — suddenly, up to the waist, a youth stood forth, his new horns wreathed with bending reeds, who, but that he was larger and all blue in the face, was Acis. But even so it was Acis, changed into a river; and the stream kept its old name.

Galatea ceased speaking, and, the company breaking up, the Nereids swim off in the calm waves. Scylla returns — for she does not dare trust herself to the open sea — and either wanders without clothes on the thirsty sand, or, when tired, finding a hidden nook of the water, cools her limbs in the enclosed wave. When, look — cutting the deep, a new dweller of the high sea, his limbs lately changed at Anthedon near Euboea, Glaucus comes, and, in his longing for the maiden he has seen, lingers and says whatever he thinks may hold her as she flees. Yet she flees, and, swift with fear, reaches the top of a mountain near the shore. In front of the sea a huge ridge rises to a single peak, curving far over the waves, treeless. Here she stops, and, safe in the place, not knowing whether he is a monster or a god, admires his color and his flowing hair that covers his shoulders and back, and how a coiling fish closes the end of his groin. He sees it, and, leaning on a nearby rock, says: "I am no monster, no wild beast, maiden, but a god of the water; nor have Proteus and Triton and Palaemon, son of Athamas, a greater right over the sea. Yet once I was mortal — but, owed even then to the deep, I was busy in it: for at one time I would haul the nets that drew the fish, at another, seated on a rock, I would work the line with the rod. There is a shore next to a green meadow, one part ringed by water, the other by grass, which no horned heifers had ever cropped, nor you, gentle sheep, nor shaggy goats; no busy bee took gathered blossoms from it, no festive garlands were made from it, no mower's hand had ever cut it. I was the first to sit on that turf, drying my dripping nets; and, to count my captive fish in order, I laid out on it those that chance had driven to my nets or their own credulity to my hooks. It is like a fiction — but what use to me to invent? — at the touch of the grass my prey began to stir, to shift its sides, and to move on land as in the sea; and while I paused and wondered, the whole crowd fled to the waves and left their new master and the shore. I stood amazed, and long in doubt sought the cause: whether some god had done it, or the juice of some herb. 'And what herb,' I said, 'has this power?' and with my hand I plucked the grass and chewed it. Scarcely had my throat swallowed the strange juices when suddenly I felt my heart throb within, and my breast seized with love of another nature; and I could not stay there, and cried, 'Land never to be revisited, farewell!' and sank my body beneath the sea. The sea-gods thought me worthy of a share in their honor, and asked Oceanus and Tethys to take from me whatever was mortal; and I was cleansed by them, and, a purging charm said over me nine times, was bidden to put my breast beneath a hundred streams. No delay: rivers from different springs, and whole seas, poured over my head. So far I can tell you what happened worth telling, so far I remember; the rest my mind did not feel. When it came back, I found myself, in all my body, another than I had been, and not the same in mind: then for the first time I saw this beard, green with its deep hue, and my flowing hair which I sweep along the wide seas, and my huge shoulders and blue arms, and my legs ending, curved, in a finny fish. But what use is this shape, what use to have pleased the sea-gods, what use to be a god, if you are not moved by these things?" As he spoke such things, and would have said more, Scylla left the god; and he, raging, stung by the refusal, made for the marvelous halls of Circe, daughter of the Sun.
