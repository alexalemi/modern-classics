Book VII

(Part 1 of 2)

And now the Minyans were cutting the sea in their ship built at Pagasae — the Argo. They had visited Phineus, who dragged out a starved old age under an endless night of blindness, and the young sons of the North Wind, Boreas, had chased the maiden-faced birds, the Harpies, away from the poor old man's table. Under their famous captain Jason they had suffered much, and at last they reached the rushing, muddy waters of the river Phasis, in the land of Colchis.

They go before the king, Aeetes, and ask for the fleece that had once been Phryxus's — the Golden Fleece. Aeetes lays down his terms: a dreadful list of mighty labors. And in that same hour the king's daughter, Medea, catches fire. She fought the flame a long time, and when reason could not master the madness, she said: "It's no use, Medea — you fight it and lose. Some god is against me, I don't know which. This is a strange thing — unless what I feel is the thing they call love, or something so like it there's no telling them apart. Why else do my father's terms look too cruel to me? And they are too cruel! Why am I terrified that a man I only just laid eyes on might die? What is the reason for a fear this great? Drive the flames out of your virgin heart, you wretched girl, if you can! If only I could — I'd be saner for it. But a new force drags me against my will. Desire pulls one way, my better judgment another. I see the right course, and I approve of it — and I follow the wrong one.

"Why are you burning for a stranger, you, a princess? Why long for a marriage bed in a world not your own? This land, too, could give you something to love. Whether he lives or dies rests with the gods. Still — let him live! That much I can pray for even without loving him. For what has Jason done wrong? Whose heart, unless it were made of stone, would not be touched by Jason's youth, his birth, his courage? And even if all the rest were missing — whose heart would his face not move? It has certainly moved mine.

"But unless I help him, the bulls will breathe their fire in his face; he'll come up against a crop of enemies sprung from the very ground he sowed; or he'll be flung to the greedy dragon as its living prey. If I let that happen, then I'll admit I was born from a tiger — that I carry iron and rock inside my chest! And why not go further? Why not watch him die, and stain my eyes by looking on? Why not urge the bulls against him, and the savage earth-born men, and the sleepless dragon? The gods forbid it! But these are not things to pray for — they are things to do. So — am I to betray my father's kingdom? To save some stranger by my art, some man I hardly know, so that once I've delivered him he can spread his sails to the wind without me, become another woman's husband, and leave Medea to her punishment? If he could do that — if he could set another woman above me — then let the ingrate die! But no: that is not his face, that is not the nobility in his spirit, not the grace of his body, for me to fear betrayal, fear he'd forget what he owes me. Besides, he'll pledge his faith first, and I'll make the gods stand witness to our bargain. What are you afraid of, when you're this safe? Get ready, then, and put off every delay. Jason will owe you his life forever; he'll join you to himself by the solemn marriage torch, and through all the Greek cities crowds of mothers will hail you as the woman who saved their sons.

"So I'll be carried off by the winds, and leave behind my sister, my brother, my father, my gods, and the soil where I was born? Well — my father is cruel, and my country is barbarous, and my brother is still a baby; my sister's prayers are on my side, and the greatest of the gods lives inside me. I won't be leaving anything great behind. I'll be chasing something great: the glory of having saved the flower of Greece's young men, and knowledge of a better land, and cities whose fame flourishes even here, and the culture and the arts of those places — and the son of Aeson himself, whom I would trade for everything the whole world holds. With him for my husband I'll be called dear to the gods, and I'll touch the stars with the top of my head. Yes — they say there are mountains that crash together in the middle of the sea, and Charybdis, the enemy of ships, who first gulps down the water and then spits it back up, and greedy Scylla, ringed with savage dogs, barking in the Sicilian deep. But so long as I'm holding what I love, cradled against Jason's chest, I'll be carried over the long seas; in his arms I'll dread nothing — or if I fear anything, I'll fear it for my husband alone.

"Marriage, you call it, Medea? You dress your crime in flattering names? No — look hard at how great a wrong you're about to do, and turn away from the guilt while you still can!"

So she spoke, and there before her eyes stood the right, and loyalty, and shame; and Cupid, beaten, was already turning his back to go. She was walking toward the ancient altars of Hecate, the daughter of Perses, hidden away in a shadowy grove deep in the woods. She was strong again now; the fever had been driven off and had ebbed away — when she caught sight of Jason, and the flame she thought was out blazed up again. Her cheeks flushed; her whole face went hot. As a spark, small while it lay buried under the ash heaped over it, feeds on the wind and grows, and, stirred, rises again to its old strength, so her love — sinking now, now so faint you'd have thought it was dying — flared up at the sight of the young man before her. And by chance Jason was more beautiful that day than usual: you could forgive her for loving him. She stares, and holds her gaze fixed on his face as if she were seeing it now for the first time, and in her madness she thinks she is not looking at any mortal face, and she cannot turn away from him. But when the stranger began to speak, and took her right hand, and asked her help in a low voice, and promised her marriage, she said, with the tears spilling over: "I see what I'm doing. It won't be ignorance of the truth that undoes me — it will be love. My gift will save you. Once saved, keep your promise."

He swears by the rites of the triple goddess Hecate, and by whatever divine power lived in that grove, and by the all-seeing Sun, the father of his future father-in-law, and by his own coming ordeals, and by dangers so great. She believed him, and then and there he took from her the enchanted herbs, learned how to use them, and went back to his lodging glad.

The next dawn had chased off the glittering stars. The people gathered on the field sacred to Mars and took their places on the slopes; in the middle of the crowd sat the king himself, in purple, marked out by his ivory scepter. Look — the bronze-footed bulls breathe out Vulcan's fire from their adamant nostrils, and the grass they graze catches and burns. Like furnaces roaring when they're full of flame, or like lime dissolving in an earthen kiln, hissing into fire when sprinkled with running water — so their chests, rolling the shut-in flames around inside them, and their scorched throats, sound and roar. And yet Jason walks straight up to meet them. As he came on, they turned their terrible faces toward him, and their iron-tipped horns, and stamped the dusty ground with cloven hooves, and filled the place with smoking bellows. The Minyans froze with dread. Jason goes in and feels nothing of the breathed-out fire — such is the power of the drugs — and with a bold right hand he strokes their hanging dewlaps, forces them under the yoke, and makes them drag the heavy weight of the plow and cut into a field that had never known iron. The Colchians are amazed; the Minyans swell it with their cheering and put fresh heart in him.

Then he takes the dragon's teeth from a bronze helmet and scatters them over the plowed field. The earth, steeped beforehand in a strong poison, softens the seed, and the sown teeth grow and turn into new bodies. Just as an infant takes on human shape inside its mother's womb, and is formed there part by part, and does not come out into the open air until it's ready, so, when the shape of a man had been finished inside the belly of the pregnant earth, it rose up out of the teeming field — and, stranger still, at the very moment it was born it clashed the weapons that came into being with it. When the Greeks saw these earth-men getting ready to hurl their sharp-pointed spears at the head of the Thessalian youth, their faces fell and their courage sank with fear. Medea herself was frightened too, the one who had made him safe. When she saw the young man, one against so many enemies, she went pale, and all at once, chilled and bloodless, she sat down; and, in case the herbs she'd already given him should prove too weak, she chants a second charm to help him and calls up her secret arts. Jason flings a heavy stone into the middle of his foes, and turns the war away from himself and onto them. The earth-born brothers die of each other's wounds, and fall in a battle among their own. The Greeks cheer, and hold their champion, and cling to him in hungry embraces. You too, barbarian girl, longed to throw your arms around him. Shame stood in the way of that; and yet you would have embraced him — but regard for your good name held you back from it. What you were free to do, you did: in silence you rejoice, in your heart, and you give thanks to your charms, and to the gods who taught them to you.

Now one thing still remained: to lull to sleep with herbs the ever-wakeful dragon, that guardian of the golden tree, marked out by his crest and his three tongues and terrible with his hooked fangs. After Jason had sprinkled him with the juice of Lethe, the herb of forgetting, and had said three times the words that bring calm sleep — words that could still a storm-tossed sea and stop rushing rivers — sleep came at last upon those eyes that had never known it, and the hero, Aeson's son, took the gold. Proud of his prize, and carrying off the woman who had won it for him as a second prize, he reached the harbor of Iolcos in victory, with his wife.

The mothers and gray-haired fathers of Thessaly bring offerings for the safe return of their sons; frankincense heaped on the flames melts away, and the promised victim, its horns gilded, drops dead. But Aeson is not among those giving thanks. He is close to death now, worn out with the years of old age. So Jason says: "My wife — to whom I confess I owe my life — you have given me everything, and the sum of all you've done for me is past believing; and yet, if your spells can do this too — and what can spells not do? — take away some of my years and add the years you take to my father's."

He could not hold back his tears. She was moved by the loyalty of his plea, and the thought of her own father, Aeetes, abandoned, so unlike Jason, rose in her mind — though she confessed nothing of the sort. "What a wicked thing," she said, "has slipped out of your loving mouth! Do I seem to you the kind who could sign over a stretch of your own life to someone else? Hecate would never allow it, and what you ask is not right. But I'll try, Jason, to grant you a gift greater than the one you ask. By my art I'll try to win back my father-in-law's long years — not by spending yours — if only the triple goddess helps me and is here to nod her blessing on so vast a venture."

Three nights were still missing before the horns of the moon would meet and close into a full circle. When the moon shone at her fullest and looked down on the earth with her disk complete, Medea went out of the house, her robe unbelted and flowing, her feet bare, her hair loose over her shoulders, and she took her wandering steps, alone and unescorted, through the still hush of midnight. Deep quiet had loosened the limbs of men and birds and beasts. No sound came from the hedgerows; the leaves hung motionless and silent; the damp air was silent; only the stars glimmered. To them she stretched out her arms, turned herself around three times, three times splashed her hair with water drawn from the stream, and three times loosed a long wailing cry from her mouth. Then, bending her knee to the hard ground, she said:

"Night, most faithful keeper of my mysteries; and you golden Stars, who with the Moon follow the fires of day; and you, three-headed Hecate, who come knowing my purpose and come to help my chants and the arts of magic; and you, Earth, who furnish the sorcerers their potent herbs; you breezes and winds and mountains, you rivers and lakes, and all you gods of the groves and all you gods of the night — be here with me. By your power, when I've willed it, the rivers have run backward to their springs while their banks stared in wonder; with my chant I still the tossing sea and rouse the calm one; I drive the clouds off and I bring them on; I banish the winds and I summon them; I split serpents' throats with my words and my spells; I move living rock, and oaks torn up with their own earth, and whole forests; I order mountains to tremble and the ground to groan and the dead to come out of their tombs. You too, Moon, I draw down, though the Temesan bronze clangs to ease your pangs during the eclipse; my chant even makes pale the chariot of my grandfather, the Sun, and my poisons make Dawn go pale. It was you who blunted the bulls' flames for me, who pressed the yoke on necks that had never borne it; you who set the dragon's brood at furious war among themselves, and lulled to sleep the never-sleeping guardian and, cheating that keeper, sent the gold off to the Greek cities. Now I need juices by which old age, made new again, can come back into bloom and gather up its first young years. And you will give them — for the stars have not flashed for nothing, and not for nothing has the chariot drawn by the necks of winged dragons arrived."

The chariot was there, let down out of the sky. As soon as she had climbed in and stroked the bridled necks of the dragons and shaken the light reins in her hands, she was snatched up on high. She looked down on Thessalian Tempe far below and steered her serpents to particular districts. She scanned the herbs that Ossa bore, and lofty Pelion, and Othrys, and Pindus, and Olympus taller than Pindus, and some she pulled up by the root and some she cut down with the curve of a bronze sickle. Many herbs on the banks of the river Apidanus pleased her too, and many on the Amphrysus; nor did you escape her, Enipeus. The Peneus contributed something, and the Spercheus, and the reedy shores of the lake Boebe. By Euboean Anthedon she plucked a herb of long life, not yet made famous by the transformation of Glaucus, whose body had not yet been changed.

And now the ninth day and the ninth night had watched her sweep over all those fields in her chariot on the wings of her dragons, when she came home. The dragons had been touched by nothing but the smell of those plants — and yet they cast off the skin of their old age, sloughing away the years. When she arrived she stopped short of the threshold and the doors, roofed by the open sky alone, and shrank from any touch of a man. She built two altars out of turf, one on the right for Hecate, one on the left for Youth. When she had wreathed these with vervain and wild boughs, she dug two trenches in the earth nearby, performed the rite, plunged the knife into the throat of a black ram, and drenched the wide trenches with its blood. Then, pouring goblets of clear wine over it, and pouring bronze goblets of warm milk, she spoke her words and called on the gods of the earth and begged the king of the shades and his stolen bride, Proserpine, not to be in any hurry to rob the old man's limbs of their life.

When she had won them over with prayers and long muttered chants, she ordered Aeson's worn-out body carried out to the altars and, lulling it into a deep sleep with her spell, stretched it out on a bed of herbs, looking like a corpse. She tells Jason to go far off, and the attendants to go far off, and warns them to keep their unhallowed eyes away from her mysteries. They scatter at her command. With her hair streaming loose, Medea circles the blazing altars like a worshipper of Bacchus in a frenzy; she dips torches split into many splinters into the trench black with blood, and lights them, so dipped, at the two altars. Three times she cleanses the old man with flame, three times with water, three times with sulfur.

Meanwhile the powerful brew is boiling in the bronze cauldron set over the fire, seething and going white with a swelling froth. In it she cooks roots cut in the valleys of Thessaly, and seeds and flowers and dark bitter juices; she throws in stones fetched from the farthest East, and sands washed by the ebbing tide of Ocean; she adds hoarfrost gathered by moonlight through the night, and the ill-omened wings of a screech-owl, flesh and all, and the guts of a werewolf, that shifting creature that changes its animal shape into a man's; nor was the thin scaly slough of the Cinyphian water-snake missing, nor the liver of a long-lived stag; and on top of these she puts the egg and the head of a crow that had lived through nine generations. When with these and a thousand other nameless things the barbarian woman had made ready her design, greater than any mortal could attempt, she stirred it all up with a long-dry branch of peaceable olive and mixed the bottom with the top. And look — the old branch, turned in the hot bronze, first goes green, then in no long time puts on leaves, and all at once hangs heavy with ripe olives. And wherever the fire flung the froth out of the hollow cauldron, and the hot drops fell to the earth, the ground blooms, and flowers and soft grass spring up.

The moment she saw this, Medea drew her sword and opened the old man's throat; and, letting the old blood run out, she filled the veins with her juices. When Aeson had drunk them in, taking them by the mouth or through the wound, his beard and his hair lost their whiteness and snatched back a black color; his gauntness fled, driven off; his pallor and his squalor were gone; his hollow wrinkles were filled out with new flesh laid under them; his limbs grew full and strong. Aeson marvels at himself and remembers that this was how he had been forty years ago.

From high above, Bacchus had watched the wonders of so great a marvel, and, learning from it that youth could be given back to his own nurses, he asked this gift from the woman of Colchis.

And, so that her arts should never rest, Medea pretends a false quarrel with her husband and flees as a suppliant to the doorstep of Pelias — the man who had wronged Jason's house. Since Pelias himself is weighed down with old age, his daughters take her in; and in a short time the cunning Colchian binds them to herself with the show of a pretended friendship. As she counts, among the greatest of her good deeds, the story that Aeson's decrepitude has been stripped away — dwelling on that part of the tale — the daughters of Pelias are seized by a hope that their own father might be made young again by the same art. This they beg of her, and press her to name her price, whatever it might be. For a little while she says nothing, and seems to hesitate, and keeps their eager minds in suspense with a put-on solemnity.

Then, when she has promised them, she says: "So that you can trust this skill of mine all the more — the ram that leads your flock, the oldest of your sheep, will be turned into a lamb by my brew." At once a fleecy ram, worn out by countless years, is dragged up, his horns curling around his hollow temples. When she had slit his withered throat with the Thessalian knife, barely staining the blade with his thin blood, the sorceress plunged the animal's limbs, and her potent juices with them, into the hollow bronze. The body's limbs shrink; he loses his horns, and with the horns his years; and out of the middle of the kettle a tender bleating is heard. Without any pause, while they are still marveling at the bleating, a lamb leaps out, frisks off in play, and goes looking for a teat of milk.

The daughters of Pelias are stunned. Now that her promises have proved good, they press her all the harder. Three times the Sun had unyoked his horses as they sank into the Spanish sea, and on the fourth night the radiant stars were glittering, when the treacherous daughter of Aeetes set plain water over a leaping fire, and herbs with no power in them at all. And now a sleep like death held the king, his slack body loosened, and held his guards along with their king — a sleep her chants and the power of her enchanting tongue had brought on. At her order the king's daughters had crossed the threshold with the Colchian and gathered around the bed. "Why do you hang back now, useless girls?" she says. "Draw your swords and drain the old blood out, so I can fill his empty veins with young blood! Your father's life and his years are in your hands. If you have any love in you, if the hopes you nurse are not idle, do your duty to your father: drive out old age with your weapons, and thrust in the steel and let the rotten blood out!" At this urging, the more loving each of them is, the more she is the first to be unnatural — and so as not to be wicked, she does a wicked thing. Yet not one of them can bear to watch her own blow; they turn their eyes away, and with their faces turned aside they deal blind wounds with cruel right hands. Streaming with blood, the old man still lifts himself on his elbow and, half-torn-apart, tries to rise from the bed, and there in the middle of all those swords he stretches out his pale arms and says, "What are you doing, my daughters? What arms you against your father's life?" Their spirits failed them, and their hands. He was about to say more when the Colchian took away his throat along with his words, and sank him, so mangled, into the boiling water.

And if she had not risen into the air with her winged dragons, she would not have escaped punishment. She flees aloft, over shadowy Pelion, the home of Chiron, son of Philyra, and over Othrys, and over the places famous for the fate of old Cerambus — who, lifted into the air on wings by the help of the nymphs, when the heavy earth was buried under the flooding sea, escaped un-drowned the waters of Deucalion's flood. On her left she leaves Aeolian Pitane behind, and the stone shape of the long dragon, and the grove of Ida, where Bacchus hid a stolen bullock under the false form of a stag; and the place where the father of Corythus lies buried under a little sand; and the fields that Maera terrified with her strange new barking; and the city of Eurypylus, where the mothers of Cos wore horns at the time Hercules's company was leaving; and Rhodes, dear to Apollo; and the Telchines of Ialysus, whose eyes ruined everything by the very act of looking, until Jupiter, loathing them, thrust them down under his brother Neptune's waves. She passed over the Cartheian walls of ancient Cea, where her father Alcidamas was one day to marvel that a gentle dove could be born from his daughter's body.

Then she looks down on the lakes of Hyrie and Cycnean Tempe, made famous by a swan that came into being there all at once. For there Phyllius, at a boy's command, had handed over birds he had tamed, and a wild lion tamed as well; ordered to master a bull too, he had mastered it; but, angry that his love had been scorned so many times, he refused the bull as the boy's last prize when he begged for it. The other, indignant, said, "You'll wish you'd given it!" and leaped from a high rock. Everyone thought he had fallen — but he had turned into a swan and hung in the air on snow-white wings. His mother, Hyrie, not knowing he was saved, melted away in her weeping and became a pool that bears her own name. Next to these lies Pleuron, where Combe, daughter of Ophis, escaped her sons' wounds on trembling wings. Then she sees the fields of Calaurea, sacred to Latona, which watched their king and his wife turned into birds. On her right is Cyllene, where Menephron was one day to lie with his own mother, in the way of savage beasts. Far off she looks back at the river-god Cephisus, mourning the fate of his grandson, whom Apollo changed into a bloated seal, and at the house of Eumelus, grieving for his son now in the air.

At last, on her serpents' wings, she reached Ephyre by the spring of Pirene — the city we call Corinth. Here the people of the earliest age told how, long ago, mortal bodies sprang from mushrooms grown out of the rain. But after the new bride burned up with the Colchian's poisons, and both seas watched the king's palace go up in flames, Medea's unnatural sword was steeped in the blood of her own sons; and the mother, having taken this monstrous revenge, fled from Jason's reach. Carried off from there by her Titan-born dragons, she came into the city of Pallas, Athens — the city that had seen the just Phene, and old Periphas, flying together as birds, and Polypemon's granddaughter borne up on new-made wings.

Aegeus takes her in — a man to be condemned for this one deed — and, as if giving her shelter were not enough, he even binds her to himself by the bond of marriage. And now Theseus had arrived, his son, unknown to his father, who by his own courage had brought peace to the Isthmus between its two seas. To destroy him, Medea mixes the wolfsbane, the aconite, that she had once brought with her from the shores of Scythia. This, they say, sprang from the teeth of Echidna's dog, Cerberus. There is a dark blind cave with a shadowy mouth, and a path sloping down, along which the hero of Tiryns, Hercules, dragged Cerberus out — the dog fighting him, twisting his eyes sideways from the daylight and the shining rays, straining against the chains of adamant. Roused to a rabid fury, Cerberus filled the air with barking from all three throats at once and flecked the green fields with white foam. This foam, they think, thickened, and, feeding on the rich and fertile soil, took on the power to do harm; and because it grows, full of life, on the hard rock, the country people call it aconite. This poison, by his wife's contriving, Aegeus himself held out to his own son, as if to an enemy. Theseus had taken the offered cup in his unsuspecting right hand when his father caught sight, on the ivory hilt of the sword, of the tokens of his own family — and struck the guilty drink away from the young man's lips. Medea got away from death by raising up a mist with her spells.

But the father, though he rejoices that his son is safe, is stunned all the same that so great a crime could have come within a hair's breadth of being done. He warms the altars with fires and loads the gods with gifts, and the axes strike the muscled necks of oxen with garlands wound around their horns. No more celebrated day, they say, ever dawned on the people of Erechtheus, the Athenians, than that one. The elders keep the feast, and the common folk, and, with wine sharpening their wit, they sing: "You, greatest Theseus — Marathon marveled at you for the blood of the Cretan bull; and that the farmer plows Cromyon safe from the boar is your gift and your work. Through you the land of Epidaurus saw the club-swinging son of Vulcan go down; the banks of the Cephisus saw the pitiless Procrustes fall; Eleusis, sacred to Ceres, saw the death of Cercyon. Sinis fell too, who put his great strength to cruel use — who could bend down whole tree trunks and used to drag pines from on high to the ground, to fling men's bodies scattered far and wide. The road to Alcathoë, the town of the Lelegians, lies open and safe now that Sciron has been laid low; and the land refuses a resting place, the water refuses a resting place, to the robber's scattered bones — which, tossed about a long time, age is said at last to have hardened into rocks. To those rocks the name of Sciron still clings. If we tried to count your titles and your years, your deeds would outnumber your years. For you, bravest of men, we take the public vows; in your honor we drink these draughts of wine." The palace rings with the people's cheers and the prayers of their applause, and there is not one sorrowing place in the whole city.

And yet — so surely is no one's pleasure ever pure, and some worry always breaks in on our joys — Aegeus did not get to enjoy his son's return untroubled. Minos is preparing for war. Though he is strong in soldiers, strong in ships, he is strongest of all in a father's anger, and he means to avenge his son Androgeus's murder with rightful arms. Before the war, though, he gathers friendly forces and ranges over the sea in the swift fleet that makes his strength. To his side he joins Anaphe and the realm of Astypalaea — Anaphe by a treaty, Astypalaea's realm by war; and then lowly Myconos, and the chalky fields of Cimolus, and Syros flowering with thyme, and level Seriphos, and Paros rich in marble, and Siphnos, which the wicked Arne betrayed. She had demanded gold in her greed, and once she took it she was changed into a bird that even now loves gold — the jackdaw, black of foot and cloaked in black feathers.
