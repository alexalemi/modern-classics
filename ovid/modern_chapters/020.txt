Book XI

(Part 1 of 2)

While the Thracian bard was drawing the woods and the hearts of wild beasts and the following rocks with such a song, look — the women of the Ciconians, their raving breasts covered with the skins of beasts, spied Orpheus from a hilltop as he set his voice to the sounded strings. One of them, tossing her hair on the light breeze, cried, "See! see — here is the man who scorns us!" and hurled her spear at the singing mouth of Apollo's bard; wreathed with leaves at the tip, it made a mark without a wound. Another's weapon was a stone, which, thrown, was overcome in the very air by the harmony of his voice and lyre, and lay before his feet like a suppliant for so mad a daring. But the reckless warfare swells, all measure is gone, and frenzied Fury reigns. All their weapons would have been softened by his song — but the huge clamor, the Berecynthian pipe with its blaring horns, the tambourines, the clapping and the Bacchic howling drowned the sound of the lyre; and then at last the stones grew red with the bard's blood, for now he could not be heard.

First the Maenads seized the countless birds still spellbound by his singing voice, and the snakes, and the throng of beasts — the glory of Orpheus's audience; then with bloodied hands they turned on Orpheus, and flocked together as birds do when they see the bird of night wandering by day; and, as when a stag doomed to die in the morning arena is a prey to the dogs in the built-up amphitheater, they set upon the bard and hurled their thyrsi, green with leaves, never made for such use. Some throw clods, some branches torn from trees, some flung flints; and, so their fury should not lack weapons, by chance some oxen were turning the earth with the driven ploughshare, and not far off some brawny farmers were digging the hard fields, winning their harvest with much sweat. Seeing the frantic troop, they flee and leave the tools of their work: hoes and heavy rakes and long mattocks lie scattered through the empty fields. Snatching these, and tearing apart the oxen with their threatening horns, the women rush back to the bard's undoing; and, as he stretched out his hands and for the first time spoke words that did nothing, moving them not at all with his voice, the sacrilegious women killed him — and through that mouth, O Jupiter, heard by rocks and understood by the senses of wild beasts, his breath went out and passed away on the winds.

The mournful birds wept for you, Orpheus, the throng of beasts, the hard flints, the woods that had so often followed your song; the tree, shedding its leaves, mourned you, shorn of its hair. They say the rivers too swelled with their own tears, and the Naiads and Dryads wore dark mourning and loosened hair. His limbs lie in scattered places. His head and lyre you receive, Hebrus; and — a marvel — as they float down the middle of the stream, the lyre laments some sad thing, the lifeless tongue murmurs something sad, and the banks answer sadly. And now, borne out to sea, they leave their native river and reach the shore of Methymnaean Lesbos. Here a fierce serpent went for the head cast up on the foreign sand and the hair dripping with dew; but at last Phoebus came, and drove it off as it made to sting, and froze the serpent's open jaws to stone, hardening the gaping mouth just as it was.

His ghost sinks under the earth, and knows again all the places he had seen before; and, seeking Eurydice through the fields of the blessed, he found her and folded her in his eager arms. Here now they walk side by side; now he follows as she goes before, now goes ahead and leads — and now, in safety, Orpheus looks back at his Eurydice.

Yet Bacchus did not let this wickedness go unpunished. Grieving for the lost bard of his rites, he at once bound down in the woods, by a twisting root, all the Edonian women who had seen the crime. He drew out the toes of each one's feet, just as she had pursued him, and drove their points into the solid earth; and, as a bird whose leg has caught in a snare the cunning fowler hid, when it feels itself held, beats its wings and, fluttering, tightens the noose by its struggles, so each of them, as she stuck fast, fixed in the ground, tried in vain to flee in her terror; but the tough root held her and checked her as she strained. And while she looks for where her toes are, where her feet, where her nails, she sees wood climbing up her shapely legs; and, trying to strike her thigh with a grieving hand, she struck oak — her breast, too, becomes oak, her shoulders are oak; and you would think her outstretched arms real branches, and thinking it you would not be wrong.

And this is not enough for Bacchus. He resolves to leave the country itself, and with a better troupe seeks the vineyards of his own Tmolus, and Pactolus — though it was not golden then, nor to be envied for its precious sand. His usual band, the Satyrs and Bacchants, throng about him, but Silenus is missing: the Phrygian country folk had taken him, staggering with age and wine, and, bound with garlands, led him to their king Midas, to whom, with the Athenian Eumolpus, Thracian Orpheus had once handed the mysteries of Bacchus. When he knew this partner and companion of the rites, Midas kept a glad festival for the coming of his guest, twice five days and as many nights joined in a row. And now the eleventh Morning Star had driven off the lofty host of the stars, when the king came rejoicing into the Lydian fields and gave Silenus back to his young foster-son. The god, glad at the return of his foster-father, gave Midas the choice of a wish — pleasing, but useless, as it turned out. Midas, doomed to make a foolish use of the gift, said, "Grant that whatever I touch with my body turn to yellow gold." Liber granted his wish and gave the harmful gift, and was sorry he had not asked for something better.

The Berecynthian hero goes off glad, rejoicing in his own ruin, and tests the truth of the promise by touching things one by one. Scarcely believing himself, he pulled a green twig from a low branch of a holm-oak: the twig turned to gold. He lifts a stone from the ground: the stone too paled with gold. He touched a clod: at the mighty touch the clod became a mass of gold. He plucked dry ears of corn: the harvest was golden. He holds an apple picked from a tree: you would think the Hesperides had given it. If he laid his fingers on the tall doorposts, the posts seemed to shine; even when he washed his hands in the clear water, the water flowing from his hands could have fooled Danaë. He can scarcely hold his own hopes in his mind, imagining all things gold. As he rejoices, his servants set before him a table heaped with food, and not lacking roasted grain; but then, whether he touched the gifts of Ceres with his hand, the gifts of Ceres went stiff, or whether he made to tear the food with hungry teeth, a yellow plate pressed the food as his teeth came down; and he mixed the giver of the gift, Bacchus's wine, with pure water — you could see molten gold flowing through his jaws.

Stunned by the strangeness of his misfortune, rich and wretched at once, he longs to flee his wealth, and hates what he lately prayed for. No plenty relieves his hunger; dry thirst burns his throat; and, rightly, he is tortured by the hated gold. Lifting his hands and shining arms to heaven, he cried, "Forgive me, father Lenaeus! I have sinned; but pity me, I pray, and snatch me from this fair-seeming ruin!" The gods' power is gentle. Bacchus, when Midas confessed his fault, restored him, and undid the gift given by their bargain: "So that you may not stay coated with the gold you so unluckily wished, go," he said, "to the river that neighbors great Sardis, and, climbing the ridge, make your way against the falling waters until you come to the stream's source; and there, where it bubbles up most fully, plunge your head under the foaming spring, and your body with it, and wash away your crime at once." The king went under the water as bidden: the golden power tinged the river and passed from the human body into the stream; and even now the fields, taking the seed of that old vein, are hard, pale with gold from their soaked clods.

Hating riches, Midas dwelt in the woods and fields, following Pan, who always lives in the mountain caves; but his dull wit stayed, and, as before, the folly of his stupid mind was fated to hurt its owner again. For lofty Tmolus, looking out wide over the sea, rises steep in its high ascent, and, stretching down on either side, is bounded on one hand by Sardis, on the other by little Hypaepae. While Pan was there boasting of his tunes to the tender nymphs, and warbling a slight song on his waxed reeds, daring to scorn Apollo's playing beside his own, he came to an unequal contest under the judgment of Tmolus. The old judge sat on his own mountain and freed his ears of the crowding trees; only oak wreathes his dark-blue hair, and acorns hang about his hollow temples. Looking at the god of the flocks, he said, "There is no delay in your judge." Pan sounds his rustic reeds and charms Midas with his rough song — for Midas happened to be there. After him, the sacred Tmolus turned his face to the face of Phoebus; his own woods followed his face. Apollo, his golden head wreathed with Parnassian laurel, sweeps the ground with a robe soaked in Tyrian purple, holds up in his left hand his lyre inlaid with gems and Indian ivory, and in the other holds the plectrum; his very posture was an artist's. Then he plucks the strings with a skilled thumb, and, caught by their sweetness, Tmolus bids Pan lower his reeds before the lyre.

The judgment and verdict of the sacred mountain pleased them all — yet it is faulted, and called unjust, by the voice of Midas alone. The Delian god does not let those stupid ears keep their human shape, but draws them out to length, fills them with gray hairs, makes them unsteady at the base, and gives them the power to move. The rest is a man's; in one part alone is he punished, and he wears the ears of a slow-moving ass. He tries indeed to hide them, and to veil his temples, loaded with the ugly shame, in a purple turban. But a servant who used to cut his long hair with the shears saw it; and though he dared not tell the disgrace he had seen, longing to bring it into the open yet unable to keep it in, he went off, dug up the ground, and, in a low voice, whispered into the dug earth what kind of ears he had seen on his master; then he buried this witness of his voice under the earth thrown back, and, the trench covered, stole away in silence. There a thick grove of quivering reeds began to rise; and, as soon as it ripened at the full year, it betrayed the planter: stirred by the gentle south wind, it repeated the buried words and gave away its master's ears.

Avenged, the son of Latona left Tmolus and, borne through the clear air, came to rest on the plains of Laomedon, this side of the narrow sea of Helle. On the right of Sigeum, on the left of deep Rhoeteum, there is an old altar sacred to the Thunderer who hears all voices. From there Apollo sees Laomedon just then building the walls of rising Troy, and that the great work is growing with hard toil and demanding no small resources; and so, with the trident-bearing father of the swelling deep, he took mortal shape, and for the Phrygian king they built the walls, a sum of gold agreed on for the defenses. The work stood done; the king denies the reward, and, to crown his treachery, adds perjury with false words. "You will not do this unpunished," said the king of the sea, and drove all his waters against the shores of greedy Troy, and turned the land into the shape of a sea, and carried off the farmers' wealth, and buried the fields in waves. Nor is this penalty enough: the king's daughter too is demanded for a sea-monster. Chained to the hard rocks, she is freed by Hercules, who demands the promised reward, the horses agreed on; and, the wage of so great a work denied, he takes the twice-perjured walls of conquered Troy. Nor did Telamon, a sharer in the war, go off without honor; he won Hesione, given to him.

For Peleus was made famous by a goddess for his wife, no prouder of his grandfather's name than of his father-in-law's — since it fell to more than one man to be Jove's grandson, but to one man alone to have a goddess for a wife. For old Proteus had said to Thetis: "Goddess of the wave, conceive: you will be the mother of a youth who, in his strong years, will surpass his father's deeds and be called greater than he." So, lest the world hold anything greater than Jove, though he had felt no gentle fire in his breast, Jupiter fled the marriage of sea-born Thetis, and bade his grandson, the son of Aeacus, take his place in the wish, and go to the embrace of the sea-maiden.

There is a bay of Thessaly curved into a bending arch; its arms run out; there, were the water deeper, would be a harbor, but the sea just covers the surface of the sand; the shore is firm, keeping no footprint, delaying no step, hidden by no weed; a myrtle grove is near, set with two-colored berries. In the middle is a cave, whether made by nature or art is doubtful — more by art; to which you, Thetis, used often to come, riding naked on your bridled dolphin. There Peleus seized you as you lay bound in sleep; and since, tried by prayers, you resisted, he made ready force, clasping your neck with both arms. And had you not fled, as often, to your familiar arts of changing shape, he would have won his daring attempt: but now you were a bird — yet a bird he held; now a heavy tree — Peleus clung to the tree; your third shape was a spotted tigress, and, frightened by that, the son of Aeacus loosed his arms from your body. Then, pouring wine on the waters, he worshiped the sea-gods with the entrails of sheep and the smoke of incense, until the Carpathian seer said from the middle of the flood: "Son of Aeacus, you will win the marriage you seek. Only, when she rests asleep in the cool cave, bind her unawares with cords and holding bonds. And let her not deceive you by faking a hundred shapes, but hold her, whatever she is, until she takes back the form she had before." Proteus said this, and hid his face in the sea, and let his own waves in over his last words.

Titan was sinking, and, with his chariot-pole tilted down, was taking the western sea, when the beautiful Nereid, leaving the deep, entered her wonted resting-place. Scarcely had Peleus well seized the maiden's limbs when she began her shapes anew, until she felt her limbs held and her arms stretched apart. Then at last she sighed and said, "You do not conquer without a god's help" — and showed herself as Thetis. The hero embraced her thus revealed, won his wish, and filled her with great Achilles.

Happy Peleus in his son, happy in his wife, and one to whom all blessings had fallen, if you take away the guilt of murdered Phocus. The land of Trachis received him, guilty of his brother's blood and driven from his native home. Here Ceyx, born of Lucifer and bearing his father's brightness in his face, held sway without force or bloodshed — Ceyx, who at that time, sad and unlike himself, mourned a brother taken from him. When the son of Aeacus, worn with trouble and the long road, came there and entered the city with a few companions, leaving the flocks and herds he brought not far from the walls in a shady valley, and when he first had the chance to approach the king, he held out the tokens of peace in a suppliant hand, and told who he was and from whom sprung — hiding only his crime, and lying about the cause of his flight; and he begs help, in the city or the country. The Trachinian answered him with gentle lips: "Our bounties are open even to the middle sort, Peleus, and I hold no unwelcoming realm. To this you add strong weights — a famous name, and Jove for a grandfather. Do not waste your time in asking! All you ask you shall have; and call these things, whatever you see, in part your own — I wish you saw them in better case!" And he wept. Peleus and his companions ask what cause moves such grief; and he tells them:

Perhaps you think this bird, which lives by prey and terrifies all birds, always had wings. He was a man — and so unshaken was his spirit that even then he was fierce, savage in war and ready for violence — Daedalion by name. We were born of the same father, the one who calls forth Aurora and leaves the sky last of all. Peace was my care, the keeping of peace and my marriage; my brother loved cruel wars. His valor subdued kings and nations — that valor which now, changed, chases the doves of Thisbe. He had a daughter, Chione, who, most richly dowered with beauty, had a thousand suitors when she came of age at twice seven years. By chance Phoebus and the son of Maia, returning, the one from his Delphi, the other from the height of Cyllene, saw her at the same moment and at the same moment caught fire. Apollo puts off his hope of enjoyment to the hours of night; the other brooks no delay, and touches the girl's face with his sleep-bringing wand: at the mighty touch she lies still and suffers the god's force. Night had strewn the sky with stars; Phoebus, taking an old woman's shape, took the joys already snatched. When her ripe womb had filled its time, from the stock of the wing-footed god was born a crafty child, Autolycus, clever at every theft, who used to make white of black and black of white, no degenerate of his father's art; and from Phoebus (for she bore twins) was born Philammon, famous for tuneful song and the lyre.

But what did it profit her to have borne two children, and pleased two gods, and sprung from a brave father with the Thunderer for ancestor? Is glory a harm to many, too? To her, at least, it was: she dared to set herself above Diana and faulted the goddess's beauty. Fierce anger was roused in the goddess: "Then we will please you by deeds," she said; and without delay she bent her bow, sent an arrow from the string, and pierced with the reed the tongue that deserved it. The tongue fell silent; no voice, no attempted words followed; and, as she tried to speak, her life left her with her blood. Wretched, I held her then, and bore my brother's grief in my heart, and spoke comfort to my loving brother — which the father took no otherwise than rocks take the murmurs of the sea, and bitterly bewailed his daughter torn away. When he saw her burning, four times he made to rush into the midst of the pyre; four times driven back, he gave his swift limbs to flight and, like an ox carrying the stings of hornets on its galled neck, rushed where there was no path. Already he seemed to me to run more than a man, and you would think his feet had taken wings. So he outran all, and, made swift by the longing for death, gained the heights of Parnassus. Apollo, pitying him, when Daedalion had thrown himself from the high rock, made him a bird and held him hovering on sudden wings, and gave him a hooked beak, curved claws on his talons, his old courage, and strength greater than his body; and now, a hawk, kind enough to none, he rages against all birds, and, grieving himself, becomes a cause of grief to others.

While the son of Lucifer was telling these wonders of his brother, panting with haste, Onetor the Phocian, keeper of his herds, ran up. "Peleus, Peleus! I come to you with news of a great disaster," he said. Peleus bids him tell whatever it is; and the Trachinian too hangs in fear at the trembling mouth. The man tells it: "I had driven the weary bullocks to the winding shore, when the Sun at his highest in mid-course could look back on as much of it as he saw still to come; and part of the oxen had bent their knees on the yellow sand and, lying, watched the plains of wide water, part wandered slowly here and there, and others swam, standing above the waves with lofty necks. There is a temple near the sea, bright with neither marble nor gold, but made of thick beams and shaded by an ancient grove: the Nereids and Nereus hold it — a sailor drying his nets on the shore told us these were its gods. Next to it is a marsh, ringed with thick willows, which the water of the standing sea has made a swamp; from there, roaring with a heavy crash, a huge beast — a wolf — terrifies the neighboring places, and comes out of the marsh thicket, its lightning jaws smeared with foam and clotted blood, its eyes suffused with red flame. Though it rages with both fury and hunger, it is fiercer with fury; for it does not care to end its fast and its dread hunger with the slaughter of oxen, but wounds the whole herd and lays them all low like an enemy. Some of us too, defending them, were killed by the deadly bite; the shore and the near waves are red with blood, and the fens are filled with lowing. But delay is ruinous, and the case does not allow us to waver: while something is still left, let us all band together and take up arms — arms, I say — and bear our weapons in one body!"

So spoke the countryman. But the loss did not move Peleus; remembering his crime, he judges that the bereaved Nereid had sent these ills as an offering to dead Phocus. The king of Oeta bids his men put on their armor and take up stout weapons; and he was making ready to go with them, when Alcyone his wife, roused by the uproar, rushed out, her hair not yet fully done, and threw even the done part into disorder, and, flung on her husband's neck, begged him with words and tears to send help without himself, and so save two lives in one. The son of Aeacus said to her: "Lay aside your lovely, loyal fears, queen! Your kindness is thanks enough. It does not please me to move arms against this new monster; the god of the sea must be worshiped." There was a high tower, a beacon on its top, a welcome sight to weary ships. They climb up there and see with groans the bulls strewn on the shore and the cruel ravager with bloody mouth, its long hair fouled with gore. Then Peleus, stretching his hands toward the open sea, prays the sea-blue Psamathe to end her wrath and bring aid; but she is not bent by the words of the pleading son of Aeacus. Thetis, interceding for her husband, won that favor. Still the wolf holds on, not called back from the fierce slaughter, keen with the sweetness of blood, until she changed it to marble as it clung to the neck of a mangled heifer. The body kept everything but its color; the stone's color shows it is no longer a wolf, and need no longer be feared. Yet the Fates do not let the exiled Peleus settle in that land; the wandering outcast goes to the Magnetes, and there receives from Thessalian Acastus his cleansing of the murder.
