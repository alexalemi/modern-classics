Book XII

(Part 1 of 2)

His father Priam mourned Aesacus, not knowing that, having taken wings, he still lived; and Hector, with his brothers, made empty offerings at a tomb that bore his name. The presence of Paris was missing from that mournful rite — Paris, who soon after brought a long war home to his country along with a stolen wife; and a thousand sworn ships followed him, and with them the whole Greek nation. Nor would vengeance have been delayed, had not the raging winds made the seas impassable, and the Boeotian land held the ships, ready to sail, in fishy Aulis. Here, when they had prepared a sacrifice to Jupiter in their fathers' way, as the old altar glowed with the kindled fire, the Greeks saw a blue serpent creep up a plane tree that stood near the sacrifice they had begun. On the top of the tree was a nest of twice-four birds; the serpent seized them all, and the mother as she fluttered around her loss, and buried them in its greedy maw. All stood amazed. But Calchas, the son of Thestor, a seer foreseeing the truth, said, "Rejoice, Greeks — we will conquer! Troy will fall, but the length of our toil will be long"; and he assigned the nine birds to the years of the war. The serpent, just as it was, coiled around the green branches, becomes stone, and keeps the stone in the shape of a serpent.

Nereus stayed rough in the Ionian waves and did not drive the sails on; and there are some who think Neptune spared Troy, because he had made the city's walls. But not so the son of Thestor: for he neither failed to know, nor kept silent, that the wrath of the virgin goddess must be soothed by a virgin's blood. When the public cause had beaten natural love, and the king the father, and Iphigenia, ready to give her chaste blood, stood before the altar while the priests wept, the goddess was appeased, cast a mist before their eyes, and — so it is said — amid the rite and the crowd and the voices of the praying, changed the Mycenaean girl for a substituted hind. So, when Diana had been soothed by a death more fitting, and at the same moment the wrath of Phoebe and of the sea passed, the thousand ships took the winds astern and, after much hardship, reached the Phrygian shore.

There is a place in the middle of the world, between land and sea and the tracts of heaven, the meeting-point of the threefold universe; from it is seen whatever is anywhere, however far off, and every voice pierces the hollow ears. Fame holds it, and chose herself a house on the top of a tower, adding countless entrances and a thousand openings, and closing the doorways with no gates. Night and day it stands open; it is all of sounding bronze, all humming, echoing the voice and repeating what it hears. Within there is no rest, no silence anywhere; yet there is no shout, but the murmur of a low voice, such as comes from the sea's waves if you listen far off, or the sound the end of the thunder makes when Jupiter has clashed the black clouds. A crowd fills the hall; the fickle rabble come and go, and a thousand rumors, false mixed with true, wander up and down and roll about their muddled words. Some of these fill idle ears with talk, some carry off elsewhere what they were told; the measure of the fiction grows, and each new teller adds something to what he heard. There is Credulity, there rash Error, and empty Joy, and startled Fears, and sudden Sedition, and Whispers of doubtful source. Fame herself sees whatever happens in heaven and sea and earth, and searches the whole world.

She had made it known that Greek ships were coming with valiant troops; nor does the enemy appear in arms unlooked-for. The Trojans block the landing and guard the shore; and you, Protesilaus, are the first, by fate's decree, to fall by Hector's spear; and the battles now joined, and the brave spirits of the Trojans, and Hector, known then for the first time, cost the Greeks dear. Nor did the Phrygians learn at small cost of blood what the Greek right hand could do; and now the shores of Sigeum were red, now Cycnus, the son of Neptune, had given a thousand men to death, now Achilles pressed on in his chariot, leveling whole ranks with the stroke of his Pelian spear; and, seeking through the lines either Cycnus or Hector, he meets Cycnus — Hector was put off to the tenth year. Then, urging on his horses, their white necks pressed under the yoke, he steered his chariot at the enemy and, shaking his quivering spear in his arm, said, "Whoever you are, young man, take this comfort in your death: that you were killed by Haemonian Achilles." So far the grandson of Aeacus; his heavy spear followed his words. But though there was no missing in the sure spear, still the sharp point, so hurled, did nothing, and only bruised the breast with a blunt blow. "Son of a goddess — for by report we knew you beforehand," said Cycnus, "why do you wonder that wounds are kept from me?" — for Achilles was wondering. "This helmet you see, tawny with a horse's mane, and the hollow shield, the burden of my left arm, are no help to me; grace alone is sought from them — Mars too takes up arms for that. All the service of this covering shall be removed, and still I will go off unscratched. It is something to be born not of a Nereid, but of one who rules Nereus and his daughters and the whole sea."

He spoke, and hurled at the descendant of Aeacus a spear meant to lodge in the shield's rim; it broke through the bronze and the next nine folds of bull's hide, but stopped in the tenth circle. The hero wrenched it out and again hurled the quivering weapon with a strong hand; again the body was without a wound, unharmed; nor could a third point so much as graze Cycnus, open and offering himself. Achilles blazed no otherwise than a bull in the open arena, when with its dreadful horns it charges the purple cloths that provoke it and feels its wounds evaded. Still he checks whether the point has fallen from the spear: it was clinging to the shaft. "Is my hand weak, then," he says, "and has it poured out on one man all the strength it had before? Surely it was strong enough when I first threw down the walls of Lyrnessus, or when I filled Tenedos and Eëtion's Thebes with their own blood, or when the Caÿcus flowed purple with the slaughter of its people, and Telephus twice felt the work of my spear. Here too, with so many slain, whose heaps along this shore I have both made and see, my right hand has proved mighty, and is mighty." He spoke, and, as if he distrusted his earlier deeds, hurled his spear at Menoetes, one of the Lycian crowd, standing opposite, and tore through both his mail and the breast beneath. As Menoetes beat the solid earth with his dying head, Achilles drew the same weapon from the reeking wound and said, "This is the hand, this the spear, with which I have just conquered; I will use them on him — and I pray the end may be the same." So saying, he aimed again at Cycnus, and the ash spear did not miss; it rang, unavoided, on his left shoulder, then was thrown back as if by a wall or a solid rock. Yet, where he had struck, Achilles saw Cycnus marked with blood, and rejoiced in vain: there was no wound — the blood was Menoetes'.

Then indeed, roaring, he leaped headlong from his high chariot, and, striking at his fearless foe hand to hand with his bright sword, he sees that the shield and the helmet are pierced by his blade, but that his weapon too is blunted on the hard body. He bore it no longer; drawing back his shield, three and four times he struck the hero's face and his hollow temples with the sword-hilt, and, pressing on as the other gave ground, confounded him, drove him back, and gave him no rest in his daze. Terror takes Cycnus, darkness swims before his eyes; and, as he carried his backward steps in retreat, a stone in the middle of the field stood in his way. Over it Achilles, with great force, threw Cycnus on his back and dashed him to the earth; then, pressing his chest with shield and hard knees, he drew tight the straps of the helmet, which, fastened under the pressed chin, crushed the throat and took away the breath and the passage of life. He was making ready to strip the beaten man — and saw only the armor left behind; the god of the sea had turned the body into a white bird, whose name he had so lately borne.
