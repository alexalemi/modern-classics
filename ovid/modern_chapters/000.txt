Book I

(Part 1 of 2)

My mind moves me to tell of forms changed into new bodies. Gods — for you are the ones who changed them — breathe your favor on what I begin, and from the world's first origin draw down one unbroken song, all the way to my own days.

Before there was any sea, or land, or sky that arches over everything, the whole face of nature wore one and the same look across the entire world. Men have named it Chaos: a raw and unsorted mass, nothing but a dead weight, a heap into which the warring seeds of things, badly matched, had all been crammed together. No Sun yet gave the world its light. No Moon renewed her horns by slow growing. No Earth hung balanced in the surrounding air, poised on its own weight, and no Amphitrite had reached out her arms along the far margins of the coasts. Land was there, and sea, and air — but the land could not be stood on, the water could not be swum, the air had no light of its own. Nothing kept its own shape. Everything got in the way of everything else, because within that single body cold fought hot, wet fought dry, soft fought hard, and things with weight fought things with none.

This quarrel a god, and a kinder nature, settled. He cut the earth away from the sky, and the waters away from the earth, and separated the clear heavens from the thick lower air. And once he had untangled these elements and freed them from that blind heap, he bound them, each in its own place, in a harmony that held peace between them. The fiery, weightless force of the domed sky leapt upward and made a place for itself at the very summit. Nearest to it, in lightness and in position, came the air. Denser than these, the earth drew down the heavier elements and was pressed together by its own gravity. The water, flowing around it all, took the lowest place and hemmed in the solid globe.

When the god — whichever of them it was — had thus divided the mass and, so divided, marshaled it into its parts, he first rolled the earth into the shape of a great ball, so that it should not be uneven on any side. Then he ordered the seas to spread out, to swell under the driving winds, and to wrap themselves around the shores of the encircled land. He added springs, and vast ponds, and lakes, and hemmed in the downhill rivers with sloping banks. These, different in different places, are partly swallowed by the earth itself; partly they reach the sea and, taken up into that wider field of freer water, beat against coastlines instead of banks. He commanded the plains to stretch wide, the valleys to sink low, the woods to clothe themselves in leaves, the stony mountains to rise. And just as the heavens are cut by two zones on the right and as many on the left, with a fifth between them hotter than the rest, so the god's care marked out the mass enclosed below with the same number, and the same number of climate-belts are pressed upon the earth. Of these, the middle one is not fit to live in, for its heat; deep snow buries two of them; and between each pair he set two more and gave them a mild climate, blending warmth with cold.

Over these hangs the air, which is heavier than fire by exactly as much as the weight of water is lighter than the weight of earth. There he ordered the mists to settle, and the clouds, and the thunder that would shake the minds of men, and, together with the lightnings, the winds that make the flashes. But even to these the world's maker did not grant the sky to hold at random. Even now, though each of them governs his own gusts in his own quarter, they can barely be kept from tearing the world apart — so fierce is the discord of these brothers. Eurus, the East Wind, drew off toward the dawn and the kingdoms of Nabath and Persia and the mountain ridges laid bare to the morning rays. The West — the evening lands, the shores that grow warm as the sun goes down — these border on Zephyr. Into Scythia and the far North burst the shuddering Boreas; the opposite quarter of the earth grows sodden with endless cloud and the rainy South. Above all these the god set the clear upper sky, the pure aether, weightless and holding not one trace of earthly dregs.

Scarcely had he fenced off everything within its own fixed bounds when the stars, long buried and hidden beneath that blind mass, began to blaze out across the whole of heaven. And so that no region should be left without its own living creatures, the stars and the shapes of the gods took the floor of heaven; the waters fell to the gleaming fish to live in; earth received the wild beasts, and the restless air the birds.

Still missing was a creature holier than these, able to hold a higher mind, one that could rule over the rest. And so Man was born. Either the maker of all things, the seedbed of a better world, fashioned him from divine stuff — or else the young earth, so lately parted from the high aether, still held the seeds of its kindred sky, and the son of Iapetus, Prometheus, mixed that earth with rainwater and molded it into the image of the gods who govern all things. And where the other animals bend their gaze down toward the ground, to Man he gave a face lifted high; he told him to look at the sky and to raise his eyes upright toward the stars. So the earth, which just now had been raw and shapeless, was changed and put on the never-before-seen forms of human beings.

The Golden Age was the first sown. With no one to enforce it, of its own free will, without laws, it kept faith and did right. Punishment and the fear of it were nowhere; no threatening words were read on tablets of fixed bronze, no crowd of the accused trembled before the face of its judge. All were safe with no one to guard them. No pine had yet been felled from its native mountains and gone down into the flowing waves to visit a foreign world, and mortals knew no shores but their own. No sheer moats yet ringed the towns; there was no straight war-trumpet, no coiled horn of bronze, no helmet and no sword. Without any use for soldiers, the untroubled peoples passed their days in soft ease. The earth herself, too, unforced, untouched by the hoe and wounded by no plowshare, gave everything of her own accord; and men, content with food that grew under no compulsion, gathered the fruit of the arbutus and mountain strawberries, cornel-cherries and blackberries clinging to the tough brambles, and the acorns that had fallen from Jupiter's spreading tree. It was an everlasting spring, and the mild west winds caressed with warm breath the flowers that had sprung up from no seed. Before long the unplowed earth was bearing grain as well, and fields that had never lain fallow whitened with heavy ears of wheat. Now rivers of milk, now rivers of nectar ran, and golden honey dripped from the green oak.

Afterward, once Saturn had been sent down into shadowy Tartarus and the world lay under the rule of Jupiter, the Silver Age came on — lower than gold, but more precious than tawny bronze. Jupiter shortened the span of that old-time spring and split the year into four seasons — winters, summers, unsteady autumns, and a brief spring. Then, for the first time, the air burned white with dry heat, and ice hung frozen hard in the winds. Then, for the first time, men went indoors; their houses were caves, dense thickets, and branches lashed together with bark. Then, for the first time, the seeds of Ceres, the grain, were buried in long furrows, and the young oxen groaned under the yoke.

After that age, third in order, followed the Bronze, fiercer in temper and quicker to reach for cruel weapons — yet not wicked outright. The last age was of hard iron. At once every kind of evil broke loose into that age of baser vein: modesty fled, and truth, and good faith, and in their place came fraud and trickery and treachery, and violence, and the criminal hunger to have more. The sailor now spread his sails to winds he still barely knew, and hulls of timber that had long stood high on the mountains now leapt over unknown waters. The land, once shared like sunlight and open air, the wary surveyor now marked off with a long boundary line. Nor was the rich soil asked only for its harvests and the food it owed; men went down into the very guts of the earth, and the wealth she had hidden away and moved close to the ghosts of the underworld was dug up — kindling for evil. And now hurtful iron had come forth, and gold more hurtful than iron; and War came forth, who fights with both, and rattles clashing weapons in his bloody hand. Men lived by plunder. Guest was not safe from host, nor father-in-law from son-in-law; even between brothers goodwill was rare. The husband schemed his wife's death, and she her husband's; ghastly stepmothers brewed pale poisons; and the son inquired into his father's years before their time. Duty and love lay conquered, and the maiden Astraea — Justice herself — last of the immortals, left the earth now soaked in slaughter.

And so that the high aether should be no safer than the earth, the Giants — so the story goes — made a grab for the kingdom of heaven and built the piled-up mountains high, toward the stars. Then the almighty Father hurled his lightning, split Olympus, and blasted Pelion off Ossa beneath it. When the monstrous bodies lay crushed under the ruin they themselves had raised, they say the Earth was drenched, soaked with the streaming blood of her sons; that she gave life to the warm gore; and, so that no trace of her offspring should remain, she turned it into the shape of men. But that breed, too, was a scorner of the gods above, greedy for savage killing, and full of violence: you could tell they were born of blood.

When the Father, Saturn's son, saw all this from his highest citadel, he groaned; and, calling to mind the foul banquet on Lycaon's table — an outrage still fresh and not yet widely known — he conceived a vast anger in his heart, an anger worthy of Jupiter, and summoned a council. No delay held back those he called.

There is a road on high, easy to see when the sky is clear; the Milky Way it is called, marked out by its very whiteness. Along this the gods go up to the halls of the great Thunderer, to his royal house. To the right and the left stand the open-doored courts of the noble gods, thronged with visitors. The lesser gods live scattered in their own quarters; here, along the front, the powerful and famous immortals have set their household shrines. This is the place — if boldness may be given to my words — that I would not be afraid to call the Palatine hill of high heaven. So when the gods had taken their seats in the marble hall, Jupiter himself, raised above them on his throne, leaning on his ivory scepter, shook the awful hair of his head three times and four; and with it he set the earth, the sea, and the stars trembling. Then, in words like these, he opened his indignant lips:

"I was no more anxious for the rule of the world even in that hour when each of the snake-footed monsters was reaching a hundred arms to seize the captured sky. For though that enemy was savage, still the whole war hung from one stock and sprang from a single source. Now, wherever Nereus roars around the whole globe, the race of mortals must be destroyed by me. I swear it by the rivers of the underworld that glide beneath the earth through the Stygian grove: everything else has already been tried, but a wound past healing must be cut away with the blade, so the sound part is not dragged down with it. I have demigods to think of; I have the country spirits, the nymphs, the fauns and satyrs and the woodland gods of the hills. Since we have not yet thought them worthy of the honor of heaven, we should at least let them live safely on the earth we have given them. But can you truly believe, gods, that they will be safe, when Lycaon — a man known for his savagery — has laid a plot against me, against the very one who holds and wields the thunderbolt and rules over you?"

Every god roared, and with burning zeal they demanded punishment for the man who had dared such a thing. It was just so when an unholy gang raged to blot out the Roman name in Caesar's blood: the human race stood stunned with sudden terror at so great a ruin, and the whole world shuddered. And the loyal love of your people, Augustus, was no less dear to you than that of the gods was to Jupiter. When he had checked their outcry with voice and hand, all of them fell silent. Once the clamor had died, held down by the weight of their ruler's authority, Jupiter broke the stillness again with these words:

"He has paid his penalty already — set that worry aside. But what he did, and what the punishment was, I will tell you. Word of the age's infamy had reached my ears. Hoping to find it false, I came down from the peak of Olympus and, a god in human shape, walked the earth. It would take too long to count up how much guilt was found everywhere: the rumor itself fell short of the truth.

"I had crossed Maenalus, dreaded for its dens of beasts, and Cyllene, and the pine woods of cold Lycaeus. From there, as the late dusk was drawing on the night, I entered the realm and the unwelcoming house of the Arcadian tyrant. I gave a sign that a god had come, and the common people began to pray. Lycaon first mocked their pious prayers; then he said, 'I'll find out by a clear test whether this fellow is a god or a mortal — and the truth will not be in doubt.' He made ready to kill me by surprise in the night, sunk in sleep: that was his idea of testing the truth. Not content with that, he took a hostage sent to him from the Molossian people, cut his throat with a blade, and softened part of the still-twitching limbs in boiling water while he roasted the rest over a fire. The moment he set this on the table, I, with avenging flame, brought his house down on its household gods — gods that deserved such a master. Terrified, he himself fled. He reached the silence of the fields and howled, and tried in vain to speak. His mouth drew rabid fury from himself, and, in his old lust for slaughter, he turned it against the flocks and even now delights in blood. His clothes became shaggy hair; his arms became legs. He is a wolf, and he keeps the traces of his former shape: the same gray, the same violence in his face, the same eyes glinting, the same look of savagery. One house has fallen — but one house alone did not deserve to die. As far as the earth reaches, the savage Fury reigns. You would think men had sworn an oath to do evil! Let all of them, and quickly, suffer the punishment they have earned — for that is my judgment."

Some of the gods approved Jupiter's words aloud and spurred him on in his anger; others played their part with silent assent. Still, the destruction of the whole human race grieved every one of them, and they asked what the earth would look like once emptied of mortals — who would lay incense on the altars? Did he mean to hand the world over to the wild beasts to ravage? To their questions the king of the gods answered that they should not be alarmed — the rest would be his care — and he promised to raise, from a marvelous source, a people unlike the one before.

And now he was about to scatter his lightning bolts over all the lands; but he feared that the sacred aether might catch fire from so many flames and the whole long vault of the sky burst into blaze. He remembered, too, that it stood in the decrees of Fate that a time would come when sea and land and the seized palace of heaven would all burn, and the hard-built mass of the world would strain in its agony. So the weapons forged by the Cyclopes' hands were laid aside. A different punishment pleased him: to destroy the mortal race beneath the waves and to loose the rains from every quarter of the sky.

At once he shut the North Wind up in the caves of Aeolus, along with every gust that drives the gathered clouds away, and sent out the South Wind. Out flew the South Wind on soaking wings, his terrible face wrapped in pitch-black gloom. His beard was heavy with storm; water streamed from his gray hair; mists sat on his brow; his wings and the folds of his robe dripped. And as he squeezed the hanging clouds in his broad hand, there came a crash, and dense rains poured down from the sky. Iris, the messenger of Juno, robed in her many colors, drew up water and carried it back as food for the clouds. The standing crops were beaten flat; the farmer's prayers lay ruined and mourned; and the work of a long year was lost, gone for nothing.

Jupiter's anger was not content with his own sky; his blue-gray brother Neptune helped him with reinforcing waves. He called the rivers together, and once they had come into their king's house he said, "There's no time now for a long speech. Pour out all your strength — the crisis demands it. Open your homes, and with every barrier thrown aside, give your streams full rein." So he ordered; and they went back, opened wide the mouths of their springs, and rolled toward the sea in an unbridled race. Neptune himself struck the earth with his trident, and it shuddered, and with the shock laid open the channels of the waters. The rivers burst their bounds and rushed across the open fields, sweeping away orchards along with the crops, flocks and men, houses, and temples too, with their sacred things. If any house stood firm and, unthrown, could resist so great a ruin, still the water rose higher than its roof and covered it, and the towers lay hidden, drowned beneath the flood. And now sea and land had no boundary between them: everything was ocean, and even the ocean had no shores.

One man seized a hilltop; another sat in his curved boat and pulled the oars where he had just been plowing; another sailed over his grainfields or the roof of his drowned farmhouse; another caught a fish in the top of an elm. An anchor, if chance so dropped it, bit into a green meadow, or curved keels grazed the vineyards below them. Where slender goats had lately cropped the grass, ungainly seals now laid down their bodies. The sea-nymphs, the Nereids, marveled at woods and cities and houses under the water; dolphins slipped into the forests and rammed the high branches and struck the shaken oaks. The wolf swam among the sheep; the wave carried tawny lions along, the wave carried tigers along. The lightning-force of his tusks did the boar no good now, nor his swift legs the stag, both swept away. And the wandering bird, long looking for land to light on, at last fell into the sea on failing wings. The boundless riot of the ocean had buried the hills, and unfamiliar waves beat against the mountaintops. The greatest part were carried off by the water; and those the water spared, long fasting broke, worn down by the want of food.

Phocis separates the fields of Aonia from those of Oeta — a fertile land, while it was land, but in that hour a part of the sea and a broad plain of waters that had risen all at once. There a steep mountain reaches toward the stars with two peaks, Parnassus by name, and its summit rises above the clouds. When Deucalion, in a small boat with the partner of his bed, ran aground here — for the sea had covered everywhere else — the two of them prayed to the Corycian nymphs and the gods of the mountain and to prophetic Themis, who at that time kept the oracle. No man was ever better than Deucalion, none more devoted to what was right, and no woman more reverent of the gods than Pyrrha.

When Jupiter saw the world one standing marsh of water, and saw that out of so many thousands only one man remained, out of so many thousands only one woman — both harmless, both worshippers of the gods — he scattered the clouds, and, the storms swept off by the North Wind, he showed the earth to the sky again and the sky to the earth. The sea's anger did not last either. Laying aside his three-pronged spear, the ruler of the deep calmed the waters and called on sea-green Triton, who stood up out of the deep with his shoulders crusted in native shellfish. He told him to blow his echoing horn and, at that signal, to call the waves and the rivers back. Triton took up his hollow, spiraling trumpet, the one that widens in coils from its narrow base — the trumpet that, once it has drawn in the sea's own air, fills the shores lying under either sunrise and sunset with its voice. Now, too, as soon as it touched the god's lips, wet with his dripping beard, and, filled with breath, sounded the ordered retreat, it was heard by all the waters of land and sea, and every water that heard it, it held in check. Now the sea had a shore again; the channels held their brimming rivers; the streams sank; the hills seemed to rise into view. The ground came up, the dry places spread as the waters shrank, and after a long day the woods showed their bare tops, holding the mud still left on their branches.

The world was restored. When Deucalion saw it emptied, and saw the desolate lands keeping a deep silence, tears welled up and he spoke to Pyrrha: "O sister, O wife, O only woman left alive, whom a shared descent and a common ancestor and then the marriage bed joined to me, and whom now our very dangers join — of all the lands the East and the West look upon, we two are the whole population. The sea has taken all the rest. And even now our hold on life is not sure enough; the clouds still frighten my mind. What would your heart be now, poor woman, if you had been snatched from destruction without me? How could you bear this terror alone? Who would comfort you in your grief? For I — believe me — if the sea had taken you too, I would have followed you, wife, and the sea would have taken me as well. If only I could remake the peoples by my father's arts and breathe souls into the molded earth! As it is, the mortal race survives in us two alone. So it has seemed good to the gods, and we remain as the last examples of mankind."

He finished, and they wept. They resolved to pray to the powers of heaven and to seek help through the sacred oracle. Without delay they went together to the waters of the Cephisus, not yet clear but already cutting their familiar channel. When they had sprinkled the drawn water on their clothes and their heads, they turned their steps to the temple of the holy goddess, whose gables were pale with ugly moss and whose altars stood without fire. As they reached the temple steps, each fell face-down on the ground and, trembling, kissed the cold stone. And they said: "If the gods can be softened, won over by just prayers — if the anger of heaven can be bent — tell us, Themis, by what art the ruin of our race can be repaired, and bring help, most gentle one, to a drowned world."

The goddess was moved, and gave this answer: "Leave the temple, veil your heads, loosen your girded robes, and throw the bones of your great mother behind your backs." For a long time they stood stunned. Pyrrha was the first to break the silence, refusing to obey the goddess's command; with trembling lips she begged pardon, afraid to wrong her mother's ghost by casting her bones. Meanwhile they turned over and over between them the dark, riddling words of the oracle they had been given. At last the son of Prometheus soothed the daughter of Epimetheus with gentle words: "Either my wit is deceiving me, or — since oracles are holy and never urge a crime — our great mother is the earth. The stones in the earth's body, I think, are what she calls her bones; those are what we are told to throw behind our backs." The daughter of Titan was moved by her husband's reading, yet her hope hung in doubt — so little did they both trust the warnings of heaven. But what harm in trying?

They went down; they veiled their heads, ungirded their tunics, and threw the stones, as ordered, behind their steps. And the stones — who would believe it, if the old tradition were not a witness? — began to lay aside their hardness and their stiffness, and little by little to soften, and, once softened, to take on a shape. Soon, when they had grown larger and a gentler nature had come over them, a kind of human form could be seen in them — not clear, but like a figure begun in marble, not yet finished off, very like rough-cut statues. The part of them that was damp with any moisture, and earthy, was turned into the stuff of a body; what was solid and could not bend changed into bone; what had just been a vein kept the same name still. And in a short while, by the will of the gods above, the stones thrown by the man's hands took on the look of men, and from the woman's throwing woman was made anew. That is why we are a hard race, well used to toil, and give proof of the source we sprang from.

The rest of the animals, in their different forms, the earth brought forth of her own accord, once the old moisture had been warmed through by the fire of the sun, and the mud and the wet marshes swelled with the heat, and the fertile seeds of things, nursed in the life-giving soil as in a mother's womb, grew and, given time, took on some shape. So it is when the seven-mouthed Nile draws back from the sodden fields and returns its waters to their old bed, and the fresh silt burns under the heavenly star: the farmers, turning the clods, come upon countless living things — some just begun, at the very moment of birth, some still unfinished and short of their limbs; and often, in the same body, one part is alive while the other is raw earth. For where moisture and heat have been mixed in just measure, they conceive, and from these two all things are born. And though fire fights with water, a moist warmth creates all things, and that discordant harmony is right for bringing life. So when the earth, still muddy from the recent flood, glowed hot again beneath the heavenly sun and a deep, soaking warmth, she gave birth to countless kinds — partly bringing back the old shapes, partly creating new monsters.

Against her will she bore you, too, then, enormous Python: a serpent never seen before, a terror to the newborn peoples, so much of the mountainside you covered. The bow-bearing god, Apollo, who had never before used such weapons on anything but fleeing deer and does, all but emptied his quiver upon him, laying him low with a thousand arrows as the venom oozed out through the black wounds. And so that the passing of years should not blot out the fame of the deed, he founded sacred games with famous contests, the Pythian games, named for the serpent he had conquered. There, whoever of the young men won with hand or foot or chariot-wheel took as his prize a wreath of oak leaves. There was no laurel yet, and Phoebus would bind his temples, lovely under their long hair, with garlands from any tree at all.
