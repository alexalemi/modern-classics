Book XV

(Part 2 of 2)

If anything is harmful, destroy it — but even so, only destroy it: keep your mouths free of blood, and take the gentler food!

His heart schooled with such teachings and others, Numa, they say, returned to his country and, invited of their own will, took up the reins of the Roman people. Blessed with a nymph for his wife and the Muses for his guides, he taught the rites of sacrifice, and led a nation used to fierce war over to the arts of peace. When, full of years, he had finished his reign and his life, the Latian matrons and the people and the fathers wept for Numa dead; but his wife left the city and lay hidden in the thick groves of the vale of Aricia, and by her groaning and lament disturbed the rites of Diana, brought there by Orestes. Ah, how often the nymphs of the grove and the lake begged her not to, and spoke words of comfort! Ah, how often the hero, the son of Theseus, said to her as she wept, "Set a limit to it; your lot is not the only one to be mourned. Look at the like disasters of others: you will bear yours the more mildly — and would that examples not my own could lighten your grief! But even mine can.

"If, in talk, it has ever reached your ears that a certain Hippolytus died through his father's credulity, by the trickery of a wicked stepmother, you will be amazed, and I shall hardly prove it — but I am he. Once the daughter of Pasiphaë, having tried in vain to tempt me to defile my father's bed, pretended I had wished what she herself wished, and, turning the charge about — whether more from fear of exposure or from spite at the rebuff — condemned me; and my father, though I was guiltless, drove me from the city and, as I went, cursed my head with cruel prayers. In my flying chariot I was making for Troezen, the city of Pittheus, and now was skirting the shores of the Corinthian gulf, when the sea rose, and a huge heap of water seemed to bend into the shape of a mountain and to grow and to give a bellowing and to split at its very peak; and out of the burst waves a horned bull was thrown, and, reared breast-high into the light air, spewed a mass of sea-water from his nostrils and open mouth. My companions' hearts quailed; my mind stayed unafraid, intent only on my exile — when the fierce horses turned their necks to the sea and shied with pricked-up ears, and, terrified of the monster, dashed the chariot over the high rocks. I struggled, with useless hand, to hold the reins white with foam, and, thrown back, pulled at the slack traces. Yet my strength would not have beaten the madness of the horses, had not the wheel, running against a stump, been broken and torn from the long axle. I was hurled from the chariot, and, the reins tangling my limbs, you might have seen my living guts dragged, my sinews caught on the stump, my limbs partly torn away and partly held back, my broken bones give a heavy sound, and my spent breath breathe out — and no part of my body you could recognize: the whole of me was one wound. Can you, or dare you, nymph, set your misfortune beside mine? I have seen, too, the lightless realm, and bathed my mangled body in the wave of Phlegethon; nor would life have been given back to me but for the powerful medicine of Apollo's son. When, by strong herbs and the healing art, I had it back, against Pluto's will, then Cynthia, that I might not by my presence deepen his anger at the gift, threw thick clouds around me; and, that I might be safe and be seen unharmed, she added years to my look and left me no face that could be known, and long she wavered whether to give me Crete or Delos to hold; and, leaving Crete and Delos, she set me here, and bade me lay aside the name that might remind me of my horses. 'You who were Hippolytus,' she said, 'be now Virbius.' Since then I haunt this grove and, one of the lesser gods, lie hidden under my mistress's power, and am numbered among her own."

Yet the misfortunes of others could not ease Egeria's grief; and, lying at the mountain's foot, she melted into tears, until the sister of Phoebus, moved by the love of her sorrow, made a cool fountain from her body and thinned her limbs into ever-flowing waters.

This new thing amazed the nymphs, and the son of the Amazon was astonished no otherwise than when the Etruscan ploughman saw the fateful clod in the middle of the field move first of itself, no one touching it, then take on a human shape and lose the earth's, and open its new-made mouth with the destinies to come — the natives called him Tages, the first to teach the Etruscan nation to unfold the future; or as when Romulus once saw his spear, fixed on the Palatine hill, suddenly put out leaves, standing now on a new-made root, not on the driven iron, and, now no dart but a tree of pliant twigs, give the wondering onlookers an unlooked-for shade; or as when Cipus saw his own horns in the stream's water — for he did see them — and, thinking the reflection false, kept bringing his fingers back to his brow and touched what he saw; and, no longer blaming his eyes, he stopped where he was, coming home victorious over the beaten enemy, and, raising his eyes and his hands to heaven, cried, "Whatever this marvel foretells, you gods above — if it is happy, let it be happy for my country and the people of Quirinus; if it threatens, let it threaten me." And he soothes the grassy altars, built of green turf, with fragrant fires, and pours wine from bowls, and consults the quivering entrails of slain sheep for their meaning. When the Etruscan seer looked at them, he saw great designs of things in them, but not clear; but when he lifted his keen eye from the sheep's flesh to the horns of Cipus, he said, "Hail, O king! For you, Cipus, you and your horns this place and the Latian towers will obey. Only break off delay, and hurry to enter the open gates! So the fates command: for, once received in the city, you will be king, and safely hold a lasting scepter." He stepped back, and, turning his stern face from the walls of the city, said, "Far, far may the gods drive all such omens! Far more rightly will I pass my life in exile than see the Capitol make me king." He spoke, and at once called together the people and the grave senate; but first he veiled his horns with the laurel of peace, and stood on a mound raised by his brave soldiers, and, praying to the old gods in the ancient way, said, "There is one here who will be king unless you drive him from the city. Who he is I will tell you by a sign, not a name: he wears horns on his brow! The augur warns you that, if he enters Rome, he will make you slaves by his laws. He could have burst through the open gates, but I opposed him — though no one is more closely bound to him than I. Keep the man from the city, Romans; or, if he deserves it, bind him with heavy chains, or end your fear by the death of the destined tyrant." Such a murmur as rises in the tall pines when the fierce east wind whistles through them, or as the sea-waves make if one hears them far off, so the people sounded; but through their confused, roaring words one cry stood out: "Which is he?" And they scan the foreheads and look for the foretold horns. Cipus spoke to them again: "The one you seek, you have," and, tearing the wreath from his head though the people forbade it, showed his temples marked with a twin horn. All cast down their eyes and groaned, and — who could believe it? — unwilling, looked on that head famous for its merits; and, no longer letting it lack its honor, they set the festive wreath on it. And the nobles, since you were forbidden to enter the walls, gave you, Cipus, as a mark of honor, as much land as you could enclose with the plow, oxen yoked, from the sun's rising to its setting; and they carved the horns, keeping their wondrous shape, on the bronze-plated doorposts, to last through the long ages.

Now tell, Muses, the present powers of poets — for you know, and the wide reach of time does not deceive you — how the island washed by the deep Tiber's stream added the son of Coronis to the sacred rites of Romulus's city. A dreadful plague had once fouled the Latian air, and pale bodies were wasted by a bloodless sickness. When, weary with funerals, they saw that mortal efforts availed nothing, and the arts of healers nothing, they sought help from heaven, and, going to Delphi that holds the middle place of the world, the oracle of Phoebus, they begged that he would come to their misery with a health-bringing answer and end so great a city's woes. The place, and the laurels, and the very quivers he holds trembled together, and the tripod gave this voice from the depths of the shrine and shook their frightened breasts: "What you seek here, Roman, you should have sought in a nearer place; and seek it now in a nearer place: you need not Apollo to lessen your grief, but Apollo's son. Go with good omens, and summon my offspring."

When the prudent senate had received the god's command, they searched out which city the young son of Phoebus dwelt in, and sent men to reach the shores of Epidaurus with the winds. As soon as those sent had touched there in the curved ship, they came to the council and the Greek elders and begged them to grant the god who, present, might end the deaths of the Ausonian nation — for so the sure oracle had said. Opinion was split and wavering; some thought aid should not be refused, many urged them to keep their own protector and not send away their guardian god. While they hesitated, the twilight had driven off the fading day, and the earth's shadow had drawn darkness over the world, when in your sleep, Roman, the saving god seemed to stand before your couch, just as he is wont to be in his temple, holding a rustic staff in his left hand and stroking the long hair of his beard with his right, and to speak such words from his gentle breast: "Lay aside your fears! I will come, and leave these statues of mine. Only look at this serpent that winds its coils about my staff, and mark it well with your eyes, so you can know it: into this I will change, but I shall be greater, and seem as great as heavenly bodies ought to be when they are transformed." At once, with these words, the god departed, and with his words and the god sleep departed, and kindly light followed the flight of sleep.

The next dawn had scattered the starry fires; unsure what to do, the nobles met in the god's rich temple and begged him to show, by heavenly signs, in what place he would abide. Scarcely had they ceased when the golden god, in the serpent's form, with high crest, sent forth a hiss to announce his coming, and at his approach shook his statue and the altars and doors and marble floor and gilded roof, and, reared breast-high in the middle of the temple, rolled around his fire-flashing eyes. The frightened crowd trembled; the priest, his chaste hair bound with a white fillet, knew the god and cried, "The god — behold the god! Be of good omen in mind and tongue, whoever is here! May you be seen to our good, most beautiful one, and aid the peoples that keep your rites!" All present worship the god as bidden, and all repeat the priest's words again, and the sons of Aeneas give holy favor in heart and voice. The god nods to this, and, shaking his crest, gives sure pledges with his darting tongue thrice repeated; then he glides down the polished steps and turns his face back, and, about to leave, looks back at his old altars and salutes his wonted home and the temple he has so long lived in. Then, through the ground strewn and covered with scattered flowers, he glides, huge, coiling his folds, and through the middle of the city makes for the harbor guarded by its curving quay. Here he stopped, and, seeming with a calm face to dismiss his train and the dutiful crowd that followed, laid his body on the Ausonian ship; it felt the god's weight, and the keel was pressed low by the god's heaviness. The sons of Aeneas rejoice, and, a bull first slain on the shore, loose the twisted cables of the garlanded ship. A light breeze drove the ship on; the god stands out on high, pressing the curved stern with his neck laid on it, and looks down on the blue water; and with gentle west winds along the Ionian sea, at the sixth rising of the dawn, he reached Italy, and was borne past the Lacinian shore, famed for the goddess Juno's temple, and the Scylacean coast; he leaves Iapygia, and flees the Amphrisian rocks with oars to the left, the steep Cocinthian on the right, and coasts Romethium and Caulon and Narycia, and crosses the strait and the narrows of Sicilian Pelorus, and the home of King Aeolus, grandson of Hippotas, and the mines of Temesa, and makes for Leucosia and the rose-beds of warm Paestum. Then he coasts Capreae, and the headland of Minerva, and the hills noble with the Surrentine vine, and the city of Hercules, and Stabiae, and Parthenope made for ease, and, past it, the temple of the Cumaean Sibyl. Then the warm springs are passed, and mastic-bearing Liternum, and Volturnus dragging much sand under its stream, and Sinuessa busy with white doves, and heavy Minturnae, and the town her foster-child gave a tomb, and the house of Antiphates, and Trachas ringed by marsh, and the land of Circe, and Antium with its rocky shore. When the sailors turned the sail-bearing ship here — for now the sea was rough — the god unfolds his coils and, gliding in many a fold and great winding, enters the temple of his father that skirts the yellow shore. The sea calmed, the god of Epidaurus leaves his father's altars, and, having enjoyed the welcome of the god akin to him, furrows the shore sand with the drag of his rattling scales, and, leaning on the helm, laid his head on the high stern, until he came to Castrum and the sacred seats of Lavinium and the mouths of the Tiber. Here all the people, a crowd of matrons and men alike, rush to meet him — and they too who keep your fires, Vesta — and hail the god with glad shouts. And where the swift ship is steered up the stream, incense crackles on the altars set in rows along both banks, and perfumes the air with smoke, and the felled victim warms the knives thrust in it. And now he had entered Rome, the head of the world: the serpent rises erect, lifts his neck that rested on the masthead, and looks around for a home fit for himself. There is a spot where the flowing river splits in two — it is called the Island — and stretches out from either side equal arms, the dry land in the middle. Here the serpent, son of Phoebus, came from the Latian ship, and, taking back his heavenly form, put an end to the mourning, and came, the bringer of health, to the City.

Yet he came a stranger to our shrines: Caesar is a god in his own city, whom, chief in war and in peace, not his wars ended in triumphs, nor his deeds at home, nor his swift-won glory turned into a new star, a comet with streaming hair, so much as his own offspring. For of all the deeds of Caesar there is none greater than that he was the father of this our Caesar. Was it more, then, to have subdued the sea-girt Britons, and steered victorious ships up the seven-mouthed streams of the papyrus-bearing Nile, and added to the people of Quirinus the rebel Numidians and Cinyphian Juba and Pontus swollen with the fame of Mithridates, and earned many triumphs and held some — than to have fathered so great a man, under whose rule over the world you gods have shown abundant care for the human race? That this man, then, might not be born of mortal seed, Julius had to be made a god. When the golden mother of Aeneas saw this, and saw too that a sad death was being prepared for the high priest, and the conspirators' arms were being moved, she went pale, and said to each of the gods as she met them: "See on how vast a scale treason is plotted against me, and with what great guile that life is sought which alone is left me from Dardanian Iulus! Shall I alone be forever harried by just cares — I, whom now the Calydonian spear of the son of Tydeus wounds, now the walls of Troy, defended in vain, grieve, who have seen my son driven through long wanderings, tossed on the sea, entering the homes of the silent, and waging war with Turnus, or, if we tell the truth, with Juno rather? Why do I now recall the old wrongs of my line? This fear does not let me remember the past. Look, you see the wicked swords whetted — keep them off, I beg, and thwart the crime, and do not, by the priest's murder, put out the fires of Vesta!"

Such anxious words Venus threw in vain through all heaven, and moved the gods above; who, though they could not break the iron decrees of the ancient sisters, still gave no doubtful signs of the coming grief. They say that arms clashing among the black clouds, and dreadful trumpets and horns heard in the sky, warned of the crime; the sad face of the sun, too, gave a lurid light to the anxious earth; often torches seemed to burn among the stars, often drops of blood fell in the rain; the Morning Star was dark, his face sprinkled with black rust, the chariot of the moon sprinkled with blood; in a thousand places the Stygian owl gave sad omens, in a thousand places the ivory statues wept, and dirges, they say, were heard, and threatening words, in the sacred groves. No victim gave a favorable omen; the entrails warned that great tumults were near, and a cut-off head was found among them; and in the Forum, and around the houses and the temples of the gods, dogs howled by night, they say, and the ghosts of the silent walked, and the City was shaken by tremors. Yet the gods' warnings could not overcome treachery and the fate to come, and drawn swords were carried into the temple: for no other place in the whole city pleased them for the crime and the dreadful murder than the senate-house. Then indeed Venus beat her breast with both hands, and made to hide the son of Aeneas in the cloud in which Paris was once snatched from the hostile son of Atreus, and Aeneas escaped the sword of Diomedes. Her father spoke to her thus: "Do you, daughter, alone try to move the unconquerable fate? You may enter the house of the three sisters yourself: there you will see, in a vast structure of bronze and solid iron, the records of things, which fear neither the crash of the sky nor the wrath of the lightning nor any ruin, safe and eternal; there you will find the fates of your line engraved in everlasting adamant. I myself have read them and marked them in my mind, and I will tell them, that you may not still be ignorant of the future. He for whom you labor, Cytherea, has filled his time, the years ended that he owed to earth. That he may enter heaven as a god and be worshiped in temples, you will bring it about, with his son, who, heir to his name, will bear alone the burden laid on him, and, our bravest avenger of his murdered father, will have us to aid him in his wars. Under his command the besieged walls of Mutina, beaten, will sue for peace; Pharsalia will feel him, and Philippi again be soaked with Emathian blood, and the great name will be beaten in the Sicilian waves; and the Egyptian wife of the Roman general, trusting ill in that match, will fall, and in vain will she have threatened that our Capitol should serve her Canopus. Why count you the barbarian lands and the nations by either ocean? Whatever the habitable earth bears will be his — the sea too will serve him! Peace given to the lands, he will turn his mind to civil law, and, most just of lawgivers, bring in laws, and by his own example rule men's ways; and, looking to the age of the time to come and his coming descendants, he will bid the child born of his holy wife bear both his name and his cares; nor, until as an old man he has matched his years to his deeds, will he reach the heavenly seats and his kindred stars. Meanwhile, this soul, snatched from the murdered body, make into a star, so that the deified Julius may always look down from his high place on our Capitol and Forum."

He had barely said this when kindly Venus, seen by none, stood in the middle of the senate-house and snatched the soul, just freed, from the limbs of her Caesar, and, not letting it dissolve into air, bore it up among the stars of heaven; and, as she bore it, she felt it take light and catch fire, and let it go from her breast: higher than the moon it flies, and, drawing a flaming trail through a wide track, gleams as a star; and, seeing his son's good deeds, it owns them greater than its own, and rejoices to be surpassed by him. Though the son forbids his own deeds to be set above his father's, still Fame, free and bound by no commands, prefers him against his will, and in this one thing disobeys him. So great Atreus yields to the glory of Agamemnon, so Theseus outdoes Aegeus, so Achilles Peleus; and, to use examples that match their own case, so too Saturn is less than Jove. Jupiter rules the heavenly halls and the realms of the threefold world; the earth is under Augustus; each is a father and a ruler. Gods, companions of Aeneas, before whom sword and fire gave way; and you, native gods; and Quirinus, father of the City; and Mars, father of unconquered Quirinus; and Vesta, held sacred among the household gods of Caesar; and with Caesar's Vesta, you, Phoebus of the home; and Jupiter, who hold on high the Tarpeian heights; and whatever other gods a poet may rightly call on — late, I pray, be that day, and beyond my own time, on which Augustus, leaving the world he rules, shall reach heaven, and, gone from us, listen favorably to those who pray.

And now I have finished a work that neither the anger of Jove, nor fire, nor sword, nor devouring time can destroy. Let that day, which has power over nothing but this body of mine, end when it will the span of my uncertain life; yet in my better part I shall be borne immortal above the high stars, and my name shall never be blotted out; and wherever Roman power reaches over the conquered lands, I shall be read on the lips of the people, and — if the prophecies of poets have any truth — through all the ages, in fame, I shall live.
