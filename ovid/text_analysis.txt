TEXT ANALYSIS — Ovid's Metamorphoses
(Publius Ovidius Naso, 8 CE; Metamorphoseon libri XV)

SOURCE: the original LATIN (The Latin Library's 15 books), TRANSLATED
FROM THE LATIN into a vivid modern PROSE retelling. Henry T. Riley's
literal 1851 prose (Gutenberg #21765 + #26073) is kept per-file under
reference/ as a COMPREHENSION CRIB ONLY — for the mythological names,
the who-does-what, and the line-by-line sense. Never copy Riley's
stilted Victorian phrasing; he is a map, not the voice. The
de-officiis/ directory is the Latin+crib pipeline precedent.

WHY THIS BOOK

The Metamorphoses is the single greatest storybook of classical
mythology — 250-odd tales strung from the creation of the world to the
death of Julius Caesar, all turning on the theme of transformation:
gods into animals, mortals into trees and stones and stars, grief and
desire and punishment freezing people into new shapes. It is the
source almost every later poet and painter drew on. Trapped behind
archaic verse translations, it reads as homework; retold as vivid
modern prose it is what it always was — a spellbinding, funny,
sometimes horrifying, endlessly inventive page-turner.

WHAT THIS IS — the structure

A continuous carmen perpetuum ("one continuous song") in 15 books, not
discrete chapters. Ovid chains the tales with deft transitions — one
story handing off to the next, often nested (a character tells a tale
inside a tale). We keep the 15 books as our chapters ("Book I".."Book
XV"), each split into 2-3 parts. The tales flow into each other; honor
Ovid's segues rather than chopping the poem into isolated fables.

THE VOICE — how to render it

- MODERN, VIVID PROSE, third-person narration. Fast, clear, sensory.
  Eighth-grade to high-school reading level, but never dumbed down —
  Ovid is witty and sophisticated and the prose should be too.
- Ovid's TONE shifts constantly and deliberately: sweeping cosmic
  grandeur (the Creation, the Flood), tender pathos (Pyramus and
  Thisbe, Orpheus and Eurydice, Ceyx and Alcyone), high horror
  (Tereus and Philomela, the flaying of Marsyas, Actaeon torn apart),
  broad comedy and irony (the gods behaving badly, Midas's ears), and
  virtuoso set-piece description (Phaethon's ride, the House of Rumor,
  the House of Sleep). MATCH the register of each tale — do not flatten
  the poem to one mood. The pathos must land; the horror must not be
  bowdlerized; the wit must actually be funny.
- Keep the SENSUAL, PICTORIAL quality — Ovid is intensely visual
  (bodies mid-transformation: bark climbing a girl's thighs, fingers
  webbing, a voice thinning to birdsong). Render transformations with
  their full uncanny, precise physicality; they are the whole point.
- Keep it COMPLETE. Translate every tale, every catalogue (the hounds
  of Actaeon, the trees that gather for Orpheus, the companions turned
  to birds). Do not summarize or skip the "boring" lists — render them
  with momentum. Prose retelling of verse runs LONG: expect modern
  English roughly 1.4-2.4x the compressed Latin word count. Let it
  fall where it does; never pad, but never compress.

THE APPARATUS OF MYTH — names and references

- PROPER NAMES: use the familiar English/Latin forms a modern reader
  meets (Jupiter, Juno, Venus, Minerva, Diana, Apollo, Bacchus, Mercury,
  Neptune, Pluto; Proserpine, Daphne, Phaethon, Actaeon, Narcissus,
  Echo, Pyramus, Thisbe, Arachne, Niobe, Tereus, Philomela, Medea,
  Daedalus, Icarus, Orpheus, Eurydice, Pygmalion, Midas, Ceyx,
  Alcyone). The crib's names are a reliable guide.
- Ovid constantly names gods by CULT-TITLES and patronymics and places
  by learned periphrasis (e.g. "the Delian god" = Apollo, "Saturnia" =
  Juno, "the Tirynthian hero" = Hercules, "the Atlantid" = Mercury).
  Do NOT leave the reader stranded: render the plain name, or name +
  brief tag, so the story stays legible. The crib usually resolves who
  is meant. When a periphrasis carries real flavor, keep it and gloss
  lightly in-line.
- Keep the ROMAN religious/astronomical furniture as Ovid has it (the
  Golden/Silver/Bronze/Iron Ages, the council of the gods, the
  apotheoses and catasterisms). No modern "corrections" of his
  cosmology, geography, or natural history.
- M0DEST GLOSSING, never footnotes: fold any needed word of context
  into the sentence as a brief phrase. No translator's notes, no
  bracketed asides, no scholarly apparatus in the body.

SENSITIVE / EDITORIAL

The Metamorphoses contains rape, incest, mutilation, and sexual
violence — Ovid treats these with a moral seriousness his reputation
for wit can obscure (Daphne, Io, Callisto, Philomela, Myrrha, the
daughters of Minyas). Render them faithfully and with gravity, in a
measured adult register: do not sensationalize, eroticize, or
sanitize the violence, and do not moralize over Ovid's shoulder — let
the tale carry its own weight, as he does, keeping the victim's
experience in view. This is scholarly modernization of the foundational
classic of Western mythology; frame subagent work that way. When a
tale is comic or bawdy (the gods' affairs, Midas), the modern prose
can be light — match Ovid's own register per tale.

FAMOUS PASSAGES (also in must_contain.txt as name-checks)

- The proem: "In nova fert animus mutatas dicere formas / corpora" —
  "My mind moves me to tell of forms changed into new bodies." This
  opening MUST survive as a clear, resonant first sentence.
- The Four Ages (Golden Age onward); the Flood and Deucalion & Pyrrha.
- Apollo & Daphne (the laurel); Phaethon's catastrophic chariot ride.
- Actaeon; Narcissus & Echo; Pyramus & Thisbe ("one blood, one tomb").
- Arachne's weaving contest; Tereus, Procne & Philomela.
- Daedalus & Icarus; Orpheus & Eurydice; Pygmalion's statue; Midas.
- Ceyx & Alcyone (the halcyon); the closing apotheosis of Caesar and
  Ovid's own epilogue "iamque opus exegi" — "And now my work is done...
  I shall live." Keep the epilogue's proud close intact.

FORMAT

15 books = 15 chapters titled "Book I" .. "Book XV", most split into
2-3 parts, no other dividers. Ratio Latin -> modern English prose
≈ 1.4-2.4 (Latin verse is very compressed; do NOT pad the low end or
compress the high end). Verify with --min-ratio 1.4 --max-ratio 2.4.
