Book II

(Part 2 of 2)

Rhadamanthus questioned Cinyras and his accomplices first, asking whether anyone else had been in on the plot. They swore no one had. So sentence was passed: they were to be flogged with rods of mallow, then tied up by the genitals and shipped off to the place of the wicked, to be tormented there. Helen, meanwhile, was all in tears, so ashamed of herself that she wouldn't show her face.

The judges also decreed that we had to leave the country — our allotted time was up — and that we could stay no later than the next day. That hit me hard. I wept bitterly at having to leave so good a place and become a wanderer again, with no idea where I was going. But they comforted me: before many years had passed, they said, I'd be back with them again — and they even showed me my chair and my couch, already prepared for the time to come, right beside the very best company.

Then I went to Rhadamanthus and begged him to tell me my future and point me on my course. He said that after long travels and many dangers I would reach my own country at last, though he refused to name the day of my return. Pointing out the neighboring islands — five of them, with a sixth a little farther off — he said: "Those nearest are the islands of the ungodly, the ones you see burning in open fire. The sixth is the Isle of Dreams, and beyond that lies Calypso's island, which you can't see from here. Once past these, you'll come to the great continent opposite your own country, where you'll suffer many hardships, pass through many nations, and meet men of inhuman habits — and at length you'll reach the other continent."

Then he pulled a mallow root out of the ground and handed it to me, with instructions to pray to it in my worst dangers. He gave me some further advice too: never stir the fire with my knife, never eat lupins, and never go near a boy once he's past eighteen. If I kept all that in mind, he said, I had good hopes of coming back to the island.

So we made ready for the voyage and feasted with them at the usual hour. The next day I went to Homer and asked him to do me the favor of composing a two-line epigram for me. He did, and I set up a pillar of beryl stone near the harbor and engraved the lines on it. The epigram ran:

    Lucian, the gods' beloved, did attain
    to see all this, then travel home again.

We stayed out that day, and the next we put to sea, with the Heroes there to see us off. As we were leaving, Odysseus slipped over to me — quietly, so Penelope wouldn't notice — and pressed a letter into my hand, to be delivered to Calypso on the island of Ogygia. Rhadamanthus also sent Nauplius the ferryman along with us, so that if we happened to put in at those islands, no one would lay hands on us, since we were traveling on other business.

The moment we sailed beyond the reach of that sweet fragrance, a horrible stench hit us, like pitch and brimstone burning — an unbearable reek, as if men were broiling over hot coals. The air was dark and thick, and a pitchy sort of dew dripped out of it. We could hear the crack of whips and the screams of the tormented.

We didn't visit all the islands. The one we landed on was like this: ringed entirely by steep, sharp, jagged cliffs, without a tree or a drop of water anywhere. We managed to scramble up among the rocks and pushed forward along a path choked with briars and thorns, through hideous, ghastly country, until we came at last to the prison and place of torment. There we marveled at the soil itself, which grew no flowers — only swords and daggers. Around the place ran rivers in rings: the first of mud, the second of blood, and the innermost of burning fire. That last one was very broad and impossible to cross; it flowed like water and rolled like the waves of the sea, and it was full of fish — some the size of burning torches, others smaller, like live coals. They call these Lychnisci — lamp-fish.

There was only one narrow entrance, with Timon of Athens, the famous hater of mankind, posted as doorkeeper. But with Nauplius's help we got in, and we saw the tormented: kings and common men alike, great numbers of them, including some I recognized. There hung Cinyras, strung up by the genitals in the smoke.

But the greatest torments of all are reserved for those who told lies in their lifetime and wrote untrue histories — Ctesias of Cnidus was there, and Herodotus, and many others. Seeing them, I felt very hopeful about my own prospects, since to my knowledge I have never told a lie in my life.

We hurried back to the ship — we couldn't stand the sight any longer — said our goodbyes to Nauplius, and sent him home.

A little later the Isle of Dreams appeared close by: a dim country, hard for the eye to fix on, with the same quality dreams themselves have. For as we approached, it kept receding and slipping away from us, until it seemed farther off than when we started. But we caught it in the end, and sailed into the harbor called Hypnus — Sleep Harbor — close by the ivory gate, where the temple of Alectryon, the Rooster, stands. We landed late in the evening.

Going in through the gate, we saw dreams of every kind and fashion. But first let me tell you something about the city itself, since no one else has ever written a description of it. Even Homer only touches on it, and to small purpose.

The whole city is surrounded by a wood, whose trees are towering poppies and mandrakes, with an infinite number of owls nesting in them — the only birds to be seen on the island. Nearby runs a river they call Nyctiporus, the Night-Stream, and at the gates are two wells, one named Negretus — Sound-Sleep — and the other Pannychia — All-Night-Long.

The city wall is high and shifts color like a rainbow. It has four gates, though Homer mentions only two. Two of them face the Plain of Sloth — one made of iron, the other of potter's clay — and through those pass the dreams of fear, blood, and cruelty. The other two face the harbor and the sea: one of horn, and one of ivory, which is the one we entered by.

As you come into the city, on the right stands the temple of Night — for of all the gods they reverence Night and Alectryon above the rest, and Alectryon has a temple of his own built near the harbor. On the left stands the palace of Sleep, for he is sovereign king over them all, and he governs through two great princes serving under him: Taraxion son of Matogenes — Trouble, son of Delusion — and Plutocles son of Phantasion — Riches, son of Fantasy.

In the middle of the marketplace is a well they call Careotis — Deep-Drowse — and beside it two temples, one of Falsehood and one of Truth. Each has its own inner shrine reserved for the priests, and its own oracle. The chief prophet there is Antiphon, the interpreter of dreams, appointed to that office of honor by Sleep himself.

The dreams themselves are not all alike, in nature or in shape. Some are long, beautiful, and pleasing; others are short and ugly. Some appear to be made of gold; others are cheap and shabby. Some have wings and monstrous forms; others come decked out as if for a parade, dressed up as kings, gods, and other grand persons.

Many of them we actually knew, having seen them before back home — and these came up and greeted us like old friends, gathered us in, lulled us to sleep, and entertained us nobly and courteously. On top of all the rest of their hospitality, which was lavish and costly, they promised to make us kings and princes. Some of them even carried us home to our own country, showed us our friends there, and brought us back again the next morning.

Thirty days and thirty nights we spent among them, sleeping and feasting the whole time — until a sudden clap of thunder woke us all. We started up, stocked the ship with provisions, and put to sea, and on the third day we landed at Ogygia. But on the way I opened the letter I was carrying and read it. It said:

"Odysseus to Calypso: greetings. Know that after I sailed away from you on the raft I built in such haste, I suffered shipwreck, and barely escaped with the help of Leucothea into the country of the Phaeacians, who sent me to my own home. There I found a crowd of suitors courting my wife and riotously eating up my estate. I killed them all, and was afterward killed myself by Telegonus, the son I fathered with Circe; and now I am on the Isle of the Blessed, where I repent daily that I refused to live with you and turned down the immortality you offered me. If I can spy a convenient moment, I will give them all the slip and come to you."

That was the gist of the letter — along with a line about us, asking that we be given hospitality.

I hadn't gone far from the sea when I found the cave, exactly as Homer describes it, and Calypso herself busy working at her wool. She took the letter and brought us inside, and first she wept and carried on grievously; but afterward she called us to dinner and treated us royally, asking question after question about Odysseus — and about Penelope: was she really as beautiful and as modest as Odysseus had so often bragged?

We gave her the answers we figured would please her best. Then we went back to the ship and slept near the shore.

In the morning we put to sea, and a violent storm caught us and tossed us for two days together. On the third we fell among the Pumpkin-Pirates (Colocynthopirates). These are a savage people from the neighboring islands who prey on passing travelers. For ships they use enormous gourds, six cubits long: when the gourds are ripe they hollow them out, scrape them clean, and sail in the rinds, rigging masts of reed and sails of gourd leaf.

They attacked us with two fully crewed ships and wounded many of us, firing gourd seeds at us instead of stones. The fight stayed even until about noon, when, behind the Pumpkin-Pirates, we spotted the Nut-Sailors (Caryonauts) coming on. The two were enemies, as it turned out: the moment the pirates saw them approaching, they forgot all about us and turned to fight them — and in the meantime we hoisted sail and got away, leaving them at each other's throats. No doubt the Nut-Sailors won the day, since they had the greater numbers — five well-equipped ships — and the stronger vessels, their hulls being nutshells split in half and scooped clean, each half fifteen fathoms long.

Once we were out of sight, we tended to our wounded, and from then on we went armed at all times, in constant fear of sudden attack. And with good cause. Before sunset, some twenty men or so — pirates also — came at us riding monstrous great dolphins, which carried them steadily and would neigh like horses when their riders mounted. When they drew near, they split up, some on one side and some on the other, and pelted us with dried cuttlefish and the eyes of sea-crabs. But when we shot back and wounded them, they wouldn't stand their ground; they fled to the island, most of them hurt.

Around midnight, with the sea calm, we ran before we knew it onto an enormous halcyon's nest — a kingfisher's nest — a good sixty furlongs around. The halcyon herself was sailing on it, hatching her eggs, and she was very nearly as big as the nest: when she took wing, the blast from her feathers nearly capsized our ship, and she flew off making a mournful cry.

At daybreak we climbed onto it and found it really was a nest, built like a giant barge out of trees plaited and woven together. In it were five hundred eggs, every one bigger than a wine cask from Chios, and so near hatching that the chicks could already be seen inside and were beginning to cry. We took an axe, chopped one of the eggs open, and cut out an unfledged chick — which was still bigger than twenty of our vultures.

When we had gone some two hundred furlongs from the nest, fearful prodigies and strange signs appeared to us. The carved goose that stood as an ornament on our ship's stern suddenly sprouted feathers and began to honk. Scintharus, our pilot, who was bald, was covered with hair in an instant. And strangest of all, the ship's mast budded, put out branches, and bore fruit at the top — figs, and great clusters of grapes, though not yet ripe. At the sight of all this we had good reason to be troubled, and we prayed to the gods to turn aside whatever evil the omens foretold.

We hadn't gone a full five hundred furlongs more when we came in view of a vast forest of pines and cypresses. We took it for land — but it was in fact a sea of infinite depth, planted with rootless trees that floated firm and upright on the water. When we reached it and saw how things stood, we had no idea what to do with ourselves. To go forward through the trees was altogether impossible — they grew so thick and close together — and to turn back safely looked just as unlikely.

So I climbed to the top of the tallest tree to find out, if I could, what lay beyond. The forest, I found, was fifty furlongs wide or thereabouts, and beyond it another ocean waited to receive us. We decided our best chance was to hoist the ship up onto the leaves of the trees, which grew dense, and try to pass over them, if it could be done, to the other ocean.

And so we did. We fastened a strong cable to the ship, wound it around the treetops, and with great effort hauled her up to that height, set her on the branches, and spread the sails — and we were carried along as if on the sea, dragging the ship after us with the help of the wind that pushed it forward. Right then a verse of the poet Antimachus came to my mind — the one where he speaks of sailing over the tops of trees.

When we had crossed the forest and come to the sea again, we lowered the ship the same way we had raised her. Then we sailed on through pure, clear water until we came to an enormous gulf, a trench in the sea, made by the dividing of the waters — the way great clefts open in the ground on land, from earthquakes and other causes. We struck sail and the ship stopped all at once, right at the pit's brim, on the very point of tumbling in. Leaning over to look down, we judged it to be no less than a thousand furlongs deep — fearful and monstrous to behold, for the water simply stood there, divided into two walls. But looking far off to our right, we noticed a bridge of water that, as well as we could tell, joined the two seas and ran across from one to the other. So we labored at the oars to reach it, crossed over it, and with great effort got to the far side — beyond anything we had hoped.

After that a calm sea received us, and on it we found an island, not very large, and inhabited by unsociable people. Living there were wild men called the Ox-Heads (Bucephalians), who have horns on their heads, like pictures of the Minotaur. We went ashore to look for fresh water and provisions, since ours had run out entirely. Water we found soon enough, but nothing else was in sight — though we heard a great bellowing and roaring a little way off. Thinking it was some herd of cattle, we walked toward the sound and ran straight into those men. The moment they spotted us, they came after us, and they seized three of my companions; the rest of us fled toward the sea.

Then we all armed ourselves — we had no intention of leaving our friends unavenged — and fell on the Ox-Heads while they were dividing up the flesh of the men they had slaughtered. We put the whole crowd to flight and chased them, killing fifty and taking two alive, and so came back with our prisoners. Food, though, we still couldn't find.

The whole crew urged me to kill the prisoners, but I didn't like that plan. I thought it better to keep them in chains until ambassadors came from the Ox-Heads to ransom them. And come they did — and I understood exactly what their business was from the nodding of their heads and their mournful, pleading lowing.

The ransom we agreed on was this: a quantity of cheeses, dried fish, and onions, plus four deer with three legs apiece — two behind and one in front. On those terms we handed over our prisoners, and after staying just one more day, we departed.

By now fish were beginning to show themselves in the sea, birds flew over our heads, and all the other signs of approaching land appeared. A little later we saw men traveling across the sea by a newfound method of navigation, in which each man serves as both ship and sailor — and I'll tell you how. They lie on their backs in the water with their members standing upright — these being of a large size, and well suited to the purpose — fasten a sail to them, and hold the lines in their hands; and when the wind fills the sail, off they go, wherever they please.

After these came others riding on corks: they yoke two dolphins together and drive them on, playing the coachman themselves, and the dolphins pull the cork along behind. These people never offered us any violence, and never once avoided us, but traveled along in our company, peaceable and unafraid, marveling at the size of our ship and looking it over from every side.

At evening we reached a small island, inhabited, as far as we could tell, only by women — women who spoke Greek. They came to us, took our hands, and welcomed us, every one of them dressed like a courtesan: beautiful, young, in long gowns trailing to the foot. The island was called Cabbalusa, and the city Hydramardia. The women received us, and each one picked out one of us for herself and made him her guest.

But I hung back a moment — my heart misgave me — and looking carefully around, I saw the bones of many men, and skulls heaped together in a corner. I thought it best not to raise an alarm, or call my crew together, or reach for my weapons. Instead I took my mallow root in hand and prayed to it earnestly to bring me out of these present dangers.

A little while later, when my strange hostess came to wait on me, I saw that she didn't have the legs of a woman, but the hooves of a donkey. At that I drew my sword, grabbed hold of her, tied her up, and questioned her on every point. Unwillingly, she confessed: they were women of the sea, called the Donkey-Legs (Onosceleans), and they fed on the strangers who traveled that way. "When we've made them drunk," she said, "we go to bed with them, and finish them off in their sleep."

When I heard that, I left her tied up where she was, climbed to the roof of the house, and raised a cry to call my crew together. When they had gathered, I told them everything, showed them the bones, and brought them in to see the woman I'd bound — who suddenly turned into water and could no longer be seen. Even so, I thrust my sword into the water to see what would come of it. It turned to blood.

Then we made all the haste we could to the ship and got away. As soon as it was fully day, we sighted the mainland, which we judged to be the continent lying opposite our own. We bowed down in worship, said our prayers, and took counsel about what to do next. Some thought it best just to set foot on shore and turn back again; others wanted to leave the ship there and march inland, to see what the inhabitants would do. But while we were still deliberating, a violent storm fell on us, drove the ship against the shore, and burst it all to pieces. With great difficulty we all swam to land, every man clutching his weapons and whatever else he could lay hands on.

These, then, are all the things I can tell you of up to the time of our landing: on the sea, on our voyage among the islands, in the air, then inside the whale, and after we got out, among the Heroes and among the dreams, and lastly among the Ox-Heads and the Donkey-Legs. What happened on land, the next books shall deliver.
