Book I
(Part 2 of 2)

When a man there reaches the end of his full age, he doesn't die. He dissolves, like smoke, and turns into air.

They all eat the same food. They kindle a fire and broil frogs over the coals — frogs are everywhere there, infinite numbers of them, flying through the air — and while the frogs are broiling, the diners sit around the fire as if around a table, lap up the smoke that rises, and feast on that. That is the whole of their eating. For drink, they pound air in a mortar until it yields a kind of moisture, very like dew.

They pass no waste of any kind, neither urine nor dung; in fact they have no opening for that purpose where we have one. Their boys take partners not the way ours do, but in the hollow of the leg, just above the calf — that's where they are open.

A bald head is considered a great mark of beauty on the Moon; hairy people disgust them. On the comet stars it's just the opposite — there, long hair is admired — I have this from travelers who had visited those coasts. Such beards as they do have grow a little above the knee. They have no toenails, since the whole foot is a single toe. Above the rump each of them grows a long cabbage, in place of a tail — always green and flourishing, and it does not break even when a man falls flat on his back.

The drippings of their noses are sweeter than honey. When they work or exercise, they rub themselves down with milk — and if a drop of that nose-honey happens to fall in, it turns the milk to cheese. They press a rich oil from their beans, as fragrant as any fine perfume.

They have plenty of vines in those parts, but the vines yield water, because the grapes hanging in the clusters are like our hailstones. My own theory is that when a strong wind shakes the vines up there, those berries break loose and come down on us as a hailstorm.

Their bellies serve them as satchels. They can open and shut them at will and stow their necessities inside, for they have no liver or entrails of any kind — only a rough, hairy lining, so that when their young children are cold, they can be tucked in there to keep warm.

The rich wear garments of glass, very soft and delicate; the poorer sort wear woven brass, which they have in great supply, working it with water to make it fit for the loom, the way we treat our wool.

As for their eyes — if I wrote down what kind of eyes they have, I'm afraid I'd be taken for a liar, the thing is so incredible. But I can't leave it out. Their eyes are removable. A man can take them out whenever he pleases and put them away until he has a use for them; then he pops them back in and sees again. Many who have lost their own eyes borrow other people's, and the rich keep several pairs lying around spare. Their ears are made of plane-tree leaves — except for the acorn-born men, whose ears alone are made of wood.

I saw one more wonder in the king's palace: a great mirror lying over a shallow well. Climb down into the well, and you can hear everything said on the Earth. Look into the mirror, and you can see every city and every nation as plainly as if you were standing among them. I had a look at my own friends and the whole country around home — whether they saw me too, I can't say. Anyone who doesn't believe this can take the trouble to go there himself; he'll find out my words are true.

Then we said our goodbyes to the king and his court, boarded our ship, and set off. At parting, Endymion gave me gifts: two of their glass mantles, five of brass, and a complete suit of lupin-shell armor — all of which I later left behind in the whale. And he sent a thousand of his Vulture Cavalry to escort us the first five hundred furlongs of the way.

On our course we coasted along many other countries, and at last put in at the Morning Star, newly colonized, where we landed and took on fresh water. From there we entered the Zodiac and passed the Sun, leaving it on our right hand, hugging the shore but never landing — though my crew badly wanted to — because the wind wouldn't allow it. We could see it was a flourishing country, rich, well watered, and full of every good thing. The Cloud-Centaurs, who serve Phaethon as mercenaries, spotted us and came at our ship at top speed, but on finding we were covered by the treaty they let us go without another word. Our Vulture Cavalry escort had already turned back.

We sailed on all that night and the next day, and around evening, still holding our course downward, we came to a city called Lychnopolis — Lamp-Town. It hangs in the air between the Pleiades and the Hyades, somewhat below the Zodiac. When we landed, there was not a man to be found — only lamps, great numbers of them, hurrying back and forth, some busy in the marketplace, some down at the harbor. Many were small and, frankly, poor things; a few were great and mighty, gloriously bright. There were lodgings built for each of them, every lamp had its own name like a person, and we heard them speak. They did us no harm at all — in fact they invited us to dinner — but we were so unnerved that we didn't dare eat or sleep the whole time we were there.

Their courthouse stands in the middle of the city, where the governor sits all night long calling each lamp by name, and any lamp that fails to answer is condemned to death for deserting its post. Their death is to be put out. We stood by, watched the proceedings, and heard the lamps plead their own cases and give their reasons for burning late. There I recognized our own house lamp. I spoke to it and asked how matters stood at home, and it told me all the news.

We spent that night there, and the next morning returned to the ship and set sail near the clouds. There we got a sight of the city of Nephelococcygia — Cloud-Cuckoo-Land — and marveled at it, though the wind was against us and we couldn't enter. Its king was Coronus, son of Cottyphion — Crow, son of Blackbird. And I couldn't help thinking of the poet Aristophanes: what a wise man he was, what a truthful reporter, and how little reason anyone has ever had to doubt a word he wrote.

Two days later the ocean came plainly into view, though we could see no land except the countries in the air, and those now looked fiery and glittering. On the fourth day, about noon, the wind eased gently and set us down fair and softly onto the sea.

The moment we felt water under us, we were beside ourselves — our joy was past expressing. We celebrated with everything we had on board, and we jumped over the side and swam around for the fun of it, for the sea was calm.

But it often happens that a change for the better is just the start of greater evils. We had sailed only two days on the water when, at sunrise on the third, we suddenly saw a great many monstrous fishes and whales — and one above the rest, fifteen hundred furlongs in length, which came at us with its mouth gaping, churning the sea for miles around, beating up froth and foam on every side, flashing teeth far taller than the tallest beech trees back home, all sharp as needles and white as ivory. We took what we assumed were our last leaves of one another, embraced, and waited for the end. The monster was on us at once, and it swallowed us down, ship and all. By sheer luck its jaws didn't catch us when they crashed shut — the ship slipped through the gaps between the teeth and on down into the deep of him.

Inside, we sat in darkness a good while and could see nothing, until the whale opened his mouth — and then we saw what we were in: a monstrous cavern, so broad and so high that a city of ten thousand people could fit inside it with room to spare. Scattered everywhere lay small fish and other sea creatures chopped to pieces, ships' masts and anchors, the bones of men, and cargo. In the middle of him there was land — actual earth, with hills — built up, I concluded, from the settling of all the mud that came down his throat. Forests had grown on it, trees of every kind, and herbs of every sort, and the whole thing looked farmed. The coastline of that land measured two hundred and forty furlongs around. Sea birds of all kinds — gulls, kingfishers, and others — had built their nests in the trees.

At first we wept, and wept freely. But at last I got my company on their feet, and we shored up the ship and struck fire. We made supper out of what lay to hand — there was fish of every sort piled around us — and we still had water left from the Morning Star.

The next morning we got up to watch for the whale's gaping, and looking out through it we could see, at different moments, mountains, then nothing but sky, then many times islands — for we could tell the beast was carrying himself at tremendous speed to every part of the sea. When we tired of that, I took seven of my company and went into the wood to see what I could find. We hadn't gone five furlongs when we came on a temple of Poseidon, as the inscription showed, and not far off a number of graves with pillars set on them, and close by a spring of clear water. Then we heard a dog barking and saw smoke rising in the distance, and judged there must be some dwelling out there.

So we hurried on, and came upon an old man and a youth hard at work in a garden plot, channeling water to it from the spring. We stopped short, caught between joy and fear, and they did exactly the same; for a long while nobody said a word. At last the old man spoke. "What are you, strangers? Spirits of the sea? Or unlucky men like us? For we are men — born and raised on land — yet now we're sea-dwellers, swimming around inside this continent of a whale, and we hardly know what to call ourselves anymore. By all reason we're dead, and yet we believe we're alive."

I answered him. "We're men too, father — new arrivals, swallowed up yesterday, ship and all. And just now we came walking into this wood, so vast and thick — some good spirit, I think, was guiding us, so we could meet you and learn we're not the only men shut up inside this monster. So tell us your story, we beg you: who you are, and how you got in here."

But he answered, "You won't hear one word from me, or get to ask another question, until you've taken some share of what hospitality we can offer." With that he brought us into his house — a sufficient one for his needs, with sleeping-couches made up and everything in order — and set before us greens and nuts and fish, and poured out his own wine for us. When we had eaten our fill, he asked what fortunes we had endured, and I told him everything in order: the storm, the doings on the island, our voyage through the air, the war, and all the rest, right down to our dive into the whale.

He marveled greatly at all this, and then told his own story in return. "By birth, strangers, I am from the island of Cyprus. I was traveling from my own country as a merchant, with this boy you see here — my son — and many friends besides, on a voyage to Italy in a great ship full of cargo. You may have seen her wreckage in the whale's mouth. We sailed in fair weather as far as Sicily, but there a storm caught us, so violent that by the third day it had driven us out into the ocean — where we had the luck to meet this whale, and were swallowed up, all of us. We two alone survived. The rest perished; we buried them here and built a temple to Poseidon over them.

"Ever since, this has been our life: we plant vegetables and live on fish and nuts. There's wood enough, as you see, and plenty of vines that yield a most delicate wine. There's a well of excellent cool water — maybe you saw it. We make our beds of leaves, we burn all the firewood we want, we snare the birds that fly around us, and we go out onto the monster's gills to catch live fish. We bathe there too, when we feel like it, for there's a saltwater lake not far off, about twenty furlongs around, full of all sorts of fish; we swim in it, and sail on it in a little boat of my own building. This is the twenty-seventh year of our shipwreck.

"And we could be content enough with all this, if it weren't for our neighbors. The borderers around us are spiteful and troublesome — completely unsociable, and savage."

"Do you mean," I said, "there are others inside the whale besides yourselves?"

"Many," he said, "and all hostile to strangers, and all of monstrous, misshapen builds. The western country, toward the tail-end of the wood, is held by the Salt-Fish Men — the Tarychanians — who look like eels with the faces of lobsters: a warlike, fierce people, eaters of raw flesh. On the right side live the Tritonomendetans, men in their upper parts and cats below; they are less offensive than the rest. The left side belongs to the Crab-Hands — the Carcinochirians — and the Tuna-Heads — the Thinnocephalians — who are in league with one another. The middle country is held by the Crab-Folk — the Paguridians — and by the Flounder-Feet — the Psettopodians — a warlike nation and swift of foot. The east, toward the mouth, is mostly desert, being washed over by the sea; even so, I make my home there, and pay the Flounder-Feet a tribute for it of five hundred oysters a year.

"That is the country, and those are its nations. So we had better work out between us either how to fight them or how to live among them."

"How many do they come to, all together?" I asked.

"More than a thousand," he said.

"And what weapons do they carry?"

"None at all," he said, "except the bones of fishes."

"Then our best course," I said, "is to fight them — armed as we are against people with no weapons. If we beat them once, we live free of fear afterward."

That was settled, so we went back to the ship and equipped ourselves. The cause of war we supplied by withholding the tribute, which had just come due. The Flounder-Feet sent their messengers to demand it; Scintharus — for that was the old man's name — gave them a harsh and scornful answer and sent them packing with it. The Flounder-Feet and the Crab-Folk, furious at Scintharus, came against us first, with a tremendous racket.

We had suspected they would, so we stood ready and waiting. I had posted twenty-five men in ambush, with orders to rise up once the enemy had passed by and take them in the rear — which is exactly what they did. The rest of us, twenty-five strong as well (for Scintharus and his son took their places in our ranks), advanced to meet them head-on and closed with great courage and strength. In the end we broke them, ran them down, and chased them all the way to their dens. The enemy dead came to one hundred and seventy. On our side we lost one man: Trigles, our pilot, run through the back with a fish's rib.

That day and the night after, we camped on the field, and set up the dry backbone of a dolphin on end, as a trophy.

By the next morning the rest of the peoples of the whale had heard what happened, and came out against us. The Salt-Fish Men were drawn up on the right wing under their captain Pelamus; the Tuna-Heads held the left; the Crab-Hands formed the center. The Tritonomendetans never stirred — they chose to join neither side. We met them by the temple of Poseidon and fell on with a great shout, which echoed around the inside of the whale like a cave. They were naked, unarmored people; we broke them quickly, chased them into the wood, and from that hour were masters of the country.

Soon after, they sent ambassadors to recover their dead and to discuss terms of peace. We had no intention of keeping friendship with them. The next day we marched on them and put them all to the sword — all but the Tritonomendetans, who, seeing how it had gone for the rest, fled out through the gills of the fish and threw themselves into the sea.

Then we walked the whole country, empty now, and settled there without an enemy left to fear. We passed the time exercising, hunting, planting vineyards, and gathering fruit from the trees — living like men of leisure with the world at their feet, inside a spacious and inescapable prison.

We led this life for a year and eight months. But on the fifth day of the ninth month, around the second opening of the mouth — the whale gaped once every hour, which is how we told time — around that second opening, I say, we suddenly heard a great commotion: shouting, like sailors' calls, and the stir of oars. Shaken, we crept all the way up to the very mouth of the fish, and standing inside his teeth we saw the strangest sight my eyes have ever looked at: giant men, half a furlong tall, sailing on great islands as if on shipboard. I know this is going to smell like a lie, but you'll get it anyway.

The islands were long but not high, each about a hundred furlongs in circumference, and each carried twenty-eight of those men. Some sat along either side of the island and rowed, using great cypress trees — branches, leaves, and all — for oars. Astern, if I can call it that, stood the helmsman on a high hill, holding a bronze rudder a furlong long. On the bow stood forty of them armed for battle, like men in every respect except their hair, which was pure fire and burned bright — so they needed no helmets. Instead of sails, the forest growing on each island did the work: the wind blew against it and drove the island forward like a ship, wherever the helmsman steered, with pilots to direct them — and under oars they were as nimble as a longboat.

At first we saw only two or three of them; later, no fewer than six hundred appeared. They divided into two fleets and went to battle. Many crashed bow-on into one another and were broken to pieces; many were rolled over and sank. The ones that grappled fought hard and would not easily let go — the soldiers stationed forward showed tremendous courage, boarding one another's islands and killing everyone they could, for no prisoners were taken. Instead of iron grappling-hooks, they threw enormous octopuses tied to lines: once an octopus gripped the trees, the island was held fast. For missiles they hurled oysters big enough to fill a wagon, and sponges the size of an acre.

The leader of one fleet was Aeolocentaurus — Wind-Centaur — and of the other, Thalassopotes — Sea-Drinker. The quarrel, it seems, was over plunder: the word was that Thalassopotes had driven off many herds of dolphins belonging to Aeolocentaurus. We could hear it in the way they shouted at one another and yelled out their kings' names.

Aeolocentaurus had the better of the day. He sank one hundred and fifty of the enemy's islands and took three more, crews and all. The rest backed off and fled. He pursued, but not far, since evening was coming on; then he returned to the wrecks, recovered the better part of them, and took up his own — for on his side, too, no fewer than eighty islands had gone down. As a monument of the island-fight they raised a trophy: one of the enemy's islands, staked up on the head of our whale. That night they camped beside the beast, made their cables fast around him, and rode at anchor close by — their anchors are made of glass, huge and remarkably strong. The next day they offered sacrifice on top of the whale, buried their dead there, and sailed away in high triumph, singing songs of victory.

And that was the manner of the battle of the islands.
