Book II
(Part 1 of 2)

By now we were thoroughly sick of living inside the whale. The endless waiting wore on us, so we put our heads together to find some way out of our captivity.

Our first idea was to dig a tunnel through his right side and escape that way. We set to work with a will, but after we'd dug five furlongs deep and gotten nowhere, we gave it up. Then we decided to set the forest on fire. That would kill him for certain, we reasoned, and once he was dead, getting out would be easy. So we put the plan into action, starting at the tail end. The fire burned for seven days and seven nights before he felt our handiwork at all. On the eighth and ninth days we could tell he was getting sick: he gaped more sluggishly than usual, and shut his mouth again sooner. By the tenth and eleventh days he was rotting through and starting to stink. On the twelfth day it occurred to us — almost too late — that unless we propped his jaws open the next time he gaped, we'd be sealed inside his dead carcass forever, and die miserably in there.

So we wedged long beams of timber upright inside his mouth to keep it from shutting, then got our ship ready, stocking her with plenty of fresh water and everything else we'd need. Scintharus agreed to serve as our pilot. The next morning, the whale died.

We hauled the ship through the gaps between his teeth, made cables fast to the teeth themselves, and little by little lowered her down into the sea. Then we climbed up onto the whale's back, sacrificed to Poseidon, and camped beside our trophy for three days, since there was no wind at all. On the fourth day we put to sea.

We kept running into the dead bodies of those who'd perished in the great sea-battle. Our ship bumped against them, and we measured the corpses with no small amazement. For a few days we sailed on in mild weather. But then the north wind blew so bitterly cold that a great frost set in, and the whole sea froze solid — not just a skin of ice on the surface, but frozen four hundred fathoms deep — so we had to abandon ship and walk out onto the ice.

The wind sat stubbornly in that quarter, and we couldn't endure the cold, so we tried a scheme that Scintharus invented: with pickaxes and other tools we dug an enormous cave down into the frozen water, and sheltered in it for forty days straight. We kept a fire going inside and lived on fish, which we found in plenty as we dug. When our supplies finally ran low, we came back out, set our frozen ship upright, spread her sails, and glided forward over the ice as smoothly as if we'd been on water — easy, gentle sledding. On the fifth day the weather warmed, the frost broke, and everything turned back to water again.

We hadn't sailed three hundred furlongs more before we reached a small deserted island, where we took on fresh water (which was running low again) and shot two wild bulls before moving on. These bulls have their horns growing not on their heads but under their eyes — which is where Momus, the god of fault-finding, always said they belonged.

Next we entered a sea made not of water but of milk, and in it appeared a white island covered with vines. The island was actually one enormous, well-pressed cheese — as we discovered later when we ate some of it — about twenty-five furlongs across. The vines were loaded with clusters of grapes, but when we squeezed them, out came not wine but milk. In the middle of the island stood a temple dedicated, according to its inscription, to Galatea the sea-nymph, one of the daughters of Nereus — her name means "milk-white." The whole time we stayed there, the soil itself supplied our food, and our drink was the milk from the grapes. The ruler of these parts, they say, is Tyro, daughter of Salmoneus — "tyro" means "cheese" — who received this reward from Poseidon after her death.

We rested on that island five days, and on the sixth put to sea again with a gentle breeze behind us and the water calm and smooth. By the eighth day we were no longer sailing in milk but in salty blue water, and we saw great numbers of men running across the surface of the sea. They were like us in every way, in both shape and size, except for their feet, which were made of cork — which is why, I assume, they're called the Cork-Feet (Phellopodes).

We were amazed to see that they didn't sink, but stayed right on top of the waves and traveled over them without a care. They came up to us, greeted us in Greek, and said they were headed for Phello, their own country. For a while they jogged along beside the ship, then turned off their own way and left us, wishing us a safe and prosperous voyage.

Soon many islands came into view. Close by on our left was Phello, where those men were headed: a city built on a huge, round cork. Farther off to the right we saw five large, mountainous islands with great fires burning on them. But straight ahead lay a broad, flat island, no more than five hundred furlongs off. As we drew near it, a wonderful fragrance drifted out over us, sweet and delicate — the kind of scent Herodotus the historian claims rises off Arabia the Blessed — a blend of roses, daffodils, gillyflowers, lilies, violets, myrtle, bay, and vine blossoms. That was the perfume that came wafting out to meet us.

Delighted by the smell, and hoping for better luck after all our hard labors, we drew close to the island. We found it ringed with harbors on every side — sheltered from rough water, yet roomy — and rivers of clear water flowing gently into the sea, with meadows and flowers and songbirds, some singing on the shore and many in the branches of the trees, while a soft, still air lay over the whole country. Whenever pleasant breezes stirred the woods, the swaying branches made a continuous, delightful melody, like the sound of pan-pipes in a lonely place. Mixed in with it you could hear a kind of murmur — not rowdy or unpleasant, but like the noise of a banquet, where some are playing flutes, some are praising the music, and some are clapping along with the pipe or the harp.

Charmed by all this, we sailed boldly into the harbor, moored the ship, and went ashore, leaving Scintharus and two of our companions aboard. As we walked through a sweet-smelling meadow, we met the guards who patrol the island. They took us prisoner and bound us with garlands of roses — the strongest restraints they have — to bring us before their governor. From them we learned along the way that this was the island called the Isle of the Blessed, and that its governor was Rhadamanthus, the judge of the dead. We were brought before him and put fourth in line among the cases waiting to be judged.

The first trial concerned Ajax, son of Telamon: whether he was fit to be admitted to the company of the Heroes or not. The charges against him were his madness and his suicide. After long arguments on both sides, Rhadamanthus delivered this sentence: that for the present he should be committed to the care of Hippocrates, the physician of Cos, to be purged with hellebore, and upon the recovery of his wits he should be granted admission.

The second was a lovers' dispute: Theseus and Menelaus both claiming the better right to Helen. Rhadamanthus ruled in favor of Menelaus, on the grounds of all the labors and dangers he had undergone for that marriage's sake — whereas Theseus had plenty of other wives to live with, including the Amazon queen and the daughters of Minos.

The third was a question of precedence between Alexander, son of Philip, and Hannibal the Carthaginian. Alexander won, and his throne was placed next to that of the elder Cyrus the Persian.

We came up fourth. He demanded to know what business we, living men, had setting foot in that sacred country, and we told him all our adventures, in order, exactly as they'd happened. He ordered us to stand aside, deliberated for a long while, and then referred the matter to his bench of assessors — there were many of them, including Aristides the Just, the Athenian. When he had settled on a verdict, he ruled that for our meddling curiosity and our needless wandering we should answer after our deaths; but that for the present we should be granted a fixed term of residence, during which we might feast with the Heroes, and then depart. The limit he set on our stay was seven months, and not a day more.

At that, our rose-garlands fell off by themselves, and we were set free and led into the city to feast with the blessed.

The city is built entirely of gold, and the wall around it of emerald. It has seven gates, each one carved from a single plank of cinnamon wood. The pavement of the city, and all the ground inside the walls, is ivory. The temples of all the gods are built of beryl, with great altars in them, each cut from a single amethyst, on which they offer their sacrifices. Around the city runs a river of the finest perfumed oil, a hundred royal cubits wide and deep enough to swim in comfortably. For baths they have great houses of glass, heated by burning cinnamon — and instead of water, the tubs are filled with warm dew.

Their only clothing is purple cobwebs. And they have no bodies at all: they're intangible and fleshless, pure shape and outline. Yet bodiless as they are, they stand, they move, they think, they speak. Each one is a naked soul, it seems, walking around wearing the likeness of a body — so long as you don't touch one, you'd never know it wasn't solid. They're like shadows standing upright, only not dark.

Nobody there grows any older: whatever age you arrive at, that's the age you stay. There's no night there, and no real daylight either, but a glow like the gray of dawn just before sunrise — that's the light they live in. They know only one season of the year, spring, and only one wind ever blows there, the soft west wind Zephyrus. The country blooms with every kind of flower and with every pleasant shade plant. Their vines bear fruit twelve times a year — once a month — and their pomegranates, apples, and other fruit trees, they say, bear thirteen times, because in the month they call Minous they bear twice. Instead of grain, the wheat-stalks there sprout loaves of bread, ready-baked, at their tips, like mushrooms. Around the city there are three hundred and sixty-five wells of water, just as many of honey, and five hundred of perfumed oil — though those, to be fair, are smaller. They have seven rivers of milk and eight of wine.

They hold their feasts outside the city in a field called Elysium — a beautiful meadow ringed by woods of every kind, so thick they shade all the guests, who recline on couches of flowers. The winds wait on them and bring them everything they need — everything except the pouring of the wine, which nobody has to do, because around the banqueting ground grow great trees of clear, pure glass, and the fruit of those trees is drinking-cups and goblets of every shape and size you could want. Each guest arriving at the feast picks one or two of these cups and sets them at his place, where they fill with wine all by themselves. That's how they drink. Instead of wearing garlands, they have nightingales and other songbirds pluck flowers with their beaks from the meadows nearby and fly overhead, singing, scattering blossoms down on them like snow.

As for perfume, this is how they're anointed: thick clouds draw the scented oil up out of the fountains and rivers, settle over the heads of the banqueters, and at the least breath of wind release it in a fine mist, like dew.

After dinner they pass the time with music and singing. Their favorite songs are Homer's verses — and Homer himself is there in person, feasting among them, with his couch just above Odysseus'. Their choirs are made up of boys and girls, led and accompanied by Eunomus the Locrian, Arion of Lesbos, Anacreon, and Stesichorus — who has had a place there ever since he made his peace with Helen. When these finish, a second choir takes over: swans, swallows, and nightingales. And when they've ended, the whole forest rings like a wind orchestra as the breeze stirs through it.

But the thing that does most for their merriment is a pair of springs beside the banqueting ground: one of laughter, the other of pleasure. Everyone drinks from both at the start of the feast, and so they spend the whole evening laughing and enjoying themselves.

Now I'll tell you which famous men I saw in that company. All the demigods were there, and everyone who fought at Troy except Ajax the Locrian — he alone, they told me, was being punished in the land of the wicked. Of the barbarians there were the two Cyruses, elder and younger; Anacharsis the Scythian; Zamolxis the Thracian; and Numa the Italian. There were also Lycurgus the Spartan, Phocion and Tellus the Athenians, and all the Wise Men except Periander.

I also saw Socrates, son of Sophroniscus, chatting away with Nestor and Palamedes. Clustered around him were Hyacinthus the Spartan, the gorgeous Narcissus, Hylas, and other beautiful young men. As far as I could tell, he was deeply in love with Hyacinthus — at any rate, he talked to him more than to all the rest put together. They said Rhadamanthus was annoyed with him for it, and had threatened more than once to throw him off the island if he kept playing the fool and wouldn't drop his endless ironic act at their banquets. Plato was the only one missing: they said he lived in a city of his own design, run under the very constitution and laws he'd written for it.

Aristippus and Epicurus rank highest in their esteem, because they're the most cheerful, agreeable, and best company at a party. Diogenes of Sinope had changed so completely from the man he used to be that he'd married Lais the courtesan, and would often get so drunk that he'd jump up and dance around the room like a man out of his mind. Aesop the Phrygian serves them as their jester. Not a single Stoic was in the company — they were all said to be still climbing the steep hill of Virtue. As for Chrysippus, we heard that he's not permitted to set foot on the island until he has purged himself with hellebore a fourth time. The Academics, they say, were perfectly willing to come, but are still holding back, suspending judgment — they can't establish for certain whether such an island even exists. Though personally I think they were just afraid of being judged by Rhadamanthus, since they themselves abolished all standards of judgment. They say many of them did start out, following after travelers bound for the island, but being too sluggish to keep up — and unable to comprehend the destination anyway — they gave up and turned back halfway.

Those were the most notable men there. The one held in highest honor among them all is Achilles, and after him Theseus.

As for sex and lovemaking, here is how things stand: they couple openly, in full view of everyone, with women and men alike, and nobody thinks the worse of anyone for it. Socrates was the lone exception — he swore up and down that his relations with the young men were purely innocent, and everybody therefore wrote him off as a perjurer; Hyacinthus and Narcissus both testified otherwise, whatever he claimed.

The women there are held in common, and no man objects — in this respect they are the most perfect Platonists in the world — and the boys, likewise, yield to whoever desires them, without any argument.

After I'd spent two or three days like this, I went to talk with Homer the poet, since we both had time on our hands. Among other things, I asked him where he was from — a question we still argue about endlessly — and he said he honestly couldn't be sure himself, since some claimed Chios, some Smyrna, and many Colophon. But in fact, he said, he was a Babylonian, and among his own people his name wasn't Homer but Tigranes; he only got the name later, when he lived as a hostage among the Greeks — "homeros" being Greek for hostage. Then I asked him about the disputed lines in his poems, the ones the critics reject as not his work: were they his or not? He said every one of them was his own, and had some harsh words for the grammarians Zenodotus and Aristarchus and their feeble judgment.

When he'd satisfied me on that, I asked him next why he opened his poem with the word "wrath," of all things. He said it just came out that way — no planning behind it whatsoever. I also wanted to know whether he wrote the Odyssey before the Iliad, as many people maintain. He said no. As for the blindness everyone attributes to him, I didn't even need to ask: I could see perfectly well that there was nothing to it.

I made a habit of this for many days afterward — whenever I found him at leisure, I'd go over and pepper him with questions, and he answered everything freely. He was especially talkative after his court case, which he won: Thersites had filed a complaint against him for the insults and mockery in the poem, and Homer was acquitted, with Odysseus arguing his defense.

Around the same time, Pythagoras of Samos arrived among us, having now changed shape seven times, lived seven lives, and completed the full cycle of his soul's migrations. The entire right half of his body was solid gold. They all agreed he should have a place among them, but couldn't settle on what to call him: Pythagoras, or Euphorbus. Empedocles showed up too, scorched from head to foot, his whole body looking broiled on the coals — but for all his begging, he was not admitted.

As time went on, the day of their athletic games came around — the Games of the Dead, which they call the Thanatusia. The presidents of the games were Achilles, serving his fifth term, and Theseus, his seventh. A full account would take too long, so I'll just give the main results. In wrestling, Carus, a descendant of Hercules, won the garland from Odysseus. The boxing match between Arius the Egyptian, who's buried at Corinth, and Epius ended in a draw. No prize was offered for the all-in fighting, and I can't remember who won the footrace. In poetry, Homer was unquestionably the best of them all — but the prize went to Hesiod. The prizes were all the same: garlands woven of peacock feathers.

Just as the games ended, news arrived that the damned souls in the prison of the wicked had broken their chains, overpowered their jailers, and were marching to attack the island, led by Phalaris of Agrigentum, Busiris the Egyptian, Diomedes the Thracian, and Sciron and Pityocamptes, the highway robbers. The moment Rhadamanthus heard it, he drew the Heroes up in battle order along the shore, commanded by Theseus, Achilles, and Ajax, son of Telamon — who by now had recovered his senses. The two sides met and fought, and the Heroes won the day, with Achilles performing most nobly of all. Socrates, posted on the right wing, also distinguished himself as a brave soldier — far braver than he'd been at the battle of Delium in his lifetime — for when the enemy charged, he neither ran nor even changed expression. For his valor he was later awarded a special prize: a beautiful, spacious garden on the outskirts of the city, where he gathered companions and held his discussions. He named the place the Necracademia — the Academy of the Dead.

Then we rounded up the defeated, bound them, and shipped them back to be punished worse than before.

Homer wrote an account of this battle too, and when I left he gave me the book to show my friends — but I lost it later, along with so much else. I do remember the opening line, though. It went: "Tell me now, Muse, how the dead Heroes fought."

When they win a battle, it's their custom to celebrate with a feast of boiled beans, banqueting together in honor of the victory. Only Pythagoras took no part: he sat far off by himself and went without dinner, because he cannot abide beans.

Six months had now passed, and we were halfway through the seventh, when trouble broke out among us. Cinyras, the son of Scintharus — a fine, tall young man — had long been in love with Helen, and it was plain to see she doted on him just as foolishly. They were forever winking and toasting each other at the feasts, slipping off alone together, and wandering through the woods. Love kept growing, and seeing no other way forward, Cinyras hit on a plan: to steal Helen away and escape with her to one of the nearby islands — Phello, or Tyroessa, the cheese island. He found her perfectly willing to run off with him. He'd already recruited three of the boldest men in my crew as fellow conspirators, but he never breathed a word to his father, knowing he'd put a stop to it.

The plan settled, they waited for their moment. When night came and I was out of the way — I'd fallen asleep at the feast — they slipped past everyone unnoticed, hurried Helen aboard, and put to sea as fast as they could. Around midnight, Menelaus woke up, found his bed empty and his wife gone, and raised an outcry. He collected his brother and went straight to the court of Rhadamanthus.

At first light, the lookouts reported a ship in sight, already far out to sea. So Rhadamanthus launched a vessel carved from a single timber of asphodel wood, manned by fifty of the Heroes, with orders to give chase. They rowed with such a will that by noon they had caught the runaways just as they entered the milky ocean near Tyroessa — that's how close they came to escaping. We took their ship in tow with a chain of roses and hauled it back again.
