Book I

(Part 1 of 2)

Athletes and wrestlers — anyone who trains the body for strength and speed — don't just worry about staying in shape and keeping up their daily routine. They also know when to rest, and they treat rest as a serious part of training. I think the same goes for scholars. After a long haul through heavy, serious books, you ought to give your mind a break, so it comes back fresher and stronger for the next stretch of study.

And the rest works best if you spend it on the right kind of reading: books that don't just entertain you with wit and good writing, but also give your mind something to chew on. I'd like to think these books of mine do exactly that. You won't enjoy them only for the novelty of the subject, or the charm of the plan, or the sheer number of outrageous lies I tell with a perfectly straight face. You'll also enjoy them because every single thing in here is a sly poke at one of the old poets, historians, and philosophers who filled their books with monstrous, shameless whoppers. I'd name names, but you'll recognize them yourself as you read.

Ctesias of Cnidus, son of Ctesiochus, wrote about India and its marvels — things he never saw and never heard from a single living soul. Iambulus wrote a pile of wonders about the great ocean; everyone knew he made it all up, but he made it up so pleasantly that people read him anyway. Plenty of others have taken the same line, publishing their so-called "travels," stuffed with enormous beasts, savage tribes, and bizarre ways of life. And the founding father of all this nonsense was Homer's Odysseus, who sat in the court of King Alcinous and spun his long yarn about winds held captive in a bag, one-eyed giants who ate men raw, many-headed monsters, and his crew turned into animals by a witch's potion — and the simple Phaeacians swallowed every word of it as solid fact.

Reading all this, I couldn't really blame ordinary people for lying, when even men with reputations as philosophers were doing it. What did amaze me was that they thought nobody would notice. So I got ambitious to leave a monument of my own behind, and I saw no reason to be the only man alive shut out from the freedom to tell tales. Since nothing worth recording has ever actually happened to me, I turned my pen to lies — but with a more honest heart than the others. For this one thing, at least, I confidently pronounce as a truth: that I lie. And I hope that confession excuses everything else, now that I've admitted up front what I'm guilty of. I am about to write of things I never saw, never experienced, and never heard about from anyone else — things that do not exist, and never possibly could. So whatever you do, don't believe a word of it.

I weighed anchor one day at the Pillars of Hercules — the Strait of Gibraltar — and with a fair wind behind me, struck out into the Western Ocean. The reason for the voyage? Simple curiosity. A hunger for new things, and a longing to find out where the ocean ends and what kind of people live on the far shore. With that in mind I laid in a generous stock of food and fresh water, recruited fifty companions who shared my itch for adventure, packed plenty of weapons, paid a handsome fee to an expert pilot to set our course, and refitted a tall, sturdy ship for a long and difficult passage.

We sailed a day and a night with a favorable wind, taking it easy as long as land stayed in sight. But at sunrise the next morning the wind rose hard, the waves swelled, and a darkness fell over us so thick we couldn't even see to take in the sails. We gave the ship up to the weather and let it carry us — and it carried us, tossing in that storm, for seventy-nine days straight. On the eightieth day the sun broke out all at once, and we sighted, not far off, an island, high and wooded, where the sea had calmed to a reasonable roar. We put in, went ashore, and threw ourselves flat on the ground, lying there a long time, completely wrecked by our ordeal at sea. At last we got up and split our company: thirty stayed to guard the ship, and I took the other twenty to explore the island. We hadn't gone half a mile through the woods when we came upon a pillar of bronze, engraved with Greek letters — worn faint and hard to read — saying: THIS FAR CAME HERCULES AND BACCHUS. Nearby, two footprints were stamped in the rock: one a full acre across, the other smaller. I figured the smaller for Bacchus and the giant one for Hercules. We paid our respects and pressed on.

We hadn't gone far before we reached a river — and the river ran wine. Rich wine, as fine as anything out of Chios, and broad enough in places to float a ship. Seeing such solid evidence of Bacchus's travels, I started taking that pillar a lot more seriously. We decided to follow the stream up and find its source, and when we reached the head of it, there was no spring at all. Instead: enormous grapevines, countless numbers of them, and from each root the pure wine came trickling out — that's what kept the whole river flowing. The stream was well stocked with fish, too, which tasted and even looked like wine; we caught a few, but everyone who ate them got drunk on the spot. When we cut them open, they were full of wine-dregs. Later we figured out the trick of mixing them with ordinary freshwater fish, which cut the strength of the wine to something manageable.

Then we crossed the river at a shallow place and came into a whole world of vines — vines of an unbelievable kind. The lower part, toward the ground, was a thick, healthy trunk. But from the hips up, they were women, perfectly formed in every detail — the way painters show Daphne at the moment she turned into a tree as Apollo grabbed her. From their fingertips grew branches loaded with grapes, and their hair was nothing but curling tendrils and leaves and clusters. When we came up, they greeted us and shook our hands, chatting away, some in Lydian, some in Indian, but most of them in Greek. They kissed us on the mouth, too — and every man they kissed went instantly drunk and staggered around for a good while after. They could not bear to have their fruit picked; if anyone tried, they roared and cried out in real pain. Some of them wanted to go further than kissing, and two of our company took them up on it — and could never be pried loose again. They fused with the women at the hip, grew into them, took root with them; their fingers were already sprouting branches and curling tendrils, as if they'd be bearing grapes of their own any day. We abandoned the two of them and fled to the ship, and when we got there we told the others everything — about our friends' entanglement, vines and all. Then we took some jars and filled them, a few with water and a few with wine straight from the river, spent the night on the beach nearby, and in the morning put out to sea with a gentle wind.

Around noon, with the island out of sight behind us, a whirlwind hit. It spun the ship around, hoisted it three thousand furlongs into the air — about three hundred and fifty miles — and never let us back down to the sea. We hung up there above the world, sails full, driven along by a mighty wind. For seven days and seven nights we sailed the sky, and on the eighth day we came in sight of a great country in the air, like an island — round, shining, and lit up with a tremendous light. We made for it, landed, and looked around: the country was inhabited, and farmed. By daylight we could see nothing else from there, but when night came, other islands appeared all around, some big, some small, all colored like fire — and below them another country, with cities and seas and rivers and forests and mountains, which we worked out to be our own Earth.

Pushing farther inland, we ran into a patrol and were arrested — by what the locals call the Hippogypi, the Vulture Cavalry. The Vulture Cavalry are men who ride enormous vultures the way we ride horses. The vultures there are tremendous, each with three heads; to give you the scale, every feather in their wings is bigger and longer than the mast of a tall ship. Their standing orders were to patrol the country and bring any strangers to the king — so naturally that was our fate, and they presented us to him. He took one look at our clothes and guessed. "Strangers," he said, "you wouldn't be Greeks, would you?" We said we were. "And how did you get here, with all that air to cross?"

So we told him the whole story of our adventures, and he told us his in return. He was a man too — Endymion by name — snatched up from the Earth long ago in his sleep and brought here, where they made him king of the country. This land, he said, was the very same one that appears to us down below as the Moon. He told us to cheer up and fear nothing; we'd want for nothing while we were his guests. "And if I win the war I've got on my hands against the Sun," he said, "you'll live with me here as happily as men can live."

We asked who his enemies were and what the quarrel was about. "Phaethon," he said, "king of the people of the Sun — for the Sun is inhabited, just like the Moon — has been making war on us for a long time. Here's why. Once, I gathered up all the poorest and neediest people in my kingdom, planning to plant a colony on the Morning Star — that's Venus — because it was empty desert with nobody living on it. Phaethon got jealous and blocked the whole project: he sent his Ant Cavalry to ambush us halfway there. We weren't equipped for a fight, so we were beaten and turned back. But now I mean to declare war again and plant my colony anyway. If you're willing to join the expedition, I'll furnish each of you with a vulture from my own stable and a full set of arms. We march tomorrow."

"With all our hearts," I said, "if it pleases you."

So we feasted and stayed with him that night, and at dawn we took our positions, because the scouts were signaling that the enemy was close. Our army numbered one hundred thousand, not counting porters, engineers, infantry, and foreign allies. Of those hundred thousand, eighty thousand were Vulture Cavalry, and twenty thousand rode the Lachanopters — the Salad-Wings — a breed of huge bird shagged all over with greens instead of plumage, their wing-feathers looking remarkably like lettuce leaves. Next to them were posted the Millet-Shooters and the Garlic-Fighters. Allies came in from the Big Bear, too: thirty thousand Flea-Archers and fifty thousand Wind-Runners. The Flea-Archers ride giant fleas — hence the name — each flea the size of a dozen elephants. The Wind-Runners are infantry, but they travel through the air without wings: each man wears a long cloak down to his feet, and the wind fills it like a sail and carries him along like a boat. Most of them fight as light troops with shields. There was talk of seventy thousand Sparrow-Acorns and five thousand Crane Cavalry expected from the stars over Cappadocia, but they never showed up, and I didn't see them myself — so I haven't dared describe them, because the reports going around were too wonderful and incredible to repeat.

The equipment was the same throughout the army: helmets of bean-shells (their beans grow huge and tough), breastplates of overlapping lupin-husks sewn into scales — the husk of a lupin there is impenetrable, hard as horn — and shields and swords of the Greek pattern. When the hour of battle came, the line of battle was drawn up as follows. The right wing was held by the Vulture Cavalry, the king in person among them with the pick of his soldiers — and we were posted there too. The Salad-Wings held the left, and the allies took the center, arranged as their luck fell out. The infantry came to about sixty million, and they were deployed by an ingenious arrangement: the spiders in those parts are enormous, every one of them bigger than one of the Cyclades islands, and they were ordered to spin a web across the air between the Moon and the Morning Star. The job was done in no time, and on that web a level plain was laid out, and the infantry was drawn up on it, under the command of Nycterion, son of Eudianax, with two other generals beside him.

On the enemy side, the left wing was the Ant Cavalry, with Phaethon himself among them. These are beasts of enormous size, winged, resembling our ants in everything but scale — the biggest of them measured two acres. The riders fought, and the ants themselves fought too, doing terrible damage with their feelers. They were reported at fifty thousand. On their right wing were posted the Sky-Gnats, also about fifty thousand, all of them archers mounted on giant gnats. Behind them came the Sky-Dancers, light-armed infantry but dangerous ones: they slung monstrous turnips from long range, and any man they hit did not last long — he died of the stench that rose from the wound. The story is that they smear their shot with mallow poison. Next to them stood the Stalk-Mushrooms, heavy troops and hard fighters at close quarters, fifty thousand strong. They are called Stalk-Mushrooms because their shields are mushrooms and their spears are stalks of asparagus. Near them were the Dog-Acorns, sent by the Dog Star to help him: dog-faced men riding winged acorns. But two of Phaethon's allies came too late — the slingers he had summoned from the Milky Way, and the Cloud-Centaurs. The battle was over before they arrived, so they were no use at all; and the slingers never came whatsoever, which is why, they say, Phaethon later burned over their country in his fury.

That was the force Phaethon brought into the field. The armies met; the signal was given; the asses on each side brayed — they use asses for trumpeters — and the battle began. The left wing of the Sunites broke at once. They never even stood to receive the charge of the Vulture Cavalry, but turned tail immediately, and we cut them down as they ran. But their right wing was too strong for our left, and drove it back all the way to our infantry. The infantry came to the rescue, and with their weight added, the enemy there broke and fled too, especially once they realized their left wing had already been smashed. It became a total rout. Great numbers were taken prisoner, great numbers killed, and the blood spilled everywhere — some of it fell on the clouds, dyeing them red, the way they look to us down here around sunset, and some dripped all the way to the Earth. That made me wonder whether something of the kind happened ages ago, and Homer simply assumed it was Zeus raining blood over the death of his son Sarpedon.

When we came back from the chase, we set up two trophies: one for the infantry battle, planted on the spider-web, and one for the air battle, set up on the clouds. We had hardly finished when the scouts brought word that the Cloud-Centaurs were coming — the allies who should have reached Phaethon before the battle. As they came on, they were a sight worth staring at: monsters compounded of winged horse and man. The human part, from the waist up, was as tall as the Colossus of Rhodes; the horse part was the size of a big cargo ship. As for how many there were — I won't write the number down. It was so large you'd take it for a lie. Their leader was the Archer out of the Zodiac. When they learned their friends had been beaten, they sent word to Phaethon to come back and renew the fight, and then they formed up and charged. The Moonites were in complete disorder — scattered everywhere, chasing fugitives and looting the field — and the Cloud-Centaurs put the whole army to flight, chased the king himself back into his city, killed the greater part of his birds, tore down the trophies we had raised, and overran the entire plain the spiders had woven. Myself and two of my companions were taken alive.

By now Phaethon had come up, and the enemy set up trophies of their own. That same day we were carried off as prisoners into the Sun, our hands tied behind our backs with a length of cobweb. The enemy decided not to lay siege to the city. Instead, on their way back, they built a wall across the middle of the air, so that the Sun's light could no longer reach the Moon at all. It was a double wall, made entirely of cloud — and the result was a total, permanent eclipse of the Moon, with the whole country locked in unbroken night. Endymion was crushed by this. He sent ambassadors begging Phaethon to pull the wall down and not leave his people to live in darkness, promising to pay tribute, to be his ally and friend, and never to make war on him again. Phaethon's council met twice over the offer. At the first session they would not soften their anger one inch; at the second, they came around, and the peace was drawn up in these terms:

"The Sunites and their allies have made peace with the Moonites and their allies, on these conditions: that the Sunites shall demolish the wall, and shall return their prisoners at a fixed ransom per head; that the Moonites shall leave the other stars their independence, and shall not raise arms against the Sunites, but each shall aid and defend the other if either is attacked; that the king of the Moonites shall pay to the king of the Sunites a yearly tribute of ten thousand jars of dew, and shall hand over ten thousand of his people as hostages for his good faith; that the colony on the Morning Star shall be planted jointly by both parties, and any other nation that wishes may take part in it; and that these articles of peace shall be engraved on a pillar of amber, to be set up in the middle of the air on the border between the two countries. Sworn, for the Sunites: Pyronides, Therites, and Phlogius. For the Moonites: Nyctor, Menius, and Polylampes."

So peace was concluded. The wall came down at once, and we prisoners were handed back. When we returned to the Moon, our friends came out to meet us — Endymion himself with all his court — and embraced us with tears in their eyes. He begged us to stay, to join the new colony, and even promised me his own son in marriage (there are no women there at all). I wouldn't be persuaded; I asked him, as the greatest favor he could do me, to send us back down to the sea. When he saw there was no changing my mind, he entertained us for seven days of feasting and then let us go.

But first, let me tell you about the strange things I observed during my stay on the Moon — all the novelties worth noting.

To begin with: they are not born of women, but of men. Marriage there is between males only, and the very word "woman" is unknown. Up to the age of twenty-five, each man is the wife; after that, he becomes the husband. They carry their children not in the belly but in the calf of the leg. Once a child is conceived, the leg begins to swell; when the time comes, they cut it open and deliver the baby — dead. Then they lay it out with its mouth open to the wind, and the wind brings it to life. I suspect that's where we Greeks got our phrase "the belly of the leg," since on the Moon the leg, not the belly, does the childbearing.

But here is something stranger still. There is a race among them called the Dendritae, the Tree-Men, who come into the world like this: they cut off a man's right testicle and plant it in the ground. From it grows a great tree of flesh, with branches and leaves, bearing fruit like acorns — but a foot and a half long. When the fruit is ripe, they harvest it and shell the men out of it. Their private parts are detachable, fitted on and taken off as the occasion requires. Rich men have them made of ivory, poor men of wood, and with these they do their begetting and keep company with their spouses.
