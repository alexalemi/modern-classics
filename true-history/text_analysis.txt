TEXT ANALYSIS AND TRANSLATION STRATEGY
True History (Vera Historia) — Lucian of Samosata, c. 175 AD
Source: Francis Hickes' English translation of 1634 (1894 edition;
Hickes' marginal footnotes removed — they are the translator's
pedantry, not Lucian)

================================================================================
1. WHAT THIS BOOK IS
================================================================================

The first science fiction story, and a deliberate, gleeful parody. Lucian —
a Syrian-Greek satirist — opens by mocking historians and travel writers
(Ctesias, Iambulus, and above all Homer's Odyssey) who pass off whoppers
as fact, then announces the joke of the whole work: everything that
follows is a lie, honestly labeled. What follows: a sea voyage past the
Pillars of Hercules; a river of wine; a whirlwind that carries the ship to
the MOON; interplanetary war between the Moon-king and the Sun-king over
the colonization of the Morning Star (with cavalry riding three-headed
vultures, fleas the size of elephants, and a battlefield spider-web spun
between Moon and Venus); the strange biology of the Moon-men; being
swallowed whole — ship and all — by a whale 170 miles long, with nations
living inside it; a sea of milk with a cheese island; the Isle of the
Blessed, where the famous dead live (Homer interviews! the trials of
Helen! Lucian gets Homer to confirm which lines the critics wrongly
deleted); the Isle of the Wicked; the Isle of Dreams; Calypso's island;
and a cliffhanger non-ending promising further adventures that — as
ancient readers already knew — was the final lie.

Two books, ~16k words. Tone: a brilliant stand-up comedian doing a
pitch-perfect deadpan parody of a travel memoir.

================================================================================
2. LUCIAN'S STYLE — AND WHAT THE 1634 ENGLISH ADDS
================================================================================

- DEADPAN ESCALATION. The narrator reports the impossible in the flat,
  precise voice of a careful historian: numbers, distances, troop counts
  ("eighty thousand of the Vulture Horsemen"), dates, names. The comedy
  lives in the precision. NEVER signal the joke with a wink; the wink is
  structural. Keep all the fake-precise numbers exactly.
- PARODY OF SOURCES. Constant riffs on Homer, Herodotus, Thucydides
  (battle scenes parody Thucydidean war reporting — casualty lists,
  treaties with absurd clauses, trophy inscriptions). Render the treaty
  and inscription passages in mock-formal register.
- SPEED. Lucian never lingers; a marvel per paragraph. Keep the pace —
  short paragraphs, brisk transitions ("From there we sailed on...").
- THE HICKES LAYER. The 1634 English is itself dense and archaic
  ("tarriance," "great sooth," "peregrinations"). Translate THROUGH it to
  Lucian's brisk comic clarity — the modern version should read like a
  fast, funny contemporary travel blog from a narrator you cannot trust,
  not like a 17th-century text lightly dusted.

================================================================================
3. FORMAT CONVENTIONS IN THE SOURCE FILES
================================================================================

- Files are book-level with "(PART n OF k)" markers; two books, two parts
  each. No internal chapter divisions and no margin notes — do NOT invent
  subheadings. Continuous narrative prose with paragraph breaks.
- Greek names in Hickes' spellings (Phæakes, Endymion, Phaethon) →
  standard modern spellings (Phaeacians, Endymion, Phaethon). The œ/æ
  ligatures all modernize.
- Hickes' long periodic sentences (colon-and-semicolon chains) → break
  into short modern sentences; the deadpan survives the breaking.

================================================================================
4. VOCABULARY MAP
================================================================================

  Vulture Horsemen (Hippogypians)   -> Vulture Cavalry (gloss the Greek
                                       once: "Hippogypi — vulture riders")
  Salletmen (Lachanopters)          -> Salad-Wings / vegetable-feather
                                       troops — keep Lucian's compound-word
                                       jokes ALIVE by translating them into
                                       fresh English compounds, with the
                                       Greek once in parentheses where the
                                       source gives it
  Millet-shooters, Garlic-fighters  -> keep as literal comic compounds
  the Moonites / Sunites            -> Moon-men and Sun-men (or Moonites/
                                       Sunites — pick in file 000, lock it)
  Endymion (Moon-king), Phaethon (Sun-king) -> keep names
  Morning Star (Lucifer/Venus)      -> the Morning Star (gloss once: Venus)
  the great whale                   -> the whale (its size in miles kept
                                       exactly as the source gives it)
  Isle of the Blest                 -> Isle of the Blessed
  Rhadamanthus                      -> Rhadamanthus (gloss once: the judge
                                       of the dead)
  Isle of Dreams, Isle of the Wicked-> keep
  Pillars of Hercules               -> Pillars of Hercules (gloss once:
                                       the Strait of Gibraltar)
  furlongs/stadia                   -> convert nothing; keep the source's
                                       units with a one-time light gloss
                                       (the fake precision is the joke,
                                       not the metric value)

================================================================================
5. FAMOUS PASSAGES — PRESERVE NEARLY VERBATIM (Hickes wording, lightly
   modernized)
================================================================================

- The preface confession — the heart of the work, land it as a punchline:
  "for this one thing I confidently pronounce for a truth: that I lie."
  and "I write of matters which I neither saw nor suffered, nor heard by
  report from others" (+ "...which are not, nor ever were, nor can ever
  be"). Keep "that I lie" exactly.
- The closing promise of Book II (more adventures in the next books) —
  render faithfully; do not add a translator's note that it was a joke.

================================================================================
6. CONTENT NEEDING CAREFUL HANDLING
================================================================================

- MOON-MEN REPRODUCTION (Book I): Lucian's Moon has no women; men marry
  men and bear children from the calf of the leg, plus the "tree-men"
  grown from planted... acorns of flesh. This is ancient comic
  fantasy-biology: translate it plainly, completely, and matter-of-factly
  in the narrator's same deadpan — neither censored nor leered at.
- ISLE OF THE BLESSED couplings (Book II): free love among the dead,
  Helen's elopement subplot, Socrates teased about the company of
  beautiful youths — same treatment: complete, deadpan, unbowdlerized;
  it is canonical classical satire.
- Classical allusions (Ajax, Theseus, Pythagoras' golden thigh, Empedocles
  half-cooked from Etna): keep all, with at most a 2-4 word inline
  identification where a modern reader needs one ("Ctesias, an old
  historian famous for tall tales").

================================================================================
7. VOICE TARGET — SAMPLE TRANSFORMATION
================================================================================

Original (Hickes):
  "for this one thing I confidently pronounce for a truth, that I lie:
  and this, I hope, may be an excuse for all the rest, when I confess
  what I am faulty in: for I write of matters which I neither saw nor
  suffered, nor heard by report from others, which are not in being,
  nor possible ever to be."

Modern target:
  "For this one thing, at least, I can confidently pronounce as a truth:
  that I lie. And I hope that confession excuses everything else — for I
  am about to write of things I never saw, never experienced, and never
  heard about from anyone else; things that do not exist and never
  possibly could."

Register: 8th grade–high school. Fast, funny, precise. Contractions
welcome in the narrator's voice — he is telling you a story over a drink.
The fake-historian passages (treaties, inscriptions, troop counts) go
contraction-free for mock formality.
