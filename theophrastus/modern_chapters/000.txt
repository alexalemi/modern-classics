Characters 1–10

Theophrastus to Polycles

I have stopped to wonder about this many times, and I expect I'll never stop: why is it that we Greeks don't all turn out the same? We share one climate from end to end of the country, and our people get the same education — and yet our characters could hardly be more different. I've been studying human nature a long time, my dear Polycles. I have lived ninety-nine years, I've talked with people of every possible temperament, and I've taken great pains to observe the good ones and the bad ones side by side. So it seemed to me I ought to set down in writing how people actually live and behave. I'm going to describe their characters, type by type, and show you the weakness that runs each man. I'd like to think, Polycles, that our children will be better for it. If we leave them portraits like these to study — patterns of good and bad laid out plainly — they'll choose, I believe, to spend their lives in the company of the best sort of people, and try to live up to that standard themselves. So, to work. Your job is to follow along and judge whether I've got it right. I'll begin the book with the Phony, skipping any preface and any fuss about the word itself. First I'll lay down a definition of phoniness, and then, with that in view, I'll draw the phony himself — how he acts, how he lives, trait by trait, as clearly as I can manage.

1. The Phony

Phoniness is, in essence, an act — in word or deed — put on to make things look other than they are. The phony is the sort of man who walks right up to his enemies and chats away pleasantly, just to show there are no hard feelings. He praises people to their faces and trashes them behind their backs — and when they lose a lawsuit, he tells them how terribly sorry he is. When people bad-mouth him, or the criticism gets loud, he's all forgiveness.

When someone has been treated badly enough to have every right to be furious, the phony's comments on the outrage are carefully, studiously neutral. And when a man is anxious for a word with him, he tells him to come back another time — he's only just gotten home, or the hour is late, or honestly, his health won't stand the strain today.

He never admits he's doing anything. At most, he's "considering it." When a friend wants a loan, or asks him to chip in for something, he sighs, "Business is dreadfully slow" — though when business really is slow, he reports it booming. Has he heard the news? He hasn't heard a thing. Did he see what happened? He didn't see it — or fine, he saw it, but he can't for the life of him recall it. One matter he says he'll look into; another he knows nothing about; a third leaves him amazed; a fourth — well, he'd already thought of that himself, actually. In short, he is a master of phrases like these: "I can't believe it." "I don't follow you." "I'm stunned." "To hear you tell it, the fellow has become a different man." "That is certainly not what he told me." "The whole thing sounds unlikely." "Go tell that one to somebody gullible." "I honestly don't know whether to doubt your story or condemn my friend." "Just be careful you're not too quick to believe things."

2. The Suck-Up

Sucking up is, in essence, degrading behavior that happens to pay off for the man doing it. The suck-up is the sort of man who, strolling along with his patron, cries: "Look at how people stare at you! There isn't a man in the city who gets the attention you do. Yesterday you were the talk of the Porch — the colonnade where the men of leisure gather. More than thirty of them were sitting there when the question came up, 'Who is our finest citizen?' They started with your name and they ended with it." While he's carrying on in this vein, he picks a speck of lint from the great man's cloak; if the wind has lodged a bit of straw in his hair, he plucks it out with a laugh and says, "See that? Two days away from me and your beard is going gray — though if any man has a beard that's black for his years, it's you."

When the patron speaks, the suck-up shushes everyone else. He praises him where he's sure to overhear, and when the great man finishes a speech, he cues the applause with a "Bravo!" If the patron makes a stale joke, the suck-up howls with laughter and stuffs his sleeve in his mouth, as if he simply cannot contain himself.

If they meet people in the street, he asks them to kindly wait until the master has passed. He buys apples and pears, carries them to the great man's house, and hands them out to the children — and with the father watching, he kisses them and exclaims, "Chips off a magnificent block!" When the patron buys a pair of shoes, he observes, "The foot is finer than the boot." When the patron calls on a friend, the suck-up trots on ahead announcing, "You are about to have the honor of his visit," then doubles back with, "I have announced you." And of course he can run the errands at the market in the blink of an eye.

At a banquet he's the first to praise the wine, and after doing it ample justice he declares, "What a table you keep!" He picks up a morsel from the board and exclaims, "Now this — this is delicious!" Then he asks his friend whether he's chilly — would he like a wrap over his shoulders? Shall he throw one around him? — and with these words he's already bending over, whispering in his ear. While his talk is aimed at the rest of the company, his eye never leaves his patron. At the theatre he takes the cushions from the slave-boy and arranges them for the master's comfort with his own hands. Of the great man's house he says, "It is beautifully built." Of his farm: "It is beautifully kept." Of his portrait: "It's the man himself."

3. The Coward

Cowardice is, in essence, a certain shrinking of the heart. The coward is the sort of man who, sailing along, decides the cliffs in the distance are pirate ships. If the waves run high, he asks whether anyone in the ship's company has skipped his initiation into the mysteries — because that's exactly the kind of thing the gods sink a ship over. He leans over to the helmsman and inquires whether he intends to keep to the open sea, and what he makes of the weather; he confides to the man next to him that he's terrified on account of a dream he had; he pulls off his tunic and hands it to his slave; and finally he begs to be set ashore.

On campaign, when the infantry marches out, he calls his comrades to stand by him and keep a sharp lookout first — it's important, he says, to establish whether that thing out there is the enemy or not. When he hears the noise of battle and sees men falling, he tells the men around him that in his hurry he forgot to bring his sword. He runs back to his tent, sends his servant outside with orders to scout where the enemy is, hides the sword under his pillow, and then wastes a long time pretending to hunt for it. While he's in the tent, he sees a wounded comrade being carried in from the field — and now he springs into action. He runs out, cries "Cheer up, man!", lends a hand with the stretcher, and stays to nurse the sufferer: washing his wounds, sitting at his side, shooing the flies away — anything, anything but fight the enemy.

When the trumpeter sounds the signal for a fresh assault, he sits in the tent and grumbles, "Oh, plague take him! The poor fellow can't get a wink of sleep with that eternal bugling." Then, smearing himself with blood from the other man's wound, he goes out to meet the troops returning from battle, and — looking every inch the man just back from the thick of it — announces, "I've saved a comrade!" And then he brings his neighbors and clansmen into the tent for a viewing, and assures each one of them, individually, that he carried the wounded man in with his own two hands.

4. The Overhelper

Overhelping is, in essence, an excess of word and deed — well intended, of course. The overhelper is the sort of man who stands up in public and promises things he cannot possibly deliver. When a point is so clear that nobody doubts it, he raises an objection — and gets refuted.

At a banquet, he makes the servants mix more wine than the guests can possibly drink. He sees two men brawling and rushes in to separate them, though he doesn't know either one. He leads his friends off the main road onto a shortcut, and presently cannot find the way. He marches up to the commander and asks when he plans to draw up the troops for battle, and what his orders will be for the day after tomorrow.

He goes and informs his father that his mother is already asleep in her room. When the doctor orders that a patient get no wine, he administers "just a little" — he only wants to set the poor fellow right. And when a woman dies, he has the tombstone carved with her husband's name, her father's, her mother's, her own name and birthplace — and underneath adds: "Fine people, every one of them." When he takes the oath in court, he turns to the bystanders and remarks, "Not my first time, you know."

5. The Tactless Man

Tactlessness is, in essence, a gift for choosing exactly the moment most painful to everyone involved. The tactless man is the sort of man who picks a man's busiest hour to come confer with him. He serenades his sweetheart when she's down with a fever. When an acquaintance has just forfeited bail-money on a friend, he hunts him up and asks him to stand surety. He shows up at the trial to give his evidence — after the verdict has been delivered. As a guest at a wedding, he holds forth against the whole female sex.

When a friend has just come home from a long journey, he invites him out for a walk. He has a talent for producing a higher bidder after the item has already been sold. In a circle of companions he stands up and explains, from the very beginning, a story everyone has just heard and perfectly understood. He is eager — positively eager — to take on the chore that nobody wants done and nobody can politely refuse.

When men are in the middle of a religious offering, money flowing out of their purses, he arrives to collect his interest. Standing by while a slave is being flogged, he tells the story of how he once flogged a slave of his own — who went off and hanged himself. When he serves as arbitrator, he sets the two sides at each other's throats again just as they're ready to settle. And when he wants to dance, he grabs a partner who isn't drunk enough yet.

6. The Moocher

Mooching is, in essence, contempt for decency in pursuit of something grubby. The moocher is the sort of man who robs a man and then comes back to borrow money from him. He sacrifices a victim to the gods — then, instead of making his supper from it, he salts the meat away for later and goes and dines at a friend's house. There he calls a servant over, helps himself to bread and meat straight off the table, and says, in a voice all can hear: "Try that, Tibios!"

When he goes to market, he reminds the butcher of all the business he's brought him over the years, and as he stands by the scales he slips an extra cut onto the pile if he can — or, failing that, a soup-bone. If he gets away with it, he's satisfied. If not, he snatches a piece of tripe off the bench and makes off with it, laughing. He buys theatre seats for friends visiting town, goes along to the show, and never pays his own share — and the next day you'll find him back with his children, and their tutor too.

Whenever anyone lands a bargain of any kind, he demands a cut. He goes around to the neighbors borrowing barley — sometimes even bran — and then actually makes the lenders deliver it to his house. And his favorite trick: he marches up to the tubs in a private bath-house, draws himself a bucket of warm water, and dumps it over his own head while the attendant shouts in protest — and on his way out he calls back, "That's a good bath. No thanks to you!"

7. The Rumormonger

Rumormongering is, in essence, the manufacture of false reports of what people are saying and doing, exactly as the gossip pleases. The rumormonger is the sort of man who spots a friend, strikes a pose, breaks into a knowing smile, and fires off: "Where have you been? What's the news? How do things stand? Any fresh word about it?" — and, pressing right on, "No later report? Well! The current rumors are certainly good."

And without giving his friend a chance to answer, he keeps rolling: "What! You haven't heard a word? Then I have a feast of news for you." He always has a source ready: some soldier nobody's ever heard of, or a slave of Asteus the piper, or Lycon the contractor — an obscure fellow, just back from the battlefield. The authorities behind his reports are always precisely the kind you can never track down. But these are the men he quotes when he tells you how Polyperchon and the king carried the day, and Cassander — the strongman of the hour — has been taken prisoner.

If anybody asks, "Do you actually believe this?" he answers: "Believe it! The story is all over the city. It's gaining ground by the hour. Everyone is of one mind — every single person agrees about the battle. It must have been an absolute slaughterhouse." He finds proof, too, in the faces of the men in power: haven't they all got a changed look about them? He says he's overheard that a man arrived from Macedonia who knows the whole history of the battle — the authorities have had him hidden away in a house for five days now. And the pathos in his voice as he tells it — you can just imagine: "Luckless Cassander! Ill-starred hero! There's the fickleness of fortune for you. All that power, and for what? But — this stays strictly between ourselves." Then he trots off and repeats the whole thing to every man in town.

8. The Penny-Pincher

Penny-pinching is, in essence, an unhealthy sparing of expense. The penny-pincher is the sort of man who shows up at a man's door to demand his half-penny of interest before the month is even out. At dinner he counts how many cups each guest drinks, and of the whole company he pours the smallest offering to Artemis.

When a friend picks up something cheap, he totals the price aloud and declares it beggars a man. If a servant breaks a pot or a plate, he deducts the cost from the man's rations. If his wife drops a three-farthing coin — the smallest there is — he moves the furniture, the beds, the cupboards, and hunts between the floorboards. When he has something to sell, he sets the price so high that the buyer gets no bargain at all. He lets no one eat a fig from his garden, or cross his field, or pick up so much as a fallen olive or date. He inspects his boundary-stones every single day to make sure nobody has moved them.

He is always ready, the instant a debt goes unpaid, to seize the collateral and pile on compound interest. When he hosts a banquet for his fellow townsmen, he carves the meat into tiny pieces and sets one small portion before each guest. He goes to market — and buys nothing. He forbids his wife to lend out salt, or a lamp-wick, or a pinch of cumin, or marjoram, or meal, or a ribbon, or a sacrificial wafer — because, he observes, these trifles add up to real money over the course of a year.

In a word: the penny-pincher's money chest is moldy from staying shut and the key is rusty. His cloak is cut too skimpy to reach his thigh. He oils himself from a stingy little jar, wears his hair cropped to the scalp, and goes barefoot until midday to save the boots. And he stands over the fuller and insists on plenty of earth in the wash, so the coat won't get dirty again too soon.

9. The Dimwit

Dimwittedness is, in essence, a sluggishness of the soul in everything a man says and does. The dimwit is the sort of man who works out a sum, writes down the total, and then turns to his neighbor and asks: "So how much does it all come to?" When he's the defendant in a lawsuit and due in court, he forgets all about it and heads out to his farm. At the theatre he's the one spectator left behind on the benches, asleep. After stuffing himself at dinner, he gets up in the night to go out — and gets bitten by the neighbor's dog. He takes a thing and puts it away carefully, then can't find it when he looks. When a friend's death is announced to him so that he can attend the funeral, his face falls, tears come to his eyes, and he says: "Well, thank goodness for that!" When he goes to collect money owed him, he brings witnesses along. In the middle of winter he scolds his slave for not buying cucumbers. He drills his children at wrestling and running until they collapse with fever. Roughing it in the country and cooking the vegetables himself, he salts the pot twice and makes the dish inedible. When it's pouring rain and everyone else remarks that the sky is blacker than pitch, he sighs: "How sweet it is to gaze at the stars!" And when someone asks him how many dead have been carried out through the Sacred Gates — the gate to the burying-ground — he answers: "May you and I have as many."

10. The Grouch

Grouchiness is, in essence, sullen rudeness of speech. The grouch is the sort of man who, when you ask him "Who is that gentleman?", snaps back, "Don't bother me." When you greet him in the street, he doesn't return it. When he has something to sell, he won't tell the buyer what he's charging — he demands, "What am I offered?" When people show him a kindness and send him a gift for the holidays, he announces that he has no need of presents.

He accepts no excuse if you accidentally smudge his cloak, or bump him in a crowd, or happen to tread on his foot. Ask him to contribute to some cause and he refuses — though later he may come around with the money after all, declaring, as he hands it over, that he's throwing it away. When he stumbles in the street, he curses the stone that tripped him.

He is not a man to wait around long for a friend, appointment or no appointment. Singing, recitation, dancing — he has no taste for any of it. And it is exactly like him to refuse to join even in a prayer to the gods.
