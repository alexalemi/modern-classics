Cato the Younger

(Part 2 of 6)

After that, every city he passed through on the way home competed with its neighbors in showing him respect and honor. There were feasts and receptions at every stop — so many that Cato asked his friends to keep strict watch over him, in case he ended up proving Curio right. Curio was a friend of his, but he disliked Cato's austere temper, and he had once asked him whether he planned to see Asia when he left the army. "Yes, by all means," Cato answered. "Good," said Curio. "You'll come back with a better temper and pleasanter manners." Those were pretty nearly his exact words.

Deiotarus, the Galatian king, was an old man by now, and he had sent for Cato to place his children and his household under Cato's protection. The moment Cato arrived, the king began pressing gifts of every sort on him, begging and pleading with him to accept. All that pleading annoyed Cato so much that although he had come in the evening, he stayed only that one night and left early the next morning. After a single day's travel he reached Pessinus — and found an even bigger store of presents waiting for him there, along with letters from Deiotarus entreating him to take them, or at least to let his friends take them, since they deserved some reward for his sake and Cato could hardly provide for them out of his own means. Cato refused even that, though he could see that some of his friends were quite willing to accept and were ready to grumble at his severity. Corruption, he answered, would never lack an excuse; and his friends would share with him in whatever he obtained justly and honestly — nothing more. He sent the presents back to Deiotarus.

When he was about to take ship for Brundisium, his friends urged him to put his brother's ashes aboard a different vessel. He said he would sooner part with his life than with those ashes, and set sail. And as it happened, we are told, his crossing was a genuinely dangerous one, while other ships making the passage at the same time came through safely enough.

Back in Rome, he spent his time for the most part either at home, in conversation with Athenodorus, or in the forum, in the service of his friends. It was now time for him to stand for the quaestorship — but he refused to run for the office until he had studied the laws governing it, questioned men of experience, and gained a precise understanding of its duties and its authority. Armed with that knowledge, he began a sweeping reform of the treasury's clerks and under-officers the moment he took office. These were men with long practice and total familiarity with the public records and the laws; and since the new magistrates who came in year after year were so ignorant and unskilled that they positively needed someone to teach them their own jobs, the clerks never submitted to them or gave way. They kept the real power in their own hands. They were, in effect, the treasurers themselves. Until Cato. He threw himself squarely into the work and showed that he possessed not just the title and honor of a quaestor but the knowledge, the understanding, and the full authority of the office. He treated the clerks and under-officers as what they were — servants — exposing their corrupt practices and correcting their ignorance. They were bold, shameless men, and they flattered the other quaestors, Cato's colleagues, and tried through them to keep up an opposition against him. But Cato convicted the chief of them of a breach of trust in the handling of an inheritance and threw him out of his place. He brought a second to trial for dishonesty — a man defended by Lutatius Catulus, the censor at that time, a man of great weight because of his office and of greater weight because of his character, for he stood above all the Romans of his age in his reputation for wisdom and integrity. Catulus was also close to Cato and admired his way of living. Seeing that he could not get his client off in a fair trial, he began openly begging him off. Cato objected to his doing it. Catulus kept pleading. At last Cato said, "It would be shameful, Catulus, if the censor — the judge of all our lives — had to be removed from court by our officers." Catulus looked as if he wanted to make some answer; but he said nothing, and — whether from anger or from shame — walked away silent and out of countenance. Even so, the man was not convicted: the votes to acquit fell just one short of the votes to condemn, and Marcus Lollius, one of Cato's colleagues, who was absent sick, was sent for by Catulus and begged to come and save the man. So Lollius was carried into court in a chair and cast his vote for acquittal. Cato, for his part, never employed that clerk again, never paid him his salary, and refused to count Lollius' vote for anything.

Having humbled the clerks and brought them to heel, he managed the books and registers as he saw fit, and within a short time he had given the treasury a higher name than the Senate house itself. Everyone said that Cato had made the office of quaestor equal to the dignity of a consul. When he found that many people owed the state money on old accounts, and that the state likewise owed money to many private citizens, he made sure the public would no longer either commit wrongs or suffer them: he exacted what was due to the treasury strictly and punctually, and paid the state's creditors promptly and in full. People watched with awe and respect as men who had expected to slip away with their plunder were made to pay, and men who had despaired of ever seeing their money received every bit of it. And whereas those who showed up with false claims and pretended senate orders had usually managed, through favoritism, to get them accepted, Cato was never once taken in. In one particular case, when a question arose over whether an order had actually passed the senate, he refused to believe the many witnesses who attested to it, and would not admit it until the consuls came and swore to it on oath.

There were at that time a great many men Sulla had used as his agents in the proscriptions — men he had paid twelve thousand drachmas apiece for their service in putting the condemned to death. Everybody hated them as wicked, polluted wretches, but nobody dared take revenge on them. Cato called every one of them to account as wrongful holders of public money, exacted repayment, and in the same breath denounced them for their lawless and unholy actions. Immediately after this they found themselves charged with murder — already, in a sense, prejudged as guilty — and they were easily convicted, and paid the penalty. The whole people rejoiced at it, feeling that the old tyranny had finally been abolished and that Sulla himself, so to speak, had at last been brought to punishment.

His sheer diligence won the people over too — the man never stopped. He was always the first of his colleagues to arrive at the treasury and the last to leave. He never missed an assembly of the people or a sitting of the senate, always anxious, always on the watch for those who would casually, or as a favor to someone, vote to remit a debt or grant away customs owed to the state. And in the end, having kept the exchequer clean of cheats and informers while filling it with treasure, he showed that the state could be rich without oppressing the people. Some of his colleagues found him irritating at first, but after a while they were well contented with him, since he was perfectly willing to take all the blame on himself when they declined to hand out public money to their friends or to pass dishonest accounts. When petitioners pressed them hard, they could simply answer that nothing could be done unless Cato consented. On the last day of his term, nearly the whole people escorted him home in honor — but on the way he learned that several powerful men were at the treasury with Marcellus at that very moment, using all their influence to get him to record a certain debt to the public revenue as if it were a gift. Marcellus had been one of Cato's friends since childhood, and so long as Cato was at his side he was one of the best colleagues in the office; but on his own he could not resist the pressure of petitioners, and was prone to do anybody a kindness. Cato turned straight around, and finding that Marcellus had given in and passed the entry, he took the book and — while Marcellus stood silently by, watching — struck it out. That done, he led Marcellus out of the treasury and took him home with him. And Marcellus never complained of it, not then and not afterward, but remained his friend and intimate ever after.

Even after he laid down the office, Cato did not stop watching the treasury. He kept servants who continually copied out the details of public expenditure, and he always had by him a set of account books containing the state revenues from Sulla's time down to his own quaestorship — books he had bought for five talents.

He was always the first man into the senate and the last one out; and often, while the others were slowly gathering, he would sit and read quietly by himself, holding his gown up in front of his book. He was never once out of town when the senate was due to meet. Later, when Pompey and his party found that Cato could be neither persuaded nor pressured into favoring their unjust designs, they tried to keep him away from the senate by tangling him up in business for his friends — pleading their cases, arbitrating their disputes, and so on. Cato saw through the trick quickly, and to defeat it he told all his acquaintances flatly that he would never touch private business while the senate was in session. For it was not in hope of honor or riches, nor on impulse, nor by chance that he had gone into politics; he had taken up the service of the state as the proper business of an honest man, and he considered himself as bound to his public duty as a bee to its honeycomb. To that end he arranged to have friends and correspondents everywhere, sending him reports of the edicts, decrees, judgments, and all the important proceedings in the provinces. Once, when Clodius, the seditious agitator, was pushing his violent, revolutionary projects by slandering some of the priests and priestesses before the people — Fabia, sister of Cicero's wife Terentia, stood in real danger — Cato stepped in boldly, made Clodius look so disgraceful that he was forced to leave town, and, when it was over, was approached by Cicero, who came to thank him for what he had done. "You must thank the commonwealth," Cato said — for the commonwealth's sake, he insisted, was the only reason he did anything. Out of all this grew a great and remarkable reputation. An advocate in one case, facing a single witness on the other side, told the judges they ought not to rely on one witness even if it were Cato himself. And it became a kind of proverb with many people, when some wildly unlikely claim was asserted, to say they would not believe it even if Cato himself affirmed it. One day, while a debauched and extravagant liver was lecturing the senate on frugality and temperance, Amnaeus jumped up and cried, "Who can stand this, sir — you feast like Crassus, you build like Lucullus, and you talk like Cato!" And in the same spirit, men whose lives were vicious and dissolute but whose language was grave and severe were mockingly called Catos.

At first, when his friends urged him to stand for tribune of the people, he thought it a bad idea: the power of so great an office, he felt, should be held in reserve, like the strongest medicines, for occasions of last necessity. But later, during a court vacation, as he was traveling with his books and his philosophers to Lucania, where he had lands and a pleasant house, they met on the road a long train of horses, carriages, and attendants — belonging, they learned, to Metellus Nepos, who was heading to Rome to stand for the tribunate. Cato stopped. He was silent for a moment; then he gave orders to turn back at once. His companions looked amazed, and he said, "Don't you know how dangerous the madness of Metellus is all by itself? Now he comes armed with Pompey's backing, and he will fall on the state like lightning and throw everything into disorder. This is no time for holidays and leisure. We must get ahead of this man and stop his designs — or die honorably in defense of our liberty." At his friends' urging he did go on first to his country house, but he stayed only a very short time before returning to town.

He arrived in the evening, and first thing the next morning he went down to the forum and began campaigning for the tribuneship, in opposition to Metellus. The power of that office lies more in blocking business than in doing it: even if every other tribune agreed to a measure, the refusal of a single one could stop the whole thing cold. At first Cato had few supporters; but as soon as his intention became known, all the good and distinguished men of the city quickly came forward to encourage and support him. They saw him not as a man asking a favor of them, but as a man offering a great favor to his country and to every honest citizen — a man who had refused this same office many times when he could have had it without trouble, and who now sought it at real risk so that he might defend their liberty and their government. It is reported that so great a crowd pressed around him that he was nearly crushed, and could barely make his way through. He was declared tribune, along with several others — Metellus among them.

Once elected, Cato saw that the election of consuls had become a matter of open purchase, and he rebuked the people sharply for the corruption, ending his speech with a vow to prosecute whomever he found handing out money — with one exception, Silanus, on account of their close connection: Silanus was married to Servilia, Cato's sister. So he let Silanus alone and instead accused Lucius Murena, who had won the consulship by bribery alongside him. There was a law allowing the accused to appoint a man to keep watch on his accuser, so that he could know fairly how the prosecution was being prepared. The watcher Murena set on Cato followed him closely at first, observing everything — and never once caught him doing anything unfair or underhanded. Cato went about the accusation generously and candidly, by the just and open methods. The man came to admire his great spirit so much, and to trust his integrity so completely, that he would simply walk up to Cato in the forum, or call at his house, and ask whether he planned to do anything on the case that day; and if Cato said no, he went away satisfied, relying on his word. When the case was pleaded, Cicero — consul that year, and defending Murena — spent a good deal of the trial being witty at Cato's expense, poking fun at the Stoic philosophers and their so-called paradoxes until the judges were roaring with laughter. Cato smiled and remarked to the bystanders, "What a pleasant consul we have, my friends." Murena was acquitted — and afterward showed himself neither resentful nor a fool, for as consul he took Cato's advice in the weightiest affairs, and treated him with honor and respect throughout his entire term. The credit for that belongs to Murena's good sense, but also to Cato's own behavior: terrible and severe as he was in matters of justice, in the senate and at the bar, once the fight was over his manner toward everyone was perfectly friendly and humane.

Before he entered on the tribunate, he supported Cicero, then consul, in many of the struggles of his office — above all in his great and noble handling of Catiline's conspiracy, which owed its final successful outcome to Cato. Catiline had plotted the total overthrow of the Roman state by sedition and open war, but Cicero exposed and convicted him, and he was forced to flee the city. Yet Lentulus and Cethegus remained behind, with several others, to carry the plot forward; and blaming Catiline for cowardice and timid, small-scale thinking, they resolved to set the whole town on fire, overthrow the empire outright, rouse whole nations to revolt, and stir up foreign wars. But Cicero discovered the design — as I have written in his life — and laid the matter before the senate. Silanus, speaking first, gave his opinion that the conspirators should suffer the ultimate punishment, and every speaker after him agreed — until it came to Caesar. Caesar was an excellent speaker, and he regarded every upheaval and commotion in the state as raw material for his own purposes — better fed than put out. He rose and delivered a very merciful, very persuasive speech: the men ought not to be put to death without fair trial according to law, and he moved that they be held instead under guard in whatever Italian towns Cicero himself might choose. The house swung almost entirely to Caesar's side, dreading also the anger of the people; even Silanus backtracked and explained that he had never meant death, only imprisonment — since that was the utmost a Roman could suffer.

The whole senate was tipping toward the milder, more merciful opinion when Cato stood up. He tore at once, with passion and vehemence, into Silanus for changing his position — and then he turned and pointed the finger of suspicion at Caesar himself. Here was a man, he said, working to ruin the commonwealth with soft words and crowd-pleasing speeches; a man trying to frighten the senate when he himself ought to be afraid, and grateful if he walked out unpunished and unsuspected, since he so openly and boldly dared to protect the enemies of the state. For his own native country, glorious as it was and brought to the very edge of destruction, this man could find no compassion; yet he could be full of pity for men who should never have been born, and whose deaths would deliver the commonwealth from bloodshed and ruin. Of all Cato's speeches, it is said, this is the only one preserved — because Cicero, as consul, had stationed around the senate house a number of exceptionally quick writers whom he had taught to make small strokes standing for many words. Up to that time Rome had no shorthand writers, as we call them; this occasion, it is said, was the first real example of the art. Cato carried the day, and turned the house around once more, and it was decreed that the conspirators should be put to death.

I will not leave out the small things that show Cato's temper and add a stroke or two to the portrait of his mind. At the very height of this duel, it is reported, with the whole senate watching the two of them, a little note was brought in and handed to Caesar. Cato declared it suspicious — some piece of sedition in motion, he said — and demanded the letter be read aloud. Caesar handed the paper straight across to Cato. And Cato found himself holding a love letter from his own sister Servilia to Caesar, who had seduced her. He flung it back. "Keep it, you drunkard," he said — and went on with his speech. Indeed, Cato seems to have had poor luck in the women of his family. This sister got an ugly name for her intimacy with Caesar; and the other Servilia, also Cato's sister, conducted herself worse still: married to Lucullus, one of the greatest men in Rome, and having borne him a son, she was eventually divorced for infidelity. Worst of all, Cato's own wife Atilia was not free from the same fault; after she had given him two children, he was forced to divorce her for her misconduct. He then married Marcia, the daughter of Philippus, a woman of good reputation — and yet a woman about whom there has been endless talk; for the life of Cato, like a stage play, contains this one scene full of perplexity and doubtful meaning.

Here is how Thrasea tells it, on the authority of Munatius, Cato's friend and constant companion. Among the many who loved and admired Cato, some stood out more than others. One was Quintus Hortensius, a man of high reputation and proven character, who wanted not merely Cato's friendship and company but some tie of marriage that would unite their two houses into one. So he set about persuading Cato that his daughter Portia — already married to Bibulus, and the mother of his two children — might nevertheless be given over to him, like a fine plot of land, to bear fruit for him as well. "In the opinion of the world," he argued, "this may seem strange; but in nature it is honorable, and good for the public, that a woman in the prime of her youth should not sit idle and let the fruit of her womb go to waste — nor, on the other side, wear down and impoverish one man by bearing him more children than he needs. If worthy men shared their families in this way, virtue would increase and spread through their bloodlines, and the commonwealth would be knit and cemented together by their alliances." And if Bibulus was altogether unwilling to give up his wife, Hortensius would return her as soon as she had borne him a child, so that he would then be bound to both families. Cato answered that he loved Hortensius well and thoroughly approved of uniting their houses — but it was a strange thing to talk of marrying a daughter who was already given to another man. At that, Hortensius simply shifted ground, and did not hesitate to speak openly and ask for Cato's own wife; for Marcia was still young and fertile, and Cato had children enough. And it cannot be supposed that Hortensius did this because he imagined Cato indifferent to Marcia — she was, we are told, pregnant at the time. Cato, seeing how much in earnest he was, did not refuse; but he said that Philippus, Marcia's father, would have to be consulted as well. So Philippus was sent for and came; and finding the two men agreed, he gave his daughter Marcia to Hortensius in Cato's presence — with Cato himself assisting at the wedding. This happened at a later time; but since I was on the subject of the women, I thought it best to tell it now.

Lentulus and the rest of the conspirators were put to death. But Caesar, finding so much insinuated and charged against him in the senate, turned to the people, and began stirring up the most corrupt and rotten elements of the state to form a party behind him. Cato, alarmed at what might follow, persuaded the senate to win over the poor, unprovided-for multitude with a distribution of grain — an act of humanity and kindness that cost the state twelve hundred and fifty talents a year, and that unquestionably dissipated the immediate danger. But then Metellus, entering on his tribunate, began holding riotous assemblies, and had prepared a decree that Pompey the Great should be summoned into Italy at once, with all his forces, to preserve the city from the danger of Catiline's conspiracy. That was the fair-sounding pretext. The real design was to hand everything over to Pompey and give him absolute power. The senate was assembled, and Cato — who so often went straight at Metellus — did not do it this time. He urged his advice in the most reasonable and moderate tone he could manage; at last he descended even to entreaty, praising the house of Metellus for having always stood with the nobility. This only made Metellus more insolent. Concluding that Cato was yielding out of fear, he let himself run on into the most audacious threats, openly declaring that he would do whatever he pleased in spite of the senate. At that, Cato's expression changed, and his voice, and his language; and after many sharp exchanges he ended with the bold declaration that while he lived, Pompey would never enter the city armed. The senate, watching the two of them, concluded that neither man was entirely in his right and sober senses. Metellus' course seemed sheer rage and frenzy — an excess of malice dragging everything toward ruin and confusion — while Cato's virtue looked like a kind of ecstasy of contention in the cause of everything good and just.
