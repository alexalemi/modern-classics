Cicero

(Part 4 of 4)

Cicero was not at the battle of Pharsalia. His health had kept him away. And when it was over, and Pompey had fled, Cato — who still held a considerable army and a great fleet at Dyrrachium — offered Cicero the supreme command. The law was on his side: Cicero had been consul, and his rank came first. But Cicero refused the command, refused, in fact, to have anything more to do with the war at all — and the refusal nearly killed him. Young Pompey and his friends called him a traitor and drew their swords on him, and it was only Cato, throwing himself between them, who plainly saved Cicero's life and got him out of the camp.

He made his way to Brundusium and waited there a long while for Caesar, who was tied up with his affairs in Asia and Egypt. When word came that Caesar had landed at Tarentum and was coming overland toward Brundusium, Cicero set out to meet him — not without hope, but not without dread, either, at the prospect of testing the temper of a conquering enemy in front of a crowd of witnesses. As it turned out, he never had to say or do anything unworthy of himself. The moment Caesar saw him coming, well ahead of the rest of the company, he got down, went to meet him, greeted him, and walked on with him for a good mile or more, talking with him alone. From that day forward Caesar treated him with honor and respect. Even when Cicero published his tribute to Cato, Caesar's reply — praising Cato was hardly something Caesar could let stand — took care to compliment Cicero's own life and eloquence, comparing him to Pericles and Theramenes. Cicero's essay was called the Cato; Caesar's, the Anti-Cato.

There is a story from the same period. When Quintus Ligarius was prosecuted for having borne arms against Caesar, and Cicero took up his defense, Caesar remarked to his friends, "Why not hear a speech from Cicero once more? Ligarius is a guilty man and an enemy — there's no question about that." But once Cicero began to speak, he was extraordinarily moving, and as the speech went on — shifting register after register of feeling, with that charm of language no one else commanded — the color came and went in Caesar's face, and it was plain that every passion in his soul was being stirred in turn. At last, when the orator touched on the battle of Pharsalia, Caesar was so shaken that his body trembled and some of the papers he was holding dropped from his hands. He was simply overpowered — and he acquitted Ligarius.

After that, with the republic changed into a monarchy, Cicero withdrew from public life. He spent his leisure teaching philosophy to any young men who wanted it, and since these tended to be from the noblest and highest-ranking families, the connection made him once again a man of great influence in the city. His real project now was to compose and translate philosophical dialogues, and to find Roman words for the technical terms of Greek logic and physics. It was Cicero, they say, who first — or at least principally — gave Latin names to phantasia, syncatathesis, epokhe, catalepsis, atomon, ameres, kenon, and the rest of that vocabulary, working by metaphor or whatever other device would serve, until the ideas could actually be said, and understood, in Latin. For recreation he turned his facility to poetry; when he set himself to it he could produce five hundred verses in a night. He spent most of his time at his country house near Tusculum, and wrote to his friends that he was living the life of Laertes — Odysseus' old father, digging his garden far from power. Perhaps he was only joking, as was his habit; more likely it was ambition talking, an appetite for public life that made him restless under the new order of things. He rarely went into the city except to pay his court to Caesar. He was usually first among those voting Caesar new honors, and forever hunting for some fresh turn of praise for the man and his works. Take his remark about the statues of Pompey, which had been torn down and which Caesar ordered set up again: Cicero said that by this act of generosity Caesar had raised Pompey's statues — and fixed his own in place.

He had a plan, it is said, to write a history of his country, weaving into it much of the history of Greece and all the stories and legends of the past he had collected. But the plan was overtaken by troubles public and private, misfortunes for most of which he had himself to blame. First, he divorced his wife Terentia. She had neglected him during the war — sent him off short of even the necessities for his journey — and she was no kinder when he returned to Italy. She never joined him at Brundusium in all his long stay there, and when their young daughter made that whole long journey to him, Terentia provided her with neither a decent escort nor the money for her expenses. She had also stripped Cicero's house bare and emptied it, while loading him with many large debts. These were the most respectable reasons given for the divorce. But Terentia denied every one of them, and her husband promptly handed her the most unanswerable defense imaginable: not long afterward he married a young girl. For love of her beauty, Terentia said scornfully. For her money, said Tiro, Cicero's freedman — to clear his debts. The girl was very rich, and Cicero was trustee of her estate, having been left her guardian; and being many millions in debt, he was persuaded by his friends and relations to marry her, disparity of age notwithstanding, and use her fortune to satisfy his creditors. Antony, who brings up this marriage in his answer to the Philippics, jeers at him for putting away a wife he had grown old with — and adds some well-aimed sarcasm about Cicero's habits: domestic, idle, nothing of the soldier about him. Not long after the marriage, Cicero's daughter died in childbirth at the house of Lentulus, whom she had married after the death of her former husband, Piso. Philosophers came from every quarter to console Cicero, but his grief was beyond all bounds — so far beyond that he put away his new bride, because she seemed pleased at Tullia's death. So stood Cicero's domestic affairs.

In the plot now forming against Caesar he had no part at all, although he was among Brutus' closest confidants, and although no man alive was more aggrieved at the present state of things or longed harder for the old republic. The conspirators simply did not trust him: not his temperament, which lacked daring, and not his age — for even the boldest natures grow timid when they are old.

So the deed was done by Brutus and Cassius. And with Caesar's friends gathering, and the city facing the fear of a new civil war, Antony, as consul, convened the senate and made a short speech recommending concord. Cicero followed with a longer one, suited to the moment, and persuaded the senate to imitate the Athenians of old: to decree an amnesty for everything done in Caesar's case, and to assign provinces to Brutus and Cassius. Neither measure came to anything. For the common people were already tender-hearted on their own account, and when they saw Caesar's dead body carried through the forum — when Antony held up the dead man's clothes, soaked in blood and stabbed through in every part — they went mad with rage, hunted the marketplace for the murderers, and ran with firebrands to burn their houses down. The conspirators, forewarned, escaped that danger; but expecting more and worse to come, they left the city.

Antony was instantly riding high, and everyone feared he meant to make himself sole ruler — Cicero more than anyone. Antony, for his part, watching Cicero's influence revive in the state, and knowing how close he stood to Brutus, did not want him in the city. There was old bad blood between them anyway; their characters could hardly have been less alike. Cicero, afraid of what was coming, first thought of going out to Syria as a lieutenant under Dolabella. But Hirtius and Pansa, the consuls-elect who would succeed Antony — good men, and fond of Cicero — begged him not to abandon them, and undertook to put Antony down if only he would stay in Rome. Cicero, neither quite trusting them nor quite distrusting them, let Dolabella go without him and promised Hirtius he would spend the summer in Athens and return when the new consuls took office. So he set out. But his crossing was delayed, and in the meantime — as so often happens — fresh news came suddenly from Rome: Antony had undergone an astonishing change, was doing everything and managing all public business according to the will of the senate, and nothing was wanting but Cicero's presence to settle affairs completely. Cursing his own cowardice, he turned back to Rome. And his first hopes were not disappointed: such crowds poured out to meet him that the greetings and compliments at the gates and along his entrance into the city took up nearly an entire day.

The next day Antony convened the senate and summoned Cicero to it. Cicero did not come. He kept to his bed, claiming to be worn out from his journey; but the true reason seems to have been fear of a plot, based on a warning that had reached him on the road. Antony took the affront badly and sent soldiers with orders to bring Cicero or burn his house down. Many people interceded and pleaded, and Antony settled for taking sureties. After that, when the two men met, they passed each other in silence, each on his guard — until the young Caesar arrived from Apollonia, took up the elder Caesar's inheritance, and fell into a dispute with Antony over twenty-five million in money that Antony was withholding from the estate.

At this point Philippus, who had married the young man's mother, and Marcellus, who had married his sister, brought him to Cicero, and a bargain was struck: Cicero would lend the young man his eloquence and his political influence with the senate and the people, and the young man would give Cicero the protection of his wealth and his arms — for he already had a large following among the dead Caesar's veterans. But there was said to be a stronger reason still for Cicero's readiness. Back when Pompey and Caesar were both alive, Cicero had dreamed that someone was summoning the sons of the senators to the Capitol, because Jupiter intended to declare one of them the future ruler of Rome. The citizens came running and stood around the temple, and the boys sat waiting in their purple-bordered robes, in silence. Suddenly the doors opened, and the boys rose one by one and filed past the god, who reviewed each of them and, to their sorrow, dismissed them — until this one boy passed, and the god stretched out his right hand and said, "Romans, this young man, when he is lord of Rome, shall put an end to all your civil wars." They say the dream left Cicero with a distinct image of the boy's face, which he retained perfectly, though he did not know who he was. The next day, going down to the Campus Martius, he met the boys just leaving their exercises — and the first of them was the very face from his dream. Astonished, Cicero asked who his parents were. And he turned out to be this young Caesar: son of Octavius, a man of no great eminence, and of Atia, daughter of Caesar's sister — which was why Caesar, having no children of his own, had made the boy heir to his house and property by his will. From then on, they say, Cicero made a point of greeting the boy whenever they met, and the boy took the attention kindly. And by one more turn of fortune, he had been born in the very year of Cicero's consulship.

Those were the reasons people talked about. But what really bound Cicero to Octavian was, first, his hatred of Antony, and then his nature, which could never resist an honor. He meant to fold Octavian's power into his own political designs — and the young man courted him so far as to call him Father. Brutus was disgusted. In his letters to Atticus he said flatly that Cicero, by courting Octavian out of fear of Antony, had shown he was not working for his country's liberty at all — he was bidding for a kinder master. All the same, Brutus took up Cicero's son, then studying philosophy at Athens, gave him a command, and employed him in various services, with good results. Cicero's own power in the city now stood at its greatest height. He did whatever he pleased. He engineered Antony's defeat and expulsion, and sent out the two consuls, Hirtius and Pansa, with an army to finish him — and meanwhile persuaded the senate to vote Octavian the lictors and insignia of a praetor, as though the young man were the republic's defender. But then Antony was beaten in battle, both consuls died of it, and the two armies combined and went over to Octavian. The senate, now frightened of this young man and his extraordinary luck, tried with honors and gifts to draw the soldiers away from him and cut his power down — arguing that with Antony in flight, the state had no further need of armies.

That alarmed Octavian, and he privately sent friends to beg Cicero to procure the consulship for the two of them together. Cicero, they said, should run things however he pleased once they were in office; he would hold the real power and govern the young man, who wanted nothing but the name and the glory of it. Octavian himself admitted afterward that this was exactly what it had been: afraid of ruin and in danger of being abandoned, he had made timely use of Cicero's ambition, coaxing him to stand for the consulship with his help and support.

And here, more than at any other moment of his life, Cicero let himself be carried away — an old man taken in by the flattery of a boy. He canvassed for him; he brought the senate around for him; and his friends blamed him for it even at the time. Soon enough he saw what he had done: he had ruined himself and betrayed the liberty of his country. For the young man, once established in the consulship, said his farewells to Cicero — and reconciled himself with Antony and Lepidus, pooled his power with theirs, and divided up the government among the three of them like a piece of family property. Together they drew up a list of more than two hundred men who were to die. The bitterest fight in all their bargaining was over Cicero. Antony would agree to nothing unless Cicero was the first man killed; Lepidus sided with Antony; Octavian held out against them both. They met secretly, just the three of them, for three days, near the town of Bononia, at a spot not far from the camp, ringed round by a river. For the first two days, it is said, Octavian fought for Cicero's life. On the third day he yielded, and gave him up.

The exchange ran like this: Octavian abandoned Cicero; Lepidus abandoned his own brother Paulus; Antony abandoned Lucius Caesar, his uncle on his mother's side. So completely did rage and fury strip them of their humanity — proof, if any were needed, that no beast is more savage than man when his power is as great as his anger.

While all this was being arranged, Cicero was with his brother at his country house near Tusculum. When news of the proscriptions reached them, they decided to move to Astura, a seaside villa of Cicero's, and take ship from there for Macedonia, to join Brutus — for word had already come of his strength in that province. They traveled in separate litters, crushed with grief, stopping again and again along the road so the litters could be brought together and the two of them could grieve side by side. Quintus was the more despairing of the two, for he had begun to think about how little he had for the journey — he had brought, as he said, nothing from home — and even Cicero's provision was slender. Better, they decided, for Cicero to hurry on ahead while Quintus went home to gather what they needed. So it was resolved; and they embraced, weeping, and parted.

Within a few days Quintus was betrayed by his own servants to the men searching for him, and was killed, together with his young son. Cicero was carried on to Astura, found a vessel there, boarded at once, and sailed as far as Circeii with a favorable wind. The pilots wanted to put straight out to sea again; but Cicero — whether he feared the sea, or had not yet wholly given up his faith in Octavian — went ashore, and walked a dozen miles inland, as if making for Rome. Then his resolution failed him. He changed his mind, turned back to the coast, and passed the night there in fearful, tangled thoughts. At moments he resolved to slip secretly into Octavian's house and kill himself on the altar of the household gods, to fasten a divine vengeance upon him; but fear of torture drove him off that course too. And so, after churning through one confused and broken plan after another, he finally let his servants carry him by sea to Caieta, where he had a house — a pleasant retreat in the heat of summer, when the cool seasonal winds come in off the water.

There was a temple of Apollo at that place, a little way up from the shore. As Cicero's vessel rowed in toward land, a flight of crows rose from the temple with a great racket and made straight for the ship, settling along both ends of the yard, some croaking, some pecking at the ends of the ropes. Everyone took it for an evil omen. Cicero went ashore anyway, entered his house, and lay down on his bed to rest. But many of the crows settled at the window, cawing dismally, and one of them flew down onto the bed where Cicero lay wrapped up, and with its beak, little by little, dragged the covering away from his face. At the sight of that, his servants turned on themselves: were they going to stand there as spectators at their master's murder, doing nothing to defend him, while the very birds came to help him and keep watch over his undeserved suffering? Partly by pleading, partly by force, they got him up and carried him in his litter down toward the sea.

But by now the assassins had arrived with a squad of soldiers — Herennius, a centurion, and Popillius, a tribune whom Cicero had once defended when he was prosecuted for the murder of his own father. Finding the doors barred, they broke them open. Cicero was not there, and the people in the house said they did not know where he was; but a young man, it is stated — a freedman of Cicero's brother Quintus, educated by Cicero himself in liberal learning, Philologus by name — told the tribune that the litter was being carried down to the sea through the walks, where the trees grew close and shady. The tribune took a few men and ran round to the spot where it would come out. Cicero saw Herennius running through the walks, and he ordered his servants to set the litter down. Then, stroking his chin with his left hand, the old familiar gesture, he looked steadily at his murderers — covered in dust, his hair and beard untrimmed, his face worn down with his troubles — and most of the men standing there covered their own faces while Herennius killed him. He stretched his neck out of the litter to take the blow. He was in his sixty-fourth year. Herennius cut off his head, and, on Antony's orders, his hands too — the hands that had written the Philippics. For that was Cicero's own name for his speeches against Antony, and the Philippics they are called to this day.

When these remnants of Cicero were brought to Rome, Antony was holding an assembly for the election of officials. Hearing the news, and seeing the head and hands, he cried out, "Now let there be an end of our proscriptions." He ordered the head and hands fastened up over the Rostra, the speakers' platform — a sight that made the Roman people shudder, for they believed they were looking not at the face of Cicero but at the image of Antony's own soul. In all of this he did do one just thing: he handed Philologus over to Pomponia, the wife of Quintus. And she, once she had the man in her power, among other terrible punishments made him cut off his own flesh piece by piece, and roast it, and eat it — so, at least, some writers have related. But Tiro, Cicero's own freedman, never so much as mentions Philologus' treachery.

A long time afterward, I have been told, Octavian — Augustus by then — was visiting one of his daughter's sons, and found the boy with a book of Cicero's in his hands. The boy, frightened, tried to hide it under his gown. Augustus saw, took the book from him, and stood there reading a great part of it; then he handed it back and said, "An eloquent man, my child — an eloquent man, and a lover of his country." And as soon as he had finally defeated Antony, being then consul himself, he chose Cicero's son as his colleague in the office. It was under that consulship that the senate took down Antony's statues, canceled every other honor he had held, and decreed that from then on no man of his family should ever bear the name Marcus. Thus the final acts of Antony's punishment were handed, by the divine powers, to the family of Cicero.
