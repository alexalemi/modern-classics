Cato the Younger

(Part 1 of 6)

The Cato family first became famous through this Cato's great-grandfather — the elder Cato, whose virtue won him such standing and authority among the Romans that I have told his story in a life of its own.

Our Cato lost both his parents and was left an orphan, along with his brother Caepio and his sister Porcia. He also had a half-sister on his mother's side, Servilia. All of these children lived together and were raised in the house of Livius Drusus, their uncle on their mother's side, who at that time carried real weight in the government. He was a powerful speaker, a man of exceptional self-control, and in dignity second to no Roman alive.

They say that even as a small child, Cato showed — in the way he spoke, in the look on his face, even in his games — a nature that would not bend. Nothing swayed him; he was steady in everything. Once he set himself a purpose, he pushed through to the end with a determination far beyond his years. He was gruff and cold toward anyone who flattered him, and even more immovable toward anyone who threatened him. It was hard to make him laugh; his face rarely relaxed even into a smile. He was not quickly or easily provoked to anger — but once his anger was up, it was just as hard to bring it down again.

When he began his lessons, he proved a dull student, slow to take things in — but whatever he did take in, his memory held with a remarkable grip. And this, in fact, is generally how nature works. Quick, brilliant minds recall things readily, but those who learn with the most pain and difficulty remember best; everything new they learn is, so to speak, burned and branded into their minds. Cato's natural stubbornness, his resistance to being persuaded, probably also made him harder to teach. For to learn is to submit — to let something be done to you — and persuasion works fastest on those with the least strength to resist it. That is why the young are more easily persuaded than the old, and the sick more easily than the healthy. In short, wherever there is the least doubt and resistance standing in the way, a new impression settles in most easily. Still, they say Cato was very obedient to his tutor and would do whatever he was told — but he would also ask the reason, and want to know the why of everything. His teacher, to be fair, was a cultivated man, more inclined to explain than to beat his students. His name was Sarpedon.

While Cato was still a child, Rome's Italian allies were petitioning to be made full Roman citizens. One of their delegates, Pompaedius Silo, a brave soldier and a man of great reputation, was a friend of Drusus and stayed at his house for several days. In that time he became familiar with the children. "Well," he said to them, "will you ask your uncle to help us in our cause?" Caepio smiled and agreed, but Cato said nothing at all — he just stared at the strangers, hard and fierce. So Pompaedius pressed him: "And you, young sir — what do you say? Won't you plead with your uncle for us, like your brother?" Cato still gave no answer, and his silence and his expression seemed to refuse the whole petition. So Pompaedius snatched him up and held him out the window, as if he meant to drop him, and told him to consent or down he would go. He put on a harsher voice, dangled the boy's body out over the drop, and shook him — more than once. Cato endured all of it, unmoved and unafraid, until at last Pompaedius set him down and murmured to his friend, "What a blessing for Italy that he is only a child. If he were a man, I don't believe we would win a single vote among the people."

Another time, a relative celebrating his birthday invited Cato and some other children to dinner. The children went off to play in another part of the house, older and younger together, and their game was to act out trials before the judges — accusing one another and hauling the convicted off to prison. In the middle of this, a very beautiful young boy was bound and dragged off to "prison" by an older one, and he cried out to Cato. The moment Cato saw what was happening, he ran to the door, shoved aside the boys standing guard, pulled the child out, and marched home in a fury, with several of his companions trailing after him.

In time Cato grew so admired among the young that when Sulla decided to stage the sacred equestrian game called Troy — a ceremonial cavalry exercise for well-born boys — he gathered the youth of good families and appointed two leaders over them. One of the two the boys accepted for his mother's sake, since he was the son of Metella, Sulla's wife. But the other — Sextus, Pompey's nephew — they flatly refused. They would not follow him or drill under him. When Sulla asked whom they wanted instead, they all shouted, "Cato!" — and Sextus willingly gave up the honor to him, as the worthier of the two.

Sulla, an old friend of their family, would send for Cato and his brother from time to time, to see them and talk with them — a favor he showed to very few once he had seized his great power. Sarpedon, alert to the advantage of this — for his students' honor and for their safety — often brought Cato to pay his respects at Sulla's house. And that house, in those days, crowded with people being dragged in and tortured, looked like nothing so much as a place of execution. Cato was then in his fourteenth year. Watching the heads of reportedly distinguished men being carried in, and noticing the stifled groans of those standing by, he asked his tutor, "Why does nobody kill this man?" "Because, child," said Sarpedon, "they fear him even more than they hate him." "Then why," Cato answered, "didn't you give me a sword, so that I could stab him and free my country from this slavery?" When Sarpedon heard that — and saw the boy's face swelling with anger and resolve — he watched him closely from then on, afraid he might attempt something desperate.

While Cato was still very young, some people asked him whom he loved best. "My brother," he answered. And whom next? "My brother," again. And the third time the same, until they gave up asking. As he grew older, this love for his brother only grew stronger. By the time he was about twenty, he never ate supper, never left town, never went into the forum without Caepio. But when his brother took to using expensive oils and perfumes, Cato refused them; in all his habits he was strict and austere. So when Caepio was praised for his moderation and self-restraint, he would admit that yes, he might count as moderate — compared with most men. "But," he said, "when I compare myself with Cato, I find I'm hardly any different from Sippius" — naming a man notorious at the time for his soft and luxurious living.

When Cato was made a priest of Apollo, he moved to a house of his own, took his share of the family inheritance — a hundred and twenty talents — and began to live even more simply than before. He became close to Antipater of Tyre, the Stoic philosopher, and devoted himself above all to the study of ethics and politics. He pursued every virtue as if driven by a kind of inspiration, but the one that seized his devotion most of all was justice — the rigid, unbending kind, the kind that cannot be worked on by favors or by pity. He also trained himself in the art of public speaking and debate, reasoning that political philosophy, like a great city, ought to keep a fighting force on hand for its defense. But he would never practice his exercises in front of company, and no one ever heard him declaim. When someone told him that people blamed him for his silence, he replied, "As long as they don't blame my life. I will begin to speak when I have something to say that is better said than left unsaid."

The great Porcian Hall, as it was called, had been built and dedicated to public use by the old Cato during his term as aedile. The tribunes of the people conducted their business there, and because one of the pillars seemed to be in the way of their seats, they debated whether to move it elsewhere or remove it altogether. This was the occasion that first drew Cato — very much against his will — into the forum. He opposed the tribunes' demand, and in doing so gave a first display of both his courage and his power as a speaker, which won him great admiration. There was nothing youthful or polished about his speech; it was direct, packed with substance, and rough. And yet there was a certain grace in his rough statements that held people's attention, and the speaker's character, showing through everything he said, gave his severe language something that people found naturally pleasing and interesting. His voice was full and carrying, strong enough to reach that whole crowd, and its stamina was simply inexhaustible — he could often speak an entire day and never stop.

Once he had won this case, he went back to his studies and his quiet life. He hardened his body with labor and strenuous exercise. He trained himself to go bareheaded in the hottest weather and the coldest, and to travel on foot in every season. When he journeyed with friends who rode on horseback while he walked, he would drop back and move up along the line, joining first one and then another to talk with them on the road. In sickness, his patience in enduring an illness — and the strict self-denial he used to cure it — were remarkable. When he had a fever, he would stay alone and let no one see him until he began to recover and knew the attack had passed. At supper, when the dice were thrown for choice of portions and he lost, and the company offered him his pick anyway, he declined — he would not, as he put it, argue with the ruling of Venus. At first he was in the habit of drinking a single cup after supper and then leaving; but over time he came to drink more, so much so that he would often sit over the wine until morning. His friends explained this by saying that public business and affairs of state took up his whole day, and that Cato, hungry for knowledge, liked to spend his nights over wine in the company of philosophers. So when a certain Memmius remarked in public that Cato spent whole nights drinking, Cicero shot back, "You should add that he spends whole days gambling."

Cato, in general, considered the customs and manners of his era so corrupt, and so badly in need of reform, that he thought it necessary to go against the ordinary way of the world in many things — deliberately. Seeing that the lightest, brightest purple was then the height of fashion, he always wore the shade nearest to black. He would often go out after his morning meal with no shoes and no tunic. He was not fishing for attention with these oddities. He was training himself to be ashamed only of what truly deserves shame, and to shrug off every other kind of disgrace.

When the estate of a cousin — another Cato — fell to him, worth a hundred talents, he turned all of it into ready cash and kept it on hand for any friend who found himself in need, lending it out without interest. For some of them, he even allowed his own land and slaves to be pledged to the public treasury as security.

When he judged himself old enough to marry — having never yet been with any woman — he was engaged to Lepida. She had previously been engaged to Metellus Scipio, but Scipio had backed out, the contract had been dissolved, and she was free. Yet afterward Scipio changed his mind, did everything he could to win her back before Cato's marriage could take place — and succeeded. Cato was furious. At first he resolved to take the matter to court, but his friends talked him out of it. Still, so hot was his young blood that he wrote a stream of iambic verses against Scipio, in the biting, sarcastic style of Archilochus — though without that poet's license and vulgarity. After this he married Atilia, the daughter of Soranus. She was the first woman he ever knew — the first, but not the only one. In that he was less fortunate than Laelius, the friend of Scipio Africanus, who through the whole course of a very long life knew only the one woman he wed in his first and only marriage.

In the slave war — the one named for Spartacus, its ringleader — Gellius held the command, and Cato went along as a volunteer for the sake of his brother Caepio, who was serving as a tribune in the army. The general's incompetence gave Cato no real opening to show his zeal or prove his courage. But amid all the corruption and disorder of that army, he showed such devotion to discipline, such bravery when the moment called for it, and such steady good judgment in everything, that it seemed clear he was in no way inferior to the old Cato. Gellius offered him substantial rewards and would have voted him the first honors; Cato refused them all, saying he had done nothing to deserve them. Which made people think him a strange, eccentric character.

There was, moreover, a law that candidates standing for office could not use prompters in their campaigns — hired men who whispered them the names of the citizens they greeted. When Cato ran for military tribune, he was the only candidate who obeyed it. He took great pains to learn, from his own memory, how to greet the voters he had to address and call each of them by name. Yet even the people who praised him for this could not do it without a touch of envy and resentment — for the more they reflected on how fine the thing was, the more it stung them how hard it would be to do the same.

Elected military tribune, he was sent to Macedon to join Rubrius, the general there. The story goes that his wife was distressed and in tears at his leaving, and Munatius, one of Cato's friends, said to her, "Don't worry, Atilia — I promise to watch over him for you." "By all means," said Cato. And after they had traveled one day's journey together, he turned to Munatius as soon as they had finished supper. "Now," he said, "so that you're sure to keep your promise to Atilia, you must not leave me day or night." From then on he ordered two beds made up in his own chamber so that Munatius would sleep there — and so it continued, Cato making a running joke of it: it was he who kept watch over Munatius. He traveled with fifteen slaves, two freedmen, and four friends. They rode on horseback; Cato always went on foot — yet he kept pace beside them, talking with each in turn as they went.

When he reached the army, which consisted of several legions, the general gave him command of one. Cato considered it a small thing, hardly worthy of a commander, to put his own personal bravery on display; what he resolved to do was make his soldiers, as far as he could, like himself. He did not soften the fear his office carried, but he joined reason to his authority. He persuaded and instructed every man individually, and handed out rewards and punishments strictly by what each deserved. In the end his men were so well disciplined that it was hard to say whether they were more peaceable or more warlike, more brave or more just. They were formidable to their enemies and courteous to their allies, afraid to do wrong and eager to earn honor. And Cato himself won, in the fullest measure, exactly what he had least set out to seek — glory and a great name. He was highly esteemed by everyone and completely beloved by his soldiers. Whatever he ordered done, he took part in doing himself. In his dress, his food, and his manner of traveling, he was more like a common soldier than an officer — but in character, in high purpose, and in wisdom, he towered over everyone who carried the names and titles of commander. And so, without knowing it, he made himself the object of universal affection. For a true love of virtue grows, in all of us, out of love and respect for the person who teaches it. People who praise good men without loving them may honor their reputation, but they do not truly admire their virtue — and they will never imitate it.

There lived at that time in Pergamus a man named Athenodorus, surnamed Cordylio, of high repute for his mastery of Stoic philosophy. He was now old, and he had always firmly refused the friendship and acquaintance of princes and great men. Cato understood this. Figuring he would never win the man over by messages or letters, and being allowed by law two months' leave from the army, he resolved to sail to Asia and see him in person, trusting that his own qualities would keep the trip from being wasted. He talked with Athenodorus, succeeded in arguing him out of his old resolutions, and brought him back to camp — as delighted and as proud of this conquest as if he had pulled off some heroic feat greater than anything done by Pompey or Lucullus, who at that very moment were subduing whole nations and kingdoms with their armies.

While Cato was still in the service, his brother, traveling toward Asia, fell sick at Aenus in Thrace. Letters with the news were sent to Cato at once. The sea was running very rough and no decent-sized ship could be found, so Cato boarded a small trading vessel with only two friends and three servants and set sail from Thessalonica. He very nearly drowned on the crossing — and arrived at Aenus just as Caepio died. On this occasion, people felt, he showed himself more a devoted brother than a philosopher: not only in the extravagance of his grief, weeping and embracing the dead body, but in the enormous cost of the funeral — the huge quantity of rich perfumes and costly garments burned with the corpse, and the monument of Thasian marble he raised, at a cost of eight talents, in the public square of Aenus. Some people made a point of carping at all this as inconsistent with his usual calm restraint. What they failed to see was that for all his steel — firm and unbending against pleasure, fear, and shameless begging — Cato was full of natural tenderness and brotherly affection. Various cities and princes of the region sent him many gifts to honor his brother's funeral. He took none of their money; he accepted only the perfumes and ornaments, and paid for those. And afterward, when the inheritance was divided between him and Caepio's daughter, he did not ask for any share of the funeral expenses to be paid out of it. Despite all this, it has been claimed that he had his brother's ashes passed through a sieve, to sift out the gold that had melted in the fire with the body. But the man who wrote that, it seems, assumed that his pen, like his sword, stood above all question and criticism.

When Cato's term of service ended, he left the army carrying not just the prayers and praises of the soldiers, but their tears and embraces. They spread their cloaks under his feet and kissed his hand as he passed — an honor the Romans of that day rarely paid even to their generals and commanders-in-chief. Having left the army, he resolved that before he went home and threw himself into public life, he would travel through Asia and study the customs, the character, and the strength of every province. He was also reluctant to refuse the kindness of Deiotarus, king of Galatia, who had been a close friend of his father's and was very eager for a visit. Cato's travel arrangements ran like this. Early in the morning he would send his baker and his cook ahead to the town where he planned to spend the next night. They would enter quietly and without fuss, and if Cato had no friend or family acquaintance in the place, they arranged for him at an inn and bothered no one. If there was no inn — then, and only then — they went to the magistrates, asked for help finding lodgings, and accepted without complaint whatever they were given. Because his servants approached the magistrates without bluster or threats, they were often not believed, or simply ignored, so that Cato frequently arrived to find nothing prepared for him. And it was worse when he appeared in person; he counted for even less. Seeing him sit down on his baggage without a word, the officials wrote him off at once as a nobody who wouldn't dare demand anything. On such occasions he would sometimes call them over and say, "You foolish people — drop this inhospitality. Not all your visitors will be Catos. Use courtesy to blunt the sharp edge of power. There are plenty of men who want nothing but an excuse to take from you by force what you give so grudgingly."

While traveling this way he had a comic misadventure in Syria. As he approached Antioch, he saw a great crowd outside the gates, lined up in order on both sides of the road — young men in long cloaks here, children neatly dressed there, and others in garlands and white robes, the priests and the magistrates. Cato, assuming all this could only be a ceremony of welcome in his honor, grew angry with the servants he had sent ahead for allowing it. He made his friends dismount, and walked in with them on foot. But as soon as he neared the gate, an elderly man who seemed to be the master of ceremonies, wand and garland in hand, came up to Cato and — without so much as a greeting — asked where he had left Demetrius, and how soon he would arrive. Demetrius was Pompey's servant; and since at that moment the whole world, so to speak, had its eyes fixed on Pompey, even this man was treated with high honor because of his influence with his master. Cato's friends collapsed into such laughter that they couldn't compose themselves as they walked through the crowd. Cato himself, ashamed and mortified, said only, "Unfortunate city!" — and not another word. Afterward, though, it always made him laugh, whenever he told the story or was reminded of it.

Pompey himself soon made the people of Antioch ashamed of their ignorance and foolishness in slighting Cato that way. For when Cato's journey brought him to Ephesus, he went to pay his respects to Pompey — the older man, loaded with honors, and then the general of a great army. Yet Pompey would not receive him sitting down. The moment he saw him he rose, went to meet him as the more distinguished of the two, gave him his hand, and embraced him with a great show of warmth. He praised Cato's virtue lavishly to his face at the reception, and even more lavishly after he had gone. And so at once everyone began to show Cato open respect — discovering, in the very habits they had despised before, an admirable mildness of temper and greatness of spirit. It was clear enough, though, that Pompey's own courtesy came from a man who respected Cato rather than loved him. The general impression was that Pompey admired him while he was present and was not sorry when he left. When other young men came to visit, Pompey usually pressed and begged them to stay on with him. Cato he did not invite to stay at all — as if his own power shrank in the man's presence, he was quite willing to let him go. And yet, of all those departing for Rome, Cato was the only one to whom Pompey entrusted his children and his wife — who was, it is true, a relation of Cato's.
