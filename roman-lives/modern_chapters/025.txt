Antony

(Part 5 of 7)

All that, however, came later. For the moment, Antony was marching his army at desperate speed through the dead of winter, through one snowstorm after another, and the march cost him eight thousand men. With his numbers badly thinned he came down to the seacoast, to a place called the White Village, between Sidon and Berytus, and there he waited for Cleopatra to arrive. She was slow in coming, and the delay ate at him. He tried to shorten the time with wine and heavy drinking, but even so he could not sit through a meal; again and again he would jump up from the table and run down to look for her ship. At last she came into port, bringing clothing and money for the soldiers — though some say she brought only the clothes, and that Antony handed out his own money in her name.

About this time a quarrel broke out between the king of Media and Phraates of Parthia. It began, the story goes, over the division of the loot taken from the Romans, and it left the Median king in real fear of losing his throne. So he sent ambassadors to Antony offering to join him in a war against Phraates. This filled Antony with hope. The one thing he had lacked against the Parthians — the thing whose absence, he believed, had cost him the last campaign — was horsemen and archers; and here was someone begging him, as a favor, to accept exactly that. He began at once to prepare a return to Armenia, planning to meet the Medes on the river Araxes and open the war afresh.

But in Rome, Octavia wanted to see her husband, and she asked Octavian's leave to go to him. He gave it — not so much, most writers say, to please his sister, as to gain a respectable pretext for war when she was received with dishonor. She had no sooner reached Athens than letters came from Antony telling her of his new expedition and instructing her to wait for him there. She was hurt, and she was not blind to the real reason for this treatment; yet she wrote back asking only where he would like her to send the things she had brought for him. And she had brought a great deal: clothing for his soldiers, baggage animals, money, presents for his friends and officers, and two thousand picked men, splendidly armed, to serve as his personal guard. This message was carried to Antony by one of his friends, a man named Niger, who added to it the praises she so thoroughly deserved.

Cleopatra could feel her rival closing in, and she was afraid. Octavia had a noble character and the power of her brother behind her; if she were ever allowed to add the quiet charm of daily company and affectionate married life to those advantages, she would become irresistible, and hold Antony absolutely and forever. So Cleopatra pretended to be dying of love for him. She starved herself thin on a meager diet. When he entered the room, she would fix her eyes on him in rapture; when he left, she would seem to sink and half faint away. She took great care that he should catch her in tears — and then, the moment he noticed, she would hastily dry them and turn aside, as if the one thing she wished was that he should know nothing of it. All this theater was played out while he was preparing for Media, and Cleopatra's creatures worked busily to help it along. They scolded Antony for his cold, hard heart — letting a woman perish whose whole soul hung on him and him alone. Octavia was his wife, yes; but she had been married to him because it suited her brother's political business, and she had the honor of the title. Cleopatra, sovereign queen of many nations, had been content to be called his mistress; she did not shrink from the name or think it beneath her, so long as she could see him, live with him, have him. Take that away, and she would not survive the loss. In the end they so melted and unmanned him that, genuinely believing she would die if he left her, he put off the war and went back to Alexandria, postponing the Median expedition until the following summer — this although word had come that Parthia was in chaos with internal feuds. Some time later he did go into that country, and sealed an alliance with the Median king by betrothing one of his sons by Cleopatra to the king's daughter, still a very young girl. Then he returned, his mind now occupied with the coming civil war.

When Octavia came back from Athens, Octavian considered that she had been insultingly treated, and ordered her to live in a house of her own. She refused to leave her husband's house. More than that, she begged her brother — unless he had already resolved on war with Antony for other reasons — to take no notice of her injuries. It would be an unbearable thing, she said, to have it told of the two greatest commanders in the world that they had plunged the Roman people into civil war, the one out of passion for a woman, the other out of resentment about one. And her conduct proved she meant every word. She stayed in Antony's house as though he were living in it, and she took the noblest, most generous care not only of her own children by him but of his children by Fulvia as well. She received every friend of Antony's who came to Rome seeking office or on any other business, and did all she could to advance their requests with Octavian. Yet this honorable behavior did Antony damage she never intended: he was hated for wronging such a woman.

And the division of kingdoms he staged among his sons at Alexandria was hated just as much; it looked like a piece of theatrical arrogance and contempt for his own country. He assembled the people in the exercise ground and had two golden thrones set up on a platform of silver, one for himself and one for Cleopatra, with lower thrones at their feet for the children. Then he proclaimed Cleopatra queen of Egypt, Cyprus, Libya, and Coele-Syria, and joint ruler with her Caesarion, the reputed son of the first Caesar, who had left Cleopatra with child. His own sons by Cleopatra were to carry the title Kings of Kings. To Alexander he gave Armenia and Media — with Parthia thrown in, as soon as it should be conquered. To Ptolemy he gave Phoenicia, Syria, and Cilicia. Alexander was presented to the people in Median dress, with the tiara and the tall upright peak; Ptolemy in boots, mantle, and a Macedonian cap circled with the diadem — for this was the costume of the successors of Alexander the Great, as the other was of the Medes and Armenians. When the boys had saluted their parents, one was received by a bodyguard of Macedonians, the other by a guard of Armenians. Cleopatra, then and whenever she appeared in public, wore the sacred robes of the goddess Isis, and gave audience to the people under the name of the New Isis.

Octavian reported all this to the senate, and complained of it often before the people, stirring the public mind against Antony. Antony sent accusations back in kind. His principal charges were these: first, that Octavian had taken Sicily from Sextus Pompey and never given him his share of it; second, that he had kept the ships Antony lent him for that war; third, that after deposing their colleague Lepidus, he had taken for himself the army, the provinces, and the revenues that had been assigned to him; and last, that he had parceled out almost all of Italy among his own soldiers and left nothing for Antony's. Octavian's answer ran as follows: he had removed Lepidus from power because of Lepidus' own misconduct; whatever he had won in war he would divide with Antony the moment Antony gave him a share of Armenia; and as for Italy, Antony's soldiers had no claim to any of it — they had Media and Parthia, those splendid additions their brave service under their general had made to the Roman empire.

Antony was in Armenia when this answer reached him. He immediately sent Canidius toward the sea with sixteen legions, while he himself went with Cleopatra to Ephesus, where ships were streaming in from every quarter to form the navy — eight hundred vessels counting the transports, of which Cleopatra furnished two hundred, along with twenty thousand talents and provisions for the whole army for the duration of the war. Antony, on the advice of Domitius Ahenobarbus and some others, told Cleopatra to return to Egypt and await the outcome there. But she dreaded any new reconciliation that Octavia might manage, so she paid Canidius a large sum to plead her case with Antony. It was not just, Canidius argued, that a woman bearing so great a share of the war's cost should be robbed of her share of its glory; nor was it good policy to offend the Egyptians, who made up so large a part of the naval forces; nor, for that matter, could he see how Cleopatra was inferior in judgment to any one of the kings serving in the campaign — she had governed a great kingdom by herself for years, and she had lived long with Antony and learned the handling of great affairs. These arguments won — for such was the will of the fate that was steering everything into Octavian's hands. When all the forces had assembled, they sailed together to Samos and gave themselves over to festivities. Every king, prince, and governor, every nation and city between Syria, the Maeotid Lake, Armenia, and Illyria had been ordered to send or deliver munitions of war; and at the same time every stage performer was summoned to appear at Samos. So while nearly the whole world around them groaned and lamented, this one island echoed for days with piping and harping, with theaters filling and choruses competing. Every city sent an ox for the sacrifice, and the kings in Antony's train outdid one another in magnificent feasts and presents — until people began to ask themselves: what will they possibly do to celebrate the victory, if this is what they spend on the opening of the war?

When it was over, he gave the town of Priene to his performers to live in, and sailed for Athens, where fresh sports and play-acting swallowed his time. Cleopatra, jealous of the honors Octavia had received in that city — for the Athenians loved Octavia dearly — set out to win the people with every sort of attention. The Athenians voted her public honors and sent a deputation of citizens to her house to deliver them, and among the deputies went Antony himself, as a citizen of Athens; it was he who made the speech. And he sent orders to Rome to have Octavia put out of his house. She left it, we are told, with all of his children in her care except the eldest son by Fulvia, who was with his father — weeping, and grieving that she would be looked upon as one of the causes of the war. But the Romans pitied Antony more than they pitied her, and most of all those who had seen Cleopatra with their own eyes and could report that she had no advantage over Octavia in either youth or beauty.

The speed and scale of Antony's preparations alarmed Octavian, who feared he might be forced into the decisive battle that very summer. He was short of many essentials, and the taxes were bitterly resented: free citizens were being called on to pay a quarter of their income, freed slaves an eighth of their property, and there were loud outcries against him and disturbances all over Italy. This is reckoned among the greatest of Antony's blunders — that he did not press the war then. He gave Octavian time to finish his preparations, and gave the unrest time to burn itself out. For while the money was being demanded of them, people were mutinous and violent; once they had paid it, they held their peace.

Then Titius and Plancus, men of consular rank and friends of Antony, defected to Octavian. They had been badly treated by Cleopatra, having resisted harder than anyone her insistence on being present in the war; and they carried with them knowledge of the contents of Antony's will. The will had been deposited with the vestal virgins, who refused to hand it over — if Octavian wanted it, they sent word, he could come and take it himself. So he did. First he read it through alone, marking the passages that would serve his purpose best; then he summoned the senate and read those passages aloud. Many of the senators were scandalized by the whole proceeding: it seemed neither reasonable nor fair to call a living man to account for what was not to happen until after his death. Octavian bore down hardest on the clause about Antony's burial, for Antony had directed that even if he died in the city of Rome, his body, after being carried in state through the forum, should be sent to Cleopatra at Alexandria. Calvisius, one of Octavian's dependents, piled on further charges connected with Cleopatra: that Antony had given her the library of Pergamus with its two hundred thousand volumes; that at a great banquet, in front of a crowd of guests, he had stood up and rubbed her feet, to settle some wager or promise; that he had allowed the Ephesians to salute her as their queen; that many times, while seated on the tribunal giving public audience to kings and princes, he had received love notes from her written on tablets of onyx and crystal, and read them then and there; and that once, when Furnius — a man of great authority and one of the finest speakers in Rome — was pleading a case before him, Cleopatra happened to pass by in her chair, and Antony leaped up, abandoned the hearing in the middle of the cause, and walked at her side all the way home.

Calvisius, it must be said, was generally suspected of inventing most of these stories. Antony's friends went up and down the city pleading his cause, and sent one of their number, Geminius, to warn him not to sit still while he was stripped of his authority by vote and declared a public enemy of Rome. But Geminius had no sooner landed in Greece than he was marked down as one of Octavia's spies. At dinner he was made the standing butt of the jokes and seated in the least honorable places. He bore all of it well, only watching for a chance to speak with Antony. Finally, at supper, he was told to state the business he had come about. The rest of it, he answered, would keep for a soberer hour — but there was one thing he had to say, drunk or fasting: everything would go well if Cleopatra would return to Egypt. Antony flared up at that. Cleopatra cut in: "You have done well, Geminius, to tell your secret without being put to the rack." A few days later Geminius found his moment and slipped away to Rome. Cleopatra's flatterers drove off many more of Antony's friends with their insolence, among them Marcus Silanus and Dellius the historian. Dellius says he was in fear for his very life, and that Glaucus the physician tipped him off that Cleopatra was planning to have him killed. He had offended her by remarking that Antony's friends were served sour wine, while back in Rome Sarmentus — Octavian's little page boy, his delicia, as the Romans call such pets — was drinking Falernian.

As soon as Octavian's preparations were complete, he had a decree passed declaring war on Cleopatra, and stripping Antony of the authority he had surrendered to a woman to exercise in his place. Octavian added that Antony had been drugged out of his own senses, and that the generals Rome would actually be fighting were Mardion the eunuch, Pothinus, Iras — Cleopatra's hairdressing girl — and Charmion: for these, he said, were Antony's chief ministers of state.

These are the prodigies said to have announced the war. Pisaurum, a colony Antony had settled on the Adriatic, was swallowed by an earthquake. At Alba, sweat ran from one of the marble statues of Antony for days on end, and though it was wiped away again and again, it did not stop. While he was in the city of Patrae, the temple of Hercules there was struck by lightning; and at Athens, a violent wind tore the figure of Bacchus out of the sculptured Battle of the Giants and laid it flat on the theater below. Both omens struck home, for Antony claimed a connection with both gods: he professed descent from Hercules, and his imitation of Bacchus in his manner of living had earned him the name of the Young Bacchus. The same whirlwind at Athens singled out, from among many statues left standing, the colossal figures of Eumenes and Attalus — the ones inscribed with Antony's name — and brought them down. And aboard Cleopatra's flagship, which was called the Antonias, came the most ominous sign of all. Swallows had built their nest in the stern of the galley; then other swallows came, drove the first ones out, and destroyed their nests.

When the armaments gathered for the war, Antony had no fewer than five hundred warships, among them many huge galleys of eight and ten banks of oars, decked out as richly as if they were meant for a triumph. He had a hundred thousand infantry and twelve thousand horse. He had vassal kings in attendance: Bocchus of Libya, Tarcondemus of Upper Cilicia, Archelaus of Cappadocia, Philadelphus of Paphlagonia, Mithridates of Commagene, and Sadalas of Thrace — all of these with him in person. Out of Pontus, Polemon sent him substantial forces, as did Malchus from Arabia, Herod the Jew, and Amyntas, king of Lycaonia and Galatia; the Median king, too, sent troops. Octavian had two hundred and fifty war galleys, eighty thousand foot, and cavalry roughly equal to the enemy's. Antony's dominion stretched from the Euphrates and Armenia to the Ionian Sea and the Illyrians; Octavian's from Illyria to the western ocean, and from the ocean back along the Tuscan and Sicilian seas. Of Africa, Octavian held all the coast facing Italy, Gaul, and Spain as far as the Pillars of Hercules; Antony held the provinces from Cyrene to Ethiopia.

But by now he was so entirely an appendage of Cleopatra that although he was far superior on land, he wanted the victory won at sea — to please her; and this although he could not help seeing that for sheer lack of sailors, his captains were sweeping up every kind of man that miserable Greece could supply — travelers, mule drivers, harvest hands, half-grown boys — and even so the ships could not fill their crews, and most of them put to sea undermanned and wretchedly rowed. Octavian's ships were another matter: built not for size or show but for service — no towering parade galleys, but light, fast, and fully manned. He kept his headquarters at Tarentum and Brundisium, and from there he sent Antony a message: stop wasting time, and come. He would provide safe anchorages and harbors for Antony's fleet, and for his army he would pull back and leave him as much landing ground in Italy, inland from the sea, as a horse could cover in a single gallop. Antony answered in equally grand style: he challenged Octavian to single combat — though Antony was much the older man — and when that was refused, proposed that they fight it out on the fields of Pharsalia, where Caesar and Pompey had fought before. But while Antony lay with his fleet off Actium — where the city of Nicopolis now stands — Octavian saw his moment, crossed the Ionian Sea, and seized a place in Epirus called the Ladle. Antony's people were badly rattled, for their land forces were still far behind. "How terrifying," said Cleopatra, mocking them. "We may well be frightened, now that Octavian has got hold of the Ladle!"

At dawn the next day, Antony saw the enemy fleet standing in toward him, and feared his ships would be captured outright for want of soldiers to fight from their decks. So he armed the rowers and paraded them on deck as if ready for battle; the oars were shipped and poised as though waiting for the stroke, and the vessels were drawn up facing the enemy at the mouth of the channel of Actium, looking fully crewed and ready to fight. Octavian was taken in by the stratagem, and withdrew. Antony was also credited with a clever piece of work in cutting the enemy off from their drinking water by a system of trenches and forts — fresh water being scarce in that country, and bad. And his conduct toward Domitius was generous, much against Cleopatra's will. When Domitius, already burning with fever, escaped in a small boat and went over to Octavian, Antony — though the desertion stung him deeply — sent his entire baggage after him, along with his friends and his servants. And Domitius, as though he wished to give the world one testimony of his repentance now that his treachery lay exposed, died soon after. Among the kings, Amyntas and Deiotarus also went over to Octavian. And the fleet proved so unlucky in every venture, and so unready at every call, that Antony was driven back, in spite of everything, to putting his faith in the army. Even Canidius, who commanded the legions, changed his mind when he saw how things stood: he now advised that Cleopatra be sent home, and that they fall back into Thrace or Macedonia and settle the quarrel in a land battle. Dicomes, king of the Getae, was promising to join them with a great army; there would be no disgrace in yielding the sea to Octavian, whose crews had trained through the long Sicilian war — but it would be simply ridiculous for Antony, the most experienced land commander alive, to throw away the strength of his numerous, disciplined infantry by scattering the men in parcels among the ships. But Cleopatra carried the day: a sea fight would decide everything. She already had her eye on flight, and was arranging all her affairs not to help win the battle, but to get away most safely once the losing of it had begun.

Between the camp and the station of the ships ran two long walls, and Antony used to walk the passage between them without a thought of danger. A servant pointed out to Octavian how easy it would be to snatch him there, and an ambush was laid. It sprang up a moment too soon, and seized the man walking just ahead of him; Antony himself barely got away at a run.

Once the decision was taken to fight at sea, they burned all the Egyptian ships except sixty; the best and largest of these, from ten banks of oars down to three, Antony manned with twenty thousand heavy infantry and two thousand archers. It was then, the story goes, that a foot captain — a man who had fought through countless battles under Antony, his body seamed all over with wounds — cried out as Antony passed: "General! What have our wounds and our swords done to displease you, that you put your trust in rotten timbers? Let the Egyptians and Phoenicians do their fighting on the water. Give us land — where we know how to stand our ground, and die on it, or win." Antony answered him nothing. He gave the man a look and a motion of the hand, as if to say, be of good courage, and passed on. But his own hopes, it would seem, were no longer very sure; for when the ships' masters proposed leaving the sails ashore, he ordered them carried aboard. "For we must not," he said, "let one enemy escape."
