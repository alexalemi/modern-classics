Brutus

(Part 3 of 5)

The city had now split into two armed factions, one gathering around Octavian and the other around Antony, while the soldiers sold themselves practically at public auction, going over to whichever bidder paid the most. Brutus despaired of anything good coming out of such a scramble. He resolved to leave Italy, traveled overland through Lucania, and came down to Elea on the coast.

From there it was decided that Portia should return to Rome. She was overwhelmed with grief at parting from Brutus, and did everything she could to hide it — but for all her self-command, a painting she happened upon betrayed her. Its subject was Greek: Hector parting from Andromache as he went out to fight, handing her their little son Astyanax while her eyes stayed fixed on her husband. The likeness to her own situation undid her. She burst into tears at the sight of it, and went back several times a day to look at the picture and weep before it. On one of these occasions, when Acilius, one of Brutus's friends, quoted the lines Andromache speaks to Hector in Homer —

  Hector, you are father and mother to me,
  and brother too, and my own dear husband —

Brutus smiled and answered, "But I could never say to Portia what Hector says back to Andromache:

  Go tend your loom, and give your maids their orders.

Her body may lack the strength for what only men's strength can do, but in spirit she fights for her country as bravely as any of us." The story comes from the memoirs of Brutus written by Bibulus, Portia's own son.

From Elea Brutus took ship and sailed to Athens, where the people received him with open delight — acclamations in the streets, public honors voted in his name. He lodged with a private friend and became a faithful listener at the lectures of Theomnestus the Academic and Cratippus the Peripatetic, throwing himself into philosophy so completely that he seemed to have set aside all thought of public business and to be entirely at leisure for study. All the while, unsuspected, he was secretly preparing for war. He sent Herostratus into Macedonia to win the commanders there over to his side, and he himself quietly recruited every young Roman then studying at Athens and kept them at his disposal. One of them was Cicero's son, whom Brutus praised everywhere, saying that sleeping or waking he could not help admiring a young man of so great a spirit and so fierce a hatred of tyranny.

At last he began to act in the open. Learning that several Roman ships loaded with treasure were coming that way from Asia, commanded by a friend of his, he went to meet him near Carystus. He found him there, persuaded him to hand over the ships, and then gave an unusually splendid dinner — it happened to be Brutus's birthday. When the drinking began and the cups were being filled with toasts to victory for Brutus and liberty for Rome, Brutus, wanting to stir the company further, called for a larger bowl. Then, holding it in his hand, with no prompting and no forethought, he suddenly recited this verse aloud:

  But deadly fate and Leto's son have killed me.

Some writers add that at the last battle he fought at Philippi, the watchword he gave his soldiers was "Apollo" — and they conclude from this that his sudden, unaccountable outburst was an omen of the defeat he suffered there.

Antistius, the commander of the treasure ships, gave him at parting five hundred thousand from the money he was carrying to Italy. And all the soldiers still left over from Pompey's army — men who had been wandering Thessaly since their general's defeat — flocked to him readily and joyfully. He also took from Cinna five hundred horsemen being led to Dolabella in Asia. Then he sailed to Demetrias and seized a great store of weapons that had been made on the dead Caesar's orders for the Parthian war and were now due to be shipped to Antony. Macedonia itself was put into his hands, surrendered by Hortensius the praetor, and all the kings and princes of the surrounding country came offering their services.

Then came word that Gaius, Antony's brother, had crossed over from Italy and was marching straight to join the forces Vatinius commanded at Dyrrhachium and Apollonia. Brutus decided to get there first and seize those troops himself, and set off at once with the men he had around him. The march was brutal — rough country, deep snow — but so fast that he left the servants carrying the morning's provisions far behind. Near Dyrrhachium, worn down by fatigue and cold, he fell into the condition called bulimia — a ravenous, fainting hunger that seizes both men and animals after hard labor, especially in deep snow. Perhaps the body's natural heat, driven inward when the cold takes hold, burns up all its nourishment at once; or perhaps the sharp, thin vapor that rises off melting snow cuts into the body and destroys the warmth as it escapes through the pores — for sweating seems to happen when the heat meets the cold at the body's surface and is quenched there. But I have discussed all this more fully in another place.

Brutus was growing faint, and no one in the whole army had anything for him to eat, so his servants were driven to turn to the enemy: they went right up to the city gates and begged bread from the sentries on duty. The moment the sentries heard what condition Brutus was in, they came out themselves, bringing food and drink with them. In return, when Brutus took the city, he showed the greatest kindness not only to those men but to all the inhabitants, for their sake.

Meanwhile Gaius Antonius reached Apollonia and summoned all the soldiers in the area to join him there. But they kept going over to Brutus instead, and suspecting that even the people of Apollonia leaned the same way, he abandoned the city and moved to Buthrotum — losing on the way three cohorts, cut to pieces by Brutus as they marched. He then tried to seize some strongpoints around Byllis that the enemy had occupied first, and was beaten in a pitched battle by young Cicero, to whom Brutus had given the command — an officer he used often, and with great success. Not long after, Gaius himself was caught in marshy ground, far from his supports. With the man in his power, Brutus would not let his soldiers attack. Instead he circled the enemy with his cavalry and gave orders that no one was to be killed — in a little while, he said, they would all be on his side. And so it proved: they surrendered, the men and their general together, and Brutus now had a large and formidable army.

For a long time he treated Gaius with every mark of honor and respect, and left him the insignia of his office, even though — as some report — letters kept arriving from Rome, from Cicero in particular, urging Brutus to put him to death. But when he found that Gaius was tampering with his officers and trying to stir up a mutiny among the soldiers, he put him aboard a ship and held him there under close guard. The soldiers Gaius had corrupted withdrew to Apollonia and sent word asking Brutus to come to them there. He answered that this was not the Roman custom: those who had given offense must come to their general themselves and beg forgiveness for it. They came, and he pardoned them.

As he was preparing to cross into Asia, news reached him of the upheaval at Rome. The young Octavian, backed by the senate against Antony, had driven his rival out of Italy and begun to grow formidable in his own right — standing for the consulship against all law, and keeping up great bodies of troops the commonwealth had no need of. And when he saw that the senate, unhappy with his conduct, was beginning to look abroad to Brutus, decreeing and confirming province after province under his command, he took alarm. He sent messengers to Antony proposing reconciliation and friendship; then he ringed the city with his troops and got himself chosen consul — though he was hardly more than a boy, barely twenty years old, as he says himself in his memoirs.

The moment he entered office he opened a judicial process against Brutus and his associates, for murdering the first man of the city — a holder of the highest offices of Rome — without trial or condemnation. He appointed Lucius Cornificius to accuse Brutus and Marcus Agrippa to accuse Cassius. No one appeared to answer the charges, so the judges were compelled to pass sentence and condemn them both. It is said that when the crier, following custom, called out from the tribunal in a loud voice summoning Brutus to appear, the people groaned aloud and the noblest citizens hung their heads in grief. Publius Silicius was seen to burst into tears — and for that, not long afterward, his name went down on the list of the proscribed. After this the three men — Octavian, Antony, and Lepidus — settled their quarrels completely, divided the provinces among themselves, and drew up the proscription list: the catalogue of those marked for slaughter, two hundred men in all. Cicero was among the slain.

When this news reached Brutus in Macedonia, he felt himself under a compulsion, and sent orders to Hortensius to kill Gaius Antonius — in revenge for Cicero his friend and for a Brutus who was his own kinsman, both proscribed, both dead. It was on this account that Antony, when he later captured Hortensius at the battle of Philippi, slaughtered him on his brother's tomb. As for Brutus, he wrote that he felt more shame at the cause of Cicero's death than grief at the event itself, and that he could not help blaming his friends at Rome. They were slaves, he said, more by their own doing than by that of the men who now tyrannized them: they could stand there, and watch, and endure things that ought to have been unbearable even to hear about.

He now moved his army — already very large — across into Asia, and ordered a fleet fitted out in Bithynia and around Cyzicus. Traveling overland himself, he made it his business to settle and secure the cities as he went, and gave audiences to the princes of every region he passed through. And he sent orders into Syria for Cassius to give up his planned expedition into Egypt and come to him. It was not to win an empire for themselves, he reminded him, but to free their country, that they were wandering like this with an army they had gathered for one purpose: destroying the tyrants. If they remembered that first resolve and meant to hold to it, they should not stray far from Italy, but hurry back and rescue their fellow citizens from oppression.

Cassius obeyed the summons and turned back, and Brutus went out to meet him. They met at Smyrna — the first time they had seen each other since parting at the Piraeus in Athens, one bound for Syria, the other for Macedonia. The sight of each other's forces filled them both with joy and confidence. They had fled Italy like the most miserable of exiles — no money, no arms, not a ship, not a soldier, not a city to rely on — and in this short time they had come together again so well furnished with shipping and money, with an army of horse and foot, that they were in a condition to contend for the empire of Rome.

Cassius wanted to show Brutus every bit as much honor as Brutus showed him, but Brutus generally beat him to it, coming to Cassius most of the time because Cassius was the older man and his health was the weaker. Men generally rated Cassius an expert soldier, but harsh-tempered and hot, a commander who preferred to rule by fear rather than love — though among his close friends he gave way easily to joking and would happily play the clown. Brutus, on the other hand, was esteemed by the people for his virtue, loved by his friends, admired by the best men, and not hated even by his enemies. For he was a man of singularly gentle nature and great spirit, untouched by anger or pleasure or greed, steady and unbending in holding to whatever he judged right and honorable. And what won him the deepest affection and the highest standing was the complete trust men placed in his intentions.

For no one had ever supposed that even Pompey the Great, had he beaten Caesar, would have submitted his power to the laws. He would have kept the management of the state in his own hands, soothing the people with the milder name of consul or dictator or some other title softer than king. And everyone was persuaded that Cassius — a man governed by anger and passion, and often carried beyond the bounds of justice for his own advantage — was enduring all these hardships of war and travel and danger to win dominion for himself, not liberty for the people. As for the earlier disturbers of Rome's peace — a Cinna, a Marius, a Carbo — it is plain that they set their country up as a stake for the winner, and all but admitted in so many words that they were fighting for empire. But even Brutus's enemies, we are told, never laid that charge against him. Many people, indeed, heard Antony himself say that Brutus was the only man who conspired against Caesar out of a sense of the glory and the apparent justice of the deed; the rest rose against the man himself, out of private envy and grudges of their own.

And it is plain from his own letters that Brutus relied not so much on his forces as on his own virtue. Shortly before the final engagement he wrote to Atticus that his affairs stood as well as fortune could make them: either he would conquer and restore liberty to the Roman people, or he would die and be beyond the reach of slavery. With everything else, he said, settled and certain, one question alone remained open — whether they would live or die as free men. He added that Mark Antony had paid a fitting price for his folly: he could have been counted alongside Brutus and Cassius and Cato, and had chosen instead to attach himself to Octavian — and even if the two of them were not beaten now, they would soon enough be fighting each other. In this he proved no bad prophet.

While they were at Smyrna, Brutus asked Cassius for a share of the great treasure he had piled up, because his own funds were all spent fitting out a fleet large enough to command the whole Mediterranean. Cassius's friends argued against it. "It is not right," they said, "that the money you keep so frugally, and gathered at the cost of so much resentment, should be handed to him to spend making himself popular and buying the favor of the soldiers." Cassius gave him a third of everything anyway. Then they parted, each to his own command.

Cassius took Rhodes and behaved there with no mercy at all — though at his first entry, when some of the people hailed him as lord and king, he had answered that he was neither king nor lord, but the destroyer and punisher of a king and lord. Brutus, for his part, sent to the Lycians demanding a supply of money and men. But Naucrates, their popular leader, persuaded the cities to resist, and they occupied a string of hills and small mountains to block Brutus's passage. Brutus first sent out a party of cavalry, who caught them at their meal and killed six hundred. Then, after taking the small towns and villages all around, he released every prisoner without ransom, hoping to win the whole nation over by goodwill. But they stayed stubborn, angry at what they had suffered and contemptuous of his kindness, until at last he drove the most warlike of them into the city of Xanthus and laid siege to it. They tried to escape by swimming and diving down the river that flows past the town, but were caught by nets let down across the channel, with little bells at the top that rang the moment anyone was taken. Then they made a sortie by night, seized some of the siege engines, and set them on fire; but the Romans spotted them and beat them back to the walls — and a strong wind was blowing, and it drove the flames onto the battlements so fiercely that several of the nearby houses caught fire. Brutus, afraid the whole city would be destroyed, ordered his own soldiers to help put the fire out.

But the Lycians were suddenly seized by a strange, unbelievable desperation — a frenzy that can hardly be described except as a violent appetite for death. Women and children, slave and free, people of every age and every condition fought from the walls to drive off the very soldiers who had come to help them, and with their own hands gathered reeds and wood and anything that would burn, spreading the fire through the whole city, feeding it with whatever fuel they could find and goading its fury by every possible means. When the flames had run through Xanthus and ringed it and blazed up on every side, Brutus, sick at heart over their calamity, rode around the walls on horseback, desperate to save the city, stretching out his hands to the Xanthians and begging them to spare themselves and their town. No one listened. They sought their own destruction by every means there was — not just men and women, but even boys and small children, with a hideous outcry, leaping into the fire, or from the walls, or onto their parents' swords, baring their throats and begging to be struck. After the city was destroyed, a woman was found who had hanged herself, her young child hanging at her neck, and in her hand the torch with which she had set her own house alight. So tragic was the sight that Brutus could not bear to look at it, and wept when it was merely described to him. He proclaimed a reward for any soldier who could save a Xanthian; and only a hundred and fifty, it is said, were found — saved against their wills. So the Xanthians, after a long stretch of years, as though the fated cycle of their destruction had come round at last, repeated by their own desperate act the calamity of their ancestors, who in the Persian war had burned their city and destroyed themselves in the very same way.

After this, finding the people of Patara resolved to resist and hold their city against him, Brutus was very reluctant to besiege it, dreading that the same madness might seize them too. But he held some of their women as prisoners, and he released them all without ransom. They went home to husbands and fathers of the highest rank and told them what a man Brutus was — how temperate, how just — and persuaded them to yield and put their city into his hands. From then on all the cities of the region came over to him, surrendering themselves, and found him kind and merciful beyond anything they had hoped. And the contrast told. Cassius, at that same time, was forcing every Rhodian to hand in all the silver and gold he privately owned — which raised eight thousand talents — and on top of that had fined the city another five hundred. Brutus took no more than a hundred and fifty talents from the Lycians, and having done them no other injury of any kind, marched away with his army into Ionia.

Through the whole course of this expedition Brutus did many memorable acts of justice, in rewards and punishments alike; but I will tell one in particular, because it gratified him — and the noblest Romans with him — more than all the rest. When Pompey the Great, stripped of his great power by Caesar, fled to Egypt and landed near Pelusium, the guardians of the boy king held a council on what to do, and could not agree — some were for receiving him, others for driving him away from Egypt. But Theodotus, a Chian by birth, then attached to the king as a paid teacher of rhetoric and admitted to the council only for want of better men, undertook to prove that both sides were wrong — those who advised welcoming Pompey and those who advised sending him off. In their situation, he argued, only one course was truly expedient: seize him and kill him. And he closed his argument with the proverb: "Dead men don't bite." The council came round to his opinion, and Pompey the Great — a lesson in how incredible and unforeseen events can be — was slain, thanks, as the sophist himself later had the impudence to boast, to the rhetoric and cleverness of Theodotus. Not long afterward, when Caesar came to Egypt, some of the murderers got their just reward and died the wretched deaths they deserved. Theodotus borrowed a little more time from fortune — time for a poor, despised, vagrant life — but he did not stay hidden from Brutus as he passed through Asia. He was seized and executed, and his death made him more memorable than his life ever had.
