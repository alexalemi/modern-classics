Brutus

(Part 1 of 5)

Marcus Brutus was descended from the famous Junius Brutus — the man the ancient Romans honored with a bronze statue on the Capitol, set among the images of their kings with a drawn sword in his hand, in memory of the courage and resolve with which he drove out the Tarquins and destroyed the monarchy. But that ancient Brutus was hard by nature, inflexible, like steel tempered too far, and his character had never been softened by study and reflection. His rage and hatred against tyrants carried him so far that when his own sons conspired with the Tarquins, he had them executed. The Brutus whose life we are now writing was a different kind of man. To a naturally good disposition he added the improvements of learning and the study of philosophy, and a temperament that was by nature serious and gentle he roused into action by throwing himself into business and public affairs — so that he seemed a character framed, piece by piece, for virtue. Even his bitterest enemies, the people who hated him on account of the conspiracy against Caesar, granted him this much: whatever was honorable or generous in that whole affair they credited entirely to Brutus, and whatever was savage and cruel they laid at the door of Cassius — his kinsman and close friend, but not his equal in honesty or purity of purpose.

His mother, Servilia, came from the family of Servilius Ahala. When Spurius Maelius was whipping the people into rebellion and scheming to make himself king, this Ahala tucked a dagger under his arm, walked out into the marketplace, and, on the pretense of having some private business with him, came up close. As Maelius bent his head to hear what he had to say, Ahala struck him with the dagger and killed him. That much — the descent on his mother's side — everyone admits. As for his father's family, those who bore Brutus any hatred or ill will over Caesar's murder claimed he was no descendant of the Brutus who expelled the Tarquins at all: that line, they said, was wiped out with the execution of the two sons, and this man's ancestor was a commoner, the son of a steward named Brutus, whose family rose to office and dignity only in recent times. But the philosopher Posidonius writes that the history is accurate about the two grown sons being put to death — and that a third son, still an infant, was left alive, and from him the family came down to Marcus Brutus. He adds that in his own day there were several distinguished men of the house whose faces bore a striking resemblance to the statue of Junius Brutus. But enough on that subject.

Cato the philosopher was the brother of Servilia, Brutus's mother, and of all the Romans it was Cato whom his nephew most admired and studied to imitate; later he married Cato's daughter Portia. As for the Greek schools of philosophy, there was hardly one Brutus had not heard out and made some progress in, but his deepest loyalty went to the Platonists — and to the ancient Academy at that, for he never much approved of what is called the modern, or middle, Academy. All his life he was a great admirer of Antiochus of the city of Ascalon, and he took Antiochus's brother Aristus into his own house as a friend and companion — a man inferior to many philosophers in learning, but the equal of the best of them in evenness of temper and steadiness of conduct. As for Empylus, whom Brutus and his friends mention often in their letters as one of the household, he was a rhetorician, and he left behind a short but well-written history of the death of Caesar, titled Brutus.

In Latin, Brutus had trained himself well enough to deliver public addresses and plead a case. But in Greek he cultivated the terse, pointed, Spartan manner of speaking — the famous Laconic style — and many passages of his letters show it. At the beginning of the war he wrote to the people of Pergamum: "I hear you have given Dolabella money. If you gave it willingly, admit that you have wronged me; if unwillingly, prove it by giving willingly to me." Another time, to the Samians: "Your counsels are careless and your performance slow. What do you suppose the end will be?" And of the Patareans: "The Xanthians distrusted my kindness and made their country the grave of their despair. The Patareans trusted themselves to me and enjoy their liberty in every particular. You may choose: the judgment of the Patareans, or the fortune of the Xanthians." That is the style for which some of his letters are remembered.

While he was still a very young man he accompanied his uncle Cato to Cyprus, on the mission against Ptolemy. When Ptolemy killed himself, Cato — detained on the island of Rhodes by some necessary business — had already sent one of his friends, a man named Canidius, to take charge of the king's treasure. But soon, not feeling sure of Canidius's honesty, he wrote to Brutus to sail for Cyprus at once from Pamphylia, where Brutus was resting after just recovering from an illness. Brutus obeyed, but with real reluctance — partly out of respect for Canidius, who was being tossed out of the job by Cato with such disgrace, and partly because he considered the whole commission beneath him: petty accounting work, unsuited to a young man in the prime of life who lived for books and study. Still, he applied himself to the business and handled it so well that Cato praised him highly. Having converted all of Ptolemy's goods into ready money, he sailed with the greater part of it in his own ship to Rome.

But when the state split into two great factions — when Pompey and Caesar took up arms against each other and the whole empire dissolved into confusion — everyone assumed Brutus would choose Caesar's side. For his father, years before, had been put to death by Pompey. Brutus, however, held that it was his duty to put the public interest ahead of his private feelings, and judging Pompey's to be the better cause, he took Pompey's part — this, though formerly he would not so much as greet Pompey or acknowledge him if they happened to meet, counting it a pollution to exchange a single word with his father's murderer. Now, looking on him as the general of his country, he placed himself under Pompey's command and sailed for Cilicia as lieutenant to Sestius, who governed that province. But he found no opportunity there for any great service, and hearing that Pompey and Caesar were now closing on each other and preparing for the battle on which everything depended, he came of his own accord to Macedonia to share the danger. At his arrival, it is said, Pompey was so surprised and so pleased that he rose from his chair in full view of everyone around him and saluted and embraced Brutus as one of the chief men of his party. All the time he was in the camp, apart from the hours he spent in Pompey's company, he gave to reading and study — and he did not neglect them even on the day before the great battle. It was the middle of summer, the heat was crushing, the camp had been pitched beside marshy ground, and the men carrying Brutus's tent took forever to arrive. Though he was worn out and out of sorts on every count, and had barely managed by midday to anoint himself and eat a sparing meal, he spent his time until evening — while most of the others were asleep, or lost in dread of what the battle would bring — writing a condensed edition of Polybius.

It is said that Caesar had such regard for him that he ordered his commanders on no account to kill Brutus in the battle, but to spare him: if he surrendered willingly, they were to bring him in safe; if he resisted, they were to let him escape rather than do him any violence. And this, it is believed, Caesar did out of tenderness for Servilia, Brutus's mother. In his youth Caesar had been intimate with her, and she passionately in love with him, and since Brutus was born about the time their love was at its height, Caesar had some belief that the boy was his own child. There is a story told about this. When the great matter of Catiline's conspiracy — the plot that came near destroying the state — was being debated in the senate, Cato and Caesar were both on their feet, arguing opposite sides, when a little note was carried in and handed to Caesar, who read it silently to himself. At this Cato cried out that Caesar was corresponding with and receiving letters from the enemies of the state; and when a number of other senators joined the outcry, Caesar handed the note, just as he had received it, to Cato — who read it and found it to be a love letter from his own sister Servilia. He threw it back at Caesar with the words, "Keep it, you drunkard," and returned to the business of the debate. That is how public and notorious Servilia's love for Caesar was.

After the great defeat at Pharsalia, with Pompey escaped to the sea and Caesar's army storming the camp, Brutus slipped out privately by one of the gates that led to marshy ground, thick with water and reeds, and traveling through the night got safely to Larissa. From Larissa he wrote to Caesar, who expressed great joy to hear he was safe, told him to come, and not only forgave him freely but honored him and esteemed him among his closest friends. At that moment no one could say with any certainty which way Pompey had fled; so Caesar took a short journey alone with Brutus and sounded out his opinion, and after some discussion between them, concluding that Brutus's guess was the right one, he dropped every other consideration and set out straight for Egypt in pursuit. Pompey had indeed reached Egypt, just as Brutus guessed he would — and there he met his fate.

Brutus meanwhile obtained Caesar's forgiveness for his friend Cassius. He also pleaded the cause of the king of Libya, and though the man was buried under the sheer weight of the charges against him, by entreaties on his behalf Brutus preserved for him a great part of his kingdom. It is reported that Caesar, hearing Brutus speak in public for the first time, said to his friends, "I don't know what this young man intends — but whatever he intends, he intends it intensely." For that was Brutus's nature: a firmness of mind that did not easily bend or grant favors to everyone who asked, but which, once set in motion by reasoned judgment and deliberate moral choice, drove toward its object in whatever direction it took and was almost certain not to fail. To unjust petitions he was unmovable; no flattery could reach him. He held that being conquered by shameless, fawning importunity — which some people dress up with the name of modesty or soft-heartedness — was the worst disgrace a great man could suffer. And he used to say that people who could deny nothing had, he suspected, not behaved very well in the flower of their youth.

When Caesar was about to cross to Africa on his expedition against Cato and Scipio, he entrusted Brutus with the government of Cisalpine Gaul — to the great good fortune of that province. For while the people of other provinces groaned under the violence and greed of their governors, suffering as much oppression as slaves or captives of war, Brutus governed so mildly that he actually made his subjects amends for their miseries under previous rulers — and he directed all their gratitude for it to Caesar himself. So it was a most welcome and pleasant spectacle for Caesar, passing through Italy on his return, to see the cities under Brutus's command flourishing, and Brutus himself adding to his honor and traveling agreeably in his company.

Now several praetorships fell vacant, and everyone expected that the most distinguished of them — the one called the praetorship of the city — would go either to Brutus or to Cassius. Some say the two men, who already had certain small differences between them, were set much further at odds by this competition, even though they were connected by family: Cassius had married Junia, Brutus's sister. Others say the rivalry was Caesar's own doing — that he had privately encouraged each of them with hopes of his favor until he led them on and provoked them into open competition. Brutus had only his reputation for honor and virtue to set against Cassius's many brilliant exploits against the Parthians. Caesar heard both sides, and deliberating on the matter among his friends, said, "Cassius has the stronger claim — but Brutus must have the first praetorship." So another praetorship was given to Cassius, who was less grateful for the one he gained than furious over the one he lost. And in all other respects Brutus had as large a share of Caesar's power as he cared to take. Had he wished, he could have been first among all Caesar's friends, with authority and command beyond any of them. But Cassius, and the company Cassius kept, kept pulling him away from Caesar. He had not yet fully reconciled with Cassius after their rivalry, but he listened to Cassius's friends, who were forever warning him not to be so blind as to let Caesar soften and tame him — to shun the kindness and favors of a tyrant, which Caesar showed him, they hinted, not to honor his merit but to unstring his strength and sap the vigor of his purpose.

Nor was Caesar entirely without suspicion of Brutus; there was no shortage of informers accusing him. But while Caesar feared the man's high spirit, his great name, and his powerful friends, he trusted in his character. When he was told that Antony and Dolabella were plotting some disturbance, he said, "It's not the fat, long-haired men I'm afraid of — it's the pale, lean ones," meaning Brutus and Cassius. And later, when people were running Brutus down to him and warning him to beware, he took hold of his own flesh with his hand and said, "What — do you think Brutus cannot wait out the time of this little body?" — as if to say that no one but Brutus was fit to succeed him in his power. And indeed it seems beyond doubt that Brutus could have been the first man in Rome if he had only had the patience to be second to Caesar for a little while — to let Caesar's power pass its peak and decline, and the fame of his great deeds fade by degrees. But Cassius — a violent-tempered man, and one who hated Caesar out of private grievance rather than love of the public, hated the man and not the tyranny — kept firing him up and driving him on. Brutus resented the rule; Cassius hated the ruler. Among the grudges Cassius counted against Caesar was the loss of his lions, which he had procured when he was aedile-elect: Caesar found them at Megara when Calenus took that city, and seized them for himself. Those beasts, they say, proved a calamity for the Megarians. When the city was on the point of falling, the Megarians broke open the lions' dens and struck off their chains, meaning to loose them on the enemy coming through the gates — but the lions turned on the Megarians themselves, tearing apart crowds of unarmed people as they ran, so that even the enemy pitied the sight.

Some say this was the chief provocation that drove Cassius to conspire against Caesar — but they are quite wrong. From his boyhood Cassius carried a natural hatred and bitterness toward the whole race of tyrants, and he showed it while still a schoolboy, when he attended the same school as Faustus, the son of Sulla. Faustus was boasting among the boys, glorifying his father's absolute power, and Cassius stood up and boxed his ears — two or three good blows. Faustus's guardians and relatives meant to take the matter to law, but Pompey forbade it, and sending for both boys, examined the business himself. And Cassius is reported to have said, "Go on, then, Faustus — dare to repeat, right here, the words that provoked me, and I'll hit you again just as I did before." That was the temper of Cassius.

Brutus, though, was roused and pushed toward the undertaking by many voices: the urging of his close friends, and letters and appeals from citizens he did not know. Under the statue of his ancestor Brutus — the Brutus who overthrew the kings — people wrote: "If only we had a Brutus now!" and "If only Brutus were alive!" And Brutus's own tribunal, the platform where he sat as praetor, was found covered every morning with writings like these: "You are asleep, Brutus." and "You are no longer Brutus." The ones responsible for all this were Caesar's flatterers, who among the other invidious honors they kept fastening on him crowned his statues at night with diadems, hoping to lead the people into hailing him king instead of dictator. But exactly the opposite came to pass — as I have told more fully in the life of Caesar.

When Cassius went around sounding out friends for the plot against Caesar, every man he approached agreed readily — on one condition: that Brutus lead it. What the enterprise needed, in their view, was not hands or daring but the reputation and authority of a man like Brutus, to consecrate the deed, as it were, by his mere presence — to justify it simply by being part of it. Without him, they felt, they would go into the act with less heart and lie under heavier suspicion afterward; for if the cause had been just and honorable, people would be certain Brutus would not have refused it. Cassius thought all this over, and then paid Brutus his first visit since their falling-out. When the courtesies of reconciliation had been exchanged and their old friendship renewed, he asked whether Brutus intended to be present in the senate on the first of March; for word was going around, he said, that Caesar's friends meant to move on that day that he be made king. When Brutus answered that he would not be there, Cassius pressed him: "But what if they send for us?" "Then it will be my business," Brutus replied, "not to hold my peace, but to stand up boldly and die for the liberty of my country." And Cassius, stirred, answered: "But what Roman will let you die? Do you not know yourself, Brutus? Do you imagine the writings on your praetor's tribunal were left there by weavers and shopkeepers, and not by the first and most powerful men of Rome? From other praetors they expect handouts and shows and gladiators — but from you they claim, as a debt of your inheritance, the destruction of tyranny. They are ready to suffer anything for your sake, if you will only show yourself to be the man they believe you are, and expect you to be." With that he threw his arms around Brutus and embraced him; and so, agreed, they parted, each to sound out his own friends.

Among Pompey's old friends there was a certain Gaius Ligarius, whom Caesar had pardoned after he was accused of bearing arms against him. This man — feeling not so much gratitude for the pardon as resentment of the power that had made a pardon necessary — hated Caesar, and was one of Brutus's most intimate friends. Brutus visited him and found him sick. "Ligarius," he said, "what a time you've picked to be sick!" At those words Ligarius raised himself on his elbow, took Brutus by the hand, and said, "But Brutus — if you are on any design worthy of yourself, I am well."

From that time on they tested the inclinations of every acquaintance they dared trust, shared the secret, and drew into the plot not only their close friends but every man they believed bold, brave, and unafraid of death. That is why they hid the plot from Cicero, though they all trusted him fully and loved him well: they feared that his naturally timid disposition, with the caution of old age now added to it — his habit of weighing every particular, refusing to take a single step without absolute security — would blunt the edge of their resolve in a business that demanded all possible speed. There were two other companions of Brutus whom he likewise left out: Statilius the Epicurean, and Favonius, the devoted admirer of Cato. His reason was this. One day, in conversation with them, testing them at a safe distance, he floated a question of the kind philosophers dispute, to see where they stood; and Favonius gave it as his judgment that civil war was worse than the most lawless monarchy, while Statilius held that a wise and sensible man ought not to plunge himself into danger and turmoil on account of wicked or foolish people. But Labeo, who was present, contradicted them both; and Brutus, as though the question were a knotty one and hard to settle, held his peace for the moment — and afterward revealed the whole design to Labeo, who readily took it up. The next step, they agreed, was to win over the other Brutus — Decimus Brutus, surnamed Albinus — a man of no great daring or courage in himself, but valuable for the number of gladiators he was maintaining for a public show, and for the great confidence Caesar placed in him. When Cassius and Labeo raised the matter with him, he gave them no answer; but he sought out a private interview with Brutus himself, and on learning that Brutus was their captain, he readily agreed to take part. Among the rest, too, it was the name of Brutus that won over the most men and the best. And though they swore no oath of secrecy and used no sacred rite to bind their loyalty to one another, they all kept the design so close, so warily, so silently among themselves, that although the gods gave warning of it through prophecies and apparitions and signs in the sacrifices, no one could believe it was real.
