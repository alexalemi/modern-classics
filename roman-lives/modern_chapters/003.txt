Caesar

(Part 4 of 6)

Caesar folded Domitius's soldiers into his own army, and he did the same in every town where he found men enlisted for Pompey's service. Now strong and formidable, he marched against Pompey himself. Pompey did not wait for him. He fell back to Brundisium, sent the consuls ahead to Dyrrhachium with a body of troops, and shortly afterward, as Caesar approached, put to sea — as will be told in more detail in his own Life. Caesar wanted to pursue at once, but he had no ships, so he turned back to Rome. In sixty days, without shedding a drop of blood, he had made himself master of all Italy.

He found the city quieter than he expected, and many senators still in it. He addressed them with courtesy and deference, and asked them to send envoys to Pompey with reasonable terms for a peace. Nobody took him up on it — whether from fear of Pompey, whom they had deserted, or because they suspected Caesar did not mean a word of it and was simply saying what sounded good. When the tribune Metellus tried to stop him from taking money out of the public treasury, citing the laws against it, Caesar answered that arms and laws each had their own season. "If what I am doing displeases you, leave. War has no room for free speech. When I have laid down my arms and made peace, come back and give all the speeches you please. And in telling you even this much," he added, "I am giving up my own just rights — for you, and everyone else who took sides against me and is now in my power, may be dealt with exactly as I please." With that he walked to the doors of the treasury. The keys could not be found, so he sent for smiths to break the doors open. Metellus objected again, and some of the bystanders cheered him on — at which Caesar raised his voice and told him he would put him to death if he made any more trouble. "And you know very well, young man," he said, "that saying this is more disagreeable to me than doing it." The words sent Metellus away in fear, and from then on everything Caesar demanded for the war was supplied promptly and without argument.

Next he set out for Spain. He meant to crush Afranius and Varro first — Pompey's lieutenants there — and take over the armies and provinces they held, so that he could then move against Pompey with no enemy left at his back. In Spain his life was in constant danger from ambushes and his army from hunger, but he never let up: pursuing the enemy, daring them to fight, walling them in with his siege-works, until by main force he made himself master of their camps and their armies. Only the generals got away, and fled to Pompey.

When Caesar came back to Rome, his father-in-law Piso urged him to send envoys to Pompey and negotiate a peace; Isauricus, angling for Caesar's favor, argued against it. The senate then made him dictator, and he used the office to call home the exiles, restore citizen rights to the children of those who had suffered under Sulla, and ease the debtors by canceling part of the interest on their debts — that, and a few other measures of the same kind, but not many. Within eleven days he resigned the dictatorship, had himself declared consul alongside Servilius Isauricus, and hurried back to the war.

He marched so fast that he outran his own army. With only six hundred picked horsemen and five legions, he put to sea in the dead of winter, at the beginning of January — which corresponds roughly to the Athenian month Posideon — crossed the Ionian Sea, took Oricum and Apollonia, and sent the ships back to Brundisium for the soldiers still on the road behind him. Those soldiers, as they marched — men past the first vigor of youth, worn down by so many wars — grumbled openly against Caesar. "When will this Caesar finally let us rest, and where? He hauls us from place to place as if we could never wear out, as if we felt nothing. Even iron gets used up by hammering. Our shields and breastplates have served so long they deserve some pity. Our wounds, if nothing else, ought to remind him that the men he commands are mortal, and feel pain and suffering like any other human beings. The gods themselves cannot force the winter season or hold back the storms — yet he pushes on as if he were running from an enemy instead of chasing one." So they talked, strolling toward Brundisium at their ease. But when they got there and found Caesar already gone, the mood flipped in an instant. Now they cursed themselves as traitors to their general, railed at their officers for the slow pace, and perched on the heights facing Epirus, scanning the sea for the ships that would carry them across to Caesar.

Caesar, meanwhile, sat at Apollonia without an army strong enough to fight, and the forces from Brundisium were so long in coming that he was in an agony of suspense over what to do. At last he settled on a desperate experiment. Without telling anyone, he would cross back to Brundisium himself in a twelve-oared boat, though the sea was covered with the enemy's fleets. He went aboard at night dressed as a slave, threw himself down like a passenger of no account, and lay flat in the bottom of the boat. The river Anius was to carry them down to the sea. Most mornings a gentle breeze off the land smoothed the waves at the river's mouth, driving them outward; but that night a strong wind had blown in from the sea and overpowered the land breeze, so that where the river met the incoming seawater and the push of the waves, it turned rough and angry. The current was beaten back with such a violent swell that the master of the boat could not force the passage, and ordered his sailors to come about and turn back. At this Caesar revealed himself, took the astonished man by the hand, and said, "Go on, my friend, and fear nothing — you carry Caesar and his fortune in your boat." The sailors forgot the storm. They threw all their strength into their oars and fought to force their way down the river. But it was no use; the boat kept taking on water, and Caesar, finding himself in real danger at the river's very mouth, very reluctantly allowed the master to turn back. When he came ashore, his soldiers ran to him in a crowd, reproaching him and demanding to know why he thought he could not win with their help alone — why he had to torment himself and risk his life for the absent, as if he could not trust the men he had.

After this, Antony came over with the forces from Brundisium, and Caesar felt strong enough to offer Pompey battle. Pompey was superbly encamped, with supplies flowing in by land and sea, while Caesar was short of everything at the start and desperately pinched before the end. His soldiers were reduced to digging up a local root, mixing it with milk, and eating that. Sometimes they baked it into a kind of bread, and they would run up to the enemy's outposts and throw the loaves over, shouting that as long as the earth produced roots like these, they would never give up the blockade of Pompey. Pompey did what he could to keep both the loaves and the words from reaching his men, who were already disheartened — terrified of the enemy's ferocity and toughness, as if they were fighting some breed of wild beast. There was constant skirmishing around Pompey's outworks, and Caesar got the better of all of it except once — one fight in which his men were routed so badly that he nearly lost his camp. Pompey's sortie swept everything before it. Not a man stood his ground; the trenches filled with the slain; men fell at their own ramparts and palisades, driven onto them in headlong flight. Caesar met the fugitives and tried to turn them around, and could not. When he grabbed at the standards, the bearers dropped them and ran — the enemy captured thirty-two of them. He himself barely escaped with his life. Seizing a big, powerful soldier who was running past, he ordered him to stand and face the enemy; and the man, out of his mind with panic, raised his sword to strike him — before Caesar's armor-bearer sliced off his arm. That day Caesar's cause was so far gone that when Pompey — whether from over-caution or plain bad luck — failed to deliver the finishing blow, and pulled back after driving the routed enemy inside their camp, Caesar said to his friends as he watched the withdrawal, "Today the victory belonged to the enemy — if they had had a general who knew how to win it." Then he went to his tent and lay down, and spent the most miserable night of his life, turning it all over and concluding that he had managed the war badly. With fertile country in front of him, and all the rich cities of Macedonia and Thessaly, he had failed to carry the war inland and had instead planted himself by the sea, where the enemy's fleet ruled — so that in truth he was less besieging his enemy with arms than being besieged himself by hunger. Wrestling with the difficulty and distress of his position, he broke camp, resolved to march against Scipio, who lay in Macedonia. Either he would draw Pompey after him into country where Pompey would have to fight without his seaborne supplies, or he would crush Scipio unaided.

The move set Pompey's whole army and officer corps on fire to chase Caesar, whom they took for a beaten man on the run. But Pompey was afraid to stake everything on a single battle. He was stocked and supplied for a long war, and he meant to let time grind down Caesar's army, which could not last. The best of Caesar's men had enormous experience and an irresistible courage in any engagement; but the endless marching, the shifting of camps, the assaults on fortifications, the long night watches, were wearing them out and breaking them down. They were getting old. Their bodies were less fit for labor, and their spirit was starting to sag as their strength failed. On top of that, an infectious disease, bred by their wretched diet, was said to be running through Caesar's army — and worst of all, he had neither money nor provisions, so that in a little while he must simply collapse on his own.

For all these reasons Pompey refused battle, and only one man thanked him for it: Cato, who rejoiced at the prospect of sparing the lives of fellow citizens. Cato had seen the bodies of the thousand men who fell on Caesar's side in the last battle, and had turned away, covered his face, and wept. Everyone else berated Pompey for ducking the fight and needled him with nicknames — Agamemnon, King of Kings — as if he were in no hurry to lay down his supreme command, and enjoyed the sight of so many commanders dancing attendance at his tent. Favonius, who liked to copy Cato's blunt way of speaking his mind, complained bitterly that thanks to Pompey's love of command they would not taste figs at Tusculum even this year. Afranius, just back from Spain and suspected — given how badly things had gone there — of having sold his army for a bribe, demanded to know why they were not fighting this merchant who bought up provinces. Goaded by talk like this, Pompey was driven, against his own judgment, into offering battle, and set off after Caesar. Caesar's march had been hard going. Nobody would supply him with provisions; since his recent defeat, his reputation had fallen everywhere. But once he took Gomphi, a town in Thessaly, he found not only food for his army but medicine too. The place was full of wine, and the men drank it freely; heated with it, romping and reveling along the march like one long festival of Bacchus, they shook off the disease entirely, their whole condition washed clean and made new.

The two armies came into the plain of Pharsalia and encamped facing each other. Pompey's thoughts swung back to their old track, away from fighting — all the more because of unlucky omens and a vision that came to him in a dream. But the men around him were so confident of success that Domitius, and Spinther, and Scipio quarreled, as if they had already won, over which of them would succeed Caesar as high priest; and many sent ahead to Rome to rent houses suitable for consuls and praetors, sure of stepping into those offices the moment the battle was over. The cavalry above all were spoiling for a fight. They were splendidly armed and superbly mounted, proud of their fine horses and of their own handsome persons — and of their numbers: five thousand of them against Caesar's one thousand. The infantry odds were just as lopsided: forty-five thousand on Pompey's side against twenty-two thousand.

Caesar called his soldiers together and told them that Corfinius was on the way with two legions, and that fifteen more cohorts under Calenus were posted at Megara and Athens. Did they want to wait for these to join them, he asked, or hazard the battle by themselves? They shouted back at him not to wait — to do whatever it took to bring on an engagement as soon as possible. When he sacrificed to the gods for the purification of his army, the first victim was no sooner killed than the augur told him that within three days matters would come to a decisive action. Caesar asked whether he saw anything in the entrails that promised a happy outcome. "That," said the priest, "you can answer better than I can. The gods announce a great change from the present state of things. If you think yourself well off now, expect worse fortune; if badly off, hope for better." The night before the battle, as Caesar walked the rounds about midnight, a light appeared in the sky, bright and flaming, and seemed to pass over his camp and fall into Pompey's. And when his soldiers came to relieve the watch at dawn, they could hear a panicked confusion in the enemy's lines. Even so, he did not expect to fight that day, and began breaking camp to march for Scotussa.

The tents were already down when his scouts rode up with word that the enemy was coming out to give battle. The news delighted him. He performed his devotions to the gods and drew up his army in three divisions. Domitius Calvinus took the center, Antony commanded the left wing, and Caesar himself the right, where he meant to fight at the head of the tenth legion. But when he saw the enemy's cavalry taking position opposite him — struck by their gleaming turnout and their sheer numbers — he quietly ordered six cohorts up from the rear of the army and posted them behind his right wing, with exact instructions for what to do when the enemy horse charged. On the other side, Pompey commanded his own right wing, Domitius the left, and Scipio, Pompey's father-in-law, the center. The whole weight of the cavalry was massed on the left, with one purpose: to swing around the enemy's right wing and rout the very part of the line where the general himself stood. No block of infantry, they reckoned, could be packed solid enough to take that shock; under the charge of so immense a force of horse, it must be broken and smashed to pieces. When both sides were ready to sound the signal, Pompey ordered his front ranks to stand their ground — to hold formation and quietly absorb the enemy's first attack until they came within javelin range. Here too Caesar finds fault with his generalship: Pompey did not understand that the first collision, delivered on the run, puts weight and force behind every blow and sets the men's spirits blazing, the roar of the whole mass fanning them to full heat. Caesar himself was just setting his lines in motion and advancing to the action when he noticed one of his captains, a steady and experienced soldier, urging his men to give their utmost. Caesar called him by name. "What are our hopes, Gaius Crassinius? What are our grounds for confidence?" Crassinius flung out his hand and shouted, "We shall conquer nobly, Caesar — and today, alive or dead, I will earn your praise." So he said, and he was the first man to run in upon the enemy, the hundred and twenty soldiers of his command at his back. He tore through the first rank and was still driving forward, cutting men down, when at last he was stopped by a sword-thrust that entered his mouth with such force that it came out at the back of his neck.

While the infantry ground against each other in the center, Pompey's cavalry rode up confidently on the flank, spreading their ranks wide to envelop Caesar's right wing. Before they could charge, Caesar's cohorts sprang out at them — and they did not hurl their javelins from a distance, or stab at thighs and legs as men usually do in close fighting. They aimed at the faces. Those were Caesar's orders. These young gentlemen, he reasoned, had seen little of battle or wounds; they wore their hair long, in the flower of their youth and the height of their good looks; and a blow like that they would dread twice over — the danger now, and the disfigurement forever. It worked exactly as he hoped. Far from standing up to the javelins, they could not even bear the sight of them. They twisted away and covered their faces to protect them; and once in disorder, they turned and fled — and most shamefully ruined everything. For the men who had beaten them wheeled at once around the flank of the infantry, fell on it from behind, and cut it to pieces. When Pompey, from the other wing, saw his cavalry scattered and flying, he was no longer himself. He no longer remembered that he was Pompey the Great. Like a man robbed of his senses by some god, he walked to his tent without a word and sat there, waiting to see how it would end, until the whole army was routed and the enemy was on the ramparts of the camp, fighting hand to hand with the men posted to defend it. Then at last he seemed to come back to himself, and saying — so it is reported — only these words, "What, into the camp too?" he took off his general's dress, put on clothes better suited to a fugitive, and stole away. What became of him afterward — how he took refuge in Egypt, and was murdered there — we tell in his own Life.

When Caesar came to look over Pompey's camp and saw some of his opponents lying dead on the ground and others still dying, he groaned and said, "They would have it so. They drove me to this. After winning so many wars, I — Gaius Caesar — would have stood condemned in their courts if I had dismissed my army." Pollio says Caesar spoke these words in Latin at the time, and that he himself later wrote them down in Greek; he adds that most of those killed in the taking of the camp were servants, and that no more than six thousand soldiers fell. Most of the infantry taken prisoner Caesar folded into his own legions, and he granted a free pardon to many distinguished men — among them Brutus, who would later kill him. Brutus did not appear right away when the battle was over, which is said to have thrown Caesar into great anxiety for him; and his pleasure was just as great when Brutus presented himself alive.

Many prodigies were said to have foretold this victory, but the most remarkable on record happened at Tralles. In the temple of Victory there stood a statue of Caesar. The ground it stood on was naturally hard and solid, and the stone paving on top of it harder still — yet a palm tree, they say, shot up beside the statue's pedestal. And at Padua, one Gaius Cornelius, a man with the reputation of a good augur — a fellow citizen and acquaintance of Livy the historian — happened to be taking the auguries on the very day the battle was fought. First, as Livy tells it, he marked the exact moment of the fight, announcing to those around him that just then the battle had begun and the men were engaging. When he looked a second time and read the signs, he leapt up as if inspired and cried out, "Caesar, you are victorious!" The bystanders were astonished; but he took the garland from his head and swore he would never wear it again until the outcome had proved his art true. Livy insists this really happened.

As a memorial of his victory, Caesar gave the Thessalians their freedom, and then went in pursuit of Pompey. When he reached Asia, he made the Cnidians a free people as a favor to Theopompus, the collector of fables, and cut the taxes of the whole province of Asia by a third. He came to Alexandria to find Pompey already murdered. When Theodotus presented him with the head, Caesar turned away and would not look at it; he took only the signet ring, and wept. Every one of Pompey's friends the king of Egypt had seized as they wandered the country, Caesar took under his protection and offered his own friendship. He wrote to his friends at Rome that the greatest and sweetest pleasure of his victory was this: that he could go on saving the lives of fellow citizens who had fought against him.

As for the war in Egypt, some say it was dangerous, inglorious, and entirely unnecessary — fought for no reason but his passion for Cleopatra. Others blame the king's ministers, above all the eunuch Pothinus, the power behind the throne, who had just had Pompey killed and Cleopatra banished, and was now secretly plotting Caesar's murder — which is why, from that time on, Caesar took to sitting up whole nights, under the pretense of drinking parties, to guard his own life. In the open, meanwhile, Pothinus was insufferable, insulting Caesar in everything he said and did. When Caesar's soldiers were issued moldy, foul grain, Pothinus told them to be content with it, since they were eating at another man's expense. He had the royal table set with wooden and earthenware dishes, claiming Caesar had carried off all the gold and silver plate against a supposed debt. In fact, the reigning king's father had owed Caesar seventeen and a half million; Caesar had earlier forgiven the king's children the greater part of it, but now demanded ten million to maintain his army. Pothinus advised him to go away for the present and attend to his great affairs elsewhere — he would receive his money later, with thanks. Caesar replied that he had no need of Egyptians as his advisers, and shortly afterward, in secret, he sent for Cleopatra from her exile.
