Caesar

(Part 3 of 6)

The crossing itself met no resistance. Even the Suevi, the most warlike people in all Germany, would not face him; they gathered up their belongings and vanished into the deepest, most thickly wooded valleys they could find. Caesar burned the enemy's countryside, encouraged the tribes that had taken Rome's side, and after eighteen days in Germany marched back into Gaul. But it was his expedition to Britain that became the most famous proof of his daring. He was the first commander ever to bring a fleet into the western ocean, the first to sail an army out onto the Atlantic to make war. And the island he invaded was so remote, and the reports of its size so extravagant, that historians had actually argued over whether it existed at all — whether "Britain" was a real place or just a name, a fiction. By landing there, you could fairly say, Caesar carried the Roman empire beyond the limits of the known world. He crossed twice from the part of Gaul that faces the island, and in several battles he did the enemy more damage than he did himself any good, for the islanders were so miserably poor that they had nothing worth plundering. Unable to finish the war the way he wanted, he settled for taking hostages from the king, imposing a tribute, and leaving.

Back in Gaul, he found letters waiting for him, ready to be carried across the water — letters from his friends in Rome, telling him that his daughter was dead. Julia had died giving birth to Pompey's child. Caesar and Pompey were both shattered by her death, and their friends were hardly less shaken, because everyone understood that the alliance which had kept the ailing republic at peace was now broken. The baby, too, died within a few days of its mother. As for Julia's body, the people took it — over the objections of the tribunes — carried it to the Field of Mars, and held her funeral there. That is where her remains lie.

Caesar's army had by now grown so large that he had to scatter it among a number of camps for the winter, and he himself went off to Italy, as he usually did. In his absence, all of Gaul erupted at once. Great armies swept across the country, attacked the Roman winter quarters, and tried to storm the forts where the legions lay. The largest and strongest body of rebels, under the command of Ambiorix, wiped out Cotta and Titurius and every man with them, and then a force sixty thousand strong laid siege to the legion commanded by Cicero and very nearly took it by storm — every Roman soldier wounded, all of them spent, holding on by an effort beyond their natural strength. Caesar was far away when the news reached him. He instantly scraped together seven thousand men and raced to relieve Cicero. The besiegers knew he was coming and marched out to meet him, fully confident of swallowing so small a force. Caesar, to feed their overconfidence, kept seeming to avoid battle, falling back and back until he found ground suited for a few men to fight many, and there he made camp. He forbade his soldiers to attack. He ordered them instead to build the ramparts higher and barricade the gates, so that everything about the camp said fear — and the enemy's contempt grew accordingly. At last, swaggering and disordered, sure of themselves, they came on to assault the walls. Then Caesar came out, threw them into flight, and killed a great many of them.

That settled most of the uprisings in this part of Gaul, and through the winter Caesar went everywhere in person, watching sharply for any new trouble. Three fresh legions had arrived to replace the men he had lost — two of them lent by Pompey from his own command, the third newly raised in the Gaul along the Po. But the seeds of war had been quietly sown and scattered long before, by the most powerful men among those warrior nations, and now they broke out into the greatest and most dangerous war ever fought in that country. Consider the scale of it: the sheer numbers of young men in their prime, gathered and armed from every quarter; the vast sums of money collected to sustain the fight; the strength of the fortified towns; the near-impossibility of the terrain. It was winter. The rivers were frozen, the forests buried in snow, the plains flooded; in some places the roads disappeared under snowdrifts, in others the overflowing of marshes and streams made every route uncertain. All of it seemed to make a move against the rebels simply impossible. Many tribes had risen together, chief among them the Arverni and the Carnutes, and supreme command of the war belonged to Vercingetorix — a man whose own father the Gauls had put to death on suspicion of aiming at absolute power.

Vercingetorix divided his forces into several armies, set officers over each, and brought over to his side all the surrounding country as far as the river Arar. He had intelligence, too, of the opposition Caesar was now facing in Rome, and his plan was to raise all Gaul at once. Had he done it only a little later, when Caesar was tied down in the civil wars, Italy would have known a terror not felt since the Cimbri. But Caesar had, above all men who ever lived, the gift of using every advantage a war offered — and above all the gift of seizing the right moment. The instant he heard of the revolt he turned around and went back the way he had come, and the sheer speed of that march, through such a brutal season, showed the barbarians exactly what kind of army was coming for them: an unstoppable one. In the time it seemed scarcely believable that a courier could have arrived with a message from Caesar, Caesar himself arrived with his whole army — ravaging the country, reducing strongholds, taking towns, receiving the tribes that came over to him. So it went until even the Aedui declared against him. They had always called themselves brothers of the Roman people and had been showered with honors, and when they joined the rebels, Caesar's army felt the discouragement badly. He pulled out of that region and crossed the country of the Lingones, making for the territory of the Sequani, who were friendly and who stood like a rampart between Italy and the rest of Gaul. There the enemy fell on him and surrounded him with many tens of thousands of men. Caesar was eager enough to fight, and after a long and bloody struggle he won, on the whole, a complete victory — though at first he seems to have taken a beating, for the Arverni still show visitors a short sword hanging in one of their temples which they claim was captured from Caesar. Caesar himself saw it there later, and smiled, and when his friends urged him to have it taken down, he refused. It had been dedicated to the gods, and he considered it sacred.

After the defeat, most of the survivors fled with their king into the town of Alesia. Caesar besieged it, though the height of the walls and the number of defenders made the place look impregnable — and meanwhile, from outside the siege lines, he faced a danger greater than words can express. The best fighting men of Gaul, picked from every nation and well armed, were converging on Alesia to relieve it: three hundred thousand of them. And inside the town were no fewer than a hundred and seventy thousand more. Caught and squeezed between two such forces, Caesar had to build two walls — one facing the town, the other facing the relief army — because he knew that if the two enemies ever joined hands, he was finished. The danger he ran at Alesia earned him fame, and deservedly, on many counts; no other contest ever gave him such scope to display his boldness and his generalship. One marvels that he could engage and defeat so many thousands outside the town without the men inside ever noticing. But stranger still: even the Romans guarding the inner wall, the one facing the town, knew nothing of the battle. They learned of the victory only when they heard the crying of the men and the wailing of the women in Alesia, who had looked out and seen, in the distance, the Romans carrying into their camp great numbers of shields decorated with gold and silver, breastplates soaked in blood, drinking cups, and tents of Gallic workmanship. That is how quickly so vast an army dissolved and vanished — like a ghost, like a dream — with most of them killed where they stood. At last, after giving Caesar and themselves plenty of trouble, the defenders of Alesia surrendered. And Vercingetorix, the mainspring of the whole war, put on his finest armor, decked out his horse, and rode out through the gates. He made a circle around Caesar, who sat waiting; then he dismounted, stripped off his armor, and sat down quietly at Caesar's feet, and there he stayed until he was led away — a prisoner reserved for the triumph.

Caesar had long since decided to bring Pompey down, and Pompey, for that matter, had decided the same about him. Crassus, whom they had both feared, and whose existence had kept the peace between them, was now dead in Parthia. From that point the logic was simple: if either man wanted to be the greatest in Rome, he had only to destroy the other; and if either wanted to avoid being destroyed, his only move was to strike first. Pompey had come to this fear only recently. Until lately he had despised Caesar, figuring it would be no great trouble to put down a man he himself had raised up. But Caesar had been planning for this contest from the beginning. Like a champion wrestler, he had withdrawn to train for the match in private. The Gallic wars were his training ground: there he hardened his soldiers and piled up glory with his victories, until men spoke of him as a rival worthy of comparison with Pompey. And he let no advantage slip — not the openings Pompey gave him, not the openings the times gave him, not the rot in Rome's own government. In Rome, candidates for office bought votes in the open, shamelessly, with cash on the table; and the people, once paid, fought for their paymasters not with ballots but with bows, swords, and slings. More than once the voting ground was left stained with blood and littered with the dead, and the city drifted into pure anarchy, like a ship swept along with no one at the helm. Sensible people could only hope that after such a storm of madness and disorder, things might end in nothing worse than a monarchy. Some said it openly: only a monarchy could cure the state now, and the remedy ought to be taken from the gentlest physician available — meaning Pompey. Pompey made a show of declining, but in reality he worked with everything he had to be named dictator. Cato saw through him, and persuaded the senate to make him sole consul instead — offering him a more legal sort of monarchy, so that he would stop demanding the dictatorship. On top of this they voted to extend his provinces. He had two, Spain and all of Africa, which he governed through deputies while maintaining armies at the public expense — a thousand talents a year out of the treasury.

At this Caesar sent to Rome and applied, in his turn, for a consulship and an extension of his own provinces. Pompey at first said nothing, but Marcellus and Lentulus — men who had always hated Caesar — opposed him, and did everything, fair or foul, to disgrace and insult him. The people of New Comum, a colony Caesar had recently planted in Gaul, were stripped of their Roman citizenship; and Marcellus, who was then consul, had one of that town's senators, who happened to be in Rome, publicly whipped — telling him the lashes were a mark to prove he was no Roman citizen, and that he should go home and show them to Caesar. After Marcellus's consulship ended, Caesar opened the floodgates of the wealth he had taken in Gaul and let it pour over the public men of Rome. He paid off the enormous debts of the tribune Curio. He gave the consul Paulus fifteen hundred talents, with which Paulus built the splendid basilica beside the forum, the courthouse that replaced the old Fulvian one. Now Pompey was alarmed. Openly, in his own person and through his friends, he pushed to have a successor appointed to Caesar's command, and he sent to demand back the soldiers he had lent him for the Gallic wars. Caesar sent them back — with a parting gift of two hundred and fifty drachmas to every man. But the officer who conducted them home to Pompey spread ugly and dishonest reports about Caesar among the people, and fed Pompey himself on flattering delusions: Caesar's own army longed for Pompey, he said; and while envy and bad government made Pompey's position difficult at home, out there the army was his for the asking — let them once cross into Italy and they would declare for him at once, so sick were they of Caesar's endless campaigns, and so suspicious that he aimed at monarchy. On this diet Pompey grew fat with confidence. He neglected every military preparation, since he feared no danger, and fought Caesar with nothing but speeches and votes — which Caesar cared nothing about. The story goes that one of Caesar's captains, sent on an errand to Rome, was standing outside the senate house when he heard that the senate refused to give Caesar an extension of his command. The captain clapped his hand on the hilt of his sword and said, "But this shall."

And yet the demands Caesar was making had the fairest look of justice imaginable. He proposed that he lay down his arms — provided Pompey did the same — and that both become private citizens and let the public reward each man's services as it saw fit. To disarm Caesar while confirming Pompey in all his power, he argued, was simply to establish one man in the very tyranny they accused the other of plotting. When Curio laid these proposals before the people in Caesar's name, they cheered him to the sky; some threw garlands at him and sent him off crowned with flowers, like a winning athlete. Antony, who was tribune, produced a letter from Caesar on the question and read it aloud, though the consuls did all they could to prevent it. But Scipio, Pompey's father-in-law, moved in the senate that if Caesar did not lay down his arms by a fixed day, he should be declared an enemy of the state. And when the consuls put the questions — should Pompey dismiss his soldiers? should Caesar disband his? — hardly anyone voted yes to the first, and nearly everyone voted yes to the second. But then Antony moved that both men give up their commands together, and all but a very few agreed to that. Scipio raged; the consul Lentulus shouted that against a robber you need weapons, not votes; and the senate adjourned, its members putting on mourning clothes in grief at the quarrel.

Then more letters came from Caesar, and their terms seemed more moderate still. He offered to give up everything else and keep only Gaul-within-the-Alps, Illyricum, and two legions, until he could stand for his second consulship. Cicero the orator, just back from Cilicia, worked hard for a reconciliation and softened Pompey up; Pompey was ready to yield on everything but the soldiers. At last Cicero persuaded Caesar's friends to settle for the provinces and a mere six thousand men, and close the quarrel there. Pompey was inclined to accept. But the consul Lentulus would not hear of it, and went so far as to drive Antony and Curio out of the senate house with insults — handing Caesar the most plausible grievance he could have asked for, and one perfectly designed to inflame his soldiers: look, he could say, at two officers of rank and authority forced to flee Rome in a hired carriage, dressed as slaves. For that is how they had to disguise themselves to get out of the city.

Caesar at that moment had no more than three hundred cavalry and five thousand infantry with him. The rest of his army was still beyond the Alps, to be brought up later by officers already under orders. But he judged that the opening move of his enterprise did not need large forces — what it needed was to be made suddenly, with a boldness that would stun his enemies. It would be easier, he reckoned, to throw them into panic by doing what they never expected than to beat them in a fair fight after his preparations had put them on guard. So he ordered his captains and officers to take Ariminum — a large city of his province, the first inside Italy — with their swords alone, no other weapons, and with as little disturbance and bloodshed as possible. He gave the command of the force to Hortensius. He himself spent the day in public, standing around watching gladiators exercise. A little before evening he bathed and dressed, went into the hall, and chatted for a while with the guests he had invited to dinner. When it was beginning to get dark, he rose from the table, politely asked the company to wait for him until he came back, and left — having already told a few close friends, privately, to follow him, not all together but by different roads. He climbed into one of the hired carriages and drove off at first in a different direction entirely, then turned toward Ariminum.

When he came to the river Rubicon, which divides Gaul-within-the-Alps from the rest of Italy, his thoughts began to work on him. Here was the edge of the danger. He wavered, turning over in his mind the sheer size of what he was about to attempt. He slowed, then ordered a halt, and sat a long while in silence, revolving the decision this way and that, changing his mind and changing it again. This was the moment his purpose hung most in the balance. Then he began to talk it through with the friends who were with him — Asinius Pollio was one — reckoning up the calamities that his crossing of that river would let loose on mankind, and what the men of the future would say of it. At last, on a kind of passion, he threw calculation aside and flung himself at whatever was coming. Speaking the words men always use on the brink of a desperate gamble — "The die is cast." — he took the river. Once across, he moved at full speed, and before daybreak he reached Ariminum and took it. It is said that the night before he crossed, he had a monstrous dream: he dreamed that he lay with his own mother.

With Ariminum taken, it was as if wide gates had swung open to let war flood in over every land and sea alike; with the border of the province, the boundaries of the laws had been crossed too. And the panic that followed was not the usual kind, with men and women fleeing in terror from one Italian town to another. This time, you would have said, the towns themselves tore loose from their foundations and fled to one another for shelter. Rome itself was overrun, as if by a flood, with people pouring in from all the surrounding country. No magistrate could govern the city, no orator's eloquence could quiet it; it came within an inch of wrecking itself on the violence of its own storm. Everywhere the most violent and contradictory passions collided. Those who welcomed the coming upheaval made little effort to hide it, and when they ran into the frightened and dejected men of the other party — as in so great a city they constantly did — their loud confidence in what was coming started quarrels on the spot. Pompey, unsettled enough on his own account, was battered from every side. Some told him he was only getting what he deserved for arming Caesar against himself and against the state. Others blamed him for letting Lentulus insult Caesar just when Caesar was making generous concessions and offering reasonable terms of peace. Favonius told him to go ahead and stamp his foot — because once, talking big in the senate, Pompey had told the senators not to trouble themselves with preparations for war: he himself, with one stamp of his foot, would fill all Italy with soldiers. Even then, Pompey still had more forces than Caesar. But he was never allowed to think his own thoughts. Buried under false reports and alarms — the enemy at the gates, the enemy sweeping all before him — he gave way and let himself be carried off by the general panic. He issued an edict declaring the city in a state of anarchy, and left it, ordering the senate to follow and declaring that anyone who stayed behind was choosing tyranny over country and liberty.

The consuls fled at once, without even performing the sacrifices custom required before a journey. Most of the senators fled too, snatching up whatever of their own property came to hand, in as much haste as if they were looting their neighbors' houses. Some who until then had been warm supporters of Caesar's cause lost their heads in the general terror and let themselves be swept along in the current, though it did them no good at all. It was a pitiful sight: the city tossed in the tumult like a ship abandoned by her pilots, left to run on any rock in her path as chance might steer her. And yet, miserable as the flight was, people counted exile itself as home because Pompey was there, and fled from Rome as if Rome had become Caesar's camp. Even Labienus deserted — Labienus, who had been one of Caesar's closest friends and his lieutenant, who had fought at his side through all the Gallic wars — he now abandoned him and went over to Pompey. Caesar sent his money and his baggage after him. Then he sat down before Corfinium, which was held by thirty cohorts under the command of Domitius. Domitius, despairing of the defense, asked a physician among his attendants for poison, took the dose, and drank it, expecting it to finish him. But shortly afterward, when he heard that Caesar was treating his prisoners with extraordinary clemency, he began to lament his misfortune and curse the haste of his decision. His physician then set his mind at rest: what he had swallowed was a sleeping draught, not poison. Overjoyed, Domitius got up from his bed, went straight to Caesar, and gave him his hand in pledge — and then, later, went over to Pompey again anyway. When news of all this reached Rome, it calmed the people who remained there, and some of those who had fled came back.
