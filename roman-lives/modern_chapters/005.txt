Caesar

(Part 6 of 6)

All this talk turned people's thoughts toward Marcus Brutus. On his father's side he was believed to descend from the first Brutus, the man who had driven out the kings; on his mother's side he came from the Servilii, another noble house; and he was Cato's nephew and son-in-law besides. But the honors and kindnesses Caesar had heaped on him had dulled whatever appetite he might have had for overthrowing the new monarchy himself. Caesar had not only pardoned him after Pompey's defeat at Pharsalia, and pardoned many of his friends at his request — he trusted him, personally and particularly. Brutus held the most honorable praetorship of the year, and was marked out for the consulship four years later, chosen over Cassius, his rival for the post. When the choice was being weighed, Caesar is said to have remarked that Cassius had the stronger claim — but he could not pass over Brutus. Later, when the conspiracy was already in motion and people came to him with accusations against Brutus, he refused to listen. Laying a hand on his own body, he told the informers, "Brutus will wait for this skin of mine" — meaning that Brutus deserved power for his virtue, but would never be base and ungrateful to get it. Meanwhile, the people who wanted a change, and who looked on Brutus as the only man — or at least the likeliest man — to bring it about, did not dare speak to him directly. Instead they came at night and left notes on the tribunal chair where he sat to hear cases: "You are asleep, Brutus." "You are no longer Brutus." When Cassius saw Brutus's ambition beginning to stir at this, he pressed him harder than before. Cassius had a private grudge of his own against Caesar, for reasons we have mentioned in the Life of Brutus. And Caesar suspected him too. He once said to his friends, "What do you think Cassius is aiming at? I don't like him — he looks so pale." Another time, when he was told that Antony and Dolabella were plotting against him, he said it was not the fat, luxurious men he feared, but the pale, lean ones — meaning Cassius and Brutus.

Fate, it seems, is not so much unexpected as unavoidable. Strange signs and apparitions were reported in the days before the event. Lights in the sky, crashing noises in the night, wild birds settling in the forum — perhaps those are not worth much notice in a case as great as this. But Strabo the philosopher records that crowds of men were seen fighting one another, all of them seemingly ablaze; and that flame streamed from the hand of a soldier's servant, so that the onlookers were sure he was burning — yet when it passed, he was unhurt. When Caesar himself offered sacrifice, the victim's heart was missing — a very bad omen, since no living creature can exist without a heart. And many writers tell us that a soothsayer had given him warning of grave danger to come: "Beware the Ides of March." When the day arrived and Caesar was on his way to the senate, he met the soothsayer and greeted him with a joke: "The Ides of March have come." "Yes," the man answered calmly, "they have come — but they have not gone." The evening before the assassination, Caesar dined with Marcus Lepidus, and as he reclined at the table signing letters, as his habit was, the conversation turned to the question of which kind of death is best. Before anyone else could speak, Caesar answered, "A sudden one."

That night, as he lay in bed with his wife, every door and window in the house flew open at once. Startled by the noise and by the light that broke into the room, he sat up, and by the moonlight he could see Calpurnia fast asleep — but murmuring broken words and groaning in her dreams. She was dreaming, in fact, that she was weeping over Caesar, holding his butchered body in her arms. Others say her dream was different: that the ornamental gable the senate had voted to raise on Caesar's house as a mark of grandeur — Livy tells us this — was tumbling down, and that this was what made her weep and cry out. Either way, when morning came she begged Caesar not to go out if he could possibly avoid it, and to postpone the senate to another day. If he thought nothing of her dreams, she begged him at least to consult his fate by sacrifices and every other kind of divination. And Caesar himself, it seems, was not free of suspicion and fear. He had never before known Calpurnia to show any womanish superstition, and here she was in real terror. When the priests reported that they had killed several victims and found every one of them inauspicious, he made up his mind to send Antony to dismiss the senate.

At this critical moment Decimus Brutus, surnamed Albinus — a man Caesar trusted so completely that he had named him his second heir, and who was nevertheless in the conspiracy with the other Brutus and with Cassius — grew afraid that if Caesar put the senate off to another day, the plot might leak out. So he scoffed at the diviners and told Caesar he was handing the senate a perfect excuse to say he had insulted them. They had assembled at his summons; they were ready to vote unanimously that he be declared king of all the provinces outside Italy, free to wear a diadem anywhere by land or sea except in Italy itself. If someone were sent now to tell them to go home and come back whenever Calpurnia happened to have better dreams, what would his enemies say? Who would listen patiently while his friends tried to argue that his rule was not arbitrary and tyrannical? And if he was truly determined to treat the day as unlucky, then it was more gracious to go in person and adjourn the senate himself. With these words Brutus took Caesar by the hand and led him out. He had not gone far from the door when another man's servant tried to reach him, but could not get through the crowd pressing around him. So the servant forced his way into the house and put himself in Calpurnia's hands, begging her to keep him safe until Caesar came home — he had matters of great importance to tell him.

Artemidorus, a Cnidian who taught Greek logic, and who through that profession had come to know Brutus and his circle well enough to learn the secret, brought Caesar a small written scroll containing the essentials of what he had to reveal. He had noticed that Caesar handed every paper he received straight to the attendants around him; so he pushed in as close as he could and said, "Read this one, Caesar — alone, and quickly. It contains matters of great importance that concern you personally." Caesar took it and tried several times to read it, but was blocked at every attempt by the crush of people who came to speak with him. It was the one paper he kept in his own hand, and he was still holding it when he entered the senate. Some say it was another man who gave Caesar this note, and that Artemidorus never got near him at all — the crowd kept him off the whole way.

All of that, you might say, could have happened by chance. But the place appointed for the scene of the murder — the hall where the senate met that day — held a statue of Pompey, and was one of the buildings Pompey himself had raised and dedicated, along with his theater, for public use. It is hard not to see some higher power guiding the action and calling it to that particular spot. Cassius, just before the act, is said to have turned toward Pompey's statue and silently asked for its help — Cassius, who inclined to the doctrines of Epicurus, which leave no room for such prayers. But the occasion, and the danger of the instant, carried him past all his reasoning and filled him with something like inspiration. As for Antony, who was loyal to Caesar and a physically powerful man, Decimus Brutus Albinus kept him outside the hall, detaining him with a long conversation invented for the purpose.

When Caesar entered, the senate rose to its feet in respect. Some of Brutus's confederates gathered around his chair and stood behind it; others came forward to meet him, pretending to add their voices to the petition of Tillius Cimber on behalf of his exiled brother, and they trailed Caesar with their pleas all the way to his seat. He sat down and refused the request, and when they kept pressing, he began to scold them one by one for their persistence — at which Tillius seized his toga with both hands and yanked it down from his neck. That was the signal. Casca struck the first blow, a cut to the neck, neither deep nor dangerous — the stroke of a man badly shaken, no doubt, at the opening of so enormous a piece of daring. Caesar spun around, seized the dagger, and held on. In the same instant both men cried out — Caesar, in Latin, "Casca, you villain, what are you doing?" and Casca, in Greek, to his brother: "Brother, help!"

At that first assault, the senators who knew nothing of the plot were so stunned, so frozen with horror at what they were watching, that they could neither run nor come to Caesar's aid — they could not even make a sound. But the men who had come prepared closed around him on every side, naked daggers in their hands. Whichever way he turned he met a blow. The blades were leveled at his face and his eyes; he was hemmed in like a wild animal in the hunters' nets. For they had agreed that every one of them would strike him, every one would flesh his blade in the blood — which is why even Brutus gave him one thrust, in the groin. Some say Caesar fought all the rest of them, twisting his body away from the blows and shouting for help; but when he saw Brutus's sword drawn, he pulled his toga over his face and gave up the struggle. He fell — whether by chance, or pushed that way by his killers — against the pedestal of Pompey's statue, which was soaked with his blood. So Pompey himself seemed to preside over the vengeance taken on his old enemy, who now lay at his feet, breathing out his life through his wounds. He had received, they say, twenty-three. And many of the conspirators wounded one another, so many blows aimed at a single body.

When Caesar was dead, Brutus stepped forward to give the reasons for what they had done, but the senate would not hear him. The senators bolted for the doors, and their flight filled the city with such alarm and confusion that some men barred up their houses while others abandoned their counters and shops. Everyone was running — some toward the place, to see the terrible sight; others away from it, having seen. Antony and Lepidus, Caesar's most faithful friends, slipped off quietly and hid in the houses of friends. But Brutus and his followers, still hot from the deed itself, marched in a body from the senate house up to the Capitol with drawn swords — not like men trying to escape, but with an air of confidence and assurance — calling on the people as they went to take back their liberty, and inviting the distinguished men they met to join them. And some did join the procession and walk up with them, as if they too had been in the conspiracy and could claim a share of the glory — Gaius Octavius, for instance, and Lentulus Spinther. Their vanity cost them dearly afterward: Antony and the young Octavian put them to death, and they lost the honor they had claimed along with their lives, since no one ever believed they had actually been involved. Even the men who punished them were not avenging the deed — they were punishing the wish. The next day Brutus and the rest came down from the Capitol, and Brutus made a speech to the people, who listened without showing either pleasure or resentment. Their silence said plainly enough that they pitied Caesar and respected Brutus. The senate passed a general amnesty for what was past and set about reconciling all parties. It decreed that Caesar should be worshipped as a god, and that nothing he had enacted while in power, down to the smallest measure, should be revoked; at the same time it gave Brutus and his followers provinces and other important posts. So everyone now assumed that matters were settled, and settled as happily as they could possibly be.

But then Caesar's will was opened, and it was found that he had left a generous legacy to every single Roman citizen. And then his body was carried through the forum, mangled with wounds. After that the crowd could no longer be kept within any bounds of order. They heaped up benches, railings, and tables into a pyre, laid the corpse on it, and burned it where it stood. Then, snatching burning brands from the pile, some ran to set fire to the conspirators' houses, while others ranged up and down the city hunting for the men themselves, to tear them to pieces. They found not one of them; the conspirators had taken good care to secure themselves.

One of Caesar's friends, a man named Cinna, happened to have a strange dream the night before. He dreamed that Caesar invited him to dinner, and that when he declined, Caesar took him by the hand and pulled him along, forcing him though he hung back. Now, hearing that Caesar's body was burning in the forum, Cinna got up and went there to pay his respects, though the dream gave him misgivings and he was running a fever. Someone in the crowd saw him and asked a neighbor who he was; the name, once learned, was passed from mouth to mouth; and in a moment it was taken for certain that this was one of Caesar's murderers — for there was in fact a conspirator named Cinna, another man entirely. Believing this was he, the mob seized him and tore him limb from limb on the spot.

That, more than anything, frightened Brutus and Cassius, and within a few days they withdrew from the city. What they afterward did and suffered, and how they died, is told in the Life of Brutus. Caesar died in his fifty-sixth year, having outlived Pompey by little more than four. The empire and the power he had chased his whole life through so much danger he did at last achieve, with enormous difficulty — and reaped from it nothing but the name itself, and a glory that drew envy from his fellow citizens. But the great genius that had watched over him all his life remained, even after his death, as the avenger of his murder, pursuing the men who had a hand in it across every sea and every land, letting not one escape — reaching every man who had either joined in the killing or in any way encouraged the plot.

Of the merely human coincidences, the most remarkable was the one that befell Cassius: defeated at Philippi, he killed himself with the same dagger he had used against Caesar. Of the supernatural signs, the greatest were the great comet, which blazed bright for seven nights after Caesar's death and then disappeared, and the dimming of the sun, whose disk stayed pale and dull for that whole year, rising without its usual radiance and giving only a weak and feeble heat. The air hung damp and heavy for want of stronger rays to clear it, and the fruits of that year never properly ripened — they withered and dropped half-formed for lack of warmth. But above all, the phantom that appeared to Brutus showed that the murder was not pleasing to the gods. The story goes like this.

Brutus was about to take his army across from Abydos to the mainland on the other side. The night before, he lay in his tent as usual — not asleep, but thinking over his situation and what might be coming. Of all the men who have ever commanded armies, they say, he needed sleep the least, with the greatest natural capacity for staying awake and working without rest. He thought he heard a noise at the door of the tent, and looking that way by the light of his lamp, which was almost out, he saw a terrifying figure — shaped like a man, but unnaturally tall, with a grim and forbidding face. He was frightened at first; but when the thing neither did nor said anything, only stood silently at his bedside, he asked who it was. The specter answered, "I am your evil genius, Brutus. You will see me at Philippi." And Brutus answered steadily, "Then I will see you," and the apparition vanished at once. When the time came, he drew up his army at Philippi against Antony and Octavian, and in the first battle he won the day, routing the enemy in front of him and plundering Octavian's camp. The night before the second battle, the same phantom came to him again. It said nothing this time; but Brutus understood that his hour had arrived, and threw himself into the thickest danger of the fighting. Yet he did not die in the battle. When he saw his men beaten, he climbed to the top of a rock, set his naked sword against his breast — helped, they say, by a friend who drove the thrust home — and so met his death.
