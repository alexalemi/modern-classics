Brutus

(Part 2 of 5)

Brutus now carried the knowledge that the best men in Rome — the noblest in birth, in character, in courage — were staking everything on him, and as he ran through all the dangers they were walking into, the weight of it began to tell. In public he mastered himself as well as he could, keeping his uneasiness locked away and his face composed. But at home, and above all at night, he was a different man. Sometimes the working of his cares jolted him out of sleep against his will; sometimes he lay awake for hours, turning his difficulties over and over. His wife, lying beside him, could not help noticing that he was full of some unusual trouble, and that he was wrestling with a question both dangerous and hard to resolve.

Portia, as was said before, was the daughter of Cato. Brutus, her cousin, had married her when she was still very young — not a girl, though, but a widow, for her first husband had died leaving her a son named Bibulus; a little book of his, the Memoirs of Brutus, survives to this day. Portia was devoted to philosophy, deeply in love with her husband, and full of a courage that had thought behind it, and she resolved not to pry into Brutus's secrets until she had first put herself to the test. She sent all her attendants out of her room, took a little knife of the kind used for trimming nails, and gave herself a deep gash in the thigh. Blood poured out, and soon afterward came violent pain and a shivering fever from the wound. Brutus was beside himself with worry for her, and at the very height of her pain she spoke to him: "I am Cato's daughter, Brutus, and I was brought into your house not like a concubine, to share only your bed and your table, but to be your partner in everything — your good fortune and your bad alike. On your side of the marriage I have no complaint. But on mine, what proof of my love, what satisfaction can I offer you, if I may not share your hidden griefs, or be admitted to the counsels that call for secrecy and trust? I know women are supposed to be too weak by nature to be trusted with a secret. But surely, Brutus, a good birth, a good upbringing, and the company of honorable people count for something in the forming of character. I can say this: I am the daughter of Cato and the wife of Brutus. Before now I put less confidence in those titles than I do today — for I have tested myself, and I find I can defy pain." With that she showed him her wound and told him of the trial she had made of her own constancy. Brutus was astonished. He lifted his hands to heaven and begged the gods to stand by his enterprise, so that he might show himself a husband worthy of such a wife as Portia. Then he set about comforting her.

A meeting of the senate was called at which Caesar was expected to appear, and the conspirators agreed this was their opportunity. There they could all gather without drawing suspicion, and there, they hoped, the noblest and leading men of the state, assembled in one place, would stand up the moment the great deed was done and claim the common liberty. Even the setting seemed heaven-sent. The senate was to meet in a portico attached to the theater, with a large recess in which stood a statue of Pompey, put up by the state when Pompey beautified that quarter of the city with the porticos and the theater. To this place the senate was summoned for the middle of March — the Ides of March, as the Romans call the day — as if some power greater than human were steering Caesar there, to meet his punishment for the death of Pompey.

As soon as it was light, Brutus went out with a dagger that no one but his wife knew he carried. The rest met at Cassius's house, and together they escorted Cassius's son down to the forum — this was the day the boy was to put on the toga of manhood, as it is called. From there they all went to Pompey's porch and waited, expecting Caesar to come to the senate without delay. And here, more than anywhere, anyone who had known what these men intended would have marveled at their unshaken calm and their steady nerve on the edge of so dangerous an undertaking. Several of them were praetors, obliged by their office to hear and decide cases, and they not only listened patiently to everyone who came before them pleading against one another, as if they had nothing else on their minds — they decided the cases with as much precision and good judgment as they had shown attention in hearing them. When one man refused to accept Brutus's ruling and appealed loudly to Caesar, with much shouting and calling of witnesses, Brutus looked around at the bystanders and said, "Caesar does not hinder me, nor will he hinder me, from acting according to the laws."

Yet a whole string of unnerving accidents was thrown in their way by pure chance. First and worst was Caesar's long delay: the day was wearing on, and he was being kept at home by his wife, forbidden to go out by the soothsayers because of some defect in his sacrifice. Then a man came up to Casca, one of the company, took him by the hand, and said, "You hid the secret from us, but Brutus has told me everything." While Casca stood there stunned, the man laughed and went on, "How did you get rich so fast, that you can afford to stand for aedile?" That was how close Casca came to letting the secret slip, on nothing more than the ambiguity of a phrase. Next Popilius Laenas, a senator, greeted Brutus and Cassius more warmly than usual, leaned in, and whispered, "My prayers are with you, that you may accomplish what you have in mind — and my advice is not to delay, for the thing is no secret now." Then he walked away, leaving them deeply afraid that the plot had leaked. Meanwhile a messenger came running from Brutus's house with the news that his wife was dying. For Portia, strung tight with waiting for the event and unable to bear the weight of her anxiety, could hardly keep herself indoors. At every little noise or cry she would start up like a woman in a bacchic frenzy, demanding of everyone who came in from the forum what Brutus was doing, and sending out one messenger after another. At last, after the long waiting, her body simply gave out. Her mind was overwhelmed by doubt and fear; she lost her hold on herself and began to faint. There was no time even to reach her room — sitting where she was among her women, she was seized by a sudden swoon and a great stupor; her color drained away and her speech failed entirely. At the sight her women screamed, neighbors came running to Brutus's door to ask what was wrong, and the report quickly spread that Portia was dead — though in fact, with her women's help, she revived after a little while and came to herself again. When the news reached Brutus he was badly shaken, and no wonder; but he did not let his private grief carry him away from his public purpose.

For now came word that Caesar was on his way, carried in a litter. Discouraged by the bad omens of his sacrifice, he had decided to take up no serious business that day but to put everything off, pleading illness. The moment he stepped out of the litter, Popilius Laenas — the same man who a little earlier had wished Brutus success in his undertaking — went up to him and talked with him at length, Caesar standing still all the while and appearing to listen closely. The conspirators (to give them that name) could not hear what was said; but guessing, from what their own consciences told them, that this conversation was the betrayal of their plot, they lost heart again, and looking at one another they agreed from each other's faces that they must not wait to be arrested but should kill themselves at once. Cassius and some of the others already had their hands on the daggers under their robes and were drawing them out when Brutus, watching Laenas's expression and gestures closely, saw that the man was earnestly pleading a case, not making an accusation. He said nothing — too many outsiders were mingled in the crowd — but with a cheerful face he steadied Cassius. And after a little while Laenas kissed Caesar's hand and went his way, making it plain that all his talk had been about some private business of his own.

When the senators had gone in ahead to the chamber where they were to sit, the rest of the company gathered close around Caesar's chair as if they had some petition to bring him, and Cassius, turning his face toward Pompey's statue, is said to have invoked it, as though the marble could hear his prayers. Trebonius, meanwhile, engaged Antony in a long conversation at the door and kept him talking outside. As Caesar entered, the whole senate rose. As soon as he sat down, the conspirators crowded around him, and they put forward one of their own number, Tillius Cimber, to plead for his brother, who was in exile. The others joined their prayers to his, taking Caesar's hand and kissing his head and his chest. At first Caesar brushed their appeals aside; then, when they would not stop, he rose violently to his feet — and Tillius caught his toga with both hands and tore it down from his neck, which was the signal. Casca, standing behind him, drew his dagger and struck the first blow, a shallow wound near the shoulder. Caesar seized the handle of the dagger and cried out in Latin, "Casca, you villain, what are you doing?" — while Casca, in Greek, shouted to his brother, "Brother, help!" By now blows were coming at Caesar from many hands. He looked around for a way to break out — and then he saw Brutus with his dagger drawn against him. At that he let go of Casca's hand, covered his head with his robe, and gave his body up to their blows. They pressed in on him so eagerly, so many daggers hacking at once, that they cut one another; Brutus took a wound in the hand, and every one of them was smeared with blood.

With Caesar dead, Brutus stepped out into the middle of the chamber meaning to make a speech, calling on the senators to stay and take heart. Instead they bolted in terror and total disorder, jamming the doorway in the crush — though no one was chasing them. For the conspirators had firmly resolved to kill no one but Caesar, and to summon everyone else to liberty. Indeed, when they had been planning the deed, all the others had thought it essential to cut down Antony along with Caesar. He was an arrogant man, they argued, a would-be monarch, and one who by his easy familiarity had built up real influence with the soldiers — and on top of his naturally lofty and ambitious temper he now held the dignity of the consulship, as Caesar's colleague. But Brutus opposed the plan, insisting first on the plain injustice of it, and then holding out the hope that Antony might change. He refused to despair of so gifted and honorable a man, such a lover of glory: once Caesar was gone, Antony might well be stirred by the nobility of their attempt to seize the chance and join them in restoring his country's liberty. So Brutus saved Antony's life. But Antony, in the general panic, put on a plebeian's clothes and fled. Brutus and his party marched up to the Capitol, holding up their bloody hands and naked swords as they went and proclaiming liberty to the people. At first every street was full of shouting and cries, and the wild running back and forth of people caught up in the shock and passion of the moment only added to the tumult in the city. But no more blood was shed, and no shops were plundered, and so the senators and many of the people took courage and went up to the men on the Capitol. When a crowd had gathered, Brutus made a speech well calculated to win the people over and well suited to the state of affairs. They applauded and shouted for him to come down, and at that the whole party took confidence and descended into the forum. Most of them walked mixed in with the crowd, but many of the most eminent men escorted Brutus in the midst of them, with great honor, all the way from the Capitol, and set him on the rostra. At the sight of him the crowd — a jumbled mass, primed for a riot — was struck with awe, and waited for what he would say in order and in silence; and when he began to speak, they heard him out quietly and attentively. But that not everyone was pleased with the deed became clear soon enough: when Cinna began to speak and to accuse Caesar, the crowd erupted in sudden rage and abused him so violently that the whole party thought it best to withdraw again to the Capitol. There Brutus, expecting a siege, sent away the most eminent of those who had accompanied them up, thinking it wrong that men who had no share in the deed should share in the danger.

The next day the senate met in the temple of the Earth. Antony, Plancus, and Cicero gave speeches urging concord and a general amnesty, and it was decreed not only that the men should be free of all fear and danger, but that the consuls should consider what honors and dignities ought to be conferred on them. With that the senate adjourned; and when Antony had sent his son up to the Capitol as a hostage, Brutus and his company came down, and greetings and invitations passed back and forth through the whole gathering. Antony invited Cassius to dinner and entertained him; Lepidus did the same for Brutus; and the rest were taken in by their various friends and acquaintances. At daybreak the senate met again and voted thanks to Antony for having smothered a civil war in its cradle; then Brutus and his associates who were present received formal praise, and the provinces were assigned and distributed among them. Crete was allotted to Brutus, Africa to Cassius, Asia to Trebonius, Bithynia to Cimber, and to Decimus Brutus (Albinus) the Gaul that lies along the Po.

After this they took up the question of Caesar's will and the arrangements for his funeral. Antony asked that the will be read publicly, and that the body be given no mean or private burial, for fear of further inflaming the people. Cassius fought this fiercely, but Brutus yielded and gave his consent — and in doing so he seems to have made his second great mistake. Sparing Antony's life had already earned him some blame from his own party, as the setting up of a dangerous and formidable enemy against the conspiracy; now, in letting Antony manage the funeral, he fell into an error that was total and beyond recovery. For first, when the will revealed that Caesar had left seventy-five drachmas to every single Roman citizen, and had given the public his gardens beyond the Tiber, where the temple of Fortune now stands, the whole city was set ablaze with love for him and a passionate sense of what they had lost. And then, when the body was carried into the forum and Antony, as custom required, delivered the funeral oration in Caesar's praise, he saw the crowd moved by his words — and shifting into the register of grief, he unfolded Caesar's bloody garment and held it up, showing them how many places it was pierced, and the number of his wounds. After that there was nothing to be seen but chaos. Some screamed for the murderers to be killed; others — as had happened long before, in the days when Clodius led the people — tore the benches and tables out of the surrounding shops and heaped them together into one great funeral pyre, laid Caesar's body on it, and set it alight, there in the middle of a district ringed with temples and sacred precincts, so that the burning took on the look of a religious rite. As soon as the flames leaped up, the crowd surged in from every side, snatched half-burnt brands off the pyre, and ran through the city to set fire to the houses of Caesar's murderers. But the conspirators had fortified themselves well beforehand, and turned the danger back.

There was, however, a certain poet named Cinna, who had no part whatever in the conspiracy — he was, on the contrary, one of Caesar's friends. This man dreamed that Caesar invited him to supper, and that he declined, but Caesar pressed and urged him until at last, taking him by the hand, he led him into a deep, dark place, where Cinna was forced against his will to follow, in dread and bewilderment. After this vision he lay in a fever most of the night. Even so, in the morning, hearing that Caesar's body was to be carried out for burial, he was ashamed to stay away from the ceremony, and went out and joined the crowd — which Antony's speech had already whipped into fury. They caught sight of him, and mistaking him not for the Cinna he was but for the man who had lately denounced Caesar in a speech to the people, they fell on him and tore him limb from limb.

It was this, above all, together with the change Antony had worked in the city, that alarmed Brutus and his friends into leaving Rome for their own safety. They stopped first at Antium, planning to return as soon as the people's fury had burned itself out — which they expected would happen soon and easily in a crowd so unsettled, so easily swept by any sudden gust of passion, especially since they had the senate on their side. The senate, though it turned a blind eye to the men who had torn Cinna to pieces, made a strict search for those who had attacked the houses of Brutus's and Cassius's friends, and arrested them for punishment. By now the people, too, were growing unhappy with Antony, who they saw was setting up a kind of monarchy of his own. They longed for Brutus to come back, and they expected him to appear in person at the games and spectacles which, as praetor, he owed the public. But Brutus had intelligence that many of Caesar's old soldiers — men who had received lands and towns from him — were lying in wait, slipping into the city a few at a time, and he did not dare come himself. In his absence, though, the people got shows of the utmost magnificence and expense: he had bought up great numbers of wild beasts of every kind and ordered that none be returned or held back — all were to be spent freely on the public spectacles. He traveled to Naples himself to engage a large company of performers, and hearing of one Canutius, an actor everyone praised, he wrote to his friends to use every persuasion to bring him to Rome — for as a Greek, Canutius could not be compelled. He wrote to Cicero as well, begging him on no account to miss the shows.

This was how matters stood when everything was suddenly changed again by the arrival in Rome of the young Octavian. He was the son of Caesar's niece, and Caesar had adopted him and made him heir by his will. At the time of the murder he was pursuing his studies at Apollonia, where he was also waiting to meet Caesar on his way to the Parthian expedition he had planned. The moment he heard of Caesar's death he came to Rome; and to win the people's favor he took the name of Caesar for himself and paid out, to the letter, the money the will had left the citizens — and by that stroke he outmaneuvered Antony. By money and gifts, freely scattered among the soldiers, he gathered to his side a great number of the men who had served under Caesar. Cicero himself, out of sheer hatred of Antony, took the young man's part — and Brutus took this so hard that he lashed him in his letters. Cicero, he wrote, evidently had no objection to a master; what he feared was a master who disliked him; and all his speaking and writing in the young man's praise showed that his real aim was a comfortable slavery. "But our forefathers," Brutus wrote, "could not stomach even gentle masters." For his own part, he said, he had not yet finally decided between war and peace; but on one point he was fixed and settled — he would never be a slave. He was amazed, he added, that Cicero should dread the dangers of a civil war and not be far more afraid of a dishonorable and shameful peace; and that the reward Cicero sought for pulling down Antony's tyranny was the privilege of setting up the young Caesar as tyrant in his place. Such is the tone of Brutus's first letters to Cicero.
