Cicero

(Part 1 of 4)

Cicero's mother, Helvia, is generally agreed to have been well born and to have lived an honorable life. About his father, the reports run to opposite extremes. Some say he was the son of a fuller — a cloth-worker — and raised in that trade; others trace the family all the way back to Tullus Attius, a famous king of the Volscians who fought the Romans with some distinction. Whatever the truth, the first man of the family to carry the surname Cicero must have been somebody worth remembering, because his descendants refused to drop the name — in fact they were fond of it — even though people made fun of it. The Latin word cicer means chickpea, and that first Cicero apparently had a notch or dimple at the tip of his nose that looked like the cleft in a chickpea. Hence the name.

The Cicero whose story I am telling had spirit on this subject. When he first stood for office and got into politics, some of his friends advised him to drop the name or trade it for a better one. "No," he told them. "I intend to make the name Cicero more glorious than Scaurus or Catulus ever were." And later, when he was quaestor in Sicily and dedicated a piece of silver plate to the gods, he had his first two names, Marcus and Tullius, engraved on it — and then, as his little joke, told the craftsman to carve a chickpea where the third name should go. So much for the name.

As for his birth, the story goes that his mother delivered him without pain or labor, on the third of January — the same day on which, now, the magistrates of Rome offer prayers and sacrifices for the emperor. It is also said that a vision appeared to his nurse and foretold that the baby at her breast would grow up to be a great blessing to Rome. Omens like these can usually be written off as fancy and idle talk, but Cicero himself soon made them look like genuine prophecy. The moment he was old enough for lessons, his talent was so obvious, and his name among the schoolboys so great, that their fathers used to drop in at the school just to lay eyes on young Cicero and be able to say they had personally witnessed the quickness in learning he was famous for. The cruder ones got annoyed with their own sons when they saw the boys walking along with Cicero respectfully placed in the middle. He had exactly the temperament Plato asks of the scholar and philosopher — hungry for every kind of learning, turning up his nose at no branch of knowledge — but his special pull was toward poetry, and a poem he wrote as a boy, in tetrameter verse, called Pontius Glaucus, still survives. As he grew and worked at these arts more seriously, he came to be considered not just the best orator in Rome but the best poet too. The glory of his oratory holds up to this day, despite all the new fashions in speaking that have come along since; but his poetry has been forgotten and fallen completely out of favor, so many gifted poets having come after him.

When he finished his boyhood studies, he attached himself to Philo the Academic, the student of Clitomachus the Romans admired most for his eloquence and loved best for his character. At the same time he kept company with the Mucii, distinguished statesmen and leaders of the senate, and picked up his knowledge of the law from them. For a short stretch he even served in the army under Sulla, in the Marsian war — Rome's war with its Italian allies. But when he saw the state splitting into factions, and the factions dragging everything toward one-man rule, he withdrew into a quiet, contemplative life, keeping company with learned Greeks and devoting himself to his books, until Sulla got control of the government and things settled into some sort of order.

Around this time Chrysogonus, a freed slave of Sulla's, had put in a claim about the estate of a man said to have been executed in the proscriptions, and had bought it himself for 2,000 drachmas. Roscius, the dead man's son and heir, protested, and demonstrated that the estate was actually worth 250 talents. Sulla was furious at having his dealings questioned, and — with Chrysogonus managing the evidence — brought a prosecution against Roscius for the murder of his own father. Not one advocate would help the man; every one of them, dreading Sulla's cruelty, backed away from the case. Abandoned on all sides, the young man came to Cicero for refuge. Cicero's friends urged him on, telling him he would never have a finer or more honorable entry into public life. So he took the defense, won the case, and was widely admired for it.

But he was afraid of Sulla, so he traveled to Greece, putting the word out that he went for his health. And in fairness, there was something to that. He was thin and undernourished, with a stomach so weak that he could keep down only a light, plain diet, and that only late in the evening. His voice was loud and strong but harsh and untrained, and in the heat and vehemence of speaking he always drove it up to such a pitch that people genuinely worried for his health.

When he got to Athens he attended the lectures of Antiochus of Ascalon, and was charmed by the fluency and elegance of the man's language, though he did not approve of his novelties in doctrine. For Antiochus had by now broken away from the so-called New Academy and abandoned the school of Carneades — maybe won over by the argument from the plain evidence of the senses, maybe, as some say, pushed by rivalry and spite against the followers of Clitomachus and Philo into changing his views and adopting, on most points, the doctrine of the Stoics. Cicero preferred the New Academy and held to it, and he made himself a plan: if he found no place in public life, he would retire here, away from the courts and politics, and live out his days quietly in the study of philosophy.

Then came the news of Sulla's death. By now Cicero's body, rebuilt by exercise, had come into vigorous condition; his voice had been trained until it was sweet and full on the ear and properly matched to his constitution. His friends at Rome were writing letter after letter begging him to come back, and Antiochus too kept urging him toward public affairs. So he set to work again on rhetoric — the orator's instrument — and summoned up his political faculties, drilling himself in practice speeches and attending the most celebrated rhetoricians of the day. He sailed from Athens for Asia and Rhodes. Among the Asian masters he worked with Xenocles of Adramyttium, Dionysius of Magnesia, and Menippus of Caria; at Rhodes he studied oratory with Apollonius, the son of Molon, and philosophy with Posidonius. Apollonius, the story goes, knew no Latin, so he asked Cicero to declaim in Greek. Cicero was glad to do it, figuring his faults would be more easily pointed out that way. When he finished, the rest of the audience was astonished, and they fell over one another competing to praise him. But Apollonius, who had shown no excitement while listening, now sat lost in thought for a long while, saying nothing at all. And when Cicero began to look crushed, Apollonius said, "You have my praise and admiration, Cicero — and Greece has my pity. The only glories she had left were learning and eloquence, and now you are carrying those off to Rome as well."

So Cicero, full of expectation, was once more set on a political career — when an oracle took the edge off his ambition. He asked the god at Delphi how he could win the most glory, and the priestess answered: by making his own nature, and not the opinion of the crowd, the guide of his life. Because of that he went cautiously at first in Rome, hanging back from public office, and so was little regarded, and picked up the labels that Rome's crude and ignorant sorts hand out so freely — "the Greek," "the Scholar." But when his own appetite for fame, and his father's and relatives' eagerness, drove him seriously into pleading, he did not rise to the top by slow degrees. He blazed out at full brilliance all at once and left every other advocate at the bar far behind. Yet at first, they say, he was as weak in delivery as Demosthenes had been, and so he studied carefully under Roscius the comic actor and Aesop the tragic one. Of this Aesop they tell a story: playing Atreus on stage, plotting his revenge on Thyestes, he was so carried out of himself in the heat of the scene that he struck a servant running across the stage with his scepter — and killed him on the spot. That was the kind of intensity Cicero learned, and his delivery became no small part of what made his eloquence so persuasive. He used to mock orators who bellowed. "They shout because they cannot speak," he said, "the way lame men get on horseback because they cannot walk." That readiness with a jab — that gift for the witty line — was thought to suit a courtroom advocate perfectly, and audiences loved it; but he overused it, offended a good many people, and got a reputation for spite.

He was appointed quaestor at a time of severe grain shortage, and drew Sicily as his province. At first he made himself unpopular there by forcing the Sicilians to ship their grain to Rome, but once they had experience of his diligence, fairness, and mildness, they honored him beyond any governor they had ever had. And when a number of young Romans from good and noble families were brought before the praetor in Sicily on charges of indiscipline and misconduct on military service, Cicero took their defense, argued it superbly, and got them all acquitted. So he headed back to Rome feeling very pleased with himself — and then, as he tells the story on himself, something absurd happened. In Campania he ran into a prominent citizen he counted as a friend, and asked him what Rome was saying about his achievements — what Rome thought of them — as if the whole city must be ringing with the glory of what he had done. His friend replied, "So where have you been all this time, Cicero?" That flattened him completely. He saw that the news of his deeds had sunk into the city of Rome as into a vast ocean, without leaving one visible ripple of reputation. Later, thinking it over, he concluded that the glory he was chasing was an infinite thing — there was no fixed finish line, no way to measure done — and he scaled back his ambitions considerably. All the same, he remained excessively delighted by his own praise, and stayed passionately hungry for glory to the very end of his life; and that hunger often got in the way of his soundest judgment.

As he threw himself more seriously into public business, one thought struck him as absurd: a craftsman working with tools and vessels — dead objects — knows the name, the place, and the use of every one of them; should a statesman, whose instruments for public work are human beings, be careless and lazy about knowing people? So he memorized not only the names but everything else: exactly where each of the more prominent citizens lived, what land he owned, who his friends were, who his neighbors were; and traveling any road in Italy, Cicero could point out his friends' and acquaintances' estates and country houses as he passed. His own fortune was small — enough to live on, no more — and so people marveled that he took neither fees nor gifts from his clients, and marveled most of all when he took on the prosecution of Verres. This Verres had been praetor of Sicily, and the Sicilians charged him with a long list of crimes committed during his governorship. Cicero got him convicted not by speaking, but, in a sense, by declining to speak. The praetors favored Verres and had stalled the trial with adjournment after adjournment until the very last day, when it was obvious there wouldn't be time for the advocates to be heard and the case brought to a verdict. So Cicero stood up and said there was no need for speeches; he simply produced and examined his witnesses, and then called on the judges to vote. Even so, the record preserves plenty of Cicero's wit from that case. When a man named Caecilius, a former slave who was said to keep Jewish practices, tried to shove the Sicilians aside and take over the prosecution of Verres himself, Cicero asked, "What does a Jew have to do with a pig?" — verres being Latin for a boar. And when Verres started sneering that Cicero lived like a woman, he shot back, "You ought to save that lecture for your sons, at home" — Verres having a son who had gone down a disgraceful road. The orator Hortensius did not dare defend Verres outright, but he was persuaded to appear for him at the assessment of the fine, and took an ivory sphinx as his fee. When Cicero worked an oblique dig at him into one of his speeches and Hortensius protested that he was no good at solving riddles, Cicero said, "Really? And you with the Sphinx in your house."

So Verres was convicted — though Cicero, who set the fine at 750,000 denarii, fell under suspicion of having been bribed to keep the figure low. Still, the grateful Sicilians came to him when he was aedile bringing all sorts of presents from the island; he took no personal profit from any of it, using their generosity only to bring down the public price of food.

He had a very pleasant place at Arpinum, plus a farm near Naples and another near Pompeii, neither worth much. His wife Terentia's dowry came to 100,000 denarii, and he received a bequest worth 90,000 denarii; on this he lived generously but temperately, surrounded by the learned Greeks and Romans of his circle. He rarely if ever sat down to eat before sunset — not because of the press of business so much as for his health and that weak stomach. He was altogether careful and precise about his body, keeping to a fixed regimen of walks and massages, and by managing his constitution this way he built it up, in time, into something healthy, equal to serious strains and hardships. His father's house he turned over to his brother; he himself lived near the Palatine hill so that the people who came to pay court to him would be spared a long walk. And come they did — every day, as many at his door as ever came to Crassus for his riches or to Pompey for his standing with the soldiers, and those two were then the most admired and powerful men in Rome. In fact, Pompey himself used to pay court to Cicero, and Cicero's political work did a great deal to build up Pompey's authority and reputation in the state.

A crowd of distinguished candidates stood with him for the praetorship, and he was elected ahead of them all; and he handled the cases before him with justice and integrity. There is a story that Licinius Macer, a man of real power in the city with Crassus backing him besides, was tried before Cicero for extortion, and was so confident in his own influence and his friends' efforts that while the judges were still voting he went home, got a quick haircut, put on a clean toga as if already acquitted, and was setting off for the Forum again — when at his front door he met Crassus, who told him he had been convicted by every single vote. Macer went back inside, lay down on his bed, and died on the spot. The verdict brought Cicero great credit for running an honest court. Another time a man named Vatinius — rough-mannered, often insolent to the magistrates in court, with his neck covered in swollen tumors — came before the tribunal with some request, and when Cicero said he needed time to consider it, Vatinius remarked that he wouldn't hesitate over such a thing if he were praetor. Cicero turned on him instantly: "Ah, but I don't have your neck."

With only two or three days left in his term, Manilius was brought before him, charged with embezzlement. Manilius had the goodwill and support of the common people, who believed he was being prosecuted only to get at Pompey, whose particular friend he was. So when he asked for time before his trial and Cicero granted him just one day — the very next — the people were furious, because the custom was for praetors to give the accused ten days at least. The tribunes hauled Cicero before the assembly and accused him; he asked to be heard, and said that he had always treated defendants with as much fairness and humanity as the law allowed, so he thought it would be hard to deny the same to Manilius — and that he had deliberately assigned the one day of which, as praetor, he was still master, since it was hardly the move of a man trying to help Manilius to toss his case into another praetor's court. These words produced a wonderful change in the crowd. Loud in his praise, they asked him to take on Manilius' defense himself. He willingly agreed — chiefly for the sake of Pompey, who was away — and taking his place before the people again, he delivered a bold attack on the oligarchical party and on the men who envied Pompey.

Yet when it came to the consulship, the nobles pushed him forward as eagerly as the common people did, for the good of the city, and both parties worked together for his election — and here is why. The new order Sulla had imposed on the state had seemed senseless at first, but with time and habit most people had come to regard it as a settlement they could live with. There were men, however, who wanted to shake and overturn the whole existing arrangement — not from any honorable motive, but for their own private gain — and with Pompey off fighting the kings of Pontus and Armenia, there was no force at Rome sufficient to put down a revolution. These men had for their leader Lucius Catiline, a bold, daring, restless character who stood accused, among other enormous crimes, of taking his own virgin daughter's honor and of killing his own brother — and, fearing prosecution for that murder, he had persuaded Sulla to enter his brother's name, as though the man were still alive, on the proscription lists of those to be executed. With this man as their captain, the city's worst citizens pledged themselves to one another, sealing the pact, among other rites, by sacrificing a man and tasting his flesh. Catiline corrupted a large part of the young men of Rome, keeping each supplied with pleasures, drink, and women, and freely covering the bills for all of it. Beyond the city, all Etruria had been stirred to the edge of revolt, along with much of Gaul this side of the Alps. And Rome itself was primed for upheaval by the lopsided distribution of wealth: men of the highest rank and proudest spirit had bankrupted themselves on shows, banquets, election campaigns, and extravagant building, and the city's riches had drained into the hands of low-born nobodies. It would have taken only a slight push to bring the whole thing down — any man with enough daring could have toppled a commonwealth that sick.

Catiline, wanting a strong base from which to launch his plans, stood for the consulship, and his hopes were high: he expected to share the office with Gaius Antonius, a man fit to lead in neither a good cause nor a bad one, but a useful addition to someone else's power. Most of the good and honest citizens saw what was coming, and they put Cicero up for the consulship. The people embraced him gladly; Catiline was rejected; and Cicero and Gaius Antonius were elected — even though, of all the candidates, Cicero was the only one whose father belonged to the equestrian order rather than the senatorial.

Though Catiline's plot was not yet public knowledge, Cicero's consulship ran into serious preliminary trouble at once, from two directions. On one side, the men whom Sulla's laws had barred from holding office — no small group, in numbers or in influence — came forward as candidates and courted the people, saying much about Sulla's tyranny that was perfectly true and just, except that they were shaking the government at the wrong and most dangerous moment. On the other side, the tribunes were pushing laws to the same effect, proposing a commission of ten men with unlimited powers: as supreme governors they would have the right to sell the public lands of all Italy and Syria, and everything Pompey had newly conquered; to try and to banish whomever they pleased; to plant colonies; to draw money from the treasury; and to levy and pay whatever soldiers they saw fit. Several of the nobles favored this law — none more than Gaius Antonius, Cicero's colleague, who hoped to be one of the ten. What frightened the nobles most was that Antonius was thought to know about Catiline's conspiracy and, buried in debt as he was, not to mind it.

Cicero dealt with that danger first. He arranged a decree assigning Antonius the province of Macedonia, while declining for himself the province of Gaul that was offered to him. That single favor so completely bought Antonius that he was ready, like a hired actor, to play the supporting part in whatever Cicero said for the good of the country. With his colleague tamed and manageable, Cicero could go after the plotters with more confidence. In the senate he delivered a speech against the law of the ten commissioners that left its sponsors with nothing to say for it. They tried again, and, with everything prepared beforehand, summoned the consuls before the popular assembly; Cicero, entirely unafraid, walked out first, ordered the senate to follow him, and not only got the law thrown out but so overwhelmed the tribunes with his oratory that they dropped the rest of their projects altogether.

For Cicero, more than any man, showed the Romans how much charm eloquence can lend to what is good; that justice is invincible if it is well spoken; and that a statesman who means to govern skillfully must, in his actions, always choose what is honest over what is popular, and, in his speaking, know how to present the right and useful course stripped of whatever might give offense. One incident in the theater during his consulship shows what his speaking could do. In the old days the knights of Rome sat mixed in among the common people in the theater, taking whatever seats they found; Marcus Otho, as praetor, was the first to separate them out and assign them their own section, a mark of distinction they hold to this day. The common people took this as an insult, and when Otho appeared in the theater they hissed him, while the knights received him with loud applause. The people hissed harder; the knights clapped louder. Then the two sides turned on each other with insults, and the whole theater fell into chaos. When Cicero heard of it, he came to the theater himself, called the people out to the temple of Bellona, and there scolded and shamed them so effectively that they went straight back into the theater and applauded Otho thunderously, competing with the knights over who could show him the greater honor and respect.
