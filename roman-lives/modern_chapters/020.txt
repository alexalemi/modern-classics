Brutus

(Part 5 of 5)

As soon as Brutus was sure that Cassius's wing had been beaten, he hurried toward him — but he heard nothing of his death until he had nearly reached the camp. There he mourned over the body, calling Cassius "the last of the Romans," for the city, he said, could never again produce another man of so great a spirit. Then he sent the body away to be buried on the island of Thasos, so that a funeral inside the camp would not stir up disorder. He gathered the soldiers and steadied them; and seeing that they had lost everything they owned, he promised every man two thousand drachmas to make up for it. The words put heart back into them, and the size of the gift astonished them. They saw him off with shouts and praise, calling him the only general of all four who had not been beaten in the battle. And the facts bore out his confidence that he would win: with only a few legions he had overthrown everything that stood against him. If all his soldiers had actually fought — instead of most of them streaming past the enemy to get at the plunder — he would very likely have swept every part of the field.

Eight thousand men fell on his side, counting the army's servants, whom Brutus calls Briges. On the other side, Messala gives it as his opinion that the dead came to more than twice that number. So it was Octavian and Antony's men who were the more disheartened — until a servant of Cassius named Demetrius came to Antony that evening, carrying the robe he had stripped from the dead body, and the sword. At the sight of these the enemy took such heart that at first light they drew out their whole force and stood in battle order. Brutus, meanwhile, found both his camps wavering and in disorder. His own was full of prisoners and needed a heavier guard than usual; and Cassius's men were unsettled by the change of general — besides which, the beaten troops nursed a certain envy and resentment toward the part of the army that had done the conquering. He decided, therefore, to put his forces in battle order but to keep out of a fight. The captured slaves — a great number of them had been found mixed in among the soldiers, not without suspicion — he ordered killed. But most of the free men and citizens he released, saying that they were more truly prisoners with the enemy than with him: over there they were captives and slaves, but with him they were free men and citizens of Rome. Even so, he had to hide them and help them slip away secretly, because he could see that his friends and officers were bent on revenge against them. Among the captives were an actor named Volumnius and a clown named Sacculio. Brutus paid them no attention at all, but his friends dragged them before him and charged that even now, in this condition, the pair would not give up their jokes and their insolent talk. Brutus, his mind full of other things, said nothing to the accusation; but Messala Corvinus gave as his judgment that they should be publicly whipped on a stage and sent naked to the enemy's commanders, to show them what sort of drinking companions they liked to bring along on campaign. Some of those present laughed at that. But Publius Casca — the man who had given Caesar the first wound — said, "It is not right for us to be joking and making merry at the funeral of Cassius. And you, Brutus, will show what esteem you have for that general's memory by how you deal with men who mock him and speak of him shamefully." Brutus, badly discomposed, replied, "Then why ask me about it, Casca, instead of doing what you think fitting yourselves?" That answer was taken as his consent to the deaths of the two wretched men, and they were carried off and killed.

After this he paid the soldiers the reward he had promised. He rebuked them, though only mildly, for charging the enemy in disorder, without the password or any word of command; and he promised that if they fought bravely in the next engagement, he would give them two cities to sack and plunder: Thessalonica and Lacedaemon — Sparta itself. This is the one indefensible thing in all that is found fault with in the life of Brutus — though it is true that Octavian and Antony were far more cruel in the rewards they gave their own soldiers after victory. They drove out, one might almost say, all the old inhabitants of Italy, to put their soldiers in possession of other men's lands and cities. But then conquest and empire had been their whole purpose in the war, while Brutus, because of his reputation for virtue, was not permitted by the world either to win or even to save himself except with justice and honor — especially now that Cassius was dead, Cassius who was generally blamed as the man who had steered him toward his less merciful acts. As sailors, when a storm has smashed the rudder, fit and nail on some other piece of timber in its place — meeting the danger not well, but as well as necessity allows — so Brutus, at the head of so great an army in such uncertain times, with no commander equal to his need, was forced to use the ones he had, and to do and say much of what they advised. And what they advised was, in effect, whatever might bring Cassius's soldiers into better order. For those troops were headstrong and hard to manage — bold and insolent in camp for want of their general, but cowardly and fearful in the field, because they remembered being beaten.

Things stood no better with Octavian and Antony. Their provisions were running short, and their camp lay on low ground, so they expected a very hard winter. They were pressed close against the marshes, and the heavy autumn rains that fell after the battle filled their tents with mud and water, which froze at once in the cold. And while they were in this misery, news reached them of their disaster at sea. Brutus's fleet had fallen upon their ships as they carried a great reinforcement of soldiers over from Italy, and had destroyed them so completely that only a handful of men escaped the slaughter — and those few, starving, were driven to eat the sails and the rigging. The moment they heard this, Octavian and Antony were in a hurry to force a deciding battle before Brutus could learn of his good fortune. For it so happened that the fighting at sea and on land had fallen on the very same day; and yet by some mischance — not through any fault of his commanders — Brutus did not learn of his victory until twenty days after. Had he known, he would never have been drawn into a second battle. He had provisions enough for his army for a long time to come; his camp was well placed, sheltered from the winter weather and nearly unreachable by the enemy; and being absolute master of the sea, and victorious on land on the wing where he himself had fought, he would have been full of hope and confidence. But it seems the Roman state could no longer endure to be governed by many men; it required a monarchy; and so the divine power, wishing to remove from the scene the one man who could still resist the man destined to control the empire, cut off that good news from Brutus's ears. And it missed him by only a little. The very evening before the battle, a deserter named Clodius came over from the enemy and announced that Octavian had learned of the loss of his fleet, and that this was the reason for his haste to fight. But the story found no credit. The man was never even brought before Brutus; they simply set him down as someone with bad information, or a liar inventing tales to buy himself favor.

That same night, they say, the phantom came to Brutus again, in the same shape as before — but this time it vanished without speaking. Publius Volumnius, though, a philosopher who had carried arms with Brutus from the beginning of the campaign, makes no mention of the apparition. He records other omens: that the first eagle-standard was covered with a swarm of bees; that one of the officers had an arm that sweated oil of roses of its own accord, and though they dried and wiped it again and again, it would not stop; and that just before the battle two eagles met and fought in the open space between the two armies, while the whole field kept an unbelievable silence and every man watched the spectacle — until at last the eagle on Brutus's side gave way and fled. And the story of the Ethiopian is famous: meeting the standard-bearer just as the camp gate was opened, he was cut to pieces by the soldiers, who took him for an evil omen.

Brutus led his army into the field and drew it up facing the enemy, and then paused a long while before he would fight. As he was reviewing the troops, suspicions were stirred and accusations laid against some of them. Besides, he could see that his cavalry had no great appetite for beginning the action and were waiting to see what the infantry would do. And then, suddenly, Camulatus — an excellent soldier, one Brutus prized highly for his courage — rode close past Brutus himself and went over to the enemy. The sight grieved Brutus to the heart. Partly out of anger, partly out of fear of some greater treason and desertion, he immediately threw his forces against the enemy, at about three in the afternoon, with the sun already going down. On his own wing Brutus had the better of it. He pressed hard on the enemy's left, which gave way and retreated, and his cavalry charged in alongside the infantry when they saw the enemy in confusion. But the other wing was a different story. To avoid being surrounded — for the numbers were against them — the officers kept stretching the line, until it was drawn so thin at the center that it could not withstand a charge; at the first onset it broke and fled. After cutting through it, the enemy at once swung round and took Brutus in the rear. Brutus himself, in that peril, did everything an expert general and a brave soldier could do — by judgment and by his own hand, everything that might yet recover the victory. But the very thing that had been his advantage in the first battle told against him in the second. In the first, the beaten part of the enemy had been killed on the spot; but of Cassius's men who fled, few had been slain, and the survivors, cowed by their old defeat, infected the greater part of the army with their own dread and disorder. It was here that Marcus, the son of Cato, was killed, fighting among the noblest and bravest of the young men. He scorned to run and would not give the least ground, but kept fighting, declaring who he was and naming his father's name, until he fell at last, leaving his enemies in awe of his courage, upon a heap of the enemy's dead. And of the rest, the bravest died defending Brutus.

There was in the field a man named Lucilius, a good man and a friend of Brutus. He saw a party of barbarian horsemen ignoring everyone else in the pursuit and galloping at full stretch after Brutus alone, and he resolved to stop them, though it should cost him his life. Letting himself fall a little behind, he told them that he was Brutus. They believed him all the more because he begged to be taken to Antony — as if he feared Octavian but could trust Antony. Overjoyed with their prize, and thinking themselves wonderfully lucky, they carried him along through the night, first sending messengers ahead to tell Antony they were coming. Antony was delighted and came out to meet them; and everyone else who heard that Brutus had been taken alive came flocking to see him — some pitying his fortune, others sneering that out of too much love of life he had let himself become the prey of barbarians, a meanness unworthy of his former glory. When they came near, Antony stood still, considering how he ought to receive Brutus. But Lucilius, brought up before him, said with perfect confidence: "Be assured, Antony, that no enemy has taken Marcus Brutus alive, and no enemy ever shall — heaven forbid that fortune should ever win such a victory over virtue. He will be found, living or dead, in a manner worthy of himself. As for me, I am here by a trick I played on your soldiers, and I am ready to suffer any severity you choose to inflict for it." Everyone stood amazed at these words. But Antony turned to the men who had brought him and said: "I see, my fellow soldiers, that you are annoyed at being deceived, and feel yourselves insulted and injured by it. But you should know that you have taken a better prize than the one you were hunting. You were searching for an enemy, and you have brought me a friend. For to tell you the truth, I do not know how I would have treated Brutus if you had brought him in alive. But of this I am sure: it is better to have men like Lucilius as our friends than as our enemies." With that he embraced Lucilius, and for the moment put him in the care of one of his friends; and ever afterward he found him a steady and faithful friend.

By now Brutus had crossed a little brook that ran among trees under steep overhanging rocks, and since it was night, he would go no farther. He sat down in a hollow with a great rock jutting out in front of it, a few of his officers and friends around him. First, looking up at the sky, which was full of stars, he recited two lines of verse. One of them, Volumnius records, was this:

  Punish, great Zeus, the author of these ills.

The other, he says, he had forgotten. A little later, naming one by one all the friends who had been killed before his eyes in the battle, he groaned aloud — above all at the names of Flavius, the chief of his engineers, and Labeo, his lieutenant. Meanwhile one of the company, who was thirsty and saw that Brutus was thirsty too, took his helmet and ran down to the brook for water. Then a noise was heard from the far side of the river, and Volumnius, taking Brutus's armor-bearer Dardanus with him, went out to see what it was. When they came back a short while later, they asked what had become of the water. Brutus smiled — a smile that carried a great deal of meaning — and said to Volumnius, "It is all drunk; but more shall be fetched for you." So the man who had brought the first helmetful was sent again; he came close to being captured by the enemy, took a wound, and escaped only with difficulty.

Brutus reckoned that not many of his men had been killed in the fighting, and Statyllius undertook to find out for certain. He would dash through the enemy — there was no other way — and see what had become of their camp; if he found everything safe there, he would hold up a torch as a signal, and then return. He got through, and the torch was raised: Statyllius had reached the camp safely. But when a long time had passed and he did not come back, Brutus said, "If Statyllius is alive, he will come back." On the return, he fell into the enemy's hands, and was killed.

The night was now far gone. Brutus, still sitting, leaned his head toward his servant Clitus and spoke to him. Clitus gave no answer, only burst into tears. Then Brutus drew aside his armor-bearer Dardanus and had some words with him in private. Last of all he spoke to Volumnius in Greek, reminding him of their studies together and the discipline they had shared, and begged him to take hold of the sword with him and help him drive it home. Volumnius refused the request, and so did the others; and when someone said there could be no staying here — they must fly — Brutus rose and said, "Yes, indeed, we must fly; but not with our feet — with our hands." Then he gave each of them his right hand, and with a face full of pleasure he told them that it was an infinite satisfaction to him that not one of his friends had proved false. As for Fortune, he was angry with her only for his country's sake. As for himself, he counted himself far happier than the men who had conquered — not only as he had been a little while ago, but even now, in his present condition. For he was leaving behind him a reputation for virtue that no force can take away — a possession that none of the conquerors, with all their arms and all their riches, would ever be able to acquire; nor could they ever prevent posterity from believing, and saying, that being unjust and wicked men, they had destroyed the just and the good, and seized a power to which they had no right. Then he urged and entreated them all to look to their own safety, and withdrew with only two or three of his closest friends. One of these was Strato, whose acquaintance he had made when they studied rhetoric together. Him he placed nearest to himself; and then, taking hold of the hilt of his sword and directing it with both hands, he fell upon it and killed himself. But others say that it was not Brutus who held the sword but Strato, who — at Brutus's earnest entreaty, and turning his face away — held it steady while Brutus threw himself upon it with such force that it passed through his breast, and he died at once.

Messala, that friend of Brutus who was afterward reconciled to Octavian, once brought this same Strato to him at a leisure moment and said, with tears in his eyes, "This, Caesar, is the man who did the last friendly service for my beloved Brutus." Octavian received him kindly, and had good use of him afterward in his labors and his battles — at Actium Strato was one of the Greeks who proved their courage in his service. And of Messala himself the story is told that when Octavian once paid him this compliment — that although at Philippi he had been his fiercest enemy for Brutus's sake, at Actium he had shown himself his most devoted friend — Messala answered, "You have always found me, Caesar, on the best and justest side."

Brutus's body was found by Antony, who ordered the richest purple mantle he owned to be thrown over it; and when the mantle was afterward stolen, he found out the thief and had him put to death. The ashes of Brutus he sent home to his mother, Servilia. As for his wife Portia, the philosopher Nicolaus and Valerius Maximus write that she was determined to die, but was prevented by her friends, who watched her constantly; and that at last she snatched burning coals from the fire, shut them close in her mouth, and stifled herself — and so ended her life in a manner worthy of her birth and her virtue. Yet there is also a letter of Brutus in circulation, addressed to his friends, in which he grieves for Portia's death and reproaches them for neglecting her, so that in her illness she preferred to die rather than linger. It seems, then, that Nicolaus was simply mistaken about the time; for the letter — if it is indeed genuine, and truly Brutus's own — lets us understand Portia's sickness, and her love, and the manner in which her death came.
