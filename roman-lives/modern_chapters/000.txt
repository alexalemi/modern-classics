Caesar

(Part 1 of 6)

When Sulla made himself master of Rome, he wanted Caesar to divorce his wife Cornelia — the daughter of Cinna, the man who had lately held sole power in the state. He could not make it happen, not with promises and not with threats, so he settled for confiscating her dowry. The root of Sulla's grudge was Caesar's family tie to Marius. The elder Marius had married Julia, the sister of Caesar's father, which made the younger Marius Caesar's first cousin. At first, with so many people to kill and so much business to get through, Sulla simply overlooked Caesar. But the boy would not keep his head down. He presented himself to the people as a candidate for a priesthood while he was still barely more than a child, and Sulla, without opposing him openly, quietly arranged his defeat. When the question was raised of whether to have him killed outright, and someone objected that it was hardly worth the trouble to contrive the death of a boy, Sulla answered that they had no eyes at all if they could not see more than one Marius in that boy.

Word of that remark reached Caesar, and he disappeared. For a long while he kept out of sight in the Sabine country, moving from house to house — until one night, shifting quarters yet again because he was ill, he fell straight into the hands of Sulla's soldiers, who were sweeping the district for men in hiding. Caesar bribed their captain, Cornelius, with two talents to let him go, and the moment he was released he made for the sea and sailed to Bithynia. After a short stay there with King Nicomedes, he was captured on the voyage home, near the island of Pharmacusa, by pirates — who in those days controlled the seas everywhere with great fleets and swarms of smaller craft.

When the pirates demanded twenty talents for his ransom, he laughed in their faces for not knowing the value of their prisoner, and volunteered to pay them fifty. Then he sent his people off in every direction to raise the money, until he was left among the most bloodthirsty men in the world, the Cilicians, with a single friend and two attendants. He treated his captors as if they hardly existed. When he wanted to sleep, he would send someone over to order them to keep the noise down. For thirty-eight days he carried on with perfect freedom, joining their games and exercises as though they were not his keepers but his bodyguard. He wrote poems and speeches and made the pirates his audience, and anyone who failed to admire the work he called an illiterate barbarian to his face — and he would often, in the middle of the joking, promise to have them all crucified. They loved this. They put his free talk down to a kind of simple, boyish playfulness. As soon as his ransom arrived from Miletus, he paid it and was set free — and went straight to the port of Miletus, manned some ships, and sailed out after the pirates. He caught them still anchored at the island, took most of them prisoner, seized their money as his prize, and locked the men up in the prison at Pergamus. Then he applied to Junius, the governor of Asia, since punishing them fell to Junius's office as praetor. But Junius had his eye on the money — the sum was considerable — and said he would consider at his leisure what to do with the prisoners. So Caesar took polite leave of him, went back to Pergamus, had the pirates brought out, and crucified every one of them — the exact punishment he had so often promised them while he was in their hands, when they never dreamed he meant a word of it.

Meanwhile Sulla's power was fading, and Caesar's friends urged him to come home to Rome. Instead he went to Rhodes and enrolled in the school of Apollonius, the son of Molon — a famous teacher of rhetoric with a reputation as a decent man, who had counted Cicero among his students. Caesar, it is said, was fitted by nature to be a great statesman and orator, and he worked so hard at developing the gift that he could without question have claimed second place in eloquence. He aimed no higher, because he chose to be first among men of arms and power instead; and so he never reached the summit of oratory that nature had put within his range, his attention being pulled away toward the campaigns and grand designs that eventually won him the empire. He said so himself. In his reply to Cicero's tribute to Cato, he asked his readers not to compare a soldier's plain way of speaking with the polished performances of an orator who had not only brilliant gifts but a whole lifetime spent perfecting them.

When he returned to Rome, he prosecuted Dolabella for corruption in office, and many of the cities of Greece supplied evidence against him. Dolabella was acquitted; but Caesar, to repay the Greeks for their support, helped them prosecute Publius Antonius for corruption before Marcus Lucullus, the praetor of Macedonia. He pressed the case so hard that Antonius had to appeal to the tribunes at Rome, claiming that in Greece he could not get a fair trial against Greeks. At Rome, Caesar's eloquence in the courts quickly earned him a serious reputation, and the warmth and courtesy of his manner won him the people's affection — he had a tact and consideration well beyond his years. And the open house he kept, the dinners he gave, the general splendor of his way of living, all built his political influence little by little. His enemies shrugged at it at first, assuming the whole thing would collapse the moment his money ran out. But while they shrugged, it grew and flowered among the common people. By the time his power was established beyond challenge — openly aimed, now, at transforming the entire constitution — they understood, too late, that no beginning is so small that persistence will not make it great, and that a danger despised at first becomes irresistible in the end. Cicero was the first to suspect what Caesar was really after. Like a good pilot who fears the storm while the sea is still smiling, he saw the calculation underneath the charm and good humor, and said that in everything Caesar did and planned he could detect the ambition for absolute power. "But when I look at his hair, so carefully arranged," he said, "and watch him adjusting it with one finger, I cannot imagine that such a man could ever conceive of anything so enormous as overthrowing the Roman state." But more of that later.

The first proof Caesar had of the people's goodwill came when he ran for a military tribuneship and finished higher on the list than Gaius Popilius. A second and clearer proof came when he delivered a magnificent oration in the forum in praise of his aunt Julia, the wife of Marius — and at her funeral had the audacity to bring out the images of Marius himself, which no one had dared display since Sulla took power and Marius's party was declared enemies of the state. A few in the crowd started shouting against Caesar for it, but the people drowned them out with cheering and applause, delighted that he had brought the honors of Marius back from the grave, as it were, after so many years lost to the city. It had always been the custom at Rome to deliver funeral orations for elderly matrons, but never for young women — until Caesar gave one at the death of his own wife. This too won him favor: the people saw the show of grief and took him for a man of great tenderness and kindness of heart. After burying his wife, he went to Spain as quaestor under one of the praetors, a man named Vetus, whom he honored ever afterward — when Caesar himself became praetor, he made Vetus's son his own quaestor. When that service was over, he married his third wife, Pompeia; he already had a daughter by his first wife Cornelia, whom he later gave in marriage to Pompey the Great. He spent money so lavishly that before he held any public office at all he was thirteen hundred talents in debt, and many people thought he was trading a solid good for a short and uncertain return, buying popularity at ruinous cost. In truth he was buying the most valuable thing in the world, and getting it cheap. When he was made curator of the Appian Way, he poured a great deal of his own money into the work on top of the public funds; and as aedile he put on three hundred and twenty single combats of gladiators, and mounted theatrical shows, processions, and public feasts on a scale so magnificent that he washed out the memory of everyone who had come before him. The people were so taken with it all that every man of them was hunting for new offices and new honors to pay him back.

There were two factions in the city: Sulla's, which was very powerful, and Marius's, which was broken and cowed. Caesar set out to revive the latter and make it his own. At the height of his popularity from the shows of his aedileship, he had images of Marius made, along with figures of Victory carrying trophies, and had them carried up at night and set in place on the Capitol. In the morning people saw them glittering with gold, beautifully worked, their inscriptions recording Marius's victories over the Cimbrians — and they were stunned at the boldness of the man who had put them there. It was not hard to guess who that was. Word spread fast, and a great crowd came running. Some cried out that this was open rebellion against the settled government — reviving honors that had been buried by laws and decrees of the senate — and that Caesar had done it to test the temper of a people he had already softened up, to see whether they were tame enough to indulge him and let his innovations pass. Marius's people, on the other hand, took heart, and it was astonishing how many of them suddenly turned out to be, and how they came pouring into the Capitol, shouting. Many wept for joy at the sight of Marius's likeness, and Caesar was praised to the sky as the one man in Rome who was worthy of his kinship to Marius. The senate met over it, and Catulus Lutatius, one of the most respected men in Rome, rose and denounced Caesar, closing with the memorable line that Caesar was no longer tunneling under the state — he was bringing up siege engines against it in the open. But Caesar made his own defense and satisfied the senate, and his admirers were more excited than ever. They urged him to yield his ambitions to no one: with the people's favor behind him, he would beat them all in the end and be the first man in the commonwealth.

At this point Metellus, the high priest of Rome, died, and Catulus and Isauricus — men of the highest standing and great influence in the senate — competed for the office. Caesar refused to step aside for them and presented himself to the people as a rival candidate. With support running about even, Catulus, who had the most reputation to lose and was the most nervous about the outcome, sent to Caesar offering a large sum of money to withdraw. Caesar answered that he was ready to borrow an even larger sum to stay in the fight. On election day, as his mother walked him to the door in tears, he embraced her and said, "Mother, today you will see me either high priest or an exile." When the votes were counted, after a hard struggle, he won — and the senate and the nobility were seized with alarm that he would now lead the people into every kind of recklessness. Piso and Catulus blamed Cicero for letting Caesar slip away when the Catiline conspiracy had given the government such a hold on him. For Catiline had planned not merely to change the government but to overthrow the whole Roman order and throw everything into chaos; yet he had fled the city while the evidence against him was still incomplete, before his deepest designs came to light, leaving Lentulus and Cethegus behind to carry on the conspiracy in his place. Whether Caesar secretly encouraged and helped them is uncertain. What is certain is that they were overwhelmingly convicted in the senate, and when Cicero, as consul, went around the chamber asking each senator what punishment they favored, every man who spoke before Caesar said death. Then Caesar stood and delivered a prepared speech, arguing that it was without precedent, and unjust, to execute men of their birth and rank without a proper trial, unless there was absolute necessity for it. Better, he said, to hold them under guard in whatever Italian towns Cicero himself might choose, until Catiline was defeated — and then the senate could decide, in peacetime and at leisure, what should be done with them.

The proposal sounded so humane, and Caesar urged it so eloquently, that it swept the room: not only did those who spoke after him adopt it, but many who had already voted for death got up and switched sides — until the turn to speak came around to Catulus and Cato. They fought it hard, and Cato, in his speech, pointed the finger of suspicion at Caesar himself, and pressed the matter so fiercely that the conspirators were handed over for execution. As Caesar was leaving the senate house, a crowd of the young men who then served as Cicero's guard rushed at him with drawn swords. But Curio, it is said, threw his own toga over him and hustled him out, and Cicero himself, when the young men looked to him for the signal, shook his head — either out of fear of the people or because he thought the killing would be unjust and lawless. If the story is true, I cannot understand why Cicero left it out of the book he wrote about his consulship. In any case, he was blamed afterward for wasting so perfect an opportunity against Caesar — for flinching, people said, out of fear of the common people, who were extraordinarily devoted to Caesar. Indeed, a short while later, when Caesar went into the senate to clear himself of the suspicions hanging over him and the session ran long amid loud attacks on him, the crowd came surging up to the senate house, surrounded it, and shouted for Caesar, demanding that the senators let him go. That was what moved Cato, who was afraid above all of a rising among the poor — the tinder of every revolution, and men who had set all their hopes on Caesar — to persuade the senate to grant them a monthly allowance of grain. The measure added seven and a half million drachmas a year to the state's expenses, but it worked: it smothered the immediate danger, and it broke up much of Caesar's power at just the right moment, since he was about to enter his praetorship and would have been all the more formidable in office.

In fact his praetorship passed without any public disturbance. The trouble that year came inside his own house. Publius Clodius was a patrician by birth, distinguished for both wealth and eloquence, but in sheer insolence and appetite for scandal he outdid every notorious degenerate of the day. He was in love with Pompeia, Caesar's wife, and she was not unwilling. But her rooms were closely watched, and Caesar's mother Aurelia, a shrewd woman, kept so constant an eye on her that any meeting between the two was difficult and dangerous. Now, the Romans have a goddess they call Bona, the Good Goddess — the same one the Greeks call Gynaecea, the women's goddess. The Phrygians claim her as their own and say she was the mother of King Midas; the Romans say she was one of the dryads and the wife of Faunus; the Greeks say she is that mother of Bacchus whose name must never be spoken — which is why the women celebrating her festival cover their tents with vine branches, and why, in keeping with the myth, a sacred serpent sits beside the goddess. While her rites are performed, no man may be present, or even inside the house. The women conduct the sacred ceremonies by themselves, and these are said to be much like the rites of Orpheus. When the festival comes around, the host — a consul or a praetor — leaves his own house, along with every male creature in it. His wife takes the house over and puts it in order, and the main ceremonies are performed at night: a night of games and play among the women, with music of every kind.

Pompeia was hosting this festival when Clodius — who had no beard yet, and figured he could pass unnoticed — dressed himself up as a singing girl, ornaments and all, and went to the house looking like a young woman. He found the doors open and was slipped safely inside by a maid who was in on the affair, and who ran ahead to tell Pompeia. But she was gone a long time, and Clodius, too restless to wait where he had been left, went drifting through the big house, room to room, keeping away from the lights — until one of Aurelia's attendants ran into him and, taking him for a woman, invited him to play, as the women were doing among themselves. When he refused, she pulled him forward into the open and asked who he was and where he had come from. Clodius answered that he was waiting for Pompeia's own maid, Abra — which happened, in fact, to be this woman's name too — and his voice gave him away. The attendant shrieked, ran back to the lights and the company, and cried out that she had found a man in the house. The women were terrified. Aurelia covered up the sacred objects and stopped the rites, ordered the doors shut, and went through the house with torches, hunting for Clodius. He was found where he had started — hiding in the room of the maid who had let him in — and seized. The women recognized him and threw him out of the house, and that same night, before dawn, they went home and told their husbands everything. By morning the story was all over Rome: Clodius had committed sacrilege, and owed satisfaction not only to the women he had insulted but to the city and to the gods. One of the tribunes formally charged him with profaning the holy rites, and some of the leading senators joined forces to testify against him — swearing that, among a long list of other outrages, he had committed incest with his own sister, the wife of Lucullus. But the people set themselves against the nobles' campaign and defended Clodius, which counted for a great deal with the judges, who took fright and had no wish to provoke the crowd. Caesar divorced Pompeia immediately. Yet when he was called as a witness at the trial, he said he knew nothing against Clodius at all. Since that sounded absurd, the prosecutor asked him: then why had he put away his wife? "Because I wished my wife," Caesar said, "to be not so much as suspected." Some say he meant it; others, that he said it to please the people, who were determined to save Clodius. Either way, Clodius was acquitted — most of the judges turning in ballots written so illegibly that they could not be read, so as not to risk their necks with the people by convicting, nor their standing with the nobility by acquitting.

His praetorship over, Caesar was assigned the province of Spain — and found himself in serious trouble with his creditors, who descended on him as he was about to leave, loud and immovable. This drove him to Crassus, the richest man in Rome, who for his part needed Caesar's youth and fire for his own contest with Pompey. Crassus agreed to take on the most impatient and unbending of the creditors and stood surety for eight hundred and thirty talents, and with that Caesar was free to leave for his province. On the journey out, crossing the Alps, he passed a shabby little native village with a handful of wretchedly poor inhabitants, and his companions began joking among themselves: was there election campaigning here too — rivalries for first place, feuds between the local great men? Caesar answered them in complete earnest. "For my part," he said, "I would rather be first man here than second at Rome." In the same way, they say that later, in Spain, in an idle hour, he was reading from the history of Alexander and sat a long time lost in thought — and then burst into tears. His friends were astonished and asked what was wrong. "Do you not think I have cause to weep," he said, "when Alexander at my age was already king over so many nations, and I have done nothing memorable at all?" The moment he arrived in Spain he went to work. Within days he had raised ten new cohorts of infantry on top of the twenty already stationed there, marched against the Callaici and the Lusitanians, beat them, and pushed on all the way to the outer ocean, subduing tribes that had never before answered to Rome. Having managed the war so well, he did equally well at governing the peace. He worked to bring the province's communities into harmony, and took particular care to heal the running war between debtors and creditors: he ordered that each creditor take two-thirds of his debtor's annual income, with the debtor keeping the remaining third, until the whole debt was worked off. This won him a fine reputation to leave with. He departed the province a rich man himself, with soldiers he had enriched too — and they had hailed him with the honorable title of Imperator, the soldiers' salute of victory.
