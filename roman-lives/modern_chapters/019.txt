Brutus

(Part 4 of 5)

About this time Brutus sent word to Cassius to join him at the city of Sardis, and when Cassius was on the road, Brutus went out with his friends to meet him. The whole army, drawn up in formation, saluted each of the two men as imperator. But as usually happens in great enterprises, where many friends and many commanders are engaged, jealousies had been building between Brutus and Cassius — grievances, private accusations, things carried back and forth by other men — and they decided to settle all of it before they took up any other business. The moment they arrived, they withdrew into a room together and shut the door. Alone, just the two of them, they began first to air their complaints, then to argue hotly, then to accuse each other outright — and at last they were so carried away by passion that they fell to harsh words and finally burst into tears. Their friends standing outside were astonished to hear them so loud and so angry, and feared something worse might follow; but they had been ordered not to enter, and no one dared interrupt. No one, that is, except Marcus Favonius. He had been a devoted admirer of Cato, and he kept up the character of a philosopher not so much by learning or wisdom as by a wild, vehement manner all his own. Now he came charging at the door, and the attendants tried to hold him back — but stopping Favonius was hard work once his wildness had seized him. He was fierce in everything he did and would try anything to get his way. He was a senator, but he considered that among the least of his distinctions; what he really prized was a kind of Cynic's license to say whatever he pleased to anyone — rudeness so free and so badly timed that people who chose to take it as a joke could sometimes forgive it. This Favonius forced his way past the men guarding the doors, burst into the chamber, and in a theatrical voice declaimed the lines Homer gives to old Nestor:

  Take my advice — for I am older than you both.

At this Cassius burst out laughing. But Brutus threw him out, calling him an impudent dog and a counterfeit Cynic. Still, for the moment, the interruption put an end to their quarrel, and they parted. That night Cassius gave a supper, and Brutus invited the guests; and when everyone had taken their places, in came Favonius, fresh from his bath. Brutus called out that he had not been invited and told him to take the farthest couch. Instead Favonius shoved his way in and lay down on the middle one, the place of honor — and the evening passed in playful talk, with no shortage of either wit or philosophy.

The next day, on a complaint brought by the people of Sardis, Brutus publicly disgraced and condemned Lucius Pella, a man who had been a censor of Rome and whom Brutus himself had trusted with responsible posts — the charge was embezzling public money. The verdict stung Cassius badly. Only a few days earlier, two friends of his own had been accused of exactly the same crime; he had scolded them in private, cleared them in public, and kept them in his service. Now he accused Brutus of too much rigor, too much strict justice, at a time that called for policy and accommodation. But Brutus told him to remember the Ides of March — the day they had killed Caesar, a man who did not himself plunder and squeeze mankind, but who was the strength and shelter of the men who did. "If there were any decent excuse for setting justice aside," he said, "it would have been better to put up with the crimes of Caesar's friends than to let our own go unpunished. Then we could have been accused of nothing worse than cowardice. Now, after all our pains and all our dangers, we lay ourselves open to the charge of injustice." That was Brutus's purpose, and that was the rule he acted by.

About the time they were preparing to cross from Asia into Europe, it is said that a strange and wonderful sign appeared to Brutus. He was by nature a man of little sleep, and by discipline and a spare diet he had trimmed his hours of rest down to almost nothing. He never slept during the day, and at night only when all his business was done and there was no one left awake to talk with. But now, with the war begun, the whole weight of it on his mind, and the outcome pressing on him, he would take a short first sleep after supper and then spend the rest of the night on his most urgent affairs. If he could dispatch them early and save a little time, he read — until the third watch of the night, when the centurions and tribunes were accustomed to come to him for orders. So it was that one night, before the army left Asia, he was very late alone in his tent, a dim lamp burning beside him, the whole camp hushed and silent. He was deep in thought, turning something over in his mind, when he sensed that someone had come in. He looked up toward the door and saw a terrible, strange apparition — a monstrous, frightening figure standing beside him, saying nothing. Brutus asked it boldly, "What are you — man or god? And what business brings you to me?" The figure answered, "I am your evil genius, Brutus. You will see me at Philippi." And Brutus, not shaken in the least, replied, "Then I will see you."

As soon as the apparition vanished, he called his servants. They told him they had heard no voice and seen no vision. So he stayed awake until morning, then went to Cassius and told him what he had seen. Cassius, who followed the philosophy of Epicurus and often used to debate these matters with Brutus, answered him this way: "It is the opinion of our school, Brutus, that not everything we feel or see is real. The senses are slippery and deceitful things, and the mind is quicker and subtler still — it sets the senses in motion and stamps every kind of change upon them with no real cause behind it at all, the way an impression is pressed into wax. And the soul of man, which contains in itself both the stamp and the wax, can most easily, by its own workings, produce and take on every shape and figure. You can see this in the sudden changes of our dreams, where the imagination, once set off by the least thing, runs through a whole parade of emotions and appearances. It is the imagination's nature to be always in motion, and its motion is fantasy — mere conception. In your case, add this: your body, worn down and strained by constant toil, naturally works upon your mind and keeps it in an excited, unnatural state. As for supernatural beings — that they exist at all, or, if they did, that they would have human shape or voice or any power that could reach us — there is no reason to believe it. Though I confess I could wish there were such beings, so that in this most sacred and honorable enterprise of ours we might not have to rely only on our arms and horses and ships, numerous and powerful as they are, but could count on the help of the gods as well." With talk like this, Cassius soothed Brutus's mind. But as the troops were boarding the ships, two eagles flew down and perched on the two foremost standards. They crossed the water with the army, followed the soldiers all the way, and were fed by them — until they came to Philippi, where, just one day before the battle, both eagles flew away.

Brutus had already brought most of the places and peoples of that region under control, but now the army marched on to the coast opposite the island of Thasos, subduing any town or strongman that still held out. There they found Norbanus encamped in a position called the Straits, near Symbolum. They surrounded him so completely that they forced him to abandon the place, and Norbanus came within a hair of losing his entire army — Octavian was lagging far behind because of sickness, and only Antony's relief saved him, arriving with such astonishing speed that Brutus and his officers refused to believe the report that he had come. Octavian arrived ten days later and camped opposite Brutus, with Antony opposite Cassius.

The stretch of ground between the two armies is what the Romans call the Campi Philippi — the plains of Philippi. Never before had two Roman armies of such size come together to fight each other. Brutus's army was somewhat smaller in numbers than Octavian's, but in the splendor of its weapons and the richness of its equipment it was far the more magnificent, for most of the men's arms were of gold and silver, which Brutus had lavished on them. In everything else he had trained his commanders to frugality and self-restraint, but he believed that the wealth a soldier carried in his hands and wore on his body would put spirit into the man who craved glory — and would make the man who loved money fight all the harder to keep the armor that was his whole estate.

Octavian held his review and ritual purification of the army inside his own trenches, and handed out only a little grain and a bare five drachmas per soldier for the sacrifice they were to offer. Brutus — whether out of pity for that poverty or contempt for that meanness of spirit — first mustered and purified his army in the open field, as custom required, and then distributed great numbers of sacrificial animals to every regiment and fifty drachmas to every soldier. In the love of his men and their readiness to fight for him, Brutus held every advantage. But at the ceremony, they say, an unlucky omen befell Cassius: his lictor, his official attendant, presented him with the garland he was to wear at the sacrifice — upside down. It was said, too, that some time before, at a solemn procession, a golden image of Victory being carried before Cassius had crashed to the ground when its bearer slipped. On top of that, birds of prey circled the camp daily, and swarms of bees were found clustering at a spot inside the trenches — a spot the soothsayers ordered walled off from the camp, to quiet the superstition that was quietly beginning to infect even Cassius himself, and to shake his Epicurean philosophy, and that had already wholly seized and conquered the soldiers. Because of all this Cassius was reluctant to stake everything on an immediate battle. He argued for stretching the war out, since they were the stronger in money and supplies but the weaker in men and arms. Brutus, on the contrary, was as eager as ever to come to a swift decision — either to restore his country's liberty or to release all those multitudes of people from the misery of the levies and services and extortions the war laid on them. And when he saw his light cavalry getting the better of skirmish after skirmish, his confidence hardened into resolve. Then some of the soldiers deserted to the enemy, and others began accusing and suspecting one another, and many of Cassius's friends in the council came over to Brutus's opinion. But one man of Brutus's own party, named Atellius, opposed him, advising that they wait out the winter. When Brutus asked him how he expected to be better off a year from now, Atellius answered, "If I gain nothing else, at least I will have lived that much longer." The answer displeased Cassius intensely, and Atellius fell into general contempt for it. And so it was resolved to give battle the next day.

That night at supper Brutus was cheerful and full of hope, and talked philosophy with his friends before going to his rest. But Messala says that Cassius supped privately with a few of his closest companions and seemed thoughtful and silent, against his nature and his habit; that after supper he took Messala warmly by the hand and said to him — speaking in Greek, as he always did when he wished to show affection — "Bear witness for me, Messala, that I am brought to the same necessity as Pompey the Great before me: to hazard the liberty of my country on a single battle. Yet let us be of good courage, and trust our fortune — it would be wrong to distrust her, even if our counsels are bad." These, Messala says, were the last words Cassius spoke to him before saying goodnight; and he was invited to supper with Cassius the following evening, which was his birthday.

At first light the signal for battle — the scarlet coat — was hung out in both Brutus's camp and Cassius's, and the two generals met in the open ground between their armies. There Cassius said: "May it be as we hope, Brutus — may we win today and live all the rest of our lives happily together. But the greatest of human affairs are the most uncertain, and if the battle goes against us, we may find it hard ever to see each other again. So tell me now: what have you resolved about flight, and about death?" Brutus answered, "When I was young, Cassius, and knew nothing of the world, I let myself utter a bold philosophical opinion — I know not how. I blamed Cato for killing himself. I called it an irreligious act, and an unmanly one, to try to slip out from under the divine order of things — to refuse to accept bravely whatever evil comes, and to run from it instead. But now, in my own fortunes, I am of another mind. If Providence does not settle this undertaking as we wish, I will put no further hopes and no further preparations for war to the test. I will die content with my fortune. I gave my life to my country on the Ides of March, and I have lived a second life since then for her sake, in liberty and honor." Cassius smiled at these words, and embraced him, and said, "With these resolutions, let us go against the enemy. For either we shall conquer — or we shall have no cause to fear the men who do." After this they discussed the ordering of the battle with their friends. Brutus asked Cassius for command of the right wing, though it was generally thought that post belonged to Cassius, as the older and more experienced man. Even in this Cassius gave way to Brutus, and he placed Messala with the bravest of all his legions in the same wing. Brutus at once led out his cavalry, splendidly equipped, and brought his infantry up close behind.

Antony's soldiers were digging trenches from the marsh where they were camped out across the plain, to cut Cassius off from the sea, while Octavian's troops were to stand by in support — though Octavian himself could not be present because of his sickness. His soldiers were not expecting the enemy to offer a pitched battle at all — a few sallies, they thought, some darts and light weapons thrown to harass the men working in the trenches — and they took no notice of the army drawn up facing them, ready to fight. So they were stunned by the huge, confused roar that came rolling from the trenches. Meanwhile Brutus had sent out tickets to his officers with the password for battle written on them, and rode along the ranks himself, encouraging the men. But few of them ever heard the password before the fighting began; most of them did not wait for it to be passed down, but with one surge and one shout ran straight at the enemy. The charge threw the line out of order and the legions were pulled apart from one another. Messala's legion first, then the next beside it, swept clean past Octavian's left wing, barely grazing its edge and killing only a handful, and wheeling around behind it fell directly on Octavian's camp. Octavian himself, as his own memoirs tell it, had been carried out of the camp only moments before — one of his friends, Marcus Artorius, had dreamed a dream commanding that Octavian be carried out. He was believed dead, for the soldiers had riddled his empty litter with darts and pikes. There was great slaughter in the captured camp, and two thousand Spartans, newly arrived to reinforce Octavian, were cut down with the rest.

The rest of Brutus's line, which had not swung around the wing but engaged the enemy head-on, found them in complete disorder, overwhelmed them easily, and cut down three legions where they stood; then, swept along by the momentum of victory, they poured into the enemy camp on the heels of the fugitives, Brutus himself among them. But the defeated men saw, in their extremity, what the victors had failed to see. The right wing, hurrying off in pursuit, had torn away from the main body and left its flank exposed — and the enemy fell on that gap. They could not break the center, which met them with fierce, stubborn resistance; but the left wing, where Cassius commanded, in disorder and knowing nothing of what had happened on the other side of the field, they put to flight. They chased the fugitives all the way into Cassius's camp and sacked and wrecked it — with neither of their own generals present, for Antony, they say, had ducked away from the fury of that first charge and withdrawn into the nearby marsh, and Octavian was nowhere to be found after being carried from his tents — though some soldiers showed Brutus their bloody swords and swore they had killed him, describing his face and his youth. By now the center of Brutus's line had driven back its opponents with great slaughter. Brutus was plainly victorious everywhere on his side of the field, and Cassius was everywhere beaten. And this one mistake ruined everything: Brutus did not go to Cassius's relief, because he believed Cassius had won as he had; Cassius did not wait for Brutus's relief, because he believed Brutus had lost as he had. As proof that the victory was Brutus's, Messala points out that he took three eagles and many standards from the enemy without losing one of his own. But now, riding back from the pursuit and the plunder of Octavian's camp, Brutus was puzzled that he could not see Cassius's tent standing tall above the rest, where it had always stood — nor anything else looking as it should, for the enemy had torn it all down and looted it the moment they burst into the camp. Some of his men, sharper-eyed and longer-sighted than the rest, told Brutus they could see a great deal of gleaming armor and silver shields moving back and forth in Cassius's camp — and that neither the number nor the style of the armor looked like the guard that had been left there; yet there did not seem to be nearly as many dead bodies lying about as there should have been after the defeat of so many legions. That was what first made Brutus suspect Cassius's misfortune. Leaving a guard in the enemy camp, he called his men back from the pursuit and rallied them to march to Cassius's relief. And this is what had become of Cassius.

He had been angry from the very first, when Brutus's soldiers charged without the password and without the order to attack. Then, when they had won, he was just as displeased to watch them rush off after loot and plunder instead of wheeling to encircle what was left of the enemy. And then — acting by delay and hesitation rather than commanding boldly and with a clear purpose — he let himself be hemmed in by the enemy's right wing. His cavalry broke and bolted for the sea, and when he saw the infantry beginning to give way as well, he did everything a man could do to stop the rout and turn them back, snatching a standard from the hands of one fleeing soldier and planting it at his own feet — though by then he could barely hold even his personal guard together. In the end he was forced to withdraw with a handful of men to a small hill overlooking the plain. He himself, being weak-sighted, could make out almost nothing — only, and only with difficulty, the destruction of his camp. But the men with him saw a large body of horsemen riding toward the hill — the very riders Brutus had sent. Cassius took them for enemies coming to hunt him down; even so, he sent out Titinius, one of his companions, to find out who they were. When Brutus's horsemen saw Titinius coming and recognized a friend, a faithful servant of Cassius, those who knew him well shouted for joy, leapt down from their horses, and embraced him and shook his hands, while the rest rode circles around him, singing and cheering in their delight. And that was the cause of the worst mischief of all. For Cassius honestly believed that Titinius had been taken by the enemy, and he cried out, "Through too much love of life, I have lived to watch a friend captured by the enemy before my face." Then he withdrew into an empty tent, taking with him only Pindarus, one of his freedmen — a man he had kept ready for exactly such a moment ever since the disaster of the Parthian expedition, when Crassus was destroyed. From the Parthians Cassius had escaped with his life. Now he pulled his cloak up over his head, bared his neck, and held it out to Pindarus, commanding him to strike — with, men said, the same dagger he had used against Caesar. The head, at any rate, was found severed from the body. But no man ever saw Pindarus again, which led some to suspect that he had killed his master unbidden. Soon enough they discovered who the horsemen really were, and here came Titinius, crowned with garlands, hurrying toward Cassius. But when the cries and wailing of his grief-stricken friends told him of his general's tragic error and death, he drew his sword, bitterly cursed his own slowness for causing it, and killed himself.
