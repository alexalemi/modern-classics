Antony

(Part 7 of 7)

The moment he understood that she was alive, Antony eagerly ordered his servants to lift him up, and they carried him in their arms to the door of the monument. Cleopatra would not open it. Instead she appeared at a sort of window above and let down ropes and cords. Antony was made fast to them, and she and her two women — the only people she had allowed inside with her — hauled him up. Those who saw it said nothing was ever more heartbreaking than that sight: Antony, covered in blood and dying, drawn slowly up the wall, still stretching out his hands to her, still lifting himself with what little strength he had left. It was no easy work for the women. Cleopatra, clinging to the rope with both hands, her whole weight against it, her face straining toward the ground, dragged him up with the greatest difficulty, while the people below shouted encouragement and lived every inch of it with her.

When she had him inside, she laid him on the bed. She tore off her own clothes and spread them over him. She beat her breasts with her hands, tore at herself, smeared her face with the blood from his wounds, calling him her lord, her husband, her commander — so lost in his misfortunes that she seemed to have nearly forgotten her own. Antony calmed her cries as best he could and asked for wine — either because he was thirsty, or because he hoped it might carry him out of his pain the sooner. When he had drunk it, he gave her his last advice. She should look to her own safety, so far as it could be done with honor; among all of Octavian's friends, the one to trust was Proculeius. And she should not pity him for this final turn of fortune, but rather be glad for him, remembering the happiness he had known — he who had been the most illustrious and the most powerful of men, and who at the end had fallen not without honor, a Roman by a Roman overcome.

Just as he breathed his last, Proculeius arrived from Octavian. For when Antony had given himself his wound and been carried in to Cleopatra, one of his guards, Dercetaeus, had picked up Antony's sword and hidden it; and when he saw his chance, he slipped away to Octavian and brought him the first news of Antony's death, showing him the bloody sword as proof. At this, Octavian withdrew into the inner part of his tent and wept — genuine tears, for a man who had been his brother-in-law, his colleague in power, his companion in so many wars and dangers. Then he came out to his friends carrying a bundle of letters, and read them aloud to show how reasonably and moderately he had always written to Antony, and what overbearing, arrogant answers he had gotten back. After that he sent Proculeius with orders to do everything possible to take Cleopatra alive. He was afraid of losing a great treasure — and besides, she would be no small ornament to his triumph.

Cleopatra, however, was careful not to put herself in Proculeius's hands. He stood outside a door at ground level, strongly barred but built so that voices carried through it, and there they held their conference: she demanding that her kingdom be given to her children, he telling her to take heart and trust Octavian in everything. Having studied the place carefully, Proculeius went back to Octavian, and Gallus was sent to talk with her a second time. Gallus came to the door and deliberately dragged out the conversation — while Proculeius set his ladders against the window through which the women had pulled up Antony. He climbed in with two men behind him and went straight down to the door where Cleopatra stood talking with Gallus. One of the two women shut up in the monument with her cried out, "Poor Cleopatra — you are taken prisoner!" She spun around, saw Proculeius, and drew the dagger she carried to kill herself. But Proculeius was on her in an instant, pinning her with both hands. "For shame, Cleopatra," he said. "You wrong yourself, and you wrong Octavian — you would rob him of this perfect chance to show his mercy, and make the world believe the gentlest of commanders is a faithless, unforgiving enemy." He took the dagger out of her hand and shook out her dress to see whether any poison was hidden in it. Afterward Octavian sent one of his freedmen, Epaphroditus, with orders to treat her with every possible gentleness and courtesy — and to take the strictest precautions to keep her alive.

Meanwhile Octavian made his entry into Alexandria with the philosopher Areius at his side, holding him by the hand and talking with him as they went — he wanted the whole city to see, from the very first moment, what honor was paid to this man, and to measure him accordingly. Then, entering the exercise-ground, he mounted a platform built for the occasion, and from it he commanded the citizens — who in their terror had thrown themselves flat at his feet — to stand up. He told them he freely acquitted the people of all blame: first for the sake of Alexander, who had founded their city; second for the sake of the city itself, so large and so beautiful; and third, to gratify his friend Areius.

That was the honor Areius received from Octavian, and by his intercession many lives were saved. Among them was Philostratus, of all professional debaters that ever lived the readiest at speaking off the cuff, but with no honest claim at all to call himself a philosopher of the Academy. Octavian, disgusted by the man's character, would not listen to his pleas. So Philostratus grew a long white beard, dressed himself in black, and trailed behind Areius everywhere, shouting the verse,

  The wise, if they are truly wise, will save the wise.

When Octavian heard of it, he pardoned him — less to spare Philostratus any harm than to spare Areius the ill will.

As for Antony's children: Antyllus, his son by Fulvia, was betrayed by his tutor Theodorus and put to death. While the soldiers were cutting off the boy's head, the tutor managed to steal the precious jewel he wore around his neck and slip it into his own pocket. He denied it afterward, but he was convicted and crucified. Cleopatra's children and their attendants were kept under guard, but treated very honorably. Caesarion, who was widely held to be the son of Caesar the dictator, had been sent off by his mother with a great sum of money, to travel through Ethiopia and cross over into India. But his tutor, a man named Rhodon — about as honest as Theodorus — persuaded him to turn back, telling him Octavian meant to make him king. While Octavian was weighing what to do with him, Areius, we are told, remarked,

  Too many Caesars is no good thing.

And so, after Cleopatra was dead, the boy was killed.

Many kings and great commanders petitioned Octavian for Antony's body, to give him his funeral rites; but he would not take the corpse away from Cleopatra, and she buried him with her own hands, with royal splendor and magnificence, being granted whatever she wished to spend on the funeral. In that extremity of grief — her breasts inflamed and ulcerated from the beating she had given them — she fell into a high fever, and she welcomed it, hoping to use the illness as an excuse to refuse food and so die quietly, without interference. She had her own physician, Olympus, and to him she told the truth, asking his advice and his help in ending her life — as Olympus himself tells us in the account he wrote of these events. But Octavian suspected what she intended and began making menacing remarks about her children, working on her fears for them; and against those siege-engines her resolve shook and gave way, and she let her attendants feed and treat her as they pleased.

A few days later, Octavian himself came to visit and console her. She was lying on a simple pallet, plainly dressed, and as he entered she sprang up from the bed with nothing on but the single garment next to her body and threw herself at his feet — her hair and face wild and disfigured, her voice trembling, her eyes sunken in her head. The marks of the blows she had given herself were visible on her bosom; her whole body, in a word, seemed no less wounded than her soul. And yet for all that, her old charm — the confidence of her youthful beauty — had not wholly left her. In spite of everything, it still sparkled from within and showed itself in every movement of her face. Octavian asked her to lie down again and sat beside her, and she seized the opportunity to justify what she had done, blaming it on necessity and on her fear of Antony. When Octavian answered her point by point and she found herself refuted, she shifted at once to pleading and appeals for pity, like a woman who wanted nothing in the world so much as to go on living. Finally she handed him a list she kept of her treasure; and when Seleucus, one of her stewards, who was standing by, pointed out that various items were missing from it and accused her of hiding them, she flew at him, caught him by the hair, and struck him blow after blow across the face. Octavian, smiling, restrained her. "Isn't it hard, Caesar," she said, "that when you do me the honor of visiting me in this condition, I should be accused by my own servant of setting aside a few women's trinkets — not to adorn my own unhappy self, believe me, but so that I might have some small presents for your Octavia and your Livia, and hope, through their intercession, to find you in some measure inclined to mercy?" Octavian was delighted to hear her talk this way, now feeling certain that she wanted to live. He told her that she might dispose of the things she had set aside exactly as she pleased, and that his treatment of her would be generous beyond her expectation; and he went away well satisfied that he had outwitted her — when in fact it was he who had been deceived.

Among Octavian's companions was a distinguished young man named Cornelius Dolabella, who was not without a certain tenderness for Cleopatra. At her request he sent her word privately that Octavian was about to march back through Syria, and that she and her children were to be sent ahead within three days. When she learned this, she asked Octavian's permission to pour funeral offerings for the departed Antony. It was granted, and she had herself carried to the place where he was buried. There, with her women around her, she embraced his tomb, tears in her eyes, and spoke: "O dearest Antony, it is not long since I buried you with these hands. They were free then; now I am a captive, and I pay you these last duties with a guard set over me, for fear my just grief and sorrow should wear down this slave's body of mine and make it less fit to appear in their triumph over you. Expect no further offerings or libations from me. These are the last honors Cleopatra can pay your memory, for now they are hurrying her far away from you. Nothing could part us while we lived — but in death, it seems, we may have to trade places: you, the Roman, lie buried in Egypt, while I, the Egyptian, am to seek my grave in your country, and that is the only favor I will ask of it. But if the gods below, with whom you now dwell, can do anything or will do anything — since the gods above have betrayed us — do not abandon your living wife. Do not let me be led in triumph to your shame. Hide me, bury me here with you; for of all my countless bitter misfortunes, none has been so heavy as this short time I have lived without you."

When she had finished these lamentations, she crowned the tomb with garlands and kissed it. Then she ordered a bath prepared for herself, and after bathing she lay down to a sumptuous meal. Presently a fellow arrived from the country carrying a little basket. The guards stopped him and asked what was in it, so he pulled aside the leaves on top and showed them it was full of figs. They marveled at how large and beautiful the figs were, and he laughed and invited them to take some; they declined, and, suspecting nothing, told him to carry them in. After her meal, Cleopatra sent Octavian a letter she had written and sealed; then, putting everyone out of the monument except her two women, she shut the doors. Octavian opened the letter, and when he found in it pathetic prayers and entreaties to be buried in the same tomb with Antony, he guessed at once what was happening. His first thought was to rush there himself; then he changed his mind and sent others at full speed to see. But the thing had been quickly done. The messengers arrived at a run and found the guards suspecting nothing; when they opened the doors, they found her stone-dead, lying on a bed of gold, arrayed in all her royal ornaments. Iras, one of her women, lay dying at her feet. Charmion, already swaying, scarcely able to hold up her head, was adjusting the diadem on her mistress's brow. Someone burst out angrily, "A fine deed this, Charmion!" "It is extremely well done," she answered, "and worthy of a queen descended from so many kings." And as she said it, she fell dead beside the bed.

Some say an asp was carried in among those figs, hidden under the leaves — that Cleopatra had arranged for it to strike her before she even knew it was there, but that when she lifted away some of the figs and saw it, she said, "So here it is," and held out her bare arm for the bite. Others say the asp was kept shut in a vase, and that she teased and provoked it with a golden spindle until it seized her arm. But what really happened, no one knows. It was even said that she carried poison in a hollow hairpin that she kept hidden in her hair; yet no spot, no symptom of poison, was found on her body. Nor was the asp ever seen inside the monument — though some claimed to have noticed something like its trail in the sand by the sea, on the side the building faced, below the windows. Some also report that two faint puncture-marks were found on Cleopatra's arm, and Octavian seems to have believed this account, for in his triumph he had carried a figure of Cleopatra with an asp clinging to her. Such are the different stories. But Octavian, though bitterly disappointed by her death, could not help admiring the greatness of her spirit, and he gave orders that her body be buried beside Antony's with royal splendor and magnificence. Her women, too, received honorable burial by his direction. Cleopatra had lived thirty-nine years. For twenty-two of them she had reigned as queen, and for fourteen she had been Antony's partner in his power. Antony was fifty-three by some accounts, fifty-six by others. His statues were all pulled down; Cleopatra's were left standing untouched, because Archibius, one of her friends, gave Octavian two thousand talents to save them from the fate of Antony's.

Antony left seven children by his three wives, and of them only Antyllus, the eldest, was put to death by Octavian. Octavia took in all the rest and raised them with her own. Cleopatra, his daughter by Cleopatra, was given in marriage to Juba, the most accomplished of kings; and Antony, his son by Fulvia, rose so high in favor that — while Agrippa was reckoned to hold first place with Octavian, and the sons of Livia the second — the third place, beyond dispute, belonged to Antony. Octavia had two daughters by her first husband, Marcellus, and one son, also named Marcellus; this son Octavian adopted and made his son-in-law, giving him his daughter in marriage, while Octavia gave one of the daughters to Agrippa. But when Marcellus died almost immediately after the wedding, and Octavia saw that her brother was at a loss to find another son-in-law he could truly trust, it was she who proposed that Agrippa should put away her own daughter and marry Julia. Octavian agreed first, then Agrippa; so Agrippa married Julia, and Octavia, taking her daughter back, married her to the young Antony. Of the two daughters Octavia had borne to Antony, one married Domitius Ahenobarbus; the other, Antonia, famous for her beauty and her good sense, married Drusus, the son of Livia and stepson of Octavian. From these parents came Germanicus and Claudius. Claudius later reigned as emperor; and of the children of Germanicus, Gaius reigned with distinction for a time before he was killed, along with his wife and child. Agrippina, who bore a son, Lucius Domitius, to Ahenobarbus, was later married to Claudius the emperor, who adopted the boy and gave him the name Nero Germanicus. This was the Nero who was emperor in our own time — who put his own mother to death, and whose madness and folly came close to ruining the Roman empire. He was Antony's descendant in the fifth generation.
