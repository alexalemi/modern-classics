Antony

(Part 6 of 7)

That day and the three days after it, the sea ran too rough for the fleets to engage. On the fifth day it fell calm, and they fought. Antony commanded the right squadron himself, with Publicola; Coelius had the left; Marcus Octavius and Marcus Insteius held the center. On the other side, Octavian gave the left wing to Agrippa and commanded the right in person. As for the armies on land, Canidius was Antony's general and Taurus was Octavian's, and both stood drawn up in order along the shore, watching. Antony went about his fleet in a small boat, rowing from ship to ship, encouraging his soldiers — stand firm, he told them, and fight from these big ships as steadily as if you were on solid ground. The ships' masters he ordered to take the enemy's attack lying still, as though riding at anchor, and to hold the mouth of the gulf, which was a narrow and difficult passage. Of Octavian this story is told: while it was still dark, as he left his tent to go the rounds of his ships, he met a man driving a donkey and asked him his name. "My name is Fortunate," the man said, "and my donkey is called Conqueror." Afterward, when Octavian decorated the place with the bronze beaks of captured ships as a monument to his victory, a bronze statue of this man and his donkey stood among them. After inspecting the rest of his fleet, he was rowed out to the right wing, and looked with real admiration at the enemy lying perfectly motionless in the straits — to all appearances a fleet at anchor. For a considerable time he actually believed they were, and he held his own ships back, about a mile off. But around noon a breeze came up off the sea, and Antony's men — tired of waiting so long, and trusting their tall, massive vessels as if they were unbeatable — began to push the left squadron forward. Octavian was overjoyed to see them move. He ordered his own right wing to back water, meaning to draw the enemy out into open sea as far as he could; his plan was to circle, and circle again, and set his light, well-manned galleys on those enormous ships, which their bulk and their shortage of rowers made slow to move and clumsy to handle.

When the fleets engaged, there was no charging, no ramming of one ship by another. Antony's ships were too ponderous to build up the speed that gives a ram its force; and Octavian's did not dare meet them head to head, since their bows were armored with solid masses and spikes of bronze, nor even to strike their sides, which were built of great squared timbers bolted through with iron — a ram would have shattered against them. So the engagement looked like a battle on land, or, more exactly still, like the storming of a walled town. Three or four of Octavian's vessels would cluster around one of Antony's, the men pressing in with spears, javelins, boarding poles, and every device of fire they could hurl, while Antony's men shot back with catapults from wooden towers on the decks. Then Agrippa began stretching his squadron wide to outflank the enemy line, and Publicola had to answer the movement, drawing gradually away from the center, where confusion and alarm set in as Arruntius attacked. And the day still hung undecided, the battle dead even, when suddenly Cleopatra's sixty ships were seen hoisting sail and making for open water in full flight — straight through the ships that were fighting. They had been stationed behind the great vessels, and they threw them into disorder as they broke through. The enemy watched in astonishment as they caught the wind and ran for the Peloponnesus. And here Antony showed the whole world that he was no longer steered by the judgment of a commander, or of a man, or by his own judgment at all. What had once been said as a joke — that the soul of a lover lives in someone else's body — he proved to be the plain and serious truth. As if he had been born a part of her and had to move wherever she moved, the moment he saw her ship sailing away he abandoned everyone who was fighting and dying for him, got himself aboard a galley of five banks of oars, and — taking with him only Alexander of Syria and Scellias — chased after the woman who had already begun his ruin, and would soon finish it.

She, seeing him follow, raised the signal for him to come aboard, and as soon as he drew alongside he was taken onto her ship. But he did not see her, and did not let himself be seen. He walked forward alone and sat down by himself in the ship's prow, without a word, covering his face with both hands. Meanwhile some of Octavian's light Liburnian galleys appeared, in pursuit. Antony ordered the ship brought about to face them, and they all sheered off — except Eurycles the Spartan, who came on hard, brandishing a lance from his deck as if he meant to throw it at Antony himself. Antony stood up at the prow and demanded, "Who is this that pursues Antony?" "I am Eurycles, the son of Lachares," came the answer, "armed with Octavian's fortune to avenge my father's death." Lachares had been condemned on a charge of robbery and beheaded on Antony's orders. Yet Eurycles did not attack Antony's ship. He drove with his full force into the other flagship — for there were two — spun her around with the blow, and captured her, along with a second ship loaded with valuable plate and furnishings. As soon as Eurycles was gone, Antony returned to exactly the same posture as before and sat silent; and so he remained for three days — whether in anger at Cleopatra, or unwilling to reproach her — until they put in at Taenarus. There the women of their company managed first to get them talking to each other, then eating together, then sleeping together. By this time several of the transport ships and some of his friends began finding their way to him out of the rout, all bringing the same news: the fleet was utterly destroyed, but the land army, they believed, still held together. So he sent messengers to Canidius with orders to march the army at full speed through Macedonia into Asia. He himself intended to cross from Taenarus to Africa; and he gave one of the merchant ships — loaded with an immense sum of coined money and with gold and silver vessels from the royal collections — to his friends, telling them to divide it among themselves and see to their own safety. They refused, with tears running down their faces, and he comforted them with all the warmth and kindness imaginable, begging them to go, and wrote letters on their behalf to Theophilus, his steward at Corinth, instructing him to keep them safe and hidden until they could make their peace with Octavian. This Theophilus was the father of Hipparchus, who had stood higher in Antony's favor than almost anyone — and who was the first of all Antony's freedmen to go over to Octavian. He settled afterward at Corinth. So matters stood with Antony.

But at Actium his fleet fought on. It held out against Octavian for hours, and took its worst punishment from a heavy sea that set in dead against the bows; it was four in the afternoon before it finally gave up the struggle. No more than five thousand men were killed, but three hundred ships were taken, as Octavian himself has recorded. Only a few had even known that Antony had fled, and those who were told of it at first simply refused to believe it — that a general with nineteen intact legions and twelve thousand cavalry waiting on the shore could abandon everything and run; he, of all men, who had known good fortune and bad so many times, and had been schooled by a thousand wars and battles in the turns of luck. His soldiers would not give up their longing for him, or their expectation. They kept imagining he would reappear from somewhere, and they showed such generous loyalty to his cause that even when his flight was beyond all doubt, they held together as an army for seven days, ignoring every message Octavian sent them. Only at the end — when Canidius, their own commanding general, had slipped out of the camp by night, and every officer had abandoned them — did they give way and submit to the conqueror. After this Octavian sailed for Athens, where he came to a settlement with Greece and distributed what remained of the grain Antony had stockpiled for his army among the cities, which were in a miserable state — stripped of their money, their slaves, their horses and pack animals. My own great-grandfather Nicarchus used to tell how the entire population of our city was conscripted to carry grain down to the sea near Anticyra, each man with a fixed measure on his shoulders, and men standing over them with whips to keep them moving. They had made one trip of that kind, and the grain for the second was measured out and they were just hoisting it onto their backs when the news came of Antony's defeat — and that news was the saving of Chaeronea, for Antony's quartermasters and soldiers fled at once, and the citizens divided the grain among themselves.

When Antony reached Africa, he sent Cleopatra ahead from Paraetonium into Egypt, and gave himself up to as complete a solitude as he could wish, roaming and wandering about with only two friends — one a Greek, the rhetorician Aristocrates, the other a Roman, Lucilius. Of Lucilius I have told elsewhere how, at Philippi, he let himself be captured by the pursuers, pretending to be Brutus, to give Brutus time to escape. Antony spared his life, and for that reason Lucilius remained true and faithful to him to the very end.

But when the officer commanding his forces in Africa — the man to whom he had entrusted everything there — took them over to Octavian as well, Antony resolved to kill himself, and was stopped by his friends. Coming at last to Alexandria, he found Cleopatra absorbed in an astonishingly bold undertaking. Between the Red Sea and the Mediterranean off Egypt lies the narrow neck of land that may be called the boundary between Asia and Africa; at its narrowest it is not much more than thirty-five miles across. Over this isthmus Cleopatra had conceived the project of hauling her fleet and launching it again in the Arabian Gulf — and then, with her soldiers and her treasure, of finding herself a home on the far side of the world, beyond the reach of war and slavery. But the first galleys carried over were burned by the Arabs of Petra; and since Antony still believed the army at Actium was holding together, she abandoned the enterprise and gave orders to fortify every approach to Egypt. Antony, meanwhile, forsook the city and the company of his friends and built himself a dwelling in the sea near Pharos, on a little breakwater he ran out into the water; and there, shut away from all mankind, he said he wanted nothing but to live the life of Timon — for his case, he said, was the same. The ingratitude and the injuries he had suffered from those he had counted his friends had made him hate and distrust the whole human race.

This Timon was a citizen of Athens who lived around the time of the Peloponnesian War, as you can tell from the comedies of Aristophanes and Plato, where he is mocked as the hater and enemy of mankind. He shunned and rebuffed every approach from every man — yet Alcibiades, then in his reckless youth, Timon would embrace and kiss with every show of affection. When Apemantus, amazed, asked him why, he answered that he knew this young man would one day do the Athenians infinite harm. This Apemantus was the only person he ever allowed into his company, and only now and then — a man of the same sour temper, who imitated his way of life. At the festival of flagons, the Athenian wine feast, the two of them kept the holiday together, and Apemantus remarked, "What a pleasant party this is, Timon." "It would be," he answered, "if you were somewhere else." One day he climbed up onto the speaker's platform before a full assembly, and when a dead silence fell — everyone wondering at so unheard-of a sight — he said: "Men of Athens, I own a little plot of ground, and in it grows a fig tree, on which many of my fellow citizens have been pleased to hang themselves. Since I have decided to build on the spot, I wanted to give public notice, so that any of you who are so inclined may go and hang yourselves before I cut it down." He died and was buried at Halae, near the sea; and after his burial it happened that the shore at that point slipped and gave way, and the sea flowed in around his tomb and made it unreachable by any human foot. The tomb bore this inscription:

  Here I lie, my miserable life at an end.
  Do not ask my name. I curse you, every one.

That epitaph he composed for himself while he was still alive. The one more commonly quoted is by Callimachus:

  Timon the man-hater am I, who lie below.
  Go ahead, traveler — curse me. Only go.

So much for Timon, though there is a great deal more that could be said. Now Canidius arrived, bringing in person the news that the army at Actium was lost. Then Antony learned that Herod of Judaea had gone over to Octavian with several legions and cohorts, that the other kings and princes were deserting him in the same way, and that outside Egypt nothing remained to him. None of it seemed to disturb him. Rather, as if he were glad to put away hope, and with it to be rid of care, he quit that dwelling by the sea, which he called the Timoneum, and let Cleopatra receive him into the palace — and set the whole city on a course of feasting, drinking, and gift-giving. Caesarion, Cleopatra's son by the great Caesar, was enrolled among the young men of age; and Antyllus, Antony's own son by Fulvia, put on the plain toga without the purple border, the dress of manhood. In honor of it, Alexandria did nothing but feast and revel for days. The two of them dissolved the Order of Inimitable Livers and founded another in its place, every bit its equal in splendor, luxury, and extravagance, which they called the Order of Those Who Will Die Together. All who declared they would die with Antony and Cleopatra put down their names, and passed the time, for the present, in every kind of pleasure and an unbroken round of banquets. Cleopatra, meanwhile, was assembling a collection of every variety of deadly poison, and to find out which of them killed with the least pain, she had them tested on prisoners under sentence of death. When she found that the fast poisons always brought sharp agony, and that the gentler ones were slow, she went on to trying venomous animals, watching with her own eyes as one creature was set upon the body of another. This became her daily practice; and she satisfied herself, in the end, that nothing compared with the bite of the asp, which brought on — without convulsions, without groaning — a heavy drowsiness and lethargy, a light sweat on the face, the senses dimming and closing down by easy degrees; the victim showing no sign of pain, but only a mild annoyance at being disturbed or awakened, like someone deep in natural sleep.

At the same time they sent ambassadors to Octavian in Asia. Cleopatra asked that the kingdom of Egypt be preserved for her children; Antony asked leave to live as a private citizen in Egypt — or, if that was too much, to retire to Athens. They were so short of friends, after so many desertions, and so unsure of those who remained, that the embassy was entrusted to Euphronius, the tutor of their children. For Alexas of Laodicea — who had come to know Antony at Rome through Timagenes' introduction, who had held more power over him than any other Greek, and who of all the instruments Cleopatra used on Antony was the most forcible, the chief subverter of whatever good impulses toward Octavia still rose up in his mind from time to time — Alexas had already been sent away, to talk Herod out of deserting. Instead he betrayed his master and stayed with Herod; and then, presuming on Herod's influence, he had the audacity to appear before Octavian. Herod's influence did him no good. He was thrown into chains on the spot, shipped back to his own country, and there, on Octavian's orders, put to death. That was the wage of his treason — paid to Alexas while Antony was still alive.

To any proposal on Antony's behalf, Octavian refused to listen. To Cleopatra his answer was that there was no reasonable favor she might not expect — if she put Antony to death, or drove him out of Egypt. Along with the returning ambassadors he sent a freedman of his own, Thyrsus, a man of intelligence, and by no means ill-suited to carry a young general's messages to a proud beauty who believed in the power of her charms. The long audiences she granted him, and the marked honors she paid him, woke Antony's jealousy. He had Thyrsus seized, whipped, and sent back to Octavian with a letter: the man's busy, insolent meddling had provoked him, and in his present circumstances he could not be expected to have much patience. "But if it offends you," he added, "you have my freedman Hipparchus with you. String him up and give him a whipping, and we'll be even." After that, Cleopatra set out to clear herself and soothe his jealousy with every attention imaginable. Her own birthday she kept modestly, as suited their fallen fortunes; but his she celebrated with such reckless splendor and magnificence that many of the guests came to the table poor and went home rich. Meanwhile letter after letter came to Octavian from Agrippa, telling him his presence was urgently needed at Rome.

So the war was put off for a season. But when winter was over, Octavian marched — he himself through Syria, his commanders through Africa. Pelusium fell, and a story went around that Seleucus had handed it over, and not without Cleopatra's consent; to clear herself, she delivered Seleucus' wife and children into Antony's hands to be put to death. She had built, adjoining the temple of Isis, a set of tombs and monuments of astonishing height and remarkable workmanship, and into these she now moved her treasure — gold, silver, emeralds, pearls, ebony, ivory, cinnamon — and, along with it all, a great quantity of torchwood and tow. Octavian began to be afraid that in some fit of desperation she would set the whole fortune ablaze; so all the while he marched his army toward the city, he kept sending her fresh assurances of his good intentions. He took up his position at the Hippodrome — and Antony made a fierce sally against him, routed his cavalry, drove them back into their trenches, and came home to the palace glowing with satisfaction. Meeting Cleopatra, still in his armor, he kissed her, and presented to her favor the one of his soldiers who had fought most brilliantly. She gave the man a breastplate and helmet of gold; he took them — and that very night deserted to Octavian.

After this Antony sent Octavian a fresh challenge: single combat, man to man. Octavian answered that Antony might find several other ways to end his life. Antony, reflecting that he could not die more honorably than in battle, resolved to attack by land and sea at once. At supper, the story goes, he told his servants to pour freely and serve him with a generous hand — for tomorrow, perhaps, they would not be doing the same; tomorrow they might be waiting on a new master, while he lay on the ground, a corpse, and nothing. His friends wept to hear him talk this way, and seeing it, he told them he would not lead them into a battle from which he expected an honorable death rather than either safety or victory. That night, it is said, around the middle of it, when the whole city lay in silence and dread, waiting on the next day, there suddenly came the sound of every kind of instrument at once — voices singing in harmony, and the cries of a great crowd shouting and dancing, like a troop of revelers of Bacchus on the march. The wild procession seemed to move through the very center of the city toward the gate that faced the enemy camp; there the noise rose to its loudest, and passed out, and was gone. Those who tried to read the sign understood it this way: Bacchus — the god whom Antony had all his life studied to copy and claim — was now abandoning him.

At first light he marched his infantry out of the city and posted them on the high ground, where he stood and watched his fleet put out toward the enemy, waiting to see what it would do. And what it did was this: as the fleets closed, his crews lifted their oars and saluted Octavian's men — who returned the salute — and the whole body of ships, falling into a single fleet, rowed together straight for the city. Antony had barely taken this in when his cavalry deserted too, going over to Octavian; and when his infantry was beaten, he fell back into the city, crying out that Cleopatra had betrayed him to the very enemies he had made for her sake. She, terrified of what he might do to her in his fury and despair, fled to her monument and let down the falling doors, massive with their bars and bolts, and sent messengers to tell Antony she was dead. He believed it. "Now, Antony," he said to himself, "why delay any longer? Fate has taken away the only excuse you had left for wanting to live." He went into his chamber, and as he unfastened and opened his armor he said, "I am not troubled, Cleopatra, to be parted from you — I shall be with you soon. What stings me is that so great a general should be proved slower in courage than a woman." He had a faithful servant named Eros, whom he had long ago bound by a promise to kill him whenever he judged it necessary; and now he called in the promise. Eros drew his sword and raised it as if to strike — then turned suddenly away and ran himself through instead. As the man fell dead at his feet, Antony said, "Well done, Eros. You show your master how to do what you did not have the heart to do yourself." And he drove the sword into his own belly, and laid himself down on the couch. But the wound did not kill him at once. The bleeding slowed when he lay down, and presently he came to himself and begged the men around him to put him out of his pain. Instead they fled the chamber, every one, and left him crying out and writhing there, until Diomede, Cleopatra's secretary, came to him with her orders: he was to be carried to her, into the monument.
