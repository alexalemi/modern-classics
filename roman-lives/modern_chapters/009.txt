Cato the Younger

(Part 4 of 6)

Word that he was coming up the river reached Rome ahead of him. Every magistrate, the priests, the whole senate, and a great part of the people went out to meet him; both banks of the Tiber were lined with spectators, so that his arrival had all the solemnity and honor of a triumph. But it struck many as strange — willful, even proud — that when the consuls and praetors appeared, Cato neither disembarked nor paused to salute them, but swept on upstream in a royal galley of six banks of oars and did not stop until he had brought his vessels to the dockyard. Still, when the money was carried through the streets, the people marveled at the sheer quantity of it, and the senate, once assembled, decreed him in handsome terms an extraordinary praetorship, along with the privilege of watching the public games in a purple-bordered robe. Cato declined every one of these honors. Instead, telling the senate what diligence and honesty he had found in Nicias, Ptolemy's steward, he asked them to grant the man his freedom.

Philippus, Marcia's father, was consul that year, and the power and authority of the office rested, in effect, with Cato — for the other consul deferred to him for his virtue's sake no less than Philippus did on account of the family connection. Cicero, meanwhile, had returned from the exile into which Clodius had driven him and had recovered great standing with the people; and while Clodius was away, he went to the Capitol and carried off by force the records of Clodius's tribuneship that had been stored there. The senate assembled, Clodius made his complaint, and Cicero answered that Clodius had never legally been tribune at all, so that everything done in his tribuneship was void and carried no authority. But Cato interrupted him as he spoke, and at last stood up and declared that he in no way justified or approved of Clodius's conduct — but if they questioned the validity of everything done in his tribuneship, they might just as well question everything Cato himself had done at Cyprus, since that expedition was unlawful if the man who sent him held no lawful office. For his own part, he thought Clodius had been legally made tribune, since the law allowed a patrician to be adopted into a plebeian family. If he had done wrong in office, he could be called to account for it — but the authority of the magistracy ought not to suffer for the faults of the magistrate. Cicero took this hard, and for a long time dropped his friendship with Cato; later, though, the two were reconciled.

Pompey and Crassus, in agreement with Caesar — who crossed the Alps to meet them — had now formed a plan: the two of them would stand for a second consulship, and once in office they would extend Caesar's command by five more years and take for themselves the greatest provinces, with armies and money to hold them. This looked like nothing less than an open conspiracy to subvert the constitution and carve up the empire. Several men of high character had intended to run for the consulship that year, but at the sight of these enormous competitors they all withdrew — all except Lucius Domitius, the husband of Cato's sister Porcia, whom Cato persuaded to stand his ground and not abandon the contest, which, he said, was not about winning a consulship but about saving the liberty of Rome. Meanwhile the more sober part of the citizens were telling one another that the power of Pompey and Crassus must not be allowed to combine — united, it would swell past all bounds and become dangerous to the state — and that one of the two must therefore be refused. So they took Domitius's side, urging and encouraging him to press on and assuring him that many who were afraid to support him openly would help him in private. Pompey's party feared exactly that, and laid an ambush for Domitius as he went down before daybreak, by torchlight, to the Field of Mars. The torchbearer walking just ahead of Domitius was struck down first and killed; then others were wounded, and everyone fled except Cato and Domitius. Cato held him there — though he himself had taken a wound in the arm — crying out, imploring the rest to stay and not abandon, while they had breath in their bodies, the defense of their liberty against tyrants who showed plainly enough, by grasping at power through this kind of violence, how moderately they were likely to use it once they had it. But in the end Domitius, unwilling to face the danger any longer, fled to his own house, and Pompey and Crassus were declared consuls.

Even then Cato refused to give up. He resolved to stand for praetor himself that year, to have some footing for his campaign against them — he did not want to be a private citizen contending with public magistrates. Pompey and Crassus saw the danger, and fearing that the praetorship in Cato's hands might carry as much weight as a consulship, they convened the senate suddenly, without notice to a great many of the senators, and pushed through an order that the praetors-elect should take office immediately, without waiting out the usual interval during which, by law, they could be prosecuted for buying votes. Having thus licensed themselves to bribe freely without being called to account, they put up their own friends and dependents as candidates for the praetorship, handing out money and standing watch over the people as they voted. Even so, Cato's virtue and reputation were on the point of triumphing over all these stratagems, for the people generally felt it was shameful that a price should be paid to reject Cato, when he was the man who ought rather to be paid to accept the office. He carried the first tribe. At that, Pompey immediately manufactured a lie, crying out that he had heard thunder, and abruptly broke up the assembly — for the Romans held thunder a matter of religion and a bad omen, and would conclude no business after it. Before the next election they distributed still larger bribes, drove the best men from the Field, and by these foul means got Vatinius chosen praetor instead of Cato. The story goes that those who had sold their votes so corruptly and dishonestly hurried out of the Field the moment it was done, as if in flight. The rest stayed, gathered together and loudly indignant; one of the tribunes kept the assembly in session, and Cato stood up and, as if by inspiration, foretold all the miseries that were afterward to befall the state. He urged the people to beware of Pompey and Crassus, men whose guilt and whose secret designs were exactly what made them so afraid of having Cato for praetor. When he had finished, a larger crowd followed him home than escorted all the new praetors-elect put together.

Gaius Trebonius now proposed the law assigning provinces to the consuls: one was to have Spain and Africa, the other Egypt and Syria, with full power to make war by land and sea however they saw fit. Once it was proposed, everyone else despaired of stopping it, and neither did nor said anything against it. But Cato, before the voting began, mounted the speaker's platform and demanded a hearing; with great difficulty he was allowed two hours. He spent them informing the people, reasoning with them, foretelling much of what was to come — and then he was permitted to speak no longer. As he pressed on, an officer came up and pulled him down; and once down, he went on shouting, and found plenty of listeners ready to share his outrage. The officer seized him again and forced him out of the forum; the moment Cato broke loose, he turned back toward the platform, shouting to the people to stand by him. After this had happened several times, Trebonius lost his temper and ordered him hauled to prison — but the crowd followed, listening to the speech Cato made as he went, until Trebonius took fright again and ordered him released. That whole day was used up, and Cato had staved the business off. But in the days that followed, some of the citizens were overawed by fears and threats, and others were won with gifts and favors. Aquillius, one of the tribunes, they penned inside the senate house with armed men; Cato, crying out that it was thundering, they drove from the forum; a number of people were wounded, and some were killed; and at last, by open force, they passed the law. Feeling ran so high that a crowd gathered to pull down the statues of Pompey — until Cato went and turned them from the attempt.

Then another law was proposed, this one concerning Caesar's provinces and legions. This time Cato did not appeal to the people; he went to Pompey himself. He told him he did not realize it yet, but he was hoisting Caesar onto his own shoulders; and when Caesar grew too heavy for him, Pompey would find he could neither set the burden down nor bear it any longer, and would come crashing with it — burden and bearer together — onto the commonwealth. Then he would remember Cato's advice, and remember that it had been as much to Pompey's own advantage as it was just and honest in itself. Pompey heard warnings like this again and again, and every time he shrugged them off and let them go, never suspecting that Caesar could change, and trusting always in his own power and good fortune.

Cato was made praetor the following year — and, it must be said, he did the office no more honor and credit by his conspicuous integrity than he did it disgrace and damage by his strange behavior. He would often come to court without shoes, sit on the bench with no tunic under his toga, and in that attire pass judgment in capital cases on men of the highest rank. It was said, too, that he used to drink wine after his morning meal and then transact the business of his office — but that report was false. What was true was this: the people at that time had been thoroughly corrupted by the handouts of office-seekers, and most of them treated the selling of their votes as a regular trade. Cato was determined to root this corruption out of the commonwealth altogether, so he persuaded the senate to order that men elected to any office, even if no one accused them, must come into court and give an account under oath of their conduct during the election. This was intensely hateful to the candidates, and more hateful still to the vast numbers who took the bribes. So it happened that one morning, as Cato was on his way to the tribunal, a great mob crowded around him, shouting curses and abuse and throwing stones. Everyone around the tribunal fled at once; Cato himself was forced off it and jostled through the crush, barely escaping the stones, until with great difficulty he caught hold of the Rostra. There he stood up, bold-faced and unshaken, and mastered the tumult at once; the clamor died, and addressing the crowd in words fit for the occasion, he was heard in complete attention and quelled the disturbance entirely. When the senate afterward praised him for it, he answered, "But I do not commend you — for abandoning your praetor in danger, and bringing him no help."

Meanwhile the candidates for office were in an agony of perplexity: each man dreaded to spend money himself, yet dreaded even more that his rivals would. At last they agreed that each should put down 125,000 drachmas, and then all canvass fairly and honestly, on the condition that anyone caught using bribery forfeited his deposit. The bargain struck, they chose Cato to hold the stakes and judge the matter; they brought him the agreed sums and signed the agreement in his presence. He chose not to take the actual money, but accepted securities from men who stood bound for the candidates. On election day he stationed himself beside the tribune who took the votes and watched everything that passed; he caught one candidate breaking the agreement and promptly ordered him to pay his forfeit to the rest. But they, loudly praising Cato's justice, let the penalty go, judging the exposure punishment enough. The affair brought Cato as much envy as reputation, and many resented his taking upon himself, single-handed, the whole authority of the senate, the courts, and the magistracies. For there is no virtue whose honor and credit earn a man more ill-will than justice — and this because justice, more than any other virtue, wins a man power and standing with the common people. Men merely honor the brave and admire the wise, but the just they also love, and trust, and rely on completely. The bold man they fear and the clever man they suspect; and besides, they credit courage and cleverness to a man's natural makeup rather than to any goodness of his will — courage being a kind of natural vigor of the mind, and wisdom a built-in sharpness. But any man can be just if he only wills it; and that is why injustice is held the most dishonorable of vices — because it is the least excusable.

For exactly this reason all the great men opposed Cato: his virtue felt like a standing rebuke. Pompey in particular regarded the growth of Cato's credit as the ruin of his own power, and kept putting men up to rail against him. Among them was the agitator Clodius, now back in league with Pompey, who openly charged that Cato had pocketed a great deal of the treasure found in Cyprus, and that he hated Pompey only because Pompey had refused to marry his daughter. Cato answered that although they had given him neither a horse nor a single soldier, he had brought home more treasure from Cyprus alone than Pompey had from all his wars and triumphs over the ransacked world; and that he had never sought an alliance with Pompey — not because he thought him unworthy of the connection, but because they differed so much in matters that concerned the commonwealth. "I laid down the province that was offered me when my praetorship ended," he said. "Pompey, on the contrary, keeps many provinces for himself and hands out many more to others. Just now he lent Caesar a force of six thousand men for Gaul — which Caesar never asked the people for, and which Pompey never got the people's consent to give. Men, horses, and arms by the thousand have become the private gifts that private men make to one another. Pompey keeps the titles of commander and general while handing over the armies and provinces to others to govern, and he himself stays home to preside over the contests of the canvass and to stir up riots at the elections — and out of the anarchy he creates among us, we can all see well enough, he is seeking a monarchy for himself." So he paid Pompey back in kind.

Cato had a devoted friend and admirer named Marcus Favonius — much what Apollodorus of Phalerum is said to have been, long before, to Socrates: a man whose master's words went to his head like strong wine and threw him into raptures bordering on frenzy. This Favonius stood for the aedileship and was on the point of losing when Cato, there to help him, noticed that all the ballots were written in a single hand; he exposed the fraud and appealed to the tribunes, who stopped the election. Favonius was afterward elected, and Cato, who assisted him in everything the office involved, also took charge of the shows exhibited in the theater. He gave the actors crowns not of gold but of wild olive, such as they give at the Olympic games; and in place of the usual magnificent presents he offered the Greeks beetroot, lettuces, radishes, and pears, and the Romans earthenware jars of wine, pork, figs, cucumbers, and little bundles of firewood. Some ridiculed the economy of it; others watched with respect this gentle unbending of Cato's usual rigor and austerity. In the end Favonius himself slipped into the crowd, took a seat among the spectators, and applauded Cato — shouting to him to reward the performers who did well, and calling on the people to give their honors to Cato, since he for his own part had placed his whole authority in Cato's hands. Meanwhile, in another theater, Curio, Favonius's colleague, was giving magnificent entertainments; but the people left his shows for Favonius's, and joined heartily in the fun, delighted to watch Favonius playing the private citizen and Cato the master of the games. Cato did all this, in truth, to mock the great sums that others spent, and to teach a lesson: that in amusements men ought to seek amusement and nothing more — the display of a decent cheerfulness, not elaborate productions and costly magnificence that demand endless care and trouble over things of no importance at all.

After this, Scipio, Hypsaeus, and Milo stood for the consulship — and not merely with the by-now familiar and accepted disorders of bribery and corruption, but with weapons and slaughter, driving their recklessness and desperation to the very edge of open civil war. It was proposed that Pompey be empowered to preside over the election. Cato at first opposed this, saying that the laws ought not to look to Pompey for protection — Pompey should look for protection to the laws. But the confusion dragged on, three armies in effect besieged the forum day after day, and when no possible end to the disorder appeared, Cato at last decided that rather than let things reach the final extremity, the senate should freely confer everything on Pompey: better to use a lesser illegality as the cure for the greatest of all, and better to set up a more legal sort of monarchy themselves than to let the chaos run on into a sedition that could only end in one anyway. So Bibulus, a friend of Cato's, moved in the senate that Pompey be created sole consul — for either he would restore lawful government, or Rome would at least be serving its best master. Cato stood up and, against all expectation, seconded the motion, concluding that any government was better than pure confusion, and that he did not doubt Pompey would deal honorably with the commonwealth committed to his charge. Pompey, declared consul, invited Cato to visit him at his house in the suburbs. When he came, Pompey greeted and embraced him warmly, acknowledged what Cato had done for him, and asked for his counsel and support in managing the office. Cato answered that nothing he had said in the past had been said out of hatred for Pompey, and nothing he had done now had been done out of love for him: all of it was for the good of the commonwealth. In private, if Pompey asked, he would freely give his advice; in public, whether he asked or not, he would always speak his mind. And he was as good as his word. First, when Pompey drew up severe laws with heavy fines for those who had bribed the people, Cato advised him to let the past alone and provide for the future — if he went digging up old misdemeanors, it would be hard to know where to stop, and if he decreed new penalties for past acts, it would be unreasonable to punish men under a law they had never had the opportunity of breaking. Later, when a number of prominent men, some of them Pompey's own relations, stood accused, and Pompey went slack and lost his appetite for prosecution, Cato reproved him sharply and pushed him on. Pompey had also made a law forbidding the custom of delivering speeches in praise of men on trial; yet he himself wrote one for Munatius Plancus and sent it in while the case was being pleaded. Cato, who was sitting as one of the judges, clapped his hands over his ears and refused to hear it read. Plancus objected to him before the verdict was given — and was condemned anyway. In fact Cato had become a great trouble and perplexity to defendants in general: they dreaded to have him among their judges, yet did not dare demand his removal. Many men had been condemned precisely because, by refusing Cato, they seemed to admit they could not trust their own innocence; and there were some whose enemies threw it in their teeth as a reproach that they had not accepted Cato for a judge.

In the meantime, Caesar kept close with his forces in Gaul and stayed in arms — while at the same time using his gifts, his riches, and above all his friends to build up his power in the city. And now Cato's old warnings began to rouse Pompey out of the careless security he had been lying in, into a kind of dawning sense of danger at hand. But seeing him slow, unwilling, and timid about taking any step to head Caesar off, Cato resolved to stand for the consulship himself and force Caesar's hand at once: either lay down his arms or show his intentions. Both of Cato's competitors were men of good standing; one of them, Sulpicius, owed a great deal to Cato's credit and authority in the city, and people thought it ungracious and ungrateful of him to run against him. Cato himself took no offense. "It is no wonder," he said, "if a man will not yield to another the thing he counts the greatest good." He had persuaded the senate to order that candidates must ask the people for their votes in person — no soliciting through go-betweens, no bringing others along to speak for them on their rounds. This made the common people bitterly hostile to him: he was taking from them not only the money, but the chance of putting important men in their debt — leaving them, at a stroke, both poorer and less courted. On top of that, Cato was by nature completely unsuited to the business of canvassing; he cared more about sustaining the dignity of his life and character than about winning the office. So he solicited in his own fashion, would not let his friends do the things that win over a crowd, and was rejected: he lost the consulship.

Now, the custom on such occasions was for the loser — and his friends and relations with him — to feel disgraced and humiliated, and to observe a kind of mourning for days afterward. Cato took it so lightly that he oiled himself and played ball in the Field of Mars, ate his breakfast, and went down to the forum as usual, shoeless and tunicless, to stroll about with his friends. Cicero faulted him for this: at a moment when affairs demanded such a consul, he said, Cato would neither exert himself nor condescend to pay the people a little court — and then he gave up and never tried again, though he had stood a second time for the praetorship. Cato answered that he had lost the praetorship the first time not by the honest vote of the people but by the violence and corrupt dealing of his adversaries, whereas in the consular election there had been no foul play at all. He could see perfectly well that the people simply did not like his manners; and an honest man ought not to change his manners for their sake — nor would a wise man attempt the same thing again while the same prejudices stood in his way.
