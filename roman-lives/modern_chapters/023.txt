Antony

(Part 3 of 7)

Antony was so captivated by her that he simply stopped functioning as a Roman commander. Consider what was happening in the world at that moment. His wife Fulvia was in Rome, waging his quarrels against Octavian with actual armies. Parthian troops under Labienus — the king's generals had made this renegade Roman their commander-in-chief — were massed in Mesopotamia, poised to pour into Syria. And Antony let himself be carried off to Alexandria to keep holiday like a schoolboy, playing and amusing himself, squandering the one commodity that, as the orator Antiphon says, is the costliest of all valuables: time. He and Cleopatra formed a sort of club with its own special name — they called it the Order of Inimitable Livers. The members entertained one another daily, each taking a turn, with spending so extravagant it defied measure or belief. Philotas, a physician from Amphissa who was then a medical student in Alexandria, used to tell my grandfather Lamprias about it. Philotas had gotten to know one of the royal cooks, and the cook, since Philotas was a young man, invited him to come see the sumptuous preparations for a supper. He was taken into the kitchen, where he marveled at the prodigious spread of everything — but what stopped him cold was the sight of eight wild boars roasting whole. "You must have an enormous number of guests," he said. The cook laughed at his innocence and told him there would be no more than twelve at supper — but every dish had to be served roasted to the exact turn, and if anything was mistimed by so much as a minute, it was ruined. "And Antony," said the cook, "may sit down to supper right now, or maybe not for an hour; maybe he'll call for wine first, or fall into conversation and put it off. So it isn't one supper we have to keep ready — it's many suppers, because there's no guessing his hour." That was Philotas's story, and he had more. He later became one of the medical attendants of Antony's eldest son by Fulvia, and was invited fairly often to the young man's table, along with his other companions, whenever the boy was not dining with his father. One day another physician at the table was talking loudly and making a nuisance of himself to the whole company, until Philotas shut his mouth with this piece of trick logic: "In some states of fever the patient should take cold water; everyone who has a fever is in some state of fever; therefore in a fever cold water should always be taken." The man was struck completely dumb, and Antony's son, delighted, laughed out loud and said, "Philotas, I make you a present of all of that" — pointing to a sideboard loaded with plate. Philotas thanked him warmly, but never for a moment imagined that a boy of that age could give away things of such value. Soon afterward, however, the whole service was brought to his rooms, and he was asked to put his seal on it. When he pushed it away, afraid to accept such a gift, the man who had brought it said, "What's the matter with you? Don't you know the giver is Antony's son, who is free to give it away if it were all solid gold? Still, if you'll take my advice, let us pay you its value in money instead — there may be some antique piece in the set, some famous bit of craftsmanship, that Antony would be sorry to lose." These are the stories my grandfather told us Philotas loved to repeat.

But to return to Cleopatra. Plato admits four kinds of flattery; she had a thousand. Whether Antony was in a serious mood or a merry one, she had at any moment some fresh delight or charm ready to meet it. She was at his side at every turn and never let him out of her grasp by day or by night. She diced with him, drank with him, hunted with him; when he drilled at arms, she was there to watch. At night she would go rambling with him through the city to knock at people's doors and windows and torment them, dressed as a servant-woman — for Antony went out disguised as a servant too. He often came home from these expeditions thoroughly insulted, and sometimes soundly beaten, though most people had a good guess who he was. The Alexandrians, for their part, rather liked it all. They joined in his fun good-humoredly and kindly, and said they were much obliged to Antony for playing his tragic roles for the Romans and saving his comedy for them. To catalogue all his follies would be trifling without end, but his fishing must not be forgotten. He went out angling one day with Cleopatra and had the bad luck to catch nothing while his mistress looked on. So he secretly ordered the fishermen to dive under the water and fasten onto his hooks fish that had already been caught — and he hauled these in so fast that the Egyptian saw through it. Pretending to be lost in admiration, she told everyone how marvelously skilled Antony was, and invited them to come out the next day and watch him again. So a crowd of them boarded the fishing boats, and the moment Antony let down his line, one of her servants beat his divers to the hook and fixed on it a salted fish from Pontus. Antony felt the line pull, drew up his prize, and — as you can imagine — the laughter was tremendous. "Leave the fishing-rod, general, to us poor sovereigns of Pharos and Canopus," said Cleopatra. "Your game is cities, provinces, and kingdoms."

While he was amusing himself with this boys' play, two dispatches arrived. One came from Rome: his brother Lucius and his wife Fulvia, after many quarrels between themselves, had joined together in war against Octavian, lost everything, and fled from Italy. The other brought news hardly better: Labienus, at the head of the Parthians, was overrunning Asia from the Euphrates and Syria all the way to Lydia and Ionia. So at last — barely — rousing himself from his long sleep and shaking off the fumes of the wine, Antony set out to attack the Parthians and got as far as Phoenicia. But then came heartbroken letters from Fulvia, and he turned back toward Italy with two hundred ships. On the voyage he took aboard friends of his who were fleeing Italy, and from them he learned that Fulvia had been the real cause of the whole war — a woman of restless spirit and plenty of daring, who had hoped that stirring up trouble in Italy would pry Antony away from Cleopatra. But it happened that Fulvia, sailing to meet her husband, fell sick on the way and died at Sicyon, which made a settlement all the easier. For when Antony reached Italy, Octavian showed no intention of pressing any charge against him, and Antony for his part shifted the blame for everything onto Fulvia. Their friends would not allow the moment to be wasted in picking that excuse apart; they made the reconciliation first, and then divided the empire between the two men, taking the Ionian Sea as the boundary — the eastern provinces falling to Antony, the western to Octavian, with Africa left to Lepidus. And they agreed that, whenever they did not choose to hold the consulship themselves, each in turn should hand the office to friends of his own choosing.

These terms satisfied everyone, but it was felt that some closer tie would be desirable, and for that, fortune supplied the occasion. Octavian had an elder half-sister — not of the whole blood, for his mother was Attia, and hers was Ancharia. He was deeply attached to this sister, Octavia, and she was, by every account, a wonder of a woman. Her husband, Gaius Marcellus, had died not long before, and Antony was now a widower by Fulvia's death — for though he did not deny his passion for Cleopatra, he disowned any talk of marriage; on that one point, reason was still fighting its case against the charms of the Egyptian. Everyone pushed to promote this new alliance, in the full expectation that once Octavia's beauty, honor, and good sense had won his affections — and it seemed certain they would — the whole arrangement would be held safely in the happy course of friendship. So, with both parties agreed, they went up to Rome to celebrate the wedding, the senate granting a dispensation from the law that forbade a widow to remarry within ten months of her husband's death.

Sextus Pompey, the son of Pompey the Great, held Sicily at this time, and with his ships — commanded by Menas the pirate and by Menecrates — he so infested the Italian coast that no vessel dared venture into those waters. Sextus had treated Antony with real humanity, having taken in his mother when she fled with Fulvia, so it was judged proper that he too should be brought into the peace. They met near the promontory of Misenum, by the mole of the harbor, Sextus with his fleet riding at anchor close by, Antony and Octavian with their troops drawn up along the shore. There it was settled that Sextus should keep the government of Sicily and Sardinia undisturbed, on condition that he sweep the seas clear of pirates and send a fixed quantity of grain to Rome every year.

With this agreed, they invited one another to supper, and the lot fell to Sextus to give the first entertainment. When Antony asked where it would be held, Sextus pointed to his flagship, a great galley of six banks of oars. "There," he said. "That is the only house Pompey inherits from his father." It was a jab at Antony, who was then living in the elder Pompey's confiscated house. Fixing the ship firmly on her anchors and laying a bridge of planks from the promontory to her deck, Sextus gave them a hearty welcome. And when the party had warmed up, and the jokes about Antony and Cleopatra were flowing freely, Menas the pirate leaned close to Sextus and whispered in his ear, "Shall I cut the cables and make you master not of Sicily and Sardinia only, but of the whole Roman empire?" Sextus considered it for a moment, then answered, "Menas, you should have done it without telling me. As it is, we must let things rest. I do not break my word." And so, after being entertained in turn by the other two, he sailed back to Sicily.

Once the treaty was completed, Antony dispatched Ventidius into Asia to check the Parthian advance, while he himself, as a courtesy to Octavian, accepted the office of priest to the deified Julius Caesar. In every affair of state and matter of consequence, the two men treated each other with consideration and friendliness. But it gnawed at Antony that in all their amusements — any contest of skill or of luck — Octavian kept winning. Antony had with him an Egyptian diviner, one of those who cast horoscopes, and this man — whether to please Cleopatra, or because his art genuinely told him so — declared openly that Antony's fortune, bright and glorious as it was, stood in the shadow of Octavian's, and advised him to keep as far away from the young man as he could. "Your Genius dreads his," he said. "Apart from him, your guardian spirit is proud and brave; in his presence it turns cowed and dejected." And events did seem to bear the Egyptian out. Whenever they drew lots for some trifle or threw dice, Antony lost; whenever they set their gamecocks or their quails against each other, Octavian's won. This nettled Antony in secret and made him trust the Egyptian's science all the more. So, leaving the management of affairs in Italy to Octavian, he left for Greece, taking with him Octavia, who had just borne him a daughter.

While he wintered in Athens, he received the first reports of Ventidius's victories over the Parthians: he had beaten them in battle and killed Labienus along with Pharnapates, the best general their king Hyrodes had. To celebrate, Antony gave a public feast throughout Greece, and at the games held in Athens he served personally as steward of the prizes. Leaving at home the insignia that are carried before a general, he appeared in public in a Greek gown and white shoes, with the steward's wands borne before him, and he did his duty in full — taking the combatants by the neck to pull them apart when they had fought long enough.

When the time came to leave for the war, he took a garland from the sacred olive tree and, obeying some oracle, filled a vessel with water from the spring of the Clepsydra to carry with him. In the meantime Pacorus, the Parthian king's son, marching into Syria with a large army, was met by Ventidius, who gave him battle in the country of Cyrrhestica, killed a great number of his men, and killed Pacorus among the first of them. This victory became one of the most celebrated feats of Roman arms, and it fully avenged the defeats under Crassus: after losing three battles in a row, the Parthians were forced to keep themselves within the bounds of Media and Mesopotamia. Ventidius chose not to push his luck further, for fear of stirring Antony's jealousy; instead he turned his forces on the peoples who had abandoned the Roman cause and brought them back to their old obedience. Among the rest, he besieged Antiochus, king of Commagene, in the city of Samosata. Antiochus offered a thousand talents for his pardon and promised submission to Antony's commands, but Ventidius told him he would have to send to Antony directly — for Antony was already on the march, and had ordered Ventidius to make no terms with Antiochus. He wanted this one exploit, at least, credited to his own name, so that people would not think every success was won by his lieutenants. But the siege dragged on and on; once the men inside saw their offers refused, they defended themselves stoutly, until at last Antony, finding he was accomplishing nothing, and ashamed and sorry that he had refused the first offer, was glad to settle with Antiochus for three hundred talents. After giving some orders for the affairs of Syria, he returned to Athens, and, paying Ventidius the honors he had richly earned, sent him home to receive his triumph. Ventidius is, to this day, the only man who has ever triumphed over the Parthians. He was of obscure birth, but Antony's friendship gave him the opportunity to show what he could do, and he made such glorious use of it that he confirmed the common observation about Antony and Octavian both: that they were more fortunate in what they did through their lieutenants than in their own persons. For Sossius, too, won great successes, and Canidius, whom Antony left in Armenia, defeated the people there along with the kings of the Albanians and Iberians, and marched victorious all the way to the Caucasus. By these means the fame of Antony's arms grew great among the barbarian nations.

Yet once again, on the strength of some unflattering stories, Antony took offense at Octavian, and set sail for Italy with three hundred ships. Refused entry at the port of Brundisium, he made for Tarentum instead. There his wife Octavia, who had sailed from Greece with him, asked leave to visit her brother. She was then heavily pregnant, having already borne Antony a second daughter. On her way she met Octavian, accompanied by his two friends Agrippa and Maecenas, and drawing those two aside, with urgent entreaties and many tears she told them that she, the most fortunate woman on earth, was in danger of becoming the most miserable. As things stood, every eye was fixed on her as the wife and the sister of the two great commanders; but if rash counsels prevailed and it came to war — "I shall be wretched," she said, "past all remedy. Whichever side wins, I lose." Octavian was overcome by her pleading and came on to Tarentum in a peaceable frame of mind, and the people there beheld a most stately spectacle: a vast army drawn up along the shore and as great a fleet in the harbor, with not one act of hostility — nothing but the greetings of friends, and every expression of joy and kindness, passing from one armament to the other. Antony entertained Octavian first, Octavian conceding even this for his sister's sake. At length they struck an agreement: Octavian would give Antony two of his legions for the Parthian war, and Antony in return would leave him a hundred armed galleys. Octavia then obtained, over and above this, twenty light ships from her husband for her brother, and a thousand foot-soldiers from her brother for her husband. So they parted good friends. Octavian went off at once to make war on Sextus Pompey for Sicily, and Antony, leaving in Octavian's care his wife and children — along with his children by Fulvia — set sail for Asia.

But the mischief that had lain quiet so long — the passion for Cleopatra, which better thoughts had seemed to lull and charm into forgetfulness — gathered strength again as he drew near Syria, and broke out in flame. In the end, like the restive, rebellious horse that Plato makes of the human soul, kicking away all good and wholesome counsel and breaking fully loose, he sent Fonteius Capito to bring Cleopatra to Syria. And when she arrived, the present he made her was no small or trifling thing: Phoenicia; Coele-Syria; Cyprus; a great part of Cilicia; the balm-producing side of Judaea; and the stretch of Arabia where the Nabathaeans reach down to the outer sea. Lavish gifts — and they deeply displeased the Romans. He had handed great governments and kingdoms to plenty of private persons before, and stripped many kings of theirs — Antigonus of Judaea he had actually beheaded, the first king ever put to death that way — yet nothing stung the Romans like the shame of these honors paid to Cleopatra. Their resentment grew sharper still when he acknowledged as his own the twin children she had borne him, naming them Alexander and Cleopatra, and adding as surnames the titles of the Sun and the Moon. But Antony, who knew how to put a fine face on the most disreputable conduct, would say that the greatness of the Roman empire lay more in the giving of kingdoms than in the taking of them, and that the way to spread noble blood through the world was to father a new line of kings in every place. His own ancestor, he said, had been born of Hercules that way — and Hercules had never confined his hopes of offspring to a single womb, never feared any law like Solon's or any audit of his procreation, but had freely let nature have her way in founding and beginning many families.

After Phraates killed his father Hyrodes and seized the kingdom, many Parthians fled the country — among them Monaeses, a man of great distinction and authority, who took refuge with Antony. Antony, seeing in his case a likeness to that of Themistocles, and comparing his own wealth and generosity of spirit to those of the old Persian kings, gave him three cities: Larissa, Arethusa, and Hierapolis, which used to be called Bambyce. But when the Parthian king soon recalled Monaeses, pledging his word and honor for his safety, Antony was quite willing to let him go — for he hoped by this to catch Phraates off guard, lulled into believing the peace would hold. All Antony asked of the king was the return of the Roman standards taken when Crassus was killed, and of whatever prisoners still remained alive. That done, he sent Cleopatra back to Egypt and marched through Arabia and Armenia. When his own forces had assembled and been joined by those of his allied kings — there were a great many of these, the most considerable being Artavasdes, king of Armenia, at the head of six thousand horse and seven thousand foot — he held a general muster. The count came to sixty thousand Roman infantry, ten thousand cavalry of Spaniards and Gauls who ranked as Romans, and thirty thousand more, horse and foot together, from the other nations. And these vast preparations — which alarmed even the Indians beyond Bactria and made all Asia shake — were rendered useless to him, we are told, because of Cleopatra. For in order to spend the winter with her, he pushed the war forward before its proper time, and everything he did, he did without clear thought — like a man with no real command of his own faculties, who, under the influence of some drug or spell, was forever glancing back toward her, his mind fixed far more on a quick return than on conquering his enemies.

Consider his mistakes. First: he ought to have taken up winter quarters in Armenia to rest his men, who were exhausted from marches totaling at least a thousand miles, and then moved at the first opening of spring to invade Media before the Parthians were out of their own winter quarters. He hadn't the patience to wait. He marched straight into the province of Atropatene, leaving Armenia on his left hand, and laid waste the whole country. Second: his haste was so great that he left behind the siege engines absolutely required for any siege — they followed the camp in three hundred wagons, and among them was a battering ram eighty feet long — none of which, once lost or damaged, could be repaired or replaced, since the provinces of upper Asia produce no trees long or hard enough for such work. Nevertheless he left them all behind as a mere drag on his speed, in the care of a detachment under Statianus, the officer of the baggage train. Antony himself laid siege to Phraata, a principal city of the king of Media, where that king's wife and children were. And when real need soon showed him the size of his blunder in leaving the siege train behind, there was nothing for it but to come up close and raise a mound against the walls — with enormous labor and a great loss of time. Meanwhile Phraates came down with a large army, and hearing that the wagons with the battering engines had been left behind, he sent out a strong force of cavalry. Statianus was caught by surprise; he himself and ten thousand of his men were killed, the engines were all broken to pieces, and many men were taken prisoner — among them King Polemon.

This great disaster at the very opening of the campaign badly discouraged Antony's army, and Artavasdes, the king of Armenia, deciding that Roman prospects were poor, withdrew with all his forces from the camp — though he had been the chief promoter of the war. The Parthians, emboldened by their success, rode up to the Romans at the siege works and taunted them freely. Antony, fearing that idleness would only deepen his soldiers' despondency and alarm, took all the cavalry, ten legions, and three praetorian cohorts of heavy infantry, and resolved to go out foraging — reckoning this the best way to draw the enemy into a battle on favorable terms. He marched a day's journey from camp, and finding the Parthians hovering around him, ready to strike while he was on the move, he had the signal for battle hung out in the encampment, but at the same time struck the tents, as if he meant not to fight but simply to lead his men home. So he marched them past the enemy, who were drawn up in a half-moon formation, with orders that the moment the legions were close enough to support them, the cavalry should charge. The Parthians, standing still as the Romans filed past, watched the army in open admiration: rank after rank passing at equal intervals, in perfect order and silence, pikes ready in hand. Then the signal was given, the horse wheeled sharply on the Parthians and charged them with loud cries, and the Parthians met them bravely, though at once they were too close for bowshot. But when the legions came on with a roar and a clashing of arms, the Parthian horses shied in terror — the men too — and they broke without holding their ground. Antony pressed them hard, in high hopes that this one victory might end the war. The infantry kept up the pursuit for six miles, the cavalry three times that distance — and yet, when they summed up the advantage, they had taken only thirty prisoners and killed a mere eighty men. Gloom and discouragement settled over them all at the thought of it: when they won, this was all their winning came to; but when they lost, as at the capture of the wagons, they lost ten thousand men.
