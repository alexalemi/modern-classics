Caesar

(Part 5 of 6)

She took a small boat and a single companion — Apollodorus the Sicilian — and came ashore near the palace at dusk. There was no way to get in unseen, until she hit on the idea of rolling herself up full-length inside a bed-sack; Apollodorus tied up the bedding, hoisted it onto his back, and carried it straight through the gates to Caesar's rooms. That piece of bold wit was what first captivated Caesar; the charm of her company did the rest. He arranged a reconciliation between Cleopatra and her brother, on the condition that she rule beside him as his colleague in the kingdom. A festival was held to celebrate the settlement, and at it Caesar's barber — a busy, listening fellow whose sheer timidity kept him poking his nose into everything — discovered that a plot was under way against Caesar, laid by Achillas, the general of the king's forces, and Pothinus the eunuch. The moment Caesar learned of it, he threw a guard around the banquet hall and had Pothinus killed. Achillas escaped to the army and raised a troublesome, dangerous war — no easy thing for Caesar to manage with his handful of soldiers against so powerful a city and so large an army. His first difficulty was water: the enemy had blocked the canals. The second came when they tried to cut him off from the sea. To head off that danger he set fire to his own ships, and the flames, after consuming the docks, spread onward and destroyed the great library. The third came in the fighting near the Pharos, when he leaped from the mole into a small boat to help his soldiers, who were in trouble. The Egyptians pressed in on him from every side, so he threw himself into the sea and swam clear only with great difficulty. This was the moment, the story goes, when he had a bundle of manuscripts in his hand and refused to let them go: though missiles rained down on him the whole way and he was forced again and again to duck his head under the water, he held the papers up dry in one hand and swam with the other, while his little boat sank behind him. At last, once the king had gone over to Achillas and his party, Caesar attacked and beat them. Many died in that battle, and the king himself was never seen again. Caesar left Cleopatra on the throne as queen of Egypt — soon afterward she bore him a son, whom the Alexandrians called Caesarion — and then set out for Syria.

From Syria he crossed into Asia, where he learned that Domitius had been beaten by Pharnaces, son of Mithridates, and had fled Pontus with a handful of men — and that Pharnaces, pressing his victory hungrily, had already made himself master of Bithynia and Cappadocia, had designs on Lesser Armenia besides, and was calling on all the kings and princes of the region to rise. Caesar marched against him at once with three legions, fought him near Zela, drove him out of Pontus, and destroyed his army entirely. Reporting the action to Amantius, a friend of his in Rome, he caught its whole speed and suddenness in three words: I came, I saw, I conquered. In Latin — veni, vidi, vici — the three words share the same clipped cadence, and the brevity could not be more fitting.

From there he crossed to Italy and reached Rome at the close of the year for which he had been chosen dictator a second time — though that office had never before lasted a whole year — and he was elected consul for the year to come. People spoke badly of him because, after mutinous soldiers killed two men of praetorian rank, Cosconius and Galba, he gave the mutineers nothing worse than the mild rebuke of addressing them as "citizens" instead of "fellow soldiers" — and then handed each man a thousand drachmas and a share of land in Italy besides. He was blamed, too, for the behavior of his lieutenants: Dolabella's extravagance, Amantius's greed, Antony's debauchery, and Corfinius, who pulled down Pompey's house and rebuilt it on the grounds that it was not magnificent enough. The Romans were disgusted by all of this. Caesar knew exactly what these men were, and disapproved of them — but for the sake of his own scheme of government he was forced to use the instruments that would serve him.

After the battle of Pharsalia, Cato and Scipio had fled to Africa, and there, with the help of King Juba, they assembled a considerable army. Caesar resolved to fight it. He crossed to Sicily around the winter solstice, and to knock every hope of delay out of his officers' heads he pitched his camp right on the beach; the moment he had a fair wind he put to sea with three thousand infantry and a few horsemen. Once he had landed them, he slipped back secretly, anxious about the larger part of his army — but he met those troops already at sea and brought the whole force into the same camp. There he learned that the enemy were leaning on an ancient oracle: the family of the Scipios would always be victorious in Africa. Now, there happened to be in his own army a man of the house of the Africani — an otherwise obscure and unimpressive fellow — whose name was Scipio Sallutio. This man Caesar put at the head of his troops, as though he were the general, in every one of the frequent battles he was compelled to fight. Whether he did it in mockery, to ridicule the enemy's Scipio, or in earnest, to steal the omen for his own side, is hard to say. And he was compelled to fight often, because he was desperately short of food for his men and forage for his horses — so short that he fed the horses on seaweed, rinsed thoroughly to take off the salt and mixed with a little grass to make it more palatable. The Numidians, numerous and superbly mounted, commanded the countryside wherever he went. One day Caesar's cavalry, having nothing to do, were amusing themselves watching an African man who danced and played the flute at the same time, marvelously well. They were so absorbed that they dismounted and handed their horses to some boys — and at that moment the enemy swept around them, killed some, and chased the rest headlong into their own camp. If Caesar himself, with Asinius Pollio, had not rushed to help and stopped the rout, the war would have ended right there. In another engagement, too, the enemy got the better of the fight, and Caesar, it is said, seized a fleeing standard-bearer by the neck, spun him around, and said, "Look — that is the way to the enemy."

Scipio, flushed with these early successes, decided to risk one decisive battle. Leaving Afranius and Juba in two separate camps not far off, he marched to Thapsus and began building a fortified camp above a lake, to serve as a center for operations and a refuge if things went wrong. While Scipio was busy with the work, Caesar moved with unbelievable speed. He made his way through thick woods and country everyone thought impassable, cut off one body of the enemy, and struck another head-on. Having routed them, he rode his opportunity and the current of his good fortune: at the first onset he carried Afranius's camp and sacked the camp of the Numidians, whose king, Juba, was glad to escape with his life. In a small part of a single day he had made himself master of three camps and killed fifty thousand of the enemy, at the cost of only fifty of his own men. That is one account of the battle. Others say Caesar was not in the action at all — that his old sickness, the epilepsy, took hold of him just as he was drawing up his army. He felt it coming, and before it could scramble his senses completely — he was already beginning to shake under it — he had himself carried into a nearby fort and rested there. Of the men of consular and praetorian rank captured after the battle, Caesar put several to death; others saved him the trouble by killing themselves.

Cato had undertaken the defense of Utica, and for that reason was not in the battle. Caesar wanted badly to take him alive, and hurried toward the city — and when word came that Cato had killed himself, he was visibly shaken, though exactly why is not agreed. What he certainly said was this: "Cato, I grudge you your death, as you grudged me the honor of saving your life." Yet the treatise he wrote against Cato after his death hardly suggests kindness, or any inclination to be reconciled. How likely is it that he would have spared the living man when he poured such bitterness on the dead one's memory? Then again, from his clemency to Cicero, to Brutus, and to many others who had fought against him, people have concluded that the book was written not so much out of hatred of Cato as in Caesar's own defense. It came about like this. Cicero had written a tribute to Cato and given it his name; and a composition by so great a master, on so excellent a subject, was bound to end up in everyone's hands. That stung Caesar, who regarded praise of his dead enemy as nothing less than an indictment of himself. So he wrote his Anti-Cato, a collection of everything that could be said against the man. The two works — like Cato and Caesar themselves — have each their own admirers to this day.

On his return to Rome, Caesar did not fail to stand before the people and deliver a magnificent account of his victory: he had subdued a country, he told them, that would supply the public every year with two hundred thousand Attic bushels of grain and three million pounds of olive oil. He then celebrated three triumphs — for Egypt, for Pontus, and for Africa — the last of them, officially, for the victory over King Juba, not over Scipio. Juba's little son was carried in that triumph, and he proved the luckiest captive who ever walked in one: from a barbarian Numidian prince, he went on to earn a place among the most learned historians of Greece. After the triumphs, Caesar distributed rewards to his soldiers and treated the people to feasts and shows. He banqueted the entire population at a single sitting, at twenty-two thousand dining couches, and put on displays of gladiators and mock naval battles in honor, as he said, of his daughter Julia — though she had been dead for years. When the shows were over, a count was taken of the people. Where there had been three hundred and twenty thousand, there were now one hundred and fifty thousand. So great was the wound the civil war had cut into Rome alone — to say nothing of what the rest of Italy and the provinces had suffered.

He was now chosen consul a fourth time, and went into Spain against Pompey's sons. They were young, but they had gathered a very large army and showed they had the courage and the skill to command it, and they put Caesar in extreme danger. The great battle came near the town of Munda. Caesar, seeing his men hard pressed and putting up a feeble resistance, ran through the ranks shouting at his soldiers, asking whether they were not ashamed to hand him over to boys. With enormous difficulty, and the best effort he had in him, he forced the enemy back, killing thirty thousand of them — though at the cost of a thousand of his own best men. Coming back from the fight, he told his friends that he had often fought for victory, but this was the first time he had ever fought for his life. He won the battle on the feast of Bacchus, the very day on which, four years earlier, Pompey had set out for the war. The younger of Pompey's sons escaped; a few days after the battle, Didius brought Caesar the head of the elder. This was the last war he ever fought — and the triumph he celebrated for it displeased the Romans beyond anything he had done. This time he had not defeated foreign generals or barbarian kings; he had destroyed the children and the family of one of the greatest men Rome had produced, however unfortunate. It did not look well to lead a parade celebrating his country's calamities — to rejoice in things whose only possible excuse, before gods or men, was that they had been absolutely necessary. And until then he had never sent so much as a letter or a messenger to announce a victory over his fellow citizens; he had seemed ashamed of such actions, not eager for credit.

Nevertheless the Romans bowed to his fortune and accepted the bit, hoping that one-man rule would give them room to breathe after so many civil wars and calamities: they made him dictator for life. This was tyranny openly admitted — his power was now not only absolute but permanent. Cicero made the first proposals in the senate for conferring honors on him, and those at least might be said to stay within the limits of ordinary human moderation. But others competed to outdo one another, and piled the honors so extravagantly high that they made Caesar odious even to mild and indifferent men, by the sheer pretension and excess of the titles they decreed him. His enemies are thought to have had a hand in this along with his flatterers: it gave them ammunition against him, a ready justification for whatever move they might make — for now that the civil wars were over, there was nothing else to charge him with. And in fairness, the senate had good reason to decree a temple to Clemency in thanks for the mildness with which he used his victory. He not only pardoned many of the men who had fought against him — to some he gave honors and offices, notably Brutus and Cassius, who both became praetors. Pompey's statues had been thrown down; Caesar set them up again, which drew from Cicero the remark that by raising Pompey's statues, Caesar had fixed his own in place. When his friends urged him to keep a bodyguard, and several volunteered for the duty, he refused to hear of it: better to suffer death once, he said, than to live always in fear of it. The affection of the people, he believed, was the best and surest guard a man could have; so he courted them again with public feasts and general distributions of grain, and to satisfy his army he sent out colonies to several places. The most remarkable were Carthage and Corinth — two cities that had been destroyed at the same time, and now, together, were rebuilt and repeopled.

As for the men of high rank, he promised some of them future consulships and praetorships, consoled others with different offices and honors, and gave everyone hope of his favor by the eagerness he showed to rule with the general good will. When the consul Maximus died with a single day of his term remaining, Caesar made Caninius Revilius consul for that one day. Crowds set off to pay the customary compliments and attentions to the new consul, and Cicero said, "Let us hurry — or the man will be out of office before we get there."

Caesar was born to do great things, and his passion was for honor. The many brilliant exploits behind him did not incline him to sit still and enjoy the fruit of his labors; they were fuel and provocation instead, kindling ideas of still greater actions and an appetite for fresh glory, as though the present supply were all used up. The feeling was really a kind of contest with himself, as if with a rival: a struggle to make his future outdo his past. His plan was to make war on the Parthians; once they were subdued, to pass through Hyrcania and march along the Caspian Sea to the Caucasus; then to swing around the Black Sea into Scythia, overrun the countries bordering Germany and then Germany itself, and return through Gaul to Italy — closing the circle of his intended empire and bounding it on every side by the ocean. While preparations for this expedition went forward, he proposed to cut a canal through the isthmus at Corinth, and appointed Anienus to superintend the work. He planned to divert the Tiber into a deep channel running straight from Rome to Circeii and into the sea near Tarracina, giving the merchants who traded to Rome a safe and easy passage. Beyond that, he intended to drain the marshes at Pomentium and Setia, reclaiming enough ground from the water to employ many thousands of men in farming; to raise great seawalls on the shore nearest Rome, to keep the sea from breaking in on the land; to clear the coast at Ostia of the hidden rocks and shoals that made it unsafe for shipping; and to build ports and harbors fit to receive the great number of vessels that would crowd into them.

All these designs stayed on paper. But his reform of the calendar, undertaken to fix the irregularity in the reckoning of time, was not only worked out with great scientific ingenuity but carried through to completion — and it proved enormously useful. From ancient times the Romans had lacked any reliable rule for keeping the cycle of their months in step with the course of the year, so their festivals and days of sacrifice drifted little by little until they were finally being kept in seasons opposite to the ones intended. Even in Caesar's day the people had no way of computing the solar year at all; only the priests could say what time it was, and they, at their own pleasure and without any notice, would slip in the extra month they called Mercedonius. Numa had been the first to insert that month, but his expedient was a poor patch, entirely inadequate to correct the errors that piled up in the annual cycles — as we have shown in his Life. Caesar called in the best philosophers and mathematicians of his time to settle the question, and out of the systems laid before him he built a new and more exact method of correcting the calendar — the one the Romans use to this day, and it serves them better than any other nation in avoiding the errors of the cycles. Yet even this gave offense to the men who looked on his position with an evil eye and felt the weight of his power. When someone in Cicero's company happened to remark that the constellation Lyra would rise the next morning, the orator answered, "Yes — by edict," as if even the stars now rose on compulsion.

But what brought down on him the most open and deadly hatred was his desire to be king. It gave the common people their first real grievance against him, and it handed the men who had long been his secret enemies their most plausible excuse. Those who wanted to procure him the title spread a story that, according to the Sibylline books, the Romans could conquer the Parthians only when they fought under the command of a king — and not before. And one day, as Caesar was coming down from Alba to Rome, some in the crowd were bold enough to hail him as king. Seeing that the people took it badly, he put on an air of being offended himself, and said his name was Caesar, not King. A general silence followed, and he passed on looking far from pleased. Another time, the senate voted him some extravagant honors, and the consuls and praetors came to deliver the message — the whole body of the senate attending them — while he sat on the rostra. He did not rise. He received them as though they were private citizens, and remarked that his honors wanted trimming back rather than adding to. This offended not only the senate but the common people too, who felt that an insult to the senate fell on the whole republic; and everyone who could decently leave walked away, deeply upset. Caesar, realizing the false step he had made, went straight home, bared his throat, and told his friends he was ready to offer it to anyone who wanted to strike. Later he made his illness the excuse for staying seated: those who suffer from it, he said, lose command of their senses if they talk long on their feet — they grow dizzy, fall into convulsions, and lose their reason entirely. But that was not the truth. He had been perfectly willing to rise for the senate, and was held back, the story goes, by Cornelius Balbus — a friend, or rather a flatterer — who said, "Will you not remember that you are Caesar, and claim the honor that is owed to your merit?"

Then came his affront to the tribunes, and fresh fuel for the resentment. The feast of the Lupercalia was being celebrated — a festival that at its founding belonged, some writers say, to the shepherds, with some connection to the Arcadian Lycaea. Many young noblemen and magistrates run through the city with their upper garments off, striking everyone they meet with strips of hide, all in sport; and many women, even of the highest rank, deliberately put themselves in the way and hold out their hands to the lash, the way schoolboys do to a master — believing that it grants an easy delivery to those who are pregnant, and children to those who are barren. Caesar, dressed in his triumphal robe, seated himself in a golden chair at the rostra to watch the ceremony. Antony, as consul, was one of the runners, and when he came into the forum the crowd parted for him. He went up to Caesar holding out a diadem — the band of kingship — wreathed with laurel. At this there was a shout, but a thin one, raised by the few men planted in the crowd for the purpose; and when Caesar pushed the diadem away, the whole forum applauded. Antony offered it a second time, and again only a few cheered; Caesar refused a second time, and again everyone applauded. The experiment had plainly failed. Caesar rose and ordered the crown carried up to the Capitol. But afterward his statues were found wearing royal diadems on their heads. Two tribunes of the people, Flavius and Marullus, went and tore the diadems off, then hunted down the men who had first hailed Caesar as king and marched them to prison. The people followed the two tribunes with cheers, calling them by the name of Brutus — because it was Brutus, long ago, who had ended the succession of kings and transferred the power once lodged in one man into the hands of the senate and the people. Caesar was stung. He stripped Marullus and Flavius of their office, and in pressing his charges against them he ridiculed the people into the bargain, calling the men over and over "Bruti" — brutes — and "Cumaeans," a byword for dullness.
