Antony

(Part 2 of 7)

So the plan was carried through, and Caesar fell in the senate-house. In the first moments Antony pulled on a servant's clothes and hid. But when he learned that the conspirators had gathered on the Capitol and had no designs on anyone else, he persuaded them to come down, sending his own son up as a hostage. That evening Cassius dined at Antony's house, and Brutus dined with Lepidus. Antony then called the senate together and spoke in favor of an amnesty — a formal act of forgetting — and of granting Brutus and Cassius provinces to govern. The senate passed both measures, and voted besides that everything Caesar had enacted should remain in force. Antony walked out of that session with the highest reputation of his life. He had, it seemed, headed off a civil war, and he had settled questions of enormous difficulty and danger in the wisest and most statesmanlike way imaginable.

But those sober counsels did not last. The tide of popular applause swept them away, and with them came a thought that would not leave him: if Brutus were overthrown, Antony would beyond any doubt be the first man in Rome. As Caesar's body was being carried out for burial, Antony delivered the funeral oration in the forum, as custom required. He could see that his praise was working powerfully on the crowd, and so he began to weave in notes of pity, and of horror at what had been done. As he came to the end of the speech he lifted up the dead man's garments, soaked in blood and stabbed through in every part, and held them out for all to see, calling the men who had done this villains and bloody murderers. The effect was volcanic. The people would not hear of delaying the funeral. They heaped up a pyre of benches and tables right there in the market-place and set the body burning — and then, each man snatching a flaming brand from the fire, they ran to attack the houses of the conspirators.

At that, Brutus and his whole party abandoned the city, and Caesar's friends closed ranks behind Antony. Calpurnia, Caesar's widow, entrusted him with the best part of the estate, worth some four thousand talents; and he also got his hands on Caesar's papers — journals of everything he had done, drafts of everything he intended to do. Antony made excellent use of these. On their authority he appointed whatever magistrates he pleased, brought whomever he liked into the senate, recalled men from exile, freed others from prison — all of it, supposedly, "as Caesar had ordered." The Romans mocked the beneficiaries with the nickname "Charonites," men of Charon's ferry, since any of them asked to prove his title would have to appeal to the papers of the dead. In short, Antony's rule in Rome was absolute. He himself was consul, and his two brothers held high office beside him: Gaius as praetor, Lucius as tribune of the people.

While things stood so in Rome, the young Octavian — Caesar's great-nephew, his niece's son, and by his will his adopted heir — arrived from Apollonia, where he had been staying when his uncle was killed. The first thing he did was call on Antony, as his father's friend. He raised the matter of the money in Antony's keeping, and reminded him that Caesar's will had left seventy-five drachmas to every single Roman citizen. At first Antony simply laughed at such talk from so young a man. He told him he must be out of his mind — that what he needed was good sense and good friends, because the burden of being Caesar's executor would crush shoulders as young as his. That answer settled nothing. Octavian went on demanding the property, and Antony went on treating him insultingly in word and deed. He blocked him when he stood for the tribune's office; and when Octavian took steps to dedicate his father's golden chair, as the senate had decreed, Antony threatened to have him thrown in prison if he did not stop courting the people. At that the young man turned to Cicero, and to everyone else who hated Antony. Through them he was recommended to the senate, while he himself wooed the common people and gathered in Caesar's old soldiers from their settlements — until Antony took alarm, met him on the Capitol, and, after some words, the two patched up an agreement.

That night Antony had an ugly dream: he fancied his right hand had been struck by lightning. And a few days later came a report that Octavian was plotting against his life. Octavian tried to explain and was not believed, and the breach was as wide as it had ever been. Each man now raced through Italy, bidding high to enlist the old soldiers scattered in their settlements, and hurrying to be first to secure the troops still under arms.

Cicero at this point was the most influential man in Rome. He used every art he possessed to inflame the public against Antony, and at last persuaded the senate to declare him a public enemy, to send Octavian the rods and axes and the other marks of honor usually given to praetors, and to order Hirtius and Pansa, the consuls of the year, to drive Antony out of Italy. The armies met near Mutina, and Octavian was present and fought in the battle. Antony was defeated — but both consuls were killed.

Antony's flight was misery of every kind, and the worst of it was famine. But it was his character, in calamity, to be better than at any other time. Antony in misfortune came closest to being a virtuous man. It is common enough for people who have fallen into great disasters to see what is right and what they ought to do; what is rare is the strength, in such extremity, to obey that judgment — to do what it approves and avoid what it condemns. Plenty of people are so weak that they collapse into their old habits all the more, and let their minds go entirely. Antony, on this occasion, was an astonishing example to his own soldiers. Fresh from all that luxury and splendid living, he made no difficulty now about drinking foul water and living on wild fruit and roots. As they crossed the Alps, the story goes, his men ate the very bark of trees, and fed on creatures that no one before had ever been willing to touch.

The plan was to join the army on the far side of the Alps commanded by Lepidus, whom Antony counted on as a friend — he had done Lepidus many good turns with Caesar. He came up and camped close by; and when no encouragement of any kind was offered him, he resolved to push his luck and risk everything. His hair was long and wild, his beard unshaved since the defeat; and in that state, with a dark cloak thrown around him, he walked up to the trenches of Lepidus's camp and began to address the army. Some of the men were moved by the sight of him, others by his words — enough of them that Lepidus, not liking where this was heading, ordered the trumpets sounded so that Antony could not be heard. That only deepened the soldiers' pity. They resolved to confer with him in secret, dressed Laelius and Clodius in women's clothes, and sent the pair to him. Attack Lepidus's trenches now, they urged; a strong party inside would welcome him, and would kill Lepidus too if he wished it. Antony wanted no such thing. Instead, the next morning, he marched his army straight into the river that divided the two camps. He was the first man into the water; and as he waded toward the far bank he could see Lepidus's soldiers in great numbers stretching out their hands to help him and tearing down their own defenses to make way for him. Once inside the camp he was its absolute master — and yet he treated Lepidus with perfect courtesy, addressed him as "Father" when he spoke to him, and, though everything was now his to command, left him the honor of being called the general. That handsome treatment brought over Munatius Plancus, who lay not far off with a considerable force. So Antony recrossed the Alps in great strength, leading seventeen legions and ten thousand horse into Italy, besides six legions left in garrison under Varius, one of his familiar friends and drinking companions, whom everyone called by his nickname: Cotylon — "the Jug."

Octavian, meanwhile, had perceived that Cicero's heart was set on liberty, and stopped paying him any further attention; he was already using his friends to work out an understanding with Antony. The three of them — Antony, Octavian, Lepidus — met on a small island, and the conference lasted three days. The empire itself was settled easily enough: they divided it among themselves like a piece of family property. What gave them real trouble was agreeing on who should die. Each of them wanted to destroy his enemies and save his friends; and in the end, hatred of enemies beat respect for kin and love of friends. Octavian yielded, and gave Cicero up to Antony; Antony gave up his own uncle, Lucius Caesar; and Lepidus was given leave to murder his brother Paulus — or, as others tell it, surrendered his brother to the other two. I do not believe anything more truly savage or barbarous was ever transacted. In this exchange of blood for blood they were equally guilty of the lives they surrendered and of the lives they took — more guilty, in fact, where their friends were concerned, since those they killed without even the excuse of hatred. To complete the reconciliation, the soldiers crowding around them demanded that the pact be sealed with a marriage: Octavian was to marry Clodia, the daughter of Antony's wife Fulvia. That too was agreed. And then three hundred men were put to death by proscription.

Antony's orders to the men sent to kill Cicero were to cut off his head, and the right hand that had written the Philippics against him. When they were brought to him he gazed at them with delight, bursting out more than once into laughter for sheer joy; and when he had looked his fill, he had them fastened up above the speakers' platform in the forum. He thought he was insulting the dead. In fact he was only putting his own wanton arrogance on display, and his unworthiness to hold the power that fortune had given him. His uncle Lucius Caesar, closely hunted, fled to his sister. When the murderers broke into her house and were pressing toward his room, she stood in the doorway, spread out her arms, and cried again and again, "You will not kill Lucius Caesar until you have first killed me — me, the mother of your general!" And by making that stand she got her brother away and saved his life.

The triumvirate was hateful to the Romans, and Antony bore most of the blame — he was older than Octavian, he carried more authority than Lepidus, and the moment his affairs were settled he rolled straight back into his old luxurious, dissolute way of living. To the general scandal of his behavior one detail added special damage: he lived in the house of Pompey the Great, a man who had been admired as much for his temperance and his sober, citizen-like habits as for his three triumphs. People could not watch without anger as the doors of that house were shut against magistrates, officers, and envoys — shamefully refused admittance — while inside it was packed with actors, jugglers, and drunken flatterers, on whom Antony poured out most of the wealth that violence and cruelty were raking in. For the triumvirs did not stop at confiscating the estates of the proscribed, cheating the widows and families as they went, nor were they satisfied with laying on every imaginable kind of tax and levy. Hearing that sums of money, deposited by foreigners and Roman citizens alike, lay in the keeping of the vestal virgins, they went and took the money by force. When it became plain that nothing would ever be enough for Antony, Octavian finally demanded a division of the property. They divided the army as well, and marched for Macedonia to make war on Brutus and Cassius, leaving Lepidus in command of the city.

Once they had crossed the sea, opened the campaign, and camped facing the enemy at Philippi — Antony opposite Cassius, Octavian opposite Brutus — Octavian did nothing worth recording; all the success and all the victory were Antony's. In the first battle Octavian was completely routed by Brutus, his camp was captured, and he himself very narrowly escaped — though by his own account in his Memoirs, he had withdrawn before the battle even began, because of a dream one of his friends had. Antony, for his part, defeated Cassius — though some have written that he was not actually present in the engagement and only joined afterward in the pursuit. Cassius, not knowing that Brutus had won, was killed at his own entreaty and order by Pindarus, one of his most trusted freedmen. A few days later they fought a second battle; Brutus lost it, and killed himself. Octavian being sick, nearly all the honor of the victory fell to Antony. Standing over Brutus's dead body, he allowed himself a few words of reproach for the death of his brother Gaius, whom Brutus had executed in Macedonia to avenge Cicero. But then, saying that Hortensius was more to blame for it than Brutus, he ordered Hortensius slain on his brother's tomb — and he threw his own scarlet cloak, a garment of great value, over Brutus's body, and charged one of his freedmen with seeing to the funeral. When Antony later learned that this man had not burned the cloak with the corpse, and had pocketed a good part of the funeral money besides, he had him put to death.

Octavian was carried back to Rome so ill that no one expected him to live long. Antony set out for the eastern provinces to lay them under tribute, and entered Greece with a large force. Every common soldier had been promised five thousand drachmas in pay, so the taxing and levying were bound to be severe. To the Greeks, however, he showed at first a fair amount of reason and moderation. He indulged his love of amusement in civilized ways — listening to the scholars dispute, watching the games, undergoing initiation into the mysteries. In matters of law he was even-handed, and he took pleasure in being called a lover of Greece — above all in being called a lover of Athens, a city to which he gave very considerable presents. The people of Megara, wishing him to know that they too had something worth seeing, invited him to come and view their senate-house. He went, looked it over, and when they asked how he liked it, told them: "Not very large — but extremely ruinous." At the same time he had a survey made of the temple of Pythian Apollo, as though he meant to restore it; indeed he had declared to the senate that he intended to.

Leaving Lucius Censorinus in Greece, Antony crossed into Asia, and there laid his hands on the stored-up wealth of the East, while kings waited at his door and queens competed with one another over who could send him the greatest presents or look most enchanting in his eyes. So while Octavian in Rome was wearing out his strength amid sedition and war, Antony, with nothing to do amid the enjoyments of peace, let his passions carry him easily back down the old familiar road. A troupe of harpers and pipers — Anaxenor and Xuthus — the dancer Metrodorus, and a whole Bacchic carnival of Asiatic performers, far outdoing in license and buffoonery even the pests that had followed him out of Italy, came flooding in and took possession of his court. It was past all patience: wealth of every kind was being squandered on people like these. The whole of Asia was like the city in Sophocles, loaded at one and the same time

  with incense in the air,
  with songs of joy — and cries of despair.

When he made his entry into Ephesus, women dressed as Bacchantes went to meet him, with men and boys costumed as satyrs and fauns; through the whole town nothing was to be seen but spears wreathed with ivy, harps, flutes, and pipes, while the songs hailed Antony as Bacchus — the Giver of Joy, the Gentle. And so he was, to some. To far more he was the Devourer and the Savage; for he stripped people of worth and standing of their fortunes to gratify scoundrels and flatterers — men who would sometimes beg the estate of someone still living, pretending he was dead, get the grant, and take possession. He gave his cook the house of a citizen of Magnesia as the reward for a single highly successful supper. And at last, when he was moving to lay a second year's tribute on Asia, Hybreas, speaking on behalf of the cities, found the courage to tell him — bluntly, but in a style suited to Antony's taste — "If you can take tribute twice a year, no doubt you can give us two summers and two harvests a year." Then he put the rest in the plainest, boldest terms: Asia had already raised two hundred thousand talents for Antony's service. "If this has not been paid to you, ask your collectors what became of it. If it has been paid, and it is all gone — then we are ruined men."

The words cut Antony to the quick, for he was simply ignorant of most of what was done in his name — not that he was lazy, but that he trusted, frankly and completely, everyone around him. There was a great deal of simplicity in his character. He was slow to see his faults; but when he did see them, his repentance was intense, and he was ready to ask pardon of the very people he had wronged. When he made amends he was lavish, and when he punished he was severe — though his generosity always ran further than his severity. His mockery was sharp and insulting, but it carried its own antidote: he was perfectly willing to take as good as he gave, and enjoyed being laughed at almost as much as he enjoyed laughing at others. And this freedom of speech was, in truth, the cause of many of his disasters. He could not imagine that men who joked with him so boldly would ever flatter or deceive him in serious business. He did not understand how deliberately a parasite mixes boldness into his flattery, the way a confectioner cuts sweetness with something sharp so that it never cloys. The impudence at the dinner table was engineered precisely so that the yes-saying in council would look like conviction instead of servility.

With a temper like his, the last and crowning ruin came in the form of the love of Cleopatra — a love that woke and whipped to fury passions that had lain quiet and dormant in his nature, and stifled, and finally corrupted, whatever goodness and sound judgment still held out in him. He fell into the snare in this way. As he was preparing for the Parthian war, he sent orders commanding her to appear before him in Cilicia, to answer a charge that she had given great assistance to Cassius in the late war. But Dellius, who carried the message, had no sooner seen her face and registered the adroitness and subtlety of her talk than he understood that Antony would never dream of harming such a woman — on the contrary, she would soon stand first in his favor. So he set about paying court to the Egyptian queen at once, and gave her his advice: to go to Cilicia "in her best attire," as Homer would put it, and to fear nothing from Antony, the gentlest and kindest of soldiers. She put some faith in Dellius's words, but far more in her own powers of attraction. They had already won her Julius Caesar, and Gnaeus, the great Pompey's son — and those conquests she had made as a girl who knew nothing yet of the world. She would meet Antony at the age when a woman's beauty is at its most splendid and her mind at its full maturity. So she gathered a great store of money, gifts, and ornaments of value — everything so wealthy a kingdom could supply — but her surest hopes she carried in herself: in her own magic, her own spells.

She received letter after letter of summons, from Antony and from his friends alike, and took no notice of any of them. At last, as if in mockery of the whole proceeding, she came sailing up the river Cydnus in a barge with a gilded stern and outspread sails of purple, while oars of silver beat time to the music of flutes and fifes and harps. She herself lay stretched at ease beneath a canopy of cloth of gold, dressed as Venus is dressed in a painting, while beautiful boys, got up like painted Cupids, stood on either side to fan her. Her maids, costumed as sea nymphs and graces, steered at the rudder and worked at the ropes. Perfume drifted from the vessel to the shore, which was covered with multitudes — part following the galley up the river along either bank, part streaming out of the city for the sight. The market-place emptied until Antony was left sitting on the tribunal alone, while the word ran through the whole crowd that Venus had come to feast with Bacchus, for the common good of Asia. On her arrival, Antony sent an invitation to supper. She thought it more fitting that he should come to her; and Antony, wanting to show his good humor and courtesy, complied, and went. He found preparations magnificent beyond expression, but nothing so admirable as the lights: all at once, so great a number of branching lights were let down, arranged with such ingenuity — some in squares, some in circles — that the whole scene was a spectacle few things have ever equaled for beauty.

The next day Antony invited her in turn, ambitious to outdo her in magnificence and in cleverness alike. He was beaten in both, and so thoroughly convinced of it that he was the first to jest at his own poverty of wit and his rough soldier's manners. She saw at once that his humor was broad and coarse — more of the barracks than the court — and she met him in exactly the same key, instantly, without the least reluctance or reserve. For her actual beauty, it is said, was not in itself so remarkable that no one could be compared with her, nor the kind that struck everyone who saw her at first sight. But the contact of her presence, if you lived with her, was irresistible. The attraction of her person, joined with the charm of her conversation and a character that colored everything she said and did, was something bewitching. There was pleasure in the mere sound of her voice; and her tongue was like an instrument of many strings, passing from one language to another as she pleased, so that there were few barbarian nations she needed an interpreter to answer. To most of them she spoke herself — Ethiopians, Troglodytes, Hebrews, Arabians, Syrians, Medes, Parthians, and many others whose languages she had learned — which was all the more astonishing because most of the kings before her had scarcely troubled to learn even the Egyptian tongue, and several of them had let their Macedonian Greek go as well.
