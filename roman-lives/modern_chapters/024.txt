Antony

(Part 4 of 7)

The next day they got the baggage in order and started back toward the camp before Phraata. Almost at once they ran into scattered enemy troops on the road. Then larger parties. Then, at last, the whole Parthian army, fresh and in good order, calling them out to battle and charging them from every side, and it took hard fighting just to get back inside the camp. There Antony discovered something worse waiting for him. During a sally by the Medes, his men had panicked and abandoned the defense of the siege mound. He resolved to punish them by decimation, as it is called: the soldiers are divided into groups of ten, and out of every ten, one man — chosen by lot — is put to death. The rest he ordered to have their grain rations issued in barley instead of wheat, the food of the disgraced.

By now the war had turned grim for both sides, and the future looked grimmer still — for Antony most of all, because famine was closing in on him. He could no longer send out foraging parties without paying for every sack of grain in wounds and dead men. But Phraates had his own fears. The autumn equinox was past, the air was already sharpening toward winter, and if the Romans dug in and kept up the siege, he was afraid his own soldiers would desert him; Parthians will endure almost anything rather than winter in an open field. So he turned to a trick. He passed the word to those of his men who had struck up the most acquaintance with the Roman soldiers: when you meet them foraging, don't press them too hard — let them carry off some provisions. More than that: praise their courage. Tell them the king has good reason to call the Romans the bravest men in the world. Once this had done its work, the riders came in closer, walking their horses alongside the Romans, and began to complain about Antony's stubbornness. Phraates, they said, wanted nothing more than peace, and a chance to show how gladly he would spare the lives of so many brave soldiers — and Antony would not give him the smallest opening. Instead he sat there waiting for the two fiercest and cruelest enemies of all, winter and famine, and even with all the goodwill of the Parthians to help them, those two would be hard to escape. These reports reached Antony from many mouths, and he began to let himself hope. Still, he would not send a word to the Parthian king until he had put the question to these friendly talkers directly: were they speaking on their king's orders? They answered that they were, and urged him again to believe them. So he sent some of his friends to demand, one more time, the return of the standards and prisoners lost with Crassus — reasoning that if he asked for nothing at all, he would look pathetically grateful just to be allowed to slink away. The Parthian king's answer came back: never mind the standards and prisoners; but if Antony wished to retreat, he could do so whenever he pleased, in peace and safety. So after a few days spent collecting the baggage, he broke camp and marched. And here something telling happened. There was no man alive who could address a crowd of soldiers like Antony, no one better at carrying an army with him by sheer force of words — and out of shame and sadness he could not bring himself to speak. He handed the task to Domitius Ahenobarbus. Some of the soldiers resented it, as if he thought them not worth addressing; but most of them understood the real reason, and pitied him for it, and decided it was one more reason to give their general more respect and obedience than ever.

Antony had planned to go back the way he had come, through open, level country bare of trees. But a certain Mardian came to him — a man who knew Parthian ways inside and out, and whose loyalty to Rome had been proved at the battle where the siege engines were lost — and gave him different advice. Keep the mountains close on your right hand, he said. Do not march heavy-armed men across broad, open riding country against a huge army of mounted archers. That was exactly why Phraates had coaxed him away from the siege with soft promises: to cut him to pieces at leisure on the retreat. If Antony was willing, the Mardian would guide him himself, by a shorter route, with better supplies for the army along the way. Antony thought it over. He did not want to seem to distrust the Parthians right after making terms with them; but the shorter road through inhabited country was plainly the better one. So he asked the Mardian for some guarantee of good faith, and the man offered to be kept in chains until the army was safe in Armenia. For two days he guided the army in chains. On the third, when Antony had stopped thinking about the enemy altogether and was marching at his ease, in no particular order, the Mardian noticed that the bank of a river had been broken open and the water let out to flood the road ahead of them. He saw instantly whose handiwork this was: the Parthians had done it to slow their march. Be on your guard, he told Antony — the enemy is close. Antony had barely begun to form his ranks, posting the slingers and javelin men at intervals where they could dash out, when the Parthians came pouring in from all sides, fully expecting to surround the army and break it apart in confusion. The Roman light troops met them and were badly stung by the arrows; but the Parthians were entertained just as warmly with sling stones and javelins, took many wounds of their own, and pulled back. They rallied and came on again, until a battalion of Gallic cavalry charged and broke them, and they were seen no more that day.

The attack taught Antony what to do. He posted slingers and javelin men not only as a rear guard but along both flanks as well, and marched the army as a hollow square, with orders to the cavalry to charge and drive the enemy off — but never to chase them far when they fled. For the next four days the Parthians took at least as much damage as they gave, and their enthusiasm began to cool. Winter was getting on, they grumbled, and they pressed to go home.

But on the fifth day, Flavius Gallus, a brave and energetic officer with a substantial command, came to Antony asking for some light infantry from the rear and some cavalry from the van. With these, he said, he could strike a real blow. Antony gave them to him, and when the enemy attacked, Gallus beat them back — but instead of withdrawing as usual and retiring on the mass of the heavy infantry, he stood his ground where he was and fought on, dangerously exposed. The officers commanding the rear, seeing how far he was drifting from the army, sent messages calling him back. He ignored them. It is said that Titius the quaestor actually grabbed the standards and turned them around, shouting at Gallus that he was leading so many brave men to their destruction. Gallus shouted back, and ordered the men around him to stand firm; so Titius withdrew. Then, while Gallus pressed the enemy in front of him, a Parthian force swung in behind and closed the circle. Surrounded at last, he sent a messenger asking for help — and here the commanders of the heavy infantry, including Canidius, a particular favorite of Antony's, made what looks like a serious blunder. Instead of wheeling the whole army around, they sent small detachments; and when those were cut up, they sent more small detachments, and by this piecemeal mismanagement the rout would have spread through the entire army if Antony himself had not come up from the van at the head of the third legion, driven it straight through the mass of fleeing men, and faced down the enemy, stopping the pursuit cold.

Three thousand men died in that engagement. Five thousand more were carried back to camp wounded — among them Gallus, shot through the body with four arrows. He did not recover. Antony went from tent to tent to visit and comfort the wounded, and he could not look at his men without tears, without open grief. But they took his hand with shining faces and told him to go and look after himself and not to worry about them, calling him their imperator, their general, saying that as long as he was well, they were safe. And in truth, history cannot show a finer army behind any general of that age. In strength and youth, in patience under labor and hardship, it had no superior; and as for the obedience and affection the soldiers bore their general — the way every man of them, officer and private, great and small, valued Antony's good opinion of him above his own life — in that particular excellence, not even the Romans of old could have surpassed them. There were many reasons for this devotion, as I have said before: his noble family, his eloquence, his frank and open manner, his lavish and princely generosity, his easy way of joking with everyone. And now, above all, there was his kindness to the sick and wounded — visiting them, grieving with them, supplying whatever they needed — so that the sick and wounded were even more eager to serve him than the healthy and strong.

Yet that one victory had put such heart into the enemy that their weariness turned to contempt. They camped through the night close to the Roman lines, expecting to plunder the tents and baggage of an army about to disintegrate, and in the morning fresh forces streamed in, until their number is said to have grown to no fewer than forty thousand horsemen. The king had even sent the guards who attended his own person — a sign that he considered the victory certain. The king himself was never present at any battle. Antony prepared to address his soldiers, and called for a mourning robe, thinking to move them more deeply; his friends talked him out of it, and he came forward instead in the scarlet cloak of a general. He praised the men who had won the recent fighting and shamed the ones who had run. The victors shouted back promises of success; the others begged his forgiveness, telling him they were ready to undergo decimation or any other punishment he chose, asking only that he stop grieving over their failure. At that, Antony lifted his hands to heaven and prayed: if any judgment from the gods was coming to balance the great favors of his life, let it fall on his head alone — and let his soldiers have victory.

The next day they marched in better order, and the Parthians, riding up expecting a plundering party rather than a battle, met a storm of missiles from men who were anything but broken — fresh, resolute, and ready. It was the Parthians who began to lose their nerve. But at a place where the Romans had to descend a steep hill, they gathered and poured arrows into the column as it moved slowly down the slope. Then the shield-bearing infantry wheeled about, took the light troops inside their ranks, and the front rank knelt on one knee with shields planted before them; the rank behind held their shields over the heads of the first, and the next rank over those, layer upon layer, like the tiles of a roof or the banked seats of a theater — a sloping shell that no arrow could do more than glance off. The Parthians, watching the Romans sink to their knees, could only imagine the men were collapsing from exhaustion. So they put down their bows, took up their spears, and charged in close — at which the Romans leapt to their feet with a roar, met them hand to hand with their javelins, killed the foremost, and sent the rest flying. It went this way day after day, and the constant fighting shrank the marches to almost nothing. Then hunger set in. They could get only a little grain, and had to fight for what they got; and they lacked even the tools to grind it and bake bread, for nearly all the mills had been left behind — the pack animals were dead, or carrying the sick and wounded. In the camp, a quart of wheat sold for fifty drachmas, and barley loaves went for their weight in silver. They turned to vegetables and roots, and finding few of the familiar kinds, they were driven to try whatever they could pull from the ground — and so they came upon an herb that was death to eat. It first destroyed the memory and the mind. A man who ate it forgot everything in the world and gave himself over to a single task: moving large stones from one place to another, which he did with tremendous earnestness and industry, as if it were the most important business of his life. All through the camp you could see men stooped over the ground, digging up stones and carrying them from place to place. And then, at the end, they vomited bile and died — for wine, the one antidote, had run out. Watching them die by the score, with the Parthians still hanging on his heels, Antony was heard to cry out, again and again, "O, the Ten Thousand!" — in admiration of Xenophon's Greeks, who had an even longer road home from Babylonia, and a more powerful enemy, and made it back safe all the same.

The Parthians, unable to break the Roman army apart or crack its formations, and beaten as often as not, once more turned friendly with the foragers. They rode up with their bows unstrung and said they were going home; this was the end of their reprisals; only a few Median troops would follow along for two or three more days — not to trouble the Romans, but to protect some outlying villages. And they took their leave with salutes and embraces and a great show of friendship. The Romans filled up with confidence again, and Antony, hearing of it, leaned toward taking the road through the plains, since the mountain route was said to be waterless. But while he was preparing to do it, a man named Mithridates rode into the camp — a cousin of that Monaeses who had taken refuge with the Romans and received the gift of the three cities from Antony. He asked for someone who could speak Syriac or Parthian. Alexander of Antioch, a friend of Antony's, was brought to him. The stranger gave his name, explained that Monaeses was the man who wished to repay Antony's kindness, and then asked Alexander a question: did he see that high range of hills in the distance? He said he did. "Under those hills," said Mithridates, "the whole Parthian army is waiting for you. The great plains run right up to their feet, and they are counting on you to trust their promises, leave the mountain road, and take the level one. It is true that on the mountain route you will face thirst and the hardships you have grown used to. But if Antony goes through the plains, he should expect the fate of Crassus."

With that, he left. Antony, alarmed, called his friends to council, and sent for the Mardian guide, who said exactly the same thing. Enemy or no enemy, he told them, the plain had no reliable track, and losing the way in it was likely enough to rule it out by itself; the other route was rough and dry, but the dry stretch lasted only a day. So Antony changed his mind and marched out that night by the mountain road, ordering every man to carry his own water. Most of them had no vessels, so they made do, filling their helmets, or carrying it in skins. They were barely underway when word of the march reached the Parthians, who — against all their custom — pursued through the night, and at sunrise struck the rear guard, which was worn out with marching and lack of sleep and in no condition to put up a real fight. They had covered thirty miles in the dark, and to find the enemy on their heels at the end of such a march broke their spirit. Worse, fighting for every step of the road sharpened their thirst. The men at the front reached a river whose water ran cool and clear — and brackish, laced with minerals, so that whoever drank it was seized at once with cramps in the bowels and a thirst fiercer than before. The Mardian had warned them of this too, but they could not hold back; they shoved aside the men who tried to stop them and drank. Antony ran up and down the column, begging them for a little patience — there was a river of good water not far ahead, he said, and beyond it the country was too rough for cavalry, so the enemy could follow no further. Then he sounded the recall for the men still fighting, and ordered the tents pitched, so the soldiers could at least rest in the shade.

The tents were scarcely up, and the Parthians were withdrawing as usual for the night, when Mithridates appeared again and told Alexander to give Antony this advice: rest the army, but not a moment longer than necessary — then push hard for the next river. The Parthians would not cross it, but that far they were determined to follow. Alexander carried the message to Antony, who sent back a load of gold plate; Mithridates took as much of it as he could hide under his clothes and rode off. Acting on his warning, Antony broke camp while it was still daylight, and the whole army marched on with no interference from the Parthians at all — and yet, by their own doing, that night was the most wretched and terrifying of the entire retreat. Men began to murder and rob anyone suspected of carrying money. They ransacked the baggage train and stole whatever they found. Finally they laid hands on Antony's own equipment, smashing his fine tables and drinking cups and dividing the pieces among themselves. With such an uproar rolling through the whole army — men surging back and forth in the dark — the belief spread that the enemy had fallen on the column and cut off part of it, and Antony believed it too. He called for one of his freedmen among his bodyguard, a man named Rhamnus, and made him swear an oath: whenever Antony gave the order, Rhamnus would run his sword through him and cut off his head, so that he would neither fall into Parthian hands alive nor be recognized when dead. His friends stood around him in tears. Then the Mardian came up and gave them all their lives back. The air, he pointed out, had turned cool and moist — they could feel it in every breath — which meant the river he had promised was near; and the time it should have taken to reach it, reckoned against the hours they had marched, came out the same, for the night was nearly spent. At the same moment, others arrived to report that the whole uproar had come from nothing but the army's own violence and thieving. To quiet the tumult and pull the men out of their frenzy back into some kind of order, Antony gave the signal to halt.

Day was breaking, and calm and discipline were just returning to the column, when Parthian arrows began to drop on the rear guard. The light troops were ordered out to fight, and the infantry covered one another with their shields as before, roof-tile fashion, and held the enemy — who chose not to press any closer — while the head of the army, marching on at an easy pace, came in sight of the river. Antony drew up his cavalry along the bank, facing the enemy, and sent the sick and wounded across first. And by now even the men still skirmishing had time to drink in peace, for as soon as the Parthians saw the river, they unstrung their bows, told the Romans to cross freely, and paid them handsome compliments on their courage. They crossed unmolested, rested a while, and then pushed on — not quite trusting fair words from that quarter. Six days after that last battle they reached the river Araxes, which divides Media from Armenia — deep, violent in its current, and dangerous-looking to cross; and a rumor ran through the ranks that the enemy lay in ambush, waiting to strike the moment they were tangled up in the crossing. But when they had gotten safely to the far bank and stood in Armenia at last, they behaved like sailors sighting land after a storm at sea: they kissed the ground, they wept, they fell into one another's arms for joy. But then, marching through a country that overflowed with everything, they ate — after their long starvation — with such abandon that they came down with dropsy and dysentery.

Here Antony held a review of his army and found he had lost twenty thousand infantry and four thousand cavalry — and more than half of them had been killed not by the enemy but by disease. The march from Phraata had taken twenty-seven days, and in that time they had beaten the Parthians in eighteen battles — victories that settled nothing and won nothing lasting, because they could never pursue. And that fact points the finger plainly: it was Artavasdes who cost Antony the fruits of the whole expedition. If the sixteen thousand horsemen the Armenian had led away out of Media — armed in the Parthian style and trained to the Parthian way of fighting — had been there to run down the enemy whenever the Romans broke them, the Parthians could never have rallied so many times after so many defeats and come back fresh to attack again. The whole army, understandably, was hot to march into Armenia and take revenge. But Antony, thinking further ahead, made no mention of the desertion and kept up every courtesy as before; his army was worn out and short of everything, and this was no time for a new war. Later, however, when he entered Armenia again, he coaxed Artavasdes into a meeting with invitations and fair promises — then seized him, put him in chains, carried him down to Alexandria, and led him there in a triumph. This was one of the things that most offended the Romans back home: the proudest and most sacred ceremony of their country, handed over to the Egyptians for Cleopatra's sake.
