Antony

(Part 1 of 7)

Antony's grandfather was the famous orator Antonius, whom Marius put to death for siding with Sulla. His father was another Antony, surnamed the Cretan — never famous or distinguished in public life, but a decent, kind man, and remarkable above all for his generosity. One story shows the whole character. He was not rich, and for exactly that reason his wife kept a tight rein on his good nature. One day a friend in need came to borrow money. Money he had none. So he told a servant to bring him water in a silver basin, and when it came he wetted his face as if he meant to shave, sent the servant off on some other errand — and handed his friend the basin, telling him to turn it into whatever he needed. Later, when a great hunt for the missing basin went up through the house, and his wife, in a very bad temper, was about to put the servants to the question one by one, he confessed what he had done and begged her pardon.

His wife was Julia, of the family of the Caesars, and in good sense and honorable conduct she was the equal of any woman of her time. It was under her that Antony was raised; after his father's death she remarried, to Cornelius Lentulus — the man Cicero put to death for his part in Catiline's conspiracy. Here, probably, is the first seed of the deadly grudge Antony bore Cicero. Antony even claimed that Lentulus' body was refused burial until Julia went and begged it from Cicero's wife. But that is plainly a mistake, for none of the men executed in Cicero's consulship was denied burial. Antony grew up a strikingly handsome young man, and then — the worst luck of his life — he fell in with Curio, a man wholly given over to his pleasures. To make Antony depend on him all the more, Curio plunged him into drinking and dissipation, and led him through spending so extravagant that at that early age he was already two hundred and fifty talents in debt. Curio stood surety for the whole sum; and when his father, the elder Curio, heard of it, he threw Antony out of his house. For a short while after that, Antony attached himself to Clodius, the most insolent and violent demagogue of the day, and ran with him through his career of riot and disorder. But he soon wearied of Clodius' madness, and grew nervous of the powerful enemies gathering against him, so he left Italy for Greece, where he spent his time in military training and the study of public speaking. He took most naturally to the so-called Asiatic style of oratory, then at the height of its fashion — a style that suited his boastful, swaggering temper very well, all empty flourishes and restless grabbing after glory.

After some time in Greece, Gabinius, a former consul, invited him to join a campaign in Syria. At first Antony refused — he had no wish to serve as a man in the ranks — but when he was offered a commission as commander of cavalry, he went along. His first action was against Aristobulus, who had persuaded the Jews to rebel. There Antony was himself the first man up the wall of the largest fortress, and drove Aristobulus out of every stronghold he held; then he beat him in a pitched battle against an army many times the size of his own, killed nearly all of them, and took Aristobulus and his son prisoner. When that war was over, Ptolemy came asking Gabinius to restore him to his kingdom of Egypt, with a promise of ten thousand talents as the reward. Most of the officers were against the venture, and Gabinius himself did not much like it, though the ten thousand talents tempted him sorely. But Antony, hungry for bold action and eager to oblige Ptolemy, helped talk Gabinius into going. Everyone agreed that the most dangerous stretch was the march to Pelusium — deep sand, no hope of fresh water, the road running past the Ecregma and the Serbonian marsh, which the Egyptians call the breathing-hole of Typhon, and which is probably water left behind by the Red Sea, or seeping through from it, since only a narrow neck of land divides the Red Sea from the Mediterranean there. Antony was sent ahead with the cavalry, and he did more than seize the passes: he captured Pelusium itself, a great city, took its garrison prisoner, and so made the march safe for the army and the road to victory easy for its general. Even the enemy got some benefit from his hunger for honor. When Ptolemy entered Pelusium, boiling with rage and spite against the Egyptians, he meant to put them all to the sword — and Antony stood in his way and stopped the slaughter. In all the many skirmishes and battles that followed, he gave proof after proof of his personal courage and his skill in command; once in particular, by swinging around and striking the enemy's rear, he handed the victory to the attackers in front, and won signal honors for it. And his decency toward the dead Archelaus was noticed just as widely. The man had once been his host and his friend; now, of necessity, Antony fought him bravely while he lived — but when he fell, Antony sought out his body and buried it with royal honors. The result was that he left a great name behind him among the Alexandrians, and every man serving in the Roman army counted him a most gallant soldier.

He had, besides, a noble and striking presence. A full, well-grown beard, a broad forehead, an aquiline nose — altogether a bold, masculine look that reminded people of Hercules as he appears in paintings and statues. Indeed there was an old tradition that the Antonys descended from Hercules through a son of his named Anton, and Antony liked to lend the story credit — by that resemblance, and by the way he dressed. Whenever he was to appear before a crowd, he wore his tunic belted low on the hips, a broadsword at his side, and a heavy, coarse cloak over everything. Even the things that others found insufferable in him — the bragging, the constant joking, the drinking in public, the way he would sit down beside the men at their meals or stand eating off the common soldiers' tables — made him the darling of the army. In matters of love he had the same easy charm: he won plenty of friends by helping them along in their affairs, and took their jokes about his own in perfect good humor. And his generous ways — the open, lavish hand with gifts and favors to friends and fellow soldiers — did a great deal to launch his rise to power, and once he was great, kept his fortunes standing long after a thousand follies had begun pulling them down. One example of that open hand is worth telling. He had ordered a payment of two hundred and fifty thousand to one of his friends — a decies, as the Romans call the sum. His steward, staggered by the extravagance, piled all the silver up in a heap where Antony would pass. Antony saw the heap and asked what it meant. "The money you ordered paid to your friend," said the steward. Antony saw the man's spite at once. "I thought a decies came to more than that," he said. "This is too little. Double it." But that belongs to a later time.

When the Roman state finally split into two hostile camps — the aristocratic party lining up behind Pompey, who was in the city, and the people's party looking to Caesar, who was off at the head of an army in Gaul — Curio, Antony's old friend, changed sides, devoted himself to Caesar, and brought Antony over with him. With the influence his eloquence won him among the people, and with a free flow of Caesar's money, Curio got Antony elected first tribune of the people, then augur. And the moment Antony took office he was worth a great deal to Caesar. First he blocked the consul Marcellus, who wanted to put the troops already assembled under Pompey's command and give him power to raise fresh levies; Antony countered with an order that the assembled troops be sent to Syria to reinforce Bibulus in his war with the Parthians, and that no one should enlist under Pompey. Next, when the senators refused to allow Caesar's letters to be received or read in the senate, Antony, by the authority of his office, read them out publicly — and to such effect that many changed their minds, since Caesar's demands, as he set them down in writing, looked nothing but fair and reasonable. At last two questions were put to the senate: should Pompey dismiss his army, and should Caesar dismiss his. A few voted for the first; for the second, all but a handful. Then Antony rose and put a question of his own: would it please them that both men give up their commands together? This proposal they seized on with real enthusiasm — they cheered him loudly and demanded a vote. But the consuls would not allow it. Caesar's friends then made some fresh offers, fair and equitable ones, but Cato opposed them fiercely, and the consul Lentulus ordered Antony out of the senate. So Antony was driven out with insults. He left cursing them, and, with Quintus Cassius, dressed as slaves, they fled in a hired carriage straight to Caesar. The moment they reached the camp they declared openly that there was no longer any order or justice in Rome — that tribunes were denied the right to speak, and that any man who stood up for common fairness was thrown out and lucky to escape with his life.

At that, Caesar put his army in motion and marched into Italy. That is why Cicero writes in his Philippics that Antony caused the civil war as surely as Helen caused the Trojan one. But this is slander. Caesar was not so soft or so easily swept away that the sight of Antony and Cassius, shabbily dressed and fleeing in a hired carriage, could carry him off into civil war against his country in a fit of momentary indignation — as if he had never thought of it or resolved on it long before. For a man who wanted a pretext for war, this made a fair and plausible occasion; but the true motive that drove him was the same that had once driven Alexander, and Cyrus before him, against all mankind: the unquenchable thirst for empire, the consuming ambition to be the greatest man in the world — which was impossible until Pompey had been pulled down. So as soon as he had advanced, occupied Rome, and driven Pompey out of Italy, Caesar decided to deal first with the legions Pompey had in Spain, then cross the sea and hunt Pompey himself with the fleet being built in the meantime. He left the government of Rome to Lepidus, as praetor, and the command of the troops and of Italy to Antony, as tribune of the people. Antony had the soldiers' hearts almost at once — he drilled with them, lived among them for the most part, and gave them everything his means allowed. But with nearly everyone else he was unpopular. He was too lazy to hear out the complaints of people who had been wronged; he listened impatiently to petitioners; and he had a bad name for meddling with other men's wives. In short, Caesar's government — which, as far as Caesar himself was concerned, looked like anything but a tyranny — got its bad reputation through Caesar's friends. And of those friends, Antony, who held the largest trust and committed the largest errors, was thought the most deeply at fault.

For all that, when Caesar came back from Spain he overlooked the charges against him — and in the war that followed, in every task he gave Antony, he never once had cause to complain of any lack of courage, energy, or soldierly skill. Caesar himself embarked at Brundisium, crossed the Ionian Sea with a small force, and sent the ships back with orders for Antony and Gabinius to embark the army and come over to Macedonia with all speed. Gabinius had no stomach for the sea in the rough, dangerous weather of midwinter, and wanted to march the army around by the long land route. But Antony was more afraid of what might happen to Caesar, hard pressed as he was by the numbers against him. He beat back Libo, who was blockading the mouth of the harbor of Brundisium, by swarming his galleys with a fleet of small boats, and with that opening he loaded twenty thousand infantry and eight hundred horsemen aboard and put to sea. The enemy spotted him and gave chase — and from that danger a strong south wind saved him, springing up and raising a sea so heavy that the enemy galleys could barely make way. But his own ships were being driven straight onto a lee shore of cliffs and rock walls dropping sheer to the water, with no hope of escape — when all at once the wind swung round to the southwest and blew from the land out to open sea. Antony, sailing now in safety, saw the whole coast covered with the wreckage of the enemy's fleet: the pursuing galleys had been driven onto it by the gale, and not a few had been smashed to pieces. Many men and much property fell into Antony's hands; he took the town of Lissus as well; and by arriving when he did, with a force that size, he gave Caesar great encouragement.

In the many engagements that now came one after another, there was not one in which Antony did not distinguish himself. Twice, when the army was in full flight, he stopped it, turned the men around, led them back into the charge, and won. Not without reason, then, his reputation in the camp stood next to Caesar's. And what Caesar himself thought of him showed plainly when the final battle came at Pharsalia — the battle that would decide everything. Caesar chose to lead the right wing himself, and gave the left to Antony, as the best officer he had. After the victory, Caesar, now named dictator, went off in pursuit of Pompey, and sent Antony to Rome as Master of the Horse — an office second in power to the dictator when the dictator is present, and, in his absence, first and very nearly alone. For once a dictator is appointed, every other magistrate except the tribunes ceases to hold any authority in Rome.

Dolabella, however, who was tribune then — a young man and hungry for upheaval — was pushing a sweeping measure to cancel all debts, and he wanted Antony, his friend and always ready to champion a crowd-pleasing project, to join him in it. Asinius and Trebellius argued the other side; and just at that moment Antony was seized by a terrible suspicion that Dolabella was carrying on with his wife. In his distress he put her away — she was his cousin, a daughter of Gaius Antonius, Cicero's colleague in the consulship — sided with Asinius, and went to open war with Dolabella, who had seized the forum, intending to force his law through. The senate voted that Dolabella should be put down by armed force, and Antony went down and attacked him, killing some of Dolabella's men and losing some of his own. The action cost him the affection of the common people; and with the better sort, and with all decent citizens, his whole way of living made him — as Cicero says — absolutely odious. They were disgusted by his drinking bouts at all hours, his wild spending, his gross affairs with women, his days spent asleep or walking off his debauches, his nights at banquets and theaters and at the weddings of comedians and clowns. The story goes that after drinking all night at the wedding of Hippias the comedian, he had to address the people in the morning; he came forward, still loaded from the night before, and vomited in front of them all, while one of his friends held out his toga for him. Sergius the actor was one of the friends with the most influence over him; so was Cytheris, an actress from the same stage, whom he doted on — when he made his official tours she rode with him in a litter, attended by an entourage in nothing inferior to his own mother's. And people were scandalized on every side: at the golden cups he carried with him on the road, fitter for a religious procession than a journey; at the pavilions pitched, and the lavish outdoor breakfasts spread by riversides and in groves; at chariots drawn by lions; at prostitutes and singing-girls quartered in the houses of sober fathers and mothers of families. And it seemed outrageous that while Caesar, out of Italy, slept in the open field and was toiling through the dangerous remnant of a hazardous war, others should use his authority to insult the citizens with their shameless luxury.

All this seems to have inflamed the party quarrels in Rome and encouraged the soldiers in outrages and plunder. Accordingly, when Caesar came home, he acquitted Dolabella; and being elected consul for the third time, he took as his colleague not Antony but Lepidus. When Pompey's house was put up for sale, Antony bought it — and then complained loudly when the price was demanded of him. He tells us himself that this, and his feeling that his services had never been properly rewarded, was why he did not follow Caesar and the army to Libya. And yet Caesar, by dealing gently with his failings, seems to have cured him of a good deal of his folly and extravagance. Antony gave up his old habits and took a wife: Fulvia, the widow of Clodius the demagogue — a woman not born for spinning or housekeeping, and not one to be content ruling a private husband. Her ambition was to govern a governor and command a commander-in-chief. Cleopatra, in fact, owed Fulvia a real debt for training Antony to obey a woman — he came to her hands tame, broken in already to taking orders from a mistress. He used all sorts of playful, boyish tricks to keep Fulvia amused. For example: when Caesar was on his way back after his victory in Spain, Antony went out with the rest to meet him. Then a rumor swept Italy that Caesar was dead and the enemy marching on Rome, and Antony turned back to the city. Disguising himself, he came to Fulvia by night, muffled up as a servant carrying letters from Antony. She, frantic, asked before she would even take the letter, "Is Antony alive?" He handed it to her without a word — and as she was opening it, caught her around the neck and kissed her. This one little story, out of many like it, I give as a sample.

When Caesar returned from Spain, everybody of any rank in Rome traveled days out to meet him, but no one was received like Antony: Caesar took him into his own carriage and rode with him the whole way, while behind them came Brutus Albinus, and Octavian, Caesar's niece's son — the boy who would afterwards take his name and rule the Romans so long. When Caesar was elected consul for the fifth time, he chose Antony as his colleague at once; but he intended to resign his own consulship in favor of Dolabella, and told the senate so. Antony fought it with everything he had, heaping abuse on Dolabella and getting as good back, until Caesar, unable to stomach the indecency of it, put the matter off. When Caesar later came before the people to proclaim Dolabella consul, Antony shouted out that the auspices were against it — and at last Caesar gave in and dropped the plan, much to Dolabella's fury. The truth is, Caesar seems to have been about equally disgusted with the pair of them. When someone was accusing them both to him, he said, "It's not these fat, long-haired men I fear, but the pale, lean ones" — meaning Brutus and Cassius, whose conspiracy would soon bring him down.

And it was Antony himself who, without meaning to, handed that conspiracy its fairest pretext. The Romans were celebrating the festival called the Lupercalia, and Caesar, in his triumphal robes, sat above the Rostra in the marketplace watching the sport. The custom is that many young men of the nobility and the magistracy, oiled for the running, race through the city with leather straps in their hands, striking in fun at everyone they meet. Antony was one of the runners. But he set the old ceremony aside: he had wreathed a diadem with laurel, and he ran up to the Rostra, was hoisted up by his companions, and moved to set it on Caesar's head — as though by that ceremony Caesar were declared king. Caesar made a show of refusing and leaned away from it, and the people applauded with a great shout. Antony pressed it on him again; again Caesar declined it. So the contest between them went on for some time — Antony's offers cheered by a handful of his friends, Caesar's refusals by the whole people at once. Which is a curious thing: men who submitted patiently to the fact of a king's power could not bear the name of it, as though the name alone were the death of their liberty. Caesar, thoroughly rattled by the whole scene, rose from his seat, pulled his robe back from his neck, and cried out that anyone who wanted could strike the blow there and then. The diadem ended up placed on one of his statues — and the tribunes Flavius and Marullus tore it down, and were escorted home by the people with shouts of applause. But Caesar resented it, and stripped them of their office.

All this put fresh heart into Brutus and Cassius. When they were choosing trustworthy friends for the enterprise, they debated whether to bring in Antony. The rest were for it, except Trebonius, who told them that on the journey out to meet Caesar he and Antony had shared lodgings and traveled together, and that he had dropped several careful hints, feeling him out. Antony had understood him perfectly, he said, and had not encouraged it — but neither had he said a word of it to Caesar. He had kept the secret faithfully. The conspirators then proposed that Antony should die along with Caesar. Brutus would not allow it: an act undertaken in defense of right and the laws, he insisted, must be kept unstained, pure of any injustice. But Antony's physical strength and his high office made him dangerous — so it was settled that when Caesar entered the senate house, and the deed was to be done, some of the party would keep Antony occupied outside with a conversation about some invented piece of business.
