Cato the Younger

(Part 3 of 6)

When the day came for the people to vote on the decree, Metellus got to the forum first and packed it with armed men — foreigners, gladiators, slaves. A good part of the crowd was for Pompey too, hoping a change of regime would pay off for them, and there was powerful help besides from Caesar, who was praetor that year. The best and most distinguished men of the city were every bit as disgusted by all this as Cato was, but they looked more likely to suffer alongside him than to be of any use to him. Meanwhile Cato's whole household was in an agony of fear for him. Some of his friends never ate or slept that night; they passed the hours arguing in circles and getting nowhere. His wife and sisters wept and pleaded. He himself, without a trace of fear, was the one comforting everyone else, steadying them with his talk. After supper he went to bed at his usual hour, and the next morning had to be shaken out of a deep sleep by Minucius Thermus, one of his fellow tribunes. The two of them walked down to the forum together, accompanied by only a handful of people but met by a great many more, who kept urging them to be careful. When Cato saw the temple of Castor and Pollux ringed with armed men, the steps held by gladiators, and Metellus and Caesar seated together at the top, he turned to his friends. "Look at this bold coward," he said, "who has recruited a regiment of soldiers against one unarmed man." And he walked on with Thermus. The men holding the approaches let those two through and no one else — though Cato caught Munatius by the hand and with a good deal of trouble hauled him along too. Then he went straight up to Metellus and Caesar and sat down between them, cutting off their conversation, which left them both staring in confusion. The honest citizens, watching Cato's face and marveling at his spirit and audacity, pressed closer and shouted to him to have courage, and called out to one another to stand together and not betray their liberty, or the man defending it.

Then the clerk produced the bill, and Cato forbade him to read it. Metellus took it to read it out himself; Cato snatched the document out of his hands. Metellus knew the decree by heart and started reciting it from memory — and Thermus clapped a hand over his mouth. Seeing the two of them determined to block him by any means, and the people cowed but leaning toward the better side, Metellus sent to his house for armed men. They came rushing in with a roar, and in the terror everyone scattered — everyone but Cato, who stood alone while the other party pelted him with sticks and stones from above, until Murena, the very man he had once prosecuted, came up to protect him, holding his own gown in front of him as a shield and shouting at them to stop throwing. In the end, half persuading and half dragging, Murena got him into the temple of Castor and Pollux. Metellus, seeing the ground clear and the whole opposition fled from the forum, thought the thing was as good as done. He ordered his soldiers to withdraw, and resuming in proper form, began to put the decree to the vote. But the other side had recovered themselves, and they came back with a bold, deafening shout — at which Metellus's followers panicked, took the noise for the arrival of armed reinforcements, and fled the place to the last man. With them scattered, Cato came forward again, praising the people's courage and cheering on their resolve, and now the majority were for stripping Metellus of his office by any means available. The senate, too, assembled and gave orders once again to back Cato and resist the bill as a measure designed to breed sedition in the city — perhaps civil war.

Metellus himself remained bold and unbending; but he could see that his followers were thoroughly frightened of Cato, whom they regarded as unbeatable, so he rushed out of the senate into the forum, called the people together, and gave a bitter, poisonous speech against him — crying out that he was fleeing from Cato's tyranny and from this conspiracy against Pompey, and that the city would soon be sorry for having dishonored so great a man. And with that he set off for Asia, to lay before Pompey, everyone assumed, the full catalogue of his injuries. Cato was showered with praise for having delivered the state from a dangerous tribuneship and for having, in the person of Metellus, dealt a blow to the power of Pompey himself. But he was admired even more for what came next: when the senate moved to disgrace Metellus and formally depose him, Cato opposed the whole proceeding and finally talked them out of it. The common people admired his moderation and humanity — he would not trample a beaten enemy for the pleasure of it — while shrewder men recognized the policy in it: he was not going to provoke Pompey needlessly.

Soon after this, Lucullus came home from the war in Asia — a war whose finishing stroke, and with it the glory of the whole campaign, had to all appearances been lifted out of his hands by Pompey. He came close to losing his triumph as well, because Gaius Memmius attacked him before the people and threatened prosecution — more out of devotion to Pompey than any real grudge of his own. But Cato was tied to Lucullus, who had married his sister Servilia, and he thought the whole business a gross injustice besides, so he stood up to Memmius — and exposed himself to a storm of slander for it. They came near to throwing him out of his office, on the pretense that he was wielding his power like a tyrant. Yet in the end Cato got the better of Memmius, who was forced to drop the charges and abandon the fight. Lucullus got his triumph, and from then on cultivated Cato's friendship all the more carefully, regarding it as his best wall and defense against the power of Pompey.

And now Pompey himself came home from the war in glory. The splendor of his reception convinced him the people would refuse him nothing, so he sent word to the senate asking them to postpone the consular elections until he could be there in person to campaign for Piso, who was standing for the office. Most of the senators were ready to go along. Cato was not — and not because he thought the delay itself mattered much. He wanted to cut down Pompey's swelling expectations at a stroke. He opposed the request and swung the senate against it. This shook Pompey more than a little. He could see he was going to fail, and fail often, unless he could bring Cato over to his side. So he sent for Munatius, Cato's friend, and made an offer: Cato had two nieces of marriageable age, and Pompey proposed to marry the elder himself and take the younger for his son. (Some say they were not his nieces but his daughters.) Munatius laid the proposal before Cato in the presence of his wife and sisters, and the women were overjoyed at the prospect of an alliance with so great and important a man. Cato, without a pause and without a moment's deliberation, made up his mind on the spot. "Go, Munatius," he said, "go tell Pompey that Cato cannot be captured by way of the women's rooms. I am grateful for the kindness he intends, and so long as his conduct is honest, I promise him a friendship more solid than any marriage alliance. But I will not hand over hostages to Pompey's glory against my country's safety." The women were badly disappointed by this answer, and even his friends thought it harsh and high-handed. But later, when Pompey was trying to get the consulship for one of his friends and paid the people outright for their votes — a bribery so notorious that the money was counted out in Pompey's own gardens — Cato said to the women that they would necessarily have shared in the dirt of these dealings if they had been joined to Pompey's family; and they admitted he had judged best in refusing. And yet, if we go by what actually happened, Cato was badly at fault in rejecting that alliance — because it fell instead to Caesar. That was the match that fused Caesar's power with Pompey's, all but ruined the Roman empire, and did destroy the republic. None of it, perhaps, would have come to pass if Cato had not been so alarmed by Pompey's small faults that he drove him into handing another man the opportunity for the greatest ones.

But all that lay in the future. In the meantime Lucullus and Pompey fell into a great dispute over their rival arrangements in Pontus, each fighting to have his own ordinances stand. Cato took the side of Lucullus, who was plainly the wronged party; and Pompey, finding himself the weaker in the senate, went over its head to the people and proposed a law dividing lands among his soldiers to buy their votes. Cato opposed him there too, and the bill was thrown out. At that, Pompey attached himself to Clodius, the most violent demagogue of the day — and struck up his friendship with Caesar. And for that, too, Cato was in a way responsible. Here is how. Caesar, back from his governorship in Spain, wanted to stand for the consulship and keep his claim to a triumph at the same time. But the law required candidates for office to be present in the city, while a man awaiting a triumph had to remain outside the walls. So Caesar asked the senate for permission to canvass through his friends in his absence. Many senators were willing to allow it. Cato was not — and seeing them inclined to oblige Caesar, he spoke for the entire day and talked the session into the ground, so that the senate could reach no decision at all. Caesar thereupon let the triumph go, came into the city, and immediately made his alliance with Pompey and stood for the consulship. The moment he was declared consul-elect, he married his daughter Julia to Pompey. And having leagued themselves together against the commonwealth, the one proposed laws distributing land to the poor, and the other stood by to push the proposals through. Lucullus, Cicero, and their friends lined up with Bibulus, the other consul, to block the laws — and at the front of them all stood Cato, who already saw the friendship and alliance of Pompey and Caesar for the dangerous thing it was. What he objected to, he declared, was not the advantage the poor would get from the land — it was the price these men meant to collect for it, this courting and cozening of the people. On that argument he carried the senate with him, along with many outside it who were disgusted at Caesar's conduct — that a consul should flatter the mob so basely, using tricks that had always belonged to the rashest and most rebellious tribunes. So Caesar and his party, afraid they could not win by fair means, turned to open force. First a basket of dung was dumped on Bibulus on his way to the forum. Then they set on his lictors and broke their rods. Finally darts flew and men were wounded, and everyone who opposed the laws fled the forum — the rest at a dead run, and Cato last of all, walking out slowly, turning back again and again to call down the vengeance of heaven on them.

So the other party not only carried their land law, but tacked on a decree that the whole senate should swear to uphold it and defend it against any attempt at repeal, with heavy penalties for refusing the oath. The senators all swore — necessity staring them in the face, and with it the memory of the elder Metellus, long ago, who had refused an oath in just such a case and been driven out of Italy for it. As for Cato, his wife and children begged him with tears, and his friends and familiars pressed and pleaded with him to give way and swear. But the man who prevailed with him above all was Cicero, the orator, who urged that it was perhaps not even right in itself for one private citizen to set himself against what the public had decreed; that with the thing done and past altering, it was folly and madness to throw himself into danger when it could do his country no good at all; and that the worst of all evils would be to seize the excuse, as it were, to abandon the commonwealth he did everything for, and hand it over to the men bent on its ruin — as if he were glad to be relieved of the trouble of defending it. "For though Cato may have no need of Rome," Cicero said, "Rome has need of Cato — and so do all his friends." Of these, Cicero professed, he himself was the first, since Clodius was even then taking aim at him, openly threatening to come after him the moment he got the tribuneship. So Cato, they say, worn down by the entreaties and arguments of his friends, went unwillingly and took the oath — last of all, except only Favonius, one of his closest acquaintance.

Riding high on this success, Caesar brought in another law, this one distributing almost the whole territory of Campania among the poor and needy citizens. No one dared speak against it but Cato — so Caesar had him pulled off the rostra and dragged toward prison. Even that did not check his freedom of speech: as he went along he kept on denouncing the law, and advised the people to put down every legislator who proposed the like. The senate and the best of the citizens followed after him with sad, dejected faces, their grief and indignation showing in their silence, so Caesar could hardly miss how offended they were; but for pure contention's sake he pressed on, waiting for Cato to beg off or file an appeal. When he saw that Cato would not so much as consider doing either, ashamed of what he was doing and of what the city thought of it, Caesar himself quietly asked one of the tribunes to intervene and release him. For all that, with these laws and gratifications the multitude was won, and they decreed Caesar the government of Illyricum and all Gaul, with an army of four legions, for five years — while Cato cried out that they were voting, with their own hands, to install a tyrant in their citadel. Publius Clodius, who had gotten himself illegally transferred from patrician to plebeian, was declared tribune of the people, having promised to do everything according to Caesar's and Pompey's pleasure on one condition: that he be allowed to banish Cicero. And for consuls they set up Calpurnius Piso, the father of Caesar's wife, and Aulus Gabinius — one of Pompey's creatures, as we are told by the people best acquainted with his life and habits.

Yet even with everything locked down — one half of the city mastered by favors, the other by fear — they were still afraid of Cato. They remembered, with some bitterness, what pains and trouble their victories over him had cost, and what shame and disgrace besides, when they had been driven at last to lay violent hands on him. Clodius, for one, despaired of forcing Cicero out of Italy while Cato remained in Rome. So, with his scheme laid in advance, the first thing he did on entering office was send for Cato and tell him that he regarded him as the most incorruptible man in Rome, and was ready to prove he meant it. "Plenty of men," he said, "have applied for the commission to Cyprus in the case of Ptolemy, and lobbied for the appointment. But you are the only man worthy of it, and I am pleased to give you the favor." Cato cried out at once that the thing was a trap — no favor, but an injury. To which Clodius answered, proud and fierce: "Then if you will not take it as a kindness, you shall make the voyage as a punishment," and going straight out to the assembly of the people, he had them pass a decree that Cato be sent to Cyprus. So Cato was shipped off — and they voted him neither ship, nor soldier, nor any staff except two secretaries, one of whom was a thief and a scoundrel, and the other a hanger-on of Clodius. And as if Cyprus and Ptolemy were not work enough, he was ordered to restore the refugees of Byzantium into the bargain. Clodius meant to keep him far away for as long as his own tribuneship lasted.

Forced to go, Cato advised Cicero — next on the list — to make no resistance, for fear of throwing the state into civil war and chaos, but to bow to the times, and so become his country's preserver a second time. He sent his friend Canidius ahead to Cyprus to persuade Ptolemy to yield without a fight, promising that if he did he would lack for neither riches nor honor, for the Romans would give him the priesthood of the goddess at Paphos. Cato himself waited at Rhodes, making his preparations and expecting an answer from Cyprus. While he waited, the other Ptolemy — the king of Egypt, who had abandoned Alexandria after a quarrel with his subjects and was sailing for Rome in hopes that Pompey and Caesar would send an army to restore him — asked, on his way, to see Cato, and sent word to him, assuming Cato would come to him. Cato had taken a purgative that morning, and sent back the answer that Ptolemy was welcome to come to him, if he saw fit. When the king came, Cato neither went out to meet him nor so much as rose from his seat; he greeted him like any ordinary visitor and told him to sit down. This threw Ptolemy off balance at first — he was startled to meet such stern, lofty manners in a man of so plain and unassuming an appearance. But when they began to talk over his affairs, he was still more astonished at the wisdom and the bluntness of what he heard. For Cato criticized the whole course he had taken. He pointed out what honor and happiness the king was walking away from, and what humiliations and miseries he was walking into — the bribing he would have to do, the greed he would have to feed when he reached the leading men of Rome, whom all Egypt turned into silver would scarcely satisfy. His advice was to sail home and be reconciled with his subjects — and he offered to go along himself and help settle the differences. Brought back to his senses by this talk, as if out of a fit of madness or delirium, and seeing the truth and good sense in what Cato said, Ptolemy resolved to follow his advice. But his friends talked him around again, and he went on to Rome as he had first planned. When he got there, and found himself cooling his heels at the door of one magistrate after another, he began to curse his own folly — feeling he had refused not the counsel of a good and wise man so much as the oracle of a god.

Meanwhile the other Ptolemy, the one in Cyprus, very conveniently for Cato, poisoned himself. He was reported to have left great riches; so Cato, who meant to go first to Byzantium, sent his nephew Brutus to Cyprus, since he did not entirely trust Canidius. Then he reconciled the refugees with the people of Byzantium, left that city quiet and at peace, and sailed for Cyprus, where he found a royal treasure — plate, furniture, precious stones, purple — all of which had to be turned into ready money. Determined to do everything with perfect exactness, and to drive the price of every item to the top, he attended the sales in person and went carefully through all the accounts. He would not trust the customs of the market; he looked with suspicion on everyone alike — the officers, the criers, the bidders, even his own friends — and ended by talking with the buyers himself, pressing them to bid higher, and running the greater part of the sales in that fashion.

This distrust gave offense to a number of his friends, and one of them in particular — Munatius, the closest of them all — was left almost beyond reconciling. When Caesar came to write his book against Cato, this affair supplied him with his most savage passages. Munatius himself, though, says the quarrel came not so much from Cato's distrust as from Cato's neglect of him — and from his own jealousy of Canidius. For Munatius also wrote a book about Cato, and it is the main authority Thrasea follows. Munatius tells it this way: he arrived in Cyprus after the others, and found miserable lodgings provided for him; he went to Cato's house and was turned away at the door, because Cato was closeted with Canidius. He later complained about this, in quite mild terms — and got a very harsh answer: too much love, according to Theophrastus, often turns to hatred. "And you," Cato told him, "because you bear me so much love, think you receive too little honor, and so you lose your temper. I employ Canidius for his industry and his fidelity. He has been with me from the first, and I have found him worthy of trust." All this was said privately, between the two of them; but Cato afterward repeated it to Canidius — and when Munatius found that out, he refused to come to Cato's suppers anymore, and refused to come when he was invited to give counsel. Cato then threatened to seize his goods, the customary penalty for the disobedient; Munatius ignored the threats, went back to Rome, and nursed the grievance a long time. But later, when Cato had come home too, Marcia — who at this time was still living with him — arranged for the two men to be invited to supper together at the house of one Barca. Cato arrived last, when the rest were already reclining, and asked where he should take his place. Wherever he pleased, said Barca. Cato looked around the room, said he would be near Munatius — and went and placed himself on the couch beside him. But that was the only mark of kindness he showed him all through the meal. Another time, at Marcia's urging, Cato wrote to Munatius that he wished to speak with him. Munatius came to the house in the morning, and Marcia kept him there until all the other company had gone. Then Cato came in, threw both arms around him, embraced him warmly, and the two were friends again. I have told this little story at full length because I think the characters and tempers of men show themselves more clearly in things of this kind than in great and famous actions.

Cato collected not much less than seven thousand talents of silver. Anxious about what might happen on so long a sea voyage, he had a great many chests made, each holding two talents and five hundred drachmas; to every chest he fastened a long rope, and to the far end of the rope a piece of cork, so that if the ship went down, the floats would mark where the chests lay under water. And in fact the money, all but a very little, was carried over safely. But he had also kept two account books, in which every transaction of his commission was carefully written out — and neither of them survived. One was in the charge of his freedman Philargyrus, who sailed from Cenchreae and went down with the ship and all its freight. The other Cato kept safe himself as far as Corcyra, where he pitched his tent in the marketplace; the sailors, bitterly cold in the night, built a great many fires, some of the tents caught, and the book was burned. He had brought along several of Ptolemy's stewards, who could vouch for his integrity and shut the mouths of enemies and false accusers; even so, the loss gnawed at him, and he was angry with himself over it — for he had intended those books not so much as proof of his own honesty, but as a pattern of exactness for other men to follow.
