Cicero

(Part 2 of 4)

Catiline's fellow conspirators had been cowed at first, even discouraged. But before long their nerve came back. They began meeting again, urging one another to strike boldly and get the thing done before Pompey returned — for word had it that he was already marching his army home toward Rome. The sharpest goads driving Catiline forward, though, were Sulla's old soldiers. They had been discharged and scattered all over Italy, and the largest and fiercest concentration of them lay seeded through the towns of Etruria, passing their days in daydreams of fresh plunder — of looting the piled-up riches of Italy all over again. With Manlius for their leader, a man who had served with real distinction in Sulla's campaigns, they attached themselves to Catiline and came into Rome to back him with their votes at the election. For he was standing for the consulship a second time, and his plan was to kill Cicero in the chaos of election day itself. Even the gods seemed to be signaling the trouble ahead, with earthquakes, lightning strikes, and strange apparitions. Human informants were not lacking either — reliable enough in themselves, but not yet enough to convict a man as noble and as powerful as Catiline. So Cicero postponed the election, summoned Catiline before the senate, and questioned him directly about the reports. Catiline, believing that plenty of senators secretly wanted a revolution — and wanting to give the conspirators in the room a taste of what he was made of — gave back an answer of pure audacity. "What harm am I doing," he said, "if I see two bodies — one lean and wasting, but with a head; the other big and strong, but with no head at all — and I simply give a head to the body that lacks one?" The riddle meant the senate and the people, and it deepened Cicero's alarm considerably. He put on a breastplate, and the leading citizens escorted him from his house in a body, with a crowd of young men accompanying him down to the Field of Mars, where the voting was held. There, quite deliberately, he let his tunic slip from his shoulder so that the armor underneath showed, putting his danger on display for everyone watching. The crowd was outraged on his behalf and packed in close around him to protect him. In the end Catiline was voted down once again, and Silanus and Murena were elected consuls.

Not long afterward, Catiline's soldiers began assembling in Etruria and forming themselves into companies, for the day fixed for the uprising was drawing near. And around midnight, some of the most prominent and powerful men in Rome — Marcus Crassus, Marcus Marcellus, and Scipio Metellus — came to Cicero's house, pounded on the gate, roused the porter, and told him to wake his master and say who was waiting. Here was the story. After dinner, Crassus's porter had handed him a bundle of letters delivered by a stranger. They were addressed to various men, but one — unsigned — was for Crassus himself. That one alone he read. It told him that a great massacre was coming at Catiline's hands, and advised him to slip out of the city. The others he did not open. He came straight to Cicero with the lot, shaken by the danger, and eager to clear himself of the suspicion he lay under because of his friendship with Catiline. Cicero weighed the matter and summoned the senate at daybreak. He brought the letters with him, handed each to the man it was addressed to, and ordered them read aloud. Every one of them told the same story of conspiracy. And when Quintus Arrius, a former praetor, reported to the house that soldiers were mustering in companies across Etruria, and that Manlius was said to be on the move with a large force, hovering near those towns and waiting for news from Rome, the senate passed its decree: everything was placed in the hands of the consuls, who were to take charge of affairs and do whatever they could to save the state. This was no routine measure. The senate reserved it for moments of imminent danger.

Once Cicero had received this power, he handed the situation outside the city to Quintus Metellus and kept the management of Rome itself in his own hands. So enormous was the escort that guarded him whenever he went out that his train filled most of the forum the moment he entered it. Catiline, unable to bear any more delay, resolved to break out and join Manlius himself — but first he ordered Marcius and Cethegus to take their swords, go to Cicero's gates early in the morning as if merely paying a courtesy call, and there fall on him and cut him down. This plot a noblewoman named Fulvia carried to Cicero by night, warning him to be on his guard against Cethegus and Marcius. They came at daybreak, and when they were refused entry they made such an angry scene at the gates that they only deepened the suspicion against them. Cicero then went out and summoned the senate to the temple of Jupiter Stator, which stands at the top of the Sacred Street on the way up to the Palatine. Catiline arrived with the rest, as though he meant to make his defense — and not one senator would sit beside him. Every man on his bench got up and moved away. When he began to speak, they shouted him down. At last Cicero rose and ordered him to leave the city: since one of them did his work with words, he said, and the other with weapons, there needed to be a wall between them. So Catiline marched straight out of Rome with three hundred armed men, surrounded himself — as though he were a lawful magistrate — with the rods, the axes, and the military standards, and made his way to Manlius. With a force now numbering nearly twenty thousand, he moved from town to town, working to talk or terrify them into revolt. It was open war now, and Antonius was sent out to fight him.

The conspirators Catiline had left corrupted inside the city were held together and kept in heart by Cornelius Lentulus, surnamed Sura — a man of noble family but a dissolute life, who had once been expelled from the senate for his debaucheries and was now serving his second term as praetor, the customary route for men trying to win back senatorial rank. The story goes that he picked up the surname Sura like this. As quaestor in Sulla's day, he had run through and squandered an enormous amount of public money. Sulla, furious, called him before the senate to account for it. Lentulus strolled in with perfect coolness and contempt and announced that he had no accounting to give — but they were welcome to this, and he stuck out the calf of his leg, the way boys do at ball when they miss a catch. And since sura is the Latin word for the calf of the leg, the name stuck. On another occasion he was on trial and had bribed some of the jurors; when he was acquitted by a margin of only two votes, he complained that he'd wasted his money on the second one — a single vote would have done the job. This was the man, such by his own nature, whom Catiline had set aflame — and false prophets and fortune-tellers had finished the corruption with empty hopes. They fed him invented verses and oracles, claiming to prove from the Sibylline prophecies that three men named Cornelius were fated to rule Rome as monarchs; that two of them, Cinna and Sulla, had already fulfilled the decree; and that destiny was now hurrying toward the third and last Cornelius with the gift of monarchy in her hands. He must seize it by every means, they said, and not ruin his chance by dawdling, the way Catiline had.

What Lentulus had in mind, then, was nothing small or trivial. He had resolved to kill the entire senate and as many other citizens as he could manage, to burn the city to the ground, and to spare nobody — nobody except Pompey's children, whom he intended to seize and hold as hostages to secure his peace with Pompey. For by now the report was loud and everywhere that Pompey was on his way home from his great eastern campaign. The night chosen for the strike was during the Saturnalia, the December festival. Swords, flax, and sulfur were carried into Cethegus's house and hidden there. They assigned a hundred men to a hundred districts, the city carved up so that with that many fires kindled at once, all of Rome would go up in a single blaze. Others had their orders too: block the aqueducts, and kill anyone who tried to carry water to the flames. While all this was in preparation, it happened that two ambassadors from the Allobroges — a Gallic people then in miserable straits and chafing badly under Roman rule — were staying in Rome. Lentulus and his circle judged them the perfect instruments for stirring Gaul to revolt, and admitted them to the conspiracy. They gave them letters for their own magistrates and letters for Catiline: to the Gauls they promised liberty; Catiline they urged to proclaim freedom for the slaves and march them on Rome. And they sent along with the ambassadors, to carry the letters to Catiline, a man named Titus, a native of Croton.

Such were the schemes of careless men who did their plotting over wine and among women — and Cicero tracked them with sober patience, hard thinking, and truly remarkable intelligence. He had agents everywhere outside, following and tracing every move, and he kept up secret communication with many men who pretended to be in the conspiracy. So he knew all their dealings with the strangers; and lying in wait by night, he took the Crotonian, letters and all — the Allobrogian ambassadors secretly working with him the entire time.

At daybreak he summoned the senate to the temple of Concord, where he read out the letters and examined the informers. Junius Silanus added that several people had heard Cethegus say three consuls and four praetors were marked for death; Piso, an ex-consul, testified to more of the same; and Gaius Sulpicius, one of the praetors, was sent to Cethegus's house, where he found a stockpile of javelins and armor, and a still larger number of swords and daggers, every one freshly sharpened. In the end the senate voted immunity for the Crotonian in exchange for a full confession, and Lentulus stood convicted. He resigned his office — he was still praetor — stripped off his purple-bordered robe there in the senate, and exchanged it for clothing better suited to his new circumstances. Then he and his confederates were handed over to the praetors to be held in open custody.

By evening the common people were massed outside, waiting. Cicero went out to them, told them what had been done, and then, with the crowd escorting him, went to the house of a friend and near neighbor — for his own house was occupied by the women, who were celebrating their secret rites in honor of the goddess the Romans call Bona, the Good Goddess, and the Greeks the Women's Goddess. Her sacrifice is performed every year in the consul's house by his wife or his mother, with the Vestal Virgins attending. Slipping quietly into his friend's house, with only a few men present, Cicero began to turn over what he should do with these prisoners. The severest penalty — the only one that fit crimes so enormous — he shrank from, hesitant and uneasy. Partly it was the gentleness of his own nature; partly he feared looking like a man drunk on his new authority, crushing too harshly men of the noblest families, with the most powerful friends in the city. Yet if he dealt with them mildly, the danger ahead looked dreadful. If they suffered anything short of death, there was no chance they would be reconciled; far more likely they would add fresh rage to their old wickedness and hurl themselves into every kind of recklessness — while he himself, whose reputation for courage did not stand especially high with the crowd as it was, would be written off as the worst sort of coward.

While Cicero sat wavering, a sign came to the women at their sacrifice. On the altar, where the fire seemed to have gone completely out, a great bright flame burst up from the ashes of the burnt wood. The other women were terrified, but the holy virgins told Terentia, Cicero's wife, to go quickly to her husband and tell him to carry out what he had resolved for his country's good — for the goddess had sent a great light on the road to his safety and his glory. Now Terentia was by no nature soft-hearted or timid; she was an ambitious woman who, as Cicero himself says, was far more likely to push her way into his public business than to share her domestic affairs with him. She delivered the message and sharpened him against the conspirators. So did his brother Quintus, and so did Publius Nigidius, one of his philosophical friends, whose counsel he used in his greatest and weightiest affairs of state.

The next day the senate took up the question of punishment. Silanus, asked his opinion first, said the men should be taken to the prison and there suffer the ultimate penalty. One after another, every senator agreed — until it came to Gaius Caesar, the man who was afterward dictator. He was young then, at the very start of his rise, but he had already set his hopes and his policy on the road by which he would eventually turn the Roman state into a monarchy. Others saw none of this coming. Cicero had strong suspicions, though he had never obtained solid proof; and some indeed said that Caesar came within a whisker of exposure and only just slipped clear of him. Others hold that Cicero deliberately looked away from the evidence against him, out of fear of his friends and his power — since it was obvious to everyone that if Caesar were charged alongside the conspirators, they were likelier to be saved with him than he was to be punished with them.

So when Caesar's turn came to speak, he stood up and proposed that the conspirators not be put to death, but that their property be confiscated and the men held instead under guard in whatever Italian towns Cicero himself might choose, until Catiline was defeated. The proposal was the moderate one, and the man delivering it was a formidable speaker — and Cicero himself added no small weight to it, for when he rose he argued the scale both ways, speaking partly for the first motion and partly for Caesar's. His friends all judged Caesar's motion the safer course for Cicero — he would carry less blame afterward if the conspirators were not executed — and so they came down on that side; and Silanus too changed course and walked his own opinion back, saying he had never meant capital punishment, only the "ultimate penalty," which for a Roman senator was imprisonment — the utmost a Roman could suffer. The first man to speak against Caesar's motion was Catulus Lutatius. Cato followed, and in his speech he pointed the finger of suspicion at Caesar himself with such vehemence, and so filled the senate with anger and resolve, that a decree was passed for the conspirators' execution. But Caesar fought the confiscation of their property, thinking it hardly fair that the senators who had thrown out the mildest part of his proposal should help themselves to the harshest. When many insisted, he appealed to the tribunes, who would do nothing; and Cicero himself gave way and dropped that part of the sentence.

Then Cicero went out with the senate to fetch the conspirators. They were not being held together in one place; each praetor had one or another of them in custody. First he took Lentulus from the Palatine and led him down the Sacred Street and through the middle of the forum, a ring of the most eminent citizens encircling and guarding him. The people, frightened by what was happening, filed past in silence — the young men especially, as if, trembling and afraid, they were being initiated into some ancient sacred mystery of aristocratic power. When he had crossed the forum and reached the prison, he handed Lentulus to the executioner and gave the order. Then Cethegus, and after him each of the rest in turn, he brought down and delivered to death. And when he saw many of the conspirators still standing about the forum in knots, ignorant of what had been done and waiting for nightfall, still supposing the men were alive and might yet be rescued, he called out to them in a loud voice: "They have lived." That is how Romans speak of the dead when they want to avoid words of ill omen.

It was evening by the time he made his way home through the forum, and now the citizens no longer escorted him in silence and good order. Instead they received him, as he passed, with shouts and applause, hailing him as the savior and founder of his country. The streets blazed with light from the lamps and torches set up at every doorway, and the women held lights out from the rooftops in his honor, to watch him going home in a magnificent procession of the city's greatest men. Among them were many who had commanded great wars, celebrated triumphs, and added to the Roman empire by land and by sea — and as they walked with him they admitted to one another that although the Roman people owed many commanders of that age a debt for riches, spoils, and power, they owed their safety — the security of everything else — to Cicero alone, for delivering them from a danger so great and so near. Not that stopping the plot and punishing the plotters seemed so wonderful in itself; the extraordinary thing was that the greatest conspiracy ever formed had been crushed with so little disturbance, so little tumult, so little cost. For most of the men who had flocked to Catiline deserted him the moment they heard the fate of Lentulus and Cethegus; and Catiline himself, giving battle to Antonius with the forces that remained to him, was destroyed along with his army.

And yet there were men quite ready to speak ill of Cicero for all this, and to do him harm as well — with leaders among the incoming magistrates of the following year: Caesar, now one of the praetors, and Metellus and Bestia, the tribunes. Taking office a few days before Cicero's consulship expired, they refused to let him address the people, and threw their benches in front of the Rostra to prevent it, telling him that he could, if he liked, take the oath of withdrawal from office and then come straight down again. Cicero accepted the terms and came forward to lay down his consulship; and when silence fell, he recited his oath — not in the usual form, but in a new one all his own: that he had, in truth, saved his country and preserved the empire. And the whole people swore the oath after him, confirming it. This only exasperated Caesar and the tribunes further, and they set about brewing new trouble for him, proposing a law to recall Pompey and his army to put an end to what they called Cicero's tyranny. But it was an enormous piece of luck for Cicero, and for the whole commonwealth, that Cato was a tribune that year. Holding equal power with the others and greater public standing, Cato could block their designs. He swept aside their other projects easily, and in a speech to the people he praised Cicero's consulship so highly that the greatest honors ever given were voted him, and he was publicly proclaimed Father of his Country. He seems to have been the first man ever to receive that title, when Cato gave it to him before the assembled people.

His authority in the city stood very high at this moment. But he made himself widely envied, and offended a great many people — not by any wicked act, but simply because he never stopped praising and glorifying himself. No senate, no assembly of the people, no court of law could convene without being treated to another round of Catiline and Lentulus. He went on to fill his books and writings with self-congratulation, until a prose style that was in itself delightful — a pleasure to hear — became tiresome and grating to his audiences; the ugly habit clung to him like a chronic disease. Still, however greedy he was for his own glory, he was remarkably free of envy toward anyone else's, and was in fact lavishly generous in praising both the ancients and his own contemporaries, as his writings show on every page. Many of these sayings are still remembered. He called Aristotle "a river of flowing gold," and said of Plato's dialogues that "if Jupiter were to speak, he would speak in language like theirs." Theophrastus he liked to call "my own special luxury." Asked which of Demosthenes's orations he thought the best, he answered, "The longest." True, certain would-be imitators of Demosthenes have made a fuss over a line in one of his letters to the effect that Demosthenes sometimes nods off in his speeches — conveniently forgetting the extravagant praise Cicero heaped on the man throughout his career, and the compliment he paid him in naming his own most labored orations, the ones against Antony, his "Philippics." As for the eminent men of his own day, in eloquence or in philosophy, there was not one of them whom he did not make more famous by speaking or writing kindly about him. He obtained Roman citizenship from Caesar, when Caesar was in power, for Cratippus the Peripatetic philosopher, and got the court of the Areopagus to pass a public decree asking Cratippus to remain in Athens to teach their young men and adorn their city. There are letters from Cicero to Herodes, and others to his own son, in which he directs the boy to study philosophy under Cratippus. There is one letter in which he blames Gorgias the rhetoric teacher for luring his son into drinking and high living, and bans the man from his company. That letter, and one other to Pelops of Byzantium, are the only two of his Greek letters written in anger — and rightly enough in the case of Gorgias, if he really was the dissolute character he was reputed to be; but with Pelops, Cicero comes off rather small, whining that the man had failed to arrange certain honors for him by decree of the Byzantines.

Here is another illustration of his hunger for praise: his habit, in the heat of making a speech more striking, of trampling right over decorum and dignity. When Munatius, who had escaped conviction thanks to Cicero's advocacy, promptly turned around and prosecuted Cicero's friend Sabinus, Cicero snapped in the heat of his resentment, "Do you imagine you were acquitted on your own merits, Munatius? Wasn't it rather that I threw so much darkness over the case that the court couldn't see your guilt?" From the Rostra he once delivered a eulogy of Marcus Crassus to great applause — and then, within a few days, publicly abused him from the same spot. "Didn't you stand in this very place two days ago," Crassus called out, "and praise me?" "Yes," said Cicero. "I was exercising my eloquence on a poor subject." On another occasion Crassus declared that no member of his family had ever lived past sixty, then later denied saying it, and demanded, "What could have put it into my head to say such a thing?" "You knew how delighted the people would be to hear it," Cicero answered. "You were fishing for their favor." When Crassus once professed admiration for the Stoic doctrine that the good man is always rich, Cicero said, "Are you sure you don't mean their doctrine that everything belongs to the wise?" — Crassus being notorious for his greed. One of Crassus's sons was thought to look so exactly like a certain Axius that it cast a shadow on his mother's honor; when the young man made a successful speech in the senate and Cicero was asked what he thought of it, he answered in Greek: "Axios Crassou" — worthy of Crassus, and of Axius too.

When Crassus was about to leave for Syria, he preferred to have Cicero as a friend rather than an enemy, so one day he greeted him warmly and said he would come and dine with him — and Cicero received him just as graciously. A few days later, some of his acquaintances came to intercede for Vatinius, then his enemy, saying the man wanted reconciliation and friendship. "What," said Cicero, "does Vatinius want to come to dinner too?" So much for his way with Crassus. As for Vatinius, who had swellings on his neck, Cicero once heard him pleading a case and called him "the swollen orator." And having been told once that Vatinius was dead, then learning shortly afterward that he was alive, Cicero said, "Then may the rascal perish — for bringing news that wasn't true."
