Caesar

(Part 2 of 6)

Roman law said that a general who wanted the honor of a triumph had to wait outside the city walls until the senate gave its answer. Another law said that a candidate for the consulship had to appear in the city in person. Caesar arrived home at the exact moment of the consular elections and found himself pinned between the two rules. So he sent word to the senate asking that, since the law obliged him to stay outside, he be allowed to stand for the consulship in absence, through his friends. Cato opposed the request, standing squarely on the letter of the law — and then, when he saw that Caesar had already talked a large part of the senate into agreeing, he switched tactics and simply played for time, speaking on and on until the whole day was wasted. At that, Caesar made his decision. He let the triumph go and went after the consulship.

He entered the city, came forward at once, and pulled off a political masterstroke that deceived everyone except Cato. He reconciled Crassus and Pompey, the two most powerful men then in Rome. There had been a quarrel between them; Caesar now patched it up, harnessed their combined strength to himself, and — under cover of an act that looked like nothing but kindness and good nature — carried out what was in reality a revolution in the government. For it was not the quarrel between Pompey and Caesar that produced the civil wars, as most people imagine. It was their union: their conspiring together, first, to pull down the aristocracy, and only afterward falling out with each other. Cato, who kept predicting exactly where this alliance would end, was written off at the time as a sour, meddling obstructionist. In the end he got the reputation of a wise counselor who was simply unlucky.

So Caesar, propped up on both sides by the interests of Crassus and Pompey, was swept into the consulship and triumphantly proclaimed, with Calpurnius Bibulus as his colleague. The moment he took office he began introducing bills that would have come more gracefully from the most reckless of the tribunes than from a consul — proposals for planting colonies and carving up public lands, designed purely to please the crowd. The best and most honorable senators opposed him, which was exactly the excuse he had been waiting for. He protested loudly that it was against all his wishes, but the senate's insults and harshness were driving him to it: there was nothing left for him now but to devote himself to the people and their cause. And out of the senate house he stormed. Presenting himself before the popular assembly, with Crassus stationed on one side of him and Pompey on the other, he asked the two men whether they approved of his bills. They said they did. He then asked them to stand by him against those who had threatened to stop the bills with their swords. They promised they would, and Pompey went further: if anyone came with swords, he said, he would bring his own sword — and a shield as well. The nobles hated this remark. It was beneath his dignity, they said, and showed none of the respect owed to the senate; it sounded like a hotheaded boy, or a madman raving. But the people loved it.

To fasten his hold on Pompey tighter still, Caesar gave him his daughter Julia — who was already engaged to Servilius Caepio — and told Servilius he could have Pompey's daughter instead, though she was not exactly free either, being promised to Sulla's son Faustus. A little later Caesar himself married Calpurnia, the daughter of Piso, and then got Piso made consul for the following year. At this Cato exploded, protesting with real heat that it was intolerable to have the government bartered away through marriages, and to watch these men trade armies, provinces, and great commands among themselves by dealing out women. Bibulus, Caesar's colleague, found that opposing the bills was useless and that he was in real danger of being murdered in the forum — as was Cato. So he shut himself up in his house and stayed there for the remainder of his term as consul. Pompey, as soon as he was married, filled the forum with soldiers, helped the people force the new laws through, and secured for Caesar the government of all Gaul — both on this side of the Alps and beyond them — together with Illyricum and the command of four legions for five years. Cato made some attempt to fight these measures, and Caesar had him seized and marched off toward prison, expecting him to appeal to the tribunes. But Cato walked along without saying a word. Caesar watched — and saw that not only were the nobles indignant, but the common people too, out of respect for Cato's virtue, were following behind him in silence with downcast faces. So Caesar quietly asked one of the tribunes, himself, to intervene and release him. As for the rest of the senators, only a few still attended the meetings; the others, disgusted, stayed away. Considius, a very old man, told Caesar one day that the senators did not come because they were afraid of his soldiers. "Then why doesn't the same fear keep you at home?" Caesar asked. Because, Considius answered, old age was his bodyguard: the little life he had left was not worth much caution. But the most disgraceful act of Caesar's entire consulship was helping win the tribuneship for Clodius — the same Clodius who had made the attempt on his wife's chastity and crashed the secret rites of the women. Clodius was elected for one purpose: to destroy Cicero. And Caesar did not leave the city to join his army until the two of them together had brought Cicero down and driven him out of Italy.

So much for Caesar's career before the wars in Gaul. From here he seems to start over — to enter a new life and a whole new field of action. The wars he now fought, and the long string of campaigns in which he conquered Gaul, proved him a soldier and a general second to none of the greatest and most admired commanders who ever led an army. Compare him with the Fabii, the Metelli, the Scipios; with the men of his own time or just before it — Sulla, Marius, the two Luculli, or even Pompey himself, whose military glory in those days seemed to reach the sky — and Caesar's achievements outstrip them all. One he surpassed in the difficulty of the country he fought in; another in the sheer extent of territory he conquered; some in the number and strength of the enemies he defeated; one in the wildness and treachery of the tribes he managed to win over; another in his humanity and mercy toward the beaten; others again in his gifts and generosity to his own soldiers; and all of them alike in the number of battles he fought and enemies he killed. For in less than ten years of war in Gaul he took more than eight hundred towns by storm, subdued three hundred nations, and — of the three million men who at one time or another stood against him — killed one million and took another million prisoner.

He commanded such devotion and loyalty from his soldiers that men who were nothing special in other campaigns became unstoppable, past defeating or even withstanding, the moment Caesar's glory was at stake. One such was Acilius, who in the sea-fight off Marseilles had his right hand sheared off by a sword — and did not drop the shield from his left. He smashed it into the enemies' faces until he had driven them all back and taken the ship. Another was Cassius Scaeva, who in the battle near Dyrrhachium had one eye shot out with an arrow, his shoulder run through with one javelin and his thigh with another, and caught one hundred and thirty darts on his shield. In that state he called out to the enemy as if he meant to surrender — and when two of them came up to take him, he sliced the shoulder off one with his sword, struck the other across the face and drove him back, and escaped with the help of his friends, who by then had reached him. In Britain, some of the leading officers blundered into a watery marsh and were attacked there by the enemy. While Caesar stood watching, a common soldier flung himself into the middle of the fight, performed one astonishing feat of courage after another, rescued the officers, and beat off the barbarians. Then, going last himself, he plunged into the marsh and struggled across, partly swimming, partly wading — but in the crossing he lost his shield. Caesar and his officers, who had seen the whole thing, came to meet him with shouts of joy and admiration. But the soldier, miserable and in tears, threw himself at Caesar's feet and begged forgiveness for letting go of his shield. And once in Africa, when Scipio captured one of Caesar's ships with Granius Petro aboard — he had just been appointed quaestor, a treasury officer — Scipio handed the other passengers over to his soldiers as plunder but offered the quaestor his life. Granius answered that Caesar's soldiers were not in the habit of taking mercy, but of giving it — and with those words he fell on his own sword and killed himself.

This appetite for honor, this passion for distinction, Caesar himself planted in his men and fed daily. He handed out money and honors without stinting, and made it plain that he was not piling up wealth from his wars for his own luxury or private pleasure: all of it was a public fund, held in trust to reward and encourage courage, and he counted every coin he gave a deserving soldier as an addition to his own riches. Beyond that, there was no danger he would not walk into willingly, no hardship he ever excused himself from. His contempt for danger did not much surprise his soldiers, because they knew how hungry he was for glory. What astonished them was his endurance of hardship — far beyond, it seemed, what his body should have been able to bear. For he was a slight man, with soft, white skin, subject to headaches and to epileptic fits, which are said to have first seized him at Corduba, in Spain. Yet he never used his frail constitution as an excuse for taking it easy. Instead he used war itself as his medicine — fighting off his ailments with relentless marches, plain food, night after night camping in the open, and constant punishing exercise, hardening his body against every attack. He usually slept in carriages or litters, putting even his rest to work. By day he had himself driven around to the forts, garrisons, and camps, with one servant riding beside him to take dictation as he traveled and a single soldier standing behind him with a drawn sword. And he drove hard: on his first departure from Rome, he reached the river Rhone in eight days. Riding had been easy for him since childhood — he used to sit with his hands clasped behind his back and put his horse to a full gallop. In this war he trained himself further still, until he could dictate letters from horseback and keep two secretaries busy at once — or, as Oppius says, even more. It is thought he was also the first to work out a way of communicating with friends in cipher, when the press of business, or the sheer size of the city, left no time to meet face to face on urgent matters. As for how little he cared about his food, one story tells it all. At Milan his host Valerius Leo served him a dinner at which the asparagus came dressed not with olive oil but with sweet perfumed ointment. Caesar ate it without a flicker of disgust — and scolded his friends for complaining. "It was enough not to eat it if you didn't like it," he said. "But a man who points out another man's bad manners shows a shortage of his own." Another time a storm drove him and his companions to shelter in a poor man's cottage on the road. Finding it held a single room, and that one barely fit to lodge one person, he told his friends that places of honor should go to the greater men, but necessities to the weaker — and so he ordered that Oppius, who was in poor health, should sleep inside, while he and the rest bedded down under a shed by the door.

His first war in Gaul was against the Helvetii and the Tigurini, who had burned their own twelve towns and four hundred villages and set out to march through the part of Gaul that belonged to the Roman province, just as the Cimbri and Teutones had done before them. They were not inferior to those invaders in courage, and in numbers they were their equals: three hundred thousand in all, of whom one hundred and ninety thousand were fighting men. The Tigurini Caesar did not engage in person; Labienus, acting under his orders, routed them near the river Arar. But the Helvetii caught Caesar by surprise, falling on him unexpectedly as he was marching his army toward an allied town. He managed to pull back to a strong position, and there, once he had mustered and drawn up his men, his horse was brought to him. "When I've won the battle," he said, "I'll use my horse for the chase. For now — let's go at the enemy." And he charged them on foot. After a long, hard fight he drove their main army off the field, but the toughest work came at their wagons and ramparts, where not only the men stood and fought but the women and children defended themselves too, until they were cut to pieces; the fighting was barely finished by midnight. To this victory Caesar added something finer still. He gathered up all the barbarians who had escaped the battle — more than one hundred thousand of them — and compelled them to return to the country they had abandoned and the cities they had burned. He did this because he feared that if the land lay empty, the Germans would cross over and take it.

His second war he fought in defense of the Gauls against the Germans — even though, not long before, he had himself arranged for their king Ariovistus to be recognized at Rome as an ally. But the Germans had become insufferable neighbors to the peoples under his government, and it was obvious that at the first opportunity they would tear up the existing arrangements and march in to seize Gaul. Caesar found his officers frightened, though — especially the young nobles who had come along on the campaign imagining it would be a road to pleasure and profit. So he called them together and told them to march off home: there was no need for men with such weak and unmanly feelings to risk a battle against their inclinations. "I will take the tenth legion alone," he said, "and march against the barbarians. I don't expect these enemies to prove more formidable than the Cimbri — and I don't think you will find me a general inferior to Marius." At this, the tenth legion sent a deputation to thank him, while the other legions turned on their own officers in shame, and the whole army, full of fire and eagerness now, followed him for many days' march, until they camped about twenty-five miles from the enemy. Their mere approach took some of the fight out of Ariovistus. He had never expected the Romans to attack the Germans — he had assumed they would not dare stand against them even in defense of their own subjects — and Caesar's boldness stunned him; he could see, too, that his own army was rattled. The Germans were discouraged even more by the prophecies of their holy women, who read the future in the eddies of rivers and took signs from the windings and murmur of streams, and who now warned them not to fight before the new moon appeared. Caesar got word of this, and seeing the Germans sitting idle, he judged it far better to attack them while they were unnerved than to wait around for their chosen moment. So he pressed assaults on the strongholds and hills where they lay camped, and stung and goaded them until at last they came storming down in a fury to fight. He won a crushing victory, and pursued them fifty miles, all the way to the Rhine, the whole distance strewn with spoils and the bodies of the dead. Ariovistus with a small remnant managed to get across the Rhine; the dead are said to have numbered eighty thousand.

After this campaign, Caesar left his army in winter quarters in the country of the Sequani and, in order to keep a hand on affairs at Rome, came down into the part of Gaul that lies along the Po, which belonged to his province — for the river Rubicon divides Gaul on this side of the Alps from the rest of Italy. There he settled in and worked at courting popularity. Crowds came to him constantly, and every man got what he asked for; no one left without something in hand and greater hopes for the future. And through this whole period of the Gallic war, Pompey never noticed what was happening: that Caesar was using the arms of Rome to conquer Gaul, and using the wealth of those conquests to buy Rome. But when Caesar heard that the Belgae — the most powerful of all the Gauls, holding a third of the country — had revolted and assembled many thousands of men in arms, he turned back immediately and moved with tremendous speed. Falling on the enemy as they plundered the lands of Rome's Gallic allies, he quickly shattered and scattered the largest and most concentrated body of them. Their numbers were huge, but they fought so feebly that the Roman infantry crossed marshes and deep rivers on the sheer mass of the dead. After that, all the rebel tribes along the ocean submitted without a fight, and Caesar led his army against the Nervii, the fiercest and most warlike people in that whole region. The Nervii live in a country of dense unbroken woods. Having stowed their families and property deep in the forest, out of harm's way, they fell on Caesar with sixty thousand men while he was still building his camp, before he was ready for them. They routed his cavalry, surrounded the twelfth and seventh legions, and killed every one of the officers. If Caesar had not snatched up a shield himself, forced his way through the press of his own men, and thrown himself at the barbarians — and if the tenth legion, seeing his danger from the hilltops where they were posted, had not come running down and smashed through the enemy ranks to reach him — in all likelihood not one Roman would have survived. As it was, with Caesar's boldness in front of them, the men fought, as the saying goes, beyond human courage. Even so they could not drive the Nervii from the field; they cut them down where they stood, still fighting. Of sixty thousand men, it is said that no more than five hundred survived the battle — and of their four hundred senators, no more than three.

When news of this reached Rome, the senate voted sacrifices and festivals to the gods, to be strictly observed for fifteen days — a longer celebration than any victory had ever earned before. The danger had been real, they felt: so many nations rising at once. And because Caesar was the people's favorite, his success shone all the brighter. He himself, after settling everything in Gaul, came back and again spent the winter in the country along the Po, working at his designs on Rome. Candidates for office lined up for his help and drew on his money to bribe the people and buy votes, and once elected, they did everything they could to increase his power. More significant still, the most eminent and powerful men in Rome came to visit him at Lucca — Pompey, and Crassus, and Appius the governor of Sardinia, and Nepos the proconsul of Spain — until there were one hundred and twenty lictors in the town at one time, and more than two hundred senators. In the conference held there, it was settled that Pompey and Crassus should be consuls again the following year, and that Caesar should have a fresh grant of money and his command renewed for five more years. Thinking people found the arrangement grotesque: the very men who had taken so much money from Caesar were now urging the senate to give him more, as if he were poor. Though in truth "urging" is the wrong word — it was compulsion, and the senators groaned over their own votes as they passed the measures. Cato was not there; they had taken care to ship him off to Cyprus in good time. But Favonius, a devoted imitator of Cato, finding his opposition useless, burst out of the senate house and loudly denounced the whole business to the people. No one listened. Some brushed him off out of deference to Crassus and Pompey; most did it to please Caesar, on whom all their hopes now hung.

Caesar then returned to his forces in Gaul, where he found the country in the grip of a dangerous war: two powerful German nations, the Usipetes and the Tencteri, had just crossed the Rhine to conquer land. Of the war with them, Caesar's own account in his commentaries is this: the barbarians sent ambassadors to negotiate with him, and then, in the middle of the truce, attacked him on the march — which is how eight hundred of their horsemen routed five thousand of his cavalry, who suspected nothing. They then sent a second embassy to try the same trick again; Caesar held the ambassadors in custody and led his army against the barbarians, judging it plain foolishness to keep faith with men who had so faithlessly broken their own agreements. But Tanusius adds this: when the senate was decreeing festivals and sacrifices for the victory, Cato rose and declared that in his opinion Caesar ought to be handed over to the barbarians — so that the guilt of his broken faith might be purged from the city and the curse carried onto the one man responsible for it. Of the Germans who had crossed the Rhine, four hundred thousand were cut down; the few who escaped found shelter with the Sugambri, a people of Germany. Caesar seized on this as his pretext to invade the Germans — and besides, he coveted the honor of being the first man ever to cross the Rhine with an army. He began throwing a bridge across it, though the river was very wide, and at that point especially the current ran full, strong, and violent, driving down tree trunks and debris that battered the bridge's foundations. But Caesar drove great piles of timber into the riverbed upstream of the crossing to catch and hold whatever came floating down — and so, setting a bridle on the stream itself, he brought his bridge to completion. It was a sight no one who saw it could believe was the work of just ten days.
