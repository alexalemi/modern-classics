Cicero

(Part 3 of 4)

When Caesar brought forward a law to divide the farmland of Campania among his soldiers, many senators opposed it. One of the oldest men in the house, Lucius Gellius, declared that it would never pass while he lived. "Then let's postpone it," said Cicero. "Gellius isn't asking us to wait long." There was a man named Octavius, rumored to be of African descent. When he complained during one of Cicero's pleadings that he couldn't hear him, Cicero answered, "Strange — you have holes in your ears." When Metellus Nepos told him he had ruined more men as a witness than he had ever saved as an advocate, Cicero agreed: "I admit it. I have more truth in me than eloquence." A young man suspected of serving his father a poisoned cake was talking loudly about the abuse he planned to heap on Cicero. "Better that," Cicero replied, "than one of your cakes." Publius Sextius hired Cicero as one of his advocates in a case, then insisted on doing all the talking himself and wouldn't let anyone else get in a word. When it was clear the judges were about to acquit him and the ballots were already being cast, Cicero called out, "Speak fast, Sextius — use your time. Tomorrow you'll be a nobody again." He once summoned Publius Cotta as a witness — a man who liked to pass for a lawyer, though he was ignorant and unread. When Cotta answered, "I know nothing about the matter," Cicero said, "You think, perhaps, that we're asking you about a point of law." When Metellus Nepos, in the middle of a quarrel, kept demanding, "Who was your father, Cicero?" he shot back, "In your case, your mother has made that question much harder to answer" — for Nepos's mother had a scandalous reputation. The son was cut from flighty cloth himself. At one point he abruptly threw up his office of tribune and sailed off to Syria to join Pompey — then sailed straight back again, with just as little reason. He buried his tutor Philagrus with far more ceremony than the occasion called for, and set a stone crow on top of the tomb. "That, at least, is fitting," said Cicero. "The man didn't teach you to speak — he taught you to fly around." And when Marcus Appius opened a courtroom speech by saying that his friend had begged him to bring industry, eloquence, and loyalty to the case, Cicero asked, "Then how did you have the heart to supply not one of the three?"

Sharp needling of opponents and rival advocates counts as fair courtroom rhetoric. But Cicero would attack anyone at all for the sake of a joke, and that earned him real hatred. A few samples will show the habit. Marcus Aquinius, who had two sons-in-law in exile, was dubbed King Adrastus, after the legendary king whose daughters likewise both married exiles. Lucius Cotta, a devoted lover of wine, happened to be censor when Cicero was running for the consulship. Cicero, thirsty at the election, took a drink of water while his friends crowded around to screen him. "You're right to be worried," he told them. "The censor might be angry with me — for drinking water." Meeting Voconius one day with his three strikingly ugly daughters, he quoted the verse:

  He raised a family without Apollo's blessing.

When Marcus Gellius, who was reputed to be the son of a slave, read out some letters in the senate in a piercing, carrying voice, Cicero remarked, "Don't be surprised. He comes from a family of criers." And when Faustus Sulla, son of Sulla the dictator — the man whose posted bills had proscribed and condemned so many citizens during his dictatorship — had so wasted his estate and sunk into debt that he was forced to post bills of his own advertising his goods for sale, Cicero observed that he liked the son's bills much better than the father's. Habits like these made him hateful to many people.

And it was over just such grievances that Clodius's faction formed against him. Here is how it began. Clodius was a man of noble family, in the flower of his youth, bold and headstrong. Being in love with Pompeia, Caesar's wife, he slipped secretly into Caesar's house dressed as a music-girl. The women were celebrating there the sacrifice of Bona, the Good Goddess — the rite no man is permitted to see — and there was no man in the house. Clodius, still young and beardless, hoped to pass among the women and reach Pompeia unnoticed. But finding himself inside a great house at night, he lost his way in the corridors, and a maidservant of Aurelia, Caesar's mother, spotted him wandering about and asked his name. Forced to speak, he said he was looking for one of Pompeia's maids, a girl named Abra — and the servant, hearing that this was no woman's voice, shrieked and called the women together. They barred the gates and searched every corner, and at last found Clodius hiding in the room of the very maid who had let him in. The scandal ran through the whole city. Caesar divorced Pompeia, and Clodius was prosecuted for profaning the sacred rites.

Cicero at this time was Clodius's friend, for during the Catiline conspiracy Clodius had been one of his most energetic supporters and protectors. But when Clodius rested his defense on the claim that he had not even been in Rome that day, but far away in the country, Cicero testified that Clodius had come to his own house that very day and talked with him about a number of matters. Which was true — though people believed Cicero testified not so much for the truth's sake as to keep the peace with his wife Terentia. She bore a grudge against Clodius on account of his sister Clodia, who, it was alleged, wanted to marry Cicero and had been working at it through Tullus, one of Cicero's closest friends. Cicero's frequent visits and attentions to Clodia, who lived in their neighborhood, had roused Terentia's suspicions; and being a woman of fierce temper who held the upper hand over Cicero, she pushed him into taking sides against Clodius and giving his testimony. Many other good and honest citizens gave evidence against Clodius as well — for perjuries, for public disorders, for bribing the people, for seducing women. Lucullus produced women-servants who testified that Clodius had slept with his own youngest sister while she was Lucullus's wife; and it was widely believed he had done the same with his two other sisters — Tertia, married to Marcius Rex, and Clodia, married to Metellus Celer. This Clodia was nicknamed Quadrantia, because one of her lovers had slipped her a purse of small copper coins instead of silver, and the smallest copper coin was called a quadrant. It was on this sister's account, above all, that Clodius's name became a byword. Yet for all this, when the common people massed against the accusers and witnesses and the whole prosecution, the judges panicked. A guard had to be posted around them, and most of them scrawled their ballots so that no one could read the letters. The count showed a majority for acquittal, and bribery was said to have done the work. Catulus, when he next met the judges, remarked, "You were quite right to ask for a guard — someone might have taken your money." And when Clodius taunted Cicero that the judges hadn't believed his testimony, Cicero answered, "On the contrary. Twenty-five of them believed me — they voted against you. And the other thirty didn't believe you, because they wouldn't vote to acquit until they had your money in hand."

Caesar, though summoned, gave no testimony against Clodius. He declared that he was not convinced of his wife's adultery, but that he had put her away because Caesar's house had to be clear not only of guilt but of rumor. "Because I wished my wife," Caesar said, "to be not so much as suspected."

Clodius escaped the danger, got himself elected tribune, and immediately turned on Cicero, piling up every charge and stirring up every person he could against him. He won over the common people with crowd-pleasing laws; he decreed rich provinces to each of the consuls — Macedonia to Piso, Syria to Gabinius; he organized the poorest citizens into a standing party for his purposes, and kept a bodyguard of armed slaves around him. Of the three men then holding the greatest power, Crassus was Cicero's open enemy, Pompey played coy with both sides, and Caesar was about to march for Gaul with an army. It was to Caesar — no friend of his, for the suspicions born in the days of the conspiracy still stood between them — that Cicero applied, asking to go along as one of his lieutenants in the province. Caesar accepted him. But Clodius, seeing that Cicero was about to slip beyond the reach of his power as tribune, put on a show of wanting reconciliation. He laid the whole blame on Terentia, spoke of Cicero always in generous terms, and addressed him kindly, as a man who felt no hatred — only a few complaints to raise, in a moderate, friendly way. He so completely talked Cicero out of his fears that Cicero resigned his post with Caesar and returned to public life. At this Caesar was exasperated. He joined Clodius's party against him, turned Pompey away from him entirely, and declared before a public assembly that in his own view the execution of Lentulus and Cethegus and their accomplices without trial had been neither fair nor legal. For this was the charge against Cicero, and this was the accusation he was summoned to answer. So, as a defendant in danger of the verdict, he changed into mourning clothes and went about with his hair untrimmed, in the dress of a suppliant, begging the people for mercy. But Clodius met him at every corner with a gang of insolent ruffians who jeered at his mourning dress and his humiliation, and often broke up his appeals to the people by pelting him with mud and stones.

Even so, nearly the whole equestrian order changed into mourning with him, and no fewer than twenty thousand young men followed him about with untrimmed hair, joining his supplications to the people. Then the senate met to decree that the people should put on mourning as in a time of public grief. When the consuls opposed it, and Clodius surrounded the senate-house with armed men, many senators ran out into the open, crying aloud and tearing their clothes. But even that sight stirred neither shame nor pity. Cicero had to flee, or settle it with Clodius by the sword. He begged Pompey for help — but Pompey had deliberately gotten out of the way and was staying at his country house in the Alban hills. First Cicero sent Pompey's son-in-law Piso to intercede; then he set out to go himself. Pompey, hearing of it, could not bring himself to face the man. He was ashamed, remembering the great battles Cicero had fought for his sake in the commonwealth and all the policy he had shaped for his advantage; but he was Caesar's son-in-law now, and at Caesar's bidding he had set aside every old kindness. He slipped out by another door and dodged the interview. Deserted by Pompey and left standing alone, Cicero turned to the consuls. Gabinius was harsh with him, as usual. Piso spoke more gently: give way, he urged, step aside for a while before the fury of Clodius, wait for the times to change — and be, once again, his country's savior from the storms Clodius was raising.

Cicero took this answer and consulted his friends. Lucullus advised him to stay, certain he would win in the end; others said to flee, because the people would want him back soon enough, once they had had their fill of Clodius's rage and madness. Cicero chose flight. But first he took a statue of Minerva that had long stood honored in his house, carried it up to the capitol, and dedicated it there with the inscription, "To Minerva, Patroness of Rome." Then, with an escort of friends, he left the city in the middle of the night and traveled by land through Lucania, making for Sicily.

The moment his flight became public, Clodius put a decree of exile to the vote and posted an edict barring him from fire and water — the Roman formula of outlawry — and forbidding anyone within five hundred miles of Italy to take him under a roof. Out of respect for Cicero, most people ignored the edict entirely, showing him every courtesy and escorting him along his way. But at Hipponium, a city of Lucania now called Vibo, a certain Vibius — a Sicilian by birth who owed Cicero many favors, among them an appointment as head of the state engineers during his consulship — refused to receive him into his house, sending word instead that he would assign him a spot somewhere in the countryside. And Gaius Vergilius, the praetor of Sicily, who had been on the closest terms with him, wrote telling him to stay out of Sicily. Disheartened by these snubs, Cicero went to Brundisium and put out with a fair wind — but a gale blowing in from the sea carried him back to Italy the next day. He put to sea again and reached Dyrrachium, and it is reported that just as he came ashore, an earthquake struck and the sea convulsed at the same moment — signs, the diviners said, that his exile would not last long, for such things foretell change. Many people visited him out of respect, and the cities of Greece competed over which could honor him most; yet he stayed dejected and inconsolable, gazing back toward Italy like a heartbroken lover. The misfortune had left him so small-spirited, so humbled and crushed, as no one would have expected in a man who had spent so much of his life with study and philosophy. He had often asked his friends, after all, to call him not an orator but a philosopher — philosophy, he said, was his real business; rhetoric was merely the tool he used to get things done in public life. But the hunger for glory has great power to wash the tinctures of philosophy out of a man's soul. Spend enough time governing the crowd and you catch the crowd's passions, by sheer custom and contact — unless a politician engages with public business very carefully indeed, taking on the problems themselves without taking on the passions that ride along with them.

Clodius, having driven Cicero out, burned his farms and country villas, and then his city house, and built a temple to Liberty on the empty site. The rest of his property he put up for auction, advertising it day after day; nobody came to buy. All this made him terrifying to the nobles, and with the common people — whom he had filled with insolence and license — trailing behind him, he finally began testing his strength against Pompey himself, attacking some of the settlements Pompey had made in his conquered territories. The disgrace of this stung Pompey into reproaching himself for his cowardice in abandoning Cicero, and, reversing course, he set himself and his friends to work for a recall. When Clodius blocked it, the senate voted to ratify no public business of any kind until Cicero was brought home. In the consulship of Lentulus the violence over the question rose so high that tribunes were wounded in the Forum, and Cicero's brother Quintus was left for dead, lying unnoticed among the corpses. At that the people's mood began to turn. Annius Milo, one of the tribunes, was the first to find the nerve to summon Clodius to trial for violence. Crowds of ordinary citizens, from Rome and the neighboring towns, formed up behind Pompey, and with them at his back he drove Clodius out of the Forum and called the people to vote. It is said the people never passed any measure more unanimously. The senate, competing to outdo the people, sent letters of thanks to the cities that had cared for Cicero in his exile, and decreed that his house and his country estates, which Clodius had destroyed, should be rebuilt at public expense.

So Cicero came home, sixteen months after his exile began. The cities were so joyful, and people so eager to meet him, that his own boast afterward — that Italy had carried him home to Rome on her shoulders — actually fell short of the truth. Even Crassus, his enemy before the exile, went out to meet him voluntarily and made peace — to please his son Publius, as he said, who was one of Cicero's devoted admirers.

Cicero had not been back long when, seizing a day Clodius was out of town, he went up to the capitol with a large following and tore down and destroyed the tribunes' tablets, on which the official acts of Clodius's term were recorded. When Clodius called him to account for it, Cicero answered that Clodius, a patrician, had gotten himself made tribune against the law, so that nothing done in his term was valid. Cato was displeased at this and spoke against Cicero — not that he had any praise for Clodius; he detested the man's whole administration — but he argued that it was an irregular and violent step for the senate to vote so many decrees and acts null, including the acts of Cato's own commission in Cyprus and at Byzantium. The clash opened a breach between Cato and Cicero. It never came to open enmity, but their friendship was cooler and more guarded afterward.

After this, Milo killed Clodius. Arraigned for the murder, he secured Cicero as his advocate. The senate, fearing that the trial of a man as prominent and hot-spirited as Milo might set off riots in the city, gave Pompey the oversight of this and the other trials, to keep both the city and the courts secure. Pompey moved in the night before, posting soldiers on the high ground and ringing the whole Forum with troops. Milo, worried that so unusual a sight would rattle Cicero and hurt his performance, persuaded him to be carried into the Forum in a covered litter and to wait there quietly until the judges were seated and the court was full. For Cicero, it seems, was timid not only in arms but in speaking too: he began nearly every speech in a fright, and in many cases he had barely stopped shaking and trembling by the time his oratory hit its full current and force. Once, defending Licinius Murena against Cato's prosecution and burning to outdo Hortensius, who had made his argument to great applause, he took so little rest the night before, and wore himself so ragged with worry and lying awake, that he spoke well below his usual level. So now, at the trial of Milo, when he stepped out of his litter and saw Pompey posted on the heights as if encamped for war, and arms glittering all around the Forum, he fell apart. He could barely begin his speech; his body shook and his voice caught — while Milo himself sat through the proceedings bold and fearless, having disdained to grow his hair out or put on mourning. That bearing, in fact, seems to have contributed as much as anything to his condemnation. Still, in Cicero's case, people judged it less cowardice for himself than sheer anxiety for his friend.

He was made one of the priests the Romans call augurs, taking the place of the younger Crassus, who had died in Parthia. Then the lot assigned him the province of Cilicia, and he sailed out to it with twelve thousand infantry and two thousand six hundred cavalry. His orders included restoring Cappadocia to the allegiance of its king, Ariobarzanes — which he settled completely without lifting a weapon. Seeing that the Cilicians, watching the great Roman losses in Parthia and the upheavals in Syria, were leaning toward revolt, he steadied them back into loyalty by governing gently. He would accept none of the presents the local kings offered him. He canceled the public banquets the province owed him, and instead entertained its educated and accomplished men daily at his own table — not lavishly, but generously. His house had no doorkeeper, and no visitor ever found him in bed: from early morning he stood or walked in front of his door, receiving whoever came to pay respects. He is said never once to have ordered a man under his authority beaten with rods or stripped of his garments; he never hurled insults in anger, never attached humiliation to punishment. He uncovered an embezzlement of public money on a large scale, and by recovering it relieved the cities of their burdens — while letting those who paid back what they had taken keep their full rights as citizens, with no further penalty. He got a taste of war, too: he defeated the bandits who infested Mount Amanus, and for it his army saluted him imperator. When Caecilius the orator asked him to send panthers from Cilicia for the shows in the theater at Rome, Cicero wrote back, in a spirit of self-congratulation, that there were no panthers left in Cilicia: they had all fled into Caria, indignant that in a province so entirely at peace, they were the only creatures anyone still hunted. Leaving his province, he touched at Rhodes and lingered a while at Athens, savoring the memory of his old studies. He called on the leading men of learning, saw his old friends and companions, and, after receiving from Greece the honors due to him, returned to Rome — where everything was now, as it were, in flames, tipping into civil war.

When the senate would have voted him a triumph, he said he would rather walk behind Caesar's triumphal chariot, if only terms could be made. Privately, he gave advice to both men — writing letter after letter to Caesar, entreating Pompey face to face — doing his best to soothe each of them and talk them back from the edge. But when the thing became incurable, and Caesar was marching on Rome, and Pompey did not dare stay but abandoned the city with many good men beside him, Cicero did not join the flight, and people counted him as siding with Caesar. It is quite clear he was torn, and swung painfully between the two. In his letters he writes, "Which way should I turn? Pompey has the honorable case for war; but Caesar has managed his affairs better, and is more able to protect himself and his friends. I know whom to flee — I just don't know whom to flee to." Then Trebatius, one of Caesar's friends, wrote to him: Caesar's view was that Cicero's best course was to join his side and share his hopes — but if he thought himself too old for that, he should go to Greece and sit quietly there, out of everyone's way. Cicero, offended that Caesar had not written himself, sent back an angry answer: he would do nothing unworthy of his past life. That, at least, is what can be gathered from his letters.

But the moment Caesar marched for Spain, Cicero sailed off to join Pompey. Everyone welcomed him — except Cato, who took him aside privately and scolded him for coming. For his own part, Cato said, it would have been dishonorable to abandon the side he had chosen in politics from the beginning; but Cicero could have been more useful to his country and his friends if he had stayed neutral at home and used his influence to soften whatever came, instead of sailing out here to make himself Caesar's enemy — without reason, without necessity — and a partner in enormous danger. The argument shook Cicero's resolve; so did the fact that Pompey made no real use of him. Though, in truth, he had himself to blame for that. He didn't deny that he was sorry he had come. He belittled Pompey's resources, sniped behind hands at his plans, and could not stop cracking jokes at the expense of his fellow soldiers. He went around the camp wearing a gloomy, sour face himself, yet was forever raising laughs in others whether they wanted to laugh or not. A few examples are worth giving. When Domitius promoted a man with no soldiering in him and defended the choice by saying he was modest and prudent, Cicero asked, "Then why didn't you keep him to tutor your children?" Hearing people praise Theophanes the Lesbian, the army's chief of engineers, for how admirably he had consoled the Rhodians on the loss of their fleet, he said, "What a thing it is to have a Greek in command!" When Caesar was getting the better of it and all but had Pompey under siege, Lentulus remarked that he'd heard Caesar's friends were downcast. "You mean," said Cicero, "that they wish Caesar ill." To a man named Marcius, newly arrived from Italy with word that a rumor was running around Rome that Pompey was blockaded, he said, "And you sailed all the way out here to see it with your own eyes?" After one defeat, Nonius tried to raise spirits by pointing out that seven eagles still remained in Pompey's camp. "That would be very encouraging," said Cicero, "if we were fighting jackdaws." When Labienus kept insisting on certain prophecies that promised Pompey the victory, Cicero said, "Yes — and by that strategy we have opened the campaign by losing our camp."
