Cato the Younger

(Part 5 of 6)

Caesar, all this while, was off fighting warlike nations and subduing them at great risk to himself. Among other things, it was widely believed that he had attacked the Germans during a truce and slaughtered three hundred thousand of them. Some of his friends moved that the senate declare a public thanksgiving for the victory. Cato's counter-proposal was that they hand Caesar over to the people he had wronged, and so wash away the offense rather than bring its curse down on the city. "Still, we do have reason to thank the gods," he added, "for sparing the commonwealth — for not taking vengeance on the army for the madness and folly of its general." Caesar answered with a letter to the senate, which was read aloud, and which was packed with insults and accusations against Cato. Cato stood up looking entirely unconcerned. Without heat, without passion — in a calm and, you might say, rehearsed manner — he made all of Caesar's charges sound like common scolding and abuse, a kind of joke Caesar was amusing himself with. Then he got down to business. He went through Caesar's whole political career, explaining and exposing his conduct and his purpose from the very beginning — not like a lifelong opponent, but like a fellow conspirator letting out the secrets — and he closed by telling the senators that it was not the sons of the Britons or the Gauls they needed to fear, but Caesar himself, if they had any sense. The speech so stirred and awakened the senate that Caesar's friends were sorry they had ever had the letter read, since it had handed Cato the opportunity to say so many reasonable things and so many hard truths. Yet nothing was actually decided. The only suggestion made was that it might be well to send Caesar a successor. At that, Caesar's friends demanded that Pompey also lay down his arms and give up his provinces — or else Caesar should not be required to do either. Now, Cato cried out, exactly what he had foretold had come to pass: it was plain that Caesar was using his army to force their judgment, turning against the state the very forces he had wheedled out of it by imposture and trickery. But outside the Senate house Cato could accomplish little. The common people were always eager to build Caesar up, and the senate, though Cato had convinced it, was afraid of the people.

Then the news arrived that Caesar had seized Ariminum and was marching on Rome with his army — and suddenly everyone, Pompey and the common people included, turned their eyes to Cato, the one man who had seen Caesar's intentions from the start and said so plainly. "If you had believed me," he told them, "or taken my advice, you would not now be reduced to fearing one man — or to pinning all your hopes on one man either." Pompey admitted that Cato had spoken like a prophet, while he himself had acted too much like a friend. And Cato advised the senate to put everything into Pompey's hands. "Those who can raise up great evils," he said, "are best at putting them down."

Pompey, finding his forces insufficient and the men he could raise halfhearted, abandoned the city. Cato, resolved to follow him into exile, sent his younger son to Munatius, who was then in Bruttium, and took his eldest with him. But he needed someone to keep his house and look after his daughters, so he took Marcia back — she was a rich widow now, for Hortensius had died and left her his whole estate. Caesar later used this, too, as ammunition, charging Cato with greed and with trafficking in marriage for profit. "If he needed a wife," Caesar asked, "why did he give her away? And if he didn't, why did he take her back? Unless he offered her to Hortensius as bait from the beginning — lent her out young to get her back rich." To that, the fair answer is the line from Euripides:

  Some things are past discussing; and the first
  is calling Hercules a coward.

Charging Cato with greed is about as sensible as charging Hercules with cowardice. Whether the marriage itself was entirely rightly handled is another question, and open to dispute. In any case, as soon as he had taken Marcia back, he put his house and his daughters in her charge, and himself followed Pompey. And it is said that from that day forward he never cut his hair or shaved his beard or wore a garland, but carried the same sadness, grief, and dejection over his country's calamities to the very end, whichever side happened to be winning or losing.

The government of Sicily fell to him by lot, and he crossed over to Syracuse. Learning that Asinius Pollio had landed at Messena with a force from the enemy, Cato sent to him demanding the reason for his coming; Pollio replied by demanding that Cato explain the reason for the whole upheaval of the times. And when word came at the same moment that Pompey had abandoned Italy altogether and lay encamped at Dyrrhachium, Cato remarked on how strange and unfathomable the divine government of the world is: "When Pompey did nothing wisely or honestly, he was always successful; and now that he wants to save his country and defend her liberty, nothing goes right for him." As for Asinius, Cato said he could drive him out of Sicily easily enough — but larger forces were on their way to reinforce him, and Cato would not plunge the island into war. So he advised the Syracusans to join the winning side and look to their own safety, and sailed away.

When he reached Pompey, his advice never varied: drag the war out. He always hoped matters could still be settled, and he had no wish to see things come to battle, for the commonwealth would suffer terribly and would be the author of its own ruin no matter whose sword won. In the same spirit he persuaded Pompey and his council to decree that no city subject to Rome should be sacked, and that no Roman should be killed except in the heat of battle. This won him great honor, and it drew many people over to Pompey's side, attracted by such moderation and humanity. Afterward he was sent into Asia, to help those who were raising men and fitting out ships there, and he took along his sister Servilia and the little boy she had borne to Lucullus. Since her widowhood she had lived with her brother, and she had recovered much of her reputation by placing herself under his care, following him on his voyages, and accepting his severe way of life. Caesar, naturally, did not fail to sling mud at Cato on her account as well.

As it turned out, Pompey's officers in Asia had little need of Cato; but he did talk the people of Rhodes over to the cause, and, leaving Servilia and her child on the island, he returned to Pompey — who by now had assembled very great forces by land and sea. And here, more than in any other single act, Pompey betrayed what he was really thinking. His first intention was to give Cato command of the navy, which counted no fewer than five hundred warships, besides a huge number of light galleys, scouts, and open boats. But then it occurred to him — or his friends pointed out — that Cato's one guiding aim was to free his country from all usurpation, and that if Cato held forces on that scale, the moment Caesar was beaten he would call on Pompey, too, to lay down his arms and submit to the laws. So Pompey changed his mind, though he had already mentioned the appointment to Cato, and made Bibulus admiral instead. For all that, he had no reason to think Cato's zeal for the cause had cooled. Before one of the battles at Dyrrhachium, we are told, Pompey himself addressed the soldiers and ordered his officers to do the same, and the men listened coldly, in silence — until Cato, last of all, came forward and spoke to them as a philosopher would, saying what the occasion demanded about liberty, and manly virtue, and death, and a good name. He delivered it all with strong natural feeling, and he finished by calling on the gods directly, addressing them as though they stood present to watch men fight for their country. At that the army raised such a shout, and showed such fire, that the officers led them into the danger full of hope and confidence. Caesar's men were routed and driven off — but Caesar's presiding fortune used Pompey's caution and self-distrust to leave the victory incomplete. That story, though, has been told in the life of Pompey. And while everyone else was celebrating and glorying in the success, Cato alone turned away, covered his face, and wept for his country, cursing the fatal ambition that had set so many brave Romans to murdering one another.

After this, when Pompey followed Caesar into Thessaly, he left behind at Dyrrhachium a mass of munitions, money, and stores, along with many of his own household and relations, and put the whole charge of it in Cato's hands — with a command of only fifteen cohorts. He trusted Cato greatly, but he feared him too, knowing perfectly well that if things went badly, Cato would be the last man to desert him; but that if he conquered, Cato would never let him use the victory however he pleased. A good many men of high rank also stayed with Cato at Dyrrhachium. When they heard of the disaster at Pharsalia, Cato settled his course in his own mind: if Pompey were dead, he would lead the men in his charge back to Italy, and then withdraw as far from Caesar's tyranny as he could and live in exile; if Pompey were alive, he would hold the army together for him. On that resolution he crossed over to Corcyra, where the fleet lay, and there he offered to hand his command to Cicero, on the grounds that Cicero had been consul and he himself only a praetor. But Cicero refused it, and was setting out for Italy. Pompey's son, furious at this, was ready in his rashness and heat to punish everyone who was quitting the cause — starting by laying hands on Cicero. Cato spoke with the young man privately and talked him out of it. So he plainly saved Cicero's life, and rescued several others from rough treatment besides.

Guessing that Pompey the Great had fled toward Egypt or Africa, Cato resolved to hurry after him, and he put all his men aboard and set sail — but first he gave free leave to depart to everyone whose heart was not in continuing the fight. On the African coast they fell in with Sextus, Pompey's younger son, who told them of his father's death in Egypt. The grief was overwhelming, and to a man they declared that after Pompey they would follow no leader but Cato. Out of compassion, then, for so many worthy men who had given such proof of their loyalty — men he could not for shame abandon in a desert country in the middle of so many dangers — he took the command upon himself, and marched toward the city of Cyrene, which opened its gates to him at once, though not long before it had shut them against Labienus. There he learned that Scipio, Pompey's father-in-law, had been received by King Juba, and that Attius Varus, whom Pompey had made governor of Africa, had joined them with his forces. Cato resolved to march to them overland, though it was now winter. He collected a train of donkeys to carry water, laid in plenty of every other provision, and gathered a number of wagons. He also brought along some of the people called Psylli, who cure snakebite by sucking the venom out with their mouths, and who have charms besides, with which they drug the serpents into a stupor.

They marched seven days straight, Cato on foot the whole time at the head of his men, never once using a horse or a chariot. Ever since the battle of Pharsalia, moreover, he had taken his meals sitting upright — adding this to his other habits of mourning, that he never lay down except to sleep.

The winter passed in Africa, and Cato led out his army, which came to little short of ten thousand men. The affairs of Scipio and Varus were going badly, because of the quarrels and dissensions between them and their fawning submission to King Juba, whose vanity, and whose swollen pride in his own power and riches, made him insufferable. The first time he came to a conference with Cato, Juba had his own seat placed in the middle, between Scipio and Cato. Cato saw it, picked up his chair, and set himself down on the far side of Scipio — putting Scipio in the place of honor in the middle, though Scipio was his enemy and had once published a pamphlet full of slanders against him. There are people who treat that moment as a trifle, and who nonetheless criticize Cato because in Sicily one day, walking with Philostratus, he gave the philosopher the middle place, to show his respect for philosophy. However that may be, he now succeeded both in humbling Juba, who had been treating Scipio and Varus like a pair of satraps under his orders, and in reconciling the two Romans with each other. All the troops wanted Cato for their general; Scipio and Varus, too, gave way and offered him the command. But he said he would not break the very laws he was fighting to defend: he was only a propraetor, and a propraetor had no business commanding in the presence of a proconsul — for Scipio had been made proconsul. Besides, people took it as a good omen to see a Scipio in command in Africa, and the mere name put hope of victory into the soldiers.

No sooner had Scipio taken up the command than he resolved — Juba urging him on — to put the whole population of Utica to the sword and level the city, on the charge that it had taken Caesar's side. Cato would not stand for it. Invoking the gods, crying out and protesting in the council of war, he managed with great difficulty to save the poor people from that cruelty. Afterward, at the request of the inhabitants and at Scipio's own instance, Cato took over the government of Utica himself, so that the city should not fall into Caesar's hands one way or another — for it was a strong place, and valuable to either side. Under Cato it became stronger and better supplied still. He brought in vast stores of grain, repaired the walls, raised towers, and ran deep trenches and palisades around the town. The young men of Utica he quartered in these outworks, having first taken their weapons from them; the rest of the inhabitants he kept inside the town, and he took the greatest care that the Romans did them no injury and offered them no insult. From this base he sent great quantities of arms, money, and provisions to the camp, and the city became the war's chief magazine.

His advice to Scipio was the same he had once given Pompey: do not stake everything on a battle against a man experienced in war and formidable in the field, but play for time — for time gradually wears down the fury of a crisis, and fury is what usurpation lives on. Scipio, out of pride, rejected the counsel, and wrote Cato a letter taunting him with cowardice: apparently it was not enough for him to sit safe himself behind walls and trenches; he had to stop other men from boldly using their own good judgment to seize the right opportunity. Cato wrote back offering to take the horse and foot he had brought to Africa and cross into Italy, to make a diversion there and pull Caesar off after him. Scipio ridiculed that proposal too. Then Cato let it be seen openly that he was sorry he had yielded the command — sorry because Scipio would clearly not conduct the war with any wisdom, and because, if against all likelihood he somehow succeeded, he would use his success just as unjustly at home. Cato had made up his mind by then, and said as much to his friends: he could have only slender hopes of generals with so much boldness and so little judgment; and yet if anything happened beyond all expectation, and Caesar were overthrown, he for his part would not stay in Rome, but would get away from the cruelty and inhumanity of Scipio, who was already flinging fierce, arrogant threats at many men.

What Cato had been bracing for came sooner than he expected. Late one evening a man arrived from the army, three days on the road, with word that a great battle had been fought near Thapsus; that everything was utterly lost; that Caesar had taken the camps; that Scipio and Juba had escaped with only a handful, and all the rest of the army was destroyed. Arriving in wartime, and at night, news like that drove the people nearly out of their minds; they could scarcely be kept inside the city walls. But Cato came out to meet them in their uproar and confusion, and did everything he could to comfort and steady them, easing their terror and bewilderment a little by suggesting that things were probably not as bad as the reports made out — rumor always exaggerates. For the moment, the panic subsided. The next morning he sent for the three hundred men he used as his council — Romans who were in Africa on business, in trade and money-lending — along with the senators who were present, and their sons, summoning them all to the temple of Jupiter. While they were gathering, Cato walked about quietly, perfectly composed, as though nothing had happened, reading a book he carried in his hand. It was his inventory of war supplies: armor, grain, ammunition, soldiers.

When they had assembled, he began. First he addressed the three hundred themselves, praising warmly the courage and loyalty they had shown, and their good service to their country with their persons, their money, and their counsel. Then he begged them on no account to scatter, as if any man alone could find safety by deserting his companions. On the contrary: as long as they held together, Caesar would have less reason to despise them if they fought, and more readiness to pardon them if they submitted. So he urged them to deliberate among themselves, and said he would find no fault with either choice. If they decided to bow to fortune, he would put their change of course down to necessity. But if they resolved to stand firm and face the danger for liberty's sake, he would do more than commend them — he would admire their courage, and he would make himself their leader and their companion, until they had tried the last fortune of their country. And their country was not Utica or Adrumetum: it was Rome, which had often, by her own sheer greatness, lifted herself up from worse disasters than this. Besides, many things argued for their safety, and chiefly this: they would be fighting a man whose affairs were dragging him urgently in every direction at once. Spain had already gone over to the younger Pompey; Rome itself, unused to the bridle and chafing under it, was ready to rise at any turn of events. As for danger — they should not flinch from it, and they could take a lesson there from their enemy, who spent his own life so freely to accomplish the most unrighteous ends, but who could never hope for the ending they could promise themselves. For all the uncertainty of war, they were sure of one or the other: a most happy life if they won, or a most glorious death if they failed. Still, the decision was theirs to make among themselves, he said; and for his part he joined them in praying that the gods, in payment for their past courage and goodwill, would prosper whatever they now determined. When Cato finished, some of his hearers were moved and heartened by the arguments themselves; but the greater part, watching his fearlessness, his generosity, his goodness, all but forgot the danger they were in. Here, they felt, was the one leader no fortune could conquer; and they begged him to use their persons, their arms, and their estates however he saw fit — better to meet death following his counsel than to save their skins by betraying such virtue. Someone in the assembly proposed a decree freeing the slaves, and most of the rest approved the motion. Cato refused: it was neither just nor lawful, he said; but if individual masters chose to free their slaves willingly, those of military age should be accepted for service. Many promised to do so; he ordered their names enrolled, and withdrew.

Soon afterward letters reached him from Juba and Scipio. Juba, with a few of his men, had withdrawn to a mountain, and was waiting to hear what Cato would decide — intending to wait there for him if he chose to abandon Utica, or to march to his relief if he were besieged. Scipio was aboard ship off a promontory not far from Utica, waiting for an answer on the same question. Cato thought it best to hold the messengers until the three hundred should come to some resolution.

The senators, for their part, showed real spirit: they freed their slaves at once and armed them. But the three hundred were businessmen and moneylenders, with much of their wealth held in slaves, and the enthusiasm Cato's speech had kindled in them did not last long. Just as some substances take heat easily and lose it the moment the fire is removed, so these men glowed while Cato was in front of them; but when they began reasoning among themselves, their fear of Caesar soon overpowered their reverence for Cato and for virtue. "Who are we, after all," they said, "and who is it we are refusing to obey? Isn't this the Caesar who now holds the whole power of Rome? And which of us is a Scipio, a Pompey, a Cato? Now, when every man is letting fear outweigh honor, shall we of all people take up the fight for the liberty of Rome — shall we, in Utica, declare war on the man before whom Cato and Pompey the Great fled out of Italy? Shall we free our slaves to fight Caesar, when we ourselves have exactly as much liberty as he chooses to allow us? No — poor creatures that we are, let us know ourselves, submit to the victor, and send envoys to beg his mercy." That was the moderate faction talking. The greater part were for seizing the senators and holding them, hoping to appease Caesar's anger by handing him prisoners. Cato saw the change come over them, and gave no sign of it; but he wrote to Juba and Scipio to keep away from Utica, because he did not trust the three hundred.

Now a considerable body of cavalry that had escaped from the lost battle came riding toward Utica, and sent three men ahead to Cato — who did not, between them, carry a single coherent message, for one party in the squadrons was for going to Juba, another for joining Cato, and some were simply afraid to enter Utica at all. On hearing this, Cato ordered Marcus Rubrius to keep an eye on the three hundred — quietly taking down the names of those who freed their slaves of their own accord, and forcing no one. Then, taking the senators with him, he went out of the town to meet the cavalry's senior officers. He pleaded with them not to abandon so many Roman senators, and not to take Juba for their commander in place of Cato, but to think of the common safety and come into the city — a city impregnable, and stocked with grain and every other provision enough for years. The senators added their own pleas, with tears. The officers went off to consult their soldiers, and Cato sat down with the senators on an embankment to await the answer. At that moment Rubrius came up in great agitation, crying out that the three hundred were all in commotion, stirring up revolt and tumult in the city. At this the rest gave way to despair, weeping and lamenting their condition; Cato did what he could to comfort them, and sent word to the three hundred asking them to be patient. Then the cavalry officers came back with demands that were anything but reasonable. They said they did not want to serve Juba for his pay, and they would not fear Caesar so long as they followed Cato; but they dreaded being shut up inside with the men of Utica — a treacherous breed, they said, of Carthaginian blood; quiet enough for now, but certain, the moment Caesar appeared, to conspire together and betray the Romans. If Cato expected them to join him, he must first drive every Utican out of the town, or kill them, and receive his cavalry into a place cleansed of enemies and barbarians alike. Cato thought this utterly cruel and barbarous; but he answered mildly that he would consult the three hundred.
