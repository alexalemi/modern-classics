Cato the Younger

(Part 6 of 6)

Then he rode back into the city, where he found the three hundred no longer making excuses or hiding their feelings out of respect for him. Now they said it openly: no one was going to force them into a war against Caesar. They were not able to fight him, they said, and they were not willing. Some of them were even muttering about holding the senators in the city until Caesar arrived — a hostage offering. Cato acted as though he had not heard it; he had, in fairness, the excuse of being slightly deaf. At that moment a man came running to tell him the cavalry were riding away. Afraid that the three hundred, left to themselves, might do something desperate to the senators, Cato immediately went out with a few of his friends, and seeing that the horsemen had already gone some distance, he took a horse and rode after them. They were delighted when they saw him coming, welcomed him warmly, and begged him to save himself along with them. And it was then, they say, that Cato wept — pleading for the senators, stretching out his hands in supplication. He turned some of their horses around bodily; he grabbed hold of the men by their armor; until at last, out of sheer compassion, they gave in and agreed to stay one day — just one — to cover the senators' escape. So they rode back with him, and he posted some of them at the town gates and gave others charge of the citadel. Now it was the three hundred who grew frightened, sure they would be made to pay for their wavering, and they sent to Cato begging him to come to them by any means. But the senators crowded around him and refused to let him go. They would not, they said, hand their guardian and savior over to a pack of traitors.

There was never, perhaps, a moment when Cato's virtue showed more plainly. Every class of person in Utica could see it, with sorrow and with admiration: nothing he was doing carried the slightest trace of hidden motive or self-interest. Here was a man who had long since settled on his own death, taking infinite pains, labor, and care — entirely for the sake of others — so that once he had made their lives safe, he could put an end to his own. For it was easy enough to perceive that he had determined to die, though he never let it show.

So, after calming the senators, he did what the three hundred asked and went to them alone, without a single attendant. They thanked him over and over, and begged him to use them and trust them in whatever came next. If they were not Catos, they said — if they could not rise to his greatness of soul — then let him at least pity their weakness. They had decided to send to Caesar and plead with him, first and above all, for Cato; and if they could not win his case, they would not accept a pardon for themselves, but would fight in his defense as long as they had breath in their bodies. Cato thanked them for their good intentions and advised them to send quickly, for their own safety's sake — but to ask nothing whatsoever on his behalf. "Entreaties are for the conquered," he said, "and begging pardon is for those who have done wrong. As for me, I have never been beaten in my life. So far as I chose to be, I have been the winner — I have conquered Caesar on every point of justice and honesty. It is Caesar who has been caught and defeated. The designs against his country that he denied for so long — he now stands convicted of them all." With that he left the assembly. Told that Caesar was already approaching with his whole army, he said, "Ah — he expects to find us brave men." Then he went to the senators and urged them not to delay another hour, but to get away while the horsemen were still in the city. He ordered every gate shut except the one facing the sea, assigned ships to those who were leaving, and handed out money and provisions to those in need — all of it done with perfect order and exactness, keeping down every disturbance, making sure no one did the townspeople any wrong.

Marcus Octavius now arrived with two legions, camped near Utica, and sent to Cato to settle the question of who held supreme command. Cato sent him no answer at all; to his friends he said, "And we wonder why everything has gone to ruin — when even here, in the wreck itself, our love of office survives." Meanwhile word came that the cavalry, on their way out, had begun to loot and plunder the citizens. Cato ran straight at them. From the first man he reached, he snatched back what had been taken; the rest threw down their spoils and rode off in silence, ashamed of themselves. Then he called together the people of Utica and asked them, for the three hundred's sake, not to inflame Caesar against them, but to join with them in seeking safety for everyone together. After that he went down to the port again to watch over those who were embarking, and there he embraced the friends and companions he had persuaded to go, and sent them on their way. His own son he did not advise to leave; he did not think it was his place to make a son abandon his father. But there was a young man named Statyllius, in the very flower of his age, high-spirited and burning to imitate Cato's iron constancy. Cato pressed him to leave — he was a known enemy of Caesar — and got nowhere. So Cato turned to Apollonides the Stoic philosopher and Demetrius the Peripatetic. "This one is your job," he said. "Cool this young man's fever, and teach him what is good for him." And so, seeing his friends off and dispatching the business of everyone who came to him, he spent that night and the greater part of the following day.

Lucius Caesar, a kinsman of Caesar's, had been chosen to go as envoy for the three hundred, and he came to Cato asking for help in preparing a persuasive speech on their behalf. "And for you yourself," he added, "it will be an honor for me to kiss Caesar's hands and fall at his knees, pleading your cause." Cato would not hear of it. "If I were willing to be saved by Caesar's favor," he said, "I should go to him myself. But I will not be indebted to a tyrant for his acts of tyranny. And tyranny is what it is — it is usurpation for him to play the rightful lord, graciously sparing the lives of men he has no title to rule at all. But come — if you like, let us work out what you should say for the three hundred." They spent some time at it together, and as Lucius was leaving, Cato commended his son and the rest of his friends to his care, took his hand, and said goodbye.

Then he withdrew to his house again and gathered his son and his friends around him. He talked with them about many things — among the rest, he forbade his son ever to go into politics. To act in public life as a Cato should, he said, had become impossible; and to act any other way would be shameful. Toward evening he went to his bath. As he was bathing, he remembered Statyllius and called out loudly, "Apollonides! Have you tamed that high spirit of Statyllius'? Has he gone, without even saying goodbye to us?" "No," said Apollonides. "I have said a great deal, and it has done very little good. He is resolute and immovable. He declares he is determined to follow your example." At this, they say, Cato smiled, and answered, "That will soon be tried."

After his bath he went to supper with a large company. He sat upright at the table, as he had done ever since the battle of Pharsalia; from that day on he never lay down except to sleep. At supper with him were all his own circle and the magistrates of Utica.

After the meal, the wine brought on a great deal of lively, agreeable conversation, and one philosophical question after another went around the table. At length they came down to the strange Stoic doctrines known as the paradoxes — and to one in particular: that only the good man is free, and every wicked man is a slave. The Peripatetic, as you might expect, objected — and Cato came down on him hard. Raising his voice, he argued the point at astonishing length and with such passionate vehemence that it became obvious to everyone at the table: he had resolved to put an end to his life, and set himself free. When he finished speaking, there was a long silence, and every face fell. Cato saw it, and to draw them off from suspecting his design, he turned the conversation back to the present — worrying aloud over the men now at sea, and over the others who were traveling by land across a dry and barbarous desert.

When the company broke up, he took his usual after-supper walk with his friends, gave the necessary orders to the officers of the watch, and went to his chamber — but first he embraced his son, and then each of his friends, with more than his usual warmth, which set them all suspecting again. Then he lay down and took up Plato's dialogue on the soul — the Phaedo. He had read more than half the book when he glanced up and noticed his sword was missing; his son had carried it off while he was at supper. He called a servant and asked who had taken his sword. The servant said nothing, and Cato went back to his reading. A little later — not pressing, not seeming in any hurry, as if he merely wanted to know what had become of it — he told the man to bring it. He waited a while; he finished the whole book; still no one brought the sword. At that he called up all his servants and demanded it in a louder voice. One of them he struck across the mouth so hard that he bloodied his own hand — and now his anger rose in earnest, and he shouted that his son and his servants were betraying him, handing him over naked to the enemy. Then his son came running into the room with the rest of his friends, and they fell at his feet, weeping and begging. But Cato rose up and fixed them with a terrible look. "When did I lose my mind," he said, "and how, without my noticing? No one argues with me. No one shows me a better course, if my judgment is supposed to be so bad — I am simply to be disarmed, and prevented from using my own reason. Why don't you go further, young man, and tie your father's hands behind his back, so that when Caesar comes he'll find me unable to defend myself? To kill myself I need no sword. I need only hold my breath a while, or dash my head once against the wall."

At this his son went out of the chamber weeping, and all the rest with him, except Demetrius and Apollonides. Left alone with those two, Cato began to speak more calmly. "And you," he said. "Do you also propose to keep a man of my age alive by force — to sit here and watch me in silence? Or have you brought me arguments — some proof that it is not base and unworthy, when Cato can find safety nowhere else, to go begging for it from his enemy? If so, make your case. Show me why we should unlearn everything we were ever taught — why we should throw over every conviction we have lived by, so that Caesar can make us wiser, and we can owe him even more than our lives. Not that I have decided anything about myself. But when I do decide, I must have the power to carry out my own resolution. And I will decide it, be assured, in counsel with you — in counsel with the doctrines your own philosophy teaches. So set your minds at rest. And go tell my son that he should not try to compel his father where he cannot persuade him." They gave him no answer, but went out weeping. Then the sword was carried in by a small boy. Cato took it, drew it, and looked it over; and when he saw the point was true, he said, "The sword is now my own." He laid it down, picked up his book again, and — so it is related — read it through twice from the beginning. After that he slept so soundly that the men outside the door could hear him snoring.

About midnight he called up two of his freedmen — Cleanthes, his physician, and Butas, his chief agent in public business. Butas he sent down to the port, to see whether all his friends had gotten safely to sea. To the physician he gave his hand to be dressed, swollen as it was from the blow he had struck his servant. At this everyone in the house took heart, hoping it meant he intended to live.

After a while Butas returned with word that all had sailed except Crassus, who had been delayed by some business but was on the point of departing; the wind, he added, was high, and the sea running very rough. Cato sighed when he heard it, out of pity for the men on the water, and sent Butas down again — in case anyone had been driven back and needed something, he was to come and report it.

By now the birds were beginning to sing, and Cato drifted off into a short sleep. At last Butas came back and told him all was quiet in the port. Then Cato lay down as if to sleep out what remained of the night, and told him to shut the door behind him. But the moment Butas was gone, he took his sword and drove it into his chest. The swollen hand robbed the blow of its force, and he did not die of it at once; struggling, he fell from the bed, knocking over a small geometrical table that stood beside it, and the crash brought the servants crying out, and his son and all his friends running into the chamber. They found him lying in his blood, with a great part of his bowels forced out of the wound — but still alive, still able to look at them — and they stood frozen in horror. The physician went to him and, finding the bowels unpierced, tried to put them back and sew up the wound. But Cato came to himself, and understood what the man intended. He thrust the physician away, tore at his own bowels with his hands, ripped the wound wide — and died.

In less time than you would think his own household could have learned what had happened, the three hundred were at the door. A little later the people of Utica came flooding in, all crying out with one voice that he was their benefactor and their savior — the only free man, the only man undefeated. And this at the very hour word arrived that Caesar was coming. Yet neither fear of the danger at hand, nor any urge to flatter the conqueror, nor their own quarrels and divisions could turn them from honoring Cato. They laid out his body magnificently, gave him a splendid funeral, and buried him by the seashore — where a statue of him now stands, sword in hand. Only when all this was done did they turn to the business of saving themselves and their city.

Caesar had been told that Cato was staying in Utica and making no attempt to escape — that he had sent the other Romans away while he himself, with his son and a handful of friends, remained there entirely unconcerned — and he could not fathom what the man intended. Holding him in great consideration, he hurried toward the city with his army. When he heard of the death, this, it is related, is what he said: "Cato, I grudge you your death, as you grudged me the honor of saving your life." And in truth, had Cato allowed himself to owe his life to Caesar, he would not so much have tarnished his own honor as added to Caesar's glory. What would actually have happened, of course, we cannot know — though from Caesar's usual clemency, we may guess it would have been the kindest outcome.

Cato was forty-eight years old when he died. His son suffered no harm from Caesar; but he grew idle, it is said, and got a reputation for wasting himself on women. In Cappadocia he stayed at the house of Marphadates, a member of the royal family there who had a very beautiful wife; and he stretched the visit so far past what was decent that he became the butt of epigrams — such as this one:

  Tomorrow — the thirtieth day — they say,
  Cato at last will go away.

and this:

  Porcius and Marphadates, friends so true —
  one Soul, they say, suffices for the two.

For Soul — Psyche — was the wife's name. And again:

  To Cato's greatness all confess:
  a royal Soul he does possess.

But every one of these stains was wiped away by the bravery of his death. At Philippi, fighting for his country's liberty against Octavian and Antony, when the ranks were breaking, he scorned to run and scorned to slip away unnoticed. He shouted his defiance at the enemy, showed himself out in front where they could see him, rallied those of his side who stood their ground — and fell at last, leaving his enemies in awe of his courage.

Nor was Cato's daughter Portia one whit behind the rest of her family in sober living and greatness of spirit. She married Brutus, the man who killed Caesar; she was in on the conspiracy; and she ended her life in a manner worthy of her birth and her virtue. All of which is told in the life of Brutus.

And Statyllius — the young man who swore he would imitate Cato — was stopped by the philosophers at the time, when he wanted to end his life. Afterward he followed Brutus, served him with complete loyalty and great usefulness, and died on the field of Philippi.
