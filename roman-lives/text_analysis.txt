TEXT ANALYSIS — Roman Lives (Plutarch, c. 100 AD)
Source: the Dryden translation revised by Clough (1859), Gutenberg
#674. This Modern Classics volume selects FIVE lives — the fall of the
Roman Republic told through its principals, the arc the American
founders studied hardest: Caesar, Cato the Younger, Cicero, Brutus,
Antony. (Plutarch's Greek pairings and the Comparisons are omitted;
this is a Roman volume with one continuous story.)

WHAT THIS IS

Plutarch writes moral biography, not history: "the most glorious
exploits do not always furnish us with the clearest discoveries of
virtue or vice in men" — a jest, a phrase, a small incident often
reveals more than battles. Each life mixes childhood omens, anecdotes,
campaigns, and death scenes, always watching for character. The five
lives overlap heavily (the Catiline conspiracy, the civil war, the
Ides of March, Philippi, Actium each appear several times from
different angles) — that repetition is a FEATURE: the reader watches
the same events through five sets of eyes.

STYLE AND TONE

- The modern voice: a master biographer telling character-driven
  stories — vivid, fast, alert to the telling detail. Plutarch's
  moralizing asides stay (they are his voice) but land conversationally,
  not sermonically.
- Dryden-Clough runs to enormous sentences; break them decisively.
  Scene-by-scene narration should read like narrative nonfiction.
- Anecdotes and one-liners are the payload. Every quip, omen, and
  deathbed detail must survive. Speeches stay in direct quotation.
- Verse quotations (Homer, tragedy tags): two-space-indented
  modernized lines.

FAMOUS LINES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- Caesar at the Rubicon: "The die is cast." (Dryden's exact phrase —
  keep it.)
- Caesar on passing the Alpine village: keep the shape "I would
  rather be first man here than second at Rome."
- Caesar's triple dispatch: "I came, I saw, I conquered."
- The warning: "Beware the Ides of March." (Dryden: "take heed of the
  Ides of March" — modernize to the famous form.)
- Caesar to Brutus at the assassination: render as "You too, my
  child?" if the Greek line appears (Dryden reports the scene; do not
  insert Shakespeare's Latin).
- Cato's verdict, quoted in Caesar: Caesar "was the only man who
  undertook to destroy the state sober."
- Brutus: "the noblest Roman" sentiment is Shakespeare, NOT Plutarch —
  do not import it.
- Antony/Cleopatra: the barge on the Cydnus — keep the pageantry at
  full length; "her own beauty... was not in itself so remarkable"
  observation preserved.

CONSISTENCY ACROSS THE FIVE LIVES (the ledger enforces)

- Names: Latin forms per project convention — Pompeius NO: use
  "Pompey" HERE (Dryden's constant form; this is a narrative volume
  and "Pompey" is the name English readers know — deliberately
  different from the Cicero volumes' rule; ledger it once). Sulla
  (not Sylla — modernize Dryden's spelling), Catiline, Clodius,
  Octavius → "Octavian" after the adoption as Dryden's "young Caesar"
  clarifies, Lepidus, Cassius, Casca, Calpurnia, Portia (Dryden's
  spelling; the Latin Porcia may be noted once), Cleopatra, Fulvia,
  Octavia, Enobarbus → Domitius Ahenobarbus as Dryden has him.
- The same events recur across lives: keep KEY PHRASES consistent
  (the ledger records each rendering at first occurrence: the Rubicon
  line, the Ides warning, Philippi's two battles, the proscription
  bargain that gave up Cicero).
- Titles: consul, praetor, tribune, dictator, quaestor, censor as-is;
  "imperator" glossed once ("the soldiers' salute of victory").

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Battle deaths, suicides (Cato's death scene is the moral climax of
  his life — render it with full gravity, exactly as Plutarch stages
  it; likewise Brutus, Cassius, Antony, Cleopatra), proscription
  murders (Cicero's death), and Caesar's assassination: plain,
  measured historical register — vivid but never gory for its own
  sake, nothing softened.
- Slavery, concubinage, and Roman marriage politics appear
  matter-of-factly — render plainly as the period reality.

FORMAT

28 files, five sections (one per life; title = the name, e.g. "Cato
the Younger"). Files have NO chapter subheadings — each is continuous
narrative with "(Part n of k)" markers. Parts 2+ never re-introduce
the life; non-final parts never conclude it. Word ratio target
0.85–1.15.
