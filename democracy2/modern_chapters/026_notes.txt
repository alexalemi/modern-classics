Translation Notes — Chapter 026 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter X, Part 1)

CONTEXT:
This is the opening of Tocqueville's massive chapter on "the three races" — one of the most extraordinary and disturbing chapters in the whole work. It introduces the framework (white, Black, Indian), then devotes its main body to the situation of Native Americans. Tocqueville is writing with a combination of genuine sympathy and analytical detachment that is remarkable for 1835. His account of the Choctaw removal at Memphis is based on personal observation and is one of the most powerful passages in the book.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Nègre" — Translated as "Black man" or "Black" throughout. The French word was the standard term of the era and carried no particular pejorative connotation in Tocqueville's usage. "Negro" in English now reads as dated and offensive; "Black" is the natural modern equivalent.

2. "Indien" / "sauvage" — "Indian" throughout, as Tocqueville uses it as a specific ethnic/racial designation. "Sauvage" translated as "savage" when Tocqueville uses it as a noun (meaning uncivilized person), "wild" or "uncivilized" when used as an adjective. Tocqueville is not being derogatory — he uses "sauvage" in its French ethnographic sense — but "savage" conveys his analytical framework faithfully.

3. "L'homme par excellence" — Kept as "the man par excellence." Tocqueville is describing white supremacist reality, not endorsing it. The phrase drips with irony given what follows.

4. "Moeurs" — Consistently translated as "customs" or "customs and values" depending on context. Never "manners."

5. "Lumières" — Translated as "knowledge," "education," or "knowledge and education" depending on context.

6. The Memphis passage — Translated with particular care. This is Tocqueville at his most powerfully observational. The detail about the dogs howling and swimming after their masters is one of the most haunting images in 19th-century political writing. Kept the prose spare and direct to match Tocqueville's own restrained style here.

7. The Cherokee petition — Translated verbatim as Tocqueville indicates he did. Their rhetoric is dignified and devastating; no modernization needed beyond basic English.

8. The final paragraph — "On ne saurait détruire les hommes en respectant mieux les lois de l'humanité." This is one of the most savage lines Tocqueville ever wrote — pure acid irony. Translated as "You could not destroy men while showing greater respect for the laws of humanity." The impersonal "on" becomes "you" for maximum impact.

TONE:
This chapter alternates between clinical analysis and barely suppressed outrage. Tocqueville maintains his scholarly posture but the anger bleeds through, especially in passages about the hypocrisy of American legalism regarding Indian removal. The irony in the final passages is devastating.
