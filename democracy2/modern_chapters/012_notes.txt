Translation Notes — Chapter 012 (Chapter VIII: The Federal Constitution — Opening)

This is the opening section of the long Chapter VIII on the federal constitution. It covers the history: the weakness of the Articles of Confederation, the call for a constitutional convention, and the ratification of the new Constitution. This is only the beginning of the chapter, which continues in subsequent files.

Key decisions:

- "secouèrent simultanément le joug" → "simultaneously threw off the yoke" — standard metaphor, kept.

- "moeurs" → "customs" here (in the context of colonial similarities), per the text analysis guidance.

- "mère-patrie" → "mother country" — standard English equivalent.

- "pouvoir constituant" → "constituent power" — consistent with chapter 010 usage. This is the power to create or amend a constitution, distinct from ordinary legislative power.

- "les rouages du gouvernement s'arrêtent" → "the gears of government are grinding to a halt" — "rouages" literally means "gears" or "workings." I made the metaphor slightly more vivid ("grinding to a halt") to match the drama Tocqueville is building.

- The famous passage comparing American and French revolutionary efforts: Tocqueville is pointedly deflating American self-mythology while celebrating French sacrifice. "Étouffant d'une main l'incendie qui dévorait ses entrailles, et de l'autre promenant la torche autour d'elle" — "smothering with one hand the fire that was devouring her own insides, and with the other waving the torch around her." This is a brilliant image of France simultaneously fighting civil war and foreign wars. Kept the two-handed image structure intact.

- "1,300 lieues de mer" → "1,300 leagues of ocean" — kept the archaic unit since Tocqueville uses it.

- "les plus beaux esprits et les plus nobles caractères" → "the finest minds and noblest characters" — "beaux esprits" here means brilliant intellects, not "beautiful spirits."

- "La révolution d'Amérique finit donc précisément au moment où commençait la nôtre" → "The American Revolution thus ended at precisely the moment ours began." — A characteristically Tocquevillean epigrammatic closing. Clean and punchy.
