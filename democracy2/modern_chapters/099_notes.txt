Translation Notes — Chapter 099 (Chapter XXIII: The most warlike and revolutionary class in democratic armies)

CONTEXT:
Tocqueville identifies three distinct classes in democratic armies — officers, non-commissioned officers (NCOs), and ordinary soldiers — and argues that the NCOs are by far the most dangerous and revolutionary. This is a remarkably prescient analysis; NCO-led coups and mutinies would become a recurring feature of modern political history.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Très-nombreuse, relativement au peuple qui la fournit" — "very large relative to the population that supplies it." Clean, direct translation. Tocqueville is noting that democracies need big armies but their citizens don't want to serve — hence conscription.

2. "L'enrôlement forcé" — "compulsory enlistment." More natural than "forced enrollment."

3. "C'est l'inégalité du poids et non le poids qui fait d'ordinaire qu'on lui résiste" — "it is inequality of burden, not the burden itself, that usually provokes resistance." A sharp Tocquevillean epigram, preserved intact.

4. "Sous le drapeau" — "under the flag." The French idiom translates perfectly.

5. "Le sous-officier" — "non-commissioned officer." This is the standard English military term. Used throughout consistently.

6. "Cette classe des sous-officiers qui, avant le siècle présent, n'avait point encore paru dans l'histoire" — "This class of non-commissioned officers, which had not appeared on the stage of history before the present century." Added "on the stage of" for English fluency while preserving the idea that this is a historically new phenomenon.

7. "Il a pris pied dans une sphère supérieure" — "He has gained a foothold in a sphere above." "Pris pied" literally means "taken foot/footing" — "gained a foothold" is the perfect English equivalent.

8. Footnote 9 — Integrated as [Note: ...] inline, per the style guide.

9. "Une ambition désespérée" — "a desperate ambition." The French carries a sense of ambition born of desperation, not just ambitious-and-also-desperate. The English works for both readings.

10. "Les moeurs nationales" — "national culture." In context, "customs and values" would have been clunkier; "culture" captures the full meaning concisely.

TONE:
Tocqueville is building a sociological typology with the analytical precision of a scientist. The three-class analysis (officer/NCO/soldier) is structured like a logical proof. The passage about the NCO's psychology is especially vivid — you can feel Tocqueville's combination of sympathy and alarm. The final paragraph's clean symmetry ("ignorant and weak" / "educated and energetic") brings the argument home with epigrammatic force.
