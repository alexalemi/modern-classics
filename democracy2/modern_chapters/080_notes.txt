Translation Notes — Chapter 080 (Part 3, Chapter IV: Consequences of the three preceding chapters)

A short synthesis chapter. Tocqueville reconciles his earlier analysis of democratic individualism with the observation that Americans are generous in practice. His resolution: equality creates a tacit mutual insurance pact — people help each other not out of deep personal bonds but from a practical awareness that they will need help themselves someday.

Key decisions:
- "zèle" → "eagerness" rather than "zeal" (too religious-sounding).
- "sauvage dans ses bois" → "savage in his forest." Kept Tocqueville's own vocabulary; "savage" here is his standard term for indigenous peoples living in the wilderness (used without contempt in this passage, as a figure of isolation).
- "individualisme" — left untranslated since Tocqueville himself coined/popularized this term and it is discussed extensively in earlier chapters.
- "accord tacite et presque involontaire" → "tacit, almost involuntary agreement." This is the key concept: mutual aid in democracy arises not from love but from rational self-interest and shared vulnerability.
- "bons offices" → "small acts of kindness." The French "bons offices" means helpful services/good deeds, not bureaucratic "good offices."
- "serviables" → "obliging." Captures the modest, everyday quality Tocqueville intends — not heroic self-sacrifice, but reliable helpfulness.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 081 is a long, important chapter on servant-master relations. Three models: aristocratic (deep bonds, total inequality), democratic (contractual, temporary, dignified), and transitional/revolutionary (confused, bitter). The military analogy (soldiers who are equals off-duty but obey on duty) is key. Handle the passage about "lackey" carefully — Tocqueville is analyzing a social type, not mocking servants.
