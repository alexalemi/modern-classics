Translation Notes — Chapter 104 (Volume II, Part 4, Chapter I)
"Equality naturally gives people a taste for free institutions."

This is the opening chapter of Part 4, the climax of the entire work — democracy's influence on political society. It is brief and programmatic, setting up the central tension: equality produces two opposite tendencies, one toward freedom, one toward servitude.

Key decisions:
- "l'habitude et le goût de ne suivre... que leur volonté" → "the habit and the taste for following no one's will but their own" — preserves both "habitude" and "goût" as distinct concepts
- "le corps social va tout à coup se trouver réduit en poussière" → "the social body suddenly reduced to dust" — kept the vivid image
- "l'anarchie n'est pas le mal principal... mais le moindre" → preserved this striking paradox exactly: anarchy is the *least* of democracy's dangers
- "par un chemin plus long, plus secret, mais plus sûr, vers la servitude" → "by a longer, more hidden, but surer path, toward servitude" — kept the tricolon rhythm
- "l'indocilité qu'elle inspire" → "the rebelliousness it inspires" — "indocilité" literally means unruliness/intractability; "rebelliousness" captures the spirit
- "déposer au fond de l'esprit et du coeur" → "planting deep in the mind and heart" — "déposer" (to deposit/lay down) becomes the more natural English "planting"

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 105 argues that democratic ideas naturally favor concentrating power. Key concept: the idea of "intermediate powers" (pouvoirs secondaires/intermédiaires) fades in democratic ages. Watch for "pouvoir unique et central" — translate consistently as "single central power." The chapter builds from theory (democratic minds favor simplicity) to practice (France, America, Europe all converging).
