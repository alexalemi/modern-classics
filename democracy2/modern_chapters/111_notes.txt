Translation Notes — Chapter 111 (Volume II, Part 4, Chapter VIII)
"General view of the subject."

This is the final chapter of the entire work — Tocqueville's grand summation and parting statement. It is brief but extraordinarily powerful, moving from an inventory of the democratic world's features, through a moment of personal melancholy, to a ringing affirmation of human agency.

Key decisions:
- "je sens ma vue qui se trouble et ma raison qui chancelle" → "I feel my vision blurring and my reason faltering" — Tocqueville's rare moment of intellectual humility
- "Le monde qui s'élève est encore à moitié engagé sous les débris du monde qui tombe" → "The world that is rising is still half buried under the ruins of the world that is falling" — magnificent image, preserved exactly
- "Le passé n'éclairant plus l'avenir, l'esprit marche dans les ténèbres" → "The past no longer illuminates the future; the mind advances in darkness" — perhaps the most quoted line in the entire book, translated to be equally memorable
- The long passage listing features of democratic society: preserved the parallel structure (short, punchy sentences in pairs) — this is Tocqueville's greatest rhetorical set-piece
- "le grand lien de l'humanité se resserre" → "the great bond of humanity is growing tighter" — the one optimistic note in the inventory
- "Le spectacle de cette uniformité universelle m'attriste et me glace" → "The spectacle of this universal uniformity saddens me and chills me" — Tocqueville's personal confession
- The God passage: "l'être tout puissant et éternel dont l'oeil enveloppe nécessairement l'ensemble des choses" → "the all-powerful and eternal being whose eye necessarily encompasses the whole of things" — Tocqueville's attempt to transcend his own aristocratic perspective by adopting God's point of view
- "L'égalité est moins élevée peut-être; mais elle plus juste, et sa justice fait sa grandeur et sa beauté" → "Equality is perhaps less elevated; but it is more just, and its justice is the source of its grandeur and its beauty" — the moral center of the entire work
- "Ce sont comme deux humanités distinctes" → "They are like two distinct humanities" — aristocratic and democratic worlds are literally different civilizations
- "un travail honnête et stérile" → "an honest but futile task" — describing those who try to cherry-pick from aristocracy
- "Ce sont là de fausses et lâches doctrines" → "These are false and cowardly doctrines" — "lâches" is deliberately harsh; Tocqueville has contempt for determinism
- "Elle trace, il est vrai, autour de chaque homme un cercle fatal, dont il ne peut sortir; mais dans ses vastes limites, l'homme est puissant et libre; ainsi des peuples" → "It is true that Providence traces around each person a fatal circle that they cannot escape; but within its vast limits, human beings are powerful and free — and so are peoples" — the book's penultimate thought: constrained freedom
- Final sentence: "il dépend d'elles que l'égalité les conduise à la servitude ou à la liberté, aux lumières ou à la barbarie, à la prospérité ou aux misères" → "it depends on them whether equality leads them to servitude or to freedom, to knowledge or to barbarism, to prosperity or to wretchedness" — the ultimate tricolon, three paired alternatives, ending the entire work on a note of radical human agency. "Lumières" → "knowledge" here (not "enlightenment") to keep it grounded.
- "FIN DU TOME QUATRIÈME" → "END OF VOLUME FOUR" — preserved the formal ending
