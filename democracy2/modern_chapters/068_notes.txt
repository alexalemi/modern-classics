Translation notes for Chapter 068 (Vol II, Part 2, Ch XIII):

This is one of Tocqueville's most famous and psychologically penetrating chapters. Every paragraph is quotable.

- "inquiets" / "inquietude" consistently rendered as "restless" / "restlessness" per the vocabulary guide, NOT "anxious" / "anxiety"
- "une sorte de nuage couvrait habituellement leurs traits" -> "a kind of cloud habitually covered their features" -- kept this beautiful image exactly
- "graves et presque tristes jusque dans leurs plaisirs" -> "grave and almost sad, even in their pleasures" -- devastating observation preserved
- "ardeur febrile" -> "feverish ardor" -- direct translation that works perfectly
- "cinq cents lieues" -> "five hundred leagues" -- kept the unit as leagues rather than converting to miles; it sounds more vivid
- "cette poursuite inutile d'une felicite complete qui fuit toujours" -> "this futile pursuit of a complete happiness that forever eludes him" -- one of the most quoted lines in the book
- "La borne a change de forme plutot que de place" -> "The barrier has changed shape rather than position" -- another devastating aphorism, preserved with its punch
- "le desir de l'egalite devient toujours plus insatiable a mesure que l'egalite est plus grande" -> "the desire for equality becomes ever more insatiable as equality increases" -- the Tocqueville paradox in one sentence
- "Leur volonte resiste, mais souvent leur raison flechit" -> "Their will holds firm, but their reason often buckles" -- kept the stark brevity
- Final paragraph's parallel structure carefully preserved: more vivid / infinitely greater / more often disappointed / more restless / more bitter
- The passage on suicide and insanity translated faithfully without editorial softening -- Tocqueville is making a clinical observation about democratic psychology
