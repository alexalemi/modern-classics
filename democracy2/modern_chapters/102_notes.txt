Translation Notes — Chapter 102 (Chapter XXVI: War in democratic societies)

CONTEXT:
The final substantive chapter of Part 3, this is a sweeping analysis of how democracy transforms the nature of war itself. Tocqueville makes several major arguments: (1) wars become rarer but bigger; (2) numerical superiority becomes decisive; (3) democratic nations are easy to conquer once their capital falls; (4) Napoleon's style of warfare was a product of democratic society, not individual genius; (5) civil wars become rarer and shorter in democracies, unless the army splits.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Une sorte d'apathie et de bienveillance universelle les apaise en dépit d'eux-mêmes" — "a kind of universal apathy and goodwill calms them in spite of themselves." The pairing of "apathy and goodwill" is characteristically Tocquevillean — peace comes from both laziness and fellow-feeling.

2. Footnotes 10, 11, and 12 — All integrated as [Note: ...] per the style guide. Footnote 11 is particularly long and important (about how democratic peoples become alike), so it is included in full.

3. "Aux plus gros bataillons appartient nécessairement la victoire" — "victory necessarily belongs to the bigger battalions." This echoes the (possibly apocryphal) saying attributed to Napoleon: "God is always on the side of the big battalions."

4. "Mettons, pour n'offenser personne, des fonctionnaires publics au lieu d'esclaves" — "Let us substitute public officials for slaves, so as to offend no one." Tocqueville's dry humor. He's saying democratic citizens are, in political terms, more like subjects than independent actors — but wrapping the observation in diplomatic courtesy.

5. "Je comparerai la guerre dans un pays aristocratique à la guerre dans un pays des montagnes" — "I would compare war in an aristocratic country to war in mountain terrain." A vivid analogy: aristocratic society has natural strongpoints (noble estates, local powers) that make conquest difficult, like mountains provide natural defensive positions.

6. Napoleon passage — Tocqueville carefully strips Napoleon of sole credit for "his" military revolution, arguing it was a product of social change. This is classic Tocquevillean analysis: individuals matter less than structural forces. Translated directly without editorial comment.

7. "Un coup de main plutôt que par une guerre" — "a coup rather than a war." "Coup de main" literally means "a stroke of the hand" — the English "coup" captures the sense of sudden seizure perfectly.

8. "L'esprit d'individualité est très-vivace / presque détruit" — "The spirit of individuality is very much alive / nearly destroyed." In the long footnote about democratic homogenization, this contrast is the key structural pivot.

TONE:
Expansive and sweeping — this is Tocqueville at his most geopolitically ambitious, making predictions about the future of European warfare. The passages about Napoleon and about democratic vulnerability to conquest are especially prescient, anticipating both the Franco-Prussian War and the fall of France in 1940. The civil war analysis reads like a theoretical blueprint for how coups d'état work in democracies.
