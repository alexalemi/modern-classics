Translation Notes — Chapter 055 (Vol. II, Part 1, Ch. XXI)

Title: "De l'éloquence parlementaire aux États-Unis" — "On Parliamentary Eloquence in the United States."

This is the final chapter of Volume II, Part 1 (Democracy's influence on intellectual life).

Key decisions:
- "un lien hiérarchique à l'aide duquel on peut maintenir chacun à sa place" — "a hierarchical chain connecting them all, which serves to keep each person in his place." Clear and direct.
- "ils ne se montrent subordonnés que quand le péril est très-grand" — "show themselves subordinate only when the danger is extreme." Kept the qualifier.
- "un désir pétulant" — "an impetuous desire." The French "pétulant" is closer to "impetuous" or "eager" than to the English "petulant" (which suggests irritability).
- "une oisiveté qui a encore de la splendeur" — "a leisured existence that still has its splendor." Captures the aristocratic consolation prize of elegant retirement.
- "c'est de ce coin de terre qu'il lui faut sortir pour s'élever à commander le peuple" — "it is from this little patch of earth that he must rise to command the people." Tocqueville's ironic sense of the gap between the local and the global.
- "fournissant ainsi une sorte de résumé brillant et complet de ses commettants et de lui-même" — "providing a sort of brilliant and complete summary of his constituents and himself." The irony is thick — voters expect their representative to be a one-man variety show.
- "embrouille le débat et fatigue l'assemblée" — "tangles up the debate and exhausts the assembly." Direct and punchy.
- "les vingt-quatre États dont l'union se compose" — kept "twenty-four states" as Tocqueville wrote it; this reflects the number of states at the time he was writing.
- "elle semble se traîner vers le but qu'on se propose plutôt qu'y marcher" — "seeming to crawl toward its goal rather than march toward it." Strong image of bureaucratic sluggishness.
- "réduirait les chrétiens en Turcs" — This phrase appeared in chapter 054, not here. In this chapter the analogous point is about people bearing bad speeches.
- The final pivot from the petty to the grand side of democratic eloquence is one of Tocqueville's signature structural moves. He first demolishes congressional speechifying, then suddenly reveals the universal power of democratic oratory. Preserved the dramatic turn.
- "c'est toujours à la nation tout entière, et au nom de la nation tout entière qu'on parle" — "it is always to the nation as a whole, and in the name of the nation as a whole, that one speaks." Key formulation; the repetition of "nation as a whole" is deliberate in the French and was kept.
- "Tous les hommes s'y intéressent parce qu'il s'agit de l'homme qui est partout le même" — "Every person takes an interest, because it deals with Man — who is everywhere the same." Capitalized "Man" to capture the universalizing force of Tocqueville's argument.

Style note: This chapter has the sharpest tonal contrast of the batch — biting satire of congressional mediocrity followed by a genuinely inspiring passage on the universal reach of democratic debate. The closing line about French orators speaking to all mankind even when addressing only their countrymen is characteristic Tocqueville: pride in France delivered with analytical detachment.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 056 begins Volume II, Part 2 — "The Influence of Democracy on the Feelings of Americans." The first chapter is on why democratic peoples show a more ardent and enduring love of equality than of liberty. This is one of Tocqueville's most important arguments. Shift to a more psychological, less literary register.
