Translation Notes — Chapter 079 (Part 3, Chapter III: American sensitivity at home vs. abroad)

One of the more entertaining chapters — Tocqueville's comedic account of trying to escape an oblivious American conversationalist is genuinely funny, and the portrait of Americans in Europe is sharp social observation that still resonates.

Key decisions:
- "susceptibilité" → "touchy" in the title (captures the colloquial sense better than "susceptibility" or "sensitivity"), "easygoing" for the opposite quality at home.
- "tempérament vindicatif" → "vengeful temperament." Tocqueville means they hold grudges, not that they are violent. The surrounding context (slow to anger, slow to forgive) makes this clear.
- "savoir-vivre" → "proper behavior" / "etiquette" depending on context.
- The comic passage about the persistent American: kept the escalating rhythm. "Il m'excede" → "he is driving me crazy" — deliberately colloquial to match Tocqueville's frustration.
- "ennemi mortel" → "mortal enemy." Tocqueville is being hyperbolic for comic effect; preserved it.
- "gentilhomme campagnard" → "country squire." Natural English equivalent of the rural lesser nobleman.
- "commerce méticuleux et difficile" → "extremely fussy and difficult in his dealings." "Commerce" here means social intercourse, not business.
- The section on Americans in Europe claiming noble descent and surrounding themselves with luxury — translated straightforwardly. Tocqueville's irony speaks for itself.
- "esprit se cacher sous des formes vulgaires" → "intelligence hiding behind ordinary exteriors." The American is scandalized that Parisian wit does not come with aristocratic trappings.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 080 is a brief synthesis chapter drawing consequences from the previous three. The key point: equality makes people willing to help each other, not out of deep attachment but out of a practical recognition of shared vulnerability. Keep it brisk — it's a short chapter.
