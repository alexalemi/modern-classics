Translation Notes — Chapter 005 (Chapter II, Part 2: Reasons for Some Peculiarities)

This short closing section serves as a corrective to the preceding argument. After building the case that America's Puritan origins explain everything, Tocqueville pauses to say: not so fast. Some features of American life are not democratic at all -- they are inherited from England's aristocratic legal tradition. This is Tocqueville the careful analyst tempering Tocqueville the grand theorist.

Key decisions:

- "Singularités" -> "Peculiarities." Could also be "oddities," but "peculiarities" is more precise and matches Tocqueville's analytical register.

- "La condition sociale, la religion et les moeurs" -> "The social conditions, religion, and customs and values." Consistent with vocabulary decisions from text analysis.

- "Nul ne saurait se dégager entièrement du passé" -> "no one can entirely break free from the past." Clean, modern phrasing. Tocqueville's point is simple and powerful: origins matter, but so does inherited culture.

- "L'esprit dominant de la législation américaine" -> "the dominant spirit of American legislation." Kept "spirit" for "esprit" here since Tocqueville means the animating principle.

- "La prison ou le cautionnement" -> "imprisonment or bail." Preserved the italics Tocqueville uses in the French to emphasize these as technical terms.

- "Insoluble" -> "unsolvable." Preferred over "insoluble" which, while technically correct in English, sounds overly formal.

- "Quoi de plus aristocratique qu'une semblable législation?" -> "What could be more aristocratic than a system like that?" Turned the rhetorical question into something slightly more conversational while keeping its bite.

- "Les légistes" -> "lawyers." Tocqueville uses "légistes" (legal professionals) rather than "avocats" (advocates). "Lawyers" captures the broader meaning.

- "Couvert d'une couche démocratique, sous laquelle on voit de temps en temps percer les anciennes couleurs de l'aristocratie" -> "covered with a democratic coat of paint, beneath which you can see, from time to time, the old colors of aristocracy showing through." This is a wonderful closing image. "Coat of paint" modernizes "couche" while preserving the metaphor of surfaces and what lies beneath them. Tocqueville is saying: scratch the surface of American democracy, and you find English aristocratic origins still at work.

- Chapter 006 will be Chapter III: "État social des Anglo-Américains" (Social Conditions of the Anglo-Americans). This is a pivotal analytical chapter where Tocqueville lays out his key concept of "état social" -- the overall configuration of American society. He will argue that equality of conditions is the fundamental fact of American life, and that it shapes everything else. The term "état social" should be translated as "social conditions" per the text analysis. The tone becomes more abstract and theoretical -- more like a sociological essay than historical narrative.
