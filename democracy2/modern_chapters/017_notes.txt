Translation Notes — Chapter 017 (Vol. I, Tome 2, Ch. IV: On Political Association in the United States)

Key decisions:

- Three degrees of association: Tocqueville's tripartite structure (intellectual bond / physical assembly / representative convention) is the backbone of this chapter. Kept the progression very clear with explicit markers: "second degree," "last degree," and the recapitulation "Thus, in the first case... in the second... in the third."

- "l'association réunit en faisceau les efforts des esprits divergents" — "An association bundles the efforts of divergent minds." The French "faisceau" (bundle/sheaf) is the root of "fascism" but here simply means bundling together. "Bundles" is the most natural English equivalent.

- "se charge seulement des considérants et s'arrête au dispositif" — "concerns itself only with the preamble and stops short of the verdict." This is legal terminology: "considérants" are the whereas-clauses (reasoning), "dispositif" is the operative part (ruling). Translated to convey the legal metaphor: the minority drafts arguments but stops short of actually ruling.

- "moeurs" — "customs" in "passé dans les habitudes et dans les moeurs" (passed into habits and into customs). Here moeurs is paired with "habitudes" so "customs" is the right complement.

- "omnipotence de la majorité" — "the majority's absolute power" in the general argument, preserving the term from the text analysis.

- "factieux" — "troublemakers" (in "il y a des factieux, mais point de conspirateurs"). The French "factieux" means agitators/seditious persons; "troublemakers" captures the sense without the archaic English connotation. The key contrast is with "conspirateurs" (conspirators) — open agitation vs. secret plotting.

- "corps secondaires" — "secondary bodies." This is Tocqueville's term for the intermediate institutions (guilds, clergy, nobility, local assemblies) that in aristocratic societies stand between the individual and the state. Kept the term as-is since it's semi-technical.

- "pétitionnent" — "petition." Tocqueville's contrast between European associations that "act and command" and American ones that "speak and petition" is one of the chapter's sharpest formulations. Preserved exactly.

- The convention of 1831 passage: Translated with precision since this is historical narrative. "Suivant l'habitude américaine, avait pris le nom de convention" — "following American custom, had taken the name of convention." The word "convention" was itself somewhat foreign to French readers.

- "l'extrême liberté corrige les abus de la liberté, et l'extrême démocratie prévient les dangers de la démocratie" — "extreme liberty corrects the abuses of liberty, and extreme democracy prevents the dangers of democracy." One of Tocqueville's most brilliant paradoxes. The chiastic structure is essential; preserved it exactly.

- Final paragraph on American vs. European associational governance: "un gouvernement civil" vs. military organization. This is the chapter's culminating point. Tocqueville's observation that American associations preserve individual judgment while European ones demand obedience is his explanation for why the same right produces different outcomes.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 018 should be "Du gouvernement de la démocratie en Amérique" — on democratic governance. Key themes: the quality of people chosen by democracy vs. aristocracy, why Americans tolerate mediocre leaders, and the relationship between democratic elections and good government. Watch for Tocqueville's comparison between democratic and aristocratic selection of leaders — he is surprisingly critical of democracy's choices while ultimately defending the system.
