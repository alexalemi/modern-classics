Translation Notes — Chapter 089 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XIII: How Equality Naturally Divides Americans Into a Multitude of Small Private Groups)

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "petites sociétés particulières" — "small private groups" or "small private societies." Used "groups" for the more natural English feel, but kept "societies" where Tocqueville is being more formal.

2. "coteries" — "cliques." The French word has the same slightly pejorative connotation as the English "cliques."

3. "les hommes se pratiquent forcément tous les jours" — "people necessarily deal with one another every day." "Se pratiquer" in this context means to have regular social dealings.

4. "de hautes barrières immobiles" vs. "une multitude de petits fils presque invisibles" — "high, immovable barriers" vs. "tiny, nearly invisible threads." This is one of Tocqueville's most vivid metaphorical contrasts. Preserved the parallelism and the sharp visual distinction.

5. "la convenance générale d'une même condition les rapproche" — "the general suitability of belonging to the same class brings them together." "Convenance" here means social propriety/fitness — the sense that people of the same class ought to associate.

TONE:
A concise, observational chapter. Tocqueville makes a sharp sociological point: equality doesn't create communal living; it creates fragmentation into self-selected micro-communities. The closing metaphor of barriers vs. threads is particularly elegant.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:
- Chapter 090 is about American manners — a longer, more reflective chapter that meditates on what democracy does to social behavior and etiquette.
- The thread/barrier metaphor here connects to broader themes about democratic social texture.
