Translation Notes — Chapter 086 (Part 3, Chapter X: The young woman becomes a wife)

Tocqueville's central paradox: the most free young women become the most disciplined wives, because freedom in youth builds the strength of character needed for self-imposed constraints. The frontier passage at the end is some of his most vivid descriptive writing.

Key decisions:
- "cloître" → "cloister." Tocqueville means this almost literally — American wives live in a domestic sphere as enclosed as a convent.
- "abnégation d'elle-même" → "self-denial." Not "self-abnegation" (too formal).
- "le joug" → "the yoke." Tocqueville uses this stark metaphor without apparent irony — the point is that the American woman places herself under it voluntarily, which transforms its meaning.
- "discipline paternelle est fort lâche" / "lien conjugal est fort étroit" → "parental discipline is very loose" / "marriage bond is very tight." Preserved the chiastic contrast.
- "cette froide et austère raison" → "that cool, austere reason." A key phrase — Tocqueville admires the American woman's rationality, even while noting it comes at the cost of warmth and charm.
- The frontier passage: "hutte mal fermée au sein d'un bois" → "a drafty cabin in the middle of the woods." Made "mal fermée" (badly closed/sealed) concrete and vivid. "Hutte" is more primitive than "cabin" in French, but "cabin" captures the American frontier context.
- "Leurs traits semblaient altérés et flétris, mais leurs regards étaient fermes" → "Their features seemed worn and faded, but their eyes were firm." Powerful portrait — physical deterioration but unbroken will.
- "tristes et résolues" → "sad and resolute." The combination captures the specific quality Tocqueville admires.
- Omitted [TN-A] Gutenberg editorial mark per guidelines.
- Closing line: "le rôle a changé, les habitudes diffèrent, l'esprit est le même" → "the role has changed, the habits are different, but the spirit is the same." Clean tricolon preserved.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 087 is about how equality of conditions helps maintain good morals in America. This is a longer, more analytical chapter. Tocqueville argues that equality promotes marital fidelity through several mechanisms: free choice of spouse, the practical demands of democratic life, and the channeling of passion into industry. The long footnote about European vs. American novels is worth including. The final section about France's transitional disorder vs. eventual democratic moralization is important for Tocqueville's larger argument.
