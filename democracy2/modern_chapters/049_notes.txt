Chapter 049 (Vol II, Part 1, Ch XV) Translation Notes

This chapter closes the literary sequence with a practical prescription: democratic nations should study Greek and Latin literature as a corrective to their natural literary tendencies. But Tocqueville is careful to say this education should be for those with literary talent or inclination, not for everyone — universal classical education in a commercial society would produce "very polished and very dangerous citizens."

Key decisions:
- "Ce qu'on appelait le peuple" — "What people called 'the people'" — the quotation marks flag Tocqueville's point that the ancient concept of "the people" was radically different from the modern one.
- "une querelle intestine entre les cadets et les aînés de la même famille" — "an internal quarrel between the younger and the elder sons of the same family." This vivid metaphor for the patrician-plebeian conflict is pure Tocqueville — reducing a grand historical struggle to a family dispute.
- "C'est une hygiène salutaire" — "It is a salutary discipline." The French "hygiène" here means a health regimen or discipline, not our narrower modern sense of cleanliness. "Discipline" captures the prescriptive, therapeutic sense.
- "Elles nous soutiennent par le bord où nous penchons" — "They hold us up on the side where we are leaning." This final sentence is one of Tocqueville's best closers: concise, vivid, and structurally balanced. The metaphor of leaning/being propped up captures his whole argument about the ancients as corrective ballast.
- "citoyens très-polis et très-dangereux" — "very polished and very dangerous citizens" — preserved the deliberate, darkly funny juxtaposition.
- "ils troubleraient l'État, au nom des Grecs et des Romains, au lieu de le féconder par leur industrie" — "they would disturb the State in the name of the Greeks and Romans instead of enriching it through their industry." A wonderfully dry warning about what happens when you give everyone a classical education in a society that needs engineers and entrepreneurs.

Consistency notes for subsequent chapters:
- This concludes the literary/intellectual section of Part 1. The next chapters move on to other aspects of democracy's influence on intellectual life (language, rhetoric, etc.)
- Tocqueville's balanced view — democracy has natural literary strengths AND weaknesses — should continue to be reflected in tone.
