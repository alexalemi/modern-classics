Translation Notes — Chapter 029 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter X, Part 4: Threats to the Union's Durability)

CONTEXT:
This section shifts from the racial analysis of chapters 026-028 into political-structural analysis: what holds the Union together, what might tear it apart. Tocqueville builds a theoretical framework (national vs provincial vs "mixed" sovereignty), applies it to America, and concludes that the preponderant force resides in the states, not the federal government. He then examines material interests (which bind), immaterial interests (shared opinions and values, which also bind), and character differences (shaped by slavery, which threaten to divide). The chapter closes with his analysis of westward expansion and the shifting center of gravity.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Objets mixtes" — "Mixed matters." Tocqueville uses this as a semi-technical term for sovereign powers that could reasonably be assigned to either level of government. Kept the directness of "mixed" rather than using a more elaborate phrase.

2. "Force preponderante" — "Preponderant force." Maintained this somewhat formal phrase because it is Tocqueville's central analytical concept in this section — which level of government has the upper hand by structural default.

3. "Moeurs" — Translated as "customs and values" in the key passage about slavery's indirect effect ("l'esclavage n'attaque donc pas directement la confederation americaine par les interets, mais indirectement par les moeurs"). This is one of the most important uses of "moeurs" in the chapter — Tocqueville's argument is that slavery doesn't create conflicting economic interests between North and South, but creates conflicting cultures and temperaments.

4. "Lumières" — Translated variously as "knowledge," "enlightenment," and "knowledgeable" depending on context, following the text analysis guidelines.

5. The North/South character comparison — Tocqueville's parallel portraits of the Southerner (proud, quick-tempered, brilliant, idle) and the Northerner (patient, practical, industrious) are presented in carefully balanced rhetorical structures. Preserved the parallelism and the punch of the final summary: "The one has the tastes, the prejudices, the weaknesses, and the grandeur of every aristocracy. The other has the qualities and the defects that characterize the middle class."

6. "Comme un deluge d'hommes" — "Like a flood of men." Tocqueville's image of westward expansion as a biblical flood, "lifted each day by the hand of God," is powerful. Translated directly without softening the providentialist language.

7. Population projections — Tocqueville's predictions (100 million by ~1896, forty states) are remarkably accurate. Kept his mathematical precision intact.

TONE:
This chapter marks a major tonal shift from the emotional intensity of the race chapters (026-028) to cool political and structural analysis. Tocqueville is at his most systematic here — building a theoretical framework, applying it, examining evidence. The North/South character portraits inject some literary energy, but the dominant mode is analytical. The final sections on westward expansion have a note of awe.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:

- Maintain: "federal government," "provincial government" (for state governments in the theoretical passages), "the Union," "confederated states," "customs and values" (for moeurs).
