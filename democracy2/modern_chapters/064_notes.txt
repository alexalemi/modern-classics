Translation Notes -- Chapter 064 (Vol. II, Part 2, Ch. IX: How Americans Apply the Doctrine of Self-Interest Properly Understood to Religion)

CONTEXT:
Tocqueville extends his analysis of self-interest properly understood to religion. He argues that American religion naturally aligns with this doctrine: it emphasizes practical benefits, worldly happiness, and rational calculation alongside spiritual devotion. The chapter is a remarkable piece of religious sociology -- sympathetic but clear-eyed about how American Christianity operates.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "intérêt bien entendu" -> "self-interest properly understood." Consistent with ch. 063.

2. "faire bien vivre un homme qui ne veut pas mourir" -> "make a person live well who does not want to die." A brilliant aphorism. The point: if you don't believe in an afterlife, a purely this-worldly doctrine of self-interest will sometimes fail to motivate sacrifice.

3. "Je les respecte trop pour les croire" -> "I respect them too much to believe them." One of Tocqueville's most elegant lines. He thinks the zealous Christians are actually better than they claim -- they act from genuine love, not calculation, but dress it up in the language of reward. Preserved the concise paradox.

4. Pascal quotation: "De se tromper en croyant la religion chrétienne vraie, il n'y a pas grand'chose à perdre; mais quel malheur de se tromper en la croyant fausse!" -> "To be mistaken in believing the Christian religion true is no great loss; but what a misfortune to be mistaken in believing it false!" This is Pascal's Wager in miniature. Translated directly from the French Tocqueville provides.

5. "au pied des autels" -> "to the foot of the altar." Direct, preserving the religious imagery.

6. "moyen âge" -> "Middle Ages." Standard capitalization in English.

7. "prédicateurs américains" -> "American preachers." "Prédicateur" = preacher, not "predictor." The context makes this clear.

8. The final observation -- American preachers can't tear their gaze from earth, and it's hard to tell if religion is about heaven or this-world happiness -- is characteristically sharp Tocqueville social observation. Translated to preserve the dry, almost amused tone.

9. "ne parlaient que de l'autre vie" -> "spoke only of the next life." Consistent use of "next life" / "next world" for "l'autre vie" / "l'autre monde."

TONE:
Sympathetic, measured, with flashes of wit. Tocqueville respects religion and sees its social utility, but he observes American religious practice with the detached eye of a French Catholic intellectual visiting a Protestant culture. The chapter has moments of genuine admiration (the passage about love of God) alongside sociological observation.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:
- Chapter 065 discusses the taste for material well-being in America. This shifts from religion to materialism -- Tocqueville will analyze America's characteristic focus on comfort and acquisition.
- "bien-être matériel" -> "material well-being" or "material comfort"
- The self-interest theme continues but now applied to economic behavior and consumption
- Class analysis will be important -- Tocqueville distinguishes between aristocratic, democratic, and middle-class attitudes toward wealth
