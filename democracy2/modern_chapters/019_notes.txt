Translation Notes — Chapter 019 (Du gouvernement de la démocratie, Part 2)

CONTEXT:
This is the second part of Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter V. It continues Tocqueville's dissection of democratic governance with sections on public spending comparisons between America and France, corruption in democracy vs. aristocracy, democracy's capacity for sustained effort in wartime, the people's self-governance, and how democracy conducts foreign affairs. The chapter ends with one of Tocqueville's most famous formulations: "An aristocratic body is a firm and enlightened man who never dies."

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "léger et enthousiaste" → "frivolous and enthusiastic." "Léger" here means lightness in temperament — flighty, lacking gravity — rather than "light" physically. "Frivolous" captures the right shade.

2. "un million dépensé en fumée" → "a million spent on fireworks." Literally "a million spent on smoke," but the reference is clearly to fireworks and public celebrations. "Fireworks" makes this concrete and vivid.

3. The long fiscal comparison passage: This is one of Tocqueville's most methodically careful sections. He systematically demolishes the possibility of comparing American and French spending by showing that every unit of comparison — national, state, departmental, county, township — is either unknown, incomparable, or incomplete. Preserved his exhaustive, almost lawyerly logic without rushing to a conclusion.

4. "intérêt bien entendu" → "enlightened self-interest." This is a key Tocqueville concept. Translated consistently with the text analysis guidelines.

5. "les buveurs sont en majorité, et la tempérance est impopulaire" → "the drinkers are in the majority, and temperance is unpopular." This is one of Tocqueville's driest, most devastating one-liners. Preserved the deadpan delivery. The anecdote about Philadelphia and the liquor tax is a perfect little parable of democratic self-governance.

6. "Le grand privilége des Américains n'est donc pas seulement d'être plus éclairés que d'autres, mais d'avoir la faculté de faire des fautes réparables." → "The great privilege of Americans, then, is not simply being more enlightened than others, but having the ability to make mistakes that can be repaired." A famous formulation. Kept it clean and punchy.

7. South America passage: "le peuple... semble obstinément attaché à se déchirer les entrailles" → "the people... seem obstinately bent on tearing out their own entrails." Vivid and dark. Tocqueville is genuinely anguished here, and the passage about despotism being "a blessing" followed by "But those two words can never be united in my mind" is one of the most powerful moral declarations in the book.

8. Washington's letter: This is Tocqueville's French translation of Washington's Farewell Address. Translated from Tocqueville's French, not from the English original, as instructed. The sense is very close to the original English, but there are subtle differences reflecting how Tocqueville read Washington.

9. "Un corps aristocratique est un homme ferme et éclairé qui ne meurt point." → "An aristocratic body is a firm and enlightened man who never dies." The chapter's final sentence — an extraordinary aphorism. "Firm" rather than "steadfast" or "resolute" because the French "ferme" is deliberately simple and strong. This personification of aristocracy as an immortal individual is one of Tocqueville's most memorable images.

10. Corruption section: "il n'y a pas moins d'hommes à vendre, mais on n'y trouve presque point d'acheteurs" → "Perhaps in democracies there are no fewer men for sale, but there are almost no buyers." The marketplace metaphor for corruption is deliberately cynical. Preserved the commercial language.

11. "esprit de cour" → "the courtier's mind." Literally "the spirit of the court." Chose "courtier's mind" to make the reference concrete and avoid the stiff "court spirit."

12. War of Independence passage: Hamilton quotation translated from Tocqueville's French. The passage about Americans giving up tea — with Tocqueville's sardonic note that "those who know that people generally cling more to their habits than to their lives will no doubt be astonished" — is a perfect example of his dry wit.

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
This chapter has many subsection headings in ALL CAPS. All translated. The section summaries (italicized in the original) are telegraphic outlines of arguments to come. The fiscal comparison section is unusually dense with footnotes containing detailed statistical data — all preserved.

TONE:
This chapter shows Tocqueville at his most nuanced. He is simultaneously admiring democracy's ultimate capacity for self-correction ("mistakes that can be repaired") while unflinchingly cataloguing its weaknesses in foreign policy and wartime. The final section on foreign affairs is particularly bold: he flatly declares democracy "decidedly inferior" in this domain. The closing image of aristocracy as an immortal man is not nostalgic — it is analytical.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:
- Key terms established: "enlightened self-interest," "common sense" (bon sens), Washington's non-entanglement policy.
- The foreign affairs section sets up tensions that will be explored in later chapters about the Union's future.
- Tocqueville's willingness to say democracy is "decidedly inferior" in foreign affairs is a benchmark for his honesty as an analyst.
