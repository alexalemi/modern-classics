Translation Notes -- Chapter 006 (Chapter III: Social Conditions of the Anglo-Americans)

This chapter is one of the analytical keystones of Volume I. Tocqueville argues that social conditions -- particularly the equality of conditions -- are the foundational fact from which everything else flows. The famous passage on inheritance law is some of his most vivid, almost poetic writing.

Key decisions:

- "etat social" -> "social conditions" throughout, per the text analysis. Avoided Reeve's "social state" which sounds stilted.

- "loi des successions" -> "inheritance law" / "inheritance laws." Reeve used "laws of inheritance" or "law of descent." I went with the simpler modern phrasing.

- "droit de primogeniture" -> "right of primogeniture." Kept the formal term since Tocqueville is being precise.

- "loi sur les substitutions" -> "law of entail." Standard English legal equivalent of the French concept (fideicommis/substitutions).

- "lumières" -> "knowledge" / "education" / "knowledge and virtue" depending on context. In the passage about the West, "cette naturelle aristocratie qui decoule des lumieres et de la vertu" becomes "that natural aristocracy that flows from education and virtue."

- "moeurs" -> "customs and values" in the final sentence, per the text analysis. This is one of the most important vocabulary choices in the whole book. Reeve's "manners" is far too narrow.

- The long rhetorical passage about inheritance law as a machine ("elle broie, ou fait voler en eclats...") is one of Tocqueville's most famous pieces of prose. I preserved the building rhythm and the final devastating image of democracy sitting down on dust, keeping it as a single extended sentence with semicolons as in the French.

- "passion male et legitime pour l'egalite" -> "virile and legitimate passion for equality." Kept "virile" (male) because Tocqueville is deliberately using a gendered term -- this is the muscular, admirable form of equality-seeking.

- "gout deprave pour l'egalite" -> "depraved taste for equality." Direct and punchy, as Tocqueville intended.

- Tocqueville's footnotes included as [Note: ...]. Omitted Gutenberg editorial markers like (_G_).

- "publicistes anciens et modernes" -> "political writers, ancient and modern." "Publiciste" in 19th-century French means political writer/thinker, not "publicist" in the modern English sense.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 007 is Chapter IV on popular sovereignty. Short and punchy. The famous last line ("Le peuple regne sur le monde politique americain comme Dieu sur l'univers") needs to land with full force. Maintain first-person voice -- Tocqueville is building his argument step by step. "Cens" = property qualification for voting. "Dogme" = he uses this word deliberately, almost ironically -- the "dogma" of popular sovereignty.
