Translation Notes — Chapter 052 (Vol. II, Part 1, Ch. XVIII)

Title: "Pourquoi les écrivains et les orateurs américains sont souvent boursouflés" — "Why American Writers and Orators Are Often Bombastic." The French "boursouflés" literally means "puffed up" or "bloated"; "bombastic" captures the literary meaning precisely.

Key decisions:
- "un langage clair et sec, dépourvu de tout ornement" — "a clear, dry language, stripped of all ornament." Kept close.
- "donnent volontiers dans le boursouflé" — "happily plunge into bombast." More vivid than "fall into pomposity."
- "un très-petit objet, qui est lui-même" — "one very small object — himself." Preserved the comic sting of the dash.
- "l'espace intermédiaire est vide" — "the space in between is empty." Clean and direct.
- "ils gonflent leur imagination sans cesse, et l'étendant outre mesure, ils lui font atteindre le gigantesque pour lequel elle abandonne souvent le grand" — "they inflate their imaginations ceaselessly, and by stretching them beyond all measure, they make them reach the gigantic — for which they often abandon the merely great." Key distinction between "gigantesque" and "grand" — the inflated vs. the genuinely great.
- "créent des monstres" — "create monsters." Kept the blunt, striking word.
- "ne fassent quelquefois regretter le monde réel" — "sometimes make us miss the real world." Captures the French sense of "regretter" as longing/wishing for.

Style note: This is one of Tocqueville's most compact, witty chapters — almost a satirical essay. Kept the dry humor throughout. The final paragraph is a beautiful piece of literary criticism delivered as prophecy.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 053 is on theater in democratic societies. Longer, more structured argument. Tocqueville draws on the French dramatic tradition (Racine, Louis XIV's theater) as his aristocratic benchmark. Keep an essayistic tone but note the shift back to a more analytical register.
