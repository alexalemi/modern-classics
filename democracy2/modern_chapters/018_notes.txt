Translation Notes — Chapter 018 (Du gouvernement de la démocratie en Amérique, Part 1)

CONTEXT:
This is the first part of Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter V — one of the most important chapters in the entire work. Tocqueville examines how democracy actually governs, starting with universal suffrage, the quality of democratic choices, public officials, discretionary power, administrative instability, public spending, and officials' salaries. His arguments are structurally careful: he does not simply praise or condemn democracy, but anatomizes its tendencies with clinical precision.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "terrain brûlant" → "burning ground." Direct and vivid. Reeve used "burning ground" too — but it is simply the natural English for the French metaphor. No alternative needed.

2. "ses propres pentes" → "its own tendencies." Literally "its own slopes" — a gravitational metaphor. "Tendencies" captures the sense of democracy naturally rolling in a certain direction without being pushed.

3. "vote universel" → "universal suffrage." Standard political term; kept throughout.

4. "les charlatans de tous genres savent si bien le secret de lui plaire" → "charlatans of every stripe know so well how to please them." "Stripe" is a natural English equivalent for "genre" in this context, and keeps the colloquial energy.

5. Pascal reference ("fuit, comme dit Pascal, d'une fuite éternelle") → "flees, as Pascal said, in an eternal flight." Preserved the attribution and the rhythmic phrase. This references Pascal's Pensées on the impossibility of grasping happiness.

6. "il s'agite, il se lasse, il s'aigrit" → "they agitate, they grow weary, they grow bitter." Preserved Tocqueville's tricolon of increasing disillusionment. Each verb is sharper than the last.

7. Chancellor Kent quotation: Translated from the French, as instructed, rather than looking up the English original. The sense is clear and Tocqueville's French rendering captures what he wanted.

8. "feuilles de la Sibylle" → "the Sibyl's leaves." Classical reference to the Cumaean Sibyl who wrote prophecies on leaves that scattered in the wind (Virgil, Aeneid, Book VI). Left as-is; the reference is evocative and educated readers will recognize it.

9. "l'arbitraire des magistrats" → "the discretionary power of officials." "Arbitraire" in this context means the freedom to act at one's own discretion, not "arbitrariness" in the pejorative English sense. Tocqueville is making an analytical, not a moral, point — that democratic officials have wide latitude precisely because the people can remove them at will.

10. "select-men" → "selectmen." Modernized the hyphenation. These are the elected officials of New England townships.

11. The salary comparison table: Preserved the format as closely as possible. The numbers are in francs, as in the original. The comparison is deliberately striking — the King of France receives 12 million francs vs. the American President's 135,000.

12. "Le pauvre n'est point, à proprement parler, le semblable du riche; c'est un être d'une autre espèce." → "The poor person is not, strictly speaking, the rich person's equal; he is a being of a different species." This is one of Tocqueville's sharpest formulations about aristocratic mentality. Kept the bluntness.

13. Long sections on public spending: Tocqueville's three-class analysis of fiscal policy (rich, middle, poor) is a masterpiece of political economy. Preserved his logical architecture — thesis, evidence, counterargument, synthesis — without flattening.

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
This chapter has many subsections, each with ALL CAPS headings and italicized summaries. Translated all headings. The summaries (in smaller text in the French) are not full sentences but telegraphic outlines; translated them as they appear.

TONE:
Tocqueville is at his most analytical here. The first-person voice is prominent but measured — "I must say it," "I was struck with surprise," "I like this natural bearing." He is not ranting; he is dissecting. The tone is that of a brilliant diagnostician who admires the patient even while cataloguing the disease.
