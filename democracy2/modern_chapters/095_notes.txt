Translation Notes — Chapter 095 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XIX: Why There Are So Many Ambitious People in the United States but So Few Great Ambitions)

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "ambition" — "ambition" throughout. Straightforward cognate that works perfectly.

2. The Pascal quote — "C'est un grand avantage que la qualité..." Translated directly. Pascal's point: aristocratic birth gives you a 30-year head start in life. In democracies, everyone starts at the same level and must work their way up slowly.

3. "On n'élargit pas graduellement son âme comme sa maison" — "You do not gradually enlarge your soul as you do your house." One of Tocqueville's finest epigrams. Preserved exactly.

4. "une même filière" — "the same filter." The French image is of a wire-drawing die or mold through which everyone must be squeezed. "Filter" conveys the sense of uniform processing.

5. Chinese novel reference — Tocqueville's wry observation that in China, the romantic hero wins the girl by passing an exam. A charming illustration of how deeply the meritocratic/examination culture penetrates.

6. "Je céderais volontiers plusieurs de nos petites vertus pour ce vice" — "I would gladly trade several of our small virtues for that one vice." A stunning closing line. Tocqueville argues that what democratic peoples need is not humility but pride — genuine ambition and self-regard.

7. "l'audace que la médiocrité des désirs" — "the audacity of desires than their mediocrity." The fundamental fear: not that democratic people will want too much, but that they will want too little.

TONE:
This is Tocqueville at his most prescient and concerned. The chapter builds from sociological observation to moral exhortation. His fear is not democratic chaos but democratic mediocrity — that equality will produce a society of small ambitions and small pleasures. The closing call for pride over humility is a deliberate provocation.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:
- Chapter 096 is a short chapter on office-seeking (l'industrie des places) — the tendency of democratic peoples in centralized states to seek government jobs.
- Key distinction: Americans seek private enterprise; Europeans seek public employment. Same equality, different outlets.
