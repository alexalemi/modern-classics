Chapter 048 (Vol II, Part 1, Ch XIV) Translation Notes

A very short, sharp chapter — almost an epigram stretched to a page. Tocqueville's argument: democracy turns literature into a business. The key insight is the inversion of the aristocratic model: in aristocracies, glory is attainable but money is not; in democracies, money is easy but glory is cheap.

Key decisions:
- "industrie littéraire" — "literary industry." The double meaning of "industrie" (both industriousness and commercial enterprise) works in English too.
- "vendeurs d'idées" — "idea-peddlers." The French is blunt and contemptuous; "peddlers" captures this better than "sellers" or "dealers."
- "âmes vénales qui naissent dans les cours, ou qui sont dignes d'y vivre" — "the venal souls — those born in courts or worthy of living there." Tocqueville's comparison of democratic readers to kings with courtiers is brilliantly cutting: the public lavishes money on writers it doesn't respect, just as kings reward courtiers they hold in contempt.
- "il suffit qu'on le goûte" — "it is enough to be enjoyed." The contrast with "admire" is important: democratic success requires only that people like you, not that they look up to you.
- Kept the chapter's punchy brevity intact — no expansion or explanatory additions.

Consistency notes for next chapter:
- The final chapter in this literary sequence (Ch XV on Greek and Latin literature) provides a constructive prescription: study the ancients as a corrective to democratic literary vices.
