Translation Notes — Chapter 107 (Volume II, Part 4, Chapter IV)
"Some particular and accidental causes that either lead a democratic people to centralize power or divert them from it."

This is the longest chapter in Part 4 so far. It moves from the general theory (chapters 105-106) to specific historical circumstances: Why does centralization differ in America, England, France, and Egypt? The answer: how and when equality arrived matters enormously.

Key decisions:
- "moeurs viriles" → "vigorous customs" — "viriles" in 19th-century French means vigorous/robust, not specifically masculine in the modern English sense
- "fils de leurs oeuvres" → "self-made men" — this French idiom means exactly that
- "la relation réciproque d'inférieur et de maître" → "the reciprocal relationship of inferior and master" — preserved the pairing
- Napoleon passage: kept Tocqueville's characteristic balance — "Il ne faut ni louer ni blâmer Napoléon" → "Napoleon should be neither praised nor blamed"
- "la menue tyrannie du peuple" → "the petty tyranny of the people" — "menue" means small/petty
- Egypt/pasha passage: Tocqueville means Muhammad Ali. "faire du pays sa manufacture, et des habitants ses ouvriers" → "turn the country into his factory and its inhabitants into his workers" — preserved the devastating metaphor
- "la science du despotisme, si compliquée jadis, se simplifie: elle se réduit, pour ainsi dire, à un principe unique" → the closing line is a masterpiece of irony, kept exact
- [TN-F] Gutenberg mark omitted
