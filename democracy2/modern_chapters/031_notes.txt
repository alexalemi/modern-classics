Translation Notes — Chapter 031 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter X, Part 6: Republican Institutions + Commercial Greatness)

CONTEXT:
This chapter contains two distinct sections. The first, shorter section argues that republican institutions are deeply rooted in America and will outlast the Union itself — a key distinction that most readers miss. The second, longer section analyzes why America is becoming a great commercial power, with the famous comparison of American sailors to French Revolutionary soldiers.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "L'Union n'est qu'un accident" — "The Union is only an accident." This is one of Tocqueville's most striking formulations. By "accident" he means something contingent, not necessary — the Union depends on circumstances, while the republic is woven into the fabric of American life. Kept the blunt phrasing.

2. "Consensus universalis" — Kept in Latin as Tocqueville has it. This phrase captures how the republic exists in America not through argument but through something deeper: unquestioned shared assumption, like the orbit of the sun.

3. The European "republic" passage — Tocqueville's acid satire of European republicans ("those who know best what is good for the people: a happy distinction that allows one to act in the name of nations without consulting them, and to claim their gratitude while trampling them underfoot") is some of his sharpest writing. He's clearly thinking of the Jacobin tradition. Kept the sarcasm at full force.

4. "Ce que les Francais faisaient pour la victoire, ils le font pour le bon marche" — "What the French did for victory, they do for low prices." This is the central analogy of the commerce section: American merchants are commercial revolutionaries, willing to take risks and endure hardships that their European competitors won't, just as French revolutionary soldiers defeated professional armies by fighting in ways that broke all conventional rules. The parallel is brilliant and Tocqueville clearly relishes it.

5. The Boston-to-Canton passage — Tocqueville's portrait of the American merchant sailing to China, seeing land only once in two years, drinking brackish water, living on salted meat — all to sell tea a penny cheaper than the English — is one of the most vivid passages in the book. Translated with the same lean, propulsive energy Tocqueville uses.

6. "Les Americains mettent une sorte d'heroisme dans leur maniere de faire le commerce" — "Americans bring a kind of heroism to their way of doing business." This is the thesis sentence of the commerce section. The word "heroism" applied to commerce is deliberate and characteristically Tocquevillian — he sees in American commercial energy the same spirit that in other societies would produce military heroes or artistic geniuses.

7. "Moeurs" — Consistently "customs and values" throughout, including in the key passage about the republic being rooted in "moeurs" as well as laws.

8. South America passage — Tocqueville's prediction that the US will become the commercial hub of the Western hemisphere, as England is of Europe, is remarkably prescient. His analysis of why South America will need Northern intermediaries (latecomers to civilization, agricultural before commercial) is colonial in its assumptions but analytically sharp. Translated without softening.

9. Final sentence — "Ils sont pousses a s'emparer des mers, comme les Romains a conquerir le monde." — "They are driven to seize the seas, as the Romans were to conquer the world." A magnificent closing line that puts American commercial ambition in the grandest possible historical frame. Kept the sweep.

TONE:
The republic section is measured and analytical, with flashes of sarcasm directed at European pseudo-republicans. The commerce section is exuberant and admiring — Tocqueville is clearly impressed and even excited by the commercial energy he witnessed. The sailor passages have a narrative momentum rare in this work. The overall effect is of a writer who sees in American commercial genius something genuinely new under the sun.

VOCABULARY MAINTAINED:
- "Customs and values" (moeurs)
- "Township" (commune)
- "Popular sovereignty" (souverainete du peuple)
- "The Union" / "the republic" — kept as distinct concepts throughout, following Tocqueville's key distinction
- "Federal government" / "provincial government" for the theoretical passages
