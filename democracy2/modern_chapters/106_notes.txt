Translation Notes — Chapter 106 (Volume II, Part 4, Chapter III)
"The feelings of democratic peoples agree with their ideas in leading them to concentrate power."

This chapter complements the previous one: Chapter 105 showed that democratic ideas favor centralization; this one shows that democratic feelings do too. The key insight: citizens in democracies are simultaneously independent and weak — proud among equals, but helpless individually, so they turn to the state.

Key decisions:
- "se replient volontiers sur eux-mêmes et se considèrent isolément" → "readily turn inward and consider themselves in isolation" — captures the reflexive quality
- "cet être immense qui seul s'élève au milieu de l'abaissement universel" → "that immense being that alone rises above the universal lowliness" — kept Tocqueville's almost theological language about the state
- Note 13 (the long footnote) is critically important — it contains the idea that democratic government grows simply by existing, and that citizens unknowingly help it grow. Translated in full as [Note: ...]
- "fiers et serviles" → "proud and servile" — Tocqueville's devastating oxymoron
- "l'amour de l'égalité croisse sans cesse avec l'égalité elle-même; en le satisfaisant on le développe" → "the love of equality should grow constantly along with equality itself; in satisfying it, you develop it further" — an addictive loop
- "La centralisation sera le gouvernement naturel" → "Centralization will be the natural form of government" — the chapter's devastating closing line

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 107 discusses specific and accidental causes that push toward or away from centralization. Key contrast: America (liberty came first, equality second) vs. Europe (equality came first via absolute monarchy). Napoleon gets a nuanced treatment. The chapter ends with the striking formula that despotism's only necessary science is to "love equality or pretend to."
