Translation Notes -- Chapter 058 (Vol. II, Part 2, Ch. III: How Individualism Is Greater Right After a Democratic Revolution Than at Any Other Time)

CONTEXT:
A short but penetrating chapter. Tocqueville argues that individualism is worst not in mature democracies but in the immediate aftermath of a democratic revolution, when old class hatreds still burn and new equals haven't yet learned to trust one another. The implicit reference is to France after 1789. The closing line about America's advantage -- born equal rather than becoming so -- is one of his most quoted observations.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "au sortir d'une révolution démocratique" -> "Right After a Democratic Revolution." The French "au sortir de" means literally "on coming out of" -- translated idiomatically.

2. "enivrés de leur nouveau pouvoir" -> "drunk with their new power." Direct, vivid. "Enivrés" = intoxicated/drunk.

3. "présomptueuse confiance" -> "presumptuous confidence." The cognate works perfectly here.

4. "haines implacables" -> "implacable hatreds." Another case where the French-English cognate is the best choice.

5. "inquiétude secrète" -> "secret restlessness." Following text analysis: "inquiétude" = restlessness, not anxiety. This fits perfectly: the newly elevated aren't anxious in a clinical sense -- they're uneasy, unsettled.

6. "regards de triomphe et de crainte" -> "looks of triumph and fear." Preserved the paradox -- they feel both victorious and afraid at the same time.

7. Final line: "être nés égaux au lieu de le devenir" -> "they were born equal instead of becoming so." Clean, memorable. This is one of Tocqueville's most famous observations about American exceptionalism.

TONE:
Brisk and observational. The chapter is short and punchy -- Tocqueville makes his point quickly and ends with a killer line. No rhetorical excess.
