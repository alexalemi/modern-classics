Translation Notes — Chapter 077 (Part 3, Chapter I: How customs soften as conditions equalize)

This is the opening chapter of Part 3 (Democracy's influence on customs/moeurs). Tocqueville's central argument: equality produces empathy because people who see themselves as alike can imagine each other's suffering. The chapter builds this through a stunning historical example — Madame de Sevigne's letters.

Key decisions:
- "moeurs s'adoucissent" → "customs soften." Kept close to the French. "Moeurs" here means the whole texture of social behavior and moral feeling.
- "égalité des conditions" → "equality of social conditions" (first use), then "conditions" alone for rhythm.
- "faits corrélatifs" → "linked phenomena" rather than the more technical "correlated facts."
- Madame de Sevigne letters: Translated the full text from the French. These are arguably the most powerful passage in the chapter — the casual tone about torture and execution reveals exactly Tocqueville's point about aristocratic insensitivity. Kept the conversational, almost breezy tone of the original letters.
- "roua le violon" → "broke on the wheel the fiddler." The "violon" was the person who played fiddle to rally the rioters. "Rouer" is breaking on the wheel, a specific execution method.
- "gentilhomme" → "gentleman" (in the sense of a member of the gentry/nobility, not modern politeness).
- "lumières" → "education" in context of what causes gentleness; "enlightenment" when referring to the Romans' intellectual peak.
- "droit des gens" → "law of nations" (the standard translation of this legal term, referring to what we now call international law).
- "égoïsme en théorie sociale" → "self-interest into a social and philosophical theory" — this refers to the doctrine of "interet bien entendu" (enlightened self-interest) discussed earlier in Volume II.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 078 is about how democracy makes everyday social relations simpler and easier. Key contrast is English stiffness vs. American openness. The England comparison is central — Tocqueville attributes English social awkwardness not to national character but to the transitional state of their aristocracy (birth-based → money-based).
