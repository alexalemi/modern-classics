Translation Notes — Chapter 082 (Part 3, Chapter VI: Democratic institutions raise rents and shorten leases)

A chapter that uses agricultural economics to illustrate broader democratic dynamics. The key moves: the shift from personal to cash relationships, rising rents as a sign of democratic transition, the shortening of leases as a symptom of democratic restlessness. The closing image is one of Tocqueville's finest epigrammatic sentences.

Key decisions:
- "fermiers" → "tenant farmers" (not "farmers" — the distinction between owner and tenant is the whole point).
- "fermages" → "rents" / "farm rents." The specific agricultural rent paid by a tenant to a landowner.
- "baux" → "leases." Standard translation.
- "patrimoines" → "family estates." More evocative than "inheritances."
- "arbre mort dans ses racines" → "a tree that is dead at the roots." Beautiful metaphor, translated directly.
- "ce qu'ils gagnent en argent, ils vont bientôt le perdre en pouvoir" → "what they gain in money they are about to lose in power." Kept the epigrammatic punch.
- Final sentence: "ce qu'il y a de plus mouvant, au milieu du mouvement de toutes choses, c'est le coeur de l'homme" → "the most restless thing in the midst of all this restlessness is the human heart." This is a quintessential Tocqueville epigram — used "restless/restlessness" per the text analysis guidance on "inquiet/inquiétude."

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 083 is about democracy's influence on wages. Tocqueville predicts rising wages as a general law of democracy, then identifies a critical exception: industrial workers trapped in a new kind of aristocracy (the manufacturing aristocracy discussed earlier). The passage about the vicious circle of industrial poverty is strikingly prescient. Keep the economic analysis clear but preserve the moral urgency of the final paragraph.
