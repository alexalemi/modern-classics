Translation Notes — Chapter 003 (Chapter I: Geography of North America)

This chapter is Tocqueville's sweeping geographic portrait of North America — part geology lecture, part prose poem, part stage-setting. It moves from the physical landscape to the indigenous peoples, then to the idea that this continent was "waiting" for a new civilization.

Key decisions:

- "Configuration extérieure" -> "Physical geography." The French literally means "external configuration" but Tocqueville is simply describing the continent's physical layout. "Physical geography" is natural and clear.

- "Mers polaires" -> "polar seas." Kept the slightly poetic phrasing rather than specifying the Arctic Ocean, since Tocqueville is being evocative.

- "Lieues carrées" -> "square leagues." Kept Tocqueville's original units. He is writing for a French audience accustomed to leagues. Converting to modern units would lose the period flavor and disrupt the footnotes.

- "Mer du Sud" -> "the Pacific." In Tocqueville's time, "la mer du Sud" (South Sea) referred to the Pacific Ocean. Used the modern name since the old one would confuse readers.

- "Le Père des eaux, ou le Mississipi" -> "the Father of Waters -- the Mississippi." Preserved the poetic naming and the dash for dramatic effect.

- Long geographic descriptions: Kept Tocqueville's flowing sentences largely intact. His description of the Mississippi Valley as a single great organism — the river as "god" of its valley — is vivid rhetoric that shouldn't be chopped up.

- "Nègres" -> "Africans." In the passage listing races ("ni blancs... ni jaunes... ni noirs comme les nègres"), used "Africans" as the natural modern parallel to "Europeans" and "Asians" in the same sentence.

- "Sauvages" -> "native people" / "native tribes" / "Indians" depending on context. When Tocqueville uses "sauvages" as a noun (common 19th-century French usage), translated variously. When he uses "sauvage" as an adjective ("indépendance sauvage," "sauvages vertus"), kept "savage" since he's being deliberate about the word choice.

- "Moeurs" -> "customs and values" per the text analysis vocabulary decisions.

- "Grossièreté" / "grossier" -> "coarseness" / "coarse" — Tocqueville is making a precise sociological observation about how inequality degrades the manners of the poor. "Coarseness" captures this better than "rudeness."

- "Insolents et bas" -> "insolent and servile." "Bas" literally means "low" but Tocqueville's point is about the combination of defiance and submission that inequality produces. "Servile" catches the meaning.

- Preserved the contrast between South America and North America as parallel set-pieces. Tocqueville's prose turns lush and tropical for the Caribbean, then austere for the North. Matched the register shift in English.

- "N'étaient là, en quelque sorte, qu'en attendant" -> "they were there, in a sense, only as a placeholder." The French "en attendant" means "while waiting" — Tocqueville's italics show he knows this is a provocative claim. "Placeholder" captures the coldness of the observation.

- Omitted Gutenberg editorial markers (_A_), (_B_), (_C_), (_D_) per instructions.

- Tocqueville's footnotes: all included as [Note: ...] inline markers.
