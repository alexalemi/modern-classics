Translation Notes — Chapter 034 (Avertissement / Preface to Volume II)

CONTEXT:
This is Tocqueville's preface to Volume II (1840), where he explains the shift from the institutional analysis of Volume I to the broader cultural and social analysis of Volume II. He also defends himself against the charge of being anti-democratic — a criticism that Volume I had attracted. The preface is methodological, personal, and quietly defiant.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Avertissement" → "Preface." Consistent with the decision made in chapter 001. The word literally means "warning/notice" but functions as a preface.

2. "État social démocratique" → "democratic social order." In the text analysis, "état social" can be rendered as "social conditions" or "social order." Here "social order" works better because Tocqueville is describing a systemic condition that generates specific laws and customs — "order" captures the structural quality.

3. "Moeurs politiques" → "political customs." Consistent with the text analysis guidance on "moeurs" — broader than "manners," encompassing habits, customs, values.

4. "L'aspect de la société civile" → "The face of civil society." Kept "face" for "aspect" — Tocqueville is using the metaphor of physiognomy (the "physionomie du monde politique" in the next clause) to suggest that democracy has changed how society looks at a fundamental level.

5. "Il faut que, sur-le-champ, je prévienne le lecteur" → "I must immediately warn the reader." "Sur-le-champ" (literally "on the spot") conveys urgency. Tocqueville is anxious to head off a misreading before it takes hold. "Immediately" captures this.

6. "Ce serait me supposer une vue bien étroite" → "That would be crediting me with a very narrow view." Slightly more conversational than a literal "that would be supposing in me a very narrow view." The word "crediting" adds a touch of irony — as if being mono-causal were a kind of backhanded compliment.

7. "Lumières acquises" → "acquired knowledge." Following the text analysis guidance on "lumières."

8. "Habitudes antérieures" → "prior habits." Straightforward.

9. "Les petites factions qui, de nos jours, le tracassent et l'énervent" → "the petty factions that today pester and exhaust it." "Tracasser" = to pester, hassle, bother. "Énerver" in 19th-century French means to weaken/enervate (not to irritate, which is the modern colloquial sense). "Exhaust" captures the weakening sense while remaining natural. Together "pester and exhaust" conveys Tocqueville's contempt for these minor political groupings.

10. "Que ces lecteurs élèvent la voix et m'accusent" → "let those readers raise their voices and accuse me." This is a challenge — Tocqueville is daring his critics. Kept the combative directness.

11. The key passage on honesty and democracy: "C'est parce que je n'étais point un adversaire de la Démocratie, que j'ai voulu être sincère envers elle." → "It is precisely because I was not an enemy of Democracy that I wanted to be honest with it." Added "precisely" to capture the emphasis of "c'est...que" (a cleft construction that highlights causality). This is one of Tocqueville's most self-revealing statements — he sees himself as a friend of democracy who insists on telling uncomfortable truths.

12. "Les hommes ne reçoivent point la vérité de leurs ennemis, et leurs amis ne la leur offrent guère" → "People do not accept truth from their enemies, and their friends rarely offer it to them." A beautifully epigrammatic line. Translated to preserve its balance and punch. Chose "people" over "men" for naturalness.

TONE:
Personal, methodological, quietly defiant. Tocqueville is doing three things simultaneously: (1) explaining what the new volume will cover, (2) defending himself against charges of being anti-democratic, and (3) making a philosophical argument about the role of honest criticism in a democracy. The modesty of the final paragraph is genuine but also strategic — he wants to be taken seriously as someone who tackled an impossibly large subject with the right spirit.
