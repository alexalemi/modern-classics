Translation Notes — Chapter 083 (Part 3, Chapter VII: Democracy's influence on wages)

A chapter that reads as strikingly modern — Tocqueville identifies the general democratic tendency toward rising wages, then diagnoses the critical exception: industrial workers trapped in what he earlier called the "manufacturing aristocracy." His analysis of the vicious circle of industrial poverty anticipates Marx by several years.

Key decisions:
- "maîtres et ouvriers" → "employers and workers." "Masters and workers" is too feudal for what is clearly an industrial context.
- "entrepreneurs d'industrie" → "small business owners." Tocqueville is distinguishing these numerous small operators from the few large industrialists discussed later.
- "lumières" → "education" (in the context of workers' limited skills/knowledge).
- "se liguer" → "band together." More natural than "league together."
- "la culture des terres" → "farming." Clean and modern.
- "cercle vicieux" → "vicious circle." Tocqueville uses the exact French equivalent of this now-standard phrase.
- The contrast between the general law (wages rise with equality) and the exception (industrial workers trapped in a new aristocracy) is the structural backbone of the chapter. Kept this clear with clean paragraph transitions.
- Final paragraph: "il est difficile, lorsque la société entière se remue, de tenir une classe immobile" → "it is difficult, when the whole of society is in motion, to keep one class standing still." This is Tocqueville at his most politically urgent — a direct warning to legislators.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 084 is on democracy's influence on the family — a major chapter. Tocqueville argues democracy weakens formal paternal authority but strengthens natural family bonds. The long footnote about Napoleon's civil code is important and should be included. The closing epigram about democracy loosening social bonds while tightening natural ones is famous.
