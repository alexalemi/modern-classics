Translation Notes — Chapter 020 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter VI)
"The Real Advantages That American Society Derives from Democratic Government"

This is a long, structurally complex chapter with multiple subsections. Tocqueville is making a careful, hedged argument: democracy is not better in its methods, but better in its purpose and its indirect effects. The rhetorical strategy is concession-and-rebuttal throughout.

Key decisions:

- "tendance générale des lois" → "general tendency of [its/those] laws." Kept Tocqueville's emphasis, including his own italics on this phrase in one key passage.

- "le grand privilège des Américains est de pouvoir faire des fautes réparables" → "the great privilege of Americans is being able to make mistakes that can be fixed." Natural English phrasing while preserving the epigrammatic punch.

- "moeurs" → varied: "customs," "customs and values," "social habits" depending on context. In the patriotism section, "simples dans leurs moeurs" → "simple in their customs."

- "lumières" → "knowledge" (in the patriotism section), "information/informed" elsewhere. Avoided the awkward "enlightenment" except where specifically about the concept.

- "esprit de cité" → "civic spirit." Cleaner than "spirit of the city."

- "Homo puer robustus" → kept in Latin with italics, as Tocqueville himself does. Added a brief gloss: "man is a robust child."

- "cupidité" → "greed." Tocqueville is being deliberately provocative here — the American works for the public good partly out of self-interest verging on greed. Kept the edge.

- "patriotisme irritable" → "prickly patriotism." More natural and vivid than "irritable patriotism."

- Long final passage (what do you want from society?) — This is one of Tocqueville's great rhetorical set pieces, a long series of conditional clauses building to a climax. Preserved the architecture: the cascading "if" clauses, the dramatic pivot at "then equalize conditions and establish democratic government," and the resigned coda about being carried by forces beyond your control.

- Tocqueville's footnote about temperance societies: included as [Note: ...] per guidelines.
- Footnote about Roman citizens under the Caesars: included as [Note: ...].
- Gutenberg editorial marks: omitted.

Structural note: This chapter has five subsections, each with its own argument summary (the indented italic text in the original). I translated these summaries and kept them as headers.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 021 is "De l'omnipotence de la majorité" — Tocqueville's most famous chapter. Contains the "tyranny of the majority" argument, the passage about democratic despotism going "straight for the soul," and the quotes from Madison and Jefferson. This requires particular care. Key terms to maintain: "tyranny of the majority," "the majority's absolute power" for "omnipotence de la majorité." The passage where the master says "You are free not to think as I do..." is one of the most quoted in the entire work — translate with full rhetorical force.
