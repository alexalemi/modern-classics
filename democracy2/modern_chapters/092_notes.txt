Translation Notes — Chapter 092 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XVI: Why American National Vanity Is More Restless and Quarrelsome Than That of the English)

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "vanité" vs. "orgueil" — "vanity" vs. "pride." Tocqueville makes a crucial distinction: "vanité" is needy, outward-directed, seeking validation; "orgueil" is self-sufficient, inward-directed, already sure of itself. Americans have vanity; the English have pride. Maintained this distinction consistently.

2. "inquiète et envieuse" — "restless and envious." "Inquiète" = restless/anxious (per the text analysis, "restless" is preferred over "anxious").

3. "Quêteuse et querelleuse" — "needy and quarrelsome." The French has a nice rhyme (quêteuse/querelleuse) that doesn't perfectly transfer to English. "Needy and quarrelsome" captures the meaning.

4. The dialogue with the American — This is one of Tocqueville's most entertaining passages. Translated the dialogue naturally, preserving the escalating humor of the American's responses. The American keeps one-upping every compliment with a boast that turns it into evidence of American superiority.

5. "Il se tient vis-à-vis du monde entier dans une réserve pleine de dédain et d'ignorance" — "He stands before the entire world in a reserve full of disdain and ignorance." The English simply don't know or care about the rest of the world. Tocqueville captures this perfectly.

6. "Son orgueil n'a pas besoin d'aliment; il vit sur lui-même" — "His pride needs no nourishment; it feeds on itself." A beautiful epigram.

7. Omitted "[TN-C]" reference — a Gutenberg editorial mark, per text analysis instructions.

TONE:
Comic and sociological at once. Tocqueville's portrait of the insecure American patriot is both affectionate and devastatingly accurate. The comparison with court behavior among aristocrats is a masterstroke — it shows that vanity is not democratic per se, but a function of insecurity about status.
