Chapter 045 (Vol II, Part 1, Ch XI) Translation Notes

This chapter examines how democratic conditions shape the arts — both useful and fine. The argument: in aristocracies, few buyers demand perfection; in democracies, many buyers accept imperfection. Democracy doesn't kill art — it changes its character, favoring quantity over quality, appearance over substance.

Key decisions:
- "arts" — Tocqueville uses this word to mean both crafts/trades and fine arts. Context makes clear which is meant. He explicitly signals the shift: "Ceci me conduit à parler de ceux des arts qu'on a nommés, par excellence, les beaux-arts."
- "médiocrité savante qui se juge elle-même" — "skillful mediocrity that judges itself clearly" — this is one of Tocqueville's most brilliant phrases about democratic craft. The worker KNOWS they could do better but deliberately holds back.
- "l'hypocrisie de la vertu est de tous les temps; celle du luxe appartient plus particulièrement aux siècles démocratiques" — preserved this epigrammatic parallel. One of the chapter's most quotable lines.
- The New York passage (whitewashed brick and painted wood columns) is a wonderful piece of concrete reportage amid the theoretical argument. Kept it vivid and direct.
- "David et ses élèves" — Jacques-Louis David, the neoclassical painter. Left as "David" since he's well-known enough.
- "Raphaël" — standardized to "Raphael" per English convention.
- "le paysan lui-même aime mieux se priver entièrement des objets qu'il convoite, que de les acquérir imparfaits" — "even the peasant would rather go entirely without the things he covets than acquire them in an imperfect form." This nicely captures the aristocratic mentality.

Consistency notes for next chapter:
- "monuments" in the next chapter means both physical monuments and any large-scale construction/building project
- Tocqueville's observation about democratic extremes (tiny private things vs. gigantic public things) directly follows from this chapter's argument
