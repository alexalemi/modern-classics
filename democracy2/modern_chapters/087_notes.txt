Translation Notes — Chapter 087 (Part 3, Chapter XI: How equality of conditions helps maintain good morals in America)

A long, analytical chapter arguing that equality promotes sexual morality through multiple mechanisms: free choice of spouse (so no one has excuses for infidelity), practical demands of democratic life (no leisure for affairs), and the channeling of passion into commerce and industry. The final section about France's transitional moral disorder is a characteristically Tocquevillian argument: the revolution's short-term moral chaos will give way to long-term moral improvement.

Key decisions:
- Opening joke about climate and morals: "se tirer d'affaire à bon marché" → "an easy way out." Tocqueville is dismissing the Montesquieu-derived theory that climate determines sexual behavior. Kept the dismissive tone.
- "bonnes moeurs" → "good morals." In this chapter, "moeurs" specifically refers to sexual morality and marital fidelity, so "morals" is the right choice (rather than the broader "customs" used elsewhere).
- "unions passagères et clandestines" → "fleeting, secret liaisons." More evocative than "temporary and clandestine unions."
- "le mari y est pris à l'école et la femme en nourrice" → "the husband is taken from the schoolroom and the wife from the nursery." Tocqueville's image of child marriages arranged between aristocratic families. Vivid parallel preserved.
- Footnote 4 (about European vs. American novels): Important cultural observation — European novelists must excuse adultery through sympathetic framing of unhappy marriages; American novelists cannot do this, so they simply avoid the topic. This explains why so few novels were published in early America.
- "galanterie" → "widespread infidelity" rather than "gallantry." In 19th-century French, "galanterie" meant sexual adventurism/casual affairs, not courtly behavior. The contrast with "prostitution" makes the meaning clear: open prostitution is less socially dangerous than the normalization of affairs among respectable people.
- "rasant la terre" → "close to the ground." The image of democratic imagination flying low, unable to soar, is characteristic of Tocqueville's ambivalent view of democracy's effect on the inner life.
- The final section on the old French aristocracy being moralized by the Revolution: this is one of Tocqueville's most paradoxical and penetrating observations. The formerly dissolute nobles, stripped of power and leisure, discovered bourgeois virtues — while the bourgeoisie, caught up in revolutionary upheaval, became temporarily dissolute. Translated clearly to preserve the irony.
