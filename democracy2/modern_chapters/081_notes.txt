Translation Notes — Chapter 081 (Part 3, Chapter V: How democracy changes servant-master relations)

This is the longest and most analytically complex chapter in this batch. Tocqueville develops three ideal types: the aristocratic servant (bound by tradition, absorbed into the master's identity), the democratic servant (contractual, temporary, dignified), and the transitional/revolutionary servant (confused, resentful). The final distinction — between democratic and revolutionary — is crucial to Tocqueville's entire project.

Key decisions:
- "domesticité" → "domestic service" throughout. Not "domesticity" (which means home life in English).
- "laquais" → "lackey." This is the direct English cognate and carries the right connotations of servile degradation.
- "honneur servile" → "servile honor." An oxymoron that Tocqueville intends — he's showing that even within servitude, aristocratic structures produce their own dignity codes.
- Footnote 2 (about servants mirroring feudal pride): included as [Note: ...] per the style guide.
- "il se déserte en quelque sorte" → "he deserts himself, in a way." Striking metaphor — the servant abandons his own selfhood to inhabit the master's identity.
- "grandeur empruntée" → "borrowed greatness."
- Military analogy (soldiers equal off-duty, obedient on-duty): kept tight and clear — this is Tocqueville's best illustration of the democratic servant-master contract.
- "affranchis" → "freed slaves." In context, Tocqueville is specifically discussing Black servants in the North who are former slaves or their descendants. "Emancipated" would be too sanitized.
- The final section on the revolutionary/transitional period: "malveillant et doux" / "malveillant et indocile" — "ill-tempered and lenient" / "ill-tempered and defiant." Both master and servant are resentful but express it differently.
- Last line: "Un pareil état n'est pas démocratique, mais révolutionnaire" — translated as a clean, punchy closing. This distinction is one of Tocqueville's most important conceptual moves.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 082 is about rents and leases — how democratic conditions raise rents and shorten lease terms. The agricultural/economic content is specific but Tocqueville uses it to illustrate broader themes: the shift from personal relationships to cash transactions, and the instability of democratic life. The final image of "the human heart" as the most restless thing in a restless age is powerful.
