Translation Notes -- Chapter 009 (Chapter V, Part 2: State Government, Decentralization)

This is the second half of Chapter V, moving from institutional detail to political philosophy. It contains Tocqueville's crucial distinction between governmental and administrative centralization, and some of his most passionate rhetoric in defense of local liberty. The portrait of the apathetic European citizen is devastating and still resonant.

Key decisions:

- "centralisation gouvernementale" -> "governmental centralization" and "centralisation administrative" -> "administrative centralization." These are Tocqueville's key analytical terms. I kept them as technical terms throughout.

- "esprit de cite" -> "civic spirit." In the passage about administrative centralization diminishing "l'esprit de cite," this captures the idea of active citizenship better than "civic feeling" or "public spirit."

- "L'Etat, c'est moi" -> kept in French with English translation: "'I am the State,' he said." This is Louis XIV's famous (possibly apocryphal) phrase; the French is well enough known that the English alone is sufficient.

- The long passage about the European citizen as "une espece de colon indifferent a la destinee du lieu qu'il habite" -> "a kind of settler, indifferent to the fate of the place he lives." "Colon" here means colonist/settler, not "colonist" in the imperial sense. The metaphor is that the person lives in his own country as though he were a stranger passing through.

- "un etranger puissant qu'on appelle le gouvernement" -> "a powerful stranger called 'the government.'" This is one of Tocqueville's most biting phrases. I preserved the scare-quote effect.

- "on y trouve encore des sujets, mais on n'y voit plus de citoyens" -> "you still find subjects there, but you no longer see citizens." The subject/citizen distinction is central to Tocqueville's argument.

- The China footnote is fascinating and prophetic. I translated it fully. "Les Chinois ont de la tranquillite sans bonheur, de l'industrie sans progres, de la stabilite sans force, et de l'ordre materiel sans moralite publique" -> four parallel contrasts, kept exactly.

- "moeurs" -> "customs" or "customs and values" depending on context. In the final section on the French Revolution, "il faut qu'elles modifient leurs lois et leurs moeurs" -> "they must either reform their laws and their customs."

- "lumieres" -> "education" where it refers to general knowledge and enlightenment; "knowledge" in more specific contexts.

- The passage comparing centralized and decentralized administration ("Elle excelle, en un mot, a empecher, non a faire") -> "In a word, it excels at preventing, not at doing." Clean, punchy translation of one of Tocqueville's best epigrammatic lines.

- Preserved the long rhetorical question about the authority that removes thorns from your path but is "maitresse absolue de ma liberte et de ma vie" -- this is passionate first-person Tocqueville at his most urgent. I kept it as a single building sentence.

- Omitted Gutenberg editorial references (_I_, _K_).

- All Tocqueville footnotes included as [Note: ...].
