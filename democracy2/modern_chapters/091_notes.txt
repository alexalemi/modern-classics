Translation Notes — Chapter 091 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XV: On the Seriousness of Americans)

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "gravité" — "seriousness" throughout. Not "gravity" (which sounds too solemn in English) or "solemnity." Tocqueville means a habitual earnestness, not pompousness.

2. "il s'enivre décemment en famille" — "he gets respectably drunk with his family." A delightful touch of Tocqueville's dry humor. "Décemment" = respectably/decently — the irony is intentional.

3. "J'ai vu les Américains et j'ai changé d'opinion" — "I saw the Americans and changed my mind." Short, punchy, perfect as-is.

4. "Il n'a guère le temps, et il perd bientôt le goût d'approfondir" — "He has little time, and he soon loses the taste for going deep." Kept this as its own short paragraph, matching the French structure. The brevity is the point.

5. "L'habitude de l'inattention doit être considérée comme le plus grand vice de l'esprit démocratique" — "The habit of inattention must be considered the greatest flaw of the democratic mind." A devastating closing sentence. "Vice" could be "vice" but "flaw" is more natural for "the democratic mind" in English.

6. "des étourderies singulières" — "remarkably foolish things." "Étourderies" = thoughtless blunders, rashness. "Remarkably foolish things" captures the slightly amused tone.

7. The analogy about speaking to crowds vs. silence — one of Tocqueville's most brilliant observations about information overload. The metaphor of whispers in silence vs. words lost in crowds anticipates modern media theory.

TONE:
Wry and observational, with Tocqueville's characteristic dry humor (the drinking American, the changed opinion about the English). The chapter builds to a serious sociological point about the paradox of democratic seriousness coexisting with democratic recklessness.
