Translation Notes — Chapter 098 (Chapter XXII: Democratic peoples desire peace, democratic armies desire war)

CONTEXT:
This is one of Tocqueville's most penetrating chapters — a brilliant analysis of the structural tension between democratic societies (which want peace) and democratic armies (which want war). The paradox arises from equality itself: the same force that makes citizens love peace and commerce also makes soldiers crave advancement that only war can provide. Tocqueville sees this as one of democracy's most dangerous internal contradictions.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "L'esprit militaire et l'esprit révolutionnaire s'affaiblissent en même temps et par les mêmes causes" — "the military spirit and the revolutionary spirit weaken together, and for the same reasons." Kept the parallel structure clean.

2. "Cette mansuétude des moeurs, cette mollesse de coeur" — "the gentleness of customs, the softness of heart." "Mansuétude" is a gentleness/mildness; "mollesse" is softness bordering on weakness. Kept both distinct rather than collapsing them.

3. "Le droit de l'ancienneté, qui est le seul privilége naturel à la démocratie" — "the rule of seniority — the only natural privilege in a democracy." "Droit" here means rule/right of seniority. The dash adds emphasis to this sharp epigram.

4. "Conséquence singulière" — "striking paradox." "Singulière" here means remarkable/paradoxical rather than literally "singular." "Paradox" captures the logical tension Tocqueville is highlighting.

5. "L'inquiétude du coeur" — "the restlessness of the heart." Consistent with the text analysis vocabulary mapping.

6. "Prolétaires" — "men with nothing." Tocqueville uses the term not in the Marxist sense but simply to mean people without property. "Men with nothing" conveys the practical meaning.

7. "Cette petite nation incivilisée possède les armes, et seule elle sait s'en servir" — "And this small, uncivilized nation possesses the weapons, and it alone knows how to use them." Preserved the ominous tone of this striking observation.

8. "À la manière de Sylla et de César" — "in the manner of Sulla or Caesar." Used standard English spellings of the Roman names.

9. "Ayez des citoyens éclairés, réglés, fermes et libres, et vous aurez des soldats disciplinés et obéissants" — "Give me educated, disciplined, steady, and free citizens, and you will have disciplined and obedient soldiers." Changed the impersonal "have" imperative to "Give me" for a more conversational English feel while preserving the direct address.

TONE:
This chapter oscillates between analytical cool and urgent warning. Tocqueville is at his most politically practical here — diagnosing a structural problem and then systematically rejecting easy solutions (war, army expansion) before arriving at the true remedy: civil liberty itself. The final paradox — that the cure for military danger is more freedom, not less — is quintessential Tocqueville.
