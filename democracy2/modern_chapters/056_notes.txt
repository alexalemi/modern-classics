Translation Notes -- Chapter 056 (Vol. II, Part 2, Ch. I: Why Democratic Peoples Show a More Passionate and Enduring Love for Equality Than for Liberty)

CONTEXT:
This is the opening chapter of Volume II, Part 2 -- "Democracy's Influence on Feelings." Tocqueville sets up the central tension of the entire Part: democratic peoples love both equality and liberty, but they love equality more, and this asymmetry has profound consequences. The chapter is one of his most famous analytical pieces.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "égalité des conditions" -> "equality of social conditions." Following the text analysis consistently. This is Tocqueville's core concept.

2. "moeurs" -> "customs and values" in the passage about what would need to change to destroy equality ("altérât ses moeurs"). The word encompasses habits, values, and social norms.

3. "délire" -> "frenzy." The French word literally means delirium, but "frenzy" captures the passionate, almost irrational quality Tocqueville describes without sounding clinical.

4. "lutte intestine" -> "internal struggle." Direct and clear.

5. "nous-mêmes" -> "ourselves." Tocqueville shifts from discussing all democratic nations to specifically France/Europe. Preserved this pivot clearly.

6. The magnificent passage near the end -- "ils veulent l'égalité dans la liberté, et, s'ils ne peuvent l'obtenir, ils la veulent encore dans l'esclavage" -- translated as "They want equality in liberty, and if they cannot have it, they want it even in slavery." This is one of Tocqueville's most famous lines. Kept it punchy and direct.

7. "les âmes les plus vulgaires" -> "the most ordinary souls." "Vulgaire" in 19th-century French means common/ordinary, not vulgar in the modern English sense.

8. The final chiasmus -- liberty cannot establish itself without equality, despotism cannot reign without it -- preserved the mirror structure.

TONE:
Analytical but building to passionate rhetoric. The chapter starts calmly, with careful distinctions, then crescendos into the devastating passage about people blindly surrendering liberty for equality. The final paragraphs about France have a personal urgency.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:
- Chapter 057 introduces "individualism" -- Tocqueville's famous coinage. The term should be kept as "individualism" and its distinction from "egoism" (égoïsme) must be sharp.
- "conditions s'égalisent" -> "conditions become more equal" (established pattern)
- Maintain the analytical-to-passionate arc that characterizes this section.
