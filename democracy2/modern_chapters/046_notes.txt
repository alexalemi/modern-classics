Chapter 046 (Vol II, Part 1, Ch XII) Translation Notes

A short, punchy chapter making a paradoxical argument: democracies produce both tiny and gigantic works, but nothing in between. Individuals think small; the state thinks enormous. Tocqueville also cautions against reading too much into grand monuments — they may reveal ignorance as much as greatness.

Key decisions:
- "Pontoise" — kept as-is. A small town near Paris; the comparison is meant to be deflating and humorous. American readers may not know it, but the point is clear from context.
- "dix lieues" — "ten leagues" — kept the period unit rather than converting to miles or kilometers. A league is roughly 2.5 miles.
- "Capitole" — "the Capitol" — straightforward English equivalent.
- "ces longs rochers artificiels qu'on nomme des voies romaines" — "those long artificial ridges of stone we call Roman roads." Tocqueville's phrase "longs rochers artificiels" (long artificial rocks) is wonderfully defamiliarizing — making us see Roman roads as if for the first time. Rendered as "artificial ridges of stone" to preserve this effect.
- "tuyaux de plomb" / "tringles de fer" — "lead pipes" / "iron rods" — Tocqueville is gesturing toward modern industrial infrastructure (plumbing, railroads) as being aesthetically modest but technologically superior to Roman aqueducts and roads.
- The final line is characteristically Tocquevillian: a quiet epigram that inverts expectations.

Consistency notes for next chapter:
- This chapter is a brief interlude. The next chapter (XIII) is a major piece — Tocqueville's analysis of the literary character of democratic ages. Much longer and more complex.
