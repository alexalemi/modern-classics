Translation Notes — Chapter 096 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XX: On the Pursuit of Government Jobs in Certain Democratic Nations)

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "l'industrie des places" — "the pursuit of government jobs." The French literally means "the industry of positions/posts." Tocqueville uses "industrie" ironically — job-seeking as a kind of industry. Translated as "pursuit of government jobs" for the chapter title, and varied the phrasing in the text.

2. "solliciteurs" — "office-seekers." A people of "solliciteurs" = a people of office-seekers/petitioners.

3. "un patrimoine" — "a patrimony." Civil servants treat their government positions as though they were inherited estates. The word choice is deliberate — it links bureaucratic careerism to aristocratic inheritance.

4. "l'art de se suffire à lui-même" — "the art of providing for themselves." The chapter's punch line: instead of creating dependency, rulers should teach self-reliance.

5. "ne point venir le troubler dans ses labeurs et d'en assurer le fruit" — "to not interfere with his labors and to protect what he earns." A pithy summary of the American relationship to government. Nearly libertarian in tone.

TONE:
Warning and analytical. Tocqueville is clearly thinking about France, not America. The American section is brief and serves as the positive counterexample. The chapter is essentially a warning to European rulers: creating dependency through patronage will backfire. Remarkably applicable to modern debates about the size of government.
