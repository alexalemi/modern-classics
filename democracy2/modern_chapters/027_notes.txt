Translation Notes — Chapter 027 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter X, Part 2: Black Americans, first half)

CONTEXT:
This section transitions from the Native American discussion to the situation of Black Americans. It is one of the most analytically brilliant and morally searching passages in the entire book. Tocqueville makes several arguments that were extraordinary for 1835: (1) that racial prejudice is worse in free states than slave states; (2) that slavery economically ruins the master; (3) that abolition, as practiced in the North, does not liberate the slave but merely transfers him South. The Ohio River passage — comparing the left bank (Kentucky, slave) to the right bank (Ohio, free) — is deservedly famous.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Nègre" — Consistently translated as "Black man," "Black people," or "Black" throughout, matching the decision from chapter 026.

2. "Le souvenir de l'esclavage déshonore la race, et la race perpétue le souvenir de l'esclavage" — Translated as "The memory of slavery dishonors the race, and the race perpetuates the memory of slavery." This chiasmus is crucial — it's Tocqueville's most compressed statement of the vicious circle of racial prejudice. Preserved the mirror structure exactly.

3. "Peu s'en faut que nous ne le prenions pour un être intermédiaire entre la brute et l'homme" — "We come close to regarding him as a being halfway between the brute and the human." Tocqueville is describing white attitudes, not endorsing them. The "we" is deliberately implicating — he includes himself and his French readers in the critique. Kept the "we."

4. "La Belle-Rivière" — Identified parenthetically that this is the Indian name for the Ohio, meaning "Beautiful River."

5. "Lumières" — In this chapter, translated variously as "knowledge," "education," "light of experience" depending on context.

6. "Ouvrier libre" — "Free worker" throughout. "Laborer" would also work but "worker" is more neutral and modern.

7. The inheritance law passage — Tocqueville's argument about primogeniture creating an idle aristocracy in the South, and its abolition creating free labor competition, is subtle. Translated carefully to preserve the logical chain.

TONE:
Tocqueville is at his most analytical here, building an economic and sociological argument about why slavery is being abolished (not for moral reasons but for economic ones). His moral judgment is implicit rather than explicit — the devastating contrast at the Ohio says more than any sermon could. The passage about prejudice being stronger in free states than slave states is delivered with cool factual precision that makes it all the more damning.
