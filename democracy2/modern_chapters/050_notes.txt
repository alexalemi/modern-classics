Translation Notes — Chapter 050 (Vol. II, Part 1, Ch. XVI)

Title: "Comment la démocratie américaine a modifié la langue anglaise" — "How American Democracy Has Modified the English Language."

Key decisions:
- "le premier instrument de la pensée" — "the primary instrument of thought." Direct and clear.
- "les classes éclairées" — "the educated classes." The French "éclairées" (literally "enlightened") is best rendered as "educated" in this context per the text analysis.
- "le jargon des partis, les arts mécaniques, la langue des affaires" — "the jargon of political parties, the mechanical arts, the language of business." Kept close to the French tricolon.
- "le repos où se tiennent toutes choses" — "the stillness that pervades everything else." Captures the stasis Tocqueville describes.
- "la chute de Constantinople" — kept as historical reference without footnote; widely known.
- "nos danseurs de corde se sont transformés en acrobates et en funambules" — "our rope dancers transformed themselves into acrobats and funambulists." Kept "funambulists" since Tocqueville's point is precisely that such pompous Latinate terms are absurd replacements.
- "une situation ambulatoire" — "a wandering state." The word retains its sense of rootlessness.
- "des mots chinois, tartares ou hurons" — "Chinese, Tatar, or Huron words." Kept as written; Tocqueville is making a rhetorical point about preferring foreign borrowings to semantic confusion.
- "une boîte à double fond" — "a box with a false bottom." The French idiom refers to a magician's trick box.
- "les capacités" / "les actualités" / "les éventualités" — Kept in the translated text as "capabilities" / "current events" / "contingencies" with explanations, matching Tocqueville's own glossing.

Style note: This chapter is one of Tocqueville's most linguistically self-aware passages. He is remarkably candid about his own democratic writing habits (using "equality" as a personified agent). Preserved the self-deprecating humor throughout.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 051 is on the sources of poetry in democratic nations. Tocqueville argues democracy dries up traditional poetic subjects (gods, heroes, the past) but opens new ones (humanity as a whole, the future, the inner life). The tone is more elevated and lyrical than the language chapter. Watch for his famous passage about man emerging from nothingness, crossing time, and vanishing into God.
