Translation Notes — Chapter 016 (Vol. I, Tome 2, Ch. III: On Freedom of the Press in the United States)

Key decisions:

- The great rhetorical passage on the impossibility of a middle ground for press freedom (second long paragraph): This is one of Tocqueville's virtuoso performances — a step-by-step walk from liberty through successive restrictions to despotism. Preserved the accumulating structure and the refrain "vous avez donc fait trop et trop peu" ("so you have done too much and too little"). Kept it as one flowing argument rather than breaking it into small paragraphs.

- "Vous étiez parti des abus de la liberté, et je vous retrouve sous les pieds d'un despote" — "You set out from the abuses of liberty, and I find you at the feet of a despot." The shift from "vous" to "je" in the French is deliberate — Tocqueville steps in as witness. Preserved this.

- "licence de la presse" — "unruliness of the press" in the opening of the argument, "license of the press" later when discussing political effects. The French "licence" means both excess/unruliness and legal permission; context determined the choice.

- "tribun" — "demagogue." The French "tribun" refers to a political orator/agitator, not specifically a Roman tribune. "Demagogue" captures the negative sense Tocqueville intends here (a people that "croit le premier tribun qui se présente" — naively trusts the first agitator).

- "lumières" — "knowledge" in "les lumières comme la puissance sont disséminées" (knowledge and power are dispersed). Follows the text analysis guideline.

- "patentes pour les imprimeurs, de timbre ni d'enregistrement" — "licensing requirements for printers, stamp taxes or registration fees." These are specific French/European regulatory mechanisms that did not exist in the US. Translated as the institutional equivalents.

- "l'esprit du barreau, l'esprit de cour" — "the spirit of the bar, the spirit of the court." Here "esprit" means the collective mentality/ethos of a profession; kept "spirit" as it works well in English.

- The Vincennes Gazette excerpt: Translated the French rendering of this originally-English newspaper article. Tocqueville says he is translating it "faithfully" from English to French; I've rendered his French version into English, not attempted to recover the original English text.

- "moeurs" — "customs and values" in the opening paragraph where it contrasts with "lois"; "morals" in "goût et la moralité du peuple américain" where the French itself uses "moralité."

- The closing section on tenacity of opinions under press freedom: This is a subtle philosophical passage about epistemology. "L'ignorance était aux deux bouts de la science" — "ignorance lay at both ends of knowledge." Tocqueville is making a point about the U-curve of conviction: the ignorant believe firmly, the learned believe firmly, and doubters are stuck in the middle.

- "vous êtes riche et je suis pauvre" — "you are rich and I am poor." A brilliantly blunt ending. Tocqueville's point: the argument between democracy and aristocracy ultimately reduces to material interest. Left exactly as is.

- Footnote 1 included as [Note: ...] inline. Gutenberg reference marks (_A_) omitted per guidelines.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 017 is about political associations. Tocqueville distinguishes three degrees of association (public declaration, physical assembly, representative convention). He draws a sustained comparison between European and American associations — European ones are military in character, American ones are civil/persuasive. The key argument is that unlimited freedom of association is less dangerous in America because of universal suffrage (associations know they don't represent the majority). Preserve the three-tier structure clearly. "Associations" should stay as "associations" — don't substitute "organizations" or "groups."
