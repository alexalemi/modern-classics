Translation Notes — Chapter 101 (Chapter XXV: Discipline in democratic armies)

CONTEXT:
A short, punchy chapter that demolishes the aristocratic claim that equality destroys military discipline. Tocqueville distinguishes between two types of discipline: blind/mechanical (aristocratic) and intelligent/voluntary (democratic). He argues the democratic kind is actually superior, citing ancient Greek and Roman armies as proof. The chapter ends with a devastating one-liner about Alexander and Rome.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Le soldat est plié à la discipline militaire" — "The soldier is molded to military discipline." "Plié" literally means "bent/folded" — "molded" captures the idea of being shaped into submission.

2. "Ce n'est plus un homme, mais c'est encore un animal très-redoutable dressé à la guerre" — "he is no longer a man, but he is still a very formidable animal trained for war." A shockingly blunt image. Tocqueville is not complimenting aristocratic discipline here — he's exposing its dehumanizing nature.

3. "Le libre essor des âmes" — "the free spirit of men." "Essor" means soaring/flight — "free spirit" captures the sense of aspiration and energy that democracy produces.

4. "Plus impétueuse et plus intelligente" — "more spirited and more intelligent." "Impétueuse" suggests energy, fire, élan — "spirited" conveys this without the negative connotation "impetuous" would carry in English.

5. "Une sorte de confraternité familière" — "a kind of familiar brotherhood." Direct and natural.

6. "On dirait des compagnons autant que des chefs" — "They were companions as much as commanders." Preserved the alliterative pair ("compagnons/chefs" becomes "companions/commanders").

7. The closing sentence about Alexander and Rome — This is one of Tocqueville's finest mic-drop endings. Kept it exactly as sharp as the original. The implicit argument: drill-sergeant discipline matters less than fighting spirit, and democracies produce the latter naturally.

TONE:
Brisk and argumentative. Tocqueville opens with a common objection, dismisses it as "an error," and then builds his counter-argument with increasing force. The chapter has a debater's rhythm — thesis, rebuttal, evidence, knockout conclusion. The reference to Plutarch gives it scholarly authority; the Alexander/Rome punchline gives it rhetorical triumph.
