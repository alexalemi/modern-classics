Translation Notes — Chapter 110 (Volume II, Part 4, Chapter VII)
"Continuation of the preceding chapters."

This is the prescriptive counterpart to the prophetic Chapter 109. Having painted the nightmare of soft despotism, Tocqueville now asks: how do we resist it? His answers are concrete and practical — associations, press freedom, judiciary, formal procedures, individual rights — while maintaining the philosophical weight of the preceding chapter.

Key decisions:
- "J'aurais, je pense, aimé la liberté dans tous les temps; mais je me sens enclin à l'adorer dans le temps où nous sommes" → "I would have loved liberty, I think, in all times; but I feel inclined to worship it in the time in which we live" — "adorer" is deliberately religious language; kept "worship"
- "leur sainte entreprise" → "their sacred enterprise" — Tocqueville means it: defending freedom is a moral calling
- "faire sortir la liberté du sein de la société démocratique où Dieu nous fait vivre" → "make liberty emerge from within the democratic society where God has placed us" — this is Tocqueville's fundamental statement of purpose
- "des personnes aristocratiques" → "aristocratic persons" — Tocqueville is using "persons" in the legal sense (juridical entities) for associations
- Press passage: "L'imprimerie a hâté les progrès de l'égalité, et elle est un de ses meilleurs correctifs" → "Printing has accelerated the progress of equality, and it is one of equality's best correctives" — the paradox is key: the same technology advances equality and fights its abuses
- "un culte éclairé et réfléchi" → "an enlightened and deliberate devotion" — regarding formal procedures
- Revolutionary habits passage: "les instincts révolutionnaires, s'adoucissant et se régularisant sans s'éteindre, ne se transforment graduellement en moeurs gouvernementales et en habitudes administratives" → "revolutionary instincts — softening and regularizing themselves without dying out — may gradually transform into governmental practices and administrative habits" — critical insight about how radicalism becomes bureaucratic
- "une nation ne peut rester longtemps forte quand chaque homme y est individuellement faible" → "a nation cannot long remain strong when every person in it is individually weak" — one of the book's great aphorisms
- Final paragraph: "cette crainte salutaire qui fait veiller et combattre, et non cette sorte de terreur molle et oisive qui abat les coeurs et les énerve" → "that salutary fear that makes us watchful and ready to fight — and not that soft and idle terror that demoralizes the heart and saps it of all strength" — a rallying cry that sets up the grand conclusion

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 111 is the final chapter of the entire work — Tocqueville's grand summation. It is relatively brief but extraordinarily powerful. The key passage is the closing: "Les nations de nos jours ne sauraient faire que dans leur sein les conditions ne soient pas égales; mais il dépend d'elles que l'égalité les conduise à la servitude ou à la liberté." Providence sets limits, but within those limits, peoples choose. The passage about God's perspective vs. human perspective is remarkable — translate with appropriate gravity.
