Translation Notes — Chapter 085 (Part 3, Chapter IX: Education of young women in the United States)

Tocqueville's admiring portrait of how Americans educate young women — by giving them freedom, knowledge, and self-reliance rather than sheltering them. His comparative framework: European (especially French) girls are over-protected then thrown into the world unprepared; American girls are equipped from the start to navigate democratic society's freedoms and risks.

Key decisions:
- "moeurs" in the opening → "customs." Here Tocqueville uses "moeurs" in the broad sense of social habits and values. The line "c'est la femme qui fait les moeurs" is a famous claim — "it is women who shape customs."
- "jeune fille" → "young woman" or "girl" depending on context. The French "jeune fille" covers both teenager and young unmarried woman. Used "girl" for the earlier stages, "young woman" for the more mature descriptions.
- "âge nubile" → "marriageable age." Direct equivalent.
- "candeur virginale au milieu des naissants désirs" → "virginal innocence in the midst of awakening desires." Tocqueville is describing what American girls lack compared to European girls — naive innocence. Translated directly.
- "moeurs pures plutôt qu'un esprit chaste" → "pure morals rather than a chaste mind." An important distinction: the American girl behaves well by choice and knowledge, not out of ignorance.
- "philosophe aurait bronché cent fois" → "a philosopher would have stumbled a hundred times." The image of young women navigating racy conversations more deftly than philosophers is one of Tocqueville's most charming observations.
- "éducation timide, retirée et presque claustrale" → "timid, sheltered, almost cloistered education." The monastic metaphor ("claustrale") captures how French aristocratic girls were raised.
- "armer sa raison" → "arm her reason." A martial metaphor Tocqueville uses deliberately — reason is the weapon that democracy gives women in place of the walls that aristocracy built around them.
- Omitted the [TN-B] Gutenberg editor's marks per the text analysis guidelines.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 086 is about how the young American woman transforms into a wife — embracing tight marital duties after enjoying broad youthful freedom. This apparent paradox is Tocqueville's point: freedom in youth produces the strength of will needed for self-imposed discipline in marriage. The frontier passage (young women following husbands into the wilderness) is vivid and moving.
