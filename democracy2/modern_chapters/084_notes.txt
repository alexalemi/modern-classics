Translation Notes — Chapter 084 (Part 3, Chapter VIII: Democracy's influence on the family)

A major chapter with one of the most famous closing lines in the book. Tocqueville argues that democracy weakens formal, institutional family authority but strengthens natural emotional bonds — a pattern consistent with his broader thesis about democracy replacing artificial social structures with natural ones.

Key decisions:
- "famille, en prenant ce mot dans son sens romain et aristocratique" → "the family — in the Roman and aristocratic sense of the word." Tocqueville means the familia as a political and economic unit centered on the paterfamilias, not the modern nuclear family.
- "dictature domestique" → "domestic dictatorship." Deliberately strong language that Tocqueville uses without irony — the father's total authority over small children is necessary and justified.
- "virilité" → "manhood." The French "virilité" here means adulthood/maturity, not masculinity per se.
- Footnote 3 (about Napoleon's civil code): This is an important aside. Tocqueville is making the point that Napoleon deliberately democratized civil/family law while keeping political law authoritarian — a compromise that could not last. "Un homme qui voyait son intérêt..." clearly refers to Napoleon without naming him.
- "se faisaient tutoyer par leurs enfants" → "let their children address them informally." The French "tutoyer" (using "tu" instead of "vous") is a powerful social marker. Explained the significance through "informally" since English lacks this formal/informal pronoun distinction.
- "Le maître et le magistrat ont disparu; le père reste" → "The master and the magistrate have disappeared; the father remains." One of Tocqueville's best one-liners. Kept it clean and punchy.
- Closing sentence: "La démocratie détend les liens sociaux, mais elle resserre les liens naturels" → "Democracy loosens social bonds, but it tightens natural ones." This is the chapter's thesis in epigrammatic form. Preserved the chiastic structure.
- "Elle rapproche les parents dans le même temps qu'elle sépare les citoyens" → "It brings family members closer together at the very moment it pushes citizens apart." "Parents" here means "relatives/family members" not just "parents" in the English sense.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 085 is about the education of young women in America. Tocqueville admires the American approach of giving young women freedom and knowledge rather than sheltering them — arguing this produces strong, clear-eyed women better equipped for democratic life. The chapter reflects 1830s gender norms but Tocqueville's observations are genuinely admiring of American women's agency and intelligence.
