Translation Notes -- Chapter 059 (Vol. II, Part 2, Ch. IV: How Americans Combat Individualism with Free Institutions)

CONTEXT:
This is the remedy chapter. After diagnosing individualism (057) and showing how it's worse after revolutions (058), Tocqueville now explains the cure: free institutions, especially local government. The argument is that political participation forces people out of their private bubbles. This is one of Tocqueville's most influential chapters -- the foundation of modern arguments about civic engagement, social capital, and participatory democracy.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "esprits turbulents et inquiets" -> "troublemakers and agitators." The French literally says "turbulent and restless spirits," but this is what a despot calls them -- it's a label, not a description. Translated as the kind of dismissive terms a tyrant would actually use.

2. "changeant le sens naturel des mots" -> "twisting the natural meaning of words." Tocqueville notes how despots corrupt language. This observation anticipates Orwell by a century.

3. "se complaire" -> "to please one another." The word means to take pleasure in each other's company, to accommodate.

4. "faut-il faire passer un chemin au bout de son domaine" -> "if you need to build a road past the edge of his property." This is one of Tocqueville's most vivid examples -- the road that makes abstract governance concrete. Translated with maximum clarity.

5. "action d'éclat" -> "a brilliant deed." Clean, direct.

6. "la simplicité des manières a des charmes presque irrésistibles" -> "simplicity of manner has an almost irresistible charm." Preserved the elegance.

7. "Ce n'est pas le sacrifice de leur argent qu'elle leur demande; c'est celui de leur orgueil." -> "What the people ask of them is not the sacrifice of their money; it is the sacrifice of their pride." One of Tocqueville's sharpest observations about democratic social dynamics. Kept the antithesis clean.

8. "Ce qui était calcul devient instinct" -> "What began as calculation becomes instinct." This concise formulation captures Tocqueville's theory of how self-interest transforms into civic habit.

9. Final paragraph: "Et moi je dis que..." -> "And I say that..." The emphatic "moi" (literally "and me, I say") shows Tocqueville stepping forward to disagree directly. Preserved the personal force.

TONE:
Prescriptive and energetic. Tocqueville is arguing for something he believes in passionately -- the power of free institutions to cure democratic isolation. The chapter builds through examples (local government, elections, the road past your property) toward the ringing conclusion.
