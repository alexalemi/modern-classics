Translation Notes -- Chapter 063 (Vol. II, Part 2, Ch. VIII: How Americans Combat Individualism Through the Doctrine of Self-Interest Properly Understood)

CONTEXT:
One of Tocqueville's most important philosophical chapters. He introduces "l'intérêt bien entendu" -- self-interest properly understood -- as the characteristic American moral philosophy. It's not noble, but it works: by convincing everyone that honesty and public service are in their own interest, it creates a functional, if modest, civic morality. The chapter is a masterpiece of measured praise -- Tocqueville clearly admires the doctrine's effectiveness while noting its limitations.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "intérêt bien entendu" -> "self-interest properly understood." Consistent with text analysis. This is the chapter's central concept. Used throughout.

2. "doctrine officielle" -> "official moral doctrine." Added "moral" for clarity since Tocqueville is discussing ethics specifically.

3. Montaigne quotation: The original is in 16th-century French ("droicture," "suyvray," "droict chemin"). Translated into modern English: "When I would not follow the right path for righteousness' sake, I would follow it for having found by experience that, at the end of the day, it is usually the happiest and the most useful." Preserved the meaning while making it accessible.

4. "l'amour éclairé d'eux-mêmes" -> "enlightened self-love." Clean, precise.

5. "Considérez quelques individus, elle les abaisse. Envisagez l'espèce, elle l'élève." -> "Consider a few individuals: it lowers them. Look at the species as a whole: it raises them." One of Tocqueville's most epigrammatic formulations. Preserved the parallel structure and the punchy rhythm. Two sentences, perfectly balanced.

6. "lumières" -> "education" in the final paragraph. Following text analysis. The context is clearly about educating citizens, not about enlightenment in the abstract.

7. "le siècle des dévouements aveugles et des vertus instinctives" -> "the age of blind devotion and instinctive virtue." Tocqueville sees a historical transition: from an era where people were virtuous without thinking to one where they must be taught to be so.

8. "l'utile n'est jamais déshonnête" / "l'honnête peut être utile" -> "what is useful is never dishonorable" / "what is honorable can be useful." Tocqueville reverses the formula to make his point: everyone teaches that utility is fine, but who teaches that honor is useful? Preserved the chiasmus.

9. "aiguillon" -> "spur." The image is of the same goad that drives the passions being turned to direct them. "Spur" captures both the prodding and the redirection.

TONE:
Philosophically measured, building to urgency. Tocqueville begins as an observer, assesses the doctrine with careful balance (not lofty, but reliable), and ends with a direct appeal: educate people, because the alternative is catastrophe. The chapter moves from description to evaluation to exhortation.
