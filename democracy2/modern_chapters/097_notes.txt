Translation Notes — Chapter 097 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XXI: Why Great Revolutions Will Become Rare)

OVERVIEW:
This is one of Tocqueville's most famous and most prescient chapters. Written in 1840, it argues that mature democracies will tend toward stability, not chaos — that equality breeds conservatism, not revolution. The chapter contains some of his most powerful prose and his most unsettling prophecy: not that democracy will produce too much change, but too little.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "grandes révolutions" — "great revolutions." Not just political upheavals but fundamental transformations of society and thought. Tocqueville uses the term broadly throughout.

2. "biens mobiliers" — "movable property." Financial assets, stocks, commercial property — as opposed to "propriété foncière" (landed property). The distinction matters: holders of movable property are even more risk-averse than landowners because their wealth is more vulnerable.

3. "Il les soulève un moment avec mille efforts, et bientôt ils lui échappent, et comme entraînés par leur propre poids, ils retombent" — "He rouses them for a moment with a thousand efforts, but soon they slip away from him and, as though pulled down by their own weight, they fall back." One of Tocqueville's greatest images — the would-be revolutionary leader trying and failing to lift a democratic people out of its inertia.

4. "À sa fougue, ils opposent en secret leur inertie; à ses instincts révolutionnaires, leurs intérêts conservateurs; leurs goûts casaniers à ses passions aventureuses; leur bon sens aux écarts de son génie; à sa poésie, leur prose" — Preserved this magnificent series of antitheses: passion vs. inertia, revolutionary instincts vs. conservative interests, adventurous passions vs. stay-at-home tastes, genius vs. common sense, poetry vs. prose. The last pair is devastating.

5. "en Europe nous avons encore des passions et des idées révolutionnaires" — "in Europe, we still have revolutionary ideas and passions." Note the chiastic reversal: America has "democratic ideas and passions"; Europe has "revolutionary ideas and passions." The word order is reversed, which was preserved.

6. Note 8 — The long footnote about conditions favorable to intellectual revolutions. Included as [Note: ...]. This is important — Tocqueville identifies a "golden age" of intellectual revolution that lies between rigid caste society and mature equality, an intermediate period when conditions are unequal enough for great minds to influence masses but fluid enough for change to be possible.

7. The prophecy about Black Americans — "elles seront amenées par la présence des noirs sur le sol des États-Unis" — Translated directly. Tocqueville correctly predicted that America's greatest crisis would come from racial inequality, not from equality. Written 21 years before the Civil War.

8. The closing paragraph — One of the most powerful passages in all of Tocqueville. "L'homme s'épuise en petits mouvements solitaires et stériles; et que, tout en se remuant sans cesse, l'humanité n'avance plus." — "humanity will exhaust itself in small, solitary, and sterile movements; and that, for all its ceaseless agitation, it will advance no more." Preserved the rhythm and the devastating final image.

9. "Oserais-je le dire au milieu des ruines qui m'environnent?" — "Dare I say it amid the ruins that surround me?" The "ruins" refer to the ongoing upheaval of European politics. In the midst of revolution, Tocqueville's fear is not revolution but stagnation.

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
The chapter has two major movements: (1) Why democratic peoples resist revolutions (property, commerce, middle-class conservatism, the difficulty of following revolutionary leaders); (2) Why democratic peoples resist changes in ideas (majority pressure, conformism, lack of leisure for new thought, the self-perpetuating power of established opinion). Both converge on the same fear: democratic immobility.

TONE:
Prophetic and deeply personal. Tocqueville speaks with increasing urgency as the chapter progresses, culminating in an almost anguished personal confession: "I tremble — I confess it." The final paragraph is one of the great passages in political philosophy — a vision of humanity in perpetual motion that goes nowhere.
