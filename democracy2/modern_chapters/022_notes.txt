Translation Notes — Chapter 022 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter VIII)
"What Tempers the Tyranny of the Majority in the United States"

This is the longest chapter of the three. It is the counterpart to Chapter VII — after making the case for the tyranny of the majority, Tocqueville now identifies the forces that keep it in check. Three major sections: (1) the absence of administrative centralization, (2) the legal profession as an aristocratic counterweight, (3) the jury as a political institution.

Key decisions:

- "esprit légiste" → "the legal mind." This is the central concept of the chapter. Tocqueville argues that lawyers have a naturally conservative, order-loving, aristocratic mentality that serves as a counterweight to democratic excess. "The legal mind" captures this better than "the lawyer's spirit" or "the legist's mentality." Used consistently throughout.

- "légiste(s)" → "lawyer(s)" in most cases; "legal professional(s)" or "man/men of law" occasionally for variation. Avoided the archaic English "legist." In some cases where the French clearly means the class of legal practitioners as a body, used "the legal profession."

- "centralisation administrative" vs. "centralisation gouvernementale" → "administrative centralization" vs. "governmental centralization." Kept these as technical terms, Tocqueville's own key analytical distinction.

- "Nouveau-Monde" → "the New World."

- "un corps" (italicized in original) → "*a body*" — preserved the emphasis. Tocqueville is making the point that lawyers form a coherent social class without consciously organizing.

- "prêtres de l'Égypte" → "priests of Egypt." Tocqueville's vivid analogy: common-law lawyers, like Egyptian priests, are the sole interpreters of an arcane science. A wonderful image that needed no modernization.

- "la branche cadette de l'aristocratie anglaise" → "the junior branch of the English aristocracy." Clean translation of a perfect metaphor.

- "l'aristocratie américaine est au banc des avocats et sur le siège des juges" → "The American aristocracy sits on the lawyers' bench and on the judges' seat." One of the most quotable lines in the chapter. Kept crisp.

- "la rouille des sociétés" (describing individual selfishness) → "the rust of societies." Preserved this vivid metaphor.

- "il n'est presque pas de question politique, aux États-Unis, qui ne se résolve tôt ou tard en question judiciaire" → "There is hardly a political question in the United States that does not sooner or later turn into a judicial question." Another famous formulation, widely cited by legal scholars. Translated directly.

- Long footnote about Louisiana legal codes: included in full as [Note: ...]. While specialized, it shows Tocqueville's comparative legal mind and his awareness of Franco-American legal hybridity.

- Gutenberg editorial marks (_B_, _C_, _D_): omitted per guidelines.

- "inamovibilité" → "permanence" (of judicial position). More natural than "irremovability."

- The jury section carefully distinguishes between the jury as judicial institution (where Tocqueville concedes its value is debatable) and as political institution (where he is enthusiastic). Preserved this rhetorical structure.

- The closing line — "le jury... est aussi le moyen le plus efficace de lui apprendre à régner" — is a perfect epigram. Translated: "the jury, which is the most energetic means of making the people rule, is also the most effective means of teaching them to rule."

Structural note: The chapter has three major sections with distinct arguments. The first (administrative centralization) is relatively brief; the second (lawyers) is the longest and most original; the third (the jury) completes the argument. Each builds on the previous one: the absence of centralization prevents the majority from having tools of tyranny; lawyers provide an aristocratic check within democracy; the jury spreads the legal mind to the people, completing the circuit.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 023 covers the causes that maintain the democratic republic in the United States. This is a pivotal transition chapter. Key themes: the role of circumstances, laws, and customs (moeurs) in sustaining democracy, with Tocqueville arguing that customs matter more than laws, and laws more than circumstances.
