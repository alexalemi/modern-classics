Translation Notes — Chapter 078 (Part 3, Chapter II: Democracy makes social relations simpler)

A short, incisive chapter contrasting English social awkwardness with American openness. Tocqueville's key insight: English stiffness comes not from national character but from the transitional state of their aristocracy — birth-based privilege is giving way to money-based privilege, creating universal status anxiety.

Key decisions:
- "rapports habituels" → "everyday interactions" / "everyday relations." More natural than "habitual relations."
- "inquiétude secrète" → "secret unease." Per the text analysis, "inquiétude" = restlessness/unease, not anxiety.
- "aux antipodes" → "on the other side of the world." More vivid and natural than "at the antipodes."
- "aristocratie de naissance... aristocratie d'argent" → "aristocracy of birth... aristocracy of money." Clean parallel preserved.
- "guerre sourde" → "silent war." Direct and punchy.
- "état social" → "social conditions" / "social order" as appropriate per text analysis.
- "constitution du pays" → "structure of their country." "Constitution" here means the overall social makeup, not the legal document.
- Preserved Tocqueville's rhetorical build: the anecdote of two Englishmen meeting abroad → analysis of why → contrast with Americans → the punch line about social conditions being the real cause.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 079 deals with American sensitivity — relaxed at home, prickly abroad. The humor in this chapter is important: Tocqueville's description of trying to shake off an oblivious American conversationalist is comedy. The second half about Americans in Europe being status-conscious is more pointed social observation.
