Translation Notes — Chapter 028 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter X, Part 3: Black Americans, second half)

CONTEXT:
This section completes Tocqueville's analysis of slavery and race relations. It covers: the economic mechanics of slavery in the South (crop dependency), the impossibility of gradual abolition in states with large enslaved populations, the Liberia colonization scheme, and his devastating analysis of how Southern slaveholders have "spiritualized despotism" — keeping slaves ignorant and removing the hope of freedom. The passage about the old man whose children are his slaves is one of the most emotionally powerful in the book.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Spiritualisé le despotisme et la violence" — "Spiritualized despotism and violence." This is one of Tocqueville's most powerful coinages — the idea that Southern slaveholders have moved from physical control (chains) to psychological control (enforced ignorance, removal of hope). Kept the exact phrase.

2. "Je rassemble toute ma haine contre ceux qui, après plus de mille ans d'égalité, ont introduit de nouveau la servitude dans le monde" — "I gather all my hatred against those who, after more than a thousand years of equality, reintroduced servitude into the world." This is Tocqueville's most emotionally raw sentence on slavery. He explicitly directs his condemnation at the originators (the 16th-century slave traders) rather than the contemporary slaveholders, whom he sees as trapped in a system they cannot safely dismantle. This is analytically honest if morally uncomfortable.

3. The Liberia passage — Tocqueville treats the colonization scheme with a mix of admiration for the idea and devastating mathematical demolition of its practicality (2,500 transported in 12 years vs. 700,000 born in the same period). Kept his analytical precision.

4. "Commerce illégitime" — "illicit union." Tocqueville is describing a sexual relationship between a master and an enslaved woman. "Illicit" captures both the legal and moral dimensions without being euphemistic.

5. "À la rigueur" — "in a pinch." This casual, conversational phrase captures Tocqueville's meaning perfectly — the South *could* theoretically abolish slavery, but only barely and with enormous difficulty.

6. The Caribbean vs. continent prediction — Tocqueville's prediction that Black people would prevail in the Caribbean and be destroyed on the continent is wrong in its harshest form but reflects his analytical framework honestly. Translated without softening.

TONE:
This is the most emotionally charged section of the chapter. Tocqueville alternates between cold analysis (the economics, the demographics) and barely controlled moral outrage (the "spiritualized despotism" passage, the story of the old slaveholding father). The final paragraphs are prophetic — he essentially predicts the Civil War.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:

- Key vocabulary to maintain: "servitude"/"slavery," "the master's interest," "free worker."
