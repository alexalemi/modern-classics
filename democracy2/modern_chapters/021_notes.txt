Translation Notes — Chapter 021 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter VII)
"The Majority's Absolute Power in the United States and Its Effects"

This is Tocqueville's most famous chapter. Several passages are among the most widely quoted in political thought. Translated with particular care to maintain the rhetorical force of the original while making the language fully modern.

Key decisions:

- "omnipotence de la majorité" → "the majority's absolute power" in most cases; "tyranny of the majority" when Tocqueville uses "tyrannie de la majorité" explicitly. Both phrases appear in the chapter, and Tocqueville uses them for different emphases — "omnipotence" for the structural fact, "tyranny" for its moral condemnation.

- THE GREAT PASSAGE: "Le maître n'y dit plus: Vous penserez comme moi, ou vous mourrez..." This is the most famous passage in the chapter and perhaps in all of Tocqueville. Translated to preserve its devastating rhetorical architecture: the shift from the old master's brutal clarity ("Think as I do, or die") to the new master's cold, comprehensive ostracism. Key choices:
  - "Vous êtes libres de ne point penser ainsi que moi" → "You are free not to think as I do" — kept simple and direct.
  - "vous êtes un étranger parmi nous" → "you are a stranger among us"
  - "je vous la laisse pire que la mort" → "I leave it to you worse than death" — preserved the devastating final blow.
  - The whole passage maintains second-person address to keep the menacing intimacy.

- "la tyrannie; elle laisse le corps et va droit à l'âme" → "tyranny... it leaves the body alone and goes straight for the soul." This is another of the most quoted formulations. Kept crisp and direct.

- "c'est là un langage d'esclave" → "that is the language of a slave." Kept Tocqueville's sharp edge. He means it.

- "il n'y a que les étrangers ou l'expérience qui puissent faire arriver certaines vérités jusqu'aux oreilles des Américains" → "only foreigners or experience can make certain truths reach American ears." Clean and pointed.

- "il n'existe pas de génie littéraire sans liberté d'esprit, et il n'y a pas de liberté d'esprit en Amérique" → "literary genius cannot exist without freedom of mind, and there is no freedom of mind in America." Preserved the devastating epigram.

- "Messieurs" → "Gentlemen" (in the description of Americans who talk as if addressing an assembly — wait, that's from Chapter 020. Moved note there.)

- The Baltimore incident footnote: translated in full as a [Note: ...]. This is a powerful concrete illustration of majority tyranny, including the devastating dialogue about Black voters in Pennsylvania.

- "nègres" → "Blacks" — standard translation; avoiding anachronistic terms while also not using modern euphemism that would obscure Tocqueville's directness.

- "nègres affranchis" → "freed Blacks."

- Madison quotation: Translated from Tocqueville's French rendering rather than pasting the English original, per the text analysis principle of translating the French. (The French version differs slightly from Hamilton/Madison's English original.)

- Jefferson quotation: Same approach — translated from Tocqueville's French.

- "esprit de cour" → "the courtier spirit." This is a key concept: Tocqueville's argument that democracy democratizes the vices of the court — sycophancy, flattery — rather than eliminating them. "Courtier spirit" captures this better than "court spirit."

- "mâle indépendance de la pensée" → "virile independence of thought." Kept "virile" because Tocqueville is deliberately using gendered language about political courage, and softening it would lose his specific rhetorical register.

- "sans les avoir acquises, et pour ainsi dire sans le vouloir" (about the majority possessing "all virtues") → "without having acquired them and, so to speak, without even wanting to." Preserves the satirical bite.

Structural note: This chapter has seven subsections, each a distinct argument. The structure builds from the structural fact of majority power → its effects on legislation → the moral argument about tyranny → its effect on thought → its effect on character → the danger it poses. Preserved this architecture carefully.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 022 is "De ce qui tempère la tyrannie de la majorité" — what tempers majority tyranny. The two main sections cover (1) the absence of administrative centralization as a check, and (2) the legal profession ("esprit légiste") as an aristocratic counterweight, plus (3) the jury as political institution. Key terms: "esprit légiste" → "the legal mind" or "the lawyer's mentality"; "légistes" → "lawyers" or "legal professionals"; maintain contrast between governmental centralization and administrative centralization.
