Translation Notes — Chapter 030 (Volume I, Tome 2, Chapter X, Part 5: Union's Durability — Continued)

CONTEXT:
This chapter continues and completes the analysis of threats to the Union. It moves from the theoretical framework of chapter 029 into concrete, contemporary examples: Virginia's declining representation, the South's grievances, the tariff crisis and nullification, the Bank of the United States, and General Jackson. The chapter concludes with Tocqueville's prediction that the federal government will continue to weaken — a prediction that proved essentially correct for the next three decades until the Civil War.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Imbecillite senile" — "Senile imbecility." Tocqueville's mordant image of the Union dying not in a dramatic breakup but in a slow, pathetic decline. Kept the harsh phrase — it's characteristic of his willingness to use vivid, even shocking language for political phenomena.

2. "Nullification" / "Nullificateurs" — Kept in English as "nullification" / "nullifiers." These are already English political terms that Tocqueville uses in French. The Calhoun speech is quoted and translated directly.

3. "Le general Jackson est l'esclave de la majorite" — "General Jackson is the slave of the majority." Tocqueville's characterization of Jackson is brilliant and counterintuitive: far from being a dictator-in-waiting, Jackson is the ultimate populist who undermines federal power by pandering to provincial jealousies. The metaphor of "slave" is deliberately provocative in a chapter about a slaveholding republic.

4. "C'est un favori qui parfois rudoie son maitre" — "He is a favorite who sometimes rough-handles his master." Tocqueville's portrait of Jackson's paradox: personally powerful but institutionally weakening. This courtly metaphor (the king's favorite) applied to a democratic president is vintage Tocqueville.

5. "Passions decentralisantes" — "Decentralizing passions." Tocqueville notes his own coinage with "(si je puis m'exprimer ainsi)" — "if I may put it that way." Preserved both the neologism and his self-conscious flagging of it.

6. "Internal improvements" — Kept in English (italicized) as Tocqueville does. This was the American political term of the era for federally funded infrastructure projects.

7. South Carolina ordinance passage — The long quoted passage from the nullification report is translated with its legalistic rhetoric intact. Tocqueville quotes both French and English terms ("a solemn contract of union," "unquestionable"), and I preserved both.

TONE:
This chapter has a forensic, case-study quality. Tocqueville is building his argument through specific examples — the tariff crisis, the Bank, Jackson's presidency — each one illustrating the same thesis: the federal government is weakening. The Jackson portrait is the most literary section, combining political analysis with character study. The conclusion is measured but pessimistic — prophetically so.
