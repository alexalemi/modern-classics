Translation Notes — Chapter 015 (Vol. I, Tome 2, Ch. II: On Political Parties in the United States)

Key decisions:

- "grands partis / petits partis" — "great parties / small parties." Kept Tocqueville's stark dichotomy. "Major/minor" would lose the moral weight he assigns.

- "Ce sont des gens qui marchent; ils paraissent immobiles à ceux qui courent" — "They are people walking; they appear motionless only to those who are running." One of Tocqueville's best aphorisms. Translated directly, preserving the parallel structure.

- "Les grands partis bouleversent la société, les petits l'agitent; les uns la déchirent et les autres la dépravent" — Preserved the tricolon structure: "Great parties shake society to its foundations; small parties merely agitate it. The former tear it apart; the latter corrupt it." The French "dépravent" is stronger than "corrupt" but "corrupt" captures it well in English.

- "l'amant exclusif de la liberté" — "the exclusive champion of liberty." The French "amant" (lover) is metaphorical here; "champion" captures the sense of passionate, proprietary claim.

- "comme enveloppé au milieu d'une inondation subite" — "engulfed as if by a sudden flood." The flood metaphor is vivid in French; kept it vivid in English.

- "le privilége du roi" — "the royal warrant." This is Tocqueville's dry analogy: politicians attaching a doctrine to their party is like old authors printing the king's permission on the first page. "Royal warrant" is more natural in English than "royal privilege."

- "un juif du moyen-âge qui craint de laisser soupçonner ses richesses" — Translated directly: "a Jew in the Middle Ages, afraid of letting anyone suspect his riches." This is historically descriptive — Tocqueville is comparing the American rich man's concealment to the self-protective behavior forced on medieval Jews. Left as written per the text analysis guidance on faithful translation.

- "moeurs" — "customs and laws" in the phrase "modeler les moeurs et les lois," since here moeurs contrasts with lois and means the broader social habits/norms.

- Chapter section heading "DES RESTES DU PARTI ARISTOCRATIQUE AUX ÉTATS-UNIS" — "Remnants of the Aristocratic Party in the United States." "Restes" is literally "remains/remnants."

- Final line: "Les deux grandes armes qu'emploient les partis pour réussir sont les journaux et les associations" — sets up the next two chapters. Translated "journaux" as "newspapers" here.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 016 is about freedom of the press. Tocqueville opens with a famous argument that there's no stable middle ground between complete freedom and complete suppression of the press. The rhetorical build-up in the second paragraph — walking step by step from freedom to despotism — is one of his virtuoso passages. Preserve the accumulating rhythm. "Presse périodique" = "the periodical press" or simply "the press." Watch for the comparison between French and American press, and his paradox about press fragmentation weakening its power.
