Translation Notes — Chapter 100 (Chapter XXIV: Democratic armies — weak at first, strong over time)

CONTEXT:
Tocqueville makes a paradoxical but compelling military argument: democratic armies are terrible at the start of a war (old leadership, peacetime lethargy, low morale) but become formidable over time as war itself unleashes the democratic energies of ambition, merit, and popular mobilization. This is arguably a prophetic analysis of what the United States and other democracies would demonstrate in the 20th century's world wars.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "Plus redoutables quand la guerre se prolonge" — "More Formidable When the War Goes On." The chapter title. "Formidable" works in English (it means "inspiring fear" in both languages), and "goes on" is more natural than "is prolonged."

2. "Le plus grand capitaine des temps modernes" — "the greatest captain of modern times." This is a reference to Napoleon. Tocqueville does not name him here but the audience would have recognized it instantly. Kept the oblique reference rather than inserting Napoleon's name.

3. "Les soldats sont voisins de l'enfance et tous les chefs sur le déclin" — "all the soldiers are close to childhood and all the leaders are in decline." A vivid, almost comic image — green recruits led by decrepit officers. Preserved the parallel structure.

4. "La même agitation d'esprit" — "the same restlessness of mind." Consistent with "restlessness" for inquiétude/agitation per the text analysis.

5. "La mort ouvre sans cesse les rangs, vide les places, ferme la carrière et l'ouvre" — "Death constantly opens the ranks, clears out positions, closes careers and opens them again." This is a remarkable four-beat sentence where Death is personified as the great equalizer and opportunity-creator. Preserved the rhythm and the chiastic ending ("closes... and opens").

6. "La plupart d'entre eux adorent le hasard, et craignent bien moins la mort que la peine" — "Most of them worship chance and fear death far less than effort." A brilliant epigram linking democratic commercial culture to military bravery. "Peine" here means effort/toil, not pain.

7. "Grandeur brillante et soudaine qu'on obtient sans travail, en ne risquant que sa vie" — "a brilliant and sudden greatness won without toil, by risking nothing but one's life." Another stunning Tocquevillean formulation. The ironic "nothing but one's life" is preserved.

TONE:
This chapter has a more dynamic, narrative quality than the previous two. Tocqueville is telling a story of transformation — from peacetime decay to wartime excellence. The passage about democratic peoples doing "prodigious things" once armed has an almost admiring energy that contrasts with the analytical coolness elsewhere.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES:

- Key theme: the connection between democratic habits and military effectiveness continues.
