Translation Notes -- Chapter 007 (Chapter IV: On the Principle of Popular Sovereignty in America)

This is a short, powerful chapter. Tocqueville traces the arc of popular sovereignty from a hidden principle to the dominant force in American politics. The chapter builds to one of the most famous lines in the book.

Key decisions:

- "dogme de la souverainete du peuple" -> "principle of popular sovereignty." Tocqueville uses "dogme" here, which literally means "dogma." But in French political writing of this era, "dogme" often means "fundamental principle" without the negative connotation "dogma" has in modern English. I used "principle" for clarity, since Tocqueville is not being ironic here -- he genuinely treats popular sovereignty as a foundational axiom. However, in some passages where the quasi-religious overtone is deliberate, I kept the sense of something absolute and sacred.

- "cens" -> "property qualification" (for voting). This is a technical term for the minimum property or tax payment required to vote. Reeve used "qualification" alone; I added "property" for clarity. "L'abaissement du cens" -> "the lowering of property qualifications."

- "la volonte nationale" -> "the national will." Kept straightforward. The key passage about despots discovering the national will "dans le silence des peuples" (in the silence of peoples) is one of Tocqueville's most cutting observations.

- "du fait de l'obeissance naissait pour eux le droit du commandement" -> "from the fact of obedience springs the right to command." Preserved the italics on "fact" and "right" that Tocqueville uses for emphasis in the French.

- "Les hautes classes se soumirent donc sans murmure et sans combat a un mal desormais inevitable" -> "The upper classes therefore submitted without murmur and without struggle to what was now an inevitable fate." I translated "mal" as "fate" rather than "evil" -- Tocqueville is being wry here, noting that the upper classes saw democracy as a misfortune, but I didn't want to editorialize by making "evil" too strong.

- The final sentence: "Le peuple regne sur le monde politique americain comme Dieu sur l'univers. Il est la cause et la fin de toutes choses; tout en sort et tout s'y absorbe." This is Tocqueville at his most epigrammatic. I kept the theological comparison front and center. "They are the cause and the end of all things" echoes the language of the catechism deliberately.

- Omitted the Gutenberg editorial reference (_H_) at the end.

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 008 is the first part of Chapter V, covering local government and the township system. This is a long, detailed chapter -- more institutional analysis than soaring rhetoric. Tocqueville loves the New England township and sees it as the seedbed of American democracy. Key terms: "commune" = township; "select-men" = selectmen (keep the English term); "town-meeting" = town meeting; "esprit communal" = township spirit / civic spirit of the township. The tone is still first-person and admiring, but more technical. Preserve the institutional detail -- Tocqueville is building his case carefully.
