Translation Notes — Chapter 094 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XVIII: On Honor in the United States and in Democratic Societies)

OVERVIEW:
This is one of Tocqueville's most original and important chapters — a full sociological theory of honor as a social code that varies by group, era, and class. He argues that "honor" is not a universal moral standard but a particular set of rules that emerges from the specific needs of a particular social group. This is genuinely groundbreaking sociology, anticipating Durkheim and Weber by decades.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "honneur" — "honor" throughout, in the specific sociological sense Tocqueville defines in his opening footnote: the set of rules by which a group distributes praise and blame. Not personal virtue or integrity.

2. Tocqueville's footnote defining "honour" — Included as [Note: ...] at the beginning, per the translation guidelines. This is essential context.

3. "roturier" — "commoner." Consistent with the text analysis vocabulary.

4. "félonie" — "felony." Kept the direct cognate since Tocqueville is explaining the etymology. The French word came from the feudal concept of vassal betrayal.

5. "vilain" — "commoner" (in the medieval context). Not "villain" — the French word means a person of low birth in feudal terminology, with no implication of moral badness.

6. "débaucher une fille de couleur nuit à peine à la réputation d'un Américain; l'épouser le déshonore" — "seducing a woman of color barely hurts an American's reputation; marrying her dishonors him." Translated directly and unflinchingly. This is Tocqueville illustrating how honor codes can reflect the worst prejudices of a society.

7. "cupidité servile" vs. "noble et estimable ambition" — "servile greed" vs. "noble and worthy ambition." The inversion of values between feudal and democratic honor.

8. Plutarch quote — Translated from Tocqueville's French rendering, maintaining the archaic flavor. "Virtus" left in Latin as Tocqueville intended — the point is the etymological connection between virtue and valor.

9. Note 7 about slavery — Included as [Note: ...]. Important context: Tocqueville explicitly limits his analysis to the free states.

10. "Nous avons atteint le point extrême, redescendons" — "We have reached the extreme point; let us now come back down." A wonderful moment of rhetorical self-awareness — Tocqueville signals the turn in his argument.

11. The closing formula — "ce sont les dissemblances et les inégalités des hommes qui ont créé l'honneur" — "it is the differences and inequalities among people that created honor." A powerful, clean summation. Preserved as a standalone concluding statement.

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
The chapter has a clear arc: abstract theory -> feudal honor as case study -> American honor as comparison -> democratic honor in general -> grand synthesis. This architecture is essential to the argument and was preserved throughout.

TONE:
Analytical and systematic, but with Tocqueville's characteristic vivid examples (the duel, the slap on the cheek, the bankrupt merchant, seducing vs. marrying a woman of color). He is at his most sociological here — building a general theory from particular cases.
