Translation Notes — Chapter 090 (Vol. II, Part 3, Ch. XIV: Some Reflections on American Manners)

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "manières" — "manners" throughout. Unlike "moeurs" (customs/values), "manières" in this context refers specifically to outward social behavior, etiquette, and deportment. "Manners" is the right English word here.

2. "moeurs" vs. "manières" — Tocqueville carefully distinguishes these in the opening paragraph. "Les manières sortent, en général, du fond même des moeurs" — manners emerge from the depths of customs/values. Translated "moeurs" as "customs and values" or "customs" depending on context.

3. "gênées et sans gêne" — "stiff and casual." A wonderfully paradoxical phrase. The French captures the idea of being simultaneously constrained and unconstrained. "Stiff and casual" preserves the contradiction.

4. "savoir-vivre" — "etiquette" or "code of etiquette." The French term is broader than English "etiquette" (it means knowing how to live/behave), but "etiquette" is the closest natural equivalent.

5. "un voile léger et mal tissé" — "a light, loosely woven veil." Beautiful metaphor — democratic manners are transparent, unlike the heavy curtain of aristocratic formality.

6. "le grand tableau de l'humanité est moins orné, il est plus vrai" — "if the great tableau of humanity is less decorated, it is more truthful." Preserved the French "tableau" as it works in English too, and kept the sharp antithesis.

7. The English critics passage — Tocqueville's wry observation that English middle-class critics of American manners are essentially mocking themselves. A delicious irony that needs no embellishment.

TONE:
Reflective and bittersweet. Tocqueville is genuinely ambivalent here — he sees the gains of democratic sincerity but mourns the loss of aristocratic grace. The final paragraph is almost elegiac.
