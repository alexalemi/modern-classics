Chapter 047 (Vol II, Part 1, Ch XIII) Translation Notes

This is one of the richest chapters in Volume II — Tocqueville's theory of democratic literature. He contrasts aristocratic literature (polished, rule-bound, refined, potentially sterile) with democratic literature (raw, bold, uneven, energetic). Neither is idealized; both have characteristic flaws.

Key decisions:
- "log-house" — kept as "log house." Tocqueville uses the English term in the French text, showing he found it an exotic, characteristically American thing. The detail of reading Henry V in a log house is one of his best concrete touches.
- "physionomie littéraire" (chapter title) — "literary character" rather than "literary physiognomy." The French metaphor of a face/character comes through better as "character."
- "le recherché et le faux" — "the precious and the artificial" — captures the kind of over-refinement Tocqueville describes in late aristocratic literature.
- "jargon aristocratique" — "aristocratic jargon" — kept direct.
- Tocqueville's footnote (Note 2) about liberty within aristocracies is included as an inline [Note:] per the style guide.
- "des clartés soudaines, de vérités ou d'erreurs brillantes qui les tirent à l'instant d'eux-mêmes" — "sudden flashes of clarity, brilliant truths or errors that snatch them instantly out of themselves" — preserved the velocity and force of the French.
- "la fécondité sans exubérance, et le mouvement sans confusion" — "fertility without excess, and movement without confusion" — a perfect Tocquevillian balanced formulation about 18th-century French literature.
- "moeurs" — "customs and values" per the text analysis guidelines. In the passage about journalists vs. literary imitators: "ils amusent l'esprit, et n'agissent point sur les moeurs" — "they amuse the mind but have no effect on customs and values."

Consistency notes for next chapter:
- The literary themes continue directly into Ch XIV (the literary industry) and XV (Greek and Latin literature)
- "industrie" in the next chapter title means both "industry" in the commercial sense and industriousness/commercial spirit applied to literature
