Translation Notes -- Chapter 065 (Vol. II, Part 2, Ch. X: On the Taste for Material Well-Being in America)

CONTEXT:
Tocqueville analyzes America's characteristic materialism -- not as a moral failing, but as a structural feature of democratic society. The argument is sociological: in aristocracies, the rich take comfort for granted and the poor cannot imagine it; in democracies, a vast middle class is obsessed with acquiring and keeping material comfort. This analysis anticipates modern discussions of consumer culture and middle-class anxiety.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. "bien-être matériel" -> "material well-being." Consistent throughout. This is Tocqueville's key term for the chapter. Sometimes shortened to "well-being" where context makes the meaning clear.

2. "le goût du bien-être" -> "the taste for well-being." Tocqueville uses "goût" (taste) deliberately -- it's a cultivated preference, not a raw drive.

3. "la possession paisible d'un objet précieux" -> "the peaceful possession of something precious." Direct translation of an important psychological insight: desire and fear drive attachment more than possession does.

4. "le superflu" / "le nécessaire" -> "the superfluous" / "the necessary." Tocqueville's paradox: aristocrats accustomed to luxury can endure deprivation, while those who fought for mere comfort cannot. Preserved the antithesis.

5. "une multitude de fortunes médiocres" -> "a multitude of middling fortunes." "Médiocre" in French = middling/moderate, not inferior. "Middling" captures this precisely.

6. "passion de classe moyenne" -> "middle-class passion." Tocqueville explicitly identifies materialism as a middle-class phenomenon. Direct translation.

7. "enivrés" -> "intoxicated." Consistent with ch. 058's use. The image of the newly rich remaining drunk on small pleasures they chased for decades is vivid and psychologically acute.

8. "fortune ennemie" -> "hostile fortune." The French personifies fortune (fate/luck) as an adversary. Preserved this.

9. "le grand courant des passions humaines porte de ce côté, il entraîne tout dans son cours" -> "the great current of human passions flows in this direction, and it carries everything in its course." River/current metaphor -- one of Tocqueville's favorite images for irresistible social forces. Translated directly.

10. "les rangs sont confondus et les priviléges détruits" -> "ranks are blurred and privileges destroyed." "Confondus" = mixed together, blurred, confused. "Blurred" captures the gradual dissolution rather than a sudden collapse.

TONE:
Observational and analytical, with a touch of melancholy. Tocqueville is not condemning American materialism -- he understands its social origins -- but there is a note of regret for the aristocratic capacity for disdaining comfort and pursuing higher things. The chapter reads like a sociological diagnosis, clinically precise.
