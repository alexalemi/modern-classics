Translation Notes — Chapter 010 (Chapter VI: Judicial Power)

This is a substantial chapter covering two related topics: (1) the distinctive nature of American judicial review and (2) the right of citizens to sue public officials. Both topics let Tocqueville draw sharp contrasts between the American, English, and French systems.

Key decisions:

- "pouvoir judiciaire" → "judicial power" or "the judiciary" depending on context. When Tocqueville uses it as an abstract concept, "judicial power" works; when referring to the institution, "the judiciary" is more natural.

- "puissance judiciaire" vs "pouvoir judiciaire" — In French these have a subtle distinction (puissance = capacity/authority, pouvoir = institutional power). I rendered both flexibly rather than forcing a rigid distinction that would sound awkward in English.

- "servir d'arbitre" → "serve as an arbiter" — kept formal since Tocqueville is laying out a definition.

- "quand elle est saisie" → "when it is 'seized'" — kept the legal term in quotes since Tocqueville himself flags it as legal jargon ("suivant l'expression légale").

- "Parlement de Paris" — kept as "Parlement" (not "Parliament") to distinguish the French judicial body from the English legislative Parliament. This is important context: the French Parlements were courts, not legislatures.

- "remontrances" → "remonstrances" — kept the English cognate, which is the standard term for this specific French legal-political institution.

- "fonctionnaire prévaricateur" → "corrupt official" — "prévaricateur" literally means one who abuses official authority. "Corrupt" captures the sense clearly.

- "force morale" → "moral authority" or "moral force" — used "moral authority" where it fits better in English.

- "jurisprudence" → "judicial precedent" — the French "jurisprudence" means case law / judicial precedent, not "legal philosophy" as in English.

- The Reeve note markers (_L_, _M_, _N_) are Gutenberg editorial references and have been omitted per instructions.

- Article 75 passage: This is a set piece where Tocqueville dramatizes explaining French administrative law to bewildered Anglo-Saxons. I kept the conversational, almost comedic tone of his escalating description — the king orders a prefect to commit an injustice, then orders a councilor of state to prevent punishment. The building indignation is central to the rhetoric.

- "Conseil d'État" → "Council of State" — standard English translation of this French institution.

- Final line: "Nous avons donc bien reculé du point où étaient arrivés nos pères" — "So we have actually gone backward from where our ancestors stood." This is a devastating closing line aimed at France, contrasting the old monarchy's at-least-visible despotism with the modern legalistic concealment. Preserved the punch.

Consistency notes for next chapters:
- "jugement politique" (next chapter) should be "political trial" or "political judgment" — the concept is impeachment/political prosecution.
- "constitution fédérale" (chapter after) — "federal constitution" is straightforward.
- The contrast between French and American systems is a recurring pattern in these institutional chapters.
