Translation Notes — Chapter 108 (Volume II, Part 4, Chapter V)
"Among the European nations of our time, sovereign power is growing even though the sovereigns themselves are less secure."

This is the longest chapter in Part 4 — a sweeping survey of how every domain of life (charity, education, religion, savings, industry, judiciary, associations) is being absorbed by the state. The paradox of the title is central: the rulers may fall, but the power of the state grows regardless.

Key decisions:
- Three substantial footnotes (14, 15, 16) translated in full as [Note: ...] — all three are important
- Note 14 on wills: "Après avoir régenté la vie entière, il veut encore en régler le dernier acte" → "After having regulated your entire life, it wants to regulate your final act as well" — devastating line
- Note 15 on civil servants: "Ils forment une nation dans chaque nation" → "They form a nation within each nation" — kept this striking formulation
- Note 16 on comfort and servitude: Two paths to servitude — kept the dual structure
- "plus inquisitive et plus détaillée" → "more inquisitive and more detailed" — "inquisitive" is exactly right here
- "L'industrie nous mène, et ils la mènent" → "Industry leads us, and they lead it" — preserved the chiasmus
- "à côté, autour et au-dessus de chaque individu" → "beside, around, and above each individual" — tricolon preserved
- Long passage on industrial vs. landed property: careful to preserve Tocqueville's historical argument about how industrial property inherited less legal protection than real estate
- Mining footnote (Note 18): translated in full — it's a concrete example of how the state absorbs private industry
- "les souverains ne se trouvent plus puissants qu'ils ne l'ont été" → "the sovereigns may find themselves more powerful than they have ever been" — the chapter's chilling final line
- "des rois... des tyrans... des tuteurs" → the progression from kings to tyrants to tutors foreshadows chapter 109

Next chapter notes:
- Chapter 109 is THE famous chapter — "What kind of despotism democratic nations have to fear." This contains the vision of soft despotism, the paternalistic state that keeps citizens in "perpetual childhood." The long paragraph beginning "Je veux imaginer" is among the most quoted passages in political theory. It must be translated with full rhetorical power. Key verb chains must be preserved: "il ne brise pas... mais il amollit, plie, dirige... il ne détruit point, il empêche de naître; il ne tyrannise point, il gêne, il comprime, il énerve, il éteint, il hébète." The chapter's structure builds from Roman tyranny (violent but limited) to modern soft despotism (gentle but total).
