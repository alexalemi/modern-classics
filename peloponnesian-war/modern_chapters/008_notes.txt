CHAPTER 8 TRANSLATION NOTES

=== KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES ===

- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout narrative and in speeches
- "Hellenes/Hellas" → "Greeks/Greece" in all contexts; no formal/emphatic
  usage warranted in this chapter
- "barbarian" → "non-Greek" on first substantive reference (the Chaonian
  contingent and other peoples north of Greece); then used contextually as
  "non-Greek troops" or simply identified by tribal name. Ch 1 notes established
  "non-Greek" as the standard; maintained that here since the Thracian/Chaonian
  peoples are central to the narrative and "barbarian" carries wrong modern
  connotations
- "confederacy" → "alliance" or "allied" throughout
- "circumvallation" → "wall of siege" / "siege line" / "siege wall"
- "proxenus" → "diplomatic representative" (for Nicias of Gortys)
- "faggots of brushwood" → "bundles of brushwood"
- "palisade" → kept as-is (clear enough in context)
- "paean" → "paean — the battle hymn of victory" (glossed on first use)
- "mound" → "siege mound" / "earthen siege mound" on first use, then "mound"
- "heavy infantry" → kept as-is (consistent with earlier chapters)
- "light troops/light-armed" → "light troops" / "light-armed troops"
- "targeteers" → "light-armed troops" (folded into the general term)
- "wattles of reed" → "woven reed mats" (more accessible)
- "cuirasses" → "cuirasses" (kept — context of armor makes it clear)
- "the rising of Arcturus" → "around the time Arcturus rose — late September"
  (added modern month gloss)
- "the Ionian Gulf" → "the Adriatic" (modern name for clarity in the Odrysian
  empire description)
- "the Euxine" → "the Black Sea" throughout
- "Hellespont" → kept as-is (recognizable geographic term)
- "Temenids" → kept as-is (clan name, clear enough in context)

=== SPEECH HANDLING ===

- Four speeches in this chapter: Plataean envoys, Archidamus's reply,
  Peloponnesian commanders' pre-battle speech, Phormio's pre-battle speech
- Archidamus's prayer to the gods of Plataea rendered in slightly elevated
  register to convey the solemnity of the religious appeal
- The paired naval speeches (Peloponnesian commanders vs. Phormio) are the
  rhetorical centerpiece — kept their contrasting tones: the Peloponnesian
  speech is defensive and excuse-making, Phormio's is confident and tactical
- Phormio's speech preserved his key tactical insight about not fighting in
  the narrows — this is the strategic core of the chapter's naval narrative
- Used horizontal rules (---) to separate major sections (Plataea siege,
  Spartolus battle, Acarnanian campaign, first naval battle, second naval
  battle, Piraeus raid, Sitalces' invasion, winter operations)

=== PROPER NOUNS INTRODUCED ===

- Archidamus (son of Zeuxidamus) — Spartan king, commander at Plataea;
  previously established in Ch 3 notes as "moderate/cautious"
- Pausanias (son of Cleombrotus) — historical Spartan commander, referenced
  in the Plataean speech; established in Ch 4
- Plataea/Plataeans — the besieged city; central to this chapter and Book III
- Cithaeron — mountain near Plataea (timber source for siege mound)
- Xenophon (son of Euripides) — Athenian commander at Spartolus (NOT the
  historian Xenophon)
- Spartolus — town in Bottiaea
- Olynthus — Chalcidian city providing reinforcements
- Cnemus — Spartan high admiral
- Phormio — Athenian admiral at Naupactus; the hero of the naval chapters
- Ambraciots — people of Ambracia, Corinthian colony
- Chaonians — non-Greek people, fierce warriors without a king
- Photys and Nicanor — Chaonian chieftains
- Thesprotians, Molossians, Atintanians, Paravaeans, Orestians — non-Greek
  allied peoples in Cnemus's army
- Sabylinthus — regent for young King Tharyps of the Molossians
- Oroedus — king of the Paravaeans
- Antichus — king of the Orestians
- Stratus — capital of Acarnania
- Machaon, Isocrates, Agatharchidas — Corinthian naval commanders
- Timocrates, Brasidas, Lycophron — Spartan commissioners sent to the fleet;
  Timocrates dies in the second battle; Brasidas will become a major character
  in later books
- Nicias of Gortys — Cretan diplomatic representative who diverted the
  Athenian reinforcements to Crete (NOT Nicias the Athenian general)
- Panormus — Achaean port, Peloponnesian base
- Rhium (Molycrian/Athenian) and Rhium (Achaean/Peloponnesian) — the two
  promontories flanking the mouth of the Crissaean Gulf
- Sitalces (son of Teres) — Odrysian king of Thrace
- Perdiccas (son of Alexander) — king of Macedonia
- Philip — Perdiccas's brother, rival claimant to the throne
- Amyntas (son of Philip) — pretender Sitalces intended for the Macedonian throne
- Hagnon — Athenian general accompanying Sitalces
- Seuthes (son of Spardacus) — Sitalces' nephew, later successor; bribed by
  Perdiccas to urge withdrawal
- Stratonice — Perdiccas's sister, given to Seuthes as wife
- Archelaus (son of Perdiccas) — future king who modernized Macedonia; mentioned
  in a forward-looking aside
- Alexander (Perdiccas's father) — founder of the expanded Macedonian kingdom
- Alcmaeon (son of Amphiaraus) — mythological figure in the Oeniadae digression
- Cynes (son of Theolytus) — restored to Coronta by Phormio
- Getae, Dii, Agrianes, Laeaeans, Treres, Tilataeans, Triballi — Thracian
  peoples in Sitalces' army and empire
- Scythians — referenced as the benchmark for military manpower in Europe

=== GEOGRAPHIC NOTES ===

- Euxine → "Black Sea" throughout (modern standard)
- Ionian Gulf → "Adriatic" (once, in the Odrysian empire description)
- Hellespont → kept as-is
- Mount Haemus, Mount Rhodope, Mount Scombrus, Mount Pangaeus — all kept as-is
- Danube — used instead of "Ister" (Crawley already uses "Danube")
- Cercine — mountain range between Sintians and Paeonians; kept as-is
- Doberus — Thracian staging ground in Paeonia; kept as-is
- Pella, Cyrrhus — Macedonian cities; kept as-is
- Echinades — islands near Oeniadae; kept as-is

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

- Plataea is now fully besieged with 400 Plataeans, 80 Athenians, and 110
  women bakers inside. The siege wall is complete. The fall of Plataea will
  come in Book III (Ch 9-11) — maintain the sense that these people are
  trapped and waiting
- Phormio has won two brilliant naval victories and is established as Athens's
  best admiral in the west; he returns to Athens at the end of this chapter
- Brasidas has been introduced as one of three Spartan commissioners sent to
  the fleet; he will become a major character later — for now he's just a name
  in a list, but note his presence
- The Piraeus raid establishes that Athens is now guarding its harbor —
  previously it was left open
- Sitalces' invasion has ended with no real results; Perdiccas has bought
  off Seuthes with his sister Stratonice. The Chalcidian war in Thrace
  remains unresolved
- "Non-Greek" continues to be used for "barbarian" — especially important in
  this chapter given the extensive treatment of Thracian peoples
- The Achelous River / Echinades Islands digression and the Alcmaeon legend
  give a nice geographical/mythological aside at the chapter's end — a rare
  moment of Thucydides the storyteller rather than Thucydides the analyst
- Oeniadae remains hostile to Athens — this thread may recur
- The third year of the war has ended; Chapter 9 begins the fourth year
- Macedonia's political situation: Perdiccas is playing all sides (makes
  promises to Sitalces, bribes Seuthes, coordinates with everyone). His
  unreliability will be a recurring theme
- Archelaus mentioned as a future modernizer of Macedonia — this is a rare
  forward-looking aside from Thucydides; note it was preserved
