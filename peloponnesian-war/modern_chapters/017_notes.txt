CHAPTER 17 TRANSLATION NOTES

=== KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES ===

- "Lacedaemonians" -> "Spartans" throughout narrative and dialogue
- "Lacedaemon" -> "Sparta" (one instance, in the Athenian speech about rival empires)
- "Hellas" -> "Greece" (used once in the Athenians' final long speech: "the greatest city in Greece")
- "the Mede" -> "the Persians" (in the Athenians' opening speech dismissing pretexts)
- "confederacy" -> "alliance" (final Athenian speech)
- "circumvallation" -> "siege wall" (per text analysis vocabulary table)
- "heavy infantry" -> kept as-is
- "light-armed" -> not present in this chapter
- "suppliants" -> not present in this chapter
- "at discretion" (surrender) -> "unconditionally"
- "tributary ally" -> "tribute-paying ally"

=== DIALOGUE FORMATTING ===

- Used speaker labels in CAPS (ATHENIANS: / MELIANS:) for the formal dialogue, formatted like a diplomatic negotiation/screenplay
- The iconic line "the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must" is set apart on its own line for emphasis, preserving this most famous quotation from the entire work
- The Melians' final answer and the Athenians' departing response are also formatted with speaker labels to maintain the dialogue structure through the end
- Thucydides' narration before and after the dialogue uses the standard narrative voice

=== THE ICONIC LINE ===

"the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must" — preserved verbatim from the Crawley translation. This is the most quoted line in the entire History and one of the most famous sentences in Western political thought. It appears in nearly every anthology. No modernization needed; it is already perfect.

=== TONE AND STYLE CHOICES ===

- The Athenian speeches are cold, logical, relentlessly pragmatic — almost corporate in their detachment. They speak of power as natural law, not as something requiring justification.
- The Melian speeches convey dignity, desperation, and a kind of stubborn faith that is both admirable and heartbreaking given what follows.
- The final four paragraphs after the dialogue deliberately accelerate: siege, betrayal, surrender, massacre, colonization. Each sentence is shorter and more brutal than the last. The final sentence — "Then they sent out five hundred of their own colonists and settled the island themselves" — is left standing alone as a blunt, chilling full stop. Thucydides provides no commentary. Neither do we.

- Tisias, son of Tisimachus — Athenian general; introduced with patronymic

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

1. Melos has been destroyed. The Athenians killed all adult males and enslaved the women and children, then colonized the island. This act of extreme brutality is presented without editorial comment — Thucydides' silence IS the commentary. The next chapter (Book VI, the Sicilian Expedition) follows immediately. The juxtaposition is deliberate: Athens at the height of its imperial ruthlessness is about to embark on the adventure that will destroy it. Do not add commentary connecting these; let the reader feel the juxtaposition.

2. Alcibiades has been mentioned as an active military commander (arresting pro-Spartan Argives). He will become the central figure of the next several chapters. His first appearance here is brief and efficient — he will need fuller treatment when the Sicilian debate begins.

3. The Spartan failure to help Melos — they never even tried — vindicates the Athenians' argument about Spartan behavior in the dialogue. The text makes this point implicitly. The Spartans were "preparing to invade Argive territory" (not Athenian territory, not sailing to Melos). Their sacrifices were unfavorable, and they went home. This matches the Athenian characterization perfectly.

4. The Peace of Nicias is effectively dead. The Corinthians are openly hostile. The Spartans have authorized private raiding of Athenian property. The Athenians at Pylos are plundering Spartan land. All of this sets up the full resumption of war in Book VI.

5. The tone should shift dramatically in the next chapter. The Melian Dialogue is philosophical, almost abstract — power versus justice, hope versus reality. The Sicilian Expedition chapters are narrative, full of drama and personality. Alcibiades, Nicias, and Lamachus will debate the expedition in speeches that should feel very different from the Melian Dialogue — more personal, more political, more reckless.

6. Speech register: the Melian Dialogue used a distinctive format (speaker labels, sharp exchanges). The next chapter's speeches should return to the standard format (introduced by narration, delivered as continuous oratory, like the Corinthian speech in Chapter 5).
