=== STRUCTURAL CHOICES ===

- Added section headers (THE CAPTURE OF TORONE, PHAEAX'S MISSION TO SICILY, etc.) to break up this long chapter and help readers navigate. The chapter covers many distinct episodes.
- Brasidas's speech formatted as a block with a header, consistent with speech formatting in Ch. 5.
- The treaty articles kept in a formal but readable register — slightly elevated compared to the narrative, to convey the legal/diplomatic weight, but with modern sentence structures.
- The alliance treaty uses parallel structure with the peace treaty for consistency.
- Thucydides' first-person interjection about calculating by seasons preserved and made conversational.

=== KEY INTERPRETIVE CHOICES ===

- "the affair of accident and panic that I have described" — rendered as "the kind of panicked rout and lucky ambush I have described" to make Thucydides' point clearer: the casualty ratio was lopsided because it was an ambush, not a fair fight.
- Cleon's characterization: Thucydides is openly contemptuous of Cleon. Preserved this without editorializing further. "He never dreamed of anyone coming out to fight him" and "who from the first had no thought of fighting" are devastating assessments; kept them sharp.
- Brasidas's death: "he lived to hear of the victory of his troops, and not long after expired" — rendered as "He lived long enough to hear that his side had won. Then he died." Short sentences for emotional weight.
- The irony of Cleon and Brasidas: Thucydides' observation that the two biggest hawks dying opened the door to peace is presented matter-of-factly, as he wrote it. The irony speaks for itself.
- "plough with a silver share" — kept as "plow with a silver plowshare" (proverbial expression meaning impoverishment/hardship; the oracle's threat).
- The slave revolt clause in the alliance: this refers to the Helots. Rendered plainly as "the slave population" which is what Thucydides means. The clause's implications are stark and need no commentary.

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

1. The Peace of Nicias is now in effect but already fraying. The Boeotians, Corinthians, Eleans, and Megarians refused to sign. Clearidas refused to hand over Amphipolis. The next chapter should show this unraveling accelerating.

2. Nicias has been established as a cautious, risk-averse leader who wants to preserve his reputation. This characterization will be important through the Sicilian expedition debate.

3. Pleistoanax has been established as politically vulnerable — always under attack from enemies who blame his restoration for Sparta's troubles. His eagerness for peace is partly self-protective.

4. The Spartan-Athenian alliance is an extraordinary development — the two great rivals formally allied. But it was born of expediency, not trust. The next chapter should show how quickly this arrangement comes under strain.

5. Argos has been flagged as a rising threat — the thirty-year truce has expired, and they refused to renew it. Argos will be a major player in the diplomatic maneuvering of the next chapters.

6. The Helot revolt clause in the alliance is a remarkable concession by Athens and shows how desperate Sparta was — they essentially got Athens to promise to help suppress their slave population. This may come up again.

7. Key proper nouns to maintain: Clearidas (still in charge at Amphipolis), Ramphias/Autocharidas/Epicydidas (the failed relief force), Ischagoras/Menas/Philocharidas (Spartan envoys to Thrace).

8. The "tribute of Aristides" has been mentioned without further explanation — it refers to the original assessment of allied contributions set by Aristides after the Persian War. If Thucydides elaborates later, gloss then.

9. Thucydides' dating method (counting by summers and winters rather than magistrates) has been highlighted. Continue using this framework: "the eleventh year of the war" etc.

10. The treaty text preserves all the named cities and territorial provisions. These specific places will come up again as the peace collapses — particularly Amphipolis (never actually returned) and Panactum (in Boeotian hands).
