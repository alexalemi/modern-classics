CHAPTER 1 TRANSLATION NOTES
=============================

KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES
- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout (used "Sparta/Spartans" consistently)
- "Hellenes/Hellas" → "Greeks/Greece" in most contexts; kept "Hellas" nowhere in this chapter since the analytical register doesn't call for the formal/emphatic usage
- "barbarian" → "non-Greek" on first occurrence, then "non-Greek peoples" or "non-Greek" in subsequent uses, with "barbarian" avoided to prevent modern connotations
- "Median War" → "Persian War" throughout
- "the Mede/Medes" → "Persians"
- "faction" → "political rivalries," "civil strife," "civil conflicts," "civil war" depending on context
- "fifty-oars" → "fifty-oared ships" or "fifty-oared vessels"
- "tyrant/tyrannies" → kept, but added a parenthetical gloss on first substantive use explaining it meant "sole ruler" not necessarily a cruel one
- "confederacy" → "alliance"
- "heavy infantry" → not used in this chapter
- "galleys" → "galleys" (kept as-is, clear enough)
- "talents" → not used in this chapter
- "cupidity" → "profit"
- "aggrandizement" → "power" or restructured
- "rapine" → "raiding"
- "supernumeraries" → "passengers"
- "munitions of war" → "equipment"

PROPER NOUNS INTRODUCED
- Thucydides — Athenian author, introduced in opening line
- Hellen, son of Deucalion — mythological ancestor of the Greeks; explained as source of "Hellenes" name
- Minos — first navy builder, ruler of the Cyclades
- Homer — referenced extensively as historical source
- Achilles — mentioned as leader of original Hellenes from Phthiotis
- Pelops — ancestor who gave name to Peloponnese; glossed as "Pelops' island"
- Agamemnon — leader of the Trojan expedition
- Eurystheus, Atreus, Perseus, Chrysippus, the Heraclids — genealogical chain establishing Agamemnon's dynasty
- Tyndareus — oath-binder of Helen's suitors
- Ameinocles — Corinthian shipwright
- Cyrus, Cambyses, Darius, Xerxes — Persian kings (mentioned in sequence)
- Polycrates — tyrant of Samos
- Themistocles — persuaded Athenians to build fleet for Salamis
- Croesus — king overthrown by Cyrus
- Pisistratus, Hippias, Hipparchus, Thessalus — Athenian tyrant family
- Harmodius and Aristogiton — tyrannicides (Thucydides corrects the popular myth)
- Chalcis and Eretria — earliest large-scale Greek conflict

GEOGRAPHIC NAMES
- All kept as-is: Thessaly, Boeotia, Peloponnese, Arcadia, Attica, Ionia, Phthiotis, Cyclades, Delos, Corcyra, Corinth, Samos, Rhenea, Marseilles, Sicily, Aegina, Troy/Ilium, Mycenae, Argos, Chersonese, Chios, Lesbos, Euboea, Arne, the Halys River
- "Hellas" → "Greece" in all geographic uses
- "Hellenic sea" → "Aegean" (modern recognizable equivalent)
- "Ionian sea" → "Ionian Sea" (kept as-is)

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
- Thucydides' first person has been preserved ("in my opinion," "I had almost said," "in my judgment")
- The famous closing line "a possession for all time" is kept verbatim
- "The growth of Athenian power, and the fear it inspired in Sparta, made war inevitable" — this is the famous thesis statement, rendered clearly. Future chapters should treat this as the established framework.
- The Thirty Years' Peace has been introduced as the broken treaty — this will be referenced extensively in subsequent chapters
- Tyrants have been glossed — no need to re-explain in future chapters
- "Non-Greek" was used instead of "barbarian" — maintain this consistently, or reintroduce "barbarian" with the established context if the text requires it frequently
- The tone established here is analytical and argumentative but accessible — maintain this register for the remaining Book I diplomatic chapters
- Homer is treated as a historical source throughout — Thucydides uses him critically, and the modernization preserves this relationship
- Dates are given relative to "the end of this war" per the original — kept as-is since Thucydides' own dating method is part of his voice
- The parenthetical aside about "tyrant" meaning is the only added editorial gloss in this chapter; keep such additions minimal going forward
