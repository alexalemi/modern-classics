CHAPTER 5 TRANSLATION NOTES

=== KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES ===

- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout narrative; "Lacedaemon" not used in this chapter
- "Hellenes/Hellas" → "Greeks/Greece" in narrative; "Hellas" retained once in the Corinthian speech ("gave freedom to all of Greece" — chose "Greece" over "Hellas" here since it was Corinthians speaking to fellow Greeks, not a solemn/emphatic moment)
- "the barbarian" → "non-Greeks" on first use (paragraph 1); "Persians" when referring specifically to Persia thereafter; "barbarian" retained in context of "intriguing with the barbarians" for Pausanias section since the Greek attitude toward Pausanias's treachery is the point
- "Median/Mede" → "Persian" throughout (e.g., "Median dress" → "Persian clothing"; "the Mede defeated" → "the Persians defeated"; "Medism" → "dealings with Persia")
- "confederacy" → "alliance" (Spartan alliance context)
- "viz." → eliminated, replaced with natural phrasing
- "tyrant" → kept, with a parenthetical gloss on first use ("the tyrant — that is, the sole ruler — of Megara")
- "ephors" → kept, glossed as "Sparta's chief magistrates" on first substantive mention
- "archons" → kept as "nine archons" with context about their governmental role
- "scytale" → kept, glossed as "the official Spartan dispatch stick"
- "talents" → kept (mentioned in context of Themistocles' revenue — fifty talents a year)
- "suppliants/supplication" → kept; the concept is explained through action rather than glossed
- "ostracized" → kept, glossed as "exiled by popular vote"
- "Helots" → kept without gloss (context makes clear they are a subject population; should be glossed in an earlier chapter if not already)
- "galley" → kept
- "Kaiadas" → kept, glossed as "the pit where Sparta disposed of criminals"

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

1. Pericles has been established as the dominant Athenian leader — "the first man of his time at Athens, ablest in both strategy and action." He should continue to be treated with this level of gravity. He is the voice of Athenian resolve.

2. The Megarian Decree has been explained as "the law that barred the Megarians from using Athenian harbors and the Athenian marketplace." Keep this framing if referenced again.

3. The tone at the end of the chapter is one of grim inevitability — "events were unfolding that amounted to a breach of the treaty and grounds for war." The next chapter (Book II) should open with that tension snapping. The war is about to begin.

4. Pausanias and Themistocles digressions are now complete — these characters should not need re-introduction unless Thucydides references them again.

5. The Spartan alliance has been characterized as slow, consensus-driven, and hampered by their council structure (per Pericles' speech). Athens has been characterized as naval, wealthy, and capable of sustained warfare. These strategic assessments will be tested by events — maintain the framing.

6. "Ostracism" was glossed in this chapter. If it comes up again, no need to re-explain.

7. The supplication customs (sitting at hearth with a child, sitting at altars) were shown through action rather than explained didactically. Continue this approach for Greek religious customs — show, don't lecture.

8. The chapter ends with communication continuing "without the formality of official heralds" — a key detail. The next chapter should mark the point where even this informal contact breaks down.

9. Speeches in this chapter used a slightly more formal register than the narrative prose, but still modern. Pericles was given a particularly sharp, direct voice — almost like a modern political leader making a case. Maintain this distinction: narrative = conversational; speeches = elevated but clear.
