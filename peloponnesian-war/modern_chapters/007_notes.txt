CHAPTER 7 TRANSLATION NOTES

=== KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES ===

- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout narrative, consistent with Ch. 1 and Ch. 3
- "Hellas/Hellenes" → "Greeks" in all narrative contexts; retained "Greek state" and
  "Greeks" in Pericles' speech where Crawley had "Hellenes" and "Hellenic state" —
  EXCEPT in Pericles' speech the line "we ruled over more Greeks than any other Greek
  state" was kept accessible rather than using "Hellenes" since it is mid-argument, not
  a formal invocation
- "barbarian" → "non-Greek peoples" for the Amphilochian context, consistent with Ch. 1
  approach; the Chaonians described as "non-Greek peoples"
- "heavy infantry" → kept as-is throughout, consistent with established convention
- "talents" → kept; glossed as "an enormous sum" on the 2,000 talents for Potidaea
  (the reader has encountered the term before in earlier chapters)
- "confederacy" → "alliance" / "allies"
- "commonwealth" → "state" (in Pericles' speech)
- "demes" → not present in this chapter's Crawley text
- "galleys" → "warships" (for the horse transports converted from old galleys)
- "the king's country" → "the Persian Empire" (plague origin passage)
- "Peloponnesians" → kept as-is for the alliance; "Peloponnesian" as adjective
- "the king" → "the Persian king" for clarity on first reference; "the king" on
  subsequent references where context is clear
- "distemper" → "disease" / "plague" / "infection"
- "heavy infantry" numbers kept exact (4,000, 1,050, etc.)
- "privateer" → "privateer" (kept; clear enough in context)

=== SPEECH HANDLING ===

- Pericles' final speech set off with horizontal rules (---), consistent with Ch. 3
  speech formatting
- Maintained the speech's argumentative structure — Pericles builds from defense of
  himself → defense of the war policy → revelation of naval supremacy → call to action
- The "tyranny" passage ("what you hold is, in effect, a tyranny") kept close to the
  original — this is one of the most quoted lines in the work and Pericles' frank
  admission about the nature of Athenian empire
- The famous "greatest name in all the world" passage kept largely intact in phrasing
- Modernized the rhetorical register to feel like a leader under pressure — direct,
  forceful, occasionally blunt ("Let me be blunt")
- Broke up Crawley's longest periods (80-120 word sentences in the speech) into
  shorter, punchier sentences while preserving the arc of each argument

=== THE PLAGUE DESCRIPTION ===

- Treated as the centerpiece of the chapter — given full weight and visceral detail
- Thucydides' first-person statements preserved and emphasized: "I caught the disease
  myself and I watched it destroy others around me"
- Clinical precision maintained — the progression from head to chest to stomach to
  bowels is kept step by step
- The social/moral collapse section (abandonment of burial rites, lawlessness, loss
  of religious belief) translated to feel like modern disaster reportage
- The observation about scavenging birds disappearing kept as a chilling detail
- "Bodies piled on top of bodies" and "half-dead people staggered through the streets"
  — kept the cinematic horror of the original

=== THUCYDIDES' EDITORIAL VOICE ===

- The famous digression on Athens's post-Pericles decline preserved in full — this
  is where Thucydides explicitly comments on the Sicilian disaster and the decline
  of Athenian leadership
- "The Athenians did the exact opposite." — set as its own paragraph for emphasis;
  this is one of the most devastating single-sentence judgments in the work
- Thucydides' voice in the plague section ("I can do this with some authority") kept
  first-person and direct

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

- Pericles is now dead (survived 2 years, 6 months after war's start). Chapter 7
  explicitly covers his death, his vindication, and the decline of leadership after him.
  Future chapters should not reference Pericles as alive.
- The Sicilian expedition has been foreshadowed explicitly in Thucydides' editorial
  digression — when it comes up in Books VI-VII, this passage can be echoed
- Phormio is stationed at Naupactus watching the Corinthian Gulf — he will likely
  feature prominently in the next chapters (Book II continues into Book III)
- Potidaea has finally fallen — it was a running thread since the pre-war chapters.
  The Athenians colonized it. This resolves that plotline.
- The three motives for empire (fear, honor, interest from Ch. 3) are echoed in
  Pericles' speech here — he speaks of honor, glory, and the impossibility of retreat
- "What you hold is, in effect, a tyranny" — this is a key line that will be echoed
  by Cleon in the Mytilenean debate (Ch. 9-10). Use similar phrasing when it recurs.
- The lawlessness/moral collapse during the plague parallels what Thucydides will
  describe during the civil war in Corcyra (Ch. 10-11) — the language of social
  breakdown should feel connected
- "Non-Greek peoples" used for the Amphilochian barbarians — maintain this for
  ethnographic passages; "Persian" used specifically for Persian contexts
- The Ambraciot-Amphilochian conflict and Phormio's presence at Naupactus will both
  carry into the next chapter's western Greece campaigns
- Melesander is dead — killed in Lycia. Brief mention, no need to follow up.
- The oracle about the Dorian war and "death vs. dearth" — this is a characteristic
  Thucydidean aside about how people twist prophecies to fit events. If similar
  observations come up, maintain the same slightly wry tone.
