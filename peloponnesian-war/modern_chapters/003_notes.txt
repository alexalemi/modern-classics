CHAPTER 3 TRANSLATION NOTES

=== KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES ===

- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout narrative; "Spartans" also used as
  the address form in speeches (the Corinthians, Athenians, and Archidamus all
  address "Spartans" rather than "Lacedaemonians" — keeps the oratory punchy)
- "Hellas/Hellenes" → "Greece/Greeks" in most cases; "Greek liberty" used where
  Crawley had "rights of Hellas"; retained "Greek" throughout for accessibility
- "the Mede/Median war" → "the Persians/Persian War" consistently
- "barbarian" → "Persian invader" or "the Persians" in context of the Persian War
  speeches; "non-Greek" concept not needed in this chapter since all barbarian
  references are specifically about Persia
- "confederacy" → "alliance" or "allies"
- "viz." → removed; items listed directly
- "ephors" → "ephors — the senior magistrates" (glossed on first use via
  Sthenelaidas introduction)
- "acclamation" → kept, with explanation of the voting system built into the
  narrative
- "heavy infantry" → kept as-is (clear enough)
- "tribute/tributary" → kept as-is
- "Long Walls" → capitalized as proper noun, kept without gloss (context makes
  function clear enough)

=== SPEECH HANDLING ===

- Used horizontal rules (---) to set off the four speeches visually
- Maintained direct address ("Spartans") at key rhetorical moments
- The Corinthian speech's famous "national character" comparison (Athenians vs.
  Spartans) was kept as a single sustained passage — the cumulative rhetorical
  effect of the contrasts is the whole point
- Sthenelaidas's speech kept deliberately short and blunt — contrast with the
  others is part of Thucydides' dramatic structure
- The Athenians' speech about Marathon and Salamis was kept detailed — these are
  real arguments, not just boasting

=== PROPER NOUNS INTRODUCED ===

- Potidaea — Corinthian colony being besieged (established in earlier chapters)
- Aeginetans — from Aegina; mentioned as secret war advocates
- Megarians — from Megara; complain about trade embargo
- Corcyra — mentioned in Corinthian speech (established in earlier chapters)
- Themistocles — Athenian commander at Salamis; named in Athenian speech
- Archidamus — Spartan king; described as wise and moderate
- Sthenelaidas — Spartan ephor who forces the vote
- Attica — Athenian home territory
- Marathon, Salamis — Persian War battle sites (no gloss needed; widely known)
- The Thirty Years' Peace — the treaty in question; kept as proper noun
- Euboea/Euboean affair — referenced at end as occasion for the treaty

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

- The vote at Sparta has been taken: treaty declared broken, but a full allied
  congress still needs to ratify the decision for war
- The Thirty Years' Peace is the treaty framework — it was signed after the
  "Euboean affair" and this vote comes in its 14th year
- Archidamus has been established as the moderate/cautious Spartan king — he will
  likely appear again as a military commander; keep his characterization consistent
- The three motives for Athenian empire have been stated explicitly: fear, honor,
  and interest (a famous Thucydidean formulation) — if these recur, use the same
  three words
- The Corinthian characterization of Athens ("born to take no rest themselves and
  to give none to anyone else") is one of the most famous lines in the work —
  it was rendered closely and should be echoed if referenced later
- Spartan voting procedure (acclamation/division) has been explained — no need
  to re-explain if it comes up again
- "The Peloponnese" used as geographic term for the alliance's territory
- Athens's allies consistently called "allies" (not "subjects" except where
  Thucydides specifically means to highlight the imperial relationship)
- The final line emphasizes Spartan fear of Athenian power as the real motive —
  this is Thucydides' famous thesis about the "truest cause" of the war; keep
  this emphasis if it recurs
