CHAPTER 22 TRANSLATION NOTES

=== KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES ===

- "Lacedaemonians" -> "Spartans" (one mention in context of Peloponnese reinforcements)
- "Hellenic and barbarian" -> "Greek and non-Greek alike" for the darters/javelin-throwers
- "heavy infantry" -> kept as-is throughout (established technical term)
- "darters" -> "javelin-throwers" (more immediately clear to modern readers)
- "counterwall" -> kept as "counterwall" throughout; explained on first use as "the wall they had built to block the Athenians from completing their siege lines around the city"
- "the first watch" -> "after the first watch of the night" (kept the term; context makes the meaning clear)
- "paean" -> kept, with immediate contextual gloss ("the battle hymn")
- "trophies" -> "victory trophies" (first use) then "trophies"
- "engines" -> "siege engines" (clearer)
- "Siceliots" -> "Sicilian Greeks" (more accessible; Siceliots were Greek colonists in Sicily)
- "the Olympieum" -> "the fortified temple area at the Olympieum" (brief context)
- "talents" -> kept (previously glossed in earlier chapters)
- "truce" -> kept

=== STRUCTURAL CHANGES ===

- Broke the massive opening paragraph into several shorter paragraphs: the arrival of reinforcements, the Syracusan reaction, the Athenian reaction, and Demosthenes' strategic reasoning. The original is a single enormous sentence-cluster that buries the key insight (Demosthenes learning from Nicias' delay) deep inside nested clauses.
- Added section headers ("THE ASSAULT ON EPIPOLAE," "THE NIGHT BATTLE," "AFTERMATH," etc.) to help readers navigate what is a long and structurally complex chapter. These do not appear in the original but follow the pattern of the Crawley edition's own chapter subtitle.
- The night battle passage was given extra paragraph breaks to create the sense of escalating chaos. Short paragraphs and sentence fragments mirror the confusion on the ground.
- Nicias' speech was broken into clear argumentative blocks: (1) don't show weakness, (2) the enemy is in worse shape, (3) the Athenian public will punish us, (4) financial argument. In the original these are tangled together.

=== TONE AND NARRATIVE CHOICES ===

- The night battle at Epipolae is one of the most cinematic passages in Thucydides. I leaned into the horror and disorientation: the moonlight that reveals shapes but not identities, the watchword becoming a weapon against its users, the paean -- a sacred battle hymn -- becoming an instrument of terror. Short, punchy sentences during the chaos sections.
- "And then the Boeotians stopped them." -- This abrupt one-line paragraph mirrors the sudden reversal. The Boeotians' stand is the hinge of the entire battle.
- "The moon was bright. But moonlight is a treacherous thing." -- Added the editorializing gloss "treacherous thing" which is not literally in the original but captures the spirit of Thucydides' point about the limits of moonlit visibility.
- Nicias' refusal to retreat is presented with sympathy but also dramatic irony. The reader can feel the tragedy building: his intelligence is wrong (or at least insufficient), his reasoning about Athenian politics is probably correct but irrelevant to survival, and his stubbornness will doom the army. I tried to make his arguments feel genuinely persuasive in the moment while letting the reader sense the catastrophe ahead.
- "And so they hesitated." -- Final sentence is a deliberate understatement. The hesitation of the other commanders is the pivot point of the entire Sicilian disaster.

=== FAMOUS/CRITICAL PASSAGES ===

- Thucydides' aside about this being "the only [night battle] that occurred between great armies during the war" -- preserved and emphasized. This is one of his rare methodological intrusions, reminding us he is a historian conscious of the uniqueness of what he is describing.
- The paean passage is one of the most psychologically acute descriptions of battlefield confusion in ancient literature. Preserved in full with the structural logic made more explicit: (1) the paeans sound the same, (2) therefore allies terrify their own side, (3) therefore everyone becomes the enemy.
- Nicias' speech about preferring death in battle to unjust execution at home -- "he would rather take his chance and die, if die he must, a soldier's death at the hand of the enemy" -- preserved closely. This is one of the most tragic moments in the work. Nicias is essentially choosing death over the political culture of his own city.

=== CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER ===

1. Demosthenes, Eurymedon, and Nicias are the three key commanders. Demosthenes and Eurymedon favor retreat; Nicias refuses. This deadlock carries forward. Menander is a subordinate commander mentioned in the assault force.

2. The army is now in dire condition: defeated at Epipolae, disease-ridden, demoralized, trapped in marshy ground near the harbor. The fleet is their one remaining advantage.

3. Nicias has been characterized as tragically stubborn -- clinging to intelligence from a pro-Athenian faction inside Syracuse and financial analysis of Syracusan weakness. His fear of Athenian domestic politics (being prosecuted at home) is a major driver of his decision-making. This should continue to be a theme.

4. Gylippus is actively recruiting reinforcements across Sicily. Sicanus has been sent to Agrigentum. The strategic situation is deteriorating for Athens -- the enemy is getting stronger while the Athenians debate.

5. The Boeotians were singled out as the force that turned the battle at Epipolae. If they appear again, this should be consistent.

6. "Epipolae" is the high ground overlooking Syracuse -- the key to the siege. The Athenians' failure to hold it means they cannot complete their encirclement of the city. This geographic reality should be maintained.

7. The tone should continue to darken. We are moving toward the final catastrophe. Every delay, every missed opportunity, every moment of hesitation brings the army closer to destruction. The next chapter should feel like the walls closing in.

8. The financial argument (Syracuse has spent 2,000 talents, is running up debts, depends on mercenaries) has been made by Nicias. Whether this is accurate or wishful thinking should remain ambiguous, as Thucydides leaves it.

9. Thapsus and Catana have been mentioned as alternative positions. If the army eventually moves, these are the reference points.
