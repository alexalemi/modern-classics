CHAPTER 2 TRANSLATION NOTES
=============================

KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES
- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout narrative; "Lacedaemon" used once in a commander's name (Lacedaemonius son of Cimon) since it is a proper noun
- "Hellenes/Hellas" → "Greeks/Greece" throughout; "Hellas" retained once inside the Corcyraean speech ("the greatest naval power in Hellas" → "the greatest naval power in Greece") -- decided to modernize even within speeches for this term since the audience is Athenian/Greek and the modern reader benefits from consistency
- "barbarians" → "non-Greek tribes" or "local tribes" on first/early uses; "non-Greek" used as the primary replacement when referring to Illyrian and Thesprotian peoples; raw "barbarian" avoided to prevent modern connotations
- "confederacy" → "alliance" consistently
- "heavy infantry" → kept as-is (clear enough, and the text uses it frequently as a unit type)
- "light troops" → "light troops" or "light infantry" (kept close to original)
- "galleys" → "warships" (the one occurrence in the Crawley text)
- "darters" → "javelin-throwers"
- "suppliants" → "suppliants" (kept; the concept of supplication at temples is important and the word is clear enough)
- "trophy" → "victory monument" (clearer for modern readers; Greek tropaion was a physical marker)
- "paean" → "battle hymn" (the pre-battle war song)
- "herald's wand" → "herald's staff" (the kerykeion/caduceus carried by official envoys)
- "in statu quo" → paraphrased as "both sides could stay where they were"
- "viz." → eliminated, replaced with direct statement
- "undergirded" → "reinforced with undergirding ropes" (kept the technical term but added explanation)
- "heavy infantry on their decks" → kept phrasing; refers to marines/hoplites fighting aboard ships
- "faction/factions" → "political divisions" or "internal political divisions"
- "the Phaeacians" → "the Phaeacians of Homer's Odyssey" (added brief context)

PROPER NOUNS INTRODUCED
- Epidamnus: colony city, no gloss added (context makes role clear)
- Corcyra: island state (modern Corfu), not glossed explicitly but role is clear from context
- Taulantians: identified as "an Illyrian people" (following Crawley)
- Phalius son of Eratocleides: founder of Epidamnus, kept with patronymic (single mention)
- Actium: identified by Crawley's own context ("at the mouth of the Gulf of Ambracia")
- Chimerium: harbor in Thesprotis, kept geographical detail
- Sybota: identified as "a deserted harbor in Thesprotis" and later "one of the Sybota islands"
- Leukimme: headland on Corcyra, kept as-is
- Perdiccas son of Alexander: identified as "king of the Macedonians" / "king of Macedonia"
- Potidaea: identified as being on "the isthmus of Pallene" and as "a Corinthian colony, but tributary allies of Athens"
- Olynthus: inland fortress city, role made clear through context
- Aristeus son of Adimantus: Corinthian commander at Potidaea, kept patronymic at introduction
- Callias son of Calliades: Athenian general, killed at Potidaea
- Phormio son of Asopius: Athenian commander who completes the siege; important figure in later chapters

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
- Phormio has been introduced as a commander at Potidaea; he becomes a major naval commander later -- maintain his characterization
- The siege of Potidaea is ongoing at chapter's end; it is still unresolved
- Aristeus has escaped and is operating among the Chalcidians; his fate may come up
- Perdiccas has switched sides (from Athens back to Potidaea's side); he is an unreliable player who switches allegiances repeatedly throughout the war
- The Corinthian grievance against Athens is now established (fighting at Corcyra during treaty time)
- The treaty between Athens and the Peloponnesians is under severe strain but not formally broken
- "Victory monument" was used for "trophy" -- maintain this choice
- "Battle hymn" was used for "paean" -- maintain this choice
- "Non-Greek" or "local tribes" was used for "barbarian" in narrative -- maintain this choice
- Speeches were kept in a formal but accessible register; diplomatic language was preserved but sentences were broken up for clarity
- Patronymics: included at first introduction of military commanders, can be dropped on subsequent mentions
- The defensive-only nature of the Athens-Corcyra alliance is important and was emphasized; this distinction matters for the treaty-violation arguments that follow
- Drachmas kept as "drachmas" (Corinthian drachmas specifically mentioned once)
