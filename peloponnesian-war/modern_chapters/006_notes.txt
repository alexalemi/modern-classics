CHAPTER 6 TRANSLATION NOTES
=============================

KEY VOCABULARY CHOICES
- "Lacedaemonians" → "Spartans" throughout narrative; "Lacedaemon" not used in this chapter
- "Hellas/Hellenes" → "Greece/Greeks" in most contexts; "Greece" used in Pericles' speech ("the school of Greece") except in the famous line which keeps the resonance of the original
- "Boeotarchs" → "Theban generals" (per text analysis: "Theban commander / Boeotian general")
- "confederacy" → "alliance" throughout
- "demes" → "townships" (e.g., Acharnae described as "the largest township in Attica")
- "heavy infantry" → kept as-is (technical term, clear enough)
- "talents" → kept as-is; amounts given in context make the scale clear
- "proxenus" → "diplomatic representative" (for Nymphodorus)
- "the Median/Mede" → "Persian/Persians" (consistent with Ch. 1 approach)
- "resident aliens" → kept (metics — clear enough in context)
- "viz." → dropped, items simply listed
- "galleys" → kept
- "trophy" → kept (Greek military custom of erecting a marker at the battle site)
- "heavy armour" → "heavy armor" (American spelling maintained)
- "light troops" → "light troops" or "light infantry" (per text analysis)
- "barbarian powers" → "foreign powers" (in the diplomatic context of seeking allies)

PROPER NOUNS INTRODUCED
- Pythangelus, son of Phyleides — Theban general (Boeotarch); patronymic given on introduction
- Diemporus, son of Onetorides — Theban general (Boeotarch); patronymic given on introduction
- Naucleides — Plataean traitor who opened the gates
- Eurymachus, son of Leontiades — influential Theban, arranged the plot; executed among the 180 prisoners
- Archidamus — Spartan king, commander of the invasion; introduced previously in Ch. 3 as a speaker, here as military leader
- Melesippus, son of Diacritus — Spartan herald sent to Athens; his words at the border ("great suffering for the Greeks") preserved
- Pericles, son of Xanthippus — Athenian general and statesman; described as "one of the ten generals"
- Pleistoanax, son of Pausanias — earlier Spartan king who turned back from Attica (historical reference)
- Brasidas, son of Tellis — Spartan officer, first to receive commendation in the war; important character later
- Cleopompus, son of Clinias — Athenian naval commander
- Carcinus, Proteas, Socrates (with patronymics) — commanders of the 100-ship fleet
- Nymphodorus, son of Pythes — Abderite diplomat, sister married to Sitalces
- Sitalces, son of Teres — king of the Odrysian Thracians; new Athenian ally
- Teres — father of Sitalces; distinguished from mythological Tereus (digression preserved)
- Sadocus — son of Sitalces, given Athenian citizenship
- Perdiccas, son of Alexander — king of Macedon; reconciled with Athens
- Phormio — Athenian commander (mentioned briefly in Chalcidian expedition; will be important later)
- Evarchus — tyrant of Astacus, expelled then restored by Corinthians

GEOGRAPHIC NAMES
- Plataea — Boeotian town allied with Athens; scene of the opening night attack
- Asopus River — swollen by rain, delayed Theban reinforcements
- Oenoe — border fortress, first target of the invasion
- Eleusis, Thriasian plain — initial Peloponnesian encampment in Attica
- Acharnae — largest Attic township, site of main encampment
- Rheiti ("the Brooks") — site of cavalry skirmish
- Mount Aegaleus, Cropia — landmarks on the army's advance
- Mount Parnes, Brilessus — landmarks defining area ravaged
- Methone — in Laconia; saved by Brasidas
- Pheia, Point Ichthys — in Elis; site of storm and temporary capture
- Aegina — islanders expelled, settled by Athenians
- Thyrea — on Argolis-Laconia border; refuge for expelled Aeginetans
- Sollium, Astacus — Acarnanian towns taken by Athenian fleet
- Cephallenia — island with four communities, won over without fight
- Atalanta — fortified island off Locris
- Ceramicus — "the most beautiful suburb," site of public cemetery
- Daulis — in Phocis; home of mythological Tereus (digression)

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
- Pericles' Funeral Oration is now complete. The famous lines preserved: "the school of Greece" (from "school of Hellas"), the passage about monuments needing no Homer, heroes having "the whole earth for their tomb" rendered as "their grave is... in every land, in every heart"
- Brasidas has been introduced as a daring Spartan officer — he will become a major character in later books. His commendation is noted.
- Phormio mentioned briefly — he will be a key naval commander in the next chapters.
- The Plataean episode is complete for now but Plataea will return in Book III (siege and fall).
- The public funeral custom has been described in detail — this will recur and can be referenced more briefly.
- Pericles' strategy (stay behind the walls, rely on the fleet, don't engage on land) has been clearly established. The next chapter should show the consequences — especially the plague, which will devastate the crowded city.
- The tone of the Funeral Oration was kept oratorical and elevated — rhythmic, with short punchy sentences alternating with longer flowing ones. Speeches are set off with "---" dividers, consistent with Ch. 3.
- Thucydides' first person preserved: "in my opinion" regarding the oracle about the Pelasgian ground.
- The reserve fund of 1,000 talents and 100 ships has been established — this will be referenced later.
- Archidamus's calculated strategy at Acharnae explained clearly — the psychological warfare angle.
- The eclipse mentioned — Thucydides' interest in natural phenomena and omens is part of his method.
- Evarchus restored to Astacus by Corinthians in the winter — minor subplot.
- The pattern of annual Megarian invasions noted — "this continued until they captured the port of Nisaea."
- American spelling used throughout (honor, favor, harbor, armor).
