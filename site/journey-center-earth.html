Chapter 1

On Sunday, the 24th of May, 1863, my uncle, Professor Lidenbrock, came rushing back to his little house at No. 19 König-strasse, one of the oldest streets in the old quarter of Hamburg.

Martha, our good housekeeper, must have thought she was terribly behind, because dinner was only just beginning to sing on the kitchen stove.

"Well," I said to myself, "if he's hungry, my uncle — the most impatient man alive — is going to howl the house down."

"Professor Lidenbrock already!" cried Martha, astonished, easing open the dining-room door.

"Yes, Martha. But dinner has every right not to be ready, because it isn't two o'clock yet. Saint Michael's has only just struck the half hour."

"Then why is Professor Lidenbrock coming home?"

"He'll no doubt tell us himself."

"Here he comes! I'm off. Master Axel, you make him see reason."

And good Martha retreated to her culinary laboratory.

I was left on my own. But making the most short-tempered of professors see reason — that was rather more than my somewhat indecisive character was equal to. So I was preparing to beat a prudent retreat up to my little room on the top floor, when the street door screamed on its hinges, a pair of big feet came cracking up the wooden staircase, and the master of the house, crossing the dining room, plunged straight into his study.

But in that quick passage he had managed to fling his nutcracker-headed cane into one corner, toss his broad, rough-napped hat onto the table, and hurl these ringing words at his nephew:

"Axel, follow me!"

I hadn't had time to stir before the professor was shouting at me again, with a sharp note of impatience:

"Well? You're not here yet?"

I bolted into the study of my formidable master.

Otto Lidenbrock was not a bad man, I'll gladly grant that; but unless something improbable changes him, he will die a terrible eccentric to the very end.

He was a professor at the Johannaeum, where he gave a course in mineralogy during which he lost his temper, as a rule, once or twice a session. Not that he cared whether his students turned up faithfully to his lessons, or how closely they paid attention, or what success they might go on to have; those little details hardly troubled him. He taught "subjectively" — to borrow a term from German philosophy — for himself, and not for anyone else. He was a selfish scholar, a well of learning whose pulley squealed whenever you tried to draw anything up out of it. In a word: a miser.

There are a few professors of this stripe in Germany.

My uncle, unfortunately, was not blessed with a great gift for pronunciation — not in private, at least, but certainly in public, and that is a regrettable flaw in a public speaker. In his lectures at the Johannaeum he would often stop dead; he'd be wrestling with some stubborn word that refused to slide out between his lips, one of those words that dig in their heels, swell up, and finally burst out in the highly unscientific form of an oath. And then: mighty rage.

Mineralogy has a great many half-Greek, half-Latin names, hard to pronounce — rough, jaw-breaking labels that would flay a poet's lips. I don't mean to run the science down. Far from it. But when a man finds himself face to face with rhombohedral crystallizations, retinasphalt resins, gehlenites, tangasites, molybdates of lead, tungstates of manganese, and titanates of zirconia, even the nimblest tongue is allowed to trip.

Now, all over town this pardonable weakness of my uncle's was well known, and people took advantage of it; they lay in wait for him at the dangerous passages, he flew into a fury, and they laughed — which is not in good taste, even for Germans. And so, if there was always a great crowd of listeners at Lidenbrock's lectures, how many of them followed the course so faithfully mainly to enjoy the professor's magnificent rages?

Be that as it may, my uncle — I can't say it too often — was a genuine scholar. Though he sometimes shattered his specimens by testing them too roughly, he combined the instinct of the geologist with the eye of the mineralogist. With his hammer, his steel point, his magnetic needle, his blowpipe, and his flask of nitric acid, he was a formidable man. From the fracture, the look, the hardness, the fusibility, the sound, the smell, or the taste of any given mineral, he would classify it without hesitation among the six hundred species that science recognizes today.

So the name of Lidenbrock rang out with honor in the schools and the learned societies. Humphry Davy, Humboldt, Captains Franklin and Sabine never failed to call on him when they passed through Hamburg. Becquerel, Ebelmen, Brewster, Dumas, Milne-Edwards all liked to consult him on the most gripping questions in chemistry. That science owed him some rather fine discoveries, and in 1853 there had appeared in Leipzig a Treatise on Transcendental Crystallography, by Professor Otto Lidenbrock — a grand folio volume with plates, which nevertheless failed to cover its own costs.

Add to all this that my uncle was curator of the mineralogical museum of Monsieur Struve, the Russian ambassador — a valuable collection with a Europe-wide reputation.

That, then, is the man who was hailing me so impatiently. Picture a tall, lean fellow, iron-healthy, with a boyish blond coloring that knocked a good ten years off his fifty. His big eyes rolled ceaselessly behind an enormous pair of spectacles; his long, thin nose was like the blade of a knife; the malicious even claimed it was magnetized and pulled in iron filings. Pure slander: it pulled in nothing but tobacco — though a great deal of that, it must be admitted, to be honest.

When I add that my uncle took mathematical strides half a fathom long, and that as he walked he kept his fists firmly clenched — the mark of an impetuous temperament — you will know him well enough not to hanker after his company.

He lived in his little house on König-strasse, a dwelling half wood, half brick, with a scalloped gable; it looked out onto one of those winding canals that crisscross the middle of Hamburg's oldest quarter, which the great fire of 1842 had mercifully spared.

The old house did lean a little, it's true, and thrust its belly out at the passersby; it wore its roof tilted over one ear, like the cap of a student of the Tugendbund; the plumb of its lines left something to be desired; but all in all it held itself up well, thanks to an old elm vigorously wedged into the front wall, which in spring pushed its budding blossoms in through the panes of the windows.

My uncle was not badly off, as German professors go. The house was his outright, contents and all. The contents were his goddaughter Graüben — a young Virland girl of seventeen — our good Martha, and me. In my double capacity as nephew and orphan, I became his assistant-preparator in his experiments.

I'll admit I bit into the geological sciences with a hearty appetite; I had mineralogist's blood in my veins, and I was never bored in the company of my precious rocks.

All in all, one could live happily in that little house on König-strasse, despite its owner's fits of impatience — because, for all his rather brutal way of going about things, he loved me no less for it. But the man simply did not know how to wait; he was in more of a hurry than nature itself.

When, back in April, he had planted seedlings of mignonette or morning glory in the china pots of his sitting room, every morning he would go and faithfully tug them by the leaves to hurry their growth along.

With an eccentric like that, there was nothing to do but obey. So I dashed into his study.

Chapter 2

That study was a genuine museum. Every specimen the mineral kingdom has to offer was there, labeled and arranged in the most perfect order, according to the three great divisions of minerals: the flammable, the metallic, and the stony.

How well I knew them, those trinkets of the science of mineralogy! How many times, instead of loafing about with boys my own age, had I amused myself dusting these graphites, these anthracites, these coals, these lignites, these peats! And the bitumens, the resins, the organic salts that had to be shielded from the least speck of dust! And these metals, from iron all the way up to gold, whose relative value vanished before the absolute equality of scientific specimens! And all these stones, enough to rebuild the house on König-strasse from scratch — even with a fine extra bedroom, which I could have made very good use of!

But as I stepped into the study, none of these wonders was on my mind. My uncle alone filled my thoughts. He had buried himself in his big armchair upholstered in Utrecht velvet, and he held a book in his hands that he was contemplating with the most profound admiration.

"What a book! What a book!" he kept exclaiming.

That outburst reminded me that Professor Lidenbrock was also, in his idle moments, a book fanatic. But an old volume had no value in his eyes unless it was impossible to find, or at the very least impossible to read.

"Well!" he said to me. "Don't you see it? It's a priceless treasure that I turned up this morning, poking around in the shop of Hevelius the Jew."

"Magnificent!" I answered, with a show of enthusiasm to order.

For really, what was all this fuss about? An old quarto whose spine and boards seemed to be made of coarse calfskin, a yellowish book with a faded bookmark dangling from it.

Meanwhile the professor's admiring exclamations went on without a pause.

"Look," he was saying, putting the questions and the answers to himself. "Isn't it beautiful? Yes, it's splendid! And what a binding! Does the book open easily? Yes, because it stays open at any page you like! But does it close properly? Yes, because the cover and the pages form a single unbroken whole, without gaping or coming apart anywhere. And this spine — not a single crack in it after seven hundred years! Ah, there's a binding that Bozerian, Closs, or Purgold would have been proud of!"

And as he talked, my uncle kept opening and closing the old book. The least I could do was to ask him about its contents, though they didn't interest me in the slightest.

"And what is the title of this marvelous volume?" I asked, with an eagerness far too enthusiastic not to be put on.

"This work!" my uncle replied, warming up. "This is the Heims-Kringla of Snorre Turleson, the famous Icelandic author of the twelfth century! It's the Chronicle of the Norwegian princes who reigned in Iceland."

"Really!" I cried, as best I could. "And no doubt it's a translation into German?"

"A translation!" the professor shot back sharply. "And what would I want with your translation? Who cares about your translation? This is the original work, in the Icelandic tongue — that magnificent language, rich and simple at the same time, which allows for the most varied grammatical combinations and countless modifications of words!"

"Like German," I ventured, rather pleased with myself.

"Yes," my uncle answered, shrugging his shoulders, "with the difference that Icelandic admits the three genders, like Greek, and declines proper nouns, like Latin!"

"Ah!" I said, my indifference a little shaken. "And are the letters of this book beautiful?"

"Letters! Who's talking about letters, you wretched Axel! Letters, indeed! Ah, so you take this for a printed book! But you ignoramus, it's a manuscript — and a runic manuscript!"

"Runic?"

"Yes! Are you going to ask me now to explain that word for you?"

"I'll be careful not to," I replied, with the tone of a man wounded in his self-respect.

But my uncle carried on more energetically than ever, and instructed me, against my will, in things I had no great wish to know.

"The runes," he went on, "were characters of writing once used in Iceland, and, according to tradition, they were invented by Odin himself! But look, then — admire them, you heathen — these letters that sprang from the imagination of a god!"

Well, for lack of any reply, I was on the point of prostrating myself — the sort of answer that ought to please gods and kings alike, since it has the advantage of never putting them on the spot — when an incident came to divert the course of the conversation.

It was the appearance of a grimy parchment that slipped out of the book and fell to the floor.

My uncle pounced on this scrap with an eagerness easy to understand. An old document, sealed away perhaps for time out of mind inside an old book, could not fail to be of the highest value in his eyes.

"What's this?" he cried.

And at the same moment he was carefully spreading out on his table a piece of parchment five inches long and three wide, across which ran, in horizontal lines, characters straight out of some book of spells.

The professor studied this string of characters for a few moments; then he said, pushing his spectacles up onto his forehead:

"This is runic. These letters are absolutely identical to those in Snorre Turleson's manuscript! But... what on earth can it mean?"

Since runic struck me as an invention of scholars meant to mystify the poor world, I was not sorry to see that my uncle understood none of it. At least, that was how it seemed to me from the movement of his fingers, which were beginning to twitch alarmingly.

"And yet it's Old Icelandic!" he muttered between his teeth.

And Professor Lidenbrock surely ought to have known, for he passed for a real polyglot. Not that he spoke fluently the two thousand languages and four thousand dialects used across the face of the globe, but he knew his fair share of them.

He was on the verge, then, faced with this difficulty, of giving free rein to the full impetuosity of his temper, and I foresaw a violent scene — when two o'clock chimed on the little clock over the mantelpiece.

At once, good Martha opened the door of the study and announced:

"The soup is served."

"To the devil with the soup," cried my uncle, "and with the woman who made it, and with anyone who eats it!"

Martha fled. I flew after her, and, hardly knowing how, found myself seated in my usual place in the dining room.

I waited a few moments. The professor did not come. It was the first time, to my knowledge, that he had missed the solemn ritual of dinner. And what a dinner it was, too! Parsley soup, a ham omelette seasoned with sorrel and nutmeg, a loin of veal with stewed plums, and, for dessert, sugared shrimp — all of it washed down with a nice Moselle wine.

That was what an old sheet of paper was going to cost my uncle. As a devoted nephew, I felt it my duty to eat for him, and for myself as well — which I did in all good conscience.

"I have never seen anything like it!" said good Martha as she served. "Professor Lidenbrock not at the table!"

"It's beyond belief."

"It's a sign of some grave event to come!" the old servant went on, shaking her head.

In my opinion it was a sign of nothing at all — nothing except a frightful scene when my uncle found his dinner devoured.

I was down to my last shrimp when a thundering voice tore me from the delights of dessert. In a single bound I was out of the dining room and into the study.

"It's runic, obviously," said the professor, knitting his brows. "But there's a secret in it, and I'll dig it out, or else—"

A violent gesture finished the thought.

"Sit down there," he added, jabbing his fist toward the table, "and write."

In an instant I was ready.

"Now, I'm going to dictate to you the letter of our alphabet that corresponds to each of these Icelandic characters. We'll see what it gives us. But, by Saint Michael, mind you don't make a single mistake!"

The dictation began. I did my best; each letter was called out one after another, and together they formed the incomprehensible string of words below:

When this labor was finished, my uncle snatched up the sheet I had just written on and studied it for a long while, closely.

"What on earth does it mean?" he kept repeating, mechanically.

On my honor, I couldn't have told him. And in any case he didn't ask me — he simply went on talking to himself:

"This is what we call a cryptogram," he said. "The meaning is hidden under letters deliberately scrambled; arrange them properly and they'd form an intelligible sentence. To think that this might hold the explanation, or the key, to some great discovery!"

For my part, I thought it held absolutely nothing at all — but I prudently kept my opinion to myself.

The professor then took up the book and the parchment and compared the two.

"These two scripts aren't in the same hand," he said. "The cryptogram is later than the book, and I can see the proof at once — it's irrefutable. Look: the very first letter is a double M, one you'd search for in vain in Turleson's book, because it wasn't added to the Icelandic alphabet until the fourteenth century. So there are at least two hundred years between the manuscript and this document."

That, I'll admit, struck me as reasonably logical.

"I'm therefore led to think," my uncle went on, "that one of the owners of this book must have traced these mysterious characters. But who the devil was that owner? Might he not have put his name somewhere in the manuscript?"

My uncle pushed his spectacles up onto his forehead, took a strong magnifying glass, and carefully went over the opening pages of the book. On the back of the second leaf — the one behind the half-title — he found a sort of smudge that looked to the eye like an ink blot. But looking closely, you could make out a few half-effaced characters. My uncle understood at once that this was the point of interest; he bore down on the smudge, and with the help of his big glass he finally recognized the following signs, runic characters that he read off without hesitation:

ᛐ ᛦ ᚳ ᛅ ᚼ ᛐ ᚴ ᚳ ᚢ ᚼ ᚼ ᛅ ᛯ

"Arne Saknussemm!" he cried, in a tone of triumph. "Why, that's a name — and an Icelandic name at that! The name of a scholar of the sixteenth century, a famous alchemist!"

I looked at my uncle with a certain admiration.

"These alchemists," he went on, "Avicenna, Bacon, Lull, Paracelsus — they were the true, the only scholars of their age. They made discoveries that we have every right to marvel at. Why shouldn't this Saknussemm have buried some astonishing invention beneath this incomprehensible cryptogram? It must be so. It is so."

The professor's imagination caught fire at the idea.

"No doubt," I ventured to reply, "but what interest could this scholar have had in hiding some marvelous discovery this way?"

"Why? Why? How should I know? Didn't Galileo do exactly that with Saturn? In any case, we'll soon see. I'll have the secret of this document, and I won't take food or sleep until I've guessed it."

"Oh," I thought.

"And neither will you, Axel," he added.

"The devil," I said to myself. "A good thing I ate enough for two!"

"And first of all," my uncle continued, "we have to find the language of this 'cipher.' That shouldn't be difficult."

At these words I looked up sharply. My uncle resumed his soliloquy:

"Nothing simpler. There are a hundred and thirty-two letters in this document, giving seventy-nine consonants against fifty-three vowels. Now, that's roughly the proportion in which the words of the southern languages are formed, whereas the tongues of the north are infinitely richer in consonants. So we're dealing with a language of the south."

The conclusions were quite sound.

"But which language is it?"

Here was where I expected my scholar to stumble — though in him, all the same, I was discovering a profound analyst.

"This Saknussemm," he went on, "was an educated man; and once he wasn't writing in his mother tongue, he'd naturally have chosen the language most current among the cultivated minds of the sixteenth century — I mean Latin. If I'm wrong, I can try Spanish, French, Italian, Greek, Hebrew. But the scholars of the sixteenth century generally wrote in Latin. So I have the right to say, a priori: this is Latin."

I jumped in my chair. My memories as a Latin student rose up in revolt against the notion that this string of grotesque words could belong to the sweet language of Virgil.

"Yes! Latin," my uncle went on, "but scrambled Latin."

"Fine and good," I thought. "If you unscramble that, uncle, you're a clever man."

"Let's examine it closely," he said, taking up the sheet I'd written on again. "Here's a series of a hundred and thirty-two letters presented in apparent disorder. There are words where the consonants stand alone, like the first, 'mrnlls'; others, on the contrary, where the vowels crowd together — the fifth, for instance, 'unteief,' or the second to last, 'oseibo.' Now, this arrangement clearly wasn't contrived; it's given mathematically by the unknown rule that governed the sequence of these letters. It seems certain to me that the original sentence was written out properly, then turned around according to some law we have to discover. Whoever held the key to this 'cipher' could read it fluently. But what is that key? Axel, have you got that key?"

To this question I said nothing — and for good reason. My eyes had come to rest on a charming portrait hanging on the wall, the portrait of Graüben. My uncle's ward was at that time in Altona, staying with a relative, and her absence made me very sad, for — I can confess it now — the pretty Virlander and the professor's nephew were in love with all the patience and all the calm of Germany; we had become engaged without my uncle's knowledge, he being too much of a geologist to understand such feelings. Graüben was a charming young woman, fair-haired, with blue eyes, of a somewhat grave temperament and a somewhat serious mind; but she loved me no less for that, and for my part I adored her, if indeed that verb exists in the Germanic tongue! The image of my little Virlander thus flung me, in an instant, out of the world of realities and into the world of daydreams, of memories.

I saw again the faithful companion of my labors and my pleasures. Every day she helped me arrange my uncle's precious stones; she labeled them with me. What a formidable mineralogist Miss Graüben was! She loved to get to the bottom of the thorny questions of science. How many sweet hours we had spent studying together, and how often I envied the lot of those insensible stones she handled with her charming hands.

Then, when the moment of recreation came, we would go out together; we'd take the leafy walks along the Alster and make our way in company to the old tarred mill that looks so fine at the end of the lake; along the way we'd talk, holding hands; I told her things that made her laugh her best; and so we'd come to the bank of the Elbe, and after saying good evening to the swans swimming among the big white water lilies, we'd come back to the quay by the steamboat.

I had gotten that far in my reverie when my uncle, banging his fist on the table, dragged me violently back to reality.

"Look here," he said, "the first idea that ought to come to mind for scrambling the letters of a sentence is, it seems to me, to write the words vertically instead of tracing them horizontally."

"Well, well," I thought.

"We have to see what that produces. Axel, throw some sentence or other onto this scrap of paper; but instead of setting the letters down one after another, place them in vertical columns, so as to group them in fives or sixes."

I understood what he was after, and immediately I wrote from top to bottom:

J m n e , b e e , t G e t' b m i r n a i a t a ! i e p e ü

"Good," said the professor, without having read it. "Now set these words out on a horizontal line."

I obeyed, and I got the following sentence:

Jmne,b ee,tGe t'bmirn aiata! iepeü

"Perfect!" cried my uncle, tearing the paper out of my hands. "There — that already has the look of the old document; the vowels are grouped together, and the consonants too, in the same disorder; there are even capitals in the middle of words, and commas, exactly as in Saknussemm's parchment!"

I couldn't help finding these remarks extremely ingenious.

"Now," my uncle went on, addressing himself directly to me, "to read the sentence you've just written, which I don't know, all I need do is take the first letter of each word in turn, then the second, then the third, and so on."

And my uncle, to his great astonishment — and above all to mine — read:

Je t'aime bien, ma petite Graüben!

"Eh!" went the professor.

Yes — without suspecting it, like a clumsy lover, I had traced that compromising sentence: I love you dearly, my little Graüben!

"Ah! so you love Graüben!" my uncle went on, in a thoroughly guardian-like tone.

"Yes... No... " I stammered.

"Ah! so you love Graüben," he repeated, mechanically. "Well then — let's apply my method to the document in question!"

My uncle, sunk back into his absorbing contemplation, had already forgotten my imprudent words. Imprudent, I say, because a scholar's head could never grasp the affairs of the heart. But, luckily, the great business of the document won out.

At the moment of making his crucial experiment, Professor Lidenbrock's eyes shot lightning through his spectacles; his fingers trembled as he took up the old parchment again; he was seriously moved. At last he coughed hard, and in a grave voice, calling out one after another the first letter, then the second of each word, he dictated to me the following series:

But — who could have foreseen it! A violent blow of his fist shook the table. The ink leaped, the pen jumped out of my hands.

"That's not it!" cried my uncle. "It doesn't make any sense!"

Then, crossing the study like a cannonball, plunging down the stairs like an avalanche, he hurled himself into König-strasse and fled away as fast as his legs would carry him.

Chapter 4

"He's gone out?" cried Martha, hurrying up at the sound of the street door, which had slammed shut hard enough to shake the whole house.

"Yes," I answered, "gone right out!"

"But what about his dinner?" the old servant asked.

"He won't be having dinner!"

"And his supper?"

"He won't be having supper either!"

"What?" said Martha, clasping her hands.

"No, my good Martha. He won't be eating at all — and neither will anyone else in this house! My uncle Lidenbrock is putting us all on a starvation diet until he cracks an old scribble that is absolutely uncrackable!"

"Heaven help us! So we're all to starve to death, then!"

I didn't dare admit that, with a man as absolute as my uncle, starving was exactly the fate in store for us.

Seriously alarmed, the old servant went back to her kitchen, groaning.

Once I was alone, the thought came to me of going to tell Graüben all about it. But how could I leave the house? What if he called for me? What if he wanted to take up that word-puzzle again — the sort of riddle that would have stumped old Oedipus himself? And if I failed to answer his summons, what then?

The wisest course was to stay put. As it happened, a mineralogist from Besançon had just sent us a collection of siliceous geodes that needed sorting. So I set to work. I sorted, I labeled, I arranged in their display case all those hollow stones with the little crystals stirring inside them.

But the task didn't absorb me. The business of the old document kept nagging at me in the strangest way. My head was seething, and I felt seized by a vague uneasiness. I had a premonition of some imminent disaster.

Within an hour my geodes were neatly shelved. I let myself sink back into the big Utrecht-velvet armchair, arms dangling, head thrown back. I lit my long pipe with the curved stem, whose carved bowl showed a naiad lounging lazily; then I amused myself watching the charring creep along, turning my naiad little by little into a thoroughly blackened figure. Every so often I listened for a footstep on the stairs. But no. Where could my uncle be just now? I pictured him striding beneath the handsome trees along the Altona road, gesticulating, thwacking the wall with his cane, whipping the tall grass with a furious arm, beheading thistles, and rousting the solitary storks out of their peace and quiet.

Would he come home triumphant or defeated? Which would win out over the other — the secret, or the man? I asked myself these questions and, without thinking, picked up between my fingers the sheet of paper on which I had copied out that unintelligible string of letters. I kept repeating to myself:

"What on earth does it mean?"

I tried grouping the letters so as to form words. Impossible. Whether I joined them in twos, threes, fives, or sixes, it came to absolutely nothing intelligible. True, the fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth letters made the English word "ice," and the eighty-fourth, eighty-fifth, and eighty-sixth spelled out "sir." And then, in the body of the document, on the second and third lines, I also spotted the Latin words "rota," "mutabile," "ira," "neo," "atra."

"The devil," I thought. "These last words would seem to prove my uncle right about the language of the document! And look — on the fourth line I can even make out the word 'luco,' which means 'sacred grove.' Yet on the third line you read 'tabiled,' with a perfectly Hebrew look to it, and on the last line the words 'mer,' 'arc,' 'mère,' which are pure French."

It was enough to drive you out of your mind! Four different languages in one absurd sentence! What possible connection could there be among the words ice, sir, anger, cruel, sacred grove, changing, mother, bow, and sea? The first and the last did fit together easily enough — no surprise that a document written in Iceland should mention a "sea of ice." But going from that to understanding the rest of the cryptogram was another matter entirely.

So there I was, wrestling with an unsolvable problem. My brain was overheating; my eyes were blinking at the sheet of paper; the hundred and thirty-two letters seemed to flutter around me like those silvery flecks that swim in the air about your head when the blood has rushed to it.

I was in the grip of a kind of hallucination. I was suffocating; I needed air. Absently, I fanned myself with the sheet of paper, and its back and front passed before my eyes one after another.

Imagine my surprise when, in one of those rapid flips — just as the back turned toward me — I thought I saw perfectly legible words appear, Latin words, among them "craterem" and "terrestre."

Suddenly a flash of light broke in my mind. Those few clues let me glimpse the truth; I had discovered the law of the cipher. To read this document, you didn't even need to read it through the reversed sheet! No. Just as it stood, just as it had been dictated to me, it could be spelled out fluently. All the professor's ingenious calculations came right; he had been correct about the arrangement of the letters, correct about the language of the document! It had come within a hair's breadth of letting him read this whole Latin sentence straight through — and that hair's breadth chance had just fallen into my lap!

You can imagine how shaken I was! My eyes clouded over; I couldn't use them. I had spread the sheet of paper flat on the table. A single glance was all it would take to make me master of the secret.

At last I managed to calm my agitation. I laid down the rule that I would walk twice around the room to settle my nerves, and then I came back and buried myself in the vast armchair.

"Let's read it," I cried, after laying in a full lungful of air.

I bent over the table; I set my finger on each letter in turn and, without stopping, without hesitating for an instant, I read the whole sentence aloud.

But what astonishment, what terror flooded through me! At first I sat as though struck by a sudden blow. What! The thing I had just learned had actually been done! A man had had the daring to venture down there! . . .

"No!" I cried, leaping to my feet. "No, no! My uncle must not know! It would be the last straw for him to learn of such a journey! He'd want a taste of it himself! Nothing could stop him! A geologist that determined — he'd go anyway, no matter what, in spite of everything! And he'd drag me along with him, and we'd never come back! Never! Never!"

I was in a state of excitement almost impossible to describe.

"No! No! It shall not be," I said forcefully. "And since I can keep such an idea from ever entering my tyrant's head, I will. Turning this document over and over, he might just stumble on the key by chance. Let's destroy it!"

There was still a bit of fire glowing in the hearth. I snatched up not only the sheet of paper but Saknussemm's parchment as well; with a feverish hand I was about to fling the whole lot onto the coals and wipe out this dangerous secret — when the study door opened. My uncle appeared.

Chapter 5

I barely had time to lay the wretched document back on the table.

Professor Lidenbrock seemed deep in thought. The one idea that ruled him wouldn't give him a moment's peace; he had plainly turned the matter over, dissected it, brought every resource of his imagination to bear on it during his walk, and now he was coming back to try some fresh angle.

Sure enough, he dropped into his armchair, took up a pen, and began setting down formulas that looked like an algebra problem.

I followed his trembling hand with my eyes; I didn't miss a single one of his movements. Was some unhoped-for result about to burst out of nowhere? I was shaking — and with no reason to, since the true arrangement, the *only* one, had already been found, which made any other line of inquiry necessarily useless.

For three long hours my uncle worked without a word, without lifting his head, rubbing out, starting over, crossing through, beginning again a thousand times.

I knew perfectly well that if he managed to set the letters in every relative position they could possibly occupy, the sentence would come out. But I also knew that twenty letters alone can form two quintillion, four hundred and thirty-two quadrillion, nine hundred and two trillion, eight billion, one hundred and seventy-six million, six hundred and forty thousand combinations. Now, there were a hundred and thirty-two letters in the phrase, and those hundred and thirty-two letters yielded a number of different sentences made up of at least a hundred and thirty-three digits — a number all but impossible to count, one that escapes any real reckoning.

On the score of that heroic method of solving the problem, I felt safe.

Meanwhile the time slipped by; night came on; the noises in the street died down; my uncle, still bent over his task, saw nothing — not even good Martha easing the door open; he heard nothing — not even the voice of that worthy servant, asking:

"Will the Professor be having any supper tonight?"

And so Martha had to go away without an answer. As for me, after holding out for a while, I was overtaken by an unconquerable drowsiness, and I fell asleep on one end of the couch, while my uncle Lidenbrock went on calculating and crossing out.

When I woke the next morning, the tireless digger was still at work. His red eyes, his pallid complexion, his hair tangled under his feverish hand, his flushed cheekbones — all of it spoke plainly enough of his terrible struggle with the impossible, and of what mental exhaustion, what strain of the brain, the hours must have cost him.

Honestly, I felt sorry for him. In spite of the reproaches I thought I had every right to fling at him, a certain tenderness was creeping over me. The poor man was so utterly possessed by his idea that he forgot to fly into a rage; all his living forces were concentrated on a single point, and since they weren't escaping through their usual outlet, there was reason to fear that the pressure might make him burst at any moment.

I could loosen that iron vise clamped around his skull with a single gesture — a single word! And I did nothing.

And yet I had a good heart. Why did I stay silent at such a moment? For my uncle's own sake.

"No, no," I kept telling myself, "no, I won't speak! He'd want to go, I know him; nothing on earth could stop him. He has a volcanic imagination, and to do what other geologists have never done, he'd stake his life. I'll keep quiet; I'll hold on to this secret that chance has made me master of. To let it out would be to kill Professor Lidenbrock. Let him guess it, if he can; I don't want to reproach myself one day for having led him to his death."

That firmly settled, I folded my arms and waited. But I'd reckoned without an incident that turned up a few hours later.

When good Martha went to leave the house for the market, she found the door locked; the big key was gone from the lock.

Who had taken it out? My uncle, obviously, when he came home the night before after his headlong walk.

Was it on purpose? Was it an oversight? Did he mean to subject us to the pangs of hunger? That struck me as going a bit far. What! Martha and I, made the victims of a situation that was none of our business in the least? No doubt about it — and I remembered a precedent well suited to alarm us. A few years back, at the time my uncle was working on his great mineralogical classification, he went forty-eight hours without eating, and the whole household had to fall in with this scientific fast. For my own part, I came away with stomach cramps that were anything but amusing in a boy of a fairly ravenous nature.

Now it looked to me as though breakfast was going to fail us the way supper had the night before. Still, I resolved to be heroic and not to give in to the demands of hunger. Martha took it very seriously and was in despair, the good woman. As for me, the impossibility of leaving the house worried me more — and with good reason. You can well understand why.

My uncle went on working; his imagination was lost in the ideal world of combinations; he was living far from the earth, and truly beyond all earthly needs.

Around noon, hunger began to prod me in earnest. Martha, all innocence, had devoured the pantry's provisions the night before; there was nothing left in the house. And yet I held firm. I made a sort of point of honor of it.

Two o'clock struck. This was getting ridiculous — intolerable, even; my eyes were opening wider and wider. I began telling myself that I was exaggerating the importance of the document; that my uncle wouldn't put any stock in it; that he'd see nothing there but a hoax; that, at the very worst, we'd hold him back by force if he tried to attempt the adventure; and that, after all, he might discover the key to the "cipher" himself, and then I'd have gone hungry for nothing.

These arguments, which I'd have thrown out with indignation the day before, struck me now as excellent; I even found it perfectly absurd to have waited so long, and I made up my mind to tell him everything.

So I was casting about for an opening, one not too abrupt, when the professor stood up, put on his hat, and got ready to go out.

What — leave the house, and lock us in again! Never.

"Uncle!" I said.

He didn't seem to hear me.

"Uncle Lidenbrock!" I repeated, raising my voice.

"Eh?" he said, like a man startled awake.

"Well — that key?"

"What key? The door key?"

"No, no," I cried, "the key to the document!"

The professor looked at me over the top of his spectacles; he must have noticed something unusual in my face, because he seized me sharply by the arm and, unable to get a word out, questioned me with his eyes. And yet no question was ever put more plainly.

I nodded my head up and down.

He shook his with a kind of pity, as if he were dealing with a madman.

I made a more emphatic gesture.

His eyes flashed with a bright glare; his hand turned menacing.

That silent conversation, under those circumstances, would have gripped the most indifferent onlooker. And really I was reaching the point of not daring to speak, so afraid was I that my uncle would smother me in the first transports of his joy. But he grew so insistent that I had to answer.

"Yes, that key!... chance!..."

"What are you saying?" he cried, with an indescribable emotion.

"Here," I said, holding out to him the sheet of paper on which I'd written it, "read this."

"But this means nothing!" he answered, crumpling the sheet.

"Nothing, if you start reading from the beginning — but from the end..."

I hadn't finished my sentence before the professor let out a cry — better than a cry, a genuine roar! A revelation had just broken over his mind. He was transfigured.

"Ah! ingenious Saknussemm!" he cried. "So you'd written your sentence backwards to begin with!"

And swooping down on the sheet of paper, his eye blurred, his voice shaking, he read the whole document aloud, working up from the last letter to the first.

It ran in these terms:

In Sneffels Yoculis craterem kem delibat umbra Scartaris Julii intra calendas descende, audas viator, et terrestre centrum attinges. Kod feci. Arne Saknussemm.

Which, out of that wretched Latin, may be translated thus:

Descend into the crater of Sneffels Yocul, which the shadow of Scartaris caresses before the calends of July, bold traveler, and you will reach the center of the earth. I have done it. Arne Saknussemm.

At this reading my uncle leapt up as though he'd suddenly touched a Leyden jar. He was magnificent — all daring, all joy, all conviction. He paced back and forth; he took his head in both hands; he shoved the chairs about; he stacked up his books; he juggled — you'd hardly credit it — with his precious geodes; he threw a punch here, a slap there. At last his nerves settled, and, like a man drained by too great an expenditure of fluid, he sank back into his armchair.

"What time is it, then?" he asked after a few moments' silence.

"Three o'clock," I answered.

"Well now! My dinner went down fast. I'm starving. To the table. And then afterward..."

"Afterward?"

"You'll pack my trunk."

"What!" I cried.

"And your own!" answered the merciless professor, striding into the dining room.

Chapter 6

At those words a shiver ran through my whole body. Still, I kept my composure. I even resolved to put on a brave face. Only scientific arguments could stop Professor Lidenbrock — and there were some, good ones, against the very possibility of such a journey. Go to the center of the earth! What madness! I saved my debating skills for the right moment and turned my attention to dinner.

No need to repeat my uncle's curses when he found the table cleared. It all got sorted out. Good Martha was set free again. She ran to the market and did so well that an hour later my hunger was satisfied and I was coming back to a sense of the situation.

During the meal my uncle was almost cheerful; a few of those harmless scholarly jokes escaped him — the kind that never do much damage. After dessert he signaled me to follow him into his study.

I obeyed. He sat down at one end of his worktable, and I at the other.

"Axel," he said in a rather gentle voice, "you're a very clever boy. You did me a fine service just now, when I was worn out and about to abandon that combination. Where might I have wandered off to? No one can say! I'll never forget it, my boy, and of the glory we're about to win, you'll have your share."

"Right," I thought. "He's in a good mood. Now's the moment to discuss this glory."

"Above all," my uncle went on, "I ask you for absolute secrecy — you understand me? I don't lack for envious rivals in the world of science, and plenty of them would love to undertake this journey, though they won't suspect a thing until we're safely home again."

"Do you really believe," I said, "that there are so many of these bold souls?"

"Of course! Who would hesitate to win such fame? If this document were known, an entire army of geologists would go charging off on the trail of Arne Saknussemm!"

"That's exactly what I'm not convinced of, Uncle, because nothing proves this document is genuine."

"What! And the book we found it in?"

"Fine! I'll grant that this Saknussemm wrote these lines. But does it follow that he actually made the journey? Couldn't this old parchment be nothing but a hoax?"

That last word was a bit reckless, and I almost regretted saying it. The professor knit his thick brow, and I feared I had wrecked the rest of the conversation. Luckily, no. My stern companion sketched something like a smile on his lips and replied:

"That's what we'll find out."

"Ah!" I said, a little vexed. "But allow me to run through the whole series of objections regarding this document."

"Speak, my boy, don't hold back. I give you complete freedom to express your opinion. You're no longer my nephew, but my colleague. So — go ahead."

"Well then, first I'll ask you: what are this Yocul, this Sneffels, this Scartaris — names I've never heard of?"

"Nothing simpler. As it happens, I received a while ago a map from my friend Peterman, of Leipzig; it couldn't have come at a better time. Take down the third atlas in the second bay of the big bookcase — series Z, plate 4."

I got up, and thanks to these precise directions I quickly found the atlas he wanted. My uncle opened it and said:

"Here's one of the best maps of Iceland — Handerson's — and I think it's going to give us the answer to all your difficulties."

I leaned over the map.

"See this island made up of volcanoes," said the professor, "and notice that they all bear the name Yocul. That word means 'glacier' in Icelandic, and at Iceland's high latitude most eruptions break out through the layers of ice. Hence the name Yocul applied to all the fire-belching mountains of the island."

"All right," I answered, "but what is the Sneffels?"

I was hoping this question would have no answer. I was mistaken. My uncle went on:

"Follow me along the western coast of Iceland. Do you see Reykjavik, its capital? Yes. Good. Now trace up the countless fjords of these sea-gnawed shores, and stop a little below the sixty-fifth degree of latitude. What do you see there?"

"A kind of peninsula, like a picked-clean bone, ending in an enormous kneecap."

"The comparison is apt, my boy. Now — do you notice anything on that kneecap?"

"Yes, a mountain that seems to have sprouted up out of the sea."

"Good! That's the Sneffels."

"The Sneffels?"

"The very one. A mountain five thousand feet high, one of the most remarkable on the island, and beyond doubt the most famous in the entire world — if its crater leads down to the center of the globe."

"But that's impossible!" I cried, shrugging my shoulders, revolted by such a notion.

"Impossible!" the professor answered sternly. "And why is that?"

"Because this crater is obviously blocked up by lava, by scorching rock, and so —"

"And what if it's an extinct crater?"

"Extinct?"

"Yes. The number of active volcanoes on the surface of the globe is currently only about three hundred; but there exist a far greater number of extinct ones. Now the Sneffels ranks among the latter, and in all of recorded history it has had only a single eruption — that of 1219. From that time on, its rumblings gradually died away, and it is no longer counted among the active volcanoes."

To these flat assertions I had absolutely nothing to reply, so I fell back on the other obscurities the document contained.

"What does this word Scartaris mean," I asked, "and what have the calends of July got to do with anything?"

My uncle took a few moments to think. I had one instant of hope — just one — for he soon answered me in these terms:

"What you call obscurity is, to me, clarity. It proves the ingenious care with which Saknussemm meant to pin down his discovery. The Sneffels is made up of several craters; it was therefore necessary to indicate which of them leads to the center of the globe. What did the learned Icelander do? He noticed that as the calends of July approach — that is, toward the last days of June — one of the mountain's peaks, the Scartaris, casts its shadow all the way to the mouth of the crater in question, and he set the fact down in his document. Could he have devised a more exact instruction? Once we've reached the summit of the Sneffels, will there be any hesitating over which road to take?"

Decidedly, my uncle had an answer for everything. I saw plainly that he was unassailable on the words of the old parchment. So I stopped pressing him on that score, and, since convincing him was the whole point, I moved on to the scientific objections — far graver ones, in my opinion.

"All right," I said. "I'm forced to admit it: Saknussemm's sentence is clear and leaves no room for doubt. I'll even grant that the document has an air of perfect authenticity. This scholar went down into the Sneffels; he saw the shadow of the Scartaris caress the rim of the crater before the calends of July; he even heard it told, in the legends of his day, that this crater led to the center of the earth. But as for reaching it himself — as for having made the journey and come back from it, if he ever set out — no, a hundred times no!"

"And the reason?" said my uncle in a singularly mocking tone.

"The reason is that every theory in science proves such a venture is impossible!"

"Every theory says so?" replied the professor, putting on a genial air. "Ah! The wicked theories! How they're going to get in our way, these poor theories!"

I saw that he was making fun of me, but I pressed on all the same.

"Yes! It is perfectly established that heat increases by about one degree for every seventy feet of depth below the surface of the globe. Now, assuming this proportion stays constant, and the earth's radius being fifteen hundred leagues, there exists at the center a temperature of two million degrees. The matter inside the earth is therefore in the state of an incandescent gas, for the metals, gold, platinum, the hardest rocks, cannot hold up against such heat. So I have every right to ask whether it's even possible to venture into such a place!"

"So, Axel, it's the heat that troubles you?"

"Naturally. If we got down to a depth of just ten leagues, we'd have reached the limit of the earth's crust, because already the temperature is above thirteen hundred degrees."

"And you're afraid of melting?"

"I'll leave the question for you to decide," I answered testily.

"Here's what I decide," replied Professor Lidenbrock, drawing himself up grandly. "It's that neither you nor anyone knows for certain what goes on inside the globe, seeing that we scarcely know one twelve-thousandth part of its radius; it's that science is eminently perfectible, and every theory is forever being demolished by a newer one. Wasn't it believed, right up until Fourier, that the temperature of the planetary spaces went on falling forever? And don't we know today that the greatest cold of the ethereal regions doesn't drop below forty or fifty degrees under zero? Why shouldn't it be the same with the internal heat? Why, at a certain depth, shouldn't it reach an impassable limit, instead of climbing to the melting point of the most stubborn minerals?"

Since my uncle had shifted the question onto the ground of hypotheses, I had nothing to answer.

"Well, I'll tell you that genuine scholars, Poisson among them, have proved that if a heat of two million degrees existed inside the globe, the incandescent gases given off by the molten matter would take on such elasticity that the earth's crust couldn't resist and would burst like the walls of a boiler under the strain of steam."

"That's Poisson's opinion, Uncle — that's all."

"Agreed, but it's also the opinion of other distinguished geologists that the interior of the globe is formed neither of gas, nor of water, nor of the heaviest stones we know — for in that case the earth would weigh half as much as it does."

"Oh! With figures you can prove anything you like!"

"And with facts, my boy — is it the same? Isn't it an established fact that the number of volcanoes has considerably decreased since the first days of the world? And if there is a central heat, can't we conclude that it's tending to weaken?"

"Uncle, if you're going to venture into the field of suppositions, I've nothing more to argue."

"And I have to say that my opinion is joined by the opinions of highly competent men. Do you remember a visit paid me by the famous English chemist Humphry Davy in 1825?"

"Not in the least, since I only came into the world nineteen years afterward."

"Well, Humphry Davy came to see me on his way through Hamburg. We discussed at length, among other questions, the hypothesis of the liquidity of the earth's inner core. We were both agreed that such liquidity couldn't exist, for a reason science has never found an answer to."

"And what reason is that?" I said, a little surprised.

"It's that this liquid mass would be subject, like the Ocean, to the moon's attraction, and consequently, twice a day there would be internal tides that, heaving up the earth's crust, would give rise to periodic earthquakes!"

"But it's nevertheless obvious that the surface of the globe underwent combustion, and one is allowed to suppose that the outer crust cooled first, while the heat took refuge at the center."

"Wrong," replied my uncle. "The earth was heated by the combustion of its surface, and in no other way. Its surface was composed of a great quantity of metals — potassium, sodium — which have the property of catching fire at the mere contact of air and water. These metals took fire when the atmospheric vapors came pouring down as rain onto the ground, and little by little, as the waters worked their way into the fissures of the earth's crust, they set off fresh blazes with explosions and eruptions. Hence the volcanoes so numerous in the first days of the world."

"But that's an ingenious hypothesis!" I cried, almost in spite of myself.

"And one that Humphry Davy made vivid to me, right here, by a very simple experiment. He made up a metallic ball, mainly of the metals I've just mentioned, which perfectly represented our globe. When a fine dew was let fall onto its surface, that surface swelled, oxidized, and formed a little mountain; a crater opened at its summit; the eruption took place and gave off, through the whole ball, a heat so great that it became impossible to hold it in the hand."

Truly, I was beginning to be shaken by the professor's arguments; and besides, he pressed them home with his usual passion and enthusiasm.

"You see, Axel," he added, "the state of the central core has raised various hypotheses among geologists; nothing is less proved than this business of an internal heat. In my view, it doesn't exist; it can't exist; and besides, we shall see for ourselves and, like Arne Saknussemm, we'll know what to make of this great question."

"Well then! Yes," I answered, feeling myself won over by his enthusiasm. "Yes, we shall see — if one can see anything down there at all."

"And why not? Can't we count on electrical phenomena to light our way, and even on the atmosphere itself, which its own pressure may render luminous as we near the center?"

"Yes," I said, "yes! That's possible, after all."

"It's certain," my uncle answered triumphantly. "But silence, do you hear! Silence about all of this, and let no one so much as dream of discovering the center of the earth before we do."

Chapter 7

And so that memorable session came to an end. The whole conversation had left me feverish. I stumbled out of my uncle's study half stunned, and there wasn't enough air in all the streets of Hamburg to bring me around, so I made for the banks of the Elbe, over by the steam ferry that links the town with the Harburg railway.

Was I really convinced by what I'd just heard? Hadn't I simply buckled under Professor Lidenbrock's sheer force of will? Was I actually supposed to take seriously his decision to go to the center of the earth? Had I just been listening to the crazed ravings of a lunatic, or the scientific deductions of a great genius? Where, in all of it, did the truth leave off and the delusion begin?

I drifted among a thousand contradictory ideas without being able to hold onto a single one.

And yet I remembered that I had been convinced, even as my enthusiasm began to cool. What I wanted was to leave that very instant, without giving myself time to think it over. Yes — right then and there, I'd have had the nerve to strap up my suitcase.

Still, I have to admit that an hour later the fever broke. My nerves unknotted, and from the deep chasms of the earth I climbed back up to its surface.

"It's absurd!" I cried. "There's no sense in it! It's not a serious thing to propose to any level-headed young man. None of it is real. I slept badly. I had a bad dream."

Meanwhile I'd followed the banks of the Elbe and rounded the town. Once past the harbor, I'd reached the Altona road. A hunch was guiding me — and a well-founded hunch, because soon I caught sight of my own little Graüben, striding briskly and bravely back toward Hamburg on those light, quick feet of hers.

"Graüben!" I called from a distance.

The girl stopped, a little flustered, I imagine, to hear herself hailed like that on the open road. In ten strides I was beside her.

"Axel!" she said, surprised. "Oh! You came out to meet me! Now that's very nice of you, sir."

But looking at me, Graüben couldn't mistake my anxious, shaken face.

"Whatever's the matter?" she said, holding out her hand.

"The matter, Graüben!" I burst out.

In two seconds and three sentences my pretty Virland girl was fully up to speed. For a few moments she said nothing. Was her heart pounding as hard as mine? I couldn't tell — but her hand didn't tremble in mine. We walked a hundred paces without a word.

"Axel!" she said at last.

"My dear Graüben?"

"That is going to be a wonderful journey."

I jumped at the words.

"Yes, Axel, and a journey worthy of a scholar's nephew. It's a fine thing for a man to make his name by some great undertaking!"

"What! Graüben, you're not going to talk me out of attempting an expedition like this?"

"No, dear Axel — and I'd gladly come along with you and your uncle, if a poor girl weren't bound to be a burden to you."

"Do you mean it?"

"I mean it."

Oh, women — girls — female hearts, forever past all understanding! When you're not the most timid creatures alive, you're the bravest! Reason has no hold on you at all. What! This child was egging me on to take part in the expedition! She wouldn't have been afraid to try the adventure herself. She was pushing me toward it — me, whom she loved, all the same!

I was thrown, and — why not admit it — ashamed.

"Graüben," I went on, "we'll see whether you still talk this way tomorrow."

"Tomorrow, dear Axel, I'll say exactly what I say today."

Hand in hand, but keeping a deep silence, Graüben and I walked on. I was worn out by the emotions of the day.

"After all," I thought, "the calends of July are still a long way off, and between now and then plenty could happen to cure my uncle of his mania for traveling underground."

Night had fallen by the time we reached the house on König-strasse. I expected to find the place quiet, my uncle in bed as usual, and good Martha giving the dining room its last flick of the feather duster for the evening.

But I'd reckoned without the professor's impatience. I found him shouting and dashing about in the middle of a squad of porters who were unloading crates of goods in the hallway. Our old servant didn't know which way to turn.

"Come on, Axel — hurry up, you wretched boy!" my uncle cried the instant he spotted me. "Your trunk isn't packed, my papers aren't in order, I can't find the key to my traveling bag, and my gaiters haven't come!"

I stood there thunderstruck. My voice failed me. My lips could barely form the words:

"So we're leaving?"

"Yes, you miserable boy, off gallivanting instead of being here where you belong!"

"We're leaving?" I repeated, my voice fainter still.

"Yes — the day after tomorrow, at first light."

I couldn't listen to another word, and I fled to my little room.

There was no room for doubt now: my uncle had spent the afternoon laying in a stock of the gear and supplies he'd need for the trip. The hallway was cluttered with knotted rope ladders, torches, water flasks, iron clamps, picks, iron-shod staffs, mattocks — enough to load down ten men at least.

As long as we were shut inside the inner spiral, all went well; but after a hundred and fifty steps the open air struck me in the face; we had reached the platform of the steeple. "Would you be a coward, by any chance? Climb!" the Professor answered pitilessly.

Nevertheless I had to stand up, hold myself straight, and look. My first lesson in vertigo lasted an hour. When at last I was allowed to go back down and set my foot on the solid pavement of the streets, I ached in every limb.

In three hours I had visited not only the town but its surroundings. The general aspect of it was singularly bleak. No trees, no vegetation, so to speak. Everywhere the sharp ridges of volcanic rock. The Icelanders' huts are made of earth and peat, with walls sloping inward; they look like roofs laid down on the ground. Only, these roofs are relatively fertile meadows. Thanks to the heat of the dwelling, the grass grows on them well enough, and it is carefully mown at haying time — otherwise the household animals would come and graze on these verdant homes.

During my excursion I met few inhabitants; coming back along the commercial street, I saw the greater part of the population busy drying, salting, and loading cod, the principal article of export. The men looked robust but heavy — a sort of blond Germans, with pensive eyes, who feel themselves a little set apart from the rest of humanity, poor exiles banished to this land of ice, whom nature really ought to have made into Eskimos, since she condemned them to live on the edge of the polar circle! I tried in vain to surprise a smile on their faces; they laughed sometimes, by a kind of involuntary contraction of the muscles, but they never smiled.

Their costume consisted of a coarse blouse of black wool, known throughout the Scandinavian countries by the name of "vadmel," a broad-brimmed hat, trousers with a red border, and a piece of leather folded into the shape of a shoe.

The women, with sad, resigned faces of a rather pleasant cast but without expression, were dressed in a bodice and skirt of dark "vadmel"; unmarried girls wore, over their hair braided into garlands, a little cap of brown knitting; married women wrapped their heads in a colored kerchief, topped with a crest of white linen.

That evening I took a short stroll along the shores of Reykjavik, then came back early to my bed of thick planks, where I slept a deep sleep.

When I woke, I heard my uncle talking away at great length in the next room. I got up at once and hurried to join him.

He was chatting in Danish with a tall man of powerful build. This big fellow had to be of uncommon strength. His eyes, set in a very large and rather guileless head, struck me as intelligent. They were a dreamy blue. Long hair, which would have passed for red even in England, fell over his athletic shoulders. The movements of this native were supple, but he stirred his arms very little, like a man who neither knew nor cared for the language of gestures. Everything about him revealed a temperament of perfect calm — not lazy, but tranquil. You sensed that he asked nothing of anyone, that he worked as it suited him, and that in this world his philosophy could be neither startled nor troubled.

I caught these shades of character from the way the Icelander listened to the passionate stream of words pouring out of his companion. He stood there with his arms crossed, motionless amid my uncle's flurry of gestures; to say no, his head turned from left to right; to say yes, it tipped forward, and so slightly that his long hair barely moved. It was economy of motion carried to the point of miserliness.

Certainly, to look at this man, I would never have guessed his profession was hunter; he would not scare off the game, that was for sure — but how on earth could he ever catch it?

It all became clear when Mr. Fridriksson explained that this placid figure was nothing more than an "eider hunter," the bird whose down makes up the island's greatest wealth. That down, in fact, is what we call eiderdown, and gathering it does not call for any great expenditure of motion.

In the first days of summer, the female eider — a kind of pretty duck — goes to build her nest among the rocks of the fjords (the name given to narrow gulfs in the Scandinavian countries) that fringe the whole coastline. Once the nest is built, she lines it with fine feathers plucked from her own breast. At once the hunter — or rather the merchant — arrives, takes the nest, and the female starts her work all over again. This goes on as long as she has any down left. When she has stripped herself bare, it is the male's turn to shed his feathers. Only, since his coarse, tough plumage has no commercial value, the hunter does not bother to rob him of his brood's bed. So the nest is finished; the female lays her eggs; the chicks hatch; and the next year the eiderdown harvest begins again.

Now, since the eider does not choose steep cliffs to build its nest, but rather easy, level rocks that slope down into the sea, the Icelandic hunter could ply his trade without much exertion. He was a farmer who had neither to sow nor to reap his crop, but only to gather it in.

This grave, phlegmatic, silent character was named Hans Bjelke; he came on Mr. Fridriksson's recommendation. He was our future guide.

His manners made a strange contrast with my uncle's.

Yet the two of them reached an understanding easily. Neither cared about the price — the one ready to accept whatever was offered, the other ready to give whatever was asked. Never was a bargain struck more easily.

By the terms of it, Hans agreed to lead us to the village of Stapi, on the southern coast of the Sneffels peninsula, at the very foot of the volcano. That meant about twenty-two miles overland, a journey of two days, in my uncle's opinion.

Four horses were to be put at his disposal, two to carry him and me, the other two for our baggage. Hans, as was his habit, would go on foot. He knew this stretch of coast perfectly, and he promised to take us by the shortest route.

Departure was fixed for the 16th of June. My uncle wanted to give the hunter an advance on the bargain, but he refused with a single word.

"Efter," he said.

"Afterward," the professor translated for my benefit.

The deal concluded, Hans withdrew all in one piece.

"A splendid fellow," cried my uncle, "but he little suspects the marvelous role the future has in store for him to play."

"So he's coming with us all the way to—"

Forty-eight hours still remained; much to my regret, I had to spend them on our preparations. All our ingenuity went into arranging each item to the best advantage — the instruments in one group, the weapons in another, the tools in this bundle, the provisions in that. Four groups in all.

The instruments included:

1. A centigrade thermometer by Eigel, graduated up to a hundred and fifty degrees, which seemed to me either too much or not enough. Too much, if the surrounding heat should ever climb that high, in which case we would be cooked. Not enough, if it came to measuring the temperature of springs or any other matter in a state of fusion.

2. A compressed-air manometer, arranged to indicate pressures greater than that of the atmosphere at sea level. In fact, an ordinary barometer would not have been sufficient, since the atmospheric pressure was bound to increase in proportion to our descent below the surface of the earth.

3. A chronometer by Boissonnas the younger of Geneva, perfectly set to the meridian of Hamburg.

4. Two compasses, one for inclination and one for declination.

5. A night telescope.

6. Two Ruhmkorff apparatus, which by means of an electric current gave off a light that was very portable, safe, and compact. (Mr. Ruhmkorff's apparatus consists of a Bunsen battery, set going by means of potassium bichromate, which gives off no odor. An induction coil puts the electricity produced by the battery in communication with a lantern of a special design. Inside this lantern is a glass coil from which the air has been evacuated, leaving behind only a residue of carbonic acid gas or nitrogen. When the apparatus is working, this gas becomes luminous, producing a whitish, continuous light. The battery and the coil are placed in a leather bag that the traveler carries slung over one shoulder. The lantern, worn on the outside, gives more than enough light in the deepest darkness. It allows one to venture, without fear of any explosion, into the midst of the most inflammable gases, and it does not go out even in the heart of the deepest streams. Mr. Ruhmkorff is a learned and skillful physicist. His great discovery is his induction coil, which makes it possible to produce high-tension electricity. In 1864 he was awarded the five-yearly prize of 50,000 francs that France reserved for the most ingenious application of electricity.)

The weapons consisted of two Purdley More & Co. rifles and two Colt revolvers. Why weapons? We had neither savages nor wild beasts to fear, I supposed. But my uncle seemed to set as much store by his arsenal as by his instruments, especially by a good quantity of guncotton, impervious to damp, whose explosive force is far superior to that of ordinary powder.

The tools included two picks, two mattocks, a silk ladder, three iron-tipped staffs, a hatchet, a hammer, a dozen iron wedges and pitons, and long knotted ropes. That made no small bundle, for the ladder alone was three hundred feet long.

Finally, there were the provisions. The package was not large, but reassuring, for I knew that in concentrated meat and dry biscuit it held six months' worth of food. Juniper spirits made up the whole of the liquid portion, and there was no water at all — but we had flasks, and my uncle was counting on the springs to fill them. The objections I had raised about their quality, their temperature, and even their possible absence had gone nowhere.

To complete the exact inventory of our traveling articles, I will note a portable medicine chest containing blunt-bladed scissors, splints for fractures, a length of unbleached tape, bandages and compresses, adhesive plaster, a lancet-plate for bleeding — all of it terrifying; on top of that, a series of vials holding dextrin, medicinal alcohol, liquid lead acetate, ether, vinegar, and ammonia, every one of them a far from reassuring drug; and finally the materials needed for the Ruhmkorff apparatus.

My uncle had taken care not to forget the supply of tobacco, hunting powder, and tinder, nor a leather belt that he wore about his waist, holding a sufficient quantity of gold, silver, and paper money. Six pairs of good boots, made waterproof with a coating of tar and India rubber, were among the group of tools.

"Clothed, shod, and equipped like this, there's no reason we shouldn't go far," my uncle told me.

The whole day of the 14th was spent arranging these various objects. That evening we dined at Baron Trampe's, in the company of the mayor of Reykjavik and Doctor Hyaltalin, the country's great physician. Mr. Fridriksson was not among the guests; I learned later that the governor and he were at odds over a matter of administration and did not see each other. So I had no chance to understand a word of what was said during that semi-official dinner. I noticed only that my uncle talked the entire time.

The next day, the 15th, the preparations were finished. Our host gave the professor real pleasure by handing him a map of Iceland incomparably better than Henderson's — the map of Mr. Olaf Nikolas Olsen, drawn to a scale of 1/400,000 and published by the Icelandic Literary Society from the geodesic surveys of Mr. Scheel Frisac and the topographical survey of Mr. Bjorn Gumlaugsonn. It was a precious document for a mineralogist.

The last evening passed in intimate conversation with Mr. Fridriksson, for whom I felt a lively liking; then, after the talk, came a rather restless sleep — on my part, at least.

At five in the morning, the whinnying of four horses pawing the ground beneath my window woke me. I dressed in haste and went down into the street. There, Hans was finishing loading our baggage without, so to speak, stirring a muscle. And yet he worked with uncommon skill. My uncle made more noise than headway, and the guide seemed to pay very little attention to his instructions.

Everything was done by six o'clock. Mr. Fridriksson shook our hands. My uncle thanked him in Icelandic, and very warmly, for his kind hospitality. As for me, I sketched out some cordial farewell in my best Latin; then we climbed into the saddle, and with his last goodbye Mr. Fridriksson sent after me this line that Virgil might have written expressly for us, travelers uncertain of the road:

Et quacunque viam dederit fortuna sequamur.

We had set out under an overcast but settled sky. No tiring heat to dread, no disastrous rain. Perfect weather for tourists.

The sheer pleasure of riding on horseback across an unknown country made me easygoing about the start of the whole business. I gave myself up entirely to the holiday-maker's happiness, all appetite and freedom. I was beginning to make my peace with the expedition.

"Besides," I said to myself, "what am I really risking? A trip through the most curious country on earth! The climb up a truly remarkable mountain! At worst, a scramble down to the bottom of a dead crater! It's perfectly obvious that this Saknussemm did nothing more than that. As for the existence of some tunnel leading to the center of the globe — pure imagination! Pure impossibility! So whatever there is to enjoy in this expedition, let's take it, and no haggling!"

I had barely finished this line of reasoning when we left Reykjavik behind.

Hans marched at the head, at a quick, steady, tireless pace. The two horses loaded with our baggage followed him without needing to be led. My uncle and I came next, and, truly, we didn't cut too poor a figure on our little mounts — small beasts, but sturdy ones.

Iceland is one of the great islands of Europe. It measures fourteen hundred miles of surface and counts no more than sixty thousand inhabitants. Geographers have divided it into four quarters, and we had to cross, almost on the diagonal, the one that goes by the name of the Country of the Southwest Quarter — "Sudvestr Fjordùngr."

On leaving Reykjavik, Hans had immediately struck out along the seashore. We crossed meager pastures that were doing their level best to be green; the yellow was having more success. The rugged summits of the trachytic masses blurred into the eastern mists on the horizon; now and then a few patches of snow, concentrating the diffused light, blazed on the slopes of the far-off peaks; certain summits, thrusting up more boldly, pierced the gray clouds and reappeared above the drifting vapors, like reefs surfacing in the open sky.

Often these chains of barren rock ran out toward the sea and bit into the pasture; but there was always room enough left to get by. Our horses, moreover, picked out the passable spots by instinct, never slackening their pace. My uncle didn't even have the consolation of urging his mount along with voice or whip; he wasn't allowed to be impatient. And I couldn't help smiling to see him so tall on his little horse — with his long legs nearly grazing the ground, he looked like a six-legged centaur.

"Good beast! Good beast!" he kept saying. "You'll see, Axel, that no animal on earth outdoes the Icelandic horse for intelligence. Snow, storms, impassable trails, rocks, glaciers — nothing stops him. He's brave, he's frugal, he's sure-footed. Never a false step, never a shy. Let some river turn up, some fjord to cross — and one will, you'll see — and he'll throw himself into the water without a moment's hesitation, like an amphibian, and make the far bank! "Oh, he doesn't worry me in the least. Men like that walk without even noticing it. This one stirs so little that he can't possibly tire. Anyway, if it comes to it, I'll give up my mount to him. Meanwhile we were pushing on at a good clip; the country was already all but deserted. These tumbledown huts had the look of imploring charity from passersby, and one felt half-inclined to give them alms. In this country roads, and even footpaths, were utterly lacking, and the vegetation, slow as it was, made short work of erasing the tracks of the rare traveler.

And yet this part of the province, lying a stone's throw from its capital, ranked among the inhabited and cultivated portions of Iceland. What, then, must the more desolate regions be, beyond this desert? Half a mile covered, and we still hadn't met a single farmer at his cottage door, nor a wild shepherd grazing a flock less wild than himself — only a few cows and sheep left to fend for themselves. What, then, would the convulsed regions be like, the ones churned up by eruptions, born of volcanic explosions and subterranean upheavals?

We were fated to know them soon enough; but, consulting Olsen's map, I saw that they were avoided by following the winding fringe of the shore. In fact, the great plutonic upheaval has been concentrated mostly in the island's interior; there the horizontal layers of stacked rock — called trapps in the Scandinavian tongue — the trachytic bands, the eruptions of basalt, of tuff, and of every volcanic conglomerate, the flows of lava and molten porphyry, have made a land of unearthly horror. Little did I suspect then the spectacle awaiting us on the Sneffels peninsula, where these ravages of a fiery nature form a formidable chaos.

Two hours after leaving Reykjavik we reached the little town of Gufunes, called "Aoalkirkja," or Principal Church. It offered nothing remarkable. A few houses only. Barely enough to make a hamlet in Germany.

Hans halted there for half an hour; he shared our frugal breakfast, answered my uncle's questions about the nature of the road with yes and no, and when he was asked where he meant to spend the night, he said only:

"Gardär."

I consulted the map to find out what Gardär was. I saw a village of that name on the shores of the Hvalfjörd, four miles from Reykjavik. I showed it to my uncle.

"Only four miles!" he said. "Four miles out of twenty-two! What a pleasant little stroll."

At that spot the fjord was at least half a mile wide; the waves broke noisily on the sharp rocks; the gulf widened out between walls of rock, a sort of sheer escarpment three thousand feet high, remarkable for its brown strata parted by beds of tuff in a reddish shade. Whatever the intelligence of our horses, I augured nothing good from the crossing of a genuine arm of the sea on the back of a four-legged animal.

I understood perfectly the necessity of waiting for a certain moment of the tide to undertake the crossing of the fjord — the moment when the sea, having reached its greatest height, stands slack. The favorable instant didn't come until six in the evening; my uncle, myself, the guide, two ferrymen, and the four horses took our places in a sort of flat, rather flimsy barge. A long, narrow, gloomy passage led into the dwelling, which was built of barely squared timbers, and gave access to each of the rooms. On the right, the chain of mountains ran on without end, like an immense system of natural fortifications, and we followed along its outer slope. Often streams presented themselves that had to be forded, and crossed without soaking the baggage too badly.

The wilderness grew steadily emptier. Now and then, though, a human shadow seemed to flee off in the distance; and if a bend in the road brought us unexpectedly close to one of these specters, I felt a sudden revulsion at the sight of a swollen head, its skin glistening and hairless, and the repellent sores betrayed by the rents in a set of wretched rags.

The unhappy creature did not come toward us with its deformed hand outstretched; on the contrary, it fled — though not so fast that Hans failed to hail it with the usual "saellvertu."

"Spetelsk," he would say.

"A leper!" my uncle would repeat.

Such apparitions were not the sort to brighten a landscape that was growing profoundly bleak; the last tufts of grass were dying away beneath our feet. That evening, after fording two rivers rich in trout and pike, the Alfa and the Heta, we were forced to spend the night in an abandoned hovel, fit to be haunted by every goblin of Scandinavian mythology. The genie of cold had certainly taken up residence there, and it plagued us all night long.

The following day brought no particular incident. Always the same marshy ground, the same monotony, the same dreary aspect. By evening we had covered half the distance we had to travel, and we slept at the "annexia" of Krösolbt.

On the 19th of June, for about a mile, a field of lava stretched beneath our feet; this formation of the ground is called "hraun" in the country. The lava, wrinkled at the surface, took the shapes of cables, sometimes drawn out, sometimes coiled on themselves; an immense flow came down from the neighboring mountains — volcanoes now extinct, though these ruins bore witness to their past violence. Here and there, however, a few wisps of steam from hot springs crept along the ground.

We had no time to study these phenomena; we had to keep moving. Before long the marshy ground reappeared under our mounts' feet, broken up by little lakes. Our course now lay to the west; we had rounded the great bay of Faxa, and the twin white peaks of the Sneffels rose into the clouds less than five miles off.

The horses marched well; the difficulties of the ground did not stop them. For my part, I was beginning to grow very tired; my uncle stayed as firm and upright as on the first day, and I couldn't help admiring him as much as I admired the hunter, who looked on this whole expedition as a simple stroll.

The ground felt the nearness of the mountain, whose granite roots pushed up out of the earth like those of an old oak. We were skirting the volcano's immense base. The professor never took his eyes off it; he gestured, he seemed to challenge it and to say: "There stands the giant I am going to tame!" At last, after twenty-four hours' march, the horses stopped of their own accord at the door of the presbytery of Stapi.

Stapi is a village of maybe thirty huts, built straight onto the lava under sunlight bounced back at it by the volcano. It sits at the head of a small fjord walled in by a rampart of the strangest kind.

As everyone knows, basalt is a brown rock of igneous origin, and it takes on regular shapes whose arrangement can catch you off guard. Here nature works like a geometer, building the way a human being would, as if she had wielded the square, the compass, and the plumb line. Everywhere else she makes her art out of great masses flung down without order, cones barely sketched, half-finished pyramids, and the odd, wandering run of her lines; but here, meaning to set an example of regularity, and getting there ahead of the very first architects, she created an order so severe that neither the splendors of Babylon nor the wonders of Greece ever surpassed it.

I'd certainly heard of the Giant's Causeway in Ireland and of Fingal's Cave in one of the Hebrides, but I had never yet laid eyes on a structure built out of basalt.

At Stapi, though, the phenomenon rose up in all its beauty.

The Sneffels is five thousand feet high. With its double cone, it caps a band of trachyte that breaks away from the island's mountain system. From where we set out, you couldn't yet make out its two peaks against the gray backdrop of the sky. All I could see was an enormous cap of snow pulled low over the giant's brow.

We walked single file, the hunter leading the way, climbing narrow paths where two people couldn't have gone side by side. That made conversation just about impossible.

Beyond the basalt wall of the Stapi fjord, the ground was at first a grassy, fibrous peat, the residue of the ancient vegetation that once choked the marshes of the peninsula. There was enough of this fuel — still untouched — to heat the whole population of Iceland for a century. Measured from the bottom of some of the ravines, this vast peat bog stood as much as seventy feet high in places, and showed layer upon layer of charred debris, separated by thin sheets of pumice tuff.

True nephew of Professor Lidenbrock that I was, I studied the mineral curiosities laid out in this huge cabinet of natural history with real interest, in spite of my worries; and at the same time I ran back through the whole geological history of Iceland in my mind.

This strange island clearly rose up out of the depths of the sea in a relatively recent epoch. Maybe it's even still rising, by some movement too slow to feel. If that's the case, its origin can only be put down to the action of underground fires. And in that case, Humphry Davy's theory, Saknussemm's document, my uncle's whole grand claim — all of it went up in smoke. This idea led me to examine the nature of the ground closely, and I soon worked out the sequence of events that had governed the island's formation.

Iceland, entirely lacking any sedimentary terrain, is made up solely of volcanic tuff — that is, an agglomerate of stones and rocks with a porous texture. Before the volcanoes existed, it was a mass of trap rock, slowly heaved above the waves by the push of the central forces. The inner fires had not yet burst out into the open.

But later, a wide fissure opened diagonally across the island, from southwest to northwest, and through it the whole trachytic paste gradually flowed out. The thing happened without violence back then; the opening was enormous, and the molten matter, thrown up from the bowels of the globe, spread quietly out in vast sheets or in rounded, breast-shaped masses. This was the age of the feldspars, the syenites, and the porphyries.

But thanks to this outpouring, the thickness of the island grew considerably, and with it, its power to resist. You can imagine what a quantity of elastic fluids got stored up inside it once it offered no outlet at all, after the trachytic crust had cooled. So there came a moment when the mechanical force of these gases was such that they lifted the heavy shell and hollowed out tall chimneys for themselves. That's how the volcano was made, from the heaving-up of the crust — and then the crater, suddenly punched through at the volcano's summit.

Then the eruptive phenomena gave way to the volcanic ones. Through the newly formed openings came, first, the basaltic discharges, of which the plain we were crossing at that very moment offered the most marvelous specimens to our eyes. We were walking over these heavy, dark-gray rocks that the cooling had cast into prisms with hexagonal bases. Far off could be seen a great many flattened cones, each of which had once been a fire-vomiting mouth.

Then, once the basaltic eruption was spent, the volcano — its force swollen by that of the extinct craters — gave passage to the lavas and to those tuffs of ash and scoria whose long streams I could see scattered down its flanks like a rich head of hair.

Such was the sequence of events that put Iceland together; all of it came from the action of the inner fires, and to suppose that the internal mass did not remain in a permanent state of incandescent liquidity was madness. Madness above all to claim you could reach the center of the globe!

So I reassured myself about how our enterprise would turn out, even as I marched to the assault of the Sneffels.

The route grew harder and harder. The ground rose; splinters of rock shifted underfoot, and it took the most scrupulous attention to avoid a dangerous fall.

Hans went forward calmly, as though over level ground. Sometimes he vanished behind the big blocks and we lost sight of him for a moment; then a sharp whistle, escaping his lips, showed us the way to follow. Often, too, he stopped, gathered a few chips of rock, arranged them in a recognizable pattern, and so built up landmarks meant to mark the road back. A good precaution in itself — but one that later events would make useless.

Three exhausting hours of walking had brought us only to the base of the mountain. There, Hans signaled a halt, and a hasty meal was shared out among us all. My uncle ate his mouthfuls double to go faster. But this refueling stop was also a rest stop, so he had to wait on the good pleasure of the guide, who gave the signal to move on an hour later. The three Icelanders, as taciturn as their comrade the hunter, didn't say a single word and ate sparingly.

Now we began to climb the slopes of the Sneffels. Its snowy summit, by an optical illusion common in the mountains, looked very close to me — and yet, what long hours before we'd reach it! What exhaustion, above all! The stones, bound together by no cement of earth, by no grass, gave way under our feet and went tumbling down into the plain with the speed of an avalanche.

In certain spots, the flanks of the mountain made an angle with the horizon of at least thirty-six degrees; it was impossible to climb them straight on, and these stony steeps had to be worked around, not without difficulty. Then we lent one another a hand with our staffs.

I have to say that my uncle stayed as close to me as he could. He never let me out of his sight, and more than once his arm gave me a solid support. Looking at the height of the Sneffels's peak, it seemed impossible to me that anyone could reach it from this side, unless the angle of the slopes eased off. Luckily, after an hour of hard labor and feats of strength, in the middle of the vast carpet of snow spread over the volcano's rump, a kind of staircase appeared out of nowhere and simplified our ascent. It was formed by one of those torrents of stone thrown out by the eruptions, whose Icelandic name is "stinâ." If this torrent hadn't been stopped in its fall by the lay of the mountain's flanks, it would have gone plunging into the sea and made new islands.

Just as it was, it served us well. The slopes were getting steeper, but these stone steps let us climb them easily — so quickly, in fact, that when I fell behind for a moment while my companions kept climbing, I already saw them shrunk by distance to a microscopic size.

By seven in the evening we had climbed the two thousand steps of the staircase, and we stood above a swelling of the mountain, a kind of shelf on which rested the cone proper of the crater.

The sea stretched out three thousand two hundred feet below us. We had passed the line of perpetual snow, which lies fairly low in Iceland because of the constant damp of the climate. The cold was fierce; the wind blew hard. I was worn out. The Professor saw clearly that my legs would carry me no further, and, in spite of his impatience, he decided to stop. "Ofvanför."

"It seems we have to go higher up," said my uncle.

Then he asked Hans the reason for his answer.

"Mistour," replied the guide.

"Ja, mistour," repeated one of the Icelanders in a frightened tone.

"What does that word mean?" I asked, uneasy.

"Look," said my uncle.

I turned my eyes toward the plain. The phenomenon — common enough when the wind blows off the glaciers — goes by the name "mistour" in the Icelandic tongue.

"Hastigt, hastigt!" cried our guide.

Without knowing any Danish, I understood we had to follow Hans as fast as we could. He began to circle the cone of the crater, but at a slant, to make the walking easier. Luckily, we were on the opposite slope, sheltered from all danger. Without the guide's precaution, our bodies — shredded, ground to dust — would have come raining down far away, like the product of some unknown meteor.

Even so, Hans did not think it prudent to spend the night on the flanks of the cone. We continued our ascent in zigzags; the fifteen hundred feet that remained to climb took nearly five hours; the detours, the slants, and the countermarches measured at least three leagues. I was finished. I was going under, from cold and hunger. The air, a little thin now, wasn't enough for my lungs to work on.

A rope fixed at the mouth would have been enough to hold us up, but how were we to unfasten it once we'd reached its lower end?

My uncle used a very simple trick to get around the problem. He uncoiled a rope about as thick as a thumb and four hundred feet long; he let the first half run out, then wound it around a projecting block of lava and flung the other half down into the chimney. Each of us could then climb down, gripping both halves of the rope together in one hand so it couldn't slip loose; and once we'd descended two hundred feet, nothing would be easier than to haul it back down by letting go of one end and pulling on the other. Then we'd repeat the maneuver usque ad infinitum — over and over, without end.

"Now," said my uncle, once these preparations were finished, "let's see to the baggage. It'll be divided into three packs, and each of us will strap one onto his back — I mean only the fragile items."

The daring professor evidently did not count us in that last category.

"Hans," he went on, "will take charge of the tools and part of the provisions; you, Axel, of a second third of the provisions and the weapons; and I, of the rest of the provisions and the delicate instruments."

"They'll go down by themselves."

"How's that?" I asked, thoroughly astonished.

"You'll see."

My uncle liked bold methods, and used them without hesitation. At his order, Hans gathered all the non-fragile items into a single bundle, and this pack, firmly roped, was simply pitched into the gulf.

I heard the deep roar it made as it displaced the layers of air. My uncle, leaning over the abyss, followed the plunge of his baggage with a satisfied eye, and only straightened up once it was out of sight.

"Good," he said. "Now for us."

I ask any honest man whether it was possible to hear such words without a shudder!

I let myself slide down, so to speak, gripping the double rope frantically in one hand while I braced myself with my iron-tipped staff in the other. A single idea dominated me: I was terrified the footing would give out under me. The rope struck me as far too flimsy to bear the weight of three people. I used it as little as I could, performing miracles of balance on the lava ledges, which my foot tried to grip like a hand.

"Gif akt!"

"Watch out!" my uncle would repeat.

After half an hour we had reached the surface of a rock firmly wedged into the wall of the chimney.

Hans pulled the rope by one of its ends; the other rose into the air; after clearing the rock above, it dropped back down, scraping loose bits of stone and lava — a kind of rain, or rather a very dangerous hail.

Leaning out over our narrow platform, I noticed that the bottom of the pit was still nowhere in sight.

The rope maneuver started up again, and half an hour later we had gained another two hundred feet of depth.

I doubt whether the most rabid geologist alive would have tried, during that descent, to study the nature of the ground around him. For my part, I hardly gave it a thought; whether the strata were Pliocene, Miocene, Eocene, Cretaceous, Jurassic, Triassic, Permian, Carboniferous, Devonian, Silurian, or primitive, it worried me very little. But the professor, no doubt, was making his observations or taking his notes, because at one of the halts he said to me:

"The farther I go, the more confidence I feel; the arrangement of these volcanic strata bears out Davy's theory absolutely. We're deep in primordial ground, ground in which the chemical reaction of the metals took place as they caught fire on contact with air and water. I flatly reject the whole notion of a central heat; at any rate, we'll soon see."

Always the same conclusion. You'll understand that I didn't amuse myself by arguing. My silence was taken for agreement, and the descent resumed.

At the end of three hours, I still couldn't glimpse the bottom of the chimney. When I lifted my head, I could see its mouth noticeably shrinking; because of their slight inward slope, the walls were tending to close in on one another, and little by little the darkness deepened.

Yet still we kept going down; it seemed to me that the stones breaking loose from the walls were being swallowed up with a duller echo, as though they must soon be striking the bottom of the abyss.

Since I'd been careful to note our rope maneuvers exactly, I was able to work out precisely the depth we'd reached and the time that had passed.

By then we had repeated fourteen times a maneuver that took half an hour each. That came to seven hours, plus fourteen quarter-hour rests, or three and a half hours — ten and a half hours in all. We had set out at one o'clock; it must now be eleven.

As for the depth we'd reached, those fourteen maneuvers of a two-hundred-foot rope gave two thousand eight hundred feet.

At that moment Hans's voice made itself heard:

"Halt!" he said.

I stopped short just as I was about to knock my feet against my uncle's head.

"We're here," he said.

"Where?" I asked, letting myself slide down beside him.

"At the bottom of the vertical chimney."

"There's no other way out, then?"

"Yes — a sort of corridor I can just make out, slanting off to the right. We'll look at that tomorrow. Let's have supper first, and then we'll sleep."

The darkness was not yet complete. We opened the provision sack, ate, and bedded down as best we could on a mattress of stones and lava fragments.

And when, stretched out on my back, I opened my eyes, I saw a bright point at the far end of that tube three thousand feet long, which had turned into a gigantic telescope.

It was a star, stripped of all its twinkling, and which, by my calculations, must be Sigma of the Little Bear.

Then I fell into a deep sleep.

The glow was strong enough to let us make out the things around us.

Monday, the 1st of July.

The whole difficulty of the route lay in not sliding too fast down a slope tilted at about forty-five degrees; luckily, certain worn spots and swellings in the rock served as steps, and we had only to climb down while letting our baggage run ahead of us on the end of a long rope.

But what became steps beneath our feet turned to stalactites on the other walls. The lava, porous in places, was studded with little rounded blisters; crystals of opaque quartz, set with clear drops of glass and hanging from the vault like chandeliers, seemed to light up as we passed. You'd have thought the genies of the abyss were illuminating their palace to receive their guests from the surface of the earth.

"It's magnificent!" I cried in spite of myself. "What a sight, Uncle! Do you see these shades of lava running from red-brown to brilliant yellow by the faintest gradations? And these crystals glowing at us like luminous globes?"

"Ah, now you're coming round, Axel!" my uncle answered. "Ah, so you find it splendid, my boy! You'll see plenty more, I hope. March! March!"

He'd have done better to say "slide," for we were letting ourselves go, effortlessly, down the tilting slopes. This was Virgil's facilis descensus Averni — the easy way down into the underworld. The compass, which I consulted often, pointed southeast with unshakable steadiness. This flow of lava swerved neither one way nor the other. It held to the straightness of a drawn line.

Yet the heat wasn't rising in any noticeable way; that bore out Davy's theories, and more than once I consulted the thermometer in astonishment. Two hours after starting out, it still read only 10° — an increase of just 4°. That gave me grounds to suppose our descent was more horizontal than vertical. The professor measured precisely the angles of deviation and slope of the route — but he kept the results of his calculations to himself.

Around eight in the evening he gave the signal to halt. Hans sat down at once; the lamps were hung from a shelf of lava. We were in a kind of cavern where the air was in no way lacking. On the contrary. Certain gusts reached us. What caused them? What disturbance in the atmosphere gave rise to them? It was a question I didn't try to settle just then; hunger and exhaustion had left me incapable of reasoning. Seven straight hours of descent aren't made without a great expenditure of strength. "This absence of springs surprises you?" he said.

"It does — it even worries me. We've only got water enough for five days."

"Set your mind at ease, Axel. I promise you we'll find water, and more than we'll want."

"But perhaps this flow runs on to great depths. It seems to me we haven't come very far yet, not vertically at least."

"What makes you suppose that?"

"Because if we were very deep inside the earth's crust, the heat would be stronger."

"Barely fifteen degrees — an increase of only nine degrees since we started."

"Well then, draw your conclusion."

"Here's my conclusion. According to the most exact observations, the rise in temperature inside the globe is one degree for every hundred feet. But certain local conditions can alter that figure. At Yakutsk in Siberia, for instance, it's been noted that the increase of one degree occurred every thirty-six feet — which obviously depends on the conductivity of the rocks. I'll add, too, that in the neighborhood of an extinct volcano, and through gneiss, it's been observed that the temperature rose by only one degree for every hundred and twenty-five feet. Let's take that last figure, then, which is the most favorable, and calculate."

"Calculate away, my boy."

"Nothing could be easier," I said, laying out the figures in my notebook. "Nine times a hundred and twenty-five feet gives eleven hundred and twenty-five feet of depth."

"Exactly right."

"Well then?"

"Well, according to my observations, we've reached ten thousand feet below sea level."

"Is that possible?"

"Yes — or figures are no longer figures!"

The professor's calculations were correct. We had already passed by six thousand feet the greatest depths ever reached by man, such as the mines of Kitz-Bahl in the Tyrol and those of Wüttemberg in Bohemia.

The temperature, which should have been eighty-one degrees at that spot, was barely fifteen. It gave one singularly much to think about.

The next morning, Tuesday the 30th of June, at six o'clock, we took up the descent again.

We were still following the lava gallery — a genuine natural ramp, gentle as those inclined slopes that still take the place of a staircase in old houses. So it went until seventeen minutes past noon, the precise instant when we caught up with Hans, who had just stopped.

"Ah!" cried my uncle. "We've reached the end of the chimney."

I looked around me. We were standing at the center of a crossroads, where two passages came to an end, both of them dark and narrow. Which one should we take? There was the difficulty.

Still, my uncle refused to appear to hesitate, either in front of me or in front of the guide. He pointed to the eastern tunnel, and before long all three of us were pushing into it.

In any case, any hesitation before that forking path would have dragged on forever, because there wasn't a single clue to settle the choice one way or the other. We had to leave it entirely to chance.

The slope of this new gallery was barely noticeable, and its cross-section wildly uneven. At times a succession of arches unrolled before our steps like the side aisles of a Gothic cathedral; medieval artists could have studied here every form of that religious architecture whose defining element is the pointed arch. A mile further on, our heads bent low beneath the flattened vaults of the Romanesque style, and thick pillars set into the rock mass sagged under the weight of the arches coming down on them. In certain places, all that gave way to low, cramped structures that looked like the work of beavers, and we crawled on our hands and knees through narrow burrows.

The heat held at a bearable level. In spite of myself I thought of how fierce it must once have been, when the lava vomited up by the Sneffels came pouring down this route that was so peaceful today. I pictured the torrents of fire smashing against the angles of the gallery, and the buildup of superheated vapors in that cramped space!

"Just so long," I thought, "as the old volcano doesn't take a late fancy to start up again!"

I said none of these reflections to my uncle. He wouldn't have understood them. His one and only thought was to press forward. He marched, he slid, he even tumbled downhill, with a conviction that, when all was said and done, you couldn't help but admire.

At six in the evening, after a not-too-tiring stroll, we had gained two leagues to the south, but barely a quarter of a mile in depth.

My uncle gave the signal to rest. We ate without much talk, and fell asleep without much thought.

Our arrangements for the night were very simple: a travel blanket to roll up in was the whole of our bedding. We had neither cold nor unwelcome visitors to fear. Travelers who plunge into the deserts of Africa, or into the heart of the New World's forests, are forced to keep watch over one another during the hours of sleep; but here, there was absolute solitude and complete safety. Savages or wild beasts — none of those dangerous breeds was anything to worry about.

We woke the next morning fresh and rested. We took up the road again. We were following a lava path as we had the day before. It was impossible to make out the nature of the ground it cut through. The tunnel, instead of sinking deeper into the bowels of the globe, was tending to become absolutely horizontal. I even thought I noticed it was climbing back up toward the surface of the earth. This became so obvious around ten in the morning — and, as a result, so tiring — that I was forced to slow our pace.

"Well, Axel?" the professor said impatiently.

"Well, I can't go on," I answered.

"What! After three hours' walk on such an easy road!"

"Easy, I won't argue — but tiring, that's for certain."

"How so, when all we have to do is go down!"

"Go up, if you don't mind!"

"Up!" said my uncle, shrugging.

"No question about it. For the last half hour the slopes have changed, and if we keep following them like this we'll certainly end up back on the soil of Iceland."

Still, I had shouldered my burden again bravely, and I was keeping close behind Hans, with my uncle out in front. I was determined not to be left behind; my great worry was to never lose sight of my companions. I shuddered at the thought of getting lost in the depths of this labyrinth.

Besides, though the rising road was growing harder, I consoled myself with the thought that it was bringing me closer to the surface of the earth. That was a hope. Every step confirmed it.

At noon a change came over the walls of the gallery. I noticed it in the weakening of the electric light reflected off them. To the lava lining now succeeded bare living rock; the mass was made up of layers that were tilted, and often set vertically. We were smack in the middle of the transition era, smack in the Silurian period (so named because the ground from this period stretches widely across England, in the regions once inhabited by the Celtic tribe of the Silures).

"It's obvious!" I exclaimed. "The waters laid down these schists, these limestones and sandstones during the second era of the earth! We're turning our backs on the granite mass! We're like people from Hamburg taking the road to Hanover to get to Lübeck!"

I should have kept my observations to myself. But my geologist's temperament won out over prudence, and Uncle Lidenbrock heard my outbursts.

"What's the matter with you?" he said.

"Look!" I answered, pointing out to him the varied succession of sandstones, limestones, and the first signs of slate-bearing ground.

"Well?"

"Here we are, arrived at the very period when the first plants and the first animals appeared!"

"Ah! You think so?"

"But look, examine, observe!"

I forced the professor to play his lamp over the walls of the gallery. I expected some exclamation from him. Far from it — he didn't say a word, and went on his way.

Had he understood me or not? Was he unwilling to admit — out of the wounded pride of an uncle and a scholar — that he'd blundered in choosing the eastern tunnel? Or was he set on following this passage all the way to its end? It was clear that we had left the lava road, and that this path could not possibly lead to the furnace of the Sneffels.

Still, I asked myself whether I wasn't granting too much importance to this change in the terrain. Was I fooling myself? Were we really crossing these layers of rock stacked on top of the granite mass?

"If I'm right," I thought, "I ought to find some remnant of a primitive plant, and then I'll simply have to bow to the evidence. Let's have a look."

I hadn't gone a hundred steps before undeniable proof offered itself to my eyes. It had to be there — in the Silurian era, the seas held more than fifteen hundred species of plants and animals. My feet, used to the hard lava floor, suddenly trod on a dust made of the debris of plants and shells. On the walls you could plainly see the imprints of fucus and lycopods; Professor Lidenbrock could not possibly have been mistaken about them — but he shut his eyes, I imagine, and went on his way with a steady, unbroken step.

This was stubbornness pushed past every limit. I couldn't stand it any longer. I picked up a perfectly preserved shell, which had belonged to an animal roughly like the modern wood louse; then I caught up with my uncle and said:

"Look!"

"Well," he answered calmly, "that's the shell of a crustacean from the extinct order of trilobites. Nothing more."

"But don't you conclude from it—?"

"What you conclude yourself? Yes. Perfectly. We've left the layer of granite and the lava road behind. It's possible I made a mistake; but I won't be certain of my error until I've reached the end of this gallery."

"And what's that?"

"The lack of water."

"Well then! We'll put ourselves on rations, Axel."

And so it came to rationing. Our supply couldn't last more than three days. That was the grim arithmetic I worked out at supper that evening. And, worst of all, we had little hope of striking a live spring in these strata of the transition period.

All the next day the gallery unrolled its endless arches ahead of us. We walked almost without a word. Hans's silence was catching.

The path didn't rise — not noticeably, anyway; at times it even seemed to tilt downward. But that faint slope was no comfort to the Professor, because the character of the rock never changed, and the transition period only asserted itself more strongly.

The electric light struck brilliant sparks from the shale, the limestone, and the old red sandstone of the walls; you might have thought yourself in a cutting driven through the heart of Devonshire — the county that gave its name to this kind of formation. Instead of the rudimentary trilobites, I could now make out the remains of a more advanced order — among them ganoid fishes and those Sauropteris in which the paleontologist's eye has learned to recognize the first forms of the reptile. It was becoming plain that we were climbing back up the ladder of animal life — the ladder whose summit is man. But Professor Lidenbrock didn't seem to notice.

He was waiting on one of two things: either a vertical shaft to open under his feet and let him resume his descent, or an obstacle to bar his way altogether. But evening came and neither hope was answered.

After ten hours of marching, I noticed that the glow of our lamps on the walls was fading strangely. The marble, the shale, the limestone, the sandstone were giving way to a dark, dull facing. At a point where the tunnel narrowed sharply, I leaned a hand against the wall.

"A coal mine!" I cried.

"A mine without miners," my uncle replied.

"Eh — who knows?"

"I know," the Professor answered curtly, "and I'm certain this gallery, cut through these beds of coal, was not made by the hand of man. But whether it's nature's work or not, I couldn't care less. It's supper time. Let's eat."

Hans put together a little food. I barely ate, and I drank the few drops of water that made up my ration. The guide's flask, half full — that was all that was left to slake the thirst of three men.

After the meal, my two companions stretched out on their blankets and found in sleep a cure for their exhaustion. As for me, I couldn't sleep, and I counted the hours until morning.

On Saturday, at six, we set off again. Twenty minutes later we reached a vast hollow; and now I could see for certain that no human hand could have dug out this seam — the vaults would have been shored up with timbering, whereas in truth they held together by a miracle of balance alone.

This sort of cavern measured a hundred feet across and a hundred and fifty high. The ground had been violently wrenched apart by some underground convulsion. The mass of earth, yielding to a mighty upward thrust, had split open, leaving this broad void — into which inhabitants of the surface were now venturing for the very first time.

The whole history of the coal age was written on those dark walls, and a geologist could easily trace its various phases. The beds of coal were separated by strata of compact sandstone or clay, and looked as if crushed flat under the weight of the layers above.

In that age of the world, which came before the secondary era, the earth clothed itself in immense vegetation, the product of two forces working together: a tropical heat and an unrelenting damp. An atmosphere of vapors wrapped the globe on every side, still hiding the sun's rays from it.

From this comes the conclusion that the high temperatures did not come from that new source; perhaps the sun itself was not yet ready to play its dazzling part. "Climates" did not yet exist, and a scorching heat spread over the whole surface of the globe, the same at the equator as at the poles. Where did it come from? From the interior of the globe.

Whatever Professor Lidenbrock's theories might say, a violent fire smoldered in the bowels of the spheroid; its action made itself felt right up to the outermost layers of the earth's crust. The plants, robbed of the sun's kindly influence, gave neither flowers nor scent, but their roots drew a fierce vitality from the burning soil of those first days.

There were few trees — only herbaceous plants, immense grasslands, ferns, lycopods, sigillaria, asterophyllites: strange families whose species numbered in the thousands back then.

And it is precisely to this exuberant vegetation that coal owes its origin. The elastic crust of the globe obeyed the movements of the liquid mass it covered. Hence fissures, hence countless subsidences; the plants, dragged down beneath the waters, little by little formed considerable heaps.

Then natural chemistry went to work: at the bottom of the seas, the masses of vegetation turned first to peat; then, under the influence of gases and the heat of fermentation, they underwent a complete mineralization.

And so were formed these immense beds of coal that the consumption of every people on earth, through long centuries yet to come, will never manage to exhaust.

These reflections came back to me as I studied the mineral wealth heaped up in this portion of the earth's mass. This coal, no doubt, will never be brought to light. Working such remote mines would demand sacrifices far too great. And what would be the point, in any case, when coal lies practically at the surface of the earth across so many regions? So there I saw these beds, untouched — and untouched they will still be when the last hour of the world strikes.

Meanwhile we kept walking, and I alone among my companions forgot the length of the road by losing myself in geological musings. The temperature stayed roughly what it had been during our passage through the lavas and shales. Only my sense of smell was troubled by a strong odor of protocarbureted hydrogen. I recognized at once, in this gallery, the presence of a notable quantity of that dangerous gas the miners call firedamp, whose explosions have so often caused appalling catastrophes.

Luckily we had the light of Ruhmkorff's ingenious apparatus. If, by ill fortune, we had explored this gallery carelessly with a torch in hand, a terrible blast would have ended the journey by wiping out the travelers.

This excursion through the coal seam lasted until evening. My uncle could barely contain the impatience the flatness of the road stirred in him. The darkness, always deep beyond twenty paces, made it impossible to judge the length of the gallery, and I was beginning to think it endless when suddenly, at six o'clock, a wall rose unexpectedly before us. To the right, to the left, above, below, there was no way through. We had reached the bottom of a dead end.

"Well — so much the better!" cried my uncle. "At least now I know where I stand. We're not on Saknussemm's road, and there's nothing to do but turn back. Let's take a night's rest, and within three days we'll have regained the point where the two galleries fork."

"Yes," I said, "if we have the strength for it!"

"And why wouldn't we?"

"Because by tomorrow the water will be gone completely."

"And will the courage be gone too?" said the Professor, fixing me with a stern eye.

I didn't dare answer him.

The next day we set out at dawn. We had to hurry. We were five days' march from the crossroads.

Had I heard right? Had my uncle gone mad? I stared at him blankly. I wouldn't let myself understand.

"Drink," he said again.

And tipping up the flask, he emptied the whole of it between my lips.

Oh — boundless bliss! A mouthful of water came to wet my burning mouth, a single mouthful, but it was enough to call back the life that was leaking out of me.

"Yes," he said, "a mouthful of water — the last! Do you hear me? The last! I'd guarded it carefully at the bottom of my flask. Twenty times, a hundred times, I had to fight down the terrible urge to drink it. But no, Axel — I was saving it for you."

"Yes, poor child, I knew that when you reached this crossroads you'd fall half dead, and I kept my last drops of water to bring you back."

"Thank you! Thank you!" I cried.

Little as my thirst had been quenched, I had recovered some strength all the same. The muscles of my throat, clenched tight until then, were loosening; the burning in my lips had eased. I could speak.

"Turn back!" said my uncle, as though he were answering himself rather than me.

"Yes, turn back, and without losing a moment."

"I see you as beaten down as before, still speaking words of despair!"

My uncle was speaking in a state of extreme agitation. His voice, tender for a moment, had turned hard and threatening again. He was battling the impossible with a grim energy. I didn't want to leave him at the bottom of this abyss, and yet, on the other hand, the instinct of self-preservation was urging me to flee from him.

The guide watched this scene with his usual indifference. And yet he understood what was passing between his two companions; our gestures made it plain enough which different way each of us was trying to drag the other. "Master," he said.

"His master!" I cried. "You fool! No — he isn't the master of your life! We have to run! We have to drag him with us! Do you hear me? Do you understand?"

I had seized Hans by the arm. I wanted to force him to his feet. I struggled with him. My uncle stepped in.

"Calm down, Axel," he said. "You'll get nothing from that unshakable servant. So listen to what I have to propose."

I folded my arms, looking my uncle full in the face.

"The lack of water," he said, "is the only thing standing in the way of my plans. In this eastern gallery, made of lava, shale, and coal, we haven't met a single molecule of liquid. It's possible we'll have better luck following the western tunnel."

I shook my head with an air of deep disbelief.

"Hear me out," the professor went on, forcing his voice. "While you were lying there without moving, I went to scout the shape of that gallery. It drops straight down into the bowels of the globe, and in a few hours it'll bring us to the granite mass. There we're bound to find abundant springs. The nature of the rock demands it, and instinct agrees with logic in backing my conviction. Now here's what I have to propose. When Columbus asked his crews for three days to find new lands, his crews — sick, terrified — nonetheless granted his request, and he discovered the New World. I, the Columbus of these underground regions, ask you for just one more day. If, when that time is up, I haven't found the water we're missing, I swear to you, we'll go back up to the surface of the earth."

In spite of my irritation, I was moved by those words, and by the violence my uncle was doing himself to speak that way.

"All right!" I cried. "Let it be as you wish, and may God reward your superhuman energy. You've only a few hours left to try your luck. Let's go!"

The descent began again, this time down the new gallery. Hans walked in front, as was his habit. We hadn't gone a hundred paces when the professor, running his lamp along the walls, cried out:

"Here are the primitive formations! We're on the right road! On, on!

"When the earth cooled little by little in the first days of the world, the shrinking of its bulk racked the crust with dislocations, ruptures, contractions, fissures. As we descended, the succession of strata that make up the primitive terrain came into sharper and sharper focus. Geology regards this primitive terrain as the very base of the mineral crust, and it has established that it is composed of three distinct layers — the schists, the gneisses, the micaschists — all resting on that unshakable rock we call granite.

Now, never had mineralogists found themselves in such marvelous conditions for studying nature on the spot. What the drill, that dumb and brutal machine, could never bring up to the surface of the globe about its inner texture, we were about to study with our own eyes, touch with our own hands.

Through the tier of schists, colored in beautiful shades of green, wound metallic veins of copper, of manganese, with a few traces of platinum and gold. I thought of these riches buried in the bowels of the globe, riches that human greed will never enjoy! The upheavals of the first days interred these treasures at such depths that neither pickaxe nor mattock will ever tear them from their tomb.

To the schists succeeded the gneisses, of a layered structure, remarkable for the regularity and parallelism of their sheets; then the micaschists, laid out in great flakes that the eye picked out by the glittering of their white mica.

The light of our lamps, thrown back by the little facets of the rock mass, crossed its jets of fire at every angle, and I imagined myself traveling through a hollow diamond, in which the rays shattered into a thousand dazzling flashes.

We were walled up in the immense prison of granite.

It was eight in the evening. Still no water. I was suffering horribly. My uncle marched on ahead. He wouldn't stop. He kept straining his ear to catch the murmur of some spring. But nothing.

At last my strength gave out; I let out a cry and fell.

"Help me! I'm dying!"

My uncle came back for me. He looked at me, arms folded; then these muffled words came from his lips:

"It's all over!"

One last terrifying gesture of rage struck my eyes, and I closed them.

When I opened them again, I saw my two companions lying motionless, rolled up in their blankets. Were they asleep? For my part, I couldn't find a moment's sleep. I was suffering too much, and above all from the thought that my ailment had to be beyond any remedy. My uncle's last words kept echoing in my ears.

"It's all over!" — because in such a state of weakness, there could be no thought whatsoever of climbing back to the surface of the globe.

There was a league and a half of the earth's crust above us! It seemed to me that this whole mass was bearing down with all its weight on my shoulders. I felt crushed, and I wore myself out in violent efforts to turn over on my bed of granite.

A few hours went by. A deep silence reigned around us, the silence of a tomb. Nothing came through these walls, the thinnest of which measured five miles in thickness.

And yet, in the middle of my drowsing, I thought I heard a noise; the tunnel had gone dark. I looked more closely, and I seemed to see the Icelander disappearing, lamp in hand.

Why was he leaving? Was Hans abandoning us? My uncle was asleep. I wanted to shout. My voice could find no passage between my parched lips. The darkness had grown deep, and the last sounds had just died away.

"Hans is abandoning us!" I cried. "Hans! Hans!"

These words — I cried them inside myself. They went no further. And yet, after that first instant of terror, I was ashamed of my suspicions against a man whose conduct had until now been nothing but blameless. His leaving could not be a flight. Instead of going back up the gallery, he was going down it. Evil designs would have drawn him upward, not down. This reasoning calmed me a little, and my thoughts turned another way. Hans, that placid man — only some serious motive could have torn him from his rest. Was he going, then, in search of something? Had he heard, during the silent night, some murmur whose whisper had not reached me?

For a whole hour my fevered brain churned through every reason that could have made our unflappable hunter act as he had. The most absurd ideas tangled themselves together in my head. I honestly thought I was going mad.

But at last a sound of footsteps came echoing up from the depths of the gulf. Hans was climbing back. A wavering light began to slide along the walls, then broke out through the mouth of the passage. Hans appeared.

He went over to my uncle, laid a hand on his shoulder, and gently woke him. My uncle sat up.

"What is it?" he asked.

"Vatten," the hunter answered.

You have to believe that under the spur of terrible suffering, every one of us becomes a linguist. I didn't know a single word of Danish, and yet I understood our guide's meaning by pure instinct.

"Water! Water!" I cried, clapping my hands and flailing about like a lunatic.

"Water!" my uncle repeated. "Hvar?" he asked the Icelander.

"Nedat," Hans replied.

Where? Down below! I understood every word. I had seized the hunter's hands and was squeezing them hard, while he looked back at me with perfect calm.

The preparations for departure were quickly made, and soon we were heading down a corridor that dropped two feet with every fathom.

An hour later, we had gone about a thousand fathoms and descended two thousand feet.

Just then we heard, quite distinctly, an unfamiliar sound running through the flanks of the granite wall — a kind of muffled roar, like distant thunder. Through that first half hour of walking, finding no sign of the promised spring, I felt the old anguish creeping back over me. But then my uncle explained where the noise was coming from.

"Hans wasn't mistaken," he said. "What you hear there is the roar of a torrent."

"A torrent?" I exclaimed.

"There's no doubt about it. An underground river is running all around us!"

We quickened our pace, keyed up with hope. I no longer felt my exhaustion. The sound of that murmuring water was already reviving me. The torrent, after running for a long time above our heads, was now coursing through the wall on our left, roaring and leaping. Again and again I ran my hand over the rock, hoping to find some trace of seepage or damp. But in vain.

Another half hour slipped by. Another half league was covered.

And now it became clear that the hunter, during his absence, could not have carried his search any farther than this. Guided by an instinct peculiar to mountain folk and to water-diviners, he had "felt" this torrent through the rock — but he certainly hadn't laid eyes on the precious liquid, and he hadn't drunk from it.

Soon it even became obvious that if we kept walking, we would be moving away from the torrent, whose murmur was steadily fading.

We turned back. Hans stopped at the exact spot where the torrent seemed to run closest.

I sat down against the wall, while the waters raced past just two feet from me with tremendous force. But a wall of granite still stood between us.

Without thinking, without asking myself whether some way of reaching that water might exist, I gave in to a first surge of despair.

Hans looked at me, and I thought I saw a smile flicker across his lips.

He rose and took the lamp. I followed him. He walked over to the wall. I watched what he did. He pressed his ear to the dry stone and slid it slowly along, listening with the greatest care. I realized he was searching for the precise point where the torrent could be heard most loudly. He found that point in the left-hand wall, three feet above the floor.

How stirred I was! I hardly dared guess what the hunter meant to do! But I couldn't help understanding it — and applauding it, and smothering him with grateful caresses — when I saw him take up his pick to attack the rock itself.

"Saved!" I cried. "Saved!"

"Yes," my uncle repeated, beside himself. "Hans is right! Ah, the brave fellow! We'd never have thought of that!"

I should think not! A trick like that, however simple, would never have crossed our minds. Nothing could be more dangerous than to drive a pickaxe into this framework of the globe. What if some collapse were to bury us? What if the torrent, forcing its way through the rock, were to come flooding in on us? These dangers were anything but imaginary — but just then no fear of cave-in or of drowning could stop us, and our thirst was so fierce that, to slake it, we would have dug at the very bed of the ocean.

Hans set to work at a task neither my uncle nor I could have managed. Our impatience would have run away with our hands, and the rock would have flown apart under our reckless blows. The guide, on the contrary, calm and steady, wore the stone away little by little with a series of small, repeated strokes, opening a gap half a foot wide. I could hear the sound of the torrent swelling, and I already imagined I could feel the blessed water splashing against my lips.

Soon the pick had sunk two feet into the granite wall. The work had been going on for more than an hour. I was writhing with impatience! My uncle wanted to resort to drastic measures. I had trouble holding him back, and he had already snatched up his pick when suddenly a hissing sound broke out. A jet of water shot from the wall and smashed against the rock face opposite.

Hans, half thrown down by the impact, couldn't hold back a cry of pain. I understood why when, plunging my own hands into the liquid jet, I let out a violent shout of my own: the spring was boiling hot.

"Water at a hundred degrees!" I cried.

"Well, it'll cool down," my uncle answered.

The corridor filled with steam, while a stream took shape and went off to lose itself in the winding passages underground. And soon after, we were scooping up our first mouthfuls of it.

Ah, what bliss! What incomparable delight! What was this water? Where did it come from? It hardly mattered. It was water — and though it was still hot, it brought back to the heart the life that had been about to slip away. I drank without stopping, without even tasting.

It was only after a full minute of pure pleasure that I exclaimed:

"Hey — but this is iron-rich water!"

"Excellent for the stomach," my uncle replied, "and highly mineralized! Here's a trip worth more than a cure at Spa or Toeplitz!"

"Ah, it's so good!"

"I should think so — water drawn from two leagues underground. It has a taste of ink about it that's not the least bit unpleasant. A splendid resource Hans has found us here! And so I propose we give his name to this life-saving stream."

"Agreed!" I cried.

And the name "Hansbach" was adopted then and there. Hans was no prouder for it. After refreshing himself in moderation, he settled into a corner with his usual composure.

"Now," I said, "we mustn't let this water go to waste."

"What's the point?" my uncle replied. "I suspect the spring is inexhaustible."

"Never mind that! Let's fill the skin and the flasks, then we'll try to plug the opening."

My advice was followed. Using chips of granite and some tow, Hans tried to stop up the hole he'd made in the wall. It was no easy matter. We scalded our hands without managing it; the pressure was too great, and our efforts came to nothing.

"It's obvious," I said, "that the upper reaches of this watercourse lie at a great height, judging by the force of the jet."

"No doubt about it," my uncle answered. "There must be a thousand atmospheres of pressure there, if this column of water is thirty-two thousand feet high. But an idea occurs to me."

"What's that?"

"Why are we so bent on plugging this opening?"

"Well — because..."

I would have been hard put to come up with a good reason.

"When our flasks are empty, are we sure of finding water to fill them again?"

"No, obviously not."

"Well then, let's let this water run. It'll flow downhill of its own accord, and it will guide the very people it refreshes along the way!"

"Now that's clever!" I cried. "And with this stream for a companion, there's no longer any reason we shouldn't succeed in what we've set out to do."

"Ah, so you're coming around, my boy," said the professor, laughing.

"I'm doing better than coming around — I'm already there."

"One moment! Let's begin by taking a few hours' rest."

I really had forgotten it was night. The chronometer took it upon itself to remind me. Soon each of us, sufficiently restored and refreshed, fell into a deep sleep.

By the next day we had already forgotten everything we'd suffered. My first surprise was that I was no longer thirsty, and I asked why. The little stream murmuring at my feet answered for me.

We ate breakfast and drank our fill of that excellent iron-rich water. I felt thoroughly revived and ready to go a long way. Why shouldn't a man as certain of himself as my uncle succeed, with a guide as resourceful as Hans and a nephew as "determined" as I was? Those were the fine ideas slipping into my brain! If anyone had suggested climbing back up to the summit of the Sneffels, I'd have refused with indignation.

Fortunately, it was only a question of going down.

"Let's go!" I cried, waking the old echoes of the globe with my enthusiasm.

We set off again on Thursday at eight in the morning. The granite corridor twisted through winding detours, threw out sudden elbow-bends, and had all the tangle of a labyrinth about it; but on the whole its main heading was still to the southeast. My uncle never stopped consulting his compass with the greatest care, keeping track of the distance we'd covered.

The gallery ran almost dead level, with two inches of drop per fathom, no more. The stream ran on unhurried, murmuring beneath our feet. I compared it to some friendly household spirit guiding us through the earth, and with my hand I stroked the warm naiad whose songs kept time with our steps. My good mood was happy to take a mythological turn.

As for my uncle, he cursed the flatness of the road — he, "the man of verticals." His path stretched on endlessly, and instead of sliding straight down the earth's radius, as he put it, he was going by way of the hypotenuse. But we had no choice, and as long as we were gaining on the center, however little, there was no cause to complain.

Besides, every now and then the slopes dropped away; the naiad went tumbling down with a roar, and we descended deeper along with her.

All told, that day and the next we covered a great deal of horizontal ground and relatively little vertical.

By the evening of Friday, July 10th, by our reckoning, we should have been thirty leagues southeast of Reykjavik and at a depth of two and a half leagues.

At that point a rather frightening well opened up beneath our feet. My uncle couldn't help clapping his hands as he calculated the steepness of its sides.

"This will take us a long way," he cried, "and easily too, because the jutting rocks make a real staircase!"

Hans arranged the ropes so as to prevent any accident. The descent began. I don't dare call it perilous, since I was already used to this sort of exercise.

This well was a narrow crack cut into the rock mass, one of the kind called a "fault"; the contraction of the earth's framework as it cooled had clearly produced it. If it had once served as a passage for the erupted matter vomited out by the Sneffels, I couldn't understand how none of that matter had left any trace. We were descending a sort of winding screw that you'd have sworn was made by human hands.

Every quarter of an hour we had to stop for a necessary rest, to give our legs back their spring. We'd sit down on some jutting ledge, legs dangling, and talk while we ate and quenched our thirst at the stream.

It goes without saying that in this fault the Hansbach had turned itself into a waterfall, at some cost to its volume; but it was more than enough to slake our thirst; besides, once the slopes grew gentler it was bound to settle back into its peaceful course. Just now it reminded me of my worthy uncle, his impatience and his tempers, whereas on the softened slopes it was all the calm of the Icelandic hunter.

On the 6th and 7th of July we followed the spirals of this fault, sinking another two leagues into the earth's crust, which put us nearly five leagues below sea level. But on the 8th, around noon, the fault took on a much gentler slope in the southeast direction, about forty-five degrees.

The way then became easy and perfectly monotonous. It could hardly have been otherwise. The journey couldn't be varied by any incidents of scenery.

At last, on Wednesday the 15th, we were seven leagues underground and about fifty leagues from the Sneffels. Though we were a little tired, our health was holding up reassuringly, and the travel medicine chest was still untouched.

My uncle kept hour-by-hour readings of the compass, the chronometer, the pressure gauge, and the thermometer — the very ones he later published in the scientific account of his voyage. So he could easily work out our position. When he told me we were fifty leagues away horizontally, I couldn't hold back an exclamation.

"What is it?" he asked.

"Nothing — only a thought struck me."

"What thought, my boy?"

"That if your calculations are right, we're no longer under Iceland."

"You think so?"

"That's easy enough to check."

I took my measurements with the dividers on the map.

"I wasn't mistaken," I said. "We've passed Cape Portland, and these fifty leagues to the southeast put us out under the open sea."

"Under the open sea," my uncle repeated, rubbing his hands.

"So," I cried, "the ocean stretches over our heads!"

"Bah! Axel, nothing could be more natural! Aren't there coal mines at Newcastle that run out under the waves?"

The professor might find the situation perfectly simple, but the thought of strolling around beneath a mass of water didn't stop nagging at me. And yet, whether it was the plains and mountains of Iceland hanging over our heads or the waters of the Atlantic, it made little difference in the end, so long as the granite framework held solid. Besides, I got used to the idea soon enough, for the corridor — now straight, now winding, as capricious in its slopes as in its turns, but always running southeast and always sinking deeper — carried us quickly to great depths.

So I woke on Sunday morning without the usual nagging worry of an immediate departure. And even though we were down in the deepest of abysses, that wasn't such an unpleasant feeling. Besides, we'd grown used to this troglodyte existence. I barely gave a thought to the sun, the stars, the moon, the trees, the houses, the towns — all those terrestrial luxuries that creatures under the sky have convinced themselves they can't live without. As fossils now, we snapped our fingers at those useless marvels.

The grotto formed a vast hall. Across its granite floor the faithful little stream ran quietly along. At such a distance from its source, its water had cooled to the surrounding temperature and could be drunk without any trouble.

"Yes. I've carefully recorded the angles and the gradients; I'm certain I haven't made a mistake. Let's see first where we are. Take the compass and tell me what direction it points."

"East-quarter-southeast."

"Precisely."

"No doubt about it."

"Twenty-seven and six-tenths degrees."

To tell the truth, I'd have had plenty to say. I didn't accept Davy's theory in any way; I still held with the idea of central heat, even though I felt none of its effects. "You're allowed to hold your tongue, Axel, whenever you feel like talking such nonsense."

"A considerable pressure."

"None — except a bit of pain in the ears."

"That's nothing, and you'll make that discomfort disappear by putting the outside air quickly in touch with the air held in your lungs."

"Absolutely," I answered, quite determined not to cross my uncle any further. "There's even a real pleasure in feeling oneself plunged into this denser air. Have you noticed how intensely sound carries through it?"

"No doubt; a deaf man would end up hearing perfectly down here."

"But surely this density will keep increasing?"

"Yes, according to a rather poorly defined law. It's true that the force of gravity will diminish as we go down. You know that it's at the very surface of the Earth that its pull is felt most strongly, and that at the center of the globe objects weigh nothing at all."

"I know that. But tell me — won't this air eventually take on the density of water?"

"No doubt, under a pressure of seven hundred and ten atmospheres."

"And lower down?"

"Lower down, that density will increase still more."

"Then how will we go down?"

"Well, we'll put stones in our pockets."

"My word, Uncle, you have an answer for everything."

I didn't dare venture any further into the field of hypotheses, for I'd have collided again with some impossibility that would have set the Professor off.

It was obvious, all the same, that air under a pressure that might reach thousands of atmospheres would eventually turn solid, and then — assuming our bodies had held up — we'd have to stop, in spite of every argument in the world.

But I didn't press the point. My uncle would only have countered again with his eternal Saknussemm, a worthless precedent — for even granting that the learned Icelander's journey had really happened, there was one very simple thing to say in reply:

In the sixteenth century, neither the barometer nor the pressure gauge had been invented; so how could Saknussemm ever have determined his arrival at the center of the globe?

But I kept that objection to myself and waited to see what would happen.

The rest of the day passed in calculations and conversation. I agreed with Professor Lidenbrock on everything, and I envied the perfect indifference of Hans, who, without hunting for effects and causes, went blindly wherever destiny led him.
