Symposium Part 2 — Translation Notes
======================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SOCRATES' CROSS-EXAMINATION OF AGATHON: Kept this quick and sharp, as
   the original intends. The logical trap snaps shut efficiently: Love desires
   beauty → desire is for what you lack → Love lacks beauty → Love isn't
   beautiful. Short exchanges, clean logic, no padding. Agathon's "I'm afraid
   I didn't really understand what I was saying" is a gracious concession that
   needed to land with its full gentle humor.

2. DIOTIMA'S SPEECH — STRUCTURE AND BUILDING INTENSITY:
   The Diotima passage is the philosophical heart of the Symposium and one of
   the great set-pieces in Western philosophy. Organized the ascent carefully:

   a) Love is neither beautiful nor ugly, neither god nor mortal — the "in between"
   b) Love as daimon (spirit/intermediary) — kept "daimon" on first use
   c) The myth of Poverty and Plenty — vivid storytelling, made the allegory
      concrete and characterful
   d) Love as philosopher — the key identification (lovers of wisdom are
      those "in between" wise and ignorant)
   e) Love as desire for the everlasting possession of the good
   f) Love as desire for immortality through creation
   g) Two kinds of pregnancy — body and soul
   h) The "ladder of love" — the ascending vision from one body → all bodies
      → beautiful souls → beautiful activities → beautiful knowledge → Beauty itself
   i) The final revelation — Beauty absolute

   Each stage was given its own clear paragraph and momentum. The ladder
   passage builds from shorter, faster steps to the expansive, almost
   breathless description of Beauty itself.

3. DIOTIMA'S VOICE: Made her authoritative, witty, occasionally teasing.
   She smiles at Socrates, corrects him firmly, uses rhetorical questions
   effectively. "Even a child could answer that" — her intellectual confidence
   comes through clearly. She's the one figure in the Symposium who is
   smarter than Socrates (or at least whom Socrates presents as his teacher).

4. THE LADDER OF LOVE (paragraphs 67-69 of original):
   This is one of the most famous philosophical passages ever written.
   Preserved the step-by-step ascending structure:
   - One beautiful body
   - All beautiful bodies
   - Beauty of the soul over the body
   - Beautiful activities and laws
   - Beautiful knowledge/sciences
   - The vast ocean of beauty
   - Beauty itself — eternal, absolute, unmixed

   Made the transitions between steps explicit and the building intensity
   palpable. The climactic description of Beauty itself was given its full
   rhetorical power — long flowing sentences that contrast with the shorter
   steps before it. "Not contaminated by human flesh or color or any mortal
   nonsense" — slightly irreverent phrasing that keeps Diotima's voice
   distinct from dry philosophical prose.

5. ALCIBIADES' ENTRANCE: This is a complete tonal shift — from philosophical
   ecstasy to drunken chaos. Marked it with a section break (---) to signal
   the disruption. Made the entrance physical, loud, funny:
   - "completely hammered"
   - "stumbled his way in"
   - "swaying in the doorway"
   - "trailing ribbons everywhere"

   The contrast with Diotima's transcendent vision of Beauty is the whole point.

6. ALCIBIADES' SPEECH — THE SILENUS COMPARISON:
   This is one of the great character portraits in literature. Key decisions:

   a) The Silenus/satyr comparison: Made it vivid and concrete — statues you
      crack open to find gods inside. The metaphor works on multiple levels
      (ugly outside/divine inside; ridiculous words/profound meaning) and I
      let both applications land clearly.

   b) The Marsyas comparison: Socrates as a kind of musician who enchants
      with words instead of instruments. Alcibiades' confession that Socrates'
      words make him weep, make his heart pound, make him feel his life is
      worthless — this is raw and passionate. Let it be.

   c) The seduction attempt: Frank, funny, and ultimately deeply moving.
      The sequence — dismissing the attendant, the wrestling, the dinner
      invitation, the late night, crawling under the cloak — builds like a
      comedy that turns into something profound. Socrates' absolute self-
      control in the face of the most beautiful young man in Athens is the
      punch line, but it's also the proof of everything Diotima said about
      ascending beyond physical beauty.

   d) The military anecdotes: Potidaea (endurance, cold, standing all night
      in thought) and Delium (courage in retreat). These ground the portrait
      in reality — Socrates isn't just a talker, he's physically extraordinary.

7. ALCIBIADES' EMOTIONAL RANGE: His speech veers between:
   - Drunk bravado ("I know perfectly well that I'm telling the truth")
   - Genuine anguish ("Many times I've wished he were dead")
   - Frank confession ("Nothing happened. When I woke up...")
   - Hero worship ("no human being has ever seen Socrates drunk")
   - Warning ("don't be fooled by this man")

   Preserved all of these shifts. The speech is a mess — deliberately so.
   Alcibiades keeps saying he's drunk and disorganized, and the speech does
   feel like it's pouring out in bursts. That's the point.

8. THE CLOSING SCENE: Iconic and handled with restraint. The party dissolves,
   everyone falls asleep or leaves, and at dawn only Socrates is still awake,
   arguing that comedy and tragedy require the same genius. His interlocutors
   nod off. He walks to the Lyceum, takes a bath, and goes about his day.
   The most extraordinary night of conversation in Western literature, and
   Socrates treats it like any other Tuesday. Kept this spare and matter-of-fact.

SPECIFIC VOCABULARY DECISIONS
------------------------------

- "daimon" → "spirit — a daimon" on first use, then "spirit" / "intermediary"
- "Poros/Penia" → "Poros — Plenty" / "Penia — Poverty" (glossed once)
- "Metis" → "Metis, or Resourcefulness" (glossed once)
- "nectar" → kept (universally known)
- "the Corybantian revellers" → "any ecstatic worshipper's" (Corybantes
  too obscure for most readers; the meaning is religious frenzy)
- "sophist" → "master teacher" in context of Diotima (she's being praised,
  not insulted — Jowett's "accomplished sophist" is misleading in modern English
  where "sophist" is pejorative)
- "palaestra" → "wrestling ground" (more accessible)
- "the wine-cooler" → kept (self-explanatory)
- "by Heracles" → kept (Alcibiades' characteristic oath)
- "by Poseidon" → kept
- "Silenus" → kept, with description of the statues
- "Marsyas" → kept, with explanation of who he was
- "Brasidas" / "Ajax" / "Nestor" / "Antenor" → kept (the comparisons
  work even if you don't know the exact references)
- "Potidaea" / "Delium" → kept as battle names
- "Lyceum" → kept (well-known)

ARCHAIC LANGUAGE MODERNIZED
-----------------------------

- "I would fain ask" → "I'd like to ask"
- "the manner is on such occasions" → "as people do on such occasions"
- "the worse for nectar" → "having drunk too much nectar"
- "straitened circumstances" → "desperate situation"
- "squalid" → "weather-beaten"
- "a philosopher at all times, terrible as an enchanter" → "a philosopher
  through and through, a formidable enchanter"
- "the oracle requires an explanation" → "That's an oracle. I need a translation."
- "parturition" → "birth" / "giving birth"
- "the ever-growing and perishing beauties" → participates in beauty in a
  way that doesn't diminish the original
- "notwithstanding" → removed/rephrased throughout
- "perchance" → dropped
- "he rejoined" → "he replied" or "he said"
- "vociferous" → "shouted"
- "ingenious circumlocution" → "elaborate satyr-praise" / "clever about hiding"

SOCRATIC IRONY IN THIS SECTION
---------------------------------

Less prominent than in other dialogues since Socrates is mostly channeling
Diotima, but preserved in key moments:
- "No — say rather, dear Agathon, that you can't argue with the truth.
  Socrates is easy enough to argue with." (classic self-deprecation)
- His mock plea for Agathon's protection against Alcibiades
- His reading of Alcibiades' speech as an attempt to create jealousy —
  Socrates sees through the drunk man's performance instantly

SENSITIVE CONTENT
------------------

Alcibiades' attempted seduction of Socrates: translated faithfully and
naturally. The passage is frank about male-male desire and sexual pursuit.
No euphemizing — the text makes clear that Alcibiades crawled into bed
with Socrates, that nothing happened, and that this was both humiliating
and awe-inspiring to Alcibiades. The emotional honesty is what makes
the passage work.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (PHAEDRUS)
------------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: In the Symposium, Socrates speaks through Diotima for
   much of his speech, so his own voice is a bit recessive. In Phaedrus,
   he's back to direct dialogue — sharp, playful, ironic. The Phaedrus is
   an outdoor pastoral conversation (by the river Ilissus), so the tone
   should be more relaxed and intimate than the party atmosphere here.

2. LOVE AS A THEME: The Symposium's "ladder of love" and the Phaedrus'
   "chariot allegory" are companion pieces. Both deal with Eros but from
   different angles. The Symposium emphasizes the ascending intellectual
   journey; the Phaedrus emphasizes the psychological struggle (reason
   vs. desire) and the vision of the Forms. Maintain continuity in how
   love, beauty, and the Forms are discussed.

3. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "the Forms" (capitalized) for Plato's metaphysical theory
   - "Beauty itself" for the Form of Beauty
   - "the Good" for the Form of the Good
   - "daimon" / "spirit" for intermediary beings
   - "philosopher" = "lover of wisdom" (the etymology was made explicit
     in Diotima's speech)
   - Contractions used freely in dialogue
   - "by Zeus" etc. for Greek oaths

4. NARRATION FORMAT: The Symposium is narrated (Apollodorus telling what
   Aristodemus told him). The Phaedrus is direct dialogue between Socrates
   and Phaedrus. Switch to dramatic dialogue format (speaker name on own
   line, then speech).

5. THE CHARIOT ALLEGORY: Should be as vivid and physically immediate as
   the "ladder of love" was intellectually building. Two very different
   modes of philosophical imagery.

6. RHETORIC THEME: The Phaedrus contains Plato's most sustained discussion
   of rhetoric and writing. After the love speeches, there's a long
   analysis of what good rhetoric requires. This is denser material —
   keep it accessible without dumbing it down.

7. THE CRITIQUE OF WRITING: The famous irony of Plato writing about the
   inferiority of writing. Let the irony breathe — the reader should feel it.
