CHAPTER 013 — Phaedrus (Part 2): Translation Notes
====================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
This chapter completes the Phaedrus, covering:
1. The conclusion of Socrates' great recantation speech (the chariot allegory's
   depiction of the beloved's experience and the outcomes of love)
2. The prayer to Eros
3. The discussion of true vs. false rhetoric
4. A survey and critique of existing rhetoric teachers (Tisias, Gorgias,
   Theodorus, Prodicus, Polus, Thrasymachus, etc.)
5. The myth of Theuth and Thamus (the invention of writing)
6. The critique of writing as "dead speech" vs. living dialectic
7. The distinction between philosophers and mere writers
8. The prophecy about Isocrates
9. The prayer to Pan

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
----------------------------

1. THE CHARIOT ALLEGORY (CONCLUSION)
   The opening section completes the elaborate metaphor of the soul as charioteer
   with two horses. Kept the vivid, physical language (blood, sweat, neighing)
   while modernizing sentence structure. "Shag-eared" became "shaggy-eared."
   "Aquiline nose" became "hawk-like nose" for slightly better accessibility
   while preserving the noble imagery. Maintained the contrast between the
   disciplined white horse and the dark, unruly one.

2. THE STREAM OF BEAUTY / MIRROR PASSAGE
   The beautiful passage about love flowing back from beloved to lover through
   the "windows of the soul" (the eyes) required careful handling. Preserved
   the imagery of the stream, the echo, the mirror. Kept "Anteros" (love
   returned for love) in spirit though not the Greek term, rendering the
   concept clearly.

3. RHETORIC AS GENUINE ART VS. MERE TECHNIQUE
   The long central discussion about what makes rhetoric a real "techne" (art/craft)
   versus a collection of tricks is philosophically crucial. Made the argument's
   structure clearer by emphasizing the analogies:
   - Rhetoric professor who knows techniques but not truth = someone who knows
     drugs but not which patient needs which treatment
   - Knowing rhetorical devices without understanding the whole = knowing notes
     but not harmony
   - The key claim: true rhetoric requires knowledge of the soul and of truth

4. THE DONKEY/HORSE ANALOGY
   Socrates' absurd example of someone confusing a donkey for a horse is
   genuinely funny. Let the comedy land naturally. Extended it to the serious
   point: confusing good for evil is the real danger of truth-free rhetoric.

5. RHETORIC PERSONIFIED
   The passage where Socrates imagines Rhetoric defending herself ("What
   ridiculous nonsense!") is a lovely dramatic moment. Kept her voice distinct
   and assertive. Then the "Spartan" counter-voice declaring rhetoric without
   truth is no art at all.

6. COLLECTION AND DIVISION
   The two great dialectical principles are introduced here:
   - Collection (synagoge): gathering scattered particulars under one idea
   - Division (diairesis): cutting at the natural joints, "like a good butcher"
   The butcher metaphor is one of Plato's best — preserved it with emphasis.
   These principles are the heart of Platonic method.

7. THE MYTH OF THEUTH AND THAMUS
   This is one of the most famous passages in Plato and deeply, beautifully
   ironic — Plato WRITING a story about how WRITING is inferior to live
   conversation. Let the irony breathe without hitting the reader over the
   head with it. Thamus's rebuke is rendered clearly: writing creates the
   appearance of wisdom without the reality, aids reminding but not memory.

   Phaedrus's skeptical response ("You have a real talent for making up
   stories about Egypt") and Socrates' counter-rebuke ("you care less about
   whether something is true than about who's saying it") are important —
   they show Plato was aware of the self-referential quality of the myth.

8. WRITING AS PAINTING / DEAD SPEECH
   The comparison of written words to paintings — both appear alive but cannot
   answer questions — is one of the most powerful images in the dialogue.
   Extended it clearly: writing "rolls around everywhere," can't choose its
   audience, can't defend itself. Versus the "living word of knowledge written
   in the soul" that knows when to speak and when to be silent.

9. THE GARDEN OF ADONIS VS. REAL FARMING
   A gorgeous metaphor: the serious thinker doesn't plant his ideas in the
   quick-sprouting but shallow "garden of letters." He plants them in living
   souls through dialectic, where they take root slowly but bear real fruit.
   Writing is for amusement and as a hedge against old age's forgetfulness.

10. PHILOSOPHER VS. MERE WRITER
    The climactic distinction: those who can defend their written words through
    live argument deserve to be called "philosophers" (lovers of wisdom), not
    just poets or lawmakers. "Wise" is reserved for the divine. This is where
    the word "philosopher" gets its Platonic definition.

11. THE ISOCRATES PROPHECY
    Socrates' prediction about the young Isocrates is historically fascinating
    and scholars debate whether it's genuine praise or subtle criticism. Kept
    it straight — the surface reads as genuine admiration with a hint that
    Isocrates may transcend mere rhetoric for philosophy.

12. THE PRAYER TO PAN
    The closing prayer is one of the most beautiful endings in all of Plato.
    Simple, sincere, and profound: inner beauty, harmony between outer and
    inner, counting wisdom as wealth, wanting only as much gold as a disciplined
    person can carry. Phaedrus's response — "friends should share everything" —
    is the perfect warm coda. Kept it clean and direct. No embellishment needed.

STYLE NOTES
-----------
- Maintained dialogue format with speaker labels throughout
- Used contractions naturally (don't, isn't, can't, I'm, you're, etc.)
- Kept the back-and-forth rhythm tight: short questions, short answers
- Preserved all the named rhetoricians and teachers (Tisias, Gorgias,
  Theodorus, Evenus, Prodicus, Hippias, Polus, Protagoras, Thrasymachus,
  Licymnius) — they're part of the intellectual landscape
- "Gardens of Adonis" — kept the reference; it's clear enough in context
  (festival gardens planted for quick show, not real agriculture)
- Maintained the pastoral warmth throughout — the grasshoppers, the midday
  heat, the plane tree setting established in Part 1

CONSISTENCY WITH PART 1
-----------------------
- Same speaker label format
- Same register: conversational, smart, warm
- Socrates' playful irony consistent throughout
- Phaedrus's character: enthusiastic, sometimes naive, genuinely engaged
- The grasshoppers callback connects to the pastoral setting from Part 1

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (014 — Republic Book 1)
-----------------------------------------------
- Major shift in format: Republic is narrated by Socrates in first person
  ("I went down to the Piraeus yesterday..." / "I said... he replied...")
- Keep narrated format — don't convert to dramatic dialogue
- Book I reads like an early Socratic dialogue embedded in the narrated frame
- Key characters: Cephalus (the old man), Polemarchus (his son),
  Thrasymachus (the aggressive Sophist)
- Thrasymachus appears here as a character, having been mentioned as a
  teacher in Phaedrus — maintain consistency in how he's characterized
- The tone shifts from pastoral warmth to urban intellectual combat
- The central question emerges: "What is justice?"
- Thrasymachus's entry should be theatrical and forceful — he's described
  as bursting in "like a savage animal"
