Laws Book 7, Part 1 — Translation Notes
=========================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's Laws Book 7 is among the most syntactically
   challenging in the entire corpus. The Athenian's speeches regularly run
   80-150 words in single sentences, with multiple nested clauses and
   parenthetical insertions. These were systematically broken into shorter
   units (average ~20-25 words), while preserving every argument and every
   logical step. The long paragraph on the bird-carrying exercise for
   pregnant women was especially tangled — Jowett packs the entire argument
   (observation about birds -> principle about motion -> application to
   pregnancy -> proposed law -> retraction) into a handful of enormous
   sentences. These were untangled into a clear logical sequence.

2. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "endeavour" -> "try" / "do my best"
   - "inasmuch as" -> "since" / "because"
   - "aforesaid" -> dropped or rephrased
   - "hitherto" -> "so far" / "until now"
   - "enquire" -> "investigate" / "ask"
   - "whilst" -> "while"
   - "nay" -> "no" (kept emphatic where needed)
   - "wont to" -> "tends to"
   - "unseemliness" -> reworked naturally
   - "I dare say" -> "I'd say" / "probably"
   - "notwithstanding" -> "despite" / "even so"
   - "whither" -> "where" / "wherever"
   - "inauspicious" -> kept (clear enough in context)
   - "prosper" -> kept (natural in context)

3. VICTORIAN INVERSIONS removed throughout:
   - "Am I not right in maintaining that..." -> "Am I not right that..."
   - "said I" constructions eliminated
   - "by reason of" -> "because of"
   - "whereby they prove" -> "This proves"
   - "the more solemn institutions" -> "the more serious institutions"
   - Complex subordinate clause chains broken into sequential statements

4. CONTRACTIONS used freely: "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "that's," "we've,"
   "can't," "it'll," "won't," "shouldn't," "wouldn't," "they'll," etc.
   Consistent with voice established across the collection.

5. THE PRENATAL EXERCISE PASSAGE: This is one of the most charming and
   unusual passages in the Laws — the Athenian arguing that pregnant women
   should walk around to give their unborn children exercise, supported by
   the wonderfully eccentric analogy of Athenian men walking their fighting
   cocks and quails for miles. The humor in this passage is real: the
   Athenian knows it sounds absurd ("No, no — the penalty we'd really face
   would fall on our own heads soon enough" i.e. ridicule). Preserved the
   self-aware comedy while making the underlying argument clear.

6. THE MOTION AND FEAR PASSAGE: The argument that external motion can
   overpower internal agitation (using Bacchic worshippers and restless
   babies as examples) is genuinely insightful psychology. Made the
   mechanism clear: external rhythmic motion -> overpowers internal
   chaotic fear -> produces calm. The connection to the Corybantes rites
   was preserved.

7. THE MIDDLE PATH ARGUMENT: The Athenian's insistence on avoiding both
   pure pleasure and pure pain in childrearing — embracing a gentle middle
   state — is quintessentially Platonic. Cleinias' suggestion to maximize
   pleasure is firmly rejected. Made the Athenian's disagreement clear
   and Cleinias' capitulation natural: "I myself agree that everyone should
   avoid a life of pure pleasure or pure pain."

8. THE UNWRITTEN CUSTOMS PASSAGE: This is one of the most important
   theoretical passages in the Laws — the argument that unwritten customs
   are the "bonds of the whole state" that sit between written laws and
   hold the structure together. The building metaphor (support beams
   slipping -> total collapse) is powerful and was preserved intact.

9. THE STABILITY OF CHILDREN'S GAMES: A remarkable and counterintuitive
   argument — that changes in children's games lead to changes in adult
   character, which leads to changes in laws and institutions. The
   Egyptian model of consecrating all musical forms is offered as the
   solution. Made the argument's logic transparent while preserving its
   radical quality. The Athenian knows this sounds extreme: "Can anyone
   who enacts such a law escape ridicule?"

10. THE AMBIDEXTERITY ARGUMENT: The Athenian's passionate case for
    training both hands equally, using the Scythians as evidence, is
    characteristically practical. The key insight — that nurses create
    handedness through bad habit, not nature — was made crisp and clear.
    The reference to Geryon and Briareus (multi-armed mythological beings)
    provides vivid comic hyperbole.

11. GYMNASTICS AND DANCE: The division of education into gymnastics (body)
    and music (soul), and the subdivision of gymnastics into dancing and
    wrestling, was laid out clearly. The distinction between useful martial
    wrestling (standing, controlled) and useless competitive tricks
    (Antaeus, Cercyon) was preserved. The reference to Athena dancing in
    full armor — our "patron goddess" — was made vivid.

12. COMPULSORY EDUCATION: The Athenian's insistence on universal,
    compulsory, co-educational training is remarkable for antiquity.
    "Children shall attend whether their parents want them to or not"
    captures the force of the original. The Sauromatian women (Sarmatians)
    are cited as living proof. The argument that a state neglecting women's
    education "cuts itself in half" is powerful and was given full weight.

13. HUMANS AS PUPPETS: The striking passage where the Athenian calls
    humans "for the most part puppets" with "some small share of genuine
    reality" drew a rebuke from Megillus ("You have a pretty low opinion
    of the human race"). The Athenian's quick retreat ("I was comparing
    us with the gods") is characteristic of the Laws' gentler dialectic.
    Preserved the exchange's warmth and humor.

14. THE MUSIC CENSORSHIP LAWS: Three laws governing music are established:
    (a) songs of good omen only, (b) prayers must ask for genuine goods,
    (c) all compositions must be approved by judges before performance.
    The passage about funeral choruses competing to make people weep at
    sacrifices is vivid social criticism. The Carian hired mourners are
    a memorable detail.

15. THE SHIPWRIGHT METAPHOR: "Like a shipwright who first lays down the
    keel and from there sketches the outline of the whole ship" — a
    beautiful image for the legislative enterprise. Preserved intact.


SPEAKER DYNAMICS
-----------------

This chapter features more three-way interaction than many Laws chapters.
Key dynamics:

- ATHENIAN: Wise, sometimes self-deprecating, sometimes boldly provocative.
  His admission that the prenatal exercise proposal would invite ridicule
  shows self-awareness. His criticism of Sparta's treatment of women is
  surprisingly direct — and Megillus notices.

- CLEINIAS: More engaged than usual. His challenge about maximizing pleasure
  for children is substantive, and his graceful concession is well-handled.
  His meta-comment about letting the discussion run its course shows
  mature intellectual flexibility.

- MEGILLUS: Gets his best moment when he asks Cleinias if they should
  "let this stranger criticize Sparta like this." A touch of wounded
  civic pride that humanizes the dialogue.


GREEK OATHS AND CULTURAL REFERENCES
--------------------------------------

- Corybantes: Priests of Cybele known for ecstatic dances; explained
  implicitly through context rather than parenthetical gloss
- Bacchic women: Followers of Dionysus; familiar enough not to need
  explanation
- Curetes: Cretan dancers; identified as performing "armed dances"
- Dioscuri: Castor and Pollux; Spartan patron heroes
- Athena: Described as "our patron goddess" dancing in armor
- Pancratium: Ancient combat sport combining wrestling and boxing;
  left as-is (clear from context)
- Antaeus, Cercyon, Epeius, Amycus: Mythological wrestlers/boxers;
  kept as names, their systems characterized as "useless"
- Geryon, Briareus: Multi-limbed mythological figures; used for comic
  effect in the ambidexterity argument
- Sauromatides/Sauromatian women: Near the Black Sea; warrior women
  cited as evidence for women's physical capability
- Carian dirges: Professional mourning tradition from Caria
- Nomoi: The Greek pun on "laws" and "songs" is noted directly in the
  translation: "the ancients used the same word — 'nomoi' — for both
  lyric songs and laws"
- Homer/Telemachus quotation: Modernized ("some things you'll discover
  in your own heart") while keeping the poetic quality


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
-------------------------------------

1. Speaker labels: "Athenian," "Cleinias," "Megillus" — consistent
   throughout.

2. The Athenian's voice in the Laws: authoritative but warm, earnest but
   occasionally self-deprecating, less ironic than Socrates but not without
   humor. The prenatal exercise passage and the "puppets" comment show a
   distinctly Platonic late-life sensibility.

3. The three-part education framework has been established:
   - Gymnastics (body): dancing + wrestling
   - Music (soul): to be continued in Part 2
   - Mathematics and astronomy: to be covered in Part 2

4. Key positions established: compulsory co-education, universal military
   training for both sexes, stability of children's games as essential to
   political stability, the middle path between pleasure and pain, music
   censorship by approved judges.

5. The discussion of women's education ends on a confrontational note —
   the Athenian has criticized Sparta directly, Megillus has bristled,
   and Cleinias has smoothed things over. Part 2 should not carry
   residual tension from this exchange.

6. Contractions, modern phrasing, and conversational tone maintained
   throughout. The Laws' more monologic style (long Athenian speeches
   with brief agreements) is preserved but made more readable through
   sentence-breaking.
