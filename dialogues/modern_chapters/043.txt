TIMAEUS (Part 2)

Timaeus (continuing)

Before going further, we need to get clear about rest and motion, or the discussion ahead will run into all kinds of trouble. I've said something about this already, but one more point needs making: motion never exists where everything is uniform. Think about it. Nothing can move without a mover, and there can't be a mover unless there's something to be moved. Motion requires both — and where both exist, things can't be uniform. So let's assign rest to uniformity and motion to non-uniformity. And what causes non-uniformity? Inequality — whose origin we've already described.

But one puzzle remains: why don't the elements, once sorted into their separate kinds, simply stop moving through one another and changing places? Here's the explanation. The revolution of the universe embraces all four elements, and since that revolution is circular and tends to press everything together, it squeezes the whole and refuses to leave any space empty. That's why fire, above all, penetrates everywhere, with air second (being the second-finest element), and the other two following according to their own degrees of fineness. Bodies made of the largest particles have the largest gaps left in their structure; bodies made of the smallest particles have the least. The compression of the cosmic squeeze pushes the small particles into the gaps between the big ones. So small sits next to large, the smaller divides the greater, the greater compacts the lesser, and everything is carried up and down and back and forth toward its proper region — because as each thing changes size, it changes its place in space. In this way an inequality is generated that never goes away, and it keeps the elements in perpetual motion, now and always.

Next, notice that there are several kinds of fire. First, flame. Second, the emanations of flame that don't burn but only give light to the eyes. Third, the glow that remains in red-hot embers after the flame has gone out. The same goes for air: the brightest and clearest kind is called aether, the murkiest kind is mist and darkness, and there are other nameless varieties produced by the inequality of the triangles.

Water divides first into two kinds: the liquid and the fusible — that is, the meltable kind, like metal. The liquid kind is made of small, unequal particles of water; it moves on its own and is easily moved by other bodies, precisely because of its non-uniformity and the shape of its particles. The fusible kind, made of large, uniform particles, is more stable, heavy, and compact, thanks to that very uniformity. But when fire gets inside it and dissolves its particles, destroying the uniformity, it becomes more mobile; turning fluid, it's pushed along by the neighboring air and spreads out over the ground. The dissolving of the solid mass we call melting, and its spreading over the earth we call flowing. Then, when the fire leaves the molten substance, it doesn't exit into a vacuum — there's no such thing — but into the neighboring air. The displaced air presses the still-fluid mass back together into the spaces the fire vacated, and blends it into one. Compressed this way, the mass recovers its uniformity and becomes one with itself again, since the fire that caused the inequality has retreated. This departure of fire is called cooling, and the contraction that follows is called solidifying.

Of all the fusible kinds, the one that's densest, formed from the finest and most uniform parts, is that most precious of possessions: gold, which hardens as it filters through rock. It's unique in kind, with its glitter and its yellow color. An offshoot of gold so dense that it's extremely hard, and dark in color, is called adamant. There's another kind whose parts are nearly like gold's, with several varieties. It's denser than gold, and it contains a small, fine portion of earth, which makes it harder — and yet it's lighter, because of the large gaps inside it. This substance, one of the bright, dense kinds of water, is what we call copper when it solidifies. The earth alloyed into it, when the two age and separate, shows itself on the surface — and that's rust.

The remaining phenomena of this sort are easy enough to reason out by the method of probabilities. In fact, a person may sometimes set aside thinking about eternal things and, for refreshment, turn to these merely probable accounts of how things come to be. It's a pleasure he won't regret — a wise and moderate recreation for as long as he lives. So let's grant ourselves that indulgence and run through the probabilities on the subjects that come next.

Take water mixed with fire. The portion that is fine and liquid (called "liquid" because of its motion and the way it rolls along the ground) and soft (because its bases give way, being less stable than earth's) — when this is separated from fire and air and left alone, it becomes more uniform and is compressed into itself by the departure of those elements. If the condensation is very great, it becomes hail when it forms above the earth, and ice when it forms on the earth. If it congeals less completely — only half solid — it's snow when formed above the earth, and when formed on the earth, condensed from dew, it's frost.

Then there are the many kinds of water that have been mixed with one another and filtered through the plants that grow in the earth. As a whole class these are called juices or saps. Because the mixtures are all unequal, they produce many varieties, most of them nameless. But four, which are fiery in nature, stand out clearly and have names. First, wine, which warms the soul along with the body. Second, the oily kind, which is smooth and splits the visual ray, and is therefore bright, shiny, and glistening to look at — this includes pitch, castor oil, olive oil itself, and other things of that sort. Third, the kind that relaxes the contracted pores of the mouth back to their natural state, producing sweetness by that very action — these all go by the general name of honey. And last, a frothy kind, unlike all the other juices, with a burning quality that dissolves flesh; it's called opos, a vegetable acid.

As for the kinds of earth: earth that is filtered through water turns to stone in the following way. The water mixed in with the earth gets broken up in the process and changes into air, and in that form it rises toward its own region. But since there's no vacuum around it, it shoves the neighboring air aside; and that air, weighed down and displaced, pours itself around the mass of earth, squeezes it hard, and drives it into the spaces the new air just left. Earth compressed by air this way forms an unbreakable bond with water and becomes rock. The finer sort is the kind made of equal, similar parts, and it's transparent; the kind with the opposite qualities is inferior. But when fire suddenly draws out all the moisture, a more brittle substance forms — what we call pottery. Sometimes some moisture remains, and earth that has been fused by fire becomes, when it cools, a certain black-colored stone.

In the same way, two substances made of finer particles of earth, briny in nature, can have their abundant water drawn off, leaving a half-solid body that dissolves again in water. One is soda, used for scouring away oil and dirt. The other is salt, which blends so agreeably into combinations that please the palate, and which is — as custom and ritual testify — a substance dear to the gods.

Compounds of earth and water can't be dissolved by water, but only by fire. Here's why. Neither fire nor air can melt masses of earth, because their particles are smaller than the gaps in earth's structure: they have plenty of room to pass through without forcing anything, so they leave the earth intact and undissolved. But particles of water are larger; they have to force a passage, and in doing so they dissolve and melt the earth. So earth, when not packed tight by force, is dissolved only by water; when it is packed tight, only by fire — for fire alone can find a way in. The cohesion of water, in turn, when very strong, is broken only by fire; when weaker, either by air or fire — air entering the gaps, fire penetrating right down to the triangles themselves. Strongly condensed air can be dissolved by nothing that fails to reach its elemental triangles; loosely condensed air yields only to fire.

Now consider bodies compounded of earth and water. As long as the water occupies the gaps in the earth, packed in tight, water particles arriving from outside can find no entrance; they just flow around the whole mass and leave it undissolved. But fire particles can enter the gaps in the water, and then fire does to water what water does to earth and fire does to air — fire alone is the cause of such a compound body melting and turning fluid. These compounds come in two types: some, like glass and the meltable kinds of stone, contain less water than earth; others, like wax and incense, contain more water than earth.

I've now shown the various classes of bodies — their diverse forms, their combinations, and their transformations into one another. Next I must try to explain how they affect us, and why. First of all, the bodies I've been describing must be perceptible by the senses. But we haven't yet discussed the origin of flesh, or of what belongs to flesh, or of the mortal part of the soul. The trouble is, none of these can be properly explained without explaining sensation, and sensation can't be explained without them — and explaining both at once is hardly possible. So we must assume one side first and come back later to examine what we assumed. To let the sensations follow directly from our account of the elements, then, let's presuppose the existence of body and soul.

First question: what do we mean when we say fire is hot? We can work it out from the cutting and dividing action fire performs on our bodies. We all feel that the sensation is sharp. Now consider the fineness of fire's edges, the sharpness of its angles, the smallness of its particles, the swiftness of its motion — all of this makes fire violent and piercing, so that it cuts whatever it meets. And remember its original shape, the pyramid: more than any other form, it dices our bodies into tiny pieces. That's what naturally produces the sensation we call heat — and in Greek, the very name reflects it: thermos, "hot," echoes kerma, "something chopped fine." (The pun works in the Greek.)

The opposite sensation is obvious enough, but let's not leave it undescribed. When the larger particles of moisture surrounding the body enter it and push out the smaller ones, but can't fit into the places those occupied, they compress the moisture inside us. Once unequal and agitated, it's forced by the compression into stillness and uniformity. But things squeezed together against their nature fight back, by their nature, and push themselves apart. This battle, this shuddering, is what we call shivering and trembling — and the whole sensation, along with what causes it, is called cold.

Hard is what our flesh yields to; soft is what yields to our flesh — and things are also hard or soft relative to one another. What yields is whatever sits on a small base. The shape that rests on square bases stands firmest and resists the most; so does whatever is most densely compacted.

Heavy and light are best understood by examining our notions of "above" and "below" — and here we must clear up a real mistake. It's quite wrong to picture the universe as split into two opposite regions: a "lower" one toward which everything with any bulk sinks, and an "upper" one toward which things rise only against their will. The universe is a sphere. All its outermost points stand at equal distance from the center, and so all of them are equally "extremities"; and the center, equidistant from them all, is equally opposite to every one of them. Given a world like that, if someone calls one of these regions "above" and another "below," isn't he simply talking nonsense? The center of the world can't rightly be called either above or below — it's just the center. And the circumference isn't the center, and no part of it relates to the center any differently than any opposite part does. When something is the same in every direction, what sense does it make to slap on labels that imply opposites? If there were a solid body balanced at the very center of the universe, nothing would pull it toward one extremity rather than another, since all are perfectly alike. And if a person walked all the way around the world, he would repeatedly stand at the antipodes of his earlier position and call the very same point both "above" and "below." No — as I said, calling one part of a sphere "above" and another "below" is no way for a sensible person to talk.

So where do these names come from, and why do we ordinarily apply them to the divisions of the sky? Here's a thought experiment that explains it. Suppose a person stood in the region of the universe assigned to fire — the great mass of fire toward which fiery bodies gather. Suppose he could climb up there, scoop out portions of fire, put them in the pans of a scale, lift the balance, and drag the fire by force into the alien element of air. Clearly, the smaller portion would give in to his force more easily than the larger. When two things are raised at once by a single power, the smaller one resists the pull less, the larger one more — and so we call the larger one heavy and say it tends "downward," and the smaller one light, tending "upward." And we can catch ourselves doing exactly the same thing right here on earth. We often pry apart earthy substances — sometimes earth itself — and drag them by force, against their nature, into the alien element of air, while both cling to their kindred element. The smaller portion yields to our pull more easily than the larger; so we call it light, and the direction we force it we call "above," and the opposite condition and direction we call "heavy" and "below."

These relations necessarily vary from place to place, because the main masses of the elements occupy opposite regions. What's light, heavy, below, or above in one location turns out to be contrary, sideways, and altogether different from what's light, heavy, below, or above in the opposite location. The one rule covering all cases is this: each thing's journey toward its kindred mass is what makes the moving body "heavy," and the place it moves toward "below" — and we give the opposite names to the opposite tendencies. So much for the causes of these conditions.

As for smooth and rough, anyone who sees them can explain them to anyone else: roughness is hardness combined with irregularity; smoothness comes from uniformity combined with density.

The most important matter concerning the body as a whole still remains: the cause of pleasure and pain in the perceptions we've been discussing, and in everything else that's sensed through the body's parts and carries pain and pleasure along with it. Here's how to think about the cause of any sensation, perceived or not. Remember the distinction we drew earlier between what's easy to move and what's hard to move — that's the trail we need to follow to catch our quarry. When a body that's naturally easy to move receives even a slight impression, it spreads the motion outward in a circle, particle passing it to particle, until it reaches the seat of consciousness and reports the quality of whatever caused it. But a body of the opposite, immobile kind doesn't pass the motion along to its surroundings. It simply absorbs the impression without stirring its neighbors, and since the parts never relay the original impact to other parts, the motion never reaches the living creature as a whole, and the impression goes unfelt. This is the case with our bones and hair and the other mostly earthy parts of the body — while the first case applies above all to sight and hearing, because they contain the greatest share of fire and air.

Pleasure and pain, then, should be understood like this. A violent disturbance of our natural state, if it comes suddenly, is painful; the sudden return to the natural state is pleasant; a gentle, gradual change goes unnoticed either way. Meanwhile, impressions that occur most easily and readily are the most distinctly perceived, but involve neither pleasure nor pain — for example, the experiences of the visual ray itself which, as we said earlier, becomes a body united with ours in daylight. Cuttings and burnings and the like don't cause the visual ray pain, nor does its return to its normal state cause pleasure. Instead its perceptions are at their clearest and strongest in proportion to how it's affected and what it meets and touches — there's no violence at all in its contraction or dilation. But bodies made of larger particles surrender to what acts on them only after a struggle, and they pass the motion along to the whole — which is why they feel pleasure and pain: pain when they're pushed out of their natural condition, pleasure when they're restored to it. Things that undergo a slow, gradual draining of their substance but a large, sudden refilling don't notice the emptying but do notice the replenishment — so they bring no pain to the mortal part of the soul, but the greatest pleasure. Sweet scents are the obvious example. But things that are altered suddenly and restored to their nature only slowly and with difficulty produce exactly the opposite effects — as is plain when the body is burned or cut.

So much for the sensations common to the whole body, and the names of the things that produce them. Now I'll try, as best I can, to describe the sensations belonging to particular parts, along with their causes.

First, let me fill in what we left out earlier when discussing juices: the sensations peculiar to the tongue. Like most sensations, these seem to be produced by certain contractions and dilations, but they involve roughness and smoothness more than other sensations do. When earthy particles enter the small vessels of the tongue — its testing instruments, which run to the heart — and strike its moist, tender flesh, they dissolve, and as they dissolve they contract and dry up the little vessels. If they're rougher, the taste is astringent; if less rough, merely sharp. Particles with a scouring action, ones that scrub the whole surface of the tongue, are called bitter when they go too far — when they're corrosive enough to eat away some of the flesh itself, like potash and soda. Particles weaker than these alkalis, scouring only moderately, taste salty — and lacking any bitterness or harshness, they strike us as rather pleasant. Particles that absorb the mouth's warmth and are smoothed by it, that catch fire and in turn scorch what heated them, that are light enough to rise up toward the sensory channels of the head, cutting everything in their path — because of all these properties, they're called pungent.

But sometimes these same particles, already broken down by decomposition, slip into the narrow vessels of the tongue, where they're just the right size relative to the particles of earth and air present there. They set those particles whirling around one another, and as they whirl, they collide, penetrate each other, and form hollow shells around the entering particles — spheres of water wrapped around air (since a film of moisture, sometimes earthy, sometimes pure, spreads itself around the air). The pure, watery ones are transparent, and we call them bubbles; the ones formed of earthy liquid, churning and rising all over, we describe as boiling or fermenting. The cause of all these effects is what we call acid.

And the opposite sensation has an opposite cause: when the entering particles, swimming in the moisture of the mouth, are naturally congenial to the tongue — when they smooth and oil over whatever is rough, relax what is unnaturally contracted, tighten what is unnaturally slack, and settle everything comfortably into its natural state — then that gentle remedy for all the violent sensations is delightful to everyone, and its name is sweet. Enough about that.

The faculty of smell has no fixed varieties. All smells are half-formed things — no element happens to have the proportions required to produce an odor. The vessels of the nose are too narrow to admit earth and water, and too wide to detain fire and air, which is why nobody ever smells any element itself. Smells arise from substances that are dampening, decaying, liquefying, or evaporating — they exist only in that in-between state, as water changes into air or air into water. All smells are either vapor or mist: mist is what's passing from air into water, vapor what's passing from water into air. That's why all smells are thinner than water and thicker than air. The proof: when breathing is blocked and a person forcibly sucks in air, no smell filters through with it — only the air, stripped of odor, gets in. So the varieties of smell have no names; they don't come in many kinds, or in definite, simple ones. The only clear distinction is pleasant versus painful: the painful kind irritates and inflames the whole cavity between the head and the navel, while the pleasant kind soothes that same region and restores it to its agreeable natural state.

The third kind of sense to examine is hearing, and we must explain its causes. In general, let's define sound as a blow delivered by air, through the ears, to the brain and blood, and passed along to the soul; and hearing as the vibration of this blow, beginning in the head and ending in the region of the liver. A swift vibration makes a high sound; a slow one makes a low sound. A regular vibration is even and smooth; an irregular one is harsh. A big vibration is loud; a small one, soft. The harmonies of sounds we'll have to discuss later.

There remains a fourth class of perceptible things, full of intricate varieties, which we must now sort out. They go by the general name of colors: a flame streaming off every body, with particles sized to match the visual ray, so that sight can perceive them. We've already explained how sight arises; this is the natural place to give a reasonable theory of colors.

The particles streaming from other bodies into the visual ray are some smaller, some larger, and some exactly equal to the particles of the ray itself. The equal ones are imperceptible — we call them transparent. The larger ones contract the ray, the smaller ones dilate it, acting on sight much as hot and cold things act on the flesh, or astringent things on the tongue, or the heating things we called pungent. White and black are the same effects of dilation and contraction, just in a different medium, which is why they look so different. So our terms should be: white is what dilates the visual ray; black is the opposite, what contracts it.

Then there's a swifter motion — a different kind of fire that strikes the visual ray and dilates it all the way to the eyes, forcing through the eye's passages and melting them, squeezing out the mixture of fire and water that we call tears. This invading fire meets the inner fire rushing out from the opposite direction: the inner fire flashes out like lightning, while the entering fire is quenched in the moisture, and in that turmoil colors of every kind are produced. We call the experience dazzling, and the object that causes it bright and flashing. Then there's an intermediate kind of fire that reaches the moisture of the eye and mingles with it without flashing; the fire glowing through that moisture produces the color of blood, which we name red. Bright mixed with red and white gives the color auburn. But as for the exact proportions in which these colors are mixed — even if a person knew them, he'd be foolish to say, since he could give no necessary reason, nor even a passably probable account.

Red mixed with black and white makes purple; it becomes umber when the colors are burnt as well as blended and more black is worked in. Flame-color comes from mixing auburn and gray; gray from black and white; pale yellow from white and auburn. White combined with bright, landing on a saturated black, produces dark blue; dark blue mixed with white gives light blue; flame-color with black gives leek-green. From these, the remaining colors and their mixtures can be worked out easily enough by anyone reasoning from probability. But anyone who tried to verify all this by actual experiment would be forgetting the difference between human nature and divine. God has both the knowledge and the power to blend the many into one and dissolve the one back into many. No human being possesses either power — none does now, and none ever will.

These, then, are the elements as necessity shaped them — and these are what the creator of the fairest and best of all created things took up when he brought forth the self-sufficient, most perfect god that is our universe. He used the necessary causes as his servants in the work, but the good in everything he made, he crafted himself. So we should distinguish two kinds of causes: the divine and the necessary. The divine we should seek in all things, as far as our nature allows, for the sake of the blessed life. The necessary we should seek only for the sake of the divine — recognizing that without them, the higher things we're really after can't be grasped or understood or shared in by us in any way at all.

So now the different classes of causes lie ready at hand, like lumber stacked for a carpenter, and from them we must weave together the rest of our account. Let's go back briefly to where we began, and try to bring our story from its beginning to a fitting end.

As I said at the start, everything was originally in disorder, and God introduced into each thing — both within itself and in its relations to everything else — all the measure and harmony it was capable of receiving. Back then, nothing had any proportion except by accident. Nothing that we now have names for — fire, water, and the other elements — even deserved a name yet. The creator first set all of these in order, and out of them he built the universe: a single living creature containing within itself all other living creatures, mortal and immortal alike. The divine creatures he made himself. But the making of mortal beings he handed over to his own offspring, the younger gods. And they, imitating him, took from him the immortal principle of the soul, and around it they built a mortal body to be the soul's vehicle. Inside that body they constructed a second soul of a different kind — a mortal one, subject to terrible and overpowering disturbances. First pleasure, evil's most powerful bait. Then pain, which scares us away from good. Then recklessness and fear, a pair of foolish advisers; anger, which is hard to calm down; and hope, which is easily led astray. They blended all of these with raw, unreasoning sensation and with desire that will dare anything, and according to the laws of necessity, they assembled the human being.

Because of all this, the gods were afraid of contaminating the divine part any more than was absolutely unavoidable. So they gave the mortal soul a separate residence in another part of the body, and built the neck as an isthmus — a boundary between head and chest to keep the two apart. The mortal soul they housed in the chest, in what we call the thorax. And since one part of this mortal soul is nobler and the other baser, they divided the chest cavity into two compartments — the way houses are divided into separate quarters — and installed the midriff as a partition wall between them. The part of the lower soul that has courage and spirit and loves a fight, they settled closer to the head, between the midriff and the neck. That way it would be within earshot of reason, and could join forces with it to hold down the desires whenever they refused to obey the orders coming down from the citadel above.

The heart — the knot where the veins tie together, the fountain of the blood that races through every limb — they stationed in the guardhouse. The idea was this: whenever the spirited part boils over because reason has announced that some wrong is being done — an attack from outside, or treachery from the desires within — then every sensitive part of the body, through all its narrow streets and alleys, would instantly hear those commands and threats, fall in line, and let the best part stay in command of them all. But the gods knew in advance that the pounding of the heart when danger looms, and the hot surge of anger, are caused by fire. So as a support for the heart they devised and implanted the lung — soft, bloodless, and riddled with little hollows like a sponge — so that by taking in breath and drink it could cool the heart, giving it relief and comfort in the heat. That's why they cut the air channels leading to the lung, and set the lung around the heart like a soft cushion: when passion runs high, the heart can pound against something that yields, cool itself down, and suffer less — and so become better able to put its passion at the service of reason.

The part of the soul that craves food and drink and everything else the body needs, they placed between the midriff and the navel. Throughout this whole region they built a kind of feeding trough for the body — and there they chained this part down like a wild animal. Untamed as it is, it has to be kept and fed if the human race is going to exist at all. So they gave it its stall down there, as far as possible from the council chamber, where it could feed at its trough making the least possible noise and disturbance, and leave the best part free to deliberate in peace for the good of the whole.

The gods also knew that this lower principle would never understand reason — and that even if it picked up some dim awareness of rational ideas, it would never naturally care about them, but would be carried away by phantoms and visions, day and night. So God devised a remedy: he constructed the liver and placed it in the lower creature's dwelling. He made it dense, smooth, bright, and sweet, but with a bitter streak in it too — so that the power of thought, streaming down from the mind, could be reflected in it as in a mirror that receives images and gives them back to the eye. When thought wants to terrify the desires, it makes use of the liver's bitter element, which is akin to it. It comes down threatening and invading, spreads that bitterness rapidly through the whole organ, raises bile-like colors, contracts the liver until it's wrinkled and rough, bends the lobe out of its proper position, twists it, blocks and shuts the vessels and passageways — and produces pain and nausea. But the opposite also happens. When some gentle breath of understanding paints images of a kinder sort, it calms the bile and the bitterness — refusing to stir up or even touch the nature opposed to itself — and instead uses the liver's natural sweetness to set everything straight, smooth, and free. It makes the part of the soul that lives around the liver cheerful and content, able to pass the night in peace and to practice divination in sleep — since it has no share in reason or understanding.

For the gods who made us remembered their father's command to make the human race as good as possible, and so, to redeem even our inferior part and give it some contact with truth, they placed the seat of divination in the liver. And here is the proof that God gave the art of divination not to human wisdom but to human foolishness: no one in their right mind achieves genuine prophetic inspiration. The inspired word comes only when a person's intelligence is shackled by sleep, or deranged by illness or some kind of possession. It takes a sane person to understand what was said — in a dream or while awake — by the prophetic, inspired nature; to work out rationally what the visions mean, and what they indicate, for this person or that, about good and evil, past, present, or future. The one still in the grip of the frenzy cannot judge his own visions or his own words. The old saying gets it right: "only the sober-minded can act and judge for themselves about their own affairs." This is why it's customary to appoint interpreters to pass judgment on inspired prophecy. Some people call these interpreters "prophets," not realizing that they are merely expounders of riddling sayings and visions. They shouldn't be called prophets at all — only interpreters of prophecy.

Such, then, is the liver — placed where I've described so that it can give prophetic signals. While a person is alive, these signals are fairly clear; after death the liver goes blind, and its oracles become too obscure to mean anything. The neighboring organ, the spleen, sits on the left side, and its job is to keep the liver bright and clean — like a napkin kept folded and ready beside a mirror, always at hand to wipe it. When impurities build up around the liver because of some bodily disorder, the spleen's loose, porous texture — it's a hollow tissue with no blood of its own — absorbs them all and clears them away. Filled with this waste, it swells and festers; then, when the body is purged, it shrinks back down to its former place, humbled.

So much for the soul: which part of it is mortal and which divine, how and why the two are separated, and where each one lives. Whether we've told the truth here, only God's confirmation could make us certain. But we can say with some confidence that our account is at least probable — and will look more probable still on further investigation. So let that stand.

Next in order comes the creation of the rest of the body, which we can examine in the same spirit. It seems most fitting that the body was framed on the following principles.

The gods who made our race knew we would be undisciplined about food and drink — that out of sheer gluttony we'd take in far more than necessary or proper. To keep disease from destroying us quickly, and to keep our mortal race from dying out before fulfilling its purpose, they took precautions: they made what we call the lower belly as a holding tank for excess food and drink, and they coiled the bowels into their winding loops, so that food couldn't pass straight through and force the body to demand more right away. That would have produced insatiable gluttony, and made the whole human race an enemy of philosophy and culture — deaf and rebellious toward the most divine part within us.

The bones, the flesh, and the other parts like them were made as follows. The starting point for all of them was the formation of marrow. For the bonds of life that tie soul to body are anchored there: marrow is the root and foundation of the human race. The marrow itself was made from special materials. God took those primary triangles that were straightest and smoothest — the ones best suited, by their precision, to produce fire, water, air, and earth — and separated them out from their kinds. Mixing them with each other in due proportion, he made marrow out of them, as a universal seed-stuff for all mortal kind. Then he planted and fastened the souls into this seed, and in the original distribution he gave the marrow exactly as many shapes, and the same varieties, as the kinds of soul it was destined to hold. The portion that was to receive the divine seed, like a field, he made perfectly round, and named that part of the marrow the brain — intending that, once each animal was completed, the vessel holding it would be the head. The portion meant to hold the remaining, mortal part of the soul he divided into shapes both rounded and elongated, and he called all of these "marrow" as well. From these, as from anchors, he ran out the mooring lines of the entire soul, and around them he proceeded to construct our whole body — beginning by building the marrow a complete protective covering of bone.

Bone he composed like this. He sifted out earth that was pure and smooth, kneaded it, and moistened it with marrow. Then he put it into fire, then into water, then back into fire, and again into water — and by transferring it repeatedly from one to the other, he made it insoluble in both. Out of this material he turned, as if on a lathe, a sphere of bone, which he set around the brain, leaving one narrow opening in it. And around the marrow of the neck and the back he formed vertebrae, setting them one beneath another like pivots, running from the head down through the whole trunk. So, to protect the entire seed, he fenced it in a stony enclosure — but he put in joints, using the nature of the Different as an intermediary between the bones, so they would have movement and flexibility.

Then he considered further: bone on its own would be too brittle and stiff, and if it were repeatedly heated and cooled, it would decay and quickly destroy the seed inside it. So he devised sinews and flesh. The sinews would bind all the limbs together — stretching and relaxing around the vertebrae — and let the body bend and straighten. The flesh would be a shield against summer heat and winter cold, and against falls too, yielding to impact softly and gently, like things made of felt. It would hold a warm moisture within itself: in summer this moisture would sweat out, dampen the surface, and spread a natural coolness over the whole body; in winter, the same inner warmth would give decent protection against the frost gripping and attacking from outside. With all this in mind, our modeler mixed earth with fire and water, blended them, added a leavening ferment of acid and salt, and so formed flesh — soft and full of sap. The sinews he made from a mixture of bone and unfermented flesh, tempered into a single substance midway between the two, and he gave them a yellowish color. That's why sinews are firmer and more rubbery than flesh, but softer and moister than bone.

With these materials God wrapped the bones and the marrow — first binding the bones together with sinews, then shrouding the whole assembly in an outer covering of flesh. The bones with the most life and sensation in them he enclosed in the thinnest layers of flesh; the ones with the least life, in the thickest and densest. At the joints, where reason showed that no more was needed, he laid on only a thin coat of flesh, so it wouldn't get in the way of bending and make the body clumsy and hard to move — and also so that thick, packed, compacted flesh wouldn't deaden sensation with its density, impairing memory and dulling the edge of intelligence. That's why the thighs, the shins, the hips, the upper arms and forearms, and all our other jointless parts — and all the inner bones, which contain so little soul in their marrow that they're empty of intelligence — are generously padded with flesh, while the parts that house intelligence carry less. (The exception is where the creator made a part purely of flesh in order to give it sensation — the tongue, for example.) But generally the rule holds: the nature that necessity forces on us as we come into being and grow does not allow dense bone and thick flesh to coexist with keen perception.

If those things could have gone together, the head would have had them more than any other part — and the human race, crowned with a strong, fleshy, sinewy head, would have enjoyed a lifespan twice as long or more, healthier and freer of pain than the life we have now. But the gods who made us weighed the choice: a longer-lived race that was worse, or a shorter-lived race that was better? They concluded that everyone should prefer the shorter and better life to the longer and worse one. So they covered the head with thin bone, but not with flesh and sinews, since the head has no joints. For all these reasons, the head that was attached to the body of every man is more sensitive and intelligent than the rest of him — but far weaker too. On the same principles God set the sinews at the base of the head, in a circle around the neck, welded them there by the principle of likeness, and fastened the ends of the jawbones to them just below the face. The rest of the sinews he distributed throughout the body, binding limb to limb.

Our makers fitted out the mouth as we now have it — with teeth, tongue, and lips — for both the necessary and the best: the way in for what's necessary, the way out for what's best. Whatever enters and feeds the body is necessary. But the stream of speech that flows out of us in the service of intelligence is the fairest and finest of all streams. As for the head, it couldn't be left as bare bone, exposed to the extremes of heat and cold through the seasons; nor could it be muffled in flesh and so made dull and insensitive. So from the flesh, which wasn't entirely used up, a sort of larger rind was peeled away and left over — what we now call skin. Thanks to the moisture around the brain, this skin met and grew together, and wrapped itself around the head like an envelope. The moisture, rising up under the seams of the skull, watered the skin and closed it at the crown, drawing it together in a kind of knot. As for the seams themselves — the sutures — their varied patterns are caused by the action of the soul's revolutions and of nourishment: the more these two forces struggle against each other, the more numerous the sutures; the gentler the struggle, the fewer.

Now the divine power pricked this skin all around with fire, and through the punctures the moisture came welling out. The pure liquid and heat escaped entirely. But the mixed portion — made of the same stuff as the skin itself — was lifted by its own momentum and stretched far outside the head, drawn out as fine as the punctures it passed through. It was too slow to escape, though, so the surrounding air pushed it back; it curled under the skin and took root there. This is how hair sprang up in the skin. It's kindred to skin — a kind of leathery thread — but harder and denser, because as each hair separated from the skin it was compressed and cooled by the chill. This is how our maker gave us a hairy head, using the causes I've described, and reasoning besides that hair, not flesh, was the right covering to protect the brain: a light shield giving shade in summer and shelter in winter, without blocking quickness of perception.

Where sinew, skin, and bone all come together at the fingertips, the blend of the three, when dried, forms a single hard skin sharing the nature of all of them. These were the auxiliary causes that fabricated it — but the truest cause was mind, designing with an eye on the future. For our makers knew that one day women, and animals of every kind, would arise out of men; in particular, they knew that many of these creatures would need claws and nails for many purposes. That's why they sketched in the rudiments of nails in human beings at their very first creation. Such was the reasoning, and such the purposes, for which the gods made skin, hair, and nails grow at the ends of our limbs.

Now all the parts and limbs of the mortal creature had been joined into a whole. But since its life was necessarily spent among fire and air, which melted it down and drained it away, it kept wasting away — so the gods devised a remedy. They blended a nature akin to the human with other shapes and other senses, and so grew a different kind of living thing: the trees, plants, and seeds that cultivation has now tamed and domesticated for our use — though originally only the wild kinds existed, and the wild are older than the cultivated. Anything that has a share in life can rightly be called a living being, and the kind we're discussing here shares in the third type of soul — the one we located between the midriff and the navel — which has no part in opinion, reasoning, or mind, but only in sensations of pleasure and pain, and the appetites that come with them. A plant is entirely passive. Its existence turns inward, around and within itself, repelling motion from outside and using only its own; nature has given it no ability to observe or reflect on its own condition. So it is alive — it really is a living thing — but it stays fixed and rooted in one spot, because it has no power to move itself.

When the higher powers had created all these species to be food for us, the inferior creatures, they cut channels through our body, the way gardeners cut channels through a garden, so that it could be irrigated as if from a running stream. First, they cut two hidden channels — veins — down the back, where skin and flesh meet, one for the right side of the body and one for the left. They ran these down along the spine, with the generative marrow between them, both to keep that marrow thriving and so that the stream, flowing freely downhill from there to the lower parts, would water the body evenly. Next, they split the veins in the region of the head and wove them across each other in opposite directions: the ones from the right side they sent to the left of the body, and the ones from the left to the right. This served two purposes — together with the skin, the veins would form a bond tying the head to the body, since there are no sinews circling the crown; and sensations from each side would be relayed to the whole body.

Next they arranged the body's waterworks in a way I'll now describe — and it will be easier to follow if we first agree on this principle: whatever is made of smaller particles can hold in larger ones, but the larger cannot hold in the smaller. Now of all natures fire has the smallest particles, which is why it passes straight through earth, water, air, and everything compounded from them: nothing can contain it. The same principle applies to the human belly. The belly can hold in food and drink when they enter it, but it cannot hold air and fire, because their particles are smaller than the particles of its own structure.

So God put air and fire to work pumping moisture from the belly into the veins. He wove a kind of basketwork out of fire and air — like a fish-trap, a weel — with two smaller traps at its entrance, one of which was itself double, forking into two openings. From these smaller traps he stretched cords, like reeds, running all around the structure to its far ends. The whole interior of this net he made of fire; the smaller traps and their hollows he made of air. Then he took the net and fitted it over the newly formed creature, like this. The pair of smaller traps he let pass into the mouth. One of them he sent down through the windpipe into the lungs, the other alongside the windpipe into the belly. The first he split into two branches, both of which he brought up to meet at the channels of the nose — so that when the route through the mouth isn't working, all its streams can still be refilled through the nose. The large outer cavity of the trap he wrapped around the hollow parts of the body. And he made the whole system pulse: at one moment everything flows gently inward into the smaller traps — gently, because they are made of air — and at the next moment the smaller traps flow back outward; and the net sinks into the body and rises out again through the porous flesh, while the rays of fire bound up inside it follow the air in and out, never stopping as long as the mortal creature holds together. This process, we say, is what the giver of names called inhaling and exhaling. And the whole performance, active and passive alike, exists so that the body can be watered and cooled, and so be nourished and stay alive. For every time the breath goes in or out, the fire bound up inside follows it; and as it surges perpetually back and forth, it passes through the belly and gets hold of the food and drink there. It dissolves them, cuts them into tiny portions, and drives them along the passages where it travels — pumping them, as from a fountain, into the channels of the veins, and making the bloodstream flow through the body like water through an aqueduct.

But let's look once more at respiration and ask what causes have made it what it is. The explanation goes like this. There's no such thing as a vacuum that a moving body could enter. Our breath moves out of us into the external air. The next step is obvious to anyone: that breath doesn't move into empty space — it shoves the neighboring air out of its place; the displaced air drives out its neighbor in turn; and by this chain of necessity, all the air is driven round to the very place the breath came from, enters the body there, and refills it, following right behind the breath. The whole thing turns like a revolving wheel — because there is no vacuum. That's why the chest and lungs, just as they release breath outward, are refilled again by the air surrounding the body, which slips in through the porous flesh and is driven around the circuit; and then, when that air is sent back out again through the body, it forces breath back inward through the passages of the mouth and nostrils.

As for what starts this motion off, we can suppose the following. In every animal, the hottest region is the one around the blood and the veins — a kind of internal fountain of fire. It's exactly what we compared to the fish-trap's netting: stretched through the middle of the body and woven entirely of fire, while everything outside it is air. Now heat, we must agree, naturally moves outward, toward its own region and its kindred element. And since there are two exits — one out through the body's surface, the other through the mouth and nostrils — whenever the heat rushes toward one exit, it drives the air around at the other; the air driven around falls into the fire and is heated, while the air going out is cooled. Then, as the warmth shifts position and the particles at the other exit grow hotter, the hotter air there inclines that way in turn, streaming toward its kindred element, fire, and pushes the air around at the first exit. The air, undergoing the same thing each time and giving back the same push each time, sets up a continuous oscillation — a circulation swinging back and forth between the two — and this double process is what we call inhaling and exhaling.

The same principle explains a whole family of other phenomena: the medical cupping-glass, the act of swallowing, and the flight of projectiles, whether launched through the air or skimmed along the ground. It explains sounds, too — fast and slow, which strike us as high-pitched and low — sometimes jarring, because the motion they set up in us is irregular, and sometimes harmonious, because the motion is even. What happens with harmony is this: when the motions of the earlier, faster sounds are dying away and have slowed to a matching pace, the slower sounds catch up with them and take over the motion. In overtaking them, they don't jolt them with a new, clashing movement; instead they fit the start of a slower motion onto the fading end of the faster one, matching it as it dies — and so they blend high and low into a single experience. From this blend comes a pleasure even the unmusical can feel, and for the wise a deeper delight: an imitation of divine harmony played out in mortal motion. The same goes for the flow of water, the fall of the thunderbolt, and the supposed marvels of attraction in amber and in the Heraclean stone — the lodestone. There is no real attraction in any of these cases. Investigate properly and you'll find that these "wonders" come down to a combination of ordinary conditions: there is no vacuum; things push each other around in a circle; and as they combine and separate, they all change places, each moving into its proper position. What looks like magic is the interweaving of these effects.

And that, as we've seen, is the nature and the cause of respiration — the topic where this whole discussion began. The fire cuts up the food, and as it follows the breath it surges up inside us; rising together, fire and breath fill the veins, drawing the chopped portions of food up out of the belly and pouring them in. This is how the streams of nourishment are kept flowing through the entire body, in all animals. The fresh-cut particles, coming from substances akin to us — fruits of the earth and plants of the field, which God planted to be our daily food — take on all sorts of colors as they mix together, but red runs through them most of all, a color produced by the cutting action of fire and the imprint it leaves on a moist medium. That's why the liquid circulating through the body has the color we see: we call it blood. It feeds the flesh and the whole body — from it every part is watered, and every emptied place refilled.

Now this refilling and draining works exactly like the universal motion that draws all kindred things toward each other. The elements surrounding us on the outside are constantly dissolving us and dispatching what they take, sending each kind off to join its own kind. Meanwhile the components of the blood, chopped fine inside us and enclosed within the frame of each living creature as if under a private sky, are compelled to copy the motion of the universe: each divided particle within us is carried toward whatever it is akin to, and so refills whatever has been emptied. When more is drained out than flows in, we waste away; when more flows in than is lost, we grow.

When a creature is young, the triangles throughout its frame are brand new — think of it as fresh from the shipyard, like the keel of a vessel just off the blocks. They lock together firmly, and yet the whole structure is soft and tender, because it was only recently formed from marrow and has been raised on milk. Now, when the triangles that make up food and drink enter the body from outside, they are older and weaker than the triangles already in place. So the young body wins the contest: its newer triangles slice up the incoming ones, and the creature grows large, nourished by a steady supply of similar particles.

But over time, after the body's triangles have fought countless battles against countless things, their roots work loose. They can no longer cut up and absorb the food that comes in — instead, they themselves are easily broken apart by the invaders. That's how every creature is overcome and declines, and we call this condition old age. And at last, when the bonds holding the triangles of the marrow together can no longer take the strain of existence and come apart, they release the bonds of the soul in turn. And the soul, set free in the natural way, flies off with joy. For whatever happens according to nature is pleasant, while whatever goes against nature is painful. So a death from disease or wounds is painful and violent, but the death that comes with old age, paying off nature's debt on schedule, is the easiest of all deaths — accompanied more by pleasure than by pain.

Where diseases come from should be obvious to anyone. The body is built out of four substances — earth, fire, water, and air — and disorders and diseases arise when there is an unnatural excess or shortage of any of them, or when one of them migrates from its own proper place to another, or when one of them takes on the wrong variety (since fire and the other elements each come in more than one kind), or anything else of that sort goes wrong. When any element is produced or changed in a way contrary to nature, the parts that were cool grow warm, the dry become moist, the light become heavy, the heavy light — every kind of change occurs. As we keep insisting, a thing can only stay itself, whole and sound, when what is added to it or taken from it is the same kind of thing, applied in the same way and in the right proportion. Whatever comes or goes in violation of these rules produces all manner of changes, and endless diseases and decay.

There is also a second class of natural structures, and these give a second window onto disease for anyone who wants to understand it. Marrow, bone, flesh, and sinew are all composed of the four elements, and so is blood, though it's formed differently. Most diseases arise in the way I just described, but the most serious ones owe their severity to something else: these substances getting generated in the wrong order. When that happens, they are destroyed. The natural order is this: flesh and sinew are made from blood — sinew from the blood's fibers, to which it is related, and flesh from the clots that form when the fibers are separated out. And the sticky, rich substance that comes off the sinews and the flesh not only glues the flesh to the bones, but feeds the bone surrounding the marrow and makes it grow. Meanwhile, because bone is so dense, only the purest, smoothest, oiliest triangles filter through it, dripping like dew from the bones to water the marrow. When each process happens in this order, the usual result is health. When it runs in the opposite order, disease.

For when flesh decomposes and dumps its waste back into the veins, the veins fill with an oversupply of blood of every variety, mixed with air — blood of strange colors and bitter properties, with acid and salty qualities too, containing every sort of bile and serum and phlegm. Everything runs backward. Once corrupted, these substances first ruin the blood itself; then, no longer providing the body any nourishment, they rush through the veins in all directions, abandoning their natural circuits. They are at war with themselves, since they get no good from one another, and they are enemies of the body's settled constitution, which they corrupt and dissolve.

The oldest portion of decomposed flesh is hard to break down. Long exposure to internal burning turns it black; being thoroughly corroded makes it bitter, and it becomes dangerous to every part of the body not yet corrupted. Sometimes, when the bitterness is refined away, the black matter turns acidic instead. Other times, the bitter stuff gets tinged with blood and takes on a redder color — and mixed with black, this turns greenish. And when fresh flesh is decomposed by the fire of the internal flame, a yellowish color blends with the bitter matter. To all of these symptoms, some physician — or more likely some philosopher, someone with the gift of seeing the single common nature in many dissimilar things — gave one shared name: bile. Its various kinds are distinguished by their colors. As for serum: the kind that is just the watery part of blood is harmless, but the kind that is a secretion of black, acid bile turns malignant when heat mixes it with anything salty — that's what we call acid phlegm. Then there's the substance formed when new, tender flesh liquefies in the presence of air. If it gets inflated and enclosed in liquid so that bubbles form — each one invisible because it's so small, but visible in the aggregate — the foam gives it a white color. All this decomposition of tender flesh mixed with air is what we call white phlegm. And the whey or watery residue of freshly formed phlegm is sweat and tears, along with the various daily discharges that cleanse the body. All of these things become causes of disease when the blood is not replenished naturally, from food and drink, but bulks up from the wrong sources, in violation of nature's laws.

When disease breaks down the various parts of the flesh but the foundation underneath holds, the disorder has only half its force, and there's still a good chance of an easy recovery. But consider what binds flesh to bone. When that becomes diseased — when, through bad living, it stops separating cleanly from muscle and sinew, stops nourishing the bone and binding flesh to it, and turns from oily, smooth, and gluey into rough, salty, and dry — then all this corrupted material crumbles away beneath the flesh and sinews and pulls free of the bone. The flesh falls away from its foundation, leaving the sinews bare and soaked in brine, and the flesh itself slides back into the bloodstream, making all the disorders I described before even worse. And severe as these bodily afflictions are, the deeper ones are worse still — as when the bone itself, smothered by flesh too dense to let air through, grows moldy and hot and gangrenes. Receiving no nourishment, it reverses the natural process: the crumbling bone dissolves into the food, the food into the flesh, the flesh falls back into the blood, and every malady becomes more virulent than the ones before. But the worst case of all is when the marrow itself becomes diseased, by excess or by deficiency. This produces the gravest and most deadly disorders of all, since the entire current of the body is thrown into reverse.

A third class of diseases arises in three different ways: from air, from phlegm, and from bile. When the lung, the body's dispenser of air, is blocked by discharges so that its passages aren't clear, air fails to reach some places while too much forces its way into others. The parts left unrefreshed by air rot, while elsewhere the excess air bullies its way through the veins, twisting them, dissolving the body as it goes, and gets trapped in the middle of the body, occupying the midriff. Countless painful diseases result from this, often with heavy sweating. And often, when flesh breaks down inside the body, wind is generated within that cannot escape — and it causes just as much agony as the air that comes in from outside. The worst pain comes when the wind collects around the sinews and the veins of the shoulders and swells them, bending the great tendons and their attached sinews backward. From the intense tension involved, these disorders are named tetanus and opisthotonus. The cure is difficult; in fact, what usually brings relief is the onset of fever.

White phlegm, dangerous when trapped inside because of its air bubbles, is milder if it can vent to the outside of the body — though it then mottles the skin, breeding white leprous eruptions and similar complaints. But when it mixes with black bile and floods the circuits of the head — the most divine part of us — the result is shattering. If the attack comes during sleep, it's less severe; but when it strikes the waking, it is very hard to shake off. And since it is an affliction of our sacred part, it is rightly called the sacred disease. Acid and salty phlegm, in turn, is the source of all the diseases we call catarrhs — though they go by many names, because the places they flow into are many.

All the inflammations of the body — so called from the burning and "flaming" involved — are caused by bile. When bile finds an outlet, it boils up and erupts in all sorts of tumors; when it's locked inside, it generates many inflammatory diseases. The worst case is when it mixes with pure blood and disrupts the fibers there. These fibers are distributed through the blood to keep it balanced between thin and thick — so that it neither gets so liquefied by heat that it leaks out through the body's pores, nor becomes so dense that it can barely creep through the veins. The fibers are made to maintain exactly this balance. Even after death, when the blood is cooling, if you gather the fibers out of it, the remaining blood stays fluid; left alone, the fibers soon make it congeal in the surrounding cold. Since the fibers have this power over blood, consider bile — which is really just stale blood, flesh dissolved back into blood again. When it first seeps in, a little at a time, hot and liquid, the fibers' power congeals it; and as it congeals and is forcibly cooled, it produces inner chill and shivering. But when bile floods in with greater force and overwhelms the fibers with its heat, boiling them into disorder — then, if it's strong enough to hold its supremacy, it penetrates straight to the marrow and burns through what we might call the soul's mooring cables, setting the soul loose. When there's less of it, though, and the body, however wasted, holds out, then the bile itself is defeated. Either it's expelled from the whole body, or it's forced through the veins into the lower or upper belly and driven out of the body like an exile fleeing a city after civil war — producing diarrheas, dysenteries, and every disorder of that kind.

A body sickened mainly by an excess of fire develops continuous heat and fevers. When excess air is the cause, the fever recurs daily. With water — a more sluggish element than fire or air — the fever returns every third day. And with earth, the most sluggish of the four, which is purged on a fourfold cycle, the result is a fever returning every fourth day — the quartan — which is very hard to shake.

So much for how diseases of the body arise. The disorders of the soul that depend on the body come about like this. We have to agree that disease of the mind is a deficiency of intelligence, and that it comes in two kinds: madness and ignorance. Whatever condition brings on either one deserves to be called disease — and excessive pleasures and pains should be ranked among the greatest diseases the soul can suffer. A person in the grip of overwhelming joy or overwhelming pain, frantically rushing to seize the one or escape the other, can't see or hear anything correctly. He is, at that moment, raving — utterly incapable of using reason at all.

Take a man whose seed runs too plentiful and overflowing around the spinal marrow, like a tree weighed down with too much fruit. He suffers many torments and grabs at many pleasures, in his desires and in what comes of them, and spends most of his life deranged, because his pleasures and pains are so extreme. His soul is made foolish and disordered by his body — and yet people treat him not as sick but as willfully bad. That is a mistake. The truth is that sexual excess is a disease of the soul, caused mostly by the moisture and fluidity that one element produces in us when the bones are loosely knit. And in general, nearly everything labeled "lack of self-control over pleasure," and treated as a disgrace — as though the wicked do wrong on purpose — is not really fair grounds for reproach. No one is willingly bad. The bad become bad through some defective condition of the body and a bad upbringing — things every man hates, and which happen to him against his will.

The same goes for pain: there too, the soul takes great damage from the body. When acid and salty phlegms, or bitter, bilious humors, roam through the body and find no exit, no escape, they get pent up inside, blending their own vapors with the motions of the soul. Mixed in this way, they produce diseases of the soul in every variety and intensity. Carried to the three seats of the soul, they create — depending on which region they attack — endless varieties of bad temper and gloom, of recklessness and cowardice, of forgetfulness and dullness.

And on top of this: when people with constitutions this bad live under bad forms of government, where the talk in public and private is equally bad, and when nothing is done in their youth, through education, to cure these evils — that is how all of us who are bad become bad, through two causes entirely beyond our control. The blame belongs to the planters rather than the plants, to the educators rather than the educated. Still, we should make every effort — through upbringing, through pursuits, through study — to flee vice and seize its opposite. But that belongs to another discussion.

It's now fitting and proper to turn the subject around and ask about the corresponding treatments — what keeps the body and mind sound. After all, it's more right to talk about the good than the bad. Everything good is beautiful, and the beautiful is never out of proportion. So a living creature that is going to be beautiful must have proportion. Now, we notice the minor proportions and reason about them, but we completely overlook the greatest and most important ones. For health and disease, virtue and vice, no proportion or disproportion matters more than the one between soul and body. Yet we never look at this. We never reflect that when a mighty soul rides in a frame too weak and small for it — or the reverse, when a little soul is housed in a big body — the creature as a whole is not beautiful, because it lacks the most important proportion of all. The right balance of mind and body, to anyone with eyes to see it, is the most beautiful and lovely of all sights.

Think of a body with one leg too long, or lopsided in some other way. It's unpleasant to look at; and when it's put to work, it strains and lurches, stumbles through its own awkwardness, and brings endless trouble on itself. We should picture the two-natured compound we call a living being the same way. When the soul in this partnership is more powerful than the body and burns hot, it shakes the whole inner man and fills him with disorders. When it throws itself into intense study and research, it wastes the body away. When it teaches or argues, in public or in private, through rivalries and disputes, it inflames the body's frame, shakes it loose, and brings on discharges — which fools most so-called doctors into blaming the very opposite of the true cause. And the reverse happens too: when a large body, too strong for the soul, is yoked to a small, feeble mind. Human beings have two natural appetites — the body's appetite for food, and the appetite of the divinest part of us for wisdom. The motions of the stronger part prevail and promote their own interest, but they render the soul dull, slow, and forgetful, producing ignorance, the greatest disease of all.

There is one protection against both kinds of imbalance: don't exercise the body without the soul, or the soul without the body. That way each keeps the other in check, and both stay balanced and healthy. So the mathematician, or anyone deeply absorbed in some intellectual pursuit, must give his body its workout too, and take up physical training; and the person devoted to sculpting his body must give the soul its own exercise in return, taking up music and philosophy in all its forms — if he wants to deserve, with any justice, the names beautiful and good.

The parts of the body deserve the same kind of care, on the model of the universe itself. The body is heated and cooled from within by what enters it, dried and moistened by what surrounds it, and suffers all the disturbances both kinds of motion bring. If a person surrenders his body, motionless, to these forces, it is overpowered and destroyed. But suppose instead he imitates what we earlier called the foster-mother and nurse of the universe: he never lets the body rest idle, but keeps it in motion, giving it regular shakings throughout, which form its natural defense against disturbances inner and outer. By moderate exercise he sorts the particles and disturbances wandering through the body, settling them into order with their natural kin — just as we described when speaking of the universe. Then he won't leave enemy stationed beside enemy, breeding wars and disease in the body; he'll settle friend beside friend, and the result is health.

Among motions, the best is the motion a thing produces in itself — for it is most akin to the motion of thought and the motion of the universe. Motion produced by something else is inferior. Worst of all is motion that, while the body lies inert, moves only its parts, and only by outside agency. So of all the ways of cleansing and toning the body, the best is gymnastic exercise. Second best is the swaying motion of a boat, or any form of transportation that doesn't tire you. The third kind may be useful when you're absolutely desperate, but no sensible person would accept it otherwise: I mean the doctor's purgative drugs. Diseases, unless they're truly dangerous, should not be provoked with medicines. Every disease has a structure that resembles, in a way, the nature of a living creature — and the living creature is put together with a fixed term of life. Not just for the species as a whole, but for each individual: every one of us is born with his lifespan set by fate, accidents aside, because our triangles are framed from the start with the power to hold together for just so long, and no man can ever prolong his life beyond it. The structure of diseases works the same way. If you fight one with drugs before its destined time has run, you tend to turn mild diseases severe, and a few into many. So you should manage all of them, as far as your free time allows, by diet and regimen, rather than provoking a stubborn enemy with drugs.

Let that be enough about the composite creature, and about its bodily part, and how a man may train himself and be trained to live the most rational life. But first and above all, we must prepare the part that's going to do the training, making it as fine and as fit for the job as possible. To work through this in detail would be a serious task in itself; but treated in passing, as before, the matter can fairly be summed up like this.

I have said more than once that three kinds of soul are housed within us, each with its own motions. So now let me say, as briefly as I can: whichever of them sits idle, with its motions stilled, inevitably becomes weakest, while the one that's exercised becomes strongest. So we must take care that their motions stay in proportion to one another.

As for the most sovereign kind of soul in us, think of it this way: God gave it to each of us as a guiding spirit — the part that we say lives at the top of the body and lifts us up from earth toward our kindred in heaven. For we are a plant rooted not in earth but in heaven, and that is no mere figure of speech. From the place where the soul first came to be, the divine power keeps our head — our root — suspended, and so holds the whole body upright.

Now, if a man devotes himself to appetite and ambition and labors endlessly to feed them, all his thoughts must become mortal; he ends up mortal through and through, as far as that's possible, because that's the part of himself he has nourished. But the man who has devoted himself to the love of learning and to true wisdom, and has exercised that part of himself above all, must — if he touches truth — think thoughts immortal and divine. So far as human nature can partake of immortality, he will not fall short of it in any way; and because he constantly tends the divine element, keeping the guardian spirit that lives within him in good order, he must be happy above all men.

Now, there is only one way to care for anything: give it the nourishment and the motions that are proper to it. And the motions akin to the divine part within us are the thoughts and revolutions of the universe. These every man should follow. By learning the harmonies and revolutions of the universe, he should correct the circuits in his own head, which were knocked off course at birth — bringing the thinking part into likeness with what it thinks about, restoring its original nature. And having achieved that likeness, he attains the goal of the best life the gods have set before mankind, both for now and for all time to come.

And with that, the task we took on at the start — to tell the story of the universe down to the creation of man — is just about complete. The origin of the other animals can be covered briefly; there's no need to go on at length, and brevity here will keep our whole account in better proportion. So let it be told as follows.

Of the men who came into the world, those who were cowards, or who lived unjust lives, were — on the most plausible account — reborn as women in the second generation. And this is when the gods devised the desire for sexual union, constructing one living thing in us men and another in women. They made each as follows. There is a channel for drink, where liquid passes through the lungs, under the kidneys, and into the bladder, which collects it and expels it under air pressure. The gods bored a connection from this channel into the column of marrow that runs from the head down through the neck and along the spine — the marrow which, earlier in our account, we called "seed." This seed, being alive and now given an outlet to breathe through, planted in that very outlet a living thirst for emission, and so produced our desire to procreate. That is why a man's generative organs are unruly and self-willed, like an animal deaf to reason, and try, in their stinging frenzy of lust, to carry all before them. In women, for the same reasons, what is called the womb or matrix is a living creature inside them with a desire for childbearing. When it stays barren long past its proper season, it grows resentful and angry, and wandering all through the body, it blocks the channels of breath and prevents respiration, driving the woman to extremes of distress and causing all kinds of disease — until at last the desire of the man and the longing of the woman bring the two together. And then, as if plucking fruit from a tree, they sow into the womb, as into plowed soil, living things too small to see and still unformed; these take shape inside, are nourished until they're matured, and are finally brought out into the light of day. So the generation of living creatures is accomplished.

That is how women, and the female sex in general, came to be. As for birds: they were remade — sprouting feathers instead of hair — from harmless but light-headed men, men interested in the heavens but simple-minded enough to think that sight provides the surest proof of things above. Wild land animals came from men who never used philosophy at all and never once wondered about the nature of the heavens, because they had stopped using the circuits in the head and followed instead the parts of the soul that live in the chest. Thanks to these habits, their forelimbs and heads were dragged down to the earth by natural kinship and came to rest there, and their skulls were stretched into all sorts of elongated shapes, wherever each man's circuits of soul had been crushed out of shape by idleness. This is why their kind was created four-footed and many-footed: God gave the more mindless ones more points of support, to bind them that much closer to the ground. And the most mindless of all, whose entire bodies trail along the earth, no longer had any need of feet, so the gods made them footless, to crawl on the ground. The fourth kind, the water animals, came from the stupidest and most ignorant men of all. The gods who were reshaping them judged these souls no longer even worthy of breathing pure air, because all their transgressions had fouled the soul within them. Instead of breathing the fine, clean air, they were pushed down to inhale the deep and murky water. From them came the tribe of fish and shellfish and every creature of the water, which have been assigned the most remote dwellings of all as punishment for the most extreme ignorance. These, then, are the laws by which all animals, then and now, pass into one another — transformed as they lose or gain intelligence and folly.

And now we may say that our account of the universe has reached its end. For this world has received mortal and immortal living creatures and been filled full of them, and has itself become a visible living creature embracing all things visible — an image of the intelligible, a perceptible god, greatest and best, most beautiful and most perfect: this one heaven, the only one of its kind that is, or ever will be.
