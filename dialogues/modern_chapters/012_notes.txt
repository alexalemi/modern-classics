Phaedrus Part 1 -- Translation Notes
=======================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. THE PASTORAL SETTING: The walk to the Ilissus, the wading, the discovery
   of the plane tree, Socrates' rapturous description of the spot -- this is
   given full space to breathe. The sensory details (cool water, cicada song,
   soft grass, chaste tree blossoms) are sharpened and made vivid. This is
   the only dialogue set outdoors, and the setting is integral to the themes
   of inspiration, nature, and divine frenzy.

2. LYSIAS' SPEECH: Deliberately kept as a competent but hollow performance.
   The arguments are there -- they're clever, they score points -- but the
   speech is repetitive, list-like, and emotionally dead. This is intentional:
   Plato wants us to feel that something is wrong with this purely rational
   approach to love, even before Socrates says so. The modern version preserves
   the surface polish while letting the emptiness show through. The speech
   sounds like a skilled lawyer making a case, not a human being talking about
   love.

3. SOCRATES' FIRST SPEECH (the covered-face speech): This is deeply ironic.
   Socrates covers his face "in shame" and claims to be improvising, but he
   delivers a devastating, systematic takedown of the lover that's better
   argued than Lysias'. The speech is clearly superior in logic and structure
   -- precisely the point. Even Socrates' "bad" speech outshines Lysias' best
   effort. The tone is mock-serious: he's performing, and he knows it.
   Key details preserved:
   - The definition of love as desire overcoming reason (the etymological
     play on eros/erromenos)
   - The lover as jailer, keeping the beloved ignorant
   - The brutal physical portrait of the aging lover
   - The "wolves love lambs" climax
   - The abrupt, slightly panicked ending

4. THE PIVOT: Socrates' divine sign (daimonion) stops him from crossing
   the river. He realizes both speeches slandered Love, a god, and resolves
   to recant. The Stesichorus story (losing and regaining sight) is told
   crisply. This moment is crucial: it shifts the dialogue from clever
   rhetoric to genuine philosophy, from head to heart.

5. THE GREAT SPEECH -- Four Kinds of Divine Madness:
   - Prophetic madness (Delphi, Dodona)
   - Ritual/purifying madness (healing hereditary curses)
   - Poetic madness (the Muses)
   - Erotic madness (love) -- the greatest of all
   The etymological argument (mantike/manike) is kept but explained more
   clearly. The point lands: inspired madness outranks sober sanity.

6. THE SOUL'S IMMORTALITY: The argument from self-motion is preserved
   step by step but broken into shorter, clearer logical steps. The
   key chain: self-moving = never ceases = immortal = cannot be created
   or destroyed = the origin of all motion = the soul.

7. THE CHARIOT ALLEGORY: This is the centerpiece. The image is introduced
   simply and powerfully: "Picture the soul as a charioteer driving a team
   of two winged horses." The gods' chariots are noble throughout; the human
   chariot is a mixed team -- one horse good, one bad. This sets up the
   drama of the celestial procession. The language is kept vivid and
   kinetic: struggling, crashing, trampling, sweating, wings breaking.

8. THE CELESTIAL PROCESSION: Zeus leads. The gods glide smoothly to the
   rim of heaven. The human souls struggle desperately. The vision beyond
   the heavens -- "the heaven above the heavens" -- is rendered with
   appropriate awe: colorless, formless, intangible Reality, visible only
   to the mind. The feeding on Truth. The return. The ambrosia. This
   passage should feel like a glimpse of something genuinely transcendent.

9. THE HIERARCHY OF SOULS: The nine-tier reincarnation ranking is kept
   complete (philosopher down to tyrant). The 10,000-year cycle, the
   philosopher's shortcut (3,000 years), the judgment -- all preserved.
   The key philosophical point is highlighted: being human requires the
   ability to reason from particulars to universals, and this ability IS
   recollection of the Forms.

10. THE EXPERIENCE OF FALLING IN LOVE: This passage is extraordinary
    and given its full weight. The physiological/spiritual description:
    the shudder, the warmth, the melting, the swelling of the wings
    like teeth coming in, the agony of separation, the relief of reunion.
    Plato is describing the overwhelming, disorienting experience of
    falling in love with absolute honesty and precision. The modern
    version preserves the physicality (throbbing, pulsing, aching,
    flooding) while keeping the language just elevated enough to
    convey that this is a spiritual event as much as a bodily one.

11. BEAUTY'S PRIVILEGE: The argument that Beauty alone, among all the
    Forms, is directly perceptible through the senses (specifically
    sight) -- this is stated clearly. Justice and Wisdom have no
    visible radiance in their earthly copies. But Beauty does. This
    is why love is the gateway to philosophy.

12. THE CUT-OFF: The chapter ends mid-sentence, matching the source
    text split. The discussion of how different souls follow different
    gods (Zeus-followers seek philosophical natures, Hera-followers
    seek royal natures, etc.) is just beginning. Chapter 013 will
    continue with the detailed chariot scene (the two horses' struggle
    when confronted with the beloved), the account of mutual love,
    and the transition to the discussion of rhetoric.


SPECIFIC VOCABULARY DECISIONS
-------------------------------

- "by the dog" -- kept as is; Socrates' characteristic oath
- "by Hera" -- kept as is
- "daimonion" / "divine sign" -- rendered as "my sign" and "the divine
  voice" to make clear this is Socrates' famous inner voice
- "dithyrambics" -- rendered as "rapturous mode" and "full-blown epic
  poetry" (more accessible than the technical term)
- "the Forms" -- used when discussing true Reality beyond the heavens
- "mantike/manike" -- the etymological play is explained in context
  rather than leaving the Greek untranslated
- "himeros" -- the etymological connection to "flow" is explained
  inline: "which is why the Greeks call it himeros, desire, from
  the word for 'flow'"
- "Stesichorus" -- kept with the etymological joke about his name
  ("son of Godly Man, from the city of Desire") intact
- Orithyia/Boreas myth -- kept as natural conversation
- "ceteris paribus" -- rendered as "all else being equal"
- "tu quoque" -- dropped; the meaning is conveyed naturally


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (PHAEDRUS PART 2)
------------------------------------------------------

1. VOICE: Socrates' Great Speech is in full flight and continues into
   ch. 013 with the detailed chariot scene. The elevated, inspired
   register should be maintained through the completion of the speech.
   After the speech ends, the tone shifts to a more relaxed, analytical
   discussion of rhetoric and writing.

2. THE CHARIOT ALLEGORY continues: Ch. 013 opens with the followers
   of Apollo and other gods, then gives the detailed, visceral
   description of the two horses struggling when confronted with a
   beautiful person. The white horse (noble) is controlled by word
   alone; the dark horse (base) fights the bit, lunges forward,
   must be physically beaten into submission. This scene should be
   as vivid and violent as a horse-breaking.

3. MUTUAL LOVE: The account of how love is reflected back (Anteros,
   "counter-love") -- the beloved seeing himself mirrored in the
   lover, the stream of beauty flowing back through the eyes -- is
   delicate and should be handled with care.

4. THE RHETORIC DISCUSSION: After the speech, the dialogue shifts
   entirely to a discussion of what constitutes good rhetoric vs.
   bad rhetoric. Lysias' speech becomes the case study for bad
   rhetoric. Socrates' approach -- start with definitions, organize
   logically, know the nature of the soul you're trying to persuade
   -- becomes the standard. The tone is lighter, more analytical.

5. THE CRITIQUE OF WRITING: The famous "Allegory of Theuth" --
   the Egyptian god who invents writing and is told by King Thamus
   that writing will produce forgetfulness, not memory. This is
   deeply ironic: Plato writing about the inferiority of writing.
   The irony should be palpable.

6. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "the Forms" for true Reality
   - "the charioteer" for the rational part of the soul
   - "the good horse" / "the bad horse" for the two appetitive parts
   - "divine madness" for theia mania
   - "recantation" for the palinode
   - Contractions throughout for natural speech
   - Speaker labels on their own lines

7. TONE SHIFT: The chapter will need to transition from the sublime
   heights of the chariot allegory to a practical, almost technical
   discussion of rhetoric. Socrates returns to his normal conversational
   voice. The outdoor setting should continue to be felt (the noon
   heat, the cicadas, the shade).
