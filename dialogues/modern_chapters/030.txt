CRATYLUS (Part 1)

Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates
  Hermogenes
  Cratylus


Hermogenes
How about we bring Socrates into our argument?

Cratylus
Sure, if you'd like.

Hermogenes
I should explain what's going on, Socrates. Our friend Cratylus here has been arguing about names. He says they're natural, not conventional -- not just arbitrary sounds that people agree to use, but that there's a truth or correctness built into them, and it's the same for Greeks and non-Greeks alike. So I asked him whether his own name, "Cratylus," is a true name, and he says yes. "And Socrates?" "Yes." So I said, "Then every person's name is simply whatever they're called." And he replies, "If the whole world called you Hermogenes, that still wouldn't be your name." When I press him for an explanation, he gets all ironic and mysterious, like he's got some private theory that he could share if he wanted to -- and if he did, he'd completely convince me. So tell me, Socrates, what does this oracle of his mean? Or better yet, tell me your own view on whether names have a natural correctness. I'd much rather hear that.

Socrates
Son of Hipponicus, there's an old saying: "The knowledge of the good is hard to come by." And the knowledge of names is a big part of knowledge. If I weren't poor, I could have taken the fifty-drachma course from the great Prodicus -- the one that's supposed to be a complete education in grammar and language (his own words). Then I'd be able to answer your question about the correctness of names right away. But I've only taken the one-drachma course, so I don't actually know the truth about these things. Still, I'm happy to investigate with you and Cratylus. As for his claim that your name isn't really Hermogenes -- I suspect he's just teasing you. The name "Hermogenes" means "born of Hermes," the god of profit, and he's joking that you don't live up to it because you're always chasing after money but never having any luck. But as I was saying, this kind of knowledge is genuinely difficult, so we'd better leave the question open until we've heard both sides.

Hermogenes
I've talked this over many times, both with Cratylus and others, and I can't convince myself that there's any principle of correctness in names other than convention and agreement. In my view, whatever name you give something is the right one. And if you change it and give another, the new name is just as correct as the old one. We change the names of our slaves all the time, and the new name works just as well. No name is given to anything by nature -- it's all convention and the habits of the people who use them. That's what I think. But if I'm wrong, I'd be happy to learn from Cratylus or anyone else.

Socrates
You may well be right, Hermogenes. Let's see. So your view is that the name of each thing is simply whatever anyone agrees to call it?

Hermogenes
That's my position.

Socrates
Whether the name-giver is an individual person or a whole city?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
All right, let me try an example. Suppose I call a man "horse" and a horse "man." You're saying the man would be correctly called "horse" by me as an individual, while correctly called "man" by the rest of the world -- and the horse would correctly be called "man" by me and "horse" by everyone else? Is that what you mean?

Hermogenes
According to my view, yes.

Socrates
But what about truth? You'd agree that words can be true or false?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
And there are true and false propositions?

Hermogenes
Of course.

Socrates
And a true proposition states what is, and a false proposition states what is not?

Hermogenes
Yes -- what other answer could there be?

Socrates
So within a proposition, there's truth and falsehood?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
But is a proposition only true as a whole, or are its parts true too?

Hermogenes
No, the parts are true as well as the whole.

Socrates
Just the large parts, or every part?

Hermogenes
Every part, I'd say.

Socrates
And is there any part of a proposition smaller than a name?

Hermogenes
No -- that's the smallest unit.

Socrates
Then a name is a part of a true proposition?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And a true part, as you said.

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And isn't a part of a falsehood also false?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
Then if propositions can be true and false, names can be true and false too?

Hermogenes
So it would seem.

Socrates
And the name of anything is whatever anyone says it is?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And will there be as many names for each thing as there are people naming it? And will all of them be true names at the moment they're spoken?

Hermogenes
Yes, Socrates. I can't see any other standard of correctness. You give one name, I give another. In different cities and countries there are different names for the same things. Greeks differ from non-Greeks, and different Greek communities differ from each other.

Socrates
But tell me, Hermogenes -- do the things themselves differ the way the names differ? Are they relative to individuals, as Protagoras says? He claims that "man is the measure of all things" -- that things are to me as they appear to me, and to you as they appear to you. Do you agree with him, or would you say that things have a permanent nature of their own?

Hermogenes
There have been times, Socrates, when I've been so confused that I've taken refuge in Protagoras's view. Not that I really agree with him.

Socrates
Wait -- have you ever been driven to admit that there's no such thing as a bad person?

Hermogenes
No, definitely not. But I've often had reason to think there are plenty of very bad people.

Socrates
And have you ever come across any very good ones?

Hermogenes
Not many.

Socrates
But you have found some?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And would you say that the very good are the very wise, and the very bad are the very foolish?

Hermogenes
I would.

Socrates
But if Protagoras is right, and the truth is just whatever appears to each person, then how can some of us be wise and some foolish?

Hermogenes
That would be impossible.

Socrates
And on the other hand, if wisdom and foolishness really are distinguishable, then I think you'd agree that Protagoras's claim can hardly be correct. Because if what appears to each person is true for them, no one can actually be wiser than anyone else.

Hermogenes
They can't.

Socrates
And you wouldn't want to go along with Euthydemus either, who says that all things belong equally to all people at the same time and always. Because on that view there can't be good people and bad people -- not if virtue and vice belong to everyone equally.

Hermogenes
No, there can't.

Socrates
So if neither view is right -- if things aren't just relative to individuals, and all things don't belong equally to everyone -- then things must have their own proper and permanent nature. They aren't pushed around by us, fluctuating according to our whims. They're independent, and they maintain their own essential nature as prescribed by how things actually are.

Hermogenes
I think, Socrates, that you've hit on the truth.

Socrates
And does this apply only to things themselves, or equally to actions? Aren't actions also a kind of reality?

Hermogenes
Yes, actions are real too.

Socrates
Then actions are also performed according to their own nature, and not according to whatever we happen to think about them? Take cutting, for example. We don't cut however we please, with any random instrument. We cut with the right instrument, and according to the natural process of cutting. The natural process works. Any other way will fail and be useless.

Hermogenes
I'd say the natural way is the right way.

Socrates
Same with burning. Not every approach works -- the right way is the natural way, with the natural instrument.

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
And this holds for all actions?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And speaking is a kind of action?

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
So will a person speak correctly just by speaking however they please? Won't the successful speaker be the one who speaks in the natural way, as things ought to be spoken, and with the natural instrument? Any other approach will result in error and failure.

Hermogenes
I quite agree with you.

Socrates
And isn't naming a part of speaking? When we give names, we're speaking.

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
And if speaking is a kind of action that relates to real things, then isn't naming also a kind of action?

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
And we established that actions aren't relative to ourselves but have a nature of their own?

Hermogenes
Precisely.

Socrates
Then the argument leads us to conclude that names should be given according to a natural process and with the proper instrument, not at our pleasure. That's the only way we'll name things successfully.

Hermogenes
I agree.

Socrates
Now, whatever needs to be cut has to be cut with something?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And whatever needs to be woven or pierced has to be woven or pierced with something?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
And whatever needs to be named has to be named with something?

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
What do we pierce with?

Hermogenes
An awl.

Socrates
And what do we weave with?

Hermogenes
A shuttle.

Socrates
And what do we name with?

Hermogenes
A name.

Socrates
Very good. So a name is an instrument?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
Suppose I ask, "What kind of instrument is a shuttle?" And you answer, "A weaving instrument."

Hermogenes
Right.

Socrates
And if I ask, "What do we do when we weave?" the answer is that we separate the warp from the woof.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
And the same kind of description could be given of an awl or any other instrument?

Hermogenes
Of course.

Socrates
So now let me ask a similar question about names. If a name is an instrument, what do we do when we name things?

Hermogenes
I can't say.

Socrates
Don't we communicate information to each other, and distinguish things according to their natures?

Hermogenes
Yes, we certainly do.

Socrates
Then a name is an instrument for teaching and for distinguishing natures -- just as a shuttle is an instrument for distinguishing the threads of a web.

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And the shuttle is the weaver's instrument?

Hermogenes
Absolutely.

Socrates
So the weaver will use the shuttle well -- meaning like a weaver? And the teacher will use names well -- meaning like a teacher?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And when the weaver uses the shuttle, whose craftsmanship is he using?

Hermogenes
The carpenter's.

Socrates
And is every person a carpenter, or only the skilled ones?

Hermogenes
Only the skilled.

Socrates
And when someone uses the awl, whose work are they using?

Hermogenes
The smith's.

Socrates
And is every person a smith, or only the skilled ones?

Hermogenes
Only the skilled.

Socrates
And when the teacher uses a name, whose work is he using?

Hermogenes
Now I'm stumped again.

Socrates
Can you at least tell me who gives us the names we use?

Hermogenes
I really can't.

Socrates
Doesn't it seem like the law gives them to us?

Hermogenes
Yes, I suppose so.

Socrates
So the teacher, when he uses a name, is using the work of the legislator?

Hermogenes
I agree.

Socrates
And is every person a legislator, or only the skilled ones?

Hermogenes
Only the skilled.

Socrates
Then not just anyone can give a name, Hermogenes. Only a name-maker can do it -- and that's the legislator, who is the rarest of all skilled craftsmen.

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
And how does the legislator make names? What does he look to? Think about it in light of our previous examples. What does the carpenter look to when making a shuttle? Doesn't he look to whatever is naturally fitted to work as a shuttle?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
And suppose the shuttle breaks while he's making it. Will he make another one by looking at the broken one? Or will he look to the ideal form that he was working from?

Hermogenes
The ideal form, I'd imagine.

Socrates
And wouldn't we be right to call that the true or ideal shuttle?

Hermogenes
I think so.

Socrates
So whatever kind of shuttle is needed -- for manufacturing thin garments or thick ones, linen or wool or any other material -- each one should have the true form of a shuttle. And the maker should produce, in each case, the form that's best suited to that particular kind of work.

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And the same goes for other instruments. When a craftsman has figured out the instrument naturally suited to each job, he has to express that natural form in whatever material he's working with. He doesn't get to make up his own design. For instance, the smith should know how to put into iron the forms of awls suited by nature to their various uses.

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
And the carpenter should know how to put into wood the forms of shuttles suited by nature to their uses?

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
Because the different forms of shuttles naturally correspond to different kinds of weaving. And this is true of instruments in general.

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
Then our legislator must also know how to put the true natural name of each thing into sounds and syllables, and to make and give all names with a view to the ideal name -- if he's going to be a real name-maker. And we should remember that different legislators won't use the same syllables, just as different smiths don't use the same iron to make the same instrument for the same purpose. The form must be the same, but the material can vary, and the instrument will be equally good regardless of what iron it's made from, whether in Greece or abroad. The material doesn't matter.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
And the legislator, whether Greek or non-Greek, shouldn't be considered worse as long as he gives the true and proper form of the name, whatever syllables he uses. The country doesn't matter.

Hermogenes
Quite true.

Socrates
But who decides whether the proper form has been given to the shuttle, regardless of what wood it's made from? The carpenter who makes it, or the weaver who uses it?

Hermogenes
I'd say the one who uses it, Socrates.

Socrates
And who uses the work of the lyre-maker? Won't it be the person who knows how to direct the work being done and can tell whether it's been done well or not?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
And who is that?

Hermogenes
The lyre-player.

Socrates
And who will direct the shipwright?

Hermogenes
The pilot.

Socrates
And who is best able to direct the legislator in his work and judge whether the job has been done well, whether here or in any other country? Won't it be the person who actually uses the product?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And that's the person who knows how to ask questions?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And how to answer them?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And someone who knows how to ask and answer -- you'd call them a dialectician?

Hermogenes
Yes, that would be the right term.

Socrates
So the carpenter's job is to make a rudder, and the pilot directs him, if the rudder is to be well made.

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
And the legislator's job is to give names, and the dialectician must be his director, if the names are to be given correctly?

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
Then the giving of names is hardly the trivial matter you think it is, Hermogenes, or the work of trivial or random people. Cratylus is right when he says that things have names by nature, and that not just anyone can be a maker of names -- only someone who looks to the name that each thing naturally has and is able to express the true forms of things in letters and syllables.

Hermogenes
I can't really argue with you, Socrates, but I'm having trouble changing my mind all at once. I think I'd be more easily convinced if you could show me what you mean by this "natural fitness" of names.

Socrates
My good Hermogenes, I have nothing to show. Didn't I just tell you -- though maybe you've forgotten -- that I don't know anything about this, and I was only proposing to investigate it together with you? But now that we've been talking, we've made some progress. We've discovered that names do have a natural truth to them, and that not just anyone knows how to give a thing its name.

Hermogenes
Very good.

Socrates
And what is the nature of this truth or correctness in names? That's the next question, if you want to pursue it.

Hermogenes
I certainly do.

Socrates
Then think about it.

Hermogenes
How should I think about it?

Socrates
The best way is to get help from experts, and you'd have to pay them well -- both in money and in gratitude. Those experts would be the Sophists, from whom your brother Callias has -- at considerable expense -- bought a reputation for wisdom. But since you haven't come into your inheritance yet, you'd better go beg him to share what he learned from Protagoras about the fitness of names.

Hermogenes
But that would be totally inconsistent of me! I just rejected Protagoras and his "truth" doctrine, so how could I then treat what he says about names as valuable?

Socrates
Well, if you won't have Protagoras, you'll have to learn from Homer and the other poets.

Hermogenes
And where does Homer say anything about names? What does he say?

Socrates
He talks about them frequently -- notably and impressively in the passages where he distinguishes between the names that gods give to things and the names that humans give. Doesn't he make a remarkable claim about the correctness of names in those passages? Because the gods surely must call things by their right and natural names, don't you think?

Hermogenes
Well, of course, if they call them anything at all, they'd call them correctly. But what passages are you talking about?

Socrates
Do you know what he says about the river in Troy that fought in single combat against Hephaestus? "The gods call it Xanthus," he says, "and men call it Scamander."

Hermogenes
I remember.

Socrates
Well then -- isn't it a significant lesson to learn that the river ought to be called Xanthus rather than Scamander? Or take the bird that, as Homer says, "the gods call Chalcis, and men call Cymindis." Isn't it worth knowing that Chalcis is the more correct name? Or the hill called Batieia by humans and Myrina by the gods? There are many similar observations in Homer and other poets. But those examples might be above our pay grade. The names of Hector's son -- Scamandrius and Astyanax, both of which Homer says were given to the boy -- are more within our range. I think we can figure out what the poet means by "correctness" from that example. You remember the lines I'm referring to?

Hermogenes
I do.

Socrates
Let me ask you, then: which name did Homer think was more correct for Hector's son -- Astyanax or Scamandrius?

Hermogenes
I don't know.

Socrates
Well, if you were asked whether the wise or the unwise are more likely to give correct names, what would you say?

Hermogenes
The wise, obviously.

Socrates
And who's generally wiser in a city -- the men or the women?

Hermogenes
I'd say the men.

Socrates
Now, Homer tells us that the Trojan men called the boy Astyanax -- "lord of the city." But if the men called him Astyanax, the other name, Scamandrius, must have been given by the women.

Hermogenes
That's a reasonable inference.

Socrates
And Homer must have considered the Trojans wiser than their wives?

Hermogenes
Surely.

Socrates
Then he must have thought Astyanax was a more correct name for the boy than Scamandrius?

Hermogenes
Clearly.

Socrates
And what's the reason? Let's think about it. Doesn't Homer himself suggest a very good reason when he says of Hector, "For he alone defended their city and long walls"? This seems like a good reason for calling the son of the city's savior "lord of the city" -- which is what Astyanax means. (In the Greek, "anax" means "lord" or "king.")

Hermogenes
I see.

Socrates
Actually, Hermogenes, I'm not sure I see it myself yet. Do you?

Hermogenes
No, not really.

Socrates
But tell me, my friend -- didn't Homer also give Hector his name?

Hermogenes
What about it?

Socrates
Well, the name Hector seems to me to be very close in meaning to Astyanax. Both are Greek names, and "king" and "holder" mean nearly the same thing -- both describe a ruler. A person is clearly the holder of that which he's king of: he rules it, owns it, holds it. (In the Greek, "Hektor" comes from "echein," to hold, and "anax" means king -- so both names point to the idea of ruling or possessing.) But maybe you think I'm talking nonsense. I'm not entirely sure myself that I've actually found a clue to Homer's views on the correctness of names.

Hermogenes
I assure you, I think you're on the right track.

Socrates
There's good reason, I think, to call a lion cub a "lion" and a foal a "horse." I'm talking about the normal course of nature, when an animal produces offspring of its own kind -- not about freaks of nature. If a horse somehow gave birth to a calf, I wouldn't call it a foal but a calf. And I wouldn't call an inhuman birth a "human," only a natural one. The same applies to trees and everything else. Do you agree?

Hermogenes
Yes, I agree.

Socrates
Good. But keep an eye on me and make sure I'm not pulling any tricks. By the same principle, the son of a king should be called a king. And it doesn't matter whether the syllables of the name are the same or different, as long as the meaning is preserved. Adding or removing a letter makes no difference, as long as the essence of the thing is captured in the name.

Hermogenes
What do you mean?

Socrates
Something very simple. Think about the names of letters. You know that the names of letters aren't usually the same as the letters themselves -- except for four: epsilon, upsilon, omicron, and omega. The names of all the other letters, whether vowels or consonants, are made up of additional letters we tack on. But as long as the meaning comes through and there's no confusion, the name of the letter is perfectly correct. Take the letter beta, for example: the addition of eta, tau, and alpha doesn't cause any problem and doesn't prevent the whole name from conveying the value the original name-giver intended. That's how well he knew how to name letters.

Hermogenes
I believe you're right.

Socrates
And the same can be said of a king. A king will often be the son of a king, a good son of a good father, a noble son of a noble one. And in the regular course of nature, like produces like, so the offspring has the same name as the parent. But the syllables can be disguised until they look completely different to an untrained eye, even though they mean the same thing. It's like how any one of us wouldn't recognize the same drug in different colors and disguises, but a doctor, who looks at the active properties, recognizes it immediately and isn't thrown off by the packaging. In the same way, an etymologist isn't thrown off by the addition, transposition, or removal of a letter or two -- or even by changing all the letters -- as long as the meaning of the thing is preserved. As we just noted, "Hector" and "Astyanax" have only one letter in common -- tau -- and yet they mean the same thing. And "Archepolis" (ruler of the city) shares almost no letters with either, yet the meaning is the same. There are plenty of other names that just mean "king." Similarly, there are several names for a general -- like Agis ("leader"), Polemarchus ("war chief"), and Eupolemus ("good warrior"). And for a doctor: Iatrocles ("famous healer") and Acesimbrotus ("curer of mortals"). I could give many more examples -- names that differ completely in their syllables and letters but mean the same thing. Wouldn't you agree?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
Then the same names -- in terms of meaning -- should be assigned to those who follow the natural course?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And what about those who don't follow the natural course -- the exceptions? For example, when a good and religious man has an irreligious son, the son shouldn't carry his father's name but the name of the class he actually belongs to -- just like when a horse gives birth to a calf, you call the offspring what it is, not what its parent is.

Hermogenes
Quite true.

Socrates
So the irreligious son of a religious father should be called irreligious?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
He shouldn't be called Theophilus ("beloved of God") or Mnesitheus ("mindful of God") or any name like that. If names are given correctly, his should mean the opposite.

Hermogenes
Certainly, Socrates.

Socrates
Then there's the case of Orestes. (In the Greek, "Orestes" suggests "man of the mountains.") He seems to be aptly named -- whether it was chance or perhaps some poet who wanted to capture the brutality, fierceness, and mountain wildness of his nature.

Hermogenes
That's very likely, Socrates.

Socrates
And his father's name also fits his nature.

Hermogenes
Clearly.

Socrates
Yes -- "Agamemnon" means "admirable for perseverance." (In the Greek, the name suggests "admirable for remaining" or "holding firm.") He's a man who is patient and determined in carrying through his plans, and crowns them with success through sheer endurance. His years-long siege at Troy with that vast army proves the "admirable endurance" signified by the name.

I also think Atreus is rightly named. His murder of Chrysippus and his terrible cruelty to Thyestes were ruinous and destructive to his reputation. The name is a bit altered and disguised, so it's not obvious to everyone, but an etymologist can see the meaning clearly. (In the Greek, you can connect "Atreus" to "ateiros" meaning stubborn, or "atrestos" meaning fearless, or "ateros" meaning destructive -- all of which fit.) The name is right from every angle.

And Pelops is also appropriately named: it means "one who sees only what's near." (In the Greek, "ta pelas horon" -- seeing nearby things.)

Hermogenes
How so?

Socrates
According to the legend, he had no foresight at all about the terrible consequences the murder of Myrtilus would bring on his entire bloodline for generations. He saw only what was right in front of him in his eagerness to win Hippodamia as his bride at any cost.

And everyone would agree that the name Tantalus fits perfectly -- if the stories about him are true.

Hermogenes
And what are those stories?

Socrates
Many terrible misfortunes happened to him during his life -- culminating in the complete ruin of his country. And after death, he had that famous stone suspended over his head in the underworld. All of this lines up wonderfully with his name. (In the Greek, "Tantalus" evokes "talanteia," meaning "weighing down" or "balancing." You could imagine someone wanting to call him "talantatos" -- "the most burdened by misfortune" -- and disguising it into "Tantalus.")

Now, the name of Zeus, who is supposedly Tantalus's father, also has an excellent meaning, though it's hard to understand because it's actually like a compressed sentence split into two parts. Some people call him Zena, using one half, and others call him Dia, using the other half. The two together express the nature of the god. (In the Greek, combining "Zena" and "Dia" gives you "di' hon zen" -- "the one through whom all living creatures always have life.") And it's the business of a name to express nature.

Now, there seems to be something disrespectful about calling Zeus the "son of Kronos" -- since Kronos is proverbially associated with stupidity. You'd expect Zeus to be the child of a mighty intellect. And in fact he is! Because that's actually what Kronos's name means. (In the Greek, "Kronos" is connected to "koros," meaning "sweeping clean" -- not "youth," but rather "the pure and uncontaminated mind.") And Kronos, tradition tells us, was the son of Uranus, whose name is also appropriate -- it comes from "looking upward." (In the Greek, "Ouranos" derives from "horan ta ano" -- "gazing at what's above.") And as the philosophers say, looking upward is the way to develop a pure mind. So the name Uranus is correct.

If I could remember Hesiod's whole genealogy, I'd keep going and try out more of these analyses on the remoter ancestors of the gods. Then I might see whether this wisdom that's suddenly come over me -- I don't know from where -- holds up all the way through.

Hermogenes
You know, Socrates, you sound like a prophet who's just been struck by divine inspiration -- delivering oracles left and right.

Socrates
And you're right, Hermogenes. I believe I caught the inspiration from the great Euthyphro of the deme of Prospalta. He gave me a very long lecture starting at dawn -- he talked and I listened -- and his wisdom and enchanting enthusiasm have not only filled my ears but taken possession of my soul. So here's my plan: today I'll let his superhuman power work through me and finish this investigation of names. But tomorrow, if you're up for it, we'll perform an exorcism and purge ourselves of him -- if we can find some priest or sophist skilled in that sort of purification.

Hermogenes
I'm all for it! I'm very curious to hear the rest of this investigation.

Socrates
Then let's keep going. Where should we start, now that we've gotten a rough framework? Are there any names that testify on their own behalf that they weren't given arbitrarily but have a natural fitness? Names of heroes and ordinary people can be misleading, because they're often named after ancestors whose names may not suit them at all, or they express a wish -- like Eutychides ("child of good fortune"), Sosias ("savior"), or Theophilus ("beloved of God"). We'd better skip those. We'll have a better chance of finding correctness in the names of unchanging realities -- the things that should have been named with the greatest care, and where perhaps some more-than-human power was at work.

Hermogenes
I think so, Socrates.

Socrates
Shouldn't we start with the gods, and show that they're correctly called "gods"?

Hermogenes
Yes, that would make sense.

Socrates
Here's my theory. I suspect that the sun, moon, earth, stars, and sky -- which are still the gods of many non-Greek peoples -- were the only gods known to the earliest Greeks. And since they saw that these things were always moving and running, they called them "theoi" (gods) from their running nature. (In the Greek, "theoi" -- gods -- sounds like "theontes" -- runners.) When they later came to know the other gods, they applied the same name to all of them. Does that seem likely?

Hermogenes
Very likely indeed.

Socrates
What should come after the gods?

Hermogenes
What about "daimones" (spirits) and heroes and humans?

Socrates
Daimones! And what do you think the word means? See if my view is right.

Hermogenes
Let me hear it.

Socrates
Do you know how Hesiod uses the word?

Hermogenes
I don't.

Socrates
Don't you remember that he speaks of a golden race of people who came first?

Hermogenes
Yes, I do.

Socrates
He says of them: "But now that fate has closed over this race, they are holy spirits upon the earth, beneficent, averters of evil, guardians of mortal men."

Hermogenes
What's the point?

Socrates
Well, I don't think he means people literally made of gold, but good and noble people. I'm convinced of this because he also says that we are the "iron race."

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
And wouldn't he say that the good people of our own time belong to that golden race?

Hermogenes
Very likely.

Socrates
And aren't good people wise?

Hermogenes
Yes, they are.

Socrates
Then I'm absolutely convinced he called them "daimones" because they were "daemones" -- wise or knowing. (In the Greek, "daimon" is connected to "daemon," meaning knowing or wise, a word that appears in the older Attic dialect.) Hesiod and other poets say truly that when a good person dies, they receive honor and a great role among the dead, becoming a "daimon" -- a name signifying wisdom. And I'd say the same: that every wise and good person, both in life and death, is more than human -- "daimonion" -- and is rightly called a daimon.

Hermogenes
Then I think I agree with you completely. But what about the word "hero"?

Socrates
That's not hard to explain, since the name has barely been altered. It indicates that heroes were born of love.

Hermogenes
What do you mean?

Socrates
Don't you know that heroes are demigods?

Hermogenes
So?

Socrates
They all sprang from the love of a god for a mortal woman, or a mortal man for a goddess. Look at the word in the old Attic dialect, and you'll see more clearly: "heros" (hero) is just a slight alteration of "eros" (love) -- the heroes sprang from eros. (In the old Attic script, "heros" was written "eros.") Either that, or they were called heroes because they were skilled rhetoricians and dialecticians -- people good at asking questions. (In the Greek, "erotan" means "to question," and "eirein" means "to speak.") So in Attic dialect, heroes turn out to be rhetoricians and questioners -- a tribe of sophists and orators, basically.

But can you tell me why humans are called "anthropoi"? That's the harder one.

Hermogenes
No, I can't. And I wouldn't try even if I could, because I think you're more likely to figure it out.

Socrates
You mean you trust in the inspiration of Euthyphro.

Hermogenes
Of course.

Socrates
And your trust isn't misplaced. Because at this very moment a new and clever idea strikes me, and if I'm not careful, by tomorrow's dawn I'll be wiser than I ought to be. Now pay attention. First, keep in mind that we often add and remove letters from words, shift accents, and give names as we please. Take "Diphilos" ("dear to Zeus"), for instance: to turn it from a phrase into a proper name, we drop one of the iotas and change the middle syllable from an acute accent to a grave. In other cases, letters are inserted rather than removed, and the accent shifts the other way.

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
Well, the word "anthropos" ("human") seems to be exactly that kind of case -- a sentence compressed into a noun. One letter, the alpha, has been removed, and the accent on the last syllable has been changed.

Hermogenes
What do you mean?

Socrates
The word "human" implies that while other animals never examine, reflect on, or look up at what they see, a human not only sees but reflects on and looks up at what they've seen. That's why humans alone are rightly called "anthropos" -- it comes from "anathron ha opopen": "one who looks up at what they have seen." (In the Greek, "anthropos" is derived from "anathrein" -- to look up and examine -- combined with "opope" -- what one has seen.)

Hermogenes
Can I ask you to examine another word that I'm curious about?

Socrates
Certainly.

Hermogenes
I'll take what seems to come next in order. You know the distinction between soul and body?

Socrates
Of course.

Hermogenes
Let's try to analyze them the way we did the previous words.

Socrates
You want me to examine the natural fitness of the word "psyche" (soul) first, and then "soma" (body)?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
If I go with what comes to mind right now, I'd say that those who first used the name "psyche" meant to express that the soul, when in the body, is the source of life -- it gives the power of breath and revival ("anapsychon"). When this reviving power fails, the body dies. And that, if I'm not mistaken, is why they called it "psyche." But wait a moment -- I think I can come up with something that Euthyphro's followers would find more impressive. They'd probably look down on that first explanation. How about this one?

Hermogenes
Let me hear it.

Socrates
What is it that holds, carries, and gives life and motion to the entire body? What else but the soul?

Hermogenes
Just that.

Socrates
And don't you agree with Anaxagoras that mind or soul is the ordering and sustaining principle of all things?

Hermogenes
Yes, I do.

Socrates
Then you could rightly call that power "physeche" -- "that which sustains and holds nature." (In the Greek, this is formed from "physin echei kai ochei" -- "holds and carries nature.") And this has been refined down to "psyche."

Hermogenes
That derivation does seem more scientific than the first one.

Socrates
It does. But I can't help laughing at the idea that this was really the original meaning of the name.

Hermogenes
But what about the next word?

Socrates
You mean "soma" -- the body?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
That one can be interpreted in several ways, and even more if you allow a little rearrangement of letters. Some say the body is the "sema" (tomb or sign) of the soul, as if the soul were buried in our present life. Others say the body is the "sign" of the soul, because the soul expresses itself through the body. (In the Greek, "sema" means both "tomb" and "sign," and "semainei" means "to indicate.") But probably the best etymology comes from the Orphic poets, who believed that the soul is being punished for some sin, and that the body is an enclosure or prison in which the soul is kept safe -- "soma" from "sozein" (to keep safe) -- until the penalty has been paid. On this view, you don't even need to change a single letter.

Hermogenes
I think, Socrates, we've said enough about this category of words. But do we have any more explanations of divine names, like the one you gave for Zeus? I'd like to know whether a similar principle applies to them.

Socrates
Yes, Hermogenes, and there's one excellent principle that we, as sensible people, should acknowledge: we know nothing about the gods -- neither about their natures nor about the names they give themselves. But we can be sure that whatever names they call themselves are true. And this is the best principle. The next best is to say, as we do in prayers, that we'll call them by whatever names or titles they prefer, since we don't know any others. I think that's a fine custom, and I'd like to follow it. So let's make an announcement to the gods first: we're not investigating them -- we wouldn't presume to be capable of that. We're only investigating what humans meant when they gave them these names. Surely there's no harm in that.

Hermogenes
I think, Socrates, you're quite right, and I'd like to proceed as you suggest.

Socrates
Shall we begin with Hestia, as is customary?

Hermogenes
Yes, that would be very proper.

Socrates
What do you think the person who named Hestia had in mind?

Hermogenes
Now that's another very difficult question.

Socrates
My dear Hermogenes, the original name-givers must have been remarkable people. They were philosophers, with a lot to say.

Hermogenes
So? What follows from that?

Socrates
I'd attribute the creation of names to people like that. Even in foreign names, if you analyze them, you can still find meaning. Take what we call "ousia" (being/essence) -- some people call it "esia" and others call it "osia." Now, it's perfectly reasonable for the essence of things to be called "hestia," which is closely related to the first form, "esia." (In the Greek, "hestia" sounds like "esia," which sounds like "ousia.") And there's good reason why the Athenians connected "hestia" to "ousia": because in ancient times they too said "esia" instead of "ousia." And this explains why sacrifices were traditionally offered first to Hestia -- it was natural enough if they thought of Hestia as the essence of things.

Those who used the form "osia," on the other hand, seem to have leaned toward Heraclitus's view that everything flows and nothing stands still. For them, the driving force ("othoun" -- pushing) is the cause and ruling power of everything, so they rightly called it "osia."

That's about all we can say, knowing as little as we do. Now, after Hestia, we should consider Rhea and Kronos, though we've already discussed Kronos. But I'm probably talking total nonsense.

Hermogenes
Why, Socrates?

Socrates
My friend, I've stumbled onto a whole hive of wisdom.

Hermogenes
What kind?

Socrates
Well, rather ridiculous -- and yet plausible.

Hermogenes
How plausible?

Socrates
I keep seeing Heraclitus repeating ancient traditions, as old as the days of Kronos and Rhea, the same kind Homer spoke of.

Hermogenes
What do you mean?

Socrates
Heraclitus says that everything is in motion and nothing is at rest. He compares things to a river's current and says you can't step into the same water twice.

Hermogenes
That's right.

Socrates
Well then, can we avoid concluding that whoever gave the names Kronos and Rhea to the ancestors of the gods held roughly the same view as Heraclitus? Is it a coincidence that they gave both of them names connected to streams and flowing? (In the Greek, "Rhea" suggests "rhein" -- to flow -- and "Kronos" can be connected to "krounos" -- a spring or stream.) Compare the line in Homer -- and I believe in Hesiod too -- about "Ocean, the origin of the gods, and mother Tethys." And Orpheus says, "The fair river of Ocean was the first to marry, and he wedded his sister Tethys, who was his mother's daughter." You see -- it's a remarkable coincidence, and it all points toward Heraclitus.

Hermogenes
I think there's something to what you say, Socrates. But I don't understand the meaning of the name Tethys.

Socrates
Well, that's almost self-explanatory. It's just the name of a spring, slightly disguised. "That which is strained and filtered" can be compared to a spring, and the name Tethys is made up of those two ideas. (In the Greek, "Tethys" evokes "diattomenon" -- strained -- and "ethoumenon" -- filtered.)

Hermogenes
That's clever, Socrates.

Socrates
Of course it is! But what comes next? We've already dealt with Zeus.

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
Then let's take his two brothers -- Poseidon and Pluto, whether we call him by that name or by his other one.

Hermogenes
By all means.

Socrates
"Poseidon" seems to mean "foot-chain" -- the person who named him was being held back by the sea in his travels and couldn't walk any further, so he called the ruler of the waters "Posidesmos." (In the Greek, "posi" evokes feet and "desmos" means chain.) The epsilon was probably just added as decoration. Or maybe not -- maybe the name was originally written with a double lambda instead of a sigma, meaning that the god "knows many things." (In the Greek, "polla eidos" -- knowing much.) Or perhaps it comes from his being the "shaker of the earth" -- from "seiein" (to shake), with pi and delta added on.

As for Pluto, he gets his name from giving wealth -- "ploutos." (In the Greek, "Pluto" directly suggests "ploutos" -- wealth.) Wealth, after all, comes out of the earth below. But most people seem to think that the name "Hades" is connected to "the invisible" ("aeides"), which is why they're afraid of him and prefer to call him Pluto instead.

Hermogenes
And what is the real derivation?

Socrates
Despite all the mistakes people make about this god's power and all their foolish fears of him -- the terror of always being with him after death, of the soul going to him stripped of the body -- I think the god's office and name actually match perfectly.

Hermogenes
How so?

Socrates
Let me explain. But first, a question: which chain holds an animal more tightly and keeps it more firmly in place -- desire, or necessity?

Hermogenes
Desire, Socrates. Desire is far stronger.

Socrates
Then don't you think many people would escape from Hades if he didn't bind the departed with the strongest possible chain?

Hermogenes
They certainly would.

Socrates
And if by the strongest chain, then by some desire -- not by necessity?

Hermogenes
That's clear.

Socrates
And there are many desires?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
Then he must bind with the greatest of all desires, if his chain is to be the greatest?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And is any desire stronger than the thought that associating with someone will make you a better person?

Hermogenes
Certainly not.

Socrates
Then that's the reason, Hermogenes, why nobody who has gone to Hades is willing to come back to us. Even the Sirens have fallen under his spell, along with everyone else. Such is the charm this god can weave into his words. On this view, he's the perfect and accomplished Sophist, and the great benefactor of those in the other world. And even to us on earth he sends abundant blessings from below, which is why he's called Pluto -- "the wealthy one."

Note also that he won't associate with people while they're in the body -- only after the soul has been freed from the body's desires and corruptions. There's real philosophy in that. Because in their liberated state, he can bind souls with the desire for virtue. But while they're confused and maddened by the body, not even his father Kronos could hold them with his famous chains.

Hermogenes
There's a lot of truth in what you say.

Socrates
And the legislator called him Hades not from "the unseen" ("aeides") -- far from it -- but from his "knowledge" ("eidenai") of all noble things. (In the Greek, "Hades" is connected to "eidenai" -- to know -- rather than "a-ides" -- unseen.)

Hermogenes
Very good! And what do we say about Demeter, Hera, Apollo, Athena, Hephaestus, Ares, and the other gods?

Socrates
Demeter means "the giving mother" -- she gives food like a mother. (In the Greek, "Demeter" evokes "didousa meter" -- "mother who gives.") Hera is "the lovely one." (In the Greek, "Hera" suggests "erate" -- lovely.) Zeus, according to tradition, loved and married her. Or possibly the name was given with the sky in mind, and is really a disguise for "air" ("aer"), with the end put at the beginning. You'll see the truth of this if you repeat "Hera" several times.

People dread the name Persephone just as they dread the name Apollo -- and with just as little reason. Their fear comes from not understanding how names work. They keep changing the name to "Phersephone," and that terrifies them. But the real name means that the goddess is wise. (In the Greek, "Pherepapha" comes from "ephaptomene tou pheromenou" -- "touching that which is in motion" -- i.e., grasping moving reality.) Since everything in the world is in motion, the principle that can embrace, touch, and follow all moving things is wisdom. And Hades, who is wise, naturally takes a wise consort. People altered her name to Persephone for the sake of euphony, caring more about how it sounds than what it means.

Now, about Apollo -- as I was saying, people generally think his name has some terrible significance. Have you noticed that?

Hermogenes
Yes, definitely, and what you say is true.

Socrates
But the name, in my view, is actually a beautiful expression of the god's power.

Hermogenes
How so?

Socrates
I'll try to explain. I don't think any single name could better capture all four of his attributes -- music, prophecy, medicine, and archery.

Hermogenes
That must be a remarkable name. I'd like to hear the explanation.

Socrates
It's a harmonious name, as befits the God of Harmony. First, consider that the purifications and cleansings that doctors and priests use -- their fumigations, their ritual washings and sprinklings -- all serve one purpose: to make a person pure in both body and soul.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
And isn't Apollo the purifier, the washer, the one who cleanses all impurities?

Hermogenes
He certainly is.

Socrates
So in terms of his purifying and cleansing role -- as the physician who prescribes these things -- he could rightly be called "Apolouon" ("the purifier"). (In the Greek, "apolouon" means "washer" or "purifier.") In terms of his prophetic powers and his truthfulness and sincerity, he could be called "Haplous" ("sincere" or "simple"), as the Thessalians do -- all Thessalians call him "Haplous." (In the Greek, "haplous" means "sincere" or "straightforward.") And as "the one who always shoots" ("aei ballon"), because he's a master archer who never misses. Or the name might refer to his musical powers: just as in "akolouthos" ("companion") and "akoitis" ("spouse"), where the alpha means "together," the meaning of Apollo would be "moving together" -- because he's the god who makes all things move together in harmony, whether the poles of heaven or the harmony of song. He moves everything in concert through a harmonious power, which is what both astronomers and musicians will tell you.

He's the god who presides over harmony and makes all things move together -- among both gods and humans. And just as "akolouthos" and "akoitis" have alpha substituted for omicron, "Apollon" is really "homopolon" ("moving all together"). The second lambda was added to avoid the unfortunate sound of "apollon" -- which sounds like "destroyer" ("apolein"). (In the Greek, "Apollon" sounds dangerously close to "apollon" -- destruction.) That suspicion of destructive power still haunts some people's minds because they don't understand the true value of the name. But as I've just shown, the name refers to all the god's powers: the sincere one, the always-shooting one, the purifier, the one who moves all things together. (In the Greek: "haplous," "aei ballon," "apolouon," "homopolon.")

The Muses and music get their name from "making philosophical inquiries" ("mosthai"). (In the Greek, "Mousai" is connected to "mosthai" -- to seek or investigate.) Leto is called by that name because she's such a gentle goddess, so willing ("ethelemon") to grant requests. Or maybe her name is really "Letho," as foreigners often call her -- suggesting her gentle, smooth, easygoing nature.

Artemis gets her name from her sound and well-ordered nature ("artemes" -- healthy, sound), from her love of virginity, or perhaps from her being a "proficient in virtue" ("arete"), or possibly from "hating intercourse" ("ton aroton misesasa"). The person who named her could have had any or all of these in mind.

Hermogenes
What about Dionysus and Aphrodite?

Socrates
Son of Hipponicus, you're asking a solemn question! There's both a serious explanation and a humorous one for each of these names. The serious one you'll have to get from someone else, but I'm happy to share the humorous version -- because the gods love a good joke too.

Dionysus is simply "the giver of wine" -- "didous oinos," which you could playfully call "Didoinusos." (In the Greek, "Dionysos" is derived from "didous ton oinon" -- "giver of wine.") And "oinos" (wine) is properly "oionous," because wine makes those who drink it think ("oiesthai") they have a mind ("noun") when they don't.

As for Aphrodite, born from the foam ("aphros"), we can accept that derivation on Hesiod's authority.

Hermogenes
There's still Athena -- and you, Socrates, as an Athenian, surely won't forget her. And there are also Hephaestus and Ares.

Socrates
I'm not likely to forget them.

Hermogenes
No, indeed.

Socrates
There's no difficulty in explaining Athena's other name.

Hermogenes
What other name?

Socrates
We call her Pallas.

Hermogenes
Right.

Socrates
We'd be right to suppose this comes from armed dancing. Lifting yourself or anything else above the earth, or moving with the hands -- we call that shaking or dancing ("pallein"). (In the Greek, "Pallas" is connected to "pallein" -- to brandish or shake.)

Hermogenes
That's quite true.

Socrates
So that's the explanation of Pallas.

Hermogenes
Yes, but what about the name Athena?

Socrates
That's a weightier matter. Modern interpreters of Homer can help us here, I think. Most of them, when explaining the poet, say he meant by Athena "mind" ("nous") and "intelligence" ("dianoia"). And the original name-giver seems to have gone even further, calling her "divine intelligence" ("theou noesis"), as if to say: "This is she who has the mind of God" -- "theonoa." (In the Greek, "Athena" is connected to "theonoa" -- divine intelligence -- using a dialectical alpha for eta and dropping some letters.) Or maybe "theonoe" means "she who knows divine things better than others" ("theia noousa"). Or perhaps the author wanted to connect the goddess with moral intelligence ("en ethei noesin" -- thinking within character), giving her the name "ethonoe," which over time was altered into "Athena."

Hermogenes
But what do you say about Hephaestus?

Socrates
You mean the noble lord of light? ("Phaeos histora" -- "one who knows light.") (In the Greek, "Hephaistos" is "Phaistos" with an eta added by attraction -- "Phaistos" being the "knower of light.")

Hermogenes
Of course.

Socrates
Well, "Hephaistos" is just "Phaistos" with an eta tacked on. That's obvious to anyone.

Hermogenes
Very probably -- unless a better idea comes along.

Socrates
To prevent that, you'd better ask me about Ares.

Hermogenes
All right, what about Ares?

Socrates
Ares could be named from his manliness -- "arren" (male) -- or from his hard, unyielding nature -- "arratos" (unbreakable). (In the Greek, "Ares" can be connected to "arren" -- male -- or "arratos" -- stubborn, unbreakable.) Either derivation fits the god of war perfectly.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
And now, by the gods, let's leave the gods alone! I'm afraid of them. Ask me about anything else, and you'll see how Euthyphro's horses can gallop.

Hermogenes
Just one more god! I'd like to know about Hermes, since I'm supposedly not a true son of his. Let's figure him out, and then I'll know whether there's anything to what Cratylus says about me.

Socrates
I'd say the name Hermes has to do with speech. He's the interpreter ("hermeneus"), the messenger, the thief, the deceiver, the bargainer -- all of which involve language. As I was saying earlier, the word "eirein" expresses the use of speech, and there's a recurring Homeric word "emesato" that means "he contrived." Out of these two words -- "eirein" and "mesasthai" -- the legislator formed the name of the god who invented language and speech. We can imagine him addressing us: "My friends, since he is the contriver of speech and tales, you should properly call him Eiremes." But we've improved this, as we think, into "Hermes."

And Iris also seems to get her name from "eirein" (to speak), because she was a messenger.

Hermogenes
Then I'm quite sure Cratylus was right that I'm no true son of Hermes -- I'm really not good with words!

Socrates
There's also good reason, my friend, for Pan being the double-natured son of Hermes.

Hermogenes
How do you figure that?

Socrates
You know that speech signifies all things ("pan") and is always turning them round and round, and has two forms -- true and false?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
Well, the truth in speech is the smooth and sacred form that dwells above among the gods, while falsehood dwells below among humans, rough like the goat of tragedy. (In the Greek, "tragoidia" -- tragedy -- is connected to "tragos" -- goat.) Tales and falsehoods generally belong to the tragic, goatish side of life, and tragedy is their natural home.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
Then Pan, who declares all things ("pan") and perpetually moves all things ("aei polon"), is rightly called "aipolos" (goatherd) -- being the double-natured son of Hermes, smooth in his upper part and rough and goatlike in his lower regions. And as Hermes' son, he is speech -- or the brother of speech. And that a brother should resemble his brother is no surprise.

But as I was saying, my dear Hermogenes, let's get away from the gods.

Hermogenes
From those gods, absolutely, Socrates. But why shouldn't we talk about another kind of "gods" -- the sun, moon, stars, earth, aether, air, fire, water, the seasons, and the year?

Socrates
You're piling a lot of work on me. Still, if you want, I won't refuse.

Hermogenes
You'd be doing me a favor.

Socrates
Where shall I begin? With the one you mentioned first -- the sun?

Hermogenes
Very good.

Socrates
The origin of the sun's name comes out more clearly in the Doric form, where they call him "halios." This could be because at sunrise he gathers ("halizoi") people together, or because he's always rolling ("aei eilein") around the earth in his course, or from "aiolein" -- to variegate -- because he variegates the things the earth produces.

Hermogenes
But what about "selene" (the moon)?

Socrates
That name is rather unfortunate for Anaxagoras.

Hermogenes
How so?

Socrates
The name seems to anticipate his recent discovery that the moon gets its light from the sun.

Hermogenes
Why do you say that?

Socrates
The two words "selas" (brightness) and "phos" (light) mean much the same thing, right?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And the light of the moon is always both new ("neon") and old ("enon"), if Anaxagoras's followers are right. The sun in its revolution constantly adds new light, while the old light from the previous month remains.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
The moon is also frequently called "selanaia."

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
And since she has a light that's always old and always new ("enon neon aei"), she could very properly be called "selaenoneoia." And this, when compressed, becomes "selanaia." (In the Greek, the name "selanaia" is built from "selas" -- brightness -- plus "enon" -- old -- plus "neon" -- new -- plus "aei" -- always.)

Hermogenes
Now that's a positively dithyrambic name, Socrates! But what do you say about the month and the stars?

Socrates
"Meis" (month) is named from "meiousthai" (to grow less), because the moon diminishes. "Astra" (stars) seems to come from "astrape" -- which is an improved form of "anastrope," meaning "turning the eyes upward." (In the Greek, "astra" is connected to "anastrephein opa" -- turning the gaze upward.)

Hermogenes
What do you say about "pyr" (fire) and "hydor" (water)?

Socrates
"Pyr" has me stumped. Either the muse of Euthyphro has deserted me, or there's some real difficulty with this word. But let me tell you about the trick I use whenever I hit a wall like this.

Hermogenes
What trick?

Socrates
I'll explain. But first, can you tell me what "pyr" means?

Hermogenes
I really can't.

Socrates
Here's what I suspect: this and several other words are actually of foreign origin. The Greeks, especially those who lived under foreign rule, frequently borrowed words from other languages.

Hermogenes
And the point is?

Socrates
That if you try to find the natural fitness of these names using Greek alone, ignoring the language they actually came from, you're likely to get nowhere.

Hermogenes
Yes, certainly.

Socrates
Well, "pyr" doesn't easily connect to any Greek roots. And the Phrygians have what appears to be the same word, just slightly modified -- as they do with "hydor" (water), "kynes" (dogs), and many others.

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
So we shouldn't force an interpretation -- something to say about them might easily be found from a foreign source. That's how I dispose of "pyr" and "hydor."

Now, "aer" (air), Hermogenes -- this can be explained as the element that "raises things" from the earth ("airei"), or as "always flowing" ("aei rhei"). Or, since the flowing of air is wind, and the poets call winds "aetai" (air-blasts), the name-giver might have meant "aetorroun" -- "air-flow" -- in the sense of "wind-flow" ("pneumatorroun"). And so he used the word "aer."

"Aither" (aether) I'd interpret as "aeitheer" -- "that which always runs in a current around the air." (In the Greek, "aither" comes from "aei thein peri ton aera rheon" -- "always running and flowing around the air.")

The meaning of "ge" (earth) comes out better in the form "gaia," because the earth is truly the "mother" ("gaia" suggesting "genneteira" -- she who gives birth). In Homer, "gegaasi" means "to have been born."

Hermogenes
Good.

Socrates
What's next?

Hermogenes
There are "horai" (the seasons) and the two names for the year: "eniautos" and "etos."

Socrates
The "horai" should be spelled in the old Attic way if you want the probable truth: they're rightly called "horai" because they divide ("horizousin") the summers and winters and winds and the fruits of the earth.

As for "eniautos" and "etos," they appear to be the same idea -- "that which brings to light the plants and growths of the earth in their turn and reviews them within itself." (In the Greek, "eniautos" comes from "en heauto exetazei" -- "examines within itself" -- and is split into two words: "eniautos" from "en heauto" and "etos" from "etazei.") This is similar to how the original name of Zeus was divided into Zena and Dia -- the single idea of a power that reviews things from within gets expressed in two words, "etos" and "eniautos."

Hermogenes
Socrates, you really are making amazing progress.

Socrates
I'm on a roll.

Hermogenes
You certainly are.

Socrates
And I haven't hit top speed yet.

Hermogenes
I'd very much like to know how you'd explain the virtues. What principle of correctness is there in those wonderful words -- wisdom, understanding, justice, and the rest?

Socrates
That's an enormous class of names you're digging up! Still, since I've put on the lion's skin, I'd better not lose heart. So I need to consider the meanings of "phronesis" (practical wisdom), "synesis" (understanding), "gnome" (judgment), "episteme" (knowledge), and all those other wonderful words you mention?

Hermogenes
We can't stop now -- not until we've figured out their meaning.

Socrates
By the dog of Egypt! A not-bad idea just occurred to me. I believe the original name-givers were a lot like many of our modern philosophers: in their constant search for the nature of things, they kept going round and round until they got dizzy. And then they imagined that it was the world spinning, not themselves. They projected their own internal dizziness onto nature, and concluded that nothing is stable or permanent -- that everything is just flux and motion and constant change. That's the framework that seems to be behind the names we've been discussing.

Hermogenes
How so, Socrates?

Socrates
Didn't you notice that the names we just examined all seem to indicate motion and flux?

Hermogenes
No, I never thought of it.

Socrates
Take the first one you mentioned. It clearly points to motion.

Hermogenes
Which one?

Socrates
"Phronesis" (practical wisdom) -- it can mean "perception of motion and flow" ("phoras kai rhou noesis"), or "the blessing of motion" ("phoras onesis"). Either way, it's connected to motion ("pheresthai"). (In the Greek, "phronesis" is built from roots meaning motion, flow, and perception.)

"Gnome" (judgment) clearly implies the pondering ("nomesis") of becoming ("genesis"). To ponder is to consider.

Or take "noesis" (thought) itself -- it's really "neou hesis" (the desire of the new). The word "neos" implies that the world is always in the process of creation. The original name-giver wanted to express the soul's longing for novelty. The original form was "neoesis," but eta replaced the double epsilon.

"Sophrosyne" (self-control) is the preservation ("soteria") of that wisdom ("phronesis") we were just discussing.

"Episteme" (knowledge) is related to this too: it indicates that the good soul follows ("hepetai") the motion of things, neither running ahead nor falling behind. So the word should really be read as "epistemene" -- "she who follows along." (In the Greek, "episteme" comes from "hepesthai" -- to follow along with.)

"Synesis" (understanding) can be seen as a kind of conclusion -- derived from "synienai" (to go along with). Like "epistasthai" (to know), it implies the soul progressing in company with the nature of things.

"Sophia" (wisdom) is darker and seems not to be native Greek. The meaning is: "touching the motion or stream of things." Remember that the poets, when describing the start of rapid motion, often use the word "esythe" (he rushed). There was even a famous Spartan named Sous ("Rush") -- the Spartans use this word for rapid motion. And the touching ("epaphe") of this motion is expressed by "sophia." Everything is supposed to be in motion.

"Agathon" (the good) names what is admirable ("agaston") in nature. Even though all things move, some move faster and some slower, and those that are most admirably swift are called "agathon."

"Dikaiosyne" (justice) is clearly "dikaiou synesis" -- understanding of the just. But the actual word "dikaion" (just) is harder. People agree up to a point about justice, and then they start disagreeing.

Those who think everything is in motion conceive most of nature as a kind of receptacle, and they say there's a penetrating power that passes through everything, the instrument of creation -- the subtlest and swiftest element. It must be the subtlest, because otherwise it couldn't pass through everything, and the swiftest, because it passes by other things as if they were standing still. This element, which superintends all things and pierces through all -- "diaion" -- is rightly called "dikaion." The kappa is just added for euphony. (In the Greek, "dikaion" comes from "dia-ion" -- "passing through.")

So far there's general agreement on justice. But I, Hermogenes, being an enthusiastic student, have been told in confidence that justice is also the cause of the world. Now, a "cause" is "that because of which" something is created. And someone whispers in my ear that justice is rightly so called because it partakes of the nature of causation. I start asking gently: "Well, fine, but if that's all true, I still want to know what justice IS." And they think I'm asking tiresome questions and jumping ahead, that I've already been sufficiently answered. They try to satisfy me with one derivation after another, until they start arguing among themselves.

One says justice is the sun -- the only thing that truly pierces and burns as guardian of nature. And when I happily repeat this beautiful idea, someone laughs and says, "So there's no justice in the world after sunset?" When I beg them to give me their honest opinion, one says "fire in the abstract" -- which isn't very illuminating. Another says, "No, not fire in the abstract, but the abstraction of heat in fire." Yet another ridicules all of this and says, following Anaxagoras, that justice is mind, since mind has absolute power, mixes with nothing, orders all things, and passes through everything.

In the end, my friend, I find myself in far greater confusion about justice than before I started learning about it. But I still think the name was given for the reasons I mentioned.

Hermogenes
I think, Socrates, you're not making this up on the spot. You must have heard it from someone else.

Socrates
And not the rest?

Hermogenes
Hardly.

Socrates
All right, then let me continue, and maybe I can convince you that the rest is original too. What comes after justice? I don't think we've discussed courage ("andreia") yet. Injustice ("adikia") is obviously just a hindrance to the penetrating principle ("diaiontos"), so we needn't bother with it.

The name "andreia" (courage) seems to imply a battle -- a battle in the world of existence. According to the flux doctrine, it's a counter-flow ("enantia rhoe"). If you remove the delta from "andreia," the name directly signifies the thing: courage is not the stream opposed to every stream, but only to the stream that's contrary to justice. Otherwise courage wouldn't be praiseworthy.

The words "arren" (male) and "aner" (man) also contain a similar allusion -- to the principle of upward flow ("tes ano rhoes"). (In the Greek, "aner" suggests "ano rhoe" -- upward flow.)

"Gyne" (woman) I suspect is the same as "gone" (birth). "Thely" (female) seems partly derived from "thele" (the breast), because the breast, like rain, makes things flourish ("tethalenai").

Hermogenes
That seems probable.

Socrates
Yes, and the very word "thallein" (to flourish) seems to capture the growth of youth -- swift and sudden. The legislator expressed this in the name, which is a compound of "thein" (running) and "hallesthai" (leaping).

Look at me galloping along once I hit smooth ground! There are still plenty of important names left to explain.

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
There's the word "techne" (art/skill), for example.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
That can be identified with "echonoe" -- "possession of mind." You just need to remove the tau and insert two omicrons. (In the Greek, "techne" becomes "echonoe" -- "having mind" -- with minor letter changes.)

Hermogenes
That's a pretty shabby etymology.

Socrates
Yes, my friend, but you have to remember that the original names have been buried and disguised over time. People stuck on and stripped off letters for the sake of euphony, twisting and decorating them in every possible way. Time has played its part too. Take the word "katoptron" (mirror) -- why is the rho in there? Someone who cared nothing for truth but only for how the mouth feels must have added it. These additions pile up until eventually no human being can figure out the original meaning. Another example: the word "sphinx" -- which should properly be "phix," but someone added the sigma.

Hermogenes
That's quite true, Socrates.
