TRANSLATION NOTES — Laws Book 6, Part 1 (Chapter 050)

OVERVIEW
This is the first half of Laws Book 6, covering the detailed machinery of
magistrate selection, military organization, council structure, courts, and
the beginning of a discussion about the relationship between legislation and
ongoing governance. It's the most procedurally dense section of the Laws so
far — essentially a constitutional blueprint.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS

1. PROCEDURAL CLARITY
   The Jowett text describes enormously complex electoral procedures in
   long, tangled paragraphs. I broke these into sequential steps with clear
   transitions ("Here's how it works," "On the first day," "On the second
   day"). The content is unchanged but the procedure is now followable.

2. TWO EQUALITIES PASSAGE
   The distinction between arithmetic equality (same for everyone) and
   proportional equality ("the judgment of Zeus") is one of the great
   political philosophy passages. Modernized the language but preserved the
   conceptual structure: the easy equality of measurement vs. the harder
   equality of proportional justice. "Distributing natural equality among
   unequals" is kept close to the original — it's a famous formulation.

3. MILITARY TERMINOLOGY
   - "taxiarchs" → "brigade commanders"
   - "hipparchs" → "cavalry officers"
   - "phylarchs" → "tribal leaders"
   - "prytanes" → "executive council members" (on first use) / "executive officers"
   - "hoplites" → "heavy infantry" / "shield-bearing infantry"
   Kept the function clear while avoiding jargon.

4. SHIP OF STATE METAPHOR
   The analogy of the city as a ship "sailing on a sea of politics" is
   preserved — it's vivid and effective. Modernized the surrounding
   language about watchmen passing the duty hand to hand.

5. COUNTRY WARDENS SECTION
   The detailed description of what the young wardens should do — trenching,
   road-building, water management, creating gymnasiums — is fascinating as
   a picture of civic life. Kept it complete. The passage about warm baths
   for the elderly and the dig at mediocre doctors is charming.

6. COUNCIL ROTATION
   The twelve-month rotation system and the compulsory/optional voting by
   class is complex. Laid it out step by step to make the graduated
   penalties clear (fourth class = no penalty, first class = quadruple fine).

7. SUPERINTENDENT OF EDUCATION
   The Athenian's declaration that this is "the greatest of all offices"
   is one of the most striking claims in the Laws. The plant/animal metaphor
   for early education is kept vivid. The idea that humans with bad
   education become "the most savage creatures on earth" is powerful.

8. COURTS OF LAW
   Three-tier system: neighborhood/friends first, then tribal courts, then
   the supreme court of "firstfruit" judges. The principle that everyone
   who has no share in justice "feels he has no share in the state" is an
   important democratic insight, particularly notable coming from Plato.

9. PAINTER ANALOGY
   The closing image of the painter who endlessly refines his work sets up
   the transition to Part 2's discussion of the legislator's similar need
   for successors. Kept it natural and clear.

TONE
   The Athenian's voice here is that of a practical constitution-builder,
   not a mystic or theorist. He's working through real organizational
   problems. The tone alternates between bureaucratic detail and genuinely
   wise observations about human nature. I tried to make the procedural
   sections as clear as possible while preserving the moments of insight
   and humor.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR PART 2
- Speaker labels: Athenian, Cleinias, Megillus (Megillus is quiet in this
  part but appears in Part 2)
- "Guardians of the law" = the 37-member body (consistent throughout)
- "Wardens of the city" / "wardens of the market" / "wardens of the country"
  = three types of local officers (keep terminology consistent)
- Property classes: first through fourth, with first being highest
- Monetary units: mina, drachma (kept as-is with no conversion)
- The new colony is being established in Crete, near Knossos
- The number 5,040 households has been established in earlier books
- "Vetting process" = consistent translation for Jowett's "scrutiny"
