Persons of the dialogue:
  Lysimachus, son of Aristides
  Melesias, son of Thucydides
  Their sons
  Nicias, Laches, Socrates

Or, Courage


Lysimachus
You've seen the man fighting in armor, Nicias and Laches. But we didn't tell you at the time why my friend Melesias and I asked you to come along and watch. I think we might as well be honest about that now — we certainly shouldn't hold anything back from you. The truth is, we wanted your advice. Some people laugh at the whole idea of asking others for advice, and when you do ask them, they won't say what they really think. They just try to guess what you want to hear and tell you that. But we know you're both men of sound judgment, and you'll say exactly what you think. So we've brought you into our confidence.

Here's what this is all about. Melesias and I have two sons. That's his boy — named Thucydides, after his grandfather. And this is mine, also named after his grandfather: Aristides. We've made up our minds to give these boys the best upbringing we can, and not just let them run wild the way young men so often do once they're past childhood. We want to start right now and do everything in our power for them.

Now, since you both have sons of your own, we figured you'd have given some thought to their training and education. And if by chance you haven't, well — consider this a reminder that you should. We'd like you to join us in fulfilling what's really a shared responsibility.

Let me tell you, Nicias and Laches — even at the risk of going on too long — how we came to be thinking about this. Melesias and I live together, and our sons live with us. And now, as I said at the start, we're going to make a confession. We often talk to the boys about the many noble things our own fathers did — in war, in peacetime, in managing the allies, in governing the city. But neither of us has any accomplishments of his own to show them. The truth is, we're ashamed of the contrast. And we blame our fathers for spoiling us when we were young — they were too busy with everyone else's affairs to pay attention to us. We point all this out to the boys, warning them that if they slack off and don't take themselves seriously, they won't amount to anything. But if they put in the work, they might actually become worthy of the names they carry.

The boys, for their part, promise to do as we say. So our job now is to find the right studies and training for them. Someone recommended the art of fighting in armor — said it was an excellent skill for a young man to learn. He praised the man whose performance you just watched and told us to go see him. So we went, and we brought you along, planning to ask your advice — if you're willing — not just about this fighting technique, but about the education of our sons in general. That's what we want to discuss with you. Please, tell us what you think.

Nicias
As far as I'm concerned, Lysimachus and Melesias, I'm glad to help, and I applaud what you're doing. I'm sure Laches feels the same way.

Laches
Absolutely, Nicias. And I think Lysimachus made a very good point about his own father and the father of Melesias — and it applies not just to them, but to us and to anyone wrapped up in public affairs. People like that tend to neglect their own children and their personal lives. There's a lot of truth in what you said, Lysimachus.

But here's my question: instead of consulting us, why don't you ask our friend Socrates about the boys' education? He's from your own deme, and he's always spending time in places where young people are pursuing some worthy study — exactly the kind of thing you're looking for.

Lysimachus
Really, Laches? Has Socrates ever taken an interest in matters like this?

Laches
He certainly has, Lysimachus.

Nicias
I can vouch for that as well as Laches can. Just recently, he recommended a music teacher for my sons — Damon, a student of Agathocles. The man is brilliant in every way, not just as a musician, but as a companion of extraordinary value for boys their age.

Lysimachus
Those of us who've reached my age, Socrates and Nicias and Laches, tend to lose touch with the younger generation — we're generally stuck at home thanks to old age. But you, son of Sophroniscus, ought to help your fellow demesmen with whatever advice you can offer. And I have a special claim on you, as an old friend of your father's. He and I were always close — companions and friends right up until the day he died. There was never a disagreement between us.

And now that your name has come up, I remember that these boys of ours often talk about Socrates at home — always with the highest praise. But I never thought to ask them whether the son of Sophroniscus was the one they meant. Tell me, boys — is this the Socrates you're always talking about?

Son
Yes, father, that's him.

Lysimachus
I'm delighted to hear it, Socrates, that you're living up to your father's name — he was an excellent man. And I'm glad at the prospect of renewing our family ties.

Laches
And you really shouldn't let him go, Lysimachus. I've seen him in action — and not just upholding his father's reputation, but his country's. He was with me in the retreat from Delium, and I can tell you: if everyone else had fought the way he did, our city's honor would have been preserved, and that terrible defeat would never have happened.

Lysimachus
That's very high praise, Socrates, from reliable witnesses who saw you in action. I'm genuinely pleased to hear about your reputation. I hope you'll count me among your warmest friends. You should have come to visit us long ago and made yourself at home. But now that we've finally found each other, I insist — from this day forward, come around and get to know me and these young men, so I can be your friend the way I was your father's. I'll hold you to that, and I'll remind you if you forget.

But now — what about the question we were starting to discuss? This art of fighting in armor — is it something the boys should learn?

Socrates
I'll do my best to advise you on this, Lysimachus, and I'm happy to help in any way I can. But since I'm younger and less experienced, I think I should hear what my elders have to say first, and learn from them. If I have anything to add after that, I'll offer my opinion to them and to you. How about one of you starts — Nicias, would you like to go first?

Nicias
I don't mind at all, Socrates. In my opinion, learning this art is useful for young men in a number of ways. For one thing, it gives them a pastime that actually improves their physical health rather than harming it — and there's no better or harder exercise. This and horsemanship are the most fitting skills for a free man, since only those trained in the use of arms are true athletes of the military profession, trained in what actually matters on the battlefield.

In actual combat, when you're fighting in formation, this kind of training is an advantage. And it's even more valuable when the ranks break and you have to fight one-on-one — whether you're chasing down someone who's defending himself, or you're the one retreating and defending against an attacker. A man who knows this art wouldn't come to any harm at the hands of a single opponent, or maybe even several. He'd have a real edge.

And there's more. This kind of training makes a man want to learn other noble skills. Anyone who's learned to fight in armor will naturally want to learn how to arrange an army — and once he's learned that, with his ambition fired up, he'll go on to study the complete art of the general. It's easy to see that knowledge of military arts is honorable and valuable, and this skill could be the starting point for all of them.

I'll add one more advantage, and it's not a small one: this training will make a man considerably braver and more self-possessed in the field. And I won't hesitate to mention something that some might consider trivial — he'll cut a better figure at the right moment, the moment when his appearance needs to strike fear into his enemies.

So that's my opinion, Lysimachus: the boys should learn this art, for the reasons I've given. But Laches may see things differently, and I'd be very glad to hear his view.

Laches
Well, I wouldn't want to say that any kind of knowledge isn't worth learning — all knowledge seems like a good thing. So if this fighting in armor really is a genuine skill, as Nicias and the instructors claim, then sure, it should be learned. But if it's not — if the people who teach it are just fakes — or if it is a skill, but not a particularly valuable one — then what's the point?

Here's my thinking. If this were really valuable, the Spartans would have figured it out. Their whole lives are devoted to finding and practicing every art that gives them an advantage in war. And even if the Spartans somehow missed it, these instructors would surely have noticed that the Spartans, of all the Greeks, care most about military training. A master of this art who was respected in Sparta could make a fortune anywhere else — just like a tragic poet who's honored in Athens. That's why anyone who thinks he can write a tragedy comes straight to Athens to put on his play — he doesn't tour around the other cities first.

But I notice these fighting-in-armor men treat Sparta like sacred ground they won't set foot on. They make a circuit of all the neighboring cities and perform for anyone rather than the Spartans — especially for people who'd be the first to admit they're not exactly the best warriors.

And here's the other thing, Lysimachus. I've run into quite a few of these fellows in actual combat, and I can give you my assessment right now: not one of these fencing masters has ever distinguished himself in war. There's a kind of curse on them. In every other art, the notable practitioners are the ones who've actually practiced it. These men seem to be a remarkable exception.

Take this very fellow Stesilaus, the one you and I just watched performing for that big crowd, making grand claims about his skills. Well, I saw him at another time giving a far better performance — completely unintentional — and it was quite a spectacle. He was a marine on a ship that rammed a transport vessel. He was armed with a weapon that was half spear, half scythe — a contraption as peculiar as the man himself. To make a long story short, let me just tell you what happened with this famous scythe-spear of his. He was fighting, and the scythe end got tangled in the rigging of the other ship and stuck fast. He pulled and pulled, but couldn't get it free. Meanwhile, the two ships were drifting past each other. At first he ran along the deck of his own ship, hanging onto the spear. But as the transport pulled away and dragged him along, the spear slid through his hands until he was clutching just the end of the handle. The people on the transport started clapping and laughing at the ridiculous sight. Then someone threw a stone that landed on the deck at his feet, and he finally let go of the spear. At that point, even the crew of his own ship burst out laughing — they couldn't help it when they saw his weapon dangling in the air from the enemy's rigging.

Now, maybe there's something to this art, as Nicias says. But I'm telling you what I've actually seen. And as I said at the start, whether it's a skill with very little real advantage or no real skill at all, just a fraud — either way, it's not worth learning. Here's my view: if the man who claims to know this art is a coward, the training will just make him reckless, and his flaws will be even more obvious. But if he's brave and makes even the smallest mistake, everyone will be watching, and he'll be torn apart. People are jealous of anyone who claims this kind of expertise. Unless a man is truly outstanding in courage, he can't help looking ridiculous if he says he has this skill.

That's my judgment, Lysimachus, on whether this art is worth pursuing. But as I said at the start — ask Socrates, and don't let him go until he's given you his opinion.

Lysimachus
I'm going to ask you for this favor, Socrates, and it's all the more necessary because our two advisors disagree. We need someone to break the tie. If they'd agreed, we wouldn't need an arbiter. But since Laches has voted one way and Nicias another, I'd like to hear which of them you side with.

Socrates
Wait — Lysimachus, you're going to go with the majority opinion?

Lysimachus
Well, yes, Socrates. What else should I do?

Socrates
And you, Melesias — would you do the same? If you were thinking about the best gymnastic training for your son, would you follow the advice of the majority of us, or would you listen to the one person who'd actually trained and exercised under a skilled master?

Melesias
The latter, Socrates. That's only reasonable.

Socrates
His one vote would be worth more than the four of ours combined?

Melesias
Certainly.

Socrates
And that's because a good decision should be based on knowledge, not on numbers. Right?

Melesias
Exactly.

Socrates
So the first thing we need to find out is whether any one of us actually has expertise in what we're discussing. If someone does, let's listen to him, even if it's just one person, and forget about the rest. If nobody does, we'd better look elsewhere. Because this isn't some trivial matter you and Lysimachus are deciding. You're risking your greatest possessions — your children. How they turn out determines everything about your household.

Melesias
That's true.

Socrates
So we'd better be very careful about this?

Melesias
Certainly.

Socrates
Now suppose, as I was just saying, we wanted to find the best athletic trainer. Wouldn't we pick someone who actually knew and had practiced the art, someone who'd studied under the best teachers?

Melesias
I think we would.

Socrates
But wouldn't there be an even more fundamental question to settle first — what exactly is the art whose masters we're trying to find?

Melesias
I don't follow you.

Socrates
Let me try to make it clearer. I don't think we've established yet what it is we're actually consulting about — what skill we need to evaluate, and who has the right teachers for it.

Nicias
Wait, Socrates — isn't the question simply whether young men should learn to fight in armor?

Socrates
Yes, Nicias. But there's a deeper question underneath it. Let me put it this way: when someone is deciding whether to apply a certain medicine to someone's eyes, is the real question about the medicine — or about the eyes?

Nicias
About the eyes.

Socrates
And when someone is deciding whether to put a bridle on a horse, and when to do it, he's really thinking about the horse, not the bridle?

Nicias
True.

Socrates
In other words, whenever someone considers a means for the sake of an end, what he's really thinking about is the end, not the means.

Nicias
Certainly.

Socrates
So when you call in an advisor, what you need to know is whether that advisor has expertise in the end you're aiming at.

Nicias
Absolutely.

Socrates
And what we're aiming at right now is something that concerns the souls of these young men, isn't it?

Nicias
Yes.

Socrates
So what we're really asking is: which of us is skilled in caring for the soul? Which of us has had good teachers in that art?

Laches
Hold on, Socrates — haven't you ever noticed that some people who've had no teachers are more skilled than those who have?

Socrates
Yes, Laches, I've noticed that. But you wouldn't be too eager to trust them unless they could show some proof of their skill — some concrete results.

Laches
That's fair.

Socrates
So here's where we stand, Laches and Nicias. Lysimachus and Melesias want our advice on how to improve their sons' minds. We should tell them who our teachers were — if we've had any — and prove that those teachers were excellent and had real experience in training young minds. Or if any of us is self-taught, he should point to specific Athenians or foreigners — slave or free — whom he's acknowledged to have made better. But if none of us can show either teachers or results, then we should tell these men to look elsewhere. We shouldn't risk ruining the children of our friends and earning the most serious accusation a man can face from those closest to him.

As for myself, Lysimachus and Melesias — I'll be the first to confess: I've never had a teacher of virtue, even though I've wanted one my whole life. But I'm too poor to pay the Sophists, who are the only ones claiming to teach moral improvement. And I've never been able to figure it out on my own.

If Nicias or Laches have discovered or learned the art, I wouldn't be surprised. They're wealthier than I am, so they might have paid for instruction. And they're older, so they've had more time to work it out. I genuinely believe they're able to educate a man. Otherwise, why would they have spoken so confidently about what's helpful or harmful for a young man?

I trust both of them. But I'm puzzled that they disagree with each other.

So, Lysimachus, here's what I suggest — since Laches told you to hold onto me and not let me go until I answered, let me turn that around. I urge you to hold onto Laches and Nicias, and press them with questions. Here's what I'd have you say: "Socrates admits he has no knowledge of these matters — he can't decide which of you is right. He's never been a student or a discoverer of anything like this. But you, Laches and Nicias — each of you should tell us: who is the most skilled educator you've ever known? Did you discover the art yourselves, or learn it from someone? If you learned it, who were your teachers, and who else practiced the same art? And if you're too busy with politics to teach the boys yourselves, send us to your teachers. We'll bring them gifts or pull strings or whatever it takes, in the hope that they'll agree to take charge of our children and yours — so the boys don't grow up to be a disgrace to their ancestors. But if you're self-taught in this field, show us the evidence. Who started out as an inferior person and became good and noble under your guidance? Because if this is your first attempt at education, there's a real danger here — you might be experimenting not on some worthless test subject, but on your own sons and the sons of your friends. As the saying goes, you'd be 'learning to make pots by breaking the big jar.'"

Tell them to answer that, Lysimachus. Don't let them off the hook.

Lysimachus
I like what Socrates says very much. But it's up to you, Nicias and Laches — are you willing to be questioned and give an account of yourselves on these matters? Melesias and I would be delighted to hear your answers. After all, we brought you in because we thought you'd have given this subject some thought, especially since your own children are about the same age as ours.

So, if you're agreeable, why not take Socrates on as a partner? Let him ask the questions, and you answer — and question each other too. As he rightly said, we're discussing the most important thing in our lives. I hope you'll agree to this.

Nicias
I can see quite clearly, Lysimachus, that you only knew Socrates' father. You don't really know Socrates himself — or at least you only knew him as a child, tagging along with his father at sacrifices and neighborhood gatherings. You obviously haven't spent time with him since he grew up.

Lysimachus
What do you mean by that, Nicias?

Nicias
Because you don't seem to realize that anyone who gets close to Socrates and starts a conversation with him will inevitably get drawn into an argument. It doesn't matter what topic you begin with — he'll lead you around and around until you find yourself having to give an account of your entire life, past and present. And once he's got you, Socrates won't let go until he's sifted through you completely and thoroughly.

Now, I'm used to his ways. I know he'll do exactly what I'm describing, and I know I'll be on the receiving end of it — because I actually enjoy his conversation, Lysimachus. And I think there's nothing wrong with being reminded of things we've done poorly, or are doing poorly now. A man who doesn't run from honest criticism is bound to be more careful going forward. As Solon says, a man should want to keep learning as long as he lives, and not assume that old age automatically brings wisdom.

Being cross-examined by Socrates is nothing unusual or unpleasant for me. In fact, I knew all along that once Socrates was here, the conversation would shift from our sons to ourselves. So I'm perfectly willing to go along with Socrates' way of doing things. But you'd better ask our friend Laches how he feels about it.

Laches
My feelings about discussions are — well, it's complicated, Nicias. You could say I'm of two minds. When I hear a man talking about virtue or wisdom, and he's a genuine man, worthy of his subject — I'm absolutely thrilled. I look at the man, then I look at his words, and I see them fitting together in perfect harmony. That's the kind of person I call a true musician — tuned not to some lyre, but to a finer harmony. He's got the harmony of words and deeds in his own life, set not to the Ionian mode, or the Phrygian, or the Lydian, but to the true Greek mode — the Dorian, and no other. A man like that makes me happy when he speaks. People think I'm some great lover of conversation because I drink in his words so eagerly.

But when a man's actions don't match his words? He's a nuisance. And the better he speaks, the more he irritates me. Then people think I'm a hater of discussion.

As for Socrates — I don't know much about his words. But I have long experience of his deeds, and his deeds show that courage and noble feeling come to him naturally. If his words match his deeds, then I'm with him completely, and I'll be glad to be questioned by him. I won't mind learning from him one bit. I agree with Solon too — I'd gladly grow old learning many things. But I'd add one condition: learning from good people only. Socrates has to be a good teacher, or I'll be a difficult student. But the fact that the teacher is younger than me, or not yet famous — that doesn't bother me at all.

So, Socrates, consider yourself invited: teach me, challenge me, learn from me — whatever you like. That's been my opinion of you ever since the day you stood beside me in danger and proved your courage the way only a truly good man can. Say whatever you want, and don't worry about the difference in our ages.

Socrates
Well, I can't say either of you seems reluctant to think things through and join the discussion.

Lysimachus
But this is our business — yours as well as ours, since I count you as one of us. So please, Socrates, take my place and find out from Nicias and Laches what we need to know, for the boys' sake. Talk it over with them. My memory isn't what it used to be — I'm old, and I forget what I was going to ask and what the answers were. If someone interrupts, I lose the thread entirely. So I'll ask you to carry on the discussion yourselves. I'll listen, and Melesias and I will act on whatever you all conclude.

Socrates
All right, Nicias and Laches, let's do as Lysimachus and Melesias ask. There's nothing wrong with asking ourselves the question they first raised: "Who have been our teachers in this kind of training, and whom have we made better?" But there's another way of approaching the same problem that might be even better — starting from first principles.

Here's what I mean. If we knew that adding something to something else would improve it, and we were able to make that addition, then clearly we'd have to understand the thing we're trying to improve. Right? Let me put it more concretely. Suppose we knew that adding sight improves the eyes, and suppose we were able to give someone sight. Then we'd obviously have to know what sight is. If we didn't know what sight was, or what hearing was, we wouldn't be very good medical advisors about eyes or ears — we couldn't tell anyone the best way to acquire sight or hearing.

Laches
That's true, Socrates.

Socrates
And aren't these two friends of ours, Laches, asking us right now to figure out how to give their sons virtue — how to improve their minds?

Laches
Exactly.

Socrates
So don't we first need to know what virtue is? Because how can we advise anyone about the best way to get something if we don't even know what it is?

Laches
I don't think we can, Socrates.

Socrates
So we're claiming, then, that we do know what virtue is?

Laches
Yes, I'd say so.

Socrates
And if we know something, we should be able to say what it is?

Laches
Of course.

Socrates
Now, I wouldn't suggest we start by trying to define the whole of virtue — that might be too much for us. Let's begin with a part of it. That'll make things easier.

Laches
Good idea. Let's do that, Socrates.

Socrates
So which part of virtue should we pick? It should be the one that fighting in armor is supposed to develop, shouldn't it? And that's generally thought to be courage.

Laches
Yes, definitely.

Socrates
All right then, Laches. Let's start by trying to define courage. After that, we can figure out how these young men might acquire it through their studies and training. So tell me, if you can: what is courage?

Laches
That's easy, Socrates. A man of courage is one who doesn't run away but holds his position and fights the enemy. No question about it.

Socrates
Good answer, Laches. And yet I'm afraid the fault is mine — I must not have stated my question clearly enough. Because you've answered a different question from the one I meant to ask.

Laches
What do you mean, Socrates?

Socrates
I'll try to explain. You'd call a man courageous who stands his ground and fights the enemy?

Laches
I certainly would.

Socrates
So would I. But what about a man who fights while retreating, instead of standing firm?

Laches
What do you mean, "fights while retreating"?

Socrates
Well, the way the Scythians fight, for instance — attacking and retreating at the same time. And Homer praises the horses of Aeneas for knowing "how to chase and flee swiftly this way and that." He even compliments Aeneas himself for his knowledge of flight, calling him "a master of fear and retreat."

Laches
Fair enough, Socrates. But Homer was talking about chariots, and you were talking about Scythian cavalry — that's how cavalry fights. But the Greek infantryman fights the way I described: holding his ground.

Socrates
Except, Laches, for the Spartans at Plataea. When they faced the Persian light-armed troops, they reportedly refused to stand and fight — they pulled back. But when the Persian ranks broke, they turned on them like cavalry and won the battle.

Laches
That's true.

Socrates
And that's what I was getting at — the fault was mine, because I asked the question badly, which led you to answer badly. I didn't just want to know about the courage of infantry. I wanted to ask about the courage of cavalry, of every kind of soldier, of people who are brave at sea, brave in the face of illness or poverty, brave in politics. And not just people who are brave against pain and fear, but people who are tough enough to resist desires and pleasures — whether they stand their ground or turn to face the enemy. Because there is this kind of courage too, isn't there, Laches?

Laches
There certainly is, Socrates.

Socrates
All these people are courageous. But some show their courage in the face of pleasures, some in pain, some in desire, some in fear. And others are cowardly in the very same situations.

Laches
Very true.

Socrates
So what I was asking about is courage in general — the quality that's common to all these cases. Do you see what I mean now?

Laches
Not entirely.

Socrates
Here's what I mean. Suppose I asked you: "What is quickness?" Quickness shows up in running, in playing the lyre, in speaking, in learning, and in countless other activities — really, in almost everything worth doing, whether it involves arms, legs, mouth, voice, or mind. You'd agree that "quickness" applies to all of them?

Laches
Absolutely.

Socrates
Now, if someone asked me, "Socrates, what is this quality you call 'quickness' in all these different contexts?" I'd say: it's the quality of accomplishing a lot in a little time — whether in running, speaking, or anything else.

Laches
And you'd be exactly right.

Socrates
Good. Now you try. Tell me in the same way: what is that common quality called courage — the one thing that's present in all these different situations I just described, from pleasure to pain to everything else?

Laches
I'd say courage is a kind of endurance of the soul — if I'm supposed to identify the universal quality that runs through all those cases.

Socrates
That's exactly what we need to do if we're going to answer the question. But I have to say, I don't think every kind of endurance deserves to be called courage. Here's my reasoning. I'm sure, Laches, that you'd consider courage to be a noble quality?

Laches
One of the noblest, absolutely.

Socrates
And endurance guided by wisdom — that's good and noble too?

Laches
Very much so.

Socrates
But what about foolish endurance? Wouldn't you say that's harmful and bad?

Laches
True enough.

Socrates
And can anything noble also be harmful and bad?

Laches
I shouldn't say so, Socrates.

Socrates
So you wouldn't call that kind of endurance courage — since it's not noble, and courage is noble?

Laches
You're right.

Socrates
Then according to you, it's only wise endurance that counts as courage?

Laches
So it seems.

Socrates
But here's the thing — "wise" in what sense? Wise about everything, big and small? For example, if a man shows endurance in spending his money wisely, knowing that in the long run he'll earn more — would you call that courage?

Laches
Definitely not.

Socrates
Or what about a doctor whose son — or patient — has an inflammation of the lungs and begs to eat or drink something, and the doctor stands firm and refuses? Is that courage?

Laches
No, that's not courage either.

Socrates
Now consider this: a soldier who endures in battle, willing to fight, but only because he's calculated wisely that reinforcements are coming, that his side outnumbers the enemy, that the enemy is weaker, and that he has the advantage of position. This man, who endures with all this wisdom and preparation — is he braver than the soldier on the other side who's outnumbered, outmatched, in a worse position, but endures and holds his ground anyway?

Laches
I'd say the second man is braver, Socrates.

Socrates
But his endurance is foolish compared to the first man's, isn't it?

Laches
That's true.

Socrates
So you'd say that in a cavalry engagement, the man who endures without knowing how to ride is more courageous than the one who endures with that knowledge?

Laches
I would.

Socrates
And the man who endures without knowing how to use a sling, or a bow, or any other weapon — he's more courageous than the one who does have that knowledge?

Laches
Yes.

Socrates
And the man who dives into a well, or holds out in any dangerous situation, without knowing how to swim or dive — he's more courageous than the trained divers?

Laches
What else can you say, Socrates?

Socrates
Nothing — if that's what you actually believe.

Laches
And that is what I believe.

Socrates
And yet, Laches, these people who take risks and endure without any skill are being foolish compared to those who do the same things with skill.

Laches
True.

Socrates
But we agreed earlier that foolish boldness and endurance are bad and harmful.

Laches
We did.

Socrates
And we agreed that courage is noble.

Laches
Yes.

Socrates
But now we're saying that foolish endurance — the very thing we called shameful — is courage.

Laches
That does seem to be what we're saying.

Socrates
And does that sound right to you?

Laches
By Zeus, Socrates, it doesn't. Not at all.

Socrates
So by your own account, Laches, you and I aren't exactly in tune with the Dorian mode — the harmony of words and deeds. Anyone who saw us in action would say we have courage. But anyone who heard us trying to talk about courage just now? I don't think so.

Laches
That's painfully true.

Socrates
And is this a satisfactory state to be in?

Laches
The opposite of satisfactory.

Socrates
Well then, suppose we take our own principle and apply it — to a certain extent, at least.

Laches
What principle? What extent?

Socrates
The principle of endurance. We need to endure and keep pushing forward in our investigation. Otherwise courage itself will laugh at us for being faint-hearted in our search for its nature — when courage might turn out to be endurance after all.

Laches
I'm ready to keep going, Socrates, even though I'm not used to this kind of inquiry. But the fighting spirit in me has been aroused by what we've been saying, and I'm honestly frustrated that I can't put my thoughts into words. I feel like I do know what courage is. But somehow the thing keeps slipping away from me — I can't grab hold of it and say what it is.

Socrates
Well, my friend, a good hunter should follow the trail and not give up.

Laches
Absolutely.

Socrates
Then shall we invite Nicias to join the hunt? He might be better at tracking than we are. What do you think?

Laches
Sure, let's do that.

Socrates
Come on then, Nicias. Help out your friends — we're drowning in the waves of this argument, going under for the last time. If you can, save us — and settle your own opinion in the bargain. Tell us: what do you think courage is?

Nicias
I've been thinking, Socrates, that you and Laches have been going about this the wrong way. You've forgotten an excellent saying that I've heard from your own lips.

Socrates
What saying is that, Nicias?

Nicias
I've often heard you say that every man is good at what he's wise about, and bad at what he's ignorant about.

Socrates
That's certainly true, Nicias.

Nicias
So if the courageous man is good, then he must also be wise.

Socrates
Did you hear that, Laches?

Laches
I heard it, but I don't quite understand it.

Socrates
I think I do. He seems to be saying that courage is a kind of wisdom.

Laches
What kind of wisdom could he possibly mean, Socrates?

Socrates
That's a question you should ask him.

Laches
Fine.

Socrates
Go ahead then, Nicias — tell him. What kind of wisdom do you mean? Surely not the wisdom of playing the flute?

Nicias
Not at all.

Socrates
Or the wisdom of playing the lyre?

Nicias
No.

Socrates
Then what is this knowledge, and what's it about?

Laches
Good question, Socrates. I'd like to hear just what kind of knowledge he has in mind.

Nicias
What I mean, Laches, is that courage is the knowledge of what is truly to be feared and what can be safely hoped for — in war, and in everything else.

Laches
That's a strange thing to say, Socrates.

Socrates
Why do you say that, Laches?

Laches
Because courage is one thing and wisdom is another. They're obviously different.

Socrates
That's precisely what Nicias is denying.

Laches
Yes, and he's being ridiculous.

Socrates
How about we try to educate him instead of insulting him?

Nicias
No, Socrates — Laches doesn't want to educate me. He's just been shown up for talking nonsense, and now he wants to prove I'm doing the same thing.

Laches
You're right about that, Nicias — and you are talking nonsense, as I'll show you. Let me ask you something: don't doctors know the dangers of disease? Are they courageous just because they do? Or are you going to tell me doctors are the same as courageous men?

Nicias
Not at all.

Laches
And farmers know the dangers of farming. And every other craftsman knows what's fearful and what's hopeful in his own line of work. But that doesn't make any of them the least bit more courageous, does it?

Socrates
What do you make of Laches' point, Nicias? He seems to be onto something.

Nicias
He's saying something, but it's not true.

Socrates
How so?

Nicias
Because a doctor's knowledge only extends to the nature of health and disease. He can tell a sick man whether he's healthy or ill — and that's it. But Laches, do you think a doctor knows whether health or sickness is more fearful for any particular person? Wouldn't many a man be better off never getting up from his sickbed? Tell me — do you think it's always better to live than to die? Isn't death sometimes the better option?

Laches
Yes, I'd certainly say so.

Nicias
And do you think the same things are fearful for those who'd be better off dead as for those who'd be better off alive?

Laches
No, I suppose not.

Nicias
And do you think the doctor can tell which is which? Or any other craftsman, for that matter? Only the man who understands the real grounds for fear and hope can make that judgment. And that's the man I call courageous.

Socrates
Do you follow his argument, Laches?

Laches
I think so. He's saying fortune-tellers are courageous. Because who else would know whether it's better for someone to live or die? Tell me, Nicias — are you claiming to be a fortune-teller yourself? Because if not, then by your own definition, you're not courageous either.

Nicias
Hold on — you think it's the fortune-teller's job to know the grounds of hope and fear?

Laches
Of course. Whose job would it be?

Nicias
The man I'm talking about — and he's very different from a fortune-teller. The fortune-teller's job is to read the signs of things that are about to happen — death, disease, financial loss, victory, defeat. But whether experiencing or avoiding those things is actually better for a particular person? That's something the fortune-teller can't judge any better than anyone else.

Laches
I don't understand what Nicias is trying to say, Socrates. He's saying the courageous man isn't a fortune-teller, and he isn't a doctor, and he isn't anything else — unless Nicias means to say he's a god. In my opinion, Nicias just doesn't want to admit that he's talking nonsense. He's shuffling back and forth trying to hide the corner he's painted himself into.

You and I, Socrates, could have played the same game a few minutes ago, if we'd wanted to avoid looking inconsistent. If we were arguing in a courtroom, there might be a reason for that kind of dodge. But why would a man dress himself up in empty words at a friendly gathering like this?

Socrates
I completely agree that he shouldn't, Laches. But maybe Nicias is serious and not just talking for the sake of talking. Let's find out exactly what he means. If he has a good reason for what he's saying, we'll agree with him. If not, we'll set him straight.

Laches
Fine. You ask him, Socrates, if you want. I think I've asked enough.

Socrates
I don't see why not. I'll be asking for both of us.

Laches
Good.

Socrates
All right then, Nicias — or rather, tell us, since Laches and I are partners in this argument: you're saying courage is the knowledge of what is truly to be feared and truly to be hoped for?

Nicias
I am.

Socrates
And this knowledge isn't something just anyone has. A doctor doesn't have it, and a fortune-teller doesn't have it. They won't be courageous unless they acquire it — that's your position?

Nicias
It is.

Socrates
So, as the proverb goes, this definitely isn't something every pig would know. No pig could be courageous on your view.

Nicias
I don't think so.

Socrates
Clearly not, Nicias. Not even a pig as famous as the Crommyonian sow would qualify. And I'm not joking here. Anyone who accepts your definition — that courage is the knowledge of what to fear and what to hope for — can't allow that any wild animal is courageous. Unless you're prepared to say that a lion, or a leopard, or a boar has enough wisdom to know things that most humans can barely figure out. No — on your view, a lion and a deer and a bull and a monkey would all have equally little claim to courage.

Laches
Brilliant, Socrates. By the gods, that's a good point. I'd love to hear Nicias tell us whether these animals, which everyone agrees are courageous, are actually wiser than humans. Or will he have the nerve to deny their courage, against the opinion of the whole world?

Nicias
I don't call animals courageous, Laches — or anything else that doesn't fear danger because it doesn't understand danger. I call that fearless and senseless. Do you think I'd call little children courageous, just because they fear nothing since they know nothing? No — there's a difference between fearlessness and courage. In my view, true courage — thoughtful courage — is a rare quality, possessed by very few. But recklessness, boldness, fearlessness without forethought? Those are common enough — in men, women, children, and animals alike. The actions you and most people call "courageous," I call rash. The actions I call courageous are the ones guided by wisdom.

Laches
Look at that, Socrates — see how beautifully he dresses himself up with words, while trying to strip the honor of courage from the very people everyone acknowledges as courageous.

Nicias
Not at all, Laches. Don't worry — I'm perfectly willing to say that you are courageous, and Lamachus too, and plenty of other Athenians. And therefore wise.

Laches
I could reply to that. But I won't give you the satisfaction of calling me a proud Aexonian.

Socrates
Don't argue with him, Laches. I have a feeling you don't realize where he's getting this wisdom of his. He picked it up from my friend Damon, and Damon spends all his time with Prodicus, the Sophist who's considered the best of them all at splitting hairs over words.

Laches
Well, Socrates, splitting hairs is a much more fitting occupation for a Sophist than for a great statesman who's been chosen to lead the city.

Socrates
Well, my dear friend, a great statesman probably ought to have a great mind. And I think there's something in Nicias' definition of courage that deserves a serious examination.

Laches
Then examine it yourself, Socrates.

Socrates
That's what I'm going to do, my friend. But don't think you're off the hook. I expect you to stay engaged and think this through with me.

Laches
All right, if you think I should.

Socrates
I do. Now, Nicias, let's go back to the beginning. Remember, we started by treating courage as a part of virtue.

Nicias
That's right.

Socrates
And you agreed that it's one part among many. The other parts, taken together with courage, make up virtue as a whole.

Nicias
Certainly.

Socrates
Now, do we agree on what those other parts are? I'd say justice, temperance, and other qualities like them are all parts of virtue, along with courage. Wouldn't you?

Nicias
Absolutely.

Socrates
Good. So far we're on the same page. Now let's take the next step and see if we agree about what's fearful and what's hopeful. I don't want us thinking different things. So let me tell you my view, and you can correct me if I'm wrong.

In my opinion, what's fearful is what causes fear. And fear isn't directed at the present or the past — it's directed at expected future evil. Do you agree, Laches?

Laches
Completely, Socrates.

Socrates
So that's my position, Nicias. The things we fear are future evils. And the things that inspire hope are future goods — or at least, future things that aren't evil. Do you agree?

Nicias
I agree.

Socrates
And knowledge of these things is what you call courage?

Nicias
Precisely.

Socrates
Now let me see if you agree with Laches and me on a third point.

Nicias
What's that?

Socrates
Here it is. Laches and I believe that when it comes to any subject, there isn't one kind of knowledge for the past, a different one for the present, and a third for the future. There's just one knowledge that covers all three. Take medicine, for example — there's one science of medicine that understands health in the present, the past, and the future. Same with farming — one science covers the earth's produce in all times.

And as for the art of the general — you two would be my best witnesses — he's supposed to have excellent knowledge of the future. He claims to be the master, not the servant, of the fortune-teller, because he knows better what's actually happening and what's likely to happen in war. That's why the law puts the fortune-teller under the general, not the other way around. Isn't that right, Laches?

Laches
That's exactly right.

Socrates
And Nicias, do you agree that the same science understands the same things, whether they're future, present, or past?

Nicias
Yes, that's my view too, Socrates.

Socrates
Now, courage, you said, is the knowledge of what's to be feared and what's to be hoped for?

Nicias
Yes.

Socrates
And we've agreed that what's fearful and hopeful are future evils and future goods?

Nicias
True.

Socrates
And the same science deals with the same things whether they're future or in any other time?

Nicias
Yes.

Socrates
Then courage isn't just the science of the fearful and the hopeful — because those are only about the future. Courage, like every other science, must cover its subject in all times — past, present, and future.

Nicias
That seems to follow.

Socrates
So, Nicias, you've only given us about a third of what courage is. But our question was about the whole nature of courage. And on your view, it seems courage isn't just knowledge of what to fear and what to hope for. It's knowledge of practically all good and evil, in every time period. Would you like to revise your definition?

Nicias
I suppose I would, Socrates.

Socrates
But think about it, my friend. A man who had knowledge of all good and evil — how they are, how they've been, and how they'll be — would he be lacking any virtue at all? Wouldn't he have justice, temperance, piety — the whole lot? He'd know which things are truly dangerous and which aren't. He'd know how to deal with both gods and men. He'd be able to secure the good and guard against the evil in every situation.

Nicias
I think there's a lot of truth in that, Socrates.

Socrates
But then, Nicias, on this new definition of yours, courage wouldn't be just one part of virtue. It would be all of virtue.

Nicias
It would seem so.

Socrates
But we said courage is one of the parts of virtue?

Nicias
Yes, that's what we said.

Socrates
And that contradicts what we're saying now?

Nicias
It appears so.

Socrates
Then, Nicias, we haven't discovered what courage is.

Nicias
We haven't.

Laches
And yet, my dear Nicias, I had such high hopes that you'd crack it — since you were so contemptuous of the answers I gave Socrates. I was really counting on the wisdom of Damon to save you.

Nicias
That's lovely, Laches. You think it doesn't matter at all that you've been exposed as someone who doesn't know what courage is — all you care about is whether I'm in the same boat. Apparently, if we're both equally ignorant, that's just fine with you. You seem like most people — always looking at your neighbor instead of at yourself.

Well, I think we've said enough for today. If anything was poorly stated, it can be corrected later — with Damon's help, and others'. And when I've worked it out to my own satisfaction, I'll share what I've found with you. It sounds like you could use the help.

Laches
Oh, you're a real philosopher, Nicias — I can see that. But I'd still advise Lysimachus and Melesias not to rely on you or me for advice about their sons' education. As I said at the start, they should ask Socrates and not let him off the hook. If my own boys were old enough, that's what I'd do.

Nicias
I completely agree — if Socrates is willing to take them on. I wouldn't want anyone else as a tutor for my son Niceratus. But every time I bring it up with him, he recommends someone else and won't do it himself. Maybe he'll listen to you, Lysimachus.

Lysimachus
That's only fair, Nicias. I'd certainly do things for Socrates that I wouldn't do for many others. What do you say, Socrates — will you help? Are you willing to take a hand in improving these young men?

Socrates
It would be wrong of me, Lysimachus, to refuse to help anyone improve. And if this conversation had shown that I have knowledge that Nicias and Laches don't, then you'd be right to ask me specifically. But we're all in the same perplexity. None of us has found the answer. So why should any one of us be preferred over the others?

I don't think any of us should be. So here's my advice — and this can stay between us. I think every one of us should seek out the best teacher he can find — first for ourselves, because we badly need it, and then for the boys. We shouldn't spare any expense or effort. But I can't advise that we just stay the way we are now.

And if anyone laughs at us for going to school at our age, I'd quote Homer, who says:

"Modesty is not good for a needy man."

Let's forget what people might say. Let the education of these young men be our own education too.

Lysimachus
I like your proposal, Socrates. And since I'm the oldest, I'm also the most eager to go back to school alongside the boys. Here's what I ask: come to my house tomorrow at dawn, and we'll discuss this further. But for now, let's end here.

Socrates
I'll come to you tomorrow, Lysimachus, as you suggest — God willing.
