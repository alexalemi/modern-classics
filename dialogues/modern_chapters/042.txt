TIMAEUS (Part 1)

Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates
  Critias
  Timaeus
  Hermocrates


Socrates
One, two, three -- but where's the fourth, my dear Timaeus? Yesterday I was your guest; today you're supposed to be mine. There were four of you. Where's our missing man?

Timaeus
He's fallen ill, Socrates. He'd never have missed this willingly.

Socrates
Then it falls to you three to fill his place. Shall we get started?

Timaeus
Absolutely. You hosted us beautifully yesterday, and the rest of us are more than happy to return the favor.

Socrates
Do you remember the main points I asked you to address?

Timaeus
Most of them, yes. And you're here to remind us of anything we've forgotten. Actually -- if it's not too much trouble, could you briefly recap the whole thing? It'll help fix the details in our minds.

Socrates
Happy to. The main topic of yesterday's discussion was the ideal state -- what it would look like, and what kind of citizens would make it work best.

Timaeus
Yes, Socrates. And what you described was very much to our liking.

Socrates
Didn't we start by separating the farmers and craftsmen from the class responsible for defending the state?

Timaeus
Yes.

Socrates
And when we'd assigned each person a single role suited to their nature, we talked specifically about the warriors -- the guardians. We said they should protect the city against threats both internal and external. They should have no other job. They should be merciful when judging their own people -- who are, after all, their natural friends -- but fierce against enemies in battle.

Timaeus
Exactly.

Socrates
We said the guardians should have a temperament that combines two qualities in high degree: passionate spirit and a love of wisdom. That way they'd be gentle with their friends and fierce with their enemies.

Timaeus
Right.

Socrates
And their education? They were to be trained in physical exercise, in music, and in all the other subjects appropriate for them?

Timaeus
Yes, that's right.

Socrates
And once trained this way, they weren't to consider gold or silver or anything else as their private property. They'd be like professional soldiers, receiving just enough pay from those they protected to support a simple life. They'd share expenses, live together, and devote themselves entirely to cultivating excellence.

Timaeus
That was also part of it.

Socrates
And we didn't forget the women. We said their natures should be developed in harmony with the men's, and that they should share the same pursuits -- in wartime and in ordinary life alike.

Timaeus
That was as you say.

Socrates
And what about the arrangement for having children? That proposal was too unusual to forget: all wives and children were to be held in common. No one would know their own child. Instead, everyone would think of themselves as one great family -- those close in age would be brothers and sisters, the older generation would be parents and grandparents, the younger would be children and grandchildren.

Timaeus
Yes, that's easy to remember, as you say.

Socrates
And do you also remember how, in order to produce the best possible offspring, we said the chief magistrates -- both men and women -- should secretly arrange marriages through a rigged lottery? The best would pair with the best and the worst with the worst, but no one would know the fix was in. They'd all think their pairings were just the luck of the draw.

Timaeus
I remember.

Socrates
And the children of the best parents would be educated, while the children of the worst would be quietly dispersed among the general population? And as they all grew up, the rulers would be on the lookout -- promoting worthy citizens from below and demoting unworthy ones from above?

Timaeus
True.

Socrates
So have I covered all the main points of yesterday's discussion? Or have I left something out, my dear Timaeus?

Timaeus
Nothing, Socrates. That's exactly what you said.

Socrates
Good. Now let me tell you how I feel about the state we described. It's like seeing beautiful animals -- either in a painting or, better still, alive but standing perfectly still -- and being seized by a desire to see them in motion, to watch them in some struggle or contest worthy of their magnificent forms. That's exactly how I feel about our ideal city. I want to hear about it in action. I want someone to tell us how it went to war against its neighbors, how it entered battle in a manner befitting its training, and how its citizens showed -- through their deeds on the battlefield and their diplomacy at the negotiating table -- a greatness worthy of their education.

Now, I'll be honest: I know I'm not the man to tell that story properly. And I don't mean that as false modesty. The truth is, I think the poets -- past and present -- couldn't do it either. Not because I look down on poets, but everyone can see they're imitators. They copy best the life they've actually lived. Anything outside their experience is hard to capture, whether in action or in words. And the Sophists? They have plenty of clever words and beautiful theories, but they're wanderers -- they drift from city to city, they've never had a real home. I worry they'd completely miss what philosophers and statesmen actually do and say in the heat of war, in the thick of battle or at the negotiating table.

That leaves people of your caliber -- men who combine political experience with philosophical depth. Timaeus here comes from Locri in Italy, a city famous for its excellent laws. He's the equal of any citizen there in wealth and rank, he's held the most important offices in his state, and -- I believe -- he's reached the very heights of philosophy. Critias is someone every Athenian knows is no amateur in matters like these. And Hermocrates -- many witnesses assure me his natural gifts and education qualify him perfectly for this kind of inquiry.

That's exactly why, when I saw yesterday that you wanted me to describe the ideal state, I agreed so willingly. I knew that if you were willing, no one alive was better qualified to take the discussion to its next stage -- to put our city in a suitable war and show it fighting in a way worthy of its character. So after I completed my part, I gave you this task in return. You discussed it among yourselves and agreed to host me today with a feast of ideas, just as I hosted you. And here I am, dressed for the banquet, ready and eager.

Hermocrates
And we won't disappoint you, Socrates, as Timaeus says. We have no excuse to refuse. In fact, yesterday, as soon as we arrived at the guest room where we're staying with Critias -- actually, even on the way there -- we were already talking it over. Critias told us about an ancient tradition. Critias, would you repeat it for Socrates? He can help us decide whether it fits what he's looking for.

Critias
I will, if Timaeus -- our other partner here -- approves.

Timaeus
Absolutely.

Critias
Then listen, Socrates. This is a story that's strange but absolutely true, vouched for by Solon himself -- the wisest of the Seven Sages. He was a relative and close friend of my great-grandfather Dropides, as he himself says in several passages of his poetry. He told the story to my grandfather Critias, who remembered it and repeated it to us.

There were, Solon said, great and marvelous deeds performed by the ancient city of Athens -- deeds that have been forgotten over the vast stretches of time and through the destruction of civilizations. One deed in particular surpassed all the rest. We'll tell that story now. It will serve as our tribute to you, Socrates, and as a true hymn of praise to the goddess Athena on this, her festival day.

Socrates
Excellent. And what is this famous ancient action of the Athenians, which Critias says -- on the authority of Solon -- is not mere legend but actual fact?

Critias
I'll tell you an old story that I heard from an old man. My grandfather Critias was nearly ninety when he told it, and I was about ten. It was the day of the Apaturia -- the festival called the Registration of Youth, where our parents traditionally gave prizes for recitations. We boys recited poems by various poets, and many of us performed poems by Solon, which were still popular at that time.

One member of our tribe -- either because he genuinely believed it or to flatter my grandfather -- said that in his opinion, Solon wasn't just the wisest of men but also the noblest of poets. The old man -- I remember this vividly -- brightened up and said with a smile: "Yes, Amynander, if only Solon had made poetry the serious business of his life and finished the tale he brought back from Egypt! But he was forced to set it aside because of all the political upheaval he found when he returned home. In my opinion, if he'd completed it, he would have been as famous as Homer or Hesiod or any poet you could name."

"And what was the tale about, Critias?" asked Amynander.

"About the greatest action the Athenians ever performed -- one that should have been the most famous of all, but through the passage of time and the destruction of those who did it, the story hasn't survived."

"Tell us the whole story," said the other. "How did Solon hear it? From whom?"

And my grandfather replied:

"In the Egyptian Delta, at the point where the Nile divides, there's a district called Sais, and the great city of that district is also called Sais -- the city that King Amasis came from. The citizens worship a founding goddess whom they call Neith in the Egyptian language, and whom they claim is the same as the Greek Athena. They're great admirers of the Athenians and say they're related to them in some way.

"Solon traveled to this city and was received with great honor. He questioned the priests who were most knowledgeable about ancient history and made a discovery: neither he nor any other Greek knew anything worth mentioning about truly ancient times.

"On one occasion, wanting to draw the priests into discussing antiquity, Solon began telling them about the most ancient events in Greece -- about Phoroneus, who is called 'the first man,' and about Niobe. He told the story of Deucalion and Pyrrha surviving the great flood, and he traced the genealogy of their descendants, trying to calculate how many years ago these events had taken place.

"At this point, one of the priests -- a very old man -- said: 'Oh Solon, Solon -- you Greeks are always children. There isn't an old man among you.'

"Solon asked what he meant.

"'I mean,' the priest replied, 'that you're all young in your minds. You have no ancient tradition handed down through the ages, no knowledge hoary with time. And I'll tell you why. There have been, and will be again, many destructions of the human race, from many causes. The greatest come from fire and water, but lesser ones come from countless other causes.

"'Take your own story about Phaethon, the son of Helios, who yoked the horses of his father's chariot but couldn't control them, and burned up everything on earth before being destroyed by a thunderbolt. That sounds like a myth, but it actually points to a real phenomenon: a shift in the heavenly bodies that orbit the earth, causing a great conflagration on the surface at long intervals. When this happens, people living on mountains and in dry, elevated places suffer more than those near rivers or the sea. The Nile, our ever-faithful savior, protects us from this kind of disaster.

"'On the other hand, when the gods cleanse the earth with a flood of water, the survivors in your country are herdsmen and shepherds living in the mountains, while those in the cities are swept by the rivers into the sea. In our land, neither then nor at any other time does water come down from above onto the fields -- it always tends to rise from below. That's why the traditions preserved here are the most ancient in the world.

"'The truth is, wherever extreme cold or heat doesn't prevent it, human beings exist -- sometimes in greater numbers, sometimes fewer. And whatever remarkable deeds have been done, whether in your country or ours or any other place we know about, they've all been recorded by us long ago and preserved in our temples. But you Greeks -- just when you and other nations are beginning to develop writing and the other tools of civilization, down comes the flood from heaven like a plague, leaving only the illiterate and uneducated survivors. And so you have to start all over again, like children, knowing nothing of what happened in ancient times, either among us or among yourselves.

"'Those genealogies you just recited to us, Solon? They're nothing but children's fairy tales. First of all, you remember only one flood, when there were many before it. Second, you don't know that the finest and noblest race of people who ever lived once dwelled in your land -- and that you and your entire city are descended from a tiny surviving remnant of them. You don't know this because for many generations after the catastrophe, the survivors died without leaving any written record.

"'For there was a time, Solon, before the greatest of all floods, when the city that is now Athens was first in war and first in the excellence of its laws -- the best governed city under heaven, performing the noblest deeds and possessing the finest constitution of any city that tradition has preserved.'

"Solon was astonished and earnestly begged the priests to tell him everything, in order, about these ancient citizens.

"'You're welcome to hear it, Solon,' said the priest. 'Both for your sake and for your city's sake, and above all for the sake of the goddess who is the common patron and parent and educator of both our cities. She founded your city a thousand years before ours, receiving from Earth and Hephaestus the seed of your race, and afterward she founded ours. The age of our civilization, as recorded in our sacred registers, is eight thousand years. As for your citizens of nine thousand years ago, I'll briefly describe their laws and their most famous deed. We can go through the full details later, at our leisure, in the sacred records themselves.

"'If you compare their laws with ours, you'll find many parallels. First, there's the priestly class, separate from all others. Then the artisans, each trade working independently and not intermixing. Then the classes of shepherds, hunters, and farmers. And you'll notice that the warriors are a class apart, commanded by law to devote themselves solely to military training. Their weapons are shields and spears -- a style of equipment the goddess taught to us Asians first, and then to you Greeks.

"'As for learning, observe how our system from the very beginning encompassed the whole order of things, extending even to prophecy and medicine, and drawing from divine principles everything useful for human life, plus all related branches of knowledge.

"'All this order and arrangement the goddess first gave to you when she established your city. She chose the spot where you were born because she saw that its favorable climate would produce the wisest people. Being a lover of both war and wisdom, she settled the place most likely to produce people most like herself. And there you lived, with laws like these and even better ones, excelling all humanity in every form of excellence -- as befitting the children and students of the gods.

"'Many great and marvelous deeds of your state are recorded in our histories. But one surpasses all the rest in greatness and valor. Our records tell of a mighty power that launched an unprovoked assault against all of Europe and Asia -- and your city put an end to it.

"'This power came from the Atlantic Ocean. In those days, the Atlantic was navigable, and there was an island situated in front of the strait that you Greeks call the Pillars of Heracles. The island was larger than Libya and Asia combined. From it, travelers could reach other islands, and from those islands they could pass to the entire opposite continent, which surrounds the true ocean. You see, the sea within the Pillars of Heracles is really just a harbor with a narrow entrance. That outer sea is the real ocean, and the land that encircles it can truly be called a boundless continent.

"'On this island of Atlantis there existed a great and wonderful empire that ruled over the whole island and several others, and also over parts of the continent. What's more, the rulers of Atlantis had conquered the parts of Libya within the Pillars as far as Egypt, and parts of Europe as far as Tyrrhenia.

"'This vast power, gathered into one force, attempted in a single campaign to enslave your country and ours and every nation within the strait. And then, Solon, your city's excellence shone forth for all the world to see. Athens stood out among all nations for courage and military skill. At first she led the Greeks. Then, when the others fell away, she was forced to stand alone. She faced the most extreme danger, but she defeated and triumphed over the invaders. She preserved from slavery those peoples not yet conquered, and she generously liberated all the rest of us who live within the Pillars.

"'But afterward, there were violent earthquakes and floods. In a single terrible day and night, all your warriors were swallowed up by the earth, and the island of Atlantis likewise sank beneath the sea and disappeared. That's why the ocean in that region is still impassable and unnavigable -- there's a barrier of shallow mud just below the surface, left behind when the island went down.'"

I've told you briefly, Socrates, what old Critias heard from Solon and passed along to us. And when you were describing your ideal city yesterday, this story came flooding back to me. I was astonished at how -- by some mysterious coincidence -- your vision matched Solon's account in almost every detail. But I didn't want to speak up at the time. It had been so long since I'd heard the story that I'd forgotten too much. I decided I needed to run through the whole narrative in my mind first, and then I'd be ready to share it.

That's why I agreed so readily to your request yesterday. I could see that in cases like this the hardest part is finding a suitable story -- and we already had one.

So as Hermocrates told you, on the way home yesterday I immediately shared the tale with my companions, filling in what I remembered. Then last night, thinking it over, I recovered nearly the whole thing. It's really true what they say about childhood memories -- they make an extraordinary impression. I'm not sure I could repeat everything we discussed yesterday, but I'd be amazed if I forgot any of this story, which I heard so long ago. I listened to it as a boy with total fascination. The old man was glad to teach me, and I made him repeat it again and again, until it was branded into my memory like a permanent picture.

First thing this morning, I rehearsed the whole story for my companions so they'd have it fresh too. And now, Socrates -- to wrap up this introduction -- I'm ready to tell you everything, not just the main points but the details, exactly as they were told to me.

The city and citizens you described yesterday as a thought experiment -- we're going to bring them into the real world. We'll say that your ideal city is ancient Athens, and the imaginary citizens of your republic were our actual ancestors, the ones the Egyptian priest described. They'll match perfectly. There'll be no inconsistency in saying that the citizens of your ideal state are these ancient Athenians.

Let's divide the work among us, and we'll all do our best to carry out the assignment you've given us gracefully. So tell us, Socrates -- does this narrative suit our purpose, or should we look for something else?

Socrates
And what could possibly be better than this, Critias? It's a natural fit for the goddess's festival, and it has the enormous advantage of being fact rather than fiction. Where else would we find anything like it? We can't, so you must tell the tale. Good luck to you -- and in return for my lecture yesterday, I'll now sit back and listen.

Critias
Let me explain the order we've arranged for our program. Our plan is this: Timaeus, who is the most accomplished astronomer among us and who has made the study of nature his specialty, will speak first. He'll start with the origin of the universe and work his way down to the creation of human beings. Then I'll take the humans he's created -- some of whom will have benefited from the excellent education you described -- and, following Solon's account and his authority, I'll bring them before the court of public attention as Athenian citizens. They'll be the very Athenians whom the sacred Egyptian records have rescued from oblivion. From that point on, we'll speak of them as Athenians and fellow citizens.

Socrates
I can see I'm going to receive a magnificent feast of ideas in return for mine. Well then, Timaeus -- it's your turn to speak next. But first, as is customary, call upon the gods.

Timaeus
Every person with any sense of reverence, Socrates, calls on God at the start of every undertaking, whether small or great. And we, who are about to discuss the nature of the universe -- how it was created, or whether it has always existed -- would be completely out of our minds not to invoke the gods and goddesses and pray that our words may be acceptable to them and consistent with each other. Let that be our prayer. But let me also urge myself to speak in whatever way will be clearest to you and most true to my own understanding.

First, then, we need to draw a fundamental distinction. Ask yourself: What is the thing that always exists and never changes? And what is the thing that is always changing and never truly exists?

The first -- that which is grasped by intelligence and reason -- is always the same. The second -- that which is perceived by the senses, believed in on the basis of opinion, without reason -- is always in a process of coming into being and passing away. It never fully is.

Now, everything that comes into being must have a cause. Nothing can be created without one. And whenever a craftsman looks to what is eternal and unchanging as his model, and shapes his work after that pattern, the result is necessarily beautiful and perfect. But when he uses something created as his model -- something that is itself in flux -- the result will not be beautiful or perfect.

So here's the question: Was the universe always in existence, without any beginning? Or was it created -- did it have a starting point?

It was created. It's visible, tangible, and physical -- and all things that can be perceived by the senses are in a process of becoming. They've come into being. And what comes into being must have been brought into being by some cause.

But the father and maker of this whole universe is hard to find. And even if we found him, it would be impossible to describe him to everyone. Still, there's another question we have to ask: which model did the craftsman use when he made the world? Did he look to the eternal and unchanging pattern? Or to something that is itself created?

If the world is truly beautiful and its maker truly good, then clearly he looked to the eternal. And the world is beautiful, and its maker is good. So he must have looked to the eternal pattern. This means the world is a copy -- an image of something eternal and intelligible.

Now, here's an important principle about how we talk about all this. Words should be appropriate to their subject matter. When we speak about what is permanent and stable and grasped by the intellect, our words should be as permanent and unshakable as possible -- irrefutable, as far as words can be. But when we speak about what is only a copy of that reality -- something that merely resembles the original -- our words need only be likely and proportionate. As being stands to becoming, so truth stands to belief.

So don't be surprised, Socrates, if in discussing the gods and the origin of the universe, we can't produce accounts that are perfectly exact and self-consistent at every point. Be satisfied if our explanations are as probable as anyone else's -- and remember that I, the speaker, and you, the listeners, are only human. On a subject like this, the best we can do is a likely story. We shouldn't demand anything more.

Socrates
Beautifully said, Timaeus, and we accept that completely. The prelude is wonderful. Now please -- give us the main performance.

Timaeus
Let me tell you, then, why the creator made this world of change and becoming.

He was good. And what is good can never feel jealousy toward anything. Being free of jealousy, he wanted everything to be as much like himself as possible. This is the deepest and truest reason for the creation of the world, and we'd be wise to accept it on the testimony of thoughtful people: God wanted everything to be good and nothing to be bad, as far as that was achievable.

So here's what he found: the whole visible realm, not at rest but moving in a chaotic, disorderly way. And he brought order out of that disorder, because he judged that order was in every way better than chaos.

Now, the work of the best maker could never be anything less than the most beautiful. And as the creator thought about it, he realized that among visible things, no unintelligent creature, taken as a whole, could ever be more beautiful than an intelligent one, taken as a whole. And intelligence can't exist in anything that doesn't have a soul.

So this is what he did: he put intelligence into soul, and soul into body. That way, his creation would be the most beautiful and excellent work possible. And so, speaking in terms of what's likely, we can say: the world is a living creature, truly endowed with soul and intelligence, brought into being by the providence of God.

Now, the next question: What kind of living creature did the creator model the world on?

It would be unworthy to compare it to any particular species -- any creature that exists as merely a part of the whole. Nothing modeled on something incomplete could be truly beautiful. No -- let's say the world is made in the image of that great Living Being of which all other living things, individually and in their species, are portions. The eternal model contains within itself all intelligible beings, just as this physical world contains us and every other visible creature.

God intended to make the world resemble the most beautiful and complete of all intelligible beings. So he created one visible living creature that contains within itself all living creatures of every kind.

But is there one world, or are there many -- perhaps infinitely many? There can only be one. If the copy is to match its original, there must be just one. The original Living Being that contains all intelligible creatures can't have a companion or a double -- because then you'd need yet another being that contained both, and the real model would be that larger one, not the two. So in order that the world might be unique, like the perfect Living Being it imitates, the creator made not two worlds or an infinite number, but one and only one. There is, and will forever be, one single created universe.

Now, what is created must necessarily be physical -- both visible and touchable. Nothing is visible without fire, and nothing is solid or touchable without earth. So God, at the very beginning of creation, made the body of the universe out of fire and earth.

But two things can't be properly joined without a third to bind them -- there needs to be some connecting bond. And the most beautiful bond is one that creates the most complete unity between itself and the things it connects. Proportion achieves this best.

Here's how proportion works: whenever you have three numbers in a series, and the middle term stands in the same ratio to the last as the first does to the middle, then -- since the middle becomes both first and last, and the extremes both become middles -- they all necessarily end up in agreement, becoming, in a sense, one and the same.

Now, if the universe's body were flat -- a surface without depth -- a single middle term would be enough to bind it to the extremes. But the world has to be solid, three-dimensional. And solid bodies are always connected not by one mean but by two.

So God placed water and air between fire and earth, and made them proportional to each other -- as far as possible: as fire is to air, so air is to water; as air is to water, so water is to earth. In this way he bound together a visible, touchable universe. These four elements, joined in proportion, gave the world the spirit of friendship, a harmony so strong that nothing could break it apart except the very being who assembled it.

The creation used up the entirety of each of the four elements. The creator built the world out of all the fire, all the water, all the air, and all the earth there is -- leaving nothing out. He had three reasons:

First, the cosmic animal should be as complete and perfect as possible, made of complete and perfect parts.

Second, it should be one of a kind, with no leftover material from which someone might build another world.

Third, it should be immune to aging and disease. He understood that when external forces -- heat, cold, and other powerful agents -- attack a body from the outside, they break it down, bring on disease and old age, and cause it to waste away. So by making the world a single whole, containing everything within itself, he made it immune to decay from outside forces.

And he gave the world the shape that suited it best. For the living creature that was to contain all other creatures, the right shape is the one that contains all other shapes. So he made it a sphere -- perfectly round, with every point on its surface equidistant from the center. The sphere is the most perfect and the most self-similar of all shapes.

He made its surface completely smooth. Why? Because the world had no need for eyes -- there was nothing outside it to see. No need for ears -- nothing outside to hear. No atmosphere around it to breathe. No need for organs to take in food or expel waste, since there was nothing outside it. Nothing entered it and nothing left it. By design, it feeds on its own waste, and everything it does or experiences takes place within itself. The creator judged that a self-sufficient being would be far more excellent than one dependent on external things.

Since it had nothing to grab or defend against, he gave it no hands. Since it had no ground to walk on, he gave it no feet. Instead, he assigned it the motion best suited to its spherical form -- the one most appropriate to intelligence and thought: rotation. He made it spin in place, turning in a circle within its own boundaries, and he took away all six of the other possible motions. Since circular motion requires no feet, the universe was made without legs.

Such was the grand plan of the eternal God for the god that was to be. He gave it a smooth, perfect body -- a sphere with every surface point equidistant from the center, whole and flawless, made from flawless elements. He placed the soul at the center and spread it throughout the entire body, also wrapping it around the outside. He set the universe spinning in a circle -- one and solitary, yet able to keep itself company through its own excellence, needing no friend or companion. With all these purposes in mind, he created the world as a blessed god.

Now, God didn't actually create the soul after the body, even though I'm describing them in that order. That's just a consequence of our somewhat haphazard way of telling a story -- we humans are creatures of chance, after all. In reality, God made the soul first, prior to the body in both origin and rank, to be its ruler and mistress.

Here's how he made the soul. He took three ingredients:

The first was the nature of the Same -- that which is indivisible and unchanging.

The second was the nature of the Other -- that which is divisible and connected to physical bodies.

From these two he blended a third, intermediate kind of substance, partaking of the nature of both Same and Other. Then he took all three -- Same, Other, and this blended Essence -- and mixed them into a single compound. This took some force, because the nature of the Other resisted being combined with the Same. But he compressed them together into one unified mixture.

Then he began to divide this mixture into portions, according to a specific mathematical pattern.

First, he took away one portion (1).
Then a second, double the first (2).
Then a third, one and a half times the second and three times the first (3).
Then a fourth, double the second (4).
Then a fifth, three times the third (9).
Then a sixth, eight times the first (8).
Then a seventh, twenty-seven times the first (27).

So picture two series interlocking: 1, 2, 4, 8 (the doubles) and 1, 3, 9, 27 (the triples).

Next, he filled in the gaps. Between each pair in these series, he inserted two kinds of means:

The first kind -- what mathematicians call the arithmetic mean -- exceeds one extreme and falls short of the other by the same amount. For example, between 1 and 2, the arithmetic mean is 4/3: it's greater than 1 by one-third of 1, and less than 2 by one-third of 2.

The second kind -- the harmonic mean -- exceeds and is exceeded by the same fraction of the extremes themselves.

Where the intervals of 3/2 and 4/3 and 9/8 were created by these connecting terms, he filled up all the remaining gaps of 4/3 with intervals of 9/8, leaving in each case a small remainder. This leftover interval had the ratio of 256 to 243.

And so the entire original mixture was completely used up.

Then he split the whole compound lengthwise into two strips. He laid these two strips across each other in the shape of the letter X, then bent each one into a circle, joining the ends so they formed two rings -- connected to each other at the point opposite where they originally crossed. He set both circles revolving uniformly on the same axis.

The outer circle he called the motion of the Same. The inner circle he called the motion of the Other -- or the Different.

The motion of the Same he directed to the right, along the horizontal. The motion of the Different he directed diagonally, to the left. He gave supremacy to the motion of the Same, leaving it single and undivided. But the inner circle -- the motion of the Different -- he split into seven unequal circles, with their intervals in ratios of two and three (three of each kind). He set these orbits moving in opposite directions: three of them (the Sun, Mercury, and Venus) at equal speed, and the remaining four (the Moon, Saturn, Mars, and Jupiter) at speeds unequal to the first three and to each other, but all in due proportion.

When the creator had finished crafting the soul, he formed the physical universe within it and united the two, center to center. The soul, spread everywhere from the center to the outermost edge of the heavens -- and wrapped around the outside too -- began spinning within itself. And in that moment, she began her divine, unending, rational life, a life that endures for all of time.

The body of the heavens is visible. The soul is invisible. But the soul participates in reason and harmony, and being the finest creation of the finest and most eternal of makers, she is the best of all created things.

Because she's composed of three elements -- Same, Other, and Essence -- and because she's divided and reunited in exact proportion, the soul, whenever she makes contact with anything, is able to declare with precision: "This is the same as that," or "This is different from that." She can say what a thing is related to, what affects it, and in what way -- whether in the world of constant change or in the world of unchanging Being.

When reason operates in the circle of the Different and that circle moves truly and communicates its perceptions to the whole soul, the result is reliable opinion and firm belief. But when reason is engaged with the intelligible world, and the circle of the Same spins smoothly and declares what it finds, the result is genuine intelligence and knowledge. And if anyone says that the thing in which both of these operate is something other than the soul, they're saying the exact opposite of the truth.

When the father and creator saw the creature he'd made -- this living, moving image of the eternal gods -- he was delighted. And in his delight, he wanted to make the copy even more like the original.

Now, the original is eternal. But to make a created thing fully eternal was impossible. So he came up with a solution: he would create a moving image of eternity. As he set the heavens in order, he made this image -- eternal, but moving according to number, while eternity itself rests in perfect unity. This image is what we call time.

Days and nights, months and years -- none of these existed before the heavens were created. He invented them when he built the universe. They're all portions of time. And when we say "was" and "will be," we're using forms of time that we carelessly apply to eternal being. But strictly speaking, only "is" properly applies to what is eternal. "Was" and "will be" should only be used for things that move through time, because they describe processes of change. What is eternally the same doesn't become older or younger. It never "was" or "has become" or "will be" in any future moment. It isn't subject to any of the conditions that affect moving, physical things -- conditions that generation produces. These are all forms of time, which imitates eternity and moves according to the laws of number. But perhaps this whole topic deserves its own separate discussion.

In any case, time and the heavens came into being at the same instant -- created together so that, if they should ever be dissolved, they would be dissolved together. The universe was made after the pattern of the eternal -- to resemble it as closely as possible.

The sun, moon, and five other stars -- the ones we call the planets -- were created to mark and preserve the numbers of time. God made their bodies and placed them in the orbits where the circle of the Different was revolving -- seven stars in seven orbits.

First, the moon in the orbit nearest to earth. Next, the sun in the second orbit above the earth. Then came the morning star (Venus) and the star sacred to Hermes (Mercury), moving in orbits that share the sun's speed but travel in the opposite direction. That's why the sun, Mercury, and Venus seem to overtake and be overtaken by each other in turn.

To describe where he placed all the other stars, and all the reasons why -- that would be a longer digression than the main subject deserves. We can save that for another time, when we have more leisure.

When all the stars necessary for the creation of time had been set in their proper motions and had become living creatures -- their bodies bound by vital chains, each one assigned its task -- they began revolving. They moved in the path of the Different, which runs diagonally and passes through and is governed by the motion of the Same. Some had larger orbits, some smaller. The ones with smaller orbits moved faster; those with larger orbits, more slowly. But because of the motion of the Same, the fastest-moving ones appeared to be overtaken by the slower ones, even though in reality they were overtaking them. The motion of the Same made them all spiral, and because some moved in one direction and some in another, the one that moved most slowly relative to the Same -- which was itself the fastest circle -- appeared to follow it most closely.

So that there might be some clear way to measure the relative speeds of the heavenly bodies as they traveled their eight courses, God lit a fire in the second orbit from the earth -- what we now call the sun -- so that it might illuminate the whole sky. This allowed all living creatures meant to know number to learn arithmetic from the revolution of the Same.

And so night and day were created -- the period of one complete revolution of the most intelligent circle. A month is completed when the moon finishes its orbit and catches up with the sun. A year is completed when the sun finishes its own orbit.

Most people haven't noticed the periods of the other planets. They have no names for them and don't measure them against each other using numbers. They barely realize that the wanderings of these stars, infinite in number and astonishing in their variety, constitute time itself. Yet it's not hard to see that the perfect number of time is fulfilled when all eight revolutions, each moving at its own proper speed, complete their cycles together and return to their starting point -- measured by the revolution of the Same, that steadiest and most uniform of all motions.

This is how and why those stars that reverse their motion in the sky were created: so that the created universe might imitate the eternal as closely as possible, and be as perfect a likeness as it could be of the perfect, intelligible Living Being.

Up to this point -- up to the birth of time -- the created universe resembled its model. But it was still incomplete, because it didn't yet contain all the kinds of living creatures. What remained, the creator proceeded to fashion after the pattern of the original.

In the eternal Living Being, the mind perceives four kinds of creatures. The creator thought the physical world should contain the same four kinds:

First, the heavenly race of the gods.
Second, winged creatures whose path is through the air.
Third, creatures that live in water.
Fourth, creatures that walk on land.

The heavenly race he made mostly out of fire, so they'd be the brightest and most beautiful of all things. He shaped them after the form of the universe -- as circles -- and set them to follow the intelligent motion of the outermost sphere, distributing them across the entire sky, which was to be a true cosmos -- a glorious world spangled all over with stars.

He gave each star two kinds of motion. The first: rotation in place, always thinking the same consistent thoughts about the same things. The second: forward motion, governed by the revolution of the Same. The other five directions of motion he denied them, so that each might achieve the highest perfection.

This is the origin of the fixed stars -- divine, eternal beings, forever revolving in the same way in the same spot. The other stars, the ones that reverse their motion and undergo deviations, were created in the way I've already described.

The earth -- our nurse, clinging to the axis that runs through the center of the universe -- he made to be the guardian and maker of night and day, the first and eldest of the gods within the heavens.

To describe all the figures of the stars circling like dancers, their alignments, their returns upon themselves as their orbits loop back, which ones meet in conjunction and which stand in opposition, which pass behind and which pass before one another, when they're eclipsed from our view and reappear again, sending signs and omens to those who can't calculate their motions -- to attempt all this without a visible model of the heavens would be a waste of effort. So let that be enough on this topic. Our discussion of the created, visible gods is complete.

As for knowing the origin of the other divinities -- the traditional gods -- that's beyond us. We have to accept the accounts handed down by the ancients, who claimed to be descendants of the gods. They must have known their own ancestors, surely. Even though they offer no rigorous proof, we should believe them when they speak of their own family history. That's the custom.

So according to tradition, the genealogy of these gods goes like this: Oceanus and Tethys were children of Earth and Heaven. From them came Phorcys, Cronos, Rhea, and all that generation. From Cronos and Rhea came Zeus and Hera and all their siblings, and their children in turn.

Now, when all the gods had come into being -- both those who revolve visibly in the sky and those of a more hidden nature -- the creator of the universe addressed them with these words:

"Gods, children of gods -- you are my works, and I am your maker and father. What I have made is indissoluble, because that is my will. Now, anything that has been bound together can be undone -- but only a malicious being would want to destroy what is harmonious and good. So although you are created beings and therefore not inherently immortal or indestructible, you will never be dissolved, because my will is a stronger bond than the physical bonds that held you together at your birth.

"Now hear my instructions. Three kinds of mortal beings remain to be created. Without them, the universe will be incomplete -- it won't contain every kind of living creature it needs to be perfect. But if I created them myself and gave them life directly from my hands, they'd be equal to gods.

"So here's what must happen: you create them, following my example. The part of them that deserves to be called immortal and divine -- the guiding principle within those who choose to follow justice and follow you -- that divine seed, I will sow myself. I'll plant the beginning. Then I'll hand the rest of the work over to you. Weave the mortal with the immortal. Make living creatures, give them food, let them grow -- and receive them back when they die."

So he spoke. And then, once more, he poured the leftover ingredients into the same mixing bowl where he'd previously blended the soul of the universe. He mixed them in roughly the same proportions, though they weren't as pure as before -- diluted to the second and third degree.

When the mixture was ready, he divided it into individual souls, one for each star. He placed each soul upon its star as if on a chariot, and showed them the nature of the universe. He declared to them the laws of destiny:

Their first birth would be the same for all -- no one would be disadvantaged. They would be sown into the instruments of time -- the planets -- and emerge as the most reverent of living creatures. Since human nature comes in two forms, the superior form would be called "man."

When they were implanted in physical bodies (as necessity required), and were constantly gaining and losing parts of their bodily substance, the following would inevitably occur:

First, they would all possess a common faculty of sensation, arising from powerful impressions.

Second, they would experience love -- a mixture of pleasure and pain. Also fear and anger, and all the emotions related to or opposed to these.

If they mastered these emotions, they'd live justly. If the emotions mastered them, they'd live unjustly.

Whoever lived well during their allotted time would return to dwell in their native star and enjoy a blessed existence there. But whoever failed -- at the second birth, they would be reborn as a woman. And if they still didn't stop doing wrong, they'd continue to be transformed into whatever animal matched the kind of wickedness they'd developed. They wouldn't be free from these cycles of transformation until they followed the revolution of the Same within themselves, overcame by reason the turbulent, irrational mob of later additions -- fire, air, water, and earth -- and returned to the form of their first and best state.

Having laid down all these laws so that he wouldn't be responsible for any creature's future evil, the creator sowed some souls in the earth, some in the moon, and some in the other instruments of time. Then he handed over to the younger gods the job of fashioning mortal bodies, and told them to supply whatever the human soul still needed. They were to govern the mortal creature as wisely as they could, steering it away from all evils except those it brought upon itself.

After giving all these instructions, the creator returned to his own customary state of being. His children heard and obeyed their father's word. They received from him the immortal principle of a mortal creature, and in imitation of their own maker, they borrowed portions of fire, earth, water, and air from the world (to be returned later). They welded these elements together -- not with the unbreakable bonds that held them together, but with tiny invisible rivets -- making each individual body out of all four elements and fastening the circuits of the immortal soul inside a body that was in constant flux.

These circuits, trapped inside this ever-flowing river of the body, could neither fully control it nor be fully controlled by it. They were swept along, lurching forward and back, so that the whole creature moved chaotically -- progressing, yes, but in a disorderly, irrational way, stumbling in all six directions: forward and backward, left and right, up and down.

The surging flood of nourishment flowing through the body was bad enough, but the disturbances caused by contact with the outside world were even worse. Whenever someone's body collided with external fire, or with solid earth, or with rushing water, or was caught in a blast of wind, the resulting motions were transmitted through the body all the way to the soul. (That's why we still call all such disturbances "sensations.")

These sensations created an enormous upheaval. They joined with the constantly flowing stream of bodily processes to shake and disturb the circuits of the soul. They completely blocked the revolution of the Same, preventing it from advancing and maintaining control. They threw the circuit of the Different into such disorder that the three double intervals (between 1, 2, 4, 8) and the three triple intervals (between 1, 3, 9, 27), along with all the connecting ratios of 3:2, 4:3, and 9:8 -- though these couldn't be fully undone by anyone except the being who originally assembled them -- were twisted and warped in every possible way. The circles were broken and thrown into chaos. Sometimes they moved backward, sometimes sideways, sometimes upside down.

Imagine someone standing on their head, feet in the air. To that person and to anyone looking at them, left and right are reversed. That's the kind of confusion the soul's circuits experienced -- and worse. Whenever the distorted revolutions encountered something in the outside world that belonged to the class of the Same or the class of the Different, they called it the exact opposite of what it was. They became deluded and foolish. No circuit maintained the guiding, directing power it should have had. And if violent sensations invaded from outside and dragged the whole vessel of the soul along with them, the soul's circuits -- though seeming to be in control -- were actually being controlled.

Because of all this, the soul, when first encased in a mortal body, starts out without intelligence. But when the flood of growth and nourishment subsides, and the soul's circuits calm down and find their proper paths as time goes on, the circles gradually return to their natural form. Their revolutions correct themselves. They begin calling Same and Different by their right names, and the person who possesses them becomes rational.

If this natural development is supported by proper upbringing and education, a person achieves the fullness and health of a complete human being and escapes the worst disease of all -- ignorance. But anyone who neglects education stumbles through life and returns to the underworld incomplete and useless.

But that's a later stage of the story. Right now we need to be more precise about what comes first: the creation of the body and its parts, and why the soul was made the way it was -- by what design and for what purpose. So let's hold to that.

First, the gods, imitating the spherical shape of the universe, enclosed the two divine circuits of the soul in a spherical body -- what we now call the head. This is the most divine part of us, the master of everything else. The gods gave the head a body to serve as its vehicle: the whole rest of us -- the trunk and limbs -- exists for the sake of the head.

So that the head wouldn't just roll around helplessly on the uneven ground, they gave it the body as a means of getting around. That's why the body has length, and four flexible limbs that can extend and grip, allowing it to travel through any terrain, carrying the dwelling place of our most sacred and divine part high above.

That's the origin of our legs and arms. And since the gods considered the front of the human body more honorable and better suited to lead than the back, they designed us to move mostly forward. That's why a person's front is distinct from their back.

On the front of the vessel of the head, they set a face and installed in it the organs that serve the soul's foresight. They decreed that this side -- the one with natural authority -- should face forward.

The first organs they created were the eyes, designed to carry light. Here's how they work:

The gods fashioned a special substance out of fire -- not the kind that burns, but the kind that gives off a gentle, mild light, similar to daylight. They made the pure inner fire of the body flow out through the eyes in a smooth, dense stream, compressing the whole eye (especially the center) so that it blocked out everything coarse and let only this pure fire pass through.

When the stream of vision meets daylight, like falls upon like, and they merge into a single unified body along the line of sight -- wherever the inner light meets an external object. This entire visual ray, being uniform throughout, transmits the motions of whatever it touches (or whatever touches it) through the whole body to the soul, producing the sensation we call sight.

But when night falls and the kindred external fire withdraws, the visual stream is cut off. Meeting an unlike medium, it changes and is extinguished -- it's no longer the same as the surrounding atmosphere, which has lost its fire. The eye stops seeing, and we feel drowsy. The eyelids -- which the gods invented to protect our sight -- close up and contain the inner fire. That fire diffuses and equalizes the internal motions, producing rest. When this rest is deep, we fall into a sleep mostly free of dreams. But where stronger motions persist, they generate corresponding dream visions, depending on their nature and location. We remember these when we wake up.

As for how images form in mirrors and on shiny surfaces -- there's no great mystery now. The inner fire and the outer fire combine and interact, and their reflections on the smooth surface produce various effects. Left appears as right and right as left, because the visual rays meet the reflected rays in the reverse of their usual direction. But if the mirror is curved so that it flips the rays -- sending the right-side stream to the left, and vice versa -- then right appears right and left appears left. And if the mirror is tilted vertically, the curved surface makes the face appear upside down, deflecting the lower rays upward and the upper rays down.

Now, all these visual phenomena are what we might call secondary causes -- the supporting cast. They're the tools that God uses as assistants in carrying out his vision of the best. But most people mistake them for the primary causes of everything, because they see things being heated and cooled, expanded and contracted, and think those physical processes explain it all.

They don't. These processes have no reason or intelligence of their own. The only being that can truly possess mind is the invisible soul. Fire, water, earth, and air are visible bodies, nothing more.

Anyone who loves intelligence and knowledge should look first for causes that involve intelligent purpose, and only second for those mechanical processes where one thing is simply pushed around by another. We need to acknowledge both kinds of causes. But we should distinguish between those that work with mind and produce what is beautiful and good, and those that operate mindlessly, producing random, unplanned effects.

So much for the secondary, supporting causes of sight. Let me now speak about its higher purpose -- the reason God gave us eyes.

In my view, sight is the source of the greatest benefit we possess. If we'd never seen the stars, the sun, and the heavens, not a single word of our present discussion about the universe would ever have been spoken. But the sight of day and night, of months and the turning of the years, has given us the concept of number, the notion of time, and the ability to investigate the nature of the universe. From this, we've derived philosophy -- and no greater gift has ever been, or ever will be, given by the gods to mortal humanity.

This is the supreme blessing of sight. Why even mention the lesser ones? Even an ordinary person, if deprived of sight, would grieve -- though the grief would be in vain.

Here's what I'll say: God invented sight and gave it to us so that we might observe the intelligent motions of the heavens and apply them to the motions of our own thought -- which are akin to them, though ours are turbulent and theirs are serene. By learning the harmonies and revolutions of the universe and sharing in the natural truth of reason, we can imitate the unerring motions of the divine and bring order to our own wandering minds.

The same goes for speech and hearing: the gods gave us these for the same purpose and the same reason. Speech was designed primarily to serve this end, and it does so above all others.

And music -- at least the part of it that involves the voice and the sense of hearing -- was given to us for the sake of harmony. Harmony has motions that are akin to the revolutions of our soul. It wasn't given for mindless entertainment, which is what most people think it's for these days. It was given to help correct any discord that has crept into the soul's circuits, and to restore the soul to harmony and agreement with itself. Rhythm, too, was given for the same reason: to remedy the graceless, irregular tendencies that are so common among us.

Up to this point, with a few small exceptions, I've been describing the works of intelligence -- the things produced by purposeful Mind. Now we need to set alongside them the things that come about through necessity. Because the creation of the world is a mixture of both: Mind and Necessity working together. Mind, the ruling power, persuaded Necessity to direct most created things toward what is best. That's how the universe was originally made -- through the influence of reason getting the better of necessity.

But if someone wants to give a truthful account of how the work was actually accomplished, they need to include the contribution of the wandering cause -- Necessity -- as well. So we have to go back to the beginning and find a new starting point, just as we did before.

We need to consider what fire, water, air, and earth were like before the heavens were created -- what was happening to them in that prior state. Because no one has yet explained how these elements first came into being. We talk about fire and the rest as if everyone knew what they were -- treating them as the basic building blocks, the "alphabet" of reality. But really, a person with any sense shouldn't even compare them to syllables, let alone letters. They're not truly basic at all.

Let me say this much: I'm not going to try to identify the absolute first principles of everything -- not in this discussion. That would be too difficult given the approach we're taking. Don't expect that I could do it. I certainly don't expect it of myself.

Instead, remembering what I said at the beginning about probability, I'll do my best to give an account as plausible as any other -- and more plausible, I hope. I'll go back to the beginning and try to speak of each thing, and of the whole. And once more, let me call on God at the start of this new and unfamiliar inquiry, and ask for safe passage to the harbor of probability.

So let's begin again.

This new beginning requires a broader framework than before. Previously, we distinguished two kinds of reality. Now a third must be revealed.

The first two were enough for our earlier discussion: one, the eternal model -- intelligible, always the same -- and two, its imitation -- generated and visible. At the time, we didn't bother to identify a third kind, thinking two would suffice. But now the argument demands it.

What is this third kind? It's hard to explain and hard even to see clearly. It is the receptacle -- a kind of nurse of all becoming.

Let me try to be clearer, though this will be difficult for many reasons, starting with a puzzle about the elements themselves. Which of them should we call "water" rather than "fire"? Which deserves any particular name more than all the others combined? It's a hard question. Here's why:

We see that what we just called "water," when it condenses, becomes stone and earth. When it evaporates and disperses, it turns into vapor and air. Air, when it catches fire, becomes flame. And fire, when it's compressed and extinguished, turns back into air. Air, when collected and squeezed together, produces clouds and mist. From these, when further compressed, comes flowing water. From water comes earth and stones again.

So generation seems to move in a circle, each element transforming into the others.

Given that no element ever stays in the same form, how can anyone confidently claim that any particular substance IS one thing rather than another? No one can. The safest approach is this:

When we see something that's continually changing -- fire, for example -- we shouldn't call it "this" as though pointing to a stable thing. We should say it is "of such a nature" -- it's fire-like. We shouldn't say of water "this is water" but always "such is water." We shouldn't apply the word "this" or "that" to any of them, as if they were permanent. They're too volatile for that. They slip through any expression that tries to pin them down as "this" or "that" or "relative to this."

We shouldn't call any of them "this." We should call them "such" -- a word that captures the recurring quality circulating through each and all of them. What should be called "fire" is whatever consistently has the nature of fire, and likewise for everything else that comes into being and passes away.

But that in which the elements appear and grow and decay -- only that should be called "this" or "that." Whatever merely has a quality -- hot or cold, or any pair of opposites, or anything composed of them -- should never be called "this."

Let me try another way to make this clearer. Imagine someone making shapes out of gold -- continuously reshaping the same gold into triangles, then circles, then squares. If someone points to one of the shapes and asks "What is that?" the safest answer by far is: "That is gold." You shouldn't call the triangle or any other temporary shape "this," as though it had a permanent existence. The shapes are changing even as you speak. You should be satisfied with the answer "such" -- the indefinite description.

The same principle applies to the universal nature that receives all bodies. It must always be called the same thing -- the receptacle. It never departs from its own nature. It receives all things into itself but never takes on the form of anything that enters it. It's the natural recipient of all impressions -- stirred and shaped by whatever enters it, appearing different at different times because of them. But the forms that enter and leave it are copies of the eternal realities, modeled after them in a wonderful and mysterious way that we'll explore later.

For now, we need to grasp three natures:

First: that which is coming into being (the generated thing).
Second: that in which it comes into being (the receptacle).
Third: the model that the generated thing resembles (the eternal Form).

We can compare the receptacle to a mother, the source to a father, and the nature between them to a child. And notice this: if the model is to be stamped into every possible variety of form, then the material receiving the stamp must itself be without any form. If it already had the shape of some particular thing, then whenever something different or opposite was impressed upon it, the impression would be distorted -- the receptacle's own shape would interfere.

Think of it like making perfume: you start with an oil that's as odorless as possible, so it can take on any scent. Or like impressing a design into soft wax: you first make the surface as smooth and blank as you can, leaving no previous impression behind.

In the same way, the receptacle -- the thing that will receive every possible form for all eternity -- must itself be devoid of any particular form. This is why the mother and receptacle of all visible and sensible things is not to be called earth, air, fire, water, or any compound of them. It's an invisible, formless being that receives all things and somehow participates in the intelligible, and it is extremely difficult to comprehend.

As far as we can tell from what's been said: fire is what this receptacle looks like when it's inflamed, water is what it looks like when it's moistened, and it takes on the appearance of earth and air insofar as it receives their impressions.

But we need to ask this more precisely: Do self-existent realities actually exist? Is there a Form of fire that exists in and of itself? Or are the only real things the ones we see and perceive through our bodies? Is what we call "intelligible essence" nothing but a name?

This is a question we can't leave unexamined or just assert an answer to. But we also can't jam a long digression into our already lengthy discussion. If we can state a big principle briefly, that would be ideal.

Here's my view: If understanding and true opinion are genuinely different from each other, then these self-existent Forms definitely exist -- imperceptible to the senses, grasped only by the mind. But if, as some people claim, true opinion is no different from understanding, then we should regard everything we perceive through the body as the most real thing there is.

But they are different. They have different origins and different natures. Understanding is produced by instruction; opinion is produced by persuasion. Understanding always comes with rational justification; opinion has none. Understanding can't be shaken by persuasion; opinion can. And while every human being shares in true opinion, genuine understanding is a gift of the gods and belongs to very few.

Given all this, we must recognize three distinct kinds of reality:

First: Being -- that which is always the same, uncreated and indestructible, neither receiving anything from outside nor going out to anything else. Invisible, imperceptible to any sense, it can only be contemplated by the intellect.

Second: that which shares the same name and resembles the first, but is perceived by the senses -- created, always in motion, appearing in a place and vanishing from it, grasped by opinion and sensation.

Third: Space -- eternal, indestructible, providing a home for everything that comes into being. It's apprehended without the help of the senses, through a kind of indirect reasoning, and is barely real. When we contemplate it, it's as if we're dreaming. We insist that everything that exists must be somewhere, must occupy some space, and that what is neither on earth nor anywhere in the heavens doesn't exist at all. But these and similar beliefs about the true, waking nature of reality are just dreamlike impressions. We can't shake off the sleep and see clearly. An image, since it doesn't own the reality it copies, and exists as the fleeting shadow of something else, must exist in something else -- in space -- clinging to existence however it can, or it would be nothing at all.

So here is my summary verdict: Being, Space, and Becoming -- these three existed in their own distinct ways before the heavens were created.

And the nurse of becoming -- the receptacle -- moistened by water and set ablaze by fire, taking on the forms of earth and air, and experiencing all the effects that go with these -- presented a wild variety of appearances. She was filled with forces that were neither alike nor balanced, and she was never in equilibrium in any part. She swayed unevenly this way and that, shaken by these forces, and by her own motion she shook them in return.

As the elements moved, they separated out, drifting in different directions -- just as grain is sorted when shaken and winnowed: the heavy, dense grains settle in one place, and the light, loose chaff goes to another. In this same way, the four kinds of elements were shaken by the receptacle, which acted like a winnowing machine. It scattered the most dissimilar elements far apart and pushed the most similar ones close together.

That's why the different elements settled into different regions even before they were arranged into an ordered universe. At first, they were completely without reason or measure. They had only faint traces of their true natures -- they were in the condition you'd expect anything to be in when God is absent. That was their state at the time.

Then God stepped in and fashioned them by form and number. Let us hold firmly to this principle throughout: God made them as beautiful and excellent as possible, out of materials that were not beautiful or good.

And now I'll try to show you how the elements were constructed and generated -- using an unusual argument, but one I think you'll be able to follow, since your education has made you familiar with scientific reasoning.

First, it's clear that fire, earth, water, and air are all bodies. And every kind of body has solidity, depth. And every solid body is necessarily bounded by flat surfaces. And every flat surface made of straight lines is composed of triangles. All triangles ultimately derive from two basic types: one with a right angle and two equal acute angles (the right isosceles triangle), and one with a right angle divided into unequal parts, with unequal sides (the right scalene triangle).

We'll assume -- combining probability with demonstration -- that these are the original elements from which fire and the other bodies are constructed. The principles that are more fundamental than these? Only God knows those, and perhaps those few mortals who are friends of God.

Our task is to determine which four solids are the most beautiful, the ones that are unlike each other, and of which some can transform into others. If we succeed, we'll know the true origin of earth, fire, and the elements in between.

We won't accept that there are any visible bodies more beautiful than these. So let's try to construct the four most beautiful solid forms, and then we can say we've adequately grasped their nature.

Now, of the two basic triangles: the isosceles right triangle has only one form. But the scalene right triangle comes in infinitely many varieties. From this infinity, we need to select the most beautiful -- if we're going to proceed properly. And if anyone can point out a more beautiful triangle than the one I'm about to propose, they can claim a friendly victory.

The most beautiful scalene right triangle, I maintain, is this: the one where, if you put two of them together, they form a third triangle that is equilateral. The full proof would take too long, but anyone who can disprove this is welcome to try.

So let's select our two fundamental triangles: the isosceles right triangle, and the scalene right triangle whose longer side squared is three times the shorter side squared (the 30-60-90 triangle).

Now let me clarify something that was unclear before. I suggested earlier that all four elements could be generated from each other. That was wrong. In fact, three of the four elements are generated from the scalene triangle, and only the fourth -- earth -- is constructed from the isosceles triangle. This means they can't all transform into each other. Three of them can -- since they all come from the same type of triangle. When the larger bodies break apart, many smaller bodies form from the pieces, taking their own proper shapes. And when many small bodies break down into their component triangles and come back together, they can form one large body of a different kind. That's how three of the elements pass into one another.

Now let me describe each one and show how it's built.

The first and simplest solid -- the one with the fewest faces -- is constructed from the scalene triangle (the 30-60-90 triangle, whose hypotenuse is twice its shortest side). When two of these triangles are joined along their longest sides, and this is done three times, with all the diagonals and short sides meeting at a single central point, you get one equilateral triangle made from six basic triangles.

Four of these equilateral triangles, joined together, create one solid angle from every three flat angles -- and this solid angle is the one closest to the most obtuse of flat angles. Four such solid angles make the first solid shape, which divides the sphere it's inscribed in into equal, similar parts.

This is the tetrahedron -- a pyramid with four equilateral triangular faces. And this is the element of fire.

The second solid is formed from the same basic triangles. Eight equilateral triangles combine so that four flat angles form each solid angle. Six such solid angles complete the figure.

This is the octahedron -- eight equilateral triangular faces. And this is the element of air.

The third solid is built from one hundred and twenty basic triangles, forming twelve solid angles, each made of five equilateral triangles, for a total of twenty equilateral triangular faces.

This is the icosahedron. And this is the element of water.

The isosceles right triangle -- our other fundamental building block -- generated the fourth figure. Four isosceles right triangles, joined at their right angles, form one equilateral square. Six of these squares, fitted together, form eight solid angles, each made of three right angles.

This is the cube -- six square faces. And this is the element of earth.

There was still a fifth combination, which God used for the design of the universe itself -- the dodecahedron, the solid with twelve pentagonal faces.

Now, anyone who thinks carefully about all this and asks whether there are infinitely many worlds or a definite number -- the idea of infinite worlds is the mark of a sadly vague and ignorant mind. The more reasonable question is whether there is truly one world or five. In my opinion, based on probability, there is one. But others, approaching the question differently, may think otherwise. Let's leave that debate and move on to distributing the elemental shapes -- which we've now constructed in theory -- among the four elements.

To earth we assign the cube, because earth is the most immovable of the four elements and the most resistant to deformation. What has the most stable base must have such a nature. Among our basic triangles, the isosceles is inherently more stable than the scalene. And among the figures built from them, the equilateral square is more firmly based than the equilateral triangle, both in its whole form and in its parts. So in assigning the cube to earth, we follow probability.

To water we assign the icosahedron -- the least mobile of the remaining three shapes. To fire, the most mobile of all: the tetrahedron. To air, the intermediate: the octahedron.

We also assign the smallest body to fire, the largest to water, and the intermediate to air. And the sharpest body goes to fire, the next sharpest to air, and the third to water.

This all follows from one principle: the shape with the fewest faces is necessarily the most mobile (being the sharpest and most penetrating in every direction) and also the lightest (being made of the fewest identical parts). The second-fewest faces comes in second, and so on.

So let it be established, by both strict reason and probability, that the tetrahedron (pyramid) is the fundamental shape and seed of fire. The octahedron is the element of air. The icosahedron is the element of water. Each of these should be imagined as so small that no single particle of any of the four kinds is visible to us. Only when vast numbers of them collect together do we see their aggregates. And as for the ratios of their numbers, their motions, and their other properties -- God, as far as necessity allowed, has everywhere perfected and harmonized them in due proportion.

From all we've said about the elements, the most likely conclusion is this:

When earth meets fire and is dissolved by fire's sharpness -- whether the dissolution happens within fire itself or in a mass of air or water -- the earth fragments drift here and there until they find other earth particles and reunite. They can never take any other form; they always become earth again.

But water, when divided by fire or air, can reform as one part fire and two parts air. And a single portion of air, when broken apart, becomes two portions of fire.

Conversely, when a small body of fire is enclosed within a larger body of air, water, or earth, and both are in motion, the fire loses the fight and breaks apart. Two volumes of fire then combine to form one volume of air. When air is overpowered and broken into small pieces, two and a half parts of air condense into one part of water.

Here's the general principle: no element can be changed by another element of the same kind in the same state. But as long as a weaker element is fighting against a stronger, it keeps being dissolved. And when a few small particles are surrounded by many larger ones and are breaking down, they only stop dissolving when they give in and join the conquering element -- fire becomes air, air becomes water.

But if a large number of the surrounding element attacks a small amount of a different kind, the small particles continue to dissolve until they either escape entirely to rejoin their own kind, or are completely assimilated by the conquering power and remain where they are, transformed from many into one.

Because of all these transformations, everything is constantly shifting its position. The motion of the receptacle sorts each kind into its proper region. But elements that become unlike themselves and similar to something else are carried by the shaking into the region of whatever they've grown to resemble.

These are the causes that produce all unmixed, primary bodies. As for the many subordinate species within each major kind, they arise from variations in the size of the two basic triangles. Each triangle type was created not in a single size but in many -- some larger, some smaller -- producing as many varieties as there are species within the four elements. When these different-sized particles combine with each other, the result is an endless variety -- a variety that anyone seeking the probable truth about nature should carefully consider.
