TRANSLATION NOTES — Laws Book 9 (Chapter 055)

OVERVIEW
--------
Laws Book 9 is Plato's most sustained treatment of criminal law: homicide,
wounding, assault, and the philosophical foundations underlying punishment.
The chapter is long and covers a remarkable range of legal territory — from
temple robbery to parricide, from the rights of self-defense to the treatment
of suicides, from animal culpability to the duties of bystanders.

The philosophical centerpiece is the Socratic/Platonic thesis that "all
wrongdoing is involuntary" — nobody does wrong willingly. The Athenian
wrestles openly with the tension between this philosophical principle and
the practical need to legislate different penalties for different kinds of
crime. His solution is ingenious: distinguish between "harm" (which can be
involuntary) and "injustice" (which involves the soul's condition), and
identify three sources of wrongdoing — passion, pleasure, and ignorance.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS
--------------------------
1. INVOLUNTARY WRONGDOING THESIS: The key passage ("all bad men are always
   involuntarily bad") is given maximum clarity. This is the Socratic
   paradox — nobody does wrong willingly — and it needs to land clearly
   for a modern reader unfamiliar with the concept. The Athenian's own
   struggle with the tension between this thesis and practical lawmaking
   is preserved; he admits the apparent contradiction openly.

2. SPEAKER LABELS: "Athenian" throughout, as specified. Cleinias is the
   primary respondent; Megillus is named in conversation but doesn't
   speak in this chapter.

3. LEGAL VOCABULARY: Jowett's archaic legal phrasing is modernized
   throughout:
   - "engraven on his face and hands" → "branded on his face and hands"
   - "cast naked beyond the borders" → kept largely as-is (vivid)
   - "shall be quit of the homicide" → "be clear of the homicide charge"
   - "surety" → retained (still in use in legal English)
   - "metic" → retained (established term for resident alien; clear from
     context)

4. PRELUDE/PREAMBLE CONCEPT: The Athenian's important idea that every
   law should come with a "prelude" (an explanatory rationale) is
   preserved clearly. The doctor analogy — slave-doctor vs. gentleman-
   doctor — is the key metaphor for this principle.

5. THE GHOST STORY: The passage about the newly dead being angry at their
   killers and haunting them is presented as the "ancient story" it is,
   preserving its eerie quality without either rationalizing or
   sensationalizing it.

6. HARM vs. INJUSTICE: The careful philosophical distinction between
   "hurt" (mere damage) and "injustice" (wrongful intent/soul-corruption)
   is preserved and clarified. This is crucial to the Athenian's legal
   framework: you can harm someone without committing an injustice, and
   you can commit an injustice that accidentally benefits someone.

7. THREE SOURCES OF WRONGDOING: Passion (anger/fear), pleasure/desire,
   and ignorance (simple vs. "double ignorance" — ignorance plus the
   delusion of wisdom). These map to different categories of crime with
   different degrees of culpability.

8. FIVE SOURCES → TWO KINDS OF LAW: The fivefold subdivision
   (passion, pleasure, and three kinds of ignorance-driven hope) leads to
   a twofold legal distinction: crimes done openly/violently vs. crimes
   done in secret/by deception. The latter are punished more severely.

9. PASSION-DRIVEN HOMICIDE: The Athenian's careful analysis of killings
   committed in anger — distinguishing between immediate impulsive
   killings (closer to involuntary) and premeditated revenge killings
   (closer to voluntary) — is preserved with full clarity.

10. SELF-DEFENSE AND JUSTIFIABLE KILLING: The final section on when
    killing is rightfully free of guilt is kept crisp and clear: night
    thieves, muggers, rapists, defense of family.

MAJOR STRUCTURAL CHOICES
-------------------------
- The chapter alternates between philosophical discussion (the nature of
  justice, voluntary vs. involuntary, the soul's condition) and specific
  legal prescriptions (exile terms, fines, purification rites). The
  translation preserves this alternation, keeping the legal provisions
  crisp and the philosophical passages flowing.

- Long Jowett paragraphs with multiple legal provisions are broken into
  shorter, more digestible segments while preserving logical grouping.

- The hierarchy of homicide categories is kept clear:
  (a) Involuntary homicide (accidents, athletics, medicine)
  (b) Passion-driven homicide without premeditation
  (c) Passion-driven homicide with premeditation
  (d) Voluntary/premeditated murder
  (e) Special cases: parricide, family killings, suicide

SENSITIVE CONTENT NOTES
-------------------------
- SLAVERY: The laws distinguish sharply between free persons and slaves,
  with slaves receiving harsher punishments and fewer protections.
  Translated faithfully without editorializing.

- CORPORAL PUNISHMENT: Branding, lashing, and public beatings are
  prescribed throughout. Presented as they are.

- SUICIDE: Treated as a form of cowardice deserving shameful burial
  (alone, in nameless places, without markers). This reflects ancient
  attitudes; translated faithfully.

- DOMESTIC VIOLENCE/FAMILY KILLINGS: Extensive provisions for murders
  and assaults within families, including between spouses and between
  parents and children. The parricide laws are especially severe.

- AGE HIERARCHY: The law mandates deference to elders and prescribes
  punishment for younger people who strike older ones. Presented as
  written.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
-------------------------------------
- The Athenian's legislative method is now well-established: prelude
  (philosophical justification) + law (specific provision) + penalty.
  Book 10 will continue this pattern.
- The threefold psychological framework (passion, pleasure, ignorance)
  carries forward as the basis for classifying crimes.
- The distinction between harm and injustice should remain consistent.
- The concept of "incurable" criminals who deserve death has been
  established and will recur.
- Magnesia's 5,040 households and the allotment system are referenced;
  these should remain consistent.
- The phrase "guardians of the law" is the standard translation for the
  chief magistrates; keep using it.
- Book 10 moves to theology and impiety — the Athenian's arguments
  for the existence of gods. The tone will shift from legal to
  philosophical/theological, but the "prelude + law" structure persists.
