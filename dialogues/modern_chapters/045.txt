Persons of the dialogue:
  An Athenian stranger
  Cleinias, a Cretan
  Megillus, a Spartan

Scene: The road from Knossos to the cave and temple of Zeus, on a hot summer day in Crete.


Athenian
Tell me, friends — is it a god or a man who's credited with creating your laws?

Cleinias
A god, stranger. Absolutely a god. Here in Crete, we say it was Zeus himself. But in Sparta, where our friend Megillus comes from, I believe they'd say Apollo is their lawgiver. Isn't that right, Megillus?

Megillus
That's right.

Athenian
And do you believe the story Homer tells, Cleinias — that every nine years, Minos traveled to meet with his father Zeus on Olympus, and that Zeus inspired him to write the laws for your cities?

Cleinias
Yes, that's our tradition. And there was his brother Rhadamanthus too — you've probably heard of him. He's said to have been the most just man who ever lived, and we Cretans believe he earned that reputation through his righteous handling of justice during his lifetime.

Athenian
A noble reputation, and worthy of a son of Zeus. Now, since you and Megillus were both raised under these institutions, I'd imagine you won't mind giving an account of your government and laws. We can pass the time talking about them as we walk. I'm told it's a considerable distance from Knossos to the cave and temple of Zeus, and there must be shady spots along the way under the tall trees — places to escape this scorching sun. We're not young men anymore. We can stop and rest beneath them whenever we need to, and make it through the whole journey without too much trouble, letting conversation carry us along.

Cleinias
Yes, and as we go further we'll come to groves of cypresses — remarkably tall and beautiful ones — and green meadows where we can sit and talk.

Athenian
Excellent.

Cleinias
Indeed — and even better once we actually see them. Let's get moving.

Athenian
I'm happy to. And first, I want to know: why has the law required you to have communal meals and gymnastic training, and to carry weapons?

Cleinias
I think that's fairly easy to explain, stranger. Just look at our landscape. Crete isn't like Thessaly — it's not a big flat plain. That's why Thessaly has horsemen and we have foot-soldiers. Our rugged, uneven terrain is better suited to getting around on foot. And if you're fighting on foot, you need light equipment — no one can run carrying a heavy load — so bows and arrows make sense because they're light. All these arrangements were designed with war in mind, and I think the lawgiver had military readiness at the heart of everything he did. The communal meals, if I'm not mistaken, were set up for the same reason. He noticed that when men are in the field on campaign, the nature of the situation forces them to eat together for mutual protection. It seems to me he thought the world was foolish for not understanding that all people are always at war with one another. And if during wartime there ought to be communal meals and organized chains of command for an army's protection, those same arrangements should continue during peacetime too. Because what most people call "peace" is really just a name — in reality, every city is in a permanent natural state of war with every other city, even if no herald has proclaimed it. If you look closely, you'll see that this was exactly what our Cretan lawgiver intended. Every institution, public and private, was arranged with war in mind. He was working on the assumption that no possessions or institutions have any value for someone who's been defeated in battle — because all the good things of the conquered pass into the hands of the conquerors.

Athenian
You seem to have been thoroughly trained in the Cretan way of thinking, and very well informed about it. But could you spell out for me a bit more precisely the principle behind it? You seem to be saying that a well-governed state should be organized so as to conquer all other states in war. Am I reading you correctly?

Cleinias
Absolutely. And our Spartan friend here will agree with me, I'm sure.

Megillus
How could any Spartan say otherwise?

Athenian
And does this principle apply only to states, or to villages as well?

Cleinias
Both.

Athenian
The same idea?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
And within the village — the same kind of war between family and family, and individual against individual?

Cleinias
The same.

Athenian
And should each person think of himself as his own enemy too? What do we say about that?

Cleinias
Now there, Athenian stranger — I won't just call you a man of Attica, because you seem to deserve being named after the goddess Athena herself — you've gone right back to first principles. You've thrown real light on the argument, and you'll be in an even better position now to understand what I was getting at: that all people are publicly each other's enemies, and privately, each person is his own enemy.

Athenian
What do you mean by that, exactly?

Cleinias
There's a kind of victory and defeat — the very first and best of victories, the very lowest and worst of defeats — that each person wins or suffers not at the hands of another, but at his own hands. This shows that there's a war going on inside every one of us.

Athenian
Let's turn the argument around. If every individual is either his own master or his own slave, can we say the same principle operates in the household, the village, and the state?

Cleinias
You mean that in each of them there's a principle of being superior or inferior to oneself?

Athenian
Yes.

Cleinias
You're absolutely right to ask that. There certainly is such a principle, and it applies above all to states. A state where the better citizens win out over the mob and the inferior classes can truly be called "better than itself" and deserves praise. When the opposite happens, it deserves blame.

Athenian
Whether the better element is ever really conquered by the worse is a question that needs more discussion, so let's set it aside for now. But I do understand your point. You're saying that citizens of the same blood who live in the same city can form an unjust conspiracy, and if they have superior numbers, they can overpower and enslave the just minority. When that happens, the state is truly "inferior to itself" — and therefore bad. When they're defeated, the state is "superior to itself" — and therefore good.

Cleinias
That's a strange-sounding claim, stranger. But we can't possibly deny it.

Athenian
Here's another case to think about. In a family, there might be several brothers born to the same parents. It's perfectly possible that the majority of them are unjust, with the just ones in the minority.

Cleinias
Perfectly possible.

Athenian
And you and I shouldn't get sidetracked arguing about whether it's technically proper to call this family "superior" when the just prevail and "inferior" when they don't. We're not quibbling over conventional language right now. We're investigating the natural principles of right and wrong as they apply to law.

Cleinias
What you say is absolutely true, stranger.

Megillus
Excellent — I agree entirely, as far as we've gone.

Athenian
Now then. Suppose there were a judge presiding over these brothers we've been discussing.

Cleinias
All right.

Athenian
Which would be the better judge — one who destroyed the bad brothers and set the good ones up to govern themselves? Or one who let the good govern but allowed the bad to live, and made them voluntarily submit? Or perhaps third in the ranking of excellence would be a judge who, finding the family in conflict, didn't destroy anyone at all but instead reconciled them with each other permanently, gave them laws they all agreed to follow, and managed to keep them friends.

Cleinias
That last judge would be far and away the best — the best sort of legislator too.

Athenian
And yet the aim of all the laws this judge would give would be the opposite of war.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Now consider: would the person who organizes a state and arranges the lives of its people have his eye on foreign war, or on that internal kind of war we call civil strife — the kind that no one would want to see in their own state, and that everyone, once it breaks out, wants to end as quickly as possible?

Cleinias
He'd be focused mainly on preventing civil strife.

Athenian
And would he prefer that civil conflict be ended by the destruction of one side and the victory of the other, or that peace and friendship be restored — so that, once reconciled, the citizens could turn their attention to foreign enemies?

Cleinias
Everyone would prefer the latter, at least in their own state.

Athenian
And wouldn't the lawgiver want the same thing?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And wouldn't every lawgiver always make laws with an eye to the best outcome?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
But war — whether foreign or civil — isn't the best outcome. The need for either kind is something to be regretted. Peace and mutual goodwill, those are what's best. And even a state's "victory over itself" shouldn't really be considered a good thing — it's a necessity. It's like saying the body is in its best condition when it's sick and being purged by medicine, while forgetting that there's also a condition of the body that doesn't need any purging at all. In the same way, no one can be a true statesman — whether they're looking after the happiness of an individual or a state — if they focus only, or even primarily, on foreign warfare. No one can be a sound lawgiver who organizes peace for the sake of war, rather than war for the sake of peace.

Cleinias
There seems to be truth in what you're saying, stranger. And yet I'd be very much mistaken if the entire aim and purpose of our Cretan institutions — and the Spartan ones too — isn't war.

Athenian
Probably so. But there's no reason we should rudely quarrel with each other about your lawgivers. Let's question them gently instead, since we're all equally serious about getting to the truth. Follow me closely here. First, let me call up Tyrtaeus as a witness — he was Athenian by birth but also a Spartan citizen, and no man was ever more passionate about war. He writes:

"I sing not, I care not, about any man,"

even if he were the richest man alive and possessed every good thing — and then Tyrtaeus gives a whole list of them — unless he's a brave warrior at all times. I imagine you've heard his poems too. Our Spartan friend here has probably heard more than enough of them.

Megillus
Very true.

Cleinias
And they've made their way from Sparta to Crete as well.

Athenian
All right, let's all put a question to Tyrtaeus together. We'd say to him: "Most inspired poet, the magnificent praise you've given to those who excel in war is proof enough that you're wise and good, and I believe Megillus, Cleinias of Knossos, and I all agree with you completely. But we want to make sure we're talking about the same kind of men. Tell us — do you agree that there are two kinds of war?" Now, even a man far less gifted than Tyrtaeus could answer without any difficulty that yes, there are two kinds. One is what everyone calls civil war — and as we were just saying, it's the worst of all wars. The other, war against foreign nations of different peoples, is a far milder form of conflict.

Cleinias
Much milder, certainly.

Athenian
Well then — when you sing your high praise of war and blame those who fall short, Tyrtaeus, which kind of war are you talking about? Which warriors are you praising? Foreign war, I'd assume, judging from lines like these:

"Who refuse to look upon fields of blood, and will not draw near and strike at their enemies."

So we'd naturally conclude: "You, Tyrtaeus, seem to be praising those who distinguish themselves in foreign and external war." And he'd have to agree.

Cleinias
Obviously.

Athenian
They are good men, those warriors. But we say there are even better men — men whose excellence shows itself in the greatest of all battles. And we have a poet of our own to call as witness: Theognis, a citizen of Megara in Sicily. He says:

"Cyrnus, he who is faithful in a civil conflict is worth his weight in gold and silver."

And we claim that such a man is far better than the other in a much more difficult kind of war — better in roughly the same way that justice, temperance, and wisdom, when combined with courage, are better than courage alone. Because a man can't be faithful and good during civil strife unless he possesses all the virtues working together. But in the external wars Tyrtaeus writes about, plenty of mercenary soldiers will plant themselves on the battlefield ready to die — and yet they're generally insolent, unjust, violent, and among the most senseless people alive.

So where does this lead? What am I trying to show? I'm saying this: the divine lawgiver of Crete — or any lawgiver worth the name — will always and above all else, when making laws, keep his eye on the greatest virtue. And according to Theognis, that's loyalty in the hour of danger, which we might call complete justice. The virtue Tyrtaeus celebrates is good enough, and the poet praised it at the right time. But in rank and importance, it's only fourth-rate.

Cleinias
Stranger, you're demoting our inspired lawgiver to a rank far beneath him!

Athenian
No — I think we'd be demoting ourselves if we imagined that Lycurgus and Minos designed the laws of Sparta and Crete primarily with war in mind.

Cleinias
Then what should we say?

Athenian
What truth and justice demand, I think, when we're speaking on behalf of truly divine excellence: that the lawgiver, when making his laws, had in view not just one part of virtue — and the lowest part at that — but all of virtue. He devised categories of law corresponding to the different kinds of virtue, and not in the way modern lawmakers do. Today's legislators investigate one narrow problem at a time and draft laws as the need arises — one set of laws about property distribution, another about inheritance, another about assault, and on and on for ten thousand other matters. But we maintain that the right way to examine laws is the way we've been doing it just now.

And I admired your approach, actually. You were absolutely right to begin with virtue and say that virtue was the aim of the lawgiver. Where I thought you went wrong was in claiming that all his legislation aimed at only a part of virtue — the least important part — and that's what prompted my subsequent remarks. So will you let me explain how I wish you'd presented the case?

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
You should have said something like this: "Stranger, the Cretan laws are rightly famous throughout Greece, because they accomplish what laws are supposed to accomplish — they make the people who live under them happy. They provide every kind of good thing. Now, goods come in two categories: human goods and divine goods. The human goods depend on the divine ones. A state that achieves the greater goods automatically gets the lesser ones too. A state that lacks the greater goods gets neither.

"Among the lesser, human goods, the first is health. The second is beauty. The third is physical strength — including speed and overall fitness. The fourth is wealth — but not the blind kind. Wealth that can see, wealth paired with wisdom.

"Among the divine goods, the first and foremost is wisdom. That's the leader. The second is temperance — self-control. And from the union of these two with courage springs justice. Courage itself comes fourth.

"All these divine goods naturally take precedence over the human goods, and this is the order the lawgiver must follow. After establishing these priorities, he'll design the rest of his laws for the citizens with an eye toward them — the human goods looking up to the divine ones, and the divine goods looking up to their leader, which is mind and wisdom.

"Some of his laws will deal with marriage contracts. Then with the bearing and raising of children, both boys and girls. The lawgiver's job is to take charge of his citizens through every stage of life — youth, maturity, old age — handing out punishments and rewards as needed. In all their dealings with one another, he should study their pains and pleasures and desires, and the intensity of all their passions. He should watch over them and use the voice of the law itself to praise and blame them rightly. He should address anger, fear, and the other disturbances of the soul that come with misfortune, as well as the recoveries that come with prosperity, and the experiences people go through in sickness, war, poverty, and their opposites. For every one of these situations, he should define and teach what's good and what's harmful.

"Next, the lawgiver must pay attention to how citizens make and spend their money, and oversee their contracts and dissolutions of contracts, whether voluntary or involuntary. He should examine how justice and injustice operate in all their various dealings with one another. He should honor those who obey the law and impose fixed penalties on those who don't — right up until the cycle of civic life is complete and it's time to consider proper funeral rites and honors for the dead.

"Finally, the lawgiver should review his entire body of work and appoint guardians to oversee all these matters — some guided by real understanding, others by sound judgment. Then wisdom will bind all his laws together and show them to be in harmony with temperance and justice, rather than with wealth or ambition."

That's the spirit in which I wanted you to explain your laws, Cleinias. And what I want to know is: how is all of this arranged in the laws they attribute to Zeus and to Pythian Apollo — the laws that Minos and Lycurgus handed down? How does the underlying order become visible to someone who has experience with legal systems, whether through study or long practice? Because to the rest of us, this order is far from obvious.

Cleinias
How should we proceed, stranger?

Athenian
I think we need to start over, the way we did before, and first examine the practice of courage. Then we'll move on to discuss another form of virtue, and then another, if you're willing. By taking courage as our model, we'll work through the whole set. And using discussions like these, we'll make the journey pass pleasantly. Once we've covered all the virtues, we'll show — God willing — that the institutions I've been describing are all aimed at virtue.

Megillus
Good plan. And why don't you start by critiquing this admirer of Zeus and Cretan law right here?

Athenian
I'll try to critique both of you and myself too, since the argument belongs to all of us. Tell me — weren't the communal meals first, and the gymnastic training second, both established by your lawgiver with an eye toward war?

Megillus
Yes.

Athenian
And what comes third, and what fourth? I think that's the right way to enumerate the remaining aspects of virtue — never mind exactly what we call them, as long as the meaning is clear.

Megillus
Well, any Spartan would say that hunting comes third.

Athenian
Let's see if we can figure out what comes fourth and fifth.

Megillus
I think I can get to fourth. It would be the routine endurance of pain — something we Spartans display in our hand-to-hand combat exercises, and also in our practice of stealing food while facing the prospect of a thorough beating if caught. Then there's the Crypteia — the secret service — which requires extraordinary endurance. Our young men roam the entire countryside day and night, even in winter, without shoes on their feet or beds to sleep in, serving as their own attendants with no help at all. And then there's the incredible endurance shown in our naked exercises, pushing through the brutal summer heat. There are many more examples like these — so many it would take forever to list them all.

Athenian
That's all well and good, my Spartan friend. But how should we define courage? Is it only about fighting against fears and pain? Or does it also involve fighting against desires and pleasures — and against those seductive flatteries that have the power to make even respectable people melt like wax?

Megillus
I'd say it includes all of those.

Athenian
Now, earlier in our conversation — you'll remember — our friend Cleinias from Knossos was talking about a person or a city being "inferior to themselves." Were you not, Cleinias?

Cleinias
I was.

Athenian
So which person is more truly "inferior" — the one who's overcome by pleasure, or the one who's overcome by pain?

Cleinias
I'd say the one overcome by pleasure. Everyone considers him inferior in a more shameful way than the person overcome by pain.

Athenian
But surely the lawgivers of Crete and Sparta didn't design courage to limp along on one leg — strong enough to fight attacks from one direction but helpless against the seductive assaults from the other?

Cleinias
Strong enough for both, I'd say.

Athenian
Then tell me: what institutions do either of your states have that give people a taste of pleasures — that don't avoid them, the way you avoid pain — but instead put a person right in the middle of them, and use incentives to compel or encourage him to master them? Where is the law dealing with pleasure that matches your laws dealing with pain? Tell me: what do you have that makes your citizens equally tough against both pleasure and pain, conquering what they should conquer, unbeaten by the enemies that are most dangerous and closest to home?

Megillus
I was able to point to many laws directed against pain, stranger. But when it comes to similar institutions dealing with pleasure, I can't think of any great or obvious examples. There might be some minor provisions I could mention.

Cleinias
And I can't point to anything like that in Cretan law either — nothing comparably prominent.

Athenian
That's not surprising, my dear friends. And if, in our search for what's truly good, one of us has to criticize the laws of the others — well, let's not take offense. Let's take it kindly.

Cleinias
You're quite right, Athenian stranger. We'll do as you say.

Athenian
At our age, Cleinias, there should be no room for hurt feelings.

Cleinias
Certainly not.

Athenian
I won't say right now whether criticisms of the Cretan or Spartan systems are justified or not. But I think I can describe what most people say about them better than either of you can. Assuming your laws are reasonably good, one of the best among them is the rule forbidding any young person from questioning whether the laws are right or wrong. Young people must speak with one voice and agree that all the laws are good, since they came from a god. Anyone who says otherwise shouldn't be listened to. But an old man who notices a flaw in the laws is free to share his observation with a ruler or with someone his own age, as long as no young people are around to hear it.

Cleinias
That's exactly right, stranger. Like a prophet who wasn't there at the time, you seem to have understood the lawgiver's intention perfectly. What you say is absolutely true.

Athenian
Well, since no young people are present, and since the lawgiver has given old men free license to speak, there's nothing improper about us discussing these matters now, just among ourselves.

Cleinias
True. So feel as free as you like in criticizing our laws. There's no shame in recognizing what's wrong. Anyone who receives criticism in a generous and friendly spirit will only be better for it.

Athenian
Good. However, I'm not going to attack your laws — not until I've examined them as carefully as I can. I'm just going to raise some questions. Because here's what's striking: you are the only people we know of, Greek or foreign, whose lawgiver commanded citizens to avoid all intense pleasures and amusements — to never touch them. Yet when it comes to pain and fear, as we've been discussing, your lawgiver understood perfectly well that people who've spent their entire lives from childhood avoiding pain, fear, and hardship will run away when they're eventually forced to face those things, and will become the slaves of those who are hardened to them.

Now, it seems to me the lawgiver should have applied the same reasoning to pleasure. He should have said to himself: "If my citizens grow up from youth entirely unfamiliar with the most intense pleasures, and never learn to endure temptation, and aren't trained to resist everything that's base, then the sweet pull of pleasure will overcome them just as surely as fear would overcome the first group. In a different way — actually, an even worse way — they'll become the slaves of people who can handle pleasure, people who've had plenty of experience enjoying it, even when those people are the worst sort of men. Half their soul will be enslaved, the other half free. And they won't deserve to be called truly free men in the fullest sense."

Tell me — do you agree with what I'm saying?

Cleinias
At first hearing, it does seem to ring true. But to rush to a conclusion about such important matters would be pretty childish and naive.

Athenian
Fair enough. Let's move on to the next virtue we planned to discuss. After courage comes temperance — self-control. What institutions will we find in Crete or Sparta that promote temperance, the way your military institutions differ from those of other states?

Megillus
That's not an easy question to answer. But I'd say the communal meals and gymnastic exercises have been admirably designed to promote both temperance and courage.

Athenian
Here's the difficulty with state institutions, though: it's hard to make theory and practice line up perfectly with no room for debate. It's like the human body — a treatment that does good in one way can do harm in another, and you can hardly say that any single regimen suits every constitution. Take the gymnasia and communal meals: they do a great deal of good, but they've also been a source of trouble during civil unrest. We can see this from what happened among the youth in Miletus, Boeotia, and Thurii, where these institutions seem to have had a tendency to corrupt the ancient and natural customs of love — degrading them below the level not only of human beings but even of animals.

This charge can fairly be leveled at your cities above all others, and it's true of most states that place heavy emphasis on athletic training. Whether we should treat these matters lightly or seriously, I think we should recognize that the pleasure arising from the natural union of men and women is natural, while the union of men with men, or women with women, is contrary to nature — a product of unbridled desire. The Cretans are often accused of having invented the story of Ganymede and Zeus specifically to justify their own practices, claiming divine precedent from the very god they believe gave them their laws.

Setting that story aside, we can observe that any serious thinking about legislation comes down almost entirely to pleasure and pain — both in states and in individuals. These are two springs that nature lets flow, and the person who draws from them at the right time, in the right place, and in the right amount is happy. This applies to humans and animals alike, to individuals and states. But whoever indulges ignorantly or at the wrong time will be the opposite of happy.

Megillus
I admit, stranger, that your words are well spoken, and I hardly know how to answer you. But still, I think the Spartan lawgiver was absolutely right to forbid excessive pleasure. As for the Cretan laws, I'll leave their defense to my friend from Knossos. But the Spartan laws regarding pleasure seem to me the best in the world. Our system has completely driven out the thing that leads most of humanity into wild pleasure, bad behavior, and every kind of foolishness. Neither in the countryside nor in the towns under Spartan control will you find revelries and all the provocations to pleasure that come with them. Anyone who encounters a drunken, disorderly person will have him punished severely on the spot — no excuses accepted, not even during a festival of Dionysus. Although I've noticed this kind of thing does happen at your performances "on the cart," as you call them. And among our Tarentine colonists, I've seen an entire city drunk at a Dionysian festival. But nothing like that happens in Sparta.

Athenian
My Spartan friend, these festivals are praiseworthy when there's a spirit of discipline behind them, but they're completely foolish when they're unregulated. An Athenian could easily fire back by pointing out the license that exists among your women. But for all accusations like these — whether they're aimed at the Tarentines, at us Athenians, or at you Spartans — there's one standard reply that settles the question of propriety. When a stranger expresses shock at some local practice, any citizen will naturally say: "Don't be surprised, stranger. This is our custom. You probably do things differently where you're from."

But that's not what we're discussing, friends. We're not talking about people in general — we're examining the merits and defects of the lawgivers themselves. So let's talk at more length about intoxication. It's actually a very important subject, and it takes real discrimination from a legislator to handle it well. I'm not talking about whether people should drink wine at all or not drink wine at all. I'm talking about intoxication itself.

Should we follow the custom of the Scythians and Persians and Carthaginians and Celts and Iberians and Thracians — all warlike peoples — or that of your countrymen, who, as you say, abstain entirely? The Scythians and Thracians, both men and women, drink their wine unmixed and pour it on their clothes, and they consider this a fine and glorious tradition. The Persians are also very fond of various luxuries that you reject, though they indulge with somewhat more restraint than the Thracians and Scythians.

Megillus
My good man, all we have to do is pick up our weapons and we send every one of those nations running.

Athenian
Don't say that, my friend. There have been — and always will be — victories and retreats that can't be explained by such simple logic. We can't use military victory or defeat as reliable proof that a society's institutions are good or bad. Larger states conquer smaller ones all the time — Syracuse has conquered Locri, which appears to have the best government in that part of the world; Athens conquered the Ceans — and there are ten thousand other examples of the same thing. None of that proves anything.

Let's try instead to evaluate each institution on its own merits and say nothing for now about victories and defeats. Let's just say: this custom is good, and that one is not. But first, let me explain how we should judge what's good and bad in these matters.

Megillus
How do you mean?

Athenian
Here's the problem. People who are ready to praise or condemn some practice the instant it's mentioned are going about it the wrong way. Let me give you an example. Suppose someone praises wheat as excellent food, and someone else immediately attacks wheat — without ever asking about its effects, how it should be used, who should eat it, with what, in what condition, or how it should be prepared. That's exactly what we're doing in this discussion. The moment the word "intoxication" comes up, one side praises it and the other condemns it. That's absurd. Each side produces their witnesses and endorsements. Some of us think we've made our case because we have many supporters. Others think they've won because they can point to nations that abstain and conquer in battle — and then we dispute that too.

If we keep discussing each law this way, we'll get nowhere. And on this particular point of intoxication, I'd like to approach it differently — the right way, I believe. Because if sheer numbers are the criterion, there are millions of people from countless nations ready to dispute the matter with just two cities.

Megillus
I'd gladly welcome any method of inquiry that's genuinely right.

Athenian
Let me put it this way. Suppose someone praises goat-keeping and says goats are wonderful animals to own. Then someone else, who's only ever seen goats wandering without a goatherd into cultivated fields and causing damage, attacks goats — or any other animal, for that matter — with no keeper or a bad keeper. Would there be any sense or justice in that kind of criticism?

Megillus
Certainly not.

Athenian
Is a captain a good captain just because he has sailing knowledge — whether or not he gets seasick? What would you say?

Megillus
I'd say he's not a good captain if he's competent in navigation but prone to seasickness.

Athenian
And what about a military commander? Can he command effectively just because he has military expertise, if he's also a coward — the kind of man who gets "sick and drunk with fear" when danger approaches?

Megillus
Impossible.

Athenian
And what if he's both a coward and incompetent?

Megillus
Then he's a wretched excuse for a commander — fit to lead old women, not soldiers.

Athenian
Now, what about someone who criticizes or praises any kind of social gathering — one that by its nature requires a leader and works perfectly well under good leadership — but the critic has never actually seen such a gathering properly run? He's only ever seen it with no leader or a bad one. When that kind of observer praises or blames such gatherings, can we trust anything he says?

Megillus
Certainly not, if he's never seen or attended one that was properly organized.

Athenian
Now think about it: wouldn't we say that banquets and drinking parties constitute a kind of social gathering?

Megillus
Of course.

Athenian
And has anyone ever seen this kind of gathering properly run? Cleinias
What do you mean by that, stranger? Explain. Given our inexperience with such things, as you say, we probably wouldn't recognize what's right or wrong about them even if we stumbled into one.

Athenian

Cleinias
Certainly.

Cleinias
We were.

Athenian
A brave man is less likely to be rattled by fear than a coward?

Cleinias
That's true too.

Athenian

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Cleinias
True.

Cleinias
The opposite of calm, yes.

Of course.

Athenian

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian

Cleinias
It would take a miracle indeed.

Cleinias
That last point is very true, stranger. And I can clearly see the advantage of having a good leader for an army — he brings victory in war, and that's an enormous benefit. Athenian
Let me put it this way. Now, education certainly helps produce victory. But victory sometimes leads to forgetting one's education. Many people have become arrogant from winning wars, and that arrogance has bred countless evils in them. Many a victory has been — and will continue to be — suicidal for the victors. Cleinias
You seem to be suggesting, my friend, that well-managed drinking parties are an important part of education.

Athenian
That's exactly what I'm suggesting.

Cleinias
And can you prove it?

Athenian
To be absolutely sure about the truth of matters where many opinions exist — that's a gift of the gods, not given to mortals. But I'll be very happy to tell you what I think, especially since we're now embarking on a discussion about laws and constitutions.

Cleinias
Your opinion about the questions we're raising is exactly what we want to hear.

The people in charge of their education should give them toy versions of real tools. They should learn in advance the basic skills they'll need later for their craft. A future carpenter should learn to use a measuring line through play. A future soldier should learn riding or some other physical skill as a game. Cleinias
Certainly.

We're not talking about that narrow kind of training. That's the only training that deserves to be called "education," in our view. The other kind — training aimed at making money, or building physical strength, or developing cleverness without wisdom and justice — is crude and unworthy of the name.

Cleinias
Very true. We agree with you completely.

Exactly right.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian

Cleinias
I'm barely keeping up. But go on.

Same here.

And this is exactly where virtue and vice part ways.

The argument says that there's one cord among all these that every person should hold onto and never let go — pulling with it against all the rest. That cord is the sacred, golden cord of reason, which we call the common law of the state. The other cords are hard and made of iron. But this one is soft, because it's golden. And there are various other kinds too.

We should always cooperate with the pull of the best cord, which is law. Because reason is beautiful and gentle, not forceful, its rule needs helpers to assist the golden principle in overcoming the others.

And so the moral of this puppet story won't be lost, and the meaning of "superior to oneself" and "inferior to oneself" will become clearer. When an individual grasps the right use of reason in this tug-of-war among the puppet's strings, he should live according to its guidance. When a state receives the same understanding — whether from some god or from someone who knows these things — it should embody that understanding in law, to guide its dealings both internal and external.

In this way, the distinction between virtue and vice will become clearer to us.
