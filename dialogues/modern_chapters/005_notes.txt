Meno -- Translation Notes
===========================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's long Victorian periods broken throughout into
   shorter, more natural sentences. Average length dropped from ~40+ words to
   ~18-20. Meno's opening swarm-of-virtues speech, for instance, was
   restructured into crisp declarative sentences while keeping the breathless
   quality of someone who thinks the answer is obvious.

2. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "enquire" -> "investigate" / "ask" / "search for"
   - "I dare say" -> "I expect" / "probably"
   - "notwithstanding" -> "anyway" / "despite"
   - "in like manner" -> "the same goes for" / "similarly"
   - "hitherto" -> "so far" / "until now"
   - "I will endeavour" -> "I'll try"
   - "whither" -> "where"
   - "torpifies" -> "numbs"
   - "the torpedo fish, who torpifies" -> "that flat torpedo fish -- the one
     that numbs anyone who comes near it"
   - "quid" / "quale" -> replaced with plain English: "what something is" /
     "what it's like"
   - "simile in multis" -> "what's common to all these cases" (the Latin
     was already a translation of Greek; no reason to keep it)
   - "effluences of existence" -> "emanations flowing off everything that
     exists" (slightly clearer while preserving the pseudo-scientific feel)
   - "commensurate with sight, and palpable to sense" -> "fits the channels
     of sight and is perceivable by the senses"

3. CONTRACTIONS used throughout for natural conversational tone:
   "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "you'd," "that's," "can't," "wouldn't,"
   "he's," "I'd," "won't," "there'd," "didn't," "we've," "I've," etc.

4. MENO'S CHARACTER: Meno is a young Thessalian aristocrat -- confident,
   smooth, a bit entitled. His character comes through in several ways:
   - His breezy opening: "That won't be hard at all, Socrates"
   - His imperious demands: "I'd rather you answered, Socrates"
   - His love of flashy answers (prefers the Empedoclean color definition
     over the mathematical shape definition)
   - His clever torpedo-fish comparison -- designed to flatter himself while
     needling Socrates
   - His persistent attempts to redirect the conversation to what interests
     him (can virtue be taught?) rather than Socrates' question (what IS virtue?)
   - Socrates' teasing about his beauty and his "tyrannical" manner

5. THE BEE SWARM METAPHOR: Made vivid and immediate. "I ask you for one
   virtue, and you hand me a whole swarm of them." Then Socrates "runs with
   the image" -- the extended analogy about what makes bees the same vs.
   different is kept intact but made conversational.

6. THE SLAVE-BOY GEOMETRY SCENE: This is the centerpiece of the dialogue.
   Every step of the mathematical argument is preserved and made as clear
   as possible:

   Step 1: Establish the original square (2x2 = 4 sq ft)
   Step 2: Boy's first guess -- double the side (4 ft) gives double area
   Step 3: Socrates shows 4x4 = 16, not 8 -- that's four times, not double
   Step 4: Boy's second guess -- try 3 ft
   Step 5: Socrates shows 3x3 = 9, not 8 -- still wrong
   Step 6: Boy admits he doesn't know
   Step 7: Socrates shows this confusion is progress (the "torpedo's shock")
   Step 8: Socrates draws four 2x2 squares, draws diagonals
   Step 9: The diagonal creates a tilted square of exactly 8 sq ft
   Step 10: Boy recognizes the answer: the square on the diagonal

   Key decision: Added "[To the Boy]" stage directions at transitions to
   keep the reader oriented about who Socrates is addressing, since the
   scene shifts between talking to the boy and commenting to Meno.

7. THE RECOLLECTION THEORY (ANAMNESIS): Presented clearly as a chain of
   reasoning:
   - The soul is immortal and has been born many times
   - Therefore it has seen/learned everything
   - "Learning" is really remembering what the soul already knows
   - The slave-boy demonstration proves this: nobody taught him geometry,
     yet he has true ideas about it
   - These ideas must come from before this life
   - Therefore the soul is immortal

   The Pindar quotation about Persephone is kept intact -- it grounds the
   theory in religious tradition. Socrates' fierce defense of the value of
   inquiry ("I'll fight for this, in word and deed") is given full force.

8. ANYTUS: His entrance is handled carefully. He arrives as a respected
   citizen, but his hostility emerges quickly. Key moments:
   - His visceral hatred of the Sophists ("By Heracles... a plague and a
     poison") -- the language is intense, almost unhinged
   - His refusal to engage with evidence ("I know exactly what kind of
     people they are, whether I've had personal dealings with them or not")
   - Socrates' devastating response: "You must be some kind of prophet"
   - His final veiled threat: "I'd advise you to be careful. There may be
     no city where it's easier to do a man harm than good"

   The ominous undertone is preserved. Anytus will later be one of Socrates'
   prosecutors. The reader who knows this history should feel the chill.

9. THE STATESMEN'S SONS ARGUMENT: Themistocles, Aristides, Pericles,
   Thucydides -- each example follows the same pattern (great father, well-
   educated son, but the son turned out mediocre). The argument is kept
   brisk and cumulative, building to the conclusion that virtue can't be
   taught.

10. KNOWLEDGE VS. TRUE OPINION: The Daedalus statues analogy is the key
    image. Unfastened statues = true opinions (valuable but they wander off).
    Fastened statues = knowledge (valuable AND permanent). The "fastening"
    is reasoning about the cause -- which connects back to recollection.
    Made this chain of images as vivid as possible.

11. THE FINAL TWIST: Virtue turns out to be neither natural, nor taught,
    nor knowledge. It comes as a "divine gift" -- through true opinion
    rather than knowledge. The Homer/Tiresias quotation at the end
    ("He alone has true understanding; the rest are flitting shades") is
    beautifully ambiguous -- suggesting that genuine, knowledge-based
    virtue might be possible but hasn't been found yet.

12. SOCRATES' IRONY: Sharpened throughout:
    - "What luck I'm having, Meno!" (when Meno gives a terrible answer)
    - "Hereditary friend of the Great King of Persia, no less" (mocking
      Meno's aristocratic connections)
    - "You're irresistible" (giving in to Meno's constant redirection)
    - "You must be some kind of prophet, Anytus" (devastating)
    - The entire torpedo-fish exchange -- Socrates refuses to be flattered
      and turns the comparison back on himself with the key qualification:
      "I confuse them because I'm genuinely confused"

13. GREEK OATHS KEPT: "By the gods," "by Zeus," "by Heracles." These are
    characteristic and flavorful.

14. CULTURAL REFERENCES:
    - Thessaly, Larisa, the Aleuadae -- kept with minimal gloss (Socrates
      provides the context himself)
    - Gorgias, Prodicus, Empedocles, Protagoras, Phidias -- kept as-is
    - Polycrates, Ismenias the Theban -- kept (Socrates explains the
      relevance)
    - Themistocles, Aristides, Pericles, Thucydides and their sons -- kept
      as-is; these are the substance of the argument
    - The Great King of Persia -- kept (Socrates is being sarcastic about
      Meno's Persian connections)
    - Pindar, Theognis quotations -- kept and formatted as verse

15. THE HYPOTHESIS METHOD: Socrates introduces the geometric analogy
    (reasoning from hypotheses about triangles and circles) to justify
    investigating whether virtue can be taught before knowing what virtue is.
    The geometric example is deliberately left somewhat opaque -- Plato
    doesn't fully explain it -- but the logical structure is clear: "If X
    belongs to class Y, then Z follows."


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (PROTAGORAS)
-------------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: Established as sharp, conversational, ironic,
   self-deprecating but always in control. Uses contractions freely.
   The Protagoras should maintain this voice but note: the Protagoras
   is narrated by Socrates to a friend, so it needs a frame narrative
   voice as well as dialogue voice.

2. SOPHISTS: The Meno establishes a complex attitude toward Sophists:
   - Socrates doesn't share Anytus's blind hostility
   - He genuinely respects Protagoras's accomplishments (40+ years of
     successful practice, enormous fees)
   - But he's skeptical that anyone has figured out how to teach virtue
   - Gorgias notably does NOT claim to teach virtue -- only rhetoric
   - This nuanced view should carry into the Protagoras, where Protagoras
     is treated as a serious and impressive figure, not a straw man

3. VIRTUE AND KNOWLEDGE: The Meno leaves the question unresolved.
   Virtue might be knowledge (which would make it teachable) or might
   be "divine gift" (true opinion without understanding). The Protagoras
   explores the same territory from a different angle. Maintain the
   sense of genuine philosophical puzzlement.

4. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "virtue" (not "excellence" -- though both are valid for arete)
   - "self-discipline" for sophrosyne (not "temperance" or "moderation")
   - "practical wisdom" or "good judgment" for phronesis
   - "true opinion" (not "right opinion" or "correct belief")
   - "knowledge" for episteme
   - "recollection" for anamnesis
   - "the soul" (kept throughout)
   - Contractions throughout for natural speech

5. MENO'S THESSALIAN CONNECTIONS: Meno is connected to aristocratic
   Thessalian families (the Aleuadae) and to the Great King of Persia.
   He's wealthy and well-connected. This context matters for the
   Protagoras, where the social dynamics of wealthy young men seeking
   out Sophists are central.

6. THE RECOLLECTION THEORY: Established here but not fully developed.
   It recurs in the Phaedrus (chariot allegory, the soul's vision of
   the Forms before birth). Maintain consistency.

7. FORMAT: Dramatic dialogue format (speaker name on own line, then
   speech). The Protagoras will need to shift to narrated format
   (Socrates telling the story to a friend).
