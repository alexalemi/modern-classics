Persons of the dialogue:
  Theodorus
  Theaetetus
  Socrates
  An Eleatic Stranger, whom Theodorus and Theaetetus bring with them
  The younger Socrates, who is a silent auditor


Theodorus
Here we are, Socrates, right on schedule as we agreed yesterday. And we've brought a guest with us -- a stranger from Elea, a student of Parmenides and Zeno. A true philosopher.

Socrates
Are you sure he's not a god in disguise, Theodorus? Homer says the gods -- especially the god of strangers -- travel alongside the humble and the just, watching how humans behave, good and bad alike. Maybe your companion here is one of those higher powers, a cross-examining deity who's come to test our arguments and expose our weaknesses.

Theodorus
No, Socrates, he's not the argumentative type -- he's too good for that. Still, I wouldn't call him a god exactly. But he's certainly divine. That's a title I'd give to all genuine philosophers.

Socrates
Well said, my friend! Though I'd add that philosophers are nearly as hard to recognize as gods. The real ones -- not the ones who just play the part -- show up in all kinds of forms, and most people don't recognize them. They "hover about cities," as Homer puts it, looking down on human life from above. Some people think nothing of them. Others can't stop thinking about them. Sometimes they show up looking like statesmen, sometimes like sophists, and sometimes -- to many people -- they look like outright lunatics. I'd love to ask our Eleatic friend here what people back in Italy think about all this, and who gets called what.

Theodorus
What do you mean? Which terms?

Socrates
Sophist, statesman, philosopher.

Theodorus
What's the difficulty? What made you ask?

Socrates
I want to know whether people in his part of the world think these are one thing, or two, or -- since there are three names -- three genuinely different kinds of person.

Theodorus
I'm sure the Stranger wouldn't mind discussing that. What do you say, Stranger?

Stranger
Not at all, Theodorus. I've got no problem answering: we consider them to be three distinct types. But defining precisely what each one is -- that's no small or easy task.

Theodorus
Well, Socrates, you've happened to ask almost the exact question we were putting to our friend on the way here. He gave us the same excuse he's giving you now -- though he admitted the topic had been thoroughly discussed before, and that he remembered the arguments well.

Socrates
Then please, Stranger, don't deny us the very first favor we ask. Just tell me this: do you usually prefer to work through a topic by giving a long speech, or do you prefer the question-and-answer method? I remember hearing a truly remarkable discussion once, when Parmenides himself used the question-and-answer approach. I was a young man then, and he was very old.

Stranger
I prefer talking with someone who responds agreeably and is easy to work with. If not, I'd rather just give a speech on my own.

Socrates
Well, anyone here would be happy to oblige, so take your pick. But I'd suggest you choose a young person -- Theaetetus, for example -- unless you'd prefer someone else.

Stranger
I'll be honest, Socrates -- being a newcomer to your group, I feel a bit awkward launching into a long monologue instead of having a proper conversation. It would look like I'm showing off. The real answer to this question is going to be very long -- much longer than you'd expect from what seems like a simple question. At the same time, it would be rude to refuse your request, especially after what you've said. So I'm happy to take Theaetetus as my conversation partner, since I've already talked with him and you recommend him.

Theaetetus
But are you sure, Stranger, that the rest of the group will be as happy with this arrangement as Socrates thinks?

Stranger
You hear them clapping, Theaetetus. That settles it. All right then -- I'll be working through this with you, and if you get tired of the argument, blame your friends, not me.

Theaetetus
I don't think I'll get tired. But if I do, I've got my friend here -- young Socrates, the namesake of the older Socrates -- to help out. He's about my age, my training partner at the gymnasium, and we work together all the time.

Stranger
Good. You can make that call as we go. For now, let's begin together by investigating the nature of the sophist -- the first of our three types. I'd like you to work out what he actually is and bring him into the light through discussion. Right now, we only agree on the name. But what exactly the thing is that we're both calling a "sophist" -- we might each have a different idea. And we should always try to reach agreement about the thing itself through a clear definition, not just agree on the name without one.

Now, the tribe of sophists isn't easy to catch or define. And it's a well-established principle that if you want to tackle big subjects properly, you should practice on smaller, easier examples first. So since the sophist is a tricky and elusive target, I'd suggest we warm up our method on something simpler before we go after him. Unless you've got a better approach?

Theaetetus
I don't, actually.

Stranger
Then how about we work through some lesser example that can serve as a pattern for the bigger one?

Theaetetus
Sounds good.

Stranger
What's something familiar and unimportant that's just as open to definition as anything grand? How about an angler? Everyone knows what one is, and he's not exactly a fascinating subject.

Theaetetus
He's not.

Stranger
And yet I suspect he'll give us exactly the kind of definition and line of inquiry we need.

Theaetetus
Works for me.

Stranger
Let's start by asking: is the angler a man with a skill, or someone without any skill who has some other kind of ability?

Theaetetus
He's clearly a man with a skill.

Stranger
And there are two kinds of skill, aren't there?

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
There's farming, the care of living creatures, the building and shaping of tools and containers, and the art of imitation -- all of these can be grouped under a single heading.

Theaetetus
What heading?

Stranger
When someone brings into existence something that didn't exist before, we say he's a producer, and the thing he brings into existence is said to be produced.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And all the skills I just mentioned have this power of producing?

Theaetetus
They do.

Stranger
Then let's group them under the name productive or creative skill.

Theaetetus
Very good.

Stranger
Now, the other whole class includes learning, gaining knowledge, trade, fighting, and hunting. None of these produces anything. They're all about acquiring -- capturing by word or deed, or preventing others from capturing, things that already exist. So across all these branches, there seems to be a skill we could call acquisitive.

Theaetetus
Yes, that's the right name.

Stranger
So all skills are either acquisitive or creative. Which class does angling belong to?

Theaetetus
Clearly the acquisitive class.

Stranger
And the acquisitive can be split into two parts. There's exchange, which is voluntary -- gifts, hiring, purchasing. And then there's the other part, which takes things by force of word or deed. We could call that conquest.

Theaetetus
That follows from what we've said.

Stranger
And can't conquest be divided further?

Theaetetus
How?

Stranger
Open force we'll call fighting. Secret force we'll call hunting.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And there's no reason we can't divide hunting further.

Theaetetus
How would you divide it?

Stranger
Into the hunting of living things and the hunting of lifeless things.

Theaetetus
Sure, if both kinds exist.

Stranger
Of course they do. But hunting lifeless things doesn't really have a proper name -- aside from a few minor cases like diving and the like -- so let's set that aside. The hunting of living things we'll call animal hunting.

Theaetetus
Fine.

Stranger
And animal hunting has two divisions: land-animal hunting, which has many forms and names, and water-animal hunting -- hunting creatures that swim.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And among swimming creatures, some live in the air and some in the water.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Hunting birds in general we call fowling.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And hunting creatures that live in the water we generally call fishing.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
Now, can't this kind of hunting be divided into two main types?

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
One type catches fish with nets, the other strikes them with a blow.

Theaetetus
What do you mean? How do you tell them apart?

Stranger
Well, anything that surrounds and encloses something to keep it from getting out can properly be called an enclosure.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
So wicker traps, casting nets, snares, creels, and the like would all count as enclosures?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
So we can call this first kind of capture "enclosure fishing," or something like that.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
The other kind, which uses a blow -- with hooks and trident-style spears -- we could call strike fishing. Unless you've got a better name, Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
Don't worry about the name. What you've suggested works fine.

Stranger
There's one kind of strike fishing done at night, by firelight. The fishermen themselves call it torch fishing or fire-spearing.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And fishing by day is called barb fishing, since the spears are barbed at the point.

Theaetetus
Yes, that's the term.

Stranger
In barb fishing, the type that strikes downward at fish below is called spearing -- that's how three-pronged spears are usually used.

Theaetetus
Yes, people do call it that.

Stranger
Now there's only one kind left.

Theaetetus
What's that?

Stranger
When you use a hook, and instead of hitting the fish on just any part of its body like a spear does, you catch it by the head and mouth, then draw it upward from below with a rod and line -- what's the right name for that, Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
I think we've just found what we were looking for.

Stranger
So now you and I agree not just on the name "angler" but on the definition of the thing itself. Here's the full chain: half of all skill is acquisitive. Half of acquisitive skill is conquest. Half of conquest is hunting. Half of hunting is animal hunting. Half of animal hunting is water hunting. The lower half of water hunting is fishing. Half of fishing is strike fishing. Half of strike fishing is barb fishing. And the part of barb fishing that strikes with a hook and draws the fish upward from below -- that's the skill we've been looking for. From the nature of the operation, it's called angling.

Theaetetus
That's been worked out very satisfactorily.

Stranger
Now, following this pattern, let's try to figure out what a sophist is.

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Stranger
Our first question about the angler was whether he was a skilled practitioner or unskilled.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
So should we call our new subject unskilled, or a thorough master of his craft?

Theaetetus
Certainly not unskilled. His very name -- "sophist" -- implies some kind of expertise.

Stranger
Then he must have some skill. But what kind?

Theaetetus
What kind?

Stranger
Good heavens -- they're cousins! I never noticed it before.

Theaetetus
Who are cousins?

Stranger
The angler and the sophist.

Theaetetus
How are they related?

Stranger
They both look like hunters to me.

Theaetetus
What does the sophist hunt? We've already covered the angler.

Stranger
Remember how we divided hunting into water-animal hunting and land-animal hunting?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And we went through the water side but left land animals alone, noting that there were many kinds?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
So up to that point, the sophist and the angler travel the same road -- both starting from the acquisitive skill.

Theaetetus
So it seems.

Stranger
They split apart at animal hunting. The angler heads for the shore, the rivers, the lakes, fishing for the creatures in them.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
But the sophist heads for dry land -- or rather, a different kind of water: rivers of wealth and rich meadows full of generous young people. He's hunting the creatures that live there.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
Land hunting has two main divisions.

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
Hunting tame animals and hunting wild ones.

Theaetetus
Wait -- are tame animals really hunted?

Stranger
They are, if you count humans as tame animals. But it's up to you: you could say there are no tame animals, or that humans aren't tame, or that humans are tame but aren't hunted. Your call.

Theaetetus
I'd say humans are tame animals, and yes, they're hunted.

Stranger
Then let's divide the hunting of tame animals into two.

Theaetetus
How?

Stranger
Piracy, kidnapping, tyranny, the entire military profession -- let's lump all of those together as hunting by force.

Theaetetus
Good.

Stranger
But lawyering, public speaking, and the art of conversation -- those we can group together as the art of persuasion.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And persuasion has two kinds.

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
Private and public.

Theaetetus
Yes, each of those is a distinct class.

Stranger
And within private persuasion, one type collects payment, and the other offers gifts.

Theaetetus
I don't follow you.

Stranger
You've apparently never noticed how lovers hunt.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
I mean that on top of their other persuasive tactics, they shower their targets with gifts.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
So let's call that the art of courtship.

Theaetetus
Fine.

Stranger
Now the other kind -- the type where a person's conversation is pleasant and he baits his hook entirely with pleasure, asking nothing in return except his own upkeep -- I think we'd all call that flattery, or the art of making things pleasant.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
But what about the type that claims to seek people's company purely for the sake of virtue, while demanding payment in cash? Doesn't that deserve a different name?

Theaetetus
It does.

Stranger
And what name would that be? Can you tell me?

Theaetetus
It's obvious enough. I think we've found the sophist. That's the right name for this class.

Stranger
So to trace his family tree: the sophist's skill belongs to the acquisitive family -- specifically the branch that hunts animals, living ones, on land, tame ones -- that is, humans -- in private, for payment, under the pretense of education. And this is called sophistry: the hunting of wealthy and prominent young men.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
But let's look at another branch of his family tree. He's a practitioner of a vast, many-sided skill, and if we look back at what we've already done, we can see he has another aspect besides the one we just described.

Theaetetus
In what way?

Stranger
There were two sorts of acquisitive skill: one concerned with hunting, the other with exchange.

Theaetetus
There were.

Stranger
And the art of exchange has two divisions: giving and selling.

Theaetetus
Let's grant that.

Stranger
Now we'll split selling into two parts.

Theaetetus
How?

Stranger
One part is selling your own products. The other is trading in other people's goods.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And isn't the part of trade that happens within a single city -- roughly half the whole thing -- called retail?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And the kind that moves goods from one city to another through buying and selling -- that's merchant trade?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And you realize that merchant trade has two branches: one deals with food for the body, the other with food for the soul, bought and sold for money.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
You want to know what I mean by "food for the soul"? The other kind you understand, I'm sure.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
Think of music, painting, puppet shows, and many other things that are bought in one city and shipped to another to be sold -- some for instruction, some for entertainment. Can't the person who hauls them around and sells them be called a merchant just as legitimately as someone who sells food and drink?

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Stranger
And wouldn't you use the same name for someone who buys up knowledge and goes from city to city trading it for money?

Theaetetus
I certainly would.

Stranger
Of this soul-merchandise, one part could fairly be called the art of display. But there's another part that's just as absurd -- it's a trade in learning, and it needs a name that suits it.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
This learning-trade should have two names: one for the sale of knowledge about virtue, and another for the sale of all other kinds of knowledge.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
"Skill-seller" covers the second kind well enough. But you'll have to come up with a name for the first.

Theaetetus
It has to be the sophist. He's the one we're tracking, and no other name fits.

Stranger
None other. So our friend the sophist turns up again -- this time as a dealer in virtue. His family tree now runs from acquisitive skill through exchange, trade, and merchandise, arriving at a commerce in learning and ideas -- specifically, the knowledge of virtue.

Theaetetus
Exactly right.

Stranger
And he could show up a third way too. Suppose he settles down in a city and, instead of just buying and reselling, he manufactures these same intellectual wares himself, intending to make a living selling them. He'd still be called a sophist, wouldn't he?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
So the part of acquisitive skill that exchanges goods -- whether selling his own products or retailing others' -- as long as it sells the knowledge of virtue, you'd call it sophistry again?

Theaetetus
I'd have to, if I want to keep up with the argument.

Stranger
Let's see if there's yet another face of sophistry.

Theaetetus
What is it?

Stranger
Within the acquisitive branch, there was a subdivision for combat or fighting.

Theaetetus
There was.

Stranger
Maybe we should divide that further.

Theaetetus
What are the divisions?

Stranger
There's the competitive kind and the combative kind.

Theaetetus
Good.

Stranger
The combative kind that involves physical contests of strength -- we could call that something like violent combat.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And when the war is waged with words, we can call it controversy.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And controversy comes in two varieties.

Theaetetus
Which are?

Stranger
When long speeches answer long speeches, and the debate is public and about justice and injustice -- that's courtroom controversy, or forensic debate.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
But there's also a private kind, chopped up into questions and answers. That's commonly called disputation.

Theaetetus
Yes, that's the name.

Stranger
And within disputation, the type that's just casual arguing about contracts and everyday matters -- done randomly, without any method -- well, we can recognize it as a distinct type, but it's never gotten a proper name, and it doesn't deserve one from us.

Theaetetus
No, because the varieties are too small and random to bother with.

Stranger
But the kind that proceeds by method and argues about justice and injustice in their own right, and about general principles -- that's what we normally call eristic, or contentious argumentation.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And eristic comes in two forms: one that wastes money, and one that makes it.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
Let's try to name each one.

Theaetetus
Let's.

Stranger
I'd say the habit that leads a man to neglect his own business for the sheer pleasure of arguing -- in a style that most listeners don't enjoy -- can fairly be called idle chatter, or talkativeness.

Theaetetus
That's the common name for it.

Stranger
And who's the other one -- the person who makes money from private disputation? It's your turn to say.

Theaetetus
There's only one possible answer: it's our wonderful sophist, popping up for the fourth time.

Stranger
Yes, and with a fresh pedigree. He's the money-making branch of the eristic, disputatious, controversial, combative, acquisitive family. The argument has proven it.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
See how true our observation was -- that the sophist is a many-sided creature, not to be caught with one hand, as they say!

Theaetetus
Then you'll have to catch him with two.

Stranger
Yes, we'll have to, if we can. So let's try another track. You know that there are certain household tasks -- the kind servants do -- that have their own names?

Theaetetus
Yes, there are plenty. Which ones do you mean?

Stranger
Things like sifting, straining, winnowing, threshing.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And on top of those, there are many others: carding, spinning, weaving the warp and woof. Thousands of similar terms are used in the various crafts.

Theaetetus
What's the point of listing these? What are they a pattern for?

Stranger
I think all of these activities involve some idea of separating or dividing.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
Then if there's one overarching skill that includes them all, shouldn't it have one name?

Theaetetus
What name?

Stranger
The art of discriminating -- or separating.

Theaetetus
Good.

Stranger
Now see if you can divide that.

Theaetetus
I'd need to think about it for a while.

Stranger
In all the processes I just mentioned, either like is being separated from like, or better from worse.

Theaetetus
I see what you mean now.

Stranger
There's no standard name for the first kind of separation. But the second kind -- which throws away the worse and keeps the better -- I do know a name for that.

Theaetetus
What is it?

Stranger
Every act of discrimination that separates the worse from the better is called purification.

Theaetetus
Yes, that's the usual term.

Stranger
And anyone can see that purification comes in two kinds.

Theaetetus
Maybe, given enough time to think. But I don't see it right now.

Stranger
There are many forms of bodily purification that can be grouped under one heading.

Theaetetus
What are they, and what's the heading?

Stranger
There's the purification of the body's interior and exterior. The internal kind is handled by medicine and exercise. The external kind is handled by the not very glamorous art of bathing. Then there's the purification of lifeless objects -- the work of fullers and polishers, with all their various specialized names that people think are rather silly.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
People do find them silly. But here's the thing, Theaetetus: the method of philosophical inquiry -- dialectic -- doesn't care whether one form of purification is grander or more ridiculous than another. It doesn't consider sponging any less dignified than medicine. What it wants to know is: what's kindred to what across all skills? Its goal is understanding. And in pursuit of that goal, it treats every skill with equal respect. When it compares them, it doesn't find one more ridiculous than another. It doesn't think a general who illustrates "hunting" is any more dignified than an exterminator -- just a bigger pretender.

Now, as for what to call the art that covers all these physical purifications -- of living bodies and lifeless objects alike -- dialectic isn't picky about fine words, so long as it's allowed to have a general term for everything that purifies things other than the soul. Because what it really cares about right now is the purification of the soul -- the purification of the mind. That's what it's aiming at.

Theaetetus
Yes, I understand. And I agree there are two sorts of purification: one concerned with the body, the other with the soul.

Stranger
Excellent. Now listen to what I'm going to say, and try to divide the first of these two -- soul-purification -- even further.

Theaetetus
Whatever line of division you suggest, I'll do my best to follow.

Stranger
Do we accept that virtue is distinct from vice in the soul?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And purification means keeping the good and casting out the bad?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
So any removal of evil from the soul can properly be called purification?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And there are two kinds of evil in the soul.

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
One is comparable to disease in the body. The other is comparable to ugliness or deformity.

Theaetetus
I don't understand.

Stranger
Maybe you've never considered that disease and internal conflict are the same thing?

Theaetetus
I'm not sure what to say to that.

Stranger
You'd agree that discord is a breakdown of things that naturally belong together, caused by some kind of disagreement?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
And deformity -- isn't that just a lack of proper proportion, which is always unsightly?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
And don't we see that in the souls of bad people, beliefs fight against desires, pleasure against anger, reason against pain -- all these elements at war with each other?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And yet all of these elements belong to the same soul -- they're supposed to be kindred?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
Then we'd be right to call vice a kind of internal discord and disease of the soul?

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Stranger
And when things that are in motion, aiming at a target, keep missing and veering off course -- is that the result of proper proportion among them, or a lack of it?

Theaetetus
Clearly a lack of proportion.

Stranger
But surely we know that no soul is voluntarily ignorant of anything?

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
And what is ignorance, really, except the wandering of a mind that's aiming at truth, where the process of understanding has gone astray?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
So we should consider an unintelligent soul to be deformed -- out of proportion?

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
So there are these two kinds of evil in the soul. The first -- what everyone calls vice -- is clearly a disease of the soul.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And then there's the other one -- ignorance -- which people won't admit is a vice, even though it exists only in the soul.

Theaetetus
I'll grant what I resisted at first: there really are two kinds of evil in the soul. We should consider cowardice, excess, and injustice to be forms of disease in the soul. And ignorance -- which comes in all sorts of varieties -- should be considered deformity.

Stranger
And for the body, aren't there two skills that deal with these two conditions?

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
Exercise deals with deformity, and medicine deals with disease.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And where there's arrogance, injustice, and cowardice -- isn't punishment the skill that's most needed?

Theaetetus
That certainly seems to be what people think.

Stranger
And for the various kinds of ignorance, wouldn't instruction be the right remedy?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Now, would we say there's one kind of instruction, or many? There are at least two principal ones. Think about it.

Theaetetus
I will.

Stranger
I think I see how we can reach the answer quickest.

Theaetetus
How?

Stranger
By finding a line that divides ignorance into two halves. If ignorance splits into two parts, then instruction must also be twofold, matching each division.

Theaetetus
And do you see what you're looking for?

Stranger
I think I see one very large and bad kind of ignorance that stands apart -- it could be weighed against all the other kinds put together.

Theaetetus
What is it?

Stranger
Thinking you know something when you actually don't. That seems to be the source of all the errors of the intellect.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And this, if I'm not mistaken, is the kind of ignorance that specifically earns the label of stupidity.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
So what should we call the kind of instruction that cures this?

Theaetetus
I'd say, Stranger, that what you're describing isn't the teaching of practical skills, but what we here in Greece have come to call education.

Stranger
Yes, Theaetetus, and nearly all Greeks call it that. But we still need to consider whether education can be divided further.

Theaetetus
We do.

Stranger
I think it can be divided at a certain point.

Theaetetus
Where?

Stranger
There's a rougher method of education and a smoother one.

Theaetetus
How do we tell them apart?

Stranger
There's the old-fashioned approach our fathers commonly used with their sons, and which many still use -- either scolding them harshly for their mistakes, or advising them gently. Both of these fall under the general heading of admonition.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
But some people have concluded that all ignorance is involuntary -- that nobody who thinks he's already clever will ever be willing to learn the very things he's convinced he already knows. And they've noticed that the admonishing approach, no matter how much effort it takes, doesn't accomplish much.

Theaetetus
They're quite right about that.

Stranger
So these people set about rooting out the spirit of false confidence in a different way.

Theaetetus
What way?

Stranger
They cross-examine a person's stated beliefs. When he thinks he's saying something meaningful but is actually saying nothing coherent, they easily expose the contradictions in his opinions. They collect these contradictions through the dialectical process, lay them side by side, and show that the person holds opposing views about the same things, in the same respects, at the same time. The person, seeing this, becomes angry with himself and gentler toward others. He's entirely freed from the big prejudices and rigid opinions that had been blocking him. And the process is wonderfully entertaining for anyone listening, while producing the most lasting benefit for the person undergoing it.

Here's the principle, Theaetetus: just as a doctor knows the body won't benefit from food until internal blockages are removed, the purifier of the soul understands that his patient won't benefit from new knowledge until he's been shown his errors. "Is 'being' the same as 'one'? Are you using two names for the same thing?"

Theaetetus
What will they say, Stranger?

Stranger
It's clear, Theaetetus, that anyone who holds the unity of being will have a hard time answering this -- or any other question.

Theaetetus
Why?

Stranger
Admitting two names while insisting there's nothing but unity is pretty ridiculous, isn't it?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And it's equally irrational to admit that a name exists as something real.

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
Because distinguishing the name from the thing implies duality.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And yet if you identify the name with the thing, you're forced to say the name is a name of nothing. Or if you say it's the name of something, the name turns out to be merely the name of a name, and of nothing else.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And "the one" will turn out to be merely the one of one -- and being, as absolute unity, will amount to nothing but a bare name.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Now, would they say "the whole" is something different from "the one that is," or the same?

Theaetetus
They'd certainly answer -- and they actually do say it's the same.

Stranger
But if being is a whole -- as Parmenides sings:

"Every way like the fullness of a well-rounded sphere,
equally balanced from the center on every side,
and neither greater nor less in any way,
neither on this side nor on that" --

then being has a center and extremities. And if it has those, it must have parts.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Now, something that has parts could still have the attribute of unity across all its parts. Being everything and being a whole, it could still in that way be one.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
But something in that condition can't be absolute unity, can it?

Theaetetus
Why not?

Stranger
Because what is truly one, according to right reason, must be absolutely indivisible.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
But something made up of many parts while being called indivisible -- that contradicts reason.

Theaetetus
I understand.

Stranger
So should we say being is one and a whole because it has the attribute of unity? Or should we say being isn't a whole at all?

Theaetetus
That's a hard choice.

Stranger
It really is. Because being, while having in a certain sense the attribute of oneness, turns out not to be the same as "the one." So "the all" is more than one.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And yet if being isn't a whole -- if it merely has the attribute of unity, and if wholeness itself exists as something real -- then being falls short of itself. It lacks something.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And on this reasoning, being, by lacking being, will become not-being.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And once again, "the all" becomes more than one, because being and the whole will each have their own separate nature.

Theaetetus
Yes.
