Crito — Translation Notes
===========================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's long Victorian sentences — some running 100+
   words — have been broken into shorter, more natural units. Average sentence
   length dropped from ~45 words to ~18. This is especially important in the
   Laws' speech, where the arguments need to build step by step rather than
   overwhelm in dense paragraphs.

2. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "repining at the approach of death" → "upset about dying"
   - "your zeal is invaluable" → "your passion is admirable"
   - "hitherto honoured and revered" → "honored my whole life"
   - "hobgoblin terrors" → "monsters under the bed"
   - "the censure and welcome the praise" → "worry about criticism and welcome praise"
   - "whither tending and what affecting" → "what kind of harm, what part of him"
   - "the halt, the blind, the maimed" → "the lame, the blind, the crippled"
   - "the flatterer of all men, and the servant of all men" → "flattering and
     groveling before everyone, living as a servant"
   - "metamorphosed as the manner is of runaways" → "like a common fugitive"
   - "want of courage" → "cowardice" / "gutless"
   - "play truant" → "break out, escape"

3. CONTRACTIONS used throughout: "I'm," "don't," "isn't," "wouldn't,"
   "he's," "I've," "can't," "won't," "you're," "they'd," etc.
   Both Socrates and Crito speak with natural contractions.

4. TONE — THE INTIMATE REGISTER: The Crito is unique among the trial
   dialogues. It's not a public defense (Apology) or a cosmic meditation
   (Phaedo). It's two old friends talking quietly before dawn in a prison
   cell. The language is kept warm and personal:
   - "my dear, dear friend" (Crito's emotional plea)
   - "Dear Crito" (Socrates' gentle address)
   - "my friend" (before the key philosophical point)
   The affection between them is palpable without being sentimental.

5. CRITO'S EMOTIONAL PLEA (his long speech urging escape): Restructured
   from one massive Jowett paragraph into a series of distinct emotional
   appeals, each landing separately:
   - The personal loss argument
   - The reputation argument
   - The money-is-available argument
   - The exile-would-work argument
   - The abandoning-your-children argument
   - The shame-on-all-of-us argument
   - The urgent time-pressure argument
   Each builds on the last. Crito is throwing everything he has at this.

6. THE ATHLETIC TRAINING ANALOGY: Kept the step-by-step Socratic method
   intact. Short questions, short answers. The argument builds from
   "listen to experts about your body" to "then listen to experts about
   your soul" through small, irrefutable steps. Each "yes" and "certainly"
   is Crito committing to a position.

7. THE PROSOPOPOEIA — PERSONIFICATION OF THE LAWS: This is the
   centerpiece of the dialogue. Major decisions:

   a) VOICE: The Laws speak like a stern but loving parent — a combination
      of authority and heartbreak. They're not cold or legalistic; they're
      genuinely hurt that their best citizen is about to betray them.

   b) STRUCTURE: Broke the Laws' speech into three distinct movements:
      - Movement 1: "We made you." The parent-child argument. You owe us
        your existence, your education, your formation. You can't treat
        us as equals.
      - Movement 2: "You chose us." The social contract argument. You
        were free to leave at any time. You stayed for 70 years. You
        raised your family here. You explicitly preferred death to exile.
      - Movement 3: "Think about what happens if you leave." The
        consequentialist argument. Your friends suffer. You become a
        laughingstock. Your children are worse off. Your principles are
        destroyed.

   c) BUILDING INTENSITY: The Laws' speech escalates from reasonable
      argument to moral indignation to genuine warning. The final passage
      — about being received as an enemy by the Laws of the underworld —
      has an almost prophetic quality.

   d) KEPT VIVID DETAILS: The goatskin disguise, eating and drinking in
      Thessaly, the image of being "more rooted in Athens than the lame,
      the blind, the crippled" — these concrete details make the
      philosophical argument tangible.

8. THE ENDING: Kept deliberately quiet and understated. After the
   thundering speech of the Laws, the dialogue ends in near-silence:
   - "I have nothing to say, Socrates."
   - "Then leave me, Crito, to fulfill the will of God, and to follow
     where he leads."
   The contrast between the Laws' passionate oratory and this still,
   resigned ending is one of the most powerful moments in Plato.

9. SOCRATIC IRONY — LIGHTER HERE: Unlike the Euthyphro (where irony is
   the dominant mode), Socrates isn't being ironic with Crito. He's being
   gentle with a friend. The irony, when it appears, is directed outward:
   - "trying to scare us like children with monsters under the bed"
   - "who'd be just as ready to bring people back to life as they are to
     kill them, and with just as little thought"
   These jabs are at the crowd, not at Crito.

10. THE SHIP FROM DELOS: Kept the historical reference without
    explanation. The context makes the meaning clear: Socrates can't be
    executed until the sacred ship returns from its annual pilgrimage.

11. THE HOMER QUOTATION: "On the third day you shall reach the fertile
    shores of Phthia" — from the Iliad (Achilles threatening to sail
    home). Socrates interprets it as meaning he'll "reach home" (die)
    on the third day. Kept the quote intact but slightly modernized
    the phrasing.

12. PHILOSOPHICAL ARGUMENTS — ALL PRESERVED:
    - Expert vs. crowd opinion (the athletic trainer analogy)
    - Body-soul analogy (if a ruined body makes life not worth living,
      a ruined soul makes it even less so)
    - "Not life, but a good life" — the key principle
    - Never return evil for evil (the radical moral claim)
    - The social contract (you agreed to obey by staying)
    - The three charges against the lawbreaker (disobeying parents,
      disobeying educators, breaking an agreement)
    - The "obey or persuade" doctrine

13. KEY PHRASES PRESERVED:
    - "Leave me then, Crito, to fulfill the will of God" — the famous
      closing line, kept nearly verbatim
    - "Not life, but a good life" — kept the formulation
    - "Never return evil for evil" — kept the principle's stark clarity


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (PHAEDO)
----------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: In the Crito, Socrates is calm, warm, gentle but
   completely resolute. The Phaedo should continue this serenity — he's
   made his peace with death. The Phaedo is narrated by Phaedo to
   Echecrates, so the format shifts to narrated dialogue ("Socrates
   said... I replied...").

2. CRITO APPEARS IN PHAEDO: He's present at the death scene. He asks
   Socrates about funeral arrangements. Maintain the warm, practical,
   slightly flustered quality established here. Crito is the friend who
   handles logistics — money, arrangements, practical matters.

3. SIMMIAS AND CEBES: Mentioned here as friends willing to fund the
   escape. They become the primary interlocutors in the Phaedo, raising
   objections to Socrates' arguments for immortality. Their introduction
   here as loyal, generous friends should color how we present them in
   the Phaedo.

4. THE LAWS' ARGUMENTS: The social contract theory and "obey or persuade"
   doctrine established here provide context for Socrates' calm acceptance
   of death in the Phaedo. He's not being passive — he's living out the
   principles he articulated to Crito.

5. DEATH AS "GOING TO THE NEXT WORLD": The Laws mention "the rulers of
   the underworld" and "the Laws of the world below." The Phaedo will
   develop the afterlife extensively with philosophical arguments for
   immortality. The groundwork is laid here.

6. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "the will of God" (Socrates' pious acceptance)
   - "justice" (consistent lowercase, the central concept)
   - Contractions throughout for natural speech
   - "the Laws" (capitalized when personified)
   - "the crowd" / "most people" / "ordinary people" (for hoi polloi)
   - "doing wrong" / "returning evil for evil" (for the moral arguments)

7. TONE SHIFT FOR PHAEDO: The Crito is intimate and quiet. The Phaedo
   is expansive and transcendent. Socrates moves from practical ethics
   (should I escape?) to cosmic philosophy (what happens after death?).
   The emotional register deepens: the Crito ends with resignation;
   the Phaedo ends with one of the most devastating death scenes in
   literature.

8. FORMAT NOTE: The Phaedo uses narrated dialogue format. Phaedo tells
   Echecrates about Socrates' last day. Use "Socrates said" / "I said"
   / "he replied" style, not dramatic speaker labels. This is important
   for the emotional framing — we're hearing about these events through
   a grieving friend's memory.
