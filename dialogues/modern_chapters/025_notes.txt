TRANSLATION NOTES — 025 (LACHES)

OVERVIEW
--------
Laches is a dialogue on courage (andreia) that begins with a practical question —
should our sons learn to fight in armor? — and evolves into a Socratic examination
of what courage actually is. It's one of the warmest of the early dialogues: Laches
and Nicias are Socrates' friends and fellow soldiers, not adversaries. The tone is
collegial even when the argument gets sharp.

MAJOR CHANGES FROM JOWETT
---------------------------

1. LYSIMACHUS' OPENING SPEECH
   Jowett's version is a single enormous paragraph of over 300 words with deeply
   nested subordinate clauses. Broken into multiple shorter paragraphs with clearer
   topic sentences. The confessional quality ("we are ashamed") is preserved but
   the syntax is modernized. Removed "in the fulfilment of a common duty" (stiff)
   in favor of "fulfilling what's really a shared responsibility."

2. NICIAS' SPEECH ON FIGHTING IN ARMOR
   Jowett's military arguments are sound but delivered in Victorian lecture style.
   Modernized sentence structure while keeping every argument intact. "He who
   possessed the art could not meet with any harm at the hands of a single person"
   → "A man who knows this art wouldn't come to any harm at the hands of a single
   opponent." Changed "let me add a further advantage, which is by no means a
   slight one" → "I'll add one more advantage, and it's not a small one."

3. LACHES' STESILAUS ANECDOTE
   This is the comic highlight of the dialogue and Laches at his most vivid. Kept
   the story's full detail and pacing — it reads like a soldier's barracks story.
   "The singularity of this weapon was worthy of the singularity of the man" →
   "a contraption as peculiar as the man himself." The physical comedy of the
   scene (running along the deck, spear sliding through hands, crew laughing)
   is given full room to breathe.

4. LACHES' SPEECH ON MUSIC AND VIRTUE
   His distinction between loving and hating discourse is one of the most eloquent
   passages in the early dialogues. Preserved the musical modes metaphor (Dorian,
   Ionian, Phrygian, Lydian) because it's central to his character — a soldier who
   expresses himself through musical analogy. "I am delighted beyond measure" →
   "I'm absolutely thrilled." "So eager am I in drinking in his words" → "I drink
   in his words so eagerly."

5. NICIAS' WARNING ABOUT SOCRATES
   His description of the Socratic method ("carried round and round... will not let
   him go until he has completely and thoroughly sifted him") is preserved in full.
   This is one of the best ancient descriptions of what it's like to talk to
   Socrates. "An intellectual affinity" → "gets close to Socrates." "Entangled" →
   "got you."

6. SOCRATIC CROSS-EXAMINATION SECTIONS
   Kept the question-and-answer rhythm tight throughout. Short questions, short
   answers, building momentum. The two main definitional sequences (Laches:
   courage = endurance; Nicias: courage = knowledge of what to fear and hope for)
   are preserved in full with every logical step intact.

7. SOCRATES' LONG SPEECH TO LYSIMACHUS
   His proposal that they all examine their credentials (who were our teachers?
   what results can we show?) is a masterpiece of ironic modesty. "I am the first
   to confess that I have never had a teacher of the art of virtue" — kept nearly
   verbatim because the irony is already clear. The "learning to make pots by
   breaking the big jar" proverb is rendered naturally.

CHARACTER VOICES
-----------------

LACHES — Blunt, practical, a soldier through and through. Speaks in concrete
examples (the Stesilaus story, the Spartans, actual combat). Gets frustrated
with abstraction ("What kind of wisdom could he possibly mean?"). His irritation
with Nicias is that of a plain-speaking man annoyed by what he sees as verbal
gymnastics. Uses shorter, punchier sentences than Nicias.

NICIAS — More polished and intellectual. Comfortable with abstract ideas.
His definition of courage as knowledge is genuinely sophisticated — he's
drawing on Socratic ideas he's actually heard. But he also has a bit of
intellectual vanity (his parting shot about Damon helping him). Uses longer,
more structured sentences than Laches.

SOCRATES — At his warmest and most collaborative here. Less adversarial than
in Euthyphro or the Republic. Genuine respect for both men. The irony is
gentler — more "help me understand" than "let me demolish you." His military
examples (Scythians, Plataea) show he knows this world.

LYSIMACHUS — An elderly, somewhat bumbling man, embarrassed by his own
mediocrity but earnest about his sons. His confession about living in his
father's shadow is genuinely touching. He freely admits his memory is failing.

VOCABULARY MAPPINGS
--------------------

"enquire" / "enquiring" → "ask" / "looking for" / "investigating"
"endeavour" → "try" / "do my best"
"perchance" → "by chance"
"in like manner" → "in the same way"
"I dare say" → "I'd guess" / "probably"
"it would seem" → "it seems" / "apparently"
"notwithstanding" → dropped/rephrased
"the acquirement of this art" → "learning this art"
"befitting to a freeman" → "fitting skills for a free man"
"hither" → rephrased contextually
"valour" → "courage" / "bravery"
"traduced" → "torn apart"
"fain" → "gladly"
"discourse" → "conversation" / "discussion"
"the vile corpus of a Carian slave" → "some worthless test subject" (with
  the original cultural reference removed as too obscure; the meaning is clear)

PHILOSOPHICAL TERMS
--------------------

"virtue" (arete) — kept as "virtue" throughout; fits naturally here
"endurance of the soul" — kept as Laches' formulation; clear and memorable
"knowledge of the grounds of fear and hope" → "knowledge of what is truly to
  be feared and what can be safely hoped for" (Nicias' definition, slightly
  expanded for clarity)
"the fearful and the hopeful" — kept this pairing as Socrates' shorthand

STRUCTURAL NOTES
-----------------

The dialogue has a clear three-act structure:
  Act I (opening through Socrates taking charge): Practical question about
    fighting in armor, character introductions, Socrates redirecting to philosophy
  Act II (defining courage): Laches' definition (endurance) examined and found
    wanting; Nicias' definition (knowledge) examined and also found wanting
  Act III (conclusion): Aporia — no one has found the answer. Socrates proposes
    they all become students together.

All three acts preserved in full. No exchanges were merged or summarized.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
-------------------------------------

- Socratic irony is gentler in dialogues with friends vs. adversaries
- "By Zeus" and "by the gods" preserved as character-specific oaths
- The Dorian mode metaphor (words matching deeds) appears in Laches' speech and
  is echoed by Socrates later — preserved this callback
- The Spartans/Lacedaemonians are referenced multiple times — used "Spartans"
  consistently in translation for clarity (Jowett alternates)
- Speaker format: speaker name on own line, speech below, blank line between
  exchanges — consistent with Euthyphro
- Contractions used throughout in speech: "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "that's,"
  "wouldn't," "can't," "I'd," "won't," "he's," etc.
- The "Or, Courage" subtitle is moved to the top with the dramatis personae,
  matching how subtitles appear in the collection
- The Crommyonian sow reference kept — it's vivid and funny; readers can
  look it up if curious (a mythical monstrous pig)
- Damon and Prodicus mentioned as Nicias' intellectual sources — these
  references may recur in other dialogues
