Charmides

Or, On Temperance

Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates, who is the narrator
  Charmides
  Chaerephon
  Critias

Scene: The Palaestra of Taureas, near the Porch of the King Archon.


The evening before, I'd gotten back from the army at Potidaea, and after being away for a good while, I was itching to visit my old haunts. So I went to the palaestra of Taureas, which is across from the temple next to the porch of the King Archon. I found a good number of people there -- most of them I knew, though not all. My visit was unexpected, and the moment they spotted me coming in, they called out greetings from every direction. Chaerephon -- who is, let's say, somewhat unhinged -- jumped up and came running over to me, grabbing my hand and saying:

"Socrates! How did you make it out alive?"

(I should explain that there'd been an engagement at Potidaea shortly before we left, and the news had only just reached Athens.)

"Well," I said, "as you can see -- here I am."

"The report was that the fighting was brutal," he said, "and that many people we know were killed."

"That's not far from the truth," I replied.

"I assume you were there?"

"I was."

"Then sit down and tell us everything! We've only gotten fragments so far."

He sat me down next to Critias, son of Callaeschrus, and once I'd greeted him and the rest of the company, I gave them the news from the army and answered all their various questions.

When we'd had enough of that, I started asking my own questions about things back home -- how was philosophy going? Were there any young men who'd distinguished themselves for their intellect or good looks, or both? Critias glanced toward the door, where some young men were coming in, chatting noisily with each other, trailed by a crowd.

"As for the good-looking ones, Socrates," he said, "I think you'll be able to judge for yourself shortly. Those fellows coming in now are the advance guard of the great beauty of the day, as he's considered. And I expect the man himself isn't far behind."

"Who is he?" I asked. "And who's his father?"

"Charmides," he said. "He's my cousin -- my uncle Glaucon's son. I think you know him, actually, though he was just a boy when you left."

"Of course I know him," I said. "He was already remarkable even as a child. By now he must be practically a young man."

"You'll see in a moment how he's turned out." He'd barely finished saying this when Charmides walked in.

Now, my friend, I have to confess that I'm a terrible judge of these things. When it comes to beauty, I'm about as reliable as a white line drawn in chalk -- almost every young person looks beautiful to me. But when I saw Charmides come in, even I was stunned by his looks and his build. Everyone in the room seemed to be in love with him. A wave of amazement and confusion went through the crowd as he entered, and a whole train of admirers followed in his wake. That grown men like us would be affected this way wasn't especially surprising. But I noticed the same thing among the boys -- every last one of them, down to the very youngest, turned and stared at him as if he were a statue.

Chaerephon nudged me. "Well, Socrates? What do you think? Doesn't he have a beautiful face?"

"Incredibly beautiful," I said.

"But you wouldn't think much of his face," Chaerephon replied, "if you could see him stripped down. His body is absolutely perfect."

Everyone else agreed.

"By Heracles," I said, "he sounds like a paragon -- if he's got just one other small addition."

"What's that?" asked Critias.

"A noble soul. And being from your family, Critias, he probably does."

"He's just as fine on the inside as on the outside," said Critias.

"Well then," I said, "before we inspect his body, shouldn't we ask him to show us his soul, stripped bare? He's just the right age for conversation."

"Oh, absolutely," said Critias. "I should tell you -- he's already quite the philosopher. And a pretty good poet too. Not just in his own estimation, but in other people's."

"That's a distinction that runs in your family, my dear Critias," I replied. "You inherited it from Solon. But why not call him over and introduce us? Even if he were younger than he is, there'd be nothing improper about him talking with us -- not with you here, his guardian and cousin."

"Fair enough," he said. And turning to an attendant, he called out: "Go get Charmides. Tell him I want him to come meet a doctor about that headache he mentioned the other day." Then, turning back to me, he added: "He's been complaining about getting headaches in the morning. Why not let him think you know a cure?"

"Why not?" I said. "But will he actually come?"

"Oh, he'll come."

And come he did. He sat down between Critias and me. What happened next was highly entertaining: everyone on the bench started shoving their neighbor as hard as they could, trying to make room for Charmides next to themselves. At one end of the row, somebody got forced to his feet. At the other end, someone was practically rolled off sideways.

Now, my friend, I was starting to feel a little flustered. My earlier confidence about being able to have a smooth conversation with him had evaporated. And when Critias told him that I was the one with the headache cure, Charmides looked at me in this indescribable way and was just about to ask me a question -- at which point the entire palaestra crowded in around us. And right at that moment, I caught a glimpse inside his cloak, and -- well, I caught fire. I couldn't contain myself. I thought of how well the poet Cydias understood the nature of love, when he warned someone about a beautiful youth: "Don't bring the fawn into the lion's sight, or the lion will eat it." That's exactly what I felt -- like some kind of wild animal seizing its prey. But I pulled myself together, and when Charmides asked me if I knew the cure for headaches, I managed to answer -- with some effort -- that I did.

"What is it?" he asked.

I told him it was a kind of herb, but it had to be accompanied by a charm. If you repeated the charm while applying the remedy, the cure would be complete. But without the charm, the herb was useless on its own.

"Then I'll write down the charm from your dictation," he said.

"With my consent?" I asked. "Or without it?"

"With your consent, Socrates," he said, laughing.

"Very good. And you're quite sure you know my name?"

"I should think so," he said. "People talk about you quite a bit among my friends. And I remember seeing you with my cousin Critias when I was a child."

"I'm glad you remember me," I said. "That puts me more at ease, and I'll be better able to explain what this charm is about. I was having trouble knowing where to start. You see, the charm does more than just cure headaches. I expect you've heard distinguished doctors say that when a patient comes to them with bad eyes, they can't just treat the eyes in isolation -- they need to treat the whole head if they want the eyes to get better. And then they say it would be the height of folly to try to cure the head without treating the rest of the body too. Following this logic, they apply their methods to the whole body, trying to heal the whole along with the part. You've heard this kind of thing?"

"Yes," he said.

"And you'd agree with them?"

"Yes, certainly."

His eager answers reassured me, and little by little my confidence started coming back, and the old fire returned.

"Well, Charmides," I said, "that's exactly how this charm works. I learned it on campaign from a doctor who served the Thracian king Zamolxis. These Thracian physicians are said to be so skilled that they can even bestow immortality. This particular Thracian told me that the Greek doctors are right as far as they go, but that Zamolxis, their king -- who is also a god -- goes further. He says: 'You shouldn't try to cure the eyes without the head, or the head without the body. And just as surely, you shouldn't try to cure the body without the soul. This is the reason,' he said, 'why so many diseases baffle the Greek doctors -- they neglect the whole, which also needs attention. The part can never be well unless the whole is well.' For all good and evil, whether in the body or anywhere else in our nature, originates in the soul and flows outward from there, the way things flow from the head into the eyes. So if the head and body are to be well, you have to begin by curing the soul. That comes first. And the cure for the soul, my dear young man, consists of certain charms -- and these charms are beautiful words. Through beautiful words, temperance is planted in the soul. And once temperance takes root, health quickly spreads not just to the head, but to the whole body."

"When he taught me this remedy and this charm, he added a strict instruction: 'Let no one persuade you to treat his head until he has first given you his soul to be treated by the charm. The great mistake of our time,' he said, 'in treating the human body, is that doctors try to separate the soul from the body.' And he made me swear an oath to follow his words: 'Let no one, however rich, or noble, or beautiful, persuade you to give him the remedy without the charm.'"

"Now, I've sworn this oath, and I intend to keep it. So if you'll let me apply the Thracian charm to your soul first, just as the stranger directed, I'll go ahead and apply the cure to your head afterward. But if not, I'm afraid I don't know what to do with you, my dear Charmides."

When Critias heard all this, he said: "What an unexpected bonus this headache will turn out to be for my young cousin -- if the pain in his head forces him to improve his mind. But I should tell you, Socrates, that Charmides is outstanding not only for his looks among his peers, but also for the very quality your charm is supposed to produce. You said that quality is temperance, right?"

"Yes," I said.

"Well, let me tell you -- he's the most temperate person alive, and for his age, second to none in any quality."

"That's only fitting, Charmides," I said. "Because I don't think there's anyone here who could easily point to two Athenian families whose union would be likely to produce a finer young man than you. Your father's house descends from Critias, son of Dropidas -- a family celebrated in the poetry of Anacreon, Solon, and many other poets for its beauty, virtue, and every other blessing. And your mother's side is equally distinguished. Your maternal uncle Pyrilampes is said to have had no equal for height and good looks -- not in Persia at the Great King's court, not anywhere on the Asian continent, in all the places he traveled as ambassador. That entire family is every bit as impressive as the other. With ancestors like these, you ought to be first in everything. And I must say, sweet son of Glaucon, your outward appearance is no disgrace to any of them."

"Now, if to beauty you also add temperance, and if you're everything Critias here says you are -- then, dear Charmides, your mother is truly blessed in her son. And here's where we stand: if, as Critias claims, you already possess this gift of temperance, then you don't need any charms -- not from Zamolxis, not from Abaris the Hyperborean -- and I might as well give you the headache remedy right now. But if you haven't yet acquired it, then I need to apply the charm before I can give you the medicine. So tell me yourself -- is Critias right? Do you already possess temperance, or not?"

Charmides blushed, and the blush only made him more beautiful -- modesty suits a young man. Then he said, quite honestly, that he really couldn't answer yes or no to my question.

"Because if I say I'm not temperate," he explained, "that would be a strange thing to say about myself, and besides, I'd be calling Critias a liar -- along with everyone else who thinks I'm temperate. But if I say I am, then I'm praising myself, which would be bad manners. So I really don't know how to answer you."

"That's a perfectly natural reply, Charmides," I said. "I think the best thing would be for us to investigate together whether you have this quality or not. That way you won't be forced to say anything you don't want to, and I won't be practicing medicine recklessly. If that sounds good to you, I'd like us to examine the question together. But I won't press you if you'd rather not."

"There's nothing I'd like better," he said. "As far as I'm concerned, go about it however you think best."

"Here's how I think we should start," I said. "If temperance is really present in you, you must have some sense of it. It must give you some hint of its nature -- some impression that would let you form an idea of what it is. Don't you think so?"

"Yes," he said, "I think that's right."

"And you speak Greek," I said, "so you should be able to put into words what you feel about it."

"I'd think so," he said.

"Good. So in order for me to get a sense of whether temperance is in you or not, tell me: in your opinion, what is temperance?"

At first he hesitated and was clearly reluctant to answer. But then he said he thought temperance was doing things in an orderly and quiet way -- things like walking in the streets, and talking, and everything else of that sort. "To put it in a word," he said, "I'd say temperance is quietness."

"Are you right about that, Charmides?" I said. "Some people would certainly say that the quiet ones are the temperate ones. But let's see if those words really hold up. First, you'd agree that temperance belongs to the category of noble and good things?"

"Yes."

"Well, which is better at the writing teacher's: writing the same letters quickly, or slowly and quietly?"

"Quickly."

"And reading quickly, or slowly?"

"Quickly."

"And in playing the lyre, or wrestling -- quickness and sharpness are far better than slowness and quietness?"

"Yes."

"The same goes for boxing and the pancratium?"

"Yes."

"And in running, jumping, and physical exercise generally -- quick and agile movements are good, while slow, inactive, quiet ones are bad?"

"Obviously."

"So in every physical activity," I said, "it's not quietness but quickness and agility that are most admirable?"

"Yes, certainly."

"And temperance is a good thing?"

"Yes."

"Then when it comes to the body, quickness -- not quietness -- would be the more temperate quality, if temperance is good?"

"That does seem to follow," he said.

"All right," I said. "Which is better -- finding something easy to learn, or hard to learn?"

"Easy."

"And ease in learning means learning quickly, while difficulty means learning slowly and quietly?"

"Yes."

"And isn't it better to teach someone quickly and energetically, rather than slowly and quietly?"

"Yes."

"And which is better -- remembering things quickly and readily, or slowly?"

"Quickly."

"And isn't cleverness a kind of quickness of the mind, not a quietness?"

"Yes."

"And isn't it better to understand what's being said -- whether at a writing lesson or a music lesson or anywhere else -- as quickly as possible, not as quietly as possible?"

"Yes."

"And when the mind is searching and deliberating, it's not the person who does it most quietly and with the most difficulty who earns our praise -- it's the one who does it most easily and quickly?"

"Exactly right," he said.

"So in everything concerning both body and mind, swiftness and energy are clearly better than slowness and quietness?"

"Clearly."

"Then temperance isn't quietness. And the temperate life isn't a quiet life -- at least not on this argument. Because the temperate life is supposed to be the good life. And here's where we end up: either quiet actions are never -- or very rarely -- better than quick and energetic ones; or even if some noble actions are quiet, just as many are quick and vigorous. Either way, temperance can't be defined as acting quietly rather than acting quickly, whether in walking or talking or anything else. The quiet life isn't more temperate than the active one, since we've agreed that temperance is something noble and good, and quick actions have been shown to be just as good as quiet ones."

"I think you're right, Socrates," he said.

"Then let's try again, Charmides," I said. "Focus your attention inward. Consider the effect that temperance has on you, and think about what kind of thing could produce that effect. Think it all over carefully, and then -- like the brave young man you are -- tell me: what is temperance?"

After a pause, during which he made a genuinely admirable effort to think it through, he said: "My view, Socrates, is that temperance makes a person feel ashamed -- makes them modest. I think temperance is the same as modesty."

"Very good," I said. "And didn't you just agree that temperance is noble?"

"Yes, certainly."

"And the temperate are also good?"

"Yes."

"And can something be good if it doesn't make people good?"

"Certainly not."

"So temperance is not only noble, but also good?"

"That's my view."

"All right," I said. "But you'd agree with Homer when he says: 'Modesty is not good for a needy man'?"

"Yes, I agree with that."

"So it seems that modesty is both good and not good?"

"Apparently."

"But temperance -- whose presence makes people only good, never bad -- is always good?"

"That seems right."

"Then temperance can't be modesty, if temperance is always a good thing but modesty is sometimes an evil as well as a good."

"I think everything you've said is correct, Socrates," he said. "But I'd like to know what you think about another definition of temperance that I just remembered hearing from someone: 'Temperance is doing your own business.' Was the person who said that right?"

"You rascal!" I said. "That's something you heard from Critias here, or from some other philosopher."

"It must have been someone else," said Critias, "because I certainly didn't say it."

"But what does it matter where I heard it?" said Charmides.

"It doesn't matter at all," I replied. "The point isn't who said it, but whether it's true."

"Now you're talking," he said.

"Good," I said. "Though I doubt we'll be able to figure out whether it's true or false very easily. It's a bit of a riddle."

"What makes you say that?" he asked.

"Because it seems to me that whoever said it meant one thing but said another. Take a scribe, for example: when he reads or writes, should we say he's doing nothing?"

"No, I'd say he's definitely doing something."

"And does the scribe only write and read his own name? Or did you boys write your enemies' names just as much as your own and your friends'?"

"Just as much."

"And was there anything meddlesome or intemperate about that?"

"Certainly not."

"And yet, if reading and writing count as 'doing,' then you were doing what wasn't your own business."

"But they do count as doing."

"And medicine, my friend -- and building, and weaving, and everything else that's done by any skill -- these all count as 'doing,' don't they?"

"Yes."

"Now, do you think a city would be well governed by a law that required every person to weave their own cloak, wash their own clothes, make their own shoes, their own oil flask and scraper -- everyone doing only their own work and keeping their hands off other people's?"

"I don't think so," he said.

"But a temperate city would be a well-governed city?"

"Of course."

"Then temperance can't be 'doing your own business' -- at least not in this literal sense."

"Clearly not."

"So as I was saying, whoever declared that temperance is doing your own business must have had some other, hidden meaning. Because surely he wasn't such a fool as to mean it literally. Was the person who told you this a fool, Charmides?"

"Not at all," he replied. "I thought he was very wise."

"Then I'm sure he put it forward as a riddle, knowing that 'doing your own business' isn't as straightforward as it sounds."

"Probably so," he said.

"So what does 'doing your own business' really mean? Can you tell me?"

"I really can't. And honestly, I wouldn't be surprised if the man who used the phrase didn't fully understand it himself."

And as he said this, he grinned slyly and glanced over at Critias.

Now, Critias had been looking uncomfortable for a while. He clearly felt he had a reputation to maintain in front of Charmides and the rest of the company. Up to this point he'd managed to hold himself back. But now he couldn't take it any longer. And I'm convinced that my suspicion at the time was right: Charmides had gotten this definition of temperance from Critias. Charmides, not wanting to defend the definition himself, was trying to provoke Critias into doing it. He kept pointing out that the argument had been refuted, and Critias got angrier and angrier, looking ready to pick a fight with him -- like a poet furious at an actor for botching his lines. Critias fixed him with a hard stare and said:

"Do you really think, Charmides, that because you don't understand what the author of that definition meant, he didn't understand it either?"

"Well, at his age," I cut in, "most excellent Critias, it would hardly be surprising if he didn't understand. But you're older, and you've studied these things. So I'd have to assume you do know what it means. And if you accept the definition and agree with it, I'd much rather argue with you about whether it's true or false."

"I accept it entirely," said Critias.

"Good," I said. "Now let me repeat my question. Do you agree, as I was just saying, that all craftsmen make or do something?"

"Yes."

"And do they make or do only their own business, or other people's as well?"

"Other people's as well."

"And are they temperate, even though they're not working only for themselves?"

"Why wouldn't they be?" he said.

"I've got no problem with it," I said. "But there might be a problem for whoever defines temperance as 'doing your own business' and then admits there's no reason why people who do other people's business can't be temperate."

"Wait," he said. "I never said that people who do other people's business are temperate. I said people who make. 'Making' and 'doing' aren't the same thing."

"What? You're saying doing and making are different?"

"Absolutely," he said. "And making isn't the same as working either. I learned this from Hesiod, who says that 'work is no disgrace.' Now, do you really think Hesiod would have said there's no disgrace in work if he meant by 'working' things like cobbling shoes, or selling pickles, or sitting in a brothel? Of course not, Socrates. I believe Hesiod distinguished making from doing and working. A 'making' can sometimes be a disgrace -- when the product isn't honorable. But 'work' is never a disgrace. Things that are nobly and usefully produced, he called 'works,' and the activity of producing them, he called 'working' and 'doing.' Only this kind of activity, he'd say, is truly a person's own business. Everything harmful is someone else's business, not yours. So when Hesiod or any other wise person says 'do your own business,' we should understand them to mean: the person who does good is wise."

"Critias," I said, "the moment you opened your mouth, I had a pretty good idea where you were heading. You're going to define what's 'proper' to a person and what's 'their own' as what's good, and you're going to say that the 'doing' of good things is what we should call 'action.' I'm no stranger to these kinds of fine distinctions about words -- Prodicus makes them endlessly. But look, you can attach whatever meaning to words you like, as long as you tell me clearly what you mean. So let's start over, and be a little more precise this time. Are you saying that doing good -- or making good, or producing good, whatever word you prefer -- is temperance?"

"I am," he said.

"Then someone who does evil isn't temperate, but someone who does good is?"

"Yes," he said. "Wouldn't you agree with that, my friend?"

"Never mind whether I agree or not. Right now we're not examining what I think -- we're examining what you're saying."

"Fair enough," he said. "What I mean is that the person who does evil and not good is not temperate, while the person who does good and not evil is. Temperance, in plain words, is doing good."

"You may well be right about that. But here's something I'm curious about: do you think temperate people can be unaware of their own temperance?"

"I don't think so," he said.

"And yet, weren't you just saying a moment ago that craftsmen can be temperate when they do someone else's work as well as their own?"

"I was. What's your point?"

"I'm not going anywhere particular with this. But tell me: can a doctor who cures a patient do good both for himself and for others?"

"I think so."

"And when he does that, he's doing his duty?"

"Yes."

"And doesn't someone who does their duty act temperately -- wisely?"

"Yes, wisely."

"But does a doctor necessarily know when his treatment will help and when it won't? Does every craftsman necessarily know when his work will benefit someone and when it won't?"

"I suppose not always."

"So sometimes," I said, "a doctor might do good or do harm without knowing which he's doing. And yet, according to you, in doing good he's acted temperately. Wasn't that your claim?"

"Yes."

"Then it would seem that a person can sometimes do good and act wisely and temperately -- and yet not know their own wisdom or temperance?"

"But that's impossible, Socrates," he said. "And if this is where my earlier admissions lead, I'd rather take them back than accept that someone can be temperate without knowing it. I'm not ashamed to admit I was wrong. Because self-knowledge, I'd insist, is the very essence of wisdom. On this point I agree with whoever dedicated the inscription at Delphi: 'Know Thyself.' That inscription, if I'm not mistaken, is placed there as a greeting from the god to those entering the temple. It's as if the god is saying that the usual greeting -- 'Hello!' -- is the wrong one, and that 'Be temperate!' would be a far better way for people to greet each other."

"That's how the dedicator understood the god's message, I believe. When a worshipper enters, the first word he hears is 'Be temperate!' But the god, like a prophet, wraps it in a riddle: 'Know thyself!' and 'Be temperate!' are really the same thing, as I'd argue and as the Greek words imply. But later sages misunderstood. They added their own sayings -- 'Nothing in excess' and 'Give a pledge, and ruin is near' -- thinking that 'Know thyself' was a piece of practical advice from the god, rather than his greeting. So they dedicated their own inscriptions, imagining they were offering equally useful wisdom."

"Now, Socrates, the reason I'm telling you all this is to set aside our earlier discussion -- where neither you nor I came to any clear conclusion -- and to make a fresh start. I want to prove, if you'll challenge me, that temperance is self-knowledge."

"Very well, Critias," I said. "But you're approaching me as if I already knew the answers to the questions I'm asking, and could agree with you whenever I felt like it. That's not how this works. I'm genuinely investigating these things alongside you, precisely because I don't know the answers. Let me think it over, and then I'll tell you whether I agree or not. Give me a moment."

"Think it over," he said.

"I'm thinking," I said. "And here's what I'm seeing: if temperance, or wisdom, involves knowledge of something, it must be a kind of science -- a science of something specific."

"Yes," he said. "The science of itself."

"Isn't medicine the science of health?"

"Yes."

"Now suppose," I said, "you asked me: 'What's the use of medicine, this science of health? What does it produce?' I'd say medicine is enormously useful -- it produces health, which is an excellent result."

"Agreed."

"And if you asked me what result architecture produces -- the science of building -- I'd say houses. And so on for every other craft, each producing its own result. Now I want you to answer the same kind of question about temperance, or wisdom, which you say is the science of itself. Given that definition, I'm asking you: what good product does temperance produce that's worthy of the name 'wise'? Tell me."

"That's not the right way to pursue this inquiry, Socrates," he said. "Wisdom isn't like the other sciences, and they're not all like each other either. But you're treating them as if they are. Tell me -- what's the product of arithmetic or geometry, in the same way that a house is the product of building, or a garment is the product of weaving? Can you point to any such product?"

"No, you can't."

"That's true," I said. "But each of these sciences does have a subject matter that's different from the science itself. I can show you, for instance, that arithmetic deals with odd and even numbers and their relationships to one another. The odd and even numbers aren't the same thing as arithmetic, are they?"

"No."

"And the art of weighing deals with heavier and lighter things, but the art of weighing is one thing and heavy and light are another. You'd agree?"

"Yes."

"Good. Now I want to know: what is the subject matter of wisdom, the thing that is not wisdom itself but that wisdom studies?"

"You're falling into the same old trap, Socrates," he said. "You keep asking how wisdom differs from the other sciences, and then you try to find some way in which they're all alike. But they aren't. All the other sciences study something outside themselves. Wisdom alone is a science both of other sciences and of itself. And I think you know this perfectly well. You're doing exactly what you just denied doing -- you're trying to refute me, instead of honestly pursuing the argument."

"How can you think that?" I said. "Even if I am refuting you, what other motive could I possibly have except the same one I'd have in examining myself -- the fear of unconsciously thinking I know something when I really don't? That's what I'm doing right now. I'm pursuing this argument primarily for my own sake -- and perhaps for the sake of my friends too. Isn't discovering how things truly are a benefit to everyone?"

"Yes, certainly, Socrates."

"Then cheer up, my friend, and give me your answer. Never mind whether it's Critias or Socrates who ends up refuted. Just focus on the argument itself and see where it leads."

"I think you're right," he said. "I'll do that."

"Then tell me again what you're claiming about wisdom."

"I'm saying that wisdom is the only science that is both a science of itself and a science of the other sciences."

"Then the science of science would also be the science of the absence of science?"

"Very true," he said.

"So the wise or temperate person, and only that person, will know themselves -- will be able to examine what they know and don't know, and to see what other people know and merely think they know. No one else will be able to do this. And this is what wisdom and temperance and self-knowledge amount to: knowing what you know and what you don't know. Is that your meaning?"

"Yes," he said.

"All right," I said. "Now, making an offering of our third argument to Zeus the Savior, let's start fresh and ask two questions. First: is it even possible for a person to know that they know and don't know what they know and don't know? Second: even if it's perfectly possible, would such knowledge be any use?"

"Those are exactly the right questions," he said.

"Here's where I'm stuck, Critias, and I'm hoping you can find a way out. Shall I tell you what the difficulty is?"

"By all means," he said.

"Doesn't your position amount to this: that there's a single science that is wholly a science of itself and of other sciences, and that this same science is also a science of the absence of science?"

"Yes."

"But think about how strange this claim is. Look at any parallel case, and the impossibility becomes obvious."

"What do you mean? What cases?"

"Consider this: suppose there were a kind of vision that doesn't work like ordinary vision -- it doesn't see colors, but only sees itself and other kinds of vision, and the lack of them. Do you think such a vision exists?"

"Certainly not."

"Or a kind of hearing that hears no sound at all, but only hears itself and other kinds of hearing, and deafness?"

"No."

"Or take all the senses together: can you imagine a sense that perceives no sensory object at all, but only perceives itself and other senses?"

"I don't think so."

"Can you imagine a desire that desires no pleasure, but only desires itself and other desires?"

"Certainly not."

"Or a wish that wishes for nothing good, but only wishes for itself and other wishes?"

"No."

"Or a love that doesn't love anything beautiful, but only loves itself and other loves?"

"No."

"Have you ever encountered a fear that fears itself and other fears, but has no object of fear?"

"Never," he said.

"Or an opinion that's an opinion about itself and other opinions, but has no opinion about the things that opinions are normally about?"

"Certainly not."

"And yet we're claiming there's a science exactly like this -- one that has no subject matter, but is a science of itself and the other sciences?"

"Yes, that's what's being claimed."

"It is strange, isn't it? But let's not rule it out just yet. Let's keep investigating."

"You're right."

"Well then. This science we're talking about is a science of something, and it has the nature of being related to something?"

"Yes."

"Just as whatever is 'greater' has the nature of being greater than something?"

"Yes."

"And that something must be less, if the first thing is greater?"

"Necessarily."

"Now, if we could find something that's greater than itself and greater than other great things, but not greater than the things those other great things are greater than -- then that thing would have the property of being both greater and less than itself?"

"That would be the inevitable conclusion," he said.

"Or if there's a double that's double of itself and of other doubles, then those things -- including itself -- would also be halves. Because the double is always relative to the half."

"True."

"And anything that's greater than itself would also be less than itself. Anything heavier than itself would also be lighter. Anything older than itself would also be younger. And the same for every other relative term: whatever has a relation to itself must also have the nature of the thing it's related to. For example: hearing, we say, is the hearing of sound."

"Yes."

"So if hearing hears itself, it must hear a sound -- because there's no other way to hear."

"Exactly."

"And if sight sees itself, my excellent friend, it must see a color -- because sight can't see anything that has no color."

"Right."

"Do you notice, Critias, that in several of the cases we've gone through, the idea of something being related to itself is simply impossible -- and in other cases, barely credible? Self-relation is impossible for magnitudes and numbers. It's nearly incredible for hearing and sight. As for the power of motion moving itself, or heat burning itself -- some people might accept these, others won't."

"What we really need is some great thinker to settle this question once and for all: is there nothing in existence that has this built-in capacity for self-relation? Or do some things have it while others don't? And if there is such a class of self-relating things, does wisdom -- as the science of science -- belong in it? I don't trust my own ability to answer these questions. I'm not even sure whether a science of science can exist at all. And even if it can, I wouldn't call it wisdom or temperance until I can also see whether it would actually do us any good. Because I have a strong intuition that temperance is beneficial -- that it's a good thing."

"So, son of Callaeschrus, since you maintain that temperance is the science of science and also of the absence of science, I'd like you to show me two things: first, that such a science is possible; and second, that it would be useful. Then maybe you'll convince me you're right about what temperance is."

Critias heard all this and could see that I was stuck. And just as one person catches the urge to yawn from watching someone else yawn, he seemed to catch my confusion from my confusion. But since he had a reputation to maintain, he was embarrassed to admit in front of everyone that he couldn't answer my challenge or resolve the question. So he attempted an unclear response to hide his difficulty.

To keep the conversation moving, I said: "All right, Critias, let's just assume for now that there can be such a thing as a science of science. Whether that assumption is right or wrong, we can examine later. Granting it exists, tell me: how would such a science enable us to distinguish what we know from what we don't know? That's what self-knowledge, or wisdom, is supposed to do, right?"

"Yes, Socrates," he said. "And I think that's certainly true. A person who possesses this self-knowing knowledge will become like the knowledge he possesses. In the same way that having speed makes you fast, and having beauty makes you beautiful, having knowledge that knows itself will make you know yourself."

"I don't doubt," I said, "that a person who has the kind of knowledge that knows itself will know himself. But why does having that necessarily mean he'll know what he knows and what he doesn't know?"

"Because, Socrates, they're the same thing."

"Maybe," I said. "But I'm still as confused as ever. I can't see how 'knowing what you know and don't know' is the same as 'knowledge of self.'"

"What do you mean?" he asked.

"Here's what I mean. I'll grant that there's a science of science. But can this science do anything more than distinguish between two things: this is science, and that isn't?"

"No, that's all it can do."

"But is knowledge of health the same as knowledge of justice?"

"Of course not."

"One is medicine, the other is politics. What we're talking about is knowledge in the abstract -- pure knowledge."

"Right."

"So if someone has only knowledge of knowledge -- pure and simple, with no further knowledge of health or justice -- all he'll be able to determine is that he knows something, that he possesses some kind of knowledge. Whether he's thinking about himself or other people."

"Yes."

"But how will this abstract knowledge tell him what he knows? He knows health -- but that knowledge came from medicine, not from wisdom. He knows music -- but that came from the study of music. He knows building -- from the study of building. None of these came from temperance or wisdom. The same goes for everything else."

"That's clear."

"Then how will wisdom, understood purely as knowledge of knowledge, ever teach him that he knows medicine? Or that he knows building?"

"It can't."

"Then a person who lacks specific expertise will only know that he knows something -- but not what he knows."

"That seems right."

"So being wise, on this view, turns out to be not knowledge of what we know and don't know, but only knowledge that we know or don't know?"

"That's the implication."

"And such a person won't be able to examine whether someone who claims to know something actually does. He'll only be able to tell that the person has some kind of knowledge -- but wisdom won't reveal what kind."

"Clearly not."

"So he won't be able to distinguish a genuine doctor from a fake one, or any real expert from a phony. Think about it this way: if the wise person -- or anyone -- wants to tell a real doctor from a fraud, how will he go about it? He can't quiz the doctor on medicine, because as we said, the only thing a doctor understands is medicine."

"Right."

"And the doctor, on the other hand, doesn't know anything about the science of science -- we've assigned that to wisdom alone."

"Right."

"And since medicine is a science, we'd have to conclude that the wise person doesn't know anything about medicine either."

"That does follow."

"So the wise person can tell that the doctor has some kind of knowledge. But when he tries to determine what kind, he'll have to ask: what's its subject matter? Because sciences are distinguished not by the mere fact that they're sciences, but by their subjects."

"Exactly."

"And medicine is distinguished from other sciences by having health and disease as its subject?"

"Yes."

"So anyone who wants to evaluate a doctor has to examine him on matters of health and disease, not on anything else?"

"Right."

"And a proper evaluation will consider whether what the doctor says about health and disease is true, and whether what he does is effective?"

"Yes."

"But can anyone assess these things without having medical knowledge?"

"No one can."

"No one, apparently, except a doctor. Which means the wise person can't do it either -- he'd have to be a doctor in addition to being wise."

"Exactly."

"So if wisdom is only the science of science and the absence of science, it can't distinguish a real doctor from a fake one, or any genuine expert from a pretender. The wise person will only recognize his fellow wise person -- another person who has knowledge of knowledge. Beyond that, nothing."

"That's what follows," he said.

"Then what's the use of wisdom, Critias, if this is all it amounts to? Because if the wise person really could do what we originally imagined -- if he could tell what he knows from what he doesn't know, and make the same judgment about others -- then there'd be a tremendous benefit to being wise. We'd never make mistakes. We'd go through life as unerring guides, both for ourselves and for everyone in our care. We wouldn't attempt things we didn't understand but would find the people who did and hand the work over to them. We wouldn't let anyone under our charge do anything they couldn't do well. A household or a city governed by wisdom would be well run, because truth would be guiding every action and error would be eliminated. People would do everything right, and they'd be happy. Wasn't that the great advantage we imagined wisdom would give us -- knowing what is known and what is unknown?"

"Yes, absolutely," he said.

"And now you see that no such science exists anywhere."

"I see that," he said.

"Well, then let's try a different angle," I said. "Let's suppose wisdom, understood as knowledge of knowledge and ignorance, does have this much going for it: a person who possesses it would learn things more easily, and everything would be clearer to them, because in addition to studying specific subjects, they'd also perceive the science behind those subjects. And this would help them better evaluate what others know about the same topics. Meanwhile, someone without this meta-knowledge would have a weaker, less penetrating insight. Aren't those the real advantages of wisdom? And aren't we looking for something grander than what wisdom can actually deliver?"

"That's very likely," he said.

"Very likely," I agreed. "And I'm starting to think we've been investigating something worthless. Here's what leads me to say this: even if we grant that wisdom is the science of science -- and even if we go further and grant the original claim, that wisdom is knowledge of what you know and don't know -- I'm still not convinced, on reflection, that wisdom defined this way would do us much good. I think we were wrong earlier when we said that a world governed by this kind of wisdom would be a tremendous benefit."

"How so?" he said.

"Because we were far too hasty in assuming that mankind would benefit enormously if everyone stuck to what they knew and handed over everything else to the real experts."

"Weren't we right to assume that?"

"I don't think so."

"That's a very strange thing to say, Socrates!"

"By the dog of Egypt," I said, "I agree with you -- it is strange. I was just thinking the same thing when I said I was afraid we were on the wrong track. Because no matter how willing we are to accept that this is wisdom, I honestly can't figure out what good it does."

"What do you mean?" he said. "Help me understand."

"I know I'm probably talking nonsense," I said. "But if a thought comes to mind, a person who respects himself can't just let it slip by without examining it."

"I like that attitude," he said.

"Then listen to my dream -- whether it comes through the gate of horn or the gate of ivory, I can't say. Here's the dream: suppose wisdom, as we've defined it, had absolute power over us. Then every action would be performed by genuine experts, and no one who claimed to be a pilot when he wasn't, or a doctor, or a general, or anything else he wasn't qualified to be, would be able to fool us. Our health would improve. Our safety at sea and in battle would be guaranteed. Our shoes and clothes and tools and everything else would be expertly made, because only real craftsmen would make them. And if you like, we can even suppose that prophecy -- knowledge of the future -- would fall under wisdom's control, so that fraudulent prophets would be weeded out and only true seers would predict what's to come."

"I fully agree that humanity, equipped this way, would live and act according to knowledge. Wisdom would stand guard, preventing ignorance from creeping in. But here's the thing, my dear Critias -- would living according to knowledge necessarily make us act well and be happy? That's the point we haven't been able to settle."

"And yet," he replied, "I think you'll have a hard time finding the crown of happiness anywhere else, if you throw out knowledge."

"Just one small question," I said. "Knowledge of what? Do you mean knowledge of shoemaking?"

"God, no."

"Working in bronze?"

"Certainly not."

"Wool? Wood? Anything like that?"

"No."

"Then we're abandoning the claim that living according to knowledge makes you happy. These people all live according to knowledge, and you won't call them happy. You seem to be restricting happiness to certain specific individuals who have certain specific knowledge -- like the prophet, maybe, who knows the future. Is it him you're thinking of?"

"Yes, him -- but others too."

"Who? Someone who knows the past and present as well as the future, and is ignorant of nothing. Let's imagine such a person exists. You'd agree he's the most knowledgeable person alive?"

"Certainly."

"But I'd like to know one more thing: which kind of knowledge is it that makes him happy? Do all kinds equally?"

"Not all equally," he said.

"Then which kind contributes most to his happiness? Knowledge of what past, present, or future thing? Is it the knowledge of how to play checkers?"

"Don't be ridiculous."

"Arithmetic?"

"No."

"Health?"

"That's getting closer," he said.

"And the knowledge that's closest of all," I said -- "closest to making him happy -- is the knowledge of what?"

"The knowledge of good and evil."

"You villain!" I said. "You've been leading me around in circles this whole time, hiding from me the fact that the life according to knowledge isn't what makes people act rightly and be happy -- not even if knowledge includes every science in the world -- but only one particular science: the science of good and evil. Because let me ask you, Critias -- if you take away this science, will medicine stop producing health? Will shoemaking stop producing shoes? Will weaving stop producing clothes? Will navigation stop saving lives at sea? Will generalship stop winning battles?"

"No, they'll all keep working."

"But none of these things will be done well and beneficially if the science of good is missing."

"True."

"But this science of good and evil isn't wisdom or temperance -- it's something different. It's not a science of other sciences, or of ignorance. It's a science of human benefit. So if this is what's useful, then wisdom -- as the science of science -- isn't what's useful."

"Wait," he said. "Why can't wisdom still be useful? Even if we accept that wisdom is the science of sciences, surely it would have this particular science -- the science of good -- under its supervision. And in that way, wisdom would benefit us."

"And will wisdom produce health?" I said. "Isn't that medicine's job? Does wisdom do the work of any of the other crafts? Don't they each do their own work? We established long ago that wisdom is only the knowledge of knowledge and ignorance -- nothing more."

"That does seem to be what we said."

"So wisdom won't produce health."

"Apparently not."

"Because the art of health is a different thing."

"Yes, different."

"And wisdom doesn't produce benefit either, since we just assigned that to a different science."

"That's right."

"Then how can wisdom be beneficial, if it produces no benefit?"

"That, Socrates, does seem impossible."

"You see, then, Critias, that I wasn't wrong to be worried -- to fear that I'd have no sound understanding of wisdom. I was right to doubt myself. Because something that's supposed to be the best of all things keeps turning out to be useless every time I look at it -- and that has to be my fault, not wisdom's."

"Here's where we ended up: we made far more concessions than we should have. We granted that there could be a science of science, even though the argument said no. We granted that this science could know the workings of other sciences, even though that too was denied by the argument -- all because we wanted to show that the wise person knows what he knows and what he doesn't know. We waved all of that through generously, without even stopping to consider the impossibility of a person knowing, in any real way, what he doesn't know at all. Our assumption was that he knows what he doesn't know. Nothing could be more irrational than that."

"And yet, after we proved so easy and accommodating, the inquiry still couldn't find the truth. It practically mocked us. It went out of its way to prove the uselessness of the very thing we'd defined -- through generous concessions and polite fictions -- as temperance or wisdom."

"As for me, I'm not especially upset by this result," I said. "But for your sake, Charmides, I am truly sorry -- that someone with your beauty, your wisdom, your temperate soul, should get no benefit in life from your temperance. And I'm even more sorry about the charm I learned with such trouble from the Thracian, only to find it's worthless -- a cure for something that turns out to be nothing. I suspect there's been a mistake, and that I must be a terrible investigator. Because I do believe that temperance is genuinely a great good, and that you're blessed if you truly possess it. So examine yourself, Charmides. See if you already have this gift and can do without the charm. Because if you can, my advice would be to consider me simply a fool who can never reason his way to the bottom of anything, and to rest assured: the more temperate you are, the happier you'll be."

Charmides said: "I honestly don't know, Socrates, whether I have this gift of temperance or not. How could I, when even you and Critias can't figure out what it is? Though I'm not sure I entirely believe you on that point. But one thing I'm sure of, Socrates: I do need the charm. And as far as I'm concerned, I'm willing to let you charm me every single day, until you say I've had enough."

"Very good, Charmides," said Critias. "If you do that, I'll take it as proof of your temperance -- that you've chosen to let Socrates charm you and never leave his side."

"You can count on it," said Charmides. "I'll follow him and never desert him. If you, my guardian, are ordering me to do this, it would be wrong of me to disobey."

"I am ordering you," Critias said.

"Then I'll start today."

"Wait a moment," I said. "What are you two plotting?"

"We're not plotting," said Charmides. "We've already plotted."

"You're going to use force on me without even going through the proper legal proceedings?"

"Yes, I'm going to use force," he replied, "since he's given me my orders. So you'd better think carefully."

"The time for thinking is past," I said. "Once you've made up your mind and turned to force, you're irresistible."

"Then don't resist me," he said.

"I won't resist you," I replied.
