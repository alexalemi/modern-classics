Protagoras (Part 2) -- Translation Notes
==========================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's sentences, already long in Part 1, reach peak
   complexity in this section -- particularly during the "measuring art" argument
   and the Simonides poem analysis. Broke long Victorian periods into shorter,
   clearer sentences. The philosophical arguments especially needed this: the
   pleasure calculus argument builds through dozens of small logical steps, and
   each step needs to land clearly before the next begins.

2. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "enquire" -> "ask" / "investigate"
   - "in like manner" -> "in the same way" / "similarly"
   - "I will endeavour" -> "I'll try"
   - "hitherto" -> (rephrased naturally)
   - "unseemly" -> "inappropriate"
   - "nay" -> "no" / "wrong" (context-dependent)
   - "rejoined" -> "replied" / "answered" / "fired back"
   - "inasmuch as" -> "insofar as" / "since"
   - "herein" -> rephrased
   - "I dare say" -> "I'd guess" / "I suspect" / "probably"
   - "peacemakers" -> kept (clear enough in context)
   - "caestus" -> "boxing straps" (nobody knows what a caestus is)
   - "laconizers" -> "Spartan-wannabes" (captures the contemptuous tone)
   - "peltasts" -> kept with context ("trained soldiers" / "light shield fighters")

3. VICTORIAN INVERSIONS removed throughout:
   - "said he" -> "he said"
   - "Is not the latter that deceiving art..." -> "Isn't it appearances --
     that deceiving force -- that makes us..."
   - "Would not the art of measuring be..." -> "Would it be the skill of
     measuring..."
   - Complex nested questions restructured into direct, clear ones

4. CONTRACTIONS used naturally throughout: "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "you're,"
   "they're," "wouldn't," "can't," "that's," "we've," "I'd," "he's," "won't,"
   "I'll," "you'd," "we'd," "couldn't," etc. Keeps narrated dialogue feeling
   like actual speech.

5. THE SIMONIDES POEM ANALYSIS: This is one of the trickiest passages in all
   of Plato -- Socrates gives a long, possibly tongue-in-cheek literary analysis
   that's part genuine philosophy, part intellectual showmanship, part parody of
   sophistic methods. Key decisions:
   - Kept the poem quotations clearly set off
   - Made the being/becoming distinction crystal clear (the philosophical crux)
   - Made Socrates's tour-de-force reading of the poem followable step by step
   - Preserved the humor of Prodicus's word-quibbling and Protagoras's annoyance
   - The Spartan philosophy digression is deliberately absurd (Socrates is
     trolling) -- kept it vivid and let the comedy come through
   - The "hard" = "bad" argument via Prodicus is a comic set piece -- preserved
     the escalating ridiculousness

6. THE MEASURING ART / PLEASURE CALCULUS: This is the philosophically crucial
   passage. Socrates argues that "being overcome by pleasure" is really just
   ignorance -- a failure to properly measure and compare pleasures and pains
   across time. Key decisions:
   - Broke the long hypothetical dialogue (Socrates imagining what "the many"
     would say) into clear exchanges with quotation marks
   - Made each logical step explicit and punchy
   - The weighing/scale metaphor made concrete and visual
   - The punchline -- that "being overcome by pleasure" = ignorance -- given
     its full dramatic weight
   - This argument is the foundation for the final proof that courage = wisdom

7. THE FINAL PROOF (COURAGE = WISDOM): This is the climax of the whole
   dialogue's argument. Protagoras's increasing reluctance is crucial:
   - "He agreed" -> "He agreed, very reluctantly" -> "He nodded" ->
     "Again he nodded, very reluctantly" -> "He wouldn't nod or speak. He was
     silent." -> "Finish the argument yourself."
   - This progression of surrender is dramatic gold. Preserved every beat.

8. THE IRONIC REVERSAL: The dialogue's final twist -- where Socrates points
   out that he and Protagoras have completely switched positions (Socrates,
   who said virtue can't be taught, has been proving it's all knowledge;
   Protagoras, who said it can be taught, has been trying to prove it's
   not knowledge). This reversal is given its full moment:
   - "If the argument had a human voice, it would be laughing at both of us"
   - The personified argument's speech is presented clearly
   - The Epimetheus/Prometheus callback ties the ending to Part 1's myth
   - Protagoras's gracious concession ("I admire you more than anyone")
     is generous but also clearly a retreat

9. SOCRATIC IRONY sharpened at key moments:
   - The "wretched doctor making the patient worse" exchange (kept punchy)
   - "What a stroke of luck that we have Prodicus with us!" (deadpan)
   - The Spartan philosophy speech (hilariously over-the-top)
   - "If you laugh at us now, you're laughing at yourselves" (devastating)
   - The final reversal speech (gentle but lethal)

10. PROTAGORAS'S DIGNITY preserved throughout. He's not a straw man:
    - His objection about confidence vs. courage is genuinely strong
    - His careful hedging on pleasure = good shows philosophical scruple
    - His final admission is reluctant but honest
    - His closing compliment to Socrates is genuine

11. HIPPIAS, PRODICUS, ALCIBIADES: Each maintained their distinct voices:
    - Hippias: pompous, well-meaning, a bit too grand
    - Prodicus: obsessed with precise terminology (the fear/dread distinction)
    - Alcibiades: blunt, impatient, fiercely loyal to Socrates

12. GREEK OATHS AND REFERENCES kept:
    - "by the gods" (Hippocrates in flashback from Part 1)
    - "by Zeus" (kept where present)
    - Homer quotations kept: "When two go together" / "sees a thing alone"
    - Scamander/Simois river reference (with quick context)
    - Seven Sages listed by name (Thales, Pittacus, Bias, Solon, etc.)
    - "Know thyself" and "Nothing in excess" (the Delphic inscriptions)
    - Hesiod quotation kept
    - Epimetheus/Prometheus callback to Part 1 myth

13. THE POET DEBATE AS META-COMMENTARY: Socrates's speech about why serious
    people don't quote poets in arguments (the flute-girl analogy) is itself
    a commentary on what he just did -- he spent a long time analyzing
    Simonides's poem, and now he's saying that's exactly the kind of thing
    intelligent people shouldn't do. The irony is delicious. Preserved the
    flute-girl/dinner-party analogy in full, making it vivid.


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (GORGIAS PART 1)
------------------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: Maintained as sharp, conversational, ironic, always in
   control. In the Protagoras, his narrated dialogue voice is warm and
   slightly self-deprecating. The Gorgias will be more combative -- Socrates
   takes on three opponents with increasing intensity.

2. FORMAT: The Gorgias is dramatic dialogue format (speaker names), not
   narrated like the Protagoras. Switch back to the speaker-on-own-line
   format used in Euthyphro.

3. PHILOSOPHICAL VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "virtue" (arete) -- the central topic
   - "wisdom" / "knowledge" / "ignorance" -- the key terms from the
     pleasure calculus argument carry forward
   - "the skill of measuring" -- Socrates's proposed solution to akrasia
   - "self-control" (temperance/sophrosyne) -- kept as "self-control"
   - "courage" = knowledge of what is and isn't dangerous (established here)
   - Contractions throughout for natural speech

4. SOCRATIC METHOD: The step-by-step question-and-answer technique is now
   fully established. In the Gorgias, it will be deployed against three
   increasingly hostile opponents: Gorgias (polite), Polus (aggressive),
   Callicles (contemptuous).

5. KEY ARGUMENT ESTABLISHED: "No one does evil voluntarily" -- this is
   Socrates's core ethical position. It appears here definitively and will
   be central to the Gorgias as well.

6. PLEASURE AND GOOD: The identity of pleasure and good is argued for here
   but somewhat provisionally (Protagoras hedges, and even Socrates frames
   it as what "the many" would say). In the Gorgias, Socrates will take a
   much harder line against Callicles's hedonism. The tension is deliberate --
   Plato may not endorse the simple pleasure = good equation.

7. TONE SHIFT for Gorgias: The Protagoras is a sparring match between
   intellectual equals who respect each other. The Gorgias is a fight.
   Gorgias himself is courteous, Polus is rude, and Callicles is openly
   hostile and contemptuous of Socrates. The stakes escalate. Keep the
   increasing intensity.
