TRANSLATION NOTES — Laws Book 5 (Chapter 049)

OVERVIEW
--------
Laws Book 5 is a dense, largely monologic chapter in which the Athenian lays out
the moral and practical foundations of the new colony of Magnesia. It moves from
high ethical principles (the priority of soul over body over possessions) through
social philosophy (truth, justice, envy, self-love) to concrete legislative
proposals (property distribution, the 5,040 household number, property classes,
currency restrictions, city layout). Cleinias speaks only the final line;
Megillus is silent throughout.

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's sentences in this book are extraordinarily long,
   often running 80-150 words with multiple nested clauses and subordinate
   constructions. Nearly every sentence was broken into 2-5 shorter modern
   sentences while preserving the logical flow. The Athenian's style is meant to
   be that of a wise elder explaining things carefully, not a Victorian essayist.

2. SPEAKER LABELS: Used "Athenian" and "Cleinias" per the project convention
   (not "Athenian Stranger"). Megillus does not speak in this book.

3. TONE: The Athenian's voice is more earnest and less ironic than Socrates'.
   I kept him warm, authoritative, and direct — like a thoughtful elder statesman
   addressing younger colleagues. He lectures at length, but I tried to make the
   lectures feel like genuine conversation rather than Victorian oration.

4. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY resolved throughout:
   - "wherefore" → "so" / "that's why"
   - "inasmuch as" → "since" / "because"
   - "aforesaid" → dropped or rephrased
   - "notwithstanding" → "despite" / "even so"
   - "enquire" → "investigate" / "look into"
   - "hitherto" → "so far" / "until now"
   - "hereafter" → "later" / "in the future"

5. KEY PHILOSOPHICAL PASSAGES:

   a) THE PRIORITY OF SOUL (opening): The Athenian's argument that the soul
      ranks second only to the gods, above body and possessions. Kept the
      systematic structure but made each form of "dishonoring the soul" concrete
      and punchy rather than abstract.

   b) SELF-LOVE AS THE GREATEST EVIL: "Every man by nature is and ought to be
      his own friend" — a key passage where the Athenian argues that excessive
      self-love blinds us to truth. Made the connection to Socratic themes
      (thinking we know what we don't) more explicit.

   c) THE PLEASURE ARGUMENT: The systematic comparison of temperate vs.
      intemperate, wise vs. foolish, courageous vs. cowardly lives. This is
      a careful hedonic calculus. Kept the logical structure clear while
      simplifying the dense conditional language.

   d) WEALTH AND VIRTUE INCOMPATIBLE: The argument that the very rich cannot
      be good — because earning from both just and unjust sources doubles
      income, while spending only on worthy causes doubles expense. Kept the
      math explicit and accessible.

6. THE 5,040 HOUSEHOLDS: This is Plato's carefully chosen population target.
   The number 5,040 = 7! (7 factorial = 1×2×3×4×5×6×7) and has remarkable
   divisibility properties (59 divisors, including every number from 1-10
   except for 1). Kept the mathematical detail since it's central to Plato's
   legislative scheme.

7. THE THREE FORMS OF GOVERNMENT: The Athenian describes (a) the ideal
   communist state where "friends share everything," (b) the practical
   second-best state they're actually designing, and (c) a third-best state
   to be described later. Kept the first-best as clearly utopian/aspirational
   while making the second-best feel like a realistic legislative program.

8. PROPERTY DISTRIBUTION AND CLASSES: The four property classes, the four-times
   maximum on wealth, the requirement to surrender excess — these are concrete
   legislative proposals. Kept them clear and specific.

9. CURRENCY RESTRICTIONS: The dual currency system (local coin for domestic
   use, Greek currency held by the state for foreign travel) is one of the
   more striking proposals. Made the mechanics clear.

10. CITY LAYOUT: The twelve-part division of both city and country, paired
    lots (one near, one far), temples on the Acropolis — kept the spatial
    planning vivid and concrete.

11. THE LAWGIVER'S REALISM SPEECH: The passage where the lawgiver
    acknowledges these plans sound like dreams or "building a city out of wax"
    but insists on pursuing the ideal as closely as possible — this is an
    important meta-moment. Let the self-awareness come through clearly.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER
------------------------------------
- Speaker: The Athenian dominates completely. Cleinias and Megillus should
  become more active interlocutors in Book 6.
- The "5,040" number is now established and will recur throughout later books.
- Four property classes have been defined — they'll need consistent reference.
- The twelve-part division of city and country is set up — Book 6 will build
  on this with specific magistracies.
- The earth-as-mother/goddess metaphor for the allotted land should be
  maintained.
- Currency restrictions (no private gold/silver, local vs. Greek currency)
  are established.
- The tone is earnest and legislative, not ironic. Maintain this for all
  Laws books — the Athenian is not Socrates.
- "Colony" terminology established for the Magnesia project.
- The Athenian's habit of addressing both Megillus and Cleinias by name
  should continue in later books.
- The idea that the lawgiver should "not fear seeming overly precise" about
  numerical order reflects a running theme in the Laws about systematic
  planning. Keep this pragmatic-idealist tension alive.
