TRANSLATION NOTES — 026: LYSIS (On Friendship)
=================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
Lysis is one of Plato's most charming early dialogues — a conversation at a
wrestling school between Socrates and two adolescent boys (Lysis and Menexenus)
about the nature of friendship. It ends in aporia: after exhausting every
theory, they still can't define what a friend is. The tone is gentle, teasing,
and intimate — Socrates at his kindest and most playful.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS
-----------------------------

1. NARRATED FORMAT — Lysis uses Socrates-as-narrator ("I said... he replied").
   Kept this format per the text analysis guidelines. Modernized speech tags:
   "said I" → "I said"; "he rejoined" → "he replied"; dropped stiff
   constructions like "I was going to say to him" → "I almost said to him."

2. DIRECT SPEECH — Jowett frequently uses indirect speech where the Greek
   implies direct address. Converted many passages to direct quotes for
   liveliness, especially in the opening scene with Hippothales and Ctesippus.
   Example: "He asked whence I came and whither I was going" →
   "'Where are you coming from, Socrates, and where are you headed?'"

3. WRESTLING SCHOOL SETTING — Jowett's "Palaestra" retained on first mention
   but rendered as "wrestling school" throughout to keep the setting vivid and
   concrete. The Apodyterium becomes "changing room." These are real places
   with real physical character.

4. SOCRATES' TONE — This dialogue demands Socrates at his most gentle.
   He's teasing the boys, drawing them out, playing with them intellectually.
   The translation avoids making him sound professorial. His manner should feel
   like a brilliant older friend, not a lecturer.

5. HIPPOTHALES' EMBARRASSMENT — The comedy of Hippothales blushing and hiding
   behind the crowd is central to the dramatic frame. Kept all these physical
   details vivid. Ctesippus's mockery is allowed to be genuinely funny.

6. THE PHILOSOPHICAL ARGUMENTS — All philosophical content preserved complete:
   - The "knowledge = freedom" argument with Lysis
   - The lover/beloved paradox with Menexenus
   - Like attracts like (and its refutation)
   - Opposites attract (and its refutation)
   - The "neither good nor evil loves the good because of evil" theory
   - The chain-of-friendship regress to a "first friend"
   - The desire/kinship theory
   - The final collapse back into aporia

7. THE APORIA ENDING — The dialogue deliberately ends in failure and
   interruption. The tutors burst in "like an evil apparition" (kept this
   image — it's very Platonic). Socrates' final self-deprecating remark is
   the thematic capstone: they call themselves friends but can't say what
   friendship is. Let this land with genuine puzzlement, not defeat.

VOCABULARY MAPPINGS
--------------------

Jowett → Modern:
  "whence... whither" → "where... where"
  "Palaestra" → "wrestling school" (after first mention)
  "Apodyterium" → "changing room"
  "the Hermaea" → "the festival of Hermes"
  "are you disposed" → "do you want" / "are you coming"
  "goodly company" → "great crowd"
  "the fair" → "beautiful people"
  "vainglory" → "vanity" / "pride"
  "want of wit" → "foolish"
  "I dare say" → "I'd guess" / "I bet"
  "let us" → "let's" (in casual speech)
  "I should greatly prefer" → "I'd much rather have"
  "in like manner" → "the same way"
  "in grandiloquent language" → "quite grandly"
  "all-wise eristics" → "clever debaters"
  "that which is neither good nor evil" → kept, but broken into shorter
    sentence structures for clarity
  "assimilated" → "changed in its nature"
  "the congenial" → "what's akin" / "what naturally belongs"
  "by the dog of Egypt" → kept (Socrates' characteristic oath, referencing
    the Egyptian god Anubis; also appears in Gorgias and Republic)
  "by heaven" → "by Zeus" (restored the Greek oath per text analysis)
  "nay" → "no" / natural rephrasing
  "stark mad" → "completely delusional"

SPECIFIC PASSAGE NOTES
-----------------------

1. OPENING SCENE (Hippothales/Ctesippus) — Jowett's version is stiff and
   indirect. Modernized heavily for conversational energy. This scene sets
   the playful, social tone for the whole dialogue. The boys should sound
   like young men ribbing each other.

2. "BY THE DOG OF EGYPT" — Socrates' unique oath (Rhadamanthine oath,
   avoiding swearing by the gods). Kept verbatim. This is one of his most
   distinctive verbal tics across multiple dialogues.

3. THE KNOWLEDGE/FREEDOM ARGUMENT — Socrates' demonstration to Lysis that
   freedom depends on knowledge, not age. The examples (charioteer, mules,
   the Great King of Persia) are vividly concrete and slightly absurd. Kept
   the escalating silliness (from mules to the King of Persia's soup pot).
   This section is meant to humble Lysis gently.

4. HIPPOTHALES' REACTION — The moment where Socrates almost blows
   Hippothales' cover ("That's how you should talk to your beloved!") and
   catches himself is a wonderful dramatic touch. Kept the internal
   narration natural: "I very nearly made a blunder."

5. LYSIS WHISPERING — "In a childish and affectionate manner, whispered
   privately in my ear" → "in that sweet, boyish way of his, leaned over
   and whispered in my ear." Preserves the tenderness of the moment.

6. THE HESIOD QUOTATION — "Potter quarrels with potter, bard with bard,
   beggar with beggar." Kept in verse format. This is a well-known ancient
   proverb from Works and Days.

7. THE WHITE LEAD / HAIR DYE PASSAGE — Socrates uses the example of
   covering Lysis's brown hair with white lead to distinguish between
   something being present in a thing vs. changing its nature. Jowett has
   "auburn locks" — modernized to "brown hair" but kept the philosophical
   precision.

8. THE SHADOW METAPHOR — "I am afraid that we have been grasping at a
   shadow only" → "we've only been grasping at shadows." A key Platonic
   image (foreshadowing the Cave allegory).

9. THE HEMLOCK EXAMPLE — "if he knew that his son had drunk hemlock" —
   a casual reference that carries enormous dramatic irony given Socrates'
   own death. Left without commentary in the text.

10. THE FINAL INTERRUPTION — The tutors "like an evil apparition" who have
    been "drinking rather too much at the Hermaea" and shout "in their
    barbarous dialect." This comedic intrusion breaks off the philosophical
    discussion, leaving it permanently unresolved. Translated "barbarous
    dialect" as "rough foreign accents" — these are likely non-Greek-speaking
    household slaves serving as tutors/escorts.

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR ADJACENT CHAPTERS
-----------------------------------------

- Socratic oath "by the dog of Egypt" appears here. Should be used
  consistently across dialogues where it occurs (Gorgias, Republic).
- The "first friend" / "ultimate principle" concept in the chain-of-
  friendship argument anticipates the Form of the Good in the Republic.
  No need to flag this explicitly, but the language should be compatible.
- Lysis and Menexenus appear nowhere else in the major dialogues, but
  Ctesippus appears in the Euthydemus. His personality here (sharp,
  outspoken, slightly mocking) should be consistent with his portrayal there.
- The wrestling school / gymnasium setting recurs in Charmides (027) and
  Euthydemus (028-029). Keep spatial language consistent.
- "Neither good nor evil" as a philosophical category is important in
  Lysis and will recur in discussions of the intermediate state. Kept the
  phrasing stable throughout.
- The aporia ending is characteristic of early Socratic dialogues. The
  emotional tone — genuine puzzlement mixed with humor — should match
  similar endings in Euthyphro and other early works.
