Critias

Persons of the dialogue:
  Critias
  Hermocrates
  Timaeus
  Socrates


Timaeus
I'm so grateful, Socrates, that I've finally reached the end -- like a weary traveler arriving after a long journey, ready to rest at last! And I pray to the being who has always existed, and whom I've just now described, that my words may endure insofar as they've been spoken truly and in a way that pleases him. But if I've unintentionally said anything wrong, I pray he'll impose on me a just punishment -- and the just punishment for someone who's gone astray is to be set right. So, wishing to speak truly in the future about the origin of the gods, I pray he'll give me knowledge, which is the most perfect and powerful of all remedies. And now, having offered my prayer, I hand the discussion over to Critias, who's supposed to speak next, according to our agreement.

Critias
And I accept the task, Timaeus. Just as you began by asking for some patience, since you were going to speak about lofty matters, I'm asking for the same -- or even more patience -- for what I'm about to say. And although I know perfectly well that my request might seem a bit presumptuous and rude, I have to make it anyway. Now, would any sensible person deny that you've spoken well? The only thing I can try to show is that I deserve even more indulgence than you, because my subject is harder. And here's my argument: speaking well about the gods to a human audience is far easier than speaking well about humans to humans. When listeners have no experience and are completely ignorant about a subject, that ignorance is actually a huge advantage for the speaker -- and we all know how ignorant we are about the gods. But let me make my point clearer, if you'll follow me.

Everything any of us says is really just imitation and representation. Think about it this way: when painters create images of divine and heavenly bodies, notice the different degrees of satisfaction we feel looking at their work. We're perfectly happy with an artist who can even roughly imitate the earth, its mountains, rivers, woods, the whole universe, and everything moving within it. Since we don't know anything precise about these things, we don't scrutinize or analyze the painting -- all that's needed is some vague, impressionistic rendering and we're satisfied. But when someone tries to paint a human body? We're instantly sharp-eyed about every flaw, and our everyday familiarity makes us harsh judges of anyone who doesn't capture every detail.

The same thing happens with words. We're easily satisfied with a picture of divine and heavenly things that barely resembles them, but we're far more demanding when it comes to mortal, human subjects. So if I can't quite express my meaning perfectly when the moment comes, you'll have to forgive me -- creating convincing portraits of human things is the opposite of easy. That's what I wanted to suggest to you, and at the same time to ask, Socrates, that I be granted not less but more indulgence for what I'm about to say. If this is a fair request, I hope you'll be willing to grant it.

Socrates
Of course, Critias -- we'll absolutely grant your request. And we'll extend the same courtesy in advance to Hermocrates as well, along with you and Timaeus. I have no doubt that when his turn comes a little later, he'll make the same request you just did. So, to give him a fresh starting point and spare him from repeating what's already been said, let him understand that the indulgence is already granted in advance. But now, my friend Critias, let me announce the judgment of the audience: they think the last performer was a stunning success, and you're going to need a great deal of indulgence to measure up to him.

Hermocrates
That warning you're giving him, Socrates, applies to me too. But remember, Critias -- faint heart never raised a trophy. So you need to charge into this argument like a man. Call on Apollo and the Muses first, and then let's hear you celebrate and display the virtues of your ancient citizens.

Critias
Easy for you to say, Hermocrates -- you're stationed last, with someone else going before you. You haven't lost heart yet. The gravity of the situation will reveal itself to you soon enough. Meanwhile, I'll take your encouragement to heart. But besides the gods and goddesses you've mentioned, I'd especially like to invoke Mnemosyne, the goddess of Memory. The entire crucial part of my story depends on her favor. If I can remember and retell enough of what the priests said and Solon brought back, I'm confident I'll satisfy this audience. And now, with no more excuses, I'll begin.

First, let me start by noting that nine thousand years had passed since the war that was said to have taken place between those who lived beyond the Pillars of Heracles and all who lived within them. This is the war I'm going to describe. On one side, the city of Athens was reported to have been the leader and to have fought the war through to the end. On the other side, the commanders were the kings of Atlantis -- which, as I mentioned before, was an island larger than Libya and Asia combined. It was later swallowed by earthquakes and became an impassable barrier of mud, blocking sailors from crossing the ocean beyond.

As the story unfolds, it will reveal the various barbarian nations and Greek families that existed at that time, as they appear one by one. But first I need to describe the Athenians of that era and the enemies they fought, along with the powers and governments of both sides. Let's start with Athens.

In the ancient days, the gods divided the whole earth among themselves by allotment. There was no quarreling -- because you can't reasonably suppose that the gods didn't know what was right for each of them, or that, knowing this, they'd try to grab through conflict what rightfully belonged to someone else. They all received their fair share and were satisfied, and they settled their territories. Once they'd populated them, they looked after us -- their children, their possessions -- the way shepherds tend their flocks. Except they didn't use force or physical violence the way shepherds do. Instead, they governed us like pilots steering from the stern of a ship, which is a gentle way of guiding living creatures. They held our souls by the rudder of persuasion, steering us as they saw fit. That's how they guided all mortal beings.

Now, different gods received different regions and set them in order. Hephaestus and Athena, who were brother and sister -- born from the same father, sharing a common nature, and united in their love of both philosophy and craft -- these two received as their joint portion this land of ours. It was naturally suited for wisdom and excellence. They planted here a race of brave, native-born people and instilled in their minds the principles of government. Their names have been preserved, but their deeds have been lost, because the people who passed down the traditions were destroyed, and ages upon ages went by. Whenever there were survivors, as I've already mentioned, they were mountain dwellers who were illiterate. They'd heard the names of the old leaders but knew almost nothing about what they'd actually done. They were happy enough to give these names to their children, but as for the virtues and laws of their ancestors, they knew only vague traditions. For many generations, they and their children struggled just to stay alive, so they focused on meeting their basic needs and talked about that -- not about events from the distant past. After all, mythology and the study of history don't appear in cities until people have leisure, until the necessities of life have been secured. Not before.

That's why the names of the ancients have come down to us but not their deeds. I draw this conclusion because Solon said that the priests, in their account of that war, mentioned most of the names recorded from before the time of Theseus -- names like Cecrops, Erechtheus, Erichthonius, and Erysichthon, and the names of the women as well. Furthermore, since military service was common to both men and women in those days, the people of that era, following their custom, set up a statue of the goddess in full armor -- a testament to the fact that among all creatures who live together, male and female alike, both sexes can, if they choose, practice the same virtues without distinction.

Now, in those days the country was inhabited by various classes of citizens. There were craftsmen, and there were farmers, and there was also a warrior class, originally set apart by divine founders. The warriors lived by themselves and had everything they needed for their upbringing and education. None of them owned anything privately -- they considered all their possessions common property. They didn't expect to receive from the other citizens anything beyond their necessary food. In fact, they practiced all the pursuits we described yesterday as belonging to our imaginary guardians.

About the land itself, the Egyptian priests said something that's not only plausible but clearly true: the borders in those days were marked by the Isthmus, and toward the mainland they extended as far as the heights of Cithaeron and Parnes. The boundary line came down toward the sea with the district of Oropus on the right, and the river Asopus as the border on the left. The soil was the finest in the world, and in those days it could support an enormous army drawn from the surrounding population. Even the remnant of Attica that exists today can rival any region on earth for the variety and quality of its fruits and the suitability of its pastures for every kind of animal -- which proves my point. But back then, the land was as beautiful as it is now, and far more productive.

How can I back this up? What part of today's land can truly be called a remnant of what it once was? The whole country is just a long promontory jutting far out into the sea from the rest of the mainland, and the surrounding ocean is deep right up to the shore. Many catastrophic floods occurred during those nine thousand years -- that's how much time has passed since the period I'm describing -- and through all these upheavals, there was never any significant buildup of soil washing down from the mountains, as happens in other places. Instead, the earth kept sliding away on all sides and disappearing. The result is that compared to what it once was, all that's left are the bones of a wasted body, so to speak -- like a small island stripped bare. All the rich, soft topsoil has washed away, leaving nothing but the skeleton of the land.

But in its original state, the mountains were tall hills covered with deep soil. The plains we now call Phelleus were full of rich earth, and there was thick forest covering the mountains. You can still see traces of this -- some mountains that now produce nothing but food for bees once had trees large enough to roof the biggest buildings. There were also many other tall trees, cultivated by people, that produced abundant food for livestock. And the land captured the annual rainfall instead of losing it as water runs off bare earth into the sea today. Back then, it had a generous supply of water everywhere, absorbing it into thick clay soil, then releasing it from the high ground into the hollows, creating abundant springs and rivers in every direction. You can still see sacred shrines at the sites where springs once flowed -- which proves that what I'm saying is true.

That was the natural state of the country, and it was cultivated, as you'd expect, by true farmers -- people who made agriculture their calling, who loved excellence, who were noble by nature, and who had the best soil in the world, abundant water, and a beautifully temperate climate overhead.

Now, here's how the city was laid out in those days. First: the Acropolis wasn't the way it is now. A single night of extreme rainfall washed away the earth and exposed bare rock; at the same time there were earthquakes, and then came the extraordinary flood -- the third before the great catastrophe of Deucalion. But in the original era, the Acropolis hill extended all the way to the Eridanus and Ilissus rivers, taking in the Pnyx on one side and bounded by Lycabettus on the other. The whole thing was covered in deep soil and level on top, except for a spot or two.

On the outer slopes and lower reaches of the hill lived the craftsmen and those farmers who worked the nearby land. The warrior class lived by themselves around the temples of Athena and Hephaestus at the summit, and they'd enclosed the whole area with a single fence -- like the wall around one household's garden. On the north side they had shared dwellings and communal dining halls for winter, along with all the buildings they needed for their common life, plus temples. But there was no gold or silver decorating any of it -- they had no use for such things. They took a middle path between poverty and showiness, building modest homes where they and their children and grandchildren grew old, passing them on to the next generation, who were always the same kind of people. In summer, they used the southern side of the hill for their gardens, exercise grounds, and dining halls.

Where the Acropolis stands now, there was a spring that was later choked off by the earthquake, leaving only the small trickles that still exist nearby. But in those days, the spring provided abundant water for everyone -- pleasantly cool in summer and warm in winter.

That's how they lived, serving as guardians of their own citizens and as leaders of the Greeks, who followed them willingly. And they took care to maintain the same number of men and women throughout the ages -- about twenty thousand, the number needed for military purposes, the same as today.

Such were the ancient Athenians. This is how they justly governed their own land and the rest of Greece. They were famous throughout Europe and Asia for the beauty of their bodies and the many virtues of their souls. Of all the people alive in those days, they were the most admired.

And now, if I haven't forgotten what I heard as a child, I'll tell you about the character and origins of their adversaries. After all, friends shouldn't keep their stories to themselves -- they should share them.

But before I go any further, I should warn you: don't be surprised if you hear Greek names given to foreigners. Here's why. Solon, who intended to use this story for a poem, looked into the meaning of the names and discovered that the early Egyptians had translated them into their own language when they first wrote them down. Solon recovered the meaning of each name and translated them back into Greek. My great-grandfather Dropides had Solon's original manuscript, which is still in my possession, and I studied it carefully as a child. So if you hear names that sound Greek, that's the reason. Now, here's how the long story began:

I mentioned earlier, when talking about the gods' allotments, that they divided the whole earth into portions of varying size and established temples and sacrifices in their territories. Poseidon received the island of Atlantis as his lot. He fathered children with a mortal woman and settled them on a part of the island that I'll now describe.

Looking toward the sea, but in the center of the whole island, there was a plain -- said to be the most beautiful and fertile of all plains. Near this plain, also in the center of the island, about fifty stadia inland, there was a mountain, not very high on any side. On this mountain lived one of the original earthborn men of that country. His name was Evenor, and he had a wife named Leucippe. They had a single daughter called Cleito.

Cleito had just reached womanhood when her father and mother died. Poseidon fell in love with her and slept with her. And then he reshaped the very earth around her: he broke the ground and enclosed the hill where she lived on every side, carving out alternating rings of sea and land, one inside the other -- two rings of land and three of water. He turned them as if on a lathe, each ring perfectly equidistant from the center in every direction, so that no one could reach the island at the center. Ships and sailing hadn't been invented yet, so the place was completely inaccessible. But Poseidon, being a god, had no trouble arranging the central island to his liking. He brought up two springs from beneath the earth -- one warm, one cold -- and made every kind of food grow abundantly from the soil.

He also fathered and raised five pairs of twin sons. He divided the island of Atlantis into ten portions and gave to the firstborn of the eldest pair his mother's home and the surrounding territory -- the largest and best portion -- making him king over all the rest. The others he made princes, giving each of them rule over many people and a vast territory.

He named them all. The eldest, the first king, he called Atlas -- and after him the whole island and the ocean were called Atlantic. His twin brother, born after him, received the far end of the island facing the Pillars of Heracles, toward the region now called Cadiz. His name in Greek was Eumelus, but in the local language he was called Gadeirus. Of the second pair of twins, one was called Ampheres and the other Evaemon. The elder of the third pair was named Mneseus, and the younger Autochthon. Of the fourth pair, the elder was Elasippus and the younger Mestor. And of the fifth pair, the elder was Azaes and the younger Diaprepes.

All of these, and their descendants for many generations, were the inhabitants and rulers of numerous islands throughout the open sea. And, as I mentioned before, they also held dominion in our direction -- over the lands within the Pillars -- as far as Egypt and Tyrrhenia.

Now, Atlas had a large and distinguished family, and they kept the kingdom in the family, the eldest son always passing it to his eldest, for many generations. They accumulated a quantity of wealth that no kings or rulers had ever possessed before -- and probably never will again. They had everything they could possibly need, both in the city and in the countryside. Because of the vastness of their empire, many goods were imported from foreign lands, but the island itself supplied most of what they required for daily life.

First of all, they mined from the earth whatever could be found there -- both solid metals and those that could be smelted. And there was one metal that's now just a name but was then something real: orichalcum. They dug it up all over the island, and in those days it was more precious than anything except gold.

There was an abundance of timber for every kind of construction, and plenty of grazing for both domestic and wild animals. In fact, there were even elephants on the island -- large numbers of them. Because just as the land provided for every other sort of creature, whether it lived in lakes, marshes, rivers, mountains, or plains, it also provided for the largest and most voracious animal of all.

And whatever fragrant things the earth produces today -- roots, herbs, woods, the essences that drip from fruits and flowers -- all of these grew and thrived on that island. Cultivated fruits too: the dry, nourishing kind and everything else we use for food -- what we generally call grain and pulse. And the hard-shelled fruits that give us drinks, oils, and ointments. Chestnuts and similar treats that we eat for pleasure and amusement. The delightful dessert fruits we bring out after dinner when we're tired of eating, to console ourselves. All of these, that sacred island, bathed in sunlight, produced in beautiful, wonderful, and limitless abundance.

Blessed with all these riches from the earth, the people set about building their temples, palaces, harbors, and docks. And they organized the entire country in the following way:

First, they built bridges across the rings of water surrounding the ancient capital, creating a road to and from the royal palace. They'd built the palace right at the beginning, on the spot where Poseidon and their ancestors had lived, and each generation of kings kept adding to its splendor, every king outdoing the one before him to the best of his ability, until they'd made the building a marvel of size and beauty.

Starting from the sea, they cut a canal three hundred feet wide, a hundred feet deep, and fifty stadia long, driving it straight through to the outermost ring of water. This created a harbor with an entrance wide enough for the largest ships to sail through. They also cut openings through the rings of land at the bridges -- just wide enough for a single trireme to pass from one ring of water to the next -- and they roofed over these channels to create covered waterways underneath for the ships, since the banks of the land rings rose well above the water.

Now, the largest ring of water -- the one they'd cut the channel to from the sea -- was three stadia wide, and the ring of land next to it was equally wide. The next two rings -- one water, one land -- were each two stadia wide. And the ring of water immediately surrounding the central island was just one stadium wide. The central island itself, where the palace stood, was five stadia in diameter.

They enclosed all of this -- the island, the rings, and the bridges (which were a sixth of a stadium wide) -- with stone walls on every side, placing towers and gates on the bridges where the sea channels passed through. The stone they quarried from beneath the central island and from under the rings, on both the inner and outer sides. It came in three colors: white, black, and red. And as they quarried, they simultaneously hollowed out double docks with roofs carved from the native rock. Some of their buildings were simple -- all one color. But in others, they mixed the different stones together, varying the colors to delight the eye, creating a natural and effortless beauty.

The outer wall, which ran around the outermost ring, they covered entirely with a coating of bronze. The next wall they coated with tin. And the third wall, the one enclosing the citadel itself, blazed with the red light of orichalcum.

Inside the citadel, the palaces were built like this: At the very center stood a sacred temple dedicated to Cleito and Poseidon, surrounded by a golden enclosure. No one was allowed to enter -- it was the spot where the family of the ten princes had first come into the world. Each year, the people brought seasonal offerings of first fruits from all ten districts to this shrine.

Here too was Poseidon's own temple -- a stadium long, half a stadium wide, and proportionately tall, with a strange, exotic appearance. They covered the entire exterior with silver, except for the pinnacles, which were gold. Inside, the ceiling was ivory, elaborately inlaid everywhere with gold, silver, and orichalcum. The walls, columns, and floor were all sheathed in orichalcum.

Inside the temple they placed golden statues. There was the god himself, standing in a chariot drawn by six winged horses, so enormous that his head touched the ceiling. Around him were a hundred Nereids riding on dolphins -- that being the number people believed existed in those days. There were also many other statues inside, dedicated by private citizens. And around the temple on the outside stood golden statues of all the descendants of the ten kings and their wives, plus many other magnificent offerings from the kings and from private citizens, both from the city itself and from the foreign cities under their rule. There was an altar, too, that matched this magnificence in size and workmanship. And the palaces were equally worthy of the greatness of the kingdom and the glory of the temple.

They also had fountains -- one of cold water and another of hot -- flowing generously, and the water was remarkably pleasant and pure. They built structures around these springs and planted appropriate trees. They constructed pools -- some open to the sky, others covered over for use as warm baths in winter. There were separate baths for the kings, separate ones for private citizens, separate ones for women, and separate ones for horses and cattle, each decorated as befitted its purpose. The overflow water they channeled to the sacred grove of Poseidon, where trees of astonishing height and beauty grew thanks to the extraordinary soil. The rest they directed through aqueducts along the bridges to the outer rings. There they'd built numerous temples dedicated to various gods, along with gardens and exercise grounds -- some for men, others for horses -- on both of the two islands formed by the land rings. In the center of the larger island, they'd set aside a racecourse a stadium wide, which ran the entire length of the island for horse racing. There were also guardhouses stationed at intervals. The most trusted guards were assigned to the smaller ring, closer to the Acropolis. The most trusted of all were given houses inside the citadel itself, near the kings.

The docks were packed with triremes and naval supplies, everything ready for use.

So much for the layout of the royal palace.

Leaving the palace and crossing out through the three harbors, you came to a wall that started at the sea and ran all the way around, everywhere fifty stadia from the largest harbor, completely enclosing the whole area, with its two ends meeting at the mouth of the canal leading to the sea. The entire space was densely packed with houses. The canal and the largest harbor were crammed with ships and merchants arriving from every direction, and the sheer number of people created a constant roar of voices and clamor of every kind, day and night.

I've described the city and the area around the ancient palace more or less in Solon's own words. Now I need to try to convey the nature and layout of the rest of the country.

The whole land was said to be very high and sheer on the side facing the sea, but the area immediately around the city was a level plain, surrounded by mountains that descended toward the coast. It was smooth and even, oblong in shape -- stretching three thousand stadia in one direction and two thousand stadia across the center from the coast inland. This part of the island faced south and was sheltered from the north. The surrounding mountains were legendary for their number, size, and beauty -- far surpassing any that exist today. They contained many prosperous villages, as well as rivers, lakes, and meadows with enough food for every animal, wild or tame, and forests of every kind of wood, abundant enough for every purpose.

Now let me describe the plain, shaped as it was by nature and by the labor of many generations of kings over long ages. It was mostly rectangular and oblong, and where it deviated from a straight line, it followed a circular ditch. The depth, width, and length of this ditch were staggering -- almost unbelievable for a manmade work, especially on top of everything else they'd built. But I have to report what I was told. It was dug to a depth of a hundred feet, a stadium wide all the way around, and ten thousand stadia in total length, encircling the entire plain. It collected the streams that flowed down from the mountains, wound around the plain, converged at the city, and drained from there into the sea. From the inland side, straight canals a hundred feet wide were cut through the plain to the ditch, spaced a hundred stadia apart. Through these canals they floated timber down from the mountains to the city and shipped agricultural produce, cutting cross-channels between the canals for transportation to the city. They harvested crops twice a year -- relying on rainfall in winter and irrigation from the canals in summer.

As for the population: each plot on the plain was responsible for providing a military commander over its able-bodied men. Each plot was a square of ten stadia on a side, and there were sixty thousand plots in total. The mountain regions and the rest of the countryside also had a vast population, distributed among the plots and assigned leaders according to their districts and villages. Each leader was required to supply, for war: one-sixth of a war chariot (making ten thousand chariots total), two horses with riders, a pair of chariot horses without a seat plus a mounted warrior who could fight on foot with a small shield and a charioteer to stand behind the fighter and drive the horses. Also: two heavy-armed soldiers, two archers, two slingers, three stone-throwers, three javelin men (all light-armed), and four sailors -- to crew a total fleet of twelve hundred ships.

That was the military organization of the royal city. The other nine kingdoms had their own arrangements, but it would be tedious to go through all the differences.

As for offices and honors, here's how things were set up from the beginning. Each of the ten kings had absolute authority over the citizens in his own territory and in his own city, and in most cases over the laws as well, with the power to punish and execute as he saw fit. But the hierarchy among the kings and their relations with each other were governed by the commands of Poseidon, handed down by law. These were inscribed on a pillar of orichalcum, which stood in the center of the island at the temple of Poseidon. The kings gathered there every fifth year, and then every sixth year, alternating to give equal honor to both odd and even numbers. When assembled, they discussed their common interests, investigated whether any of them had broken the law, and passed judgment.

But before passing judgment, they'd pledge themselves to each other in this way: there were bulls that roamed freely in the temple of Poseidon. The ten kings, left alone in the temple, would pray to the god to let them capture a victim acceptable to him. Then they hunted the bulls -- with no weapons, only wooden staves and nooses. The bull they caught, they led to the pillar and cut its throat so the blood spattered over the sacred inscription.

Now, inscribed on the pillar, alongside the laws, was an oath calling down terrible curses on anyone who disobeyed. So after they'd slaughtered the bull in the traditional manner and burned its limbs, they'd fill a mixing bowl with wine and drop in a clot of blood for each of them. The rest of the bull they burned on the fire, after purifying the column all around. Then each king drew from the bowl with a golden cup, poured a libation onto the fire, and swore that he would judge according to the laws on the pillar, that he would punish anyone who'd already broken them, that in the future he would not willingly violate what was written there, and that he would neither give orders nor obey any ruler who commanded him to act contrary to the laws of their father Poseidon. Each king made this prayer for himself and his descendants, then drank from the cup and dedicated it in the temple of the god.

After they'd eaten dinner and taken care of their needs, when night fell and the sacrificial fire had died down to embers, they all put on magnificent dark blue robes. Then, sitting on the ground in the darkness, by the glow of the embers over which they'd sworn, with every other fire in the temple extinguished, they received and delivered judgment, in case anyone had charges to bring against another. At dawn, they wrote their verdicts on a golden tablet and dedicated it, along with their robes, as a memorial.

There were many specific laws governing the individual kings, inscribed around the temples, but the most important were these: they must never take up arms against one another, and they must all come to the defense of any city where someone tried to overthrow the royal house. Following their ancestors' example, they were to deliberate together about war and other matters, with supreme authority given to the descendants of Atlas. And the king could not impose the death penalty on any of his kinsmen without the consent of a majority of the ten.

Such was the vast power that the god established on the lost island of Atlantis. And eventually he turned this power against our land. Here's why, as tradition tells it:

For many generations, as long as the divine nature within them remained strong, they obeyed the laws and were loyal to the god whose blood they carried. They possessed spirits that were truly great in every way -- combining gentleness with wisdom through whatever life threw at them, and in their dealings with each other. They looked down on everything except virtue. They thought little of their wealth, regarding their mountains of gold and other possessions as a burden rather than a blessing. They weren't intoxicated by luxury, and wealth never robbed them of their self-control. They were clear-headed. They understood that all these material goods are increased by virtue and mutual friendship, but that obsessing over wealth and chasing it destroys both the wealth itself and virtue along with it.

Thanks to this kind of thinking, and because the divine element in their nature endured, all the qualities I've described kept growing and flourishing in them.

But when the divine portion began to fade -- diluted again and again by the mortal element, as it was mixed in too heavily -- and when human nature gained the upper hand, then they could no longer handle their good fortune. They behaved disgracefully. To anyone with eyes to see, they were visibly degraded, because they were losing the most precious of their gifts. But to those who couldn't see what true happiness was, they appeared more glorious and blessed than ever, right at the moment when they were most consumed by greed and unjust power.

Zeus, the god of gods, who rules according to law and has the ability to perceive such things, saw that this honorable race had fallen into a wretched state. Wanting to punish them so they might learn and improve, he gathered all the gods into their most sacred dwelling place -- the one at the center of the universe, which looks out over all of creation. And when he had assembled them, he spoke as follows --
