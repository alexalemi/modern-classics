Timaeus Part 1 -- Translation Notes
=====================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. STRUCTURE AND FORMAT: The Timaeus is essentially a monologue with a brief
   dramatic frame. After the opening conversation (Socrates recapping the
   Republic, Critias telling the Atlantis story), Timaeus delivers an enormous
   cosmological lecture. I preserved the speaker-label format throughout, but
   the vast majority of the text is Timaeus speaking. The rare "yes"
   interjections from Socrates were kept where they appear in the source.

2. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's Timaeus is notoriously dense -- sentences
   regularly run 60-100 words with nested subordinate clauses. I broke these
   aggressively into shorter sentences (average 18-25 words), using paragraph
   breaks to separate distinct ideas. The mathematical and cosmological
   content demands extra clarity, so I prioritized clean logical flow over
   any attempt at poetic compression.

3. THE MATHEMATICAL CONSTRUCTION OF THE WORLD-SOUL: This is the most
   technically demanding passage. I used several strategies:
   - Listed the seven portions explicitly with their values (1, 2, 3, 4, 9, 8, 27)
   - Identified the two series (doubles and triples) as interlocking
   - Defined arithmetic mean and harmonic mean in plain language with examples
   - Used concrete visual language: "split lengthwise into two strips,"
     "laid across each other in the shape of the letter X," "bent into circles"
   - Named the planets parenthetically where the source does (Sun, Mercury,
     Venus, Moon, Saturn, Mars, Jupiter)

4. THE GEOMETRIC SOLIDS: I named each Platonic solid explicitly after
   describing its construction:
   - Tetrahedron (4 triangular faces) = fire
   - Octahedron (8 triangular faces) = air
   - Icosahedron (20 triangular faces) = water
   - Cube (6 square faces) = earth
   - Dodecahedron (12 pentagonal faces) = the universe
   The source describes the construction process in excruciating geometric
   detail; I preserved every step but broke it into digestible paragraphs
   with clear labels.

5. THE TWO BASIC TRIANGLES: Identified clearly as:
   - The right isosceles triangle (45-45-90)
   - The 30-60-90 right triangle (whose hypotenuse is twice the shortest side,
     and whose longer leg squared is three times the shorter leg squared)
   This is crucial for understanding why three elements can interconvert
   (they share the 30-60-90 triangle) but earth cannot join them (it uses
   the isosceles).

6. THE RECEPTACLE (CHORA): This is one of the most obscure concepts in all
   of Plato. I used several clarifying strategies:
   - Called it "the receptacle" and "a kind of nurse of all becoming" on
     first mention, as the source does
   - Used the gold analogy extensively -- gold being reshaped into different
     forms while remaining gold -- as this is Plato's own best illustration
   - The "this" vs. "such" distinction: you shouldn't call fire "this"
     (pointing to a stable thing) but "such" (describing a recurring quality)
   - Mother/father/child analogy preserved
   - Perfume and wax analogies preserved
   - Summary: three natures = Being, Space, Becoming

7. THE ATLANTIS STORY: Preserved complete. Critias tells the story he heard
   from his grandfather, who heard it from Solon, who heard it from Egyptian
   priests. The nested narration is handled with clear attributions.
   Key details preserved:
   - The Egyptian priest's rebuke: "You Greeks are always children"
   - Atlantis as larger than Libya and Asia combined
   - Its location beyond the Pillars of Heracles
   - Athens standing alone against the Atlantean invasion
   - The catastrophe: earthquakes and floods, single day and night
   - The shoal of mud making the Atlantic impassable

8. SOCRATES' RECAP OF THE REPUBLIC: The opening section where Socrates
   recapitulates Republic V (communism of wives and children, guardian
   education, eugenics program) is kept complete but tightened. These
   ideas are already familiar from our Republic translation.

9. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "enquiry" -> "investigation" / "inquiry"
   - "whilst" -> "while"
   - "hitherto" -> "until now"
   - "aforesaid" -> dropped or rephrased
   - "notwithstanding" -> "despite"
   - "inasmuch as" -> "since" / "because"
   - "wherefore" -> "so" / "that's why" / "for this reason"
   - "betake yourselves" -> "get to work" / "turn yourselves to"
   - "artificer" -> "craftsman" / "maker"
   - "I dare say" -> "I'd guess" / "probably"

10. THE DEMIURGE: Consistently translated as "the creator," "the maker,"
    "the craftsman," or "God" -- following context. Jowett alternates between
    these himself. The Demiurge is not the God of monotheism but a divine
    craftsman who works with preexisting materials. I preserved this
    distinction -- the Demiurge doesn't create ex nihilo; he brings order
    out of preexisting chaos.

11. THE DEMIURGE'S SPEECH TO THE GODS: This is a dramatic set piece --
    God addressing the lesser gods, telling them to create mortal beings.
    Kept in direct speech, formatted as a distinct quoted passage. The
    logic is important: if God made mortals directly, they'd be immortal
    (equal to gods). So the lesser gods must do it, using mortal materials,
    while God provides the immortal soul-seed.

12. THE THEORY OF VISION: Plato's theory (a stream of inner fire meets
    external light and they coalesce along the line of sight) is wrong by
    modern standards but beautifully coherent on its own terms. I preserved
    the full account, including the mirror/reflection explanations and the
    explanation of sleep and dreams. The key insight -- that sight is an
    active process, not passive reception -- is made as clear as possible.

13. PRIMARY vs. SECONDARY CAUSES: This is a critical philosophical
    distinction. "Secondary causes" are mechanical processes (heating,
    cooling, expanding, contracting). "Primary causes" involve intelligent
    purpose. Most people mistake the secondary for the primary. This
    anticipates the modern debate between mechanistic and teleological
    explanation.

14. TIME AS "MOVING IMAGE OF ETERNITY": One of the most famous phrases in
    the history of philosophy. I kept it prominent and clear. Key point:
    time was created along with the universe. "Was" and "will be" are forms
    of time that we mistakenly apply to eternal being. Strictly, only "is"
    applies to what is truly eternal.

15. TONE: More formal than the earlier dialogues -- necessarily so. Timaeus
    is delivering a cosmological lecture, not having a Socratic conversation.
    I used contractions more sparingly than in Euthyphro or the Republic,
    but still kept the language accessible and modern. Where the content
    permits conversational phrasing, I used it; where the ideas demand
    precision, I prioritized clarity over casualness.


PHILOSOPHICAL TERMS AND CONSISTENCY
-------------------------------------

- "the Forms" (capitalized) -- the eternal, intelligible models
- "the receptacle" -- the third nature, the "nurse of becoming"
- "the Same" and "the Other/Different" -- the two fundamental motions/natures
  composing the world-soul
- "the Demiurge" not used directly; instead "the creator," "the maker,"
  "the craftsman," "God" -- context-dependent
- "necessity" -- the mechanical, purposeless aspect of causation
- "mind" / "intelligence" -- the purposeful, rational aspect
- "likely story" / "probable account" -- Timaeus's epistemological disclaimer
  about the status of his cosmology (it's the best we mortals can do)
- "Being" / "Becoming" / "Space" -- the three fundamental natures


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR PART 2
------------------------------

1. VOICE: Timaeus's lecture continues. Same tone -- scholarly, precise,
   occasionally vivid with analogies. He'll move from cosmology to
   physiology: the human body, sensation, disease, the mortal soul.

2. THE ELEMENT TRANSFORMATIONS: Part 1 established the rules for how
   elements interconvert (three can transform; earth cannot). Part 2
   will apply these rules to explain specific physical phenomena: metals,
   liquids, various kinds of earth and stone.

3. THE RECEPTACLE: Part 2 will use the receptacle concept to explain
   why elements are in constant motion -- the receptacle itself shakes
   them like a winnowing fan, sorting similar to similar.

4. THE TWO KINDS OF SOUL: Part 1 established the divine, immortal soul
   (housed in the head). Part 2 will introduce the mortal soul -- divided
   into a spirited part (in the chest) and an appetitive part (in the
   belly). The neck is the "isthmus" separating the two.

5. MEDICAL/ANATOMICAL CONTENT: Part 2 contains extensive (and largely
   wrong) anatomy and physiology -- the lungs as cooling system for the
   heart, the liver as mirror for the mind's images, the spleen as
   cleaning cloth for the liver. Translate faithfully without commentary.
   The wrongness is part of the intellectual history.

6. REINCARNATION HIERARCHY: Part 1 established that wicked men are
   reborn as women, then as animals. Part 2 will elaborate: birds come
   from light-minded men who trusted sight over reason; land animals
   from those who never looked up at the heavens; fish from the most
   ignorant of all. This is offensive by modern standards but must be
   translated faithfully per the text analysis guidelines.
