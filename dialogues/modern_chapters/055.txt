Athenian
The next natural step in our legislation, after everything we've covered, is lawsuits. We've already dealt with suits related to agriculture, but the more important ones are still ahead. Let me go through them by their standard categories, lay out the punishments for each offense, and specify who should judge them.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
There's something embarrassing about legislating, as we're about to do, for every detail of criminal behavior in a state that's supposed to be well-governed and perfectly suited for the practice of virtue. To assume that in such a state someone will commit crimes as terrible as those found in the worst places, and that we have to anticipate this person's existence by writing laws to deter and punish him -- that's a bit disgraceful, frankly. But we're not like the ancient lawmakers, who gave laws to heroes and sons of gods, being themselves (according to popular belief) descendants of gods, legislating for others who were also children of divine parents. We're just men, making laws for the sons of men. So there's nothing uncharitable in suspecting that some of our citizens may be like a seed that's touched an ox's horn -- with a heart so hard that no amount of fire can soften it. Among our citizens, there may be people who can't be tamed by all the strength of the laws. For their sake -- though it's an unpleasant task -- I'll begin with my first law, about robbing temples, in case anyone dares to commit such a crime. I don't expect or imagine that any well-raised citizen would ever catch this infection, but their servants, foreigners, and foreigners' servants might be guilty of many impieties. With them especially in mind -- though also keeping a watchful eye on the weakness of human nature in general -- I'll proclaim the law about temple robbers and similar incurable, or nearly incurable, criminals.

As we've already agreed, every such law should come with a short prelude. So here's what we'll say to the criminal -- the one whom some tormenting desire tempts, night and day, to go and rob a temple. We'll address him in as few words as possible:

"Friend, the impulse driving you to rob temples isn't an ordinary human affliction. It's not a visitation from heaven, either. It's a kind of madness, born from ancient and unexpiated crimes in your family line -- a recurring curse. You must guard against it with all your strength. Here's how: When this thought enters your mind, go and perform rites of purification. Go as a humble petitioner to the temples of the gods who ward off evil. Seek out the company of those who are called good men among you. Listen to them describe how every person should honor what's noble and just, and try to repeat their words yourself. Run from the company of the wicked -- run and don't look back. If these remedies lighten your condition, well and good. But if they don't, then accept that death is nobler than such a life, and depart from this world."

That's the prelude we sing to all those who have thoughts of unholy and treasonous acts. For the one who listens, the law has nothing more to say. But for the one who's disobedient once the prelude is over, we cry out loudly:

Anyone caught in the act of robbing a temple -- if he's a slave or a foreigner -- shall have his crime branded on his face and hands. He'll be beaten with as many stripes as the judges see fit and cast out naked beyond the borders of the land. This punishment will probably bring him to his senses and reform him, since no penalty the law inflicts is designed to cause evil. It always aims to make the offender either better, or at least not as much worse as he would have been otherwise.

But if any citizen is found guilty of some great and unspeakable crime -- against the gods, or his parents, or the state -- let the judge consider him incurable. After all, if someone who received an excellent education and upbringing from youth onward still couldn't keep himself from the worst crimes, there's no saving him. His punishment shall be death, which for him will actually be the least of evils. His example will benefit others: he'll perish in disgrace and be cast beyond the borders. But let his children and family, if they reject their father's ways, be honored. Let them receive public recognition for having nobly and courageously escaped from evil into good. None of their property should be confiscated by the state -- the citizens' allotted land must always remain the same and equal.

As for collecting fines: when someone clearly deserves a fine, he shall pay it from whatever he owns beyond his assigned lot, but not a penny more. To ensure accuracy, the guardians of the law should check the property registers and report the precise figures to the judges, so that no lot goes uncultivated for lack of funds. But if someone deserves a greater penalty, let him undergo a long and public imprisonment and be stripped of his civic honors -- unless some of his friends are willing to post bail and help him pay the fine.

No criminal shall go unpunished -- not for a single offense, not even if he's fled the country. The penalty should match the crime: death, imprisonment, beatings, degrading positions in public, removal to some temple on the borders, or fines, as we've said. In capital cases, the judges shall be the guardians of the law together with a court selected on merit from the previous year's magistrates. The procedural details -- how cases are brought to court, how summonses are served, and so on -- can be left to the next generation of lawmakers. But the manner of voting we must determine ourselves.

The vote shall be given openly. Before voting, the judges shall sit in order of seniority, facing the plaintiff and defendant. All citizens who can spare the time should attend and take a serious interest in listening to these cases. First, the plaintiff makes one speech. Then the defendant makes one. After the speeches, the eldest judge begins examining the parties and conducts a thorough inquiry into what's been said. After the eldest has spoken, the remaining judges proceed in order, each examining whichever party seems to have left gaps in the evidence -- whether something was stated poorly or left out entirely. Any judge with nothing to ask passes the examination to the next. All the judges shall set their seals on whatever testimony they find relevant and place these written records on the altar of Hestia. The next day, they'll meet again, conduct the same examination, and again seal the evidence. After they've done this three times and have gathered sufficient witnesses and evidence, each judge shall cast a sacred vote, having sworn by Hestia to decide justly and truthfully to the utmost of their ability. And so the suit comes to an end.

Next, after crimes against the gods, come crimes against the state itself. Whoever subjects the city to faction by handing power to a tyrant, who enslaves the laws and stirs up civil strife through violence and illegal means -- that person is the greatest enemy of the entire state. But the man who takes no part in such schemes, yet holds high office and either doesn't know about the treason or, knowing about it, is too cowardly to act on behalf of his country -- he's nearly as bad. Every citizen worth anything will report the conspiracy to the authorities and bring the traitor to trial for his violent and illegal attempt to overthrow the government. The judges for such cases shall be the same as for temple robbery, the procedure shall be the same, and a majority vote shall condemn to death.

But here's a general rule: the disgrace and punishment of the father must not be visited on his children -- except when someone's father, grandfather, and great-grandfather have all been executed. In that case, the state shall send the family away with all their possessions to the city and country of their ancestors, keeping only the original allotted lot. And from citizens who have more than one son of at least ten years of age, the family shall select ten candidates, as nominated by the father or grandfather on either the mother's or father's side. They shall send these names to Delphi, and whomever the god chooses shall be established as heir of the failed house. May he have better fortune than his predecessors!

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
And there shall be a third general law: the same rules about judges and trial procedure -- and the same rules about whether descendants may remain or must depart -- shall apply equally to all three categories: traitors, temple robbers, and those who violently subvert the laws of the state.

Now, for theft. Whether someone steals much or little, there shall be one law and one punishment for all: first, pay back double the amount stolen, provided the thief has enough property beyond his allotment. If he doesn't have enough, he shall be imprisoned until he either pays or persuades the person who won the verdict to forgive him. But if someone is convicted of stealing from the state -- well, if he can persuade the city to pardon him, or if he pays back twice the amount, he'll be released from prison.

Cleinias
Wait -- are you really saying that theft is all the same, whether the thief takes a lot or a little, from sacred or secular places? There are all kinds of differences in thefts. Shouldn't the lawmaker adapt to these differences and impose completely different penalties?

Athenian
Excellent point. I was running ahead too fast, Cleinias, and you've pulled me back to my senses -- reminding me of something I'd already been thinking about: that legislation has never yet been properly worked out. Remember the comparison I made earlier? I compared the people we're legislating for to slaves being treated by slave-doctors. Imagine this: one of those empirical physicians -- the kind who practice medicine without any real understanding of the science -- walks in on a gentleman-doctor talking to his gentleman-patient, using language that's practically philosophy, starting from the root causes of the disease and discussing the entire nature of the body. The empirical doctor would burst out laughing. He'd say what most so-called doctors always have on the tip of their tongue: "You fool! You're not healing the sick man -- you're educating him! He doesn't want to become a doctor. He just wants to get well."

Cleinias
And wouldn't he have a point?

Athenian
Perhaps. And he might make the same observation about us -- that in all this talk about laws, we're really giving our citizens an education, not a legal code.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
But here's where we're fortunate.

Cleinias
How so?

Athenian
We're not under any pressure to produce laws on a deadline. We're free to examine every form of government, figure out what's best and what's most practical, and how both goals can actually be achieved. Right now, in this very moment, we can choose: do we aim for what's best, or what's most immediately necessary? Which shall it be?

Cleinias
There's something funny about putting it as an either-or choice, as if we were legislators under some urgent deadline that can't wait until tomorrow. But we're more like people gathering stones and raw materials, piling them up at our leisure, and then selecting what's suitable for our construction when we're ready. Let's think of ourselves that way -- not as men frantically building, but as people partly collecting materials and partly assembling them. Some of our laws, like stones, are already set in place. Others are just lying around, ready to be used.

Athenian
That's right -- our approach to law will be more natural that way. But there's another issue facing lawmakers that I need you to think about seriously.

Cleinias
What is it?

Athenian
In every city you'll find all sorts of writings. Some are by legislators; others are by different kinds of writers.

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
Should we pay more attention to those other writers -- poets and the like, who've recorded their advice about how to live, whether in verse or prose -- rather than to the writings of legislators? Or should we give the lawmakers' writings priority above all?

Cleinias
The lawmakers', far above all others.

Athenian
And shouldn't the lawmaker, alone among writers, feel free to share his views on what's beautiful, what's good, and what's just -- and to teach how these things should be pursued by anyone who wants to live a happy life?

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Athenian
And isn't it true that if it's disgraceful for Homer and Tyrtaeus and other poets to lay down bad principles about life in their writings, it's even more disgraceful for Lycurgus and Solon and other lawmaker-writers? Of all the writings found in any city, those about laws -- when you actually open them up and read them -- should be by far the noblest and the best. Everything else should either agree with them, or be laughed off the page if it disagrees. Here's the question we should ask ourselves: shouldn't the laws of a state have the character of loving and wise parents, rather than tyrants and masters who just issue commands and threats, scribble their decrees on the walls, and walk away? That's the gentler vision of law we should aim for -- whether or not we can fully achieve it, at least we'll show we're willing to try. And may the result be good. God willing, it will be.

Cleinias
Excellent. Let's do it that way.

Athenian
So let's return to our careful examination, as we proposed, of everything relating to temple robbery, theft of all kinds, and offenses in general. And we shouldn't get frustrated if, in the middle of legislating, we've enacted some laws but haven't made up our minds about others. We're not finished lawmakers yet -- we're works in progress. If you don't mind, let's take these matters up now.

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
On the subject of honor and justice, let's try to figure out where we're being consistent with ourselves and where we're not -- and where ordinary people, from whom we'd certainly like to differ, agree or disagree among themselves.

Cleinias
What inconsistencies are you noticing in us?

Athenian
I'll try to explain. If I'm not mistaken, we all agree that justice -- just people, just things, just actions -- is always admirable. If someone argued that just people, even when they're physically ugly, are still perfectly beautiful because of the excellence of their sense of justice, nobody would call that inconsistent.

Cleinias
They'd be quite right.

Athenian
Maybe so. But let's think more carefully. If everything that's just is also admirable, then we have to include just punishments in that "everything" -- since just punishments go hand in hand with just actions.

Cleinias
And what follows from that?

Athenian
The conclusion is that a just action, insofar as it participates in justice, also participates equally in what's admirable.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And wouldn't a just punishment, by the same logic, also have to be admirable to the same degree -- if we're being consistent?

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
But what if we admit that a punishment is just yet also shameful? Then the word "shameful" gets applied to justice, and the just and the admirable end up at odds.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
It's not hard to understand. The laws we've already enacted seem to announce principles that are the exact opposite of what we're saying.

Cleinias
The opposite of what?

Athenian
We enacted, if you recall, that the temple robber and the enemy of lawful order may justly be put to death. And we were about to enact all sorts of similar penalties. But we stopped, because we realized these punishments are infinite in number and severity, and they are at once the most just and also the most shameful of all sufferings. So doesn't justice end up being sometimes the same as what's admirable, and other times the exact opposite?

Cleinias
That does seem to be the case.

Athenian
And that's how ordinary people use their language -- they rip apart what's admirable and what's just, treating them as completely disconnected.

Cleinias
Very true, stranger.

Athenian
Then let's look at our own position, Cleinias, and see how consistent we are on these matters.

Cleinias
Consistent in what way?

Athenian
I think I stated this clearly in our earlier discussion, but if I didn't, let me say it now --

Cleinias
Say what?

Athenian
That all bad people are bad involuntarily -- always. And from this, I need to draw a further conclusion.

Cleinias
What conclusion?

Athenian
That the unjust person may be bad, but he's bad against his own will. Now, it's a contradiction to say that a voluntary action is done involuntarily. So anyone who maintains that injustice is involuntary must also hold that the unjust person commits injustice involuntarily. That's my position too: I maintain that all people commit injustice involuntarily. If some argumentative person objects -- "Sure, people are unjust against their will, but plenty of them do wrong willingly" -- I disagree. No one does wrong willingly.

But then, how do I avoid contradicting myself? Suppose you, Cleinias, and you, Megillus, were to say to me: "Well, stranger, if that's all true, then what about legislating for our city of Magnesia? Should we legislate or not?" Of course we should, I'd reply. "Then will you distinguish between voluntary and involuntary crimes, and make the punishments heavier for voluntary offenses and lighter for involuntary ones? Or will you punish everything the same, on the grounds that there's no such thing as voluntary crime?"

Cleinias
Good question, stranger. So what's the answer?

Athenian
A very fair question. Here's what we need to do first --

Cleinias
Do what?

Athenian
Let's remember what we established earlier: that our ideas about justice are deeply confused and contradictory. With that in mind, let's ask ourselves whether we've found a way through the difficulty. Have we ever properly determined how these two categories of action -- voluntary and involuntary -- actually differ from each other? In every state, every lawmaker who's ever lived has recognized two kinds of actions: voluntary and involuntary. And they've legislated accordingly. But is our new principle -- that nobody does wrong willingly -- just going to sit there like an oracle from god, spoken but never explained or defended? You can't base legislation on an idea that nobody understands. That's impossible. Before we legislate, we have to prove that these are genuinely two different categories and explain what the difference between them is. That way, when we assign a penalty to either one, everyone can understand our reasoning and have some basis for judging whether the penalty fits.

Cleinias
I agree with you, stranger. One of two things has to be true: either we stop claiming that all unjust acts are involuntary, or we explain what we mean and prove it.

Athenian
The first option is completely intolerable to me. I can't refuse to speak what I believe is the truth -- that would be wrong and unholy. But if acts of injustice can't be divided into voluntary and involuntary in the usual way, then I need to find some other way to distinguish between them.

Cleinias
You're absolutely right, stranger. There can't be two opinions among us on that point.

Athenian
All right, then think about this: people harm each other in all kinds of ways in the course of daily life, and there are plenty of examples of both voluntary and involuntary harm.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Now, I don't want anyone to assume that all harms are automatically "injuries," and that these injuries come in two flavors -- voluntary and involuntary. The involuntary harms that people cause are just as numerous and just as serious as the voluntary ones. And please consider carefully whether what I'm about to say is right or wrong, because here's my key point: Cleinias and Megillus, I deny that someone who harms another involuntarily is committing an involuntary "injury." I wouldn't legislate about such an act as if it were an involuntary injury. I'd say that such a harm, whether great or small, isn't really an injury at all.

And here's the flip side: when a benefit is wrongly given, the person who gave it can often be said to have committed an injustice. You see, my friends, the mere act of giving or taking something away shouldn't automatically be labeled just or unjust. What the lawmaker needs to examine is this: did the person act from a just intention and principle, or not? He has to keep his eye on the distinction between injustice and mere harm. When there's harm, the law should, as far as possible, make it good -- saving what's been ruined, raising up what's fallen, mending what's been broken or wounded. And once compensation has been provided for the injustice, the law should always try to turn the parties involved from feelings of hostility into friendship.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
Now, as for unjust harms -- and unjust gains, too, when injustice brings profit -- we can heal as many of these as are capable of being healed. Think of them as diseases of the soul. And the cure for injustice runs in this direction:

Cleinias
What direction?

Athenian
When anyone commits an injustice, large or small, the law will instruct and compel him either never to do such a thing again, or never to do it willingly, or at least to do it far less often. And in addition, he must pay for the damage he caused. Whether the goal is achieved through words or actions, through pleasure or pain, through granting or removing privileges, through fines or rewards -- whatever method the law uses to make a person hate injustice and love the nature of justice, or at least stop hating it -- this is the noblest work that law can do.

But if the lawmaker sees that someone is incurable, he'll assign a law and a penalty for that person. He knows perfectly well that for people like this, there's no benefit in continuing to live. By departing, they'd do a double service to everyone else: they'd serve as a warning to others not to offend, and they'd rid the city of bad citizens. In such cases -- and only such cases -- the lawmaker should impose death as the punishment.

Cleinias
What you've said seems very reasonable. But could you do me a favor and explain a bit more clearly the difference between harm and injustice, and how the voluntary and involuntary get tangled up in them?

Athenian
I'll try. Here's what's generally understood about the soul: one element in its nature is passion -- whether you call it a condition or a part of the soul -- and it's hard to fight against and resist. By sheer irrational force, it overturns a great many things.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
And pleasure is not the same as passion. It operates with a different kind of power, achieving its goals through persuasion and the force of deception.

Cleinias
Quite true.

Athenian
And we can truthfully say that ignorance is a third cause of crimes. But the lawmaker should usefully divide ignorance into two kinds. There's simple ignorance, which leads to lighter offenses. And then there's double ignorance -- ignorance combined with the delusion that you're wise. Someone under this second kind's influence thinks he knows everything about matters he actually knows nothing about. When this double ignorance is backed by power and strength, the lawmaker will treat it as the source of enormous and monstrous crimes. But when it's accompanied by weakness, it produces only the mistakes of children and old people. The lawmaker will treat these as mere errors and write the mildest and most merciful laws for them.

Cleinias
You're perfectly right.

Athenian
We all observe that some people are superior to pleasure and passion, and others are inferior to them. That's a common thing to say.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
But nobody has ever heard anyone say that one person is "superior to ignorance" and another "inferior to it."

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Yet all three of these -- passion, pleasure, and ignorance -- drive people toward fulfilling their desires, often pulling them in opposite directions at the same time.

Cleinias
Yes, that happens often.

Athenian
And now I can define clearly and without ambiguity what I mean by justice and injustice. When anger and fear, pleasure and pain, jealousy and desire tyrannize over the soul -- whether or not they actually cause harm -- I call that injustice. But when a person's belief about what's best -- wherever in human nature you think this faculty resides, whether in states or in individuals -- has control of the soul and directs the person's life, then even if it's sometimes mistaken, whatever is done in accordance with it should be called just. The governing principle in each person that obeys this rational rule is what's best for their whole life, and it's to be called just -- even though harm done through honest mistake is often thought by many to be "involuntary injustice."

Let's not quarrel about terminology. We've identified three sources of wrongdoing. Let me bring them back into sharper focus. The first was the painful kind -- what we call anger and fear.

Cleinias
Right.

Athenian
The second consists of pleasures and desires. And the third involves hopes and aspirations toward what's truly best -- though this last category subdivides into three. So now we have five sources of human action, and for these five we'll create two kinds of law.

Cleinias
What are the two kinds?

Athenian
One kind covers acts of violence done openly, in broad daylight. The other covers acts done in darkness, through secret deception -- and sometimes with both violence and deception combined. The laws dealing with this second kind should be especially severe.

Cleinias
Naturally.

Athenian
Now let's return from this digression and finish the work of legislation. We've already enacted laws about temple robbers, traitors, and those who corrupt the laws to overthrow the government. It's quite possible that someone commits one of these crimes while in a state of madness, or while suffering from disease, or under the influence of extreme old age, or in a fit of childish foolishness -- essentially being no better than a child. If this is demonstrated to the judges trying the case, on the appeal of the criminal or his advocate, and the judges determine that this was indeed his condition when he committed the offense, then he simply has to pay for whatever damage he caused. Beyond that, he's exempt from other penalties -- unless he killed someone and has blood on his hands. In that case, he must leave for another land and country, and live there for a year. If he returns before the legally appointed time, or even sets foot in his native land, the guardians of the law shall confine him in the public prison for two years, and then release him.

Now that we've begun talking about homicide, let's try to lay down laws for every different kind. First: violent and involuntary homicides.

If anyone kills a friend accidentally during an athletic contest at the public games -- whether the victim dies immediately or later from the blows -- or if the same thing happens during war, military exercises, or practice combat (whether armed or unarmed), ordered by the authorities, then once the killer has been purified according to the law from Delphi governing such matters, he shall be innocent.

The same applies to doctors: if their patient dies against their will, the law holds them blameless.

If someone kills another person with his own hand, but unintentionally -- whether unarmed or with some weapon or projectile, or by giving food or drink, or through the application of heat or cold, or by suffocation, whether by his own hand or through the agency of others -- he shall be considered the responsible party and shall suffer one of the following penalties:

If he kills someone else's slave, believing the slave was his own, he must compensate the dead man's master for the loss, or pay a fine of twice the slave's value as assessed by the judges. He must also undergo purification rites -- greater and more numerous than those for someone who killed at the games. What exactly these rites are, the divinely appointed interpreters shall have authority to declare.

If a man kills his own slave, he shall undergo the legally required purification and be clear of the homicide charge.

If a man unintentionally kills a free person, he shall undergo the same purification as someone who killed a slave. But he should also take to heart an ancient story, which goes like this: A person who has suffered a violent death, when newly dead -- if he had the soul of a free man in life -- is angry at the one who killed him. Being himself filled with terror and panic from his violent end, he's shaken when he sees his killer walking around in all his familiar haunts. This disturbs the dead man's spirit, and his disturbance, combined with the killer's guilty conscience, is transmitted to the murderer with overwhelming force, destabilizing both his actions and his mind. For this reason, the killer must stay away from all the victim's usual places for an entire year, keeping clear of any spot the dead man frequented, throughout the whole country. If the dead man was a foreigner, the killer must also stay away from the foreigner's home country for the same period.

If someone voluntarily obeys this law, the victim's next of kin, seeing that everything has been done properly, shall take pity on him, make peace with him, and show him every kindness. But if someone disobeys -- if he dares to visit any of the temples and sacrifice while still impure, or refuses to stay in exile for the appointed time -- then the next of kin shall bring him to trial for murder. If convicted, every part of his punishment shall be doubled.

And if the next of kin fails to prosecute the crime? Then the pollution falls on his own head. The murdered man will fix the guilt on his kinsman, and anyone who wishes may compel the negligent relative to leave the country for five years, according to law.

If a foreigner unintentionally kills another foreigner living in the city, anyone who chooses may prosecute the case under the same rules. If the killer is a resident alien, he must leave for one year. If he's a complete foreigner, then in addition to the purification -- whether he killed a foreigner, a resident alien, or a citizen -- he shall be banished for life from the country governed by our laws. If he returns illegally, the guardians of the law shall put him to death and hand over his property, if he has any, to the victim's nearest relative. If he's shipwrecked and driven onto the coast against his will, he may camp on the seashore with his feet in the water, watching for a chance to sail away. If he's brought ashore by land and isn't his own master, the first magistrate he encounters in the city shall release him and send him safely across the border.

Next: if someone kills a free person with his own hand, and the deed is done in a fit of passion. Here we have to draw an important distinction. There are two scenarios: First, when someone suddenly and without any intention to kill causes another's death through blows or the like, on a momentary impulse, and immediately regrets it. Second, when someone is insulted in word or deed, nurses the desire for revenge, and intentionally kills, feeling no remorse afterward.

We have to treat these as two distinct kinds of homicide, both arising from passion. They occupy a middle ground between the fully voluntary and the fully involuntary, though each is really only a shadow or likeness of one or the other. The person who nurses his anger and takes revenge deliberately, after an interval and with calculated planning, resembles the voluntary killer. The person who acts instantly, in the heat of the moment, without premeditation, approaches the involuntary -- but even he isn't truly involuntary. He's only an image of the involuntary.

So when it comes to homicides committed in hot blood, it's genuinely difficult to decide whether we should classify them as voluntary or involuntary. The best and truest approach is to treat them as likenesses -- one resembling the voluntary, the other the involuntary -- and distinguish them based on whether there was premeditation or not. We should punish more severely those who kill with angry premeditation, and more leniently those who strike in the moment without forethought. What resembles the greater evil deserves heavier punishment; what resembles the lesser evil, lighter punishment. That's the rule our laws will follow.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Let's proceed. If anyone kills a free person with his own hand, and the deed is done in a moment of anger without premeditation, the offender shall suffer the same penalties as someone who committed involuntary homicide, plus an exile of two years -- to learn to control his passions.

But someone who kills from passion with premeditation shall likewise suffer the base penalties, plus an exile of three years instead of two. His punishment is longer because his passion was greater.

Here's how their return should work -- and this is where the law runs into difficulty, because sometimes the killer judged by law to be worse is actually the less cruel one, while the one judged less cruel may really be worse and may have carried out the killing more savagely, whereas the other may have been gentler. But generally, the degrees of guilt will be as I've described. The guardians of the law must weigh all these factors.

When a killer of either kind has completed his term of exile, the guardians shall send twelve judges to the borders of the land. These judges will have spent the intervening time investigating the criminals' behavior during exile. They'll decide whether to pardon the killer and allow his return, and the killers must accept their judgment.

But if someone who has returned home commits the same act again in a moment of anger, he shall be exiled permanently. If he returns, he shall suffer the same fate as the foreigner in the analogous case.

If someone kills his own slave, he shall undergo purification. If someone kills another person's slave in anger, he shall pay the owner twice the slave's value.

If any killer disobeys the law and pollutes the marketplace, the games, or the temples without undergoing purification, anyone who wishes may bring suit against the victim's next of kin for permitting this, and against the murderer as well. The accuser may compel the one to demand, and the other to undergo, double fines and double purification rites. The accuser himself shall receive the fine in accordance with the law.

If a slave kills his master in a fit of passion, the dead man's relatives may do with the killer whatever they please -- provided they don't spare his life. They'll be considered ritually pure.
