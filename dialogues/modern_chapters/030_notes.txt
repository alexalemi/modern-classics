CRATYLUS PART 1 — TRANSLATION NOTES

OVERVIEW
This is Part 1 of the Cratylus, covering:
1. The setup: Hermogenes explains the dispute with Cratylus about whether names are natural or conventional
2. Socrates dismantles Hermogenes' conventionalism through a series of arguments about the nature of actions and instruments
3. The "name-maker as craftsman" analogy — the legislator, the dialectician, the ideal Form of each name
4. The massive etymology section: Socrates etymologizes dozens of Greek words (names of heroes, gods, cosmic bodies, virtues, abstract concepts)
5. The theory of primary elements in language — letters as mimetic units

CENTRAL CHALLENGE: THE ETYMOLOGIES
The Cratylus is the hardest dialogue to translate because its central section consists of Socrates performing extended Greek wordplay — deriving words from other words based on phonetic similarity. Almost none of this works in English.

Strategy employed throughout:
- State clearly what Socrates claims each word means and what he derives it from
- Use parenthetical notes: "In the Greek, [word X] comes from / sounds like / is connected to [word Y]"
- Don't attempt to fabricate English puns that don't exist in the source
- Keep the reader oriented: the POINT is that Socrates is demonstrating (or parodying) the idea that names reveal the nature of things
- Preserve the humor and self-awareness — Socrates repeatedly signals that he knows this is getting ridiculous

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS

1. ETYMOLOGY FORMAT: Each etymology includes:
   - The English meaning Socrates claims
   - A parenthetical noting the Greek mechanism
   - The Greek terms preserved in transliterated form where necessary
   This ensures the reader understands THAT a pun is happening even when the pun doesn't land in English.

2. GREEK TERMS: Retained transliterated Greek in parentheticals where the etymology cannot be expressed any other way. The reader needs to see that "Hera" sounds like "aer" (air) or that "Kronos" sounds like "koros" (sweeping clean) even if they can't read Greek.

3. TONE: Preserved the growing absurdity and Socrates' self-aware humor. He's simultaneously brilliant and ridiculous. The "inspiration of Euthyphro" conceit (blaming his etymological mania on Euthyphro's lecture) is a running joke throughout and has been kept prominent.

4. PHILOSOPHICAL ARGUMENT: Underneath all the wordplay is a serious argument:
   - Things have their own natures (against Protagorean relativism)
   - Actions have natural methods (you cut with a cutting instrument)
   - Naming is an action, so it has a natural method
   - Names are instruments for teaching and distinguishing natures
   - The name-maker (legislator) looks to the ideal Form of the name
   - The dialectician judges whether names are well-made
   This chain of reasoning has been kept crystal clear.

5. THE HERACLITEAN THREAD: Socrates notices that the original name-givers seem to have been Heracliteans — believers in universal flux. Nearly every etymology he produces connects to motion, flow, or change. This is both an observation and a warning flag: the etymologies may reveal not the truth about things, but only the (possibly mistaken) worldview of the name-givers. This irony has been preserved.

SPECIFIC NOTES

- "Son of Hipponicus": Hermogenes' patronymic, kept as a mode of address (Socrates uses it twice in this section — at the opening and when asking about Dionysus/Aphrodite).

- "By the dog of Egypt": Socrates' characteristic oath. Kept as-is per translation guidelines. This is his substitute for swearing by the gods — he swears by the Egyptian dog-god (Anubis) instead.

- Prodicus's courses: The joke about the one-drachma vs. fifty-drachma course has been preserved — it's classic Socratic humor about being too poor for the premium version.

- Euthyphro reference: Socrates attributes his sudden etymological brilliance to Euthyphro's dawn lecture. This is a comic callback (Euthyphro appeared in the first dialogue in our collection) and a way for Socrates to distance himself from the etymologies — they're not really "his" ideas.

- Pan etymology: The double-natured Pan (smooth above, rough/goatish below = truth above, falsehood below) is one of the more philosophically rich etymologies. The connection between Pan ("all"), speech ("signifying all things"), and tragedy ("tragos" = goat) has been made as clear as possible.

- Justice digression: The long passage on "dikaion" (justice) where Socrates consults various unnamed philosophers is a miniature parody of philosophical disputes. Each expert gives a different answer — the sun, fire, abstract heat, mind — and Socrates ends up more confused than when he started. This comic structure has been preserved.

CONSISTENCY WITH PREVIOUS TRANSLATIONS
- Speaker label format: name on its own line, then speech
- Contractions in dialogue: "don't," "isn't," "can't," "I'd," etc.
- Greek oaths kept: "by the gods," "by the dog of Egypt"
- "The Forms" capitalized when discussing Plato's metaphysical theory (appears in the shuttle/craftsman passage)
- Natural, conversational rhythm with short back-and-forth exchanges

NOTES FOR PART 2
- Part 2 opens with Socrates continuing the etymology marathon, then shifts to the crucial philosophical section with Cratylus
- The argument with Cratylus is where the real philosophical payoff comes — questioning whether names can be trusted as guides to reality
- The Heraclitean worldview built up through the etymologies gets challenged: maybe the name-givers were wrong
- The dialogue ends with Socrates pointing toward the Forms as the true ground of knowledge, not names
- Maintain the same parenthetical format for remaining etymologies
- The tone shifts from playful/manic (etymology section) to serious/philosophical (Cratylus section)
