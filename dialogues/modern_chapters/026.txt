Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates, who is the narrator
  Menexenus
  Hippothales
  Lysis
  Ctesippus

Scene: A newly built wrestling school outside the walls of Athens.


Or, Friendship

I was walking from the Academy straight to the Lyceum, taking the outer road that runs close along the city wall. When I reached the postern gate by the fountain of Panops, I ran into Hippothales, the son of Hieronymus, and Ctesippus from the deme of Paeania, along with a group of young men standing around them. Hippothales spotted me coming and called out, "Where are you coming from, Socrates, and where are you headed?"

"I'm going from the Academy straight to the Lyceum," I said.

"Well, come straight to us instead," he said. "Come on, join us. It'll be worth your time."

"Who's 'us'?" I said. "And where exactly am I supposed to come?"

He pointed to an enclosed area with an open doorway set into the wall. "That's the building where we all hang out. And we've got a great crowd."

"What is this place?" I asked. "And what do you do here?"

"It's a brand new wrestling school," he said. "We mostly just talk. And you're welcome to join."

"That's kind of you," I said. "Is there a teacher?"

"Yes," he said. "Your old friend and admirer, Miccus."

"Really!" I said. "He's quite a distinguished instructor."

"So, are you coming?" he said. "Want to come see the group?"

"Sure," I said. "But first tell me something -- what's expected of me? And who's the boy you're all in love with?"

"Different people have different favorites, Socrates," he said.

"And who's yours?" I asked. "Tell me that, Hippothales."

He blushed. So I said to him, "Hippothales, son of Hieronymus! Don't bother telling me whether you're in love or not -- the confession is already too late. I can see not only that you're in love, but that you're in deep. I'm a simple, foolish sort of person, but the gods have given me the ability to spot this kind of thing."

He blushed even more.

Ctesippus jumped in: "I love watching you blush, Hippothales, and getting all tongue-tied about telling Socrates the name. If Socrates spent even a little time with you, you'd bore him to death talking about nothing else. Honestly, Socrates, the man has deafened us and stuffed our ears with his praises of Lysis. And if he's had a little wine? We might as well kiss our sleep goodbye -- it'll be 'Lysis this' and 'Lysis that' all night long. His prose performances are bad enough, but they're nothing compared to his poetry. And when he drowns us in poems and songs dedicated to his beloved -- that's truly unbearable. The worst part is his singing voice. It's genuinely horrifying, and we can't escape it. And now you ask him a question and look -- he's blushing."

"Who is Lysis?" I said. "He must be young, because the name doesn't ring a bell."

"Well," said Ctesippus, "his father is pretty well known, so people still call the boy by his father's name. You might not know his name, but I'm sure you'd recognize his face -- that's enough to pick him out."

"Tell me whose son he is," I said.

"He's the eldest son of Democrates, from the deme of Aexone."

"Well, Hippothales!" I said. "What a noble and altogether impressive love you've found! Now do me a favor -- give me the same performance you've been putting on for these fellows. I want to judge whether you know how a lover ought to talk about his beloved, either to the boy himself or to others."

"Socrates, please," he said. "You're not really taking what Ctesippus says seriously?"

"What do you mean?" I said. "Are you denying you're in love with the person he mentioned?"

"No," he said. "But I deny that I write poems or compositions about him."

"He's lost his mind," said Ctesippus. "He's talking nonsense. Completely delusional."

"Hippothales," I said, "I don't actually want to hear the poems or songs you've written for your favorite. I just want to know what they're about -- the general approach -- so I can judge your technique for winning him over."

"Ctesippus can tell you," he said. "If, as he claims, the sound of my words is constantly ringing in his ears, he must have them practically memorized."

"Oh, I know them only too well," said Ctesippus, "and the whole thing is ridiculous. Here's a man desperately in love, and he can't think of anything to say to his beloved that a child couldn't come up with. Isn't that absurd? All he talks about is the wealth of Democrates, and grandfather Lysis, and the boy's other ancestors, and their stable of horses, and their victories at the Pythian Games, and the Isthmus, and Nemea -- with four-horse chariots and single horses. That's the kind of stuff he composes and recites to us. But there's even sillier stuff. Just the other day he wrote a poem about Heracles being entertained as a guest, because Heracles was supposedly a relative of the family. He traced how one of Lysis's ancestors received Heracles on account of this connection -- and this ancestor was himself descended from Zeus through the daughter of the deme's founder. These are the kinds of old grandmother's tales he sings and recites to us, and we're forced to listen."

When I heard all this, I said, "You ridiculous man, Hippothales! You're composing and singing victory hymns about yourself before you've even won?"

"But my songs and poems aren't about myself, Socrates," he said.

"You think they're not?" I said.

"Well, what do you think?" he said.

"They absolutely are," I said. "Here's the thing: if you win this beautiful love of yours, everything you've said and sung will reflect brilliantly on you -- they'll be victory hymns for the man who conquered and won such a prize. But if he slips away from you, the more you've praised him, the more ridiculous you'll look for having lost something so wonderful. So a wise lover doesn't praise his beloved until he's actually won him, because he's afraid of how things might turn out. And there's another danger too: beautiful people, when they're praised and made much of, get puffed up with pride and vanity. Don't you agree?"

"I do," he said.

"And the more vain they become, the harder they are to catch?"

"I believe so."

"What would you say about a hunter who scared away his prey and made the animals he's trying to catch harder to get?"

"He'd be a terrible hunter, obviously."

"Right. And using words and songs to excite rather than soothe -- that would be pretty foolish, wouldn't you say? So think about it, Hippothales -- make sure you're not guilty of all these mistakes in your poetry. Because I doubt you'd say a man is a good poet who hurts himself with his own work."

"No, of course not," he said. "That would be stupid. And that's exactly why I'm coming to you for advice, Socrates. Tell me -- what should I say or do to make my beloved like me?"

"That's not easy to figure out," I said. "But if you'll bring your beloved to me and let me talk with him, I might be able to show you how to have a conversation with him -- instead of singing and reciting the way they've been accusing you of."

"That won't be hard," he said. "Just go into the wrestling school with Ctesippus and sit down and start talking. I'm pretty sure Lysis will come over on his own -- he loves listening, Socrates. And since today is the festival of Hermes, the boys and young men are all mixed together with no separation. He'll come for sure. But if he doesn't, Ctesippus knows him, and his cousin Menexenus is Lysis's best friend. Menexenus can call him over."

"That's the plan," I said. And with that, I led Ctesippus into the wrestling school, and the others followed.

When we went in, we found that the boys had just finished making their sacrifices, and that part of the festival was wrapping up. They were all dressed in white, and dice games were going on among them. Most of them were out in the courtyard having fun, but some were off in a corner of the changing room playing odds-and-evens with a bunch of dice they were pulling out of little wicker baskets. A ring of spectators stood around watching. Among them was Lysis. He stood there with the other boys and young men, a garland on his head, looking like a vision -- and every bit as deserving of praise for his character as for his looks.

We moved to the other side of the room, found a quiet spot, and sat down to talk. This caught Lysis's attention. He kept turning around to look at us -- obviously wanting to come over. For a while he hesitated, too shy to come alone. But then his friend Menexenus came in from the courtyard, and when he spotted Ctesippus and me, he started heading over to sit with us. Lysis saw him and followed, sitting down at his side. Then the other boys drifted over too.

I noticed that Hippothales, when he saw the crowd forming, positioned himself behind them where Lysis couldn't see him -- afraid of annoying the boy. And from there he stood and listened.

I turned to Menexenus and said, "Son of Demophon, which of you two is older?"

"That's something we argue about," he said.

"And which of you is more noble? Do you argue about that too?"

"Oh yes, definitely."

"And I suppose you also argue about which is better looking?"

The two boys laughed.

"I won't ask which of you is richer," I said, "because you're friends, aren't you?"

"Definitely," they both said.

"And friends share everything in common -- so neither of you can be richer than the other, if you truly are friends."

They agreed. I was about to ask which of them was more just and which was wiser, but just then someone came and called Menexenus away, saying the gymnastics teacher needed him. I figured he had to go help with a sacrifice. So off he went, and I turned back to Lysis.

"Lysis," I said, "I'd guess your father and mother love you very much."

"They sure do," he said.

"And they'd want you to be perfectly happy."

"Of course."

"But do you think anyone is happy who's basically a slave and can't do what he wants?"

"I really don't think so," he said.

"So if your father and mother love you and want you to be happy, they must go out of their way to make sure you are."

"Of course," he said.

"So they let you do whatever you like and never scold you or stop you from doing what you want?"

"Actually, Socrates, there are tons of things they won't let me do."

"Wait, what?" I said. "They want you to be happy but stop you from doing what you like? For example -- if you wanted to climb into one of your father's chariots and grab the reins during a race, they wouldn't let you, would they?"

"No," he said. "They definitely wouldn't."

"Well, who do they let drive?"

"There's a charioteer my father pays."

"So they trust a hired man more than their own son? They let him do whatever he wants with the horses and pay him for it?"

"But I bet they let you take the whip and drive the mule cart, right? They'd allow that?"

"Allow it?" he said. "They would not!"

"So is nobody allowed to whip the mules?" I said.

"The muleteer is," he said.

"And is he a slave or a free man?"

"A slave," he said.

"So they value a slave more than you, their own son? They trust their property to him rather than to you, and let him do what he likes while you're forbidden? Answer me this: are you your own master, or don't they even allow that?"

"No," he said. "Of course they don't."

"So who's your master?"

"My tutor -- there he is," he said.

"And is he a slave?"

"He sure is -- he's our slave."

"That's remarkable," I said. "A free person being governed by a slave. And what does this tutor do with you?"

"He takes me to my teachers."

"Don't tell me your teachers boss you around too?"

"They sure do."

"So apparently your father takes great pleasure in loading you up with masters and rulers. But at least when you go home to your mother, she lets you have your way, right? She wouldn't interfere with your happiness. When she's working with her wool or weaving at the loom, she lets you help yourself to her things -- the shuttle, the comb, the rest of her weaving tools. I'm sure nothing stops you from touching them."

"Ha!" he said, laughing. "Socrates, she doesn't just stop me -- I'd get a beating if I touched any of them."

"Good heavens!" I said. "Have you ever done something terrible to your father or mother?"

"Absolutely not," he said.

"Then why on earth are they so determined to keep you from being happy and doing what you like? They keep you in somebody's charge all day long, doing nothing you want to do. So it seems like you get no benefit at all from your family's enormous wealth -- everyone controls it but you -- and no use of your own body either, since someone else looks after that too. You, Lysis, are master of nothing and can do nothing you choose."

"Well, Socrates," he said, "the reason is that I'm not old enough."

"I doubt that's really it," I said. "After all, I'd imagine there are plenty of things your father Democrates and your mother do let you do without waiting for you to grow up. For instance, if they need something read or written, I'd bet you're the first person in the household they'd call on."

"True," he said.

"And they let you write the letters in any order you want, and pick up the lyre and tune the strings and pluck them however you please. They don't interfere with any of that."

"That's right," he said.

"So what's the reason, Lysis? Why do they let you do some things and not others?"

"I suppose," he said, "it's because I understand some things and not others."

"Exactly, my young friend," I said. "It's not about your age at all -- it's about your knowledge. And the moment your father decides you're wiser than he is about something, he'll hand it right over to you -- himself and his property included."

"I think so."

"And your neighbor too -- doesn't the same rule apply? If he's convinced you know more about running a household than he does, will he keep managing his own affairs, or hand them over to you?"

"I think he'd hand them over."

"And what about the Athenian people? Won't they trust you with their affairs once they see you have the wisdom to handle them?"

"And here -- let me push this further. The Great King of Persia has an eldest son, the Prince of Asia. Now suppose you and I went to the Great King and convinced him we were better cooks than his son. Would he let us manage the kitchen -- toss in whatever we liked while the pot was boiling -- rather than his own son?"

"Us, clearly."

"And we'd be allowed to throw in salt by the handful, while the prince couldn't put in so much as a pinch?"

"Right."

"Or suppose the prince had bad eyes. Would the king let him touch his own eyes if the king thought the prince didn't know anything about medicine?"

"He wouldn't."

"But if the king believed we knew medicine, he'd let us do whatever we wanted -- even pry the prince's eyes wide open and sprinkle ashes in them -- because he'd trust that we knew best?"

"That's true."

"And anything at all where we seemed wiser than the king or his son -- he'd leave it to us?"

"Necessarily so, Socrates," he said.

"So here's the point, Lysis," I said. "In anything we truly understand, everyone will trust us -- Greeks and foreigners, men and women alike. We'll be free to do as we see fit, no one will want to interfere with us, we'll be in charge, and these will truly be ours, because we'll benefit from them. But in areas where we have no understanding, no one will trust us to do what we think is best. Everyone will hold us back as much as they can -- and not just strangers, but our own father and mother, and anyone else who's even dearer to us. In those areas, we'll be subject to others, and those things won't really be ours, because we won't benefit from them. Do you agree?"

He agreed.

"And will anyone be our friend, will anyone love us, in areas where we're useless to them?"

"Certainly not."

"So as it stands, not even your father or mother can love you, and nobody else can love anybody, insofar as they're useless."

"And so, my boy, if you become wise, everyone will be your friend and everyone will feel close to you -- because you'll be useful and good. But if you're not wise, no one will be your friend -- not your father, not your mother, not your own family. And in matters you don't yet understand, can you really be full of yourself about them?"

"That's impossible," he said.

"And you, Lysis, if you still need a teacher, haven't yet reached wisdom."

"True."

"And so you're not puffed up -- since you have nothing to be puffed up about."

"Honestly, Socrates, I don't think I am."

When I heard him say this, I glanced over at Hippothales. I very nearly made a blunder -- I almost said to him, "That's how you should talk to your beloved, Hippothales -- humbling him and bringing him down to size, instead of what you do, which is puff him up and spoil him." But I saw that Hippothales was in a state of excitement and confusion over what had just happened. And I remembered that, even though he was standing nearby, he didn't want Lysis to notice him. So I thought better of it and kept quiet.

Just then Menexenus came back and sat down in his spot next to Lysis. And Lysis, in that sweet, boyish way of his, leaned over and whispered in my ear so Menexenus wouldn't hear: "Socrates, tell Menexenus what you were just telling me."

"Why don't you tell him yourself, Lysis?" I said. "I'm sure you were paying attention."

"Oh, I was," he said.

"Then try to remember what we said, and repeat it to him as exactly as you can. If you forget something, ask me again next time you see me."

"I will, Socrates, I promise. But go on and tell him something new. Let me listen for as long as I'm allowed to stay."

"Well, I can hardly refuse," I said, "since you're asking. But fair warning -- Menexenus is pretty combative. You may need to come to my rescue if he tries to trip me up."

"Oh, I know," he said. "He's very combative. That's exactly why I want you to argue with him."

"So I can make a fool of myself?" I said.

"No!" he said. "So you can put him in his place."

"That's easier said than done," I said. "He's formidable -- he's been trained by Ctesippus. And look, Ctesippus himself is right here."

"Never mind that, Socrates. Just argue with him."

"All right, I suppose I must," I said.

At this point Ctesippus complained that the two of us were whispering and keeping the feast to ourselves.

"I'll be happy to share," I said. "Here's the situation: Lysis didn't quite follow something I was saying, and he wants me to ask Menexenus about it, since he thinks Menexenus might know."

"Well, why don't you ask him?" said Ctesippus.

"That's what I'll do," I said. "So, Menexenus, answer me this. But first, let me tell you something about myself. Ever since I was a boy, I've had my heart set on one thing. Everyone has their passion -- some people want horses, some want dogs, some want gold, some want fame. None of those things get me very excited. But friends? I have a passion for friends. I'd rather have a good friend than the finest rooster or quail in the world. I'd go even further -- I'd rather have one than the best horse or dog alive. And by the dog of Egypt, I'd much rather have a true friend than all the gold of Darius -- or even Darius himself. That's how much I value friendship.

"And that's why, when I look at you and Lysis -- so young, and yet you've already gotten hold of this treasure so easily and so quickly, each of you having the other -- I'm amazed. I'm delighted. Because here I am, getting on in years, and I'm so far from having found anything like that, I don't even know how one goes about making a friend.

"But that's exactly what I want to ask you about, since you have actual experience. Tell me: when one person loves another, which one is the friend? The one who loves, or the one who's loved? Or could either of them be the friend?"

"Either one, I'd think," said Menexenus.

"Wait," I said. "You mean that if only one of them loves the other, they're both friends?"

"Yes," he said. "That's what I mean."

"But what if the lover isn't loved back? That happens all the time."

"Sure."

"Or what if he's actually hated? Lovers sometimes imagine exactly that -- that they're not loved in return, or even that they're despised. And nothing exceeds their love. Isn't that true?"

"Yes," he said. "Quite true."

"So in that case, one person loves and the other is loved?"

"Yes."

"Then which one is the friend? Is the lover the friend of the beloved, whether the beloved loves him back or even hates him? Or is the beloved the friend? Or is there no friendship at all unless both love each other?"

"It seems like there's no friendship at all," he said.

"Hm. That doesn't line up with what we said before. We were saying that both are friends if just one of them loves. But now it seems like neither is a friend unless they both love."

"That does seem to follow."

"So nothing that doesn't love back can be a friend to its lover?"

"I don't think so."

"Then there are no horse-lovers, if the horses don't love them back? No lovers of quail, or dogs, or wine, or athletics? No lovers of wisdom -- unless wisdom loves them in return? Or do they love these things even though the things aren't their friends? Was the poet wrong who said:

"'Happy the man to whom his children are dear, and single-hoofed steeds, and hounds of the chase, and a friend in a foreign land'?"

"I don't think the poet was wrong," he said.

"You think he's right?"

"Yes."

"Then, Menexenus, it seems the conclusion is this: what is loved, whether it loves back or even hates, can still be dear to the one who loves it. Think about very young children -- too young to love, or even actively resenting their father or mother when they're being punished. They're never dearer to their parents than at the very moment when they hate them."

"I think you're right," he said.

"So it's the beloved, not the lover, who is the true friend?"

"It seems so."

"And the hated one, not the hater, is the true enemy?"

"Apparently."

"Then many people are loved by their enemies and hated by their friends -- friends to their enemies and enemies to their friends. But how absurd is that, my friend? How impossible -- being an enemy to your friend, or a friend to your enemy?"

"I completely agree, Socrates. That can't be right."

"So if that's impossible, then the lover must be the friend of the beloved?"

"It seems so."

"And the hater must be the enemy of the hated?"

"That follows."

"But wait -- we're running into the same problem as before. A person can be the friend of someone who isn't his friend, or who's even his enemy -- if he loves someone who doesn't love him back, or who actually hates him. And he can be the enemy of someone who isn't his enemy, or who's actually his friend -- if he hates someone who doesn't hate him, or who even loves him."

"That seems to be true."

"Then if the lover isn't necessarily a friend, and the beloved isn't necessarily a friend, and the two of them together aren't necessarily friends -- what are we left with? Who are we supposed to call friends? Is there anyone left?"

"Honestly, Socrates, I'm stuck," said Menexenus.

"Maybe," I said, "we've been going about this the wrong way entirely?"

"I'm sure we have, Socrates," said Lysis. He blushed as he spoke. The words seemed to slip out on their own -- his whole mind had been absorbed in the argument. You couldn't miss the intensity on his face as he'd been listening.

I was pleased by Lysis's engagement, and I wanted to give Menexenus a break, so I turned to Lysis and said, "I think you're right, Lysis. If we'd been on the right track, we wouldn't have gone this far astray. Let's not keep going down this road -- it's getting rough. Let's try a different path. Let's look at what the poets say, since they're a kind of father and source of wisdom for us. They don't treat the subject of friendship lightly. They say it's God himself who brings friends together, drawing like to like. I think they put it something like this:

"'God always draws like to like, and brings them together.'

"You've heard that, haven't you?"

"Yes," he said.

"And haven't you also come across the philosophers who write about nature and the universe? They argue that like must love like."

"Yes, I have," he said.

"And are they right?"

"Maybe," he said.

"Perhaps half right," I said. "Or maybe even completely right -- if we understood their meaning correctly. Because the more time a bad person spends with another bad person, the more closely they're thrown together, the more they're likely to hate each other. Bad people harm each other, and you can't be friends with someone who harms you. Right?"

"Right," he said.

"So half the saying is already wrong, if wicked people are supposed to be like one another and therefore friends."

"That's true."

"But I think what the saying really means is this: good people are alike, and they're friends with one another. Bad people, on the other hand -- as people often say about them -- are never really at peace with each other or even with themselves. They're restless and unstable. And anything that's at war with itself is hardly going to be in harmony with anything else. Don't you agree?"

"I do."

"So, my friend, what they're really saying -- if I understand them correctly -- is that only the good are friends with the good. Bad people never achieve true friendship, not with good people and not with each other."

He nodded.

"Then we have our answer about who friends are: the good are friends."

"That seems right," he said.

"It does," I said. "And yet -- I'm not entirely satisfied. By Zeus, let me tell you what's bothering me. Suppose like is the friend of like, insofar as they're alike -- is that really useful? Or let me put it another way: can someone who's just like you do you any good or any harm that you couldn't do to yourself? Can you experience anything from your likeness that you wouldn't experience on your own? And if neither of you can be any use to the other, how can you be dear to each other? Can you?"

"No, we can't."

"And can someone who isn't loved be a friend?"

"Certainly not."

"All right, but forget the 'like' part for a moment. Maybe the good can still be friends with the good, not because they're alike, but because they're good?"

"Fair enough."

"But think about this: won't the good person, insofar as he's good, be self-sufficient? And someone who's self-sufficient doesn't need anything -- that's what self-sufficient means."

"Of course."

"And someone who doesn't need anything won't desire anything."

"No."

"And someone who doesn't desire anything can't love what he doesn't desire."

"True."

"And someone who doesn't love isn't a friend."

"Clearly not."

"Then where does friendship fit in? If good people don't need each other when they're apart -- since they're self-sufficient even alone -- and have no use for each other when they're together, why would they value each other at all?"

"They couldn't."

"And they can't be friends unless they value each other."

"True."

"So, Lysis, are we being led astray here? Are we completely wrong?"

"How so?" he said.

"Haven't I heard someone say -- and now I'm starting to remember -- that like is actually the greatest enemy of like, and good of good? And he cited Hesiod as his authority:

"'Potter quarrels with potter, poet with poet, beggar with beggar.'

"And he claimed the same was true of everything: the things most alike are most full of envy, rivalry, and hatred toward each other, while the things most unlike are most full of friendship. The poor man is compelled to be friends with the rich, the weak with the strong, the sick with the doctor. Everyone who's ignorant has to seek out and court the one who knows. In fact, he went on quite grandly to say that the whole idea of like being friendly with like is completely wrong -- it's the exact opposite. The most opposed things are the most friendly. Everything desires not what's like it, but what's most unlike it. The dry desires the moist, the cold desires the hot, the bitter desires the sweet, the sharp desires the blunt, the empty desires the full, the full desires the empty. Opposites feed on opposites, while like gets nothing from like.

"I have to say, it was a charming argument, and well put. What do you all think?"

"At first hearing," said Menexenus, "it sounds right."

"So we should say the greatest friendship is between opposites?"

"Hold on, Menexenus. Won't the clever debaters pounce on us and triumphantly ask: isn't love the exact opposite of hate? And what will we say -- won't we have to admit they're right?"

"We will."

"Then they'll ask: is an enemy the friend of a friend? Or a friend the friend of an enemy?"

"Neither," he said.

"Is a just person the friend of an unjust person? Or a self-controlled person the friend of someone with no self-control? Or a good person the friend of a bad person?"

"I don't see how that's possible."

"And yet," I said, "if friendship works by opposites, then opposites have to be friends."

"Then neither like and like nor unlike and unlike are friends."

"It seems not."

"But let's consider one more thing. Maybe we've been wrong about all of this, and the truth is something different entirely. Maybe what is neither good nor evil can sometimes be a friend of the good."

"What do you mean?" he said.

"Well, honestly," I said, "I hardly know what I mean -- my head is spinning from all this argument. So let me take a guess. Maybe 'the beautiful is the friend,' as the old proverb says. Beauty is a soft, smooth, slippery thing -- it slides right into our souls almost without us noticing. And I'd say the good is the beautiful. Would you agree?"

"Here's my hunch: what is neither good nor evil is a friend of the beautiful and the good. Let me explain why. I'm going to say there are three categories of things: the good, the bad, and what's neither good nor bad. You'd agree?"

"Yes."

"And we've already ruled out that the good is the friend of the good, the evil of the evil, or the good of the evil. Our earlier arguments took care of all that. So if friendship exists at all, what's neither good nor evil must be the friend of the good, or of what's also neither good nor evil. Nothing can be friends with the bad."

"Right."

"But like can't be friends with like, as we just said."

"Correct."

"So what's neither good nor evil can't be friends with other things that are neither good nor evil."

"Clearly not."

"Then the good alone is the friend of what is neither good nor evil."

"That seems to follow."

"And doesn't this point us in the right direction? Think about it this way: a body that's healthy doesn't need medicine or any kind of help -- it's perfectly fine on its own. A healthy person has no love for the doctor, because he's healthy."

"He doesn't."

"But a sick person does love the doctor, because he's sick?"

"Yes."

"And sickness is a bad thing, while medicine is a good and useful thing?"

"Right."

"And the human body, considered just as a body, is neither good nor evil in itself?"

"Yes."

"And the body is driven, because of disease, to seek out and befriend the art of medicine?"

"Yes."

"So what is neither good nor evil becomes the friend of the good, because of the presence of evil?"

"It looks that way."

"And clearly, this has to happen before the thing has become completely corrupted by evil. Because if it had become evil itself, it wouldn't still desire the good or be friendly with it -- we said the evil can't be friends with the good."

"Impossible."

"Now, here's an important distinction. Some things are changed in their nature when something else is present in them, and some things aren't. Take an ointment or dye applied to a surface, for example."

"Is the substance that's been dyed the same thing as the color applied to it?"

"What do you mean?" he said.

"Here's what I mean. Suppose I covered your brown hair with white lead -- would your hair actually be white, or would it just look white?"

"It would only look white," he said.

"And yet whiteness would be present in it?"

"Yes."

"But it wouldn't really be white at all, despite whiteness being present. It wouldn't be white any more than it would be black."

"Right."

"But when old age brings whiteness naturally into hair, then it really does become white -- it's made white by the genuine presence of white."

"Obviously."

"So here's my question: when something is present in something else, is the thing always changed in its nature? Or only when the presence is of a particular kind?"

"The latter," he said.

"Then what is neither good nor evil can have evil present in it without actually becoming evil -- at least not yet?"

"Right."

"And when something has evil present in it but hasn't yet become evil itself, the presence of evil makes it desire the good. But the presence of evil that actually makes a thing evil -- that destroys its desire for the good and its friendship with it. Because at that point it's no longer something that's both good and evil, but purely evil. And evil, we said, can't be friends with the good."

"True."

"And this is why we say that people who are already wise -- whether gods or mortals -- no longer love wisdom. And people who are so deeply ignorant that they've become bad can't love wisdom either, because no truly evil or ignorant person is a lover of wisdom. What's left are the people who have the misfortune to be ignorant but aren't yet hardened in their ignorance -- people who don't know what they don't know, but haven't lost their ability to recognize it. These are the lovers of wisdom: neither fully good nor fully bad. The bad don't love wisdom any more than the good do -- because, as we've seen, unlike doesn't befriend unlike, and like doesn't befriend like. Remember?"

"Yes," they both said.

"So, Lysis and Menexenus, we've discovered the nature of friendship -- no question about it. Friendship is the love that what is neither good nor evil has for the good, because of the presence of evil -- whether in the soul, the body, or anywhere else."

They both agreed and gave their wholehearted assent. And for a moment I felt that rush of satisfaction, like a hunter who's just grabbed hold of his prey.

But then the strangest suspicion crept over me. I had a feeling our conclusion was wrong. It was painful, and I said, "Oh no, Lysis and Menexenus -- I'm afraid we've only been grasping at shadows."

"Why do you say that?" said Menexenus.

"I'm afraid," I said, "that our argument about friendship is false. Arguments, like people, can be imposters."

"What do you mean?" he said.

"Look at it this way," I said. "A friend is always the friend of someone, right?"

"Certainly."

"And does he have a reason and an object in being a friend? Or no reason and no object?"

"He has a reason and an object."

"And is the object that makes him a friend something dear to him? Or something neither dear nor hateful?"

"I don't quite follow," he said.

"That's fair," I said. "Let me put it differently. A sick man -- we were just saying -- is the friend of the doctor, right?"

"Yes."

"And he's the doctor's friend because of disease, and for the sake of health?"

"Yes."

"And disease is a bad thing?"

"Obviously."

"And health -- is it good, bad, or neither?"

"Good," he said.

"So we were saying that the body, which is neither good nor evil, because of disease -- that is, because of something bad -- is a friend of medicine. And medicine has taken on this friendship for the sake of health, which is good."

"Right."

"And health is a friend?"

"Yes."

"And disease is an enemy?"

"Certainly."

"Then what is neither good nor evil is a friend of the good, because of the bad, and for the sake of the good?"

"So it seems."

"The friend is a friend for the sake of a friend, and because of an enemy?"

"Apparently."

"All right, boys -- let's be careful here and watch out for being tricked. I won't bother going back to the point that 'the friend is a friend of a friend' and 'like is a friend of like,' since we already ruled that out. But let's look closely at this new idea so it doesn't fool us. We said medicine is a friend -- for the sake of health, right?"

"Yes."

"And health is also a friend?"

"Yes."

"And if it's a friend, it must be a friend for the sake of something?"

"Yes."

"Something that's also a friend, given what we've agreed?"

"Right."

"And that friend involves another friend?"

"Yes."

"But if we keep going like this, won't we eventually arrive at some first principle of friendship -- some ultimate thing that isn't 'for the sake of' anything else, and for the sake of which everything else is dear? That's where we'll have to stop."

"Yes."

"Here's what worries me: all those other things we call dear 'for the sake of something else' -- they might just be illusions. The real friend, the true foundation -- that's where genuine friendship lives. Let me put it this way: imagine a man has a treasure -- say, a son, who's more precious to him than anything else he owns. Now, if the father found out his son had drunk hemlock, and he believed wine could save him -- would he value the wine?"

"Of course."

"And the cup that held the wine?"

"Sure."

"But does he value the wine or the clay cup the same way he values his son? Of course not. All his concern isn't really about the means -- it's about the thing those means are meant to protect. We often say we place great value on gold and silver, but that's not really true. What we truly value is some further thing -- whatever it may be -- for the sake of which we pursue gold and everything else. Right?"

"Absolutely."

"And can't we say the same thing about friendship? When we call something dear 'for the sake of something else,' we're not using the word properly. The truly dear thing -- the real friend -- is whatever all those chains of 'for the sake of' finally lead to."

"That seems right," he said.

"And this truly dear thing, this ultimate friend -- it's not for the sake of any further friend?"

"No."

"Good. Then we're done with the idea that friendship has some further object beyond itself. Can we say, then, that the good is the true friend?"

"I think so."

"And the good is loved because of evil? Here's what I mean: suppose that of our three categories -- good, evil, and neither -- evil just vanished entirely, and in no way affected soul or body or anything else. Would the good still be any use to us? If nothing could hurt us anymore, we'd have no need of anything to help us. And then it would be clear that we only loved the good because of evil -- the good was the cure, and evil was the disease. But take away the disease, and there's no need for the cure. Is this the nature of the good -- that it's loved by us, who stand between good and evil, only because of evil? And that the good, in itself and for its own sake, is useless?"

"It looks that way."

"Then the ultimate friend -- the endpoint where all other friendships lead -- is of a completely different nature from those other friendships. Those other things are called 'friends' only for the sake of some further friend. But the truly dear thing works in reverse: it's proved to be dear because of the hated, and if the hated were removed, it would no longer be dear."

"That seems right," he said. "At least if our current argument holds."

"But wait," I said. "Tell me this: if evil were to vanish entirely, would we stop feeling hungry? Or thirsty? Or having any desire at all? Or would hunger still exist as long as humans and animals exist -- just not in a harmful way? Would the same go for thirst and all other desires -- they'd still be there, but wouldn't be evil, because evil itself had disappeared? Or is it silly to even ask what would or wouldn't exist in a world so different from ours? I don't know. But this much I do know: right now, in our world, hunger can hurt us and it can also help us. True?"

"True."

"And the same for thirst, and every other desire -- sometimes good, sometimes bad, sometimes neither?"

"Exactly."

"But is there any reason why, just because evil vanishes, the things that aren't evil should vanish too?"

"No."

"Then even if evil disappeared, desires that are neither good nor evil would remain?"

"Obviously."

"And won't a person love and desire the thing he's longing for?"

"Of course."

"So even without evil, there would still be love and friendship?"

"Yes."

"But not if evil was the cause of friendship. In that case, once evil is destroyed, nothing can be anyone's friend -- because you can't have the effect without the cause."

"Right."

"And didn't we say earlier that the friend loves something for a reason? Back then, we thought the reason was that what's neither good nor evil loves the good because of evil."

"We did."

"But now our view has changed. There must be some other cause of friendship."

"It seems so."

"Maybe the truth is what we said a moment ago: desire is the cause of friendship. What desires is dear to what it desires, at the moment of desiring. And maybe that whole earlier theory was just a long story about nothing?"

"Could be."

"But surely," I said, "whoever desires, desires what he lacks?"

"Yes."

"And what he lacks is dear to him?"

"Of course."

"And he lacks what's been taken from him?"

"Yes."

"Then love, desire, and friendship are really about what naturally belongs to us -- what's akin to our own nature. That, Lysis and Menexenus, is what the argument seems to show."

They both agreed.

"So if you two are truly friends, you must have natures that are naturally akin to each other."

"Absolutely," they both said.

"And I'd say, boys, that nobody who loves or desires another person would ever feel that love or desire unless he were somehow akin to the beloved -- in soul, in character, in temperament, or in appearance."

"Yes, yes!" said Menexenus. But Lysis said nothing.

"Then the conclusion is this: what is naturally akin to us must be loved."

"It seems to follow," he said.

"So the true lover -- the genuine article, not the fake -- must of necessity be loved by his beloved."

Lysis and Menexenus gave a faint nod of agreement. And Hippothales -- Hippothales was turning every color of the rainbow with delight.

Now, wanting to reconsider the argument, I said, "But can we point to any real difference between 'what's akin' and 'what's alike'? Because if we can, then maybe, Lysis and Menexenus, our argument about friendship is getting somewhere. But if 'akin' just means 'alike,' we're back to our old problem: like is useless to like, precisely insofar as they're alike. And to say something useless is dear to us would be absurd. So suppose we agree -- in the dizzy excitement of argument -- that 'akin' and 'alike' are different things."

"All right."

"And shall we say that the good is akin to everything, while evil is alien to everything? Or that evil is akin to evil, good to good, and what's neither good nor evil to what's neither good nor evil?"

They chose the second option.

"Then, boys, we've stumbled right back into the same mistake we threw out before. The unjust will be the friend of the unjust, and the bad of the bad, just as much as the good of the good."

"That seems to be where we've ended up."

"But what if we say 'akin' is the same as 'good'? Then only the good can be friends with the good."

"But we already proved that doesn't work either. Remember?"

"We remember."

"Then what's left? What can we possibly say? I feel like one of those lawyers summing up for the jury: if the beloved is not a friend, and the lover is not a friend, and like is not a friend of like, and unlike is not a friend of unlike, and the good is not a friend, and the akin is not a friend -- and whatever else we've gone through, because there were so many I can't even keep track -- if none of these are friends, then I'm at a total loss."

I was on the verge of turning to one of the older people there, hoping for some help. But suddenly we were interrupted. The tutors of Lysis and Menexenus descended on us like some kind of evil apparition, brothers in tow, and told the boys to come home -- it was getting late. At first we tried to shoo them away, the bystanders and I. But they wouldn't budge. They just kept shouting in their rough foreign accents and getting more and more irritated and calling for the boys. They seemed to have had a bit too much to drink at the festival, which made them hard to deal with. So we finally gave in and broke up the gathering.

But as they were leaving, I managed to say a few words to the boys: "Well, Lysis and Menexenus, we've made ourselves pretty ridiculous today -- you two boys, and me, an old boy who'd love to count himself one of you. The bystanders are going to walk away saying: these people call themselves friends, but they couldn't even figure out what a friend is."
