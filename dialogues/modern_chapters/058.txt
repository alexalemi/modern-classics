The discussion continues along the road from Knossos to the cave of Zeus.


Athenian
If a herald or ambassador delivers a false message from our city to another, or brings back a false report from the city he was sent to, or if he's proved to have misrepresented — whether dealing with allies or enemies — what they actually said, he shall be charged with violating the sacred duties imposed on him by Hermes and Zeus, acting contrary to the law. And a penalty shall be set: if convicted, he'll pay or suffer whatever the court determines.

Theft is a low act, and robbery is outright shameless — and none of the sons of Zeus take pleasure in fraud or violence, or ever practiced either. So don't let anyone be misled by poets or mythmakers into thinking such behavior is acceptable. Don't let anyone suppose that when he steals or robs, he's just doing what the gods themselves do. Those stories aren't true, and they aren't plausible. Anyone who steals or robs in violation of the law is never a god or a god's child — the lawmaker should know that better than all the poets put together. Happy is the person — and may they always be happy — who listens to these words and takes them to heart. But whoever disobeys will have to contend with the following law:

If someone steals public property, whether the amount is large or small, the punishment shall be the same. Because the person who steals a little has the same intent as someone who steals a lot — just less ability to pull it off. And the person who takes a larger sum that wasn't theirs is completely unjust. The law isn't inclined to go lighter on one than the other just because one stole less. Rather, the distinction is this: one thief may still be fixable, and another may not be.

If a foreigner or a slave is convicted in court of stealing public property, the court shall determine the appropriate punishment or fine, keeping in mind that the offender is probably still capable of being reformed. But the citizen who was raised the way our citizens will be raised — if he's found guilty of robbing his own country through fraud or force, whether caught in the act or not — he shall be punished with death. He's beyond saving.

Now, when it comes to military campaigns, a great deal of thought and many laws are needed. The overriding principle is this: no one, male or female, should ever be without a commander. No one's mind should get accustomed to doing anything on its own initiative, whether in jest or in earnest. In war and in peace alike, every person should look to their leader and follow orders — being guided even in the smallest things. When told to stand, stand. When told to move, move. Exercise, wash, eat meals, get up in the night for guard duty or to carry messages — all when ordered. In the heat of battle, don't pursue and don't retreat except on your commander's orders. In short, train the mind never to know how to act apart from others.

The soldier's life should be, in all things and at all times, as communal and collective as possible. There never has been, and never will be, a higher or better or more effective principle than this for achieving success and survival in war. And we should practice this habit in peacetime too, from youth onward — the habit of commanding others and being commanded by them. Anarchy should have no place in human life, or in the life of any animal under human control.

I'd add that all dances should be performed with an eye toward military fitness. Agility and flexibility should be cultivated for the same purpose, along with endurance of hunger and thirst, winter cold and summer heat, and sleeping on hard ground. And above all, we should be careful not to ruin the natural qualities of the head and feet by wrapping them in unnecessary coverings, stunting the natural growth of hair and the toughening of the soles. These are the body's extremes, and whether they're kept strong or not makes a huge difference — the feet serve the whole body, and the head commands it, since that's where all the governing senses are naturally housed.

Let our young men take what I've just said as a eulogy of the military life. Here's the law itself:

Anyone whose name is on the military roster, or who's been assigned to special service, shall serve. If anyone is absent due to cowardice, without leave from the generals, he shall be charged before the military commanders with failure to serve when the army returns home. His fellow soldiers shall be his judges — heavy infantry judged by heavy infantry, cavalry by cavalry, and each branch by its own peers. Anyone found guilty shall never be allowed to compete for prizes of valor, or to bring charges against anyone else for failing to serve, or to act as an accuser in any military matter whatsoever. The court shall also determine whatever additional punishment he deserves.

When the trials for failure to serve are done, the leaders of each branch shall hold another assembly to award prizes of valor. Anyone who wants to can seek recognition within his own branch of service, speaking only about the current campaign — no references to previous expeditions, no outside evidence or witnesses, just the matter at hand. The crown of victory shall be an olive wreath, which the winner shall dedicate at the temple of whatever war-god he chooses, with an inscription that will last his lifetime recording that he received first, second, or third prize.

If anyone goes on campaign but comes home before the appointed time — when the generals haven't withdrawn the army — he shall be charged with desertion before the same judges who handle failure-to-serve cases. If convicted, the same punishment applies.

Now, anyone involved in a legal case should be extremely careful about bearing false witness, whether intentionally or not, whenever it's in their power to avoid it. Justice is rightly called an honorable maiden, and falsehood is naturally repugnant to honor and justice. A witness needs to be especially careful not to offend justice in cases involving the throwing away of arms — he has to distinguish between dropping your weapons out of genuine necessity and doing it out of cowardice, and not make the former a disgrace or bring charges against innocent people on that account.

Making this distinction can be difficult, but the law has to try to draw the line somehow. Let me illustrate with an old story: if Patroclus had been brought back to the tent still alive but without his armor (and that kind of thing has happened countless times), with the original arms — the ones the gods gave to Peleus as a wedding gift when he married Thetis — still in Hector's hands, then the petty spirits of that era might have accused Menoetius' son of throwing away his arms. And then there are cases of people who've been hurled off cliffs and lost their weapons, or people at sea in rough waters who've been suddenly overwhelmed by floods — there are endless situations like this that could be cited to excuse what's really just bad luck that's easily misrepresented.

So we have to do our best to separate the truly serious offense from the lesser one. And there's a useful distinction in the language of reproach itself: a person doesn't always deserve to be called someone who "threw away his shield." He might simply be someone who "lost his weapons." There's a vast — really an absolute — difference between a person who's stripped of his arms by overwhelming force and one who voluntarily lets his shield go.

Here's the law: if a person carrying arms is overtaken by the enemy and doesn't turn to fight, but instead voluntarily drops or throws away his weapons — choosing a shameful escape and a quick run over a courageous, noble, and blessed death — in such a case of throwing away arms, justice shall be done. But the judge shouldn't concern himself with the case I just described, where the loss was unavoidable. Bad men should always be punished, in the hope they might be reformed. But there's no point in punishing the merely unfortunate.

And what punishment fits the person who threw away his weapons of defense? There's a legend that Caeneus of Thessaly was changed by a god from a woman into a man. It'd be poetic justice if we could work the reverse miracle on someone who drops his shield — but since that's impossible, let's make a law that comes as close to it as we can. The man who loves his own life too much shall spend the rest of his days in no danger whatsoever, living forever under the stigma of cowardice.

Here's the law in formal terms: anyone found guilty of disgracefully throwing away his arms in war — no general or military officer shall allow him to serve as a soldier or give him any position in the ranks. Any officer who assigns the coward a post shall be fined by the public examiner: a thousand drachmas if he's in the highest property class, five minas for the second, three minas for the third, one mina for the fourth. And the person found guilty of cowardice shall not only be dismissed from the manly dangers of war — a disgrace fitting his nature — but shall also pay: a thousand drachmas for the highest class, five minas for the second, three for the third, and one mina for the fourth.

Now, what about public auditors? We have magistrates who are elected by lot for a year, and others chosen from select candidates for longer terms. Who's going to be a competent examiner of these officials — what if one of them, crushed by the demands of office or by his own inability to carry the role with dignity, goes crooked?

It's extremely hard to find a magistrate who's better than other magistrates in virtue, but we still have to try to find some examiner or auditor who's better than the rest — almost superhuman. Because the truth is, a state has many potential points of failure, just like a ship or a living body. They all have their ropes, their supports, their sinews — one vital force running through many parts, called by many names. And the office of public examiner is one of the most critical factors in a state's survival or collapse. If the examiners are better people than the magistrates, and they do their job fairly and impartially, then the whole state and country flourishes. But if the examination of magistrates is conducted badly, then the justice that holds every institution together starts to unravel. Every power in the state pulls away from every other, they stop pulling in the same direction, and the city fills with factions — turning one city into many — and before long, everything is destroyed.

That's why our examiners must be outstanding in every kind of virtue. Let's design a way to create them:

Every year, after the summer solstice, the entire city shall gather in the shared precinct of Helios and Apollo and present to the god three men from their own number, chosen as follows. Each citizen shall nominate — not himself, but another citizen he considers the very best in every way, who is at least fifty years old. From those who receive the most votes, they shall make a further cut, reducing the field to half if the total is even. If the total is odd, they drop the person with the fewest votes to make it even, then keep the half with more votes. If two candidates are tied and this pushes the number past half, they drop the younger of the two. They keep voting until three candidates remain with unequal vote counts. But if all three, or two of the three, are tied, they'll leave it to fate and fortune, separating first, second, and third place by lot.

These three shall be crowned with olive wreaths and given the prize of excellence, with a public proclamation that the city of the Magnetes, by the gods' providence, is once again preserved, and presents to the Sun and to Apollo its three best men as an offering, a shared gift to both gods in accordance with ancient custom — for as long as their lives prove worthy of the honor.

In their first year, these three shall appoint twelve examiners, who serve until each reaches seventy-five years of age. After that, three new examiners shall be added each year. These examiners shall divide all magistracies into twelve categories and test the holders of each by every kind of scrutiny appropriate for a free person. While in office, the examiners shall live in the precinct of Helios and Apollo where they were chosen. Each examiner shall form judgments on some matters individually and on others together with his colleagues. He shall post a written report in the marketplace about each magistracy, detailing what punishment or fine the magistrate deserves according to the examiners' decision.

If a magistrate doesn't accept that the judgment against him was fair, he can bring the examiners before the select judges. If acquitted by their decision, he may — if he wishes — turn around and prosecute the examiners themselves. But if he's convicted and the examiners have sentenced him to death, then he dies — you can only die once, after all. For any other penalties that can be doubled, he'll pay twice.

Now let's consider the examiners themselves — what will their own review look like?

During their lifetimes, these men whom the whole city considers worthy of virtue's highest rewards shall have the first seat at all public assemblies, at all Greek religious festivals and sacred delegations, and at every public and holy ceremony they attend. The leaders of each sacred mission shall be chosen from among them. They alone of all citizens shall wear the laurel crown. They shall all be priests of Apollo and Helios, and one of them — judged the foremost among the priests appointed that year — shall be high priest. His name shall be recorded each year to serve as a marker of time for as long as the city endures.

After their deaths, they shall be laid out and carried to their graves in a manner different from all other citizens. They shall be dressed in robes entirely of white, and there shall be no weeping or mourning. Instead, a chorus of fifteen girls on one side of the bier and fifteen boys on the other shall sing hymns to the departed priests in alternating verses, proclaiming their blessedness in song throughout the entire day.

At dawn, a hundred of the finest young athletes — chosen by the deceased's family — shall carry the bier to the tomb. The young men shall march in front, dressed for war: cavalry with their horses, heavy infantry in full armor, and each branch in its proper gear. Boys shall walk near the bier and before it, singing the national hymn. Girls shall follow behind, and after them, women past the age of childbearing. Then — although priests and priestesses are normally forbidden from attending other burials — let them follow this one, unless the oracle at Delphi forbids it. This burial carries no pollution.

The place of burial shall be an underground chamber, vaulted and oblong, built of tufa stone that will last forever, with stone couches set side by side. There they'll lay the blessed person, cover the tomb with a circular mound of earth, and plant a grove of trees all around except on one side, where the burial ground can be extended indefinitely without needing a new mound.

Every year, the city shall hold contests in their honor — music, athletics, and horsemanship — in tribute to the dead.

These are the honors for examiners who pass their own review without fault. But if any of them — trusting that the hard part is over — later reveals the common wickedness of human nature, here's what happens: anyone who wants can bring charges against him. The case shall be tried before a court composed of the guardians of the law, the surviving examiners, and the court of select judges. The accuser shall state that so-and-so is unworthy of his prize of virtue and his office. If convicted, the examiner shall be stripped of his office, his special burial rights, and all the other honors he was given. But if the accuser fails to win even a fifth of the votes, he pays a fine: twelve minas for a first-class citizen, eight for second, six for third, two minas for fourth.

The so-called judgment of Rhadamanthus is worth real admiration. He knew that the people of his time genuinely believed in the gods — and that was reasonable, since most men in those days were sons of gods, and tradition says he was one himself. He seems to have decided that judgments shouldn't be left to men at all, but only to the gods. So he had both parties swear oaths on the points in dispute, and that settled matters quickly and reliably.

But things are different now. A certain number of people don't believe in the gods at all. Others figure the gods don't care about human affairs. And the opinion of the worst sort of people is that with a small sacrifice and a few flattering words, the gods will help them get away with large-scale theft and shield them from terrible punishments. The method of Rhadamanthus doesn't suit the needs of justice anymore. People's beliefs about the gods have changed, and the laws need to change with them.

When it comes to lawsuits, a rational system should eliminate oaths from both parties. The person bringing a suit should put the charges in writing, but shouldn't add an oath. The defendant should likewise submit a written denial to the magistrates, without swearing. Because it's a terrible thing to realize — when you've got many lawsuits going on in a city — that nearly half the people you meet at public dinners and in everyday social life have committed perjury, and don't even seem bothered by it.

So here's the law: a judge who's about to render a verdict shall take an oath. Anyone selecting magistrates for the state shall vote either under oath or with a voting tablet brought from a temple. Similarly, judges of dance and music, officials overseeing athletic and equestrian contests — wherever, as far as human judgment can tell, there's nothing to be gained from a false oath. But in all cases where a sworn denial clearly gives the oath-taker a major advantage, the case shall be decided without the parties' oaths. The presiding judges shall not let either side use oaths to persuade, or call down curses on themselves and their families, or resort to undignified pleading and emotional scenes. Instead, they shall teach and learn what justice requires, using fitting language. Whoever speaks otherwise shall be considered off-topic, and the judges shall bring him back to the matter at hand.

On the other hand, foreigners dealing with other foreigners shall continue to have the power to give and receive oaths, as they do now. They won't often grow old in our city or leave behind a brood of young ones to become its heirs.

As for the process of initiating private suits: the procedure for deciding cases between citizens shall be the same as in cases where a free person is disobedient to the state in minor matters — the kind that don't warrant flogging, imprisonment, or death. But regarding attendance at choruses, processions, and other public events, and regarding public duties — whether celebrating sacrifices in peacetime or paying contributions in wartime — in all these cases, the first priority is providing a remedy for the loss. Those who refuse to comply shall put up security with the officers whom the city and the law empower to collect what's owed. If they forfeit their security, let the pledged goods be sold and the money given to the city. If they owe more than that, the relevant magistrates shall impose an appropriate penalty and bring them before the court until they're willing to do as they're told.

Now, a state that makes its money entirely from agriculture and has no foreign trade needs to think carefully about its people traveling abroad and about receiving visitors from elsewhere. The lawmaker needs to consider these matters, and he should start by trying to persuade rather than compel.

Contact between cities tends to create a jumble of customs. Foreigners are always introducing novelties to other foreigners. When states are well-governed under good laws, this mixing causes enormous harm. But since most cities are the opposite of well-ordered, the confusion that comes from receiving visitors and from their own citizens rushing off to other places — when anyone, young or old, wants to travel abroad for whatever reason — doesn't really matter, because things are already a mess.

On the other hand, flatly refusing to admit foreigners, and never letting your own citizens go anywhere else, is completely impractical. And to the rest of the world it looks harsh and uncivilized — the kind of policy associated with ugly words like "xenelasia," the expulsion of foreigners, and with harsh, unfriendly attitudes. Whether or not the rest of the world thinks well of you is no small matter. The majority of people may be far from virtuous themselves, but they're not bad at recognizing who else is good and who isn't. Even wicked people have a kind of instinct that rightly sizes others up, and many thoroughly corrupt individuals form accurate judgments about the difference between good and bad people.

That's why most cities rightly encourage us to value a good reputation abroad. And there's no greater or more important truth than this: whoever is truly good — and I'm talking about someone who would be genuinely excellent — should seek a good reputation, but not without the reality of goodness to back it up. Our Cretan colony should likewise earn the finest and noblest reputation for virtue from the rest of the world. And there's every reason to expect that if the reality lives up to the idea, this city will be one of the few well-ordered states that the sun and the other gods look upon with approval.

So here's what we'll establish regarding travel abroad and receiving visitors:

First, no one under forty shall be permitted to travel to a foreign country at all. And no one shall go in a private capacity — only on some official mission: as a herald, an ambassador, or a member of a sacred delegation. Military campaigns and wartime service don't count as the kind of travel we're regulating here.

Citizens should be sent to the festivals of Apollo at Delphi, Zeus at Olympia, and to Nemea and the Isthmus, to take part in the sacrifices and games dedicated to the gods. They should send as many as possible, and the best and finest people they can find. These representatives will make the city famous at sacred gatherings in peacetime — earning a kind of glory that's the mirror image of what's won in war. And when they come home, they'll teach the young that the institutions of other states are inferior to their own.

They should also send another kind of observer, if the guardians approve: citizens who want to take a more leisurely look at how other people do things. No law should prevent this. Because a city that has no experience with good and bad people, and no contact with them, can never be truly civilized. And citizens can't properly uphold the laws through mere habit alone, without genuinely understanding them.

The truth is, there are always a few genuinely inspired people in the world — people whose company is beyond price — and they turn up just as often in badly governed cities as in well-governed ones. Citizens of a well-ordered state should always be seeking these people out, traveling by land and sea to find the one who is incorruptible — so they can strengthen the good institutions they already have and fix what's lacking. Without this kind of investigation and inquiry, no city will ever remain perfect. And it won't work if the investigation is done badly, either.

Cleinias
How can we have both an investigation and a good one?

Athenian
Like this: our observer shall be at least fifty years old. He must be a man of distinguished reputation, especially in military matters, if he's going to represent the guardians of the law to other cities. But once he's past sixty, he shall no longer continue in this role.

When he's carried out his observations for however many years he chooses during his ten-year term, he shall come home and present himself to the assembly that reviews the laws.

This shall be a mixed body of younger and older members, and they'll be required to meet every day between dawn and sunrise. The assembly shall consist of, first, the priests who have earned the rewards of virtue; second, the ten eldest guardians of the law; and third, the current superintendent of education, along with all the previous holders of that office. Each member shall bring along as a companion a young man of his own choosing, between thirty and forty years old.

This assembly shall be in constant conversation and discussion about the laws of their own city, about any especially good laws they hear about from elsewhere, and about branches of knowledge that seem useful — the kind that shed light on the examination of laws and without which the subject becomes murky and uncertain. Whatever knowledge the elders approve of, the younger members shall study with complete dedication. If any of the invited young men proves unworthy, the whole assembly shall blame the person who invited him. The rest of the city shall keep watch over the young men who distinguish themselves in this assembly — honoring them especially if they succeed, but holding them in even lower regard than others if they turn out to be inferior.

This is the assembly that the returning observer shall report to immediately upon coming home. If he's discovered anyone who has something valuable to say about the making of laws, education, or upbringing — or if he's made observations of his own — he shall share them with the whole assembly.

If he comes home neither better nor worse than when he left, he should at least be praised for his dedication. If he comes home significantly improved, he deserves even more praise. And not only while he lives — after his death, the assembly shall honor him with fitting tributes.

But if on his return he appears to have been corrupted — if he pretends to be wise when he isn't — then he shall have no contact with anyone, young or old. If he listens to the authorities and keeps to himself, he may live as a private citizen. But if he won't, and if he's convicted in court of meddling with education and the laws, let him die. And if he deserves to be indicted but none of the magistrates indicts him, that shall count as a mark against those magistrates when the rewards of virtue are being decided.

That's the kind of person who should go abroad, and those are the conditions. Now let's talk about receiving foreigners. There are four kinds of visitors we need to address.

The first kind is the seasonal trader — like a migratory bird — who shows up in summer, flies across the sea chasing commerce as long as the weather holds, and then moves on. These people shall be received in marketplaces and harbors and public buildings near the city but outside it, supervised by the magistrates appointed for that purpose. Those magistrates shall ensure that every foreign visitor receives fair treatment, but the visitor shall not be allowed to introduce any innovations. Official dealings with him shall be kept to the necessary minimum.

The second kind is a genuine sightseer — someone who comes to see and hear the festivals of the Muses. Hospitable accommodations should be provided for them at the temples, and the priests and temple staff should look after them. But they shouldn't stay longer than a reasonable time. Let them see and hear what they came for, and then let them go, having neither suffered nor caused any harm. If any dispute arises involving them — up to fifty drachmas — the priests shall be the judges. For anything larger, the case goes before the market wardens.

The third kind is someone who comes on official business from another country. He shall be received with public honors, hosted exclusively by the generals and cavalry commanders, and the person who entertains him — along with the city council — shall have sole responsibility for his care.

The fourth kind corresponds to our own observers — a visitor from another land who comes to study ours. These visits will be rare, and the visitor should be at least fifty years old. He may be coming to see something remarkable in other states, or to show something equally remarkable from his own. Let such a person go uninvited to the doors of the wise and the distinguished — being such a person himself. Let him go, for example, to the house of the superintendent of education, confident that he's a fitting guest for such a host. Or let him visit the homes of those who've won the prize of virtue, to exchange ideas — learning from them and teaching them in turn. When he's seen and heard everything, he shall depart as a friend taking leave of friends, and they shall honor him with gifts and appropriate marks of respect.

These are the customs by which our city should receive all visitors of either sex from other countries, and should send forth its own citizens — showing respect to Zeus, the god of hospitality. We won't forbid foreigners from our meals and sacrifices, the way they do in Egypt, and we won't drive them away with brutal proclamations.

When someone acts as a surety, let him formalize the arrangement clearly in a written document, in the presence of at least three witnesses if the amount is under a thousand drachmas, and at least five witnesses if it's above a thousand. The agent of a dishonest or untrustworthy seller shall be personally responsible — both agent and principal shall be equally liable.

If someone wants to search for something in another person's house, he shall enter wearing nothing but a short tunic, without a belt, after first swearing by the customary gods that he expects to find the item there. Then he may conduct his search, and the homeowner shall open the house and allow him to search everything, both sealed and unsealed.

If the homeowner won't allow the search, the person who was prevented shall take him to court, estimating the value of what he's looking for. If the homeowner is convicted, he pays double the item's value.

If the homeowner happens to be away, the people living in the house shall let the searcher examine the unsealed property. On the sealed items, the searcher shall place an additional seal and appoint whoever he likes to guard them for five days. If the homeowner is away longer than that, the searcher shall bring the city wardens with him, open the sealed property as well, and then — together with the household members and the wardens — seal everything back up as it was.

There shall be time limits on disputed property, after which the possessor can no longer be challenged.

Houses and land can't be disputed in our state. But for other property: if someone has been openly using and displaying items in the city, the marketplace, or the temples, and no one has claimed them — and someone later says they were searching for those items the whole time while the possessor made no attempt to hide them — if this goes on for a year, the seeker loses his right to claim after the year is up. If the items aren't shown in the marketplace or the city but only in the countryside, and no owner comes forward within five years, the claim is barred forever. If the items are used in the city but kept inside the house, the time limit is three years — or ten years if they're on private property in the country. And if someone has the items in a foreign country, there's no time limit at all. The owner can claim them whenever he finds them.

If anyone forcibly prevents another person from being present at a trial — whether that person is a principal party or a witness — and the person prevented is a slave (whether his own or someone else's), the case shall be declared incomplete and invalid. But if the person prevented is a free citizen, besides the case being invalid, the one who prevented him shall be imprisoned for a year and can be prosecuted for kidnapping by anyone who wants to bring the charge.

If anyone forcibly prevents a rival competitor from being present at an athletic, musical, or any other kind of contest, the person who discovers this shall inform the presiding judges, who shall ensure the would-be competitor is free to participate. If they can't manage it, and the person who blocked the competitor wins the prize, then the prize of victory shall be awarded to the one who was blocked. He can have himself inscribed as the winner in whatever temples he chooses. The one who interfered shall not be allowed to make any dedication or inscription referencing that contest, and in any case he'll be liable for damages — whether he won or lost.

If anyone knowingly receives stolen property, he shall suffer the same punishment as the thief. If someone harbors an exile, the penalty is death.

Every person should regard the state's friends and enemies as their own. If anyone makes peace or war on his own account, without the state's authority, he too shall face the death penalty — just like the one who harbors an exile. And if any faction of the city declares war or peace independently, the generals shall indict the instigators, and if convicted, death shall be the penalty.

Those who serve their country should serve without accepting bribes. And we shouldn't make excuses or approve the saying that "you should take gifts as a reward for good deeds, just not for bad ones." Knowing which you're really doing, and standing by that knowledge, is no easy thing. The safest course is simply to obey the law that says: accept no bribes for services. Whoever disobeys and is convicted shall simply die.

For purposes of taxation, every citizen ought to have his property assessed. Each tribe's members should also file a register of annual income with the country wardens, so that there are two valuations available. Public officials can then use whichever assessment they think is better in any given year — whether they prefer to take a percentage of total value or of annual revenue, after deducting what's paid to the common dining tables.

Regarding offerings to the gods: a moderate person should show moderation in what he offers. The land itself and the hearth of every home are already sacred to all the gods, so no one should dedicate them to the gods a second time.

Gold and silver, whether in private hands or in temples, breed envy in other cities. Ivory, being the product of a dead body, isn't a proper offering. Iron and bronze are instruments of war. But offerings of wood are fine — let someone bring whatever he likes, as long as it's carved from a single block. Same with stone for public temples. For woven work, no one should offer more than one woman can produce in a month. White is the color that suits the gods, especially for woven goods. Dyes should only be used for military decorations.

The most divine gifts are birds and painted images, and they should be the kind a single artist can complete in a day. All other offerings should follow a similar rule of modesty.

Now that the entire city has been divided into its parts — the kind and number we've described — and we've established laws covering all the most important contracts, as far as we could, the next step is to see that justice is properly administered.

The first tier of courts shall consist of judges chosen jointly by the plaintiff and the defendant. These should really be called arbiters rather than judges. The second tier shall be the village and tribal courts corresponding to our twelve-part division of the land, where litigants go when the stakes are higher and the first arbiters couldn't settle things. The defendant, if he loses a second time, shall pay a fifth more than the original damages. If he still isn't satisfied and wants to try a third time, he can take the case to the select judges. If he loses there too, he pays the full damages plus half again as much.

For the plaintiff: if he loses before the first judges but presses on to the second court and wins, he gets the original damages plus an extra fifth. If he loses, he pays that same amount. If he's still not satisfied and insists on going to a third court, then if he wins he gets the damages plus half again. If he loses, he pays half the amount he was claiming.

As for the assignment of judges to courts by lot, filling out their numbers, appointing court officers, scheduling cases, managing votes and procedural delays, the order in which cases are heard, the deadlines for filing responses and appearing — all of these things we've already addressed. But there's no harm in saying what's right twice or even three times. All the smaller, easier procedural details that the senior lawmaker left out can be filled in by a younger colleague. Private courts will be adequately regulated this way.

For public and state courts, and the courts that magistrates use in administering their offices — many respectable systems already exist in other states, designed by good people. The guardians of the law can draw on these through careful study, adapting what's needed for our new state, testing things through experience, and adjusting until every detail seems right. Then they'll set the final seal on these procedures and make them permanent.

As for the silence that should be maintained by judges, the avoidance of ill-omened words and their opposites, and the different conceptions of justice, goodness, and honor that exist in our state compared with others — some of this has already been mentioned, and more will be said as we approach the end.

Anyone who wants to be a fair judge should keep all these things in view and should possess written texts about them, so he can study and learn. Of all forms of knowledge, the knowledge of good laws has the greatest power to improve the person who studies it. Otherwise there'd be no meaning in the fact that our divine and admirable law has a name related to "mind" — nomos and nous. And of all other words — praise and blame of individuals found in poetry and prose, whether written down or spoken in daily life, whether argued over contentiously or agreed with too easily — the one sure touchstone is the writings of the lawmaker. The righteous judge should carry these in his mind as an antidote to all other words. In this way he makes himself and the city stand upright: for the good, he ensures the continuance and growth of justice; for the bad, he works toward conversion — from ignorance, from lack of self-control, from every kind of wrongdoing — as far as their flawed minds can be healed.

But for those whose life's thread is truly finished — death is the only remedy for souls in their condition. And I'll say it again, as I've said before. Judges and chief judges who act on these principles will deserve the praise of the whole city.

When the year's lawsuits are completed, the following rules shall govern their enforcement: First, the judge shall assign to the winning party all the property of the losing party, except for bare necessities. This assignment shall be announced by a herald immediately after each decision, in the hearing of the judges. When the month following the court session arrives — unless the winner has already been paid — the court shall follow up on the case and hand over the loser's assets. If the loser doesn't have enough to pay, and the shortfall is at least one drachma, the insolvent person shall have no right to bring any lawsuit against anyone else until he's satisfied the winner's claim. Other people, however, can still bring suits against him.

If anyone who's been convicted refuses to acknowledge the authority of the court that convicted him, the magistrates whose authority is being flouted shall bring him before the court of the guardians of the law. If he's found guilty there, he shall be put to death as a subverter of the entire state and its laws.

And so a man is born and raised. He begets and raises his own children. He has his dealings with other people — suffers punishment when he does wrong, receives satisfaction when he's been wronged. And eventually, in the fullness of time, he grows old under the protection of the laws, and his end comes in the natural order.

Regarding funeral rites for the dead, of either sex: the religious ceremonies that are properly performed — whether they belong to the gods of the underworld or of this world — shall be determined by the interpreters, and their authority on this shall be absolute.

Burial sites must not be on land suited for farming. There shall be no large or small monuments on productive ground. The dead shall occupy that part of the country naturally suited for receiving and concealing bodies, with as little impact on the living as possible. No one — living or dead — shall deprive the living of the sustenance that their foster-mother, the earth, is naturally disposed to provide.

The burial mound shall be piled no higher than five men could build in five days. The headstone shall be no larger than what's needed to hold the dead person's eulogy in four lines of verse. The body shall not be laid out at home any longer than necessary to confirm the person is truly dead and not merely unconscious. Generally speaking, the third day after death is a reasonable time for carrying the body to the grave.

Now, we must believe the lawmaker when he tells us that the soul is in every way superior to the body, and that even in life, what makes each of us who we are is the soul alone. The body merely follows us around, shaped in our likeness. That's why it's quite right to say that the bodies of the dead are our images, our shadows — for the true and immortal being of each of us, which we call the soul, departs to give an account before other gods. That's an inspiring hope for the good, but terrifying for the wicked, as our ancestral traditions tell us.

Those traditions also say there isn't much you can do for someone after they're dead. The living — that's who everyone should be helping, all their kin. While a person is alive, help them be the holiest and most just they can be, so that after death they won't have serious offenses to answer for in the world below.

If that's true, then no one should waste money under the illusion that this lifeless mass of flesh being buried has anything to do with the real person. The real person — son, brother, beloved, whoever it is they think they're laying in the earth — has gone away to fulfill his own destiny. The duty of the living is to tend to the present, to spend moderately, and to treat the lifeless body as a fitting altar to the gods below. But moderation shouldn't be confused with stinginess.

Here's the law: total funeral expenses for someone of the highest property class shall not exceed five minas. For the second class, three minas. For the third, two minas. For the fourth, one mina. These are reasonable limits.

The guardians of the law should take special care of every stage of life — childhood, adulthood, every age. And at the very end, there should be one guardian of the law presiding over the funeral, chosen by the deceased's friends. It shall be to his credit if things are managed with fairness and moderation, and a mark against him if they aren't.

The laying out of the body and other ceremonies should follow custom. But when a statesman adopts custom as his guide, we have to allow him certain changes. It would be wrong, for example, to order anyone to weep or to refrain from weeping over the dead. But he may forbid wailing, and he may require that the voice of mourning not be heard outside the house. He may forbid bringing the body into the open streets and processing through them with mourners. And he may require that the funeral party be outside the city before daybreak.

These are our laws on these matters. Whoever obeys is free from penalty. Whoever disobeys even one of the guardians of the law shall be punished by all of them with a suitable penalty.

Other forms of burial — and the denial of burial, which shall be refused to temple robbers, patricides, and the like — have already been addressed in earlier laws. So our work of legislation is very nearly complete.

But in every endeavor, the end doesn't consist in simply doing something, acquiring something, or establishing something. The real end is reached only when we've provided for the perfect and lasting continuation of what we've built. Until we do that, our creation remains unfinished.
