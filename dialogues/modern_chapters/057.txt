Laws — Book 11

Athenian
Next, we need to properly regulate how people deal with each other in everyday life. The principle is actually very simple: don't touch what's mine, and don't take even the smallest thing that belongs to me without my permission. And may I be wise enough to treat others the way I'd want them to treat me. Let's start with buried treasure. I should never pray to the gods to find treasure that someone else buried for themselves and their family — someone who wasn't one of my own ancestors. And if I do stumble across such a treasure, I shouldn't dig it up. I should never have dealings with those so-called fortune-tellers who advise me to take what was entrusted to the earth, because the gain to my wallet wouldn't come close to matching what I'd lose in justice and virtue of soul. Justice in the soul is a better possession, in a better part of myself, than any amount of money in the bank. There's an old saying that applies to many things — "Don't move what shouldn't be moved" — and this is one of them. And we'd do well to believe the common tradition that says people who do such things end up without descendants.

Now, as for someone who doesn't care about having children and couldn't care less about the lawgiver — someone who digs up what neither he nor any ancestor of his buried, without the permission of the person who put it there, violating the simplest and most noble of laws, a rule laid down by no ordinary man: "Don't pick up what you didn't put down" — well, what this person deserves from the gods, only god knows. But here's what I'd have happen in practice: the first person who sees him should report him to the wardens of the city, if it happened in the city; to the wardens of the agora, if it happened in the marketplace; or to the wardens of the country and their commanders, if it happened in the countryside. Once the report is filed, the city shall send to Delphi, and whatever the oracle says about the money and the person who took it, the city shall carry out. If the informer is a free person, he'll earn the honor of having done the right thing. If he's a free person who fails to report it, he'll earn the dishonor of having done the wrong thing. If the informer is a slave, the state shall free him and pay his master the purchase price. But if a slave knows and doesn't report it, he'll be punished with death.

The same principle applies, in matters great and small, to lost property: if someone leaves something behind, whether on purpose or by accident, anyone who comes across it should leave it alone. These things are under the protection of the Goddess of the Roads, and the law dedicates them to her. But if someone defies this law and takes the property home — well, if the item is small and the person who takes it is a slave, any citizen at least thirty years old who catches him may give him a thorough beating. If the person is free, he'll be considered a mean-spirited law-breaker and shall pay ten times the value of what he took to the person who left it.

Now, if someone accuses another of possessing something that belongs to him — whether it's a large amount or small — and the other person admits he has the item but denies it belongs to the accuser, here's the procedure: if the property is registered with the magistrates according to law, the claimant shall summon the possessor, who must bring the item before the magistrates. If the public register shows who the rightful owner is, that person takes it and goes on his way. If the property is registered to someone who isn't present, anyone who posts sufficient bond on the absent owner's behalf can take the item as that person's representative. But if the property in question isn't registered with the magistrates at all, it shall stay in the custody of the three eldest magistrates until the case is tried. If the item is a living animal, the losing party shall pay the magistrates for its upkeep, and they shall settle the case within three days.

Anyone of sound mind may arrest his own slave and do with him whatever is lawful. He may also arrest the runaway slave of a friend or relative, for safekeeping. But if someone tries to take away a person who's being recaptured as a slave, claiming he wants to free him, the person doing the recapturing shall release the slave — provided the intervener posts three sufficient bonds. Only with those bonds may he take the slave away. If he takes the slave by any other means, he'll be guilty of assault, and if convicted, he'll pay double damages to the person deprived of the slave.

A person may also reclaim a freedman who doesn't show proper respect to the one who freed him. And here's what "proper respect" means: the freedman shall visit the hearth of his former master three times a month and offer to perform whatever services he reasonably can. He shall also agree to marry someone his former master approves of. He's not allowed to own more property than the person who freed him — anything over that amount belongs to his former master. A freedman can't remain in the state for more than twenty years. Like other foreigners, he must leave and take his property with him — unless he gets permission from both the magistrates and his former master to stay. If a freedman or any other foreigner accumulates property exceeding the census of the third class, he has thirty days from that point to pack up and leave. In this case, not even the magistrates can grant him permission to stay. If he disobeys and is brought to court and convicted, the punishment is death and confiscation of property.

Lawsuits on these matters shall be heard before the tribal courts, unless the plaintiff and defendant have already settled things through their neighbors or through privately chosen arbiters.

If someone claims that an animal or other piece of property belongs to him, the person in possession should point to the seller or to some honest, trustworthy person who legitimately transferred the property. If that person is a citizen or a resident alien living in the city, this must happen within thirty days. If the property was received from a foreigner, the timeframe is five months, with the middle month including the summer solstice.

When goods are bought and sold, the exchange — goods for payment — shall take place at a designated spot in the agora, and that's that. No buying or selling in any other location, and no credit. If an exchange happens elsewhere, or if the seller gives credit, he does so with the understanding that the law offers no protection for transactions conducted outside these rules. The same goes for voluntary contributions: anyone who likes may take up a collection among friends, but if any dispute arises about it, the law won't step in. That's his risk.

Anyone who sells goods worth more than fifty drachmas must stay in the city for ten days, and the buyer must be told where the seller lives. This is to handle the kind of complaints and returns that tend to come up. Here are the rules on returns:

If someone sells a slave who has tuberculosis, kidney stones, urinary disease, epilepsy, or some other chronic and incurable condition of body or mind that isn't obvious to a layperson — and the buyer is a doctor or trainer — there's no right of return. There's also no right of return if the seller disclosed the condition up front. But if a professional sells such a slave to a non-professional, the buyer can demand a return within six months — or within a year in the case of epilepsy. The case shall be decided by physicians agreed upon by both parties. If the seller loses, he pays double the sale price.

If one private person sells to another private person, there's still a right of return on the same terms, but the seller only has to refund the original price if he loses.

If someone sells a slave who's committed a homicide and both parties know about it, there's no right of return. But if the buyer doesn't know, he has the right of return whenever he discovers the truth. The case shall be decided by the five youngest guardians of the law. If the seller is found to have known, he must ritually purify the buyer's house according to the rules of the religious authorities, and pay back three times the purchase price.

When anyone exchanges money for money, or anything for anything else — whether living or not — the goods must be genuine and unadulterated, in accordance with the law.

And let's have a prelude on this whole subject of fraud, just as we've had preludes for our other laws. Everyone should recognize that adulteration belongs in the same category as lying and deceit. Now, plenty of people are fond of saying that lying and cheating are perfectly fine "at the right time and place." But they leave "the right time" and "the right place" completely vague, and this lack of precision causes enormous harm to themselves and others. A lawgiver can't afford to leave these things undefined — he needs to draw a clear line.

Here's the rule: no one shall invoke the gods to back up something false, deceitful, or dishonest — unless he wants to be the most hateful person in their eyes. And who's most hateful to the gods? First, the person who swears a false oath without any fear of them. Second, the person who tells lies in the presence of his superiors. Better people are the superiors of worse people; elders are the superiors of the young; parents are the superiors of children; men are the superiors of women and children; and rulers are the superiors of those they govern. Everyone should show reverence to those in positions of authority, especially those holding state office.

This is exactly why I'm bringing it up. Every person guilty of selling adulterated goods in the marketplace is telling a lie and committing fraud. And when he invokes the gods — swearing by the customs and cautions of the marketplace wardens — he's swearing with no respect for god or man. It's an excellent practice not to take the gods' names lightly, the way most people do, caring nothing about piety or purity in their religious dealings.

But if someone won't follow that excellent practice, here's the law: anyone who sells goods in the agora shall name one price and one price only. If he doesn't get it, he takes his goods home. On that day, he shall not raise or lower the price. And there shall be no puffing up of merchandise and no oath-swearing about it. If someone disobeys this rule, any citizen at least thirty years old may, without penalty, give the oath-swearer a beating. But if a citizen witnesses this and does nothing, he'll be liable for betraying the laws.

If someone sells adulterated goods and refuses to comply with these rules, anyone who can prove it in front of the magistrates — if the informer is a slave or resident alien — shall receive the adulterated goods as a reward. If the informer is a citizen and fails to pursue the charge, he'll be called a scoundrel and deemed to have robbed the gods of the marketplace. But if a citizen does prove the charge, he shall dedicate the confiscated goods to the gods of the agora.

Anyone proved to have sold adulterated goods shall not only lose the goods themselves but shall also be beaten — one lash for every drachma of the goods' price. A herald shall publicly announce in the agora what offense he's being beaten for. The wardens of the agora and the guardians of the law shall consult with experts about the various kinds of fraud and adulteration sellers commit. They shall write up clear rules about what sellers must and must not do, and inscribe these rules on a column in front of the court of the agora wardens, so that anyone doing business in the marketplace has clear guidance. We've said enough already about the wardens of the city — if anything's been left out, they should work with the guardians of the law to fill in the gaps and inscribe both the primary and secondary regulations on a column at the court of the city wardens.

After adulteration, retail trade naturally comes next. Let's start with a word of reason before we get to the law itself. Retail trade, by its nature, isn't meant to do harm — quite the opposite. Isn't someone who smooths out the inequalities in the distribution of goods actually a public benefactor? That's exactly what money does, and you could say that's what merchants are appointed to do. Innkeepers, hired workers, and all sorts of other occupations — some more respectable than others — all serve the same purpose: satisfying our needs and evening out our possessions. So let's try to understand what's given retail trade such a bad name, and why people consider it dishonorable and unseemly. If we can figure that out, we can at least partially fix the problem through legislation. Though I'll warn you — it's not easy, and it takes a great deal of virtue.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
My dear Cleinias, only a very small number of people — people who must be exceptionally gifted by nature and well-trained by education — can resist temptation when their desires press in on them, hold to moderation, and choose a reasonable profit over an enormous one. The vast majority of people are the exact opposite: their desires have no limits, and when they could make a decent living, they'd rather chase unlimited profits. That's why everything connected with retail trade, commerce, and innkeeping gets lumped together as dishonorable.

Here's a thought experiment — and I know it sounds ridiculous, but bear with me. Suppose we could somehow force the very best people in society to run taverns for a while, or to work in retail trade, or to do anything of the sort. Or suppose some twist of fate compelled the finest women to do the same. Then we'd see just how pleasant and respectable these occupations really are. If they were run on honest principles, we'd honor them the way we honor a mother or a nurse.

But here's what actually happens. Some man sets up shop in an isolated spot, reachable only by a long journey, where travelers are desperate for shelter. He takes in strangers who are battered by storms or roasting in the heat. He gives them shelter and rest. And then — instead of treating them like friends and showing them proper hospitality — he treats them like prisoners of war at his mercy, refusing to let them go until they've paid the most outrageous, extortionate ransom he can squeeze out of them. That's the kind of behavior that gives these professions such a terrible reputation. And a lawgiver should always be looking for remedies for evils like this.

There's an old saying — a true one: "It's hard to fight on two fronts." And that applies here too, because the war is against two enemies: wealth and poverty. Wealth corrupts the soul with luxury, and poverty drives people into total shamelessness through sheer desperation. What can a sensible city do about this disease? Three things. First, keep the number of retail traders as small as possible. Second, assign the occupation to the class of people whose moral corruption would cause the least harm to the state. Third, devise some system so that the people in these occupations don't easily fall into habits of unlimited greed and pettiness.

With that prelude, let our law run as follows — and may fortune be with us:

No landowner among the Magnetes — no one from the 5,040 households whose city the god is restoring — shall become a retail trader, voluntarily or involuntarily. No landowner shall be a merchant, or perform personal services for private individuals unless they provide equal service in return — the only exceptions being service to his father, mother, grandparents, or elders who are free persons, served in a manner worthy of a free person. Now, it's hard to define precisely what's worthy or unworthy of a free person, but let those who have won prizes for virtue make that judgment based on their sense of right and wrong. Anyone who engages in the pettiness of retail trade may be indicted for dishonoring his family, by any citizen who wishes, before the tribunal of those judged most virtuous. If he's found to be degrading his father's household through an unworthy occupation, he'll be imprisoned for a year and required to stop. For a second offense: two years. And every subsequent conviction doubles the sentence.

Second law: anyone who engages in retail trade must be either a resident alien or a foreigner.

Third law: to make sure the retail traders living in our city are as good — or as little bad — as possible, the guardians of the law must remember that they're not only responsible for watching over the wellborn and well-bred, who are relatively easy to keep in line. They should pay even more attention to people of a different sort, who follow occupations with a strong tendency to make them bad. So regarding the many varieties of retail trade — those that are allowed to continue because they're clearly necessary to the state — the guardians of the law should meet with people experienced in each type of trade, just as we directed earlier regarding adulteration, which is a related issue. In those meetings, they shall determine what level of revenue, after deducting expenses, will produce a moderate profit for the retail trader. They shall set in writing and strictly enforce what they find to be the right profit margin. This shall be overseen by the wardens of the agora, the wardens of the city, and the wardens of the country. Done properly, retail trade will benefit everyone while doing the least possible harm to those who practice it.

When someone makes an agreement and then doesn't follow through — unless the agreement was one that the law or the assembly doesn't allow, or was made under unjust pressure, or was prevented from being fulfilled by some unexpected circumstance beyond his control — the other party may take him to the tribal courts for breach of agreement, provided they haven't already settled things through arbiters or their neighbors.

The class of craftsmen who've furnished humanity with the arts is sacred to Hephaestus and Athena. And there's another class of craftsmen — those who protect the works of all craftsmen through the arts of defense — who are sacred to Ares and Athena. Both classes serve the country and the people throughout their lives. Some are military leaders; others produce tools and works for hire. They should never deceive in their business, out of respect for the gods who are their patrons.

If a craftsman fails through laziness to complete his work on time — not respecting the god who provides his livelihood, foolishly thinking he's his own god and will let himself off easy — well, first, the god will deal with him. And second, the law will follow suit. He'll owe the person who hired him the price of the work he failed to complete, and he'll have to redo the job for free within the original timeframe.

When a person undertakes a job, the law gives him the same advice we gave to sellers: don't try to charge more than what it's worth. Just ask a fair price. A craftsman knows the true value of his work, and in a free society, he shouldn't try to exploit laypeople by hiding behind his expertise. If someone is cheated in this way, he has a right of action against the person who cheated him.

And the reverse is equally true: if someone hires a craftsman and then doesn't pay him according to their lawful agreement, he's disrespecting Zeus, guardian of the city, and Athena, partner of the state. He's undermining the foundations of society for a little bit of savings. Let the law and the gods defend the bonds that hold our community together. If someone has received the completed work but doesn't pay within the agreed time, he shall pay double the price. And if a year goes by — although we don't allow interest on loans — for every drachma he owes the craftsman, he shall pay an additional obol per month in interest.

These suits shall be decided by the tribal courts. And since we've mentioned craftsmen, we shouldn't forget that other craft — the craft of war. Generals and military strategists are craftsmen too, who take on, voluntarily or not, the work of keeping us safe, just as other craftsmen take on other public projects. If they do their work well, the law will never tire of praising anyone who gives them the honors that brave soldiers justly deserve. But if someone has already benefited from noble military service and then fails to give those soldiers the honor they've earned, the law will condemn him.

So let this be the law, framed with an ingredient of praise: it doesn't compel but advises the great body of citizens to honor the brave men who are the saviors of the whole state, whether through their courage or their military expertise. They should honor them, I say, in second place. For the first and highest honor goes to those who are able, above all others, to honor the words of good lawgivers.

We've now regulated the greater part of dealings between people, with one major exception: orphans and the supervision of orphans by their guardians. These have to come next.

But to get there, we need to start with the testamentary wishes of the dying and the cases of people who happen to die without a will. When I said we need to regulate these things, Cleinias, I had in mind just how difficult and tangled these matters always are. You can't leave them unregulated, because individuals would make their own arrangements that clash with one another, contradict the laws and habits of the living, and even contradict their own prior wishes. If people were simply allowed to make any will they pleased, to take effect in whatever mental state they happened to be in at the end of their life — well, most of us lose our clarity of mind, in a way, and feel overwhelmed when we think we're about to die.

Cleinias
What do you mean, Stranger?

Athenian
Oh, Cleinias — a dying man is an impossible creature. He tends to say things that cause the lawgiver no end of anxiety and trouble.

Cleinias
In what way?

Athenian
He wants absolute control over every last piece of his property, and he'll use some pretty angry words to get it.

Cleinias
Like what?

Athenian
"By the gods!" he'll cry. "How monstrous that I'm not allowed to give my own property to whoever I want — or not give it! Less to those who've treated me badly, and more to those who've treated me well — people whose true character I've tested through sickness and old age and every kind of fortune!"

Cleinias
Well, Stranger, doesn't he have a fair point?

Athenian
In my opinion, Cleinias, the ancient lawgivers were too soft-hearted. They made their laws without paying enough attention to how human beings actually are.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
I mean they were afraid of the dying man's complaints, so they passed a law that let a person dispose of his property however he wished. But you and I — if I'm not mistaken — will have something better to say to our departing citizens.

Cleinias
What?

Athenian
"Dear friends," we'll say to them, "it's hard for you, creatures of a day, to know what's truly yours. It's hard, as the Delphic oracle says, to even know yourselves — especially at this hour. Now, I, as your lawgiver, don't regard you or your possessions as belonging entirely to you. They belong to your whole family, past and future. And even more than that, both your family and your possessions belong to the state. So if some flatterer comes along and catches you when you're tossed on the waves of illness or old age, and talks you into disposing of your property in a way that isn't for the best — I'm not going to let that happen, not if I can help it. I'll legislate with an eye to the whole picture, considering what's best for both the state and the family, rating an individual's feelings, as I should, at a lower value. And I hope you'll depart in peace and goodwill toward us, since you're going the way all humankind must go. We'll take impartial care of all your affairs, and we won't neglect a thing — not if we can possibly help it."

Let this be our prelude and our consolation to the living and the dying, Cleinias. And let the law be as follows:

A father who makes a will shall first name one of his sons as his heir — whichever one he thinks best. If he's giving any of his children to be adopted by another citizen, the adoption shall be recorded. If he has a son left over who hasn't been assigned to any household lot and who may be expected to be sent to a colony according to law, the father may give this son as much of his remaining property as he likes — except for the ancestral lot and the fixtures on it. If there are still more sons, he may divide whatever exceeds the lot among them in whatever proportions he chooses. But he shall not give money to a son who already has a household of his own, nor to a daughter who's been betrothed — though he may give money to an unbetrothed daughter. If any son or daughter later acquires another lot of land in the country after the will has been made, they shall leave their inherited lot to the original heir designated in the will.

If the testator has no sons but has daughters, he shall choose a husband for whichever daughter he pleases, designate that man as his son and heir, and record it. If a man has lost a son — his own or adopted — while the child was still young and hadn't yet reached manhood, the testator shall note the circumstance and name whomever he chooses as a second son, in hope of better fortune. If the testator has no children at all, he may give up to a tenth of his acquired property to anyone he likes. But he shouldn't be blamed if he leaves all the rest to his adopted son, making him a friend according to the law.

If a man's sons need guardians and he dies leaving a will that appoints them, those named guardians — whoever they are and however many — shall be recognized as guardians according to the will's provisions, provided they're able and willing to take on the job. But if he dies without a will, or with a will that names no guardians, then the next of kin — two from the father's side, two from the mother's side, and one friend of the deceased — shall serve as guardians, appointed by the guardians of the law whenever orphans need them. The fifteen eldest guardians of the law shall have overall care and responsibility for all orphans, divided into groups of three by seniority — one group of three serving for one year, then the next group the following year, cycling through all five groups. This rotation shall continue indefinitely.

If a man dies without any will at all and leaves sons who need guardians, they'll be protected by these same laws.

Now, if a man dies unexpectedly and leaves behind daughters, he should forgive the lawgiver if, in arranging their marriages, the lawgiver considers only two out of three conditions: closeness of kinship and preservation of the household lot — but leaves out the third condition that a father would naturally consider, which is choosing a husband based on character and disposition. The father should understand that this third consideration is, frankly, beyond the lawgiver's ability. So let the law on these matters be as follows, where practicable:

If a man dies without a will and leaves daughters behind, his brother — whether from the same father or the same mother — if he has no lot of his own, shall marry the daughter and take the dead man's lot. If there's no brother, but there's a brother's son, they shall marry, provided they're of suitable age. If there's no brother's son, but there's a sister's son, the same applies. In the fourth degree: the testator's father's brother. In the fifth: his father's brother's son. In the sixth: the child of his father's sister.

Kinship shall always be reckoned this way: when a person leaves daughters, the line of relationship proceeds upward through brothers and sisters, then their children — males first, then females in the same family. The judge shall assess whether the ages are suitable for marriage, inspecting the men unclothed and the women unclothed down to the waist.

If the family lacks kinsmen all the way out to grandchildren of a brother or grandchildren of a grandfather's children, the young woman may choose — with her guardians' consent — any willing citizen she wishes. That man becomes the heir of the deceased and the husband of his daughter. Circumstances vary, and sometimes there's an even greater shortage of relatives within the state. If a young woman has no living relatives in the city and there's someone who's been sent to a colony, she may make him heir to her father's estate if he's truly her kinsman — in which case he takes the lot according to the law. If he isn't her kinsman but is chosen by the daughter and approved by her guardians, he may return home and take the lot of the man who died without a will.

If a man dies without children of either sex and without a will, the previous law applies in general. A man and a woman from the family shall come forward and share the empty household, and the lot shall belong absolutely to them. The order of inheritance for the heiress: first degree, a sister; second, a brother's daughter; third, a sister's daughter; fourth, the father's sister; fifth, the daughter of the father's brother; sixth, the daughter of the father's sister. They shall live with their male kinsmen according to degree of relationship and right, as we established earlier.

Now, let's not kid ourselves — laws like these can be oppressive. Sometimes there's real hardship in the lawgiver commanding a dead man's relative to marry another relative. The lawgiver might seem not to have considered the countless obstacles that can arise. There may be cases where the parties refuse to obey and would do anything rather than marry, because there's some physical or mental illness or disability among those being told to marry. People might imagine the lawgiver never thought of this, but they'd be wrong.

So let's compose a shared prelude on behalf of both the lawgiver and his subjects. The law asks the subjects to forgive the lawgiver, because he has to look after the common good and can't simultaneously account for every individual circumstance. And the law asks the lawgiver to forgive the subjects, because sometimes they naturally can't fulfill a command that he, in his limited knowledge, imposes on them.

Cleinias
And how, Stranger, can we handle this most fairly?

Athenian
We need to appoint arbiters to deal with these laws and the people affected by them.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
Here's an example. A nephew whose father is rich might be unwilling to marry his uncle's daughter. He feels he can do better and wants to aim higher. Or there might be cases where the lawgiver is effectively imposing a terrible calamity on someone — requiring him to take a wife who's mentally ill, say, or who has some other awful condition of body or mind that makes life unbearable. So let's put this into law:

If anyone objects to the established laws on wills and inheritance — particularly regarding marriage — and claims that the lawgiver, if he were alive and present, wouldn't force them to obey; that is, wouldn't force those our law requires to marry or be given in marriage to actually do so — and some kinsman or guardian disputes this claim — then here's the answer: the lawgiver left fifteen of the guardians of the law to serve as arbiters and surrogate fathers for orphans of both sexes. The disputants should bring their case to them, and their decision shall be final.

But if anyone feels this gives too much power to the guardians of the law, he may take his adversaries to the court of select judges and have the disputed points settled there. The loser shall bear the censure and blame of the lawgiver — which, for anyone with sense, is a penalty far heavier than a large financial loss.

And so orphan children will receive, in a way, a second birth. After their first birth, we talked about how they'd be raised and educated. After their second birth — when they've lost their parents — we need to make sure the misfortune of being orphaned is as painless as possible. First, we declare that the guardians of the law are lawmakers and fathers to them — no less capable than their natural fathers. They shall take charge of them year by year as if they were their own family. We've already given suitable guidance to both them and the children's own guardians about how to raise orphans.

And I think we spoke well in our earlier discussion when we said that the souls of the dead have the power, after death, to take an interest in human affairs. There are many stories and traditions about this — long stories, but true ones. Since they're so numerous and so ancient, we should believe them. And we should believe the lawgivers who tell us these things are true — unless we're prepared to call them complete fools.

If these things really are so, then first, people should fear the gods above, who watch over the loneliness of orphans. Second, they should fear the souls of the departed, who by nature are especially attentive to their own children — kindly disposed toward those who honor them, hostile toward those who mistreat them. People should also fear the souls of the living who are old and honored: wherever a city is well-governed and prosperous, their descendants take good care of them and live happily as a result. Old people are quick to notice everything that concerns them. They're favorable to those who treat orphans justly, and they punish those who wrong the orphaned and the desolate, considering them the most sacred and important of all trusts.

Every guardian and magistrate with any brains should take all this to heart, paying careful attention to the upbringing and education of orphans, doing them every possible good — because in doing so, he's making a contribution to his own well-being and that of his children.

Anyone who heeds this prelude and does no wrong to an orphan will never experience the lawgiver's wrath. But anyone who disobeys and wrongs a child who's lost a father or mother shall pay twice the penalty he'd have paid for the same wrong done to a child whose parents were alive.

As for other legislation about guardians' duties toward orphans, or magistrates' oversight of guardians — the guardians already have the example of how they bring up their own freeborn children and manage their own property. They already have well-stated laws about exactly these things. There's no need to treat orphans as some special category requiring completely separate rules. As things stand, the condition of orphans in our state isn't fundamentally different from that of children with fathers — though in practice, the honor and attention given to each group aren't usually equal.

That's why the law on this subject speaks in the most serious tones, with both persuasion and threat. And here's a threat that's entirely appropriate:

Every guardian of an orphan of either sex, and every guardian of the law assigned to supervise that guardian, shall love the unfortunate orphan as if the child were his own. He shall be just as careful and diligent in managing the orphan's property as he would with his own — or even more so. Everyone who has charge of an orphan shall observe this law.

If a guardian violates it, the magistrate may fine him. If the offender is a magistrate himself, the guardian may bring him before the court of select judges, and if convicted, the fine shall be doubled. If a guardian appears — to the orphan's relatives or to any citizen — to be acting negligently or dishonestly, they may bring him before the same court. Whatever damages are awarded, he'll pay fourfold — half to the orphan, half to the person who brought the case. If an orphan comes of age and believes he was mistreated by his guardians, he has five years after the guardianship ends to bring them to trial. If any guardian is convicted, the court shall determine what he must pay or suffer. If a magistrate is found to have wronged the orphan through neglect and is convicted, the court shall determine what he must pay or suffer. If there was dishonesty in addition to neglect, he shall not only pay the fine but be removed from his office as guardian of the law, and the state shall appoint a replacement for both the city and the country.

Sometimes the conflicts between fathers and sons grow larger than they should. Fathers may feel the lawgiver should let them officially disown their sons — announcing it through a herald for all the world to hear. Sons may feel they should be allowed to charge their fathers with incompetence when disease or old age has taken its toll. These things really only happen when both parties have thoroughly bad natures. When only one side is bad — the father good but the son bad, or vice versa — no great disaster results from that level of conflict.

But in our state, unlike others, a disowned son doesn't simply remain a citizen. He must emigrate, because not a single household can be added to the 5,040. So someone who deserves this fate must be renounced not just by his father alone but by the entire family. Here's how the law should work:

If a father — in the sad turmoil of his soul, whether justly or unjustly — wants to expel a son he begot and raised, he can't do it hastily or on a whim. First, he must assemble his own relatives out to the cousin level, plus his son's relatives on the mother's side. Before all of them, he shall make his case that the son deserves to be expelled from the family. The son shall have an equal opportunity to address them and show that he doesn't deserve it.

If the father persuades them — and gets more than half the votes of all the adult family members of both sexes, not counting the father, the mother, and the accused son himself — then and only then may the father disown his son.

If any other citizen wants to adopt the disowned son, no law shall prevent it — young men's characters go through many changes over the course of their lives. But if ten years pass and nobody adopts him, those in charge of excess population destined for colonies shall see to it that he's properly placed in a colony.

And if disease, old age, or harshness of temperament — or all three together — makes a man more out of his mind than most people, but this is only visible to those who live with him, and he's ruining his household while still controlling the property — and his son is hesitant about charging him with insanity — then the law directs the son to first go to the eldest guardians of the law and tell them about his father's condition. They shall investigate thoroughly and advise whether or not to proceed. If they advise going forward, they'll serve as both his witnesses and his advocates. If the father is found incompetent, from that day on he'll have no power to manage the smallest detail of his life. He shall live in his house as a child for the rest of his days.

If a husband and wife are hopelessly incompatible, ten impartial guardians of the law and ten of the women who oversee marriages shall look into the matter. If they can reconcile the couple, the reconciliation is official. But if the couple's passions are too volatile, the committee shall try to find each of them a better-matched partner. These people aren't likely to have the mildest temperaments, so we should try to pair them with deeper, gentler natures. Couples who have no children — or very few — at the time of their separation should choose new partners with a view to having children. But couples who already have enough children should separate and remarry so they'll have someone to grow old with and take care of each other in their later years.

If a wife dies leaving children, male or female, the law will advise — but not compel — the husband to raise them without bringing in a stepmother. But if he has no children, he must remarry until he's fathered enough sons for both his family and the state.

If a husband dies leaving enough children, the mother shall stay with them and raise them. But if she seems too young to live virtuously without a husband, her relatives should consult with the women who oversee marriages, and together they should do what seems best. If there's a shortage of children, the new match should be made with an eye to having more. Two children — one of each sex — shall be considered sufficient in the eyes of the law.

When a child is acknowledged as the offspring of certain parents, but a decision must be made about which parent the child follows — here are the rules: if a female slave has a child with a male slave, or with a free man, or with a freedman, the child always belongs to the master of the female slave. If a free woman has a child with a male slave, the child belongs to the master of the slave. But if a child is born to a slave woman by her master, or to a master's wife by a slave — and this is proven — then the women who oversee marriages shall send away the woman's child along with its father to another country, and the guardians of the law shall send away the man's child along with its mother.

Neither god nor any person of understanding would ever advise anyone to neglect their parents. As a prelude to our discussion of honoring and dishonoring parents, let me offer this introduction about serving the gods.

There are ancient and universal customs regarding the gods, and they come in two kinds. Some gods we can see with our own eyes, and we honor them. Others we honor through their images — we set up statues and worship those. And even though these statues are lifeless, we believe the living gods feel goodwill and gratitude toward us on account of this devotion.

Now, if a man has a father or mother — or their parents — living in his house, old and frail, he should understand this: no statue is more powerful in granting his prayers than those elderly parents sitting right there at his hearth — if only he knows how to serve them properly.

And what do you call serving them properly?

Athenian
I'll tell you, my friend — this is worth hearing.

Cleinias

Athenian
The stories tell us that Oedipus, when his sons dishonored him, called down curses on them — curses that everyone agrees the gods heard and fulfilled. Amyntor in his rage cursed his son Phoenix. Theseus cursed Hippolytus. Countless others have called down the gods' wrath on their children. And this makes one thing clear: the gods listen to the prayers of parents. The curses of parents against their children are more powerful than any others, as is only right.

So consider: if the gods hear a father's or mother's prayers when they've been dishonored, and rain down punishment accordingly — shouldn't we suppose they also hear a parent who's been honored, and who joyfully prays to the gods for blessings on their children? If the gods answered curses but ignored blessings, they'd be very unfair distributors of good — and we've said that's contrary to their nature.

As I was saying, there's no image more honored by the gods than a living father or grandfather, or a mother grown old. When a person honors them, the god's heart rejoices and he's ready to answer their prayers. Truly, the figure of a living ancestor is a marvelous thing — far more powerful than any lifeless statue. The living, when we honor them, join their prayers to ours. When we dishonor them, they curse us. Statues do neither. So if a person treats his father, grandfather, and other elderly relatives as they deserve, he'll have the most powerful advocates for winning the gods' favor — better than any image ever carved.

Every person with any sense fears and respects their parents' prayers, knowing full well that those prayers have been answered many times for many people. Given this natural order of things, good people consider it a blessing from heaven when their parents live to a ripe old age. And if their parents are taken before their time, they grieve deeply. But for bad people, parents are always a source of terror.

So let every person honor their parents with every lawful honor, in the spirit of what we've just said. But if this prelude is meaningless noise in someone's ears, let the law follow, and let it be imposed in these terms:

If anyone in this city fails to take proper care of his parents — if he doesn't consider and satisfy their wishes above those of his sons, his other children, or even himself — the mistreated parent may come in person, or send someone, to report the matter to the three eldest guardians of the law and three of the women who oversee marriages. They shall look into it and punish the offenders — men under thirty and women under forty — with beatings and imprisonment. If the offenders are older than that and still continue the same neglect, or do actual harm to a parent, they shall be brought before a court where every single one of the eldest citizens serves as judge. If convicted, the court shall determine what the offender must pay or suffer, and no penalty shall be off the table.

If the mistreated person can't get word to the magistrates, any free person who learns about it must report it. If he doesn't, he'll be considered contemptible, and anyone who wants may bring a suit for damages against him. If a slave provides the information, he shall be freed. If he's the slave of either the offender or the victim, the magistrates shall free him. If he belongs to another citizen, the state shall pay the owner his price. And the magistrates shall make sure no one retaliates against the slave for giving information.

We've already discussed cases where one person fatally poisons another. But we haven't yet addressed cases where someone intentionally and maliciously harms another with substances — food, drink, or ointments — in a non-fatal way. There are actually two kinds of harm by substances that people practice, and the line between them isn't clear. There's the straightforward kind, which I just mentioned, that injures bodies through other bodies according to natural cause and effect. Then there's another kind — sorcery, spells, and what people call "magic knots" — which convinces the bolder sort that they can do real damage through supernatural means, and makes other people believe they're being harmed by a magician's powers.

It's not easy to know the true nature of all these things. And even if you do know, it's hard to convince others. When people see wax figures nailed to their doors, or placed at crossroads, or left on their parents' graves, it's useless trying to persuade them to dismiss such things just because nobody really knows how they work. So we need a law in two parts, covering both kinds of harmful practices. And we should urge, encourage, and advise people not to resort to such things — scaring the public out of their wits like children, forcing the lawgiver and judge to cure the fears the sorcerer creates.

The first thing to tell people is this: anyone who tries to poison or enchant someone doesn't actually know what he's doing — not regarding the body, unless he has medical training, and not regarding enchantments, unless he happens to be a prophet or diviner.

So let the law on poisoning and sorcery be as follows: if someone uses poison to cause non-fatal injury to a person or his servants, or any injury — fatal or not — to his livestock or bees, and the poisoner is a physician convicted of the crime, the punishment is death. If he's a private citizen, the court shall determine what he must pay or suffer.

If someone appears to be the kind of person who harms others through magic knots, enchantments, spells, or similar practices — and he's a prophet or diviner — he shall die. If he's not a prophet but is convicted of sorcery, the court shall decide what he must pay or suffer, just as in the previous case.

When someone injures another through theft or violence, the damages shall be proportional — greater damages for greater injury, lesser for lesser. But in every case, the compensation must be enough to cover the loss. On top of that, the offender shall pay an additional penalty for the correction of his crime. If the wrong was committed under someone else's influence, through the recklessness of youth or something similar, the penalty shall be lighter. But if the wrong came from the offender's own weakness — overcome by pleasure or pain, by cowardly fear, or lust, or envy, or implacable anger — the punishment shall be heavier.

Not because he's being punished for having done wrong — what's done can never be undone — but so that in the future, both he and everyone who sees him corrected will utterly hate injustice, or at least significantly reduce their wrongdoing. With all this in mind, the law, like a skilled archer, should aim at the right measure of punishment — and in every case, the deserved punishment. The judge shall be the lawgiver's partner in this work, whenever the law leaves it to him to determine what the offender must pay or suffer. And the lawgiver, like a painter, shall provide a rough sketch of the cases where the law applies.

This is what we must do, Megillus and Cleinias, as well and as fairly as we can — spelling out the punishments for all acts of theft and violence, and crafting laws of the kind that the gods and children of gods would have us craft.

If a person is insane, he must not be allowed to roam the city freely. His relatives shall keep him at home by whatever means they can. If they don't, they'll be fined: a member of the highest property class shall pay a hundred drachmas — whether it's a slave or free person he's neglecting. Members of the second class shall pay eighty drachmas, the third class sixty, and the fourth class forty.

Now, there are many kinds of madness. Some arise from disease, which we've already discussed. But others spring from a naturally bad and passionate temperament, made worse by poor upbringing. From the slightest quarrel, this type of person will erupt in a storm of abuse — and nothing like that should be tolerated in a well-ordered state.

So here's the law on verbal abuse, covering all cases: no one shall speak evil of another person. When someone has a dispute, he should try to teach and learn from the other person and from those present, but he must abstain from insults. Because from the curses people hurl at each other — the petty habit of slinging mud and using foul language, words as light as air — real and terrible enmities and hatreds spring up. The speaker indulges his anger, which is the lowest element of his nature. He nurses his rage by dwelling on hostile thoughts, makes savage again the part of his soul that education once civilized, and ends up living in a state of bitter misery — paying a steep price for his anger. And in almost every case, people in disputes resort to ridicule. But no one who makes a habit of mocking others ever achieves genuine virtue and seriousness — or at the very least, they lose the better half of greatness.

Therefore: no one shall utter taunting words in a temple, at public sacrifices, at the games, in the agora, in a courtroom, or in any public gathering. The presiding magistrate shall punish the offender — and shall face no blame for doing so. But if the magistrate fails to act, he shall forfeit any claim to the prize of virtue, since he's someone who ignores the laws and doesn't do what the lawgiver commands.

In any other place, if someone engages in this kind of abuse — whether he started it or is retaliating — any elder present should uphold the law and physically restrain those indulging their passions, using force if necessary. If the elder fails to act, he'll be liable to pay the standard penalty.

Now here's the thing: anyone who insults another person inevitably tries to be funny about it. And when it's done in a moment of anger, that's exactly what we condemn. But where does this leave comedy? Do we allow comic writers who love to make fun of people — if they do it good-naturedly — to turn their wit against our citizens? Or do we draw a line between jest and earnest, allowing someone to use humor playfully and without anger about anything or anyone, but not if he's angry and has a serious agenda?

The earnest prohibition stands — that's final. But we still need to decide who gets permission and who doesn't when it comes to harmless humor.

Here's the rule: no comic poet, no writer of satirical iambic or lyric verse, shall be permitted to ridicule any citizen — whether by words or by caricature, whether in anger or without it. If anyone disobeys, the judges shall either expel him from the country immediately or fine him three minae, to be dedicated to the god who presides over the contests. Only those who've received official permission may write humorous verses about each other, and only in jest, without anger. In anger and with serious intent, it's forbidden. The superintendent of the general education of the young shall decide what gets approved. Whatever he licenses may be produced. Whatever he rejects, the poet may not perform himself or teach to anyone else — slave or free — under penalty of being publicly dishonored and declared disobedient to the laws.

The person who truly deserves pity isn't someone who's merely hungry or suffering physical hardship. It's someone who possesses temperance or some other virtue — and suffers misfortune despite it. It would be extraordinary if such a person, whether slave or free, were completely abandoned and fell into the worst kind of poverty in any reasonably well-governed state.

So the lawgiver can safely lay down this rule: there shall be no begging in our state. If anyone goes around begging, trying to scrape together a living through hopeless appeals, the wardens of the agora shall drive him out of the marketplace, the wardens of the city shall drive him out of the city, and the wardens of the countryside shall escort him across the border and out of the land entirely — so the country may be cleared of this kind of creature.

If a slave of either sex damages someone else's property through inexperience or carelessness, and the person who suffered the damage isn't partly to blame himself, the slave's master must either make full restitution or hand over the slave. But if the master argues that the charge is a scam — a collusion between the injured party and the slave to get the slave transferred — he may sue the person claiming injury for fraud. If he wins, he receives double the value the court sets as the slave's price. If he loses, he must pay for the damage and hand over the slave.

If a pack animal, horse, dog, or any other animal damages a neighbor's property, the owner shall pay for the damage in the same way.

If anyone refuses to serve as a witness, the person who needs him shall issue a summons, and the summoned person must appear at the trial. If he has relevant knowledge and is willing to testify, let him testify. If he says he doesn't know anything, let him swear by the three divinities — Zeus, Apollo, and Themis — that he doesn't know, and then he's free from any further involvement in the case.

A person summoned as a witness who fails to answer the summons shall be liable for any resulting harm, according to law.

If someone calls on a person currently serving as a judge to be a witness, that person may give testimony but shall not afterward vote on the case.

A free woman over forty may give testimony and argue a case in court, and she may bring an action if she has no husband. But if her husband is alive, she's only permitted to serve as a witness.

A slave of either sex, and a child, may give evidence and argue in court — but only in murder cases. They must provide sufficient bonds guaranteeing they'll remain available through the trial, in case they're later charged with perjury.

Either party in a lawsuit may bring an accusation of perjury against witnesses, regarding their testimony in whole or in part, if the party believes false evidence was given. But the perjury charge must be filed before the final verdict in the case.

The magistrates shall preserve perjury accusations under seal from both parties and produce them on the day the perjury trial takes place.

If a person is convicted of perjury twice, he shall no longer be required to serve as a witness. If convicted three times, he shall not be allowed to testify at all. If he dares to testify after three convictions, anyone may report him to the magistrates, who shall turn him over to the court. If convicted again, the punishment is death.

In any case where testimony is rightly found to be false and that testimony gave the victory to the winning party, and more than half the witnesses are condemned for perjury, the original verdict won by those means shall be thrown out. There shall be a hearing to determine whether the original case was decided on the basis of that false testimony. However the determination goes, the original case shall be resolved accordingly.

There are many noble things in human life, but most of them have evils attached that seem fated to corrupt and spoil them. Isn't justice noble? Justice has been the great civilizer of humanity. And if justice is noble, how can the advocate of justice be anything less? And yet this profession — which presents itself to us under the fine name of an "art" — has acquired a terrible reputation.

Here's why: we're told that through clever arguments and a skilled advocate, the law can be made to hand victory to either side — just or unjust. The art itself, and the persuasive power it provides, are available to anyone willing to pay.

In our state, this so-called art — whether it's truly an art or just experience and practice masquerading as one — should ideally never come into existence at all. And if it does exist among us, it should heed the lawgiver's request: leave for another country, and don't speak against justice.

If the practitioners obey, we'll say nothing more. But for those who don't, here's what the law says:

If anyone is thought to be perverting the power of justice in the judges' minds — bringing frivolous lawsuits or engaging in dishonest advocacy — any citizen may indict him for malpractice of law and dishonest advocacy before the court of select judges.

If convicted, the court shall determine whether he acted out of love of money or out of pure combativeness. If it was combativeness, the court shall set a period during which he's barred from filing or arguing any case. If it was love of money: a foreigner shall be banished from the country and never allowed to return, on pain of death. A citizen shall die — because he's a person who loves money by any means. And likewise, if someone is convicted a second time for combativeness alone, the punishment is death.
