On the second day, the same process for the second class, under the same rules. This method of election hits a middle ground between monarchy and democracy, and a state should always aim for that middle ground. Servants and masters can never truly be friends, and neither can good and bad people — not just by declaring them equal. Treating unequals as equals creates its own kind of inequality, if it's not balanced with good judgment. Both kinds of imbalance — forced equality and unchecked inequality — fill cities with conflict.

There's an old saying: "equality makes friendship." It's a fine sentiment, and true enough — but there's real confusion about what kind of equality we're talking about. There are actually two kinds that go by the same name, but they're almost opposites in practice.

The first kind is easy — any state or lawmaker can apply it. It's the equality of measurement, weight, and number: give everyone the same amount.

But the second kind is better and harder to recognize. It's the judgment of Zeus, you might say. Among human beings it's rarely achieved — but that small measure of it that we do achieve is the source of the greatest good, both for individuals and for states. This kind of equality gives more to the greater and less to the lesser, proportional to the nature of each — and above all, it assigns greater honor to greater virtue and lesser honor to lesser virtue, in proportion to each person's measure of excellence and education. And this is justice — the true principle that states should aim for. This is what we should have in mind when ordering our new city, and any city that might be founded in the future.

The lawmaker should focus on this — not on the interests of tyrants, whether one or many, and not on the raw power of the people, but on justice. Justice means distributing natural equality among unequals in a way that's proportional.

Still, there are times when every state is forced to use the words "just" and "equal" in a looser sense, just to keep the peace. Equity and flexibility are compromises with strict justice. That's why we're sometimes forced to use the lottery — to prevent popular discontent. When we do, we pray to god and fortune to guide the lot toward what's truly just. Even though we have to use both kinds of equality, we should rely on chance as little as possible.

That, my friends, is how and why a state that wants to survive should operate. A city is like a ship sailing a treacherous sea of politics, vulnerable to all sorts of threats. Rulers must pass the watch from hand to hand, day and night, in an unbroken chain. A large body can never maintain that kind of vigilance with real energy. So the majority of senators will need to spend most of the year tending to their own affairs. We should therefore divide them into twelve groups corresponding to the twelve months, with each group providing one month of service as guardians of the state.

Their job is to be available and responsive — ready to receive any foreigner or citizen who approaches them, whether to share information or to raise one of those questions that cities need to answer when asked by other states, and that they sometimes need to ask themselves. When internal disturbances threaten — as they inevitably will in some form — they must try to prevent them; or, if they've already started, act immediately to inform the city and heal the damage. This rotating body is essentially the executive branch of the state, and it should always control the convening and dissolving of assemblies, both regular and emergency. All of this is to be managed by whichever twelfth of the council is on duty, keeping watch alongside the other officers of state during their month, and resting during the other eleven.

That's how the city will be properly organized. Now — who should oversee the countryside, and how should that be arranged? The entire city and territory have been divided into twelve portions. They'll manage rainwater — when it comes pouring down from the mountains into the valleys, they'll channel it with earthworks and ditches so the lowlands absorb the rainfall and produce springs and streams, giving even the driest areas a reliable water supply. They'll beautify the springs and rivers with plantings and buildings. All around such places, the young men should build gymnasiums for themselves and warm baths for the elderly, stocking them with plenty of dry firewood — a welcome relief for anyone suffering from illness. Building these works will be both useful and beautiful — a delightful pastime, but also serious business. Every citizen should remember this universal principle: anyone who isn't a good servant will never be a good master. A person should take more pride in serving well than in commanding well — serving the laws first of all (which is also service to the gods), and serving honorable elders during his youth.

In general, they should be prepared to live independently, serving themselves and each other.

Beyond that, in every season — summer and winter — they must stay armed and patrol the entire country thoroughly. This serves a double purpose: they stand guard, and they gain expert knowledge of every corner of the territory. There may be no more valuable kind of knowledge than an intimate familiarity with your own country. This whole corps — whether you call them the "secret service" or the "wardens of the country" (the name doesn't much matter) — represents a vital institution. Everyone who cares about the safety of the state should devote themselves to this service wholeheartedly.

The city wardens will be three, and they'll divide the city's twelve districts among themselves. Like the country wardens, they'll oversee the roads and the various highways connecting the countryside to the city, making sure all buildings conform to the law. They'll also manage the water supply — ensuring that what the country guardians send flows to the city fountains pure and plentiful, serving as both an ornament and a benefit. These should be men of influence who have the leisure to devote to public affairs.

To elect them: every citizen nominates whoever he likes from the highest property class. Once the votes are tallied and the field is narrowed to six, the election officers choose three by lot. After passing the vetting process, they take office according to the established laws.

Next, the wardens of the market: five shall be elected from the first and second property classes. The procedure mirrors the city wardens' election — ten are elected by vote, then five are chosen by lot from those ten and confirmed through the vetting process.

Everyone must vote for every candidate. Anyone who refuses to vote, if reported to the magistrates, will be fined fifty drachmas and branded a bad citizen. Attendance at the general assembly and council meetings is required for citizens of the first and second classes, with a ten-drachma fine for no-shows. The third and fourth classes face no penalty for absence unless the magistrates have declared attendance mandatory due to some urgent situation.

For education, we're talking about officials who oversee order and instruction in gymnasiums and schools, manage attendance, and maintain school buildings for both boys and girls. For contests, we need judges — some for athletic competitions among men and horses, others for musical performances.

In music, we should separate the judges: one group for solo performances — rhapsodes, harp players, flute players, and similar artists — and another group for choral performances.

First, let's choose directors for the choruses — boys, men, and young women — who will lead them in dance and handle our other musical arrangements. One director is sufficient for the choruses, and he should be at least forty years old. One director is also enough for the solo performers, judging competitions and guiding the contestants. He should be at least thirty.

The choral director is elected like this: anyone who regularly takes an interest in these matters goes to the meeting. Failure to attend means a fine — the guardians of the law will judge. After passing the vetting, he leads the choruses for the year. The same process applies to the solo music director. Next: judges for the athletic and equestrian contests. These should be selected from the second and third property classes. The top three classes must participate in the election; the lowest class may abstain without penalty. Twenty are elected by vote, then three are chosen by lot. These three must also pass the vetting process.

If anyone is rejected during the vetting at any stage, replacements are chosen by the same method and undergo the same scrutiny.

One office remains: the superintendent of education for youth — both male and female. One superintendent is sufficient. He must rule according to law, be at least fifty years old, and have legitimate children of his own — both sons and daughters if possible, but at a minimum, one or the other.

Both the person elected and the person doing the electing should understand that of all the great offices of state, this is the greatest. Think about it: the first growth of any plant, if it gets a good start toward its natural potential, has the biggest impact on how it matures. This is true not just of plants but of all animals, wild and tame — and of human beings. We say that humans are civilized creatures, and with proper education and a good nature, they become the most divine and civilized beings of all. But with insufficient or poor education? The most savage creatures on earth.

That's why the lawmaker must never treat the education of children as a secondary or casual concern. The first step is to make sure that the person elected to this role is truly the best citizen in every respect. The lawmaker should spare no effort in making that happen.

Here's how the election works: all the magistrates — except the council and the executive officers — go to the temple of Apollo and vote by secret ballot for whichever guardian of the law they believe would make the best superintendent of education. The candidate with the most votes undergoes a vetting process conducted by all the magistrates who elected him, excluding the guardians of the law. He serves for five years. In the sixth year, a successor is elected the same way.

If anyone holding public office dies with more than thirty days remaining in their term, the relevant officials elect a replacement through the same process. If a legal guardian of orphans dies, the relatives on both the father's and mother's side — including cousins living in the area — must appoint a new guardian within ten days, or face a fine of a drachma per day of delay.

Now, a city without proper courts of law isn't really a city at all. But a judge who sits silently through the proceedings, saying nothing more than the parties themselves — the way it happens in arbitration — will never be able to judge fairly. That's why a large panel of judges rarely judges well, but a small panel of bad judges is just as problematic. The point of contention between the parties needs to be made clear, and time, careful deliberation, and repeated examination are what help resolve doubts.

So here's the procedure: anyone with a legal dispute should first go to his neighbors and friends — the people who know the facts best. If he can't get a satisfactory decision from them, he should try another court. And if two courts can't settle it, a third one gets the final say.

Now, setting up courts of law is essentially choosing another kind of magistrate. Every magistrate has to judge certain matters, and a judge — even if he doesn't hold any other office — is really a very important official on the day he decides a case. So let's treat judges as magistrates too, and talk about who should serve, what kinds of cases they should decide, and how many judges each case needs.

The highest tribunal should be the one that the litigants themselves create by mutual agreement, choosing their own judges. Beyond that, there should be two other courts: one for private disputes, when one citizen accuses another of wronging him and wants a ruling; and one for public cases, when a citizen believes the state itself has been harmed by an individual and wants to defend the public interest.

We still need to address the qualifications and identity of the judges. First, there should be a final court of appeal, open to all citizens who've already been through two lower courts. It's composed as follows: at the end of each year, on the last day but one before the new year begins — in the month after the summer solstice — all the magistrates, both annual and longer-serving, gather at a temple. Calling god to witness, each office dedicates one judge from its ranks as a "firstfruit" — choosing whoever they think is the best and most likely to decide cases fairly and honorably in the coming year. After the selection, these judges undergo vetting in front of the electors themselves, and any who are rejected are replaced by the same process.

These vetted judges hear the cases of people who've exhausted the lower courts. They vote openly. Councilors and the magistrates who elected them are required to attend as observers, and any other citizen may attend if he wishes.

If anyone accuses a judge of deliberately ruling unjustly, he can bring the charge before the guardians of the law. A judge found guilty must pay the injured party damages equal to half the harm done. If the offense warrants a greater penalty, the judges determine what additional punishment is appropriate and how much more the guilty judge owes — to both the public treasury and the plaintiff.

In cases of offenses against the state, the people must participate. When someone wrongs the state, everyone is wronged, and citizens have a right to be upset if they're shut out of the decision. These cases should originate with the people and be finally decided by them, but the trial itself should take place before three of the highest magistrates, agreed upon by both the plaintiff and the defendant. If the parties can't agree, the council chooses between the two proposed options.

In private suits, too, as many citizens as possible should have a role. Anyone who has no share in the administration of justice tends to feel he has no share in the state at all. For this reason, there should be a court in every tribe, with judges chosen by lot, who give their verdicts immediately and are immune to lobbying.

The final word always rests with that highest court — the one we've established to be as incorruptible as anything human can be. That's the court for people who can't resolve their disputes in the neighborhood courts or the tribal courts.
