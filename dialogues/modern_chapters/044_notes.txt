CRITIAS — Translation Notes

OVERVIEW
--------
The Critias is Plato's unfinished sequel to the Timaeus. It is the primary source
of the Atlantis legend — everything humanity knows about Atlantis traces back to
this dialogue and a brief passage in the Timaeus. The text breaks off mid-sentence,
one of the great tantalizing interruptions in world literature.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS
-----------------------------

1. THE ABRUPT ENDING
   The dialogue famously breaks off in the middle of a sentence: "And when he had
   called them together, he spake as follows—" (Jowett). I preserved this with
   a trailing em-dash: "And when he had assembled them, he spoke as follows --"
   The reader should feel the sudden silence, the sense that something enormous
   was about to be said and never was. No editorial note, no explanation — just
   the break.

2. THE ATLANTIS DESCRIPTION
   This is the heart of the dialogue and the source of one of humanity's most
   persistent legends. I prioritized visual clarity and vividness throughout.
   The concentric rings of water and land, the three-colored stone (white, black,
   red), the walls of bronze, tin, and orichalcum, the golden statues, Poseidon's
   massive chariot — all of this needed to land with the force of vivid
   world-building. I broke Jowett's dense paragraphs into shorter, more
   visual passages.

3. MEASUREMENTS AND SCALE
   I kept Plato's measurements in stadia throughout (a stadium is roughly
   600 feet / 185 meters). Converting to modern units would destroy the ancient
   flavor, and the relative proportions are what matter for visualization.
   The sheer scale — the canal 50 stadia long, the ditch 10,000 stadia in
   circumference — is meant to be staggering and slightly incredible, and
   Critias himself acknowledges this ("almost unbelievable for a manmade work").

4. ORICHALCUM
   Kept untranslated as "orichalcum" — the mysterious metal that was "more
   precious than anything except gold." This is one of the great unsolved
   puzzles of ancient metallurgy. Plato himself notes it "is now only a name
   and was then something more than a name." The mystery is part of the legend.

5. SPEAKER FORMAT
   Unlike the Timaeus which is essentially one long monologue, the Critias has
   a genuine multi-speaker opening before Critias launches into his narrative.
   The four-way exchange (Timaeus wrapping up, Critias asking for indulgence,
   Socrates granting it with a playful challenge, Hermocrates encouraging)
   has real dramatic energy. Once Critias begins his story, it becomes
   continuous narrative with no further dialogue.

6. ANCIENT ATHENS AS THE REPUBLIC REALIZED
   Critias explicitly describes ancient Athens as embodying the ideal state
   from the Republic — guardian warriors who own nothing, live communally,
   share meals, and disdain gold and silver. The line "they practiced all the
   pursuits we described yesterday as those of our imaginary guardians" is
   the key connection. I kept this reference clear without over-explaining it.

7. THE ENVIRONMENTAL PASSAGE
   The remarkable section where Critias describes how Attica has been
   environmentally degraded — soil erosion, deforestation, loss of springs —
   is one of the earliest descriptions of environmental change in literature.
   I made this passage vivid and clear, using the "bones of a wasted body"
   metaphor prominently.

8. THE BULL SACRIFICE RITUAL
   The ten kings hunting bulls in Poseidon's temple with staves and nooses,
   the blood on the orichalcum pillar, the oath by firelight in blue robes —
   this is extraordinarily vivid ritual description. I kept every detail and
   made the sequence flow cinematically.

9. THE MORAL DECLINE
   The final passage — how the divine element faded and human nature took
   over, how they became greedy and debased while appearing glorious to the
   blind — is Plato's philosophical point. The Atlantis story isn't just
   adventure; it's a parable about what happens when a civilization loses its
   virtue. I made the moral clarity of this passage as sharp as possible.

VOCABULARY NOTES
-----------------
- "Pillars of Heracles" — kept as-is (the Strait of Gibraltar)
- "stadia" — kept as the plural of "stadium" (the unit of measurement)
- "Mnemosyne" — goddess of Memory, Critias' patron for this tale
- "Gades/Cadiz" — Jowett has "Gades," I added "Cadiz" for modern recognition
- "Tyrrhenia" — the Etruscan region of Italy, kept as-is
- "orichalcum" — the legendary metal, kept untranslated
- "Deucalion" — the Greek Noah figure, kept as-is
- "trireme" — kept; the ancient warship is well-known enough

STRUCTURAL NOTES
-----------------
The dialogue has a clear three-part structure:
1. PROLOGUE (speakers 1-4): Timaeus wraps up, Critias asks for patience,
   Socrates challenges, Hermocrates encourages
2. ANCIENT ATHENS (the virtuous state): geography, society, warriors
3. ATLANTIS (the magnificent empire): origins, geography, architecture,
   government, ritual, moral decline, and the abrupt break

CONSISTENCY WITH TIMAEUS (Chapters 042-043)
---------------------------------------------
This dialogue picks up immediately where the Timaeus left off — Timaeus
literally finishes his speech and hands off to Critias. The Atlantis story
was briefly introduced in the Timaeus; here it gets its full telling.
The reference to "what we described yesterday" about the ideal guardians
points back to the Republic, which in the dramatic fiction was discussed
the day before.

WHAT'S LOST
------------
We'll never know what Zeus was about to say, or how the war between Athens
and Atlantis played out, or how (and whether) Plato intended Hermocrates
to speak after Critias. The dialogue's incompleteness is itself part of its
legend. Was Plato unable to finish? Did he choose not to? Did he decide the
Laws was a better use of his remaining time? Nobody knows.
