TRANSLATION NOTES — Euthydemus Part 2 (Chapter 029)

OVERVIEW:
Part 2 continues and concludes the Euthydemus. It begins with the philosophical
puzzle about the "kingly art" (what does the supreme political knowledge actually
produce?), then delivers the dialogue's most famous and hilarious sophistic
arguments, and closes with a thoughtful coda between Socrates and Crito about
the value of philosophy.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. THE KINGLY ART PUZZLE: The opening exchange between Socrates and Crito about
   the kingly/political art is genuinely philosophical — it's Plato showing that
   even sincere philosophical inquiry can hit walls. The art that governs all
   others seems to produce... only the knowledge of itself, leading to infinite
   regress. Kept this section clear and let the intellectual vertigo come through
   naturally: "the same old tune playing again."

2. "YOU KNOW ALL THINGS": Euthydemus's argument that since you can't be knowing
   and not-knowing at the same time, anyone who knows one thing must know
   everything — this is one of the great comic set-pieces. The key is Socrates'
   mounting mock amazement as the absurdity escalates: "Oh heavens!" "Including
   carpentry?" "And stitching?" Kept the escalation building naturally.

3. CTESIPPUS AND THE TEETH: Ctesippus's demand ("tell me how many teeth
   Euthydemus has") is a brilliant moment of common sense cutting through
   sophistry. The Sophists' refusal to answer is telling. Then Ctesippus starts
   asking progressively more outrageous questions, and the Sophists fearlessly
   answer yes to everything — "like wild boars rushing onto the spear points."

4. THE DOG-FATHER ARGUMENT: The most famous joke in the dialogue. Through a
   chain of seemingly logical steps, Dionysodorus "proves" that Ctesippus's
   dog is his father. This has to land as genuinely funny. Kept the logical
   steps crystal clear so the reader can see exactly where the trick operates
   (equivocation on "yours" and "father"), while letting the absurd conclusion
   hit with full comic force. Ctesippus's comeback — "What was he thinking
   when he produced such brilliant sons?" — is perfect.

5. THE HERACLES AND HYDRA METAPHOR: Socrates compares himself to Heracles
   fighting the Hydra (a "she-Sophist") and the sea-crab (another Sophist),
   with Dionysodorus as the crab bearing down from the left. This is Plato
   at his most vividly comic. Preserved the full mythological comparison.

6. CHAEREDEMUS/SOPHRONISCUS: The argument that Socrates has no father (because
   Chaeredemus, being "different from a father," is not a father, and therefore
   Sophroniscus, being different from a father, is also not a father) is another
   classic equivocation trick. Kept each logical step explicit.

7. YOUR MOTHER IS MOTHER OF SEA URCHINS: The escalation from "your father is
   everyone's father" to "your mother has given birth to sea urchins" to
   "baby fish and puppies and piglets are your brothers" to "your papa is a
   dog" — each step is more outrageous than the last. Preserved the rapid-fire
   back-and-forth rhythm.

8. THE COOK ARGUMENT: If the cook's business is killing, skinning, and chopping,
   then someone who kills and chops the cook is doing the cook's business.
   Pure logical nonsense presented with perfect deadpan. Socrates' response —
   "Poseidon! This is the crowning achievement of wisdom!" — is classic mock
   praise.

9. THE GODS ARGUMENT: The final trap — since Socrates admits the gods are his,
   and his animals are things he can sell or sacrifice, the gods must be things
   he can sell or sacrifice. Socrates sees it coming ("sensing the final move
   like a man caught in a net who makes one desperate twist") but can't escape.
   This is the climactic absurdity.

10. "BRAVO HERACLES / HERACLES BRAVO": Dionysodorus's instant wordplay on
    Ctesippus's exclamation is a beautiful final touch — even in defeat, the
    Sophists can't stop playing word games.

11. SOCRATES' MOCK ENCOMIUM: The long ironic speech praising the Sophists is
    devastating satire. Every compliment is actually a criticism: their
    "magnificent indifference to opinions," their denial of "all distinctions,"
    the fact that "anyone can learn it in almost no time." The advice not to
    perform publicly (because the art is too easily learned) is the knife
    wrapped in silk.

12. THE CRITO CODA: The closing exchange between Socrates and Crito about the
    unnamed critic of philosophy and the proper attitude toward education is
    the serious philosophical heart of the dialogue. Socrates' defense of
    philosophy — judge the pursuit, not the practitioners — is genuinely moving
    and contrasts beautifully with the comedy that preceded it.

VOCABULARY AND CULTURAL NOTES:
- "Talents of gold" — kept the monetary terms; the image of gold stuffed in
  someone's belly and skull is funny enough on its own
- "Scythians" — Ctesippus's retort about Scythians drinking from gilded skulls
  is a real cultural reference, used here as a devastating comeback
- "Geryon and Briareus" — mythological many-armed/many-bodied monsters; the
  joke is about needing many shields
- "Hellebore" — ancient remedy/purgative; kept the term as it's clear in context
- "Statue at Delphi" — refers to a famously large statue
- Greek oaths preserved: "by the gods," "By Zeus," "Poseidon," "God forbid"
- "Prodicus" — referenced again as an authority on word distinctions
- "Castor and Pollux" — twin divine rescuers; Socrates sarcastically casts
  the Sophists in this role
- "Ancestral Zeus" (Zeus Patroios) — genuine Athenian religious distinction;
  kept with brief context

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
- Part 2 contains: the kingly art puzzle, the "you know everything" argument,
  the Ctesippus-vs-Sophists sequence (teeth, dog-father, sea urchin brothers,
  medicine, gold, garments that see, silence of the speaker), the cook/potter
  argument, the gods argument, the universal applause, Socrates' mock encomium,
  the unnamed critic's complaint, and the philosophical coda on philosophy's value
- The dialogue ends with Socrates' beautiful injunction: "Think about philosophy
  herself... If she is what I believe she is, then follow her and serve her"

TONE:
- The comedy builds throughout Part 2 to a crescendo at the gods argument
- Then pivots sharply to genuine philosophical reflection in the Crito coda
- This tonal shift is deliberate and important — Plato is saying that even
  though bad philosophy (sophistry) is laughable, philosophy herself is sacred

CONSISTENCY WITH PART 1:
- Same speaker format and narrative voice maintained
- Ctesippus's character stays consistent: hot-headed, witty, combative
- Socrates' mock amazement escalates appropriately
- The Sophists' tag-team technique (one sets up, the other finishes) continues
