Socrates
Well, we'll talk about pleasures that are corrupt and caused by corruption later, if we want to continue the inquiry. For now, I'd rather use a different argument to show that many false pleasures exist -- or come into existence -- in us, because that might help us reach our final verdict.

Protarchus
Fair enough -- assuming such pleasures exist.

Socrates
I think they do, Protarchus. But this shouldn't rest on a bare assertion -- it needs to be backed up.

Protarchus
Very good.

Socrates
Then let's step into the ring with this new argument, like wrestlers squaring off.

Protarchus
Let's go.

Socrates
We were saying a little while ago that when desires exist in us, the body has its own feelings separate from the soul. Remember?

Protarchus
Yes, I remember you said that.

Socrates
And the soul was supposed to desire the opposite of what the body was experiencing, while the body was the source of whatever pleasure or pain was being felt.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
So now you can figure out what happens in these cases.

Protarchus
What am I supposed to figure out?

Socrates
That in such cases, pleasures and pains come simultaneously -- opposite sensations sitting right next to each other, as we've already shown.

Protarchus
Clearly.

Socrates
And there's another point we agreed on.

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
That pleasure and pain both admit of more and less -- that they belong to the class of infinites.

Protarchus
We did say that.

Socrates
Then how can we judge them accurately?

Protarchus
How can we?

Socrates
Are we trying to judge their relative size and intensity, measuring pleasure against pain, pain against pain, pleasure against pleasure?

Protarchus
Yes, that's what we're doing, and that's how we'll judge.

Socrates
Well, think about sight. Doesn't the nearness or distance of objects distort their true size and make us form false impressions? Don't we find the same illusion happening with pleasures and pains?

Protarchus
Yes, Socrates, and to a far greater degree.

Socrates
So what we're saying now is actually the opposite of what we said before.

Protarchus
How so?

Socrates
Before, it was the opinions that were true or false, and they infected the pleasures and pains with their own truth or falsity.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
But now the pleasures and pains themselves are said to be true or false because they're seen at various distances and compared with each other. When set alongside pains, pleasures appear greater and more intense than they really are, and pains appear worse when set alongside pleasures.

Protarchus
That's bound to happen, for exactly the reason you're giving.

Socrates
So if you strip away from pleasures and pains the element that makes them appear larger or smaller than they really are, you'll agree that this element is illusory. And you'd never call the resulting excess or shortfall of pleasure or pain real or true.

Protarchus
Certainly not.

Socrates
Next, let's see whether we can find pleasures and pains in living beings that are even more false than these, coming from a different direction.

Protarchus
What are they, and how do we find them?

Socrates
Haven't I said repeatedly that pains, aches, suffering, and discomfort of every kind arise from corruption of our natural state -- from buildups and breakdowns, from excess and depletion, from growth and decay?

Protarchus
Yes, you've said that many times.

Socrates
And we've agreed that the restoration of the natural state is pleasure?

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
But now let's suppose there's a stretch of time during which the body isn't going through any of these changes.

Protarchus
When could that happen, Socrates?

Socrates
Your question doesn't help the argument, Protarchus.

Protarchus
Why not?

Socrates
Because it doesn't stop me from asking mine.

Protarchus
And what was yours?

Socrates
Protarchus, even granting that no such interval actually occurs, I can still ask: what would happen if it did?

Protarchus
You mean what would happen if the body weren't being changed for either better or worse?

Socrates
Yes.

Protarchus
Well then, I'd suppose there would be neither pleasure nor pain.

Socrates
Very good. But unless I'm mistaken, you also believe that we must always be experiencing one or the other -- that's what the wise men say. They claim everything is always in flux, constantly flowing up and down.

Protarchus
Yes, and they're not minor thinkers saying this.

Socrates
Of course not -- they're major thinkers. But I'd like to dodge the force of their argument. Shall I tell you my escape plan? You can come along for the ride.

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
We'll say to them: "Fine. But are we, or living creatures in general, always conscious of what's happening to us? Take growth, for example -- are we always aware of that? On the contrary, aren't we almost entirely unconscious of it and similar processes?" You answer for them.

Protarchus
The second option is clearly correct.

Socrates
Then we weren't right to say, just now, that constant changes always produce pleasures and pains.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
A better and more defensible way to put it would be --

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
That large changes produce pleasures and pains, but moderate and small ones produce neither.

Protarchus
That's the more accurate way to say it, Socrates.

Socrates
But if that's true, then the life I mentioned earlier comes back into view.

Protarchus
Which life?

Socrates
The one we said was free of both pain and pleasure.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
So we can assume there are three kinds of life: one pleasant, one painful, and a third that's neither. What do you say?

Protarchus
I'd say the same -- there are three.

Socrates
If so, then the absence of pain is not the same thing as pleasure.

Protarchus
Certainly not.

Socrates
So when you hear someone saying that the most pleasant thing in the world is to live permanently without pain -- what do you think they actually mean?

Protarchus
I think they're confusing the absence of pain with pleasure.

Socrates
Let's take any three things. Or to make it fancier, let's call the first gold, the second silver, and the third something that's neither gold nor silver.

Protarchus
Fine.

Socrates
Now, can the thing that's neither gold nor silver be gold or silver?

Protarchus
Impossible.

Socrates
And no more can that neutral middle life be rightly thought of or spoken of as pleasant or painful.

Protarchus
Certainly not.

Socrates
And yet, my friend, there are people who think and say exactly that.

Protarchus
There certainly are.

Socrates
And do they think they're experiencing pleasure when they're free from pain?

Protarchus
That's what they say.

Socrates
Then they must think they're experiencing pleasure, or they wouldn't say so.

Protarchus
I suppose not.

Socrates
But if pleasure and the absence of pain are genuinely different things, they're wrong.

Protarchus
And they are undoubtedly different.

Socrates
So shall we take the view that there are three states, as we just said? Or only two -- pain being an evil, and freedom from pain being a good that's called "pleasant"?

Protarchus
But why are we even asking this, Socrates? I don't see the point.

Socrates
That's because you've never heard of certain enemies of our friend Philebus, Protarchus.

Protarchus
Who are they?

Socrates
Certain people who are reputed to be experts in natural philosophy. They deny that pleasure exists at all.

Protarchus
Really!

Socrates
They say that what Philebus' school calls "pleasures" are nothing but escape from pain.

Protarchus
And do you want us to agree with them, Socrates?

Socrates
No -- but I'd like to use them the way you'd use fortune-tellers who get the truth not through any proper method, but through an instinctive revulsion. They have a noble nature's deep disgust for the power of pleasure, and they find nothing wholesome in it. They consider her seductive influence a kind of sorcery, not genuine goodness. That's how you should use their views. After you've considered the various grounds for their hostility, you'll hear from me what I think the true pleasures are. That way we'll examine pleasure's nature from both sides before we render our verdict.

Protarchus
Well said.

Socrates
Then let's form an alliance with these philosophers and follow the trail of their hostility. I imagine they'd argue something like this. They'd start at the beginning and ask: if we wanted to understand any quality -- hardness, say -- would we learn more by looking at the hardest things or the least hard? You answer these stern gentlemen the way you answer me, Protarchus.

Protarchus
By all means. And I'd tell them: look at the most extreme examples.

Socrates
So if we want to see the true nature of pleasure as a class, we shouldn't look at the mildest pleasures but at the most extreme and intense ones?

Protarchus
Everyone would agree with that.

Socrates
And the most obviously intense pleasures, as we've often said, are bodily pleasures?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And do we experience these more intensely when we're sick or when we're healthy? Now we need to be careful here -- we don't want to trip up.

Protarchus
How would we trip up?

Socrates
Because we might be tempted to answer "when we're healthy."

Protarchus
Yes, that's the natural answer.

Socrates
True, but aren't the greatest pleasures the ones that satisfy the greatest desires?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And don't people with a fever or similar illness feel thirst, cold, and other bodily needs far more intensely? They have deeper wants and greater pleasure when those wants are satisfied?

Protarchus
That's obviously right, once you say it.

Socrates
Then aren't we right to say that if someone wants to see the greatest pleasures, they shouldn't look at health but at sickness? Now, be careful -- don't think I'm asking whether very sick people have more pleasures than healthy people overall. I'm asking about the intensity of pleasure -- where pleasure reaches its most extreme heights. Our task, remember, is to discover what pleasure really is, and what those people mean who say pleasure doesn't even exist.

Protarchus
I think I follow you.

Socrates
You'll have a chance to show me soon enough. Answer this: do you see more intense, more excessive pleasures in self-indulgence or in self-control? Think before you speak.

Protarchus
I see what you're getting at, and the difference is enormous. The self-controlled person is restrained by the old motto "nothing in excess" -- that's their rule. But excessive pleasure, when it takes over the minds of fools and the undisciplined, drives them to madness and makes them shout out loud.

Socrates
Good. And if that's true, then the greatest pleasures and pains will obviously be found in some corrupt state of body and soul, not in a virtuous one.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
So shouldn't we pick out some of these and see what makes them so great?

Protarchus
We should.

Socrates
Take the pleasures that arise from certain disorders.

Protarchus
What disorders?

Socrates
The pleasures of shameful conditions -- the ones that our stern philosophical friends absolutely detest.

Protarchus
What pleasures specifically?

Socrates
The relief of itching by scratching, for instance, and other ailments where scratching is the only remedy needed. Because -- by heaven -- what are we supposed to call the feeling this produces? Pleasure or pain?

Protarchus
A nasty mixture of both, I'd say, Socrates.

Socrates
I didn't bring this up to embarrass Philebus personally. But without considering these kinds of pleasures and others like them, we can't settle the question before us.

Protarchus
Then we'd better analyze this family of pleasures.

Socrates
You mean the pleasures that are mixed with pain?

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
Some of these mixtures are purely bodily -- in the body alone. Others are purely in the soul -- in the soul alone. And still others are mixtures of pleasure and pain shared by both body and soul, which in their combined state are sometimes called pleasures and sometimes pains.

Protarchus
How does that work?

Socrates
Whenever the body is going through restoration and breakdown at the same time, a person experiences two opposite feelings -- say, being cold while warming up, or being hot while cooling down. He wants one feeling and wants to be rid of the other. The sweet is mixed with the bitter, as the saying goes, and both sensations cling to him, creating frustration that eventually drives him to distraction.

Protarchus
That's a very accurate description.

Socrates
And in these sorts of mixtures, the pleasures and pains are sometimes equal, and sometimes one or the other dominates?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
For cases where pain exceeds pleasure, think of itching, which we were just discussing -- the tingling you feel when there's a burning, inflamed condition inside, and rubbing and scratching only reaches the surface without touching the affected parts. If you go further and try fire, or as a last resort cold, you might produce the most intense pleasure or pain in the deeper parts, which contrasts and mingles with the pain or pleasure of the outer parts. This happens because what's naturally united gets forcibly separated, or what's separated gets pushed together, and pleasure and pain sit right next to each other.

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
Other times the element of pleasure dominates, and the slight undercurrent of pain makes the person tingle and creates a gentle irritation. Or the excessive infusion of pleasure whips them into a frenzy -- they leap for joy, strike all kinds of poses, change color, gasp for breath, and end up completely beside themselves, shouting the most irrational things.

Protarchus
Yes, indeed.

Socrates
They'll say, and others will say about them, that they're "dying" from these delights. And the more reckless and dissolute a person is, the more intensely he pursues them in every way. He declares them the greatest of all pleasures and considers whoever lives in the most constant enjoyment of them to be the happiest person in the world.

Protarchus
That's a very accurate portrait of what most people think about pleasure, Socrates.

Socrates
Quite right, Protarchus -- at least about the mixed pleasures that arise from the combination of external and internal bodily sensations. But there are also cases where the mind contributes an opposing element to the body -- whether pleasure or pain -- and the two merge into a single mixture. We talked about this earlier: when a person is hungry, he hopes for food -- pleasure from hope, pain from emptiness. But I need to add what I left out before: in all these cases, and countless others like them, where body and mind are at odds, pleasure and pain combine into one experience.

Protarchus
I believe that's entirely true.

Socrates
There's still one more type of pleasure-pain mixture left.

Protarchus
What is it?

Socrates
The combination that the mind experiences entirely on its own -- purely mental feelings.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Don't we speak of anger, fear, desire, grief, love, jealousy, envy, and the like as pains belonging to the soul alone?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And won't we find them full of the most extraordinary pleasures too? Need I remind you of that anger

  "Which stirs even a wise man to violence,
  And is sweeter than honey and the honeycomb?"

And you remember how pleasures mingle with pains in grief and mourning?

Protarchus
Yes, there's a natural connection between them.

Socrates
And you remember how at tragedies the audience smiles through their tears?

Protarchus
I certainly do.

Socrates
And are you aware that even at a comedy, the soul experiences a mixture of pain and pleasure?

Protarchus
I don't quite follow.

Socrates
I'll admit, Protarchus, that it's not easy to recognize this mixture of feelings at a comedy.

Protarchus
Not easy at all.

Socrates
But the more obscure the case, the more worthwhile it is to examine it. If we can detect the mixture here, it'll be easier to spot in other cases.

Protarchus
Go on.

Socrates
I just mentioned envy. Would you call that a pain of the soul?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
Yet the envious person finds something pleasing in his neighbor's misfortunes?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And ignorance -- what people call cluelessness -- is an evil, right?

Protarchus
Sure.

Socrates
From these observations, learn to understand the nature of the ridiculous.

Protarchus
Explain.

Socrates
The ridiculous is basically the specific name for a particular kind of vice. And among vices in general, it's the one that most directly violates the inscription at Delphi.

Protarchus
You mean "Know thyself."

Socrates
I do. And the opposite would be: "Don't know thyself."

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
Now, Protarchus, try dividing this into three kinds.

Protarchus
I'm afraid I can't.

Socrates
So you want me to do the dividing?

Protarchus
Yes -- and please do.

Socrates
Aren't there three ways a person can fail to know himself?

Protarchus
What are they?

Socrates
First, about money -- the ignorant person may think he's richer than he actually is.

Protarchus
Yes, that's a very common delusion.

Socrates
Even more often, he'll think he's taller, better-looking, or more physically impressive than he really is.

Protarchus
Of course.

Socrates
But by far the most people deceive themselves about the qualities of the mind -- they imagine they're much better, wiser, and more virtuous than they actually are.

Protarchus
Yes, that's the most common delusion of all.

Socrates
And of all the virtues, isn't wisdom the one that most people lay claim to -- the one that most stirs up competitive posturing and false pretensions?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And we can call this entire condition an evil?

Protarchus
A very great evil.

Socrates
But we need to divide it one more step, Protarchus, if we want to see how this childish sort of envy involves a curious blend of pleasure and pain.

Protarchus
How do we make this further division?

Socrates
Everyone who's foolish enough to hold this inflated view of themselves can be divided -- like the rest of humanity -- into two groups: those with power and those without it.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
There's your dividing line. Those who are weak and can't retaliate when they're laughed at may truly be called ridiculous. But those who can defend themselves are better described as strong and dangerous. Ignorance in the powerful is hateful and horrible, because it actually hurts people -- in real life and in stories. But powerless ignorance can fairly be classified as ridiculous -- and genuinely is.

Protarchus
That's very true, but I still don't see where the mixture of pleasure and pain comes in.

Socrates
Then let's examine the nature of envy.

Protarchus
Go ahead.

Socrates
Isn't envy an unjust pleasure and also an unjust pain?

Protarchus
It certainly is.

Socrates
There's nothing envious or wrong about feeling happy when your enemies suffer, is there?

Protarchus
No, nothing wrong with that.

Socrates
But feeling joy instead of sorrow at your friends' misfortunes -- that's wrong?

Protarchus
Obviously.

Socrates
And we said that ignorance is always an evil?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
Now, those three kinds of false self-image we listed -- about beauty, wisdom, and wealth -- are ridiculous when they belong to people who are weak, and hateful when they belong to the powerful. So can we say, as I was saying before, that when our friends are in this deluded state, and they're harmless, they're simply ridiculous?

Protarchus
They are ridiculous.

Socrates
And don't we acknowledge that their ignorance is a misfortune for them?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
But do we feel pain or pleasure when we laugh at it?

Protarchus
Clearly we feel pleasure.

Socrates
And wasn't envy the source of this pleasure we feel at our friends' misfortunes?

Protarchus
It must be.

Socrates
Then the argument shows that when we laugh at the foolishness of our friends, we mix pleasure with envy -- which means we're mixing pleasure with pain. Because we've already agreed that envy is a mental pain, and laughter is pleasant. So we envy and laugh at the same moment.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And the argument implies that mixtures of pleasure and pain occur in lamentation, in tragedy and comedy -- not only on the stage but on the much larger stage of human life. And in countless other cases too.

Protarchus
I don't see how anyone could deny what you're saying, Socrates, no matter how badly they want to argue the other side.

Socrates
I mentioned anger, desire, grief, fear, love, jealousy, envy, and similar emotions as examples where we'd find a mixture of those two elements we keep naming. Didn't I?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And you realize that everything we've just concluded applies only to grief, envy, and anger?

Protarchus
I see that.

Socrates
Which means many other cases remain?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And why do you think I went into such detail about the mixture in comedy? To convince you that it's not hard to show the mixed nature of fear, love, and the rest. I figured once I'd given you that illustration, you'd let me off the hook and agree as a general principle that body without soul, soul without body, and the two together are all susceptible to every kind of pleasure-pain mixture -- and further discussion would be unnecessary. So now, do I get to leave? Or will you keep me here until midnight? I think I can earn my release without many more words if I promise to give you an account of all these other cases tomorrow. But right now I'd rather sail in a different direction and move on to the issues that still need settling before we can reach the verdict Philebus is demanding.

Protarchus
Very good, Socrates. Handle what remains however you see fit.

Socrates
After the mixed pleasures, the unmixed ones should have their turn. That's the natural and necessary order.

Protarchus
Excellent.

Socrates
I'll try to describe them now. I don't agree with the people who say all pleasures are just cessation of pain. But as I said, I use them as witnesses that some pleasures only seem to be pleasures but aren't really, and that others, though they appear in many impressive forms, are actually tangled up with pain -- partial relief from intense bodily and mental suffering.

Protarchus
Then which pleasures, Socrates, should we rightly consider true?

Socrates
True pleasures are the ones that come from beauty of color and form, most of those that come from scents, those from sounds, and in general any pleasures where the lack isn't painful or even noticed, and the fulfillment is perceptible, pleasant, and unmixed with pain.

Protarchus
I need you to explain that more clearly, Socrates.

Socrates
What I mean is certainly not obvious, so I'll try to be clearer. When I say "beauty of form," I don't mean the beauty of animals or paintings -- which is probably what most people would assume. I mean straight lines and circles, and the flat and solid shapes made from them with lathes, rulers, and protractors. These, I'm saying, are not merely relatively beautiful, the way other things are. They are eternally and absolutely beautiful, and they have their own distinctive pleasures, completely different from the pleasure of scratching an itch. And there are colors with the same character and similar pleasures. Do you follow?

Protarchus
I'm trying, Socrates. Could you make it even clearer?

Socrates
When sounds are smooth and clear, producing a single pure tone, I'm saying they're not just relatively beautiful but absolutely beautiful, and they carry natural pleasures of their own.

Protarchus
Yes, there are such pleasures.

Socrates
The pleasures of smell are a somewhat less elevated type, but they involve no necessary mixture of pain. And any pleasures, however and wherever experienced, that come without any accompanying pain -- I assign them all to a similar class. So there are two kinds of pure pleasures.

Protarchus
I understand.

Socrates
We can add to these the pleasures of learning -- assuming no hunger for knowledge and no pain from that hunger come first.

Protarchus
That seems right.

Socrates
But what if someone who's full of knowledge later loses it -- aren't there pains of forgetting?

Protarchus
Not necessarily. Though there might be times of reflection when a person feels grief over losing what he knew.

Socrates
True, my friend, but right now we're cataloguing only natural, immediate experiences -- not reflective ones.

Protarchus
In that case you're right: the loss of knowledge isn't accompanied by pain.

Socrates
So the pleasures of learning are unmixed with pain. And they don't belong to the masses but to a very few.

Protarchus
Quite true.

Socrates
Now, having properly separated the pure pleasures from those that can rightly be called impure, let me add this to our description: the pleasures that go to excess have no measure, while those that don't are measured. The great, excessive pleasures -- whether they come more or less often -- belong in the class of the infinite, of the more and less, which flows through both body and soul. And the moderate ones belong to the class of measure.

Protarchus
Exactly right, Socrates.

Socrates
There's still more to consider about pleasures.

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
When we talk about purity and clarity, or about excess, abundance, greatness, and sufficiency -- how do these terms relate to truth?

Protarchus
Why do you ask, Socrates?

Socrates
Because I want to test pleasure and knowledge in every way possible, Protarchus. If there's a pure and an impure element in either of them, I want to isolate the pure element for judgment. That'll make it easier for you, me, and everyone to evaluate them.

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
Let's examine all the pure types. We'll start by selecting a single example.

Protarchus
Which one?

Socrates
Let's start with whiteness.

Protarchus
Very good.

Socrates
What makes whiteness pure? Is it being the greatest in quantity, or the most unadulterated -- the freest from any admixture of other colors?

Protarchus
Obviously the most unadulterated.

Socrates
Right, Protarchus. So the purest white, not the most abundant, is to be considered the truest and most beautiful?

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
And we'd be perfectly right to say that a small amount of pure white is whiter, more beautiful, and truer than a large amount of mixed white.

Protarchus
Perfectly right.

Socrates
Then we don't need a parade of examples to make this point about pleasure. A single case is enough to prove that a small, pure pleasure -- unalloyed with pain -- is always more pleasant, truer, and more beautiful than a large amount of impure pleasure.

Protarchus
Absolutely -- and one example is plenty.

Socrates
But here's another question: haven't we heard that pleasure is always a process of becoming and never has true being? Certain ingenious thinkers teach this, and shouldn't we be grateful to them?

Protarchus
What do they mean?

Socrates
I'll explain by asking you a question, dear Protarchus.

Protarchus
Go ahead.

Socrates
Let's say there are two kinds of things in the world: one that exists for its own sake, and another that's always striving after something else.

Protarchus
What do you mean by these two kinds?

Socrates
One is noble and self-sufficient. The other is subordinate.

Protarchus
That's pretty cryptic.

Socrates
You've seen beautiful, worthy things, and you've seen devoted lovers pursuing them.

Protarchus
I suppose so.

Socrates
Now search the whole universe for two terms that match this pair and are found everywhere.

Protarchus
Please, Socrates -- be a little clearer, for the third time!

Socrates
It's not complicated, Protarchus. The argument is just being playful. It's hinting that some things exist for the sake of something else -- they're means to an end. And other things are the ends themselves -- what the first group serves.

Protarchus
I think your many repetitions are slowing me down, not speeding me up.

Socrates
As the argument goes on, my boy, the meaning will probably get clearer.

Protarchus
I hope so.

Socrates
Here are two more principles.

Protarchus
What are they?

Socrates
One is becoming -- the process of coming into existence. The other is being -- actual existence.

Protarchus
I accept both -- becoming and being.

Socrates
Good. Now would you say that becoming exists for the sake of being, or being for the sake of becoming?

Protarchus
You're asking whether what we call "being" actually exists for the sake of "becoming"?

Socrates
Yes.

Protarchus
By the gods, you'd better repeat that!

Socrates
Let me put it this way, Protarchus: would you say that shipbuilding exists for the sake of ships, or ships for the sake of shipbuilding? And the same question for every similar case.

Protarchus
Why don't you answer that yourself, Socrates?

Socrates
I'm happy to, but you have to participate.

Protarchus
Of course.

Socrates
Here's my answer: all instrumental, remedial, and material things are given to us for the sake of some process of becoming. And each becoming exists for the sake of some particular being or reality. And all becoming, taken as a whole, exists for the sake of all being, taken as a whole.

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
Then pleasure, being a process of becoming, must exist for the sake of some being?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And that which other things serve -- the end they exist for -- must be placed in the class of the good. While that which exists for the sake of something else belongs in a different class, my friend.

Protarchus
Most certainly.

Socrates
So pleasure, being a process of becoming, will rightly be placed in some class other than the good?

Protarchus
Quite right.

Socrates
Then, as I said at the start, we should be very grateful to the thinker who first showed that pleasure is only a process of becoming and has no true being at all. He's obviously laughing at the idea that pleasure could be the good.

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
And he'd surely laugh at people who make becoming their ultimate goal.

Protarchus
Who are you talking about? What do they mean?

Socrates
I'm talking about people who feel delight when they cure their hunger, thirst, or any other deficiency through some process of restoration, and who treat the process itself as though it were pleasure. They say they wouldn't want to live without these experiences, and others like them.

Protarchus
That does seem to be how they think.

Socrates
And everyone agrees that destruction is the opposite of coming-into-being?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
So anyone who makes this choice would be choosing a life of constant becoming and perishing rather than that third kind of life we discussed -- the one with neither pleasure nor pain, but only the purest possible thought.

Protarchus
It's deeply absurd, Socrates, to claim that pleasure is the good.

Socrates
Deeply. And there's yet another absurdity.

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
Isn't it absurd to say that there's nothing good or noble in the body or anywhere else -- that good exists only in the soul, and that the only good of the soul is pleasure? And that courage, self-control, understanding, and every other virtue of the soul are not really good at all? And on top of that, to be forced to say that someone feeling pain and no pleasure is bad at the moment they're suffering -- even if they're the best person alive? And conversely, that someone feeling pleasure is virtuous to the degree they're pleased, in the very act of being pleased?

Protarchus
Nothing could be more irrational than all of that, Socrates.

Socrates
Good. Now, having subjected pleasure to every kind of test, let's not seem too easy on mind and knowledge. Let's strike them hard and see if they ring true or if there's anything unsound. We need to find the purest elements in each, and then bring the truest forms of both pleasure and knowledge forward for judgment.

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
Knowledge has two branches: one productive, and the other educational?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And in the productive arts and crafts, isn't one part more closely related to true knowledge, and the other less so? Can we treat one as the pure element and the other as the impure?

Protarchus
We can.

Socrates
Let's separate out the dominant, superior element in each.

Protarchus
What is it? How do you separate them?

Socrates
Here's how: if you take away arithmetic, measurement, and weighing from any craft, what's left isn't much.

Protarchus
Not much at all.

Socrates
What remains would be just guesswork -- the trained use of the senses that comes from experience and practice, plus a knack for estimating that's commonly called "skill" and is developed through effort and repetition.

Protarchus
Nothing more than that.

Socrates
Music, for instance, is full of this guesswork. Its harmonies are tuned not by measurement but by practiced estimation -- the flute player is always trying to guess the pitch of each vibrating note. So music is mixed up with a great deal of uncertainty and has little precision.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And you'll find the same holds for medicine, farming, navigation, and military strategy.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
But construction, on the other hand, uses a whole array of measures and instruments, and achieves a much higher degree of precision than those other crafts.

Protarchus
How so?

Socrates
In shipbuilding, house-building, and other forms of carpentry, the builder has his ruler, lathe, compass, plumb line, and a very clever device for straightening wood.

Protarchus
Very true, Socrates.

Socrates
So let's divide the arts we've been discussing into two categories: those that, like music, are less exact in their results, and those that, like building, are more exact.

Protarchus
Let's make that division.

Socrates
And of the more exact category, the most exact of all are the ones we just called primary.

Protarchus
You mean arithmetic and the related arts of weighing and measuring.

Socrates
Exactly, Protarchus. But aren't even these divisible into two kinds?

Protarchus
Two kinds?

Socrates
Take arithmetic first. There are two kinds: one popular, one philosophical.

Protarchus
How would you distinguish them?

Socrates
There's a big difference, Protarchus. Some arithmeticians count unequal units -- two armies, two oxen, two very large things or two very small things. The other school insists that every unit in ten thousand must be exactly the same as every other unit.

Protarchus
That is indeed a significant difference, and there's good reason to say there are two kinds of arithmetic.

Socrates
And when we compare the practical measurement used in building with philosophical geometry, or the arithmetic used in commerce with pure mathematical calculation -- should we say each pair is one art or two?

Protarchus
Following the logic of what came before, I'd say each pair is two.

Socrates
Right. But do you see why I've brought all this up?

Protarchus
I think so, but I'd like to hear you say it.

Socrates
The argument has been looking for a parallel to our analysis of pleasure. It's been asking whether one kind of knowledge is purer than another, just as one pleasure is purer than another.

Protarchus
That was clearly the goal.

Socrates
And hasn't the argument already shown that different arts have different domains and vary in their degrees of certainty?

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And just now, the argument first gave a particular art a single name -- making us think it was one art -- and then, as if talking about two different things, asked whether the philosophical version or the practical version has more certainty and purity?

Protarchus
That's exactly the question being raised.

Socrates
So how do we answer, Protarchus?

Protarchus
Socrates, we've reached a point where the difference in clarity between different kinds of knowledge is enormous.

Socrates
Then the answer should be easy.

Protarchus
Certainly. And let our answer be: the arts that involve arithmetic and measurement far surpass all others. And of those, the ones driven by genuine philosophical inquiry are infinitely superior in accuracy and truth.

Socrates
That's your judgment, then. And on your authority, we'll give this answer to anyone who tries to twist our words?

Protarchus
What answer?

Socrates
That there are two kinds of arithmetic, two of measurement, and many other arts that have the same double nature -- yet only one name.

Protarchus
Let's boldly give that answer, Socrates, and hope for the best.

Socrates
So we've identified what we consider the most exact arts and sciences.

Protarchus
Very good.

Socrates
And yet, Protarchus, dialectic will refuse to acknowledge us if we don't give her first place.

Protarchus
And what exactly is dialectic?

Socrates
Obviously the discipline that deals with everything we've been talking about -- all knowledge of being and reality, of sameness and unchangeability. I'm sure anyone with a shred of intelligence would agree that the knowledge dealing with true being and eternal reality is by far the truest knowledge of all. But how would you decide, Protarchus?

Protarchus
I've often heard Gorgias argue that the art of persuasion surpasses every other art by far. It's the best of them all, he says, because everything submits to it -- not by force, but willingly. I wouldn't want to quarrel with either you or him.

Socrates
Sounds like you wanted to desert but were too embarrassed.

Protarchus
Take it however you like.

Socrates
Maybe I've led you into a misunderstanding?

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
My dear Protarchus, I wasn't asking which art is the greatest, the best, or the most useful. I was asking which one has the most clarity, accuracy, and truth -- however humble and impractical it might be. As for Gorgias, he won't quarrel with you if you say his art has the edge in practical usefulness, while the discipline I'm describing is superior in the pursuit of essential truth. It's like our comparison of white colors: a small amount that's pure was said to be truer than a large amount that's mixed. So let's focus carefully. Let's not ask about the comparative usefulness or reputation of the various disciplines, but about the power or faculty -- if there is one -- that the soul has for loving truth and doing everything for its sake. Let's examine the pure element of mind and intelligence, and then we'll be able to say whether this discipline is most likely to possess that faculty, or whether some other has a stronger claim.

Protarchus
Well, I've been thinking about it, and I can hardly imagine any other science or art has a firmer grip on truth than this one.

Socrates
Are you saying that because you notice that the arts in general -- and people engaged in them -- work with opinion and are deeply absorbed in investigating matters of opinion? Even someone who thinks he's studying nature is really dealing with this world -- how things are created, how they act and are acted upon. Isn't that the kind of inquiry that fills his life?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
He's working not with eternal being, but with things that are becoming -- things that will be, have been, or are coming to be.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And can we say that any of these things -- which neither are, nor have been, nor will be unchanging -- can ever become certain when judged by the strict standard of truth?

Protarchus
Impossible.

Socrates
How could anything stable be built on what has no stability?

Protarchus
How indeed?

Socrates
So mind and knowledge, when applied to things that are always changing, can't achieve the highest truth?

Protarchus
I'd think not.

Socrates
Then let's set aside you, me, Philebus, and Gorgias, and make one claim on behalf of the argument itself.

Protarchus
What claim?

Socrates
That what is stable, pure, true, and unmixed belongs to the things that are eternal, unchangeable, and unalloyed. Or if not to those exactly, then to whatever is most closely related to them. Everything else must be ranked in a second, inferior class.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And when it comes to the terms we use for knowing -- shouldn't the finest terms be given to the finest objects?

Protarchus
That's natural.

Socrates
And aren't "mind" and "wisdom" the most honored terms?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And these terms apply most truly and precisely when the mind is engaged in contemplating true being?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And these were the very terms I put forward as the rivals of pleasure?

Protarchus
They were indeed, Socrates.

Socrates
Now then -- as for the mixture. Here are our ingredients: pleasure and wisdom. We're like craftsmen with our materials laid out before us.

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
Time to start mixing?

Protarchus
By all means.

Socrates
But shouldn't we review the key points first, to refresh our memories?

Protarchus
About what?

Socrates
About what I've already said. As the proverb goes, good things are worth saying twice -- even three times.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Well then, by Zeus, let's proceed. I'll try to give a fair summary of the argument.

Protarchus
Let's hear it.

Socrates
Philebus says that pleasure is the true goal of every living being -- that everyone should aim at it, that it's the ultimate good, and that the words "good" and "pleasant" really just name one and the same thing. Socrates, on the other hand, starts by denying this. He goes further: in reality, as in name, they're two different things, and wisdom has a greater share in the good than pleasure does. Isn't that what we were saying, Protarchus?

Protarchus
It is.

Socrates
And wasn't there a further point we agreed on?

Protarchus
What was that?

Socrates
That the good differs from everything else.

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
Whatever possesses the good is always, everywhere, in every way perfectly sufficient -- it never needs anything else.

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
And didn't we try an imaginative separation -- assigning wisdom and pleasure to separate lives, so that pleasure had no wisdom and wisdom had no pleasure?

Protarchus
We did.

Socrates
And did we think either one was sufficient on its own?

Protarchus
Absolutely not.

Socrates
And if we went wrong anywhere, let anyone take up the inquiry again. Assuming that memory, wisdom, knowledge, and sound judgment all belong to the same class, consider: would you want to have pleasure, no matter how abundant or intense, if you couldn't actually perceive that you were enjoying yourself -- couldn't be aware of what you were feeling, couldn't hold even the briefest memory of it? And the same question about wisdom: would you choose all the wisdom in the world if it came with absolutely zero pleasure, rather than having at least some pleasure? Or all the pleasure in the world without a drop of wisdom, rather than having at least some wisdom?

Protarchus
Of course not, Socrates. Why keep repeating the same thing?

Socrates
So neither one, on its own, can possibly be the perfect, universally desirable, completely good life?

Protarchus
Impossible.

Socrates
Then we need to figure out the nature of the good more precisely, so we can properly assign second place.

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
Haven't we already found a road that leads toward the good?

Protarchus
What road?

Socrates
If you needed to find a particular person and you could figure out which house he lived in, that would be a big step toward finding the person himself, wouldn't it?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Well, reason is now telling us -- just as it did at the start -- that we should seek the good not in the unmixed life but in the mixed one.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
We have better hope of finding what we're looking for in a well-mixed life than in an unmixed one?

Protarchus
Far better.

Socrates
Then let's mix, Protarchus! And let's offer a prayer to Dionysus or Hephaestus -- or whichever god oversees the art of mixing.

Protarchus
By all means.

Socrates
Think of us as cupbearers standing beside two fountains. One of them -- pleasure -- can be compared to a fountain of honey. The other -- wisdom -- is a sober, healthy stream with no wine in it, nothing sweet, just clear, wholesome water. From these we need to create the best possible blend.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Tell me first: would we be most likely to succeed if we just dumped in every kind of pleasure with every kind of wisdom?

Protarchus
Maybe.

Socrates
But I think that's risky. I think I can show a safer approach.

Protarchus
What is it?

Socrates
We agreed that one pleasure is truer than another, and one art more exact than another.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And we agreed that there are differences among the branches of knowledge: some deal with the transient and perishable, others with the permanent, eternal, and unchangeable. When judged by the standard of truth, the latter are truer than the former.

Protarchus
Good -- that's right.

Socrates
So if we start by mixing the truest parts of each -- the truest pleasures and the truest knowledge -- would that combination be enough to give us the best possible life? Or would we still need other ingredients?

Protarchus
I think we should do what you're suggesting.

Socrates
Imagine a person who understands justice and has rational insight into its true nature -- and the same for every other reality.

Protarchus
Let's imagine such a person.

Socrates
Would they have enough knowledge if they knew only the divine circle and sphere, but nothing about our human, practical circles and spheres? Could they even build a house using only divine measurements?

Protarchus
Knowledge that's only otherworldly, Socrates, is pretty useless for a human being.

Socrates
So you're saying I should throw the impure, imprecise art -- the one that uses approximate measures and rough estimates -- right into the mixing cup?

Protarchus
We have to, if any of us is ever going to find our way home.

Socrates
And should I include music, which I was just calling full of guesswork and imitation, and short on precision?

Protarchus
I think you have to, if human life is going to be a life at all.

Socrates
All right then -- suppose I give in. Like a doorkeeper who gets pushed and overwhelmed by the crowd, I'll throw the doors wide open and let every kind of knowledge stream in, the pure mingling with the impure.

Protarchus
I don't see that much harm would come of it, Socrates -- not as long as you have the pure kind first.

Socrates
Then shall I let them all flow together into what Homer poetically calls "a meeting of the waters"?

Protarchus
By all means.

Socrates
There -- I've let them all in. Now I need to go back to the fountain of pleasure. We weren't allowed to start by mixing only the true portions of both, as we originally planned. Our love of all knowledge forced us to let every kind of knowledge flow in first, before the pleasures.

Protarchus
That's true.

Socrates
And now it's time to deal with the pleasures. Should we let them all in at once, or only the true ones first?

Protarchus
It's much safer to start with the true ones.

Socrates
Let them flow in, then. And now -- if there are certain necessary pleasures, just as there were necessary arts and sciences, shouldn't we mix those in too?

Protarchus
Yes -- the necessary pleasures certainly have to be included.

Socrates
And if it's harmless and useful to know all the arts throughout our lives, should we say the same about all pleasures -- that every pleasure is good and harmless for everyone at all times? Should we just let them all in?

Protarchus
What should we say about them? What should we do?

Socrates
Don't ask me, Protarchus. Ask the daughters of pleasure and wisdom to speak for themselves.

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
"Tell us, beloved ones -- whether we should call you 'pleasures' or something else -- would you rather live with wisdom or without it?" I think they'd answer like this:

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
They'd say -- as we said before -- that for any single class to exist by itself, pure and isolated, is neither good nor entirely possible. If we must compare one class with another and choose companions, there's no better partner than knowledge of all things, and especially perfect self-knowledge.

Protarchus
And our response would be: "Well spoken!"

Socrates
Exactly. Now let's go back and interrogate wisdom and mind. "Would you like any pleasures in the mixture?" And they'll reply: "What pleasures do you mean?"

Protarchus
Probably so.

Socrates
And we'll follow up: "In addition to the true pleasures, do you want the greatest, most intense pleasures as companions too?" And they'll say: "How could we, Socrates? They're the source of ten thousand problems for us. They disturb the souls we inhabit with their madness, prevent us from being born in the first place, and are usually the ruin of whatever children we do produce -- causing them to be forgotten and neglected through carelessness. But the true and pure pleasures you mentioned -- recognize those as our family. And add the pleasures that go with health and moderation -- the ones that follow every Virtue around like attendants in a goddess's procession, wherever she goes. Mix those in. But to mix in the pleasures that always tag along with foolishness and vice? That would be the height of stupidity for anyone who wants to see a beautiful, perfect mixture and discover from it the highest good in human life and in the universe. It would make no sense to contaminate mind with pleasures that are always accompanied by folly and depravity."

Protarchus
That's an entirely reasonable response, spoken on behalf of wisdom, memory, and sound judgment.

Socrates
But there's still one more essential ingredient for any mixture.

Protarchus
What is it?

Socrates
Unless truth is part of the composition, nothing can truly be created or endure.

Protarchus
Impossible.

Socrates
Quite impossible. And now, you and Philebus need to tell me: is anything still missing from the mixture? As I see it, the argument is now complete -- it's like an immaterial law that will govern a living body with fairness.

Protarchus
I agree with you, Socrates.

Socrates
And can we say that we're now standing at the doorstep of the good?

Protarchus
I think we are.

Socrates
Then what element in the mixture is the most precious -- the main reason why everyone finds this kind of life desirable? Once we discover that, we'll ask whether this quality is more closely related to pleasure or to mind.

Protarchus
Exactly right -- that will help us make the final judgment.

Socrates
And it's not hard to see what makes any mixture either supremely valuable or worthless.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Everyone knows this.

Protarchus
Knows what?

Socrates
That any mixture lacking measure and proportion is inevitably fatal -- both to the ingredients and to the mixture itself. It's not really a mixture at all, just a jumbled mess that brings ruin on whoever has it.

Protarchus
Absolutely true.

Socrates
And now the power of the good has taken refuge in the territory of the beautiful. Because measure and proportion are beauty and excellence -- everywhere and always.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And we said that truth must be part of the mixture too.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
So if we can't capture the good with a single idea, let's use three: Beauty, Proportion, and Truth. We can treat these three together as the single cause of the mixture's goodness -- the reason the mixture is good is because these qualities have been infused into it.

Protarchus
Exactly right.

Socrates
And now, Protarchus, anyone could easily judge whether pleasure or wisdom is more closely related to the highest good and more honored among gods and humans.

Protarchus
That's clear enough, but let's finish the argument properly.

Socrates
Let's take each of the three -- Beauty, Truth, Proportion -- and examine them one at a time in relation to pleasure and mind. We need to see which of the two each one is more closely related to.

Protarchus
You mean beauty, truth, and measure?

Socrates
Yes, Protarchus. Take truth first. Review mind, truth, and pleasure in your mind, then pause and ask yourself: is pleasure or mind more closely related to truth?

Protarchus
No need to pause -- the difference is obvious. Pleasure is the world's greatest impostor. As the saying goes, even the gods forgive perjury in matters of sexual pleasure -- because pleasures, like children, don't have a particle of reason in them. Mind, on the other hand, is either the same thing as truth, or the closest thing to truth that exists.

Socrates
Next, let's look at measure the same way. Does pleasure have more measure than wisdom, or wisdom more than pleasure?

Protarchus
Another easy question. I can't imagine anything more excessive than the transports of pleasure, or anything more measured and proportionate than mind and knowledge.

Socrates
Very good. But there's still the third test. Does mind have a greater share of beauty than pleasure? Is mind or pleasure the more beautiful?

Protarchus
No one, Socrates -- awake or dreaming -- has ever seen or imagined wisdom or mind to be ugly or unseemly, at any time, in any way.

Socrates
Right.

Protarchus
But when we see someone indulging in pleasures -- especially the most intense ones -- the ridiculous or disgusting nature of what they're doing makes us ashamed. We hide them away and push them into darkness, feeling they shouldn't see the light of day.

Socrates
Then you'll proclaim it everywhere, Protarchus -- to this company and to the world -- that pleasure is not the first of possessions, nor even the second. The first prize goes to measure, the mean, the fitting, and all things of that kind -- the eternal nature has been found there.

Protarchus
Yes, that seems to be where the argument has led us.

Socrates
In the second class belongs the symmetrical, the beautiful, the complete, the sufficient, and everything in that family.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And if you place mind and wisdom in the third class, I don't think you'll be far wrong.

Protarchus
I'd think not.

Socrates
And wouldn't you put in the fourth class the goods we said belong specifically to the soul -- the sciences, arts, and true opinions, as we called them? They come after the third class and form the fourth, since they're certainly more closely related to the good than pleasure is.

Protarchus
Surely.

Socrates
The fifth class would be the pleasures we defined as painless -- the pure pleasures of the soul itself, as we called them -- the ones that accompany knowledge and sometimes sensation.

Protarchus
Perhaps.

Socrates
And now, as Orpheus says,

  "With the sixth generation, let the glory of my song come to an end."

Here, at the sixth place, let us finish. All that remains is to crown our discussion.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
Then let's sum up and restate what we've said, offering the third libation to savior Zeus.

Protarchus
How so?

Socrates
Philebus claimed that pleasure was always and absolutely the good.

Protarchus
I see -- this "third libation" you mentioned, Socrates, was meant as a final recapitulation.

Socrates
Yes. But listen to what comes next. Convinced of what I've been saying, and indignant at a doctrine maintained not just by Philebus but by thousands of others, I declared that mind is a far better, far more excellent element of human life than pleasure.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
But suspecting there were other things better still, I went on to say that if something turned out to be better than both, I'd claim second place for mind over pleasure -- and pleasure would lose second place as well as first.

Protarchus
You did say that.

Socrates
And nothing could be more thoroughly demonstrated than the inadequacy of both.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
The claims of both pleasure and mind to be the absolute good have been completely disproven in this argument. Both of them lack self-sufficiency, both are inadequate, both are imperfect.

Protarchus
Absolutely right.

Socrates
But though both must step aside in favor of another, mind is ten thousand times closer and more akin to the nature of the winner than pleasure is.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And according to the ranking we've just established, pleasure comes in fifth.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
Not first -- no, not even if all the cattle, horses, and animals in the world, in their pursuit of enjoyment, declare her to be so. The masses trust these animals the way fortune-tellers trust birds. They decide that animal appetites are better evidence than the insights of divine philosophy.

Protarchus
And now, Socrates, we all declare that what you've been saying is the truth.

Socrates
So will you let me go?

Protarchus
There's a little bit left, and I'll remind you of it -- because I'm sure you won't be the first to walk away from an argument.
