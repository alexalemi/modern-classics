Euthydemus (Part 1)

Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates, who narrates the whole thing to Crito
  Crito
  Cleinias
  Euthydemus
  Dionysodorus
  Ctesippus

Scene: The Lyceum


Crito
So who was that person you were talking with yesterday at the Lyceum, Socrates? There was such a mob around you I couldn't get close enough to hear anything. But I caught a glimpse of him over everyone's heads, and it looked like you were talking with some foreigner. Who was he?

Socrates
There were two of them, Crito. Which one do you mean?

Crito
The one sitting second from you on the right. In the middle was young Cleinias, son of Axiochus — he's really shot up, by the way. He's about the same age as my Critobulus, but he's way ahead of him, and very handsome. The other one looked thin and younger than he probably is.

Socrates
The one you mean is Euthydemus. And on my left was his brother Dionysodorus, who also got in on the conversation.

Crito
I don't know either of them, Socrates. Another fresh import of Sophists, I take it? Where are they from, and what's their particular brand of wisdom?

Socrates
As for their origin, I believe they're originally from this part of the world. They migrated from Chios to Thurii, got driven out of Thurii, and have been knocking around these parts for years now. As for their wisdom — well, Crito, it's something else. Absolutely staggering. I never knew what a true all-around fighter looked like until I met these two. They're basically one-stop shopping for combat. And I don't mean like the Acarnanian brothers, who only fight with their bodies — oh no. This pair of heroes is not only unbeatable in physical fighting, they're invincible in every kind of warfare. They're experts at fighting in armor and will teach the skill to anyone who pays. They're also brilliant at legal warfare — they'll argue cases themselves and teach others how to speak and compose speeches that win in court. And all of that? That was just the warm-up. Now they've taken the all-around combat art to its absolute pinnacle, mastering the one form of fighting they'd previously neglected. Now nobody dares even stand up to them — that's how devastating they are in the war of words. They can refute any proposition, whether it's true or false. So I'm thinking, Crito, of putting myself in their hands. They claim they can pass on their skill to anyone in no time.

Crito
But Socrates, aren't you a bit old for that? I'd be worried about that.

Socrates
Not at all, Crito, and here's why I'm not worried: they only picked up this art of argumentation quite late in life themselves. Last year, or maybe the year before, they didn't have any of this new wisdom. My only real concern is that I might embarrass them — the same way I've embarrassed Connus, son of Metrobius, the harp teacher, who's still giving me music lessons. The boys who attend his classes see me showing up alongside them, and they laugh and call him "Grandpa's teacher." So I'm afraid these two foreigners might get the same treatment. The fear of being mocked might make them refuse to take me on. That's why, Crito, I plan to recruit some fellow old-timers to come along with me — just like I recruited them to go to Connus with me. I'm hoping you'll be one of them. And maybe we should bring your sons along as bait — they'll want the boys as students and agree to take us as part of the deal.

Crito
I have no objection to that, Socrates. But first, give me a proper account of their wisdom so I know what we're signing up for.

Socrates
You'll hear it all in no time, because I can't claim I wasn't paying attention — I watched them like a hawk and remember everything, and I'll do my best to tell you the whole story. As it happened, I was sitting by myself in the changing room of the Lyceum — the very spot where you saw me — and was just about to leave. Then I felt the familiar divine sign, so I sat back down. A little while later, the two brothers, Euthydemus and Dionysodorus, came in with a bunch of others — their students, I believe — and started walking around in the covered court. They hadn't made more than two or three laps when Cleinias walked in. He really has improved, as you rightly say. Trailing behind him was a whole entourage of admirers, including Ctesippus of Paeania — a well-bred young man, but with all the wildness you'd expect from youth. Cleinias spotted me from the entrance, sitting by myself, and came straight over to sit on my right, just as you described. When Dionysodorus and Euthydemus noticed him, they first stopped and talked to each other, glancing our way now and then — I was watching them carefully. Then Euthydemus came and sat down next to the young man, and Dionysodorus sat on my left. The rest scattered wherever they could find seats.

I greeted the brothers — I hadn't seen them in ages — and then said to Cleinias, "Here are two wise men, Cleinias — Euthydemus and Dionysodorus. And I don't mean wise in some small way. These two know everything about warfare — everything a good general needs to know about strategy, commanding troops, and the whole art of fighting in armor. And they know about law too — they can teach a man how to use the weapons of the courts when someone's done him wrong."

They heard me say all this and just looked down on me. I noticed them exchange a glance, and they both laughed. Then Euthydemus said, "Those subjects, Socrates, are things we no longer pursue seriously. For us they're just side projects."

"My God!" I said. "If subjects like those are merely side projects to you, your main business must be something truly extraordinary. Tell me, I'm begging you — what is this noble study?"

"Teaching virtue, Socrates," he replied. "That's our principal occupation. And we believe we can teach it better and faster than anyone."

"By the gods!" I said. "And where did you learn that? I always thought — as I was just telling Cleinias — that your chief talent was fighting in armor. That's what you were advertising when you were here before. But if you really do have this other knowledge now — well, forgive me! I'm addressing you as I would superior beings, and I beg your pardon for my earlier remarks. But are you absolutely sure about this, Dionysodorus and Euthydemus? The promise is so enormous that a certain incredulity creeps over me."

"You have our word, Socrates."

"Then I consider you luckier than the Great King of Persia with his whole empire. But please tell me — do you plan to give a demonstration of your wisdom? Or what's the plan?"

"That's exactly why we've come, Socrates — to demonstrate, and also to teach anyone who wants to learn."

"Oh, I can promise you," I said, "that every person who lacks virtue will want to learn. I'll be first in line. And here's young Cleinias, and Ctesippus over there, and several others" — I pointed to Cleinias's admirers, who were gathering around us.

Now Ctesippus had been sitting at some distance from Cleinias. When Euthydemus leaned forward to talk with me, he was blocking Ctesippus's view of Cleinias, who was between us. So Ctesippus — partly because he wanted to gaze at his beloved and partly because he was interested — jumped up and stood facing us. All the other admirers of Cleinias, along with the students of Euthydemus and Dionysodorus, followed suit. These were the people I pointed out to Euthydemus, telling him they were all eager to learn. Ctesippus and the whole crowd enthusiastically confirmed it and urged him to show off the power of his wisdom.

So I said, "Euthydemus and Dionysodorus, I'm earnestly asking you to do me and this whole company the favor of giving a demonstration. Now, showing us the whole thing might be a big undertaking, but tell me just this one thing: can you make someone good only if he's already convinced he needs to learn from you? Or can you also work on someone who isn't convinced — either because he doesn't think virtue can be taught at all, or because he doesn't think you two are the right teachers? Does your art have the power to persuade even that kind of skeptic that virtue can be taught — and that you're the ones to learn it from?"

"Absolutely, Socrates," said Dionysodorus. "Our art can do both."

"And you and your brother, Dionysodorus," I said, "are the people most likely to inspire him to pursue philosophy and the study of virtue?"

"Yes, Socrates, I rather think we are."

"Then here's what I'd like you to do: skip the rest of the demonstration for now and just try to persuade this young man that he should become a philosopher and devote himself to virtue. Do that, and you'll be doing me and everyone here a tremendous favor. Here's the situation: we're all deeply concerned about this young man. His name is Cleinias, son of Axiochus, grandson of the famous Alcibiades, and cousin of the current Alcibiades. He's quite young, and we're naturally worried that someone might get to him first and point his mind in the wrong direction, and he'll be ruined. So your timing couldn't be better. If you don't mind, please have a go at the young man and talk with him right here in front of us."

Those were more or less my exact words. And Euthydemus, in a confident and encouraging tone, replied, "No objection at all, Socrates — as long as the young man is willing to answer questions."

"Oh, he's quite used to that," I said. "His friends are always coming to him with questions and arguments, so he's right at home with the format."

Now, Crito — what happened next, how can I possibly do it justice? It's no small task to rehearse infinite wisdom in its every detail. So like the poets, I really ought to begin by invoking Memory and the Muses.

Euthydemus, if I remember correctly, kicked things off roughly like this: "Tell me, Cleinias — when people learn things, is it the wise who learn, or the ignorant?"

The boy, overwhelmed by the question, blushed and looked at me for help. I could see he was flustered, so I said, "Take courage, Cleinias, and answer like a man — whichever option you think is right. Trust me, you're going to get enormous benefit from their questions."

Meanwhile, Dionysodorus leaned over to whisper in my ear, his face beaming with laughter: "I'm going to prophesy something for you, Socrates. Whichever way he answers, he's going to get refuted."

While he was whispering to me, Cleinias gave his answer — so I didn't get a chance to warn the boy about the trap he was walking into. He answered that the wise are the ones who learn.

Euthydemus continued: "There are people you'd call teachers, right?"

The boy agreed.

"And they're the teachers of the people who learn? Like how your grammar teacher and music teacher used to teach you and the other boys — and you were the learners?"

Yes.

"And when you were learning those things, you didn't yet know what you were learning?"

"No," he said.

"And were you wise back then?"

"No, definitely not," he said.

"But if you weren't wise, you were ignorant?"

"Yes."

"So when you were learning things you didn't know, you were ignorant while you were learning?"

The boy nodded.

"Then it's the ignorant who learn, Cleinias — not the wise, as you said."

At this, the followers of Euthydemus — the little fan club I mentioned — burst out laughing and cheering like a chorus on cue from their director.

Then, before the boy had a chance to catch his breath, Dionysodorus swooped in and took over. "Yes, Cleinias — and when your grammar teacher dictated something to you, which boys were the ones who actually learned the dictation? The smart ones, or the slow ones?"

"The smart ones," said Cleinias.

"Then after all, it's the wise who learn, not the ignorant — so the answer you just gave Euthydemus was wrong."

Once again the fans of the two heroes erupted in ecstatic laughter and applause at their wisdom, while the rest of us sat there silent and stunned.

Euthydemus noticed this and decided to press on with the boy. To heighten the effect, he threw out another question of the same kind — like the double spin of an expert dancer. "Tell me," he said, "do people who learn things learn what they already know, or what they don't know?"

Dionysodorus leaned over and whispered to me again: "That one, Socrates, is just another of the same sort."

"Good heavens," I said, "and the last question was so brilliant!"

"All our questions are like that, Socrates," he replied. "Inescapable."

"I can see why you have such a reputation among your students," I said.

Meanwhile, Cleinias had answered Euthydemus that people learn what they don't know. And Euthydemus put him through the same wringer as before.

"You know your letters?"

Yes.

"All of them?"

Yes.

"And when the teacher dictates to you, he's dictating letters?"

Yes.

"And if you know all the letters, then what he dictates is something you already know?"

Cleinias had to admit this.

"So," said Euthydemus, "you're not actually learning what he dictates — only someone who doesn't know their letters would be learning."

"But I do learn!" said Cleinias.

"Ah, but you learn what you already know — since you know all the letters."

He had to admit that too.

"So you were wrong in your answer," Euthydemus declared.

The words were barely out of his mouth when Dionysodorus snatched up the argument like catching a ball on the fly and took another shot at the boy. "Cleinias," he said, "Euthydemus is tricking you. Tell me — isn't learning the process of acquiring knowledge of whatever you're learning?"

Cleinias agreed.

"And knowing means already having knowledge at the time?"

Yes.

"And not knowing means not having knowledge at the time?"

He admitted it.

"And the people who acquire something — are they the ones who already have it, or the ones who don't?"

"The ones who don't."

"And you've admitted that those who don't know are among those who don't have?"

He nodded.

"So those who learn belong to the class of those who acquire — those who don't yet have — rather than those who already do?"

Yes.

"So, Cleinias," he said, "it's those who don't know who learn, not those who know."

Now Euthydemus was gearing up to give the boy a third throw, but I could see Cleinias was in deep water. I wanted to give him a break before he lost heart, so I said reassuringly, "Don't be surprised, Cleinias, if their way of arguing seems strange. You may not realize what these two visitors are actually doing with you. They're just initiating you, the way the Corybantes do in the mysteries. This is the enthronement ceremony — the part that comes with dancing and entertainment, if you've ever been through an initiation. Right now they're just dancing around you, and next they'll proceed to the actual initiation. So imagine you're going through the preliminary stage of the Sophist ritual, which, as Prodicus says, begins with learning the correct use of words.

"What our two distinguished visitors are trying to show you is that you didn't realize the word 'to learn' has two different meanings. It can mean acquiring knowledge of something you previously didn't know, but it can also mean reviewing or examining something using knowledge you already have. That second sense is usually called 'knowing' rather than 'learning,' but the word 'learning' gets used for both. You didn't notice that they were using the same word in two opposite senses — applying it both to those who know and to those who don't know. The second question was a similar trick: when they asked whether people learn what they know or what they don't know, they were just playing the same game.

"These are the party tricks of learning. They're not serious. That's why I call these gentlemen playful — they're just having fun with you. If a man mastered every trick of this kind, he wouldn't be one bit wiser. He'd only be able to trip people up with word games, like someone who yanks a stool out from under you right as you're sitting down, then cracks up laughing when you're sprawled on the floor. So consider everything that's happened so far as pure entertainment.

"But what comes next — I'm certain — is when they'll show you their serious side and keep their promise. I'll lead the way and show them the kind of thing I mean. They promised to give us a demonstration of how to encourage a young man toward philosophy, but I suppose they wanted to tease you a bit first.

"So now, Euthydemus and Dionysodorus, enough playing around. Let's see you explain to this young man how he should devote himself to the pursuit of virtue and wisdom. But first, I'll show you what I have in mind — the kind of discussion I want to hear. And if I do this in a clumsy, amateurish way, don't laugh at me. I'm only improvising in front of you because I'm so eager to hear your wisdom. So please — you and your students — hold back the laughter.

"Now, son of Axiochus, let me ask you: Don't all people want to be happy? Or is that one of those ridiculous questions that shouldn't even be asked, because what human being doesn't want to be happy?"

"Everyone wants to be happy," said Cleinias. "There's no one who doesn't."

"Good," I said. "So since we all want happiness, how do we get it? Wouldn't we be happy if we had many good things? And that's probably an even simpler question than the first — the answer's obvious."

He agreed.

"So what sorts of things do we consider good? This doesn't require some solemn sage to answer — anyone would say that wealth is a good."

"Certainly," he said.

"And health and good looks are goods — and other natural gifts?"

He agreed.

"And can there be any doubt that good birth, power, and honors in your own country are goods?"

He agreed.

"Then what other goods are there?" I said. "What about self-control, justice, courage — do you honestly think, Cleinias, that we'd be more right to count these as goods than not? Someone might actually argue about this. What do you say?"

"They're goods," said Cleinias.

"Very well," I said. "And where do we put wisdom in our lineup — among the goods, or not?"

"Among the goods."

"Now think," I said. "Have we left out any important goods?"

"I don't think so," said Cleinias.

"Actually, wait," I said. "I'm afraid we've left out the biggest one of all."

"Which one?" he asked.

"Good luck, Cleinias. Everyone — even the most foolish people — agrees that's the greatest good of all."

"True," he said.

"Hold on," I said. "Actually, son of Axiochus, you and I nearly made fools of ourselves in front of these visitors."

"What do you mean?"

"Because we've already listed good luck — and now we're about to say it again. We'd be repeating ourselves."

"How so?"

"Well, it's pretty silly to put something on the list when it's already there, and say the same thing twice."

He asked what I meant, and I said: "Wisdom IS good luck. Even a child could see that."

The innocent young man was amazed. Seeing his surprise, I said, "Think about it, Cleinias. Who has the best luck when it comes to playing the flute — isn't it the skilled flute players?"

He agreed.

"And with reading and writing — the skilled scribes are the luckiest?"

Yes.

"What about the dangers of the sea — aren't wise pilots luckier overall than foolish ones?"

"Definitely."

"And if you were at war, whose company would you rather be in — a wise general's or a foolish one's?"

"A wise one's."

"And if you were sick, who would you rather have by your side in a dangerous illness — a wise doctor or an ignorant one?"

"A wise one."

"So you'd say," I said, "that acting with a wise person is luckier than acting with an ignorant one?"

He agreed.

"Then wisdom always makes people fortunate. Because with wisdom, a person would never go wrong, so they'd always act rightly and succeed — otherwise it wouldn't be wisdom."

After some back and forth, we eventually agreed on this general conclusion: whoever has wisdom has no need of luck.

Then I reminded him where we'd been. "Remember," I said, "we agreed that we'd be happy and fortunate if we had many good things?"

He nodded.

"But would having good things actually make us happy if they didn't benefit us?"

"Only if they benefited us," he said.

"And would they benefit us if we just had them but never used them? Like, if we had mountains of food but never ate any, or oceans of drink but never drank — would that do us any good?"

"Of course not."

"Or what about a craftsman who has every tool he needs for his work but never uses them — would he be any better off? Like a carpenter with all his tools and plenty of wood who never actually makes anything?"

"Not at all," he said.

"And if someone had wealth and every good thing we just listed but never used any of it — would he be happy because he possessed all that stuff?"

"No way, Socrates."

"So," I said, "a person who wants to be happy doesn't just need to have good things — he has to use them. There's no advantage in just having them sitting around."

"Right."

"Well, Cleinias, is having and using good things enough to make you happy?"

"Yes, I think so."

"Even if you use them badly? Or does it matter whether you use them rightly or wrongly?"

"You have to use them rightly."

"Exactly right," I said. "And using something the wrong way is actually much worse than not using it at all. Wrong use is a positive evil, while non-use is neither good nor evil. Agreed?"

He agreed.

"Now, in working with wood, what produces the right use? Isn't it just the carpenter's knowledge?"

"Nothing else," he said.

"And in making tools and equipment, isn't it knowledge that guides the right way of making them?"

Yes.

"And with those goods we mentioned at the start — wealth, health, beauty — isn't knowledge what guides us to use them rightly and well?"

He agreed.

"So in every possession and every use of a thing, knowledge is what gives a person not just luck but success?"

He agreed again.

"So tell me," I said, "what good are possessions to anyone without good sense and wisdom? Would a person be better off having and doing many things without wisdom, or a few things with wisdom? Think about it: if he did fewer things, he'd make fewer mistakes. Fewer mistakes means fewer disasters. And fewer disasters means less misery."

"Definitely," he said.

"And who would do less — a poor man or a rich man?"

"A poor man."

"A weak man or a strong man?"

"A weak man."

"A nobody or a person of high status?"

"A nobody."

"And a coward would do less than a brave man? And a lazy person less than an energetic one?"

He agreed.

"And a slow person less than a quick one? And someone with dull senses less than someone with sharp ones?"

All of this we mutually accepted.

"So, Cleinias," I said, "here's the bottom line: all those things we called goods aren't actually good in themselves. It works like this. Under the guidance of wisdom, they're greater goods than their opposites — because they're more capable of serving the wise principle that directs them. Under the guidance of ignorance, they're greater evils — because they're more capable of serving the foolish principle that rules them. And in themselves, they're nothing, neither good nor bad."

"That," he said, "is obvious."

"So what's the conclusion of everything we've said? It's this: nothing else really matters. Wisdom is the only true good, and ignorance is the only true evil."

He agreed.

"Then let's take the next step," I said. "We've established that all people want happiness. Happiness comes from using the things of life — and using them rightly. Right use comes from knowledge. Therefore the conclusion is clear: everyone should do everything in their power to make themselves as wise as possible."

"Yes," he said.

"And when you realize this treasure is more valuable than money, it's no disgrace to seek wisdom from a father, a guardian, a friend, an admirer — anyone, citizen or foreigner. Whatever honorable thing you need to do or say to get someone to share their wisdom with you, there's no shame in it. Do you agree?"

"Yes," he said. "I completely agree. You're right."

"That is," I said, "if wisdom can be taught and doesn't just come to people on its own. That's a point we haven't settled between us yet —"

"But I do think it can be taught, Socrates," he said.

"Excellent!" I said. "I'm delighted to hear you say that — and grateful to you for saving me from a long, tedious investigation into whether wisdom can be taught. So now, since you believe it can be taught, and that wisdom alone can make a person happy and fortunate, wouldn't you say we all ought to love wisdom? And will you, personally, try to pursue it?"

"Absolutely, Socrates," he said. "With everything I've got."

I was pleased to hear it. Then I turned to Dionysodorus and Euthydemus and said, "There you go. That's an example — clumsy and tedious, I admit — of the sort of encouragement I'd like you to give. And I hope one of you will now do what I've been doing but in a more polished style. Or at least pick up where I left off and show the young man whether he needs to acquire every kind of knowledge, or whether there's one particular kind that will make him good and happy — and what that knowledge is. Because as I said at the start, what matters most to us is this young man's improvement in virtue and wisdom."

That's what I said, Crito, and then I sat back, all ears, waiting to see what would come next. I wanted to see how they'd tackle the question — where they'd start in encouraging the young man to pursue wisdom and virtue. Dionysodorus, the older brother, spoke first. Everyone's eyes were on him, expecting something amazing. And they weren't far wrong, because the man, Crito, launched into a truly remarkable speech — wonderfully persuasive, if you think of it as an encouragement to virtue.

"Tell me," he said, "Socrates, and the rest of you who say you want this young man to become wise — are you joking, or are you actually serious?"

This made me think they'd assumed we were joking earlier when we asked them to talk with the boy, and that's why they'd been joking around and not being serious. So I was even more emphatic: "We are absolutely, profoundly serious."

Dionysodorus said, "Think carefully, Socrates. You might end up having to take back your words."

"I've thought carefully," I said. "And I'll never take back my words."

"All right," he said. "So you're saying you want Cleinias to become wise?"

"Absolutely."

"And right now he's not wise?"

"Well, his modesty wouldn't allow him to claim he is."

"So you want him to become wise," Dionysodorus said, "and to stop being ignorant?"

"That's right."

"You want him to become what he is not, and to no longer be what he is?"

I was completely thrown off by this.

Pressing his advantage while I was still reeling, he added: "You want him to no longer be what he is — which can only mean you want him to die. What lovely friends and admirers you must be, wanting your dear boy to cease existing!"

When Ctesippus heard this, he got seriously angry — as any good boyfriend would — and said, "Stranger from Thurii — if politeness allowed it I'd say something a lot worse — how dare you tell such a disgusting lie about me and the others, that we want Cleinias to die! It's practically unspeakable!"

Euthydemus cut in: "And do you think, Ctesippus, that it's actually possible to tell a lie?"

"Yes," said Ctesippus. "I'd have to be insane to think otherwise."

"When you tell a lie, do you say the thing you're speaking about, or not?"

"You say the thing you're speaking about."

"And the person who says a thing says that thing and no other?"

"Yes," said Ctesippus.

"And that thing he says is one definite thing, distinct from everything else?"

Yes.

"And the person who says that thing says what is?"

Yes.

"And whoever says what is speaks the truth. So Dionysodorus, if he's saying what is, is speaking the truth about you and telling no lie."

"Yes, Euthydemus," said Ctesippus, "but when he says what he said, he's saying what is not."

Euthydemus replied: "And what is not — doesn't exist?"

Right.

"And what doesn't exist is nowhere?"

Right.

"And can anyone do anything to what doesn't exist? Can anyone do to Cleinias something that doesn't exist and is nowhere?"

"I guess not," said Ctesippus.

"Well then — when politicians speak in the assembly, they're doing something, aren't they?"

"Yes, they're doing something," he said.

"And doing is a kind of making?"

Yes.

"And speaking is doing, which is making?"

Yes.

"Then nobody says what is not — because in saying it he'd be doing something, and you've already agreed that nobody can do what doesn't exist. Therefore, by your own logic, nobody speaks falsely. So if Dionysodorus says anything at all, he's saying what is true."

"Yes, Euthydemus," said Ctesippus, "but he's talking about things in a way that doesn't match how they actually are."

"Ctesippus," said Dionysodorus, "are you claiming that anyone talks about things as they actually are?"

"Yes," he said. "Any decent, honest person does."

"And aren't good things good and bad things bad?"

He agreed.

"And you say that decent people describe things as they are?"

Yes.

"Then decent people speak badly of bad things — since they describe things as they are!"

"They certainly do," said Ctesippus. "And they speak badly of bad people too. So let me give you some friendly advice — watch out, or they'll speak badly of you, since I can assure you that good people speak badly of the bad."

"And do they speak big things of the big," Euthydemus jumped in, "and hot things of the hot?"

"Absolutely," said Ctesippus. "And they speak coldly of those who are cold — the way these cold dialecticians are."

"You're being abusive, Ctesippus," said Dionysodorus. "You are being abusive!"

"Not at all, Dionysodorus," he replied. "I actually like you, and I'm just giving you friendly advice. I'm trying to convince you, as a friend, not to be so rude as to say right in front of me that I want my beloved — whom I value above all people — to die."

I could see they were getting heated with each other, so I cracked a joke. "Ctesippus," I said, "I think we have to let our visitors use language however they like and not fight over words. We should be grateful for what they give us. If they know how to 'destroy' people in a way that turns bad, foolish people into good, wise ones — whether this is their own discovery or something they learned — this new kind of death and destruction that lets them get rid of a bad person and turn him into a good one — if they know how to do this, and they clearly do (they just told us that their newly invented art was exactly this) — then let them, in their own terminology, destroy the young man and make him wise. The rest of us too, if they like. But if you young people are nervous about being their test subjects, then let the experiment be tried on old me! Like Medea and her cauldron — I'll offer my elderly person to Dionysodorus. He can kill me, boil me, do whatever he wants — as long as he makes me good in the end."

Ctesippus said, "And I'm volunteering too, Socrates! Let the strangers skin me alive if they want — I'm pretty well skinned by them already — as long as my skin ends up not like poor Marsyas's, turned into a leather bag, but into something made of virtue. And here's Dionysodorus thinking I'm angry with him, when I'm not angry at all. I'm just pushing back when I think he's saying something wrong about me. But don't confuse contradiction with abuse, my illustrious Dionysodorus — they're completely different things."

"Contradiction?" said Dionysodorus. "There's no such thing as contradiction."

"Of course there is," said Ctesippus. "There can't be any question about it. You're not going to tell me, Dionysodorus, that there's no such thing?"

"You'll never prove to me," he said, "that you've ever heard one person contradict another."

"Really?" said Ctesippus. "Then listen right now, because you can hear me contradicting Dionysodorus."

"Can you back that up?"

"Certainly," he said.

"All right then. Doesn't everything have words that describe it?"

Yes.

"Describing its existence or its non-existence?"

"Its existence."

"Right, Ctesippus — and we just proved, if you recall, that nobody can assert a negative. Nobody can affirm what is not."

"So what?" said Ctesippus. "You and I can still contradict each other."

"But can we contradict each other," said Dionysodorus, "if we're both describing the same thing? In that case we'd be saying the same thing, wouldn't we?"

He had to agree.

"And what if neither of us is talking about the same thing? Then neither of us is saying anything about it at all."

He conceded that too.

"But what if I describe one thing and you describe something different — or I say something and you say nothing — is that a contradiction? How can the person who speaks contradict the person who doesn't speak at all?"

Ctesippus fell silent. And I, absolutely amazed, said, "What do you mean, Dionysodorus? I've heard this argument many times before, and I'm always astonished by it. The followers of Protagoras used to be fond of it, and others before them. But it seems to me perfectly marvelous — and wonderfully self-destructive. Here's what it comes down to: there's no such thing as saying something false. When a person speaks, he either speaks the truth or says nothing at all. Isn't that your position?"

He agreed.

"But even if he can't speak falsely — can he think falsely?"

"No, he can't," he said.

"So there's no such thing as a false opinion?"

"No."

"Then there's no such thing as ignorance — no people who are ignorant? Because isn't ignorance — if such a thing exists — just getting the facts wrong?"

"Certainly," he said.

"But that's impossible?"

"Impossible," he said.

"Are you saying this as a clever paradox, Dionysodorus, or do you seriously believe no person is ignorant?"

"Refute me," he said.

"But how can I refute you, if — as you say — it's impossible for anyone to speak falsely?"

"Exactly right," said Euthydemus.

"And I never asked you to refute me," said Dionysodorus. "How can I ask you to do something that doesn't exist?"

"Euthydemus," I said, "I confess these subtle and brilliant devices of wisdom are a bit above my pay grade. I'm afraid I can barely follow them, so forgive me if I ask what might seem like a really stupid question. If there's no such thing as falsehood, or false opinion, or ignorance — then there can't be any such thing as making a mistake either. A person can't get wrong what he's doing, because he's doing what he's doing — right? Isn't that what you're saying?"

"Yes," he said.

"All right — here's my stupid question. If nobody can make a mistake in action, word, or thought, then what on earth have you two come here to teach? Weren't you just telling us you could teach virtue to anyone willing to learn better than anyone else?"

"Socrates," Dionysodorus shot back, "are you really such an old fossil that you're dragging up what I said at the beginning? What's next — are you going to throw my words from last year in my face? You can't deal with what I'm saying now?"

"Well," I said, "your arguments aren't easy to deal with. They're the words of wise men. And actually, I'm not sure what to make of that word 'nonplussed' you just used. What exactly do you mean by it, Dionysodorus? You must mean I can't refute your argument. Tell me if the word means something else."

"No," he said, "it means what you said. Now answer my question."

"What — before you answer mine, Dionysodorus?" I said.

"Answer," he said.

"Is that fair?"

"Perfectly fair," he said.

"On what principle?" I said. "I can only assume you're some supremely wise man who shows up claiming to be a master logician and knows exactly when to answer and when not to. And right now you won't open your mouth because you know you don't have to."

"Stop chattering," he said, "and answer the question."

"All right," I said. "I suppose I must obey, since you're the boss. Ask away."

"Do things that have minds — are they alive or lifeless?"

"They're alive."

"And do you know of any word that's alive?"

"Can't say I do."

"Then why did you ask me just now what 'sense' my words had?"

"Well, because I was being stupid and made a mistake. Or then again — maybe I was right after all to say that words have sense. What do you say, wise man? If I wasn't mistaken, then even you can't refute me, and all your wisdom is stuck. But if I did make a mistake, then you're wrong when you say mistakes are impossible — and you didn't say that very long ago either. I'm starting to think, Dionysodorus and Euthydemus, that this argument is stuck right where it was and isn't going anywhere. Even your amazing skill in the subtleties of logic hasn't figured out how to throw someone else without falling down yourself — no more now than before."

Ctesippus said, "Gentlemen of Chios — or Thurii — or whatever you call yourselves — I'm amazed at you, because you obviously have zero objection to talking complete nonsense."

Fearing a real blowup, I tried to calm Ctesippus down again. "Ctesippus," I said, "what I told Cleinias applies to you too: you don't understand the ways of these foreign philosophers. They're not being serious. They're like the Egyptian sorcerer Proteus — they keep shapeshifting to trick us. So let's be like Menelaus and refuse to let them go until they show us their serious side. When they get serious, their true brilliance will appear. Let's beg and plead with them to let it shine through. And I think maybe I should lead by example again, to show them what I'm looking for. I'll pick up where I left off, as best I can, in hopes that I might touch their hearts and move them to pity — so that when they see how deeply serious and invested I am, they'll get serious too.

"Cleinias," I said, "remind me where we left off. Didn't we agree that a person should study philosophy?"

"Yes," he said.

"And philosophy is the pursuit of knowledge?"

"Yes."

"And what knowledge should we pursue? Can't we say, quite simply — a knowledge that will do us good?"

"Certainly."

"And would we be any better off if we ran around knowing where every deposit of gold was buried in the earth?"

"Maybe," he said.

"But haven't we already proven," I said, "that we'd be no better off even if all the gold in the earth were ours without any effort at all? Even if we knew how to turn stones into gold, that knowledge would be worthless unless we also knew how to use the gold. Remember?"

"I remember perfectly," he said.

"And the same goes for any knowledge — whether it's moneymaking, medicine, or any other skill that knows how to produce something but not how to use what it produces. None of those would do us any real good, right?"

He agreed.

"And if there were a knowledge that could make people immortal, but didn't include the knowledge of how to use immortality — even that would be useless, if we can reason from what we've already established?"

He agreed to all of this.

"Then, my dear boy," I said, "the knowledge we need is one that both produces and knows how to use what it produces."

"True," he said.

"And we definitely don't want to be lyre-makers or anything of that sort. In those crafts, the skill of making and the skill of using are completely separate — they deal with the same object but are totally different arts. The person who makes a lyre and the person who plays it are worlds apart. Right?"

"Right."

"And we obviously don't want the flute-maker's art either — same problem."

He agreed.

"Well then," I said, "suppose we learned the art of speechwriting — would that be the knowledge that makes us happy?"

"I'd say no," said Cleinias.

"Why not?" I asked.

"Because I notice," he said, "that there are some speechwriters who don't know how to use the speeches they compose — just like the lyre-makers who can't play their lyres. And there are other people who can't write speeches themselves but can brilliantly deploy the speeches others write for them. So the art of composing speeches clearly isn't the same as the art of using them."

"Yes," I said, "and I think that's solid proof that speechwriting isn't the knowledge that will make you happy. And yet I did think we might find our answer somewhere in that neighborhood. Because whenever I run into these speechwriters, Cleinias, they always strike me as truly extraordinary people — their art seems lofty and almost divine, and no wonder. Their art is really a branch of the great art of enchantment, barely inferior to it. The difference is that the enchanter's art works on snakes, spiders, scorpions, and other pests, while theirs works on juries, assemblies, and crowds of people — charming and soothing them. Do you agree?"

"Yes," he said. "I think you're right."

"So where do we go from here?" I said. "What art should we turn to?"

"I don't see my way," he said.

"But I think I do," I said.

"What's your idea?" asked Cleinias.

"I think the art of the general is the one most likely to make a person happy."

"I don't think so," he said.

"Why not?"

"The art of the general is basically an art of hunting — hunting men."

"So? What's wrong with that?"

"Because no art of hunting goes beyond the hunting and capturing stage. Once the prey is taken, the hunter or fisherman can't use it — they hand it over to the cook. And it's the same with geometers, astronomers, and mathematicians. They belong to the hunting class too, because they don't create their diagrams — they only discover what was already there. And since they can't use what they find, only catch it, they hand over their discoveries to the dialecticians to actually apply — at least, the ones with any sense do."

"Bravo!" I said. "Very good, my brilliant Cleinias! Is this really true?"

"Absolutely," he said. "Just as a general who captures a city or a camp hands his conquest over to the statesman — because the general doesn't know how to govern what he's won. Or as a quail-catcher hands his birds over to the quail-keeper. So if we're looking for the art that will make us truly happy — one that can not only acquire things but also use them — the art of the general isn't it. We need to look elsewhere."

Crito
Wait — are you telling me, Socrates, that the young man actually said all that?

Socrates
You don't believe me, Crito?

Crito
I certainly don't! Because if he really said those things, then in my opinion he needs neither Euthydemus nor anyone else as his teacher.

Socrates
Well, maybe I'm misremembering. Maybe it was actually Ctesippus who said it.

Crito
Ctesippus? No way.

Socrates
All I know is that I heard these words, and they weren't said by either Euthydemus or Dionysodorus. Perhaps, my dear Crito, they were spoken by some higher being. I'm certain I heard them.

Crito
Yes, Socrates — someone considerably higher, I'd say. But did you take the investigation any further? Did you find the art you were looking for?

Socrates
Find it? My dear fellow, hardly. We were like children chasing songbirds — always just about to catch the knowledge we wanted, but it kept slipping away. Why go through the whole story? Eventually we arrived at the kingly art and asked whether it was the one that produces and causes happiness. And then we fell into a labyrinth — every time we thought we'd reached the exit, we found ourselves right back at the entrance, with as far to go as ever.

Crito
How did that happen, Socrates?

Socrates
I'll tell you. We identified the kingly art with the political art.

Crito
And then what?

Socrates
All the other arts — including the general's art — seemed to hand over their authority to this one. It was the only art that knew how to use what the others produced. So obviously this was the very thing we'd been looking for — the art that sits at the helm of the ship of state, as Aeschylus would put it, steering and governing everything, putting it all to use.

Crito
And weren't you right, Socrates?

Socrates
You can judge for yourself, Crito, when you hear what happened next. Because we picked up the investigation again and asked a question like this: does the kingly art, which has supreme authority, actually produce anything for us? "Of course," was the answer. Wouldn't you say the same, Crito?

Crito
I would.

Socrates
And what would you say the kingly art produces? Suppose medicine had supreme authority over all the subordinate arts. If I asked you a similar question about medicine, you'd say — it produces health?

Crito
I would.

Socrates
And what about your own art, farming — if it had supreme authority over its subordinate arts, what does it produce? Doesn't it supply us with the fruits of the earth?

Crito
Yes.
