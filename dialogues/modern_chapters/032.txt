THEAETETUS (Part 1)

Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates
  Theodorus
  Theaetetus


Euclid and Terpsion meet in front of Euclid's house in Megara. They go inside, and a servant reads the dialogue aloud to them.

Euclid
Terpsion! Did you just get in from the country?

Terpsion
No, I've been here a while. Actually, I was looking for you in the marketplace and couldn't figure out where you'd gone.

Euclid
That's because I wasn't in the city.

Terpsion
Where were you, then?

Euclid
On my way down to the harbor, I ran into Theaetetus -- he was being carried up to Athens from the army camp at Corinth.

Terpsion
Alive or dead?

Euclid
Barely alive. He's badly wounded, but what's really destroying him is the sickness that's spread through the army.

Terpsion
The dysentery, you mean?

Euclid
Yes.

Terpsion
What a loss that man will be!

Euclid
A terrible one, Terpsion. He's a noble man. Just today I heard people praising the way he conducted himself in the battle.

Terpsion
That doesn't surprise me at all -- I'd be more surprised to hear anything else about him. But why didn't he stop here in Megara?

Euclid
He wanted to get home. I begged him to stay, I gave him every reason to, but he wouldn't listen. So I walked with him part of the way, and as I was heading back, I found myself remembering what Socrates once said about him -- and marveling at how exactly the prediction came true, the way his predictions always did. I believe it was shortly before Socrates' own death that he met Theaetetus, who was just a teenager then. They had a remarkable conversation, and Socrates repeated it to me when I next came to Athens. He was full of admiration for the boy's mind, and he said that if Theaetetus lived, he was certain to become a great man.

Terpsion
Well, the prophecy certainly came true. But what was the conversation? Can you tell it to me?

Euclid
Not from memory, no. But here's the thing: I took notes as soon as I got home that day, then filled them out from memory when I had time. And whenever I went to Athens, I'd ask Socrates about any details I'd forgotten and make corrections when I got back. So I have nearly the whole conversation written down.

Terpsion
That's right -- you told me about this once! I've been meaning to ask you to show me the manuscript ever since, and I've never gotten around to it. Why don't we read it now? I've just come in from the country, and I could badly use a rest.

Euclid
I could use one too -- I walked with Theaetetus all the way to Erineum. Let's go in, and the servant can read to us while we put our feet up.

Terpsion
Perfect.

Euclid
Here's the scroll, Terpsion. One note: I wrote it with Socrates actually talking to the people in the conversation -- Theodorus the geometer from Cyrene, and Theaetetus -- rather than narrating it to me secondhand. I cut out all the connecting phrases, the "I said" and "I remarked" when Socrates spoke about himself, and the "he agreed" or "he disagreed" for the answers. They get tiresome when they're repeated over and over.

Terpsion
Good call, Euclid.

Euclid
Now, boy, take the scroll and read.

Euclid's servant reads.

Socrates
If I cared more about Cyrene, Theodorus, I'd ask you about the scene there -- whether any promising young geometers or philosophers are coming up in that part of the world. But honestly, I'm more interested in our own Athenian youth. I want to know which of them show real promise. I keep an eye out myself, as much as I can, and I ask around about whose company the young men seek. Plenty of them flock to you -- and rightly so, given your eminence in geometry and everything else. So tell me: have you come across anyone worth watching?

Theodorus
I have, Socrates. There's one Athenian boy I've gotten to know who is truly remarkable, and you should absolutely meet him. Now, if he were beautiful, I'd hesitate to praise him this much -- someone might think I was in love with him. But he's no beauty, so I can speak freely. In fact -- don't take this the wrong way -- he looks like you: snub nose, bulging eyes, though both features are less pronounced in him than in you. So with that disclaimer out of the way, let me say this: of all the young people I've ever met -- and I've met a great many -- I've never known anyone with natural gifts like his. He picks things up faster than almost anyone alive, yet he's extraordinarily gentle, and on top of that he's as brave as they come. I've never seen that combination in anyone else, and I wouldn't have believed it could exist. Usually the quick, sharp, retentive minds also come with hair-trigger tempers -- they're like ships without ballast, darting around, more manic than courageous. And the steady, calm types tend to turn sluggish when they face real study, and can't remember anything. But this boy moves through learning and investigation so surely, so smoothly, so effectively -- all with perfect gentleness, like a river of oil flowing without a sound. It's astonishing in someone his age.

Socrates
That is good news. Whose son is he?

Theodorus
I've forgotten the father's name, but the boy himself is the one in the middle of that group coming toward us. He and his friends have been oiling up in the outer courtyard -- looks like they've just finished. See if you recognize him.

Socrates
I know him, though I don't know his name. He's the son of Euphronius of Sunium -- a man who was, by all accounts, exactly the kind of person you're describing in his son. A distinguished man. I believe he left a sizable fortune.

Theodorus
Theaetetus, Socrates -- that's his name. As for the fortune, I think the trustees managed to make it disappear. And yet he's wonderfully generous with money anyway.

Socrates
He sounds like a fine person. Tell him to come sit with me.

Theodorus
I will. Theaetetus! Come over here and sit by Socrates.

Socrates
Yes, do, Theaetetus -- so I can study my own reflection in your face. Theodorus says we look alike. But think about this: if each of us were holding a lyre, and he claimed the two were tuned alike, would we just take his word for it? Or would we first ask whether he was a musician?

Theaetetus
We'd ask.

Socrates
And if he turned out to be one, we'd believe him; if not, we wouldn't?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So if we cared at all about this supposed resemblance between our faces, we'd want to know whether the man making the claim is a painter or not.

Theaetetus
Definitely.

Socrates
Well -- is Theodorus a painter?

Theaetetus
Not that I've ever heard.

Socrates
Is he a geometer?

Theaetetus
Of course he is, Socrates.

Socrates
And an astronomer, a mathematician, a musician -- an educated man across the board?

Theaetetus
I think so.

Socrates
Then if he comments on our physical resemblance -- whether as a compliment or an insult -- there's no particular reason to take him seriously.

Theaetetus
I suppose not.

Socrates
But if he praises the virtue or wisdom in either of our minds? Then anyone who hears that praise will naturally want to examine the person being praised -- and the person being praised should be willing to show what he's got.

Theaetetus
Very true, Socrates.

Socrates
Then now's the moment, my dear Theaetetus -- time for me to examine and for you to show what you've got. Because believe me, Theodorus has praised plenty of people to me, citizens and foreigners alike, but he has never praised anyone the way he just praised you.

Theaetetus
That's nice to hear, Socrates. But what if he was joking?

Socrates
No, that's not Theodorus' style. And don't think you can wriggle out of your agreement by pretending he might have been kidding. If you try it, he'll have to testify under oath -- and nobody is going to accuse him of perjury. So be brave and stand by your word.

Theaetetus
All right. If that's what you want, I will.

Socrates
First things first: tell me what you study with Theodorus. Some geometry, I assume?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And astronomy, harmony, calculation?

Theaetetus
I do my best.

Socrates
So do I, my boy, so do I -- I'm eager to learn from him, or from anyone who understands these things. And I manage well enough overall. But there's one small difficulty nagging at me, and I'd like you and the others here to help me investigate it. Answer me this: learning means becoming wiser about whatever it is you're learning, doesn't it?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
And it's wisdom that makes the wise wise?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And is that any different from knowledge?

Theaetetus
Is what different?

Socrates
Wisdom. Aren't people wise about exactly the things they know?

Theaetetus
Sure.

Socrates
So knowledge and wisdom are the same thing?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And right there is the difficulty I can never settle to my own satisfaction: What is knowledge? Can we answer that? What do you all say? Who wants to go first? Whoever misses sits out and gets to be "donkey," as the boys say in their ball game -- and whoever survives every round without missing is crowned king and gets to ask the rest of us any questions he likes... Why so quiet? Theodorus, I hope my love of conversation isn't making me rude. I only want to get us talking, like the friendly, sociable people we are.

Theodorus
Rude? The opposite, Socrates. But do ask one of the young ones instead. The truth is, I'm not used to your style of question-and-answer, and I'm too old to get used to it now. The young men are better suited -- and they'll improve more than I would, since the young improve at everything. You've already started on Theaetetus, so my advice is: keep going, and don't let him off the hook.

Socrates
You hear that, Theaetetus? Theodorus says so -- and you're not going to disobey him. When a wise man gives orders, a young man obeys. So take courage, and tell me, nobly and honestly: what do you think knowledge is?

Theaetetus
Well, Socrates, since the two of you insist, I'll answer. And if I get it wrong, you'll correct me, I'm sure.

Socrates
We will -- if we can.

Theaetetus
All right then. I think the subjects I study with Theodorus -- geometry and the others you listed just now -- are knowledge. And I'd add the craft of the shoemaker and the other craftsmen too. Each and every one of those is knowledge.

Socrates
Theaetetus, that's too much! Generous and openhanded as you are, I ask you for one simple thing and you hand me a whole assortment.

Theaetetus
What do you mean, Socrates?

Socrates
Maybe nothing. But let me try to explain what I think I mean. When you say "shoemaking," you mean the craft of making shoes, right?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Socrates
And when you say "carpentry," you mean the craft of making things out of wood?

Theaetetus
I do.

Socrates
So in both cases, you're defining what each craft is about?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
But that wasn't my question, Theaetetus. I didn't ask what the various crafts are about, or how many of them there are -- we weren't taking a head count. I asked what knowledge itself is. Am I making sense?

Theaetetus
Perfectly.

Socrates
Look at it this way. Suppose someone asked us about something completely ordinary and trivial -- say, "What is clay?" -- and we answered: "Well, there's potters' clay, and oven-makers' clay, and brickmakers' clay." Wouldn't that be a ridiculous answer?

Theaetetus
It would.

Socrates
Ridiculous first of all because we'd be assuming the questioner understands what clay is just because we tacked on "of the figurine-makers" or "of" some other craftsmen. How can anyone understand the name of a thing when he doesn't know what the thing is?

Theaetetus
He can't.

Socrates
Then someone who doesn't know what knowledge is can't understand "knowledge of shoemaking" either.

Theaetetus
No.

Socrates
Nor "knowledge of" anything else.

Theaetetus
Right.

Socrates
So when someone is asked "What is knowledge?" it's ridiculous to answer with the name of some particular craft. The question was "What is knowledge?" -- and he answers, "Knowledge of this" or "knowledge of that."

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Besides, he could have given a short, simple answer, but instead he goes the long way around. Take the clay question: he could simply have said, "Clay is earth mixed with water" -- and never mind whose clay it is.

Theaetetus
Now that you put it that way, Socrates, it seems easy. In fact, I think you're asking the same kind of question that came up recently when I was talking with your namesake -- my friend Socrates here.

Socrates
What question was that, Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
Theodorus was drawing diagrams for us about square roots -- showing that the roots of three and five aren't measurable by the unit -- and he took the cases one by one, up to the root of seventeen, where he stopped for some reason. Since there are infinitely many roots, it occurred to us to try to gather them all under a single name or class.

Socrates
And did you find one?

Theaetetus
I think we did. But see what you think.

Socrates
Go on.

Theaetetus
We divided all numbers into two classes. The numbers you can make by multiplying equal factors -- like four, which is two times two -- we pictured as squares, and called them square or equilateral numbers. That was one class.

Socrates
Very good.

Theaetetus
The numbers in between -- like three and five, and any other number you can't make from equal factors, only from a larger number times a smaller one, so that the figure they form has unequal sides -- we pictured as rectangles and called them oblong numbers.

Socrates
Excellent. And then what?

Theaetetus
Every line that forms the side of a square number we called a length. And every line that forms the side of an oblong number we called a root, or power -- because while those lines aren't measurable by the lengths themselves, the squares built on them are. And we did something similar for solids.

Socrates
Boys, that's superb. I don't think Theodorus is in any danger of a perjury conviction.

Theaetetus
But Socrates, I can't answer your question about knowledge the way I answered the one about roots -- and that's clearly what you want. So maybe Theodorus is a fraud after all.

Socrates
Hold on. Suppose someone praised your running and said he'd never met a boy your equal -- and then you lost a race to a grown man in his prime, a champion sprinter. Would the praise be any less true?

Theaetetus
No.

Socrates
And do you think figuring out what knowledge is, is a small matter, like I suggested a moment ago? Isn't it the kind of problem that would strain the very best minds anywhere?

Theaetetus
By heaven, the very best of the best!

Socrates
Then relax. Don't decide that Theodorus was wrong about you. Just give everything you've got to finding out the truth about knowledge -- and about everything else, while you're at it.

Theaetetus
If effort can get us to the truth, Socrates, I'm willing.

Socrates
Come on, then -- you just showed us how it's done. Take your answer about the roots as your model. Just as you gathered all those many roots into one class, try to gather the many kinds of knowledge into one definition.

Theaetetus
I have to tell you, Socrates, I've tried this many times -- whenever reports of your questions reached me. But I can never convince myself that I have a good answer, and I've never heard anyone else give the kind of answer you're looking for. And yet I can't stop worrying at it.

Socrates
Ah -- those are labor pains, my dear Theaetetus. You're not empty. You're pregnant with something, and it's trying to be born.

Theaetetus
I don't know about that, Socrates. I'm just telling you how it feels.

Socrates
You ridiculous boy -- have you never heard that I'm the son of a midwife? A fine, sturdy one named Phaenarete?

Theaetetus
I've heard that, yes.

Socrates
And have you heard that I practice midwifery myself?

Theaetetus
No. Never.

Socrates
Well, I do, my friend -- though keep it to yourself, because the world hasn't found me out. People only say that I'm the strangest of men, and that I drive everyone I talk to into total confusion. Have you heard that one?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Shall I tell you the reason?

Theaetetus
Please.

Socrates
Then think about how midwifery actually works, and you'll see what I mean. You probably know that no woman who can still conceive and bear children works as a midwife -- only women who are past childbearing age.

Theaetetus
Yes, I know.

Socrates
They say the reason is Artemis, the goddess of childbirth, who is herself childless. She honors women who resemble her. But she couldn't let completely barren women be midwives, because human beings can't master a skill they have no experience of. So she gave the job to women who have borne children but are now too old to bear more.

Theaetetus
Makes sense.

Socrates
And here's something else that makes sense -- in fact I'm certain of it: midwives can tell, better than anyone, who is pregnant and who isn't.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
And with their drugs and incantations they can trigger labor pains or soothe them, at will. They can bring on delivery for women who are struggling, and, if they judge it best, they can end a pregnancy in its early stage.

Theaetetus
They can.

Socrates
Have you also noticed that they're shrewd matchmakers -- absolute experts at knowing which pairings will produce the best children?

Theaetetus
No, I never knew that.

Socrates
Well, let me tell you, they're prouder of that than of cutting the umbilical cord. Think about it: the same skill that knows how to grow and harvest crops must also know which soil suits which plant or seed. It's one art, not two.

Theaetetus
Yes, the same art.

Socrates
And you don't suppose it's any different with women?

Theaetetus
Probably not.

Socrates
It isn't. But midwives are respectable women with reputations to protect, so they steer clear of matchmaking. They're afraid of being called procuresses -- the name for people who pair off men and women in shady, unskilled ways. And yet the true midwife really is the one true matchmaker.

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
So that's what midwives do -- important work, but not as important as mine. Here's why. Women don't give birth to real babies one day and counterfeit babies the next, fakes so convincing you can hardly tell the difference. If they did, then telling true births from false ones would be the crowning skill of the midwife's art. Don't you think?

Theaetetus
I do.

Socrates
Well, my art of midwifery is just like theirs -- with a few differences. I attend men, not women. And I watch over souls in labor, not bodies. The supreme moment of my art is this: testing, by every means I have, whether the idea a young mind delivers is a false phantom or something alive, genuine, and true. And there's one more way I'm like the midwives: I'm barren. I can't give birth to wisdom myself. The accusation people have thrown at me for years -- that I ask everyone else questions but never answer any myself, because I have no wisdom in me -- is absolutely true. And here's the reason: the god compels me to deliver others, but has forbidden me to give birth. So no, I'm not wise at all, and I have no discovery to show for myself, no brainchild of my own soul. But the people who spend time with me? Something different happens to them. Some seem hopelessly slow at first; but as we keep at it, all of them -- all the ones the god favors -- make progress that astonishes everyone, themselves most of all. And here's the thing: it's obvious they never learned anything from me. The many beautiful discoveries they bring forth came entirely from inside themselves. The delivery, though -- that was the god's work, and mine.

Here's the proof. Plenty of them didn't realize this. Giving themselves all the credit and despising me, they left me sooner than they should have -- some on their own conceit, some because others lured them away. And after they left, what happened? They miscarried everything that was still in them, and the ideas I had already delivered they ruined through bad nurture -- because they cared more for falsehood and shams than for truth. In the end they looked like complete fools, to themselves and everyone else. Aristeides, son of Lysimachus, is one of them; there are plenty more. And when the runaways come back, begging to be taken in again -- ready to get down on their knees, practically -- then, if my inner voice allows it (and it doesn't always), I take them back, and they begin to grow again.

One more thing: the people who keep my company suffer something the women in labor suffer too, except worse. They feel labor pains -- they're filled, day and night, with confusion and aching perplexity far beyond anything the women endure. And my art can both stir those pangs up and soothe them. So much for them. And then there are others, Theaetetus, who come to me carrying nothing at all. Since I know my art is useless to them, I kindly arrange a match -- and with the god's help I'm pretty good at guessing whose company will do them good. I've matched many of them with Prodicus, and many with other wise and inspired men.

Now, why have I told you this whole long story, my friend? Because I suspect -- and you suspect it yourself -- that you're in labor. You're pregnant with something. So come to me, the midwife's son and a midwife myself, and when I ask my questions, do your very best to answer them. And if, when I examine one of your statements, I conclude it's a phantom and not a true birth, and I gently take it from you and discard it -- don't rage at me the way first-time mothers do over their babies. People before you, my friend, have gotten so furious with me they were literally ready to bite, just because I removed some cherished nonsense of theirs. They never grasped that I do it out of pure goodwill. They have no idea that no god ever wishes evil to man, and that I don't act out of malice either. I simply cannot accept a falsehood and bury a truth.

So -- back to the beginning, Theaetetus. What is knowledge? Try to tell me. And never say you can't. If the god is willing and you take courage, you can.

Theaetetus
Well, Socrates, after a pep talk like that, I'd be ashamed not to give it everything I have. All right. It seems to me that someone who knows something perceives the thing he knows. So, as it looks to me right now: knowledge is nothing other than perception.

Socrates
Good -- bold and direct! That's exactly how you should speak your mind. Now let's examine this child of yours together and find out whether it's a real birth or just a wind-egg. Knowledge is perception, you say?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And in fact you've just delivered no ordinary theory of knowledge -- it's the very one Protagoras used to teach, though he put it differently. He said that man is the measure of all things: of things that are, that they are; and of things that are not, that they are not. You've read him, I assume?

Theaetetus
Oh yes -- many times.

Socrates
And doesn't he mean something like this: things are, for you, the way they appear to you, and they are, for me, the way they appear to me -- you and I each being a man?

Theaetetus
Yes, that's what he says.

Socrates
Well, a wise man doesn't usually talk nonsense, so let's try to follow him. Sometimes the same wind is blowing, and yet one of us feels chilly and the other doesn't -- or one feels slightly cool and the other freezing. True?

Theaetetus
Quite true.

Socrates
So in that moment, is the wind itself, taken on its own, cold or not cold? Or should we side with Protagoras and say it's cold for the one who feels cold, and not for the one who doesn't?

Theaetetus
The second, I suppose.

Socrates
And it appears that way to each of them?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And "appears to him" means the same as "he perceives it."

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So appearing and perceiving are the same thing, in matters of hot, cold, and everything like them. The way each person perceives things is presumably the way they are -- for that person.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Then perception is always perception of what is -- and since it's the same as knowledge, it never makes a mistake.

Theaetetus
Apparently.

Socrates
In the name of the Graces! Protagoras must have been an almighty genius. He tossed out this saying as a riddle for the common herd -- people like you and me -- while telling the real Truth (that was actually the title of his book) in secret to his own students.

Theaetetus
What do you mean by that, Socrates?

Socrates
I'm about to lay out a serious doctrine -- one that says nothing is anything in itself; everything is relative. You can't correctly call anything by any name -- "big," "small," "heavy," "light" -- because the big will turn out small and the heavy light. Nothing is any one thing or any one quality. The truth is that everything comes to be -- through motion, change, and mixture with everything else -- always relative to something. We say things "are," but that's sloppy talk: nothing ever is. Everything is always becoming. And on this point, line up all the philosophers one after another -- Protagoras, Heraclitus, Empedocles, the lot -- and they'll all agree, with the single exception of Parmenides. Line up the great poets of both genres too: Epicharmus, the king of comedy, and Homer, the king of tragedy. When Homer sings of

"Ocean, source of the gods, and mother Tethys,"

doesn't he mean that all things are born of flux and motion?

Theaetetus
I think so.

Socrates
And who would dare take up arms against an army that size, with Homer as its general, and not look ridiculous?

Theaetetus
Who indeed, Socrates?

Socrates
Exactly, Theaetetus. And there's plenty more evidence that motion is the source of everything we call being and becoming, while stillness is the source of non-being and destruction. Fire and warmth -- supposedly the parent and guardian of everything else -- are themselves born from movement and friction, and friction is a kind of motion. Isn't that where fire comes from?

Theaetetus
It is.

Socrates
And every animal is generated the same way -- from motion?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And what about the body? Doesn't rest and laziness ruin its condition, while motion and exercise preserve it for years?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And the mind? Isn't the soul taught, improved, and maintained by study and practice -- which are motions -- while at rest (which for the soul just means not studying, not paying attention) it learns nothing and quickly forgets what it knew?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So motion is good for the soul as well as the body, and rest is bad for both?

Theaetetus
Apparently.

Socrates
I could go further: dead calms and windless stillness rot and decay things, while wind and storm keep them fresh. And the crown jewel of the whole argument -- I insist on this one -- is Homer's golden chain. By it he means nothing other than the sun, and his point is this: as long as the sun and the heavens keep circling in their orbits, everything in heaven and earth exists and is preserved. But if they were chained in place and their motion stopped, everything would be destroyed and, as the saying goes, turned upside down.

Theaetetus
I think you've explained his meaning exactly, Socrates.

Socrates
Then apply the doctrine to perception, my friend -- start with vision. What you call "white color" isn't a thing in your eyes, and it isn't some separate thing existing outside them either. Don't assign it any location at all -- because if it had a fixed position, it would just be, sitting still, and there'd be no becoming.

Theaetetus
Then what is color?

Socrates
Follow the principle we just laid down -- that nothing exists on its own -- and you'll see it: white, black, and every other color arise from the eye meeting the right kind of motion. What we call a color is neither the thing striking the eye nor the eye being struck, but something that passes between them -- and it's private to each perceiver. Tell me: would you swear that every color appears to a dog, or to any other animal, exactly the way it appears to you?

Theaetetus
Definitely not.

Socrates
And would you swear that anything appears the same to you as it does to another person? Are you really so sure of even that? Wouldn't it be closer to the truth to say it never appears exactly the same even to you -- because you are never exactly the same?

Theaetetus
The latter, I think.

Socrates
Now, if the thing we measure ourselves against, or the thing we touch, really were big or white or hot in itself, then bumping into something different couldn't change it -- unless the thing itself actually changed. And on the other side, if the perceiver were really big or white or hot in himself, then he wouldn't become different when something different approached him or happened to him -- unless something in him changed. Yet in everyday speech, we casually say things that drive us straight into the most absurd contradictions -- as Protagoras and anyone arguing his line would happily point out.

Theaetetus
What kind of contradictions? What do you mean?

Socrates
A small example will make it clear. Take six dice. Set four next to them, and we say the six are more -- one-and-a-half times as many. Set twelve next to them, and we say the six are fewer -- only half as many. The same six dice: more and fewer at once. Can you or anyone deny it?

Theaetetus
No, that's right.

Socrates
Well then, suppose Protagoras or someone else asks you: "Theaetetus, can anything become bigger or more in any way other than by increasing?" What's your answer?

Theaetetus
If I answer what I really think about this last question, Socrates, I'd say no. But if I keep my earlier answer in mind, I'd say yes -- to avoid contradicting myself.

Socrates
Wonderful! Spoken like an oracle, my boy! And notice: if you answer "yes," it'll be a scene straight out of Euripides -- the tongue convinced, but not the mind.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Now, if you and I were professional debaters -- those thoroughbred Sophists who know everything about the mind and argue purely from a surplus of cleverness -- we'd have had a regular sparring match over this by now, banging our arguments together for sport. But we're amateurs. All we want is to see what these ideas actually are and whether they're consistent with one another or not.

Theaetetus
Yes, that's exactly what I'd want.

Socrates
Me too. And since we feel that way, and there's no rush, why don't we calmly and patiently go back over our own thoughts and examine what these appearances in us really are? If I'm not mistaken, we'll start by laying out three claims. First: nothing can become bigger or smaller, in size or in number, as long as it stays equal to itself. Agreed?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Second: a thing to which nothing is added and from which nothing is taken away neither grows nor shrinks — it stays equal.

Theaetetus
Quite true.

Socrates
Third: what wasn't there before can't exist later without having come into being — without becoming.

Theaetetus
Yes, definitely.

Socrates
Now, these three principles, if I'm not mistaken, go to war with each other in our minds when we think about the dice — or take this example. Suppose I'm a certain height, taller than you. Within a year, without growing or shrinking at all, I might be shorter than you — not because I lost any height, but because you grew. So I am, later on, something I wasn't before — and yet I never became it. But that's impossible: nothing can come to be without becoming, and I couldn't have become shorter without losing some height. I could give you ten thousand examples of contradictions like this, if we accept them at all. I think you're following me, Theaetetus — I suspect you've puzzled over questions like these before.

Theaetetus
Yes, Socrates, and I'm amazed when I think about them — by the gods, I am! I want to know what on earth they mean. Sometimes my head literally swims just contemplating them.

Socrates
I see, my dear Theaetetus, that Theodorus read your nature correctly when he called you a philosopher. Wonder is the philosopher's signature feeling — philosophy begins in wonder. Whoever said that Iris, the messenger of heaven, is the daughter of Thaumas — Wonder — wasn't a bad genealogist. But are you starting to see how the theory we're attributing to Protagoras explains all this perplexity?

Theaetetus
Not yet.

Socrates
Then you'll owe me one if I help you dig up the hidden "truth" of a famous man — or rather, a famous school of thought.

Theaetetus
Of course I'll owe you. Very much so.

Socrates
Then take a look around and make sure none of the uninitiated are eavesdropping. By "the uninitiated" I mean people who believe in nothing except what they can grab with their two hands — people who refuse to accept that actions or processes or anything invisible could possibly be real.

Theaetetus
Yes, Socrates — hard, stubborn characters, those people.

Socrates
Complete barbarians, my boy. The initiates whose mysteries I'm about to reveal to you are far more sophisticated. Here's their first principle: everything is motion. There is nothing but motion, and it comes in two forms — one active, one passive — both in endless variety. From their union and friction comes an endless brood of offspring, also in two forms: the senses, and the objects of sense, which are constantly bursting forth and being born at the very same moment. The senses have names — hearing, sight, smell, the sense of heat and cold, pleasure, pain, desire, fear, and many more — plus countless others with no names at all. And each sense has its matching object: every variety of color has a corresponding variety of seeing, every sound a corresponding hearing, and so on for all the senses and their kindred objects. Do you see, Theaetetus, what this story has to do with our earlier argument?

Theaetetus
Honestly, no.

Socrates
Then pay attention, and I'll try to finish the tale. The point is this: everything is in motion, as I said, and the motion comes in two speeds, slower and quicker. The slow elements move in place, in relation to whatever is near them — and that's how they generate offspring. But the offspring are faster: they travel, moving from place to place. Now apply this to perception. When an eye and a suitable object come together, they give birth to whiteness, along with the sensation that's twinned with it — a birth that never would have happened if either of them had wandered off elsewhere. As sight flows from the eye and whiteness streams from the object, the two combine to produce the color. The eye is filled with seeing — at that moment it really does see, and it becomes not sight itself but a seeing eye. And the object that partnered in producing the color is filled with whiteness, and becomes not whiteness itself but a white thing — white wood, white stone, whatever the thing happens to be.

And the same goes for everything we perceive — hard, warm, all of it. None of it has any independent existence on its own, just as we said before. Everything, of every kind, is generated by motion in the encounter between things. As for "agent" and "patient" — the thing acting and the thing acted on — you can't form any reliable conception of either one existing on its own. There is no agent until it pairs with a patient, and no patient until it pairs with an agent. And what acts as agent in one encounter turns up as patient in another.

The upshot of all this, as I said at the start, is that nothing exists by itself, in its own right. Everything is always becoming, always in relation to something else. "Being" should be struck from our vocabulary entirely — although, out of habit and ignorance, we've kept using the word even in this very discussion. But the great philosophers tell us we mustn't allow even words like "something" or "somebody's" or "mine" or "this" or "that," or any other word that pins a thing down. In the language of nature, things are forever being created and destroyed, coming into being and passing into new forms. No name can fix or hold them — and anyone who tries to nail them down is easily refuted. And we should talk this way not just about individual things but about collections of them — the kinds of bundles we name "man" or "stone" or any animal or class of things.

Well, Theaetetus, aren't these speculations sweet as honey? Don't you like the taste of them?

Theaetetus
I don't know what to say, Socrates. Honestly, I can't even tell whether you're giving your own opinion or just testing me.

Socrates
You're forgetting, my friend, that I don't know any of this, and I don't claim to. None of these ideas are mine. You're the one in labor; I'm just the midwife — barren myself. That's why I'm soothing you, offering you one delicacy after another for you to taste. I'm hoping that in the end I can help bring your own opinion out into the light of day. And once it's born, we'll examine it and decide whether what you've delivered is a real, living child or just a wind-egg — an empty shell. So keep your courage up, and answer like a man: what do you think?

Theaetetus
Ask away.

Socrates
Then tell me once more: is it your view that nothing is, but everything is always becoming — the good and the beautiful included, along with everything else we just listed?

Theaetetus
When I hear you lay it out like that, it seems to make a great deal of sense, and I'm very much inclined to accept it.

Socrates
Then let's not leave the argument unfinished, because there's still an objection to deal with — about dreams and diseases, especially madness, and all the illusions of hearing and sight and the other senses. In every one of these cases, the theory that "to be is to be perceived" looks flatly refuted. In dreams and hallucinations our perceptions are clearly false. Far from saying that whatever appears to someone is, we'd be tempted to say the opposite: nothing that appears is.

Theaetetus
Very true, Socrates.

Socrates
Then how, my boy, can anyone keep claiming that knowledge is perception, or that whatever appears to each person is, for that person?

Theaetetus
I'm afraid to say I have no answer, Socrates, since you scolded me just now for saying exactly that. But I really can't argue that madmen and dreamers believe truly — not when some of them imagine they're gods, and others dream they have wings and are flying through the air.

Socrates
Do you see yet another puzzle hiding in this, especially in the question of dreaming versus waking?

Theaetetus
What puzzle?

Socrates
One I'm sure you've heard people raise: what proof could you give if someone asked us, right now, this very moment, whether we're asleep and dreaming all our thoughts, or awake and actually talking to each other?

Theaetetus
Honestly, Socrates, I don't see how you'd prove one any more than the other — the two states match each other point for point. There's nothing to stop us from supposing this entire conversation is something we're dreaming. And when we dream that we're telling each other our dreams, the resemblance between the two states is downright uncanny.

Socrates
So you see, doubt about the reality of perception is easy to raise — there's even doubt about whether we're awake or dreaming. And since our time is split equally between sleeping and waking, in each state the soul insists that whatever it's thinking at the moment is the truth. Half our lives we swear by the one set of perceptions, half our lives by the other — and we're equally confident both times.

Theaetetus
Absolutely true.

Socrates
And doesn't the same go for madness and the other disorders? The only difference is that the time isn't split equally.

Theaetetus
Right.

Socrates
And surely truth and falsehood aren't going to be decided by a stopwatch — by how long a state lasts?

Theaetetus
That would be ridiculous in all kinds of ways.

Socrates
But do you have any other way of proving for certain which of these opinions is true?

Theaetetus
I don't think I do.

Socrates
Then listen to what the champions of appearance would say on the other side of the argument. As I imagine it, they'd argue like this: can something that is wholly other than another thing have any of the same properties as the thing it differs from? And notice, Theaetetus — by "other" we don't mean partly different. We mean wholly other.

Theaetetus
Put that way, then no — what is wholly other can't be the same in any respect or any sense.

Socrates
So it must also be admitted to be unlike?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So if a thing happens to become like or unlike anything — itself or something else — we say it's the same when it becomes like, and other when it becomes unlike?

Theaetetus
Right.

Socrates
And weren't we saying earlier that there are many agents — infinitely many — and many patients, infinitely many of those too?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And that different pairings produce different results, not the same ones?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
Then let's use you and me as the example — or anything, really. Take Socrates healthy and Socrates sick. Are those two alike, or unlike?

Theaetetus
You mean Socrates-when-sick taken as a whole, compared with Socrates-when-healthy as a whole?

Socrates
Exactly — you've got my meaning.

Theaetetus
Then my answer is: unlike.

Socrates
And if unlike, then other?

Theaetetus
Right.

Socrates
And you'd say the same about Socrates asleep versus awake, or in any of the other states we mentioned?

Theaetetus
I would.

Socrates
So anything that acts on Socrates is dealing with a different patient depending on whether I'm well or ill.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
And in each of the two cases, I as the patient and the thing acting on me will produce a different result together?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
Now, the wine I drink when I'm healthy seems sweet and pleasant to me?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Because — as we already agreed — the agent and patient meet and generate sweetness plus the perception of sweetness, both in motion at once. The perception, coming from the patient's side, makes my tongue a perceiving tongue; the sweetness, arising on the wine's side and swirling around it, makes the wine both be sweet and appear sweet to a healthy tongue.

Theaetetus
Right — that's exactly what we agreed.

Socrates
But when I'm sick, the wine is really acting on a different person, isn't it?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
So the pairing of the gulp of wine with the sick Socrates produces an entirely different result: a sensation of bitterness on the tongue, and bitterness arising and moving about the wine — which becomes not bitterness itself but something bitter, just as I become not perception itself but something perceiving.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And I'll never get this exact perception from any other object — a different object would generate a different perception and make the perceiver different too. Nor can the thing acting on me ever produce the same result by meeting some other person and stay the same itself — from a different subject it will produce a different result, and become different in the process.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Nor can I have this sensation all by myself, nor the object have this quality all by itself.

Theaetetus
No, certainly not.

Socrates
When I perceive, I have to be perceiving something — there's no such thing as perceiving and perceiving nothing. And the object, whether it becomes sweet or bitter or anything else, has to become that for some perceiver — nothing can become sweet without being sweet to someone.

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Socrates
Then the conclusion is that we — the agent and the patient — exist, or rather become, only in relation to each other. Necessity binds us together, but binds each of us to the other and to nothing else — not even to ourselves. So we can only be bound to each other. Which means that whenever someone says a thing "is" or "becomes," they must say it is or becomes for someone, or of something, or in relation to something. They must never say — or let anyone else say — that anything is or becomes absolutely, all on its own. That's where our argument lands.

Theaetetus
Very true, Socrates.

Socrates
So since whatever acts on me is related to me and to no one else, I and no one else am the one who perceives it?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
Then my perception is true for me — because it's always a perception of my own reality. And, just as Protagoras says, I am the judge of what is for me, and of what is not for me.

Theaetetus
So it seems.

Socrates
Then if I'm never wrong, and my mind never stumbles in its grasp of what is or what becomes, how could I fail to know what I perceive?

Theaetetus
You couldn't.

Socrates
So you were exactly right to say that knowledge is nothing but perception. And it all turns out to be the same theory — whether you say with Homer and Heraclitus and that whole crowd that everything is motion and flux, or with the great sage Protagoras that man is the measure of all things, or with Theaetetus that, given all this, perception is knowledge. Am I right, Theaetetus? Shall we say this is your newborn child, and I'm the midwife who delivered it? What do you say?

Theaetetus
I can't disagree, Socrates.

Socrates
Then this is the baby — however it turns out — that you and I have labored to bring into the world. And now that it's born, we have to perform the naming ceremony: carry it in a circle around the hearth and take a good hard look at it, to see whether it's worth raising or is just a wind-egg, a fake. Or do you think your child must be raised no matter what, and not abandoned? Can you stand to watch it cross-examined and rejected, without flying into a rage if your firstborn is taken from you?

Theodorus
Theaetetus won't be angry — he's far too good-natured. But tell me, Socrates, in heaven's name: is the theory actually wrong?

Socrates
You're a lover of theories, Theodorus, and you have the charming notion that I'm a sack stuffed full of them, and can just reach in and pull out one that will overturn the last. But you don't see what's really happening: none of these theories come from me. They always come from whoever's talking with me. I know nothing — only just enough to draw an idea out of someone else's wisdom and receive it fairly. So now I'll say nothing of my own, and instead try to coax something out of our young friend here.

Theodorus
You put it well, Socrates. Do it your way.

Socrates
Shall I tell you, Theodorus, what astonishes me about your friend Protagoras?

Theodorus
What's that?

Socrates
On the whole I'm delighted with his doctrine that what appears to each person is, for that person. But the opening of his book amazes me. Why didn't he begin his "Truth" by declaring that a pig is the measure of all things — or a dog-faced baboon, or some even weirder creature, so long as it has the power of perception? That would have been a magnificently contemptuous way to open: announcing that while we were all gawking at him like he was a god for his wisdom, he was actually no wiser than a tadpole — let alone wiser than any other human being. What an opener that would have been! Because if truth is just whatever each person perceives, if no one can judge another's experience better than that person can, no one is better positioned to say whether another's opinion is true or false, and — as we've said over and over — each person alone is the judge of his own beliefs, and everything he judges is true and right, then tell me, my friend: why exactly is Protagoras the wise one? Why does he deserve to teach others and charge gigantic fees, while we're the ignorant ones who have to go to school to him — when every one of us is the measure of his own wisdom? How can this not be Protagoras playing to the crowd? And I won't even mention what it does to me and my midwife's art — we'd both be cosmically ridiculous — and the same goes for the whole practice of philosophical conversation. Examining and testing each other's opinions and beliefs would be a long, colossal exercise in stupidity, if every person's opinions are right for them — and that's exactly what follows if Protagoras' "Truth" is really true, and not just the great man joking around from the inner sanctum of his book.

Theodorus
He was my friend, Socrates, as you said yourself. I couldn't bear to be the one whose answers get him refuted — but I can't oppose you either, since I agree with you. So please, take Theaetetus back. He was answering you very nicely.

Socrates
Theodorus, if you walked into a wrestling school in Sparta, would you really think you had the right to sit there watching the wrestlers stripped down — some of them looking pretty pathetic — without stripping down yourself and giving them a look at your physique?

Theodorus
Why not, if they'd let me off? Which I'm hoping you will, out of respect for my age and stiff joints. Find some limber young man to grapple with, and leave me out of the gymnasium.

Socrates
Well, Theodorus, if that's your will, it's no ill will of mine, as the proverb-lovers say. So back to the wise Theaetetus. Tell me, Theaetetus — about what we were just saying — aren't you stunned, like I am, to discover that you've suddenly been promoted to the level of the wisest of men? Or rather of the gods — since I assume Protagoras' "measure" applies just as much to gods as to men?

Theaetetus
I assume so too. And yes, I admit it: I'm stunned. When I first heard the doctrine — that whatever appears to each person is, for them — it seemed completely satisfying. Now everything looks different.

Socrates
That's because you're young, my dear boy. You've got quick ears for crowd-pleasing arguments, and they sway you. Protagoras — or someone arguing on his behalf — will have an answer ready. He'll say: "Good people, young and old, you sit around making speeches. You drag in the gods — whose existence or nonexistence I refuse to discuss in speech or in writing. You go on about how dreadful it would be for human reason to sink to the level of the beasts — which plays great with a crowd. But proof? Demonstration? You offer not one word of it. Everything with you is plausibility. If Theodorus or any other geometer tried to do geometry on the strength of what's merely plausible and likely, he wouldn't be worth a thing. So you and Theodorus had better ask yourselves whether, on matters this important, you're willing to settle for probabilities and pretty phrases."

Theaetetus
Well, that wouldn't be fair, Socrates — not by your standards or ours.

Socrates
Then apparently you and Theodorus think we have to come at it some other way?

Theaetetus
Yes, a completely different way.

Socrates
Then here's the way: let's ask whether knowledge and perception are the same thing or different things. That was the real target of our whole argument, wasn't it? That's why we stirred up all these strange questions.

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Socrates
So — shall we agree that we know everything we see and hear? Take languages: before we've learned a foreign language, when foreigners speak, shall we say we don't hear them? Or that we both hear and know what they're saying? Or take reading: when we look at letters we can't read, shall we insist we don't see them? Or that since we see them, we must know them?

Theaetetus
We'll say, Socrates, that we know exactly as much of them as we see and hear. With the letters, we see and know their shape and color; with the foreign speech, we hear and know the rise and fall of the sound. But what reading teachers and interpreters could tell us about them — that we don't perceive by sight or hearing, and don't know.

Socrates
Excellent, Theaetetus! I won't argue with you there — you deserve room to grow. But look, here comes another difficulty, and you'll have to fight this one off too.

Theaetetus
What is it?

Socrates
This. Someone will ask: "Can a man who has come to know something, and who still has the memory of it, preserved intact, not know the very thing he's remembering, at the moment he remembers it?" I'm being long-winded, I'm afraid. All I'm asking is: can a person who has learned something, and remembers it, fail to know it?

Theaetetus
Impossible, Socrates. That would be monstrous.

Socrates
Am I talking nonsense, then? Think it through. Seeing is perceiving, and sight is a perception — right?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And by our recent definition, anyone who sees something knows it?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And you'd grant that there's such a thing as memory?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Memory of something, or of nothing?

Theaetetus
Of something, obviously.

Socrates
Of things one has learned and perceived — that kind of something?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
So a man sometimes remembers what he has seen?

Theaetetus
He does.

Socrates
Even with his eyes shut? Or does he forget the moment he closes them?

Theaetetus
That would be an outrageous thing to say, Socrates.

Socrates
And yet say it we must, if our earlier argument is going to survive.

Theaetetus
What do you mean? I can't quite pin it down — though I have a strong suspicion you're right.

Socrates
Here's the problem. We say the one who sees knows what he sees, because sight, perception, and knowledge are all agreed to be the same thing.

Theaetetus
Right.

Socrates
But the man who saw something, and so knows it, still remembers it when he closes his eyes — even though he no longer sees it.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And "not seeing" is "not knowing," since "seeing" is "knowing."

Theaetetus
That follows.

Socrates
Then look at the conclusion: a man who has come to know something, and still remembers it, doesn't know it — because he isn't seeing it. And that's the result we just called monstrous.

Theaetetus
So it is.

Socrates
So the claim that knowledge and perception are the same leads straight to an impossibility?

Theaetetus
It looks that way.

Socrates
Then they must be different things?

Theaetetus
I suppose they must.

Socrates
So we're back to the beginning: what is knowledge? Although — Theaetetus, hold on. What are we doing?

Theaetetus
About what?

Socrates
We're behaving like a no-account fighting rooster: hopping away from the argument and crowing victory before we've actually won.

Theaetetus
How so?

Socrates
We've been acting like professional debaters. We made our agreements about the meanings of words, scored our point through verbal consistency, and congratulated ourselves on winning. We claim to be philosophers, not prize-fighting word-twisters — and yet, without noticing it, we've sunk to the level of those clever gentlemen.

Theaetetus
I still don't see what you mean.

Socrates
Then let me try to explain. We asked whether someone who has learned a thing and remembers it can fail to know it. We pointed out that a man who has seen something can shut his eyes and remember it while no longer seeing it — and so he'd remember and not-know at the same time. We agreed that was impossible. And so the Protagorean tale perished — and yours along with it, the claim that knowledge is perception.

Theaetetus
So it seems.

Socrates
But I suspect, my friend, things would have gone very differently if the father of the first of those two brainchildren were still alive. Protagoras would have had plenty to say in its defense. As it is, he's dead, and we're kicking his orphan around. Even the guardians he left behind won't lift a finger — and our friend Theodorus here is one of them. In the name of justice, it looks like I'll have to come to the child's defense myself.

Theodorus
Not me, Socrates — Callias, son of Hipponicus, is the real guardian of his estate. I drifted away from abstract argument to geometry early on. Still, I'll be grateful if you come to his rescue.

Socrates
Well said, Theodorus. Watch, then, and see what my rescue looks like — because a person can land in even worse paradoxes than the ones we just saw, if he doesn't pay attention to how words are actually used in argument. Should I explain this to you, or to Theaetetus?

Theodorus
To both of us — but let the younger one give the answers. There's less shame for him if he gets tripped up.

Socrates
Then here comes the truly fearsome question. It's this: can the same man know something and also not know the very thing he knows?

Theodorus
Well, Theaetetus? What's our answer?

Theaetetus
He can't — I'd say.

Socrates
Oh yes he can — if you're going to maintain that seeing is knowing. What will you do when some self-assured fellow has you cornered — "trapped in a well," as the saying goes — claps his hand over one of your eyes, and asks whether you can see his cloak with the eye he's covering? How will you answer him? There's no escaping this man.

Theaetetus
I'd say: "Not with that eye, but with the other one."

Socrates
So you see and don't see the same thing at the same time?

Theaetetus
Well — yes, in a certain sense.

Socrates
"None of that," he'll snap. "I didn't ask in what sense. I asked one thing: do you know what you don't know? You've just been caught seeing what you don't see. And you've already admitted that seeing is knowing and not-seeing is not-knowing. Add it up yourself."

Theaetetus
And when I add it up, I get the exact contradiction of what I claimed.

Socrates
Yes, you marvel — and you might have suffered far worse if your opponent had kept going. He could have asked whether you can know sharply and also know dully; whether you can know something up close but not at a distance; whether you can know the same thing intensely and faintly — and on and on without end. Questions like these are the ammunition of the mercenary skirmisher of debate, the fighter who argues for pay. The moment you took the position that knowledge is perception, he'd have ambushed you — launched his assault on hearing and smelling and the rest of the senses, shown you no mercy, kept you pinned down while you stood there marveling at his prodigious wisdom. By the time he had you tangled in his net, you'd be paying whatever ransom the two of you agreed on before he'd let you out. But now, you'll ask, what would Protagoras himself say in defense of his doctrine? Shall I speak for him?

Theaetetus
Please do.

Socrates
He'll repeat everything we've already said on his behalf, and then — I imagine — he'll close with us in open contempt. He'll say:

"This fine fellow Socrates found a little boy, asked him whether the same person can remember a thing and not know it, and the boy got scared, couldn't see the trap coming, and said no — and so, Socrates made a laughingstock of me. Here's the truth, you slapdash Socrates. When you cross-examine some doctrine of mine by asking questions, and the person answering gets caught out, then I am refuted only if he answered the way I would have answered. If he says something different, the refuted party is him, not me. Start with this: do you really expect anyone to grant that the memory of a past impression is the same as the impression itself was, at the moment it was happening, to the person having it? Not even close. Or that anyone would shy away from admitting that the same man can know and not know the same thing? Or — if that scares him — that he'd ever concede a man who has become unlike his former self is the same person he was before the change? Indeed, that there is such a thing as 'a man' at all — one man — rather than an endless series of men, a new one for every change he undergoes? I'm choosing my words precisely, you see, since we have to watch out for each other's verbal traps.

"No, my good sir," he'll say. "Come at my actual argument, and do it more generously. Show, if you can, that our perceptions are not private and individual to each of us. Or, granting that they're individual, show that it doesn't follow that what appears to a person thereby becomes — or, if you insist on the word, is — for that person alone. As for your pigs and your baboons — you're not just talking like a pig yourself, you're training your audience to root through my writings like pigs, and that does you no credit. Because I say it again: the truth is exactly as I've written it. Each of us is the measure of what is and of what is not. But people differ from each other enormously in precisely this way: different things are, and appear, to different people. As for wisdom and wise men, far be it from me to deny they exist. Here's what I call a wise man: anyone who can take someone for whom things seem and are bad, and turn things around so they seem and are good for him.

"And don't go chasing the letter of my words this time — let me make my meaning even plainer. Remember what was said earlier: to a sick man, his food seems bitter, and is bitter; to a healthy man, it both is and seems the opposite. Now, there's no call to make either of these men out to be wiser than the other — that's not even possible. And there's no call to declare the sick man ignorant for thinking what he thinks, or the healthy man wise for thinking differently. What's needed is to change the man from one condition to the other — because the other condition is better. Education is the same: a change from a worse condition to a better one. The difference is that a doctor produces the change with drugs, while a sophist does it with words. Not that anyone ever made a man whose beliefs were false start believing truly — it's impossible to believe what is not, or to believe anything beyond what one experiences, and what one experiences is always true. What happens, I'd say, is this: a man whose soul is in a worthless condition believes things that match that condition, and a sound soul gives him sound things to believe. Those beliefs — the ones some people naively call 'true' — are simply better than the others. Not truer. Better.

"And as for the wise, dear Socrates — I'm not calling them tadpoles, far from it. Where bodies are concerned, the wise are the doctors; where plants are concerned, the farmers. Yes, farmers too: when plants are sickly, I say the farmer strips away their bad and diseased sensations and instills good, healthy, sound ones — true ones, even. And wise, skilled public speakers do the same for cities: they make sound things, rather than rotten things, seem just. Because whatever each city judges just and admirable really is just and admirable for that city, as long as it holds those beliefs. But the wise man takes each rotten thing that is and seems just to them, and makes a sound thing be and seem just instead. By the same logic, the sophist who can educate his pupils this way is wise, and worth every penny of his fees. And so: some people are wiser than others, and yet nobody believes falsely. And you, Socrates, like it or not, will have to put up with being a measure — because that's where the argument stands, solid as ever.

"You're welcome to attack it from the ground up, if you like — lay out a counterargument in a full speech. Or do it by questions, if you prefer; no sensible person runs away from questioning, quite the opposite. But do observe one rule: don't ask questions unfairly. It's wildly inconsistent to claim you care about virtue and then spend your time cheating at argument. Cheating, here, means failing to keep the two modes separate — the competitive debate and the genuine discussion. In a debate, play games and trip people up as much as you can. But in genuine discussion, be serious. Set your partner back on his feet; show him only the slips that he made on his own, or that were drilled into him by the company he kept before you. Treat people that way, and those who spend time with you will blame themselves for their own confusion and dead ends, not you. They'll follow you and love you — and hate themselves enough to run from who they are into philosophy, hoping to become different people and shed the selves they used to be. But take the usual approach — the opposite one — and you'll get the opposite result: instead of philosophers, you'll turn your companions into people who, by the time they grow up, despise the whole enterprise. So if you take my advice, you'll come to us not with hostility and combativeness but with goodwill, and honestly examine what we mean when we declare that all things are in motion, and that whatever appears to each person or each city, is — for that person or city. Build on that to investigate whether knowledge and perception are the same or different. But don't do what you did just now — making your case out of the everyday use of words and phrases, which ordinary people yank around any way they please, creating no end of confusion for each other."

There, Theodorus — that's my contribution to your old friend's defense. The best I can scrape together from my small means. If he were alive, he'd have defended his own ideas in far grander style.

Theodorus
You're joking, Socrates. Though I have to admit, your defense of him was downright heroic.

Socrates
Thank you, my friend. But I hope you noticed Protagoras scolding us to take this seriously. His text — "Man is the measure of all things" — was meant as a solemn pronouncement, and he complained that we were running the argument through a boy, letting the boy's timidity count as evidence against his position. He said we were treating him as a joke.

Theodorus
How could I have missed all that, Socrates?

Socrates
Well then — shall we do what he asks?

Theodorus
By all means.

Socrates
But if we're going to respect his wishes, then you and I have to take up the argument ourselves, in complete seriousness, asking and answering each other directly. Because compared to him, the rest of us here are just boys. There's no other way to escape the accusation that we're re-examining his thesis by playing games with children.

Theodorus
Come now — isn't Theaetetus better at following a philosophical investigation than plenty of men with long beards?

Socrates
Yes, Theodorus, but not better than you. So don't imagine that I'm obligated to defend your departed friend by every means I've got while you defend nothing and nobody. No, my good man — don't slip away until we find out whether you really are the measure of diagrams, or whether everyone is equally his own measure, fully self-sufficient in astronomy and geometry and all the other subjects where you're supposed to be the expert.

Theodorus
Sitting next to you, Socrates, nobody escapes getting pulled into an argument. I was talking nonsense just now when I said you'd excuse me — that you wouldn't be like the Spartans and force me to strip and wrestle. Actually, you're worse. The Spartan rule is "strip or leave." You're more like Antaeus, the giant who wrestled every traveler who came near: you won't let anyone who approaches you go until you've stripped him down and made him take a fall with you in argument.

Socrates
That's it exactly, Theodorus — you've diagnosed my condition perfectly. But I'm more relentless than the giants of old, because I've faced an endless parade of heroes. Many a Heracles, many a Theseus, mighty in words, has cracked my head open. And still I keep coming back for more of this rough sport — it grips me like a passion. So come on: take a fall with me. It'll do us both good.

Theodorus
Fine, I give in. Lead me wherever you like. When it comes to argument, you're like fate itself — no one escapes the web you weave. But I won't go a step further than what we've agreed.

Socrates
One round will be enough. And now let's be careful not to slip back, without noticing, into the kind of talk that gets us accused of acting like children.

Theodorus
I'll do my best to avoid it.

Socrates
First, then, let's go back to our earlier objection. Were we right to fault Protagoras — to take offense at his claim that everyone is equally sufficient in wisdom — when he himself admitted that some people are better and some worse, and that the better ones, the ones he called "the wise," really do excel the rest?

Theodorus
Very true.

Socrates
If Protagoras were alive and answering for himself, instead of us answering on his behalf, there'd be no need to review or shore up the argument. But he isn't here, and someone might accuse us of putting words in his mouth. So shouldn't we pin down exactly what he meant? A great deal could be riding on it.

Theodorus
True.

Socrates
Then let's get the basis of agreement not from any third party, but from his own statement, in the fewest possible words.

Theodorus
How?

Socrates
Like this. His words are: "What seems true to a man, is true — for him."

Theodorus
Yes, that's what he says.

Socrates
Well then, Protagoras, we too are voicing the opinion of a man — or rather of all mankind — when we say that everyone considers himself wiser than other people in some areas, and less wise in others. In moments of danger — in war, at sea, in sickness — don't people look up to their commanders as if they were gods, expecting to be saved by them, for exactly one reason: that these men know more than they do? The whole world is full of people looking for teachers and leaders, for themselves and for their animals — and full of others who believe they're qualified to teach and qualified to lead. And all of this implies that people themselves believe that wisdom and ignorance exist among them.

Theodorus
Certainly.

Socrates
And they take wisdom to be true thinking, and ignorance to be false opinion.

Theodorus
Exactly.

Socrates
So how should we handle the argument, Protagoras? Should we say that people's opinions are always true? Or sometimes true and sometimes false? Either way it comes out the same: their opinions are not always true, but sometimes true and sometimes false. Think about it, Theodorus. Would any follower of Protagoras — would you yourself — want to insist that no person ever considers another person ignorant or mistaken?

Theodorus
That's unbelievable, Socrates. Of course people do.

Socrates
And yet that absurdity is exactly what's built into the thesis that man is the measure of all things.

Theodorus
How so?

Socrates
Like this. Suppose you settle something in your own mind and state your opinion to me. By Protagoras' rule, let's grant that it's true — for you. But what about the rest of us? Don't we get to judge your judgment? Or do we somehow always judge that your opinion is true? Aren't there thousands of people, every time you form an opinion, ready to take up arms against you with the opposite opinion, convinced that you've judged falsely?

Theodorus
Oh yes, Socrates — thousands and tens of thousands, as Homer says, and they give me no end of trouble.

Socrates
Well then, shall we say that in those cases your opinion is true for you, but false for the ten thousand others?

Theodorus
That seems to be the only conclusion.

Socrates
And what about Protagoras himself? Suppose neither he nor the multitude believed that man is the measure of all things — which in fact they don't. Then the "Truth" that Protagoras wrote wouldn't be true for anyone at all. And suppose he did believe it, but the multitude doesn't agree. Then you have to start by admitting this: by however much the many outnumber the one, by that much his truth is more false than true.

Theodorus
That follows — if truth really varies with each person's opinion.

Socrates
And here's the best part of the joke. When he admits that everyone's opinions are true, he's admitting the truth of his opponents' opinion — and their opinion is that he's wrong.

Theodorus
Certainly.

Socrates
So he concedes that his own view is false, if he grants that the people who think he's wrong are thinking truly?

Theodorus
Of course.

Socrates
But the other side doesn't admit that they're wrong?

Theodorus
No, they don't.

Socrates
And he — judging from his own writings — has to agree that their opinion is true as well.

Theodorus
Clearly.

Socrates
Then everyone, starting with Protagoras himself, will dispute his doctrine — or rather, he'll concede it. The moment he grants that his opponent holds a true opinion, Protagoras himself is admitting that neither a dog nor any random man is "the measure" of anything he hasn't studied. Am I right?

Theodorus
Yes.

Socrates
So, since everybody disputes Protagoras' truth, it turns out to be true for no one — not for anyone else, and not even for Protagoras himself.

Theodorus
Socrates, I think we're running my old friend a bit too hard.

Socrates
Maybe — but I'm not sure we've overshot the truth. Of course, since he's older, we should expect him to be wiser than we are. If he could just pop his head up out of the underworld right now, he'd probably knock us both flat in no time — me for talking nonsense and you for agreeing with me — and then duck back underground and be gone. But he's beyond our reach, so we have to make do with our own faculties, such as they are, and say what actually seems true to us. And here's one thing nobody will deny: there are enormous differences in human understanding.

Theodorus
On that I completely agree.

Socrates
And isn't the firmest ground exactly the distinction we sketched out on Protagoras' behalf? Namely: with most things — especially immediate sensations like hot, dry, sweet — things really are just as they appear to each person. But if there's any area where we have to allow that one opinion can be better than another, surely it's health and disease. Not every woman or child — or animal, for that matter — knows enough about health to cure themselves. If anywhere, here one person really is wiser than another.

Theodorus
I quite agree.

Socrates
The same in politics. The Protagoreans will say that just and unjust, honorable and shameful, holy and unholy really are, for each city, whatever that city believes them to be and writes into law — that on these questions no individual and no city is wiser than another. But when it comes to deciding what's actually beneficial for the community, even they won't deny that one city's judgment is wiser than another's, one adviser better than the next. They'd hardly dare claim that whatever a city enacts, believing it beneficial, will actually turn out to be beneficial. It's only in that other realm — justice and injustice, piety and impiety — that they confidently insist none of it has any nature or essence of its own: the truth is whatever gets agreed on, at the moment of agreement, for exactly as long as the agreement lasts. And that's the philosophy of plenty of people who don't even follow Protagoras the whole way. But now, Theodorus, a new question is rising up — a bigger one than the last.

Theodorus
Well, Socrates, we have plenty of free time.

Socrates
So we do. And that remark of yours reminds me of something I've noticed many times: how absurdly helpless people who've spent their lives in philosophy look when they have to show up and speak in a courtroom. And it's no wonder!

Theodorus
What do you mean?

Socrates
I mean that people raised in philosophy and free inquiry, compared to people who've been knocking around courtrooms since they were young — well, the difference is like the difference between a free man's upbringing and a slave's.

Theodorus
How does the difference show?

Socrates
It shows in exactly that free time you just mentioned. The free man always has it. He talks through his subject in peace, at leisure. Like us, right now, he wanders from one topic to another, and on to a third; if a new thought strikes him, he starts over — as we're doing — and he doesn't care whether the discussion is long or short. His only goal is to reach the truth.

But the courtroom man is always in a hurry. The water-clock is running, driving him on — no rambling allowed. His opponent stands over him, enforcing the rules, holding the sworn indictment that lays out exactly what may and may not be discussed; he can't stray from it by an inch. He's a slave arguing with a fellow slave before a master who sits there holding the case in his hands. The stakes are never about something neutral — they're always about himself, and often the race is for his very life.

All this has made him tense and sharp. He's learned to flatter his master with words and grovel before him in deeds. But his soul has come out small and crooked. The slavery he's lived in since youth has robbed him of growth, of straightness, of independence. Dangers and fears hit him early, when he was too young and tender to face them honestly, so he was forced into bent and devious ways. He learned to lie and to retaliate from the very beginning, and he's grown warped and stunted. He passes from youth into manhood with no soundness left in him — and yet he's convinced he's become formidable and wise. That's your courtroom man, Theodorus. Shall I paint the companion portrait — the philosopher, one of our own brotherhood? Or should we get back to the argument? We don't want to abuse this freedom of digression we keep claiming.

Theodorus
No, Socrates — not until we've finished. You said it truly yourself: we belong to a free brotherhood, and we are not the servants of the argument. The argument is our servant, and it can wait until we're ready. Who's our judge here? Where's the spectator with any right to censor us or hurry us along, the way they can with the poets?

Socrates
Then since that's what you want, let's talk about the leaders of philosophy — there's no point discussing the second-rate ones. From their youth on, the true philosophers have never known the way to the marketplace, or the law courts, or the council chamber, or any other political gathering. They neither see nor hear the laws and decrees of the city, whether published or proclaimed. The scrambling of political clubs for office, the meetings, the dinners, the parties with hired flute-girls — none of this enters even their dreams. Has something gone well or badly in the city? Has some family scandal come down to a man from his ancestors? The philosopher knows no more about it than he knows how many pints of water are in the sea, as the saying goes. And he doesn't even realize he doesn't know — because he's not staying aloof for the sake of his reputation. The truth is that only his body is here in the city, residing among us. His mind, having concluded that all these human concerns are small and worth nothing, treats them with contempt and flies in every direction — "soaring far and wide," as Pindar says — measuring the earth and the heavens, the things below the earth and above the sky, investigating the entire nature of everything that exists, each thing as a whole, and never lowering itself to anything close at hand.

Theodorus
What do you mean by that, Socrates?

Socrates
Let me illustrate it, Theodorus, with the joke that a clever and witty Thracian servant girl is said to have made about Thales. He was studying the stars, looking up — and fell into a well. She teased him: he was so eager to know what was happening in the heavens that he couldn't see what was right in front of his feet. The same joke applies to everyone who lives in philosophy. The philosopher genuinely doesn't know his next-door neighbor — not just what the man is doing, but practically whether he's a human being or some other creature. Meanwhile, what is a human being? What should such a nature do or undergo, that's different from every other nature? That is what he's asking, that is what he's busy investigating. You follow me, Theodorus?

Theodorus
I do, and it's true.

Socrates
And so, my friend, in private and in public, just as I said at the start: whenever the philosopher is dragged into court, or anywhere else he's forced to deal with what's at his feet and in front of his eyes, he becomes a laughingstock — not just for Thracian servant girls but for the crowd in general — falling into wells and every kind of trouble because he has no experience. His awkwardness is appalling; people take him for a complete fool. When he's insulted, he has no personal slanders to fire back, because he doesn't know anything scandalous about anybody — he's never been interested. So he just stands there at a loss, and everyone laughs at him for it. And when people are being praised and congratulating each other, he can't help laughing out loud — not for show, but honestly — and that makes him look like an outright idiot.

When a tyrant or a king is being glorified, he feels like he's hearing some farmer praised — a pig-keeper, a shepherd, a cowherd — congratulated for the gallons of milk he squeezes out of his herd. Except that the animal kings tend and milk is more dangerous and treacherous than cattle, and the king himself, with no leisure to learn anything, penned in by the walls of his fortress like a herdsman in his mountain pen, ends up just as rough and uneducated as any shepherd. When the philosopher hears about some landowner with ten thousand acres or more — an astonishing fortune! — to him it sounds like next to nothing, because he's used to thinking about the whole earth. And when people sing the praises of family — "he's a gentleman; he can show seven generations of wealthy ancestors" — he thinks the praise comes from dim, shortsighted people who can't see the whole picture: that every single person has had thousands upon thousands of ancestors, and among them, for anyone you pick, there have been rich men and beggars, kings and slaves, Greeks and barbarians, beyond counting. When someone takes pride in a list of twenty-five forebears stretching back to Heracles, son of Amphitryon, the pettiness of it baffles him. Amphitryon had a twenty-fifth ancestor too — who was whatever luck made him — and that man had a fiftieth, and so on back forever. Why can't they do the math? He laughs to himself at their inability to count, and thinks a little arithmetic would cure them of their swollen vanity. In all these situations, the crowd mocks the philosopher — partly because he seems arrogant, partly because he doesn't know the everyday things at his feet and is hopeless in every practical situation.

Theodorus
That's exactly how it goes, Socrates.

Socrates
But, my friend, watch what happens when the philosopher drags one of them up into the heights. When some lawyer is willing to leave behind his "Who did what to whom?" and climb up to look at justice and injustice themselves — what each of them is, how they differ from each other and from everything else. When he's pulled away from his stock phrases about whether a king with piles of gold is a happy man, up to the examination of kingship itself, and of human happiness and misery in general — what they really are, and how a human being should pursue the one and escape the other. When that narrow, sharp, little legal mind has to give an account of all that — then the tables turn, and the philosopher gets his revenge. Dangling at that height, staring down into so much open space, the lawyer gets dizzy. Terrified, lost, stammering broken phrases, he becomes the laughingstock — not to Thracian servant girls or other uneducated people, who can't even see what's happening, but to everyone whose upbringing was the opposite of a slave's.

So there are the two characters, Theodorus. One is the man raised in genuine freedom and leisure — the one you call a philosopher. He can look simple and useless when menial tasks fall to him: he doesn't know how to bundle up bedding, or sweeten a sauce, or sweeten a speech — and we shouldn't hold it against him. The other can do all those little services briskly and neatly, but he doesn't know how to wear his cloak like a free man, much less how to strike up the music of true discourse and rightly sing the life that gods live, and human beings blessed by heaven.

Theodorus
Socrates, if you could persuade everyone of what you're saying, the way you persuade me, there would be more peace and less evil in the world.

Socrates
Evil can never be destroyed, Theodorus — there must always be something standing opposed to the good. And it can't be housed among the gods in heaven, so by necessity it haunts our mortal nature and this earthly realm. That's why we should try to escape from earth to heaven as quickly as we can. And escaping means becoming like God, as far as that's possible — and becoming like God means becoming just and holy, with wisdom.

But, my friend, it isn't easy to convince people of the right reason for pursuing virtue and fleeing vice. The reason isn't the one the world gives — so that you'll seem good rather than bad. That, in my judgment, is just an old wives' tale. Here's the truth. God is never, in any way, unjust. God is perfect justice, and nothing is more like God than the person among us who becomes as just as possible. This is the test of a man's true ability — or of his emptiness and cowardice. To know it is real wisdom and real virtue; not to know it is obvious folly and vice. Everything else that passes for ability and wisdom — political cleverness, technical know-how — is coarse and common by comparison.

So when a man acts unjustly and speaks or does unholy things, it's far better not to let him believe his rottenness is cleverness. People like that glory in the insult. They imagine they're hearing: "These aren't useless dead weight — these are real men, the kind a city needs to survive!" So let's tell them the truth: they are exactly what they don't think they are, and all the more so because they don't know it. Because they don't know the penalty of injustice — and that's the last thing anyone should be ignorant of. The penalty isn't what they think it is. It isn't whippings and executions, which unjust men sometimes dodge entirely. It's a penalty that cannot be escaped.

Theodorus
What penalty do you mean?

Socrates
There are two patterns set up in reality, eternally: the divine pattern, which is supremely happy, and the godless pattern, which is supremely wretched. But these men can't see this. Their foolishness, their utter blindness, hides from them what their evil actions are doing — making them grow more like the one pattern and less like the other. And the penalty they pay is exactly that: they live the life that matches the pattern they're coming to resemble. If we tell them that unless they shed their "cleverness," the place untouched by evil will refuse them after death, and that here on earth they'll live a life shaped like themselves — evil men in the company of evil — they'll hear all this as the babbling of idiots. They're far too clever for it.

Theodorus
Very true, Socrates.

Socrates
True indeed, my friend — I know it well. But there's one odd thing about these people. When they have to reason privately, one-on-one, about the philosophy they despise, and they're brave enough to hear the argument through to the end without running away — then, strangely, they end up dissatisfied with themselves. Their famous rhetoric withers, and they're left as helpless as children. But all this is digression, and we'd better stop, or it will keep flooding in and drown our original argument. Shall we go back to it?

Theodorus
Honestly, Socrates, I'd rather have the digressions — at my age they're easier to follow. But by all means, let's return to the argument.

Socrates
Well then, here's roughly where we were. We were talking about the people who preach that everything is in flux, and that what seems so to each person is so — for that person. We said they're prepared to stand firm on most things, and especially on questions of justice: whatever a city decrees and believes to be just is just, for that city, as long as the decree stands. But when it comes to the good, nobody is bold enough to keep fighting and claim that whatever a city enacts believing it beneficial really is beneficial as long as it's on the books. Anyone who said that would just be playing with the word "beneficial" — and that would make a mockery of our actual question, wouldn't it?

Theodorus
It certainly would.

Socrates
He shouldn't talk about the word, but about the thing the word points to.

Theodorus
Right.

Socrates
Whatever word a city uses, the good — the beneficial — is what it's aiming at when it legislates. Every city makes all its laws with a view to what will benefit it most, as far as its judgment and power can reach. Can lawmaking have any other goal?

Theodorus
Certainly not.

Socrates
But does it always hit the mark? Don't cities often miss?

Theodorus
Yes, I think mistakes do happen.

Socrates
We can see the possibility of error even more clearly if we ask about the whole category that "beneficial" belongs to. The beneficial is about the future, isn't it? When we make laws, we make them on the theory that they'll be useful in the time to come — which is to say, the future.

Theodorus
Very true.

Socrates
Then here's a question for Protagoras, or any of his disciples. "Protagoras," we'll say, "man, by your account, is the measure of all things — of white, heavy, light, all things of that sort. He carries the criterion inside himself, so when he believes things are as he experiences them, he believes what is true — for him." Isn't that so?

Theodorus
Yes.

Socrates
"But Protagoras," we'll go on, "does your doctrine cover the future too? Does each man carry the criterion of what will be, so that whatever he expects to happen actually happens — for him? Take heat. When an ordinary man thinks he's about to catch a fever — that this hot flush coming on is the start of one — and a doctor thinks otherwise, whose prediction should we bet on? Or are both of them right: he'll be hot and feverish in his own judgment, and not feverish in the doctor's?"

Theodorus
That would be ridiculous!

Socrates
And on the question of whether next season's wine will be sweet or dry — surely the vinegrower's judgment beats the harp player's?

Theodorus
Certainly.

Socrates
And on what will sound harmonious or off-key, the musician will be a better judge than the gym instructor — even about what the gym instructor himself will later find harmonious?

Theodorus
Of course.

Socrates
And when a feast is being prepared, the cook will be a better judge than the guest of the pleasure to come — the guest being no cook. Notice we're not yet arguing about present or past pleasure, only about future pleasure: is each person really his own best judge of what will seem pleasant, and be pleasant, to him later? Or take your own case, Protagoras: wouldn't you predict better than any ordinary person which arguments will persuade a jury?

Theodorus
On that point, Socrates, he certainly used to insist — emphatically — that he was better at it than anybody alive.

Socrates
Of course he did, my friend. Nobody would have paid him those enormous fees for conversation if he'd actually convinced his customers that no one — not even a prophet — could judge what will be, and will seem to be, any better than they could judge for themselves.

Theodorus
Nobody at all.

Socrates
And lawmaking, and the question of what's beneficial, are entirely about the future — and everyone will admit that cities, when they pass laws, must often miss what would benefit them most.
