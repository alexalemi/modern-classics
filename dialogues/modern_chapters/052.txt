Athenian
Now, assuming children of both sexes have been born, it's time for us to consider their upbringing and education. This isn't something we can just ignore, and yet it might seem like a topic better suited to advice and guidance than to actual legislation. In private life, there are all sorts of little things — arising from individual pleasures, pains, and desires — that aren't always obvious, and that run counter to what the lawgiver intends. They make citizens' characters varied and inconsistent, and that's a real problem for a state. These matters are too small and frequent to make into criminal offenses — and if you did criminalize them, you'd actually undermine the written law, because people would get into the habit of constantly breaking the law over trivial things. The result is that you can't legislate about them, but you can't stay silent either. I realize I'm being a bit vague, but I'll try to bring my meaning into the light. I'll admit that what I'm saying right now lacks clarity.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Am I not right that a good education is one that develops both mind and body as much as possible?

Cleinias
Without a doubt.

Athenian
And isn't it obvious that the healthiest bodies are those that grow up from infancy in the best and straightest way?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Here's something else we can observe: the first burst of growth in every living thing is by far the greatest and most dramatic. Many people would even argue that a person at twenty-five isn't even twice the height they were at five.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
And isn't rapid growth without proper, abundant exercise the source of countless problems in the body?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
And the body should get the most exercise during the period when it's receiving the most nourishment?

Cleinias
But wait, Stranger — are we really going to impose all this exercise on newborn infants?

Athenian
Not newborns — even earlier. On babies still in the womb.

Cleinias
What do you mean? During pregnancy?

Athenian
Exactly. I'm not at all surprised you haven't heard of this particular kind of exercise for such tiny creatures. It's unusual, but I'll do my best to explain it to you.

Cleinias
Please do.

Athenian
The practice is easier for us Athenians to understand than for you, because of certain pastimes we take to extremes back home. In Athens, not just boys but often grown men keep quails and roosters that they train to fight each other. And they're far from thinking that the fights themselves provide enough exercise. On top of the fighting, they carry the birds around — the smaller ones in their hands, the bigger ones tucked under their arms — and take them for walks covering many miles, not for the sake of their own health, but for the health of the birds. This proves to anyone with sense that all bodies benefit from being shaken and moved, as long as the movement doesn't lead to exhaustion — whether the motion comes from the body itself, or from being rocked in a cradle, or from the motion of a ship at sea, or from riding a horse, or from any other kind of movement. By mastering their nourishment through motion, bodies gain beauty, health, and strength.

So, accepting all this — what follows? Should we pass a ridiculous law requiring pregnant women to walk around and shape the embryo the way we shape wax before it hardens? And after birth, swaddle the infant for two years? Should we force nurses, under threat of legal penalties, to always be carrying the children somewhere — to temples, to the countryside, to relatives' houses — until the children can stand properly? And require them to make sure the babies' limbs don't get distorted from bearing weight too young? The nurses should continue carrying them until the child completes its third year. They should be strong women, and there should be more than one per child. Should these be our rules, with penalties for neglecting them? No, no — the penalty we'd really face would fall on our own heads soon enough.

Cleinias
What penalty?

Athenian
Ridicule. And the sheer difficulty of getting nurses — with their stubborn and resistant natures — to actually comply.

Cleinias
Then why bring the whole thing up at all?

Athenian
Because when the leading citizens of a state hear about it, they're very likely to arrive at an important realization: that without proper regulation of private life in cities, you can't expect stability in your laws. And anyone who grasps this point may voluntarily adopt the practices I just described, run their household well, and be happy for it.

Cleinias
That's likely enough.

Athenian
So let's continue with our legislation until we've determined the exercises suited to children's souls, just as we've been working through the rules for their bodies.

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
Let's take this as our first principle for both the body and soul of very young children: nursing them and keeping them in motion day and night is good for all of them, and the younger they are, the more they need it. Ideally, infants should live as if they were always rocking on a ship at sea. This is the lesson we can draw from the experience of nurses, and also from the motion used in the rites of the Corybantes. When mothers want their restless children to fall asleep, they don't use stillness — just the opposite. They rock them in their arms. And they don't give them silence — they sing to them and wrap them in sweet melodies. The Bacchic women, too, are cured of their frenzy in the same way: through dance and music.

Cleinias
And what's the reason for that, Stranger?

Athenian
The reason is pretty clear.

Cleinias
What is it?

Athenian
The agitation of both the Bacchic worshippers and the children is a kind of fear, rooted in an unhealthy condition of the soul. When someone applies external movement to this kind of inner disturbance, the motion from outside overpowers the terrible, violent motion within, and produces peace and calm in the soul. It quiets the restless pounding of the heart — which is exactly what you want — sending children to sleep, and bringing the Bacchic worshippers, while still awake, to dance to the pipe with the help of the gods to whom they offer their sacrifices. It replaces their frenzy with a sound mind. To put it simply, there's a great deal to be said for this treatment.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
But if fear has this kind of power, we should also realize something: any soul that's been exposed to a lot of fear from early childhood will become more prone to fear. And everyone would agree that this is the path to forming a habit of cowardice, not courage.

Cleinias
No doubt.

Athenian
On the other hand, the habit of overcoming fears and terrors from childhood onward — that can be called an exercise in courage.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
So we can say that exercise and motion in the earliest years of life contribute greatly to building a part of virtue in the soul.

Cleinias
Quite true.

Athenian
Furthermore, a cheerful temperament — or the lack of one — plays a big role in developing either courage or cowardice.

Cleinias
To be sure.

Athenian
So now we need to work out how, and to what extent, we can implant either character in young children, if we choose.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
There's a common view that luxury makes young people discontented, irritable, and wildly overreactive to trivial things. On the other hand, excessive and harsh servitude makes people mean, downtrodden, and hostile to their fellow humans — making them unfit as companions.

Cleinias
But how is the state supposed to educate children who don't yet understand the language of their country and can't appreciate any kind of instruction?

Athenian
Here's how. Every animal that's born tends to make some kind of cry, and this is especially true of human beings. Humans are also more inclined to weep than any other animal.

Cleinias
Quite true.

Athenian
When nurses want to figure out what an infant needs, they use these signs: when something is brought to the baby and it stays quiet, they assume it's happy. When it cries and screams, they assume it's not. Tears and cries are the signals by which children show what they love and hate. And this period — these first three years — is a very significant portion of a life, spent either well or poorly.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Now, doesn't it seem to you that a discontented and unpleasant nature is one that's full of more grief and sorrow than a good person ought to have?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Well then — if during those first three years, every possible effort were made to ensure that our young child experienced as little sorrow, fear, and pain as possible, wouldn't we expect to make their soul more gentle and cheerful in early childhood?

Cleinias
Obviously, Stranger — especially if we could provide them with a variety of pleasures.

Athenian
There I can't agree with you, Cleinias. You're amazing! That approach would be the child's absolute ruin. The beginning is always the most critical stage of education. Let's see if I'm right.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
This point where you and I disagree is extremely important, and I hope you, Megillus, will help settle it. Here's what I maintain: the right kind of life shouldn't chase after pleasures, but it also shouldn't completely avoid pain. It should embrace a middle state — what I just described as gentle and moderate — which we rightly associate with the divine. Now I'm saying that any person who wants to approach the divine should pursue this same middle path. They shouldn't rush headlong toward pleasures, because that won't free them from pain. And we shouldn't let anyone — young or old, male or female — live that way, and least of all the newborn infant. Infancy is when character is most deeply shaped by habit.

In fact, if I weren't afraid of sounding ridiculous, I'd go further and say that a woman during her year of pregnancy should be cared for more carefully than anyone else. She should be kept from violent or excessive pleasures and pains, and should cultivate gentleness, kindness, and a calm spirit during that time.

Cleinias
You don't need to ask Megillus which of us has spoken more truly, Stranger. I myself agree that everyone should avoid a life of pure pleasure or pure pain, and should always pursue a middle course. You've spoken well, and I'm happy to say you've been well answered.

Athenian
Very good, Cleinias. Now let the three of us consider one more point.

Cleinias
What is it?

Athenian
All the things we've been describing are commonly known as "unwritten customs," and what people call "ancestral laws" are of the same kind. The thought we had a moment ago — that we can neither formally call these things laws nor leave them unmentioned — is justified. These customs are the bonds that hold an entire state together. They sit between the written laws that already exist and those yet to be enacted. They're ancestral practices of great antiquity that, when properly maintained, shield and preserve the written law. But if they fall into disorder, they're like the support beams of a building that slip out of place and cause a total collapse — one part drags another down, and the whole beautiful structure falls because the old foundations have been undermined.

Keeping this in mind, Cleinias, you should bind your new state together in every possible way, leaving out nothing — whether great or small — that falls under the heading of laws, customs, or practices. A city is held together by all these things, and they're only durable when they depend on one another. So we shouldn't be surprised to find that many apparently trivial customs and habits keep pouring in and extending our legislation.

Cleinias
Very true. We're inclined to agree with you.

Athenian
Up to age three, for both boys and girls, if someone strictly follows the guidelines we've laid out and makes them a priority, it'll do a great deal of good for the young children. But at three, four, five, and six, the child's nature will need games and play. Now is the time to curb self-will by punishing it — but not in a way that humiliates the child. We said the same thing about slaves: you shouldn't add insult to punishment, which just makes them angry, but you also shouldn't leave them unpunished, which makes them spoiled. The same principle applies to freeborn children.

Children at this age have natural ways of playing that they discover for themselves when they get together. All children between three and six should meet at the village temples, with the families of each village gathering in one place. The nurses must make sure the children behave properly and in an orderly way. The nurses and their groups will be supervised by twelve matrons — one for each group — chosen annually from the women we previously mentioned, the ones who oversee marriage. These matrons will be selected by those marriage-supervisory women, one from each tribe, all of the same age. As soon as each matron is appointed, she'll take up her duties and go to the temples every day, punishing any offenders — slaves or foreigners — with the help of public servants. If any citizen disputes the punishment, she'll bring the case before the city wardens. If there's no dispute, she can handle the punishment herself.

After age six, it's time to separate the sexes — boys with boys, girls with girls. Now the real learning begins. Boys go to teachers of horsemanship, archery, javelin-throwing, and the sling. Girls too — at the very least until they know how to handle these weapons, and especially how to manage heavy armor. Because let me point out something: the way things are done now is almost universally misguided.

Cleinias
In what way?

Athenian
People assume that the right hand and the left hand are naturally suited for different tasks. But when it comes to our feet and legs, no one sees any difference. The truth is, we're essentially crippled in our hands by the foolishness of nurses and mothers. Our limbs are naturally balanced, but we create an artificial difference through bad habits. In some cases this doesn't matter much — for example, when we hold the lyre in the left hand and the plectrum in the right. But in other cases, it's pure foolishness to maintain this distinction.

The Scythians prove our error: they hold the bow out with the left hand and draw the arrow with the right, but they can use either hand for both. And there are many similar examples in chariot-driving and other activities, showing that people who make the left side weaker than the right are working against nature. With something like a horn plectrum or similar instruments, it doesn't really matter. But it makes a huge difference when a warrior has to use iron weapons — bows, javelins, and the like — and especially when fighting in heavy armor against other heavily-armed opponents. There's an enormous gap between someone who's been trained and someone who hasn't, between someone who's practiced gymnastics and someone who hasn't.

Think of it this way: a person who's perfected the Pancratium, or boxing, or wrestling isn't helpless when fighting from the left side. They don't limp and stumble in confusion when their opponent forces them to switch positions. The same principle should hold for heavy-armed combat and everything else. Someone with the ability to attack and defend from both sides should never leave either capability unused or untrained. If a person had the nature of Geryon or Briareus, they ought to be able to throw a hundred darts with their hundred hands!

Now, the magistrates — both men and women — should oversee all of this. The women will supervise the nursing and play of the children, and the men will supervise their education. The goal is that all children, boys and girls alike, should be sound in hand and foot, and shouldn't — if it can be helped — ruin nature's gifts through bad habits.

Education has two branches: gymnastics, which is concerned with the body, and music, which is designed to improve the soul. Gymnastics itself has two branches: dancing and wrestling. One kind of dancing imitates musical recitation and aims at preserving dignity and grace. The other aims at producing health, agility, and beauty in the body's limbs, giving each the proper range of movement — a harmonious motion flowing through everything, forming a natural accompaniment to the dance.

As for wrestling: the tricks that Antaeus and Cercyon invented in their systems, born from petty competitiveness, or the boxing tricks devised by Epeius or Amycus — these are useless for war and don't deserve much discussion. But the art of wrestling while standing upright, keeping the neck, hands, and sides free, working with energy and determination, with controlled strength, for the sake of health — that's always useful and shouldn't be neglected. When we reach that part of our legislation, we'll require both teachers to give instruction freely and students to receive it gratefully.

We also shouldn't leave out war dances from our choruses. Here in Crete, you have the armed dances of the Curetes, and in Sparta, the dances of the Dioscuri. And our own patron goddess Athena, who delights in dance, didn't think it was fitting to dance with empty hands. She put on a full suit of armor and danced in that attire. Our young men and women should imitate her in every way, honoring the goddess, both for the demands of war and for festive occasions.

It'll also be right for the boys, until they reach military age, to march in processions and offer prayers to all the gods — armed, on horseback, in fast and slow formations, performing dances and marches. They should offer prayers to the gods and the sons of gods, and engage in contests and practice bouts with exactly this purpose. These kinds of exercises, and no others, are useful in both peace and war, and beneficial to states and private households alike. Other physical labors, sports, and exercises are unworthy of free citizens, Megillus and Cleinias.

I've now described the kind of physical training I said at the start should be covered. If you know of anything better, please share your thoughts.

Cleinias
It's not easy, Stranger, to set aside these principles of gymnastics and wrestling and propose better ones.

Athenian
Now we need to turn to what still needs to be said about the gifts of the Muses and of Apollo. Earlier, we thought we'd covered everything and only gymnastics remained. But now we can see clearly what points were left out and should be addressed first. So let's discuss them.

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
Let me tell you once more — though you've heard me say this before — that extreme caution is needed, both from the speaker and the listener, when dealing with anything that's really unusual and out of the ordinary. Because what I'm about to say is the kind of thing many people would be afraid to put forward. And yet I have a confidence that pushes me ahead.

Cleinias
What do you have to say, Stranger?

Athenian
Here's what I'm saying: in states generally, no one has noticed that children's games have a great deal to do with whether laws remain stable or fall apart. When games are set up so that children always play the same games, entertain themselves the same way, and take delight in the same toys, then the more serious institutions of the state are left undisturbed. But when games are constantly disrupted and changed, when young people never agree about what they like, when they don't have any settled sense of what's tasteful or vulgar — whether in how they carry themselves or how they dress — and when whoever invents something new and unusual in style and appearance is given special honor: well, I'd say there's no greater disaster that can befall a state.

The person who changes children's games is secretly changing the character of the young — training them to despise old traditions and embrace new ones. And I'm telling you, there's nothing more damaging to a society than this kind of talk and this kind of thinking. Do you want to hear just how serious I think this problem is?

Cleinias
You mean the problem of disrespecting tradition in a state?

Athenian
Exactly.

Cleinias
On that subject, you'll find us very receptive listeners — more than receptive, in fact.

Athenian
I'd expect so.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
Well then, let's pay especially close attention to each other's words here. The argument is this: any change whatsoever, except from what's bad, is the most dangerous of all things. This is true of the changing of seasons, of winds, of bodily habits, of the habits of the mind — true of everything, except, as I said, what's bad. Consider someone whose body has adapted to eating any kind of food, drinking any kind of drink, doing any kind of work: at first, these things cause disorder, but over time, the body adapts. The person learns to appreciate variety, and enjoys good health and a pleasant life. But if afterward they're restricted to a superior diet, they're initially disturbed by the change and have difficulty adjusting to the new food.

We can imagine the same principle applies to people's minds and souls. When they've been raised under certain laws that — by some divine providence — have remained unchanged for long ages, so that no one has any memory or tradition of them ever being different, then everyone is afraid and ashamed to change what's established.

The legislator needs to find some way to instill this reverence for tradition, and here's what I'd propose. People tend to think — as I was saying — that when children's games change, they're just games. They don't see the serious and damaging consequences that follow. They go along with whatever children want instead of discouraging them, never considering that these children who innovate in their games will grow up to be different adults from the previous generation. Being different, they'll want a different kind of life. Under that desire, they'll want different institutions and different laws. And none of them stops to think that what follows is what I just called the greatest of evils for a state. Changes in outward fashion aren't such a serious matter. But constant changes in what a society praises and blames in conduct — those are the greatest of evils, and require the utmost foresight.

Cleinias
To be sure.

Athenian
And do we still hold to our earlier claim that rhythms and music in general are imitations of good and evil characters in people? What do you say?

Cleinias
That's the only position we can take.

Athenian
Then we must try by every means possible to prevent our young people from wanting to imitate new styles in either dance or song. And no one should be allowed to tempt them with novel pleasures.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Can any of us think of a better way to accomplish this than the method of the Egyptians?

Cleinias
What is their method?

Athenian
To make every kind of dance and melody sacred. First, we should establish festivals — calculating what the year requires, at what times, and in honor of which gods, sons of gods, and heroes they should be celebrated. Next, we should determine which hymns should be sung at each sacrifice, and which dances should accompany each festival. This should be arranged initially by designated officials, and once arranged, the entire citizen body should offer sacrifices and libations to the Fates and all the other gods, and consecrate the various songs to the appropriate deities and heroes.

If anyone introduces different hymns or dances to any of the gods, the priests and priestesses, working together with the guardians of the law, should exclude that person — with the backing of both religion and law. If the person refuses to submit, they'll be liable for the rest of their life to a charge of impiety, which anyone may bring against them.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
While we're on this subject, let's remember what we owe to ourselves.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
I mean this: when any person, young or old, encounters something strange or unfamiliar, they shouldn't immediately rush to embrace the new thing. Instead, they should stand and think it over, like someone who's reached a fork where three roads meet and doesn't quite know which way to go. Whether alone or with others, they should say to themselves: "Which is the right path?" — and not move forward until they're satisfied they're going the right way.

That's what we need to do right now. A strange discussion about law has come up that requires the most careful consideration. At our age, we shouldn't be too quick to speak about such weighty matters, or pretend we can settle them instantly.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Then let's allow ourselves time for reflection, and decide after we've given the subject enough thought. But in the meantime, so we don't get sidetracked from completing the natural order of our laws, let's proceed to the end of them in due order. If God is willing, perhaps the whole exposition, once complete, will shed light on our current difficulty.

Cleinias
Excellent, Stranger. Let's do as you suggest.

Athenian
Then let's accept this paradox: musical compositions are our laws. In fact, the ancients used the same word — "nomoi" — for both lyric songs and laws, so they probably wouldn't have objected much to the way we're using the term. Someone, whether dreaming or waking, must have had an intuition about their true nature.

So let our decree be this: no one, in singing or dancing, shall violate the publicly established and consecrated standards — the general musical customs of our society — any more than they'd violate any other law. Whoever observes this law will face no penalty. But whoever disobeys, as I said, will be punished by the guardians of the law and by the priests and priestesses. Let's consider this our law.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
Now, can anyone who enacts such a law avoid looking ridiculous? Let's think about this. I believe our only protection will be to first establish certain standards for composers. Here's one of those standards: imagine a sacrifice is taking place, the offerings are burning on the altar according to custom — and then someone standing next to the altar, a son or brother of the celebrant, starts pouring out horrible blasphemies. Wouldn't his words fill his father and relatives with dread and dark forebodings?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
And yet this is exactly what happens in almost all our cities. A magistrate performs a public sacrifice, and then not one but many choruses show up, take their positions near the altar, and proceed to pour out every sort of awful lamentation over the sacred rites, stirring the audience's souls with the most sorrowful words, rhythms, and melodies imaginable. And the one who makes the citizens weep the most at the very moment the city is offering sacrifice — he takes the prize!

Now, shouldn't we ban music like this? And if our citizens ever do need to hear such laments, then let it be on some unlucky, inauspicious day, with choruses of foreign hired performers — like those professionals who accompany funerals with harsh Carian dirges. That's the sort of setting that would be appropriate for such music, if we allow it at all. And let the singers' costumes not be garlands and gold ornaments, but the opposite.

Enough of all that. Let me simply ask one more time whether we'll adopt this as one of our principles for music —

Cleinias
What principle?

Athenian
That we should avoid every word of ill omen, and that songs of good omen should be heard everywhere and always in our state. I barely need to ask — I'll assume you agree with me.

Cleinias
Absolutely. That law has the full approval of all of us.

Athenian
And what should our next musical regulation be? Shouldn't prayers be offered to the gods whenever we sacrifice?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And our third rule, if I'm not mistaken, should be this: our poets, understanding that prayers are requests we make to the gods, must take special care not to accidentally ask for bad things instead of good ones. Making a prayer like that would be truly absurd.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Weren't we convinced just a little while ago that no golden or silver Plutus — no god of wealth — should have a place in our state?

Cleinias
We were indeed.

Athenian
And what has our whole argument been trying to show? Isn't it that poets aren't always capable of knowing what's truly good or evil? If a poet composes a misguided prayer in song or verse, he'll lead our citizens to pray for the exact opposite of what's good — on matters of the highest importance. As I was saying, there can be few bigger mistakes than that. So shall we propose this as one of our laws and standards for the Muses —

Cleinias
What exactly? Please spell out the law more precisely.

Athenian
The law shall be this: no poet may compose anything contrary to the city's accepted standards of what is lawful, just, beautiful, or good. No poet shall be allowed to share their compositions with anyone until they've been shown to the appointed judges and the guardians of the law, and those officials are satisfied with them. We've already indicated who should serve as our legislators for music and as the director of education. So, once more — as I've asked repeatedly — shall this be our third law, standard, and model? What do you say?

Cleinias
Let it be so, by all means.

Athenian
After this, it'll be appropriate to have hymns and praises to the gods, mixed with prayers. And following the gods, prayers and praises should be offered in the same way to demigods and heroes, each suited to their character.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Next, there should be no objection to a law honoring citizens who have died and who performed good and heroic deeds — whether of the soul or the body — and who were obedient to the laws. It would be very fitting for them to receive public praise.

Cleinias
Quite true.

Athenian
But honoring living people with hymns and panegyrics is a risky business. A person should run their course and reach a worthy end before we praise them. And praise should be given equally to women and to men who've distinguished themselves through virtue.

Here's how songs and dances should be organized. There are many excellent ancient musical compositions and dances, and the newly founded city may freely choose from among them whatever is proper and suitable. Judges of not less than fifty years of age shall make the selection. Any of the old works they consider adequate, they'll include. Anything deficient or completely unsuitable, they'll either reject entirely or revise and improve, calling on poets and musicians for assistance and making use of their creative talent — but always explaining the legislator's wishes, so that the artists adjust their dancing, music, and choral works according to the judges' vision, not their own personal tastes and whims.

Now, irregular, undisciplined music always becomes immeasurably better when it acquires order and structure — when it rejects the sweet, honeyed Muse — though I don't mean we should eliminate pleasure entirely, since pleasure is the very nature of all music. The point is this: if a person is raised from childhood to maturity on orderly, disciplined music, they'll detest the opposite kind when they hear it and call it vulgar. But if they're trained on sweet, popular music, they'll find the more disciplined kind cold and unpleasant. So, as I was saying, neither kind gives more pleasure than the other to someone who hears them — the advantage of one over the other is simply that the disciplined kind makes people better, while the other makes them worse.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
We also need to distinguish, on some general principle, which songs are appropriate for women and which for men, and assign the proper melodies and rhythms to each. It would be terrible for a whole piece to be musically incoherent — rhythmically or harmonically off — and this is what happens when the melody doesn't match its subject. So the legislator must set guidelines for these things too. Both sexes have melodies and rhythms that naturally belong to them, and the difference is clearly indicated by their natural character. What's grand and tends toward courage we can fairly call manly. What inclines toward moderation and temperance — both in law and in common speech — can be called the more womanly quality. That'll be our general framework.

Now let's talk about how these things should be taught, to whom, and when. Like a shipwright who first lays down the keel and from there sketches the outline of the whole ship, I'm trying to map out the patterns of life according to the different natures of people's souls — genuinely asking how, and by what means, we can best navigate this voyage of life.

Now, human affairs are hardly worth taking seriously — and yet we must take them seriously. It's a sad necessity. And having come this far, it makes sense to finish the job if we can find a suitable approach. But what do I mean by that? Someone might well ask, and rightly so.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
What I mean is this: serious matters deserve serious attention, but trivial matters don't. God is the natural and proper focus of our deepest seriousness and devotion. But humanity — as I said before — was made to be God's plaything, and this, rightly understood, is actually the best thing about us. Every man and woman should therefore live accordingly, spending their lives in the noblest forms of play, with a completely different mindset from the one that prevails today.

Cleinias
In what way different?

Athenian
Right now, people think their serious activities should serve their recreation — they view war as a serious pursuit, something that must be managed well for the sake of the peace that follows. But the truth is that there never was, never is, and never will be any real amusement or meaningful instruction in war — which, all the same, we treat as the most serious thing we do. What I'm saying is this: every one of us should live the life of peace as long and as well as they can.

And what's the right way to live? Should we spend our whole lives in play? If so, what kind of play? We should live our lives making sacrifices, singing, and dancing. Through these, a person can win the favor of the gods, defend against enemies, and conquer them in battle. The kind of songs and dances that will accomplish this has already been described, and the paths have been laid out. We should go forward in the spirit of the poet:

"Telemachus, some things you'll discover in your own heart, but other things the gods will suggest to you; for I believe you were not born and raised without the will of the gods."

And this ought to be the outlook of our young citizens. They should trust that what we've told them is enough, and that their own divine inspiration will guide them in what remains — telling them to whom, and when, and to which gods they should offer sacrifices and dances, winning the gods' favor and living according to nature. Humans are, for the most part, puppets — though we do possess some small share of genuine reality.

Megillus
You have a pretty low opinion of the human race, Stranger.

Athenian
Don't be too surprised, Megillus — forgive me. I was comparing us with the gods, and that's what moved me to speak that way. All right, let's grant it: the human race isn't to be despised, but deserves some consideration.

Moving on — next come the buildings for gymnasiums and schools, all open to the public. These should be placed in three locations in the center of the city. Outside the city and in the surrounding countryside, also in three locations, there should be grounds for horse-riding and spacious fields designed for archery and javelin practice, where young people can learn and train. We've already mentioned these, but if the earlier description wasn't detailed enough, let's fill it in now and set it in law.

In each of these schools, there should be living quarters for teachers, who'll be brought in from abroad and paid by the state. They'll teach their students the arts of war and of music. Children shall attend whether their parents want them to or not — there will be compulsory education, as the saying goes, for absolutely everyone, as far as it's possible. The students shall be regarded as belonging to the state rather than to their parents.

My law would apply to girls exactly as it does to boys. Both shall go through the same exercises. I say without any fear of contradiction that gymnastics and horsemanship are as suitable for women as for men. I'm convinced of this by ancient tradition, and in our own time, there are said to be vast numbers of women near the Black Sea — the Sauromatian women — who not only ride on horseback like men but are required to use bows and other weapons just as men do.

And let me go further: if these things are possible, then nothing could be more absurd than what happens in our own societies, where men and women don't pursue the same activities with all their strength and with a shared purpose. A state that does this cuts itself in half instead of being whole — and yet still has the same taxes to pay and the same burdens to bear. What bigger mistake could any lawgiver make?

Cleinias
Very true. And yet much of what you've said, Stranger, goes against what's customary in most states. Still, you spoke well when you said we should let the discussion run its full course, and then choose what seems best. I feel some regret for challenging you earlier. So tell us — what would you like to say next?

Athenian
Just what I said before, Cleinias: if the possibility of these things weren't sufficiently proven by actual evidence, then there might be grounds for objecting. But the evidence is as I've described it, so anyone who rejects this law will have to find some other reason to object. Failing that, our recommendation stands. No one will deny that women should share as fully as possible in education and everything else along with men. Think about it this way: if women don't share in men's way of life entirely, then they must have some other way of life altogether.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And what alternative arrangement can we find anywhere that's better than this partnership we're proposing? Shall we follow the Thracians and many other peoples who use their women to till the soil, herd their flocks, and serve them like slaves? Or shall we do as we do in our part of the world — gathering all our possessions into one household and handing them over to our women as stewards, who also preside over the spinning and weaving?

Or should we take a middle course, as in Sparta, Megillus? In Sparta, girls share in gymnastics and music. Grown women, no longer working at spinning wool, are busy weaving the web of life — no small or mean occupation — and they serve in caring for the household and raising the children. They take a kind of middle path, not participating in the toils of war. But if there were any real need for them to fight for their city and families — unlike the Amazons — they'd be unable to pick up a bow or any other weapon with skill, or to carry a shield or spear. They couldn't even stand their ground and fight nobly for their country when it was being destroyed, striking terror into their enemies simply by being seen in organized formation.

Living the way they do, they'd never dare to imitate the Sauromatian women, who compared to ordinary women would look like men. Let whoever wants to praise your lawmakers do so — but I have to say what I think. The lawgiver ought to be complete and whole, not half a craftsman. He shouldn't let the female sex live softly, spend money freely, and have no structure to their lives while he takes the greatest care of the male sex. That leaves half the state's life blessed with happiness, when he could have made the whole state happy.

Megillus
What should we do, Cleinias? Are we going to let this stranger criticize Sparta like this?

Cleinias
Yes. We've granted him freedom of speech, and we should let him continue until we've completed our work of legislation.

Megillus
Fair enough.

Athenian
Then may I continue?

Cleinias
By all means.
