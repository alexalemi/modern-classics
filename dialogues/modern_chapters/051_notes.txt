TRANSLATION NOTES — Laws Book 6, Part 2 (Chapter 051)

OVERVIEW
This second half of Laws Book 6 transitions from the constitutional
machinery of Part 1 into genuinely fascinating territory: the
legislator's mortality and the need for successors, the number mysticism
of 5,040, marriage law (with its surprising detail and occasional
comedy), the question of regulating women, the treatment of slaves,
city planning, and the three fundamental human drives. The Athenian's
voice becomes more personal and animated here.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS

1. THE PAINTER ANALOGY / LEGISLATOR'S MORTALITY
   The opening extends Part 1's closing image — the painter who needs
   successors to maintain his masterpiece. Applied to law: no lawmaker is
   so foolish as to think he won't leave gaps. This is a rare moment of
   Platonic humility about legislation. Kept it direct and poignant.

2. MARRIAGE PRELUDE — "THE CUP OF WINE"
   The Athenian's advice to young men about choosing wives is one of the
   most charming passages in the Laws. The key metaphor: a city should be
   "well mixed like wine in a cup" — hot, fiery wine tempered by a calmer
   element produces an excellent balanced drink. Marriage should do the same.
   The Athenian acknowledges this principle would provoke "both anger and
   laughter" if made into explicit law — a surprisingly self-aware moment.
   Let the humor land naturally.

3. BACHELOR TAX
   The penalties for remaining unmarried past 35 are both funny and harsh:
   graduated fines by class, and — most humiliating — loss of all deference
   from younger citizens. The bachelor can't discipline anyone; if he tries,
   bystanders must intervene. Anyone who doesn't help is declared "a coward
   and a bad citizen." This is social engineering through shame, and it's
   quite entertaining. Kept the absurd specificity intact.

4. MARRIAGE SUPERVISORS ("THE OVERSEERS")
   The women overseers who monitor young couples' reproductive conduct are
   the "amusing detail" mentioned in the instructions. They gather daily at
   the temple of Eileithyia (goddess of childbirth), share intelligence on
   non-compliant couples, and have authority to enter homes to deliver
   "advice and threats." If that doesn't work, they escalate to the
   guardians of the law, and ultimately to public shaming. The progression
   from friendly advice to home visits to public posting is comedic in its
   bureaucratic thoroughness. Translated faithfully — the humor is inherent.

5. WEDDING SOBRIETY LECTURE
   The Athenian's extended argument that newlyweds absolutely must not be
   drunk on their wedding night — because a drunk person is "the worst
   possible seed-sower" who produces "unsteady" offspring — is vintage
   Plato. The earnestness with which he argues this point (the drunk man
   "lurches through everything — his body is all over the place, and so
   is his mind") is both genuinely interesting as ancient genetics and
   inadvertently funny. Kept the full passage.

6. WOMEN AND COMMUNAL DINING
   The Athenian's argument that women should participate in communal dining
   is remarkable — and he knows it. He's genuinely nervous about proposing
   it ("like combing wool into the fire"). His frank admission that women
   would furiously resist being forced to eat in public, and that "no
   sensible person would even dare bring up the subject," is one of the
   most candid passages in Plato. The underlying argument — that leaving
   half the population unregulated means more than half the damage — is
   mathematically precise and socially radical for its time.

7. SLAVERY DISCUSSION
   The passage on slaves is handled carefully. The Athenian presents two
   contradictory views (slaves are loyal heroes vs. slaves are utterly
   corrupt), quotes Homer on Zeus taking away "half the understanding"
   of the enslaved, and arrives at a characteristically Platonic solution:
   treat slaves justly — not for their benefit alone but as a test of your
   own character. "A person who genuinely reveres justice reveals his
   character most clearly in how he deals with those he could easily wrong."
   This is one of Plato's more admirable passages on the subject.
   Translated faithfully without editorial commentary.

8. CITY PLANNING / WALLS
   The Athenian's anti-wall argument is pure Sparta (he credits it to
   Megillus's homeland): walls make citizens soft, encourage them to hide
   instead of fight, and create a false sense of security. His alternative —
   designing houses so the city itself becomes one defensive wall — is a
   genuine architectural innovation. The old saying "walls should be of
   steel and iron, not of earth" is preserved.

9. THREE FUNDAMENTAL DRIVES
   The catalogue of human nature's three basic desires — hunger, thirst,
   and sexual passion — is presented as the foundation of all law. The
   third is "the greatest and sharpest," erupting last and kindling "every
   kind of reckless and irrational behavior." This is Plato's late-life
   psychology, more direct and less metaphysical than the Republic's
   tripartite soul. The three remedies — fear, law, and right reason — map
   neatly onto the three drives.

10. THE NUMBER 5,040
    The mathematical properties of 5,040 (divisible by 1-12, except 11,
    which is "easily fixed" by subtracting 2 households) are presented with
    genuine enthusiasm. This numerological passion is characteristic of
    Plato's late work. Kept the mathematical detail — it's part of the
    charm.

TONE
    Part 2 is warmer and more personal than Part 1's constitutional
    machinery. The Athenian becomes a marriage counselor, an urban planner,
    and a moral psychologist in turn. His nervousness about the women's
    communal dining proposal is endearing. The marriage advice is genuinely
    wise ("balance and symmetry contribute infinitely more to excellence
    than concentrated, unmixed qualities"). The bachelor tax is funny.
    The slave passage is morally serious. This is the Laws at its most
    human.
