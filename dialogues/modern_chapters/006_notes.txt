Protagoras (Part 1) -- Translation Notes
==========================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. NARRATED FORMAT: Preserved Socrates' first-person narration to his unnamed
   Companion throughout. The opening exchange (Companion/Socrates) uses the
   dramatic speaker-name format, since it's a frame dialogue. Once Socrates
   begins narrating, we shift to his voice with embedded dialogue. Speech
   tags kept natural: "I said," "he replied," "he said."

2. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's enormous sentences (some 80-100+ words) broken
   into shorter, more natural units. The Protagoras myth in particular needed
   restructuring -- Jowett has it in continuous, breathless Victorian prose.
   Broke it into clear narrative beats while maintaining the storytelling flow.
   Protagoras' Great Speech was the biggest challenge: long, rhetorical, layered
   arguments. Kept the argument intact but broke into paragraph-sized units with
   clearer logical progression.

3. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "truckle-bed" -> just "the foot of my bed" (nobody knows "truckle-bed")
   - "hither/thither" -> "here"/"there"
   - "unearthly hour" -> "ungodly hour"
   - "at all hazards" -> "no matter what"
   - "apologue" -> "myth" / "story" (Protagoras' own word choice)
   - "in like manner" -> "in the same way" / "similarly"
   - "I will endeavour" -> restructured naturally
   - "ex cathedra" -> "from his throne with definitive authority"
   - "encomia" -> "praise and examples"
   - "notwithstanding" -> "despite" / "and yet"
   - "vestibule" kept (it's a real architectural term people know)
   - "colonnade" used instead of "cloister" (more accurate for Greek stoa)

4. CONTRACTIONS throughout: "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "wouldn't," "he's,"
   "I'd," "can't," "we'll," "that's," "you're," "I've," "they're," etc.
   Both Socrates and Protagoras speak with natural contractions. The Companion
   in the frame dialogue uses them too.

5. HIPPOCRATES' CHARACTERIZATION: Preserved his breathless youthful energy.
   The early-morning banging, the rush of words, the blushing at dawn -- all
   kept vivid. His excitement is charming and slightly ridiculous, which is
   exactly how Socrates presents it. The blush is a lovely detail: "day was
   just beginning to break, so that I could see his face."

6. THE DOORKEEPER SCENE: Kept the comedy. The eunuch's hostility, the double
   rejection, the slammed door -- this is physical comedy and it works.
   "More Sophists" is punchier than Jowett's longer version.

7. THE SCENE AT CALLIAS' HOUSE: This is one of Plato's great descriptive
   set-pieces. Three vignettes:
   - Protagoras with his perfectly choreographed entourage (like Orpheus)
   - Hippias on his throne, dispensing scientific wisdom
   - Prodicus in bed under a mountain of blankets
   Each has a distinct comic flavor. The Orpheus comparison is wonderful --
   kept and enhanced with "like watching a perfectly rehearsed dance."
   The Homeric allusions ("I lifted up my eyes," "my eyes beheld Tantalus")
   kept as literary echoes -- they signal Socrates is having fun with the
   grandiosity of the scene.

8. PROTAGORAS AS GENUINELY IMPRESSIVE: This is critical. Protagoras is not
   a fool or a fraud. His speech about openly declaring himself a Sophist
   is bold and principled. His myth of Prometheus is wonderful storytelling.
   His argument about why virtue can be taught is genuinely persuasive.
   The translation preserves his dignity, intelligence, and rhetorical power.
   The audience should find him impressive before Socrates begins his
   cross-examination.

9. THE PROMETHEUS MYTH: Treated as vivid narrative storytelling. Broke from
   Jowett's dense paragraph into clear story beats:
   - Epimetheus distributes all qualities to animals
   - Realizes he's left humans with nothing
   - Prometheus steals fire and crafts from Athena/Hephaestus
   - Humans develop practical skills but lack political wisdom
   - Zeus sends justice and respect to all humans
   This is great mythmaking and should read like it.

10. PROTAGORAS' GREAT SPEECH: The argument has several layers:
    - Why Athenians accept advice from anyone on political matters (Zeus
      gave virtue to all, unlike specialized skills)
    - Why they believe virtue can be taught (evidence from how we punish)
    - Why good fathers can have bad sons (the flute-player analogy)
    - Why everyone teaches virtue (like everyone teaches Greek)
    Each layer preserved in full. The flute-player analogy is particularly
    clever and was kept intact.

11. SOCRATES' CROSS-EXAMINATION: The argument about the unity of virtue is
    dense philosophical reasoning. Kept every step of the logic:
    - Are parts of virtue like parts of a face (different in kind) or
      like pieces of gold (different only in size)?
    - Justice and holiness: are they the same or different?
    - The opposites argument: if foolishness is the opposite of both
      wisdom and self-control, and everything has only one opposite,
      then wisdom and self-control must be the same
    This is vintage Socrates: small concessions building to a devastating
    conclusion.

12. THE LONG-SPEECH vs. SHORT-ANSWER DISPUTE: A meta-argument about
    methodology that's both funny and important. Socrates' "bad memory"
    is clearly a strategic move. Protagoras' resistance reveals his
    discomfort with being cross-examined. The intervention of Callias,
    Alcibiades, Critias, and Prodicus -- each with their own personality
    and agenda -- is a wonderful dramatic moment.

13. PRODICUS' DISTINCTION between "esteem" and "praise," "satisfaction"
    and "pleasure" -- this is a running joke about Prodicus throughout
    Plato's dialogues. He's obsessed with fine verbal distinctions. Kept
    the pedantic precision while making it readable.

14. PHILOSOPHICAL VOCABULARY:
    - "virtue" (arete) kept throughout -- it's the dialogue's central term
    - "self-control" used for sophrosyne (instead of Jowett's "temperance")
    - "reverence" for hosiotes (instead of "holiness" in most places,
      though "holiness" kept where Socrates discusses it as a discrete
      concept in the logical argument)
    - "good judgment" for phronesis/prudence
    - "political virtue" / "political wisdom" for the civic dimension of arete
    - "the art of politics" for politike techne

15. GREEK OATHS kept: "by the gods," "by the gods!"

16. ALCIBIADES' INTERVENTION: His defense of Socrates is confident and
    sharp -- consistent with his characterization as brilliant and bold.
    His vouching for Socrates' memory ("I'll vouch for that, even when
    he jokes about having a bad memory") is a nice touch of insight.


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (PROTAGORAS PART 2)
-------------------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: Established as narrator telling a story to his Companion.
   Conversational, ironic, generous. Uses contractions. Describes scenes
   with vivid detail and humor. In argument, methodical and relentless.

2. PROTAGORAS' VOICE: Dignified, confident, eloquent. Not afraid to push
   back. Gets increasingly uncomfortable with Socratic cross-examination.
   He's agreed reluctantly that wisdom and self-control might be the same,
   and that justice and holiness are alike. He's losing ground but hasn't
   conceded the main point.

3. THE DISPUTE ABOUT METHOD: Resolved by having Protagoras ask questions
   first, then Socrates. Part 2 opens with Hippias proposing a compromise,
   then Protagoras begins by asking about poetry (Simonides).

4. KEY CHARACTERS PRESENT: Protagoras, Socrates, Hippocrates, Alcibiades,
   Critias, Callias, Prodicus, Hippias, and various others. Alcibiades
   has been established as Socrates' ally. Prodicus as the word-distinction
   pedant. Hippias as the polymath on his throne.

5. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "virtue" for arete throughout
   - "self-control" for sophrosyne (NOT "temperance")
   - "reverence" / "holiness" for hosiotes (context-dependent)
   - "good judgment" for phronesis
   - "Sophist" always capitalized
   - Contractions used freely by all speakers
   - "the art of politics" for the teachable civic skill

6. THE ARGUMENT SO FAR: Socrates has begun to show that the "parts" of
   virtue may not be as distinct as Protagoras claims. Justice ≈ holiness.
   Wisdom = self-control (via the opposites argument). Protagoras is
   conceding reluctantly. Part 2 will continue this through the Simonides
   poem digression and the final argument about courage, pleasure, and
   the "measuring art."

7. TONE: The dialogue is a genuine intellectual contest between two
   brilliant minds. Protagoras is formidable. Socrates is relentless.
   The audience (both the characters and the reader) should feel the
   tension and the excitement.
