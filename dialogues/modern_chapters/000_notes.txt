Euthyphro — Translation Notes
================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Broke Jowett's long Victorian periods into shorter,
   punchier sentences. Average sentence length dropped from ~40 words to ~18.
   Example: Euthyphro's long narration about the laborer's death was kept as
   one speech (it's a monologue) but restructured with shorter internal
   sentences and clearer cause-and-effect.

2. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "enquire" → "ask" / "examine" / "investigate"
   - "I dare say" → "I'd guess" / "probably" / "I'm sure"
   - "nay" → "no" / "ha, no" (for Euthyphro's quip about flying)
   - "in like manner" → "so" / "and" / direct transition
   - "I will endeavour" → "I'll try"
   - "I adjure you" → "I'm begging you"
   - "soothsayers" → "fortune-tellers"
   - "the fugitive has wings?" → "can the defendant fly?" (cleaner joke)
   - "neologian" → "inventing new religious ideas" (nobody knows "neologian")
   - "volatile" → "hardly the flying type" (preserves the joke's rhythm)

3. VICTORIAN INVERSIONS removed throughout:
   - "Not in a suit, Euthyphro; impeachment is the word which the Athenians use"
     → "Not a case, Euthyphro. 'Impeachment' is the word the Athenians are using."
   - "Are not these the points..." → "Aren't these the things..."

4. CONTRACTIONS used naturally throughout: "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "you'd,"
   "that's," "can't," "wouldn't," "he's," "I'd," "won't," "there'd," etc.
   Makes the dialogue sound like actual conversation.

5. SOCRATIC IRONY sharpened:
   - "He must be some kind of genius" — the sarcasm is audible
   - "you, who are so well informed about these things" — deadpan
   - "You remarkable man!" — the reader can hear the undertone
   - "Your abundance of wisdom is making you lazy" — the bite is clear
   - "since you say you're the most expert in religious matters of any
     person alive" — devastating when Euthyphro has just been refuted

6. EUTHYPHRO'S POMPOSITY preserved and sharpened:
   - "The thing that sets Euthyphro apart from other men, Socrates —
     the best thing about me — is my exact knowledge of all these matters."
     He refers to himself in the third person. The self-regard is unmissable.
   - His breezy confidence that he'd destroy Meletus in court
   - His casual "another time, Socrates" exit when cornered

7. PHILOSOPHICAL ARGUMENTS: Every logical step preserved intact:
   - The gods-disagree-about-morality argument
   - The active/passive distinction (carrying/being carried)
   - The Euthyphro dilemma: "loved because holy, or holy because loved?"
   - The justice-as-broader-category argument (fear/reverence analogy)
   - The service-to-gods argument and its collapse
   - The circular return to "what's dear to the gods"

8. GREEK OATHS kept: "by Zeus," "by the powers," "for the love of Zeus,"
   "good heavens," "by all means."

9. CULTURAL REFERENCES kept with light context:
   - "deme of Pitthis" kept as-is (minor detail)
   - King Archon's court (the setting)
   - Panathenaea and robe of Athena (kept — Socrates explains it himself)
   - Daedalus reference (kept — Socrates explains it himself)
   - Proteus reference (kept — the context makes the meaning clear)
   - Tantalus reference (kept alongside Daedalus)

10. STASINUS QUOTE: Kept the verse format. The quote is integral to the
    fear/reverence argument.

11. THE DAEDALUS PASSAGE: This is a key comic moment — arguments that
    refuse to stay still, like Daedalus' living statues. Kept the playful
    back-and-forth about who's really making the arguments walk away.


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (APOLOGY)
----------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: Established as sharp, conversational, ironic, generous
   with self-deprecation but always in control. He uses contractions freely.
   He's funny but serious. The Apology should maintain this voice but shift
   to an oratorical register — he's addressing a jury, not having a chat.

2. MELETUS: Introduced here as a young man with a beaky nose, long straight
   hair, and a scraggly beard. The charge: making new gods and denying old
   ones (corrupting the youth). These details recur in the Apology.

3. THE CHARGE: "Impiety" — inventing new gods, denying old ones, corrupting
   the youth. Euthyphro frames it as being about the "divine sign" (inner
   voice). The Apology will address all of this directly.

4. SOCRATES' SELF-PRESENTATION: He describes himself here as someone with
   a "generous habit of pouring myself out to everyone" who would "practically
   pay someone to listen." This chatty, irrepressible quality should carry
   into the Apology, where he says similar things to the jury.

5. TONE SHIFT for Apology: Euthyphro is comedy. The Apology is drama.
   Socrates is still ironic, still funny, but the stakes are real — his life
   is on the line. The humor should have a harder edge.

6. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "impiety" (not "irreligion" or "ungodliness")
   - "the charge" (not "the allegation" or "the indictment")
   - "corrupting the youth" (standard phrase)
   - "divine sign" or "inner voice" for Socrates' daimonion
   - Contractions throughout for natural speech

7. FORMAT: The Apology is NOT a dialogue — it's a speech. Switch from
   speaker-name-on-own-line format to continuous prose. But keep the
   conversational voice. Socrates addresses the jury as "men of Athens"
   or "gentlemen of the jury."
