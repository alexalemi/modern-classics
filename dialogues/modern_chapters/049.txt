Athenian
Now listen, all of you who've just heard our laws about the gods and about honoring our ancestors. Of everything a person possesses, the soul is the most divine -- second only to the gods themselves -- and the most truly his own. In every person there are two parts: a better, ruling part, and a worse, subordinate part. The ruling part should always take priority. So I'm right when I say that after the gods -- our masters -- and the divine spirits who follow them, a person should honor his own soul above all else.

Now, everyone thinks they honor their soul. But almost no one actually does it properly. Here's the thing: honor is a genuinely divine good, and nothing evil can be honorable. So when someone thinks he's honoring his soul by flattering it -- by praising it, indulging it, letting it do whatever it wants without making it any better -- he's not honoring it at all. He's actually hurting it. Take a young man, for instance: from boyhood, he's convinced he already knows everything. He thinks he honors his soul by praising it and letting it have its way. But what he's really doing is damaging it, when he ought to be honoring it as second only to the gods.

Or take a man who blames others -- never himself -- for the mistakes he's made and the suffering that followed. He imagines he's honoring his soul, but he's doing the exact opposite: he's injuring it. And when someone chases pleasure in defiance of what the lawgiver recommends, he's not honoring his soul -- he's dishonoring it, filling it with evil and regret. When someone can't endure the hardships, fears, and pain that the lawgiver says are necessary -- when he gives up instead of pushing through -- that's not honoring the soul either. All such behavior makes the soul disgraceful. And when someone thinks that life at any price is a good thing, that's another form of dishonor -- because the soul gets this notion that the underworld is nothing but misery, and the person gives in to that fear instead of resisting it. He doesn't stop to consider that the world of the gods below might actually be the greatest of all goods.

Then there's the person who values physical beauty over virtue. What is that except a genuine insult to the soul? It amounts to saying the body is more honorable than the soul -- and that's simply false. Nothing born from the earth is more honorable than what comes from heaven, and anyone who thinks otherwise has no idea how much he's undervaluing this extraordinary possession. And what about the person who's willing to get rich through dishonest means? He's not honoring his soul with gifts -- far from it. He's selling her glory and honor for a handful of gold. But all the gold on earth or under it isn't enough to trade for virtue.

To put it simply: anyone who doesn't evaluate what's base and what's noble by the lawgiver's standard -- anyone who doesn't avoid the one and practice the other with everything he's got -- that person is abusing his soul in the worst possible way, even though the soul is the most divine part of a human being. And here's the thing hardly anyone considers: the greatest penalty for doing evil isn't some external punishment. It's that you grow to resemble evil people. As you become like them, you drift away from the company of the good. You're cut off from them and drawn instead toward the company of the wicked. And whoever joins their company must do and suffer what such people naturally do and say to one another. This isn't justice -- it's retribution, the natural consequence that follows injustice. And whether a person escapes this retribution or endures it, he's miserable either way. If he escapes it, he's not cured. If he doesn't escape it, he's destroyed so that everyone else might be saved.

Speaking broadly, our glory lies in following what's better and improving what's worse, wherever improvement is possible. And of all human possessions, the soul is the one most naturally inclined to flee evil and seek out the highest good. Once a person finds that good, he should make it the center of his life for the rest of his days.

So the soul ranks second in honor, after the gods. And third -- as anyone can see -- comes the body, in the natural order of things.

But now we need to examine this further. The body has its own kind of honor, and some forms of that honor are genuine while others are counterfeit. Sorting out which is which falls to the lawgiver. And I suspect he'd say something like this: the honor doesn't belong to the beautiful body, the strong body, the fast body, or the tall body, or even to the healthy body -- though plenty of people think otherwise. And it certainly doesn't belong to their opposites either. No, the moderate conditions -- the middle ground in all these qualities -- are by far the safest and most balanced. Going to one extreme makes the soul arrogant and reckless; the other extreme makes it servile and small. The same principle applies to money, property, and social status. An excess of any of these breeds hatred and division in cities and among individuals. A deficiency usually leads to slavery.

So don't pile up riches for the sake of your children, hoping to leave them as wealthy as possible. Great wealth isn't actually useful -- not for them, not for the state. The best condition for young people is to be free from flattery yet not lacking life's necessities. That's the most balanced and harmonious state, the one most in tune with our nature, the one that makes life most free from suffering.

So what should parents leave their children? Not heaps of money, but a spirit of respect and reverence. Now, we tend to think children will learn reverence from us if we scold them when they're disrespectful. But that's not really how it works. A wise lawgiver would instead urge the older generation to show reverence toward the younger -- and above all, to make sure no young person ever sees or hears an elder doing or saying something disgraceful. Where old people have no shame, young people will certainly have no reverence. The best way to train the young is to train yourself at the same time. Don't just lecture them -- live out your own principles in practice.

A person who honors his family and reveres those who share the same gods and the same bloodline can reasonably hope that the gods who watch over birth and generation will bless him. And someone who considers the favors his friends do for him to be greater and more important than those friends themselves think, while considering his own favors to them to be smaller than they really are -- that person will always have their goodwill throughout life.

In his relationship to the state and his fellow citizens, the best man is the one who'd rather win the prize of obedience to his country's laws than any Olympic victory or any triumph in war or peace -- the one who has the reputation, above all others, of having obeyed those laws throughout his entire life.

When it comes to strangers, a person should consider a contract to be a most sacred thing. And wrongs done to strangers are, if anything, more directly under the protection of the gods than wrongs done to fellow citizens. The stranger has no family or friends to stand up for him, which makes him more deserving of pity from both gods and humans. So whoever has the most power to avenge the stranger is the most zealous in his cause -- and the one with the most power is the guardian spirit and the god of strangers, who serve in the company of Zeus, the god of hospitality. Anyone with even a spark of caution will do his best to get through life without wronging a stranger.

And of all offenses, whether against strangers or fellow citizens, the greatest is the offense against those who come seeking protection as suppliants. The god who witnessed the agreement made with the suppliant becomes that person's special guardian. The suppliant will certainly not go unavenged.

So we've now covered how a person should act toward his parents, toward himself, toward his own affairs, toward the state, toward friends and family -- both fellow citizens and strangers. Next we need to consider what kind of person someone should be in order to live the best possible life when it comes to those other matters that aren't regulated by law but shaped by praise and blame -- the things that educate a person and make him more receptive to the laws we're about to establish.

Truth is the foundation of every good thing, for both gods and humans. Anyone who wants to be blessed and happy should embrace truth from the very beginning, so he can live as a truthful person for as long as possible. A truthful person can be trusted. But someone who loves deliberate falsehood can't be trusted, and someone who loves accidental falsehood is a fool. Neither condition is enviable. The untrustworthy and the ignorant end up without friends, and as time goes on, their reputation catches up with them. They store up loneliness for a bitter old age when life is fading -- so that whether their children or friends are still alive or not, they're equally alone.

Worthy of honor is the person who does no injustice. But worthy of more than double that honor is the person who not only avoids injustice himself but also prevents others from committing it. The first counts as one good citizen; the second is worth many, because he alerts the rulers to the injustice of others. And even more highly esteemed is the one who actually cooperates with the rulers in correcting the citizens as far as he can -- that person deserves to be called the great and complete citizen, the one who carries off the prize of virtue.

The same kind of praise applies to self-control, wisdom, and every other good quality that can be shared with others. The person who shares these goods should be honored as a true leader among people. The person who's willing but unable deserves second place. But the one who's jealous and deliberately refuses to share any good with others in a friendly spirit -- that person deserves blame. Still, we shouldn't undervalue the good qualities he possesses just because he possesses them. We should still strive to acquire those qualities ourselves as best we can.

Let everyone compete freely for the prize of virtue, and let there be no envy. A person who competes without envy helps make his city great -- he runs his own race without tearing down anyone else's reputation. But the envious person, who thinks he can get ahead by slandering others, puts less effort into pursuing genuine virtue himself and drives his rivals to despair with his unfair attacks. In this way, he sends the whole city into the competition of virtue untrained and unprepared, and diminishes its glory as much as he can.

Now, every person should be brave, but he should also be gentle. The only way to escape the cruel, barely curable, or completely incurable injustices that others inflict on you is to fight back, defend yourself, and conquer -- and to never stop holding the wrongdoers accountable. No one who lacks a noble spirit can do this.

But when it comes to people who do wrong yet whose wrongdoing can be corrected, we need to remember something first: the unjust person isn't unjust by choice. No one would willingly harbor the greatest of evils in the most honorable part of himself. And the soul, as we've said, is universally recognized as the most honorable part of a person. So no one would deliberately let the greatest evil take root in his soul if he could help it. The wicked and the unrighteous always deserve our pity. And when the wrongdoer is curable, we can afford to be forgiving -- to restrain our anger, to stay calm instead of nursing grudges like a bitter, petty person. But for the one who's truly beyond reform and wholly evil? On that person, we should pour out our full anger. That's why I say good people should be both gentle and fierce, as the occasion demands.

The greatest of all evils is one that's built into most people's souls -- an evil that everyone excuses in himself and no one bothers to correct. It's captured in the saying that "every person is naturally and rightly his own best friend." But in reality, this excessive self-love is the root cause of all our failures. The lover is blind when it comes to the beloved, and so a person in love with himself misjudges what's just, good, and honorable, always thinking he should put himself ahead of the truth. But anyone who wants to be truly great shouldn't focus on himself or his own interests -- he should focus on what's just, whether the just action is his own or someone else's.

It's the same error that leads people to mistake their own ignorance for wisdom. We know practically nothing, yet we think we know everything. And because we refuse to let others handle what we don't actually understand, we end up doing it badly ourselves. So let everyone guard against excessive self-love, and be willing to follow someone better than himself without letting false pride get in the way.

There are also smaller but equally useful principles that deserve frequent repetition. A person should remind himself of them often. After all, when something is flowing out, something should be flowing in -- and recollection flows in while wisdom flows out.

So I'd say that a person should avoid extremes, whether of laughter or tears, and encourage his neighbor to do the same. He should keep his excessive joy or sorrow under wraps and try to behave with dignity -- whether his good fortune stays with him, or whether at some critical moment, when he seems to be climbing high and steep places, the gods throw obstacles in his path. Still, a good person can always hope that whatever hardships the future brings, god will lighten them, that present troubles will change for the better, and that blessings opposite to current misfortunes will come in time. These should be our hopes, and this is how we should encourage one another -- never missing a chance, on every occasion, to remind ourselves and each other of all these things, in both serious moments and lighter ones.

Now, we've said enough about divine matters -- both the practices people should follow and the kinds of people they should strive to be. But we haven't yet talked about human things, and we need to -- because we're talking to human beings, not gods. Pleasures and pains and desires are part of human nature, and every mortal being is inevitably consumed by them. So we need to praise the noblest life not just because it looks the best on the surface, but because, if a person will actually try it -- and not abandon it while still young for something else -- he'll find it surpasses every other way of life in the very thing we all want most: more pleasure and less pain throughout our whole lives.

This will become obvious if a person gets a genuine taste of these different lives. But what counts as a genuine taste? That's what the argument has to teach us -- the question being what's according to nature and what isn't.

Here's how to compare one life with another, the more pleasant with the more painful: We want pleasure, and we don't want pain -- that much is obvious. A neutral state we're willing to accept instead of pain, but not instead of pleasure. We prefer less pain with more pleasure, but we don't prefer less pleasure with more pain. When both are equal, we can't say with confidence what we'd choose. All these preferences differ in number, magnitude, intensity, and balance -- and these differences determine what we actually want.

Given this natural ordering, we desire a life that contains many intense pleasures and pains, with the pleasures outweighing the pains. We don't want a life where the pains outweigh the pleasures. And we don't want a life where both pleasures and pains are small, few, and feeble, but the pains still come out on top. When there's a balance between pleasure and pain, we should regard that as the balanced, neutral life. We prefer lives that give us more of what we like and reject lives that give us more of what we don't like. These are the possible lives available to us, and we should understand that. If anyone desires some other kind of life, it's only because of ignorance and inexperience of the lives that actually exist.

So what are these lives, and how many are there? Let's lay them out. Say there's the self-controlled life, the rational life, the courageous life, and the healthy life. Against these four, let's set four opposites: the foolish life, the cowardly life, the undisciplined life, and the sickly life.

Someone who knows the self-controlled life would describe it as gentle in all respects -- mild pains, mild pleasures, calm desires, love that isn't obsessive. The undisciplined life, by contrast, is violent in everything -- sharp pains, intense pleasures, fierce and stinging desires, and love that's driven to insanity. In the self-controlled life, the pleasures outweigh the pains. In the undisciplined life, the pains outweigh the pleasures, both in magnitude, frequency, and sheer number. So one of these two lives is naturally and inevitably more pleasant, and the other more painful. And if someone wants to live pleasantly, it follows that he can't choose to live without self-control.

If this is true, the conclusion is clear: no one is undisciplined by choice. The great mass of humanity lacks self-control either from ignorance or from weakness of will -- or both. The same applies to the healthy and the sickly life: both involve pleasure and pain, but in health the pleasure exceeds the pain, while in sickness the pain exceeds the pleasure.

Our goal in choosing a way of life isn't to maximize pain -- it's to live the life where pleasure outweighs pain. We've established that. And the self-controlled life has fewer, smaller, and less frequent pleasures and pains than the undisciplined life; the wise life has fewer than the foolish life; and the courageous life has fewer than the cowardly life. In each pair, one life exceeds the other in pleasure -- the courageous surpasses the cowardly, the wise surpasses the foolish. So overall, the lives of self-control, courage, wisdom, and health all exceed their opposites in pleasure. Put simply: any life that possesses virtue, whether of body or soul, is more pleasant than the vicious life -- and far superior in beauty, correctness, excellence, and reputation. It makes the person who lives that way immeasurably happier than the person who lives the opposite way.

That's enough for the preamble. Now the actual laws should follow -- or, to be more precise, an outline of them. Think of it like weaving a piece of fabric: the warp and the woof can't be made from the same material. The warp has to be stronger and firmer, while the woof is softer and more flexible. In the same way, people who are going to hold high offices in the state need to be clearly distinguished from those whose education has been less rigorous. Let's say there are two parts to a constitution: one is the creation of offices, and the other is the body of laws those offices will administer.

But before any of that, there's something we need to consider first.

A shepherd or herdsman or horse breeder, when he receives his animals, won't start training them without first sorting through the herd. He separates the healthy from the unhealthy, the well-bred from the poorly bred, and sends the bad ones to other herds. He tends the rest, knowing that all his work would be wasted on animals whose bodies and souls have already been corrupted by nature and bad upbringing -- and that these damaged animals would drag down the healthy ones if left among them.

Now, with other animals this matters, but it's only worth mentioning for the sake of illustration. When it comes to human beings, the stakes are the highest imaginable. The lawgiver needs to investigate and determine what's appropriate for each person in terms of sorting and every other procedure.

Take the purification of a city, for example. There are many kinds -- some easier, some harder. The best and most difficult purifications are ones that a lawgiver with absolute power might be able to carry out. But a lawgiver who doesn't have absolute power -- who's setting up a new government and a new legal system -- should count himself lucky if he can manage even the mildest form of purification.

The best kind of purification is painful, like a drastic medical treatment. It involves righteous punishment and, in the most extreme cases, sentences of death or exile. That's how we typically deal with the worst offenders -- the incurable criminals who cause the greatest harm to the state.

The milder form of purification works like this: when people who have nothing, who are desperate for food, start showing a willingness to follow leaders in an attack on the property of the wealthy -- these people, who are like a natural plague upon the state -- the lawgiver sends them away as kindly as possible. The polite term for this is "founding a colony."

Every lawgiver should try to do something like this early on. But our present situation is unique. We don't need to arrange any colony or separating purification under the current circumstances. Instead, think of it this way: when many streams flow together from different sources -- springs, mountain torrents -- into a single lake, you need to make sure the combined water is perfectly clear. You pump out and divert the impurities. Similarly, in any political arrangement, there's going to be trouble and effort involved in this process.

But since we're just talking theoretically and not actually doing this, let's assume our selection has been completed and the desired purity achieved. As for the bad people who want to join and become citizens of our state -- after we've tested them through every form of persuasion over a sufficient period of time, we'll keep them out. But the good ones we'll welcome as friends with open arms, as warmly as we possibly can.

Now, there's another piece of good fortune we shouldn't forget. Like the Heraclid colonists before us, we've escaped the need to deal with redistribution of land and cancellation of debts -- which are always a source of dangerous conflict. A city that's forced to legislate on these matters is stuck: it can't let things stay as they are, but it also doesn't dare change them. All that's left is what amounts to prayers -- hoping that gradual, cautious change might be accomplished over a long period of time. And this kind of change can only happen when those who have plenty of land and many debtors are willing, in a generous spirit, to share with those in need -- sometimes forgiving debts, sometimes giving outright -- holding to a path of moderation, and recognizing that poverty is really an increase in a person's desires, not a decrease in his property.

This principle is the foundation on which a state's salvation rests. Whatever political structure you build must be erected on this foundation. But if the change is built on an unsound principle, the whole future administration will be plagued with problems. That's a danger we've escaped. But even so, it's worth saying how we would have escaped it if we hadn't -- and we can now assert that there's no other way out, whether narrow or broad, except through freedom from greed and a sense of justice. On this rock our city must be built. There should be no disputes among citizens about property. If there are longstanding property disputes among them, no lawgiver with any sense will take a single step in organizing the state until those disputes are settled.

But for people who've been given, as the gods have given us, the chance to found a new state that's still free from enmity -- for those people to create hostility among themselves through their method of distributing land and houses would be the height of folly and wickedness.

So how do we properly arrange the distribution of land? First, we need to determine the number of citizens, then the number and size of the divisions they'll be organized into. After that, we divide the land and houses among them as fairly as we can.

The number of citizens can only be determined properly in relation to the territory and to neighboring states. The territory needs to be large enough to support a certain number of people at a moderate standard of living -- no more than that. And the population needs to be large enough to defend itself against unjust neighbors and to help those neighbors effectively when they're wronged.

After surveying both our territory and our neighbors', we'll set these limits in practice as well as in theory.

Now, let's proceed to legislate with a view to completing the form and outline of our state. The number of citizens will be 5,040 -- a particularly useful number. These citizens will own the land and protect their allotments. The houses and land will be divided so that every person corresponds to a lot.

First, divide the total into two parts, then into three. The number can also be divided into four, five, or any number up to ten. Every lawgiver should know enough arithmetic to figure out which number is most useful for all cities. We're choosing the number that contains the greatest and most regular series of divisions.

The entire number system has every possible division, and 5,040 can be divided by exactly fifty-nine different divisors. Ten of these proceed in an unbroken sequence from one to ten. This gives us numbers ready-made for matters of war and peace, for all contracts and transactions, including taxes and divisions of the land.

Those who are charged by law with knowing these things should verify these mathematical properties at their leisure -- they're true. And they should be announced at the founding of the city for practical use.

Now, whether the lawgiver is founding a new state or restoring an old one that's fallen into decay, when it comes to gods and temples -- which temples should be built in each part of the city, and which gods or lesser divinities they should be named after -- a person with any sense won't disturb anything that the oracle at Delphi, or Dodona, or the god Ammon, or any ancient tradition has sanctioned. However these traditions were established -- whether through visions, reported divine inspiration, or whatever means led people to institute sacrifices connected with sacred rites, whether homegrown or imported from Etruria, Cyprus, or elsewhere -- and on the basis of which they consecrated oracles, images, altars, and temples, and set aside sacred land for each -- not the smallest part of any of this should be disturbed by the lawgiver.

Instead, he should assign to each district some god, lesser divinity, or hero. And in distributing the land, he should give these divine patrons their chosen territory first, along with everything appropriate to them. This way, the inhabitants of each district will gather at fixed times, and they'll have the means to supply each other's needs and entertain one another with shared sacrifices and feasts, becoming friends and getting to know each other.

There's no greater good for a state than its citizens being well acquainted with one another. When darkness and ignorance of each other's characters prevail, no one gets the honor, the authority, or the justice he truly deserves. So in every state, above all else, every person should strive to be genuine and free from deceit -- always true and straightforward -- and should make sure no deceitful person takes advantage of him.

Now, the next move in our game of legislation is an unusual one -- like moving a piece off the sacred line in a game of draughts. It'll probably cause some surprise when first mentioned. But if you think it through carefully, you'll see that our city is organized in a way that is, if not the very best, then the second best. Maybe some people won't approve because they think this kind of constitution isn't suited to a lawgiver who doesn't have absolute power. But here's the truth: there are three forms of government -- the best, the second best, and the third best. Let's briefly mention all three and leave the selection to the person in charge of the settlement. Following this approach, we'll describe the first, second, and third forms of the state, and then leave the choice to Cleinias -- or to anyone in the future who faces a similar task and wants to incorporate into his state whatever features suit his character and his country.

The first and highest form of state and government and law is the one where the old saying prevails most completely: "Friends share everything in common." Whether it exists anywhere now, or ever will exist -- this community of wives and children and property, where the private and individual is completely eliminated from life, where things that are naturally private, like eyes and ears and hands, somehow see and hear and act in common, where everyone praises and blames and feels joy and sorrow on the same occasions, where the laws unite the city to the absolute maximum -- whether all this is possible or not, I say that no one, working from any other principle, will ever create a state that's truer or better or more exalted in virtue. Whether it's governed by gods or sons of gods, one or many -- happy are the people who live that way. This is the pattern we should look to, the ideal we should cling to, the model we should pursue with all our might.

The state we have in hand right now, once it's created, will come closest to that immortal ideal and take second place. After that, god willing, we'll describe the third. But for now, let's discuss the nature and origin of this second-best state.

Let the citizens distribute their land and houses right away, without farming the land in common -- since communal ownership goes beyond what our proposed colony's origins, upbringing, and education can support. But in making the distribution, every landowner should understand that his particular lot also belongs to the whole city. And since the earth is their mother, let them tend her even more carefully than children tend their own mother. She is a goddess and their queen, and they are her mortal subjects. The same feelings should extend to the gods and divine spirits of the region.

To make sure the distribution remains stable forever, they should also keep in mind that the current number of households must always be maintained -- neither increased nor decreased. Here's how to secure this for the whole city: let the owner of a lot choose his most beloved child -- and only one -- to be the heir of his household and the successor in his duty of service to the gods, the state, and the family, both the living and the dead. As for his other children, if he has more than one, he should give the daughters in marriage according to the law we'll establish later, and distribute the sons as adopted children to citizens who have no children of their own and are willing to receive them.

If there aren't enough willing adoptive families, or if particular individuals have too many or too few children -- whether due to an excess of births or to infertility -- in all these cases, the highest and most honorable magistracy we'll establish should judge what's to be done and figure out how to keep the number of 5,040 households constant.

There are many ways to regulate population. Those who produce children abundantly can be encouraged to slow down. On the other hand, special attention can be given to increasing births through rewards and social incentives. Or elders can advise and counsel the younger generation -- that approach can also work.

And if, despite all this, we still face serious trouble maintaining the exact number of 5,040 households -- if there's a surplus of citizens because of the deep love between those who live together and we're at our wit's end -- there's always the old solution we've mentioned before: founding a colony of suitable people who'll leave us on friendly terms. On the other hand, if a wave of disease or plague of war sweeps through and the population falls far below the target because of widespread death, we shouldn't bring in citizens of inferior birth and education -- not if we can possibly avoid it. But even god, they say, can't fight against necessity.

So let's imagine that this "high argument" of ours addresses us directly, saying: "Best of all people, never stop honoring -- in keeping with nature -- the principles of similarity, equality, consistency, and agreement, especially in regard to number and every good and noble quality. Above all, maintain the number 5,040 throughout your lives. Second, don't undermine the modest proportions of the inheritances you received in the original distribution by buying and selling them among yourselves.

"Because if you do, neither the god who assigned you the lot nor the lawgiver will be on your side. And the law makes clear to the disobedient that these are the terms on which the lot may or may not be held. First, the earth is sacred to the gods -- that's a given. And second, priests and priestesses will offer prayers over a first, second, and even third sacrifice, praying that whoever buys or sells the houses or lands he was given will receive the punishment he deserves. They'll inscribe these prayers in the temples on tablets of cypress wood for future generations to read. And they'll station watchful magistrates over all of this -- the magistracy with the sharpest eyes will be assigned to make sure any violation of these rules is detected and punished as an offense against both the law and the gods.

"How much good this kind of system does for all the cities that follow it -- no dishonest person can ever appreciate, as the old saying goes. Only a person of experience and good character can understand."

In a society organized this way, there won't be much opportunity for making money. No one should -- or will be allowed to -- practice any degrading occupation. The vulgarity of such work is a matter of reproach for a free person, and no one should ever want to accumulate wealth through such means.

Furthermore, the law will forbid any private citizen from possessing gold or silver. There will be a local currency for everyday transactions -- practically necessary for dealing with craftsmen and for paying wages to hired workers, whether they're slaves or immigrant laborers. Everyone who needs these services will use this currency.

So our citizens should have a coin that circulates among themselves but isn't accepted anywhere else in the Greek world. However, for expeditions and journeys abroad -- embassies, dispatching heralds, or any other official business that requires travel -- the state itself must maintain a supply of common Greek currency.

If a private citizen ever needs to travel abroad, let him get permission from the magistrates and go. When he returns, if he has any foreign currency left over, he must hand the surplus to the treasury and receive an equal amount in local currency. If he's caught keeping foreign money for himself, it gets confiscated. Anyone who knows about it and doesn't report it will be subject to the same curse and dishonor as the person who brought it back -- and will pay a fine no less than the amount of the foreign money involved.

When it comes to marriage, no one shall give or receive a dowry of any kind. No one shall deposit money with someone he doesn't trust as a friend. No one shall lend money at interest. And the borrower should be under no obligation to repay either principal or interest.

That these are the best principles, anyone can see by comparing them with the foundational purpose of a state. The purpose of a well-governed state, as we've said, is not what the majority claim a good lawgiver should aim for -- namely, making the state as great and as rich as possible, with gold and silver overflowing and the widest possible empire by sea and land. That's what they think legislation is about, and they inconsistently add that the true lawgiver should want the city to be as good and happy as possible. But they don't see that some of these goals are compatible and some aren't, and that the person ordering the state should pursue what's possible without wasting time on impossible fantasies.

Yes, the citizen must be happy and good, and the lawgiver will try to make him so. But being very rich and very good at the same time? That he cannot be -- at least, not rich in the way most people mean it. When they say "the rich," they mean the handful of people with the most valuable possessions -- possessions that any scoundrel could own just as easily. And if that's true, I can never agree that the rich person will be happy. He has to be good as well as rich. And to be good in the highest degree and rich in the highest degree at the same time is impossible.

"Why not?" someone will ask. Here's why: income from both just and unjust sources combined is more than double what comes from just sources alone. And expenditures that are neither honorable nor dishonorable are only half as great as those spent honestly on worthy purposes. So the person who earns double and spends half will always be wealthier than the person who does the opposite and is a good man. The first type -- the saver, not the spender -- isn't always bad. He might even be completely bad in some cases. But a genuinely good person? Never. Someone who acquires money both justly and unjustly and spends neither well nor badly will be rich -- if he's also thrifty. But the truly bad person is generally a spendthrift, and therefore very poor. Meanwhile, the person who spends on noble causes and only earns money through honest means can hardly become exceptionally wealthy any more than he can become desperately poor.

So our statement stands: the very rich are not good. And if they're not good, they're not happy.

The whole intent of our laws was to make citizens as happy and as friendly to one another as possible. But people who are constantly in court with each other, people among whom many wrongs are committed, can never be friends. Only those among whom crimes and lawsuits are few and minor can achieve real friendship.

That's why we say that gold and silver shouldn't be allowed in the city, nor should there be much of the ordinary sort of commerce based on lending money or breeding the meaner kinds of livestock. Only the produce of agriculture should be the basis of wealth -- and only so much of that as won't force us to neglect what wealth actually exists to serve. I mean the soul and body, which are worthless without exercise and education. So, as we've said not once but many times, the pursuit of riches should be our lowest priority.

There are three things every person has a stake in. The interest in money, when properly understood, is the third and lowest. Above it comes the interest of the body. And first and foremost comes the interest of the soul. The state we're describing will be properly constituted if it distributes honors according to this scale. But if any of the laws we've established puts health above self-control, or wealth above health and moderate habits, that law is clearly wrong.

So the lawgiver should constantly ask himself: "What do I really want?" and "Am I actually achieving it, or missing the mark?" Only by doing this can he fulfill his duty and free everyone else from the burden of legislation.

Let the person who receives a lot, then, hold it under the conditions we've described.

It would be ideal if every person came to the colony with equal possessions. But since that's impossible, and one person will inevitably have more than another -- for many reasons -- and especially to maintain fairness during special crises of the state, property qualifications need to be unequal. Offices, contributions, and distributions should be proportioned to the value of each person's wealth -- not solely to the virtue of his ancestors or himself, nor to physical strength or good looks, but also to how wealthy or poor he is. By this kind of proportional inequality, each person will receive honors and offices as equitably as possible, and there'll be no quarrels or disputes.

To this end, four property classes should be established. Citizens will be ranked first, second, third, or fourth class based on their holdings, and they'll be called by these or similar names. A person may stay in the same class or move to another if his wealth increases or decreases.

Here's the law I'd propose as the natural next step: in a state that wants to be saved from the greatest plague of all -- not mere faction, but outright disintegration -- there should be neither extreme poverty nor excessive wealth among the citizens, because both conditions produce these evils.

So the lawgiver needs to set limits in both directions. Let the minimum be the value of the allotted lot. This must be preserved, and no ruler or anyone who cares about his reputation for virtue should ever allow it to be diminished. The lawgiver establishes this as the baseline.

Beyond that, a person may accumulate up to double, triple, or as much as four times the value of the lot. But if anyone comes to possess more than that -- whether he's found it, received it as a gift, earned it in business, or gained it through some stroke of fortune -- he should give the surplus back to the state and to the patron gods of the state. If he does this, he'll suffer no penalty or loss of reputation. But if he disobeys this law, anyone who wants may report him and receive half the value of the excess. The offender will then pay a fine equal to the surplus from his own property, and the other half goes to the gods.

Every possession of every citizen, apart from the lot itself, should be publicly registered with the magistrates appointed by law, so that all lawsuits about money can be straightforward and simple.

Next: the city should be placed as close as possible to the center of the country. We should choose a location that has everything a city needs -- which is easy enough to imagine and describe. Then we'll divide the city into twelve portions.

First, found temples to Hestia, Zeus, and Athena on a spot we'll call the Acropolis, and surround it with a circular wall. From this central point, divide the entire city and surrounding country into twelve sections. Make the twelve portions equal by making those with better soil smaller and those with poorer soil larger.

The total number of lots will be 5,040, and each will be split in two: one section of land near the city, one section farther away. A lot near the city gets paired with a lot on the borders to form one complete allotment, and the next nearest gets paired with the next farthest, and so on. In both sections of each lot, the same principle of equalization applies -- differences in soil quality are compensated by differences in size.

The lawgiver will divide the citizens into twelve groups and arrange the rest of their property to form twelve equal parts as far as possible. A full registration of everything will be made. Then twelve lots will be assigned to twelve gods, each portion named after and dedicated to its god, and the tribes will take their names from these gods. The twelve divisions of the city will mirror the twelve divisions of the country. And every person will have two homes: one near the center and one on the outskirts.

That covers the method of settlement.

Now we need to face something honestly: there can never be such a perfect alignment of circumstances as we've described. It's unrealistic to expect people who'll never take offense at this way of living together, who'll accept having their property capped at a moderate level for their entire lives, who'll have children according to our regulations, who'll tolerate being forbidden from possessing gold and other things the lawgiver -- as is obvious from these provisions -- will certainly prohibit, and who'll put up with the city being in the middle with their houses spread around it.

All of this sounds like a lawgiver telling his dreams, or building a city and its citizens out of wax. There's some truth in these objections, and everyone should take to heart what I'm about to say.

Let the lawgiver step forward and address us one more time: "My friends," he'd say, "don't think I'm unaware that there's real truth in your objections. But here's my view: when someone is planning for the future and presenting a model of what he's aiming for, he should hold nothing back from the highest and truest ideal. If he finds that some part of this plan is impossible to carry out, he should set that part aside and not attempt it. But he should work to accomplish whatever comes closest to it and is most akin to the ideal. Let the lawgiver complete his design first. Then you should join him in evaluating which parts of his legislation are practical and which will meet resistance. Surely any craftsman worth respecting should always make his work internally consistent."

So, having established that the distribution will be into twelve parts, let's consider how to carry this out. The twelve parts naturally admit of the greatest number of subdivisions of whatever they contain -- and it's easy to see the additional numbers that follow from them, all the way up to 5,040. The law should therefore require that military units, administrative districts, and villages, as well as currency, and dry and liquid measures, and weights, all be made to harmonize and correspond with one another.

And we shouldn't be afraid of seeming overly precise if the law requires that all vessels a person owns follow a common standard of measurement. We should recognize that the divisions and variations of numbers are useful for all kinds of purposes -- for measuring height and depth, for sounds, and for all types of motion, whether straight up and down or circular.

The lawgiver should take all of this into account and instruct the citizens never to lose sight of numerical order as much as possible. No single tool in the education of the young has as much power -- for household management, for politics, and for every practical skill -- as the study of arithmetic. Above all, arithmetic wakes up the naturally sleepy and dull mind, making a person quick to learn, sharp of memory, and shrewd. With this almost divine assistance, he makes progress far beyond his natural abilities.

All of these studies will be excellent and effective tools for education -- provided the lawgiver, through other laws and institutions, can banish pettiness and greed from the citizens' souls, so they can use these tools properly and for their own benefit. But if he can't, he'll unintentionally cultivate in them not wisdom but cunning -- the same unfortunate tendency we can observe among the Egyptians and Phoenicians and many other peoples, whose pursuits and acquisitions have been made vulgar, whether by incompetent legislators, bad luck, or unfavorable natural conditions.

And this brings up an important point, Megillus and Cleinias: we shouldn't overlook the fact that different places produce different kinds of people -- some better, some worse -- and we need to legislate accordingly. Some locations are subject to strange and powerful influences -- unusual winds, extreme heat, the quality of the water, or the character of the food the land produces. These things affect not only people's bodies, for better or worse, but produce similar effects in their souls as well.

And the places that excel above all others are those where there's a divine inspiration, where divine spirits have their appointed territories and look favorably upon the settlers rather than opposing them. To all these matters the lawgiver should attend as far as humanly possible, and frame his laws accordingly.

And this is what you need to do, Cleinias. You need to give careful attention to these matters, since you're about to found a new colony.
