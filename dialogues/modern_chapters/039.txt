STATESMAN (Part 2)

Stranger
So here's the principle I'd like both of us to remember: we should praise or blame the length or shortness of discussions not by comparing them with each other, but by measuring them against what's fitting — using that part of measurement we said we should keep in mind.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
But even fitness isn't the only standard. We shouldn't seek a length that pleases us — that's a secondary consideration at best. And reason tells us that ease and speed of inquiry should be our second priority, not our first. The highest priority of all is to uphold the great method of division according to real kinds — whether the discussion ends up longer or shorter. No one should be offended by length. Longer and shorter should be used freely, whichever is better for sharpening the minds of the participants. And if someone complains that a discussion is too long, they shouldn't just grumble about the length — they should prove that a shorter version would have made the participants better thinkers and more capable of finding the truth. Any other praise or blame isn't worth hearing.

But we've spent enough time on this, and you probably agree. Let's get back to our Statesman and apply the weaving example we've been developing.

Young Socrates
Good — let's do that.

Stranger
The king's art has been separated from the similar arts of shepherds, and indeed from all the arts that deal with herds. But there remain the causal and cooperative arts that are directly involved in running a state, and these need to be distinguished from one another.

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
You should know that these won't divide neatly into two halves. The reason will become clear as we proceed.

Young Socrates
Then let's get on with it.

Stranger
We'll have to carve them up like butchering an animal at the joints, since we can't bisect them cleanly. We should still divide into as few parts as possible.

Young Socrates
What do we do in this case?

Stranger
The same thing we did with weaving: all the arts that make tools were classified as cooperative.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
Just so, all the arts that produce any kind of implement in a state — large or small — should be considered cooperative. Without them, there would be no state and no statesmanship. But we're not inclined to say that any of them is a product of the royal art itself.

Young Socrates
No, indeed.

Stranger
Separating this class from the rest is tricky, because there's always a plausible case that anything in the world is "a tool for doing something." But there's another class of possessions in a city that I want to mention.

Young Socrates
What class?

Stranger
A class that doesn't have this productive power. Unlike a tool, it's not designed for making things — it's designed for preserving what's already been made.

Young Socrates
What are you referring to?

Stranger
The broad class of containers — vessels of all kinds, built for preserving both liquids and solids, things prepared over fire and things prepared without it. It's a very large class, and if I'm not mistaken, it has absolutely nothing to do with the royal art we're investigating.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
There's also a third class of possessions to note — very different from these, and also very extensive. Things that move or rest, on land or water, honorable and dishonorable. This entire class has one name, because everything in it is designed to be sat upon — it's always a seat for something.

Young Socrates
What is it?

Stranger
A vehicle. And that's certainly not the work of the Statesman — it belongs to the carpenter, the potter, and the coppersmith.

Young Socrates
I see.

Stranger
And isn't there a fourth class, again distinct, that contains most of the things we've mentioned? Every kind of clothing, most types of weapons, walls and enclosures of earth and stone, and a thousand other things? All of these are made for defense, and should properly be considered the work of the builder or the weaver, not the Statesman.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Should we add a fifth class — ornamentation and drawing, and all the imitations produced by visual art and music, designed purely for amusement? They can all be covered by one name.

Young Socrates
What name?

Stranger
Plaything.

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
That one word fits them all, because none of these things has a serious purpose. Entertainment is their only aim.

Young Socrates
That I understand.

Stranger
Then there's a class that provides raw materials for all the others — the stuff from which the arts I've mentioned fabricate their products. This varied class, the offspring of many different arts — can I count it as the sixth?

Young Socrates
What do you mean exactly?

Stranger
Gold, silver, and other metals; everything that timber-cutting and textile-shearing provide for carpentry and plaiting; the process of stripping bark and skin from plants and animals — the currier's art and similar crafts. These make cork, papyrus, cord, and enable the production of complex things from simple materials. The whole class can be called the primitive, unprocessed materials of human life — and the royal science has nothing whatsoever to do with it.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
The provision of food and everything else that mingles with the particles of the human body to sustain it — this makes a seventh class, which we can call nourishment, unless you have a better term. This belongs to the farmer, the hunter, the athletic trainer, the doctor, and the cook — not to the Statesman's art.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
So these seven classes cover nearly every kind of property, except for tame animals. Let me review: first, raw materials — which should really have come first. Then tools, containers, vehicles, defenses, playthings, and nourishment. I'm leaving out small items like coins, seals, and stamps, which don't really form a major category of their own — some can be squeezed in with ornaments, others with tools. As for the management of tame animals — which we've already divided into parts — that covers all animal property except one kind: slaves.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
And it's in the class of slaves and attendants that I suspect we'll finally discover the real pretenders to the throne — the rivals of the king in weaving the political fabric. Just as spinners, carders, and the rest were the rivals of the weaver. All the others — the ones we called cooperators — have been cleared away among the occupations already mentioned, separated from the royal and political science.

Young Socrates
I agree.

Stranger
Let's move closer and examine these remaining figures more carefully.

Young Socrates
Let's.

Stranger
Looking from our current vantage point, the most obvious servants turn out to be in the opposite situation from what we might have expected.

Young Socrates
Who are they?

Stranger
People who have been purchased and thereby become property. These are unmistakably slaves, and they certainly don't claim to possess the royal science.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
What about free people who voluntarily serve the other classes in a state — who exchange and equalize the products of farming and the other arts? Some sit in the marketplace, others travel from city to city by land or sea, trading money for goods or goods for money — the money-changers, the merchants, the ship-owners, the retailers. Do any of them claim statesmanship or political science?

Young Socrates
No — unless you mean the politics of commerce.

Stranger
And surely the hired laborers and day workers, who are happy to turn their hand to anything for pay — they don't claim to share in the royal science?

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
But what about some other class of public servants?

Young Socrates
Who are they? What services do they perform?

Stranger
Heralds, and scribes perfected through long practice, and various other highly skilled officials connected with the administration of government — what do we call them?

Young Socrates
They're officials and servants of the rulers, as you just said — not rulers themselves.

Stranger
There might seem to be something odd about a servant claiming to be a ruler. And yet I have a feeling that the principal claimants to political science are lurking somewhere in this neighborhood.

Young Socrates
Indeed.

Stranger
Well, let's move closer and test some claimants we haven't examined yet. First, there are the diviners — they have a share of ministerial knowledge and are considered interpreters of the gods to human beings.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
There's also the priestly class. According to tradition, priests know how to give the gods gifts from us in the form of sacrifices the gods will accept, and how to ask for blessings in return. Both of these — divination and priesthood — are branches of ministerial art.

Young Socrates
Yes, clearly.

Stranger
And here I think we're getting warm. Priests and diviners are swollen with pride and prestige. The grandeur of their undertakings inspires genuine awe. In Egypt, the king himself isn't allowed to rule unless he has priestly authority, and if he comes from another class and seizes power, he has to be enrolled in the priesthood. Throughout Greece, the duty of performing the most solemn public sacrifices is assigned to the highest officials. And right here in Athens, the most ancient and sacred national sacrifices are supposed to be performed by the King Archon — the man chosen by lot.

Young Socrates
Precisely.

Stranger
But who are these other kings and priests, chosen by lot, who now come into view — followed by their retinues and a vast crowd — as the previous group exits and the scene changes?

Young Socrates
Who do you mean?

Stranger
A very strange crew.

Young Socrates
How strange?

Stranger
A moment ago, I thought they were animals of every kind. Many of them look like lions and centaurs; many more like satyrs and other weak, shape-shifting creatures — all rapidly changing forms and natures like Proteus. And now, Socrates, I think I finally see who they are.

Young Socrates
Who? You seem to be gazing at some strange vision.

Stranger
Yes — everyone looks strange when you don't know them. And that's exactly what just happened to me. At first sight, coming on them suddenly, I didn't recognize the politician and his troupe.

Young Socrates
Who are they?

Stranger
The chief of Sophists. The most accomplished wizard of them all. He must be separated from the true king and statesman at any cost, if we're ever going to see daylight in this investigation.

Young Socrates
That's not a hope we can afford to abandon.

Stranger
Never, if I can help it. But first, a question.

Young Socrates
What?

Stranger
Monarchy is a recognized form of government?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And after monarchy, next comes the rule of the few?

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
And the third form is the rule of the many, called democracy?

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And don't these three expand in a sense into five, producing two additional names from within themselves?

Young Socrates
What are they?

Stranger
People today apply certain criteria — voluntary versus involuntary, wealth versus poverty, law versus lawlessness. Using these criteria, they split the first two forms: monarchy divides into royalty and tyranny.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
And the rule of the few divides into aristocracy and oligarchy.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Democracy, on the other hand — whether it strictly observes the laws or not, whether the majority rules over the wealthy with their consent or without it — always keeps the same name.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
But do you think any form of government defined by these characteristics — one ruler, few rulers, many rulers; rich or poor; voluntary or forced; written law or no law — can be the right one?

Young Socrates
Why not?

Stranger
Think carefully. Follow me.

Young Socrates
Where?

Stranger
Are we going to stand by what we said at the start, or take it back?

Young Socrates
What did we say?

Stranger
We said that royal power is a form of knowledge — a science.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And a distinctive kind of science at that — one we selected from all the rest as having both a judicial and a directive character.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And within the directive branch, we distinguished authority over lifeless things from authority over living beings, and proceeded step by step to this point — never losing the idea of science, but unable to determine exactly what kind of science it is.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
Then we're led to this conclusion: the distinguishing principle of the state can't be "the few or the many," or "willing or unwilling," or "rich or poor." Some concept of science has to be part of it, if we're going to be consistent with everything we've said.

Young Socrates
And we must be consistent.

Stranger
So the question is: in which of these various forms of government is the science of governing actually found — the science that's among the greatest and most difficult of all to acquire? We need to discover that, and then we'll be able to see who the false politicians are — the ones who claim to be statesmen but aren't, even though they've fooled a lot of people — and separate them from the truly wise ruler.

Young Socrates
Yes, as our argument has already indicated, that's exactly what we need to do.

Stranger
Do you think the general population of a state could attain this political science?

Young Socrates
Impossible.

Stranger
But in a city of a thousand people, maybe a hundred could? Or say fifty?

Young Socrates
If that were the case, political science would be the easiest of all sciences! You couldn't find that many top-ranked board-game players in a city that size, judged against the best in all of Greece. And you'd certainly find even fewer genuine kings. Remember: we can rightly call anyone who possesses the royal science a king, whether they actually hold power or not. That's what our earlier argument showed.

Stranger
Good reminder. The consequence is that any true form of government can only be the government of one, or two, or at most a few.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And these rulers — whether they govern with their subjects' consent or without it, with written laws or without them, whether they're rich or poor, whatever the outward form — must be understood to rule on the basis of a scientific principle. It's exactly like a doctor: whether he treats us with or against our will, whether he cuts or burns or inflicts some other painful treatment, whether he works from a textbook or improvises, whether he's rich or poor, whether he purges us or slims us down or even fattens us up — he's a doctor all the same, as long as he exercises authority through genuine medical knowledge, and as long as he actually does his patients good, healing and saving them. And this we lay down as the only proper test of any art of healing — or any art of command.

Young Socrates
Exactly right.

Stranger
Then the only true form of government is the one where the rulers genuinely possess this science and aren't mere pretenders — whether they rule with or without laws, over willing or unwilling subjects, and whether they're personally rich or poor. None of those factors can properly be included in the definition of a ruler.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And whether they serve the public good by purging the state — exiling some citizens, executing others — or whether they shrink the population by sending out colonies like bees swarming from a hive, or enlarge it by bringing in outsiders and granting them citizenship: as long as they act according to wisdom and justice, using their power for the general security and improvement of the state — a state with these characteristics deserves to be called the only true state. All other governments are imitations of this one, some better and some worse. The better imitations are said to be well governed, but they're still just imitations.

Young Socrates
Most of what you've said seems right, Stranger. But the part about ruling without laws — that's a hard thing to hear.

Stranger
You're a step ahead of me, Socrates! I was just about to ask whether you objected to anything. I see we'll need to take up this question of good government without laws.

Young Socrates
Indeed we must.

Stranger
There's no doubt that lawmaking is in some sense the business of a king. And yet the best thing of all is not for the law to rule, but for a wise person to rule — one who possesses wisdom and royal power. Do you see why?

Young Socrates
Why?

Stranger
Because law can't perfectly capture what's most noble and just for everyone, and therefore can't enforce what's truly best. People are different. Actions are different. And the endless, irregular movement of human affairs doesn't allow for any universal, simple rule. No art whatsoever can lay down a principle that will hold good for all time.

Young Socrates
Of course not.

Stranger
But that's exactly what the law is always trying to do — like a stubborn, ignorant tyrant who won't allow anything to be done contrary to his orders, or any questions to be asked. Even when circumstances suddenly change and something would clearly be better for someone than what the law commands — the law won't budge.

Young Socrates
That's exactly how the law treats us.

Stranger
A perfectly simple principle can never be properly applied to a state of affairs that's anything but simple.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
Then if law isn't the perfection of justice, why do we make laws at all? That's the next question to investigate.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Tell me — don't you have athletic competitions in your city, like in other cities, where people compete in running, wrestling, and so on?

Young Socrates
Yes, they're very common here.

Stranger
And what kind of rules do the professional trainers impose on their athletes? Can you recall?

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
The trainers don't create individualized programs for each person, tailored precisely to their constitution. They work more broadly, prescribing a general regimen that will benefit the majority.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
They assign the same amount of exercise to everyone. They send them out together, let them rest together — whether they're running, wrestling, or doing anything else.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
Now notice: the legislator who has to preside over the whole community and enforce justice can't be expected to prescribe exactly what's right for every individual case, either.

Young Socrates
He certainly can't.

Stranger
He'll lay down laws in general terms for the majority — roughly covering individual cases. Some of these he'll put in writing; others will remain as unwritten traditional customs.

Young Socrates
That makes sense.

Stranger
Of course it does. Because how could anyone sit at every person's side throughout their entire life, prescribing exactly what they should do at each moment? Who could manage that? No one who truly possessed the royal science — if they could actually do this — would impose the constraint of a written law on themselves.

Young Socrates
That follows from what we've said.

Stranger
Or rather, my friend, from what's about to be said.

Young Socrates
And what's that?

Stranger
Picture a doctor or a trainer who's about to travel abroad and expects to be away for a long time. Worried that his patients or athletes won't remember his instructions, he writes them down as notes for them to follow.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
But what if he came back sooner than expected? And what if, due to some unexpected change — unusual weather or some other unforeseen factor — a different treatment turned out to be better? Would he refuse to deviate from his original prescription? Would he stubbornly stick to the old rules, never issuing new instructions, while his patients dared not do anything other than what was originally written — on the grounds that only the prescribed course was healthy, and anything else was harmful and wrong? Viewed in the light of genuine science and real expertise, wouldn't all such rigidity be utterly absurd?

Young Socrates
Utterly.

Stranger
And what about the lawgiver — the one who established written or unwritten rules about what's good or bad, honorable or dishonorable, just or unjust, for the communities of people who live together in cities and are governed by these rules? If the wise lawgiver himself were to return — or someone like him — should he be prohibited from changing the laws? Wouldn't that prohibition be every bit as absurd as the medical case?

Young Socrates
Absolutely.

Stranger
Do you know a popular saying that's relevant here?

Young Socrates
I can't think which one you mean at the moment.

Stranger
People say that if someone knows how to improve the existing laws, he must first persuade his own state to accept the improvement — and only then may he change the laws.

Young Socrates
And aren't they right about that?

Stranger
Perhaps. But suppose a wise person uses a bit of gentle force for his people's own good — what do we call that? Actually, before you answer, let me frame the question using our earlier examples.

Young Socrates
Go ahead.

Stranger
Suppose a skilled doctor has a patient — of whatever age or sex — and compels that patient to do something for their own good that goes against the written rules. What do we call this compulsion? Would anyone dream of calling it a violation of the medical art, or a breach of the laws of health? Nothing could be more unjust than for the patient to accuse the doctor of malpractice or of making the disease worse.

Young Socrates
Absolutely true.

Stranger
In political life, the equivalent of "disease" isn't called disease — it's called evil, or disgrace, or injustice.

Young Socrates
Right.

Stranger
So when a citizen is compelled — against the written law and against custom — to do what's actually juster, better, and nobler than what they were doing before, the very last thing anyone should say about this use of force is that it's disgraceful, evil, or unjust.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
Then let me ask: does it matter whether the person exercising this force is rich or poor? With or without written laws? With or against the citizens' consent? The real question is this: isn't the true principle of government that the wise and good person will direct the affairs of the community in the way that's best for it? Just as a pilot — by continuously watching over the interests of the ship and crew, not by laying down rigid rules but by making his expertise the law — preserves the lives of his fellow sailors? In the same way, couldn't a true form of government be created by people who govern in this same spirit, making the strength of their art superior to the law? Wise rulers can't go wrong as long as they maintain this one great principle: distributing justice to the citizens with intelligence and skill, preserving them, and as far as possible making them better rather than worse.

Young Socrates
No one can argue with that.

Stranger
And no one can argue with the other statement either.

Young Socrates
Which one?

Stranger
That no large number of people, whoever they may be, can attain genuine political knowledge. True government must be found in a small group, or in an individual. All other states are merely imitations of this — some better, some worse.

Young Socrates
What do you mean? I'm not sure I followed your earlier remark about imitations.

Stranger
And yet the point is extremely important, even if we just raise it and move on without fully exploring it.

Young Socrates
What point?

Stranger
Here's the idea — it's not easy or familiar, but let me try to put it this way: suppose the government I've been describing is the only true model. Then all the other forms of government must rely on the written laws of this model if they want to survive. They'll have to follow the practice that most people now approve — even though it's not the best thing in the world.

Young Socrates
What practice?

Stranger
That no citizen should do anything contrary to the laws, and anyone who violates them should be punished with the most extreme penalties, including death. This is perfectly right and good — as a second-best option, once you set aside the first and best option I just described. Let me explain why I call this the second best.

Young Socrates
Please do.

Stranger
I need to go back to my favorite analogies. Only through them can I describe kings and rulers.

Young Socrates
What analogies?

Stranger
The noble pilot and the wise physician — "worth many another man," as the saying goes. Let's use them to discover an image of the king.

Young Socrates
What kind of image?

Stranger
Here's one: think about what we actually suffer at the hands of doctors and pilots. A doctor can save whoever he wants to save, but he can also mistreat whoever he wants — cutting them, burning them, causing pain, and on top of it all demanding payments that amount to a kind of tax on the sick, most of which goes to the doctor and his household rather than to the patient. In the end, he might even take money from the patient's relatives or enemies and finish the patient off. And pilots are just as bad — they intentionally leave you stranded on shore when the ship's about to sail, they cause disasters at sea, they dump the cargo overboard, and are guilty of all kinds of treachery.

Now suppose that, keeping all this in mind, we decided that neither of these arts should be allowed to exercise authority without oversight — over free people or over slaves. So we convene an assembly, either of all the citizens or only the wealthy, and anyone at all — whether they're in the profession or not — is allowed to offer opinions about how to navigate ships or treat diseases: what surgical instruments and medicines to use on patients, what nautical equipment to employ, how to handle dangers from wind and waves, what to do when encountering pirates, and how to fight naval battles with old-fashioned ships. And whatever the crowd decides, based on advice from both experts and non-experts alike — all of that gets inscribed on tablets and columns, or enacted as unwritten national customs. And from that point on, all navigation and all medical treatment must be conducted according to these regulations.

Young Socrates
What a bizarre idea!

Stranger
Now suppose further that the pilots and doctors are appointed annually — chosen either from the wealthy classes or from the whole population by lot. And once elected, they navigate ships and treat the sick strictly according to the written rules.

Young Socrates
That's even worse.

Stranger
But wait — there's more. When the year of office has expired, the pilot or doctor must appear before a review board. The judges are either drawn from the wealthy or chosen by lot from the entire population. And anyone who wants to can bring charges — claiming that during the past year, these officials didn't navigate their ships or heal their patients according to the letter of the law and the ancient customs of their ancestors. If convicted, the judges determine the punishment or the fine.

Young Socrates
Anyone who'd willingly hold office under those conditions deserves whatever they get.

Stranger
And there's one more law we'd need to pass. If anyone is caught investigating the true nature of piloting and navigation, or studying health and the genuine principles of medicine, or inquiring into the winds or atmospheric conditions — in ways that go beyond the written rules — if he has any original ideas about such matters, he's not to be called a pilot or a doctor, but a "stargazing windbag" — a Sophist. And on the grounds that he's corrupting the young by persuading them to practice medicine or piloting in unauthorized ways and exercise arbitrary authority over patients or ships, anyone who meets the legal requirements can file charges against him. If he's found guilty of persuading anyone — young or old — to act contrary to the written law, he's to be punished with the utmost severity. Because no one should presume to be wiser than the laws. After all, the principles of healing and navigation are available to everyone — anyone can learn the written laws and national customs.

Now, Socrates — if this is how we ran things in all the sciences — in generalship, in hunting, in painting, in every branch of craftsmanship, in farming, in horse-breeding, in herding, in divination, in any kind of service work, in board games, in every mathematical science, in every art involving motion — if everything were done according to written regulations instead of genuine expertise — what would happen?

Young Socrates
Every art would be utterly destroyed, with no hope of recovery — because inquiry itself would be illegal. And human life, which is already hard enough, would become completely unbearable.

Stranger
And what if, on top of forcing all these activities to follow written law, we appointed someone — elected by show of hands or by lot — to be the guardian of the laws, and this person ignored the laws and acted against them out of self-interest or favoritism, without any real knowledge? Wouldn't that be even worse than the previous scenario?

Young Socrates
Much worse.

Stranger
Because to go against laws that are based on long experience — laws that reflect the wisdom of advisors who carefully recommended them and persuaded the public to adopt them — that would be a far greater and more destructive error than mere rigid adherence to written law.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Therefore, since this danger exists, the next-best policy is this: don't allow either any individual or any group to violate the laws in any way whatsoever.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
The laws are copies of the truth — written-down versions of the real principles of action, drafted by people who actually have knowledge. They're the best approximation that writing can achieve.

Young Socrates
They certainly are.

Stranger
And as we were saying, a person who genuinely possesses knowledge — a true Statesman — will do many things within his own sphere by the light of his art, without worrying about the laws, whenever he judges that something better has come up than what he originally wrote down and prescribed for the time when he'd be away.

Young Socrates
Yes, we said that.

Stranger
And any individual or group who has established laws, and then acts contrary to those laws in pursuit of something genuinely better — they'd be doing exactly what the true Statesman does, to the best of their ability.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Now, if they did this without knowledge, they'd be imitating the truth — and imitating it badly. But if they did it with knowledge, it wouldn't be imitation at all — it would be the genuine article.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
And we've already established that no large number of people can acquire genuine knowledge of any art.

Young Socrates
Yes, we have.

Stranger
Then the royal or political art will never be attained by the wealthy class as a whole, or by the general population.

Young Socrates
Impossible.

Stranger
So the closest these lesser forms of government can ever come to the true government of the one scientific ruler is this: never do anything contrary to their own written laws and national customs.

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
When the wealthy class imitates the true form by following the laws, that government is called aristocracy. When the wealthy disregard the laws, it's called oligarchy.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And when one individual rules according to law, in imitation of the one who truly knows — we call him a king. We give him the same name whether he rules by genuine knowledge or merely by correct opinion.

Young Socrates
That seems right.

Stranger
And when an individual truly possesses knowledge and rules — his title will certainly be the same: king. So the five common names for governments really collapse into one.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
But when a single ruler governs without law and without custom, imitating the true statesman while actually being driven by appetite and ignorance — pretending that the only way to act is to override the laws, when in fact he's motivated by selfishness and stupidity — shouldn't he be called a tyrant?

Young Socrates
Absolutely.

Stranger
And this, we believe, is the origin of the tyrant and the king, of oligarchies and aristocracies and democracies. People are disgusted by the idea of one-person rule. They can never be made to believe that anyone could actually be worthy of such authority — someone who is willing and able to rule with virtue and knowledge, dispensing justice fairly to all. They imagine that such a person would be a despot who'd wrong and kill and harm whoever he pleased. But if such a ruler as we describe actually existed, people would have to acknowledge that they'd be lucky to have him. He alone would be the happy ruler of a truly perfect state.

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
But as it stands, the state isn't like a beehive with a natural-born leader who's obviously superior in both body and mind. And so people are forced to gather together, write laws, and try to get as close as they can to the true form of government.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And when the foundation of politics rests on nothing but the letter of the law and custom, with knowledge divorced from action — can we really be surprised at the miseries that exist, and always will exist, in states? Any other art built on such a foundation would destroy everything it touched. What's really remarkable is the natural resilience of the political bond. States have endured this kind of thing forever, and yet some of them survive — even though many, like ships at sea, founder and sink through the incompetence of their pilots and crews, who are completely ignorant of the most important truths. I mean people who know nothing about genuine politics, yet believe they've mastered it more thoroughly than any other science.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
So the question arises: among these imperfect forms of government, which is the least oppressive — though they're all oppressive? And which is the worst? This is somewhat beside our main topic, but considering how it affects everything we do, we need to examine it.

Young Socrates
Yes, we must.

Stranger
Here's a striking fact: of the three forms, the same one is simultaneously the hardest to live under and the easiest.

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
I'm talking about the three forms I mentioned at the start — monarchy, the rule of the few, and the rule of the many.

Young Socrates
Right.

Stranger
If we divide each of these, we get six, from which the true government can be distinguished as a seventh.

Young Socrates
How do you divide them?

Stranger
Monarchy splits into royalty and tyranny. The rule of the few splits into aristocracy — a name with an auspicious meaning — and oligarchy. And democracy, which was a single category before, must now be divided too.

Young Socrates
On what principle?

Stranger
The same one as before, even though the name stays the same in both cases. The distinction between ruling with law and ruling without law applies here too, just as it applies to the others.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
This distinction didn't matter when we were looking for the perfect state, as we showed earlier. But now that we've set the perfect state aside, and only the other forms remain — the principle of law versus lawlessness divides each of them in two.

Young Socrates
That seems to follow.

Stranger
Monarchy, when bound by good laws, is the best of all six. Lawless monarchy is the most bitter and oppressive.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
The rule of the few, which sits between the one and the many, is also intermediate in quality — both its good and bad forms. But the rule of the many is weak in every respect — unable to accomplish either great good or great evil, compared with the others, because power is divided up too finely among too many people. This makes it the worst of all the lawful governments, and the best of all the lawless ones. If all governments are lawless, democracy is the most tolerable to live under. If all are well-ordered, democracy is the least desirable. Lawful monarchy — royalty — is the best of them all, with the exception of the seventh form: the rule of genuine knowledge. That stands above them all, the way God stands above human beings.

Young Socrates
You're absolutely right, and we should choose that above all.

Stranger
The members of all these states — except the one guided by genuine knowledge — should be viewed not as real statesmen but as partisans. They're defenders of the most monstrous illusions, and they're illusions themselves. Being the greatest imitators and magicians, they're also the greatest Sophists.

Young Socrates
It seems like the label "Sophist," after many twists and turns in the argument, has been very justly applied to politicians.

Stranger
And so our satirical drama has reached its finale. The troop of Centaurs and Satyrs — reluctant as they were to leave the stage — has at last been separated from political science.

Young Socrates
So it seems.

Stranger
But there remain characters who are even more troublesome, because they're more closely related to the king and harder to identify. Examining them is like refining gold.

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
Goldsmiths start by sifting away the obvious impurities — earth and stones and the like. What remains is a confused mixture of valuable elements related to gold — copper, silver, and other precious metals. These are only separated through the fire of further testing, until the gold is left pure and unalloyed.

Young Socrates
Yes, that's how it's said to be done.

Stranger
In the same way, we've sifted away everything alien and uncongenial from political science. What remains is precious and closely related: the noble arts of the general, the judge, and the higher form of oratory that allies itself with the royal art, persuading people to do what's just and helping to guide the helm of state. How do we clear away these final contenders, leaving the one we seek alone and pure?

Young Socrates
That's obviously what we have to try to do.

Stranger
If trying is all that's needed, he'll certainly come to light. And I think the analogy of music can help us reveal him. Let me ask you a question.

Young Socrates
What question?

Stranger
There is such a thing as learning music — or any of the practical arts, for that matter?

Young Socrates
There is.

Stranger
And is there a higher art or science that has the power to decide which of these arts should and shouldn't be learned?

Young Socrates
I'd say there is.

Stranger
And we'd agree this higher science is distinct from the ones it oversees?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And should the practical sciences be superior to this governing one? Or should no individual science outrank any other? Or should this governing science be the overseer and master of all the rest?

Young Socrates
The latter.

Stranger
You're saying that the science which decides whether we should learn something must be superior to the science that's being learned or taught?

Young Socrates
Far superior.

Stranger
And the science that determines whether we should use persuasion must be superior to the science that actually persuades?

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
Good. Now, to which science do we assign the power of persuading a crowd through compelling stories rather than through teaching?

Young Socrates
That clearly belongs to rhetoric.

Stranger
And to which science do we assign the power of deciding whether we should use persuasion, or force, or neither — in dealing with any group of people?

Young Socrates
To the science that governs the arts of speech and persuasion.

Stranger
Which, if I'm not mistaken, would be politics.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
So rhetoric is quickly distinguished from politics — it's a different species, but one that serves politics.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
Now, what about another kind of power?

Young Socrates
What kind?

Stranger
The science that deals with military operations against our enemies — is that a science or isn't it?

Young Socrates
How could anyone say that generalship and military tactics aren't a science?

Stranger
And is the art that decides when to go to war and when to make peace the same as generalship, or different?

Young Socrates
If we're being consistent with everything we've said, it must be different.

Stranger
And we must say this higher art rules the lower one, if we're not going to abandon our earlier principle.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And given how vast and formidable the entire art of war is — can we imagine any art superior to it except the truly royal art?

Young Socrates
No other.

Stranger
So the art of the general is merely ministerial — it serves but isn't itself political.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
Now let's consider the righteous judge.

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
Does the judge do anything beyond deciding whether the dealings between citizens are just or unjust — applying the standards he receives from the king and legislator? He shows his particular excellence only in this: that he's not corrupted by bribes, fear, pity, or any kind of favoritism or hostility into judging contrary to what the lawgiver has established.

Young Socrates
No, that's precisely what his office involves.

Stranger
Then the judge's power isn't royal. It's the power of a guardian of the law — a minister serving the royal power.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
Looking at all these sciences together, the conclusion is clear: none of them is the political or royal science. The truly royal art should not act directly itself, but should rule over those who can act. The king should know what is and isn't the right moment to take the initiative on matters of the highest importance, while others carry out his decisions.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And therefore the arts we've been describing — each concerned with its own specific domain, and having no authority over one another — properly have their own special names corresponding to their particular activities.

Young Socrates
I agree.

Stranger
But the science that oversees them all, that takes responsibility for the laws and for every aspect of the state's affairs, and that truly weaves them all into a single fabric — if we want to capture its essential nature in a name, we'd most accurately call it politics.

Young Socrates
Exactly so.

Stranger
So now that we've identified the various classes in a state, shall I analyze politics using the model that weaving provided?

Young Socrates
I very much wish you would.

Stranger
Then I need to describe the nature of the royal web — how the various threads are woven into one fabric.

Young Socrates
Clearly.

Stranger
This is a difficult task, but it needs to be done.

Young Socrates
It certainly must be attempted.

Stranger
Here's the first challenge: claiming that one part of virtue is fundamentally different in kind from another is a position that's easily attacked by argumentative people who appeal to popular opinion.

Young Socrates
I don't follow.

Stranger
Let me put it differently. You'd consider courage to be a part of virtue?

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And temperance — self-control — is different from courage, but also a part of virtue?

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
I'm going to make a bold claim about these two.

Young Socrates
What claim?

Stranger
That in many ways they're fundamentally opposed to each other — two principles that are hostile to one another across a great deal of nature.

Young Socrates
How strange!

Stranger
Very strange — because everyone usually says that all the parts of virtue are friendly to each other.

Young Socrates
Yes, they do.

Stranger
Then let's look carefully at whether this is universally true, or whether some parts of virtue are actually at war with their own kin.

Young Socrates
Tell me how to investigate this.

Stranger
We need to look at all the things we call beautiful but place into two opposite categories.

Young Socrates
Explain — which things?

Stranger
Sharpness and speed — whether in body, in mind, or in the movement of sound — and the artistic imitations of these qualities in painting and music. You've praised such things yourself, or heard others praise them.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And do you remember the terms in which they're praised?

Young Socrates
Not really.

Stranger
Let me see if I can capture the thought in words.

Young Socrates
Why not?

Stranger
You think it's easy? Well, consider these qualities in relation to the opposite kinds of action. When we praise speed, energy, and sharpness — whether of mind or body or sound — we express our admiration with a single word: we call them courageous, or manly.

Young Socrates
How so?

Stranger
We call an action energetic and brave, or quick and vigorous, or sharp and powerful. When we use this common term of praise — "courageous" — we're admiring all of them.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And don't we also praise the quiet mode of action?

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
And don't we say the opposite of what we said about the other type?

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
We exclaim "How calm! How measured!" — admiring the slow, deliberate working of the mind, steadiness and gentleness in action, smoothness and depth of voice, every rhythmical movement, and music that carries a proper gravity. We use a different term of praise for all of these — not "courageous," but something that conveys orderliness, or temperance.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
But when either quality is out of place, the names flip to terms of blame.

Young Socrates
How so?

Stranger
Too much sharpness or quickness or intensity gets called violent, or crazy. Too much slowness or gentleness gets called cowardly, or sluggish. And here's the crucial observation: for the most part, these qualities — courage and temperance — line up as enemies on opposite sides. They don't mix with each other in actual life. And if we follow this further, we'll find that people who embody these different qualities also differ from each other.

Young Socrates
In what way?

Stranger
In all the ways I just mentioned — and probably many more. People who have an affinity for one type of action distribute their praise and blame accordingly: they praise what resembles them and criticize what's different. And from this, many quarrels arise.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
Now, when this difference between types is about trivial matters, it's no big deal. But when it affects truly important matters in a state, it becomes the most dangerous of all civic diseases.

Young Socrates
What are you referring to?

Stranger
Nothing less than the entire direction of human life. The orderly, temperance-minded people are always ready to lead a peaceful life, quietly minding their own business. They behave this way at home and are just as eager to keep the peace with foreign states. Because of this excessive love of peace — often indulged at the wrong time — when their influence dominates, the whole population gradually becomes unwarlike. They raise their young people to be just like them. They're at the mercy of their enemies. And so within a few years, they and their children and the whole city often slide imperceptibly from freedom into slavery.

Young Socrates
What a terrible fate.

Stranger
And now think about the courageous types. Aren't they always inciting their country to go to war, because of their excessive love of military life? They raise up powerful enemies against themselves, and they either completely ruin their homeland or else reduce it to slavery under foreign rule.

Young Socrates
That's true too.

Stranger
Then we have to admit that where these two types exist, they always feel the most intense opposition and hostility toward each other.

Young Socrates
We can't deny it.

Stranger
And returning to our original question: haven't we found that significant parts of virtue are in fact at odds with each other — and that this opposition shows up in the characters of the people who embody them?

Young Socrates
Yes, it seems so.

Stranger
Let's take this further.

Young Socrates
Where?

Stranger
Does any constructive art — even in the most trivial project — deliberately mix bad materials with good, when it has a choice? Doesn't every art reject the bad as far as possible, keep what's good and suitable, and from these selected elements — whether alike or different — bring them all together to produce something with a unified nature and purpose?

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
Then the true and natural art of statesmanship will never willingly form a state out of a combination of good and bad people — not if it can help it. It will first test human natures through play and education. After testing them, it will entrust them to the appropriate teachers — who serve as its agents — while the art itself retains command and oversight. Just as the art of weaving constantly directs and oversees the carders and everyone else who prepares the material, ordering the supporting crafts to do the work the master art deems necessary for making the fabric.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
In the same way, the royal science — being the mistress of all lawful educators and instructors — won't allow them to train people in ways that produce characters unsuited to the political fabric it wants to create. It will only permit the cultivation of suitable characters. Those who have no share of courage or temperance or any other virtuous disposition — those who are driven by their evil nature into godlessness, arrogance, and injustice — these it removes by death, exile, or the most severe punishments.

Young Socrates
That's commonly said.

Stranger
And those who are wallowing in ignorance and degradation, it yokes into slavery.

Young Socrates
Quite right.

Stranger
As for the rest of the citizens — those from whom education can forge something noble, those who are capable of being blended by a master craftsman — the royal art weaves them together. It takes those whose natures tend toward courage — the stronger element, which serves as the warp — and those who incline toward orderliness and gentleness — the softer element, which serves as the woof. These two types are naturally opposed, and the art seeks to bind and weave them together in the following way:

Young Socrates
How?

Stranger
First, it takes the eternal part of the soul — the divine element — and binds it with a divine cord.

Young Socrates
What does that mean?

Stranger
I mean that true and reasoned conviction about what's honorable, just, and good — and their opposites — when it takes root in the soul, is a divine principle, implanted in a nature that's heaven-born.

Young Socrates
What else could it be?

Stranger
And only the Statesman and the good lawmaker — inspired by the royal muse — can implant this conviction. And they can only do it in people who have been properly educated, the ones we just described.

Young Socrates
That makes sense.

Stranger
As for anyone who can't receive this education — we won't dignify them with any of the titles we've been discussing.

Young Socrates
Right.

Stranger
Now, when the courageous soul takes hold of this truth, it becomes civilized — genuinely capable of participating in justice. Without this truth, it tends toward brutality. Isn't that so?

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And conversely, the peaceful, orderly nature — when it shares in these true convictions — becomes genuinely wise and temperate, at least to the degree possible in public life. Without these convictions, it rightly earns the shameful name of foolishness.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
Now, can we say that this kind of bond — between truth and the soul — will permanently unite the bad with one another, or the bad with the good? Would any genuine science even attempt to use such a bond on bad material?

Young Socrates
Impossible.

Stranger
But in those who were noble from the start, and who have been nurtured in noble ways — and in them alone — this bond is implanted through law. This is the medicine that art prescribes for them. And of all the bonds that unite the naturally opposed parts of virtue, isn't this the most divine?

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
Once this divine bond is in place, the other bonds — the merely human ones — are easy to create.

Young Socrates
What bonds? What do you mean?

Stranger
The bonds of intermarriage — the ties formed when states or families give and receive children in marriage. Most people make marriage connections without thinking about what's actually best for producing good offspring.

Young Socrates
In what way?

Stranger
They chase after wealth and power in marriage — objectives that are hardly worth criticizing seriously, they're so obviously shallow.

Young Socrates
There's no need to even discuss them.

Stranger
But there's better reason to examine the practice of people who make family connections their chief aim — and to point out their error.

Young Socrates
What error?

Stranger
They act on no sound principle whatsoever. They take the easy path: welcoming people who are like themselves and avoiding those who are different, giving too much weight to what feels comfortable.

Young Socrates
How so?

Stranger
The quiet, orderly type seeks out natures like its own and marries within that class as far as possible. And the courageous type does the same — seeking its own kind. But both types should be doing exactly the opposite.

Young Socrates
Why?

Stranger
Because courage, when it goes untempered by a gentler nature for many generations, may start out blooming and strong — but eventually it explodes into outright madness.

Young Socrates
That makes sense.

Stranger
And the soul that's overly full of modesty, with no element of courage, after many successive generations grows too passive — and eventually becomes completely paralyzed and useless.

Young Socrites
That's quite likely too.

Stranger
These are the bonds I said would be easy to create — once both types already share the same true convictions about what's honorable and good. In fact, this single task encompasses the entire work of royal weaving: never allow the temperate natures to be separated from the courageous ones. Weave them together like the warp and the woof — through shared convictions, shared honors, shared reputation, and mutual pledges. From them, form one smooth, even fabric — and to them, entrust the offices of the state.

Young Socrates
How exactly?

Stranger
Where only a single official is needed, choose a ruler who combines both qualities. Where many officials are needed, blend some of each type. Because the temperate ruler is careful, fair, and steady — but lacks drive and decisiveness.

Young Socrates
That certainly seems true.

Stranger
The courageous character, on the other hand, falls short in fairness and caution — but has a remarkable power of action. A city can't thrive in either its public or private life when either quality is missing.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
And this, we declare, is the completion of the political web. The royal science draws courageous and temperate natures together into a single community through shared understanding and friendship, creating the noblest and finest of all the fabrics that political life can produce. It enfolds within this fabric all the other inhabitants of the city — free and slave alike — binding them together under one woven whole. It governs and presides over them, and in every way that happiness is granted to a city, it never fails to secure it.

Young Socrates
Your portrait of the king and statesman, Stranger, is every bit as perfect as your portrait of the Sophist.
