Athenian
What will daily life look like for people whose food and clothing are provided in moderation, who've handed the practice of crafts over to others, and whose farmland — worked by slaves who pay a share of the harvest — brings in enough for a moderate life? Imagine they have common dining halls, with the men eating separately, and nearby, common tables for their families — their daughters and mothers. Each day, male and female officers inspect the diners, observe the company's behavior, and then dismiss them. Afterward, the presiding magistrate and attendants pour libations to whichever gods that day and night are sacred to, and then everyone goes home.

Now, for people whose lives are arranged this way — is there really no important work left to be done? Should each of them just live like a fattened animal? That's neither just nor honorable, and a person who lives like that won't escape the consequences they deserve. The proper fate of an idle, fattened beast is to be torn apart by some other brave animal whose body has been hardened by courageous deeds and honest toil.

Of course, these arrangements will never be perfectly carried out under present circumstances — not as long as women, children, houses, and everything else remain private property. But if we can achieve the second-best form of government, we'll be doing very well. And for people living under this second-best system, there's still a task to accomplish that's far from small or trivial. In fact, it's the greatest task of all, ordained by righteous law.

Here's what I mean: the life that's truly devoted to excellence of both body and soul requires twice — or more than twice — the effort that goes into training for the Pythian or Olympic Games, which already demands a person's full time and attention. There should be no side jobs interfering with the essential work of providing proper exercise and nourishment for the body, and instruction and education for the soul. Day and night aren't long enough to accomplish all of this perfectly. Therefore, all free citizens should organize every hour of their day — from morning to evening and from evening to the next sunrise.

Now, there might seem to be something improper about a legislator dictating the countless details of household management — including something as specific as the duty of staying awake for those who are to be permanent guardians of the whole city. The idea that any citizen should sleep through an entire night instead of being seen by all his servants as the first to wake and rise — whether we call this a law or just a standard of behavior — should be considered disgraceful and unworthy of a free person. Likewise, the idea that the mistress of the house should be awakened by her maids rather than waking them first — this is the kind of thing that every slave, servant, and helper in the house should view as shameful.

If they all rise early, they can get a great deal of both public and private business done — magistrates managing city affairs, and masters and mistresses managing their households — before the sun even comes up. Lots of sleep isn't what nature really requires, either for our souls, our bodies, or the work they do. No one who's asleep is good for anything — they might as well be dead. The person among us who values life and reason most will stay awake as long as possible, setting aside only as much time for sleep as health requires. And that's not much, once the habit of moderation is properly established. Magistrates who keep watch at night are feared by the wicked — whether enemies or their own citizens — and honored and respected by the just and temperate. They're useful to themselves and to the entire state.

A night spent this way, in addition to all the advantages I've mentioned, also instills a kind of courage in the citizens' minds.

When day breaks, it's time for young people to go to their teachers. Now, no animal — not sheep, not any creature — can survive without a shepherd, and children can't be left without tutors, any more than slaves can manage without masters. And of all animals, the boy is the most difficult to handle. The fountain of reason in him isn't yet regulated, making him the most cunning, sharp-witted, and unruly of creatures. He needs to be restrained with many bridles.

First, when he leaves the care of his mother and nurses, he needs tutors to manage him on account of his childishness and foolishness. Then, since he's a free citizen, he needs teachers to instruct him in various subjects. But he's also, in a sense, under the authority of everyone: any free person who comes across him may correct both the boy and his tutor or teacher if any of them is doing something wrong. And whoever encounters such wrongdoing and fails to punish it as it deserves will earn the greatest disgrace. The guardian of the law who serves as director of education should keep a sharp lookout for anyone who encounters these offenses and fails to correct them properly. He should take special care with the training of our children, guiding their natures and always steering them toward what's good, as the law directs.

But how can our law adequately train the director of education himself? So far, everything we've said has been incomplete — nothing has been stated clearly or satisfactorily on this point. Now, as far as possible, the law should leave nothing to his personal discretion but should explain everything, so that he can serve as an interpreter and guide to others.

About dances, music, and choral works, I've already spoken — both about how they should be selected and how they should be revised and consecrated. But we haven't yet addressed, my distinguished director of education, what your students should study in prose. You've been told what military music they should learn and practice. But as for reading and writing, playing the lyre, arithmetic — which, as we were saying, everyone needs to learn — and other subjects required for warfare and the management of households and cities; and beyond that, what's useful to know about the motions of the heavenly bodies — the stars, the sun, and the moon — and the various rules about these matters that the whole state needs for organizing days into months and months into years, so that seasons, sacrifices, and festivals maintain their proper, natural order, keeping the city alert and active, the gods receiving their due honors, and people gaining a better understanding of these things — all of this, my friend, has not yet been adequately explained to you by the legislator.

So pay attention to what I'm about to say. We were telling you that your education in reading and writing was incomplete. The objection was this: you were never told whether a citizen who aspires to be respectable should study these things in detail or not study them at all. The same was true of learning the lyre. But now we're saying: yes, he should study them.

A fair amount of time for a boy of ten to spend on reading and writing is three years. At thirteen, it's the proper time for him to start learning the lyre, and he should continue for another three years — no more, no less. Whether the boy or his father likes or dislikes the subject, he's not to be allowed to spend more or less time on music than the law permits. Anyone who disobeys will be denied the youthful honors we'll describe later.

But first, listen to what young people should actually learn in these early years, and what their teachers should teach them. They should study reading and writing until they can read and write competently. But if nature hasn't given them the ability to achieve perfect handwriting or great speed within the allotted years, let it go. No need to force it.

Now, what about written compositions that aren't set to music — prose works, whether in verse without musical accompaniment or in plain prose with no rhythm or melody at all? We've inherited a great many writings from authors of this kind, and some of them are quite problematic. What are we going to do with them, my excellent guardians of the law? How can the lawgiver properly guide you here? I think he'll find himself in considerable difficulty.

Cleinias
What's troubling you, Stranger? Why are you so perplexed?

Athenian
A fair question, Cleinias. To you and Megillus, my partners in this work of legislation, I need to lay out both the harder parts and the easier parts of the task.

Cleinias
What are you getting at in this case?

Athenian
I'll tell you. The problem is opposing the opinion of countless people.

Cleinias
But haven't we already gone against popular opinion on many important matters?

Athenian
That's quite true. And what you're implying is that the path we're taking may be unpopular with some, but is welcome to just as many others — or if not as many, then at least to people who are no less worthy. You're telling me to press forward along this legislative road that our discussion has opened up, to take heart and not falter.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
And I won't falter. Here's the thing: we have a great many poets writing in hexameter, trimeter, and every other meter — some serious, some just trying to get a laugh. And the universal opinion is that young people who receive a proper education should be immersed in this poetry, saturated with it. Some people insist they should be constantly hearing it read aloud, always memorizing it, learning entire poets by heart. Others select the best passages and long speeches and compile anthologies, saying these should be committed to memory if a person is to become good and wise through broad learning.

And now you want me to tell all these people plainly where they're right and where they're wrong.

Cleinias
Yes, I do.

Athenian
But how can I sum up my judgment on all of them in a single statement? I think — and if I'm not mistaken, there's general agreement — that every one of these poets has written some things well and some things badly. And if that's true, then I maintain that too much of this kind of learning is actually dangerous for young people.

Cleinias
So how would you advise the guardian of the law to act?

Athenian
In what respect?

Cleinias
What standard should he follow when deciding what the young should learn and what they should be kept from learning? Don't hold back — just tell us.

Athenian
Well, my good Cleinias, I think I'm actually rather fortunate.

Cleinias
How so?

Athenian
I'm not entirely without a standard to offer. When I think back over the words we've spoken from early this morning until now — words that I believe have been inspired by heaven — they seem to me very much like a poem. When I reflected on everything we've said, I felt a natural pleasure, because of all the discourses I've ever learned or heard, whether in poetry or prose, this one seemed to me the most balanced, the most fitting for young people to hear. I can't imagine a better standard than this for the guardian of the law who also serves as director of education.

He can't do better than to instruct the teachers to teach young people these very words, and anything else of a similar nature he might find — whether in poetry, in prose, or even in unwritten discussions that share our spirit. He should make sure to preserve such works and commit them to writing. First, he should require the teachers themselves to learn and approve of these materials. Any teacher who won't should be dismissed. Only those who share his judgment should be employed and entrusted with the instruction and education of our youth.

And with that, let my little digression about reading, writing, and their teachers come to an end.

Cleinias
I don't think, Stranger, that we've strayed outside the proper scope of our discussion. But whether our whole approach is right, I can't say with complete certainty.

Athenian
The truth, Cleinias, will probably become clearer when — as we've said many times — we reach the end of our entire discussion about laws.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Now that we've dealt with the teacher of reading and writing, it's time to give our instructions to the teacher of the lyre.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
I think we only need to recall our earlier discussions to give suitable guidelines for this part of instruction and education.

Cleinias
What are you referring to?

Athenian
We were saying, if I remember correctly, that the sixty-year-old members of the chorus of Dionysus should have an especially keen sense of rhythm and musical composition, so they can distinguish between good and bad imitation — that is, imitation of good character versus bad character when under the influence of strong feeling. They should reject the one and display the other in hymns and songs, using them to charm young people's souls and invite them to pursue virtue through the power of imitation.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
With this in mind, the teacher and the student should use the sounds of the lyre because its notes are pure. The player and the student should stay in unison, rendering note for note together. But complexity and variation — where the strings produce one sound while the poet or composer creates another, or where they combine smaller and larger intervals, fast and slow tempos, high and low notes into elaborate harmonies — or again, where they create complex rhythmic variations layered over the lyre's notes — all of that is not suitable for students who need to gain a practical and useful knowledge of music in three years. Mixing opposite principles creates confusion and makes learning difficult. And our young people need to learn quickly — their essential studies are neither few nor trivial, as we'll show in due course.

Let the director of education follow the musical principles we're laying down. As for the songs and lyrics themselves that the chorus masters should teach — their content and character — we've already covered that. They're the same ones which, once consecrated and adapted to the different festivals, we said would benefit the cities by providing wholesome enjoyment.

Cleinias
That too is true.

Athenian
Then let whoever has been elected as director of music take these rules from us as the genuine truth, and may he succeed in his duties! Now let's move on to lay down additional rules beyond the ones already given, about dancing and physical training in general. We've covered what remained to be said about music education — so let's address gymnastics in the same way. Boys and girls both should learn to dance and do gymnastic exercises, shouldn't they?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Then boys should have male dance instructors and girls should have female dance instructors to train them.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
Then let's call in once again the person with the greatest responsibility here — the superintendent of youth, that is, the director of education. He'll have plenty on his plate, being in charge of both music and gymnastics.

Cleinias
But how will an elderly man manage such enormous responsibilities?

Athenian
Oh, don't worry, my friend. The law has already given — and will continue to give — him permission to choose any citizens, male or female, as his assistants. He'll know whom to choose, and he'll be careful not to make a mistake, both from a proper sense of responsibility and from awareness of how important his role is. He'll understand that if young people have been well brought up, everything goes smoothly. But if not — well, it's not fitting to describe what happens, and we won't, in case we seem to be speaking ill omens over our infant state.

We've already said a great deal about dancing and general physical movements — since we include under gymnastics all military exercises: archery, throwing weapons, using the light shield, all heavy-armed combat, military formations, troop movements, and encampment, plus everything related to horsemanship. There should be public teachers for all of these, paid by the state, and their students should include men and boys, as well as girls and women, in the entire state.

While they're still girls, they should practice armed dancing and the full art of fighting. Once they're grown women, they should train in military formations, tactics, and the proper way to take up and set down arms — if for no other reason, then in case the entire army has to march out of the city for a campaign, so that the women left behind are capable of guarding the children and the rest of the city. And on the other hand, if enemies — whether foreign or Greek — should attack with overwhelming force and push the fighting right to the city itself (which is far from impossible), what a tremendous disgrace it would be if the women had been so poorly trained that they couldn't fight for their children the way birds fight for their young against any predator, no matter how strong — ready to die or face any danger. Instead, what would they do? Rush straight to the temples, crowd the altars and shrines, and bring upon the entire human race the reproach that of all animals, we're the most cowardly!

Cleinias
Such a lack of training, Stranger, would certainly be both shameful and disastrous for any state.

Athenian
Then suppose we go so far as to make it law that women shall not neglect military training, and that all citizens — male and female alike — shall attend to it?

Cleinias
I fully agree.

Athenian
Now, about wrestling — we've discussed it partly, but I'd say the most important part hasn't been covered. And it's not easy to explain without demonstrating the movements physically, combining words with actions. Only when word and gesture come together will we be able to explain clearly that, of all forms of physical training, wrestling is the one most closely related to the military arts. Wrestling should be pursued for the sake of warfare — not warfare for the sake of wrestling.

Cleinias
Excellent.

Athenian
Enough about wrestling. Let's move on to other forms of bodily movement. This kind of motion can generally be called dancing, and it falls into two categories: one that imitates noble figures, and another that imitates base ones. Each of these has two further subdivisions.

Of the serious kind: one form involves people engaged in war and intense action — it's the exercise of a noble person with a courageous spirit. The other depicts a moderate soul enjoying prosperity and modest pleasures — this can truly be called the dance of peace.

The warrior dance is different from the peaceful one and can rightly be called the Pyrrhic. It imitates the ways of avoiding blows and missiles — ducking, giving way, springing aside, rising up, or dropping down. It also imitates the opposite: attacking postures, like the motions of archery, javelin-throwing, and delivering all sorts of strikes. When the imitation is of brave bodies and courageous souls, and the movement is direct and muscular — giving the limbs a straight, strong motion — that's the true form. But the opposite is wrong.

In the dance of peace, what we need to consider is whether a person moves naturally and gracefully, in the manner of people who properly follow the law.

But before going further, I need to distinguish the type of dancing that's in question from the type that isn't. Which kind is doubtful, and how do we tell them apart?

There are Bacchic-type dances — the kind where they say they're imitating drunken men, named after the Nymphs, Pan, Silenus, and the Satyrs. There are also the dances used in purification rites and mystery ceremonies. This whole category of dancing can't properly be classified as either warlike or peaceful — it doesn't really have a clear meaning at all. I think the best thing is to say it's distinct from both the war dance and the peace dance, and not suitable for a city. So let's set it aside.

Leaving that where it lies, let's turn to the dances of war and peace — those are undoubtedly our concern. The peaceful, non-warlike muse that honors the gods and the sons of gods in dance is entirely associated with a sense of well-being and prosperity. This type can be split into two subtypes: one expresses relief — an escape from some hardship or danger into safety, bringing greater pleasure; the other expresses the preservation and increase of existing blessings, with a less intense pleasure.

In all these cases, the greater the pleasure, the more a person moves their body; the less the pleasure, the less they move. Furthermore, if someone is more disciplined and has learned self-control through training, they move less. But if they're a coward with no training or restraint, they make bigger, more violent movements. And in general, when a person is speaking or singing, they can't keep their body completely still — and so from the imitation of words through gestures, the entire art of dancing was born.

In these various kinds of imitation, some people move in an orderly way, others in a disorderly way. And the ancients, who gave many excellent names to things according to their true nature, gave a truly fine name to the dances of people who are moderate in their pleasures during times of prosperity. They called these dances "Emmeleiai" — dances of order. With this, they established two kinds of noble dance: the war dance, which they called the Pyrrhic, and the peace dance, which they called the Emmeleia, the dance of order. Each received its fitting and proper name.

The legislator should outline these categories in general terms. The guardian of the law should then investigate them in detail, combining dancing with music and assigning to each sacrificial festival what's appropriate. Once he's consecrated everything in proper order, he should make no further changes — whether to the dances or the songs. From that point on, the city and its citizens will continue to enjoy the same pleasures, being as alike as possible in their tastes, and will live well and happily.

I've now described the dances appropriate to noble bodies and generous souls. But we also need to consider and understand the opposite: ignoble characters and thoughts — the kind meant to produce laughter in comedy, with a comic character in their style, song, and dance. Here's the thing: serious matters can't be understood without laughable ones. You can't understand any quality without understanding its opposite — not if you really want to grasp either one. But you can't practice both if you want to have any degree of virtue.

This is exactly why people should learn about comic imitation: so they can avoid doing or saying anything ridiculous or inappropriate out of ignorance. But the actual performance of such things should be left to slaves and hired foreigners. No one should ever take a serious interest in performing comedy, and no free man or woman should be caught making an effort to learn it. There should always be some element of novelty in the comic imitation.

So let these be our regulations — both legal and advisory — for the laughable entertainment we generally call comedy.

Now, what if the serious poets — the ones who write tragedy — come to us and say, "Strangers, may we come to your city and country? May we bring our poetry with us? What's your decision?"

How shall we answer these divine artists? I think our answer should go like this: "Most excellent strangers," we'll say, "we too are poets of a kind — tragic poets, in fact. And our tragedy is the finest and noblest. Our entire state is itself an imitation of the best and noblest life, which we believe is the truest tragedy of all. You are poets, and so are we — creators of the same art, rivals and competitors in the noblest of dramas, the kind that only true law can bring to perfection. So don't expect us to casually allow you to set up your stage in the marketplace, or to let your actors' fine voices ring out above our own, or to let you address our women and children and the general public about our institutions in language different from ours — and often the very opposite of ours. A state would have to be out of its mind to grant you that kind of license before its magistrates had reviewed your poetry and determined whether it was fit for public performance. So, sons and heirs of the gentler Muse: first show your songs to the magistrates. If they find your work is the same as ours, or better, we'll give you a chorus. But if not — well, my friends, we can't."

Let these, then, be the customs established by law regarding all forms of dance and their teaching. And let matters concerning slaves be kept separate from those concerning free citizens — if you don't object.

Cleinias
We can't possibly object when you put it that way.

Athenian
There are still three areas of study appropriate for free citizens. Arithmetic is one. The measurement of length, area, and depth is the second. The third concerns the motions of the stars in relation to one another.

Not everyone needs to work through all these subjects with strict scientific rigor — only a select few, and we'll say who they are later, at the proper point. But for everyone else, not knowing what's necessary for general human knowledge — not knowing the truth of these things — is a disgrace. And yet diving deep into the details isn't easy or even possible for everyone.

There's something in these subjects, though, that's genuinely necessary and can't be set aside. The person who first coined the proverb "not even God can fight against necessity" probably had this in mind — divine necessity, that is. Because when ordinary people use that phrase about mere human necessities, the application is completely ridiculous.

Cleinias
And what kind of necessary knowledge is divine rather than human, Stranger?

Athenian
The kind without which no one can be a god, or a demigod, or a hero who could genuinely guide humanity — or even take serious responsibility for human affairs. A divine being would hardly be one who couldn't count to three, or distinguish odd from even, or count at all, or reckon night from day, or who knew nothing about the motions of the sun, moon, and other heavenly bodies.

It would be absurd to imagine that any of this isn't essential knowledge for someone who aspires to the highest forms of understanding. But exactly which subjects, how many, and when they should be learned — and what should be studied together versus separately, and how they all relate to one another — all of this must be properly grasped first. Then, using these as guides, we can proceed to everything else. That's how natural necessity works — the kind we say no god fights against, or ever will.

Cleinias
I think, Stranger, that what you've just said is very true and consistent with nature.

Athenian
Yes, Cleinias, it is. But it's difficult for the legislator to begin with these subjects. We'll make detailed regulations for them at a more suitable time.

Cleinias
You seem to be worried about our habitual ignorance of these matters, Stranger. But that's no reason to hold back from speaking your mind.

Athenian
I'm certainly concerned about those difficulties. But I'm even more afraid of people who study these subjects badly. Complete ignorance isn't such a terrible or extreme evil, and it's far from the worst thing that can happen. Much worse is excessive cleverness and too much learning combined with a poor upbringing — that's far more destructive.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Every free citizen should learn at least as much of these subjects as every child in Egypt is taught when learning the alphabet. In Egypt, they've invented arithmetic games for little children, which they learn as a pleasure and amusement. They have them distribute apples and garlands — using the same number for larger and smaller groups of people. They arrange boxers and wrestlers into pairs, whether matched by lot or left over from the draw, and show how their turns come in natural order. Another way they teach is by distributing cups — sometimes gold, sometimes brass or silver, sometimes mixed — and through this, they work the principles of everyday arithmetic into children's play. This makes students far more useful to themselves in practical life and far more alert. It also helps with measurements of length, width, and depth — freeing us from that natural ignorance of these things that's so laughable and embarrassing.

Cleinias
What kind of ignorance do you mean?

Athenian
Oh, my dear Cleinias — I, like you, only learned late in life just how ignorant we are about these matters. To me, we seem more like pigs than humans in this regard. I'm thoroughly ashamed — not just of myself, but of all Greeks.

Cleinias
About what? Tell us what you mean, Stranger.

Athenian
I will — or rather, I'll show you what I mean through a question. Please answer me: you know what length is?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And breadth?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
And you know that these are two distinct things, and that there's a third called depth?

Cleinias
Obviously.

Athenian
And doesn't it seem to you that all of these are measurable against each other?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
That is: length is naturally measurable against length, breadth against breadth, and depth against depth?

Cleinias
Undoubtedly.

Athenian
But what if some things are measurable against each other while others are completely incommensurable — and you believe they're all commensurable? Where does that leave you?

Cleinias
Clearly in a very bad position.

Athenian
Now consider: when we compare length and breadth with depth, or breadth and length with each other — don't all Greeks assume these can always be measured against one another?

Cleinias
Quite true.

Athenian
But if they're actually incommensurable in some cases, and yet all of us treat them as commensurable — don't we have reason to be ashamed of our fellow citizens? Couldn't we say to them: "You fine Greeks, isn't this exactly the kind of thing we were saying it's disgraceful not to know — and yet merely having a surface knowledge of it is nothing to be especially proud of?"

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And there are related matters where similar errors crop up.

Cleinias
What are they?

Athenian
The relationship between commensurable and incommensurable quantities. A person of any worth should be able to think through and distinguish these. Different people should challenge each other with these kinds of problems — it's a far better and more elegant way to spend their time than playing checkers as old men do.

Cleinias
I'd say you're right — and these mathematical challenges aren't so different from a game of checkers, in a way.

Athenian
Well, Cleinias, I maintain that these are the studies our young people should pursue. They're harmless, they're not difficult, and learning them will be enjoyable — and they'll benefit the state. If anyone disagrees, let them make their case.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Then if these studies hold up to scrutiny, we'll include them. If not, we'll drop them.

Cleinias
Agreed. But can't we now, Stranger, formally prescribe these studies as required and fill in this gap in our laws?

Athenian
Let's consider them provisional pledges that can be taken back and removed from our system if they don't satisfy either us, the givers, or you, the recipients.

Cleinias
A fair condition.

Athenian
Next, let's consider whether we're willing to require our young people to study astronomy.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
Here we run into something truly bizarre — something that absolutely cannot be tolerated.

Cleinias
What are you referring to?

Athenian
People say that we shouldn't investigate the nature of the supreme God or the workings of the universe, that we shouldn't busy ourselves searching out the causes of things, and that such inquiries are impious. But the exact opposite is true.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
What I'm saying might seem paradoxical and out of step with what you'd expect from someone my age. But when a person has a good and true idea that benefits the state and is in every way acceptable to God, they can't keep quiet about it.

Cleinias
That sounds reasonable enough. But will we actually find any good and true ideas about the stars?

Athenian
My friends, at this moment, all of us Greeks are telling lies — if I can put it that way — about those great gods, the Sun and the Moon.

Cleinias
What kind of lies?

Athenian
We say that the sun, the moon, and various other heavenly bodies don't follow the same path — that they wander. We even call them "planets," which means "wanderers."

Cleinias
That's very true, Stranger. In the course of my own life, I've often seen the morning star, the evening star, and various others not following their usual courses but wandering all over the sky. And I've seen the sun and the moon doing what we all know they do.

Athenian
Exactly, Megillus and Cleinias. And I maintain that our citizens and our young people should learn enough about the heavenly gods to be able to offer sacrifices and pray to them in reverent language — and not blaspheme against them.

Cleinias
You're right about that — provided such knowledge is actually attainable. If we're currently speaking incorrectly and could be better informed and learn to use better language, then I fully agree that we should acquire enough knowledge to speak properly. So please, try to explain your whole meaning, and we'll do our best to follow you.

Athenian
There's some difficulty in understanding what I mean, but it's not enormous, and it won't take a great deal of time. Here's my proof: I didn't know these things long ago or in my youth, and yet I can explain them to you fairly quickly. If they were truly hard, I certainly couldn't explain all of them — old man to old men — in any reasonable time.

Cleinias
True. But what is this knowledge you describe as wonderful and fitting for young people to learn, though we're ignorant of it? Try to explain it as clearly as you can.

Athenian
I will. You see, my friends, the common belief about the "wandering" of the sun, the moon, and the other heavenly bodies is not the truth — it's the very opposite of the truth. Each of them follows a single path — not many paths, but one, which is circular. The apparent variety in their movements is just that: apparent.

What's more, we're wrong to think the fastest of them is the slowest, or the slowest is the fastest. Now, if what I'm saying is correct, just imagine we had the same confusion at the Olympic Games about runners. Suppose we called the fastest runner the slowest and the slowest the fastest, and praised the loser as if he were the winner. Our praise would be false, and it certainly wouldn't be welcome to the runners — even though they're only human. But to make the same mistake about the gods — what was merely ridiculous and wrong in the case of humans becomes something far worse, doesn't it?

Cleinias
Worse than ridiculous, I'd say.

Athenian
At the very least, the gods can't be pleased with us spreading a false account of them.

Cleinias

That covers our laws about education and learning. But now we need to turn our attention to hunting and similar pursuits. The legislator's duty turns out to extend beyond mere law-making. There's something that lies in a region between advice and law, and it's come up repeatedly in our discussion — for example, in the education of very young children, where there are things that can't be precisely defined, and treating them as formal legislation would be absurd.

Now, with our laws and the whole framework of our state laid out, the praise of a virtuous citizen isn't complete when we describe them merely as someone who serves and obeys the laws better than anyone else. The higher praise describes the good citizen as someone who passes through their whole life uncorrupted, obedient to the words of the legislator — both when he prescribes laws and when he assigns praise and blame. This is the truest commendation a citizen can receive.

The real legislator should not only write laws but should also weave in everything he considers honorable and dishonorable. And the ideal citizen should strive to uphold these standards no less than the legal rules backed by punishments.

Let me give an example to make my meaning clear — a kind of evidence for what I'm saying. Hunting is a broad subject that covers many things. There's hunting creatures in the water, hunting creatures in the air, and a great deal of hunting land animals of every kind — and not just wild beasts. The hunting of human beings also deserves attention: there's hunting people in war, and there's often a hunting of people through friendship — which can be either praised or blamed. Then there's the "hunting" done by thieves and robbers, and that of armies against armies.

Now, the legislator laying down laws about hunting can neither ignore all these varieties nor create detailed threatening ordinances with rules and penalties covering every one of them. What's he supposed to do? He should praise and blame different kinds of hunting with an eye toward the exercise and development of young people. And the young person, in turn, should listen obediently. Neither pleasure nor pain should deter them from following the legislator's commendations and instructions rather than the penalties he imposes by law.

With that understood, here comes our moderate praise and censure of hunting — praising the kind that makes young people's souls better, censuring the kind that has the opposite effect.

So let's address our young people directly, as a kind of prayer for their well-being: "Dear friends," we'll say to them, "may no desire or passion for sea-fishing, angling, or catching water creatures ever take hold of you — whether you're awake or asleep — by hook or by net, that lazy method. And may no desire for capturing human beings — through piracy at sea — ever enter your souls and make you cruel, lawless hunters. As for the temptation of stealing in the city or countryside, may it never even cross your mind. And may the sneaky art of bird-catching, which is hardly worthy of free people, never appeal to any young person among you."

What remains for our citizens, then, is only the hunting of land animals. And here, the kind that involves sleeping in shifts while hunting by night — where the hunters are basically lazy — is not to be commended. Nor is the kind that relies on rest between bouts of activity, where the wild strength of beasts is overcome by nets and traps rather than by the victory of a hardworking spirit.

The only kind of hunting that's truly approved is the best kind: hunting four-footed animals with horses, dogs, and one's own body, where the hunters pursue their quarry by running it down, striking it, and hurling weapons at it with their own hands. Those who care about developing godlike courage should practice this kind of hunting personally.

The praise and blame assigned to all these activities has now been declared. And let the law be as follows: no one shall prevent these sacred hunters from pursuing the chase wherever and however they wish. But the hunter who relies on nets and snares at night shall not be allowed to hunt anywhere. The bird-hunter in the mountains and wild places shall be permitted, but on cultivated land or consecrated wilderness, he shall not — and anyone who encounters him may stop him. As for the hunter in waters: he may hunt anywhere except in harbors, sacred streams, marshes, or pools — with the single requirement that he not pollute the water with poison.

And now we can say that all our regulations about education are complete.
