STATESMAN (Part 1)

Persons of the dialogue:
  Theodorus
  Socrates
  The Eleatic Stranger
  Young Socrates


Socrates
I owe you a real debt of thanks, Theodorus, for introducing me to both Theaetetus and our visitor from Elea.

Theodorus
And before long, Socrates, you'll owe me three times as much — once they've finished working out the definition of the Statesman and the Philosopher for you, on top of the Sophist.

Socrates
Sophist, statesman, philosopher! My dear Theodorus, are my ears hearing right? Is that really how the great mathematician and geometer ranks them — all at the same value?

Theodorus
What do you mean, Socrates?

Socrates
I mean you're treating them as equals, when in reality they're separated by a distance no geometric ratio could express.

Theodorus
By Ammon, god of Cyrene — that's a fair hit, Socrates, and it shows you haven't forgotten your geometry! I'll get you back for that another time. But for now, let me ask our visitor — who I hope won't tire of being so generous with us — to take up whichever he prefers next, the Statesman or the Philosopher.

Stranger
That's my obligation, Theodorus. I've started, so I can't leave the job unfinished. But what should we do about Theaetetus?

Theodorus
What do you mean?

Stranger
Should we give him a break and swap in his companion, Young Socrates, instead? What do you think?

Theodorus
Yes, rotate them in, as you suggest. Young people always do better with rest breaks.

Socrates
I think, Stranger, that both of them could be called relatives of mine in some way. One of them, as you've pointed out, has the same ugly face I do. The other shares my name. And we should always be on the lookout for family resemblances in conversation. I was talking with Theaetetus yesterday and just now listened to him answer your questions. But my namesake I haven't examined yet — that'll have to wait for another time. Today let him answer you.

Stranger
Very well. Young Socrates, do you hear what the elder Socrates is proposing?

Young Socrates
I do.

Stranger
And you agree?

Young Socrates
Absolutely.

Stranger
Since you have no objection, I certainly can't either. After the Sophist, then, the Statesman naturally comes next in our investigation. So tell me — should the statesman also be counted among those who possess a kind of knowledge?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
Then we'll need to divide the branches of knowledge, as we did before?

Young Socrates
I'd think so.

Stranger
But the division won't be along the same lines.

Young Socrates
How will it differ?

Stranger
We'll cut at a different point.

Young Socrates
All right.

Stranger
Where do we find the path to the Statesman? We need to locate it, separate it out, and mark it clearly, while putting a different label on every diverging path. That way the mind can conceive of all forms of knowledge under two main classes.

Young Socrates
Finding that path is your job, Stranger, not mine.

Stranger
True, Socrates, but once it's found, it belongs to both of us.

Young Socrates
Fair enough.

Stranger
Now, aren't arithmetic and certain related disciplines purely abstract knowledge — completely separate from action?

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
But in carpentry and all the other manual crafts, the craftsman's knowledge is embedded in his work. He doesn't just know things — he also makes things that didn't exist before.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Then let's divide all branches of knowledge into two groups: those that are purely theoretical, and those that are practical.

Young Socrates
Let's treat these as two divisions of knowledge, which is one unified whole.

Stranger
Now, are "statesman," "king," "master of a household," and "slave-owner" all the same thing? Or is there a distinct skill corresponding to each of these titles? Actually, let me put it another way.

Young Socrates
Go ahead.

Stranger
If someone in private life has the expertise to advise one of the public physicians, shouldn't he also be called a physician?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And if someone in private life is capable of advising the ruler of a country, wouldn't we say he possesses the same knowledge the ruler ought to have?

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And the knowledge of a true king is the royal science?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
So anyone who possesses this knowledge — whether he happens to hold power or is just a private citizen — would properly be called "royal," purely on the basis of his expertise?

Young Socrates
He certainly should be.

Stranger
And a household manager and a master amount to the same thing?

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
And a large household can be compared to a small state — as far as governance goes, is there any real difference?

Young Socrates
None at all.

Stranger
Then, returning to the point we were just discussing, it's clear that there's one science covering all of these. Call it royal, political, or household management — we won't quarrel about the name.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
This much is also clear: a king doesn't maintain his rule primarily through manual labor or physical strength. He does it through the intelligence and power of his mind.

Young Socrates
Obviously.

Stranger
So would we say the king has more in common with theoretical knowledge than with manual crafts and practical life in general?

Young Socrates
Definitely.

Stranger
Then we can treat statesmanship and the statesman as identical with the royal science and the king.

Young Socrates
Clearly.

Stranger
And now we'll be proceeding correctly if we subdivide the sphere of theoretical knowledge?

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
See if you can find a natural joint in it — a dividing line.

Young Socrates
What kind of division do you have in mind?

Stranger
Something like this: remember how we identified the art of calculation?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
That's unmistakably a branch of theoretical knowledge?

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And the only function we'd assign to calculation is judging the differences between numbers — it doesn't do anything beyond that?

Young Socrates
How could we say otherwise?

Stranger
Now, you know that a master builder doesn't work with his own hands — he directs the workers.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
He contributes knowledge, not manual labor.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
So he can fairly be said to participate in theoretical knowledge?

Young Socrates
Quite right.

Stranger
But unlike the mathematician, he shouldn't consider his job done once he's made a judgment. He has to assign the individual workers their specific tasks and keep directing them until the work is complete.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
So all such knowledge is theoretical, just like arithmetic. But there's a difference between the two types: one merely judges, while the other also directs.

Young Socrates
That seems right.

Stranger
So can we say that all theoretical knowledge divides into two branches — one that judges and one that directs?

Young Socrates
I'd say so.

Stranger
And when people work together, isn't it desirable that they be of one mind?

Young Socrates
Very much so.

Stranger
Well, as long as we agree between ourselves, we needn't worry about what others think.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
Now, in which of these divisions do we place the king? Is he a judge and spectator? Or do we assign him to the directive branch — since he's a ruler?

Young Socrates
The directive branch, clearly.

Stranger
Then we need to see whether the art of directing can be further divided. Here's what I'm thinking: there's a distinction similar to the one between a manufacturer and a retailer, and it separates the king from a herald.

Young Socrates
How so?

Stranger
Well, doesn't a retailer receive goods that others have already produced and sell them on?

Young Socrates
He certainly does.

Stranger
And similarly, a herald receives orders from above and passes them on to others?

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
Then should we lump the king's art together with the arts of the herald, the interpreter, the boatswain, the prophet, and all the similar arts that involve passing along commands? Or — just as we earlier distinguished manufacturers who sell their own products from retailers — should we recognize that supreme rulers issue their own commands? The class of "self-directing rulers" barely has a name, but that's where kings belong. Let's assign them to a science of supreme direction, or self-direction, and leave the others for someone else to name. We're looking for the ruler, not the messenger.

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
So we've drawn a solid line between the person who gives his own commands and the person who passes along someone else's. Now let's see if supreme authority allows further division.

Young Socrates
By all means.

Stranger
I think it does — help me with this.

Young Socrates
Where should we cut?

Stranger
Can we say that all rulers give commands for the sake of producing something?

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And it's not hard to divide the things produced into two classes.

Young Socrates
How would you divide them?

Stranger
Of all things produced, some are living and some are not.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And using this distinction, we can subdivide the directive branch of knowledge.

Young Socrates
At what point?

Stranger
One part directs the production of lifeless things, the other the care of living ones. That gives us our division.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Good. We can set one half aside and pick up the other — which can itself be divided into two.

Young Socrates
Which half do you mean?

Stranger
The one that exercises authority over animals, of course. The royal science isn't like being a master builder overseeing lifeless materials. The king has a nobler function — the management and care of living beings.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And the care of living creatures sometimes involves tending individuals, and sometimes managing creatures in groups — in herds?

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
But the statesman isn't a keeper of individual animals — not like a groom who tends a single horse. He's more like the manager of an entire herd of horses or cattle.

Young Socrates
Yes, I see what you mean.

Stranger
Should we call this art of tending many animals together "herd management" or "collective care"?

Young Socrates
Either one — whichever comes up naturally in conversation.

Stranger
Good instinct, Socrates. If you keep being relaxed about terminology, you'll be richer in wisdom when you're old. Now, leaving the name aside — can you see how we might divide herd management in two, so that what we're looking for in twice as large a field can be sought in half the space?

Young Socrates
I'll try. It seems to me there's one kind of management for humans and another for animals.

Stranger
You divided them boldly and directly, and I respect that. But you've fallen into an error we should avoid going forward.

Young Socrates
What error?

Stranger
You shouldn't slice off one small piece and set it against many larger ones. The division should be into genuine classes. It's always better to cut through the middle. You jumped to "humans" because you could see it would get us to our destination, and that made you rush. But you shouldn't chip off too small a piece, my friend. The safer way is to cut down the middle, and that's more likely to find real categories. This principle makes all the difference in any investigation.

Young Socrates
What do you mean, Stranger?

Stranger
I'll try to be clearer, out of appreciation for your sharp mind. I can't explain myself completely right now, but I'll try as we go along.

Young Socrates
What exactly was our mistake in the division?

Stranger
It was like this: imagine someone wanted to divide the human race and did it the way people do around here — splitting off "Greeks" as one category and lumping all the countless other peoples, who have nothing in common and don't even speak the same language, under the single label "barbarians." Because they share one name, they're supposed to be one species. Or imagine dividing numbers by cutting off "ten thousand" as one class and treating everything else as another — just because you've given it one name. You'd get a much better, more balanced division if you split numbers into odd and even, or the human race into male and female. You should only separate off specific groups like Lydians or Phrygians when you can't divide any further into parts that are also genuine classes.

Young Socrates
Very true. But I wish the distinction between a mere part and a true class could be made even clearer.

Stranger
Excellent Socrates, you're asking me something really difficult! We've already wandered further from our original topic than we should, and you want us to wander even further. Let's get back on track for now, and we can follow up on that another time. But I do want to make sure you heard me correctly —

Young Socrates
About what?

Stranger
That a class is always a part, but a part isn't necessarily a class. That's the view I'd want you to always attribute to me.

Young Socrates
Done.

Stranger
Now there's something else I'd like to clarify.

Young Socrates
What's that?

Stranger
Where exactly we went off track. If I'm not mistaken, it was when I asked how you'd divide herd management, and you answered a bit too quickly that there were two kinds of animals: human beings and everything else.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
I noticed that you carved off one part and assumed the remainder was a class, just because you could call all non-human animals "brutes."

Young Socrates
That's right.

Stranger
Now imagine, most courageous of dialecticians, that some intelligent creature — say, a crane — made the same kind of division. Cranes would be one distinguished species, and all other animals, including humans, would be lumped together as "brutes." That's the kind of error we need to avoid.

Young Socrates
How do we stay safe?

Stranger
By not dividing the whole class of animals in one stroke.

Young Socrates
We shouldn't try to take the whole thing at once?

Stranger
That's where we went wrong before.

Young Socrates
How?

Stranger
Remember, the branch of directive knowledge that dealt with living creatures had to do with animals in herds?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
Well, that already implied a division of all animals into tame and wild. Animals whose nature allows them to be tamed we call tame; those that can't be tamed we call wild.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And the political science we're searching for deals — and has always dealt — exclusively with tame animals, and specifically with those that live in groups.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
So we shouldn't divide by grabbing the whole class at once. And let's not be in too big a hurry to reach political science. That kind of haste has already brought on us the misfortune the proverb warns about.

Young Socrates
What misfortune?

Stranger
Too much haste, too little speed.

Young Socrates
Fair enough, Stranger — we got what we deserved.

Stranger
All right, let's start fresh and try to divide the collective care of animals properly. Completing the argument carefully will probably show you what you're so eager to learn. Tell me —

Young Socrates
What?

Stranger
Have you ever heard of — or maybe you've actually seen — the fish preserves in the Nile and the ponds of the Great King of Persia? Or similar fish ponds closer to home, in wells?

Young Socrates
Yes, I've seen them myself, and I've heard plenty of descriptions of the others.

Stranger
And you've heard about the goose and crane nurseries on the plains of Thessaly, even if you haven't traveled there?

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
I bring this up because here's a new division of herd management: management of water herds and management of land herds.

Young Socrates
There is.

Stranger
And do you agree we should divide collective herd care into these two branches — water and land?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And we surely don't need to ask which branch contains the royal art — that's obvious to everyone.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Anyone could further divide the herds that graze on dry land?

Young Socrates
How would you divide them?

Stranger
Into those that fly and those that walk.

Young Socrates
Exactly right.

Stranger
And where do we look for the political animal? Even a fool could tell he's a pedestrian, a walker.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Now the art of managing walking animals needs to be divided again, like halving an even number.

Young Socrates
Clearly.

Stranger
I can see two paths leading toward the class we're aiming at. One's a shortcut — it chops off a small piece and leaves a large one. The other follows our principle of cutting down the middle, but it's longer. We can take whichever we prefer.

Young Socrates
Can't we take both?

Stranger
Both at once? That's asking a lot! But one after the other — sure.

Young Socrates
Then let's take them in turn.

Stranger
Easy enough, since we're near the end. If we'd been at the beginning or the middle, I'd have pushed back. But since we're close to done, let's start with the longer path while we're fresh. Here's the division. Pay attention.

Young Socrates
Go ahead.

Stranger
Tame walking herd animals divide naturally into two classes.

Young Socrates
On what basis?

Stranger
Some grow horns, and some don't.

Young Socrates
Clearly.

Stranger
Now divide the science of managing walking animals into two corresponding parts and define them. If you try to invent specific names, you'll get tangled up in complexity.

Young Socrates
How should I describe them, then?

Stranger
Like this: the science of managing walking animals divides into one part for the horned herd, and another for the hornless herd.

Young Socrates
Consider it said — everything you've laid out has been well established.

Stranger
The king is clearly the shepherd of a hornless herd.

Young Socrates
That's evident.

Stranger
Shall we break this hornless herd into smaller sections and try to assign the king what's his?

Young Socrates
By all means.

Stranger
Should we distinguish them by split hooves versus solid hooves, or by whether the breed can interbreed with others? You know what I mean.

Young Socrates
What exactly?

Stranger
Horses and donkeys naturally breed with each other.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
But the rest of the tame hornless herd doesn't interbreed.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
And does the Statesman have charge of the mixed-breeding kind or the pure-breeding kind?

Young Socrates
The pure-breeding kind, clearly.

Stranger
I suppose we need to divide this again, as before.

Young Socrates
We do.

Stranger
Now every tame, gregarious animal has been split up, except for two species. I don't think we should count dogs among herd animals.

Young Socrates
Certainly not. But how do we divide the two remaining species?

Stranger
With a measurement that you and Theaetetus should appreciate, since you're students of geometry.

Young Socrates
What measurement?

Stranger
The diagonal — and then the diagonal of the diagonal.

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
How does the human being walk? Isn't our stride like a diagonal whose square equals two?

Young Socrates
Just so.

Stranger
And the stride of the other kind — its square being the square of two, that is, four — could be called the diagonal of our diagonal.

Young Socrates
Yes! I think I almost understand you.

Stranger
And in this division, Socrates, I spot something that would make for another good joke.

Young Socrates
What's that?

Stranger
Human beings have ended up classified alongside the most free and easygoing creature around — and they've been running a race together!

Young Socrates
That is a very odd coincidence.

Stranger
And wouldn't you expect the slowest to finish last?

Young Socrates
Indeed.

Stranger
And here's an even more ridiculous result: the king turns out to be running alongside the herd, neck and neck with the bird-catcher — the man who, of all people, is most at home in the "airy life."

Young Socrates
Quite right.

Stranger
And this proves something we said during our investigation of the Sophist.

Young Socrates
What's that?

Stranger
That the method of logical division plays no favorites. It doesn't rank the great above the small. It always arrives at the truth in its own way.

Young Socrates
Clearly.

Stranger
Now, I won't wait for you to ask — I'll take you by the shorter road to the definition of a king, on my own initiative.

Young Socrates
By all means.

Stranger
Here's what I think we should have done from the start: divide land animals into bipeds and quadrupeds. Since the winged herd — and that alone — ended up in the same class as humans, we should divide bipeds into feathered and featherless. Once that's done and the art of managing humanity is brought to light, it's time to install our Statesman and ruler in his place like a charioteer, and hand him the reins of state — for that office truly belongs to him.

Young Socrates
Excellent. You've paid me the debt — that is, you've completed the argument. And the digression was the interest, I take it.

Stranger
Now then, let's go back to the beginning and link together the whole chain that makes up the definition of the Statesman's art.

Young Socrates
By all means.

Stranger
Here it is: the science of pure knowledge had a part that was the science of directing. From this we derived a further part — self-direction, as distinct from passing along others' commands. A major division of self-direction was the management of living creatures, and this was narrowed further to the management of herds. Within herds, we focused on walking animals. The chief division there was the art of managing hornless walking animals, and this could only be captured by combining three qualifiers — pure-bred, non-interbreeding herds. The only further subdivision is the art of managing human herds — bipeds — and that's what we were looking for. That is both the royal and the political art.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
But have we really accomplished what we claim, Socrates?

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
I mean — have we truly worked this out? There's been a discussion, sure, but it seems to me the investigation isn't perfectly complete. Something is still off.

Young Socrates
I don't follow.

Stranger
Let me try to make the thought clearer for both of us.

Young Socrates
Please do.

Stranger
There were many arts of shepherding, and one of them was the political art, which had charge of one particular herd?

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
And our argument defined this as the art of rearing — not horses or other animals, but human beings collectively?

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
But notice a crucial difference that sets the king apart from all other shepherds.

Young Socrates
What are you referring to?

Stranger
Does any other herdsman have rivals who claim to share in the management of his herd?

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
Think about it: merchants, farmers, food suppliers, athletic trainers, and doctors all compete with the so-called statesmen — the herdsmen of humanity. They all insist that they're the ones who truly rear and manage human beings, and not just ordinary people but the rulers themselves.

Young Socrates
Don't they have a point?

Stranger
They very well might, and we'll examine their claims. But here's what we can be sure of: no one challenges the ordinary herdsman as sole feeder and doctor for his flock. He's also their matchmaker and midwife — no one else knows that part of the job. And he's their entertainer and musician, as far as their natures allow; no one soothes his herd better than he does, whether with his natural voice or with instruments. The same goes for animal keepers in general.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
So given all that, can our definition of the king really be correct and complete? Were we right to single him out from ten thousand other claimants as the shepherd and rearer of the human flock?

Young Socrates
I suppose not.

Stranger
Weren't we right to suspect that although we may have sketched a rough outline of the royal form, we haven't yet produced a precise portrait of the Statesman? We can't reveal him as he truly is until we've separated him from all those who crowd around him and claim to share his authority.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
And that's exactly what we must do, Socrates, if we don't want to disgrace our argument at the finish line.

Young Socrates
We certainly can't let that happen.

Stranger
Then let's make a fresh start and travel by a different road.

Young Socrates
What road?

Stranger
Let's mix in a bit of entertainment. There's a famous tale, and a good portion of it will serve our purposes nicely. Then we can go back to our systematic divisions and follow the old path to our destination. Shall we?

Young Socrates
By all means.

Stranger
Then listen to a story that a child would love to hear. And you're not too old for a bit of childish amusement.

Young Socrates
Let's hear it.

Stranger
There really did happen — and will happen again — something like the many marvels preserved in ancient tradition. I'm thinking of the portent associated with the quarrel of Atreus and Thyestes. You've heard about it and remember what they say happened?

Young Socrates
I suppose you mean the golden lamb?

Stranger
No, not that — the other part of the story. The one about how the sun and stars once rose in the west and set in the east, and how a god reversed their course and gave them the motion they have now, as a sign confirming the rights of Atreus.

Young Socrates
Yes, there's that legend too.

Stranger
And we've also heard many times about the reign of Cronus.

Young Socrates
Yes, many times.

Stranger
And the tradition that people in former ages were born from the earth, rather than being born from each other?

Young Socrates
Yes, that's another old story.

Stranger
All these stories, and ten thousand others even more astonishing, spring from the same origin. Most of them have been lost over the ages or survive only in fragments. But the source of them all has never been properly explained — and it's worth explaining now, because the tale sheds light on the nature of the king.

Young Socrates
Wonderful — please tell the whole story and don't leave anything out.

Stranger
Listen, then. There is a time when God himself guides the universe and helps turn it on its course. And there is a time, at the completion of a certain cycle, when he lets go. The universe, being a living creature endowed with intelligence by its maker, then turns around by its own inherent nature and revolves in the opposite direction.

Young Socrates
Why does it do that?

Stranger
Because only the most divine things remain forever unchanged and constant, and the physical universe isn't in that category. The heavens and the cosmos, glorious as they are — having been richly gifted by the Creator — still partake of bodily nature, and so they can't be entirely free from change. But their motion is as uniform and steady as possible; it takes the form of a reversal, which is the smallest possible alteration. Only the lord of all moving things can truly move by himself. And to say that he sometimes pushes the universe one way and sometimes the other would be impious. So here's the picture: sometimes the universe is guided by a divine external power, and it receives fresh life and a kind of immortality from the Creator's renewing hand. Other times, when it's released, it moves on its own. It can do this for immense cycles of time because of its perfect balance, vast size, and the fact that it turns on the tiniest possible pivot — it reverses direction and runs the other way.

Young Socrates
That's a very reasonable account.

Stranger
Now let's think about what follows from this and grasp the phenomenon that explains all these wonders. It's this:

Young Socrates
What?

Stranger
The periodic reversal of the universe's motion.

Young Socrates
How does that explain everything?

Stranger
Of all possible changes in the heavenly motions, this is the greatest and most complete.

Young Socrates
I'd imagine so.

Stranger
And it would naturally produce the greatest changes for the beings living in the universe at the time.

Young Socrates
That makes sense.

Stranger
And we know that living creatures don't survive well when hit by many dramatic changes all at once.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
So a massive destruction necessarily follows — wiping out most of the human race as well. The few survivors are then subject to a series of remarkable experiences, the most notable of which occurs during the transition to the cycle opposite the one we're living in now.

Young Socrates
What experience is that?

Stranger
First, every living thing stopped aging. Mortal nature ceased to grow older — in fact, it reversed. Everything began growing younger and softer. The white hair of the elderly darkened again. The cheeks of bearded men grew smooth and regained their youthful bloom. The bodies of young men in their prime grew smaller and softer, returning day by day and night by night toward the condition of a newborn child — in mind as well as body. Then they continued to waste away until they vanished entirely. And those who had died by violence at the time of the reversal passed through these same changes even faster, disappearing within days.

Young Socrates
But then how were animals created in that era, Stranger? How did they reproduce?

Stranger
They didn't reproduce from each other — that's the point. There was no procreation as we know it. Instead, there was the earthborn race we hear about in legend — people who rose up out of the ground. Our earliest ancestors, who lived closest to the end of the previous cycle and the beginning of this one, are the ones who passed down these traditions to us. And notice how the rest of the story fits together: after age returns to youth, the dead who lie in the earth return to life. The wheel of generation turns backward along with the reversal of the cosmos, and the dead are reassembled and rise again, living in the opposite direction — unless a god has taken some of them away to a different fate. That's why they're called "earthborn," and that's where the legend comes from.

Young Socrates
Yes, that all fits perfectly. But tell me — the blessed, spontaneous life you described under the reign of Cronus — did that belong to the previous cycle or this one? Because the reversal of the stars and sun would have occurred in both.

Stranger
Good — you're following me closely. No, that blessed and effortless life doesn't belong to our current cycle. It belongs to the previous one, when God oversaw the entire revolution of the universe. The various regions of the world were each assigned to subordinate divine beings, the way some places still have local gods today. These demigods were the shepherds of each species and herd of animals, each one providing everything his flock needed. There was no violence, no creatures devouring one another, no war, no conflict of any kind. I could list ten thousand other blessings of that era. The reason human life was said to be "spontaneous" is this: God himself was their shepherd and ruled over them directly, just as humans — who are comparatively godlike — still rule over the lower animals. Under his care, there were no governments, no private ownership of wives and children. Everyone was born from the earth with no memory of the past. They didn't need any of that. The earth gave them fruit in abundance — growing on trees and bushes without being planted or cultivated. They lived naked, mostly outdoors, because the climate was always mild. They had no beds but lay on soft couches of grass that sprang up plentifully from the ground. That was human life in the age of Cronus, Socrates. Our current life, which is said to be under Zeus — well, you know that from your own experience. So which era would you say was the happier one?

Young Socrates
Impossible to say.

Stranger
Want me to decide for you, as best I can?

Young Socrates
Please do.

Stranger
Well, suppose the nurslings of Cronus, with all that boundless leisure and the ability to converse not just with each other but with animals too — suppose they used all those advantages for philosophy, talking with beasts and with one another, and learning from every creature that had some special ability or insight to contribute to the store of wisdom. In that case, it would be easy to see that they were a thousand times happier than people today. On the other hand, if they just ate and drank until they were stuffed, and told stories to each other and to the animals — the kind of stories still attributed to that age — well, that answer would be easy too. But until someone can tell us whether the people of that era loved knowledge and discussion, we'd better let the question drop and get back to the point of the story.

In the fullness of time, when the change was due, and the earthborn race had all passed away, and every soul had completed its appointed number of rebirths and been sown into the earth the required number of times — then the Pilot of the universe let go of the helm and withdrew to his place of observation. At that moment, Fate and the universe's own innate tendency reversed its motion. All the subordinate deities who shared in governing the world, understanding what was happening, also let go of the regions under their control.

And the universe, turning around with a sudden jolt — driven in the opposite direction from beginning to end — was shaken by a tremendous earthquake that caused a new wave of destruction among all living things. Afterward, when enough time had passed, the chaos and confusion and trembling subsided. The great living creature found its calm and settled back into its own orderly course, taking charge of itself and everything within it, carrying out the instructions of its Father and Creator as best it could remember them — more precisely at first, but with diminishing accuracy over time.

The reason for this decline was the element of matter in its nature — the legacy of its original primordial state, which had been full of chaos before achieving its present order. From God the Creator, the world received everything good in it. But from its former condition came seeds of evil and disorder, which the world first absorbed and then passed on to the animals within it.

As long as the divine Pilot was helping nurture the animals, evil was small and good was abundant. But after the separation, when the world was left on its own, at first things went well enough. But as time passed, the forgetting grew worse and worse. The old discord regained its hold and eventually burst forth in full force. Good became scarce and evil overwhelming, until there was a danger of the world and everything in it being destroyed in the storm and sinking into infinite chaos.

That's when God — the orderer of all things — in his tender care, seeing the world in dire straits and fearing that everything might dissolve into chaos, sat down once more at the helm. He gathered up the elements that had fallen into dissolution and disorder, restored the motion that had prevailed under his guidance, set everything back in order, and made the world deathless and immortal once more.

And that is the complete tale. But the first part is what we need for understanding the king.

When the world turned toward our current cycle, the aging process resumed. The tiny creatures that had nearly disappeared began growing again. The newly born children of the earth grew gray, died, and sank back into the ground. Everything changed to match the condition of the universe, necessarily following its pattern in conception, generation, and growth. No animal was any longer brought into being through divine agency — the world was now its own master, and all its parts were meant to grow, reproduce, and sustain themselves under the same self-governing principle.

And so we've arrived at the real purpose of this story. There would be much more to say about the lower animals and the conditions from which they changed, but about human beings, there's less to say — and it's more to the point. Deprived of God's care — the God who had possessed and tended them — humans were left helpless and defenseless. They were torn apart by wild beasts, which had become fierce now that they were no longer under divine control. In those early ages, humans had no skills and no resources. The food that once grew on its own had failed, and they didn't know how to produce their own, because they'd never had to. Pressed by every kind of necessity, the gifts that tradition tells us the gods gave to humanity were bestowed — along with whatever teaching and education was essential. Fire came from Prometheus. The crafts came from Hephaestus and his partner Athena. Seeds and plants came from other gods. From all of these, human civilization was built. For divine care had failed, as I was saying, and people had to manage their own affairs, to be their own masters — just like the universe they imitate, forever changing, living and growing now one way, now another.

Enough of the story. Now let's see how it helps us understand where we went wrong in our earlier portrait of the king and statesman.

Young Socrates
What was the great error you're talking about?

Stranger
There were two. The first was smaller, the second was much larger and grander.

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
When we were asked about a king and statesman of our present age, we actually described the shepherd of the human flock from the other cycle — a being who was a god, when he should have been a man. That was the greater error. The lesser error was that we declared him ruler of the entire state without spelling out how he rules. That wasn't completely wrong, but it wasn't clear or complete either.

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
So before we can have a perfect portrait of the statesman, we need to define the nature of his office.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
And the myth was introduced for two reasons: not just to show that all others are rivals of the true shepherd — the one we're searching for — but also to give us a clearer picture of the one person who deserves the title. He alone among shepherds and herdsmen, in keeping with our analogy, has the care of human beings.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
And I can't help thinking, Socrates, that the divine shepherd from the age of Cronus is on a much higher level than any king. The statesmen of our own time are much more like their subjects — much closer to them in nature, upbringing, and education.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
Still, we need to investigate them all the same — to see whether they stand above their subjects or on the same level.

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
Let's pick up where we were. Remember, we spoke of self-directed authority exercised over animals — not individually but collectively — which we called the art of herd management?

Young Socrates
Yes, I remember.

Stranger
That's where we went wrong. We never actually included the Statesman — he had no place in our classification.

Young Socrates
How did that happen?

Stranger
All other herdsmen "rear" their herds. But "rearing" isn't quite the right term for what the Statesman does. We need a word that applies to all of them.

Young Socrates
True, if such a word exists.

Stranger
Well, isn't "care" applicable to all? "Caring for" herds implies no specific activity like feeding — it's broad enough. We could say "tending," "managing," or "having the care of" herds, and the same word would cover everyone, including the Statesman. That seems to be what the argument requires.

Young Socrates
Exactly right. But what's the next step in the division?

Stranger
The same as before. We divided the art of "rearing" herds by land versus water, winged versus walking, mixed-breeding versus pure-breeding, horned versus hornless. We can now divide the art of "caring for" herds along the same lines, covering both the kingship of today and the rule of Cronus.

Young Socrates
That's clear. But I'm still asking — what comes next?

Stranger
If we'd used the word "managing" instead of "feeding" or "rearing" from the start, no one could have argued that there's no care for humans in the case of the statesman. The objection was fair — plenty of people have a better claim to feeding humans than any king does. But no other art has a better claim to the overall care of human society and the right to rule people in general than the royal science.

Young Socrates
Quite true.

Stranger
Now, Socrates, we have to recognize another major error at the end of our analysis.

Young Socrates
What was it?

Stranger
Even if we were absolutely certain that an art of rearing bipeds existed, we had no right to immediately call it the royal or political art, as if the job were done.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
As we were saying, we first needed to rename it — to shift from "feeding" to "caring" — and then to divide further. There may be significant divisions still to make.

Young Socrates
What kinds of divisions?

Stranger
First, separating the divine shepherd from the human guardian.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
And the art of human guardianship itself needs to be subdivided.

Young Socrates
On what principle?

Stranger
Voluntary versus compulsory.

Young Socrates
Why?

Stranger
Because we made a crucial mistake earlier — we were too naive. We lumped the king and the tyrant together, when they're completely different, just as their modes of government are completely different.

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
So now let's correct that and divide the care of human beings into two parts: voluntary and compulsory.

Young Socrates
Agreed.

Stranger
And if we call the compulsory management of people by violent rulers tyranny, and the voluntary management of willing herds of willing bipeds politics — then can we say that the person who possesses this art of voluntary management is the true king and statesman?

Young Socrates
I think so, Stranger. It seems like we've finally completed our account of the Statesman.

Stranger
That would be nice, Socrates. But I have to satisfy myself as well as you. And in my judgment, the figure of the king isn't finished yet. Like sculptors who get carried away and make parts too big in their rush, then have to waste time cutting them back down — we too, partly from haste, partly from a grand desire to expose our earlier mistake, and partly because we thought a king deserved grand illustrations — we've taken up a huge lump of myth and had to use more material than was necessary. The story never quite came to an end, and yet our discussion could be compared to a painting that's been outlined nicely but hasn't yet received the life and vividness that come from blending colors. Of course, for intelligent people, a living being is better portrayed in words and argument than in any painting or sculpture.

Young Socrates
Very true. But what imperfection still remains? Tell me.

Stranger
The highest ideas, my friend, can hardly be expressed without using examples. Every person seems to know everything in a dreamy, half-conscious way, and then wakes up and realizes they know nothing.

Young Socrates
What do you mean?

Stranger
I'm afraid I've awkwardly raised a question about our experience of knowledge.

Young Socrates
Why awkwardly?

Stranger
Because my "example" of examples itself needs an example.

Young Socrates
Go ahead — don't worry about tiring me out.

Stranger
Good — you're an eager listener. Think about when children are first learning their letters.

Young Socrates
What about it?

Stranger
They can recognize individual letters perfectly well in short, easy syllables, and identify them correctly.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
But when those same letters show up in other, harder syllables, the children get confused. They misidentify them — they think and speak incorrectly about them.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
Now, isn't the best and easiest way to teach them the letters they don't yet know —

Young Socrates
To do what?

Stranger
To first take them back to cases where they correctly identified those same letters, and then set those cases side by side with the ones they got wrong — showing them that the letters are exactly the same in both combinations, and always have the same character and the same name. Once you've lined up all the cases they got right alongside all the cases they got wrong, you've given them examples. And through these examples, they learn that each letter is always itself, always the same, regardless of where it appears.

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger
So this is how examples work: we take something, compare it with a distinct instance of the same thing that we already understand correctly, and out of the comparison, a single true concept emerges that covers both cases.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
Is it any wonder, then, that the mind has the same kind of trouble with the alphabet of reality? Sometimes, in simple cases, it grasps the truth firmly. But then, when those same elements show up in the long and difficult syllables of actual experience, it gets confused all over again.

Young Socrates
Nothing surprising about that.

Stranger
And could anyone who starts from a false belief ever hope to arrive at even a small portion of truth and attain wisdom?

Young Socrates
Hardly.

Stranger
So you and I won't be far off if we try to understand the nature of "example" itself through a small, specific instance — and then move from lesser things to the grandest class, the royal class, and use systematic method to discover what the management of cities really is. That way our dream will become reality.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
So let's return to our earlier argument. Since there are countless rivals of the royal class who claim to have the care of states, we need to separate all of them from the true statesman. And as I was saying, we first need a model — an example of this process.

Young Socrates
Exactly.

Stranger
What model is small enough to work with, yet has a real analogy to the political art? How about this, Socrates: if we can't find anything better, let's choose weaving — specifically, the weaving of wool. That should be enough to illustrate our point without dragging in all of weaving.

Young Socrates
That works.

Stranger
Then let's apply to weaving the same process of division and subdivision we've been applying to everything else, moving as quickly as we can through the steps until we reach what we need.

Young Socrates
How do you mean?

Stranger
I'll show you by actually doing it.

Young Socrates
Very good.

Stranger
Everything we make or acquire is either creative or preventive. Preventive things include remedies — both divine and human — and also defenses. Defenses include military weapons and protective gear. Protective gear includes screens of various kinds, and shields against heat and cold. Shields against heat and cold include shelters and coverings. Coverings include blankets and garments. Garments are either single pieces or assembled from multiple parts. Among the assembled kind, some are stitched and some aren't. Among the unstitched, some are made from plant fibers and some from hair. Among the hair-based ones, some are cemented together with water and earth, and others hold together on their own. These last — defenses and coverings that are held together by themselves — we call clothes. And the art that produces them, taking its name from the nature of the work, we can call the art of clothing. Just as we derived "statesmanship" from the state, so the art of weaving — at least the largest part of it, which deals with making clothes — differs only in name from this art of clothing. Just as, in the previous case, the royal science differed only in name from the political science.

Young Socrates
Exactly right.

Stranger
Now let's notice something: the art of weaving clothes has been separated from several other arts in the same family, but not yet from the arts that cooperate with it.

Young Socrates
Which family arts do you mean?

Stranger
I see I've lost you. Let me back up and start from the end. Just now we separated the weaving of clothes from the making of blankets — the difference being that one is put around you and the other under you. Those are the "family arts" I meant.

Young Socrates
I understand.

Stranger
And we've also set aside all articles made from flax and cord, and everything I metaphorically called "plant sinews." We've also separated out the process of felting and the various arts of stitching and sewing, the most important of which is shoemaking.

Young Socrates
Precisely.

Stranger
Then we separated the currier's art, which makes coverings from whole pieces of leather, and the art of building shelters. We subtracted the various waterproofing arts used in construction and carpentry, and all the arts that provide barriers against theft and violence — making lids for containers and fixing doors, which are branches of the joiner's art. We also cut off the manufacture of weapons, which is a section of the vast art of making defenses. And at the very beginning, we separated off the entire art of making antidotes and magical remedies. What we're left with, apparently, is the very art we were looking for: the art of protection against cold weather, which fabricates woolen defenses, and which we call weaving.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
But that's not the whole story, my boy. Because the first step in working with the material is the opposite of weaving.

Young Socrates
How so?

Stranger
Weaving is a kind of joining together, right?

Yes.

Stranger
But the first step is separating the tangled, matted fibers.

What do you mean exactly?

I mean carding. We can't say that carding is weaving, or that the carder is a weaver.

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

And if someone called the art of making the warp and the woof "the art of weaving," they'd be saying something false and paradoxical.

Young Socrates
Of course.

Stranger
And what about fulling and mending? Do they have nothing to do with the care and treatment of clothes? Or should we call them weaving?

Young Socrates
Certainly not.

Stranger
And yet all these arts will insist that they're involved in the production and treatment of clothes. They'll contest weaving's claim to exclusive authority — conceding weaving the larger role, but reserving a substantial one for themselves.

Young Socrates
Very true.

Stranger
Besides all these, there are the arts that make the tools and instruments of weaving. They too will claim to be at least cooperative causes in every piece of weaving.

Young Socrates
Absolutely.

Stranger
So if we simply define weaving as the greatest and noblest of the arts concerned with woolen garments — is that definition complete? It's true, but isn't it unclear? Don't all those other arts need to be cleared away first?

Young Socrates
True.

Stranger
Then our next step is to separate them out, so the argument can proceed in an orderly way.

By all means.

Let's start by noting that there are two kinds of arts involved in everything we do.

What two kinds?

A very reasonable distinction.

So the arts that make spindles, combs, and other tools for producing clothes are cooperative, while those that treat and fabricate the clothes themselves are causal.

Very true.

Among the causal arts, the work of washing, mending, and all the other preparatory treatments can be grouped together under what we call the fuller's art — it's a division of the broader art of adornment.

Very good.

Carding, spinning, and all the processes involved in actually manufacturing a woolen garment make up a single art that everyone recognizes — the art of working in wool.

Of course.

Carding, half the work of the comb, and the other processes that separate fibers apart — all of these belong both to wool-working and to one of two universal arts: the art of combination and the art of division. Specifically, they belong to division.

Young Socrates
Yes.

Stranger
The art of dividing wool and yarn — done one way with the comb and another way by hand — goes by various names, but it all falls under this category.

Very true.

Done.

We must.

By "twisting," do you mean making the warp?

There's no other way.

Young Socrates
Perfectly put.

I thought there was nothing useless in what you said, Stranger.

Let's do that.

Stranger

Length and shortness, excess and deficiency — the art of measurement deals with all of these.

Yes.

Stranger

Where do you draw the line?

What do you mean?

Yes.

Young Socrates
Clearly.

Stranger

Stranger

True.

Stranger

Young Socrates
Certainly.

Stranger

Very true.

Stranger

Young Socrates
Yes, we absolutely need to do that again.

Stranger

Young Socrates
What?

Stranger

Young Socrates
True. So what's next?

Stranger

In one part, place all the arts that measure number, length, depth, breadth, and speed against their opposites. Young Socrates

Stranger

They also make the opposite mistake: dividing things that should be grouped together. The right method is this: when you've first perceived the unity of things, keep investigating until you've found all the real differences within it that constitute distinct classes. And conversely, when you see a multitude of different things, don't rest until you've grasped everything they have in common and gathered them into a single real category. Young Socrates
We won't forget.

Stranger

Young Socrates

Stranger

Young Socrates
All words, clearly.

When you want to satisfy a questioner's soul about such things, you can't hold anything up for the eye to see — you can only present them through thought and reason. Very good.
