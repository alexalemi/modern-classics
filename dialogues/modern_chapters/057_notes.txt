TRANSLATION NOTES — Laws Book 11 (Chapter 057)

OVERVIEW
--------
Laws Book 11 is the most "legal code" section of the entire Laws. It reads like
a legislative manual — covering property disputes, buried treasure, slavery
regulations, retail trade, contracts, wills, orphan guardianship, family law,
poison and sorcery, theft and violence, insanity, verbal abuse, comedy
censorship, begging, animal liability, witnesses and perjury, and corrupt
advocacy. The challenge was finding the human drama underneath the legal
minutiae, and the underlying vision of human nature that motivates every rule.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS
----------------------------

1. OPENING GOLDEN RULE: Jowett's "Thou shalt not, if thou canst help, touch
   that which is mine" is deliberately archaic-biblical even in the Victorian.
   Modernized to "don't touch what's mine, and don't take even the smallest
   thing that belongs to me without my permission" — keeping the simplicity
   and directness without the King James flavor.

2. BURIED TREASURE SECTION: The Athenian's opening monologue is essentially a
   prayer-like statement of ethical principles before the actual law. Preserved
   the meditative, first-person quality ("May I never pray the Gods...") while
   making it read naturally. The key philosophical point — "justice in the soul
   is a better possession than wealth" — is Plato's moral psychology in miniature,
   so it needed to land clearly.

3. TREASURE-TROVE MAXIMS: "Move not the immovables" and "Take not up that which
   was not laid down by thee" — kept these as quoted sayings (in quotes) since
   the Athenian presents them as proverbial wisdom. Modernized the second to
   "Don't pick up what you didn't put down."

4. SLAVERY SECTIONS: Translated faithfully without euphemism or commentary,
   per the text_analysis guidelines. The laws on slaves, freedmen, and their
   property rights reflect Athenian legal reality. The section on recapturing
   runaway slaves, freedman obligations, and property limits for foreigners
   is presented straightforwardly.

5. RETAIL TRADE: This is one of the most interesting sections philosophically.
   The Athenian's thought experiment — "what if we forced the best people to
   run taverns?" — is surprisingly modern. The portrait of the innkeeper who
   "treats [travelers] like prisoners of war at his mercy" is vivid and angry.
   Preserved the energy of this passage, which is some of the most emotionally
   charged writing in the Laws.

6. ADULTERATION AND MARKETPLACE FRAUD: The "one price only" rule and the
   physical punishments (one lash per drachma) are striking. Kept these
   concrete and visceral rather than softening them.

7. CONSUMER PROTECTION ON SLAVE SALES: The detailed rules about selling
   slaves with undisclosed medical conditions (tuberculosis, epilepsy, kidney
   stones) read remarkably like modern consumer protection law, with different
   standards for expert-to-expert vs. expert-to-layperson sales. Modernized
   the disease names where possible: "consumption" → "tuberculosis,"
   "disease of the stone" → "kidney stones," "strangury" → "urinary disease."

8. WILLS AND INHERITANCE: The dying man's speech — "By the gods! How monstrous
   that I'm not allowed to give my own property to whoever I want!" — is one
   of the most human moments in the Laws. The Athenian's response, speaking
   as the lawgiver to the dying citizen, is genuinely moving: "creatures of a
   day" who can't really know what's theirs. Gave this passage full emotional
   weight.

9. ORPHAN GUARDIANSHIP: The Athenian's appeal to supernatural enforcement —
   the dead watching over their children, the gods caring about orphans — is
   more earnest than anything Socrates would say. This reflects the Laws'
   distinctive tone: less ironic, more spiritually invested. Preserved the
   genuine warmth and seriousness.

10. FATHER-SON CONFLICTS: The disownment procedure is fascinatingly democratic —
    a family vote, majority required, the accused gets to speak. The insanity
    proceedings are surprisingly compassionate: the son must consult the
    guardians before acting, and they serve as both witnesses and advocates.

11. DIVORCE AND REMARRIAGE: Translated "incompatibility of temper" as
    "hopelessly incompatible." The committee of twenty (ten guardians + ten
    marriage-overseeing women) trying to reconcile couples, then matching them
    with "deeper, gentler natures" if reconciliation fails, is oddly charming.

12. PARENTAGE AND STATUS: The rules about children of mixed-status unions
    (slave/free) are harsh by modern standards. Translated faithfully per
    guidelines.

13. HONORING PARENTS: The comparison of living parents to statues of the gods —
    "no statue is more powerful in granting his prayers than those elderly
    parents sitting right there at his hearth" — is beautiful and theologically
    interesting. The examples of divine curses (Oedipus, Amyntor, Theseus)
    ground the argument in mythological tradition.

14. POISON AND SORCERY: The Athenian's distinction between physical poisoning
    and magical/psychological harm is fascinating — he's essentially agnostic
    about whether magic works, but legislates against it anyway because of the
    fear it causes. "Wax figures nailed to their doors" at crossroads and on
    graves is wonderfully specific and creepy.

15. PUNISHMENT PHILOSOPHY: The passage on why we punish — "not because he did
    wrong, for what's done can never be undone, but so that in the future, he
    and everyone who sees him corrected will utterly hate injustice" — is one
    of the clearest statements of deterrence theory in ancient philosophy.
    The metaphor of law as "a skilled archer" aiming at the right punishment
    is compact and powerful.

16. MADNESS AND VERBAL ABUSE: The psychological insight that angry insults
    "make savage again the part of his soul that education once civilized" is
    pure Platonic moral psychology — the tripartite soul from the Republic
    applied to everyday social behavior.

17. COMEDY CENSORSHIP: The law on comic poets connects directly to Republic
    Books II-III. The distinction between humor in jest vs. in earnest, and
    the requirement for official licensing, is presented without editorial
    softening. The superintendent of education as arts censor is a striking
    detail.

18. BEGGING: "So the country may be cleared of this sort of creature" —
    Jowett's "animal" becomes "creature," which is harsh but faithful. This
    is one of the Laws' more uncomfortable passages.

19. CORRUPT ADVOCACY: The final section is remarkably harsh — death for a
    citizen who practices law dishonestly for money, and death for repeated
    combative litigation. The observation that justice "has been the great
    civilizer of humanity" but its advocates have a "terrible reputation"
    resonates across millennia.

STYLE AND TONE
--------------
- The Athenian's voice throughout is that of a wise elder legislator — less
  playful than Socrates, more earnest and administrative, but with flashes of
  real passion (the innkeeper passage, the dying man's speech, the orphan
  protection).
- Cleinias has relatively few lines in this book. His interjections ("What do
  you mean?" "In what way?" "Like what?") serve to break up the Athenian's
  extended monologues and signal topic transitions. Kept these brief and natural.
- Megillus is addressed once near the end ("This is what we must do, Megillus
  and Cleinias") but doesn't speak. This is typical of the later books of the
  Laws, where Megillus recedes.
- Modern contractions used throughout for conversational flow.
- Legal content kept specific and concrete rather than abstracted.

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY
-----------------------
- "Guardians of the law" — consistent throughout (Plato's nomophylakes)
- "Wardens of the agora/city/country" — consistent (agoranomoi, astynomoi, etc.)
- "Metic" / "resident alien" — used interchangeably with explanation on first use
- "The 5,040 households" — kept as-is, the number is essential to Magnesia's design
- "Court of select judges" — consistent
- "Tribal courts" / "courts of the tribes" — consistent
- "Drachma," "mina," "obol" — kept as Greek monetary terms

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES (058 — Laws Book 12)
-----------------------------------------
- Book 12 is the final book of the Laws and of the entire Platonic corpus
- Key topics: the Nocturnal Council (a body of wise elders who guard the laws),
  military law, foreign travel restrictions, final theological reflections
- The conclusion is abrupt but not unfinished — maintain the sense of a work
  that ends mid-thought without the desperation of the Critias
- The Athenian's tone should carry the weight of finality — this is Plato's
  last word
- Continue using "Athenian" as speaker label
- Megillus may speak more in Book 12 — keep his Spartan practicality distinct
  from Cleinias's Cretan curiosity
- The Nocturnal Council is philosophically rich — it's Plato's final attempt
  at institutional wisdom, a body that combines practical governance with
  philosophical understanding
- Watch for the emotional register: this is an old man completing his life's
  work in political philosophy
