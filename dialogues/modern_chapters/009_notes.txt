Gorgias Part 2 — Translation Notes
=====================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's sentences in this section are extraordinarily
   long — some of Socrates' monologues run 200+ words in a single sentence
   with multiple nested subordinate clauses. These have been broken into
   shorter, punchier sentences while preserving the logical chain. The long
   summary monologue near the middle (where Socrates recaps the argument
   while Callicles refuses to participate) is kept as a continuous speech
   but restructured with short internal sentences and a question-and-answer
   rhythm Socrates plays with himself.

2. CALLICLES' BIG SPEECH: The centerpiece of this section — Callicles'
   manifesto that "luxury and intemperance and licence... are virtue and
   happiness." This was carefully modernized to be genuinely persuasive
   and seductive before Socrates dismantles it. Key choices:
   - "allow his desires to wax to the uttermost" → "let his desires grow
     as large as possible and never hold them back"
   - "courage and intelligence to minister to them" → "courage and the
     intelligence to satisfy every one of them"
   - "luxury and intemperance and licence, if they be provided with means,
     are virtue and happiness" → "luxury and excess and freedom, when backed
     by power — that's virtue, that's happiness"
   - The speech builds to a real crescendo. Callicles is allowed to sound
     dangerous and compelling — a genuine philosophical adversary, not a
     straw man.

3. CALLICLES' GROWING FRUSTRATION: This is a key dramatic element. His
   hostility escalates through the chapter:
   - Early: dismissive ("You talk about meats and drinks and physicians
     and other nonsense")
   - Middle: openly hostile ("I couldn't care less about a word you're
     saying. I've only been answering this whole time to be polite to Gorgias")
   - Late: refusing to engage ("You shall judge for yourself" / "Can't you
     finish it on your own?")
   - Final capitulation: still hostile but resigned ("Fine, go ahead. Let's
     get this finished.")
   Each stage of frustration is preserved and sharpened. The reader should
   feel the conversation becoming genuinely uncomfortable.

4. THE LEAKY JARS IMAGE: Socrates' parable of the two men with storage
   jars — one with sound jars, one with cracked ones — is central to the
   pleasure-vs-good argument. Modernized for clarity but kept the concrete
   imagery. The philosophical point (that the uncontrolled life is one of
   perpetual unfulfilled wanting) comes through the vivid picture.

5. THE ITCHING/SCRATCHING PASSAGE: Socrates' reductio ad absurdum of
   Callicles' hedonism — if all pleasure is good, then a life spent
   scratching an itch would be the good life, and so would worse things.
   The escalation from scratching to the catamite example is kept intact,
   including Callicles' outrage. This is Socrates at his most provocative.

6. THE PLEASURE ≠ GOOD ARGUMENT: The logical sequence proving that
   pleasure and good can't be identical (because pleasure and pain can
   coexist simultaneously, while good and bad cannot) is preserved step
   by step. Every "yes" and "certainly" matters — they're Callicles
   committing to positions that will trap him.

7. CALLICLES' RETREAT: His admission that "some pleasures are good and
   others bad" is a major turning point — he's abandoned his original
   position. His accusation that Socrates "grabs things like a child"
   is a beautiful moment of frustration. Kept the childishness accusation
   and Socrates' wounded-yet-strategic reply.

8. THE STATESMEN PASSAGE: The extended argument about Pericles, Cimon,
   Miltiades, and Themistocles is historically rich and philosophically
   crucial. Socrates' argument: if these men were good leaders, their
   citizens should have become better, not worse. But all of them were
   eventually punished by the very citizens they led. The animal-handler
   analogy (you'd call someone a bad trainer if the animals got wilder)
   is devastating. Kept all the historical detail.

9. THE DOCTOR AND THE CHILDREN: Socrates' analogy for his own trial —
   a doctor prosecuted before a jury of children by a cook — is one of
   the most famous passages in the Gorgias and eerily prophetic of his
   actual trial. Modernized for clarity but preserved the vivid scenario.

10. THE MYTH OF JUDGMENT: The closing myth about Zeus reforming the
    judgment of the dead is vivid storytelling. Key decisions:
    - Zeus speaks in direct quotation, conversationally
    - The imagery of naked souls being examined for scars of injustice
      is kept vivid and concrete
    - The distinction between curable and incurable souls is clear
    - The references to Homer (Tantalus, Sisyphus, Tityus, Thersites)
      and to Aristeides are preserved
    - The tone shifts from philosophical argument to genuine myth-telling

11. THE CLOSING EXHORTATION: Socrates' final speech is one of the great
    passages in Plato — he turns Callicles' own challenge back on him
    and delivers a ringing call to the life of justice. The line about
    "the great contest — the contest of life" is powerful. Preserved the
    emotional weight and the structural parallel with Callicles' earlier
    reproaches.

ARCHAIC VOCABULARY MODERNIZED:
  - "wax to the uttermost" → "grow as large as possible"
  - "chastise" → "discipline" / "punish"
  - "intemperance" → "excess" / "lack of discipline" / "self-indulgence"
  - "I dare say" → "I'd imagine" / "I suppose" / "I'm sure"
  - "hitherto" → "this whole time"
  - "mob-orator" → "demagogue"
  - "word-catching" → "playing word games"
  - "faint from want of soul" → "faint from lack of nerve"
  - "civility to Gorgias" → "to be polite to Gorgias"
  - "adduce" → "bring up"
  - "wiseacre" → "brilliant advisor" (ironic)
  - "catamite" → kept as-is (the word is clear enough and historically apt)
  - "laconising set who bruise their ears" → "pro-Spartan crowd who bash
    their ears up wrestling" (added brief explanation of the ear-bruising
    reference to Spartan wrestling)
  - "the Prytanis" → "the chief magistrate" (with context)
  - "Foxton" (Jowett's rendering of Alopece) → "Alopece" (standard name)

GREEK OATHS/EXCLAMATIONS KEPT:
  - "by the gods"
  - "by Zeus"
  - "by heaven"
  - "by the god of friendship"

CULTURAL REFERENCES:
  - Epicharmus quote about "two men spoke before" — kept with attribution
  - Amphion and Zethus reference — kept as-is (the meaning is clear from
    context: Callicles gave his "Zethus" speech earlier, Socrates would
    reply with an "Amphion")
  - Euripides quote about life and death — kept in verse format
  - Sicilian/Italian philosopher (probably Empedocles or a Pythagorean) —
    identified as "some clever thinker, probably Sicilian or Italian"
  - The Thessalian sorceresses who "drag down the moon" — kept as vivid
    cultural color
  - Homer's underworld punishments (Tantalus, Sisyphus, Tityus) — kept
  - Thersites — kept (the contrast between powerless and powerful sinners
    is self-explanatory)
  - Aristeides son of Lysimachus — kept with "famous across all of Greece"
  - The Mysian reference — kept ("Call yourself a Mysian peasant")

SPEAKER LABELS:
  - Gorgias briefly returns to intervene when Callicles tries to quit.
    This is a wonderful dramatic moment — the elderly master asking his
    young protege to keep going for the sake of the argument.

PHILOSOPHICAL TERMS:
  - "natural justice" — kept (Callicles' term for his might-makes-right view)
  - "temperance" → "self-control" throughout (more accessible)
  - "virtue" / "excellence" — used interchangeably depending on context
  - "the good" vs "the pleasant" — kept as the central distinction
  - "flattery" — kept as the key pejorative for false rhetoric/sophistry
  - "Cosmos" / "Order" — kept with the etymological point made explicit

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (SYMPOSIUM, Part 1)
-------------------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: In the Gorgias he's at his most combative — relentless,
   ironic, occasionally stung by Callicles' contempt but never thrown off
   course. The Symposium requires a dramatically different register:
   lighter, more playful, more generous. Socrates is a guest at a party,
   not a street-corner philosopher in intellectual combat.

2. DIALOGUE FORMAT: The Symposium has nested narration — Apollodorus
   telling what Aristodemus told him about the party. This is different
   from the dramatic format (speaker names on their own line) used in
   the Gorgias. The narration frame needs to be established smoothly.

3. TONE: The Gorgias ends on a solemn, almost prophetic note (the myth
   of judgment, the exhortation to virtue). The Symposium opens in a
   completely different key — conversational, anecdotal, with the framing
   device of people trying to reconstruct what happened at a party years
   ago. The tonal shift should be sharp and deliberate.

4. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "self-control" for sophrosyne (not "temperance")
   - "flattery" as the key term for bad rhetoric
   - "the good" vs "the pleasant" as distinct categories
   - Contractions throughout
   - Greek oaths maintained (by Zeus, by the gods, etc.)

5. CALLICLES' POSITION: His "might makes right" argument and his vision
   of the strong man who satisfies all desires are among the most famous
   positions in ancient philosophy. This has been translated to be genuinely
   compelling. Callicles should be remembered as a formidable opponent,
   not a caricature.

6. THE DOCTOR/COOK ANALOGY: Socrates' prediction of his own trial is
   established here and will resonate with the Apology (chapter 001).
   The image of the doctor tried before children by a cook should be
   recalled if it comes up again.
