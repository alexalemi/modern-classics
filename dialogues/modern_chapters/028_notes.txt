TRANSLATION NOTES — Euthydemus Part 1 (Chapter 028)

OVERVIEW:
The Euthydemus is Plato's great comic dialogue. Two Sophist brothers, Euthydemus
and Dionysodorus, use verbal trickery and logical fallacies to "prove" absurd
conclusions. The comedy relies on deadpan presentation of ridiculous arguments,
Socrates' exaggerated amazement, and the growing exasperation of everyone watching.
The whole thing is narrated by Socrates to his friend Crito, giving it a story-
within-a-story structure.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS:

1. NARRATIVE WRAPPER: Preserved the Socrates-to-Crito framing throughout. This
   wrapper is essential — it lets Socrates editorialize and adds comedic distance.
   Kept it natural and conversational: "Now, Crito — what happened next, how can
   I possibly do it justice?"

2. COMIC TONE: The Sophists' arguments are presented with full deadpan seriousness.
   The humor comes from the absurdity being treated as profound wisdom. Kept
   Dionysodorus's whispered asides ("I'm going to prophesy something for you,
   Socrates — whichever way he answers, he's going to get refuted") as gleeful
   and conspiratorial.

3. THE FAN CLUB: Euthydemus and Dionysodorus have a group of followers who
   laugh and cheer on cue. Translated them as a "fan club" with "chorus on cue
   from their director" to capture the theatrical absurdity.

4. SOCRATES' MOCK AMAZEMENT: Socrates' ironic praise of the Sophists is the
   comic engine of the dialogue. Phrases like "My God!", "Good heavens!", "I can
   see why you have such a reputation among your students" — all delivered with
   straight-faced faux admiration.

5. CTESIPPUS: The hot-headed young boyfriend of Cleinias who actually gets angry
   at the Sophists. He provides the emotional contrast to Socrates' ironic calm.
   Kept his outbursts raw and genuine: "how dare you tell such a disgusting lie."

6. CORYBANTES PASSAGE: Socrates compares the Sophists' word tricks to mystery
   religion initiation rites. Kept the reference but made the analogy clear —
   it's Socrates sarcastically saying "this is just the warm-up ritual, the real
   stuff is coming." Also preserved the Prodicus reference about correct use of
   terms.

7. SOCRATES' OWN DEMONSTRATION: The middle section where Socrates shows how
   a real philosophical exhortation works (proving that wisdom is the only true
   good) is played straight — it's the genuine philosophy embedded in the comedy.
   Made the argument clear and step-by-step while keeping conversational rhythm.

8. "CANNOT TELL A LIE" SEQUENCE: The Sophists' argument that lying is impossible
   (because whoever speaks says what is, and what is is true) is one of the most
   famous sophistic arguments. Presented it with complete logical clarity so the
   reader can follow each step and see exactly where the trick is.

9. "CONTRADICTION IS IMPOSSIBLE": Another classic sophistic move. Made each step
   of the argument clear while preserving the deadpan delivery.

10. THE STOOL-PULLING METAPHOR: Socrates compares the Sophists' tricks to yanking
    a stool from under someone about to sit down, then laughing when they fall.
    This perfectly captures Plato's attitude toward the Sophists and I kept it
    vivid.

11. CLEINIAS'S SURPRISING WISDOM: The passage where young Cleinias gives
    startlingly good philosophical answers (the general as hunter, the dialectician
    as the one who uses what others discover) is a lovely dramatic surprise.
    Crito's incredulous interruption ("Wait — the YOUNG MAN said all that?")
    is one of the funniest moments.

VOCABULARY NOTES:
- "pancratiast" (all-around fighter) → explained as "one-stop shopping for combat"
  and "all-around fighter" to make the athletic metaphor clear
- "Corybantes" → kept the name, explained as mystery religion initiation
- "Prodicus" → kept, as he's well known from other dialogues
- "Medea" and "Marsyas" → kept mythological references, which work as jokes here
- "Proteus" → kept, explained briefly as Egyptian sorcerer who shapeshifts
- Greek oaths preserved: "by the gods," "By Zeus," "Poseidon"

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
- Part 1 contains: the frame setup, the Sophists' first round of tricks on Cleinias,
  Socrates' own philosophical demonstration (wisdom as the only good), and the
  beginning of the Sophists' second round including the "lying is impossible"
  and "contradiction is impossible" arguments
- Part 2 will continue with more sophistic arguments and the dialogue's conclusion
