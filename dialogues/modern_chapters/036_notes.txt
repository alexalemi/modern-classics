TRANSLATION NOTES — Sophist Part 1 (Chapter 036)

OVERVIEW
This is the first half of Plato's Sophist, covering the opening dramatic frame, the
practice exercise with the angler, six attempts to define the sophist through the
method of division, the discovery that the sophist is an imitator, and the beginning
of the crisis about not-being and Parmenides' dictum.

VOICE AND TONE
- The Eleatic Stranger leads this dialogue, not Socrates. He is polite, methodical, and
  precise -- a clinical investigator rather than an ironic provocateur. I kept his tone
  professional but accessible: think of a careful, patient teacher walking through a
  systematic classification exercise.
- Socrates appears only at the very beginning (with characteristic wit about the
  Stranger being a disguised god) and one brief interjection later. His playful irony
  in the opening contrasts deliberately with the Stranger's methodical approach.
- Theaetetus is an eager, intelligent young respondent. His answers are willing and
  sometimes enthusiastic, but he's clearly following the Stranger's lead.
- The overall tone is more clinical and systematic than typical Socratic dialogues.
  Less irony, more precision -- as specified in the text analysis notes.

KEY DECISIONS
1. "Stranger" -- kept this label for the Eleatic Stranger throughout, matching the
   source text's convention. He's from Elea, a student of Parmenides and Zeno.

2. Method of division -- The elaborate classification exercise (angler as practice case,
   then six definitions of the sophist) is the heart of Part 1. I kept every step of
   every division, making each branching point clear and conversational. The
   challenge was making a systematic classification feel like a natural conversation
   rather than a dry taxonomy. Used shorter sentences and natural speech patterns
   to keep it moving.

3. The angler exercise -- This extended warm-up classification (all skills → acquisitive →
   conquest → hunting → animal hunting → water hunting → fishing → strike fishing →
   barb fishing → angling) serves as a pattern for the sophist divisions. Kept it
   complete and step-by-step.

4. Six definitions of the sophist:
   (1) Paid hunter of wealthy young men
   (2) Wholesale dealer in intellectual goods
   (3) Retailer of the same
   (4) Manufacturer and seller of his own intellectual wares
   (5) Eristic debater who argues for money
   (6) Purifier of souls who clears away false beliefs
   Each gets its own complete treatment through the method of division.

5. The wolf/dog comparison -- The Stranger's warning that the sophist resembles the
   soul-purifier "the same way a wolf resembles a dog" is a crucial moment.
   Kept it vivid and direct.

6. The pivot to imitation -- When the six definitions prove insufficient, the Stranger
   identifies the sophist's core trait as producing apparent knowledge through
   imitation. The painter analogy (showing pictures to children from a distance)
   is key and kept intact.

7. Parmenides' dictum -- "Keep your mind from this way of inquiry, for you will never
   show that not-being is." This is the philosophical climax setup. The Stranger's
   reluctance to commit "parricide" against his intellectual father Parmenides is
   a major dramatic moment. Kept the emotional weight: his trembling, his request
   for forgiveness, his sense of being forced into it.

8. The not-being paradox -- The Stranger's demonstration that you can't even talk
   about not-being without attributing being to it (saying "not-being IS unsayable"
   is already a contradiction) is presented step by step. Every logical move is
   preserved.

9. The image problem -- The Stranger's point that the sophist will challenge them
   to define "image" (something that's "really not real") without falling into
   contradiction about not-being. This sets up Part 2's resolution.

10. The monist critique -- The examination of those who say "all is one" follows
    Parmenides' own poem about being as a "well-rounded sphere." The logical
    dilemma (if being has parts it can't be absolutely one; if it's not a whole
    it lacks something and becomes not-being) is preserved in full.

VOCABULARY
- "Skill" for techne throughout (consistent with text analysis vocabulary mappings)
- "Acquisitive" and "creative/productive" for the two main branches of skill
- "Eristic" preserved as a term (with "contentious argumentation" as gloss)
- "Dialectic" / "dialectical" preserved for Plato's philosophical method
- Modern contractions used throughout: "isn't," "don't," "can't," "we've," "he's"
- "Not-being" kept as the standard translation for me on (consistent with the
  philosophical tradition and necessary for the argument to work)

STRUCTURAL NOTES
- Maintained the dramatic dialogue format with speaker names on separate lines
- Blank lines between exchanges for readability
- Every exchange from the source text is translated -- nothing summarized or cut
- The classification chains are formatted as natural conversation, not as lists
  or diagrams, following the original's dialogue form

CHALLENGES
- The method of division is inherently repetitive (divide, name, divide again).
  The challenge was keeping it lively without distorting the methodical nature
  of the investigation. Solution: varied sentence structures, used natural
  conversational rhythms, let Theaetetus's occasional confusion or enthusiasm
  break up the pattern.
- The not-being paradox section requires extreme logical precision. Every
  step matters because the Stranger is building toward a self-refuting
  demonstration. Kept all logical moves intact while making the language
  more accessible.

NEXT CHAPTER NOTES
- Part 2 continues directly from Part 1's cliffhanger about being and not-being
- The Stranger has just shown that monists face insoluble problems with "being"
- Part 2 will cover: the "Battle of Giants and Gods" (materialists vs. idealists),
  the five greatest kinds (Being, Rest, Motion, Same, Other), the resolution
  of not-being as "difference" rather than "opposite," the theory of false
  statement, and the final definition of the sophist
- The Stranger's tone should remain clinical and methodical throughout
- Key moment to handle carefully: "being is simply power" (the Stranger's
  proposed definition of being as the capacity to act or be acted upon)
- The five greatest kinds section is the philosophical heart of Part 2 --
  every step of the argument about how Same and Other relate to Being,
  Rest, and Motion must be crystal clear
- The resolution (not-being = difference from being, not the opposite of being)
  is the philosophical climax of the entire dialogue
- Final definition of the sophist follows from this resolution: he deals in
  appearances, which are possible because falsehood is possible, which is
  possible because not-being (as difference) has genuine existence
