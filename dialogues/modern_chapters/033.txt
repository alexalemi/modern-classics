THEAETETUS (Part 2)

Theodorus
Quite true.

Socrates
Then we have a fair argument against your master: he has to admit that one person is wiser than another, and that the wiser person is a measure. As for me — the one who knows nothing — I'm under no obligation to accept the honor that Protagoras' defender was just trying to force on me, like it or not: the honor of being a measure of anything.

Theodorus
That's the strongest refutation of him, Socrates. Though he's also trapped another way: by granting that everyone's opinions are true, he grants the truth of the people whose opinion is that he's flat wrong.

Socrates
There are plenty of ways, Theodorus, to refute the doctrine that every opinion of every person is true. But it's much harder to prove that the feelings actually present to a person — the ones that give rise to sensations and the opinions that match them — are false too. Maybe I've been talking nonsense about them. Maybe they really are unassailable, and the people who say they're clear evidence — actual knowledge — may well be right. In that case our friend Theaetetus wasn't so far off the mark when he said perception and knowledge are the same thing. So let's move in closer, as Protagoras' defender wanted, and give this theory of universal flux a good knock to hear whether it rings true or cracked. One thing is certain: there's no small war raging over it, and no shortage of fighters on each side.

Theodorus
No small war indeed. In Ionia the movement is spreading fast — the followers of Heraclitus are the doctrine's most energetic champions.

Socrates
All the more reason, my dear Theodorus, to examine the question from the ground up, the way they themselves lay it out.

Theodorus
Absolutely. Though about these Heraclitean ideas — which, as you say, are as old as Homer or even older — the Ephesians who claim to understand them are completely mad. You can't have a conversation with them about it. True to their own textbooks, they're perpetually in motion. As for staying put on one argument or one question — calmly asking and answering in turn — they can no more do that than they can fly. Actually, "no more" understates it: there isn't a negative strong enough to capture how determined these men are not to have a single particle of rest in them. Ask one of them a question and he pulls little cryptic sayings out like arrows from a quiver and shoots them at you. Ask him to explain what he just said, and you get hit with another freshly coined riddle. You'll never get anywhere with any of them — and they get nowhere with each other. They take enormous care never to let anything settled appear in their arguments or in their own minds, believing, I suppose, that anything settled would be standing still. And they're at war with stillness; they do everything they can to banish it from the universe.

Socrates
Maybe, Theodorus, you've only seen them in combat and never spent time with them during a truce — they're not your crowd, after all. I imagine they save their "peacetime doctrines" for the students they hope to mold into copies of themselves.

Theodorus
Students! My good man, they don't have any. Men like that aren't anyone's students. They just spring up on their own, each one catching his inspiration wherever he happens to find it, and each one convinced his neighbor knows nothing. So as I was about to say: you'll never get a reasoned account out of these men, willingly or unwillingly. We'll have to take the question out of their hands and work through the analysis ourselves, as if it were a geometry problem.

Socrates
Quite right. But on this question — haven't we heard it from the ancients too, who hid their wisdom from the masses inside poetic imagery? They said that Oceanus and Tethys, the origin of all things, are flowing streams, and that nothing stands still. And now the moderns, being so much wiser, declare it openly — so even the cobbler can hear their wisdom and learn it, and stop foolishly imagining that some things rest while others move. Once he's learned that everything is motion, he'll honor his teachers properly. But I almost forgot the opposite camp, Theodorus:

"Alone Being remains unmoved, which is the name for the all."

That's the language of Parmenides, Melissus, and their followers, who insist that all being is one, complete in itself, with nowhere to move. What are we going to do with all these people? Step by step we've accidentally wandered right between the two armies, and unless we can cover our retreat, we'll pay for our recklessness — like players in the wrestling school caught on the center line and dragged in both directions by both teams. I think we'd better start with the group we approached first, the "river gods" of flux. If we find any truth in them, we'll let them pull us over to their side and try to escape the others. But if the partisans of "the whole" seem to speak more truly, then we'll flee to them, away from the people who would set the immovable in motion. And if it turns out neither side has anything reasonable to say, we'll look ridiculous — imagining that nobodies like us could be right while ancient and famous men are wrong. O Theodorus, do you think it's worth going forward when the danger is this great?

Theodorus
Socrates, leaving these two positions unexamined would be intolerable. We have to go on.

Socrates
Then examine we must — since you, who were so reluctant to start, are now so eager to continue. The nature of motion seems to be the place to begin. What exactly do they mean when they say all things are in motion? Is there only one kind of motion, or — as I'm inclined to think — two? I'd like your opinion on this along with mine, so that if I'm going to be wrong, at least I'll be wrong in good company. Tell me: when something changes from one place to another, or spins around in the same place, isn't that what we call motion?

Theodorus
Yes.

Socrates
So there's one kind of motion. But when something stays in the same spot but grows old, or turns black from white, or hard from soft, or undergoes any other change — isn't it fair to call that a second kind of motion?

Theodorus
I think so.

Socrates
Say rather that it must be so. So motion comes in these two kinds: "change" and "motion in place."

Theodorus
You're right.

Socrates
Now that we've drawn this distinction, let's address the people who say everything is in motion and ask them: do all things, on your view, have both kinds of motion — changing as well as moving in place — or do some things move in both ways and others in only one?

Theodorus
Honestly, I don't know how to answer. But I think they'd say all things move in both ways.

Socrates
They'd have to, my friend. Otherwise they'd be admitting that the same things are both moving and at rest, and then it would be no more true to say "everything moves" than to say "everything rests."

Theodorus
That's certain.

Socrates
And if things are to be in motion, with nothing exempt from motion, then everything must always have every kind of motion?

Theodorus
Quite true.

Socrates
Now consider a further point. Didn't we understand them to explain the generation of heat or whiteness or anything else roughly like this: each of these things moves, along with a perception, between the agent and the patient — the thing acting and the thing acted upon. The patient stops being a mere capacity for perceiving and becomes an actual perceiver, while the agent becomes a "qualified thing" rather than a quality. I suspect "quality" sounds like a strange, awkward word to you, and the abstract phrasing isn't landing. So let me give concrete examples. I mean that the producing power, the agent, becomes not heat or whiteness but hot and white, and so on for everything else. Remember, as I said before: neither the agent nor the patient has any independent existence on its own. It's when they come together and generate sensations and the things sensed that one becomes a thing with a certain quality and the other becomes a perceiver. You remember all this?

Theodorus
Of course.

Socrates
We can skip the fine print of their theory. But we mustn't forget to ask them the one question that concerns us: Are all things in motion and flux?

Theodorus
Yes, they'll say.

Socrates
And moving in both the ways we distinguished — moving in place and also changing?

Theodorus
Of course — if the motion is to be complete.

Socrates
Right, because if things only moved in place without changing, we'd still be able to say what kind of things these moving, flowing things are. Wouldn't we?

Theodorus
Exactly.

Socrates
But as it stands, not even white keeps flowing white. Whiteness itself is a flux, a change, always slipping into another color, never to be caught standing still. So can the name of any color ever be used correctly at all?

Theodorus
How could it be, Socrates — for color or any other quality — if the object is escaping in the flux even while we're saying the word?

Socrates
And what about perceptions like sight and hearing, or any other kind? Does the act of seeing or hearing ever stand still?

Theodorus
Certainly not, if everything is in motion.

Socrates
Then we have no more right to speak of "seeing" than of "not-seeing," and no more right to speak of any perception than of any non-perception — if everything shares in every kind of motion?

Theodorus
We don't.

Socrates
Yet perception is knowledge — at least that's what Theaetetus and I were saying.

Theodorus
Very true.

Socrates
So when we were asked "what is knowledge?", our answer was no more an answer about knowledge than about non-knowledge?

Theodorus
I suppose not.

Socrates
A fine result! We polished up our first answer by being so eager to prove that nothing stands still — and if nothing stands still, then every answer on every subject is equally correct. You can say a thing is "thus," or "not thus" — or if you prefer, "becomes thus," so we don't trap them with words that imply rest.

Theodorus
Quite true.

Socrates
Yes, Theodorus — except for the words "thus" and "not thus." They can't even use "thus," because there's no motion in "thus" or in "not thus." The defenders of this doctrine don't yet have any words to express themselves with; they need to invent a whole new language. The only phrase I know that would suit them is "no how" — perfectly indefinite.

Theodorus
Yes, that's exactly the kind of talk they'd feel at home with.

Socrates
And so, Theodorus, we've gotten free of your friend without conceding his doctrine that every man is the measure of all things — only a wise man is a measure. Nor can we accept that knowledge is perception, certainly not on the hypothesis of perpetual flux — unless perhaps our friend Theaetetus can convince us otherwise.

Theodorus
Excellent, Socrates. And now that the argument about Protagoras' doctrine is finished, I'm released from answering — that was the deal.

Theaetetus
Not yet, Theodorus! Not until you and Socrates have dealt with the people who say everything is at rest, the way you proposed.

Theodorus
You young rogue, Theaetetus — teaching your elders to break their promises! No, you should be getting ready to answer Socrates for the rest of the argument.

Theaetetus
Fine, if he wants me to. But I'd rather have heard about the doctrine of rest.

Theodorus
Inviting Socrates into an argument is like inviting cavalry onto open ground. Just ask him — he'll answer.

Socrates
Even so, Theodorus, I'm afraid I can't grant Theaetetus' request.

Theodorus
Can't grant it! Why not?

Socrates
Because I feel a kind of reverence — not so much for Melissus and the others who say "All is one and at rest," as for their great leader himself: Parmenides, "venerable and awe-inspiring," as Homer might put it. I'd be ashamed to approach him in any spirit unworthy of him. I met him once, when he was an old man and I was barely more than a boy, and he struck me as having a glorious depth of mind. I'm afraid we wouldn't understand his words, and would fall even further short of understanding his meaning. Most of all, I'm afraid that the nature of knowledge — the real subject of our discussion — would get shoved out of sight by the crowd of uninvited guests who'd come pouring into our feast of conversation if we opened that door. Besides, the question now stirring is enormous. Treat it in passing and you treat it unfairly; treat it fully and at length, and it eclipses the question of knowledge. We can't allow either. No — I have to use my midwife's art to deliver Theaetetus of the ideas he's carrying about knowledge.

Theaetetus
Very well. Go ahead, if that's what you think best.

Socrates
Then take another look at the subject, Theaetetus. You answered that knowledge is perception, right?

Theaetetus
I did.

Socrates
And if someone asked you, "With what does a person see black and white colors? With what does he hear high and low sounds?" — you'd say, if I'm not mistaken, "With the eyes and with the ears."

Theaetetus
I would.

Socrates
Now, using words loosely instead of with fussy precision is usually the mark of a well-educated mind — the opposite is pedantic. But sometimes precision is necessary, and I think the answer you just gave is, strictly speaking, incorrect. Which is more accurate: to say that we see and hear with the eyes and ears, or through the eyes and ears?

Theaetetus
"Through," Socrates, I'd say — rather than "with."

Socrates
Yes, my boy. Because nobody can seriously suppose that each of us is like a kind of Trojan horse with a crowd of disconnected senses perched inside, never meeting in any single nature. There must be something — call it the mind, or whatever you like — in which they all converge: something that uses the senses as instruments, and through them perceives whatever can be sensed.

Theaetetus
I agree with that.

Socrates
Here's why I'm being so precise. I want to know whether, when we perceive black and white through the eyes, and other qualities through other organs, we perceive them all with one and the same part of ourselves. If asked, you could refer all such perceptions to the body — but maybe I should let you answer for yourself instead of putting words in your mouth. Tell me: the organs through which you perceive warm and hard and light and sweet — they belong to the body, don't they?

Theaetetus
To the body, certainly.

Socrates
And you'd agree that what you perceive through one faculty, you can't perceive through another? You can't perceive the objects of hearing through sight, or the objects of sight through hearing?

Theaetetus
Of course not.

Socrates
So if you have a thought about both of them together, that common perception can't be coming to you through either organ alone?

Theaetetus
It can't.

Socrates
Now, about sounds and colors. First of all, you'd agree that they both exist?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And that each is different from the other and the same as itself?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And that together they are two, and each one is one?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And you can also consider whether they're like or unlike each other?

Theaetetus
I suppose so.

Socrates
But through what do you perceive all this about them? Neither hearing nor sight can grasp what they have in common. Here's an illustration of the point. Suppose there were any sense in asking whether sounds and colors are salty or not — you could tell me which faculty would handle that question. Not sight, not hearing, but something else.

Theaetetus
Certainly — the faculty of taste.

Socrates
Very good. Now tell me: what is the power that discerns — not just in objects of sense but in everything — those universal notions like "being" and "not-being," and the others we were just asking about? What organs will you assign for perceiving these?

Theaetetus
You mean being and not-being, likeness and unlikeness, sameness and difference — and also unity and the other numbers we apply to things we sense. And you're asking through what bodily organ the soul perceives odd and even numbers and the other concepts of arithmetic.

Socrates
You follow me beautifully, Theaetetus. That's exactly what I'm asking.

Theaetetus
Honestly, Socrates, I can't answer — except that I don't think there's any special organ for these things, the way there is for objects of sense. It seems to me that the mind, by a power of its own, contemplates these universals in everything.

Socrates
You're beautiful, Theaetetus — not ugly, whatever Theodorus said — because the one who speaks beautifully is beautiful and good himself. And besides being beautiful, you've done me a great kindness: you've saved me from a very long discussion, if it's truly clear to you that the soul views some things by itself and others through the organs of the body. That was exactly my own view, and I wanted you to reach it on your own.

Theaetetus
It's quite clear to me.

Socrates
Then to which class do you assign being, or existence? Of all our notions, that's the most universal.

Theaetetus
To the class the soul reaches for by itself.

Socrates
And the same for like and unlike, same and different?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And what about noble and base, good and evil?

Theaetetus
Those seem to me to be essentially relative notions — ones the soul perceives by comparing, within itself, things past and present against the future.

Socrates
Hold on, though. The hardness of something hard — doesn't the soul perceive that through touch? And the softness of something soft, equally through touch?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
But their being — the fact that they exist, what the two of them are, their opposition to each other, and the nature of that opposition — these the soul tries to determine for us by itself, reviewing them and comparing them?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
So the simple sensations that reach the soul through the body — nature gives those at birth to humans and animals alike. But reflections about them — about their being and their usefulness — come slowly and with difficulty, if they come at all, through education and long experience.

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Socrates
And can someone attain truth if he can't even attain being?

Theaetetus
Impossible.

Socrates
And if someone misses the truth about a thing, can he ever have knowledge of that thing?

Theaetetus
He can't.

Socrates
Then knowledge doesn't live in our sense impressions at all, but in our reasoning about them. There — and not in the raw impression — is where truth and being can be reached.

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
And would you call two processes by the same name when they're so different from each other?

Theaetetus
That certainly wouldn't be right.

Socrates
So what name would you give to seeing, hearing, smelling, feeling cold, feeling hot?

Theaetetus
I'd call all of them perceiving. What else could you call them?

Socrates
So "perception" is the collective name for all of them?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
Which, as we've said, has no share in reaching truth — since it has none in reaching being?

Theaetetus
None.

Socrates
And therefore no share in knowledge?

Theaetetus
No.

Socrates
Then perception, Theaetetus, can never be the same thing as knowledge?

Theaetetus
Clearly not, Socrates. And now knowledge has been proved, as plainly as anything could be, to be different from perception.

Socrates
But the original goal of our discussion was to find out what knowledge is — not what it isn't. Still, we've made progress: we now know not to look for knowledge in perception at all, but in that other activity — whatever we call it — where the mind is alone, occupied with being.

Theaetetus
You mean, Socrates, if I'm not mistaken, what's called thinking, or forming opinions.

Socrates
You've got it exactly. So now, my friend, start fresh from this point. Wipe everything that came before out of your memory and see if the view is any clearer from here. Tell me, once more: what is knowledge?

Theaetetus
I can't say that all opinion is knowledge, Socrates, because opinions can be false. But I'll venture this: knowledge is true opinion. Let that be my answer. If it gets disproved down the road, I'll try to find another.

Socrates
That's the way to answer, Theaetetus — boldly, not in your old hesitant style. If we're bold, one of two good things will happen: either we'll find what we're looking for, or we'll be less inclined to think we know what we don't know. Either way, we come out richer. So — what are you saying, exactly? That there are two kinds of opinion, one true and one false, and you define knowledge as the true kind?

Theaetetus
Yes. That's my view at the moment.

Socrates
Then is it worth picking up an old thread about opinion?

Theaetetus
Which thread do you mean?

Socrates
There's something that troubles me — often — and leaves me genuinely perplexed, both about myself and about others. I can't make out the nature or origin of this mental experience.

Theaetetus
What experience?

Socrates
How false opinion is even possible. The problem still nags at my mind's eye, and I'm torn between dropping the question and starting over on it in a new way.

Theaetetus
Start over, Socrates — if you think there's the slightest need. Weren't you and Theodorus just saying, quite rightly, that in discussions like this we can take all the time we want?

Socrates
Good point. And maybe there's no harm in retracing our steps and beginning again. Better to do a little well than a lot badly.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
Well then, what's the difficulty? We do speak of false opinion, don't we? We say one person holds a false opinion and another a true one, as though there were some real distinction between them.

Theaetetus
We certainly do.

Socrates
Now, everything whatsoever is either known or not known. (I'm setting aside the in-between cases of learning and forgetting — they don't matter for our present question.)

Theaetetus
With those excluded, Socrates, there's no doubt: a thing must be either known or not known.

Socrates
With that settled, mustn't we say that whoever holds an opinion holds it about something he either knows or doesn't know?

Theaetetus
He must.

Socrates
And if you know a thing, you can't not know it; if you don't know it, you can't know it?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
Then what happens with false opinion? When a man holds one, does he take something he knows to be some other thing he knows — so that knowing both, he's somehow ignorant of both?

Theaetetus
That's impossible, Socrates.

Socrates
Then does he take something he doesn't know for some other thing he doesn't know? For example: he knows neither Theaetetus nor Socrates, yet somehow gets it into his head that Theaetetus is Socrates, or Socrates is Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
How could he?

Socrates
But surely he can't take what he knows for what he doesn't know, or what he doesn't know for what he knows?

Theaetetus
That would be monstrous.

Socrates
Then where can false opinion be? If everything is either known or unknown, every opinion falls under one of those two headings — and false opinion gets squeezed out entirely.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
Then suppose we move the question out of the territory of knowing and not knowing, and into the territory of being and not-being.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Maybe the simple truth is this: whoever thinks what is not — thinks something that doesn't exist — necessarily thinks falsely, whatever else is going on in his mind.

Theaetetus
That sounds plausible, Socrates.

Socrates
Then suppose someone puts this to us, Theaetetus: "Is it possible for any man to think that which is not — either as a thing existing on its own, or as a property of something else?" And we answer, "Yes, he can — when he thinks something untrue." Is that what we'll say?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
But does anything parallel happen anywhere else?

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Can a man see something — and yet see nothing?

Theaetetus
Impossible.

Socrates
Right. If he sees any one thing, he sees something that exists. Or do you suppose a "one" could ever be found among things that don't exist?

Theaetetus
I don't.

Socrates
So whoever sees some one thing, sees something that is?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
And whoever hears anything, hears some one thing — and so hears something that is?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And whoever touches anything, touches some one thing — which, being one, is?

Theaetetus
That's true too.

Socrates
And doesn't whoever thinks, think some one thing?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And whoever thinks some one thing, thinks something that is?

Theaetetus
I agree.

Socrates
Then whoever thinks what is not, thinks nothing?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
And whoever thinks nothing isn't thinking at all?

Theaetetus
Obviously.

Socrates
So no one can think what is not — neither as a thing existing on its own, nor as a property of something else?

Theaetetus
Clearly not.

Socrates
Then thinking falsely must be something different from thinking what is not?

Theaetetus
So it seems.

Socrates
Then false opinion has no existence in us — not in the realm of being, and not in the realm of knowledge?

Theaetetus
Apparently not.

Socrates
But maybe what we mean by this name is really something like the following.

Theaetetus
Like what?

Socrates
Maybe false opinion is a kind of mistaken-swapping of thoughts: a person makes an exchange in his mind and declares that one real thing is another real thing. That way, he's always thinking of something that is — but he's put one thing in the place of another. And since he's missed the target he was aiming at, we could fairly say he holds a false opinion.

Theaetetus
Now I think you've hit it exactly. When someone mentally swaps in "base" where "noble" belongs, or "noble" where "base" belongs — that's when he truly holds a false opinion.

Socrates
I see, Theaetetus, that your fear of me is gone and you're starting to look down on me.

Theaetetus
What makes you say that?

Socrates
You think, if I'm reading you right, that your phrase "truly false" is safe from attack — that I'll never ask whether there can be a swift that is slow, or a heavy that is light, or any other self-contradictory thing that operates not according to its own nature but according to its opposite's. But I'll let that go; I don't want to discourage you for no reason. So — you're satisfied that false opinion is this kind of mental swap, thinking one thing in place of another?

Theaetetus
I am.

Socrates
So on your view, it's possible for the mind to take one thing for another?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
But when the mind mixes them up like that, doesn't it have to have some conception of both objects, or at least one of them?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
Either at the same time, or one after the other?

Theaetetus
Right.

Socrates
And when you say "thinking," do you mean the same thing I mean?

Theaetetus
What do you mean by it?

Socrates
I mean the conversation the soul has with itself when it's considering something. I'm telling you this as someone who barely understands it himself — but the soul, when it thinks, seems to me to be simply talking: asking itself questions and answering them, saying yes and saying no. And when it finally reaches a decision — whether it gets there gradually or in a sudden leap — when it settles on one answer and stops doubting, that's what we call its opinion. So I'd say that forming an opinion is a kind of speaking, and an opinion is a statement spoken — not out loud to someone else, but silently to yourself. What do you think?

Theaetetus
I agree.

Socrates
Then when someone thinks one thing is another, he's saying to himself that one thing is the other?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
But think back: have you ever said to yourself that the noble is definitely shameful, or that the unjust is just? Or — here's the real test — have you ever tried to convince yourself that one thing is another thing? Even in your sleep, have you ever dared to tell yourself that odd is even, or anything like that?

Theaetetus
Never.

Socrates
And do you suppose anyone else — sane or insane — ever seriously tried to persuade himself that an ox is a horse, or that two is one?

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Socrates
So if thinking is talking to yourself, then no one who is speaking and thinking about two things, holding both of them in his soul, will say or think that one of them is the other. And by the way — fond of arguing as you are, you'd better not quibble over the word "other" here (I mean, don't insist that "one" and "other" amount to the same thing). My point is simply this: nobody thinks the noble is shameful, or anything of the kind.

Theaetetus
I'll drop the word "other," Socrates. And I agree with you.

Socrates
So if a man has both things in his thoughts, he can't think that one of them is the other?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And if he has only one of them in his mind and not the other, he can't think that one is the other either?

Theaetetus
True — because then he'd have to be grasping something that isn't in his thoughts at all.

Socrates
Then no one who has both objects in mind, or only one of them, can think that one is the other. So anyone who defines false opinion as "mistaking one thing for another" is talking nonsense. This route, like the one before it, leads nowhere — false opinion can't exist in us this way either.

Theaetetus
No.

Socrates
And yet, Theaetetus, if we don't admit that it exists, we'll be forced into some very strange conclusions.

Theaetetus
Like what?

Socrates
I won't tell you until I've tried looking at the problem from every angle. I'd be ashamed of us if, stuck in our confusion, we got forced into admitting the absurdities I have in mind. If we find a way out and escape them, then we can talk about them calmly as other people's problems, and the embarrassment won't stick to us. But if we end up completely stumped — well, then I suppose we'll have to swallow our pride and let the argument walk all over us, like seasick passengers getting stepped on by the sailors, and let it do whatever it wants with us. So listen, and I'll tell you where I think I see a way out.

Theaetetus
I'm listening.

Socrates
I think we were wrong to deny that a man can mistake something he knows for something he doesn't know. There is, in fact, a way that kind of mistake can happen.

Theaetetus
You mean what I suspected at the time — that I might know Socrates, and then see someone in the distance whom I don't know, and mistake him for you? That's how the error happens?

Socrates
But didn't we abandon that position, because it forced us into the absurdity of knowing and not knowing the very things we know?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Then let's frame the claim differently. Maybe it will hold up, maybe it won't — but we're in a tight spot, and every argument deserves to be turned over and tested. Tell me: am I right that you can learn something you didn't know before?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And then another thing, and another?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Then here's what I want you to imagine. Suppose there's a block of wax in each person's mind. It's bigger in some people, smaller in others; harder in some, moister in others; purer in some — and in some, somewhere in between.

Theaetetus
I'm imagining it.

Socrates
Let's say this wax tablet is a gift from Memory, the mother of the Muses. Whenever we want to remember something we've seen or heard or thought up ourselves, we hold the wax under our perceptions and thoughts, and they stamp an impression into it, the way a signet ring stamps a seal. Whatever gets imprinted, we remember and know — as long as the image lasts. But when the image wears away, or never takes in the first place, we forget. We don't know it anymore.

Theaetetus
Very good.

Socrates
Now, take a person with this kind of knowledge stamped in his wax, who is considering something he sees or hears. Couldn't false opinion arise in the following way?

Theaetetus
What way?

Socrates
By his thinking that what he knows is, sometimes, what he knows — and sometimes, what he doesn't know. We were wrong before when we denied this was possible.

Theaetetus
So how would you fix our earlier statement?

Socrates
I'd start by listing the impossible cases — the ones we have to rule out. First: no one can mistake one thing for another when he isn't perceiving either of them but has the memory-seal of both stamped in his mind. Nor can he mix up two things when he knows only one of them and has no impression at all of the other. Nor can he think that one thing he doesn't know is another thing he doesn't know, or that something he doesn't know is something he knows. Second: he can't think that one thing he perceives is another thing he perceives; or that something he perceives is something he doesn't perceive; or that something he doesn't perceive is some other thing he doesn't perceive; or that something he doesn't perceive is something he does perceive. Third: he can't think that something he both knows and perceives — where his impression matches his perception exactly — is some other thing he also knows and perceives with the impression matching the perception. That case, if anything, is even more inconceivable than the others. Fourth: he can't think that something he knows and perceives, with the memory lined up correctly with the sensation, is some other thing he knows; nor, as long as everything lines up, can he think that a thing he knows and perceives is another thing he merely perceives. And a thing he neither knows nor perceives can't be confused with another thing he neither knows nor perceives; nor can a thing he neither knows nor perceives be confused with another thing he doesn't know, or with another thing he doesn't perceive. In all these cases, false opinion is absolutely and completely impossible. If it's possible anywhere, it's only in the cases that remain.

Theaetetus
And what are those? Tell me — maybe then I'll understand you better. Right now I can't follow you at all.

Socrates
The remaining cases are these: a person may think that things he knows are other things he knows and perceives; or things he doesn't know but perceives are things he knows and perceives; or things he both knows and perceives are other things he knows and perceives.

Theaetetus
Now I understand you even less than before.

Socrates
Then let me put it another way. I know Theodorus, and I hold in my memory what kind of person he is — same with you. But sometimes I see the two of you, and sometimes I don't; sometimes I touch you, sometimes not; sometimes I hear you or perceive you in some other way, and sometimes I have no perception of you at all — yet I still remember you, and I still know you in my own mind.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
So here's the first point I want you to grasp: a person can know something while perceiving it, or know it while not perceiving it.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And something he doesn't know — sometimes he won't perceive it either, and sometimes he'll perceive it and nothing more?

Theaetetus
That's also true.

Socrates
Now see if you can follow me. Suppose Socrates knows Theodorus and Theaetetus, but he sees neither of them, and has no other perception of them. In that case, there's no way he could ever form the thought, in his own mind, that Theaetetus is Theodorus. Am I right?

Theaetetus
Quite right.

Socrates
That was the first of the cases I described.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
The second case: knowing one of you but not the other, and perceiving neither, I could never think that the one I know is the one I don't know.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Third case: knowing neither of you and perceiving neither, I can't possibly think that one stranger is some other stranger. And you can take it that I've run through the whole catalog of excluded cases again — all the ones where I can't form a false opinion about you and Theodorus, whether I know both of you, or neither, or one and not the other. And the same goes for perceiving. You follow me?

Theaetetus
I do.

Socrates
So the only opening left for false opinion is this: I know you and I know Theodorus — I have impressions of both of you on my wax block, like seal-stamps from a ring. Then I see you both, but poorly, at a distance. I rush to match the right memory-impression to the right visual impression — to fit each sight into its own stamp, so to speak. If I succeed, recognition happens. But if I fail and swap them — putting the foot in the wrong shoe, fitting the sight of one of you onto the impression of the other — or if my mind slips the way mirrors do, flipping right to left, then mismatching happens, and false opinion is born.

Theaetetus
Yes, Socrates! That's a marvelously exact description of what happens to opinion.

Socrates
There's also the case where I know both of you, and perceive one but not the other, and my knowledge of the one I perceive doesn't line up with my perception — that was the case I described before that you couldn't follow.

Theaetetus
Right, I couldn't.

Socrates
What I was saying was this: if someone knows and perceives one of you, and his knowledge agrees with his perception, he'll never think that person is somebody else whom he also knows and perceives with knowledge agreeing with perception. That was the situation, wasn't it?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
But what we left out was the case we're describing now — where false opinion actually arises: when I know both of you, and see both of you (or have some other perception of both), but I fail to hold each memory-seal in line with its matching sensation. Like a bad archer, I shoot wide and miss the mark. And missing the mark, in fact, is exactly what we call being wrong — falsehood.

Theaetetus
And rightly so.

Socrates
So: when perception is present for one of the impressions but not for the other, and the mind fits the seal of the absent perception onto the perception that's actually present, the mind is deceived. In short, if our account is sound: about things a person has never known and never perceived, there can be no error, no deception at all. It's only among things we know and perceive that opinion turns and twists, coming out alternately true and false. True, when the impressions line up straight and square with their sensations. False, when they go crooked and land askew.

Theaetetus
Isn't that beautifully put, Socrates?

Socrates
Beautifully — well, wait until you hear the rest, and then you'll say so with even better reason. Because holding true opinions is a beautiful thing, and being deceived is an ugly one.

Theaetetus
No doubt about that.

Socrates
And here, supposedly, is where the difference comes from. When the wax in someone's soul is deep and plentiful, smooth and worked to the perfect consistency, then the things that come in through the senses stamp themselves into this "heart" of the soul — as Homer calls it, in a phrase that hints at the likeness between heart and wax (in the Greek, his word for "heart," ker, echoes keros, "wax"). In these souls the impressions come out clean and deep, and they last. People like this learn easily, remember well, and don't get their impressions confused. They think truly. Since the impressions are clear and there's plenty of room, they quickly file each one in its proper place on the block — and these are the people we call wise. Do you agree?

Theaetetus
Completely.

Socrates
But when someone's heart is shaggy — the condition our all-wise poet praises — or when the wax is muddy and impure, or too soft, or too hard, the mind has a matching defect. Soft-waxed people learn quickly but forget quickly; hard-waxed people are the reverse. Those whose wax is shaggy and rough and gritty, mixed with earth or filth, get blurry impressions. So do the hard-waxed, because their impressions have no depth — and the soft-waxed too, because their impressions smear together and rub away. And it's blurrier still when all the impressions are crammed and jostled together in some tiny soul with no room. These are the natures that breed false opinion. When they see or hear or think about anything, they're slow matching things to the right impressions; they misassign them, in their dullness, and so they see wrong, hear wrong, think wrong. And that's what we mean when we say someone is deceived about things and ignorant.

Theaetetus
No one could put it better, Socrates.

Socrates
So then — we can now say that false opinion exists in us?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And true opinion too?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
At last we've established it, beyond any doubt: these two kinds of opinion exist?

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Socrates
Oh, Theaetetus. What a tiresome creature a talkative man is!

Theaetetus
What? Why do you say that?

Socrates
Because I'm annoyed at my own slowness — at my endless chatter. What else can you call it, when a man drags his arguments back and forth in every direction, too dense to be convinced, and simply will not let a question go?

Theaetetus
But what's bothering you?

Socrates
I'm not just bothered — I'm in despair. I don't know what to say if someone asks me: "So, Socrates, you've discovered that false opinion arises neither in our perceptions, when compared with each other, nor in our thoughts, but precisely at the junction where perception meets thought?" "Yes," I'll have to say — beaming, no doubt, like a man who thinks he's made a great discovery.

Theaetetus
I don't see anything to be ashamed of in what we've just shown, Socrates.

Socrates
"So," he'll go on, "your claim is this: a man we merely think of but don't see can never be confused with a horse we also don't see or touch, but merely think of?" Yes, I'll have to say, that's exactly my claim.

Theaetetus
And quite right too.

Socrates
"Well then," he'll say, "by that argument, the number eleven — which is purely a thought — could never be mistaken for twelve, which is also purely a thought." How would you answer him?

Theaetetus
I'd say a person might easily make a mistake between eleven and twelve things he sees or handles — but about the eleven and twelve in his mind alone? No. There's no making that kind of mistake.

Socrates
Really? Do you think no one has ever set five and seven before his mind — not five or seven men or horses, I mean, but five and seven themselves, the abstract numbers, which we agreed are stamped on the wax block, the ones we said could never be the subject of false opinion — has no one ever asked himself what they add up to, with one person answering eleven, and another answering twelve? Or does everyone always think and say twelve?

Theaetetus
By Zeus, no — plenty of people say eleven. And with bigger numbers, they slip up even more. You're talking about numbers in general, I assume.

Socrates
Exactly. And now consider: when that happens, isn't the person taking the twelve on his wax block to be eleven?

Theaetetus
It does seem so.

Socrates
Then haven't we circled right back to our original problem? Whoever makes that mistake is thinking that one thing he knows is another thing he knows. And we said that was impossible — in fact, that very impossibility was our airtight proof that false opinion couldn't exist, since otherwise the same person would have to know and not know the same thing at the same time.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
So false opinion can't be explained as a mismatch between thought and perception after all — because if that were the whole story, we could never go wrong about pure thoughts. Which leaves us stuck: either false opinion doesn't exist, or it's possible for a man not to know what he knows. Which option do you prefer?

Theaetetus
That's an impossible choice, Socrates.

Socrates
And yet the argument won't let us have both. Still — since we're completely stuck — what if we did something shameless?

Theaetetus
Like what?

Socrates
Like trying to explain what "knowing" actually means.

Theaetetus
What's shameless about that?

Socrates
You don't seem to realize that our entire conversation, from the very beginning, has been a hunt for knowledge — on the assumption that we don't know what it is.

Theaetetus
No, I realize that.

Socrates
Then isn't it brazen of us — not knowing what knowledge is — to go around explaining what "to know" means? The truth is, Theaetetus, our whole discussion has been contaminated from the start. We've said "we know" and "we don't know," "we have knowledge" and "we don't have knowledge," a thousand times, as if we understood each other perfectly while still being in the dark about what knowledge even is. In fact, look — even at this very moment I've used the words "understand" and "in the dark," as if we were entitled to those words when knowledge is exactly the thing we're missing.

Theaetetus
But if you ban all those expressions, Socrates, how will you argue at all?

Socrates
I couldn't — not being the man I am. If I were a true champion of dialectic, things would be different. If only such a man were here! He'd order us to avoid all these terms, and he'd come down hard on the sloppy way I've been using them. But since we're amateurs, what do you say I just go ahead and try to define what knowing is? I think the attempt is worth making.

Theaetetus
Then by all means, go ahead. And no one will blame you for using the forbidden words.

Socrates
Well — have you heard how people usually explain the verb "to know"?

Theaetetus
Probably, but I can't recall it at the moment.

Socrates
They say "to know" means "to have knowledge."

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
I'd like to make one small change, and say: "to possess" knowledge.

Theaetetus
What's the difference?

Socrates
Maybe there isn't one. But hear me out anyway, and help me test the idea.

Theaetetus
I will if I can.

Socrates
I'd distinguish "having" from "possessing" like this. Suppose a man buys a coat, and it's his, under his control — but he isn't wearing it. We wouldn't say he has it on; we'd say he possesses it.

Theaetetus
That would be the right way to put it.

Socrates
Now ask yourself whether knowledge can work the same way: possessed, but not actually "had" in hand. Imagine a man who has caught wild birds — doves, say, or any birds you like — and keeps them in an aviary he's built at home. In one sense, we'd say he always "has" them, because he possesses them. Right?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
But in another sense he "has" none of them. He merely has power over them. They're under his control, shut up in an enclosure of his own, and he can hunt down and take hold of any bird he wants, whenever he likes, and let it go again — and he can do that as often as he pleases.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Then just as before we set up a contraption of wax in the mind, let's now build a different image: suppose that in every man's mind there's an aviary full of birds of all kinds. Some flock together in big groups away from the rest, some gather in small clusters, and some are loners, flying wherever they happen to fly.

Theaetetus
Done — I'm imagining the aviary. What next?

Socrates
Let's say the birds are pieces of knowledge. When we're children, the cage is empty. And whenever someone captures a piece of knowledge and shuts it up in his enclosure, we say he has learned or discovered the thing that knowledge is about — and that's what knowing is.

Theaetetus
Granted.

Socrates
Now then. When he wants to hunt down one of these pieces of knowledge, catch it, hold it, and then release it again — what words should we use? The same ones we used for the original capture, when he first came to possess it? Let me make this clearer with an example. You agree there's such a skill as arithmetic?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
Picture that as a hunt for the knowledge of every odd and even number.

Theaetetus
I'm with you.

Socrates
It's by that skill, I take it, that the arithmetician has the numbers under his control — and can hand them over to someone else.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And when he hands them over, we call it teaching, and when someone takes them in, we call it learning, and when he has them in his possession in our aviary, we call it knowing.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Socrates
Now watch what follows. A perfect arithmetician knows all numbers, doesn't he? He has the knowledge of every number in his mind.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And such a person sometimes counts — either the abstract numbers in his head, or external things that can be counted?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
And counting is just figuring out how big some number actually is?

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
So he's investigating something he knows as if he didn't know it — because we've agreed he knows all numbers. You've heard these puzzles debated, I imagine?

Theaetetus
I have.

Socrates
Then let's use our image of the doves and pigeons, and say there are two kinds of hunt. One happens before you possess the bird, for the sake of possessing it. The other happens after — when you want to catch and hold in your hands what you've owned all along. That's how it is with knowledge too: a man may have learned something long ago and possessed the knowledge ever since, and then go after it all over again — recapturing the knowledge of this or that, knowledge he's possessed for years but didn't have ready at hand in his mind.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
That was the point of my question a moment ago: what's the right way to describe an arithmetician who sets out to count, or a literate man who sets out to read? Should we say that, although he knows, he's going back to learn from himself what he already knows?

Theaetetus
That sounds absurd, Socrates.

Socrates
Then should we say he's about to read or count what he doesn't know — when we've granted that he knows all the letters and all the numbers?

Theaetetus
That's just as absurd.

Socrates
Then suppose we say this: we don't care at all about the words. Let people drag "knowing" and "learning" around however they like. We've established that possessing knowledge is one thing, and having it in hand is another. So our position is: it's impossible for a man not to possess what he possesses — and therefore it never happens that he doesn't know what he knows. But he can still grab a false opinion about it. Why? Because he can lay hold of the wrong piece of knowledge — not the knowledge of this thing, but of some other thing instead. When all the various kinds of knowledge are fluttering around his aviary, and he goes hunting for one particular kind out of the whole flock, he can grab the wrong bird by mistake. He thought eleven was twelve because he caught the ring-dove inside him when he was reaching for the pigeon.

Theaetetus
That makes very good sense.

Socrates
But when he catches the bird he was actually trying to catch, then he isn't deceived — then his opinion matches what's real. And so true opinion and false opinion both exist, and all the problems that were tormenting us before vanish into thin air. I suppose you'll agree with me? Or no — what will you do?

Theaetetus
I agree.

Socrates
Yes — we're finally rid of the "not knowing what you know" problem, since it never turns out, on this account, that we don't possess what we possess, deceived or not. But I'm afraid an even worse problem is peering in at the window.

Theaetetus
What problem?

Socrates
How can swapping one piece of knowledge for another ever produce a false opinion?

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Think about it. First: how can a man who has knowledge of something be ignorant of that very thing — not because of ignorance, but because of his own knowledge? And second, isn't it completely absurd that he should mistake this for that, and that for this — that with knowledge sitting right there in his mind, he should know nothing and be ignorant of everything? On that logic, you might as well say that ignorance can make a man know things, or blindness make him see, if knowledge can make a man ignorant.

Theaetetus
Maybe, Socrates, our mistake was stocking the aviary only with birds of knowledge. We should have included birds of ignorance too, flying around in the mind alongside them. Then the hunter would sometimes catch a piece of knowledge and sometimes a piece of ignorance about the same thing — and the ignorance would give him a false opinion, while the knowledge would give him a true one.

Socrates
I can't help admiring you, Theaetetus — but I do have to ask you to think that over again. Let's grant your suggestion. Then, on your account, the man who catches a piece of ignorance will hold a false opinion — right?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
He certainly won't think he has a false opinion?

Theaetetus
Of course not.

Socrates
He'll think his opinion is true, and he'll believe he knows the very things he's been deceived about?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
So he'll think he's caught hold of knowledge, not ignorance?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
And so, after this long detour, we're right back face to face with our original problem. Our champion of debate will laugh at us and say: "My excellent friends, if a man knows the form of ignorance and the form of knowledge, can he really think the one he knows is the other one he knows? And if he knows neither of them, can he think the one he doesn't know is some other thing he doesn't know? And if he knows one but not the other, can he think the one he knows is the one he doesn't know — or the one he doesn't know is the one he knows? Or are you going to tell me there are still further forms of knowledge — knowledge of the right and wrong birds — which their owner keeps in yet other aviaries, or carved on yet other wax blocks, to use your silly images, and which he 'knows' because he possesses them even when they aren't ready at hand in his mind? At this rate you'll be chasing yourselves in circles forever and getting nowhere." What do we say to that, Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
Honestly, Socrates, I have no idea what we're supposed to say.

Socrates
And isn't his scolding fair? Doesn't the argument show that we were wrong to go hunting for false opinion before we know what knowledge is? That has to come first; only then can we work out the nature of false opinion.

Theaetetus
As far as we've gotten, Socrates, I can't disagree.

Socrates
Then back to the beginning: what shall we say knowledge is? We're not giving up yet, are we?

Theaetetus
Not unless you do.

Socrates
What definition would fit best with everything we've said so far?

Theaetetus
I can't think of anything except our old answer, Socrates.

Socrates
Which was?

Theaetetus
That knowledge is true opinion. True opinion can't go wrong, surely, and everything that comes of it is fine and good.

Socrates
The man leading the way across the river said, "The water itself will show how deep it is," Theaetetus. Same here — if we push ahead with the search, maybe we'll trip over what we're looking for. Stand still and we'll learn nothing.

Theaetetus
Right. Let's push ahead.

Socrates
Well, the trail doesn't run far. An entire profession stands in our way.

Theaetetus
What do you mean? Which profession?

Socrates
The profession of those great wise men known as orators and lawyers. These people use their art to persuade — they make their audience believe whatever they want them to believe. They don't teach. Or do you imagine there are teachers brilliant enough to convey the truth about a robbery or an assault to people who weren't there to see it — and to do it in the few minutes the water-clock allows for a courtroom speech?

Theaetetus
No, of course not. All they can do is persuade.

Socrates
And by "persuading" them, you mean making them form an opinion?

Theaetetus
Sure.

Socrates
So consider: jurors are rightly persuaded about facts that can only be known by an eyewitness — facts there's no other way to know. Judging from hearsay, they reach a true opinion about the case. They've decided without knowledge — yet they were correctly persuaded, assuming they reached the right verdict.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
But my friend, if true opinion and knowledge were the same thing, then the best juror in the world could never reach a right verdict without knowledge. As it stands, the two appear to be different.

Theaetetus
Oh — that reminds me of something, Socrates. I'd forgotten it, but I once heard someone make exactly this distinction. He said that true opinion combined with an account is knowledge, and true opinion without an account falls outside of knowledge. Things that can't be given a rational account are "unknowable" — that was the odd word he used — and things that can are knowable.

Socrates
Excellent! But how did he divide the knowable from the unknowable? Tell me what he said. I want to find out whether you and I have heard the same story.

Theaetetus
I'm not sure I can remember it. But if someone else laid it out, I think I could follow along.

Socrates
Then let me trade you a dream for a dream. I seem to have dreamed it too. In my dream, I heard some people saying that the primary elements, so to speak — the letters out of which we and everything else are composed — have no account. Each of them, by itself, can only be named. You can't say anything more about it. You can't say that it is, or that it is not — because then you'd be tacking existence or nonexistence onto it, and you mustn't add anything if you're going to speak of the thing alone, just by itself. You shouldn't even call it "itself" or "that" or "each" or "alone" or "this," because words like those circulate everywhere and get attached to everything — they're distinct from the things they get attached to. If an element could be described in its own right, if it had an account all its own, that account would have to leave out everything else. But as it is, none of these primary elements can be defined. They can only be named — a name is all they have. The things composed of them, though, are complex, and just as the things are woven together, so the names get woven together into an account — because an account is exactly that: a weaving-together of names. So: the elements are without account and unknowable, but they are perceivable; while the complexes — the "syllables" — are knowable and expressible and can be grasped by true opinion. So when someone forms a true opinion of something without an account, his mind has hit on the truth about it, but he doesn't know it. Whoever can't give and receive an account of a thing has no knowledge of it. But add the account, and he's got it all — he's complete in knowledge. Is that the version of the dream you heard?

Theaetetus
That's it exactly.

Socrates
So you accept this and propose that true opinion with an account is knowledge?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Socrates
Theaetetus, can it really be that today, just like that, we've found what so many wise men have grown old searching for and never found?

Theaetetus
Well, I'm satisfied with the current statement, anyway, Socrates.

Socrates
And it probably is right — after all, what could knowledge be apart from an account plus true opinion? But there's one part of the theory that bothers me.

Theaetetus
Which part?

Socrates
The part that sounds cleverest of all: that the elements are unknowable, but the class of complexes — the syllables — is knowable.

Theaetetus
Is that wrong?

Socrates
We're about to find out. We hold hostages, you see: the very examples the author of the theory used.

Theaetetus
What hostages?

Socrates
The letters — the elements of language — and the syllables, their combinations. That's what he was reasoning from, wasn't it? The letters of the alphabet?

Theaetetus
Yes, it was.

Socrates
Then let's take them up and put them to the test — or rather, put ourselves to the test, and ask how we actually learned our letters. First question: do syllables have an account while letters have none? Is that right?

Theaetetus
I think so.

Socrates
I think so too. Suppose someone asked you about the first syllable of my name: "Theaetetus, what is SO?"

Theaetetus
I'd answer: S and O.

Socrates
And that's your account of the syllable?

Theaetetus
It is.

Socrates
Good. Now give me the same kind of account of the S.

Theaetetus
But how can anyone state the elements of an element, Socrates? The S is just a consonant, nothing but a noise, a sort of hissing of the tongue. B, like most letters, isn't even that — neither a vowel sound nor a noise. So it's quite right to call the letters unaccountable. Even the clearest of them, the seven vowels, have only a sound. No account can be given of them at all.

Socrates
So far, then, friend, we've gotten this much about knowledge right?

Theaetetus
It seems so.

Socrates
But wait — have we been right in saying the syllable can be known but the letter can't?

Theaetetus
I'd think so.

Socrates
When we say "the syllable," do we mean the two letters — or all of them, if there are more than two? Or do we mean some single form that comes into being when they're combined?

Theaetetus
I'd say we mean all the letters.

Socrates
Then look at the case of the two letters S and O — the first syllable of my name. If someone knows the syllable, he knows both letters, doesn't he?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
So he knows the S and the O?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
But can he be ignorant of each one separately and yet know the two of them together?

Theaetetus
That would be monstrous, Socrates. It makes no sense at all.

Socrates
And yet — if knowing both requires knowing each, then anyone who's ever going to know a syllable has to know the letters first. And just like that, our beautiful theory sprouts wings and flies off.

Theaetetus
With remarkable speed, too.

Socrates
Yes, we weren't guarding it carefully. Maybe we should have said the syllable is not the letters, but a single form produced out of them, with its own unified character — something different from the letters.

Theaetetus
Right. That may well be more plausible than the other view.

Socrates
Careful, now. We shouldn't be cowards and betray a grand, imposing theory.

Theaetetus
No, we shouldn't.

Socrates
Then let it be as we now say: the syllable is one single form, arising out of the fitting-together of the several elements — letters or any other kind of element.

Theaetetus
Very good.

Socrates
Then it must have no parts.

Theaetetus
Why?

Socrates
Because whatever has parts must be the whole of all its parts. Or do you say a whole, even though formed out of parts, is a single thing different from all the parts?

Theaetetus
I do.

Socrates
And do you say "all of them" and "the whole" are the same, or different?

Theaetetus
I'm not certain. But since you want a quick answer, I'll take a chance and say: different.

Socrates
Your boldness I approve of, Theaetetus. Whether I approve of your answer — that needs some thought.

Theaetetus
Yes, the answer is what matters.

Socrates
On this new view, then, the whole is different from all the parts taken together?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Well now: is there any difference between "all of them" in the plural and "the all" in the singular? Take numbers. When we say one, two, three, four, five, six — or twice three, or three times two, or four plus two, or three plus two plus one — are we talking about the same number each time, or different ones?

Theaetetus
The same.

Socrates
Namely, six?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And in each way of putting it, we've spoken of all six?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And when we say "all of them," aren't we still talking about one thing?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Socrates
Namely, the six?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
So in everything made up of number, "the all" in the singular and "all of them" in the plural pick out the same thing?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
Now then. The number of an acre and the acre are the same thing, aren't they?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And likewise the number of a mile-stretch is the mile-stretch?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And an army is the number of the army — and the same in every case like this: the total number of a thing is the total thing?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
And the number of each thing is nothing other than its parts?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Socrates
So whatever has parts consists of parts?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
And we've agreed that all the parts are "the all," since the total number is "the all"?

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
Then the whole does not consist of parts — because if it consisted of all the parts, it would be "the all."

Theaetetus
That seems to follow.

Socrates
But can a part be a part of anything except the whole?

Theaetetus
Yes — of the all.

Socrates
A gallant defense, Theaetetus! But tell me: isn't "the all" precisely that from which nothing is missing?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And isn't a whole the very same thing — that from which nothing is absent? Whereas if something is missing from a thing, it's neither a whole nor an all. The same loss disqualifies it from both at once.

Theaetetus
I'm now thinking there's no difference between a whole and an all.

Socrates
Now, weren't we saying that when a thing has parts, the whole — the all — will be all the parts?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
Then back to what I was trying to say before. The alternatives are these: either the syllable is not the letters — in which case the letters are not parts of it — or the syllable is the same as the letters, in which case it must be exactly as knowable as they are.

Theaetetus
You're right.

Socrates
And it was to avoid the second option that we supposed the syllable to be different from the letters?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
Well then — if the letters aren't parts of the syllable, can you name any other parts of a syllable that aren't its letters?

Theaetetus
No way, Socrates. If I admitted the syllable has parts at all, it would be ridiculous to throw out the letters and go hunting for parts of some other kind.

Socrates
Quite right, Theaetetus. So on our present view, the syllable must be some absolutely indivisible single form?

Theaetetus
It seems so.

Socrates
But do you remember, my friend, what we accepted and approved just a little while ago — that the primary elements, the things everything else is composed of, can have no account, because each of them taken alone is incomposite, and it isn't even correct to apply words like "being" or "this" to them, since those are alien, borrowed words — and that this is why the elements are unaccountable and unknowable?

Theaetetus
I remember.

Socrates
And isn't that — being simple and indivisible — the entire reason they're unknowable? I can't see any other.

Theaetetus
No, there is no other.

Socrates
Then doesn't the syllable land in exactly the same category as the elements, if it has no parts and is a single form?

Theaetetus
It certainly does.

Socrates
So: if the syllable is a whole made up of many letters as its parts, then the letters are exactly as knowable and expressible as the syllable, since all the parts turned out to be the same as the whole.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
But if it's one and partless, then syllable and letter alike are unaccountable and unknowable — for the very same reason.

Theaetetus
I can't argue with that.

Socrates
Then we should refuse to accept it when anyone says the syllable is knowable and expressible but the letter isn't.

Theaetetus
Agreed — if we trust the argument.

Socrates
And here's something else. Wouldn't your own experience of learning to read make you reject that claim anyway?

Theaetetus
What experience?

Socrates
This: when you were learning, what you spent your time doing was trying to distinguish each letter by itself, by eye and by ear, so that their arrangement in speech and writing wouldn't confuse you.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
And at the music teacher's, wasn't a student fully trained exactly when he could follow each note and say which string it belonged to? And notes, as everyone would agree, are the letters — the elements — of music.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Socrates
So if we're to argue from the elements and combinations we ourselves have experience of to all the others, we'll say that the class of elements admits of knowledge that is far clearer, and more decisive for mastering any subject, than knowledge of the complex. And if anyone claims that the complex is by nature knowable and the element unknowable, we'll assume he's joking — whether he means to or not.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Socrates
I think more proofs of this could be found, too. But let's not lose sight of our question while chasing them. The question was: what does it actually mean to say that the most perfect knowledge is true opinion plus an account?

Theaetetus
Right — we need to look at that.

Socrates
Well then, what is this "account" supposed to mean? I think it must mean one of three things.

Theaetetus
What are they?

Socrates
The first would be: making your thought plain through your voice, with verbs and nouns — your opinion mirrored in the stream of speech flowing from your lips, the way a reflection appears in water or a mirror. Doesn't "giving an account" sound like that sort of thing?

Theaetetus
It does. At least, we do say a person who does that is "expressing himself."

Socrates
But that's something anyone can do, sooner or later — say what he thinks about a thing — anyone, that is, who isn't deaf or mute from birth. And then everyone who has a right opinion will turn out to have it "with an account," and right opinion without knowledge will be found nowhere at all.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So let's not be too quick to convict the man who defined knowledge this way of talking nonsense. Maybe that's not what he meant. Maybe he meant the second thing: that when you're asked what something is, you should be able to give your questioner an answer in terms of its elements.

Theaetetus
Meaning what, for example, Socrates?

Socrates
For example — Hesiod says of a wagon, "a hundred timbers make a wagon." Now, I couldn't name all hundred, and neither could you. If someone asked us what a wagon is, we'd be happy to say: wheels, axle, body, rails, yoke.

Theaetetus
We certainly would.

Socrates
And he'd probably laugh at us — the way he'd laugh if we were asked your name, answered syllable by syllable, and then claimed to be expert grammarians. We might think and speak the syllables correctly, but we'd be holding a true opinion, not knowledge. Knowledge, on this view, means going through the thing element by element — true opinion plus a complete run-through of the elements — just as we said before.

Theaetetus
Yes, we did say that.

Socrates
In the same way, a person can have a true opinion about a wagon; but the one who can lay out its being by going through all hundred timbers — he, by doing that, has added the account to his true opinion. Instead of mere opinion, he's gained mastery and knowledge of what a wagon is, by working through the whole by means of its elements.

Theaetetus
And doesn't that seem right to you, Socrates?

Socrates
Tell me if it seems right to you, my friend. Is going through a thing element by element an account of it, while going through it by syllables — or any bigger chunks — is no account at all? Settle that first, so we can examine it.

Theaetetus
Yes — I say it's exactly the element-by-element way.

Socrates
Then tell me: do we think a man knows a given element if, at one time, he counts it as part of one thing and, at another time, counts it as part of something else — or if he believes that the same thing has now one element, now another?

Theaetetus
Definitely not.

Socrates
And don't you remember that's exactly what you and everyone else did when first learning letters?

Theaetetus
You mean we'd assign the wrong letters to syllables — think a letter belonged in one syllable one time, and a different one the next?

Socrates
That's what I mean.

Theaetetus
Then by Zeus, I do remember, and I'd never say people at that stage have knowledge.

Socrates
So look: at that stage of learning, someone writing "Theaetetus" thinks he should write TH and E, and does; then, setting out to write "Theodorus," he thinks he should write T and E, and does. Are we going to say he knows the first syllable of your names?

Theaetetus
No — we've just agreed that someone in that condition doesn't yet know.

Socrates
And nothing stops him from being in the same condition about the second syllable, and the third, and the fourth?

Theaetetus
Nothing at all.

Socrates
Now, suppose at some point he writes "Theaetetus" with every letter in order. At that moment, he has the element-by-element run-through plus right opinion?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
But he's still without knowledge — though his opinion is right. We agreed?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And yet he has the account along with the right opinion. He wrote the letters out in their proper order, and that's what we said the account was.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So there is such a thing, my friend, as right opinion with an account that doesn't yet deserve to be called knowledge.

Theaetetus
It looks that way.

Socrates
So what we thought was the perfect definition of knowledge was, apparently, only a dream after all. But let's not pass sentence yet. There's still the third meaning of "account" left — remember, we said the person who defines knowledge as right opinion with an account had three options. Maybe this is the one he'd choose.

Theaetetus
Good reminder — one meaning does remain. The first was the image of thought in speech; the second, the one we just finished, was the road to the whole through the elements. What's the third?

Socrates
What most people would say: being able to state some mark by which the thing you're asked about differs from everything else.

Theaetetus
Can you give me an example?

Socrates
Take the sun, if you like. I imagine you'd accept this as an account of it: the sun is the brightest of the bodies that move through the heavens around the earth.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Socrates
And here's the point of the example. It's what we just said: if you grasp the difference that sets a thing apart from everything else, then — so some people claim — you've grasped its account. But as long as you've only got hold of something it shares with other things, your account will cover everything that shares that common feature.

Theaetetus
I see. And calling that kind of thing an "account" seems right to me.

Socrates
So whoever takes a right opinion about any thing and adds its difference from everything else will have come to know what he previously had only an opinion about.

Theaetetus
That's our claim, yes.

Socrates
Well, Theaetetus, now that I've come up close to this theory, like a painting viewed at arm's length, I can't make out a thing. From a distance it looked like it might amount to something.

Theaetetus
What? Why?

Socrates
I'll try to explain. Say I have a right opinion about you. If I add your account to that, then I know you; if not, I merely have an opinion.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
And an account, we said, is the interpretation of your differentness.

Theaetetus
True.

Socrates
So back when I was a mere opinion-haver — my mind didn't grasp a single one of the points where you differ from everyone else?

Theaetetus
Apparently not.

Socrates
Then what I had in mind was some common feature — something that belongs no more to you than to anybody else.

Theaetetus
That must be so.

Socrates
But then, by Zeus, how on earth was it you I had an opinion about, rather than anyone else? Suppose my thought is: "Theaetetus is the one who's a man, with a nose and eyes and a mouth," and so on through every limb. Will that thought make me think of Theaetetus rather than Theodorus — or, as they say, the remotest barbarian?

Theaetetus
How could it?

Socrates
And if I think not just "the one with a nose and eyes" but "the one with a snub nose and bulging eyes" — will my opinion be about you any more than about me, or about anyone else who fits that description?

Theaetetus
Not at all.

Socrates
No — I won't have Theaetetus in my mind, I think, until that snub-nosedness of yours has stamped and lodged in me a memory-trace different from every other snub nose I've ever seen — and the same for every other feature of you. Then, if I run into you tomorrow, that trace will remind me and give me a right opinion about you.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Socrates
So right opinion, too, must already involve the difference of its object?

Theaetetus
Clearly.

Socrates
Then what's left for this "adding an account" to right opinion? If it means forming an opinion of how the thing differs from everything else, the instruction becomes downright comical.

Theaetetus
How so?

Socrates
It tells us to get a right opinion of the way things differ from other things — when we already have a right opinion of the way they differ from other things. At that rate, a treadmill spinning in the same circle — or a pestle, or any machine that goes round and round — is nothing compared to this instruction. "The blind leading the blind" would be the fairer name for it. Telling us to acquire what we already have, so that we can learn what we already believe — that's the work of a mind completely in the dark.

Theaetetus
Then what's the alternative? What were you about to suggest when you raised the question just now?

Socrates
Well, my boy — if "adding an account" means coming to know the difference, not just having an opinion about it, then this finest of all definitions of knowledge turns into a fine joke. Because knowing is acquiring knowledge, isn't it?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Socrates
So when someone asks "What is knowledge?" our splendid definition will apparently answer: "Right opinion plus knowledge — of the difference, or of whatever." Since that's what "adding the account" turns out to mean.

Theaetetus
So it seems.

Socrates
And it's utterly silly, when we're trying to find out what knowledge is, to answer "right opinion plus knowledge" — whether of difference or of anything else. So, Theaetetus, knowledge is not perception, and not true opinion, and not true opinion with an account added either.

Theaetetus
Apparently not.

Socrates
Well then, my friend — are we still pregnant? Is anything about knowledge still kicking inside us, or have we delivered it all?

Theaetetus
Honestly, Socrates, thanks to you I've said more than I ever had in me.

Socrates
And my midwife's art declares the whole brood stillborn — wind-eggs, all of them, not worth raising. Doesn't it?

Theaetetus
It absolutely does.

Socrates
Then here is what we've gained. If you ever try to conceive again, Theaetetus, and you succeed, what you carry will be the better for today's examination. And if you stay empty, you'll be easier on the people around you — gentler, and humble enough not to think you know what you don't know. That much my art can do; no more. I know none of the things that other men know — the great and admired men of today and yesterday. But this midwife's work my mother and I received from God: she delivered women, and I deliver young men — young men who are noble and fair.

And now I have an appointment at the Porch of the King, to answer the indictment Meletus has filed against me. But tomorrow morning, Theodorus, let's meet here again.
