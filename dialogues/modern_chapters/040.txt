PHILEBUS
Part 1

Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates
  Protarchus
  Philebus


Socrates
All right, Protarchus, pay attention. You're about to take over the position that Philebus has been defending, and here's the position I'm arguing against it. If you don't like mine, you'll need to challenge it. Shall we lay out both sides?

Protarchus
By all means.

Socrates
Philebus has been saying that enjoyment, pleasure, delight, and everything in that family are what's good for every living being. I'm arguing the opposite -- that it's not pleasure but wisdom, intelligence, memory, and their relatives -- sound judgment and true reasoning -- that are better and more worth having for anyone capable of them. For anyone who can share in these things, now or in the future, they're the most beneficial things in the world. Have I stated both positions fairly, Philebus?

Philebus
Perfectly fair, Socrates.

Socrates
And you, Protarchus -- do you accept the position you've been handed?

Protarchus
I don't have much choice, since our excellent Philebus has bowed out.

Socrates
Well, we certainly need to get to the truth of this matter, one way or another.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Can we also agree on this --

Protarchus
On what?

Socrates
That you and I should try to identify some condition of the soul, some state of being, that has the power to make all people happy.

Protarchus
Yes, absolutely.

Socrates
And you're saying that pleasure is that state, while I'm saying it's wisdom?

Protarchus
That's right.

Socrates
But what if there's a third option that's better than either one? In that case, we'd both lose. But if this truly happy life turns out to be closer to pleasure than to wisdom, then the life of pleasure still beats the life of wisdom.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And if the better life turns out to be closer to wisdom, then wisdom wins and pleasure loses. Agreed?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
What do you say, Philebus?

Philebus
I say, and I'll always say, that pleasure wins easily. But you'll have to make up your own mind, Protarchus.

Protarchus
You've handed the argument over to me, Philebus -- you don't get a say anymore.

Philebus
Fair enough. I'm washing my hands of this. And I call the goddess herself as my witness that I'm doing so.

Protarchus
You can appeal to us too -- we'll be witnesses to your words. Now, Socrates, whether Philebus likes it or not, let's get on with the argument.

Socrates
Then let's start with the goddess herself -- Philebus says she's called Aphrodite, but her real name is Pleasure.

Protarchus
Very good.

Socrates
The reverence I feel about the names of the gods, Protarchus, goes beyond ordinary human fear -- it surpasses every other worry I have. So I won't risk offending Aphrodite by naming her wrongly. Let her be called whatever she likes. But Pleasure -- that's what I want to talk about, and as I was just saying, we need to begin by understanding her nature. She has one name, so you might think she's one simple thing. But she actually takes the most varied and even contradictory forms. Think about it: don't we say that a reckless person gets pleasure, and that a self-controlled person gets pleasure from his very self-control? Don't we say that a fool feels pleasure when he's full of foolish daydreams and hopes, and that a wise person feels pleasure in his wisdom? Wouldn't anyone who claimed all these opposite pleasures were the same be talking nonsense?

Protarchus
Well, Socrates, they come from opposite sources, sure, but they're not opposites in themselves. I mean, pleasure has to be the one thing most completely like pleasure -- like itself, right?

Socrates
Sure, my friend -- just like color is like color. As far as being colors goes, there's no difference between them. But we all know that black isn't just unlike white -- it's the exact opposite. And shape is like shape -- all shapes belong to one category -- but particular shapes can be totally opposed to each other, and there's an infinite variety of them. We could find similar cases in lots of other things. So don't rely on this argument that would prove the most extreme opposites are really the same. I suspect we'll find the same kind of opposition among pleasures.

Protarchus
Very likely. But how does this undermine the argument?

Socrates
Because even though pleasures are different from each other, you're applying a single label to all of them: you're calling them all "good." Now, nobody can deny that pleasure is pleasure. But someone can argue -- as we're doing -- that pleasures are more often bad than good. Yet you call them all good, while being forced to admit, if pressed, that they're quite different from each other. So tell me: what identical quality exists in both good and bad pleasures that makes you call them all "good"?

Protarchus
What do you mean, Socrates? Do you think anyone who says pleasure is the good is going to accept that some pleasures are good and others bad?

Socrates
And yet you'll admit they're different from one another, and sometimes even opposed?

Protarchus
Not insofar as they're pleasures.

Socrates
That's just going in circles, Protarchus. So we're supposed to say there's no difference between pleasures -- that they're all alike? And those examples I just gave don't penetrate our thick skulls, and we just keep arguing the same way, like the weakest, most inexperienced debaters?

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
I mean that in self-defense I could follow your example and boldly claim that two completely unlike things are absolutely identical. And the result would be that we'd both prove ourselves to be complete amateurs in reasoning, and the whole argument would be blown away. Let's back up and start fresh -- maybe we can actually come to an understanding.

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
Protarchus, should I have you put my own question to me?

Protarchus
What question?

Socrates
Ask me whether wisdom, knowledge, and intelligence, and those other qualities that I said were good when you first asked me -- aren't they in the same boat as your pleasures?

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
The kinds of knowledge are numerous and will turn out to have major differences between them. But even if some of them prove to be opposed to each other as well as different -- just like pleasures -- would I deserve to be called a philosopher if I tried to dodge this by saying there's no difference between one kind of knowledge and another? The argument would sink like a bad story, and we'd only survive by clinging to a fallacy.

Protarchus
Well, let's not let that happen to us -- except the surviving part! But I do like the even-handed fairness being applied to both sides. So let's agree: there are many diverse pleasures, and many different kinds of knowledge.

Socrates
And let's not hide the differences between my candidate for the good and yours, Protarchus. Let's bring them into the light, because in the process of testing them, they may show us whether pleasure should be called the good, or wisdom, or some third thing. We're not just competing so that my view or yours wins -- we should both be fighting for the truth.

Protarchus
We certainly should.

Socrates
Then let's nail down the principle this argument rests on, and make our understanding more precise.

Protarchus
What principle?

Socrates
A principle that everyone always has trouble with, and that sometimes catches people off guard.

Protarchus
Be more specific.

Socrates
The principle that just came up -- it's a genuine marvel of nature. The idea that one thing should be many, or many things should be one -- these are extraordinary claims, and anyone who asserts either one is wide open to attack.

Protarchus
Do you mean when someone says that I, Protarchus, am by nature one person but also many -- splitting the single "me" into many versions of "me," even making them opposites of each other: big and small, light and heavy, and ten thousand other ways?

Socrates
Those are the common, well-known puzzles about the one and the many, Protarchus. Everyone has basically agreed by now to set those aside as childish and obvious and actually harmful to real thinking. And people aren't any more impressed by that other trick -- where someone proves that the parts and members of something can be divided up, then admits they're all one thing, and says with a laugh, refuting his own words: "Look at this miracle! The one is many and infinite, and the many are only one!"

Protarchus
But Socrates, what are those other marvels connected with this subject that, as you're suggesting, haven't yet become common knowledge?

Socrates
When the "one," my boy, doesn't belong to the class of things that are born and perish -- like in those everyday examples -- because in those cases, where the unity is something concrete, there's universal agreement that no serious argument is needed. But when someone claims that "human being" is one, or "beauty" is one, or "the good" is one -- that's when genuine interest and controversy arise from the attempt to divide these unities.

Protarchus
What kind of controversy?

Socrates
First: do these unities even really exist? Then: how can each one, being always the same and incapable of coming into being or being destroyed, maintaining its permanent identity -- how can it be conceived as dispersed and multiplied across the infinite world of things that come and go? Or as being somehow whole and yet divided from itself -- which seems like the greatest impossibility of all, since how can one and the same thing be in one place and in many at the same time? These, Protarchus, are the real difficulties about the one and many. If you resolve them badly, they're a source of endless confusion. But if you resolve them well, they're enormously helpful.

Protarchus
Then, Socrates, let's start by clearing up these questions.

Socrates
That's exactly what I'd like to do.

Protarchus
And I'm sure all our friends here will be glad to hear them discussed. Philebus, luckily for us, isn't inclined to stir -- we'd better not provoke him with questions.

Socrates
Good. So where should we begin this great and many-sided battle, with so many issues at stake? How about this?

Protarchus
How?

Socrates
We say that the one and the many become identified through thought, and that now, as always, they run around together in and out of every word we speak. This union of them will never stop -- it's not something that just started. It is, I believe, an eternal property of thought itself, which never grows old. Any young person who first encounters these puzzles is thrilled -- he thinks he's discovered a treasure of wisdom. In the first flush of excitement, he turns over every stone -- or rather, every idea. First he rolls the many into one, kneading them together; then he unfolds and divides them again. He confuses himself first and foremost, then goes on to confuse everyone around him -- older, younger, his own age, makes no difference. He doesn't spare his father, his mother, anyone with ears to hear. He'd barely spare his dog, and a foreigner would have no chance of escaping him -- if only an interpreter could be found.

Protarchus
Socrates, considering how many of us there are, and that we're all young men, aren't you worried that we and Philebus might gang up on you if you insult us like this? We get what you're saying, though. But isn't there some way to clear up all this confusion -- some better method of getting at the truth? If there is, we hope you'll guide us to it, and we'll do our best to follow. This inquiry we're engaged in, Socrates, is not a trivial one.

Socrates
Far from trivial, my boys -- as Philebus calls you. And there never was and never will be a better method than my own favorite, though it has often abandoned me and left me helpless in my hour of need.

Protarchus
Tell us what it is.

Socrates
A method that's easy to point out but very hard to apply. It's the parent of every discovery that any skill or art has ever produced.

Protarchus
Tell us!

Socrates
A gift from heaven, which I believe the gods tossed down to humans through some new Prometheus, along with a blaze of light. And the ancients -- who were better than us and lived closer to the gods -- passed down this tradition: whatever things are said to exist are composed of one and many, and have the finite and the infinite built into them. Since that's how the world is ordered, we should begin every inquiry by positing a single Form for whatever we're investigating. We'll find this unity in everything. Once we've found it, we look for two beneath it -- if there are two -- or if not, three, or some other number, subdividing each of those units in the same way, until we see that the original unity is not just one and also many and infinite, but a specific number of kinds. We must not let the infinite touch the many until we've discovered the entire number of species that fall between the one and the infinite. Only then, after that, may we stop dividing and let the endless individuals drop off into infinity. This is the way of investigating, learning, and teaching one another that the gods handed down to us. But the clever people of our day jump too fast or too slow between one and many. They have no method. They go from unity straight to infinity, and the intermediate steps never occur to them. And that's what separates mere argument from genuine dialectic.

Protarchus
I think I partly follow you, Socrates, but I'd like a clearer picture of what you're saying.

Socrates
I can illustrate with the letters of the alphabet, Protarchus -- the ones you learned as a child.

Protarchus
How do they illustrate it?

Socrates
The sound that passes through our lips -- whether from one person or everyone -- is one, and yet infinite in variety.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
But we don't become experts in language by knowing just that sound is one, or that sound is infinite. What makes someone a grammarian is knowing the number and nature of the different sounds.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And the same kind of knowledge makes someone a musician.

Protarchus
How so?

Socrates
Sound is one thing in music too, just as in grammar, right?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And there are higher notes and lower notes, and notes of equal pitch -- we can agree on that much?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
But you wouldn't be a real musician if that were all you knew. On the other hand, if you didn't even know that much, you'd know practically nothing about music.

Protarchus
Nothing at all.

Socrates
But when you've learned which sounds are high and which are low, and the number and nature of the intervals between them, and their limits and proportions, and the systems built from them -- the ones our predecessors discovered and passed down to us under the name of "harmonies" -- and the corresponding patterns in bodily movement, which when measured by numbers are called rhythms and meters, and that this same principle should be applied to every case of the one and the many -- when you've learned all that, my dear friend, then you've truly mastered the subject. And you can be said to understand any other subject when you've grasped it the same way. But the infinity of types and the infinity of individuals within each type, when they haven't been classified, makes every one of us infinitely ignorant. And someone who never looks for number in anything will never be numbered among the wise.

Protarchus
I think what Socrates is saying is excellent, Philebus.

Philebus
I think so too, but how does this relate to us and our argument?

Socrates
Philebus is right to ask us that question, Protarchus.

Protarchus
He certainly is, and you need to answer him.

Socrates
I will. But first let me add one small point. I was saying that someone who starts with a single unity shouldn't jump to infinity, but should proceed to a definite number of kinds. Now I'll say the reverse: someone who has to start with infinity shouldn't leap straight to unity either. He should look for some definite number that represents the real variety, and only through those arrive at one. Let's go back to the alphabet for an illustration.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Some god -- or divine man, who in the Egyptian legend is said to have been Theuth -- noticed that the human voice was infinite in variety, and was the first to distinguish within that infinity a definite number of vowels. Then he identified other letters that have sound but aren't pure vowels -- the semivowels -- and these too exist in a definite number. Third, he identified a class of letters we now call mutes -- without voice and without sound -- and divided these, and the vowels and semivowels too, into their individual sounds, counting the number of each. Seeing that none of us could learn any one of them without learning all of them, and recognizing this common bond that united them, he assigned them all to a single art and called it grammar.

Philebus
The illustration, Protarchus, has helped me understand the original point better, but I still feel the same gap I just complained about.

Socrates
You're going to ask, Philebus, what this has to do with our argument?

Philebus
Yes -- that's the question Protarchus and I have been asking for a while now.

Socrates
But you've actually already arrived at the answer, haven't you?

Philebus
How so?

Socrates
Didn't we start by asking which is the better choice -- pleasure or wisdom?

Philebus
Certainly.

Socrates
And we're saying each of them is one thing?

Philebus
True.

Socrates
Well, the exact question that our whole discussion has been building toward is this: how are they each one and also many -- how does each have one general kind but many specific types? And what definite number of types belongs to each of them before they dissolve into infinite individual instances?

Protarchus
That's a very serious question, Philebus, and Socrates has cleverly steered us right to it. Consider which of us should answer him. There's something a bit absurd in my not being able to answer when I've taken over the whole argument -- but if neither of us can answer, that's even worse. Let's think about what to do. Socrates, if I understand you correctly, is asking whether there aren't different kinds of pleasure, and what their number and nature are -- and the same for wisdom.

Socrates
Exactly right, son of Callias. And our earlier discussion showed that if we can't identify the kinds within anything that has unity, likeness, or sameness -- or their opposites -- none of us will be the slightest use in any inquiry.

Protarchus
That seems very close to the truth, Socrates. It would be wonderful if the wise person could know all things, and the next best thing is to know yourself. But why am I saying this right now? I'll tell you. You, Socrates, have given us this chance to talk with you and are ready to help us figure out what the greatest human good is. When Philebus said it was pleasure and delight and enjoyment and all that, you disagreed and offered a different class of goods. And we keep reminding ourselves of what you said -- quite rightly -- so we can examine and compare the two. These goods that you consider superior to pleasure, the real objects worth pursuing, are mind and knowledge and understanding and skill, and the like. There was a dispute about which were better, and we playfully warned you that we wouldn't let you go home until the question was settled. You agreed and put yourself at our disposal. And now, as children say, what's been fairly given can't be taken back. So stop fighting us like this.

Socrates
Fighting you how?

Philebus
Stop confusing us and keep asking questions we can't properly answer yet. Let's not imagine that the whole point of this discussion is just to baffle everyone. If we can't answer, you answer -- you promised you would. So make up your mind: are you going to divide pleasure and knowledge into their different kinds, or would you rather drop it and find some other way to resolve our disagreement?

Socrates
Well, if you put it that way, I've got nothing to worry about, because the words "if you're willing" take away all my fear. And besides, a god seems to have reminded me of something.

Philebus
What's that?

Socrates
I remember hearing, a long time ago -- whether I was awake or dreaming, I can't say -- certain arguments about pleasure and wisdom. The conclusion was that neither one is the good, but rather some third thing, different from both and better than either. If that's clearly established, then pleasure loses the contest, because the good won't be identified with her anymore. Right?

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
And there won't be any need to sort out the different kinds of pleasure -- or at least, that's what I'm inclined to think. But this will become clearer as we go.

Protarchus
Excellent, Socrates. Please continue.

Socrates
But first, let's agree on a few small points.

Protarchus
What are they?

Socrates
Is the good perfect, or imperfect?

Protarchus
The most perfect of all things, Socrates.

Socrates
And is the good sufficient?

Protarchus
Yes, certainly -- sufficient beyond everything else.

Socrates
And this much nobody can deny: every conscious being desires and pursues the good, is eager to get hold of it, and doesn't care about getting anything that isn't accompanied by good.

Protarchus
Undeniable.

Socrates
Now let's separate the life of pleasure from the life of wisdom and examine each one.

Protarchus
How do you mean?

Socrates
Let's imagine a life of pleasure with no wisdom in it, and a life of wisdom with no pleasure. Because if either of them really is the supreme good, it shouldn't need anything else. But if either one turns out to be lacking something, then it can't really be the supreme good.

Protarchus
That makes sense.

Socrates
Will you help me put both lives to the test?

Protarchus
Of course.

Socrates
Then answer me.

Protarchus
Ask.

Socrates
Would you choose, Protarchus, to live your entire life enjoying the greatest pleasures?

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
And if you had perfect pleasure, would you feel that anything was still missing?

Protarchus
Not at all.

Socrates
Think carefully. You wouldn't want wisdom, or intelligence, or the ability to plan ahead? You wouldn't even want eyesight?

Protarchus
Why would I? If I have pleasure, I have everything.

Socrates
And living like this, you'd enjoy the greatest pleasures throughout your whole life?

Protarchus
I would.

Socrates
But if you had no mind, no memory, no knowledge, no sound judgment -- you wouldn't even know whether you were enjoying yourself or not, since you'd be completely devoid of intelligence.

Protarchus
Right, I suppose.

Socrates
And without memory, you wouldn't remember that you'd ever felt pleasure. Not the slightest trace of the pleasure you're feeling right now would stay with you from one moment to the next. Without sound judgment, you wouldn't even think you were feeling pleasure while you were feeling it. And without the ability to reason, you couldn't anticipate future pleasure. Your life wouldn't be a human life at all -- it would be the life of an oyster, or a jellyfish. Could it be otherwise?

Protarchus
No.

Socrates
Is that a life anyone would choose?

Protarchus
I can't answer you, Socrates. The argument has struck me speechless.

Socrates
Don't lose heart. Let's turn now to the life of the mind and examine that.

Protarchus
What do you mean by the life of the mind?

Socrates
Would anyone choose to live with all the wisdom, intelligence, knowledge, and memory in the world, but with absolutely no sensation of pleasure or pain -- completely unaffected by those feelings?

Protarchus
Neither life appeals to me, Socrates, and I can't imagine anyone else would choose either one.

Socrates
What about a combination of the two -- a life made from joining both together?

Protarchus
You mean pleasure combined with mind and wisdom?

Socrates
Exactly.

Protarchus
Everyone -- not just some people, but everyone -- would choose this third life over either of the other two, hands down.

Socrates
But do you see what follows from that?

Protarchus
Of course. Two out of three proposed lives are neither sufficient nor desirable -- not for humans, not for any living thing.

Socrates
So now it's clear that neither pleasure alone nor wisdom alone contains the good. Because whichever one contained it would be completely sufficient and perfect and desirable for every living creature capable of such a life. And anyone who chose differently would be choosing against the nature of what's truly desirable -- not by free choice, but from ignorance or some unfortunate compulsion.

Protarchus
That certainly seems right.

Socrates
So I think I've shown well enough that Philebus' goddess can't be identified with the good.

Philebus
Your "mind" isn't the good either, Socrates -- it's vulnerable to the same objections.

Socrates
Maybe my "mind" is, Philebus -- but the true, divine mind? That's a different story. Still, I won't claim first place for mind over the mixed life just yet. What we need to figure out now is the second place. You might say pleasure is the cause of the mixed life's goodness. I might say mind is. And in that case, even though neither would be the good itself, one of them might be the cause of the good. And I could go further and argue against Philebus that the element making this mixed life desirable and good is more closely related to mind than to pleasure. If that's true, pleasure can't honestly claim even second place -- let alone first. She doesn't even reach third.

Protarchus
Honestly, Socrates, it does look like pleasure has taken a fall. She stepped into the ring fighting for the championship, and the argument has knocked her flat. And I have to say, mind showed good judgment in not making the same claim to first place -- she would have been knocked down too. But if pleasure loses not just first place but second as well, she'd be badly damaged in the eyes of her fans. Even they wouldn't find her as attractive as before.

Socrates
Well, shouldn't we just leave her alone then? Why subject her to the ultimate test and expose her further?

Protarchus
Don't be ridiculous, Socrates.

Socrates
What -- because I said we shouldn't "pain" pleasure? That's impossible, isn't it?

Protarchus
That, and the fact that none of us is going to let you go home until you finish the argument.

Socrates
Good grief, Protarchus, that's going to take a while. And right now it's not going to be easy. Going to battle on behalf of mind, competing for second place -- I'm going to need different weapons from the ones I used before. Though some of the old ones might still work. Do I really have to finish?

Protarchus
Of course you do.

Socrates
Then let's be very careful in laying the foundation.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Let's divide everything that exists into two classes -- or rather, if you don't mind, three.

Protarchus
What principle of division?

Socrates
Let's use some of the ideas we just discovered.

Protarchus
Which ones?

Socrates
We were saying, weren't we, that god revealed a finite element in existence, and also an infinite one?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Let's take those as two of our principles, and add a third: what's produced by combining them. Though I'm afraid I'm rather clumsy at these processes of classification and counting.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
I'm realizing we need a fourth class too.

Protarchus
What would that be?

Socrates
The cause of that combination -- the thing that produces the third class by mixing the other two. Add that as a fourth.

Protarchus
And would you like a fifth as well -- a cause of separation, to go with the cause of combination?

Socrates
I don't think so, not right now. But if I need a fifth later, you'll let me add one?

Protarchus
Of course.

Socrates
Let's start with the first three. Two of them are enormously spread out and scattered, so let's try to gather each of them back together and see how each is both one and many.

Protarchus
If you could explain a bit more, maybe I could follow.

Socrates
The two classes I mentioned are the same ones from before: the finite and the infinite. I'll show first that the infinite is in a certain sense many, and we can deal with the finite afterward.

Protarchus
All right.

Socrates
Now think carefully, because this is a tricky, contested question. When you talk about "hotter" and "colder," can you imagine any fixed limit in those qualities? Doesn't the "more" and "less" that lives in their very nature prevent them from having any endpoint? Because if they had an endpoint, the "more" and "less" would themselves have to end.

Protarchus
That's absolutely true.

Socrates
We're always saying that the hotter and the colder always have a "more" and a "less" in them.

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
So the argument shows that they never reach an end. And being endless, they must also be infinite.

Protarchus
Yes, Socrates, that's exactly right.

Socrates
Good catch, my dear Protarchus. And your answer reminds me that this word "exactly" that you just used, and words like "gently" or "extremely," have the same significance as "more" and "less." Wherever they show up, they don't allow for fixed quantity -- they're always introducing degrees into things, setting up a comparison of more intense versus less intense, more gentle versus less gentle. At each step, definite quantity disappears. Because as I was saying, if quantity and measure were allowed into the realm of more-and-less and the other comparatives, those comparatives would be driven out of their own territory. Once you introduce a definite quantity, there can't be any "hotter" or "colder" anymore -- because those are always progressing, never standing still. But definite quantity stands still and stops progressing. This proves that comparatives like "hotter" and "colder" belong in the class of the infinite.

Protarchus
What you're saying certainly looks true, Socrates, though as you mentioned, these subjects are hard to follow at first. I think if I could hear the argument one more time, we'd be in solid agreement.

Socrates
Fair enough, and I'll try to accommodate you. But to save time rather than going through endless examples, let me ask: can I use this as the hallmark of the infinite?

Protarchus
Use what?

Socrates
Whatever seems to admit of "more" and "less," or is described by words like "extremely," "gently," "intensely," and the like -- can we assign all of that to the class of the infinite? That's its unifying principle. Remember our earlier point -- that things which are scattered and divided should be brought together and stamped with the mark of a single nature, if possible?

Protarchus
I remember.

Socrates
And everything that doesn't admit of more and less, but instead admits precise opposites -- equality, the equal, the double, any definite ratio of number and measure -- all of that, I think, we can properly assign to the class of the finite. What do you say?

Protarchus
Excellent, Socrates.

Socrates
Now, what nature should we assign to the third class -- the combined one?

Protarchus
I think you'll have to tell me that.

Socrates
No, god will tell you -- if any god will listen to my prayers.

Protarchus
Then pray, and think!

Socrates
I am thinking, Protarchus, and I believe some god has come to our aid.

Protarchus
What do you mean? What's your evidence?

Socrates
I'll tell you -- just listen.

Protarchus
Go on.

Socrates
Weren't we just talking about hotter and colder?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
Add to them drier and wetter, more and less, faster and slower, greater and smaller -- everything that we previously placed under the unity of "more and less."

Protarchus
In the class of the infinite, you mean?

Socrates
Yes. Now mix that with the other class.

Protarchus
Which other class?

Socrates
The class of the finite, which we should have gathered together just as we did the infinite. But we can do it now -- the result will be the same. When the two are combined, a third thing will emerge.

Protarchus
What do you mean by the class of the finite?

Socrates
The class of the equal and the double -- any class that puts an end to opposites being at war with each other, and by introducing number creates harmony and proportion among the different elements.

Protarchus
I see -- you're saying that when you mix the infinite with the class of the finite, various definite forms emerge.

Socrates
Yes, that's exactly what I mean.

Protarchus
Go on.

Socrates
Doesn't the right participation of the finite bring about health? In cases of disease, for example?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And whereas high and low, fast and slow are infinite and unlimited, doesn't the introduction of those same principles of limit bring them under control and perfect the entire framework of music?

Protarchus
Yes, certainly.

Socrates
Or again, when cold and heat are running wild, doesn't the introduction of limit take away the excess and indefiniteness, and bring moderation and harmony?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And from a similar mixture of the finite and the infinite come the seasons, and all the good things of life?

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
I'm leaving out ten thousand other examples -- beauty, health, strength, and the many excellences of body and soul. Philebus, my handsome friend, the goddess saw the universal excess and wickedness that comes when there's no limit to pleasures and self-indulgence, and she devised the limit of law and order. You say, Philebus, that she torments the soul by doing this. I say she saves it. What do you think, Protarchus?

Protarchus
Her methods suit me perfectly, Socrates.

Socrates
You'll notice I've described three classes?

Protarchus
Yes, I think I understand. You mean the infinite is one class, and the finite is a second class of things. But I'm not entirely sure about the third.

Socrates
That's because the amazing variety of the third class overwhelms you, my friend. But the infinite wasn't difficult -- even though it also contained many varieties -- because they were all unified by the mark of "more and less," and so they appeared as one.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And the finite didn't have many divisions either -- we easily recognized it as naturally one.

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
Right. So when I speak of the third class, understand me to mean any offspring of these two -- a birth into true existence, brought about by the measures that the limit introduces.

Protarchus
I understand.

Socrates
But we said there was a fourth class still to investigate, and you need to help. Doesn't everything that comes into existence necessarily come into existence through some cause?

Protarchus
Yes, of course -- how could anything exist without a cause?

Socrates
And isn't the agent the same as the cause, differing only in name? The agent and the cause are really one and the same thing?

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
And the same goes for the patient, or effect -- they differ only in name too, don't they?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
The cause always naturally leads, and the effect naturally follows?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
So the cause and what is produced by it are not the same, but different?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And weren't the things that were produced, along with the materials from which they were produced, what filled our first three classes?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And the maker or cause of them has been satisfactorily shown to be distinct from all three -- so it can be called a fourth principle?

Protarchus
Yes, let's call it that.

Socrates
Good. Now that we've distinguished all four, I think we should refresh our memories by reviewing each one in order.

Protarchus
By all means.

Socrates
The first I'll call the infinite, or unlimited. The second is the finite, or limited. Third comes the combination -- a mixed reality that's both compound and generated. And I don't think I'll be far wrong in calling the fourth the cause of mixture and generation.

Protarchus
Not far wrong at all.

Socrates
Now, what's our next question? How did we get here? Weren't we asking whether second place belongs to pleasure or to wisdom?

Protarchus
We were.

Socrates
And now, having worked through these four classes, won't we be better equipped to settle the original dispute about first and second place?

Protarchus
I'd think so.

Socrates
We said, if you remember, that the mixed life -- pleasure combined with wisdom -- was the winner. Right?

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
And we can see what this life is and which class it belongs to?

Protarchus
Without a doubt.

Socrates
It clearly falls in the third, the mixed class -- which isn't a mixture of just two particular ingredients, but of all the elements of the infinite, bound together by the finite. So we can truly say that it includes the winning life.

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
And what about your life, Philebus -- the life of pure sweetness? Which of these classes does that belong in? Let me ask you a question before you answer.

Philebus
Go ahead.

Socrates
Do pleasure and pain have a limit, or do they belong to the class that admits of more and less?

Philebus
They belong to the class that admits of more, Socrates. Pleasure wouldn't be perfectly good if she weren't infinite in quantity and degree.

Socrates
And pain wouldn't be perfectly evil either, Philebus. So it can't be the infinite that gives pleasure its degree of goodness. Let's set that aside. But let me ask this -- and Protarchus and Philebus, please think carefully, because making a mistake here would be very serious: which class do wisdom, knowledge, and mind belong to?

Philebus
You're making too big a deal of your favorite god, Socrates.

Socrates
And you're making too big a deal of your favorite goddess. But still, please answer the question.

Protarchus
Socrates is right, Philebus -- we have to answer.

Philebus
Didn't you volunteer to answer for me, Protarchus?

Protarchus
I did, but I'm stuck now. I have to ask you, Socrates, to be our spokesperson, so we don't say something wrong or disrespectful about your candidate.

Socrates
I'd better do as you say, Protarchus. It's not that hard, actually. But did I really throw you off with my playful seriousness when I asked what class mind and knowledge belong to, as Philebus is suggesting?

Protarchus
You certainly did, Socrates.

Socrates
And yet the answer is easy. All philosophers agree with one voice that mind is the king of heaven and earth -- which is really just a fancy way of flattering themselves. And maybe they're right! But I'd like to examine this class of mind a bit more thoroughly, if you don't object.

Philebus
Take whatever approach you like, Socrates. We won't get tired of you -- don't worry about going long.

Socrates
Thank you. Let's begin, then, Protarchus, with a question.

Protarchus
What question?

Socrates
Is this entire thing we call "the universe" ruled by unreason and blind chance? Or, as our predecessors declared, is it ordered and governed by a marvelous intelligence and wisdom?

Protarchus
Those two views couldn't be more different, my dear Socrates! What you just suggested first sounds almost like blasphemy. But the other view -- that mind orders all things -- is worthy of the beauty of the sun, the moon, the stars, and the entire sweep of the heavens. I'll never say or think otherwise.

Socrates
Then should we agree with our predecessors and maintain this doctrine? And not just lazily repeat other people's ideas at no risk to ourselves, but actually take our stand and share the criticism that comes when some clever person says everything is disorder?

Protarchus
That's certainly what I'd want.

Socrates
Then consider the next step of the argument.

Protarchus
Let me hear it.

Socrates
We can see that the elements making up the bodies of all animals -- fire, water, air, and earth (as the storm-tossed sailor cries, "Land!") -- also appear in the makeup of the universe.

Protarchus
That proverb fits us well -- we're certainly storm-tossed in this argument, and at our wits' end!

Socrates
Now here's something to notice about each of these elements.

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
Only a tiny fraction of any one of them exists in us, and a weak, impure version at that -- nothing worthy of its true nature. One example will prove the point for all of them. There's fire within us, and there's fire in the universe.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
The fire in us is small, weak, and dim. But the fire in the universe is magnificent in quantity, beauty, and every power that fire possesses.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And is the fire in the universe fed, generated, and governed by the fire in us? Or is it the other way around -- the fire in you and me and every other animal depends on the universal fire?

Protarchus
That question hardly deserves an answer.

Socrates
Right. And you'd say the same about the earth in animals versus the earth in the universe, and the same for all the other elements?

Protarchus
Anyone who answered differently would have to be out of his mind.

Socrates
Fair enough. Now, when we see all these elements gathered together in one place, we call them a body, don't we?

Protarchus
We do.

Socrates
And the same can be said of the cosmos -- for the same reason it can be considered a body, since it's made up of the same elements.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And does our body get its nourishment entirely from this cosmic body, or does the cosmic body get its nourishment from ours?

Protarchus
That's another question that doesn't deserve to be asked.

Socrates
Well, how about this question -- is this one worth asking?

Protarchus
What question?

Socrates
Can our body be said to have a soul?

Protarchus
Obviously.

Socrates
And where did that soul come from, my dear Protarchus -- unless the body of the universe, which contains elements like those in our bodies but finer in every way, also has a soul? What other source could there be?

Protarchus
No other source, Socrates -- that's clearly the only one.

Socrates
Because surely we can't imagine the following, Protarchus. We have these four classes -- the finite, the infinite, their combination, and the cause -- and this fourth one, the cause, enters into all things, giving our bodies souls, providing the art of self-care and medicine, operating in countless other ways to heal and organize, possessing all the attributes of wisdom. We can't seriously imagine that while these same elements exist in the entire heaven and throughout great regions of the universe -- only finer and purer -- this same cause somehow failed to create the noblest and finest things up there too.

Protarchus
That would be completely unreasonable.

Socrates
So if we reject that idea, shouldn't we adopt the other view and maintain that there is in the universe a mighty infinite and a sufficient limit -- as we've often said -- along with a presiding cause of real power that orders and arranges years, seasons, and months? This cause may justly be called wisdom and mind.

Protarchus
Most justly.

Socrates
And wisdom and mind can't exist without soul?

Protarchus
Certainly not.

Socrates
So you'd say that in the divine nature of Zeus, there is soul and mind belonging to a king, because in him there is the power of the cause? And other gods have other attributes that they like to be known by?

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
Don't think these words are thrown around carelessly, Protarchus. They're in harmony with the ancient thinkers who said that mind rules the universe.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And they give us our answer. They imply that mind belongs to that class of the four which we called the cause of everything. Now you have my answer.

Protarchus
I do indeed -- though I didn't quite notice when you gave it.

Socrates
A joke can be refreshing sometimes, Protarchus, when it breaks up a serious argument.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
So, my friend, I think we've now established pretty clearly which class mind belongs to and what its power is.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And the class that pleasure belongs to was identified long ago?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
Then let's remember both results. First, mind is related to the cause and essentially belongs to that family. Second, pleasure is infinite and belongs to the class that has no beginning, middle, or end of its own, and never will.

Protarchus
I'll be sure to remember.

Socrates
Our next task is to examine where pleasure and pain arise and under what conditions they're generated. And we'll start with pleasure, since we examined her class first. But pleasure can't be properly tested apart from pain.

Protarchus
If that's the road, let's take it.

Socrates
I wonder whether you'd agree with me about where pleasure and pain come from.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
I'd say their natural home is in the mixed class.

Protarchus
Can you remind me again, dear Socrates, which one is the mixed class?

Socrates
I'll do my best, good sir.

Protarchus
Please do.

Socrates
The mixed class is the one we placed third in our list of four.

Protarchus
The one that followed the infinite and the finite -- the one where you placed health and, if I'm not mistaken, harmony?

Socrates
Excellent. Now please give me your full attention.

Protarchus
Go ahead -- I'm listening.

Socrates
I'm saying that when the harmony in living beings is disrupted, their natural state breaks down, and that's when pain occurs.

Protarchus
That sounds very plausible.

Socrates
And when harmony is restored and nature returns to its proper state, that's the source of pleasure -- if I can put the biggest idea into the fewest words.

Protarchus
I think you're right, Socrates, but could you try to be a little clearer?

Socrates
Don't ordinary, everyday experiences provide the simplest illustrations?

Protarchus
Like what?

Socrates
Hunger, for example, is a disruption -- and painful.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And eating is a restoration -- and pleasurable?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
Thirst is a destruction and a pain, but when moisture replenishes what's dried out, that's pleasure. And when unnatural heat causes separation and breakdown, that's painful, but when the body's natural coolness is restored, that's pleasant.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And when moisture in the body freezes unnaturally, that's pain, but the natural process of thawing and returning to the normal state is pleasure. In short, here's the general principle -- tell me if it seems right: whenever the natural combination of finite and infinite that makes up living beings is destroyed, that's pain. And whenever everything returns to its proper nature, that's pleasure.

Protarchus
Granted -- what you say has a general truth to it.

Socrates
So here we have one kind of pleasure and pain, each arising from the two processes we've described.

Protarchus
Good.

Socrates
Now let's add that in the soul itself, there's an anticipation of pleasure -- sweet and refreshing hope -- and an expectation of pain -- anxious and fearful.

Protarchus
Yes -- that's another kind of pleasure and pain, belonging to the soul alone, apart from the body, produced by expectation.

Socrates
Right. And in analyzing these pure cases -- pleasure unmixed with pain, and pain unmixed with pleasure -- I think we'll be able to see clearly whether the entire class of pleasure is something desirable, or whether that quality of being wholly desirable really belongs to one of the other classes we've mentioned. We might find that pleasure and pain are like heat and cold -- sometimes desirable, sometimes not -- because they're not good in themselves, but only sometimes participate in the nature of good.

Protarchus
You're absolutely right that this is the trail our investigation should follow.

Socrates
Well then: if we accept that pain arises from the disruption of harmony, and pleasure from its restoration, let's ask what happens when a living creature is experiencing neither disruption nor restoration. Think about this carefully: I'm asking whether any creature in that condition could possibly feel any pleasure or pain, great or small.

Protarchus
No, certainly not.

Socrates
So here we have a third state -- beyond both pleasure and pain?

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
Keep this in mind -- it'll make a big difference in how we judge pleasure. Let me say a few words about it.

Protarchus
Go ahead.

Socrates
There's no reason why a person who chooses the life of wisdom can't live in this neutral state.

Protarchus
You mean he could live without either rejoicing or suffering?

Socrates
Yes. And if I remember correctly, when we compared the different lives, we agreed that no degree of pleasure, great or small, was necessary for someone who chose the life of thought and wisdom.

Protarchus
Yes, we did say that.

Socrates
So such a person would live without pleasure. And who knows -- maybe that's the most divine life of all.

Protarchus
If so, then the gods, at least, must experience neither joy nor sorrow.

Socrates
Certainly not -- there would be something deeply inappropriate about attributing either to them. But whether the gods are indifferent to pleasure is a point we can take up later if it becomes relevant. Whatever we decide, we'll credit it to mind's account in her contest for second place -- assuming she has to concede first.

Protarchus
Fair enough.

Socrates
Now, the other class of pleasures -- the ones we said are purely mental -- comes entirely from memory.

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
I need to analyze memory first -- or really, perception, which comes before memory -- if we're going to get clear on this.

Protarchus
How will you proceed?

Socrates
Let's imagine that some physical sensations die out before they reach the soul, leaving her unaffected. And other sensations vibrate through both body and soul, sending a shock through each of them.

Protarchus
Granted.

Socrates
And we'd be right in saying the soul is unaware of the first kind but not the second?

Protarchus
Quite right.

Socrates
Now, when I say "unaware," don't think I mean "forgetfulness" in the literal sense. Forgetfulness is losing a memory, but in this case no memory was ever formed. You can't lose something that never existed and never entered your mind. You see the point?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
So just change the terminology for me.

Protarchus
How should I change it?

Socrates
Instead of saying the soul "forgets" when she's unaffected by bodily sensations, call it "unconsciousness."

Protarchus
I see.

Socrates
And the union or communion of soul and body in a single feeling and movement -- we'd properly call that "consciousness"?

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
So now we know what we mean by that term?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And memory, I think, can be rightly described as the preservation of consciousness?

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
But don't we distinguish memory from recollection?

Protarchus
I think so.

Socrates
By recollection, don't we mean the soul's power to recover on her own some feeling she originally experienced in company with the body?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And when she recovers on her own a lost memory of some conscious experience or piece of knowledge, that recovery is what we call recollection and reminiscence?

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
There's a reason I'm going through all this.

Protarchus
What is it?

Socrates
I want to get the clearest possible picture of pleasure and desire as they exist in the soul alone, apart from the body. And the analysis we've just done helps reveal the nature of both.

Protarchus
Good -- let's move on to the next point, Socrates.

Socrates
There are certainly many things to consider about how pleasure works and what it really is. But first we need to figure out the nature and origin of desire.

Protarchus
Yes, let's look into that -- we've got nothing to lose.

Socrates
Actually, Protarchus, we've got something to lose -- the puzzle! Finding the answer means losing the problem.

Protarchus
Fair point! But let's keep going.

Socrates
We placed hunger, thirst, and similar feelings in the class of desires, didn't we?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
But they're very different from each other. What common nature do we have in mind when we call them by a single name?

Protarchus
By heaven, Socrates, that's not an easy question to answer. But it has to be answered.

Socrates
Then let's go back to our examples.

Protarchus
Where shall we start?

Socrates
What do we mean when we say "a person is thirsty"?

Protarchus
What do we mean?

Socrates
We mean he's empty.

Protarchus
Of course.

Socrates
And isn't thirst a desire?

Protarchus
Yes -- a desire for drink.

Socrates
For drink, or for being filled with drink?

Protarchus
For being filled with drink, I'd say.

Socrates
So a person who is empty desires the opposite of what he's experiencing -- he's empty and desires to be full?

Protarchus
Clearly.

Socrates
But how can someone who's empty for the first time reach any idea of fullness, which he has no present experience of and no past memory of?

Protarchus
He can't.

Socrates
And yet the person who desires certainly desires something?

Protarchus
Of course.

Socrates
He doesn't desire what he's experiencing -- he's experiencing thirst, which is emptiness. What he desires is fullness.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
So there must be something in the thirsty person that somehow grasps the idea of fullness?

Protarchus
There must be.

Socrates
And it can't be the body -- the body is the thing that's empty.

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
The only remaining possibility is that the soul grasps fullness through memory. What else could it be?

Protarchus
I can't think of anything else.

Socrates
But do you see what follows?

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
There's no such thing as bodily desire.

Protarchus
Why not?

Socrates
Because the argument shows that every living creature's striving is directed toward the opposite of its bodily condition.

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And the impulse driving it toward the opposite proves that it has a memory of that opposite state.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
So by showing that memory draws us toward the objects of desire, the argument also proves that all impulses, desires, and the driving force in every living being originate in the soul.

Protarchus
That's absolutely right.

Socrates
The argument simply won't allow us to say that our bodies hunger or thirst or experience anything similar.

Protarchus
Exactly right.

Socrates
Let me make one more observation. The argument seems to point to a certain kind of life that consists in these experiences.

Protarchus
What experiences, and what kind of life?

Socrates
I'm talking about being emptied and filled, and everything related to the preservation and destruction of living beings -- the pain felt in one state and the pleasure that follows in the other.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
But what about the in-between state?

Protarchus
What do you mean by "in-between"?

Socrates
When someone is actually suffering but remembers past pleasures that would relieve him if they'd only come back -- but he doesn't have them yet. Can't we say he's in an in-between state?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Would you say he's entirely in pain or entirely in pleasure?

Protarchus
Neither, I'd say. He has two pains: in his body there's the actual experience of pain, and in his soul there's the longing and the waiting.

Socrates
What do you mean by "two pains," Protarchus? Couldn't someone who's empty sometimes have a confident hope of being filled, and at other times be in complete despair?

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And when he's hoping to be filled, isn't the memory giving him pleasure -- even while his emptiness is causing him pain at the same time?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
So humans and other animals can experience both pleasure and pain simultaneously?

Protarchus
I suppose so.

Socrates
But when someone is empty and has no hope of being filled, that's when the double experience of pain occurs. You noticed this and concluded it was the only possibility.

Protarchus
Exactly right, Socrates.

Socrates
Now, should this investigation of feelings give rise to another question?

Protarchus
What question?

Socrates
Whether we should say that the pleasures and pains we're discussing are true or false. Or are some true and some false?

Protarchus
But how, Socrates, can there be false pleasures and pains?

Socrates
And how, Protarchus, can there be true and false fears, or true and false expectations, or true and false opinions?

Protarchus
I'll grant that opinions can be true or false, but not pleasures.

Socrates
What's that? I'm afraid we're opening up a very serious inquiry here.

Protarchus
I agree with that much.

Socrates
But we should consider, my boy -- and you are one of Philebus' boys -- whether this inquiry is relevant to our argument.

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
We can't afford long, irrelevant digressions. What we say should be to the point.

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
I've always been fascinated by the question that's just come up.

Protarchus
How so?

Socrates
You deny that some pleasures are false and others true?

Protarchus
I certainly do.

Socrates
Would you say that no one ever seems to be happy but isn't really, or seems to be in pain but isn't really -- whether dreaming or waking, sane or insane?

Protarchus
That's always been our assumption, Socrates.

Socrates
But were you right? Should we look into whether that assumption holds up?

Protarchus
I think we should.

Socrates
Then let's put the question about pleasure and opinion into more precise terms. Is there such a thing as opinion?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And such a thing as pleasure?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And an opinion must be about something?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And a person must be pleased by something?

Protarchus
Correct.

Socrates
And whether the opinion is right or wrong, it's still an opinion?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And whether someone is rightly or wrongly pleased, won't they still genuinely feel pleasure?

Protarchus
Yes, that's true too.

Socrates
So how can opinion be both true and false, while pleasure is always true -- when both are equally real?

Protarchus
Yes, that's the question.

Socrates
You're saying that opinion takes on the quality of truth or falsity, so it becomes not just opinion but opinion with a certain character. And that's what you think needs to be examined?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And further: even if we admit that other things have qualities, perhaps pleasure and pain are simple experiences without quality?

Protarchus
Right.

Socrates
But it's not hard to see that pleasure and pain do have qualities -- they can be great or small, more intense or less intense. We said that long ago.

Protarchus
Quite true.

Socrates
And if badness attaches to any of them, Protarchus, we should speak of a bad opinion, or a bad pleasure?

Protarchus
Quite true, Socrates.

Socrates
And if rightness attaches to any of them, shouldn't we speak of a right opinion or a right pleasure? And likewise for their opposites?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And if an opinion is wrong about its object, can't we say the opinion is incorrect -- that it's not judging rightly?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And if we see a pleasure or pain that's mistaken about its object, shall we call it right or good, or give it any honorable name?

Protarchus
Not if the pleasure is mistaken -- how could we?

Socrates
And surely pleasure often accompanies an opinion that isn't true but false?

Protarchus
It certainly does. And in that case, Socrates, as we were saying, the opinion is false -- but nobody would call the actual pleasure false.

Socrates
How eagerly you rush to defend pleasure, Protarchus!

Protarchus
No, Socrates -- I'm just repeating what I've heard.

Socrates
But isn't there a difference, my friend, between a pleasure that's linked to right opinion and knowledge, and one that's linked to falsehood and ignorance?

Protarchus
There must be a very great difference.

Socrates
Then let's investigate that difference.

Protarchus
Lead the way -- I'll follow.

Socrates
Here's my view.

Protarchus
What is it?

Socrates
We agree, don't we, that there's such a thing as false opinion and true opinion?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And pleasure and pain, as I just said, often follow from these -- from true and false opinion.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
And don't opinion and the attempt to form opinions always spring from memory and perception?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Could the process work something like this?

Protarchus
Like what?

Socrates
Someone often sees an object in the distance, not very clearly, and wants to figure out what it is.

Protarchus
That happens.

Socrates
Soon he starts asking himself questions.

Protarchus
In what way?

Socrates
He asks himself: "What's that standing by the rock under the tree?" That's the sort of question he'd put to himself when he sees something like that.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
Then he might guess the right answer, whispering to himself: "It's a person."

Protarchus
Very good.

Socrates
Or he might get it wrong and say: "No, it's a statue set up by some shepherds."

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And if someone's with him, he says his thought out loud, and what was just an opinion becomes a statement.

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
But if he's walking alone when these thoughts occur, he may keep them in his mind for quite a while.

Protarchus
Very true.

Socrates
Now, I wonder if you'd agree with my explanation of what's happening here.

Protarchus
What's your explanation?

Socrates
I think the soul at times like these is like a book.

Protarchus
How so?

Socrates
Memory and perception meet up, and together with their attendant feelings they seem to write words in the soul. When this internal scribe writes truly, true opinions and true statements result. But when the scribe within us writes falsely, the results are false.

Protarchus
I agree completely.

Socrates
Then let me introduce you to another artist working alongside the scribe in the chambers of the soul.

Protarchus
Who's that?

Socrates
A painter, who after the scribe has done his work, draws images in the soul of the things the scribe described.

Protarchus
But when and how does he do this?

Socrates
When a person takes opinions or statements -- received through sight or some other sense -- and then visualizes the subjects in his mind. Isn't that a very common mental experience?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And the images that match true opinions and statements are true, while those that match false ones are false?

Protarchus
They are.

Socrates
If we're right so far, another question arises.

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
Do we have this experience only in relation to the present and the past, or the future as well?

Protarchus
I'd say all three -- all times equally.

Socrates
Didn't we say earlier that purely mental pleasures and pains can be anticipations of bodily ones? This means that anticipatory pleasures and pains are about the future.

Protarchus
Absolutely.

Socrates
And all those "writings" and "paintings" that we said are produced in us -- do they relate only to past and present, or to the future too?

Protarchus
To the future, very much so.

Socrates
When you say "very much," you mean that all these mental images are essentially hopes about the future, and that human beings are filled with hopes throughout their entire lives?

Protarchus
Exactly.

Socrates
Now answer me another question.

Protarchus
What?

Socrates
A just, pious, and good person is a friend of the gods, isn't he?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And an unjust, thoroughly bad person is the opposite?

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And as we were just saying, all people are always filled with hopes?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
And these hopes are propositions -- statements that exist in the minds of each of us?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
And the images of hope are also painted in us. A person may often have a vision of a pile of gold, with pleasures piling up, and in the picture he sees himself rejoicing wildly at his good fortune.

Protarchus
True.

Socrates
And can't we say that good people, being friends of the gods, generally have true pictures presented to them, while bad people have false ones?

Protarchus
Certainly.

Socrates
Bad people have pleasures painted in their imagination too, just like good people -- but theirs are false pleasures.

Protarchus
They are.

Socrates
So bad people generally delight in false pleasures, and good people in true ones?

Protarchus
That follows.

Socrates
On this analysis, then, there are false pleasures in people's souls -- ridiculous imitations of the true ones -- and pains of a similar character?

Protarchus
There are.

Socrates
Didn't we agree that someone who holds an opinion has a real opinion, even if it's about things that have never existed, don't exist now, and never will?

Protarchus
Quite true.

Socrates
And that was the source of false opinion -- opining about what doesn't exist. Right?

Protarchus
Yes.

Socrates
So shouldn't we attribute the same kind of character to pleasure and pain -- real but illusory?

Protarchus
What do you mean?

Socrates
I mean this: a person must be admitted to have real pleasure if he's pleased about anything in any way. And he may be pleased about things that don't exist, never existed, and more often than not, never will exist.

Protarchus
Yes, Socrates, that's undeniable.

Socrates
And can't the same be said about fear, anger, and similar emotions -- that they're often false?

Protarchus
Quite so.

Socrates
And can opinions be good or bad in any way other than being true or false?

Protarchus
No other way.

Socrates
And pleasures can't really be called bad except insofar as they're false.

Protarchus
No, Socrates, that's the exact opposite of the truth! Nobody would call pleasures and pains bad because they're false -- they're bad because of some other deep corruption they're susceptible to.
