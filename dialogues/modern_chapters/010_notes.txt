Symposium Part 1 -- Translation Notes
========================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. NESTED NARRATION: The Symposium has a unique frame structure -- Apollodorus
   tells a companion about speeches he heard from Aristodemus, who was at the
   original party. Smoothed this out so the frame doesn't feel awkward. The
   opening is Apollodorus speaking directly to his companion (first person,
   conversational). When we shift into Aristodemus' account of the party,
   the transition is natural. Occasional reminders of the frame ("Aristodemus
   said...") are preserved but kept light so they don't disrupt the flow.

2. SPEAKER VOICES -- Each speaker on Love was given a distinct voice:

   PHAEDRUS: Earnest, literary, a little breathless. He's genuinely moved by
   his subject. References to mythology and poetry are plentiful -- he's the
   well-read young man who believes deeply in the nobility of love. Preserved
   his enthusiasm and his slightly naive sincerity.

   PAUSANIAS: Legalistic, analytical, fond of making distinctions. His speech
   is structured like a legal brief -- "on the one hand... on the other..."
   He's the lawyer of the group. Kept his systematic approach but broke up
   his most convoluted Jowett sentences into clearer logical steps. His
   Heavenly/Common Love distinction is presented as a carefully argued case.

   ERYXIMACHUS: Scientific, pompous, a bit self-important. He can't help
   bringing everything back to his medical expertise ("my craft," "my art").
   He extends the two-Loves framework to medicine, music, astronomy, and
   prophecy with the enthusiasm of someone who thinks he's discovered a
   grand unified theory. Preserved his intellectual ambition and his slight
   ridiculousness -- he's a man who thinks Love explains meteorology.

   ARISTOPHANES: The comic genius. His speech is the crown jewel of this
   section. Made it vivid, physical, funny -- these round creatures rolling
   around on eight limbs like gymnasts, Zeus slicing them like apples,
   Apollo knotting up navels like a cobbler. But underneath the comedy,
   the emotional core is devastating: the idea that each of us is searching
   for our other half, that love is the name for our pursuit of wholeness.
   Let the comedy build to genuine pathos. The Hephaestus passage -- "do
   you want me to melt you into one being?" -- should hit the reader hard.
   Added a few light stage directions ("the party guests staring," "a laugh
   from the party") to bring the scene alive.

   AGATHON: Flowery, poetic, performative. He's a playwright and it shows.
   His speech is all style -- beautiful phrases, elegant structures, but
   philosophically shallow. Socrates will later demolish it. Preserved
   the surface beauty while letting the reader sense the hollowness. His
   catalog of Love's virtues (justice, temperance, courage, wisdom) sounds
   impressive but doesn't hold up to scrutiny, which is exactly the point.

3. THE HICCUP INTERLUDE: This is pure comic business. Aristophanes gets the
   hiccups right when it's his turn to speak, the doctor gives him absurdly
   specific medical advice, and later Aristophanes mocks the whole concept
   of bodily "harmony" by pointing out that his cure involved sneezing and
   tickling. Preserved all the humor. The interlude also serves a structural
   purpose -- it swaps Aristophanes and Eryximachus in the speaking order,
   so the comic genius follows the pompous doctor. Plato knew what he was
   doing.

4. THE DRINKING PARTY ATMOSPHERE: These are friends at a party, hungover
   from last night, negotiating how hard to drink tonight. Made this feel
   real and natural -- like actual friends at an actual party. The decision
   to send the flute-girl away and talk instead should feel like a group
   of intellectuals who genuinely prefer conversation to entertainment.

5. APOLLODORUS' FRAME: He's an intense, slightly manic Socrates-devotee
   who openly insults his listener ("no better than you are right now")
   and is called "Apollodorus the madman" for it. Kept his abrasive
   energy and his unapologetic philosophical snobbery.

6. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's sentences in Pausanias' and Eryximachus'
   speeches are particularly brutal -- sometimes 80+ words with nested
   clauses within nested clauses. Broke these systematically into shorter,
   clearer sentences while preserving the logical structure of the arguments.
   Aristophanes' speech was already more naturally readable in Jowett but
   benefited from more vivid, physical language.

7. SOCRATES' QUESTIONING OF AGATHON: The Socratic method kicks in at the
   end of this section. Kept the question-and-answer rhythm tight and
   natural. Socrates' critique of the other speeches -- "apparently truth
   doesn't matter" -- is sharp and a little rude, as it should be. He's
   genuinely irritated by beautiful falsehoods.

8. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY:
   - "whither he was going" -> "where he was going"
   - "hitherto" -> "until now" / "before"
   - "methinks I have a plan" -> "I've got it"
   - "nay" -> "no" (but kept emphatic uses)
   - "ere long" -> "later" / "soon"
   - "inasmuch as" -> "since" / "because"
   - "the veriest coward" -> "the biggest coward on earth"
   - "I dare say" -> "I'd guess" / "probably"
   - "rejoined" -> "replied" / "said"
   - "enquire" -> "ask"
   - "extempore" -> "off the cuff"
   - "basso-relievo" -> "relief carving"
   - "privy members" -> "genitals"
   - "commence drinking" -> "time for drinking"

9. PAUSANIAS' SPEECH ON LOVE CUSTOMS: This is the most culturally specific
   section. Pausanias discusses Athenian customs around male-male love at
   length, comparing them to customs in other Greek cities. Translated
   faithfully without commentary. The key distinction (Heavenly vs Common
   Love) and the argument that only love pursued virtuously is honorable
   are philosophical points that remain clear in modern English.

10. THE "BALANCED PHRASING" JOKE: Jowett has "Pausanias came to a pause --
    this is the balanced way in which I have been taught by the wise to
    speak." This is Apollodorus cracking a pun on Pausanias/pause. Kept
    the pun and added a parenthetical "(Apollodorus' little joke)" to make
    sure the reader catches it, since puns are easy to miss in translation.

11. GREEK CULTURAL REFERENCES preserved:
    - Aristogeiton and Harmodius (the tyrannicide lovers)
    - The story of Alcestis, Orpheus, Achilles and Patroclus
    - Hephaestus as the smith-god
    - Zeus, Apollo, Asclepius
    - Heraclitus' aphorism about the bow and lyre
    - Homer, Hesiod, Parmenides, Euripides references
    - The deme of Cydathenaeum (kept as-is, minor detail)

12. ARISTOPHANES' MYTH: This is one of the most famous passages in all
    of Plato. Made it vivid and physical -- the round humans rolling
    around, Zeus cutting them like apples, Apollo tying off navels. But
    also let the emotional depth come through: the image of divided
    halves clinging to each other, refusing to eat, dying of grief. The
    Hephaestus passage ("I can weld you together") is deeply moving and
    was treated with care. The speech moves from broad comedy to genuine
    philosophical insight: love is the desire and pursuit of wholeness.

13. SOCRATES' PREAMBLE TO HIS OWN SPEECH: His mock-terror at following
    Agathon, his comparison to Gorgias/Gorgon, his confession that he
    thought truth was the point of praise -- this establishes his method
    before Diotima's speech (which comes in Part 2). Kept his ironic
    self-deprecation while making clear that he's about to upend
    everything the others have said.


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (SYMPOSIUM PART 2)
------------------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: Established here as sharp, ironic, conversational.
   His critique of the other speeches sets up his own approach: truth
   over rhetoric. Diotima's speech should maintain philosophical rigor
   while building emotionally -- the "ladder of love" is both argument
   and vision.

2. THE QUESTIONING METHOD: Socrates has begun his cross-examination of
   Agathon (Love desires what it lacks). This continues at the start of
   Part 2 and leads into the Diotima narrative. The logical chain must
   be preserved step by step.

3. KEY POINT ESTABLISHED: Love is love "of something" and desires what
   it doesn't possess. This is the foundation for everything Socrates/
   Diotima will argue: Love is not beautiful or good (contra Agathon)
   but is the desire FOR beauty and goodness.

4. AGATHON'S POSITION: He's argued Love is the most beautiful, youngest,
   most tender, most just, temperate, courageous, and wise of the gods.
   Socrates is about to dismantle all of this. The reader should remember
   Agathon's claims.

5. PARTY ATMOSPHERE: The group is relaxed, mostly sober, enjoying the
   conversation. Alcibiades hasn't arrived yet. His drunken entrance
   in Part 2 will be a dramatic tonal shift -- should feel like a bomb
   going off in a philosophy seminar.

6. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "Heavenly Love" / "Common Love" (Pausanias' distinction)
   - "Heavenly Aphrodite" / "Common Aphrodite"
   - Contractions throughout
   - "the god Love" / "Love" (capitalized as a deity)
   - Speaker labels on own lines for dramatic exchanges
   - Section breaks (---) between speeches for clarity

7. TONE: The speeches build in quality and sophistication: Phaedrus
   (sincere but simple) -> Pausanias (clever but dry) -> Eryximachus
   (learned but pompous) -> Aristophanes (brilliant and moving) ->
   Agathon (beautiful but hollow) -> Socrates (devastating). Part 2
   should complete this arc with Socrates/Diotima as the philosophical
   climax, followed by Alcibiades' wild, personal, emotionally raw
   speech as the dramatic climax.

8. FORMAT: Using section breaks (---) between speeches with headers
   (PHAEDRUS' SPEECH, etc.) to help the reader navigate. Continue
   this in Part 2 for Socrates' speech and Alcibiades' speech.
