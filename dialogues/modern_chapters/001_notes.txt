Apology — Translation Notes
==============================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. STRUCTURE: Divided the speech into three sections separated by "---"
   as specified: (1) the defense speech, (2) the counter-penalty proposal
   after the guilty verdict, (3) the final addresses to those who condemned
   him and those who acquitted him. These divisions correspond to the three
   actual moments Socrates spoke in court.

2. FORMAT SHIFT: Unlike Euthyphro (dramatic dialogue with speaker labels),
   the Apology is continuous prose — a speech to a jury. Maintained the
   oratorical quality while keeping the conversational voice established in
   Euthyphro. Socrates addresses the jury directly throughout.

3. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's long Victorian periods broken into shorter,
   more muscular sentences. Average sentence length dropped from ~40+ words
   to ~18-22. Rhetorical passages kept slightly longer for oratorical effect;
   argumentative passages kept tight and punchy.

4. THE MELETUS CROSS-EXAMINATION: This section (the back-and-forth about
   who improves the youth, the horse analogy, the atheism contradiction)
   was treated as a dialogue within the speech. Kept the rapid-fire
   question-and-answer rhythm. Socrates' demolition of Meletus should feel
   like a courtroom takedown — his logic is relentless, and the sarcasm
   ("What extraordinarily bad luck I have!") is withering.

5. ARCHAIC VOCABULARY MODERNIZED:
   - "O Athenians" / "O men of Athens" → "men of Athens" (dropped the "O")
   - "enquire" → "investigate" / "question"
   - "hitherto" → "until now" / "so far"
   - "inasmuch as" → "because" / "since"
   - "thither" → "there"
   - "wherefore" → "so" / "that's why"
   - "I dare say" → "I suspect" / "probably"
   - "forsooth" → dropped (no modern equivalent that isn't affected)
   - "the aforesaid doctrines" → "the same things" / rephrased
   - "sundry times and in divers places" → "many times before"
   - "I will endeavour" → "let me try"
   - "deme" → kept as-is (minor detail, context clear)
   - "Prytaneum" → kept, glossed as "the city hall" on first significant use

6. FAMOUS PASSAGES — handled with special care:

   a. THE ORACLE STORY: Preserved the full narrative arc — Chaerephon's
      question, Socrates' bafflement, the systematic testing of politicians,
      poets, and craftsmen. Kept the building pattern and the punchline
      structure of each encounter.

   b. "BY THE DOG" (Socrates' unique oath): Preserved as "by the dog, I
      swear!" Socrates' characteristic oath — an idiosyncratic swearing
      formula (in Greek, "nē ton kuna") that was distinctly his. He used
      it instead of swearing by the gods, perhaps from piety, perhaps
      from irreverence — scholars debate.

   c. THE GADFLY PASSAGE: "I'm like a gadfly — if you'll forgive the
      ridiculous comparison — that the god has attached to this city.
      And the city is like a big, well-bred horse that has grown sluggish
      because of its size and needs to be stung awake." Made the image
      vivid and immediate. Extended the metaphor through the "swatting"
      and "falling back to sleep" — this is one of Socrates' most
      memorable self-descriptions and it needs to land with force.

   d. "THE UNEXAMINED LIFE IS NOT WORTH LIVING": Preserved exactly.
      This is perhaps the most famous sentence in the history of
      philosophy. Placed it in its proper context — Socrates explaining
      why he can't simply shut up and go into exile.

   e. THE CLOSING LINE: "We go our separate ways — I to die, and you to
      live. Which of us goes to the better fate? Only God knows." Kept
      the parallel structure and the devastating final uncertainty.
      Changed "the hour of departure has arrived" to keep it but
      modernized slightly. The singular "God" follows Jowett — Socrates
      uses both singular and plural throughout.

7. SOCRATIC IRONY sharpened throughout:
   - The opening: being "amazed" at his accusers' audacity
   - The Callias conversation: "Lucky Evenus" (deadpan)
   - To Meletus: "What extraordinarily bad luck I have!"
   - "That is exactly my claim" / "Are you so much wiser at your age
     than I am at mine?" — the withering politeness
   - The Prytaneum proposal: suggesting free meals as his "penalty"
     is peak Socratic provocation

8. TONE MANAGEMENT across the three sections:
   - Section 1 (Defense): Ironic, defiant, intellectually devastating.
     Socrates is fighting for his life but refuses to grovel. The humor
     has a hard edge — these are jokes told by a man who knows he might die.
   - Section 2 (Counter-penalty): Provocative, almost impish. The free
     meals proposal is deliberately outrageous. But there's real pain
     underneath — "I have no money" is just a fact.
   - Section 3 (Final words): The tone splits. To his condemners:
     prophetic, stern, warning. To his friends: warm, philosophical,
     serene. The meditation on death as dreamless sleep or as meeting
     Homer — this is Socrates at his most moving. The final line is
     devastating in its simplicity.

9. CONTRACTIONS: Used liberally throughout, as established in Euthyphro.
   "don't," "isn't," "I'm," "that's," "can't," "wouldn't," "I'd,"
   "you'd," "won't," "he's," "we've," "they've," "I'll," "you'll."
   Socrates sounds like a person talking, not a text being read.

10. CULTURAL REFERENCES handled:
    - Aristophanes' "Clouds" — referenced clearly without over-explaining
    - The Sophists (Gorgias, Prodicus, Hippias) — named, roles clear
    - Anaxagoras — identified as source of sun-is-a-rock doctrine
    - Achilles / Hector / Patroclus — the Iliad reference kept in full
    - Battle of Arginusae — context provided within the narrative
    - The Thirty Tyrants — called "the oligarchy of the Thirty"
    - Leon of Salamis — the story told clearly
    - Minos, Rhadamanthus, etc. — kept as judges of the underworld
    - Orpheus, Homer, etc. — kept, no gloss needed
    - "By Hera" — kept (Socrates' oath, used especially in ironic moments)

11. THE WITNESS LIST: Preserved the full catalogue of names (Crito,
    Critobulus, Lysanias, Antiphon, Nicostratus, Paralus, Adeimantus,
    Plato, Aeantodorus, Apollodorus). This passage matters — Socrates
    is pointing at real people in the courtroom. Kept it but made the
    relationships clearer.

12. "MEN OF ATHENS" vs. "GENTLEMEN OF THE JURY": Used "men of Athens"
    as the primary form of address (matching Jowett and the Greek
    "ō andres Athēnaioi"). Occasionally varied with "gentlemen" or
    "my friends" for natural rhythm. In Section 3, where Socrates
    addresses the acquitting jurors, used "my judges" and "my friends"
    — following the text's own distinction.


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (CRITO)
--------------------------------------------

1. SOCRATES' VOICE: Now firmly established across two works — sharp,
   conversational, ironic, generous with self-deprecation but always
   in control. Uses contractions freely. Funny but dead serious. In
   Euthyphro he was playful; in the Apology he was defiant. In Crito
   he should be calm and resolute — a man at peace with his decision.

2. THE VERDICT: Socrates has been condemned to death. The vote was
   close — about 280 to 221 (he says 30 votes would have changed the
   outcome, from a jury of ~501). He proposed free meals at the
   Prytaneum as his counter-penalty, then thirty minae. The jury
   chose death.

3. KEY RELATIONSHIPS established:
   - Crito: named as "my contemporary from the same deme" — old friend
   - Plato: identified as present in court, brother of Adeimantus
   - Apollodorus: named as present
   - Meletus: the young accuser, demolished in cross-examination
   - Anytus: the real power behind the prosecution
   - Chaerephon: dead, but his brother testified about the oracle

4. PHILOSOPHICAL POSITIONS established:
   - Socrates doesn't know whether death is good or bad
   - He won't do what he knows is wrong to avoid what might not be bad
   - The god assigned him his philosophical mission
   - The "divine sign" (daimonion) warns him away from wrong actions
   - He'd rather die than stop philosophizing
   - "The unexamined life is not worth living"

5. TONE for Crito: The Apology ends with serene acceptance. Crito
   opens in prison, with Crito desperately trying to arrange an escape.
   Socrates should be gentle but immovable — the arguments of the
   Apology have already been made. He's not going to flee. The tone
   should be intimate (two old friends talking at dawn) rather than
   oratorical.

6. VOCABULARY ESTABLISHED:
   - "impiety" (consistent with Euthyphro)
   - "corrupting the youth" (the standard charge)
   - "divine sign" / "inner voice" for the daimonion
   - "the god" (singular, for Apollo/the divine generally)
   - "cross-examine" for the elenchus
   - Contractions throughout
   - "men of Athens" as courtroom address (won't recur in Crito)

7. FORMAT: Crito returns to dramatic dialogue format (speaker names
   on their own lines, as in Euthyphro). The Apology's continuous
   prose was unique to its genre (defense speech).

8. THE PERSONIFICATION OF THE LAWS: Crito contains the famous passage
   where Socrates imagines the Laws of Athens speaking to him. This
   should have a different texture from Socrates' normal voice —
   authoritative, parental, slightly stern. It's a rhetorical tour
   de force that needs to feel like a real voice breaking into the
   quiet conversation.
