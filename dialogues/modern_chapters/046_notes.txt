TRANSLATION NOTES — Laws Book 2 (Chapter 046)

OVERVIEW
--------
Laws Book 2 develops the Athenian Stranger's theory of education through music
and dance, connecting it back to Book 1's discussion of drinking parties. The
central argument: education is fundamentally about training pleasure and pain
so that people love what's good and hate what's bad. Music, dance, and even
regulated wine-drinking are tools for shaping the soul.

The book builds to the proposal of three choruses (children, young men, and
elders) and culminates in the "Chorus of Dionysus" — the idea that older
citizens should drink together and sing, using wine to soften their inhibitions
so they can participate in the musical education of the city.

MAJOR TRANSLATION DECISIONS
----------------------------

1. TONE AND VOICE: The Athenian speaks as a wise elder with genuine warmth and
   earnestness — less ironic than Socrates, more legislative and deliberate.
   Maintained this throughout with straightforward, dignified modern English.
   Cleinias and Megillus (who is silent in this book) are treated as respected
   equals, not foils to be demolished.

2. "CONVIVIAL INTERCOURSE" → "communal social gatherings": Jowett's phrase is
   impossibly Victorian. The Greek refers to the shared drinking and socializing
   that Book 1 discussed at length.

3. EDUCATION DEFINITION: The Athenian's extended definition of education
   (pleasure/pain training → harmony with reason → virtue) is one of the most
   important passages in the Laws. Broke Jowett's single massive sentence into
   several clearer ones while preserving the logical chain.

4. THE THREE CHORUSES: Children (sacred hymns), young men under 30 (invoking
   Apollo), and men 30-60 (the Chorus of Dionysus). Kept the structure clear
   and the ages explicit.

5. CHORUS OF DIONYSUS: The central proposal of the book. The Athenian argues
   that older men need wine to overcome their self-consciousness about singing.
   Wine softens the soul "like iron in fire" — a metaphor used twice in the
   book, kept both times for emphasis.

6. EGYPT PASSAGE: The Athenian praises Egypt's practice of fixing artistic forms
   by law for ten thousand years. "Literally true and no exaggeration" is
   Jowett's rendering of the Athenian's emphatic claim — kept because the
   Athenian is clearly making a deliberate rhetorical point about Egyptian
   conservatism.

7. PLEASURE VS. TRUTH IN ART: The extended argument about judging music by
   truth rather than pleasure is the philosophical heart of the book. Key
   distinction: (a) know what the imitation represents, (b) know if it's
   accurate, (c) know if it's well executed. Kept this three-part framework
   crystal clear.

8. POETRY CRITICISM: The passage about poets mixing incompatible elements
   (men's words with women's gestures, free citizens' melodies with slaves'
   rhythms, etc.) anticipates and parallels Republic Book 3's critique.
   Modernized the examples while preserving the social distinctions Plato
   draws.

9. THE JUSTICE-PLEASURE ARGUMENT: The Athenian argues that the most just life
   is also the most pleasant — and that even if it weren't, this would be the
   most useful "noble lie" a legislator could tell. This echoes Republic themes
   but is more pragmatic and less idealistic. The Athenian is willing to say
   "even if this were a lie, it would be the best lie" — kept this frankness.

10. CLEINIAS'S RESISTANCE: Cleinias pushes back at two key points — he's
    reluctant to call the unjust-but-powerful person "miserable" and reluctant
    to call such a life "bad" (as opposed to merely "shameful"). This is
    realistic resistance from a practical Cretan, not philosophical naivety.
    Preserved the gradations of his agreement/disagreement.

11. WINE REGULATIONS: The final passage lays out extremely specific rules about
    who may and may not drink, and when. Kept every rule (no wine before 18,
    moderation until 30, fuller drinking after 40, restrictions for soldiers,
    magistrates, judges, pilots, etc.). The reference to Carthaginian law is
    notable — Plato drawing on comparative legislation.

12. CINYRAS AND MIDAS: Kept these mythological references to extreme wealth
    without added explanation — the context ("wealth surpassing that of
    Cinyras or Midas") makes the meaning clear enough.

13. CADMUS STORY: The "sowing of dragon's teeth" that produce armed warriors.
    Added "dragon's teeth" for clarity since Jowett's "teeth" alone is opaque.

14. TYRTAEUS: Spartan war poet. The Athenian's recurring dig that courage is
    only the fourth virtue (not the first) is a deliberate provocation to his
    Cretan and Spartan companions. Cleinias calls him out on it again here.

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY
-----------------------
- "Stranger" — kept as Cleinias's form of address for the Athenian (matching
  source text)
- "Chorus" / "choral" — kept throughout (not "choir" which implies religious
  singing)
- "Harmony and rhythm" — standard pair throughout
- "Self-control" for sophrosyne / temperance in context
- "The Muses" — always capitalized
- "Dionysus," "Apollo" — standard forms throughout
- Contractions used freely in dialogue: "don't," "isn't," "we've," "they'd,"
  "can't," "wouldn't," "I'd," "you'll," etc.

STRUCTURAL NOTES
-----------------
- Book 2 follows directly from Book 1's discussion of drinking parties
- The book divides roughly into: (1) theory of education through pleasure/pain,
  (2) the three choruses, (3) art criticism and the standard of truth vs.
  pleasure, (4) the Chorus of Dionysus and wine regulations
- The transition to gymnastics at the end sets up Book 3, though the Athenian
  circles back for a "final word" on drinking first
- Megillus is present but silent throughout Book 2
