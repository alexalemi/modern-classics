Athenian
So now, what will this city actually look like? I'm not asking about its name -- that'll probably be determined by geography or the original settlement. Some river or spring, or maybe a local deity, will lend its name to the new colony. What I really want to know is: what's the location? Is it on the coast, or inland?

Cleinias
I'd say, Stranger, that the city we're talking about is roughly eighty stadia from the sea.

Athenian
And are there harbors along the coast?

Cleinias
Excellent harbors, Stranger -- you couldn't ask for better.

Athenian
Oh no. That's not great news. And is the surrounding countryside productive, or does it need a lot of imports?

Cleinias
It hardly needs anything from outside.

Athenian
Is there a neighboring state nearby?

Cleinias
None at all -- and that's actually why the site was chosen. Long ago, the inhabitants migrated away, and the region has been empty for as long as anyone can remember.

Athenian
And does the land have a good mix of hills, plains, and forests?

Cleinias
It's like the rest of Crete in that regard.

Athenian
You mean more rocky than flat?

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
Well, that gives us some hope that your citizens might actually turn out virtuous. If the city were right on the sea, with fine harbors and importing instead of producing its own goods, you'd need some kind of superhuman savior -- a more-than-mortal lawgiver -- to have any chance of keeping the state from moral decay. But the eighty stadia of distance gives us some breathing room, even though the sea is still closer than I'd like, especially with those excellent harbors.

Here's the thing: the sea is a perfectly pleasant daily companion, but it also has a bitter, corrosive quality. It fills the streets with merchants and shopkeepers, and it breeds unreliable, untrustworthy habits in people's souls -- making the state faithless both toward its own citizens and toward other nations.

But there's a silver lining: the country produces what it needs on its own. And because the soil is rugged, it doesn't produce huge surpluses. If it did, there'd be a massive export trade, and gold and silver would come flooding in -- and as we can safely say, nothing is more destructive to a state that aims at justice and noble character. We made this point in our earlier discussion, if you recall.

Cleinias
I remember, and I think we were right then and we're right now.

Athenian
Good. But let me ask -- how's the country supplied with timber for shipbuilding?

Cleinias
There's no fir worth mentioning, no pine, and very little cypress. You won't find much stone-pine or plane-wood either -- the kinds of timber shipwrights always need for the interior of vessels.

Athenian
That's actually another advantage.

Cleinias
How so?

Athenian
Because no city should be able to easily copy its enemies in doing harmful things.

Cleinias
What does that have to do with anything we've been discussing?

Athenian
Remember, my good friend, what I said at the very beginning about Cretan laws -- that they were aimed at one thing only, which was war. You both agreed with that. And I replied that such laws were fine insofar as they promoted virtue, but I criticized them for focusing on only one part of virtue instead of the whole. So now I hope you'll both watch me carefully as I legislate, and hold me accountable if I aim at anything other than virtue -- or at only a piece of it.

Because I believe a true lawgiver is like an archer: he aims only at what's eternally and genuinely good, and he dismisses everything else -- wealth, or any other supposed benefit -- when it's separated from virtue.

Now, when I said that imitating enemies was a bad thing, here's what I was thinking of: consider a situation where a coastal people are harassed by enemies -- the way the Athenians were by Minos. I'm not bringing up old grievances here, but Minos was a great sea power who forced the people of Attica to pay him a terrible tribute. Back then, they had no warships -- not like they do now -- and the country didn't have much shipbuilding timber, so they couldn't easily build a fleet. That meant they couldn't learn to copy their enemy's naval tactics and fight back at sea by becoming sailors themselves.

And honestly? It would've been better for them to have lost those seven youths many times over than to have their heavy-armed infantry -- soldiers who fight on solid ground -- transformed into sailors. Sailors get used to constantly jumping ashore and then sprinting back to their ships. They think there's nothing disgraceful about throwing down their weapons and running away -- and they always have "good reasons" for it. That's the language of naval warfare, and it's anything but worthy of praise. We shouldn't be teaching bad habits -- least of all to the best citizens.

You can see the problem even in Homer. Odysseus rebukes Agamemnon for wanting to drag the ships down to the sea while the Greeks are locked in battle with the Trojans. Odysseus gets angry and says:

"You'd have us haul the ships to sea while the battle rages? That would only fulfill the Trojans' prayers and bring ruin crashing down on us. The Greeks won't keep fighting once the ships are being dragged to the water -- they'll look back over their shoulders and lose heart. Your plan would destroy us."

You see? Homer understood perfectly well that having warships at sea, right next to soldiers on the battlefield, is dangerous. With habits like those, you could train lions to run from deer.

And there's more: naval powers that depend on their fleet for survival can't properly honor the kind of military excellence that actually deserves it. When your safety depends on the pilot, the captain, the oarsman -- all sorts of rather ordinary people -- how can you give honor where it's truly due? A state like that can't get its values straight.

Cleinias
That may be so. But still, Stranger, we Cretans like to say that the battle of Salamis saved all of Greece.

Athenian
Yes, and that's a widely held opinion among both Greeks and non-Greeks. But Megillus and I would say something different: that the battle of Marathon was the beginning of the great deliverance, and the battle of Plataea was its completion -- and that these land battles actually made the Greeks better people. The sea battles at Salamis and Artemisium -- and I'll lump them together, if you don't mind -- well, they helped save Greece, I won't deny that. But they didn't make anyone better.

When we're evaluating what makes a state truly good, we have to look at both its geographical situation and the quality of its laws. Because mere survival -- just staying alive -- isn't the most honorable thing for human beings, no matter what most people think. What matters is living the best possible life while we're alive. I believe we've made this point before.

Cleinias
Yes, we have.

Athenian
Then we only need to ask: are we taking the approach that we've already agreed is best for founding and governing a state?

Cleinias
The best by far.

Athenian
Good. Now let me move to the next question: who will the colonists be? Can anyone come from anywhere in Crete? Is the idea that the population in various cities has grown too large for the available resources? Because I don't think you're planning to send out an open invitation to any Greek who feels like showing up. And yet I notice that settlers have come to Crete from Argos, Aegina, and other parts of Greece. So tell me -- where are you recruiting from for this venture?

Cleinias
They'll come from all over Crete. And among other Greeks, Peloponnesians will be the most welcome. As you rightly noted, there are Cretans of Argive descent. The Cretan people with the finest reputation today are the Gortynians, and they originally came from Gortys in the Peloponnese.

Athenian
Colonization is easier in some ways when the settlers come from a single people -- like a swarm of bees sent out from one hive. This happens when friends leave friends because of population pressure or some similar necessity, or when a faction within a state is forced to emigrate. Sometimes whole cities flee after being completely conquered in war.

Now, this uniformity is an advantage in one respect, but it also creates a problem. There's natural friendship among people who share the same race, language, laws, temples, and religious practices. But colonies made up of such a homogeneous group tend to resist any laws or constitution that differ from what they had back home. Even if the faults of their old laws were what caused the divisions among them in the first place, they still cling to those same customs out of sheer habit -- the very customs that ruined them. This makes them troublesome and rebellious for the leader of the colony, who is supposed to be their lawgiver.

On the other hand, a population drawn from many different sources might be more willing to accept new laws. But getting such a mixed group to work together -- to pull in the same direction, as they say of horses -- is extremely difficult, and it takes years.

Still, nothing does more for the improvement of humanity than good legislation and thoughtful colonization.

Cleinias
I'm sure that's true. But I'd like to understand why you say so.

Athenian
Well, my friend, I'm afraid where my thinking is leading me might sound a bit unflattering to lawmakers. But if the point is relevant, there's no harm in saying it. And really, why am I worrying? I think the same principle applies to pretty much everything human.

Cleinias
What are you getting at?

Athenian
I was going to say that human beings never really legislate at all. It's accidents of every kind that do our legislating for us. Wars overturn governments. The crushing pressure of poverty rewrites laws. Diseases force political innovations -- plagues sweep through, or years of bad harvests pile up one after another. Looking at all this, a person naturally rushes to the conclusion I was heading toward: no mortal truly legislates anything. In human affairs, chance rules almost everything.

And you could say the same about the arts of the sailor, the pilot, the doctor, and the general -- and it would seem perfectly reasonable. But there's something equally true that can be said about all of them.

Cleinias
What's that?

Athenian
That God governs all things, and that chance and opportunity work alongside him in the management of human affairs. But there's also a third factor, a more moderate view: that human skill should play its part too. In a storm, it's surely a huge advantage to have a skilled pilot on board. Wouldn't you agree?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
And doesn't the same principle apply to lawmaking? Even when all the favorable conditions are in place for a state's happiness, the right lawgiver still needs to appear on the scene from time to time.

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Athenian
In every field, the practitioner could rightly pray for certain conditions, and if fortune grants them, then all that's left is to exercise his skill.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And all those other experts we just mentioned -- if asked to name what they'd pray for -- would each offer up their own particular wish?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
And the lawgiver would do the same?

Cleinias
I believe so.

Athenian
"All right, lawgiver," we'll say to him, "what conditions do you need in a state before you can organize it properly?" How should he answer? Shall I give his reply?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
He'll say: "Give me a state governed by a tyrant. And let the tyrant be young, with a good memory, quick to learn, courageous, and noble by nature. And let him have that quality which, as I said earlier, must accompany all the other parts of virtue if any of them are to be worth anything."

Cleinias
I think, Megillus, that the companion virtue the Stranger is talking about must be self-control?

Athenian
Yes, Cleinias -- self-control in the ordinary sense. Not the kind that certain philosophers inflate into some grand notion of "prudence," but the natural restraint that even children and animals can display -- some living with self-discipline, others without it. When this quality stands alone, without the other virtues, we said it's hardly worth counting as a real good. You understand what I mean.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
So our ideal tyrant must have this quality along with all the others, if the state is to acquire the best possible form of government in the shortest time. Because there isn't -- and never will be -- a faster or more efficient way to establish a good political system than through a tyranny.

Cleinias
Wait -- how on earth can anyone accept such a shocking claim, Stranger?

Athenian
It's really not hard to see, Cleinias -- it just follows the natural order of things.

Cleinias
You're saying we should assume a tyrant who's young, self-controlled, quick to learn, with a good memory, courageous, and noble?

Athenian
Yes -- and add "fortunate." His good fortune must be that he lives at the same time as a great lawgiver, and that some happy accident brings them together. When that happens, God has done the best thing he ever does for a state he wants to make truly prosperous. The second-best scenario is a state with two such rulers working together, and the third-best is three. The difficulty grows as the number increases, and shrinks as it decreases.

Cleinias
So you're saying that the best government emerges from a tyranny -- specifically, from the combination of a good lawgiver and a well-ordered tyrant -- and that the transformation from tyranny to an ideal constitution happens most easily? Less easily from an oligarchy? And least easily from a democracy?

Athenian
Not quite. What I mean is: the transformation is easiest starting from a tyranny, second easiest from a monarchy, third from some form of democracy. Oligarchy comes fourth -- it has the hardest time making this kind of change, because power is held by many potentates.

Here's my reasoning: I'm assuming the lawgiver is the real deal -- a genuinely great one -- and that his strength is united with that of whoever holds power. When the ruling element is small in number but very strong -- as in a tyranny -- the change is likely to be fastest and smoothest.

Cleinias
How so? I don't understand.

Athenian
I've said this several times now. But maybe you've just never actually seen a city under a tyrant?

Cleinias
No, and I can't say I have any great desire to see one.

Athenian
Well, if you did, you'd see exactly what I'm describing.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
I mean you'd see how easily, and how quickly, a tyrant can change the character of an entire state -- if he wants to. He just has to lead the way, whether toward virtue or vice. He sets the example through his own behavior, praising and rewarding certain actions, criticizing others, and disgracing those who disobey.

Cleinias
But how can we expect ordinary citizens to just follow the leader's example overnight? How can he have the power both to persuade and to compel them?

Athenian
Don't let anyone tell you, my friends, that there's a faster or easier way for states to change their laws than when the rulers lead from the front. That kind of change has never happened any other way, and it never will.

No, the real impossibility -- the truly rare thing -- is something different. It's the thing that's almost never achieved across entire ages. But when it does happen, ten thousand blessings follow -- or rather, every blessing imaginable.

Cleinias
What are you talking about?

Athenian
The difficulty is finding a genuine love of self-control and justice in any powerful form of government -- whether it's a monarchy or an oligarchy based on wealth or birth. You might as well hope to see another Nestor, who is said to have surpassed all men in eloquence but surpassed them even more in his self-restraint. That, however, was back in the days of Troy. In our own time, there's nothing of the sort. But if such a person has ever existed, or does exist now, or will someday exist -- blessed is he, and blessed are those who hear the wise words from his lips.

And this holds true as a general principle: when supreme power coincides with the greatest wisdom and self-control in the same person, that's when the best laws and the best constitution come into being. Never any other way. So let's treat what I've been saying as a kind of sacred legend, an oracle. And let this be our proof that, from one angle, it can be terribly difficult for a city to get good laws -- but from another angle, nothing could be easier or quicker, if our ideal conditions are met.

Cleinias
How do you mean?

Athenian
Let's have some fun, shall we? Old boys like us, amusing ourselves by shaping in words the laws that would suit your state.

Cleinias
Let's get on with it, without delay.

Athenian
Then let's invoke God at the founding of our state. May he hear us and be gracious, and come help us establish our city and its laws!

Cleinias
May he come!

Athenian
But what form of government are we going to give this city?

Cleinias
Could you spell out what you mean a bit more? Are you talking about democracy, oligarchy, aristocracy, or monarchy? Surely you're not suggesting tyranny.

Athenian
All right -- which of you will go first and tell me what his own government should be called?

Megillus
Shouldn't I answer first, since I'm the elder?

Cleinias
Perhaps you should.

Megillus
Well, Stranger, now that I think about it, I honestly can't say what to call the government of Sparta. It looks like a tyranny to me in some ways -- the power of our Ephors is astonishingly tyrannical. And yet sometimes it strikes me as the most democratic of all cities. But then again, it would be absurd to deny it's an aristocracy. And we also have a kingship held for life, which everyone -- not just Spartans -- agrees is the most ancient monarchy of all. So when you ask me point-blank, I really can't say precisely which of these the Spartan system is.

Cleinias
I'm in the same boat, Megillus. I can't confidently say that the government of Knossos fits neatly into any of those categories either.

Athenian
That's because, my excellent friends, you actually have genuine political systems. But the states I was just listing aren't really systems of government at all -- they're just cities where people are dominated by and enslaved to one faction within the state. Each gets its name from whatever group holds power. But if we're going to name a state after its ruler, then a true state ought to be named after the God who rules over those who are wise.

Cleinias
And who is this God?

Athenian
May I use a myth to help answer your question? Would that be all right?

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
Long ago, in the primeval world -- well before the cities we've been discussing were ever founded -- there's said to have been, in the age of Cronus, a blessed form of rule and a blessed way of life. The best-governed states that exist today are merely copies of it.

Cleinias
Then we definitely need to hear about this.

Athenian
I agree completely -- that's exactly why I brought it up.

Cleinias
Good. Since the story is directly relevant, please give us the whole thing.

Athenian
Here's what the tradition says. In those early days, human life was one of spontaneous abundance -- everything provided without toil. And the reason, they say, was this: Cronus understood what we ourselves have been arguing -- that no human nature, when given supreme power, can manage human affairs without becoming swollen with arrogance and injustice.

Knowing this, he didn't appoint men to rule over other men. Instead, he placed over us beings of a higher and more divine race -- spirits, or what we might call demigods -- to be the kings and rulers of our cities. He did what we do with our flocks of sheep and other domesticated animals. We don't appoint oxen to lord over oxen, or goats over goats. We ourselves, as the superior species, rule over them. In the same way, God, in his love for humanity, set over us these spirits -- a superior race -- who cared for us with great ease and pleasure, both for themselves and for us. They gave us peace, respect for law, an abundance of justice, and freedom from internal strife. They made the human race happy and united.

This tradition -- and it's a true one -- tells us that any city where a mere mortal rules instead of God has no escape from troubles and suffering.

The lesson is this: we should do everything we can to imitate the life that's said to have existed in the days of Cronus. Whatever part of us is immortal -- our reason -- that's what we must listen to, both in our private lives and in public affairs. We should organize our cities and households according to law, and by "law" I mean the rational ordering of mind.

But if a single ruler, or an oligarchy, or a democracy is driven by a soul that chases after pleasures and desires -- endlessly craving, never satisfied, afflicted with an insatiable sickness -- and this corrupted spirit tramples the laws underfoot and becomes master of a state or an individual, then salvation is hopeless.

So, Cleinias -- will you accept this account?

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Athenian
You're aware, aren't you, that people say there are as many types of law as there are types of government? And we've already listed all the commonly recognized forms. Now, this isn't a minor point -- it's actually crucial. Because what we're really arguing about, once again, is the standard for justice and injustice.

Some people say that laws shouldn't aim at military virtue or virtue in general. They say the only proper aim of law is to protect the interests, the power, and the survival of whatever group currently holds power. That, they claim, is the natural definition of justice.

Cleinias
What do you mean exactly?

Athenian
They say that justice is the interest of the stronger.

Cleinias
Could you be more specific?

Athenian
Sure. Their argument goes like this: "Obviously, whoever holds power in any state makes the laws, right?"

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
"And do you really think," they continue, "that any conquering power -- whether it's a tyranny or a democracy or anything else -- makes laws for any reason other than to keep itself in power? That's the first and foremost goal of every set of laws."

Cleinias
What other goal could they have?

Athenian
"And anyone who breaks these laws is punished as a criminal by the very lawgiver who calls those laws 'just.'"

Cleinias
Naturally.

Athenian
"So that's what justice always is, and how it always works."

Cleinias
Well, that's their view -- if they're right about it.

Athenian
Yes -- and this is precisely one of those false principles of government we were discussing.

Cleinias
Which one do you mean?

Athenian
One of the principles we examined when we asked: who ought to rule whom? We concluded that parents should rule children, the older should rule the younger, the noble should rule the common. There were many principles, and they didn't always agree with each other. One of those principles was exactly this one -- the right of the stronger. And we noted that Pindar considered violence natural and justified.

Cleinias
Yes, I remember.

Athenian
So think about it: to whom should our new state be entrusted? Because here's something that has happened time and again throughout history --

Cleinias
What?

Athenian
When there's a power struggle and one side wins, they monopolize the government completely. They refuse any share to the losing side or their descendants. Both sides live in perpetual suspicion -- the winners terrified that someone with memories of past wrongs will rise up against them.

Now, according to our view, these aren't real political systems at all, and the laws they produce aren't real laws. Laws passed for the benefit of one faction rather than the whole state don't deserve the name. States with such laws aren't genuine communities -- they're just warring parties. And their notion of "justice" is meaningless.

I'm saying all this because in our state, I don't intend to give power to anyone simply because he's rich, or strong, or tall, or well-born. The person who is most obedient to the laws -- that's the one who wins the prize. The highest office and the chief religious authority goes to whoever stands first in this competition. The second-highest office to whoever comes second. And so on down the line.

When I call these rulers "servants of the law" or "ministers of the law," I'm not just playing with words. I genuinely believe that the survival or destruction of a state depends on this principle. Where the law is subordinate to the rulers and has no real authority -- I can see that state heading straight for ruin. But where the law stands above the rulers, and the rulers see themselves as servants of the law -- there I see salvation, and every blessing that the gods can give.

Cleinias
Truly, Stranger, you see with the sharp vision of age.

Athenian
Well, yes -- that's how it works. A man's eyesight for these things is weakest when he's young and sharpest when he's old.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Now then -- what's the next step? Shall we imagine the colonists have arrived and are assembled before us, and deliver a speech to them?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
"Friends," we'll say to them, "God, as the ancient tradition tells us, holds in his hands the beginning, middle, and end of all things. He moves in a straight line, following his own nature, toward the fulfillment of his purpose. And Justice walks always at his side, ready to punish those who fall short of the divine law.

"Whoever wants to be happy clings to Justice and follows in her company with humility and good order. But the person who's puffed up with pride -- intoxicated by wealth, or status, or physical beauty -- whose soul burns hot with arrogance, who's young and foolish and thinks he needs no guide or ruler, who imagines he's fit to lead others all by himself -- that person is abandoned by God. And once abandoned, he gathers around him others like himself, and they go leaping about together, throwing everything into chaos. To many people he looks like a great man. But before long, Justice catches up with him, and he pays a penalty no one could argue against. He's utterly destroyed -- along with his household and his city.

"Given that this is how human affairs work, what should a wise person do -- or think, or avoid doing, or avoid thinking?"

Cleinias
It's obvious: every person should resolve to be one of God's followers. There's no question about it.

Athenian
Then what kind of life is pleasing to God and fitting for those who follow him? There's really only one answer, captured in the old saying: "Like is at home with like, when both keep to the right measure." But things that lack measure are at odds with each other and with everything that has it.

Now, God should be our standard of measurement in all things -- not man, as people commonly say (a view attributed to Protagoras). Those words are far more truly said of God than of man. And whoever wants to be dear to God must become, as far as possible, like God. Which means: the person with self-control is God's friend, because that person is like God. The person without self-control is unlike God -- different from him, and unjust. The same principle applies to everything else.

And from this follows what is both the noblest and truest thing that can be said: for a good person, offering sacrifice to the gods, conversing with them through prayers and offerings and every form of worship -- this is the noblest, best, and most effective path to a happy life. It's perfectly right and fitting.

But for a bad person, the opposite is true. A bad person's soul is impure, and neither a good person nor God can properly accept gifts from someone who's polluted. So all the religious devotion of the wicked goes to waste, while the same devotion from a holy person is deeply pleasing to the gods.

This, then, is the target we should aim at. But what weapons do we use, and how do we direct them? Here's where we begin:

First, we declare that after the Olympian gods and the gods of the state, honor should be given to the gods of the underworld. To these, one should offer everything in even numbers, of the second quality, and associated with the unlucky side. The odd numbers, the first-quality offerings, and the things of good omen go to the gods above -- for those who want to hit the mark of true piety.

After these gods, a wise person will honor the spirits or demigods, then the heroes, and after them the private ancestral gods, worshipped according to custom in their sacred places.

Next comes the honor due to living parents -- and this is the first, greatest, and most ancient of all debts. A person should understand that everything he has belongs to those who gave him birth and raised him. He owes them everything he can give -- first from his property, then from his physical service, and then from his soul -- repaying the endless care and labor they poured into him during his helpless infancy, now that they're old and in their greatest need.

Throughout his entire life, he should never speak -- and should never have spoken -- a harsh or disrespectful word to them. For careless, cutting words carry the heaviest penalties. Nemesis, the messenger of Justice, watches over all such matters.

When parents are angry and want to vent their feelings in word or deed, a son should give way to them. A father who feels wronged by his son is understandably furious, after all.

When parents die, the most modest funeral is best -- neither exceeding the traditional expense nor falling short of the honor shown by previous generations. And every year afterward, a person should faithfully pay tribute to the dead, honoring them above all by never neglecting their memory, and setting aside a reasonable portion of his resources for the deceased.

Living this way, we'll receive our reward from the gods and from the powers above us. We'll spend most of our days filled with good hope.

As for how a person should conduct himself toward his children, relatives, friends, fellow citizens -- and how he should handle sacred duties of hospitality and all the relationships that grow from these obligations, with a view to adorning and ordering his life -- these matters the laws themselves will address as we proceed. Partly through persuasion, and partly through force when people's natures don't yield to the persuasion of custom, the laws will, with the gods' help, make our state prosperous and happy.

But there are things that a lawgiver who thinks the way I do needs to say -- things that absolutely must be said -- but that would be out of place if cast in the form of a law. I think he should offer a sample of these things, for his own guidance and for the instruction of those he's legislating for. Then, once he's gone through all these preliminaries as thoroughly as he can, he can turn to the actual work of legislation.

Now, what form will these preliminary statements take? It might be hard to capture them all under a single heading, but I think we can get a sense of the idea if we can guarantee one thing."

Cleinias
What's that?

Athenian
I want the citizens to be as open to persuasion toward virtue as possible. Surely that will be the lawgiver's aim in every law he writes.

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
What I'm proposing seems genuinely valuable to me. I think a person will listen with more patience and goodwill to the lawgiver's instructions when his soul isn't completely unprepared to receive them. Even a small gesture of conciliation wins a hearing -- and that's always worthwhile. Because human beings don't have much natural inclination to become as good as possible, as quickly as possible.

Most people prove the wisdom of Hesiod, who said that the road to wickedness is smooth and can be traveled without breaking a sweat, because it's so short:

"But before virtue, the immortal gods have placed the sweat of labor. Long and steep is the way, and rough at the start. But once you reach the summit, what was hard becomes easy."

Cleinias
Yes, he puts it well.

Athenian
Very true. And now let me tell you what our whole preceding discussion has made me realize.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
Imagine we have a little conversation with our lawgiver. "Well, lawgiver," we say, "speak up. If you know what we ought to say and do, surely you can tell us."

Cleinias
Of course he can.

Athenian
"Didn't we just hear you saying that poets shouldn't be allowed to write whatever they please? That they wouldn't know when their words clashed with the laws and harmed the state?"

Cleinias
That's true -- we did say that.

Athenian
Now, may we respond on behalf of the poets?

Cleinias
What response would we give?

Athenian
This one: "According to a tradition that has always held among us and is accepted by everyone, when a poet sits down on the tripod of the Muse, he's not in his right mind. He's like a fountain, letting whatever flows into him pour freely out. And since his art is imitation, he's often forced to portray people with opposite characters, and so he ends up contradicting himself. He can't even tell which of the things he's said is closer to the truth."

But that's not how law works. The lawgiver can't give two different rules about the same thing -- he must give one and only one.

Take what you just said as an example. There are three kinds of funeral -- too extravagant, too cheap, and just right. You chose the middle one and approved it without qualification. But if I had an extremely wealthy wife and she told me to describe her burial in a poem, I'd praise the lavish kind. A poor, stingy man with little money would go for the cheap option. And a man of moderate means, who was moderate himself, would recommend a moderate funeral.

But you, as lawgiver, can't just say "a moderate funeral." You have to define what "moderate" actually means and how much it costs. Unless you're specific, you're not speaking a language that can become law.

Cleinias
Absolutely not.

Athenian
So should our lawgiver put no preface before his laws? Should he just say "Do this, don't do that" -- with the threat of punishment dangling over people's heads -- and then move straight to the next law, never offering a word of advice or encouragement to the people he's legislating for?

That would be like certain doctors. And speaking of doctors -- some have a gentler approach, others a rougher one. And just as children beg the doctor to be gentle with them, let's ask our lawgiver to treat our problems with the gentlest remedies possible.

Here's what I mean. Besides doctors, there are doctors' assistants who are also called doctors.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Whether these assistants are slaves or free men makes no difference. They learn medicine by following their masters around, watching and obeying -- picking it up by experience rather than through the kind of systematic, scientific education that free-born doctors receive and then pass on to their own students. You're aware that there are these two classes of doctors?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
And have you noticed that there are also two classes of patients in any state -- slaves and free people? The slave doctors rush around treating slaves, or they wait for them at the dispensary. These practitioners never explain anything to their patients or let the patients describe their own symptoms. The slave doctor just prescribes whatever his experience suggests -- as if he had perfect knowledge -- and then dashes off to the next sick slave with the same brisk confidence. He's relieving the master of the burden of looking after his sick slaves, nothing more.

But the free-born doctor, who treats free people, takes a different approach entirely. He investigates thoroughly, tracing the illness back to its origins. He talks with the patient and with the patient's family, both gathering information and giving instruction. He won't prescribe anything until he's first persuaded the patient to understand and agree. Then, once he's brought the patient around through explanation and built a cooperative relationship, he begins the actual treatment.

Now -- which is the better approach for a physician or a trainer? The one who achieves his goals through both education and treatment? Or the one who works in just one way -- the cruder way?

Cleinias
I'd say, Stranger, the approach that uses both methods is far better.

Athenian
Would you like to see an example of the double and the single method applied to legislation?

What will our first law be? Won't the lawgiver, following the natural order, start by making regulations about births?

Cleinias

Athenian

Cleinias

So, by rights, marriage laws should be the first laws established in any state?

Athenian
All right. Here's the marriage law in its simple form. It might go something like this: "A man shall marry between the ages of thirty and thirty-five. If he doesn't, he'll pay a fine of a specified amount and lose certain privileges."

That's the simple version. The double version would go like this:

"A man shall marry between the ages of thirty and thirty-five, keeping in mind that the human race has a natural share in immortality, which every person instinctively desires to the fullest. The wish to become famous and not lie nameless in the grave is nothing other than this love of continuity. Humanity is as old as time itself, following time's course forever, and so we're immortal in a sense -- because we leave children and grandchildren behind us, participating in immortality through the unbroken chain of generations.

"For a man to deliberately deprive himself of this gift -- as someone who refuses to marry and have children deliberately does -- is an act of impiety. Whoever obeys this law will be left in peace and pay no fine. But whoever disobeys and remains unmarried past the age of thirty-five will pay a yearly fine of a specified amount, so that he doesn't get the idea that bachelorhood brings him comfort and profit. And he'll be excluded from the honors that younger citizens pay to their elders."

Now, comparing these two versions, you can judge for yourself whether laws in general should be double in length -- even at their shortest -- because they need to persuade as well as command. Or whether they should only threaten and be half as long.

Megillus
The shorter form would be more in keeping with Spartan custom, Stranger. But personally, if someone asked which I'd prefer in a state, I'd definitely choose the longer version. And I'd want every law written the same way. But I think Cleinias is the one to ask -- since it's his state that's going to use these laws.

Cleinias
Thank you, Megillus.

Athenian
Whether words should be many or few is a pretty pointless question to argue about in the abstract. What matters is the best form, not the shortest. Of the two versions of the law I just recited, one isn't just twice as long as the other -- it's twice as effective in practice. The analogy I drew to the two kinds of doctors fits perfectly.

And yet it seems like lawgivers have never realized that they have two tools available to them -- persuasion and force. When dealing with the uneducated masses, they use only one tool, as far as they can. They don't blend persuasion with compulsion. They use force, pure and simple.

But there's also a third point, my friends -- something that ought to be considered but never is, in any existing system of laws.

Cleinias
What is it?

Athenian
Something that arises naturally from our whole discussion -- and it's coming to me now almost mysteriously. Think about it: all morning, from daybreak until noon, we've been talking about laws in this beautiful spot. And everything we've said so far has been preliminary -- a prelude. Now we're about to start actually laying down laws. So what was all that earlier discussion? It was the prelude.

Why am I pointing this out? Because all kinds of discourse -- lyric compositions, music of every sort -- have preludes and overtures, carefully crafted artistic introductions designed to prepare the way for what follows. But the higher and truer art of law and politics? No one has ever composed a prelude for that, as though such a thing doesn't exist.

But our discussion today suggests that it does exist. These "double laws" we've been talking about aren't simply double in length. They're actually in two parts: the law itself, and the prelude to the law.

The bare command -- the part I compared to the orders given by the slave-doctor -- that's the law, pure and simple. And the part that comes before it, the part our friend here called merely "persuasive" -- that functions as a genuine prelude, exactly the way a preamble functions before a speech.

Because I'm sure all that conciliatory language the lawgiver uses in the preface to a law is meant to create goodwill in the listener, so that he'll receive the actual command -- the law itself -- with a more receptive and understanding mind. And so, in my view, this preliminary material is more properly called the "preamble" than the substance of the law.

And let me add this: every individual law the lawgiver writes should have its own preamble. He should keep in mind what an enormous difference it makes whether a law has such a preface or not -- as we've just demonstrated with our example.

Cleinias
If you ask me, the lawgiver should absolutely legislate in the way you're recommending.

Athenian
I think you're right, Cleinias, to insist that all laws should have preambles, and that throughout this entire work of legislation, every single law should have an appropriate one at the beginning. Because what follows the preamble is the most important part, and it makes all the difference whether people clearly remember the preamble or not.

At the same time, we shouldn't demand that all laws -- small and great alike -- have preambles of the same kind, any more than all songs or speeches need the same kind of introduction. Preambles may be natural to all laws, but they're not always necessary. Whether to use one or not must be left to the judgment of the speaker, or the musician, or in this case, the lawgiver.

Cleinias

Let's treat them as a proper preamble and not just random topics of conversation.

We've said enough about honoring the gods and respecting parents. Let's move on to the topics that come next, until you consider the preamble complete. After that, you can begin going through the actual laws.

Athenian

Cleinias

Athenian
