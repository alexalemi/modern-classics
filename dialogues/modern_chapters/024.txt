Persons of the dialogue:
  Socrates
  Ion

Ion is a rhapsode -- a professional performer who recites Homer's poetry at
festivals and competitions, part actor, part lecturer, part rock star of the
ancient Greek literary world.


Socrates
Ion! Welcome. Are you just coming from your hometown of Ephesus?

Ion
No, Socrates -- from Epidaurus, where I was at the festival of Asclepius.

Socrates
Do the Epidaurians have competitions for rhapsodes at that festival?

Ion
Oh yes -- for rhapsodes and all kinds of musical performers.

Socrates
And were you competing? How did you do?

Ion
I took first prize, Socrates. First out of everyone.

Socrates
Well done! And I hope you'll do the same for us at the Panathenaea.

Ion
I will, god willing.

Socrates
You know, I've always envied your profession, Ion. You get to wear beautiful clothes -- looking gorgeous is literally part of the job. And on top of that, you spend all your time in the company of great poets, especially Homer, the best and most divine of them all. And you don't just memorize his words -- you have to truly understand what he means. That's the enviable part. Because a rhapsode has to be an interpreter of the poet's mind for his audience, and you can't do that well unless you really grasp what the poet is saying. There's a lot to envy there.

Ion
That's absolutely true, Socrates. Interpretation has been the hardest part of my craft, without question. And I honestly believe I can talk about Homer better than anyone alive. Not Metrodorus of Lampsacus, not Stesimbrotus of Thasos, not Glaucon -- nobody who's ever lived has had as many brilliant insights about Homer as I have.

Socrates
That's wonderful to hear, Ion! I'm sure you won't mind sharing some of those insights with me.

Ion
You really should hear me sometime, Socrates. The way I perform Homer is exquisite. I think the Homeridae themselves should give me a golden crown.

Socrates
I'll definitely make time to hear your performance. But right now I'd like to ask you a question. Does your expertise extend to Hesiod and Archilochus too, or just to Homer?

Ion
Just Homer. He's more than enough all by himself.

Socrates
Are there things Homer and Hesiod both talk about?

Ion
Sure, I'd say quite a lot.

Socrates
And on those topics where they agree -- could you explain what Homer says better than what Hesiod says, or equally well?

Ion
Equally well, Socrates, at least where they agree.

Socrates
But what about topics where they disagree? For example, both Homer and Hesiod have things to say about divination --

Ion
That's true.

Socrates
So who would be the better interpreter of what both poets say about divination, whether they agree or disagree -- you, or a skilled prophet?

Ion
A prophet.

Socrates
And if you were a prophet, wouldn't you be able to interpret them just as well when they disagree as when they agree?

Ion
Obviously.

Socrates
So how did you end up with this talent for Homer alone, and not for Hesiod or any other poet? Doesn't Homer cover the same topics every other poet covers? Isn't war his main subject? And doesn't he also deal with human society -- how people interact, good and bad, skilled and unskilled -- and with the gods talking to each other and to mortals, and with what happens in heaven and the underworld, and with the origins of gods and heroes? Aren't those the themes Homer sings about?

Ion
They absolutely are, Socrates.

Socrates
And don't the other poets sing about the same things?

Ion
Yes, Socrates -- but not nearly as well as Homer.

Socrates
You mean they're worse?

Ion
Far worse.

Socrates
And Homer is better?

Ion
Incomparably better.

Socrates
Now think about this, my friend. When several people are discussing arithmetic and one of them speaks better than the rest, there's someone who can tell which speaker is good. Right?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
And the person who can spot the good speaker will also be the one who can spot the bad speakers?

Ion
The same person.

Socrates
And that person would be the mathematician?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Or take a discussion about whether certain foods are healthy. If several people are speaking and one of them speaks better than the rest, will the person who recognizes the better speaker be different from the person who recognizes the worse one?

Ion
Obviously the same person.

Socrates
And who is that? What do we call him?

Ion
The doctor.

Socrates
So here's the general principle: in any discussion on a given topic, the person who can identify the good speaker will also be able to identify the bad ones. Because if you can't recognize bad speakers on a subject, you won't be able to recognize the good ones either, when the topic is the same.

Ion
True.

Socrates
And the same person is skilled at judging both?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Now, you say Homer and the other poets -- Hesiod, Archilochus -- all deal with the same subjects, just not equally well. Homer does it brilliantly, the others not so much?

Ion
Yes, and I'm right about that.

Socrates
Well, if you can recognize the brilliant speaker, you'd also be able to recognize that the inferior ones are inferior?

Ion
That's true.

Socrates
Then, my friend, I don't think I'm wrong when I say this: if Ion is equally skilled at judging Homer and all other poets -- since he himself agrees that the same person who can judge good speakers on a subject can also judge bad speakers on that same subject, and since virtually all poets deal with the same subjects -- then Ion should be equally skilled with all of them. Right?

Ion
Then how do you explain this, Socrates: when anyone talks about any other poet, I lose interest, I can barely keep my eyes open, I've got absolutely nothing to contribute. But the moment someone mentions Homer, I'm wide awake, fully engaged, and I've got plenty to say?

Socrates
The reason isn't hard to see, my friend. It's obvious. You don't speak about Homer through any skill or knowledge. If you could talk about Homer because of a skill, you'd be able to talk about all the other poets too -- because poetry is a unified whole.

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
And when someone masters any skill as a whole, the same applies to everything within it. Would you like me to explain what I mean, Ion?

Ion
Yes, absolutely, Socrates. I love hearing you brilliant thinkers talk.

Socrates
I wish we deserved the compliment, Ion! But you rhapsodes, actors, and the poets whose work you perform -- you're the brilliant ones. I'm just an ordinary person who speaks the truth. And consider what an ordinary, obvious thing this is that I've said -- something anyone could see: when you've mastered a skill as a whole, the ability to judge good and bad within it is one and the same. Let's think about this. Isn't painting a complete skill -- a unified art?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
And there are and have been many painters, some good, some bad?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Have you ever met anyone who was brilliant at analyzing the work of Polygnotus, son of Aglaophon, and pointing out what's excellent and what's flawed in his paintings, but then when shown the work of any other painter, went blank -- fell asleep, had nothing to say -- and only woke up and had opinions when Polygnotus came up?

Ion
No, I've certainly never met anyone like that.

Socrates
Or in sculpture -- have you ever met someone who could brilliantly discuss the merits of Daedalus, son of Metion, or Epeius, son of Panopeus, or Theodorus of Samos, but when shown the work of other sculptors in general, went blank and had nothing to say?

Ion
No, not that either.

Socrates
And I'd bet you've never met a flute player, or a harpist, or a singer, or a rhapsode who could hold forth brilliantly about Olympus, or Thamyras, or Orpheus, or Phemius the rhapsode of Ithaca -- but drew a complete blank when it came to Ion of Ephesus, and had no idea whether he was any good or not?

Ion
I can't argue with that, Socrates. But here's what I know in my bones, and the whole world agrees with me: I do speak about Homer better than anyone else, and I have more to say about him. But I don't speak equally well about the others. So tell me -- why is that?

Socrates
I can see what's going on, Ion, and I'm going to explain it to you. This talent you have for speaking brilliantly about Homer -- it's not a skill. It's an inspiration. There's a divine force moving you, like the power in that stone Euripides calls a magnet -- most people know it as the Heraclean stone.

That stone doesn't just attract iron rings on its own. It also gives those rings the power to attract other rings. Sometimes you can see a whole long chain of iron rings hanging from each other, one after another, and every single one of them gets its power from that original stone.

That's exactly how it works with the Muse. She starts by inspiring people directly -- and from those inspired people, a chain of others hangs down, each one catching the inspiration from the one above.

Here's the thing about all the great poets, whether they write epics or lyrics: they don't compose their beautiful poems through skill. They compose them because they're inspired -- because something has taken hold of them. It's like the Corybantes, those wild dancers: when they're in their ecstatic dance, they're not in their right minds. That's how it is with the lyric poets when they're creating their gorgeous verses. They fall under the power of music and rhythm, and they become possessed -- like the Bacchic women who draw milk and honey from rivers when Dionysus has them in his grip, but can't do it when they're sober.

And the poet's soul works the same way. The poets themselves tell us this -- they say they gather their songs from honey-flowing springs, plucking them from the gardens and meadows of the Muses. They're like bees, flying from flower to flower. And they're telling the truth.

Because here's what a poet really is: a light, winged, sacred thing. And there's no creative power in a poet until he's been inspired and knocked out of his senses -- until his rational mind has stepped aside. As long as he's still in possession of his reason, he's powerless. He can't compose a thing.

That's why poets speak so many noble words about human action, just like you do when you speak about Homer -- not through any technique, but because the Muse drives them to say exactly that, and nothing else. And when the inspiration hits, one poet writes dithyrambs, another writes hymns, another choral songs, another epics, another satire -- and the one who's great at one kind is no good at the others. This proves it's not skill. If it were skill, they'd be able to work in every genre. No -- God takes away their minds and uses them as his instruments, the same way he uses prophets and oracles. So when we hear them, we should understand that it's not these poets speaking -- these people who've lost their rational minds and are saying priceless things without even knowing it. It's God himself speaking, and he's using them to talk to us.

And here's a perfect example: Tynnichus of Chalcis. He never wrote a single poem worth remembering -- except for that famous paean that everyone knows, one of the most beautiful poems ever composed. He called it "an invention of the Muses," and he was right. It's as if God deliberately chose the worst poet to write the best poem, just to make sure we'd understand the point: these beautiful poems aren't human creations. They're divine. The poets are just interpreters of the gods, each one possessed by whichever god has claimed them. Isn't that the lesson God was teaching us when he had the worst poet sing the finest song? Am I right, Ion?

Ion
Yes, by Zeus, Socrates -- I think you are. Your words really do touch my soul. I'm convinced that good poets, through divine inspiration, interpret the things of the gods for us.

Socrates
And you rhapsodes -- you're the interpreters of the poets?

Ion
Right again.

Socrates
So you're interpreters of interpreters?

Ion
Exactly.

Socrates
Now hold on -- I want you to be completely honest with me about something. When you're performing and you really hit the audience hard -- when you're reciting one of those electrifying passages, like Odysseus leaping onto the threshold, revealing himself to the suitors, and scattering his arrows at their feet, or Achilles charging at Hector, or the grief of Andromache, or Hecuba, or Priam -- are you in your right mind at that moment? Or are you carried out of yourself? Doesn't your soul feel like it's actually there, in the places you're describing -- in Ithaca, in Troy, wherever the poem takes you?

Ion
That hits home, Socrates. I'll be completely honest: when I'm reciting something sorrowful, my eyes fill with tears. When I'm describing something terrifying, my hair stands on end and my heart pounds.

Socrates
Well then, Ion -- what should we say about a man at a festival, dressed in his finest clothes, wearing golden crowns on his head that nobody has stolen from him, standing in front of more than twenty thousand friendly faces -- and he's weeping? Or he's terrified, when nobody is threatening him or doing him any harm? Is that man in his right mind?

Ion
No, Socrates -- strictly speaking, I'd have to say he's not.

Socrates
And you're aware that you produce the same effect on most of your audience?

Ion
Oh, I'm very aware. I look down at them from the stage and I can see it on their faces -- pity, wonder, intensity -- stamped right across their expressions when I'm performing. And I have to pay very close attention to them, too. Because if I make them cry, I'll be the one laughing -- all the way to the bank. But if I make them laugh, I'll be the one crying when it's time to collect my fee.

Socrates
Now, do you realize that this audience member is the last ring in the chain I was describing -- the chain that gets its power from the original magnet? You, the rhapsode -- and the actor too -- you're the middle links. And the poet is the first link. Through all of these, God pulls the souls of people in whatever direction he wants, making one person hang from the next. It's an enormous chain -- choruses, chorus directors, assistant directors -- all suspended from the Muse like rings hanging from that stone.

And every poet hangs from a particular Muse, and we say he's "possessed" by her -- and that's basically what it means: he's been seized. From those first rings, the poets, other rings hang down. Some people get their inspiration from Orpheus, others from Musaeus, but the greatest number are possessed by Homer and held in his grip.

You're one of them, Ion. You're possessed by Homer. When someone recites another poet, you fall asleep, you've got nothing to say. But when someone sings a passage from Homer, you snap awake, your soul leaps, and the words come pouring out. And it's not through skill or knowledge about Homer that you say what you say -- it's through divine inspiration and possession. Just like the Corybantes: they have an uncanny ear for the one particular melody that belongs to the god who possesses them, and they've got endless dances and words for that melody, but they don't care about any other.

That's you, Ion. When Homer comes up, you've got a world of things to say. When anyone else comes up -- nothing. You asked me, "Why is this?" Here's the answer: your brilliance about Homer comes not from skill, but from divine inspiration.

Ion
That's a nice speech, Socrates. But still, I doubt you'll ever be eloquent enough to convince me that I praise Homer because I'm mad and possessed. If you could hear me perform, I'm sure you'd never think that.

Socrates
Oh, I'd love to hear you. But first you have to answer a question. Which parts of Homer do you speak well about? Surely not every part?

Ion
Every part, Socrates. There's no part of Homer I can't speak well about. I guarantee it.

Socrates
Surely not about things in Homer that you have no knowledge of?

Ion
And what is there in Homer that I don't know about?

Socrates
Well, doesn't Homer talk about all sorts of specialized skills? For instance, chariot-driving. If I can just remember the lines...

Ion
I remember them. I'll recite them.

Socrates
Tell me what Nestor says to his son Antilochus, advising him about the turn in the chariot race held in honor of Patroclus.

Ion
"Lean gently," he says, "in the polished chariot to the left of them, and urge the horse on the right with whip and voice, giving him rein. At the turning post, let the left horse draw close, so close that the hub of the well-made wheel seems almost to graze the edge -- but be careful not to hit the stone."

Socrates
Good enough. Now, Ion -- who's the better judge of whether those lines are accurate: the charioteer or the rhapsode?

Ion
The charioteer, obviously.

Socrates
Because that's his skill -- or is there some other reason?

Ion
No, that's the reason.

Socrates
Right. Every skill is designed to give knowledge of a particular domain. What we know through the art of navigation, we don't know through medicine.

Ion
Certainly not.

Socrates
And what we know through carpentry, we don't know through medicine.

Ion
Certainly not.

Socrates
And this holds for every skill: what we know through one, we don't know through another. But let me ask a more basic question first: you do agree that different skills exist?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
And here's how I'd distinguish them -- and I think you'd agree: when one skill gives us knowledge of one kind of thing, and another skill gives us knowledge of another kind of thing, then they're different skills.

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Right -- because if two skills gave us knowledge of the same thing, there'd be no point in calling them different. For example, I know there are five fingers here, and you know the same thing. If I asked whether we both learned this through the same skill -- arithmetic -- you'd agree?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Then tell me: does this hold as a general rule? The same skill always covers the same domain of knowledge, and different skills cover different domains?

Ion
That's how I see it, Socrates.

Socrates
Then someone who doesn't have a particular skill won't be able to judge the things that fall under that skill?

Ion
True.

Socrates
So back to those lines about chariot-racing that you recited from Homer -- who's the better judge, you or the charioteer?

Ion
The charioteer.

Socrates
Right -- because you're a rhapsode, not a charioteer.

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
And the rhapsode's skill is different from the charioteer's?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
And if they're different skills, they cover different domains of knowledge?

Ion
True.

Socrates
Now, you know the passage where Homer describes Hecamede, Nestor's companion, giving a healing drink to the wounded Machaon:

"She made it with Pramnian wine, and grated goat cheese into it with a bronze grater, and set an onion beside it as a relish for the drink."

Now -- is the rhapsode or the doctor better equipped to judge whether Homer got those lines right?

Ion
The doctor.

Socrates
And when Homer says:

"She plunged to the depths like a lead weight set in a piece of ox-horn, that speeds along carrying death to the ravenous fish" --

is the art of fishing or the art of the rhapsode better equipped to judge whether those lines are well-composed?

Ion
The fisherman's art, obviously, Socrates.

Socrates
Now, suppose you turned it around on me and said: "All right, Socrates, since you're so good at assigning passages in Homer to their proper skills, why don't you tell me which passages should be judged by a prophet?" I'd answer you easily and accurately. There are plenty of examples, especially in the Odyssey -- like the passage where Theoclymenus, the prophet descended from Melampus, says to the suitors:

"Wretched men! What's happening to you? Your heads, your faces, your limbs are wrapped in night. The sound of wailing breaks out. Your cheeks are wet with tears. The porch is full of ghosts, the courtyard is full of them, streaming down to the darkness of Erebus. The sun has been blotted from the sky. An evil mist has spread over everything."

And there are many such passages in the Iliad too -- like the description of the battle near the rampart:

"As they were eager to cross the ditch, an omen came to them: a soaring eagle, holding back the army on the left, carrying a huge, bloody serpent in its talons, still alive and struggling. The serpent hadn't given up the fight -- it twisted back and struck the eagle on the breast near the neck, and the eagle, in pain, dropped it to the ground among the crowd. Then, with a shriek, the eagle was carried away on the wind."

Those are the kind of passages I'd say a prophet should examine and judge.

Ion
And you're absolutely right to say so, Socrates.

Socrates
Good -- and you're right to agree. So just as I've picked out passages from the Iliad and the Odyssey that belong to the prophet, the doctor, and the fisherman, now it's your turn, Ion. You know Homer so much better than I do -- pick out the passages that belong specifically to the rhapsode and the rhapsode's art, the ones that a rhapsode should be able to examine and judge better than anyone else.

Ion
All of them, I'd say, Socrates.

Socrates
All of them? Really? Have you already forgotten what you just said? A rhapsode ought to have a better memory than that.

Ion
What am I forgetting?

Socrates
Don't you remember saying that the rhapsode's skill is different from the charioteer's?

Ion
Yes, I remember.

Socrates
And you agreed that since they're different skills, they'd cover different domains of knowledge?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Then by your own reasoning, the rhapsode -- and the rhapsode's skill -- won't know everything.

Ion
Well, I'd exclude certain things, Socrates.

Socrates
By "exclude certain things," you basically mean you'd exclude the domains of all the other skills. So what's left? What does the rhapsode actually know?

Ion
What a man should say and what a woman should say. What a free person should say and what a slave should say. What a ruler should say and what a subject should say.

Socrates
So you're saying the rhapsode knows better than the pilot what the commander of a ship in a storm should say?

Ion
No, the pilot would know that better.

Socrates
And the rhapsode knows better than the doctor what a person treating a sick patient should say?

Ion
No.

Socrates
But he'd know what a slave should say?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
Let's say the slave is a cowherd. Would the rhapsode know better than the actual cowherd what he should say to calm down some enraged cattle?

Ion
No.

Socrates
What about a woman spinning wool? Would the rhapsode know what she should say about working with wool?

Ion
No.

Socrates
But a general rallying his troops -- the rhapsode would know what he should say?

Ion
Yes! That's exactly the sort of thing a rhapsode would know.

Socrates
Wait -- is the art of the rhapsode the same as the art of the general?

Ion
I'm sure I'd know what a general ought to say.

Socrates
Well, maybe you would, Ion -- maybe you happen to have some knowledge of generalship on top of being a rhapsode. You might also know about horsemanship on top of playing the lyre. But if I asked you, "When you judge whether horses are well-managed, which skill are you using -- your horsemanship or your lyre-playing?" what would you say?

Ion
My horsemanship.

Socrates
And if you were judging lyre players, you'd say you were judging them as a lyre player, not as a horseman?

Ion
Yes.

Socrates
So when you're judging matters of generalship, are you judging as a general or as a rhapsode?

Ion
I don't see any difference between the two.

Socrates
You don't see any difference? Are you really saying the art of the rhapsode and the art of the general are the same thing?

Ion
Yes, one and the same.

Socrates
So anyone who's a good rhapsode is also a good general?

Ion
Absolutely, Socrates.

Socrates
And anyone who's a good general is also a good rhapsode?

Ion
No, I wouldn't say that.

Socrates
But you do say that anyone who's a good rhapsode is a good general?

Ion
Definitely.

Socrates
And you're the best rhapsode in all of Greece?

Ion
The best by far, Socrates.

Socrates
And that also makes you the best general in Greece?

Ion
You bet, Socrates. I learned it all from Homer.

Socrates
Then in the name of all the gods, Ion -- if you're really both the best rhapsode and the best general in all of Greece, why on earth are you going around performing poetry at festivals? Why aren't you leading armies? Do you think Greece needs another rhapsode in a golden crown more than it needs a general?

Ion
Well, Socrates, here's the thing. My people -- the Ephesians -- are subjects and soldiers of Athens, so they don't need a general of their own. And you Athenians and the Spartans aren't likely to pick me, since you think you've got plenty of generals already.

Socrates
My dear Ion, haven't you heard of Apollodorus of Cyzicus?

Ion
Who's he?

Socrates
A foreigner who's been chosen as a general by the Athenians multiple times. And there's Phanosthenes of Andros, and Heraclides of Clazomenae -- Athens has given them military commands and other high offices too, even though they're foreigners, once they proved their worth. So you think Athens wouldn't choose Ion of Ephesus as a general and give him every honor, if he proved himself worthy? Weren't the Ephesians originally Athenians? And Ephesus is certainly no second-rate city.

But look, Ion -- here's where we really are. If you're right that it's through genuine skill and knowledge that you're able to praise Homer, then you're not dealing fairly with me. You've been boasting about all the glorious things you know about Homer, promising to show me, and then you just keep dodging. You're like Proteus -- you shift into a hundred different shapes, twisting and turning every which way, and just when I think I've got you, you slip away in the disguise of a general to avoid actually demonstrating your supposed expertise in Homer.

So here are your two options. If you really do have a skill -- an actual art of Homer -- then you've been breaking your promise to show it to me, and that's dishonest. But if, as I believe, you don't have any skill at all, and you say all these beautiful things about Homer unconsciously, under his divine influence, without really understanding what you're doing -- then you're not dishonest. You're just inspired.

So which would you rather be called, Ion: dishonest or inspired?

Ion
There's a huge difference between those two, Socrates. Inspired is far nobler.

Socrates
Then let's go with the nobler option, Ion. When it comes to your brilliance about Homer, you are a man not of skill, but of divine inspiration.
