Enough of all that. So what should we consider the origin of government? Isn't the best way to approach this question to look at it from the perspective of how states progress and change — for better or worse — over time?

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
I mean we could trace the changes that happen to states over vast stretches of time and observe how they transform.

Cleinias
How would we do that?

Athenian
Well, do you think you could calculate how much time has passed since cities first came into existence and people began living as citizens?

Cleinias
Hardly. That would be impossible.

Athenian
But you'd agree it must be an incomprehensibly long time?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And over that span, thousands upon thousands of cities have come into being and just as many have been destroyed, haven't they? Each one cycling through every form of government over and over — sometimes growing larger, sometimes shrinking, sometimes improving, sometimes falling apart?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
Let's try to figure out the cause of these changes. That'll probably help us explain the very first origin and development of government.

Cleinias
Good idea. You share your thoughts with us, and we'll do our best to follow.

Athenian
Do you believe there's any truth in the ancient traditions?

Cleinias
Which traditions?

Athenian
The stories about how humanity has been destroyed many times over — by great floods, plagues, and all sorts of catastrophes — with only a tiny remnant surviving each time.

Cleinias
Everyone's inclined to believe those stories.

Athenian
Let's focus on one of them — the destruction caused by the great flood.

Cleinias
What should we notice about it?

Athenian
That the people who survived would have been hill shepherds — small sparks of the human race preserved on the tops of mountains.

Cleinias
Clearly.

Athenian
And these survivors would've known nothing about the arts, the clever inventions, or all the scheming and manipulation that city life breeds. They'd have been completely cut off from all that.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
So let's assume that all the cities in the plains and along the coasts were completely wiped out.

Cleinias
All right.

Athenian
Wouldn't every tool and every brilliant invention — political systems, technologies, all forms of expertise — have been utterly lost?

Cleinias
Absolutely. And you know, my friend, if things had always remained as they are now, how would anything ever have been discovered in the first place? The truth is, the arts were unknown for periods of time so vast they're beyond counting. It's only been a thousand or two thousand years since the discoveries of Daedalus, Orpheus, and Palamedes, or since Marsyas and Olympus invented their musical forms, or since Amphion created the lyre — not to mention countless other inventions that are practically from yesterday.

Athenian
Cleinias, have you forgotten the name of a friend who is quite literally from yesterday?

Cleinias
I suppose you mean Epimenides.

Athenian
That's the one. He really does leap ahead of everyone else with his inventions. What Hesiod merely talked about in theory, Epimenides actually put into practice — or so your tradition says.

Cleinias
Yes, that's what we're told.

Athenian
So after the great destruction, let's imagine what the state of humanity would have been. Picture it: a vast, empty wilderness stretching in every direction. Maybe a herd or two of cattle survived somewhere, and a few goats — barely enough to keep the shepherds who tended them alive.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
And as for cities, governments, and legislation — the things we're discussing right now — do you think anyone could have had any memory of them at all?

Cleinias
None whatsoever.

Athenian
And yet from that state of affairs, everything we now have has developed: cities and governments, arts and laws, and a great deal of both vice and virtue?

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
Well, my friend, how could we possibly expect that people who knew nothing about the good and evil of city life could have reached the heights of virtue or the depths of vice?

Cleinias
I see your point. You're quite right.

Athenian
But as time went on and the population grew, the world gradually became what we see today.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
And the change didn't happen overnight. It took place little by little, over an enormously long period.

Cleinias
That's very likely.

Athenian
At first, the terror of the flood would still be ringing in their ears, and they'd have been too afraid to come down from the highlands into the plains.

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
And the tiny number of survivors would've been desperate to see one another. But the means of traveling — by land or sea — had been almost entirely lost along with all the other arts. Getting from one place to another would have been incredibly difficult. Iron and bronze and all the metals had been jumbled together and buried in the destruction. There was no way to extract ore. And they had almost no way to fell timber either. Even if some tools had survived up in the mountains, they would've quickly worn out and disappeared, and there'd have been no way to replace them until the art of metalworking was rediscovered.

Cleinias
No, there couldn't have been.

Athenian
And how many generations would that have taken?

Cleinias
Clearly a great many.

Athenian
During that whole period, and for some time after, every craft that depends on iron and bronze and similar metals would have vanished.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And war and internal conflict would have died out too, for several reasons.

Cleinias
How so?

Athenian
First, the isolation and desolation these early people experienced would have created strong bonds of affection and goodwill among them. Second, they wouldn't have had any reason to quarrel over food. Pastureland would have been abundant — with so few people left, there'd have been plenty for everyone — except perhaps right at the beginning, in some isolated cases. From their pastures they'd have gotten most of their food: plenty of milk and meat. They'd also have supplemented this with hunting, and there'd have been no shortage of game. They'd have had plenty of clothing, bedding, shelters, and household goods too — both fireproof and regular — because the arts of pottery and weaving don't require iron. God gave humans those two crafts specifically so that even when reduced to the direst circumstances, the human race could still survive and grow.

So people in those days weren't desperately poor. And poverty didn't cause divisions among them. But they couldn't have been rich either, since they had neither gold nor silver. That was just their situation. And a community that has neither poverty nor wealth will always produce the noblest character. Where there's no great wealth, there's no arrogance or injustice. And where there's no poverty, there's no resentment or envy. So they were good people. They were also what you'd call simple-minded — and I mean that as a compliment. When they were told something was good or bad, they believed it in their simplicity and lived accordingly. Nobody was clever enough to suspect anyone else of lying, the way people do now. What they heard about gods and men, they took to be the truth and shaped their lives around it. In every way, they were exactly as we've described.

Cleinias
That fits perfectly with my own views, and Megillus' too.

Athenian
Now, wouldn't these people — living simply for generation after generation, rougher perhaps, more ignorant of the arts generally, and certainly of land and naval warfare, and all those clever tricks that cities breed: legal maneuvering, political factions, every imaginable way of hurting each other in word and deed — wouldn't they, despite being less sophisticated than the people who lived before the flood or than people of our own day, have been simpler, braver, more self-disciplined, and on the whole more just? We've already explained why.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
I want you to understand that everything I've been saying, and everything that follows, is meant to explain one thing: what did the people of that era need in the way of laws, and who were their lawmakers?

Cleinias
And so far, you've been making your case very well.

Athenian
They wouldn't have needed formal lawmakers yet. Nothing of the sort was likely to exist in their time, since they didn't even have writing. They lived by habit and by what are called ancestral customs.

Cleinias
Probably so.

Athenian
But there was already a form of government in existence — one that, if I'm not mistaken, is generally called patriarchy. It still survives in many places, among both Greeks and non-Greeks. Homer tells us the Cyclopes lived this way:

"They have no councils and no formal laws, but they live in hollow caves on the tops of high mountains, and each one lays down the law for his own wife and children, and they don't concern themselves with one another."

Cleinias
That seems like a charming poet of yours. I've read some other verses of his that are quite clever, though I don't know him well. Foreign poets aren't widely read in Crete.

Megillus
But they certainly are in Sparta! Homer seems to be the prince of all poets. Although the way of life he describes isn't Spartan — it's more Ionian, I'd say. But he does seem to confirm exactly what you're arguing, tracing the ancient condition of humanity back to a kind of primitive life through traditional stories.

Athenian
Yes, he does confirm it. And we can accept his testimony that these kinds of governments do arise.

Cleinias
Agreed.

Athenian
And weren't these communities made up of people who had been scattered into isolated families by the devastation? In these groups, the eldest would naturally have ruled — since government originated in the authority of a father and mother. Like a flock of birds, the rest would have followed, forming a single group under patriarchal rule and the sovereignty of their parents — which is, of all forms of authority, the most natural and just.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
After this, they started coming together in larger numbers, expanding the size of their communities. They turned to farming, first at the foot of the mountains. They built rough stone walls and other defenses to keep out wild animals, creating a single larger shared settlement.

Cleinias
Yes, that's how it would likely have happened.

Athenian
And here's something else that probably occurred.

Cleinias
What?

Athenian
When these larger settlements grew out of the smaller original family groups, each family would have survived within the larger community. Every family would still be governed by its eldest member. And because of their long separation from one another, each family would have developed its own customs — its own ways of doing things, both religious and secular — inherited from the parents who'd raised them. Orderly parents would have passed along orderliness; courageous parents, courage. They would naturally have stamped their own values on their children, and their children's children. So by the time these families came together into the larger society, each one already had its own distinct customs and traditions.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And naturally, every group prefers its own customs and isn't as fond of other people's.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
So it seems we've stumbled onto the beginnings of legislation.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
The next step would be that the people who'd come together would choose some arbiters — people who'd review the laws and customs of all the different families and present the ones they approved to the tribal leaders, who were essentially their kings. These arbiters would let the leaders choose whichever laws they thought best. The arbiters themselves would end up being called "legislators," and they'd appoint magistrates. Out of the old patriarchies, they'd construct some sort of aristocracy — or perhaps a monarchy. And in this transformed system of government, they'd carry on living.

Cleinias
Yes, that would be the natural progression.

Athenian
Now let's talk about a third form of government — one in which all sorts of political arrangements and constitutional forms come together.

Cleinias
What form is that?

Athenian
The one Homer describes as following the second stage. This third form emerged when, as he tells it, Dardanus founded Dardania:

"For holy Ilium had not yet been built on the plain to be a city of speaking men; they were still dwelling at the foot of many-fountained Ida."

In these verses, and in what he said about the Cyclopes, Homer speaks with the voice of divine truth. Poets are a divinely inspired race, and often — with the help of the Muses and the Graces — they hit on the truth.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Let's continue with our story, then. It'll probably help illustrate the point we're working toward. Shall we?

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
Ilium was built after people descended from the mountains. They settled on a broad, beautiful plain, on a low hill, watered by many rivers flowing down from Mount Ida.

Cleinias
That's how the story goes.

Athenian
And we'd have to assume this happened a very long time after the flood?

Cleinias
Yes, there must have been a very long interval.

Athenian
It's remarkable how completely they'd forgotten the earlier destruction. They placed their city right beneath numerous streams flowing from the heights, trusting for protection to hills that weren't even very high. That takes an extraordinary loss of collective memory.

Cleinias
Clearly a very long time had passed.

Athenian
And as the population grew, many other cities would have been founded.

Cleinias
No doubt.

Athenian
These cities eventually made war against Troy — by sea as well as land, because by that time people were losing their fear of the ocean.

Cleinias
Clearly.

Athenian
The Greeks — the Achaeans — besieged Troy for ten years and finally destroyed it.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
But during those ten years while the Achaeans were camped outside Troy, things back home were falling apart. The young people left behind revolted. And when the soldiers finally came home to their own cities and families, they weren't received properly or fairly. The result was widespread death, murder, and exile. The exiles eventually regrouped under a new name — no longer Achaeans, but Dorians, named after Dorieus, who gathered them together. The rest of the story is told by you Spartans as part of your own history.

Megillus
That's right.

Athenian
So after our long digression from the subject of laws into music and drinking parties, the argument has — almost providentially — circled back to where we started. We've now reached the founding of Sparta, which you rightly say is a sister state to Crete in its laws and institutions. And we're better off for the digression, because we've traced the development of government from a first state through a second and third, each succeeding the other over vast stretches of time. And now a fourth state appears on the horizon — one that was once in the process of being founded and has continued to this day. If we can learn from all this what makes a state well or badly governed, which laws save cities and which destroy them, and what changes produce happiness — then, Megillus and Cleinias, we can start fresh. Unless you have some objection to what we've said so far?

Megillus
If some god promised us that our next investigation into legislation would be as rich and full as this one, I'd walk a very long way to hear it. I'd say a day as long as this one — and we're approaching the longest day of the year — is too short for the discussion.

Athenian
Then I suppose we'd better keep going?

Megillus
Definitely.

Athenian
Let's put ourselves mentally at the moment when Sparta, Argos, and Messene — and the rest of the Peloponnese — were all completely under the control of your ancestors, Megillus. Megillus
True.

Athenian
Temenus became king of Argos, Cresphontes of Messene, and Procles and Eurysthenes of Sparta.

Megillus
Yes.

Athenian
And all the people of that time swore oaths that they would help defend these kings if anyone tried to overthrow their rule.

Megillus
True.

But can a kingdom really be destroyed by anyone other than its own rulers? By Zeus — have we already forgotten what we said just a short while ago?

Megillus
No.

We're not theorizing in the abstract anymore — we're talking about things that actually happened. Here's how it went: three royal families swore oaths with three cities, and the cities swore oaths with the kings, that both rulers and subjects would govern and be governed according to their shared laws. The rulers promised that as time went on, they wouldn't make their power more tyrannical. The subjects promised that as long as the rulers kept their word, they would never overthrow or allow others to overthrow those kingdoms. The kings were to help defend each other's kings and peoples, and the peoples were to help defend each other's peoples and kings. Isn't that right?

Megillus
Yes.

Athenian

Megillus

Megillus
True.

Athenian
Now, many people say that legislators should only impose laws that the general public will readily accept. But that's like telling athletic trainers or doctors to treat their pupils and patients only in ways that feel pleasant.

Megillus
Exactly.

Whereas a doctor is often doing well enough if he can restore health and make the body whole without causing too much pain.

Megillus
Certainly.

Athenian
The lawmakers of that era also had another advantage that made their job much easier.

What advantage?

Athenian
When they distributed property equally, they avoided the biggest political firestorm there is — the one that erupts whenever someone tries to redistribute land or cancel debts because they can see that without reform, there can never be real equality. Normally, when a lawmaker attempts anything like that, everyone screams, "Don't disturb vested interests!" People curse him for proposing land reform and debt cancellation until the poor man doesn't know which way to turn. But nobody could object to the Dorians distributing land — there was nothing to redistribute. And they had no significant debts, certainly no long-standing ones.

Megillus
Very true.

Athenian
Then why, my friends, did the settlement and legislation of their country turn out so badly?

What do you mean? Why are you criticizing them?

Athenian
Three kingdoms were established. Two of them quickly corrupted their original constitution and laws. Only Sparta survived intact.

Megillus
That's not an easy question to answer.

Megillus
True. We have to figure out why this happened.

What laws are more worthy of study than those that governed these cities? What state settlements were ever more significant or more famous?

Megillus
I can't think of any.

Can there be any doubt that your ancestors intended these institutions not just for the protection of the Peloponnese, but for all of Greece — in case of an attack by non-Greek powers? The second destruction of Troy was a serious offense against the Assyrians, since Troy was part of their empire. To meet this threat, the army was divided among three cities by the royal brothers, the sons of Heracles. A clever arrangement, it seemed — far better than the original expedition against Troy. For one thing, the people of that day believed they had better leaders in the Heraclidae than in the Pelopidae. And they considered their army superior in courage to the one that went against Troy — after all, the Trojans had been conquered by the Achaeans, but the Achaeans themselves had been conquered by the Heraclidae, Dorians overcoming Achaeans. Isn't it reasonable to think that this is what the men of that era had in mind when they set up their constitutions?

Megillus
Quite reasonable.

Athenian
And wouldn't people who had shared many dangers together, who were governed by a single royal family of brothers, and who had the blessing of oracles — especially the oracle at Delphi — naturally assume that their states would stand firm and last forever?

Megillus
Of course they would.

Athenian
And yet these institutions, which everyone had such high hopes for, all fell apart almost immediately — with the single exception, as I said, of the Spartan portion. And that one surviving third has never stopped fighting against the other two. Whereas if the original plan had held together and they'd stayed united, their military power would have been absolutely unstoppable.

Megillus
No doubt about it.

Athenian
So what destroyed this magnificent alliance? Here's a question truly worth investigating.

Megillus
Absolutely. You won't find more dramatic examples anywhere of how laws and governments can either save or ruin great and noble enterprises.

Athenian
Well, it seems we've arrived at a genuinely important question.

Megillus
Indeed we have.

Athenian
Have you ever noticed, my wise friend, how people — ourselves included, right at this very moment — often look at something and think, "What a magnificent thing! If only someone had known how to use it properly, it could have achieved wonders!" And yet this way of thinking might turn out to be completely mistaken — not just in our case, but in general?

Megillus
What are you getting at? What do you mean?

Athenian
I was just catching myself doing exactly that — admiring the Heraclid expedition, which was so noble and could have produced such wonderful results for Greece, if only it had been managed properly. And I was laughing at myself for it.

Megillus
But weren't you right to say what you said? And weren't we right to agree with you?

Athenian
Perhaps. But I can't help noticing that whenever anyone sees something great and powerful, the immediate reaction is: "If only the owner knew how to use this magnificent possession, imagine how happy they'd be and what great things they'd accomplish!"

Megillus
And isn't that a fair reaction?

Athenian
Think about it. In what sense is this kind of praise actually justified? Let's apply it to our case. If the commanders back then had known how to organize their forces properly, how would they have succeeded? Wouldn't it have been something like this: they would have bound the three states firmly together, preserved the alliance forever, and given themselves freedom and dominion as they pleased — the power to do whatever they wanted, anywhere in the world, Greek or non-Greek. What else could they have been after?

Megillus
Very good.

Athenian
And suppose someone were to express the same kind of admiration at the sight of great wealth, or distinguished ancestry, or anything like that. They'd be praising these things on the assumption that through them, they'd get everything — or at least most of what — they desire.

Megillus
They would.

Athenian
Well now, doesn't our argument show that there's one universal desire shared by all human beings?

Megillus
What is it?

Athenian
The desire that everything — or at the very least, everything within human reach — should turn out in accordance with one's own wishes.

Megillus
Certainly.

Athenian
And since we have this desire at every stage of life — in youth, in adulthood, in old age — we can't help praying for its fulfillment constantly.

Megillus
No doubt.

Athenian
And we join in our friends' prayers too, wishing for them what they wish for themselves.

Megillus
We do.

Athenian
A father loves his son — the elder loves the younger.

Megillus
Of course.

Athenian
And yet the son often prays to get things that the father prays he won't get.

Megillus
You mean when the son is young and foolish?

Athenian
Yes. Or consider this: when the father himself, whether from the foolishness of age or the recklessness of youth, has lost his sense of right and wrong and prays passionately — like Theseus when he cursed his innocent son Hippolytus — do you think a son who does have a sense of right and wrong would join in that prayer?

Megillus
I see what you're saying. A person shouldn't rush to have everything go according to their wishes, because their wishes might conflict with their reason. What every state and every individual really ought to pray and strive for is wisdom.

Athenian
Yes! And if you remember, I said at the very beginning that a statesman and lawmaker should design laws with wisdom as their goal. But you were arguing that a good lawmaker should organize everything with a view to war. And I replied that war involves only one of the four virtues, whereas the lawmaker should keep all of them in view — and especially the one that leads all the rest: wisdom, which has practical judgment and true understanding as its companions, with passion and desire following in their train.

So the argument has come back to the same point. And I'll say it again — call it a joke if you like, or take it seriously: the prayer of a fool is a dangerous thing, because it's likely to produce the exact opposite of what he wants. If you'd rather take that seriously, go ahead. And I suspect you'll find, as I've already argued, that what ruined the Dorian kings and their whole grand plan wasn't cowardice. It wasn't military ignorance, either on the part of the rulers or their subjects. Their downfall was caused by their general moral deterioration — and above all, by their ignorance of what matters most in human life. That was true then, it's true now, and it always will be. And I'll try to demonstrate this as clearly as I can throughout our discussion, if you'll allow me.

Cleinias
Please go on, friend. Compliments are tedious. We'll show how much we value your words not by flattering you, but by giving you our full attention — which is the best way a free man can show his approval.

Megillus
Well said, Cleinias. Let's do exactly that.

Cleinias
Absolutely — God willing. Continue.

Athenian
All right then. Picking up where we left off: the greatest ignorance was the ruin of the Dorian power. And the same is true now as then — ignorance is what destroys. So if that's the case, the lawmaker must do everything possible to cultivate wisdom in states and drive out ignorance.

Cleinias
That's obvious.

Athenian
Now think about this: what really is the greatest ignorance? I'd like to know whether you and Megillus agree with what I'm about to say.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
The greatest ignorance is this: when a person hates what they actually believe, deep down, to be good and noble — and loves and embraces what they know to be wrong and evil. This conflict between pleasure and rational judgment in the soul is, in my view, the worst kind of ignorance. It's also the most widespread, because it affects the largest part of the soul. The part of the soul that feels pleasure and pain is like the common people in a state. And when the soul rebels against knowledge and reason and good judgment — which are its natural rulers — that's what I call folly. Just as in a state, when the masses refuse to obey their rulers and laws. Or in an individual, when sound reasoning is present in the soul but accomplishes nothing — or worse, the opposite of what it should. All of these I call the worst kind of ignorance, whether in individuals or in states. You understand, friend, that I'm talking about something very different from the ignorance of tradespeople who lack technical knowledge.

Cleinias
Yes, my friend. We understand, and we agree.

Athenian
Then let's establish this principle: a citizen who suffers from this kind of ignorance should never be entrusted with any position of authority. He must be labeled ignorant — even if he's a mathematical genius, skilled in every kind of clever intellectual feat. And the opposite kind of person should be called wise — even if, as the proverb says, they can't read or swim. These are the people to whom authority should be given, because they have good sense.

After all, my friends, how can there be even a shadow of wisdom where there's no inner harmony? There can't. The noblest and greatest of all harmonies is rightly called the greatest wisdom. And anyone who lives according to reason shares in it. But someone who lacks reason isn't just failing to save his household — he's actively destroying it. And he's the exact opposite of a savior of the state. He's utterly ignorant of political wisdom. Let that be our established principle.

Cleinias
So established.

Athenian
I take it there must be rulers and subjects in any state?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
So what are the principles by which people rule and obey — in large states and small ones, and in families too? How many such principles are there? First, isn't there one claim to authority that's always legitimate — the right of parents, and ancestors generally, to rule over their children?

Cleinias
There is.

Athenian
Second, the principle that those of noble birth should rule over the less noble. And third, that older people should rule and younger people should obey?

Cleinias
Naturally.

Athenian
Fourth, that slaves should be ruled and masters should rule?

Cleinias
Obviously.

Athenian
Fifth, if I'm not mistaken, comes the principle that the stronger should rule and the weaker submit?

Cleinias
There's a rule that's hard to disobey!

Athenian
Yes — and a rule that prevails throughout the entire natural world. As the Theban poet Pindar once pointed out. But the sixth principle — and the greatest of all — is that the wise should lead and the ignorant should follow. And this, my dear Pindar — I'd tell him if I could — is not contrary to nature at all. It is the rule of law over willing subjects, not the rule of brute force.

Cleinias
Absolutely right.

Athenian
There's a seventh kind of authority, too — one assigned by lot, which is dear to the gods and a sign of good fortune. Whoever wins the lot rules; whoever doesn't, steps aside and submits. And we'd call this perfectly just.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
"So you see, legislator" — as we might playfully say to anyone who takes up lawmaking too lightly — "the principles of government: how many there are, and how naturally they conflict with one another. Here we've uncovered a fountainhead of political upheaval, and you'd better pay attention to it."

First, let's consider what happened with the kings of Argos and Messene. How did they violate these principles and bring ruin on themselves and the once-great power of Greece? Wasn't it because they failed to understand what Hesiod meant when he said that the half is often more than the whole? His point was this: when taking the whole is dangerous, and taking the half is the safe and moderate course, then the moderate path is actually better than the immoderate one.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
And which is more dangerous — this immoderate spirit appearing in kings, or in ordinary people?

Cleinias
It's probably more common and more destructive in kings, because they lead proud, luxurious lives.

Athenian
Isn't it obvious that the main goal of those kings was to override the established laws — to break the very agreements they'd sworn to uphold by word and oath? This lack of inner harmony may have looked like cleverness, but it was actually the greatest ignorance, and it completely destroyed the whole enterprise through discord and clashing.

Cleinias
Very likely.

Athenian
Good. So what precautions should the original lawmaker have taken to prevent this disaster? Here's the thing: it doesn't take much wisdom to diagnose a problem after the fact. Hindsight is easy. But to have foreseen the solution at the time — that would have taken a much wiser mind than ours.

Megillus
What do you mean?

Athenian
Looking at what happened with you Spartans, Megillus, anyone can see in retrospect what should have been done.

Megillus
Could you be a bit more specific?

Athenian
Nothing could be clearer than what I'm about to say.

Megillus
Which is?

Athenian
This: if you give too much of anything — too large a sail to a ship, too much food to the body, too much power to the mind — everything is thrown off balance. Too much food leads to sickness. Too much power leads to injustice, which is the child of excess. Here's what I mean, my friends: no human soul — especially a young, irresponsible one — can withstand the temptation of unchecked power. Nobody who gets it is immune. They inevitably become infected with the worst of all diseases: folly. And their closest friends end up hating them. When that happens, their kingdom is undermined from within, and all their power slips away. Great lawmakers who understand the principle of moderation should watch out for this danger.

As best we can tell from this distance in time, here's what happened with Sparta:

Megillus
What?

Athenian
A god watching over Sparta, seeing into the future, gave you two royal families instead of one — pulling you closer to the right balance. Then, some human wisdom blended with this divine arrangement. Noticing that Sparta's constitution was still running hot, someone tempered your inborn pride and strength with the moderating influence of age by creating the Council of Twenty-Eight Elders, whose power equaled the kings' on the most important matters. But your third savior saw that the government was still churning and swelling. So he established the Ephors, whose authority resembled that of officials chosen by lot. And with this arrangement, the royal office was blended from the right ingredients and properly balanced — which preserved it and, through it, preserved everything else.

Because if the original lawmakers — Temenus, Cresphontes, and their generation — had been left to their own devices, even the portion of Aristodemus wouldn't have survived. They simply didn't have the experience in lawmaking. Otherwise, they'd never have imagined that oaths alone could keep young men in check once they'd been handed power that could easily become tyranny.

Now that the gods have shown us what kind of government lasts, there's no great wisdom in seeing it after the fact — as I've already said, that's easy. But if anyone could have foreseen all this at the time and found a way to moderate the three kingdoms and unite them into one, they could have saved all those excellent institutions. And if they had? No Persian army — no foreign power at all — would have dared attack us, or would have dared to treat Greece as a pushover.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Our defeat of the Persians was hardly something to brag about, Cleinias. The disgrace wasn't that the victors failed to win glorious victories on land and sea — they certainly did. No, what brought disgrace was this: of the three allied cities, only one actually fought for Greece. The other two were completely useless. One was so busy waging war against Sparta that it prevented Sparta from helping. And Argos — which had held pride of place when the territories were originally divided — refused to answer the call or send help when asked to resist the barbarian invasion.

There's a lot more that could be said about Greece's conduct in that war, and much of it isn't flattering. You can't really say that "Greece" repelled the invader. The truth is, if Athens and Sparta hadn't fought together, all the Greek peoples would have been thrown into chaos — Greeks mixed with Greeks, Greeks mixed with non-Greeks, non-Greeks mixed with Greeks. It would have been like what we see now in the nations under Persian rule, torn apart by unnatural separations and forced combinations, living in misery.

These, Cleinias and Megillus, are the charges we have to bring against so-called statesmen and legislators, past and present, if we want to analyze why they failed and what could have been done differently. For instance, we said just now that there should be no great, unmixed concentrations of power. The idea was that a state should be free, wise, and internally harmonious, and that a lawmaker should legislate with these goals in mind. And we shouldn't be surprised if we keep proposing different goals for the lawmaker that seem to keep changing. When we say the aim should be self-discipline, or wisdom, or friendship — these all amount to the same thing. If we use a variety of expressions, that shouldn't confuse us.

Cleinias
Let's continue in that spirit. Now, when it comes to friendship, wisdom, and freedom — what exactly should the legislator be aiming for, in your opinion?

Athenian
Listen, then. There are two mother-forms of government from which all others are truly derived. One you can call monarchy, and the other democracy. Persia has the most extreme version of the first, and we Athenians of the second. Almost all other governments, as I was saying, are variations of these two. Now, if you want to have liberty combined with friendship and wisdom, you absolutely must incorporate elements of both forms. That's the emphatic point of our argument: no city can be well governed if it doesn't draw from both.

Cleinias
Impossible to disagree.

Athenian
If a state commits itself exclusively and excessively to monarchy, or exclusively and excessively to democracy, neither one will maintain any balance. Your states — Sparta and Crete — have more balance than most. The same used to be true of Athens and Persia in their early days. But now they've lost it. Want me to explain why?

Cleinias
Absolutely — if it helps clarify our subject.

Athenian
All right, listen. There was a time when the Persians hit a better balance between slavery and freedom. Under Cyrus, the Persians were free men who also ruled over many others. Because the rulers gave their subjects a share of freedom and treated them as equals, the soldiers felt closer to their generals and fought more bravely in times of danger. And if there was a wise man among them who could offer good counsel, he was free to share it publicly. The king wasn't jealous — he allowed full freedom of speech and honored anyone who could give useful advice. The result was that the nation flourished in every way, because there was freedom, friendship, and a genuine sharing of ideas.

Cleinias
That does seem to have been the case.

Athenian
So how was this advantage lost under Cambyses, and then recovered under Darius? Let me take a guess.

Cleinias
This certainly has a bearing on our subject.

Athenian
I'd say that Cyrus, though a great and patriotic general, never gave any attention to education, and never bothered to put his own household in order.

Cleinias
What makes you say that?

Athenian
I think that from his youth onward he was a soldier, constantly at war, and he left the education of his children entirely to the women. And the women raised those boys as pampered darlings of fortune — blessed already, needing nothing more. No one was allowed to cross them in any way. Everyone was forced to praise everything they said and did. That's how they were raised.

Cleinias
A splendid education, that!

Athenian
The kind of education you'd expect from royal women who'd recently become wealthy — and with the men away, occupied with wars and dangers, and no time to supervise.

Cleinias
Fair enough.

Athenian
Meanwhile, their father had vast herds of cattle, sheep, and men. But he didn't realize that the boys he was going to hand everything over to weren't being trained in his own tradition — the Persian way. The Persians are a people of shepherds, sons of a harsh land, a stern mother that produces a tough breed capable of living in the open, going without sleep, and fighting when necessary. But Cyrus didn't notice that his sons were being raised differently. They were educated in the Median style — by women and eunuchs — in what people called the "blessings" of royalty. The result was exactly what you'd expect when children grow up without anyone ever telling them "no."

So after Cyrus died, his sons — stuffed with luxury and self-indulgence — took the throne. First one killed the other because he couldn't stand having a rival. Then the killer himself, driven mad by wine and brutality, lost the kingdom to the Medes and the man they called "the Eunuch," who despised the stupidity of Cambyses.

Cleinias
So the story goes, and it probably happened more or less like that.

Athenian
And tradition tells us that the empire was restored to the Persians through Darius and the seven conspirators.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Now let's follow the rest of the story. Notice this: Darius was not the son of a king, and he hadn't been raised in luxury. When he came to the throne as one of the seven, he divided the country into seven provinces — and some faint traces of this arrangement still survive. He made laws based on the principle of universal equality within the state. And he enshrined in law the system of tribute that Cyrus had promised, creating a sense of friendship and shared purpose among all Persians. He used money and gifts to win people's loyalty. The result was that his armies enthusiastically conquered territories as vast as those Cyrus had left behind.

Then Darius was succeeded by his son Xerxes. And Xerxes was raised in that same royal, pampered style. "Oh Darius" — we might fairly say — "how could you raise Xerxes the same way Cyrus raised Cambyses? How could you not see the fatal mistake?" Because Xerxes, the product of the same kind of upbringing, met pretty much the same fate. And from that time until now, there has never been a truly great Persian king — though they're all called "Great."

And their decline isn't a matter of bad luck. It's the result of the terrible life that's typically led by the sons of extremely rich and royal families. No boy, no man — young or old — will ever become excellent in character if he's been raised that way. This is exactly the kind of thing the lawmaker needs to think about, and it's what we need to think about right now.

And it's only fair to give you Spartans credit, Megillus: you don't give special honors or a special education based on wealth rather than poverty, or on royal birth rather than common origin, unless your inspired lawmaker has specifically ordered it. Because no one should receive the highest honors in a state simply because they surpass others in wealth — any more than because they're a fast runner or good-looking or physically strong, unless they also have real virtue. And not just any virtue, either — they'd better have self-discipline.

Megillus
What do you mean, friend?

Athenian
Courage is a part of virtue, right?

Megillus
Of course.

Athenian
So think about this: would you want a housemate or neighbor who was extremely brave but had no self-control?

Megillus
God, no!

Athenian
Or how about a craftsman who was brilliant at his trade but a complete scoundrel?

Megillus
Absolutely not.

Athenian
And justice doesn't exist apart from self-discipline, does it?

Megillus
It can't.

Athenian
And our model of a wise person — someone whose pleasures and pains are in harmony with reason — such a person can't lack self-discipline, can they?

Megillus
No.

Athenian
There's another important question about how states should distribute honors.

Megillus
What's that?

Athenian
I want to know this: should self-discipline by itself, existing alone in a person's soul without the other virtues, be praised or criticized?

Megillus
I really can't say.

Athenian
And that's the perfect answer. Because whichever way you'd gone, I think you'd have been wrong.

Megillus
Then I'm glad I held back.

Athenian
Exactly. A quality that's merely an accessory — something that attaches to things that might be praiseworthy or blameworthy — doesn't really call for a verdict on its own. It's better left without comment.

Megillus
You're talking about self-discipline?

Athenian
Yes. But among the other virtues, the one that's most beneficial while also possessing this quality of self-discipline deserves the highest honor. The next most beneficial deserves the next highest honor. And so on — each virtue honored in its proper order.

Megillus
True.

Athenian
And shouldn't it be the lawmaker's job to establish these rankings?

Megillus
It certainly should.

Athenian
How about we leave the detailed rankings to the lawmaker? But the broad classification of laws into first, second, and third priority — that's something we lovers of law can work out ourselves.

Megillus
Sounds good.

Athenian
Here's what we maintain, then: a state that wants to be as safe and happy as human nature allows must distribute honor and dishonor in the right way. And the right way is this: put the goods of the soul first and highest — always assuming self-discipline as the foundation. Put the goods of the body second. And put wealth and property third.

If any lawmaker or state departs from this order by putting money in the place of honor, or by elevating what truly belongs last — can't we say they're committing an unholy and unpatriotic act?

Megillus
Yes. Let's declare that plainly.

Athenian
It was our examination of Persian government that led us into this longer discussion. What we found is that the Persians kept getting worse. And the reason, we argue, is that they stripped their people of too much freedom and imposed too much despotism, destroying friendship and the sense of common purpose. And once those are gone, the rulers stop governing for the benefit of their subjects and the people, and start governing for themselves. If they think they can gain even the smallest advantage, they'll devastate cities and burn friendly nations to the ground. They hate ruthlessly and horribly — and are hated in return. When they need their people to fight for them, they find no shared loyalty, no willingness to risk life on their behalf. Their armies may number in the millions, but those millions are useless on the battlefield. So they end up thinking their salvation depends on hiring mercenaries and foreigners, as if they didn't have enough men of their own. And they can't help being foolish, since their actions broadcast that the ordinary distinctions between right and wrong in a society are trivial compared to gold and silver.

Megillus
Exactly right.

Athenian
So we're done with the Persians, and our conclusion is that their current misgovernment is the result of too much slavery and too much despotism.

Megillus
Good.

Athenian
Next, we need to examine the government of Athens in the same way. We need to show that total freedom and the complete absence of any higher authority isn't nearly as good as moderate government under properly limited authority. That's what Athens had in our ancient constitution, back when the Persians launched their attack on Greece — or rather, on all of Europe.

There were four classes of citizens, arranged by a property assessment, and respect for law was our queen and mistress. It made us willing to live in obedience to the laws of that time. On top of that, the sheer scale of the Persian threat — by land and sea — created such overwhelming fear that we became even more obedient to our rulers and our laws. All these factors combined to produce an extraordinary sense of harmony among us.

About ten years before the naval battle at Salamis, a Persian general named Datis arrived, leading an army sent by King Darius with explicit orders to capture the Athenians and the Eretrians and carry them off. He was told to accomplish this on pain of death. Datis and his enormous force quickly conquered Eretria completely. Then he sent a terrifying report back to Athens: not a single Eretrian had escaped. His soldiers had literally linked hands and swept through all of Eretria like a net.

Whether that report was entirely accurate or not, it struck terror into every Greek heart — and the Athenians most of all. They sent ambassadors in every direction begging for help, but nobody was willing to come — except the Spartans. And they were either delayed by the Messenian war that was going on at the time, or by some other reason we're not told about, and arrived one day too late for the Battle of Marathon.

Then, after a while, reports started coming of even more massive preparations and endless threats from the Persian king. Time passed, and word arrived that Darius was dead and his young, hot-headed son had taken the throne and was pressing ahead with the invasion plan. The Athenians felt sure the whole expedition was aimed at them because of Marathon.

Then they heard about the bridge over the Hellespont, the canal cut through Mount Athos, and the vast fleet of ships. They could see no path to safety — not by land, not by sea. No one was going to help them. They remembered that in the first Persian invasion, when Eretria was destroyed, nobody had come to their rescue or risked an alliance with them. They assumed the same thing would happen again on land. And when they looked to the sea, they couldn't see any hope of survival there either — not against more than a thousand warships.

One slender, desperate chance remained. Their only chance. They looked at Marathon, at that seemingly impossible victory, and drew strength from it. They realized that their only refuge was in themselves and in the gods.

All of this created a powerful spirit of solidarity among them. There was the immediate fear of the moment. But there was also that deeper kind of fear — the reverence they'd developed through obedience to their ancient laws, which I've called "reverence" several times in our discussion. It's the fear that a good person willingly serves, while a coward knows nothing of it. If this reverence hadn't possessed them, they would never have stood together to defend their temples, their burial grounds, their country, and everything they held dear. Little by little, they would have scattered and been picked off one by one.

Megillus
Your words are true, Athenian, and worthy of both you and your country.

Athenian
They are true, Megillus. And it's fitting to share these things with you, since you've inherited your ancestors' virtues. But I'd like both you and Cleinias to consider whether my words don't also bear on our subject — legislation. I'm not just talking for the pleasure of it; I'm making an argument. Notice that the experience of both Athens and Persia was, in a certain sense, the same: just as the Persians led their people into utter slavery, so we Athenians led ours into utter freedom. The question now is: where does this take us? Our earlier arguments have a great deal to say here.

Megillus
True. But I'd like a fuller explanation.

Athenian
I'll give you one. Under the ancient laws, my friends, the people of Athens weren't the masters — they were the willing servants of the laws.

Megillus
Which laws do you mean?

Athenian
Let's start with the laws governing music — that is, the music of that era — so we can trace the growth of excessive freedom from its roots. In those days, music was divided into clearly defined categories. One kind consisted of hymns — prayers to the gods. Another, opposite kind was called lamentations. Then there were paeans, and also dithyrambs — songs celebrating the birth of Dionysus. There was yet another kind they actually called nomoi — "laws" — adding the term "citharoedic" to distinguish them. All of these, and others too, were precisely classified. Performers were not allowed to mix one style with another.

And the authority that maintained these standards and judged the performances and punished those who broke the rules wasn't expressed through hissing, or the crude shouting of the crowd, or through applause and clapping — the way it is today. No: the directors of public education insisted that the audience listen in silence all the way through. Boys, their teachers, and the general public were kept in line with a stick if necessary. The public was willing to maintain this good order. No one would have dreamed of making noise to express their judgment.

Then, over time, the poets themselves ushered in a reign of vulgar, lawless innovation. They were talented people, but they had no understanding of what was right and proper in music. They went wild — like Bacchants possessed with uncontrollable passion — mixing lamentations with hymns, paeans with dithyrambs, imitating flute music on the lyre, jumbling everything together. In their ignorance, they declared that music has no real truth, and that the only way to judge it — good or bad — is by how much pleasure it gives the listener.

By composing these reckless works, and setting equally reckless words to them, they infected the public with lawlessness and boldness. They made ordinary people think they were qualified to judge music and poetry for themselves. And that's how the theaters — which used to be silent — became vocal, as if the audience actually understood what was good and bad in art. Instead of a wise aristocracy of taste, what grew up was a wretched "theatrocracy."

Now, if this democratic judging had been limited to educated people, the damage would have been minor. But once the principle took root in music — this universal delusion of expertise, this general lawlessness — freedom followed. People thought they knew things they didn't know. They lost all sense of fear. And the absence of fear breeds shamelessness. What is shamelessness, after all, but the arrogant refusal to respect the judgment of one's betters — born from an excessive, reckless kind of liberty?

Megillus
Very true.

Athenian
After this kind of freedom comes another: disobedience to rulers. Then the attempt to escape the guidance of parents, mothers, fathers, and elders. As they near the end of this path, they try to escape the control of the laws too. And at the very end comes contempt for oaths and promises and total disregard for the gods. At that point they're acting out the old story of the Titans, who rebelled against the gods — returning to that same ancient pattern and living a life of endless misery.

But why have I said all this? I need to rein the argument in now and then, hold it with bit and bridle, so that — as the proverb says — we don't fall off the donkey. So let's ask again: what was the point of all this?

Megillus
Good idea.

Athenian
The point was this —

Megillus
Which was?

Athenian
We were saying that the lawmaker should keep three goals in mind: that the city he's legislating for should be free, internally unified, and wise. Those were our principles, weren't they?

Megillus
Yes.

Athenian
With those goals in mind, we picked two forms of government — the most despotic and the most free — and we've been asking which one gets it right. What we saw was that each one achieves its best form at a moderate level. When either is pushed to the extreme — extreme slavery or extreme liberty — neither produces good results.

Megillus
Very true.

Athenian
And that was our reason for tracing the settlement of the Dorian army, and the city Dardanus built at the foot of the mountains, and the migration of cities to the seashore, and the earliest humans who survived the flood. Everything we said earlier about music and drinking parties — all of it — served the same purpose: figuring out how a state can best be governed and how an individual can best order their own life.

Now, Megillus and Cleinias: how do we test whether all this has been worth saying?

Cleinias
Friend, I think I see exactly how we can put it to the test. This whole discussion seems to have come at the perfect time — it's exactly what I need right now. Your arrival here with Megillus feels like a kind of omen, and I'll tell you why.

Most of Crete is about to send out a colony. They've put the Knossians in charge of the project, and the Knossian government has appointed me and nine others to manage it. Our instructions are to give the colony whatever laws we think best — whether borrowed from the Cretan model or from anywhere else. They don't care if the laws are foreign, as long as they're good.

So do me this favor — and it'll benefit you as well: let's select from everything we've discussed, and let's imagine we're the original founders of a state. That way, we can continue our inquiry while also building a practical framework that I can actually use for the city we're about to establish.

Athenian
That's wonderful news, Cleinias! If Megillus has no objection, you can count on me to do everything I can.

Cleinias
Thank you.

Megillus
And you can count on me too.

Cleinias
Excellent. Now let's begin building our state.
