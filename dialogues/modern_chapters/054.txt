LAWS
Book 8

Athenian
Next, with the help of the Delphian oracle, we need to establish festivals and make laws about them. We have to determine which sacrifices will be good for the city and to which gods they should be offered. But when and how often they should be offered -- that's something we can partly regulate ourselves.

Cleinias
The number, yes.

Athenian
Then let's start by setting the number. Let the total be 365 -- one for every day -- so that at least one magistrate is always sacrificing to some god or divine spirit on behalf of the city, its citizens, and their property. The interpreters, priests, priestesses, and prophets should meet with the guardians of the law and fill in the details that the legislator necessarily has to leave out. And really, they're exactly the right people to notice what's been left out.

The law will say this: There are twelve feasts dedicated to the twelve gods after whom the tribes are named. Each month, the citizens shall sacrifice to each of these gods and organize choruses along with musical and athletic contests, arranging them to suit both the gods and the seasons of the year. They'll also have festivals specifically for women, distinguishing between those that should be separate from men's festivals and those that needn't be.

Furthermore, they must not mix up the rites of the underworld gods with those of the heavenly gods, but keep them separate. They should give Pluto his own rites in the twelfth month, which is sacred to him, as the law requires. Warriors shouldn't feel any aversion toward such a deity -- they should actually honor him as humanity's constant best friend. Because the connection of soul and body is in no way better than their separation, and I'm prepared to argue that point seriously.

Beyond this, the people who organize these matters need to consider that our city has no rival among existing cities when it comes to leisure and access to life's necessities. Like an individual, it ought to live happily. And those who'd live happily should first of all do no wrong to one another, and shouldn't be wronged by others either. The first part isn't that hard; the great difficulty is acquiring the power to not be wronged. Nobody can be perfectly secure against being wronged unless they've become perfectly good. The same goes for cities: a good city enjoys a life of peace, while a bad one has war both inside and outside its walls.

Since that's how things are, the citizens ought to practice for war -- not during wartime, but while they're at peace. Every city with any sense should take the field for at least one day every month, and more if the magistrates see fit. They shouldn't worry about winter cold or summer heat. They should go out as a full body, including their wives and children, whenever the magistrates decide to lead out the whole population -- or in separate groups when summoned.

They should always make sure there are games and sacrificial feasts alongside these exercises. They should hold tournaments that imitate real battles as closely as possible. They should award prizes for victory and valor to the competitors, passing judgments and public honors on one another based on how they perform in the contests and in their whole lives -- praising those who seem the best, and criticizing those who are the opposite.

As for poets who celebrate the victors -- not just any poet should be allowed. First, a poet must be at least fifty years old. And he shouldn't be someone who has musical and poetical talent but has never done anything noble or distinguished in his own life. Only those who are themselves good people and honored by the state, creators of noble deeds -- their poems should be sung, even if they're not the most musically gifted.

The judgment of these poems shall rest with the director of education and the other guardians of the law. They alone shall grant this privilege, and they alone shall be free to compose songs. Nobody else shall dare to sing a song that hasn't been approved by the guardians of the law -- not even if his music is sweeter than the songs of Thamyras and Orpheus. Only poems judged sacred and dedicated to the gods, and works by good people in which praise or blame has been properly assigned and that have been deemed to serve their purpose well.

The same regulations about warfare and freedom of expression in poetry should apply equally to men and women. The legislator should think through the question in his own mind: "Who are my citizens, the ones I've organized this city for? Aren't they competitors in the greatest of all contests, with countless rivals?" "Of course," would be the natural reply. Well, if we were training boxers or wrestlers or any other kind of athlete, would they never train until the actual contest arrived? Would we do nothing to prepare in advance? Surely, if we were boxers, we'd have been training for many days beforehand, practicing all those punches and blocks we planned to use in the real fight. And to get as close to reality as possible, we'd put on boxing gloves instead of cestuses, so we could practice our strikes and defenses with everything we had.

If there were a shortage of sparring partners, we wouldn't let the mockery of fools stop us from hanging up a dummy and practicing on that. And if we had no opponent at all, living or otherwise, wouldn't we just shadow-box by ourselves? How else would we ever learn self-defense?

Cleinias
That's really the only way, Stranger.

Athenian
So shall the warriors of our city -- who are destined to enter the greatest of all contests when the time comes, fighting for their lives, their children, their property, and the whole city -- be worse prepared than boxers? Should the legislator hold back from ordering them to train, just because he's afraid their practice sessions might look ridiculous to some people?

No. He should command soldiers to perform lighter exercises without weapons every day, making all dancing and gymnastics serve this purpose. And he should also require more serious armed exercises at least once a month, with contests against one another throughout the country -- seizing positions, setting ambushes, and imitating every aspect of real warfare. They should fight with boxing gloves and hurl javelins, using weapons that are somewhat dangerous and as close to the real thing as possible, so that the training isn't completely free from fear. It should have real terrors that, to some degree, reveal who has courage and who doesn't. Honor and dishonor should be distributed accordingly, preparing the whole city for the genuine struggles of life.

If someone dies in these practice battles, the killing is involuntary. We'll have the killer purified according to law and declare him clean, reasoning this way: if a few people die, others just as good will be born to replace them. But if fear itself dies -- if the citizens never find a way to test who's superior and who's inferior -- that's a far greater evil for the state than losing a few people.

Cleinias
We're in complete agreement, Stranger. The whole state should practice these things, and we should make laws about them.

Athenian
Now, does anyone know why these kinds of dances and contests hardly exist in actual cities -- at least not to any significant degree? Is it because people and their legislators are just ignorant?

Cleinias
Perhaps.

Athenian
Not at all, my dear Cleinias. There are two causes, and they're more than enough to explain the problem.

Cleinias
What are they?

Athenian
The first is the love of wealth. It completely absorbs people and never gives them a moment to think about anything beyond their own private possessions. Every citizen's soul is fixated on daily profit. People are ready to learn any skill, pursue any career that leads to money, and they laugh at everything else. That's one reason why a city won't take these contests -- or any other good and honorable pursuit -- seriously.

Because of this insatiable love of gold and silver, every person will stoop to any trick or trade, respectable or not, in hopes of getting rich. They'll do anything, sacred or profane, utterly degrading -- so long as they can stuff themselves like animals with every kind of food and drink and satisfy their every appetite.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
So let's mark this down as one reason states fail to properly cultivate the art of war or any other noble aim. It turns decent, moderate people into merchants and ship captains and servants. And it converts the naturally brave into thieves and burglars and temple-robbers and violent, tyrannical bullies -- many of whom are actually quite talented, but deeply unfortunate.

Cleinias
What do you mean by "unfortunate"?

Athenian
How can you call anyone anything other than truly unfortunate whose soul is forced to go through life constantly starving?

Cleinias
All right, that's one cause, Stranger. But you mentioned another.

Athenian
Thank you for the reminder.

Cleinias
You were saying that the insatiable, lifelong love of wealth is one thing that absorbs people and prevents them from properly practicing the arts of war. Granted. Now tell me -- what's the other?

Athenian
You think I'm stalling because I'm stuck?

Cleinias
No, but we think you're being a bit too hard on the money-loving temperament. You seem to have a particular grudge against it in this discussion.

Athenian
That's a very fair pushback, Cleinias. I'll move on to the second cause.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
I say it's the forms of government themselves -- democracy, oligarchy, tyranny -- which I've discussed at length already. But really, none of these are true governments at all. None of them exercises voluntary rule over willing subjects. They should really be called states of discord: the government may rule by choice, but the subjects always obey against their will and have to be coerced. And the ruler lives in fear of his subjects, so he won't allow them to become noble, or rich, or strong, or brave, or warlike in any way.

Those are the two chief causes of almost all the evils I've been talking about. But our state has escaped both of them. Its citizens have the greatest leisure, they're not subject to one another's tyranny, and I think our laws will make them the opposite of money-lovers. So we may reasonably suppose that this is the only existing constitution that would accept the education we've described and the military training we've outlined.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Now, the next point to remember about athletic contests: only the military kind should be practiced and should have prizes for victory. Non-military contests should be dropped. Let's describe and establish the military ones by law. And first, let's talk about running and speed.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
The most militarily useful quality of all is general physical agility, whether on foot or with the hands. For escaping or capturing an enemy, you need speed of foot. For hand-to-hand combat, you need power and strength.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
But neither reaches its peak performance without weapons.

Cleinias
How could it?

Athenian
So our herald, following prevailing practice, will first summon the runner -- who will appear armed, because we won't give prizes to unarmed competitors. First to enter will be the one running the single course in armor. Next, the runner in the double course. Third, the horse-course runner. Fourth, the long-distance runner. Fifth, we'll send out a man in heavy armor to run a course of sixty stadia to a temple of Ares. And we'll send another, more heavily armed, to run over smoother ground. Finally, the archer: he'll run in full archer's equipment over a hundred stadia across mountains and every kind of terrain, to a temple of Apollo and Artemis. That's the order of the contest. We'll wait for them to return and award prizes to the winners.

Cleinias
Very good.

Athenian
Let's set up three age divisions for these contests: boys, beardless youths, and men. The youths will run two-thirds of the full course, and the boys half, whether they're competing as archers or heavy-armed. As for the women: girls who haven't yet reached maturity should compete in the stadium, the double course, the horse-course, and the long-distance race, running on the racecourse itself. Those from thirteen up until marriage should continue competing, provided they're no more than twenty years old. They must compete up to age eighteen. They'll enter the arena in appropriate clothing.

Those are the regulations for running contests, for both men and women.

Now, for contests of strength: instead of wrestling and similar heavy-contact sports, we'll institute armed combat -- one against one, two against two, and so on up to ten against ten. As for what a combatant should and shouldn't suffer or do to win -- just as wrestling masters have laid down rules about what's fair and unfair -- we should bring in expert judges to help us legislate these armed fights. They'll determine who deserves to be called the victor, what moves are forbidden, and what counts as a defeat. These same rules shall apply to women until they're married, as well as to men.

The pancration will have a counterpart in light-armed combat: contestants will fight with bows, light shields, javelins, and by throwing stones with slings and by hand. Laws shall be made about this too, and prizes awarded to whoever best follows the rules.

Next in order, we'll need to legislate about horse-racing. But we don't actually need many horses -- in a country like Crete, they can't be much use, so naturally we don't put great effort into breeding them or into horse races. Nobody among us keeps a chariot, and any rivalry in such matters would be completely out of place. It would make no sense to establish contests that don't fit our country's character.

So we'll give prizes for individual horses -- for colts that haven't yet shed their baby teeth, for intermediate-age horses, and for fully grown ones. This way our equestrian games will suit the land's nature. Let them compete according to the law, and let the cavalry colonels and generals jointly decide about all courses and the armed competitors in them.

For unarmed contestants -- whether in gymnastics or horse-racing -- we have nothing to say. But the Cretan archer or javelin-thrower who fights in armor on horseback is genuinely useful, so we might as well include competitions for that among our games. As for women: they shouldn't be forced to compete by law. But if their previous training has made them strong enough and they want to take part -- girls and grown women alike -- let them, with no disgrace attached.

So that covers athletic competitions and how to learn them. We've also discussed the daily training routines under the guidance of instructors. And most of what relates to music has already been settled. As for rhapsodes and similar performers, and the choral contests to be held at festivals -- all of that will be arranged once the months, days, and years have been assigned to their respective gods and divine spirits, whether the festivals occur every three years, every five years, or however the gods inspire people to organize them.

At the same time, we can expect that the musical contests will be overseen by the judges, the director of education, and the guardians of the law, meeting together for this purpose and acting as legislators for the timing, nature, and conditions of the choral contests and all dancing. The original legislator has already laid down frequently what these should be in terms of language, song, and the blending of harmony with rhythm and dance. His successors should follow his lead, matching games and sacrifices appropriately to their proper times and establishing public festivals.

It's not hard to figure out how to put these matters in good order. And adjusting the details won't do any great good or harm to the state. But there is another matter of great importance and difficulty -- the kind of thing a god should legislate on, if it were possible to get a divine ruling. But since we can't count on divine help, it seems we need some bold person who especially values plain speaking and will say outright what he thinks is best for the city and its citizens. Someone who'll prescribe what's good and fitting for the whole state amid the corruption of human souls, opposing the most powerful appetites, with nobody to help him -- standing alone and following reason only.

Cleinias
What are you talking about, Stranger? We don't understand what you mean yet.

Athenian
That's fair. Let me try to explain more clearly. When I came to the subject of education, I pictured young men and women socializing freely with each other. And naturally, a kind of worry came over me. I couldn't help thinking: how do you manage a city where the young people are well-fed and well-raised, where they don't have to do the kind of backbreaking, soul-crushing labor that stamps out desire, and where their only real obligations their whole lives are sacrifices, festivals, and dances? In a city like that, how are they going to resist the desires that drive so many men and women to ruin -- desires that reason, acting as law, commands them to resist?

Now, the rules we've already established may succeed against most of these desires. The prohibition on excessive wealth goes a long way toward promoting self-control. Our entire educational system imposes discipline on the young. And the watchful eye of the rulers is always on them. These measures do, as far as humanly possible, regulate desire in general.

But how do we guard against unnatural sexual loves in either sex -- the source of countless evils for individuals and cities alike? How do we devise a remedy and an escape from such a serious danger?

This is genuinely difficult, Cleinias. In many areas, Crete and Sparta provide great help to those who make distinctive laws. But on the matter of sexual love, I have to say frankly -- they're completely against us.

If someone were to follow nature and lay down the law that existed before the time of Laius, declaring these desires to be contrary to nature -- pointing to the animal kingdom as proof that such unions are unnatural -- he might well prove his point. But he'd be completely at odds with the customs of your own states.

Furthermore, these practices clash with a principle we say the legislator should always observe: we're always asking which of our laws promotes virtue and which doesn't. Now suppose we grant that such loves are considered, by current law, to be honorable -- or at least not disgraceful. To what extent will they contribute to virtue? Will such passions plant the habit of courage in the soul of the one who's pursued? Or the principle of self-control in the soul of the pursuer? Nobody would believe that. In fact, it's the opposite -- everyone will criticize the weakness of the person who gives in to pleasure and can't resist it. Everyone will condemn as effeminate the man who plays the woman's role. So who would ever think of establishing such a practice by law? Certainly nobody who had a clear picture of what true law looks like.

How can I prove this? You need to understand the nature of friendship and desire, and of these so-called loves. There are really two kinds, and from those two a third arises that shares the same name. It's this shared name that causes all the confusion and difficulty.

Cleinias
How so?

Athenian
There's the love between equals -- where like is dear to like in virtue, and equal to equal. And there's the love born from need -- where the one who has plenty is dear to the one who lacks. When either of these friendships becomes excessive, we call it love.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Now, the friendship that arises from opposites -- where one has and the other wants -- is raw and crude, and often has no deep bond of community. But the friendship that arises from likeness is gentle and creates a bond that lasts a lifetime.

As for the third kind, the mixture of the two -- well, first of all, it's hard even to figure out what the person gripped by this mixed love actually wants. He's pulled in two directions and caught between two principles. One urges him to enjoy the beauty of youth; the other forbids him.

The one is a lover of the body. He hungers after beauty the way you'd hunger after ripe fruit, and he wants to satisfy himself with no regard for the character of the person he desires. The other holds physical desire to be secondary. He looks with his soul rather than lusting with his body, his soul desiring the other's soul in the proper way. He regards satisfying bodily lust as degrading. He reveres and respects self-control, courage, nobility of spirit, and wisdom, and wants to live purely with the one he loves, who is equally pure.

The love that mixes these two is the third kind I've described.

Since there are these three kinds of love, should the law prohibit and ban all of them from our state? Or isn't it obvious that we'd want the kind of love that's rooted in virtue and desires the beloved to become the best person possible? The other two, if we can manage it, we should try to prevent.

What do you say, friend Megillus?

Megillus
I think you're absolutely right in everything you've been saying, Stranger.

Athenian
I knew I'd have your agreement, my friend, and I accept it. I don't need to analyze your Spartan customs on this point any further. Cleinias can give me his opinion some other time. For now, let's move on to the laws.

Megillus
Very good.

Athenian
On reflection, I see a way to impose this law that's easy in one respect but incredibly difficult in another.

Megillus
What do you mean?

Athenian
We all know that most people, despite their lawless natures, are very strictly and precisely restrained from one particular kind of sexual intercourse -- and not against their will, but entirely with their will.

Megillus
When is that?

Athenian
When someone has a brother or sister who's attractive. The same unwritten law applies to sons and daughters, and it's a perfect safeguard. No one -- openly or in secret -- ever has sexual contact with them. The very thought never even enters most people's minds.

Megillus
Very true.

Athenian
A single little word extinguishes all such desires, doesn't it?

Megillus
What word?

Athenian
The declaration that these acts are unholy, hateful to the gods, and utterly shameful. And isn't the reason simply this: no one has ever said otherwise. From earliest childhood, every person hears people speaking the same way about this -- always, everywhere. They hear it in comedy, they hear it in the solemn language of tragedy. When the poet puts a Thyestes or an Oedipus or a Macareus on stage -- someone who's had secret intercourse with a sibling -- he shows the character, once discovered, choosing death as the penalty for their sin.

Megillus
You're quite right. Tradition, when no opposing voice ever challenges it, has an extraordinary power.

Athenian
So doesn't this confirm what I said? That the legislator who wants to tame any passion that dominates people can easily see how to subdue it? He should consecrate the tradition that these things are evil in the eyes of everyone -- slaves and free, women and children, throughout the entire city. That will be the surest foundation for his law.

Megillus
Yes. But will he ever succeed in getting all of humanity to speak with one voice on this?

Athenian
Good objection. But wasn't I just saying that I had a way to make people follow natural love and abstain from unnatural -- a way that doesn't intentionally destroy the seeds of human reproduction or sow them in barren ground where they can't take root? A way that commands people to abstain from any sexual act that isn't likely to produce life?

Now, if a law to this effect could be made permanent and gain the kind of authority that already prevents sexual relations between parents and children -- if it could achieve that level of power over other desires too -- it would be the source of ten thousand blessings.

First, it would be following the order of nature. It would restrain people from sexual frenzy and madness, from adultery, from excessive eating and drinking. It would make them true friends to their own spouses. And countless other benefits would follow if such a law could be enforced.

Now, I can picture some vigorous young man standing nearby, overhearing this proposal, and yelling in crude language that we're making foolish, impossible laws, filling the room with his protests. And that's exactly why I said I knew a way to establish and maintain such a law that's very easy in one respect but extremely difficult in another.

It's not hard to see that such a law is possible, and how it could work. As I was saying, once the principle is truly consecrated as sacred, it would master every person's soul and terrify them into obedience. But things have now reached such a point that even then, the desired result seems almost unattainable -- just as people say it's impossible for a whole city to maintain the practice of communal meals.

And yet that's partly disproven by the fact that communal meals already exist among you. Still, even in your cities, communal meals for women are considered unnatural and impossible. I was thinking about the rebelliousness of the human heart when I said that establishing these things permanently is very difficult.

Megillus
Very true.

Athenian
Shall I try to find some persuasive argument that proves such laws are possible and not beyond human nature?

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
Is a person more likely to resist the pleasures of sex and follow the rules when their body is in good condition through training, or when they're out of shape?

Cleinias
They'll be far more self-controlled when they're in training.

Athenian
Haven't we heard of Iccus of Tarentum? In his dedication to the Olympic and other contests -- driven by both his devotion to his sport and his naturally disciplined character -- he never had sexual contact with a woman or a man during his entire training period. The same is said of Crison, Astylus, Diopompus, and many others. And yet, Cleinias, these men were far less educated in mind than your citizens and mine, and far more vigorous in body.

Cleinias
You're right -- the ancients did consistently confirm this about those athletes.

Athenian
So they had the discipline to abstain from what's generally considered a great pleasure, all for the sake of winning at wrestling or running or the like. And our young people will be incapable of the same endurance for a far nobler victory -- the noblest of all? We'll tell them this from youth onward, charming them, as we hope, into believing it through stories, sayings, and songs.

Cleinias
What victory are you talking about?

Athenian
Victory over pleasure. If they win that battle, they'll live happily. If they lose it, the opposite. Beyond that, won't the fear of impiety help them master what other, lesser people have already mastered?

Cleinias
I'd expect so.

Athenian
Well, since we've reached this point in our legislation and hit a wall because of human weakness, I'll state simply what our law should say:

Our citizens ought not to be worse than birds and other animals, which are born in great numbers and yet remain virgin and unmated until the proper age for breeding. When they reach that age, they pair off -- male with female -- and live out the rest of their days in devotion and faithfulness, holding firmly to their original bond.

Surely, we'll say to our citizens, you should be better than the animals.

But if they're corrupted by the rest of the Greeks and the common practices of other peoples -- if they see with their own eyes and hear with their own ears about so-called "free love" everywhere around them, and they can't resist the temptation -- then the guardians of the law, acting as secondary legislators, shall devise a second law to deal with them.

Cleinias
And what law would you recommend if the first one fails?

Athenian
Obviously, Cleinias, the one that naturally follows.

Cleinias
Which is?

Athenian
Our citizens shouldn't let their pleasures grow stronger through indulgence. Instead, they should redirect that energy into other parts of the body through hard physical work. And this will happen if sexual activity is never practiced shamelessly.

They should be ashamed of frequent intercourse, and they'll find that pleasure, when rarely enjoyed, is a less tyrannical master. Not being caught should be considered honorable -- sanctioned by custom and established as law by unwritten tradition. Being caught, on the other hand, should be considered disgraceful -- though it's not as honorable as abstaining entirely.

This way, there will be a second standard of honor and dishonor, creating a second level of moral conduct. Three principles will encompass all those weak-willed people whom we call "inferior to themselves" -- they form a single class -- and will compel them not to transgress.

Cleinias
What are these three principles?

Athenian
Reverence for the gods, love of honor, and the desire for beauty -- not of the body, but of the soul. These may sound like romantic ideals, but they're the noblest ideals imaginable, if only they could be realized in every state. And God willing, on the matter of sexual conduct, we may be able to enforce one of two standards.

Either, first: no one shall touch any freeborn or noble person except their lawfully wedded spouse, nor scatter their seed among prostitutes or in barren, unnatural unions.

Or, failing that, at the very least: we'll abolish sexual relations between men entirely. And as for women -- if any man has relations with any woman other than the one who came into his household through sacred marriage rites (whether she was formally married to him or acquired in any other legitimate way), and he commits this offense openly in the sight of all -- we'll be right to decree that he be stripped of his civic honors and privileges and considered what he truly is: a foreigner to the city's values.

Let this law, then -- whether it's one law or should really be called two -- be established concerning sexual love in general and the sexual relations that arise from desire, whether rightly or wrongly pursued.

Megillus
For my part, Stranger, I'd gladly accept this law. Cleinias can speak for himself and tell you his opinion.

Cleinias
I will, Megillus, when the right opportunity comes. But for now, I think we should let the Stranger continue with his laws.

Megillus
Very good.

Athenian
We'd gotten about as far as establishing the communal meals, which would be difficult in most places but which no one in Crete would think of changing. The question might come up about what form they should take -- should they be like they are here in Crete, or like those in Sparta, or is there some third kind that might be better than either? The answer wouldn't be hard to find, but the discovery wouldn't do much good, since they're already quite well organized as they are.

Setting communal meals aside, then, let's move on to the means of providing food. In most cities, people make their living in many ways and from many sources -- generally from two: sea and land. But our city relies on land alone. And this makes the legislator's job much easier. Half as many laws will be enough -- actually, much less than half. And they'll be laws better suited to free people.

Our legislator has nothing to do with laws about shipowners, merchants, retailers, innkeepers, tax collectors, mines, money-lending, compound interest, and countless other such things. He can say goodbye to all of that. He gives laws to farmers and shepherds and beekeepers, and to the guardians and superintendents of their tools. He's already legislated about the bigger matters -- marriage, the begetting and raising of children, education, the establishment of offices. Now he has to turn his attention to those who provide food and the labor of preparing it.

So let's start with a class of laws we'll call the laws for farmers. And let the very first be the law of Zeus, god of boundaries:

No one shall move a boundary marker -- not of a fellow citizen who's a neighbor, nor of a stranger who lives at the border of the land. Understand that this truly means "moving the immovable." Everyone should be more willing to move the biggest boulder that isn't a landmark than the smallest stone that marks the sworn boundary between neighbors, the line of friendship and enmity. Zeus, the god of kinship, witnesses the citizen's oath, and Zeus, the god of strangers, witnesses the stranger's. When they're roused, terrible are the wars they stir up.

Whoever obeys this law will never know the fatal consequences of disobedience. But whoever breaks it shall face a double punishment -- the first from the gods, the second from the law. Let no one willfully move a neighbor's boundary markers. If anyone does, let any landowner report him, and let them bring him to court. If he's convicted of secretly or forcibly re-dividing land, let the court determine what he should suffer or pay.

Next: many small injuries between neighbors, through their accumulation, can pile up into a crushing weight of resentment and make living next door to someone a genuinely bitter thing. A person ought to be very careful about offending their neighbor, especially about encroaching on their neighbor's land. It's easy for anyone to cause harm, but not everyone can do good.

Whoever encroaches on a neighbor's land and crosses their boundaries shall make good the damage. And to cure him of both his rudeness and his pettiness, he shall pay double damages to the injured party. The country wardens shall handle these matters and act as judges, assessing the damage. In more important cases, as we've already said, the full panel of wardens from whichever of the twelve districts is relevant shall decide. In lesser cases, the commanders shall judge.

If anyone grazes their cattle on a neighbor's land, the wardens shall assess the damage and set the penalty. If anyone steals another person's bees by luring them away with noise, he shall pay damages. If anyone sets fire to their own wood and takes no care to protect their neighbor's property, they'll be fined at the magistrates' discretion. If someone plants too close to their neighbor's land without leaving a proper distance, they'll be punished in accordance with the rulings of many lawgivers, which we may freely use. The great legislator of our state needn't bother determining every little thing that any ordinary person could decide.

For instance, farmers have long had excellent laws about water, and there's no reason for us to reinvent them. Anyone who wants may draw water from the main stream onto their own land, as long as they don't cut off the spring that clearly belongs to another owner. They can direct the water however they please, except through a house, temple, or tomb -- but they must be careful not to cause damage beyond their own channel.

If there's a naturally dry area that soaks up the rain and causes a water shortage, let the landowner dig on their own property down to the clay layer. If they find no water at that depth, they may get water from their neighbors -- as much as their household servants need for drinking. If the neighbors are also running short, they'll receive a fixed daily measure set by the country wardens, on those terms sharing their neighbors' water.

If heavy rain falls and someone on lower ground injures a farmer above them by refusing to let the water drain off -- or if someone on higher ground recklessly dumps water onto their lower neighbor -- and they can't work it out between themselves, let either party call in a city warden (if in the city) or a country warden (if in the country) and get a ruling on what each of them must do. Anyone who won't abide by the decision shall suffer for their stubborn and spiteful temperament and pay a fine to the injured party worth double the damage, because they refused to submit to the magistrates.

Now, the sharing of the harvest shall be regulated as follows. The goddess of autumn gives us two gracious gifts: one is the joy of Dionysus -- grapes and fruit that aren't meant to be stored. The other is fruit that nature intends for keeping. So let this be the law concerning the fruits of autumn:

Anyone who tastes the common, storable fruits of autumn -- whether grapes or figs -- before the harvest season that coincides with the rising of Arcturus, whether on their own land or someone else's, shall pay a fine: fifty drachmas (sacred to Dionysus) if they pick from their own land, one mina if from a neighbor's, and two-thirds of a mina if from anyone else's.

As for the "choice" grapes and "choice" figs, as they're currently called: if someone picks them from their own land, they may do so however and whenever they like. But if they take them from someone else's land without permission, they shall always be punished under the law that says you shouldn't take what you didn't put there.

If a slave touches any of this fruit without the landowner's consent, he shall receive one lash for every grape on the bunch or every fig on the tree.

A resident alien may purchase the "choice" fruit, and then pick it as he pleases. But if a stranger is just passing through and wants something to eat, let him take some "choice" grapes for himself and one companion, free of charge, as a gesture of hospitality. The law does, however, forbid strangers from sharing in the storable kind. If anyone -- master or slave -- takes the storable fruit out of ignorance, the slave shall be beaten, and the free person dismissed with a warning and told to take from the other kinds of fruit -- the ones that aren't suitable for making raisins, wine, or dried figs.

As for pears, apples, pomegranates, and similar fruits: there's no disgrace in taking them secretly. But anyone caught under the age of thirty shall be struck and driven off -- without serious injury -- and no free person shall have any legal claim for such blows. Strangers may eat these fruits just as they may eat the autumn fruits. And if a citizen over thirty eats them on the spot without carrying any away, he shall be treated the same as the stranger -- allowed to have his fill. But if he won't follow this rule and tries to carry some off, let him risk disqualification from the competitions of virtue, in case anyone reports his behavior to the judges when the time comes.

Water is the most important nutrient for gardens, but it's easily polluted. You can't poison the soil, the sun, or the air -- the other elements that nourish plants -- nor can you divert or steal them. But all of these things can easily happen with water, which is why it needs legal protection.

Here is the law: If anyone intentionally pollutes another person's water -- whether it's a spring or water collected in a reservoir -- by poisonous substances, by digging, or by theft, the injured party shall bring the case before the city wardens and submit a written claim for the value of the loss. If the accused is found guilty of contaminating the water with harmful substances, he shall not only pay damages but also purify the stream or cistern that holds the water, in whatever manner the religious interpreters prescribe for each specific case.

Regarding the transport of harvested crops: a person may carry their own produce through any place where they either do no harm to anyone, or where they stand to gain three times as much as their neighbor loses. The magistrates should oversee these matters, as they should all cases where one person intentionally harms another or their property through fraud or force, or by misusing their own property.

For all these matters, a person should bring their complaint before the magistrates and receive damages, as long as the injury is no more than three minas. If the claim involves a larger amount, they should bring their suit to the public courts and have the offender punished. And if any magistrate appears to be imposing penalties in an unfair spirit, they shall be liable to pay double to the injured party. Anyone may bring charges against magistrates for wrongdoing in any particular case before the public courts.

There are countless little procedural details about methods of punishment, filing suits, issuing summonses, and witnesses for summonses -- for example, whether two witnesses are required for a summons, or how many -- and all such details that can't be left out of legislation but are beneath the dignity of an experienced legislator. These lesser matters, lesser as they truly are compared to the greater ones, should be regulated by the younger generation through laws modeled on the patterns we've set down, reflecting their own experience of what's useful and necessary. Once those rules are properly established, let there be no changes. Let the citizens live by them.

Now, regarding craftsmen and artisans, the rules should be as follows:

First, no citizen or servant of a citizen should work in the manual trades. A person who's going to preserve and maintain the public order of the state already has a full-time job -- one that requires extensive study and wide-ranging knowledge. It doesn't allow for moonlighting. And hardly any human being is capable of doing two jobs or practicing two trades well, or of practicing one trade themselves while supervising someone else in a different one.

So let this be our first principle: no one who's a metalworker should also be a carpenter. And if someone is a carpenter, he shouldn't supervise metalwork alongside his own craft, using the excuse that overseeing multiple workers brings in more revenue than his own trade alone. Every person in the state shall have one craft and earn their living from that alone.

The city wardens shall enforce this law. If any citizen turns toward any occupation other than the study of virtue, they shall punish him with disgrace and loss of standing until they bring him back to his proper course. If any foreigner practices two trades, they shall punish him with imprisonment, fines, and expulsion from the state, until they compel him to be one person, not many.

As for payments, contracts for work, and disputes: if anyone wrongs a citizen or is wronged by one, for amounts up to fifty drachmas, the city wardens shall decide the case. For larger amounts, the public courts shall decide according to law.

No one shall pay any import or export duty. As for frankincense and similar perfumes used in worship that come from abroad, and purple dye and other colors not produced locally, and materials for any craft that must be imported but aren't essential -- no one should bring these in. Likewise, no one should export anything the country needs.

There shall be inspectors and superintendents for all of this, drawn from the guardians of the law -- specifically the twelve next in seniority after the five senior ones.

For weapons and all military equipment -- if there's a need to import any craft, plant, metal, chain, or animal for use in war -- the cavalry commanders and generals shall have authority over imports and exports. The city shall send them out and receive them, and the guardians of the law shall make proper laws about them.

But there shall be absolutely no retail trade for profit -- whether in these or any other articles -- anywhere in the city or the country.

Regarding food and the distribution of the country's produce, the right approach seems to be roughly the Cretan custom. Everyone should divide the fruits of the earth into twelve parts and consume them that way.

The twelfth portion of each product -- wheat, barley, and the rest of the earth's produce, along with the animals for sale in each of the twelve districts -- shall be divided into three parts: one for free citizens, one for their servants, and one for craftsmen and foreigners in general, whether they're permanent residents who have to earn a living like everyone else, or visitors who've come on business with the state or some individual.

Only this third portion of necessities should be required to be sold. Nobody shall be forced to sell from the other two-thirds.

Next, our citizens should have separate, well-ordered houses. Here's the proper arrangement for people like them:

There shall be twelve hamlets, one in the center of each twelfth of the territory. In each hamlet, they shall first set aside a marketplace, along with temples to the gods and their attendant divine spirits. If there are any local deities of the Magnetes, or sacred sites of ancient gods whose memory has been preserved, they shall pay them their traditional honors. But Hestia, Zeus, and Athena shall have temples everywhere, together with the god who presides over each of the twelve districts. The first houses shall be built around these temples, on the highest ground, to provide the safest and most defensible place of retreat for the guards.

For the rest of the country, they shall organize things like this: They'll divide the craftsmen into thirteen groups. One group they'll establish in the city proper, subdivided into twelve sections distributed among the twelve city districts. The rest shall be settled in the villages across the countryside. In each village, they'll settle various classes of craftsmen, chosen to suit the needs of the farmers.

The chief officers of the country wardens shall oversee all of this, determining how many craftsmen each place needs, and of what kind, and settling them where they'll cause the least trouble and do the most good for the farmers. The city wardens shall handle similar arrangements within the city.

Now, the wardens of the marketplace should attend to the marketplace's details. Their first concern, after seeing to the temples in the marketplace, should be to prevent anyone from cheating in their dealings. Second, as guardians of good behavior and public order, they should punish anyone who needs it.

Regarding goods for sale, they should first make sure that the products citizens are required to sell to foreigners are actually being sold as the law prescribes. And the law is as follows:

On the first day of the month, the persons in charge -- whether foreigners or slaves acting on behalf of citizens -- shall present to the foreigners their share. First, a twelfth of the grain: foreigners shall purchase their grain for the whole month, along with other cereals, on the first market day. On the tenth of the month, one party shall sell and the other buy enough liquids to last the whole month. On the twenty-third, there shall be a sale of animals by those willing to sell to those wanting to buy, along with farming implements and other goods that farmers sell -- things like hides and all kinds of clothing, whether woven, felted, or otherwise made -- which foreigners must purchase.

As for retail trade in these items -- barley or wheat flour, or any other kind of food -- no one shall sell them to citizens or their slaves, nor shall any citizen buy them. Instead, foreigners shall sell these goods in the foreigners' market to craftsmen and their slaves, exchanging wine and food -- what's commonly called retail trade. Butchers shall sell cuts of meat to foreigners, craftsmen, and their servants.

Any foreigner who wants may buy firewood wholesale on any day from those who manage it in the countryside, and may sell it to other foreigners as much as he wants, whenever he wants.

All other goods and equipment that are likely to be needed shall be sold in the common market, at whatever locations the guardians of the law and the wardens of the market and city select at their discretion. At these locations, goods shall be exchanged for money and money for goods, with neither party giving credit. Whoever gives credit must accept the consequences -- whether they get their money back or not -- because in such transactions, the law won't protect them.

Whenever property has been bought or sold in amounts greater in quantity or value than the law allows -- the law having set limits on how much a person may increase or decrease their possessions -- the excess shall be recorded in the books of the guardians of the law. If there's been a decrease, there shall be an erasure. The same rules shall apply to the registration of property belonging to resident aliens.

Anyone who wishes may come and settle as a resident alien, on certain conditions. A foreigner may dwell in the land if he's willing and able, but he must practice a trade and not stay longer than twenty years from the time he registers. He shall pay no residency tax, however small, except good behavior -- no other tax for buying and selling either.

When the twenty years are up, he shall take his property and leave. If during those years he happens to distinguish himself by doing the state some significant service, and he believes he can persuade the council and assembly to grant him an extension or allow him to stay permanently, let him go make his case. Whatever the city agrees to at his request shall take effect.

For the children of resident aliens who are craftsmen: the clock starts at age fifteen. Let them stay for twenty years and then go where they please. Any who wish to remain may do so, if they can persuade the council and assembly. And if a resident alien departs, let him erase all the entries that were made in his name in the register kept by the magistrates.
