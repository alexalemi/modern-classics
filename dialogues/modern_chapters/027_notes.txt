Charmides -- Translation Notes
================================

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE
-----------------------------

1. NARRATED FORMAT: Preserved Socrates' first-person narration throughout,
   consistent with the Protagoras and Republic translations. Speech tags
   modernized: "said I" -> "I said"; "he rejoined" -> "he said"/"he replied."
   Embedded dialogue uses quotation marks. Stage directions and scene-setting
   kept vivid (the bench-shoving scene, the glimpse inside the cloak, the
   blushing). These ground the philosophy in real life.

2. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Jowett's long, nested Victorian sentences -- some
   running 80-100+ words -- broken into shorter, natural units. Target of
   15-30 words per sentence on average, with variation for rhythm. Some of
   the densest philosophical passages (the "science of science" arguments,
   the self-relation parallels) required the most restructuring. The goal
   was clarity without losing any logical step.

3. THE SOCRATIC IRONY: This dialogue is dripping with irony, especially:
   - Socrates' exaggerated praise of Charmides' ancestry ("sweet son of
     Glaucon, your outward appearance is no disgrace to any of them") --
     preserved with the layered compliment structure intact
   - Socrates' mock helplessness ("I must be a terrible investigator")
   - The headache-cure pretext -- a transparent excuse to examine
     Charmides' soul, which everyone present understands
   - "Most excellent Critias" when needling Critias about stepping in
   - The final exchange where Charmides and Critias "conspire" to attach
     themselves to Socrates -- deeply ironic given Critias' later tyranny

4. CRITIAS' CHARACTERIZATION: His intellectual arrogance comes through in
   his Hesiod argument (the making/doing/working distinction), his wounded
   pride when Charmides seems to fail with "his" definition, and his
   reputation anxiety. The text analysis notes that this foreshadows his
   later tyranny as leader of the Thirty Tyrants. Preserved the moments
   where Critias is described as uncomfortable, angry, and too proud to
   admit confusion -- these are character details that matter.

5. CHARMIDES' CHARACTERIZATION: Modest, charming, genuinely thoughtful.
   His blush, his honest refusal to either claim or deny temperance, his
   sly glance at Critias when the third definition is challenged -- all
   kept vivid. He's the most likeable figure in the dialogue.

6. THE BEAUTY SCENE: Socrates' account of being struck by Charmides'
   beauty is extravagant and deliberately excessive ("I caught fire,"
   the Cydias quotation about the fawn and the lion, "wild animal seizing
   its prey"). Translated faithfully without softening. The irony is that
   Socrates, who claims to be a terrible judge of beauty, gives the most
   elaborate description of it. The bench-shoving scene is physical comedy
   and kept as such.

7. THE ZAMOLXIS/THRACIAN CHARM: This extended passage sets up the entire
   dialogue. The charm = beautiful words = philosophy. The principle that
   the soul must be treated before the body, and the whole before the part,
   is the philosophical foundation. Kept it clear and flowing, preserving
   the chain of reasoning: eyes -> head -> body -> soul.

8. THE PHILOSOPHICAL ARGUMENTS: Three definitions of temperance, each
   examined and found wanting:
   (a) Temperance = quietness. Refuted by showing that quickness is
       superior to quietness in most activities.
   (b) Temperance = modesty. Refuted by Homer's line that modesty
       isn't always good.
   (c) Temperance = doing your own business. This is where Critias
       takes over. Leads to the "science of science" discussion.
   Then the extended examination of whether a "science of science" is
   possible and whether it would be useful -- the self-relation parallels
   (vision that sees only vision, hearing that hears only hearing, etc.)
   are crucial and all preserved.

9. THE "SCIENCE OF SCIENCE" ARGUMENT: This is the most abstract section.
   Kept every step of the reasoning intact:
   - Can anything be self-related? (Parallels with vision, hearing, desire,
     love, fear, opinion)
   - The greater/less paradox (if X is greater than itself, it must also
     be less than itself)
   - Even granting a science of science, it can only tell you THAT you
     know, not WHAT you know
   - A science of science can't evaluate specific expertise (the doctor
     example)
   - The final twist: the knowledge that actually produces happiness isn't
     knowledge-of-knowledge but knowledge of good and evil
   Used paragraph breaks generously to keep the logical structure clear.

10. THE APORETIC ENDING: The dialogue ends without a definition of
    temperance, but with Charmides volunteering to be Socrates' student.
    The "conspiracy" at the end has a playful, almost flirtatious quality
    ("We're not plotting -- we've already plotted"). Preserved this comic
    ending fully.

  "palaestra" -- kept (with context from the scene description)
  "enquire" / "enquiries" -> "ask" / "questions" / "investigate"
  "hitherto" -> "up to this point"
  "notwithstanding" -> "despite" / "even so"
  "I dare say" -> "I expect" / "probably"
  "whereupon" -> "and as he said this"
  "asseverated" -> "established"
  "inutility" -> "uselessness"
  "aforesaid" -> dropped/rephrased
  "ingenuously" -> "honestly"
  "in like manner" -> "in the same way"
  "let us consider" -> "let's think about"
  "perchance" -> "maybe" / "perhaps"
  "nay" -> "not at all" / "wait" (context-dependent)
  "pray" -> dropped/rephrased
  "inasmuch as" -> "since" / "because"
  "he rejoined" -> "he replied" / "he said"
  "saluted" -> "greeted"

  "panegyrical" -> dropped; rephrased as "celebrated in the poetry of"
  "prosopopoeia" -> not present (wrong dialogue)
  "strigil" -> "scraper" (with context about Greek bathing implements)

  "he's," "you're," "I've," "that's," "we've," "didn't," "they're,"

GREEK TERMS AND CULTURAL REFERENCES
--------------------------------------

  "sophrosyne" -- translated as "temperance" throughout, per text analysis
    vocabulary mapping. The subtitle "Or, On Temperance" signals this.

  "Palaestra of Taureas" -- kept as-is. A palaestra is a wrestling school;
    the setting is clear from context.

  "King Archon" -- kept. The archon basileus who handled religious and
    homicide cases. Context provided by scene description.

  "Potidaea" -- kept. The battle (432 BC) that opens the dialogue is
    historical. No further context needed; the text explains it.

  "by Heracles" -- kept (Greek oath, per text analysis: keep all Greek
    exclamations).

  "by the dog of Egypt" -- kept. This is Socrates' characteristic oath
    (variant of "by the dog"), referring to the Egyptian god Anubis.

  "Zeus the Savior" -- kept. The offering of the third libation to Zeus
    Soter was customary; "making an offering of the third argument" is
    Socrates' playful allusion to this ritual.

  "Zamolxis" -- kept. The Thracian king/god. No further context added;
    Socrates explains the reference within the text.

  "Abaris the Hyperborean" -- kept. A legendary healer/priest from the
    far north. Brief enough that the name alone works.

  "Prodicus" -- kept. The Sophist famous for fine verbal distinctions.
    Socrates' aside about Prodicus is a characteristic joke.

  "Hesiod" -- kept. "Work is no disgrace" is from Works and Days.

  "Delphi" / "Know Thyself" -- kept. The famous inscription. Critias'
    interpretation (that it means "be temperate") is his own argument.

  "the gate of horn or the gate of ivory" -- kept. From Homer's Odyssey:
    true dreams come through the gate of horn, false dreams through ivory.
    No annotation needed; the context makes the meaning clear.

  "pancratium" -- kept. A combined boxing/wrestling event in Greek athletics.


CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR ADJACENT CHAPTERS
------------------------------------------

  - This dialogue shares characters with the Lysis (026) and overlaps
    thematically with Laches (025). Critias appears in the Protagoras
    (006-007) and the Timaeus/Critias (042-044).

  - Critias is identified as "son of Callaeschrus" and as Charmides'
    cousin and guardian. His family connection to Solon is noted. These
    identifiers should remain consistent if Critias appears elsewhere.

  - The narrated format follows the same conventions established in
    Protagoras (006-007) and Republic (014-023): Socrates as first-person
    narrator, "I said" / "he said" speech tags, embedded dialogue in
    quotation marks.

  - "Temperance" used consistently for sophrosyne throughout, matching
    the text analysis vocabulary mapping.

  - Socrates' self-deprecation ("I must be a terrible investigator,"
    "I'm probably talking nonsense") follows the established pattern of
    Socratic irony across all dialogues.

  - The dialogue's aporetic ending (no conclusion reached) is consistent
    with other early dialogues: Euthyphro (000), Laches (025), Lysis (026).

  - Speaker format: Because this is narrated, no speaker-name-on-own-line
    format. Dialogue is embedded in Socrates' narration. This matches the
    Protagoras convention.

  - The Jowett source includes some passages where Charmides' responses
    are missing or implied (particularly in the "quietness" refutation
    and some of the later philosophical exchanges). These are marked by
    questions without explicit answers. Preserved these as rhetorical
    questions or with implied "yes" answers matching the flow, since
    Jowett's text itself treats them this way.
