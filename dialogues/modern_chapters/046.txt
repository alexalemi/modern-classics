Athenian
Now we need to consider whether understanding human nature is the only benefit we get from properly run drinking parties, or whether there are other advantages worth pursuing. The argument seems to suggest there are. But we'll need to think carefully about how exactly we get these benefits, or we'll end up confused.

Cleinias
Go on.

Athenian
Let me come back to our theory of good education. If I'm not mistaken, it depends on properly managing communal social gatherings.

Cleinias
That's a pretty grand claim.

Athenian
Here's what I mean. Pleasure and pain are the first things children experience. They're the channels through which virtue and vice first enter a child's soul. As for wisdom and firmly held true beliefs -- well, a person's lucky if they acquire those even in old age. Anyone who has them, along with everything they bring, is a truly complete human being. What I mean by "education" is the training that shapes a child's earliest instincts toward virtue through the right habits. When pleasure and friendship, pain and hatred are planted correctly in souls that can't yet understand why -- and then later, when reason develops, those feelings turn out to be in harmony with reason -- that harmony of the whole soul is what we call virtue. But the specific training in pleasure and pain -- the part that teaches you to hate what you should hate and love what you should love, from the beginning of life to the end -- that, in my view, is what properly deserves the name "education."

Cleinias
I think you're absolutely right in everything you've said about education, Stranger.

Athenian
I'm glad you agree. Because here's the thing: this training in pleasure and pain, which is the foundation of education when it's properly managed, has a tendency to break down and get corrupted over the course of human life. And so the gods, taking pity on the hardships that our species is born to endure, established sacred festivals as periods of rest from labor. They gave us the Muses, and Apollo as leader of the Muses, and Dionysus as companions in our celebrations -- so that through these divine festivals, people could restore and improve their education.

Now, I'd like to know whether a common observation rings true to you. People say that all young creatures -- all of them -- can't keep still, either in their bodies or their voices. They're always wanting to move and make noise: leaping, skipping, bursting with playful energy, crying out in every possible way. But here's the key difference: while other animals have no sense of order or disorder in their movements -- no sense of what we call rhythm or harmony -- the gods who were appointed as our companions in the dance have given us humans the pleasurable sense of harmony and rhythm. Through these gifts they stir us to life, and we follow their lead, joining hands in dances and songs. These they called "choruses" -- a word that naturally suggests joy and cheerfulness.

So shall we start from this point: that education begins with Apollo and the Muses? What do you say?

Cleinias
I agree.

Athenian
And the uneducated person is one who hasn't been trained in the chorus, while the educated person is one who's been well trained in it?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And the chorus combines two elements -- dance and song?

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
So a well-educated person will be able to sing and dance well?

Cleinias
I suppose so.

Athenian
But wait -- let's look at what we're actually saying.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
He sings well and dances well. But do we need to add that he sings what is good and dances what is good?

Cleinias
Yes, let's add that.

Athenian
All right. Now suppose someone knows that what's good is good and what's bad is bad, and acts accordingly. Which person is better trained in dance and music: the one who can move his body and use his voice in technically correct ways but takes no delight in what's good and feels no hatred of what's evil? Or the one whose technique might be imperfect but whose sense of pleasure and pain is right -- who welcomes what's good and is repelled by what's evil?

Cleinias
There's a huge difference between those two kinds of education, Stranger.

Athenian
So if the three of us can figure out what's genuinely good in song and dance, then we'll also know who's truly educated and who isn't. But if we can't figure that out, we certainly won't be able to tell whether education has any real safeguard -- or if it even has one.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Then let's follow the scent like hounds and track down beauty in physical movement, melody, song, and dance. If these things escape us, there's no point talking about true education -- whether Greek or foreign.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
So what is beauty of movement, or a beautiful melody? When a courageous soul is in trouble and when a cowardly soul faces the same situation, are they likely to adopt the same postures and gestures, or produce the same sounds?

Cleinias
How could they? Even the color of their faces would be different.

Athenian
Good point, my friend. Though I should note, in passing, that in music there are indeed patterns of movement and there are melodies, and music deals with harmony and rhythm. So you can correctly say a melody or pattern has good rhythm or good harmony. But you can't properly say -- the way chorus directors sometimes do -- that a melody or pattern has a "good color." That's just a metaphor. What you can say is that the patterns and melodies of the brave and the cowardly are different, praising one and criticizing the other. And to keep things simple, let's just establish this: patterns and melodies that express virtue of soul or body, or images of virtue, are all good without exception. Those that express vice are the opposite.

Cleinias
That's an excellent principle. Let's agree on it.

Athenian
One more thing: are all of us equally delighted by every kind of dance?

Cleinias
Not at all.

Athenian
Then what leads us astray? Are beautiful things not the same for everyone? Or are they the same in themselves, but we just don't agree about them? Because no one's going to admit that dance forms expressing vice are more beautiful than those expressing virtue, or that they personally enjoy watching depictions of vice while other people prefer a different kind of art. And yet most people say that the standard of excellence in music is the amount of pleasure it gives to the soul. But that's intolerable -- it's practically blasphemous. There is, however, a much more plausible explanation for why people get confused.

Cleinias
What is it?

Athenian
It's the way art adapts to different characters. The performances of a chorus are imitations of people's behavior in various situations, circumstances, and temperaments -- each detail is imitated. And people whose own nature, habits, or both are in tune with the performance can't help but enjoy it, applaud it, and call it beautiful. But people whose natures or habits don't match find no pleasure in it and call it worthless. Then there are people whose natures are right but whose habits are wrong, or whose habits are right but whose natures are wrong. These people praise one thing but take pleasure in another. They'll say that all these performances are enjoyable but "not good." And in the presence of people they consider wise, they're ashamed to dance and sing in the baser style, or to openly endorse such performances. But secretly, they enjoy them.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Now, does it actually harm anyone to take pleasure in worthless dances and songs? And does it actually help anyone to enjoy the opposite kind?

Cleinias
I think so.

Athenian
"I think so" isn't strong enough. I'd say "I'm certain." Because the effect must be the same as when someone spends time with bad characters -- people he likes and approves of rather than criticizing, only offering playful complaints because he half-suspects his own weakness. If someone takes pleasure in such company, they'll inevitably become like the people they enjoy spending time with, even if they're ashamed to praise them openly. And what greater good or evil could any fate inflict on us?

Cleinias
I can't think of any.

Athenian
Then in a city that has good laws -- or that will have them in the future -- can we allow poets to teach whatever they want in dance, using whatever rhythms, melodies, or words they choose for the young children of respectable parents? Should the poet be free to train his choruses however he pleases, with no regard for virtue or vice?

Cleinias
That's completely unreasonable and shouldn't even be considered.

Athenian
And yet that's exactly what poets are allowed to do in almost every state -- except Egypt.

Cleinias
Egypt? What are the laws about music and dance in Egypt?

Athenian
You'll be amazed when I tell you. Long ago, the Egyptians seem to have recognized the very principle we're discussing now -- that young citizens must be immersed in patterns and forms of virtue. They established these patterns and displayed them in their temples, and no painter or artist was allowed to innovate or depart from the traditional forms to invent new ones. To this day, no changes are permitted in these arts or in any aspect of music. And if you look at their works, you'll find that paintings and sculptures made ten thousand years ago -- I mean this literally, not as an exaggeration -- are neither better nor worse than the work produced today. They're made with exactly the same skill.

Cleinias
That's extraordinary!

Athenian
I'd say it's more like extraordinarily wise governance -- worthy of a true legislator. There are other things in Egypt that aren't so impressive. But what I'm telling you about their music is true and worth serious consideration, because it shows that a lawgiver can confidently establish melodies that are naturally correct and true. Doing this, however, would have to be the work of a god, or at least a godlike person. In fact, the Egyptians have a tradition that the ancient chants preserved for all those ages were composed by the goddess Isis. So, as I was saying, if someone can discover the naturally correct melodies, they can confidently enshrine them in a fixed legal form. The love of novelty that comes from craving the new and getting bored with the old isn't strong enough to corrupt consecrated song and dance by claiming they've become outdated. In Egypt, at any rate, such corruption is far from happening.

Cleinias
Your arguments seem to prove your point.

Athenian
Then can we confidently say this about the true purpose of music and choral festivals? We rejoice when we think we're doing well, and we think we're doing well when we rejoice.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
And when we're rejoicing in our good fortune, we can't keep still?

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
Our young people burst into dancing and singing, and we older folks think we're doing our part in life by watching them. Having lost our own agility, we delight in their energy and playfulness because it reminds us of our younger selves. So we gladly set up contests for those who can reawaken the memory of our youth.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Now, is it entirely wrong to say, as ordinary people do about festivals, that the wisest person and rightful winner of the prize is whoever gives us the greatest amount of pleasure and enjoyment? After all, when the occasion calls for celebration, shouldn't the person who brings the most joy to the most people be honored above all? Is this the right way to think and act?

Cleinias
Possibly.

Athenian
But hold on, my dear friend -- let's not rush to judgment. Let's think about this more carefully by imagining a scenario. Picture a festival with all kinds of entertainment -- athletic, musical, and equestrian competitions. The citizens are assembled, prizes are offered, and it's announced that anyone who wants may enter. The winner will be whoever gives the most pleasure to the audience -- no rules about how. Whoever succeeds in giving the most pleasure gets crowned as the most delightful performer. What do you think would happen?

Cleinias
In what way do you mean?

Athenian
Well, there'd be all sorts of acts. One person would perform epic poetry, like Homer. Another would play the lyre. Someone would present a tragedy, someone else a comedy. And it wouldn't be at all surprising if someone thought they could win with a puppet show. With these competitors and countless others all in the mix -- can you tell me who should win?

Cleinias
I don't see how anyone could answer that question, or claim to know, without actually hearing the competitors. The question is absurd.

Athenian
Well, if neither of you can answer, shall I answer this "absurd" question for you?

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
If very small children get to decide, they'll pick the puppet show.

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
Older children will be fans of comedy. Educated women, young men, and the general public will probably prefer tragedy.

Cleinias
Very likely.

Athenian
And I suspect that we old men would take the greatest pleasure in hearing a skilled performer recite the Iliad and Odyssey, or one of Hesiod's poems -- we'd give the prize to him. But who would really be the rightful winner? That's the question.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Clearly, you and I would have to say that whoever we old men judge to be the winner should win -- because our standards are far superior to any that currently exist anywhere in the world.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
So here's where I'd agree with popular opinion, up to a point: the excellence of music should be measured by the pleasure it gives. But not the pleasure of just anyone. The finest music is that which delights the best and best-educated people -- and above all, the single person who is preeminent in virtue and education. That's why judges must be people of character. They'll need both wisdom and courage. A true judge shouldn't take his cues from the crowd in the theater, and he shouldn't be rattled by the noise of the masses or by his own self-doubt. Nor should he, knowing what's right, deliver a false verdict out of cowardice and spinelessness -- using the very same lips that just appealed to the gods before he gave his judgment. He's sitting there not as a student of the theater audience but as their teacher, and he should be the enemy of anyone who panders to the spectators' pleasure.

The ancient Greek custom, which still survives in Italy and Sicily, was to let the audience decide the winner by show of hands. But this practice has been the ruin of the poets. They've gotten into the habit of composing to please the bad taste of their judges, with the result that the audience ends up educating itself. And it's ruined the theaters too. The audience ought to be watching characters better than themselves, receiving a higher kind of pleasure. Instead, by their own doing, the opposite has happened.

So what's the lesson from all this? Shall I tell you?

Cleinias
What?

Athenian
Here's the conclusion we've reached for the third or fourth time: education is the process of directing and guiding young people toward that right principle which the law affirms and which the experience of the best and wisest elders has confirmed to be truly right.

The goal is this: the child's soul shouldn't become accustomed to feeling joy and sorrow in ways that conflict with the law and with those who follow it. Instead, the child should follow the law, rejoicing and grieving at the same things the wisest elders do. And this is exactly why songs were invented -- songs that truly "enchant," designed to plant in souls that harmony we've been talking about. Because the young mind can't endure serious training, these are called "play" and "songs" and are performed as entertainment -- just as when people are sick, their caregivers serve wholesome food in pleasant flavors and unhealthy food in unpleasant ones, so patients learn to like the one and dislike the other.

In the same way, the true legislator will persuade -- and if persuasion fails, compel -- the poet to express what he ought: the movements and melodies of temperate, courageous, and thoroughly good people, using beautiful and noble words, proper rhythms, and fitting harmonies.

Cleinias
But do you really think, Stranger, that this is how poets actually compose in most states today? As far as I can tell, except among us Cretans and the Spartans, there are no regulations like the ones you're describing. In other places, novelties are constantly being introduced in dance and music -- not under the authority of any law, but driven by lawless pleasures. And those pleasures are so far from being constant and principled, like the Egyptian ones you mentioned, that they're never the same from one day to the next.

Athenian
Absolutely right, Cleinias. And I'm afraid I may have been unclear, which led you to think I was describing how things actually are, when I was really talking about how I'd like things to be regarding music. Criticizing evils that are deeply entrenched and past the point of easy remedy is never pleasant, though sometimes it's necessary. But since we don't really disagree, let me ask you this: do you think regulations like these are more common among the Cretans and Spartans than among the other Greeks?

Cleinias
They certainly are.

Athenian
And if these regulations were extended to the rest of the Greek world, would that be an improvement over the current state of affairs?

Cleinias
A tremendous improvement -- as long as the customs were like what we have in Crete and Sparta, and like what you were just saying they ought to be.

Athenian
Let's make sure we're on the same page. Aren't the principles of education and music that prevail among you something like this: you require your poets to teach that the good person, if they're self-controlled and just, is fortunate and happy -- whether they're big and strong or small and weak, rich or poor? And on the other hand, if someone has wealth surpassing that of Cinyras or Midas but is unjust, they're wretched and live in misery? As the poet says -- and truly: "I don't sing about, I don't care about the person who accomplishes all great deeds but lacks justice. Let the one who 'draws near and stretches out his hand against enemies' be a just person." But if he's unjust, I wouldn't want him to "look calmly upon bloody death" or "outrun the North Wind." And let none of the other so-called goods ever be his.

Because the things most people call "good" aren't really good in the truest sense. The popular list goes: health first, beauty second, wealth third -- and then countless others, like sharp eyesight, keen hearing, and all the senses working perfectly. Or being a tyrant and doing whatever you want. And the ultimate happiness, supposedly, is to have all of these things and then become immortal on top of it.

But you and I say something different. We say that for just and virtuous people, all these things are the greatest blessings. But for unjust people, they're all -- including even health -- the greatest curses. Because truly, to have sight and hearing and the use of all your senses, or even to be alive at all, without justice and virtue, even if you're loaded with every so-called blessing of fortune -- that is the worst evil imaginable, especially if such a life went on forever. It's not quite as bad if the wicked person only lives a short time.

These are the truths that, if I'm not mistaken, you would persuade or compel your poets to express with fitting harmonies and rhythms, and to use in training your young people. Am I right?

Because let me be plain about this: evils, as people call them, are actually goods for the unjust -- they're only evils for the just. And so-called goods are truly good for good people, but harmful to the wicked.

So let me ask again: are we agreed on this?

Cleinias
I think we partly agree and partly don't.

Athenian
Here's where you're getting stuck, I suspect. When a person has health, wealth, and absolute political power that lasts; when they're preeminent in strength and courage and blessed with immortality; and when they have none of the so-called counterbalancing evils but only the injustice and arrogance of their own nature -- you're reluctant to believe that such a person is miserable rather than happy.

Cleinias
That's exactly right.

Athenian
Let me try once more. Suppose a person is brave, strong, handsome, and rich, and gets to do whatever they please their entire life -- but they're unjust and arrogant. Wouldn't you both agree that such a person would necessarily live shamefully?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And live badly too?

Cleinias
I'm not so ready to grant that.

Athenian
Live painfully, then? And against their own real interests?

Cleinias
How can I possibly agree to that?

Athenian
How? Well, may heaven bring us to one mind, because right now we're of two. To me, dear Cleinias, the truth of what I'm saying is as plain as the fact that Crete is an island. And if I were a lawgiver, I'd try to make the poets and all the citizens speak this way, and I'd impose the heaviest penalties on anyone in the entire land who dared to say that wicked people lead pleasant lives, or that what's profitable and gainful is one thing while what's just is another. There are many other points on which I'd make my citizens speak differently from what Cretans and Spartans say today -- and indeed differently from most of the world.

For think about this, my good friends. By Zeus and Apollo, tell me: if I asked these same gods -- the ones who gave you your laws -- "Is the most just life also the most pleasant? Or are there two kinds of life, one the most just and the other the most pleasant?" And suppose they answered, "There are two." And then I followed up -- which would be the right way to pursue the inquiry -- "Which people are happier, those who lead the most just life or those who lead the most pleasant?" And they said, "Those who lead the most pleasant" -- well, that would be a very strange answer, and I wouldn't want to put it in the mouths of gods.

The question comes more naturally from the lips of fathers and legislators, so let me replay the conversation with one of them, and suppose he gives the same answer -- that the person who leads the most pleasant life is the happiest.

Then I'd reply: "Father, didn't you want me to live as happily as possible? Yet you never stopped telling me I should live as justly as possible. Well, now the person giving this advice -- whether legislator or father -- would be in a bind, unable to stay consistent with himself."

But if instead he declared that the most just life is also the happiest, then anyone hearing him would want to know: "What is this good and noble quality in life that the law endorses, something higher than pleasure? What good does the just person have that's separate from pleasure?"

Think about it. Shall we say that glory and fame from gods and mortals, though good and noble, are nevertheless unpleasant -- and that disgrace is pleasant? Certainly not, dear legislator. Or shall we say that refraining from wrongdoing is good and honorable but brings no pleasure, while doing wrong is pleasant but shameful and evil?

Cleinias
Impossible.

Athenian
So the view that identifies the pleasant with the just, the good, and the noble has an excellent moral and religious tendency. And the opposite view -- the one that separates them -- is the most destructive view a legislator can face. Because no one, if they can help it, will be persuaded to do something that brings them more pain than pleasure.

But since seeing things at a distance tends to make us dizzy -- especially in childhood -- the legislator will try to clear away the confusion and reveal the truth. Through customs, praise, and persuasive arguments, he'll convince the citizens that justice and injustice are like shadow paintings: injustice, which seems opposed to justice, appears pleasant when viewed by the unjust and wicked person, while justice seems deeply unpleasant to them. But from the just person's perspective, the exact opposite is true of both.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
And which judgment should we trust more -- that of the worse soul, or the better one?

Cleinias
Surely the better soul's.

Athenian
Then the unjust life must be not only more shameful and degraded, but also more unpleasant than the just and holy life?

Cleinias
That does seem to follow from our argument.

Athenian
And even if this weren't the case -- even if the argument hadn't proven it -- any lawgiver worth the name, if he ever dared to tell a lie for the good of the young, couldn't possibly invent a more useful lie than this one, or one more likely to make people do what's right not by force but by choice.

Cleinias
Truth, Stranger, is a noble and enduring thing -- but it's remarkably hard to convince people of.

Athenian
And yet the story of Cadmus the Phoenician, which is wildly improbable, has been readily believed -- along with countless other tales.

Cleinias
Which story do you mean?

Athenian
The story that armed warriors sprang from the earth after dragon's teeth were sown. This should serve as proof to any legislator that you can persuade young minds of just about anything. So all the legislator needs to do is figure out which beliefs will be of the greatest public benefit, and then devote all his efforts to making the entire community speak with one voice in their songs, stories, and conversations throughout their lives. But if you disagree with me, by all means argue the other side.

Cleinias
I don't see how either of us could fairly argue against what you're saying.

Athenian
Then here's my next proposal. All three of our choruses should sing to the young and impressionable souls of children, and the central message woven through all their songs should be this: the life that the gods deem happiest is also the best. We'll declare this to be the most certain truth, and the minds of our young students will be more likely to accept this message from us than any other we could give them.

Cleinias
I agree with what you're saying.

Athenian
First will come the sacred chorus of children, in their natural order, singing enthusiastically to the whole city with their heaven-taught hymn. Next will come the chorus of young men under thirty, who'll call upon Apollo the Healer to witness the truth of their words and pray for his grace upon the youth and his guidance for their hearts. Third will come the chorus of older men, aged thirty to sixty. And those who are past the age for singing will tell stories illustrating the same virtues, like voices of an oracle.

Cleinias
Who are these people in the third chorus, Stranger? I don't quite understand what you mean about them.

Athenian
And yet nearly everything I've been saying has been leading up to them.

Cleinias
Could you try to be a little clearer?

Athenian
You'll remember that at the beginning of our discussion I talked about the fiery nature of young creatures -- how they can't keep still in either body or voice, and how they cry out and jump around in a chaotic way. I said that while no other animal develops a sense of order in movement, humans alone do. The order of physical movement is called rhythm, and the order of the voice, where high and low notes are properly blended, is called harmony -- and the two together are what we call choral performance.

I also said that the gods took pity on us and gave us Apollo and the Muses as our companions and leaders in the dance -- and Dionysus as well, as I'm sure you'll recall.

Cleinias
I remember perfectly.

Athenian
So far I've talked about the chorus of Apollo and the Muses. I still need to talk about the remaining chorus -- the chorus of Dionysus.

Cleinias
How does that work? I have to say, it sounds rather strange at first to hear about a Dionysiac chorus of old men -- you really mean that people over thirty, maybe fifty or even sixty, are supposed to dance in his honor?

Athenian
It does sound strange, and that's exactly why I need to show that there's good reason for the proposal.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Are we agreed on what we've established so far?

Cleinias
About what exactly?

Athenian
That every person -- man and boy, slave and free, both sexes, the entire city -- should never stop immersing themselves in the kinds of songs we've been describing? And that there should be all kinds of variety and variation to prevent the effect of monotony, so that the singers always take pleasure in their hymns and never grow tired of them?

Cleinias
Everyone would agree with that.

Athenian
Then where should the best part of our city -- those who, by reason of age and intelligence, carry the greatest influence -- sing these most beautiful of songs, the ones that are going to do so much good? Would we be so foolish as to let off the hook the very people who could give us the most beautiful and useful performances?

Cleinias
The argument does say we can't let them off.

Athenian
Then how do we carry this out with proper decorum? Here's the problem.

Cleinias
What?

Athenian
As a person gets older, they become reluctant and self-conscious about singing. They take no pleasure in performing. The older and more sensible they become, the more embarrassed they feel if forced to sing. Isn't that true?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And they'd be even more embarrassed standing up to sing in a theater before a mixed audience. And if on top of that they're forced to perform like the other competition choruses, training under a singing master while feeling stiff and out of practice -- they'd be thoroughly uncomfortable and deeply unwilling.

Cleinias
No doubt about it.

Athenian
So how do we encourage them and get them to sing? Here's what I propose. First, we enact a law that boys shouldn't taste wine at all until they're eighteen. We'll tell them that you shouldn't pour fire on fire, whether in the body or the soul, until they're old enough to begin serious work -- this is a precaution against the excitability of youth. After that, they may drink wine in moderation up to the age of thirty, but while young they should completely avoid drunkenness and excess.

Then, when a person finally reaches forty, after dinner at a public feast, he may invite not just the other gods but especially Dionysus to join the celebration of the elder men. He may make use of the wine that Dionysus gave to humanity to ease the harshness of old age -- so that in our later years we might renew our youth and forget our sorrows, and so that the soul's nature, like iron softened in the fire, might become more pliable and receptive.

Now -- in the first place, won't anyone who's been mellowed this way be more willing and less self-conscious about singing? Not before a huge crowd, necessarily, but before a moderate company -- not among strangers, but among friends and familiar faces. They'll be ready, as we've said many times, to chant and to enchant.

Cleinias
Far more ready.

Athenian
Then there's nothing improper about using this method to persuade them to join us in song.

Cleinias
Nothing at all.

Athenian
And what kind of song will they sing? What music will they perform? Obviously, something fitting for them.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And what's fitting for heroic spirits? Shall they sing a choral piece?

Cleinias
Well, Stranger, we Cretans and Spartans don't really know any kind of song other than what we've learned and been accustomed to sing in our choruses.

Athenian
Of course you don't. Because your military way of life is modeled on an army camp, not on city life. You keep your young men herding and feeding together like young colts in a field. No one pulls an individual colt away from the herd, raging and foaming, to give him a personal groom who trains and grooms him privately and gives him the kind of education that would make him not just a good soldier, but also someone capable of governing a state and managing cities. That kind of person, as we said at the beginning, would be a greater warrior than the one Tyrtaeus celebrates in his poems. Such a person would honor courage in every context, but always as the fourth part of virtue, not the first -- whether in individuals or in states.

Cleinias
Once again, Stranger, you're putting down our lawmakers.

Athenian
Not intentionally, my good friend -- not at all. But wherever the argument leads, let's follow. If there really is some kind of song more beautiful than the standard chorus or public theater fare, I'd like to share it with the people who, as we've said, are embarrassed by those ordinary performances and want something better.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Now, here's a general principle. Whenever something has an accompanying charm, either the best thing about it is that charm itself, or there's some rightness or usefulness the thing possesses separately. Take eating and drinking, for example. The accompanying charm is the pleasure -- what we call enjoyment. But the real rightness and value is the healthfulness of what's served. That's where the true quality lies.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
Similarly, learning has an accompanying charm, which is the pleasure of it. But the real rightness, the real profit -- what's genuinely good and noble about it -- comes from truth.

Cleinias
Precisely.

Athenian
And the same goes for the imitative arts. When they succeed in creating good likenesses and are accompanied by pleasure, we can say their works have charm.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
But what gives these works truthfulness or rightness is accuracy of proportion -- in quality and quantity -- not the pleasure they happen to produce.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Then the only thing that can properly be judged by the standard of pleasure alone is something that produces no real usefulness, no truth, no faithful likeness -- and on the other hand does no harm either. It exists solely for the sake of the accompanying charm. The word "pleasure" applies most precisely when those other qualities -- utility, truth, likeness -- are absent.

Cleinias
You're talking about harmless pleasure, right?

Athenian
Yes. And this is what I call "amusement" -- when it does neither significant harm nor significant good.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
So if these are our principles, we have to insist that works of imitation should not be judged by pleasure or by popular opinion. This applies to every kind of proportionality and correctness. Things aren't equal because someone thinks they are, or symmetrical because someone likes them. They must be judged by the standard of truth -- and by no other standard whatsoever.

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Athenian
And don't we regard all music as representational and imitative?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Then when someone says music should be judged by the pleasure it gives, we can't accept that. If there's any music whose standard is pleasure, that music shouldn't be considered especially valuable. The music we should seek out is the kind that imitates what's good.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
So those who seek the finest music shouldn't look for what's most pleasant, but for what's most true. And the truth of an imitation consists, as we were saying, in reproducing the thing imitated accurately -- in its right proportions and qualities.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And everyone will admit that all musical compositions are imitative and representational -- poets, audiences, and performers would all agree on this point, wouldn't they?

Cleinias
They would.

Athenian
So the person who wants to judge correctly must know what each composition is. If they don't know the character and meaning of a piece -- what it's supposed to represent -- they'll never be able to tell whether its aim has been achieved or missed.

Cleinias
Certainly not.

Athenian
And can someone who doesn't know what's true be able to judge what's good or bad? Maybe I'm not being clear enough. Let me put it another way.

Cleinias
Go ahead.

Athenian
There are countless visual representations of things, aren't there?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Now, can someone who doesn't know what the original object is ever judge whether a copy of it has been faithfully made? I mean, for example, whether a statue has the right proportions of a human body, with each part in its correct position -- their proper sizes, how the parts fit together in the right arrangement -- and whether the colors and shapes are all correct, or whether it's all a confused mess. Do you think someone who doesn't even know what the animal being imitated looks like could judge any of this?

Cleinias
Impossible.

Athenian
But suppose we do know that the thing painted or sculpted is meant to be a human being, and that the artist has given it all the right parts, colors, and shapes. Even then, don't we also need to know whether the work is truly beautiful, or whether it falls short in any respect?

Cleinias
If that weren't also required, Stranger, we'd all be judges of beauty.

Athenian
Exactly right. So can we say that for everything imitative -- whether in drawing, music, or any other art -- a competent judge must possess three things? First, they must know what the imitation is meant to represent. Second, they must know whether the representation is accurate. And third, they must know whether it's been well executed -- in its words, melodies, and rhythms.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Then let's not back away from discussing the particular challenges of music. Music is more celebrated than any other kind of imitation and therefore requires the greatest care of all. If someone makes a mistake here, they can do themselves serious harm by welcoming corrupt influences into their soul -- and the mistake can be very hard to detect.

The reason is that poets are artists far inferior in character to the Muses themselves. The Muses would never make the kind of gross errors poets commit -- like assigning women's words to men's music and movements, or combining the melodies and gestures of free citizens with the rhythms of slaves and vulgar people. They'd never start with the rhythms and gestures of free people and then pair them with melodies or words of the opposite character. And they'd never mash together the voices and sounds of animals and humans and instruments and every other kind of noise as if it were all one thing.

But human poets love doing exactly this kind of incoherent mixing, and they make themselves ridiculous to anyone who, as Orpheus says, is "ready for true pleasure." People with experience see through all this confusion, and yet the poets keep making things worse by separating rhythm and dance from melody -- setting bare words to meter -- and by separating melody and rhythm from words, using the lyre or flute alone. When instruments play without words, it becomes extremely difficult to tell what the harmony and rhythm are supposed to mean, or to see that anything worthwhile is being imitated. We have to acknowledge that this kind of thing, aimed only at speed and technical flash and brute noise, using flute and lyre as something other than accompaniments for dance and song, is terribly crude and tasteless. Using either instrument without accompaniment leads to every kind of gimmickry and disorder.

So much for that point. But we're not considering how our senior choristers -- men from thirty to fifty or beyond -- should avoid the Muses. We're considering how they should properly engage with them. And everything we've discussed suggests how these older choristers can be expected to be better trained than others. They need a quick perception and deep knowledge of harmonies and rhythms. Otherwise, how could they ever know whether a melody should properly be sung in the Dorian mode, or whether the rhythm the poet assigned to it is the right one?

Cleinias
Clearly they can't.

Athenian
Most people are ridiculous in their belief that they know what's in proper harmony and rhythm and what isn't, when the only reason they can sing and keep time at all is that they've been drilled into it by force. It never occurs to them that they're ignorant of what they're actually doing. Every melody is correct when it has the right harmony and rhythm, and wrong when it doesn't.

Cleinias
That's absolutely certain.

Athenian
But can someone who doesn't actually understand a thing -- as we've been saying -- know whether that thing is done correctly?

Cleinias
Impossible.

Athenian
Then it seems we're arriving at this conclusion: our newly appointed senior choristers, whom we're hereby inviting -- and though they're their own masters, effectively requiring -- to sing, must be educated well enough to follow the steps of the rhythm and the notes of the song. They need to know the harmonies and rhythms so they can select what's fitting for men of their age and character to sing. They must sing these songs and take genuine, wholesome pleasure in their own performance, while also leading younger people to welcome good moral qualities with proper delight.

With this kind of training, they'll achieve a more precise understanding than ordinary people have, or even the poets themselves. Because the poet doesn't need to know the third criterion -- whether the imitation is truly good or not -- though he can hardly avoid knowing the principles of melody and rhythm. But the elder chorus must know all three, so they can choose the best or what comes closest to the best. Otherwise, they'll never be able to charm the souls of young people in the direction of virtue.

And now we've accomplished what we originally set out to do -- to make a proper case for the Chorus of Dionysus. Let's see if we were right. I'd expect that a drinking gathering is likely to become more and more rowdy as the drinking goes on. That was our starting point, wasn't it?

Cleinias
It certainly was.

Athenian
Every person feels a heightened sense of himself. His heart is glad, and he'll say anything. At such times, nobody can hold him back. He imagines he's capable of ruling both himself and everyone else.

Cleinias
Absolutely true.

Athenian
Now, weren't we saying that on such occasions the souls of drinkers become like iron heated in a fire -- softer, more youthful, more easily shaped by anyone who knows how to educate and mold them, just as when they were young? And that this shaper should be the same person who guided them in their youth -- namely, the good legislator?

The legislator should enact laws for the banquet that work like this: when a person becomes overconfident, bold, and shameless, unwilling to take turns in silence and speech, in drinking and music -- these laws should transform his character into the opposite. They should instill a just and noble fear to counter the approach of arrogance -- that divine fear we've been calling reverence and sense of honor.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
And the guardians of these laws, the ones who work alongside them, are the calm, sober commanders of the drinkers. Without their help, fighting the effects of drink is even harder than fighting enemies in battle when the general himself isn't sober. Anyone who refuses to obey them and the directors of Dionysiac feasts -- who should be men over sixty -- should face a disgrace as great as or greater than that of someone who disobeys military leaders.

Cleinias
Right.

Athenian
So if drinking and entertainment were regulated this way, wouldn't the participants actually benefit? They'd part as better friends than when they arrived, not as enemies, the way things usually go now. Their entire interaction would be governed by law and guided by it, with the sober leading the drunk.

Cleinias
I think so too, if drinking were regulated as you propose.

Athenian
Then let's not simply condemn the gift of Dionysus as something bad and unfit for a well-run state. Wine has many fine qualities, and one especially outstanding one -- though there's a difficulty in explaining it to the general public, because people are so likely to misunderstand.

Cleinias
What are you referring to?

Athenian
There's a story that's been floating around the world: that Dionysus was driven mad by his stepmother Hera, and that in revenge he inflicts Bacchic frenzies and dancing madness on others -- which is why he gave people wine.

Stories like this about the gods I'll leave to those who think they can tell them safely. All I know is this: no animal is born with its full intelligence. During that in-between period, before a creature has developed its proper faculties, it rages and flails without rhyme or reason. And once it gets on its feet, it jumps around without rhyme or reason. This, you'll remember, is what we said was the origin of both music and physical training.

Cleinias
Yes, I remember.

Athenian
And didn't we say that the sense of harmony and rhythm arose from this -- that the gods we have to thank for these gifts are Apollo, the Muses, and Dionysus?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
So the other story -- the popular one -- implies that wine was given to humanity out of spite, to make us crazy. But our account says the opposite: wine was given as a healing remedy, to plant modesty in the soul and health and strength in the body.

Cleinias
That, Stranger, is precisely what we said.

Athenian
Then half our subject has now been discussed. Shall we move on to the other half?

Cleinias
What's the other half, and how are you dividing the subject?

Athenian
The entire choral art, in our view, is the whole of education. And of this art, the part that deals with the voice is made up of rhythms and harmonies.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
The movement of the body shares rhythm with the movement of the voice, but gesture is unique to the body, while song is purely the movement of the voice.

Cleinias
Exactly right.

Athenian
And the sound of the voice that reaches and educates the soul -- that's what we've been calling music.

Cleinias
We were right to call it that.

Athenian
And the movement of the body, when pursued for enjoyment, we called dancing. But when that movement is extended and developed with a view to physical excellence, that structured training is what we call gymnastics.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
So music, which was one half of the choral art, can now be considered fully discussed. Shall we move on to the other half, or not? What would you prefer?

Cleinias
My dear friend, you're talking with a Cretan and a Spartan, and we've discussed music but not gymnastics -- what answer do you think either of us is going to give to a question like that?

Athenian
Your question is its own answer, and I take it not just as an answer but as a command to proceed with gymnastics.

Cleinias
You understand me perfectly. Go ahead.

Athenian
I will. And there shouldn't be any difficulty explaining this to you, since you're both far more familiar with this subject than with music.

Cleinias
There shouldn't be.

Athenian
Doesn't gymnastics, too, trace its origin to the natural tendency toward rapid motion that exists in all animals? As we were saying, humans developed a sense of rhythm and from that created and invented dancing. And when melody awakened and joined with rhythm, the two together formed the choral art.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
And one part of this subject we've already discussed, while another remains?

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
But first, let me add one final word about drinking, if you'll allow me.

Cleinias
What more do you have to say?

Athenian
Here's my position. If a city seriously adopts the practice of regulated drinking as a means of cultivating self-discipline -- and applies the same principle to other pleasures, with the aim of mastering them -- then all these pleasures can be put to good use.

But if the city treats drinking as mere amusement, where anyone can drink whenever they want, with whoever they want, and throw in any other indulgence on top of it -- I'll never agree that such a city, or such a person, should practice drinking at all.

In fact, I'd go further than the Cretans and Spartans. I'm inclined toward the Carthaginian law: that no one on military campaign should be allowed to taste wine at all -- they should drink only water the entire time. In the city, no slave, male or female, should ever drink wine. No magistrate should drink during their year of office. No ship's pilot or judge should taste wine while on duty. No one going into an important consultation should drink. No one should drink during the daytime at all, unless it's for exercise or medical reasons. And no one should drink at night when they intend to conceive children.

There are countless other situations where people with good sense and good laws ought not to drink. If what I'm saying is right, no city would need very many vineyards. Their agriculture and way of life would follow an orderly plan, and grape cultivation would be the most limited and least common of their activities.

And that, my friends, shall be the crowning statement of my discussion of wine -- if you agree.

Cleinias
Excellent. We agree.
