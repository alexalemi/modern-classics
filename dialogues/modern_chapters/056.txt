Athenian
Now that we've covered assaults, let's wrap up all acts of violence under one general law. It goes like this: No one shall take or carry off anything belonging to their neighbor, and no one shall use another person's property without the owner's consent. These offenses are the source of all the problems we've been discussing — they always have been, and they always will be. The worst of them are the excesses and outrages of the young, and they're most serious when directed against religion — especially when they violate public and sacred rites, or the ceremonies shared by tribes and brotherhoods. The second most serious are offenses against private religious rites and burial places. Third in severity — leaving aside the cases we've already discussed — are insults against parents. Fourth are acts of violence against the authority of public officials, when someone takes, carries off, or uses their property without consent. And fifth is any violation of an individual citizen's rights that calls for legal remedy. We need a single law covering all of these.

Now, we've already laid out the punishments for sacrilege, whether committed through fraud or force. What we still need to determine is the punishment for those who speak or act with contempt toward the gods. But first, let's give them a warning. It goes something like this:

No one who genuinely believes in the gods, as the laws require, has ever willingly committed an unholy act or spoken an unlawful word. If someone does, it's because they hold one of three beliefs: first, that the gods don't exist at all; second, that they exist but don't care about human beings; or third, that they exist and care, but can be bought off with sacrifices and prayers.

Cleinias
So what should we say or do about these people?

Athenian
Well, my friend, let's first hear the objections they'll throw at us. I suspect they feel rather superior, and they won't hold back.

Cleinias
What objections?

Athenian
Something disrespectful, along these lines: "People of Athens, Sparta, and Knossos — fair enough: some of us deny the gods exist entirely, while others, as you say, think they don't care about us, and still others think they can be swayed by gifts. Now, you yourselves agreed that before punishing us and making threats, you should first argue your case and try to convince us. So we're taking you up on that. Try teaching us first — persuade us that gods exist, using solid evidence. Show us they're too good to be bribed or turned from the path of justice. Because when we hear the most respected poets, orators, prophets, and priests saying these things about the gods, most of us don't resolve to avoid wrongdoing — we figure we can do wrong and then make up for it afterward. From lawmakers who claim to be reasonable rather than harsh, we expect persuasion first. Prove to us that gods exist — if not more eloquently than others have tried, then at least more truthfully. Who knows? Maybe we'll listen. If that's a fair request, then accept our challenge."

Cleinias
But is it really that hard to prove the gods exist?

Athenian
How would you go about it?

Cleinias
How? Well, for starters — the earth, the sun, the stars, the entire universe, the beautiful order of the seasons, the way they're divided into years and months. And then there's the fact that every Greek and every foreigner believes in them.

Athenian
I'm worried, my dear friend — though I won't say I'm too concerned — about the contempt these godless people would pour on arguments like that. You don't really understand what's driving their unbelief. You assume they've just given in to pleasure-seeking.

Cleinias
Well, what other reason is there?

Athenian
One that you, living in a different world, would never guess.

Cleinias
What is it?

Athenian
A particularly devastating kind of ignorance — one that passes itself off as the highest wisdom.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
In Athens, there are writings preserved — the kind of thing your more disciplined society refuses to tolerate. They discuss the gods in both prose and verse. The oldest of these works describe the origins of the heavens and the world, and before long they get into stories about how the gods were born and how they behaved toward each other. Whether these stories have been good or bad for people in other respects, I'd rather not criticize too harshly — they're ancient, after all. But when I consider them from the standpoint of how children should think about their parents, I can't call them praiseworthy, useful, or even true. But I'll say no more about the old writers.

It's the younger generation and their so-called wisdom that I can't let pass. Just look at what their ideas lead to. When you and I argue for the existence of the gods and point to the sun, moon, stars, and earth as evidence of divine power, someone schooled in these new philosophies will say those things are just earth and stone — lifeless matter that couldn't possibly care about human affairs. They'll say all religion is just a tissue of pretty words and make-believe.

Cleinias
Even one such teacher would be bad enough, Stranger. And you're saying there are many of them, which is far worse.

Athenian
So then — what do we do? Should we imagine that one of these godless people is confronting us, challenging the very basis of our legislation? Should we picture them saying, "How outrageous that you're making laws on the assumption that gods exist!" Should we defend ourselves? Or should we drop it and get back to the laws, before this introduction becomes longer than the legal code itself? Because if we're going to engage with the godless on their own terms — partly arguing, partly warning, partly reasoning — this discussion is going to stretch on for quite a while before we get to the actual laws.

Cleinias
Yes, but Stranger — how many times have we already said that on this occasion there's no reason to prefer brevity over thoroughness? As the saying goes, nobody's chasing us. It would be pitiful and absurd to choose the shorter path over the better one. It matters enormously that we prove, one way or another, that gods exist, that they're good, and that they care about justice more than humans do. A demonstration of this would be the finest and noblest introduction to all our laws. So let's take our time, without impatience or hurry, and marshal every argument we can. Let's bring the full force of persuasion to bear.

Athenian
Given how serious you are about this, I feel like offering a prayer that I'll succeed. But I need to press forward at once. Who can stay calm when called upon to prove that gods exist? Who can help feeling anger toward the people who have forced us into this argument? I'm talking about those who refuse to believe the stories they heard as infants from their mothers and nurses — stories repeated in play and in earnest, like charms; stories they heard in sacrificial prayers, accompanied by the sights and sounds that so delight children; stories they watched their parents act out with absolute conviction during sacrifices, praying to the gods with intense sincerity, prostrating themselves in worship. They've seen Greeks and foreigners alike, at sunrise and sunset, through every twist of fortune, kneeling and bowing — not as people who suspect the gods might not exist, but as people for whom the gods' existence is beyond all question and all doubt.

When people dismiss all of this on no solid grounds — as anyone with a shred of intelligence would agree — and when they force us to make the argument we're now making, how is anyone supposed to address them gently? And yet the attempt has to be made. It wouldn't do for half of humanity to go mad with indulgence while the other half goes mad with outrage. So we'll speak to these lost and misguided souls without passion. Let's imagine we've picked out one of them and are reasoning with him calmly, holding back our anger.

"My son," we'll say, "you're young. Time will change many of the opinions you hold now. Wait a while. Don't rush to judgment on the highest matters — and the highest of all, which you currently think is nothing, is this: knowing the gods rightly and living accordingly.

"Let me point out something important, something I'm quite sure about: you and your friends are not the first to hold this view about the gods. In every generation, more or fewer people have caught the same disease. I've known many of them, and I can tell you this: no one who adopted the belief that gods don't exist while young ever held that same view into old age. The other two opinions sometimes persist — the idea that gods exist but don't care about humans, or that they care but can be bribed — but the outright denial of the gods? That almost never lasts.

"So my advice is this: wait. Consider whether it might be true. Ask others, and above all, ask the lawgiver. In the meantime, be very careful not to offend against the gods. For the lawgiver's duty — now and always — is to teach you the truth about these matters."

Cleinias
Everything you've said so far, Stranger, is excellent.

Athenian
Quite true, Megillus and Cleinias. But I'm afraid we've stumbled onto a strange doctrine without realizing it.

Cleinias
What doctrine?

Athenian
One that many people consider the wisest of all.

Cleinias
Please, speak more plainly.

Athenian
The doctrine that everything that exists, has existed, or will exist comes about either by nature, by design, or by chance.

Cleinias
Isn't that true?

Athenian
Well, these philosophers are probably onto something. In any case, let's follow their reasoning and see what they and their students actually mean.

Cleinias
By all means.

Athenian
Here's what they say: the greatest and most magnificent things in the universe are the products of nature and chance, while the lesser things are the work of human craft. Art, they say, takes the raw materials that nature has already produced on a grand scale and shapes them into the smaller, less impressive things we typically call "artificial."

Cleinias
What do you mean exactly?

Athenian
I'll spell it out. They say that fire, water, earth, and air all exist by nature and chance — none of them by design. The larger bodies that came next — earth itself, the sun, the moon, the stars — were created from these entirely lifeless elements. These elements move around according to chance and various natural affinities: hot attracts cold, dry attracts moist, soft attracts hard, and so on through every combination of opposites thrown together by necessity. This is how the entire heavens were created, according to them — all the celestial bodies, all animals, all plants, all the seasons — not through the action of mind, they insist, not through any god or purposeful design, but purely through nature and chance.

Art, they say, came later — mortal art born from mortal beings. It produced certain playful imitations of reality, images that don't share much truth in themselves — the kind of thing music, painting, and the companion arts create. Some arts do have a more serious purpose and cooperate with nature: medicine, agriculture, gymnastics, for example. Politics, they say, cooperates with nature too, but to a lesser degree and with more artifice. And legislation, they claim, is entirely artificial — built on assumptions that have no basis in truth.

Cleinias
What do you mean by that?

Athenian
In the first place, my dear friend, these thinkers say that the gods don't exist by nature — they're human inventions, products of law and convention. Different gods in different places, depending on what the locals agreed upon when they wrote their laws. And they say that what's truly honorable is one thing by nature and another thing by law. Justice, they insist, has no existence in nature at all. People are constantly arguing about it and changing the rules, and whatever changes get made by human art and legislation have no natural basis — they're just whatever happened to get decided at the time.

This, my friends, is the kind of thing being said by people considered wise — by poets and prose writers alike — and it's seeping into the minds of the young. They're told that the highest form of right is might, and so the young fall into godlessness, believing the gods aren't the kind of beings the law tells them to imagine. This breeds political factions — philosophers inviting young people to live a "natural" life, which really means living in dominion over others rather than in lawful obedience.

Cleinias
What a terrifying picture you've painted, Stranger! Think of the damage this does to young people, and through them to entire families and cities!

Athenian
You're right, Cleinias. So what should the lawgiver do when this corruption has been festering for a long time? Should he simply stand up and threaten everyone — declaring that if they won't say and believe the gods are as the law describes, if they won't shape their conduct to match the law's standards of honor, justice, and virtue, then they'll face death, or beatings, or imprisonment, or loss of citizenship, or confiscation of property and exile? Or should the lawgiver, while making his laws, try to weave in the spirit of persuasion and soften his words as much as possible?

Cleinias
Why, Stranger, if there's any possibility of persuasion, then a lawgiver worth his salt should never tire of persuading! He should leave nothing unsaid in defense of the ancient belief that gods exist, and of all the truths you were just discussing. He should defend the proposition that law and craft both exist by nature — no less than nature itself — since they're creations of mind working in accordance with right reason. That's what you seem to be arguing, and I'm inclined to agree.

Athenian
Enthusiastic Cleinias! But aren't these things difficult for a crowd to grasp? And don't they take a dismally long time to explain?

Cleinias
Stranger, if we didn't lose patience when the subjects were drinking and music, shall we grow tired now when the subject is the gods and divine matters? And here's the great advantage of written legislation: the laws, once set down, sit still. They can be studied and reconsidered at any future time. So if they seem difficult at first hearing, there's no cause for alarm — even a slow reader can go over them again and again. And if they're long but useful, then it seems to me neither reasonable nor pious for anyone to refuse to examine them with all their might.

Megillus
Stranger, I agree with everything Cleinias is saying.

Athenian
Yes, Megillus, and we should do exactly as he proposes. If godless arguments weren't scattered all across the world, there'd be no need to defend the existence of the gods. But since they are everywhere, such arguments are needed. And who should come to the defense of the most fundamental laws when they're under attack from bad people, if not the lawgiver himself?

Megillus
No one is better suited for it.

Athenian
All right then, Cleinias — I need you as my partner here. Tell me: doesn't this materialist view hold that fire, water, earth, and air are the first elements of everything? That these people call this "nature"? And that they think the soul was formed afterward, out of these elements? This isn't just my guess about what they mean — it's exactly what they're saying.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
Then, by heaven, we've found the source of this misguided theory held by all these natural philosophers! Examine their arguments very carefully. This isn't a trivial matter — their godlessness is deeply serious. It's not just that they reason badly. It's that their bad reasoning misleads others. That's what I truly object to.

Cleinias
You're right. But I'd like to understand how this works.

Athenian
I'm afraid the argument will seem unusual.

Cleinias
Don't hold back, Stranger. I can see you're worried this discussion will carry us beyond the bounds of legislation. But if there's no other way to demonstrate our shared belief that gods exist — the very gods our laws are said to honor — then let's take this path, my friend.

Athenian
Then I need to lay out the strange argument these people make — the ones who manufacture the soul according to their godless theories. What they claim is the first cause of generation and destruction is actually last, and what's actually last they put first. That's the root of their mistake about the true nature of the gods.

Cleinias
I still don't follow.

Athenian
Nearly all of them, my friends, seem to be ignorant of the nature and power of the soul — especially its origin. They don't realize that the soul is among the first of all things, that it existed before all physical bodies, and that it's the primary cause of all their changes and transformations. And if this is true — if the soul is older than the body — then mustn't the things that belong to the soul's nature also be prior to the things that belong to the body?

Cleinias
They must be.

Athenian
Then thought, attention, mind, art, and law are prior to hard and soft, heavy and light. And the great original works and actions are works of design — they come first. Nature and the works of nature — though people wrongly apply the word "nature" to them — come second, and are governed by art and mind.

Cleinias
But why is the word "nature" wrong?

Athenian
Because the people who use it mean "nature" is the first creative power. But if soul turns out to be the primeval element — not fire or air — then in the truest sense, soul is what most deserves to be called "natural." And this is true if you can prove that soul is older than the body. Otherwise, it isn't.

Cleinias
You're quite right.

Athenian
Shall we take this up as our next point of investigation?

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Athenian
Let's be careful, though. This argument has a youthful, deceptive appeal — it could easily trip us old men up and make fools of us. We might be aiming at something grand and end up missing even the basic point. Here's what I suggest. Imagine the three of us have to cross a swift river. I'm the youngest of us and the most experienced with rivers, so I'll go first by myself, while you two wait safely on the bank. I'll test whether the river is safe for older men like yourselves. If it is, I'll call you over, and my experience will help guide you across. If it's too deep, the only one at risk will be me. Fair proposal?

What I mean is this: the argument ahead is probably going to be too difficult and too deep for you, since you're not accustomed to this kind of philosophical questioning. A torrent of questions could leave you dizzy and confused — not a pleasant experience. So I think it's better if I ask the questions and answer them myself while you listen from safe ground. That way I can work through the entire proof that the soul is prior to the body.

Cleinias
Excellent, Stranger. Please do exactly that.

Athenian
Come, then. If ever there was a time to call on the gods, this is it. Let's invoke them with all seriousness to help us demonstrate their own existence. Holding tight to that rope, let's venture into the deep.

Here's how I'd start. If someone asks me: "Stranger, is everything at rest and nothing in motion? Or is the opposite true? Or are some things in motion and some at rest?" I'd answer: some things are in motion, some at rest.

"And don't things that move move in a place, and things at rest stay in a place?" Of course. "And some move or rest in just one place, while others move through more than one?" By "moving in one place," we mean the kind of motion you see when a globe spins — rotation around a center, like a ball on a lathe. In that rotation, the same motion carries both the larger and the smaller circle around at the same time, distributed proportionally — faster at the rim, slower at the center. Here's something remarkable: the same motion produces different speeds in proportion to the size of the circles. "Quite true."

And by "moving through more than one place," we mean things that travel from point to point, sometimes having one center of motion, sometimes more than one, as they spin on their axes. When they collide with something stationary, they split apart. When they meet other bodies moving toward them from the opposite direction, they combine into a unity. "Agreed."

And when they combine, they grow. When they divide, they diminish — assuming their basic constitution holds. If it doesn't, that's a different kind of destruction entirely.

"When are all things created, and how?" They're created when a first principle grows from one dimension to two, from two to three, and at the third dimension becomes perceptible to the senses. Everything that changes and moves in this way is in the process of coming into being. It has real existence only when it's at rest. When it passes into a different state, it's destroyed completely.

Have we now covered all the kinds of motion and classified them — with the exception, my friends, of two?

Cleinias
Which two?

Athenian
Precisely the two that our whole inquiry is really about.

Cleinias
Say more.

Athenian
Our inquiry is ultimately about the soul, isn't it?

Cleinias
It certainly is.

Athenian
Then let's say there's one kind of motion that can move other things but can't move itself. That's one kind. And there's another kind that can move both itself and other things — working through combination and separation, growth and diminution, generation and destruction. That's another kind of motion.

Cleinias
Granted.

Athenian
Let's call the motion that moves other things but is itself moved by something else the ninth kind. And the motion that moves itself and everything else — the motion involved in every action and every experience, the true source of change and motion in all things — let's call that the tenth.

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
Now, of these ten kinds of motion, which should we say is the mightiest and most powerful?

Cleinias
I'd have to say the motion that can move itself is ten thousand times superior to all the others.

Athenian
Well said. But may I correct something I said earlier?

Cleinias
What correction?

Athenian
When I called self-motion the tenth kind, that wasn't quite right.

Cleinias
What was wrong about it?

Athenian
In the true order of things, what I called the tenth is actually the first — first in origin and first in power. And what comes after it, the motion I strangely labeled the ninth, is really second.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
Think about it. When one thing is moved by another, and that thing by something else, and so on — can there ever be a first mover in such a chain? How could something moved by something else ever be the beginning of change? That's impossible. But when the self-moved moves other things, and those move still others, and so thousands upon thousands of bodies are set in motion — mustn't the very beginning of all this motion be the change of the self-moving principle?

Cleinias
Absolutely. I agree completely.

Athenian
Or let me put it another way. If, as most of these philosophers have the nerve to claim, everything were originally at rest in one undifferentiated mass — which of our principles of motion would be the first to arise?

Cleinias
Obviously self-motion. There could be no change arising from an external cause in a mass where everything is at rest. The change would have to begin within.

Athenian
Then we must conclude that self-motion, as the origin of all motions — the first to arise among things at rest and the first among things in motion — is the oldest and mightiest principle of change. And what is changed by another thing but sets other things in motion is second.

Cleinias
That's exactly right.

Athenian
Now let me ask a question at this point in the argument.

Cleinias
What question?

Athenian
If we saw this self-moving power existing in something made of earth, water, fire, or any combination of elements — what would we call it?

Cleinias
Are you asking whether we'd call a self-moving power "life"?

Athenian
That's exactly what I'm asking.

Cleinias
We certainly would.

Athenian
And when we find soul in anything, we have to say the same thing — that it has life?

Cleinias
We do.

Athenian
Now, bear with me here — you'd agree that we can know things in three ways?

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
We can know the essence of something, the definition of that essence, and the name. And there are two kinds of questions we can ask about anything.

Cleinias
Two?

Athenian
Sometimes a person gives you the name and asks for the definition. Other times they give you the definition and ask for the name. Let me illustrate.

Cleinias
Go ahead.

Athenian
Take numbers. Some numbers can be divided into two equal parts. When a number can be divided that way, we call it "even." So the name is "even," and the definition is "a number divisible into two equal parts."

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
My point is this: whether we start from the definition and arrive at the name, or start from the name and arrive at the definition, we're talking about the same thing. "Even" means "a number divisible into two equal parts" — same thing, two ways of expressing it.

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
So what's the definition of what we call "soul"? Can we give it any other definition than the one we've already arrived at — the motion that can move itself?

Cleinias
Are you saying that the essence we've defined as "the self-moved" is the same thing that goes by the name "soul"?

Athenian
Yes. And if that's true, can we still say there's anything lacking in the proof that soul is the first origin and moving power of everything that exists, has existed, or will exist — and of all their opposites? Soul has been clearly shown to be the source of change and motion in all things.

Cleinias
Nothing is lacking. It's been proven beyond any doubt that soul, as the source of all motion, is the oldest of all things.

Athenian
And the kind of motion produced in one thing by the action of another — motion that never involves any self-moving power, being merely the change of lifeless matter — that's second in rank, or as far down the scale as you like?

Cleinias
Exactly.

Athenian
Then we're right — absolutely and perfectly right — when we say that soul is prior to body, that the body is second and comes afterward, and that the body was born to obey the soul, which is its ruler.

Cleinias
Nothing could be more true.

Athenian
And do you remember what we agreed to earlier — that if soul is prior to the body, then the things belonging to soul must also be prior to the things belonging to the body?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
Then character, temperament, wishes, reasoning, true beliefs, reflections, and memories are all prior to length, width, depth, and physical strength — if soul is indeed prior to the body.

Cleinias
Necessarily so.

Athenian
And the next step: mustn't we accept that soul is the cause of good and evil, of what's noble and base, of justice and injustice, and of all other opposites — if we're saying soul is the cause of all things?

Cleinias
We must.

Athenian
And since soul orders and inhabits everything that moves, in whatever way it moves, mustn't we also say that soul orders the heavens?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
One soul, or more than one? More than one — I'll answer that for you. We certainly can't suppose there are fewer than two: one that's the source of good, and another that's the source of evil.

Cleinias
Very true.

Athenian
Yes. So soul directs all things in heaven, on earth, and in the sea through its own movements — movements we describe with words like will, deliberation, attention, reasoning, true and false belief, joy, sorrow, confidence, fear, hatred, love, and all the other primary impulses of the soul. These in turn set in motion the secondary, physical changes in material things, guiding everything toward growth and decay, composition and decomposition, and all the qualities that accompany them — heat and cold, heaviness and lightness, hardness and softness, whiteness and blackness, bitterness and sweetness.

When soul truly receives divine mind and works with it, she governs everything rightly, guiding all things toward their proper fulfillment. But when she keeps company with folly, she does exactly the opposite. Shall we accept all this, or do we still have doubts?

Cleinias
No room for doubt at all.

Athenian
So shall we say that the soul which governs heaven and earth and the entire world is a principle of wisdom and virtue — or a principle that has neither?

Cleinias
How would you answer?

Athenian
Like this: if the whole path and movement of the heavens, and of everything in them, is by nature akin to the movement, revolution, and calculation of mind — if it proceeds according to kindred laws — then clearly we must say that the best soul governs the world and guides it along the path of good.

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
But if the world moves wildly and erratically, then it's an evil soul that guides it.

Cleinias
Also true.

Athenian
Now, what kind of motion does mind exhibit? This is a hard question to answer well, so let me help.

Cleinias
Good.

Athenian
Let's not try to look directly at the answer, as if staring straight into the sun at noon — we'd only blind ourselves. I mean, let's not pretend we can fully grasp the nature of mind with mortal understanding. It's safer to look at an image.

Cleinias
What image?

Athenian
Of our ten kinds of motion, let's pick the one that most closely resembles mind. I'll remind you of it and then answer the question on behalf of all of us.

Cleinias
That sounds right.

Athenian
You remember our distinction between things at rest and things in motion?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
And that among things in motion, some move in one place while others move through more than one?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
Of these two, the motion that stays in one place — rotating around a center like a sphere on a lathe — is most closely related to the circular movement of mind.

Cleinias
What do you mean?

Athenian
I'm saying that both mind and circular motion share the same qualities: they move in the same way, in the same place, around the same center, in relation to the same thing, according to a single proportion and order — like the motion of a spinning globe. That's a fair image of mind, and it does us credit.

Cleinias
It certainly does.

Athenian
And the opposite kind of motion — the kind that's irregular, never in the same place, never around the same center, with no consistent proportion, order, or pattern — that would be akin to mindlessness and folly.

Cleinias
Absolutely.

Athenian
So after everything we've said, it's not hard to state clearly: since soul drives all things in their courses, either the best soul or its opposite must of necessity govern the revolution of the heavens.

Cleinias
And judging by our argument, Stranger, it would be impious to suggest that any soul other than the most perfect governs the heavens.

Athenian
You've grasped my meaning perfectly, Cleinias. Now let me ask you another question.

Cleinias
Go ahead.

Athenian
If soul drives the sun and moon and the other stars through their courses, doesn't it drive each one individually?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
Then let's take one of them as our example. The same reasoning will apply to all.

Cleinias
Which one?

Athenian
Everyone can see the body of the sun. But no one sees its soul — or the soul of any other body, living or dead. And yet there's strong reason to believe that this invisible nature surrounds all of them, though it can only be perceived by the mind. So let's use mind and reflection to grasp the following point.

Cleinias
What point?

Athenian
If soul drives the sun, we won't go far wrong in proposing one of three possibilities.

Cleinias
Which three?

Athenian
Either the soul that moves the sun resides within that round, visible body — the way our soul lives within us and carries us around. Or the soul provides itself with an external body made of fire or air, as some thinkers claim, and physically pushes the sun along. Or third, the soul has no body at all but guides the sun through some extraordinary and wondrous power.

Cleinias
Yes — the soul must operate in one of these three ways.

Athenian
And this soul of the sun, which is necessarily greater than the sun itself — whether it carries the sun along in a chariot to give us light, or acts from outside, or guides it by whatever mysterious means — this soul ought to be considered a god by every person.

Cleinias
Yes — by anyone with the slightest trace of sense.

Athenian
And what about the stars, the moon, the years, the months, and the seasons? Mustn't we say the same thing? Since souls possessing every kind of excellence are the causes of all these things, those souls are gods — whether they inhabit physical bodies and govern the heavens from within, or whatever their mode of existence may be. Could anyone who accepts all this still dare to deny that the universe is full of gods?

Cleinias
No one would be that mad, Stranger.

Athenian
Good. Now, Megillus and Cleinias, let's offer terms to the person who has until now denied the existence of the gods, and then move on.

Cleinias
What terms?

Athenian
Either he must teach us that we were wrong to say that soul is the origin of all things, and produce a better argument — or, if he can't do better, he must yield to us and live the rest of his life believing that gods exist. Let's consider whether we've said enough, or not enough, to those who deny the existence of the gods.

Cleinias
More than enough, Stranger.

Athenian
Then we'll say no more to them. Now we need to address the person who believes gods exist but thinks they don't care about human affairs.

"My friend," we'll say to him, "your belief in the gods is driven by a kind of natural kinship — something in you is drawn to them, leading you to honor and believe in them. But then you see wicked, unjust people prospering in both private and public life — people who aren't truly happy but are wrongly judged to be, people celebrated by poets and writers alike. And this shakes your faith. Or perhaps you've personally witnessed monstrous acts of impiety, and you've seen people use criminal means to climb from nothing to the heights of power and wealth. When you observe all this, you don't want to blame the gods — they're your relatives, after all — but you can't make sense of it either. So, caught between confused reasoning and unwillingness to criticize the gods, you've ended up believing they exist but don't concern themselves with human affairs.

"To prevent this opinion from deepening into still greater impiety, let's see if we can reason it away before it takes root. We'll build on the argument we already made to the person who denied the gods entirely. Megillus and Cleinias, answer for our young friend as you did before. And if we hit any rough patches, I'll take over and carry you across the river, just as I did last time."

Cleinias
Very good. Do as you say, and we'll help however we can.

Athenian
It probably won't be hard to prove that the gods care about small things as much as great ones. After all, our friend was present and heard what we said — that the gods are perfectly good, and that caring for all things is fundamental to their nature.

Cleinias
Yes, he certainly heard that.

Athenian
So let's consider together what we mean by the virtue we're attributing to them. Surely we'd agree that being wise and self-controlled belongs to virtue, and the opposite belongs to vice?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
And courage is part of virtue, cowardice part of vice?

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
And one is honorable, the other disgraceful?

Cleinias
Of course.

Athenian
And whatever is base and contemptible — those are human failings. The gods have no share in anything of the sort.

Cleinias
Everyone would agree with that.

Athenian
Now — would we count carelessness, laziness, and self-indulgence as virtues? What do you think?

Cleinias
Absolutely not.

Athenian
They belong to the opposite category?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
And their opposites — diligence, attentiveness, discipline — belong to the category of virtue?

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
So anyone who truly possesses all these good qualities wouldn't be lazy, heedless, or self-indulgent — like those useless drones the poets compare to stingless bees?

Cleinias
A perfect comparison.

Athenian
Then we certainly can't attribute to God a nature that God himself would despise. Anyone who says such a thing is completely out of line.

Cleinias
Of course not. How could God have a nature he hates?

Athenian
Think about it this way. If someone is responsible for managing something — whether they're god or human — and they pay attention to the big picture while neglecting the small details, wouldn't we be entirely wrong to praise them? It has to be for one of two reasons.

Cleinias
What are they?

Athenian
Either they think neglecting small things doesn't matter to the whole, or they know it matters but neglect those things out of carelessness and laziness. Is there any other explanation? Obviously, when something is genuinely beyond someone's power to manage — when even a god or lesser being lacks the strength or ability to handle it — that's not negligence. That's just limitation.

Cleinias
Obviously not.

Athenian
Now, let's put the question to both groups of offenders at once — the ones who say the gods can be bribed, and the ones who say the gods don't care about details. We're three, and they're two, so let's say to them: "First, you both acknowledge that the gods hear and see and know all things, and that nothing within the range of sense or knowledge can escape them. Do you accept that?"

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
"And do you accept that they possess all the power that mortals and immortals can possess?"

Cleinias
Of course they'll accept that too.

Athenian
"And all five of us — the three of us and the two of them — have agreed that the gods are good and perfect."

Cleinias
Without question.

Athenian
But if they're everything we've agreed they are, is it even conceivable that they'd act out of carelessness or laziness? Think about it: in human beings, inactivity is born of cowardice, and carelessness is born of inactivity and self-indulgence.

Cleinias
Exactly right.

Athenian
Then no god could ever be negligent out of laziness or carelessness — because gods have no cowardice in them.

Cleinias
Perfectly true.

Athenian
So if the gods do neglect the smaller and lesser matters of the universe, the only remaining explanation is that they know such things don't need their attention. What other explanation could there be?

Cleinias
None.

Athenian
So, my excellent friend — which interpretation should I take from your position? That the gods are careless because they're ignorant and don't know they ought to attend to these things? Or that they do know what's right but, like the lowest sort of people, choose the worse course because they're overcome by pleasure or pain?

Cleinias
That's impossible.

Athenian
Consider this: doesn't everything that has a soul share in the divine nature? And isn't man the most religious of all creatures?

Cleinias
That can't be denied.

Athenian
And we agree that all mortal creatures belong to the gods, just as the whole of heaven does?

Cleinias
Certainly.

Athenian
So whether someone considers these possessions great or small, it would go against the gods' nature to neglect them — since the gods own us, and they're the most attentive and excellent of all owners. But there's another point to consider.

Cleinias
What's that?

Athenian
Perception and power work in opposite ways when it comes to difficulty. It's harder to perceive small things than large ones — but it's easier to manage and control small things than large ones.

Cleinias
Much easier.

Athenian
Consider a doctor who's willing and able to cure a patient's whole body. If he pays attention only to the major organs and ignores the smaller parts, how will the patient fare?

Cleinias
Not well at all.

Athenian
The same goes for generals, pilots, household managers, statesmen — every kind of manager you can name. The big things don't hold together without attention to the small. As builders say: the large stones don't sit right without the smaller ones.

Cleinias
Of course not.

Athenian
Then let's not consider God inferior to human craftsmen. A good craftsman uses the same skill to perfect both large and small work. And God, the wisest of all beings — who is both willing and able to take care of things — can't be compared to some lazy good-for-nothing who gives up on the easy tasks and only bothers with the hard ones.

Cleinias
Never, Stranger! Let's never entertain such an impious and false idea about the gods.

Athenian
I think we've argued enough with the person who accuses the gods of negligence.

Cleinias
Yes.

Athenian
He's been forced to see his error. But I think he still needs some words of comfort.

Cleinias
What comfort would you offer?

Athenian
Let's say to the young man:

"The ruler of the universe has arranged all things with an eye to the excellence and preservation of the whole. Every part, as far as possible, acts and is acted upon in a way appropriate to its nature. Over every part, down to the smallest fraction, overseers have been appointed who have worked out their governance with infinite precision. And your life, insignificant man, is one of those parts. Small as it is, it contributes to the whole. But what you fail to realize is this: every created thing exists for the sake of the whole, so that the life of the whole may flourish. You were created for the sake of the whole — not the whole for the sake of you.

"Every doctor, every skilled craftsman, does everything for the sake of the whole — working toward the common good, shaping the part to serve the whole, not the whole to serve the part. And you're resentful because you don't understand how what happens to you serves the good of the universe as far as the laws of creation allow.

"Now, since the soul passes through different bodies and undergoes all kinds of changes — whether through its own choices or through the influence of other souls — all that remains for the cosmic player is to move the pieces accordingly: sending the better nature to a better place, the worse to a worse place, giving each its proper portion."

Cleinias
What do you mean by that?

Athenian
I'm describing how the gods' oversight of everything could be perfectly easy. Imagine if someone were to rearrange all things at random, without regard to the whole — creating a living creature of water from fire, for example, instead of following the orderly progression of transformations, where many things come from one and one from many in a regular sequence. The complexity would be infinite. But the ruler of the world actually has a remarkably straightforward task.

Cleinias
How so?

Athenian
Here's how. When the king — the cosmic ruler — saw that all our actions are animated by soul, and that there's much virtue and much vice in them; that soul and body, though not eternal like the popular conception of gods, are nonetheless indestructible once they've come into being (for if either could be destroyed, living creatures would cease to be generated); and when he observed that goodness of soul naturally benefits, while evil naturally harms — seeing all this, he arranged each part so that goodness would most easily and effectively triumph over evil in the grand scheme of things.

He devised a universal plan determining where each type of soul would go and what place it would occupy. But the development of character and quality he left to individual free will. Each of us becomes what we are through the direction of our desires and the nature of our soul.

Cleinias
Yes, that's probably right.

Athenian
Everything that has a soul carries within itself a principle of change. As it changes, it moves in accordance with the law and order of destiny. Souls that have undergone only minor changes move less and stay closer to the earth's surface. But those that have changed more drastically and become more corrupt sink down into the abyss — into Hades and the regions below, the very places whose names fill people with terror, places they glimpse in dreams both during life and after death.

And whenever the soul takes on more goodness or more evil — through its own efforts or the powerful influence of other souls — when it enters into communion with divine virtue and becomes divine, it's carried to a different and better place, one perfect in holiness. But when it keeps company with evil, it changes its place accordingly.

"This is the justice of the gods who dwell on Olympus."

Young man — you who imagine the gods have forgotten you — know this: if you become worse, you'll join worse souls. If you become better, you'll join better ones. In every cycle of life and death, you'll do and suffer what like justly suffers at the hands of like. This is heaven's justice, and neither you nor any other unlucky soul will ever escape it. The powers that ordained it made sure of that. Take it seriously — because it will certainly take you seriously.

If you say, "I'm small — I'll burrow into the earth and hide," or "I'm great — I'll fly up to heaven and escape" — you're neither small enough nor great enough to avoid the fitting consequence, whether it comes in this life, in the world below, or in some still harsher place.

And this also explains those cases that troubled you — wicked people who seemed to rise from misery to greatness through unholy deeds. You thought you were seeing proof of divine neglect. But you were seeing a mirror, and you didn't know how to read the reflection. You didn't understand how the gods make all things work together for the good of the whole.

Bold man — do you really think you don't need to understand this? Without understanding it, no one can form any true idea of happiness or unhappiness, or hold any rational discussion about the meaning of life.

If Cleinias here and this worthy company of ours can convince you that you don't know what you're talking about when it comes to the gods, then God himself will help you. But if you want to hear more, listen to what we say to our third opponent — assuming you have any capacity to understand at all. I believe we've sufficiently proven that the gods exist and that they care about human beings. What remains is the third claim: that the gods can be bribed by the wicked and turned aside by gifts. This is something we must not concede to anyone, and every person should refute it with all their strength.

Cleinias
Very good. Let's do exactly that.

Athenian
Well then — I call on the gods themselves as my witnesses — tell me: if the gods are to be bribed, how would that work? Who are they, and what's their nature? They must at least be the rulers who constantly govern the entire heavens, mustn't they?

Cleinias
True.

Athenian
And what earthly rulers can we compare them to? Or who can be compared to them? Where can we find a smaller image of what's greater? Are they like charioteers driving teams of horses? Or like pilots steering ships? Maybe they're like generals commanding armies? Or doctors fighting against the diseases that attack the body? Or farmers anxiously watching the seasons' effect on their crops? Or shepherds tending their flocks?

We've established that the world is full of many good things and also many evils — more evils than goods, in fact. There's an unending battle raging, one that requires extraordinary vigilance. The gods and lesser divine beings are our allies in this battle, and we are their charges. What destroys us is injustice, arrogance, and folly. What saves us is justice, self-control, and wisdom. These virtues exist in their fullest form in the life of the gods, though traces of them can be found among human beings too.

But here on earth, we know there are souls driven by injustice — souls that can be compared to animals fawning on their keepers, whether those keepers are dogs, shepherds, or the most accomplished masters. For these corrupt souls, as wicked people openly declare, use flattery, prayers, and incantations to ingratiate themselves with their keepers, and they're allowed to profit at others' expense. And this sin, which goes by the name of dishonesty, is really the same kind of evil as disease in a body, pestilence in a season, and — when it appears in cities and governments — injustice.

Cleinias
Exactly right.

Athenian
So what must someone actually be claiming when they say the gods are always lenient to wrongdoers, as long as the wrongdoers share the spoils? It's as if wolves tossed a piece of their prey to the guard dogs, and the dogs, bought off by the offering, let the wolves tear the flock apart. Isn't that exactly the argument of anyone who says the gods can be bribed?

Cleinias
Precisely.

Athenian
Now, to which of those categories of guardian could anyone compare the gods without absurdity? Would anyone say the gods are like pilots who get drunk on offerings of wine and rich food, and end up capsizing the ship with everyone aboard?

Cleinias
Certainly not.

Athenian
Surely they're not like charioteers bribed to throw the race and let another team win?

Cleinias
That would be a horrifying image of the gods.

Athenian
And they're not like generals, or doctors, or farmers, or shepherds. No one would compare them to guard dogs that have been silenced by wolves.

Cleinias
Unspeakable.

Athenian
And aren't the gods the supreme guardians of all, watching over our highest and most important concerns?

Cleinias
The very highest.

Athenian
Then shall we say that these guardians of our noblest interests — the best guardians in existence — are inferior in virtue to dogs, or even to moderately decent human beings, who would never betray justice for the sake of bribes impiously offered by wicked people?

Cleinias
Absolutely not. The very idea is intolerable. Anyone who holds such a view deserves to be singled out as the most wicked and impious person alive.

Athenian
Then can we say that our three propositions have been sufficiently demonstrated? That gods exist, that they care about human beings, and that they can never be persuaded to commit injustice?

Cleinias
You have our complete agreement.

Athenian
I've spoken with some heat, I know — because I'm passionate in my opposition to wicked people. Let me tell you why, dear Cleinias. I didn't want the wicked to think that because they have clever arguments on their side, they can do whatever they please and believe whatever they want about the gods. That passion pushed me to speak too forcefully. But if we've succeeded even a little in making these people hate their own vices and love their opposites, then this introduction to our laws on impiety will not have been in vain.

Cleinias
Let's hope so. And even if we haven't fully succeeded, our style of argument won't disgrace the lawgiver.

Athenian
Now, after this introduction, the law itself must follow — the law that will put our arguments into practice. To all impious persons, the message is this: abandon your ways and turn to piety. And for those who refuse, the law on impiety shall be as follows:

If anyone is guilty of impiety in word or deed, any person who witnesses it shall report it to the magistrates, in support of the law. The magistrates who first receive the report shall bring the accused before the appropriate court, as the law requires. And if any magistrate, after receiving such a report, fails to act, that magistrate shall himself be tried for impiety by anyone willing to uphold the laws. If anyone is convicted, the court shall determine the punishment for each specific act of impiety. All such criminals shall be imprisoned.

There shall be three prisons in the state. The first is the ordinary prison, near the marketplace, for the general run of offenders. The second shall be near the Nocturnal Council and shall be called the "House of Reformation." The third shall be located in some remote, desolate region in the heart of the country, and shall bear a name suggesting retribution.

Now, there are three causes of impiety, as we've discussed, and each cause produces two varieties — making six types of impiety in total, which deserve to be distinguished and should not all receive the same punishment.

Consider the person who doesn't believe in the gods but has a naturally righteous character — someone who hates wickedness, despises injustice, avoids unrighteous people, and is drawn to the righteous. Then consider the person who, besides believing the world has no gods, is also intemperate — someone who happens to have a sharp memory and a quick wit. Both are unbelievers, but the second does far more harm than the first.

The first type may talk loosely about the gods, about sacrifices and oaths. By laughing at other people's beliefs, he may lead others into similar views — if he goes unpunished. But the second type, who holds the same opinions and is called "clever," is full of cunning and deceit. From this class come tyrants and demagogues, generals and founders of secret cults, and the so-called Sophists with their ingenious schemes. There are many varieties of unbelievers, but legislation is needed for only two: the hypocritical type, whose crime deserves death many times over, and the type who merely needs confinement and a talking-to.

Similarly, the belief that the gods don't care produces two more types of offense, and the belief that the gods can be bribed produces two more — six types in all.

Those who have fallen into error purely from lack of understanding — not from malice or corrupt character — shall be placed by the judge in the House of Reformation for a period of no less than five years. During this time, they shall have no contact with other citizens, except for members of the Nocturnal Council, who shall visit them with the aim of healing their souls. When their imprisonment is up, if any of them has come to their senses, they shall be restored to normal society. But if they haven't reformed and are convicted a second time, they shall be put to death.

As for the truly monstrous cases — those who not only deny the gods or claim the gods are negligent or bribable, but who go further, holding humanity in contempt, claiming to conjure the souls of the living, promising to summon the dead, and offering to charm the very gods with sacrifices and prayers, all for the sake of money — people who would destroy individuals, entire families, and whole cities for profit — anyone convicted of such crimes shall be confined in the prison in the center of the country. No free person shall ever visit them. They shall receive their food rations from public servants, as directed by the guardians of the law. And when they die, their bodies shall be cast beyond the borders, unburied. If any free citizen helps to bury them, that citizen shall face prosecution for impiety by anyone willing to bring the charge. However, if the convicted person leaves behind children fit for citizenship, the guardians of orphans shall care for them, just as they would for any other orphans, from the day of their parent's conviction.

In all these cases, there should be one additional law that will make people in general less likely to offend in word or deed, and less foolish — because they won't be allowed to practice unauthorized religious rites. Here is the straightforward rule:

No one shall maintain private religious shrines. When someone wishes to sacrifice, they shall go to the public temples and hand their offerings to the priests and priestesses who oversee these sacred matters. They may pray, and anyone who wishes may join them in prayer.

The reasoning is this: setting up gods and temples properly is no small matter — it requires great intelligence. And women especially, but men too, when they're sick or in danger or facing any kind of difficulty, or when they experience some stroke of good fortune — they have a way of consecrating the moment, vowing sacrifices, promising to build shrines to gods, demigods, and divine heroes. And when people are shaken by terrifying visions and dreams, they seek remedies in altars and temples, and they'll fill every house and every village square with them — placing them in the open air, or wherever the vision occurred. For all these reasons, the law must be obeyed.

There's another consideration as well. The law keeps an eye on the impious, ensuring they don't imagine they can propitiate the gods in secret — raising private temples, building private altars, offering private sacrifices and prayers — while actually multiplying their offenses infinitely. Such behavior brings divine punishment not only on themselves but on those who tolerate it, people who may be far better than they are. And in the end, the whole state reaps the harvest of their impiety — which, in a sense, it deserves.

God will not blame the lawgiver for enacting the following: No one shall possess shrines of the gods in private houses. If someone is found maintaining them and performing any sacred rites not publicly authorized — assuming the offender isn't also guilty of some other grave and impious crime — the person who discovers it shall inform the guardians of the law. The guardians shall order the offender to transfer their private rites to the public temples. If they refuse to comply, penalties shall be imposed until they do.

And if someone is proven guilty of impiety — not the trivial kind that comes from youthful foolishness, but the serious kind that grown adults commit — whether they have sacrificed publicly or privately to unauthorized gods, their sacrifice is impure, and they shall be punished with death. Whether the act was committed in earnest or from childish frivolity shall be determined by the guardians of the law before they bring the case to court and prosecute the offender for impiety.
