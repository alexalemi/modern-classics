PARMENIDES (Part 2)

---

"But consider a new point. For something to touch something else, mustn't it be right next to it -- occupying the adjacent position?"

"It must."

"So the One, if it's to touch itself, would need to be situated right next to itself, occupying the position next to its own."

"Yes."

"But that would require the One to be two things, in two places at once. And as long as it's one, that can never happen."

"It can't."

"So the One can't touch itself, any more than it can be two."

"Right."

"And it can't touch the others either."

"Why not?"

"Because for two things to be in contact, whatever touches the other must be separate from it but right next to it, with nothing between them."

"True."

"So you need at least two things to have contact."

"Yes."

"And if you add a third item in sequence, you get three terms and two contacts."

"Right."

"And every additional term adds one more contact. So the number of contacts is always one fewer than the number of terms. The first two terms exceeded the contacts by one, and every term added after that maintains the same pattern: the total number of terms always exceeds the total number of contacts by one."

"Right."

"So whatever the total number of things, the contacts are always one fewer."

"Yes."

"But if there's only one thing, and not two, there can be no contact."

"How could there be?"

"Now, we said that the others -- being other than the One -- are not one and have no part in the One."

"Right."

"So they have no number, since they have no one-ness in them."

"Of course not."

"Then the others are neither one, nor two, nor can they be called by any number at all."

"No."

"So the One alone is one, and two doesn't exist."

"Clearly not."

"And without two, there's no contact."

"There is not."

"So neither does the One touch the others, nor the others touch the One -- since there is no contact."

"There isn't."

"Therefore, for all these reasons, the One both touches and does not touch -- both itself and the others."


EQUAL AND UNEQUAL

"Next: is the One equal and unequal to itself and the others?"

"How do you mean?"

"If the One were greater or less than the others, or the others greater or less than the One, that wouldn't be because of their being the One and the others as such. It would only be if, in addition to being what they are, they also had equality, or if one side had Greatness and the other Smallness -- or vice versa."

"Of course."

"So there must be two real natures -- Greatness and Smallness. If they didn't exist, they couldn't be opposed to each other or present in things."

"Right."

"Now, if Smallness is present in the One, it would be either in the whole or in a part of it."

"It would have to be."

"Suppose it's in the whole. Then it would either be spread equally throughout the One, co-extensive with it -- or it would contain the One."

"Yes."

"If Smallness were co-extensive with the One, it would be equal to the One. And if it contained the One, it would be greater than the One."

"Yes."

"But can Smallness be equal to something, or greater than something? Those are the functions of Equality and Greatness -- not of Smallness."

"Impossible."

"So Smallness can't be in the whole of the One. If anywhere, only in a part."

"Right."

"But not in all of a part either -- because then the same problem recurs. It would be equal to or greater than whatever part it's in."

"Right."

"So Smallness won't be in anything -- not in a whole, not in a part. Nothing will be small except Smallness itself."

"It seems so."

"And Greatness won't be in the One either. Because if Greatness were in something, there would be something else -- besides Greatness itself -- that is greater: namely, the thing containing it. And this would be so even though Smallness isn't present for the great thing to exceed. But that's impossible, since Smallness is entirely absent."

"True."

"Now, absolute Greatness is only greater than absolute Smallness, and absolute Smallness is only smaller than absolute Greatness."

"Right."

"So the others are neither greater nor less than the One -- since they have neither Greatness nor Smallness. And Greatness and Smallness have no power of exceeding or being exceeded in relation to the One -- only in relation to each other. And the One can't be greater or less than them or the others -- since it too has neither Greatness nor Smallness."

"It seems not."

"So if the One is neither greater nor less than the others, it can't exceed them or be exceeded by them."

"No."

"And what neither exceeds nor is exceeded must be on an equal footing. And being on an equal footing, it must be equal."

"Yes."

"And the same holds for the One in relation to itself. Having neither Greatness nor Smallness within itself, it can't exceed or be exceeded by itself. So it's on an equal footing with and equal to itself."

"That follows."

"Then the One is equal to both itself and the others."

"Clearly."

"And yet, the One, being in itself, also surrounds itself and is outside itself. As the container, it's greater than itself; as the contained, it's less than itself. So the One is both greater and less than itself."

"It is."

"Now, there can't possibly be anything that isn't included in either the One or the others, can there?"

"Of course not."

"And whatever exists must be somewhere."

"Yes."

"And what's in something is less than what it's in -- the container is always greater than the contained."

"Right."

"Since there's nothing besides the One and the others, and they must be in something, they must be in each other -- the One in the others and the others in the One. There's nowhere else for them to be."

"That's clear."

"Now, insofar as the One is in the others, the others are greater than the One because they contain it, and the One is less because it's contained. But insofar as the others are in the One, the One is greater than the others by the same principle, and the others are less than the One."

"It seems so."

"So the One is equal to, and greater than, and less than both itself and the others."

"It is."

"And if greater, less, and equal -- then it has equal measures, more measures, and fewer measures than itself and the others. And if it has different measures, it also has different parts."

"Of course."

"And having equal, more, and fewer measures -- it would also be equal, greater, and less in number than itself and the others."

"How so?"

"It has more measures than what it exceeds, and as many parts as measures. The same for what it's equal to, and for what it's less than."

"Yes."

"So being greater, less, and equal to itself -- it has equal measures, more measures, and fewer measures than itself. And if measures, then parts."

"Right."

"And having equal parts, it's numerically equal to itself. Having more parts, more in number. Having fewer parts, fewer in number."

"Obviously."

"And the same applies to its relation to the others: insofar as it's greater, it's more in number; insofar as it's smaller, fewer; insofar as it's equal in size, equal in number."

"Necessarily."

"So once again, the One is equal in number to, and more than, and fewer than, both itself and all the others."

"It is."


TIME AND BECOMING

"Does the One also participate in time? Does it become older and younger than itself and the others -- and also neither younger nor older?"

"What do you mean?"

"If the One is, then 'being' must be attributed to it."

"Yes."

"And 'to be' is just participation in being in the present time. 'To have been' is participation in being at a past time. 'To be about to be' is participation in being at a future time."

"Right."

"So the One, since it participates in being, participates in time."

"Yes."

"And isn't time always moving forward?"

"It is."

"Then the One is always becoming older than itself, since it moves forward in time."

"Yes."

"And remember -- what's older is older relative to what's younger?"

"I remember."

"So since the One becomes older than itself, it's becoming older than something that's becoming younger. And that something is itself. So it becomes younger than itself at the same time."

"It must."

"So the One becomes both older and younger than itself."

"Yes."

"And it is older when, in the process of becoming, it reaches the present moment -- the point between 'was' and 'will be.' Because surely, in moving from the past to the future, it can't skip over the present."

"No, it can't."

"And when it reaches the present, it stops becoming older. It no longer becomes -- it is. If it kept going, the present would never catch it. It's the nature of what's in motion to touch both the present and the future -- letting go of the present and reaching for the future -- passing between them as it becomes."

"True."

"But what's in the process of becoming can't skip the present. When it reaches the present, it stops becoming and just is whatever it happens to be becoming."

"Right."

"So the One, when in the process of becoming older it reaches the present, stops becoming and is older."

"Yes."

"And it's older than what it was becoming older than -- namely, itself."

"Yes."

"And what's older is older than what's younger?"

"Yes."

"So the One is younger than itself -- at the moment when, becoming older, it reaches the present."

"That follows."

"But the present is always present with the One throughout all its existence. Whenever it is, it's always now."

"Of course."

"Then the One always both is and is becoming older and younger than itself."

"It seems so."

"And does it exist or become for a longer time than itself, or the same time?"

"The same time."

"But what exists for the same time as itself is the same age as itself."

"Of course."

"And what's the same age is neither older nor younger."

"No."

"So the One, existing and becoming for the same time as itself, is neither older nor younger than itself."

"I'd say not."

"Now, what about its relation to the others? Does it become older or younger than they?"

"I can't say."

"But you can at least say this: the things other than the One are more than one -- since the others are plural, not singular."

"Yes, they're many."

"And a multitude has a number greater than one."

"Of course."

"Now, which comes first -- the lesser or the greater?"

"The lesser."

"And the least is the first. And that's the One."

"Yes."

"So the One, of all things that have number, came into being first. And all the other things also have number, since they're many."

"They do."

"And what came first must have come before the others. The others came later. And what comes later is younger than what came before."

"Yes."

"So the others are younger than the One, and the One is older."

"It would seem so."

"But what about this: can the One have come into being contrary to its own nature?"

"Impossible."

"And yet, we showed that the One has parts -- and if parts, then a beginning, middle, and end."

"Yes."

"And a beginning -- of the One itself, and of everything else -- comes into being first, doesn't it? After the beginning come the rest of the parts, all the way to the end."

"Yes."

"And all these 'rest of the parts' are parts of the whole -- of the One. The One becomes whole and one only when the end is reached."

"Exactly."

"But the end comes last. And the One, by its nature, comes into being with the last part -- simultaneously with the end. Since the One can't violate its own nature, its nature requires it to come into being together with the end, after everything else."

"That's right."

"So the One is younger than the others, and the others are older."

"That also seems right."

"But consider: must not a beginning, or any other part of something -- if it's truly a part and not multiple parts -- be itself one thing?"

"Necessarily."

"So the One comes into being together with each part as it comes into being -- with the first part, the second, and all the rest. It's present throughout the process. It isn't absent from any part being added to any other, all the way to the last, when the whole becomes one. The One is missing from nothing -- not the middle, not the first, not the last, not any of them."

"Right."

"Then the One is the same age as all the others. So if the One doesn't contradict its own nature, it came into being neither before nor after the others -- but simultaneously. And by this argument, the One is neither older nor younger than the others, nor they than it. But by the previous argument, the One was both older and younger than the others, and the others than the One."

"Exactly."

"So much for how it is and has become. But what about whether it becomes older and younger, or neither? The same question arises for becoming as for being. What do we say?"

"I can't answer."

"But I can venture this much: even if one thing is older or younger than another, it can't become older or younger by a greater difference than it started with. When you add equals to unequals -- whether periods of time or anything else -- the difference between them stays the same."

"Of course."

"So something that exists can't become older or younger than something else that exists -- the age difference is always the same. The One is older and has become older, and the other is younger and has become younger. But they don't keep becoming more so."

"True."

"So the One, which exists, doesn't keep becoming older or younger than the others, which exist."

"No."

"But consider whether they might become older and younger in another way."

"What way?"

"The One was shown to be older than the others, and the others older than the One."

"Yes."

"When the One is older than the others, it has been in existence for a longer time."

"Right."

"But think: if you add an equal amount of time to a longer time and a shorter time, the longer will differ from the shorter by a smaller proportion than before."

"Yes."

"So the difference in age between the One and the others won't be as great afterwards as it was at first. If equal time is added to both, the age difference becomes proportionally smaller."

"Right."

"And what differs in age from something else by less than before -- isn't it becoming younger relative to what it used to be older than?"

"Yes, younger."

"And if the One is becoming younger relative to the others, then the others are becoming older relative to the One."

"Yes."

"So what had become younger keeps becoming older relative to what had become and was older. They never actually are older or younger -- they're always in the process of becoming so. The One keeps becoming younger relative to the others because it started out older and prior, and the others keep becoming older relative to the One because they came into being later. And the same relationship runs in the other direction too: the others were shown to be older and prior to the One, so the same process applies in reverse.

"They can't have finished becoming, though -- because if they had, they would be, not just be becoming. But that's impossible. They're always in the process of becoming both older and younger than each other."

"That's clear."

"So: insofar as the One and the others don't become older or younger than each other -- because they always differ by the same amount -- the One doesn't become older or younger than the others, nor the others than the One. But insofar as what came into being earlier and what came later must differ by a continually changing proportion -- in that respect, they must keep becoming older and younger than each other: the One relative to the others, and the others relative to the One."

"Exactly."

"For all these reasons, then, the One both is and becomes older and younger than itself and the others, and neither is nor becomes older or younger than itself and the others."

"Precisely."

"And since the One participates in time and in becoming older and younger, it must also participate in past, present, and future."

"Of course."

"Then the One was, and is, and will be. It was becoming, is becoming, and will become."

"Right."

"And there is, was, and will be something that stands in relation to it and belongs to it."

"Yes."

"And since right now we have opinion and knowledge and perception of the One, there is opinion and knowledge and perception of it."

"Quite right."

"And there's a name and a description for it. It is named and described. And everything of this kind that belongs to other things also belongs to the One."

"That is certainly true."


THE INSTANT (The Moment Between)

"Now, one more time -- for the third time -- let's consider this: if the One is both one and many, as we've described, and is also neither one nor many, and participates in time -- then, insofar as it's one, mustn't it sometimes participate in being, and insofar as it's not one, sometimes not participate in being?"

"Necessarily."

"But can it participate in being at the very time it's not participating? Or not participate at the very time it is?"

"Impossible."

"So it participates and doesn't participate at different times. That's the only way it can both have and lack the same thing."

"Right."

"And isn't there a time when it takes on being and a time when it lets go of being? How else could it have the same thing at one time and lack it at another, unless it receives and gives it up?"

"No other way."

"Receiving being -- isn't that what you'd call coming into being?"

"I would."

"And giving up being -- isn't that destruction?"

"Yes."

"So the One, it seems, comes into being and is destroyed -- by taking on and letting go of being."

"Necessarily."

"And being both one and many, and in the process of becoming and being destroyed -- when it becomes one, it ceases to be many; when it becomes many, it ceases to be one."

"Of course."

"And as it becomes one and many, mustn't it experience separation and combination?"

"It must."

"And whenever it becomes like and unlike, it must undergo assimilation and dissimilation?"

"Yes."

"And when it becomes greater, less, or equal, it must grow, diminish, or be equalized?"

"Right."

"But when it's in motion and then comes to rest, or when it's at rest and then begins to move -- it surely can't be in any time at all."

"How so?"

"Think about it: something that's first at rest and then in motion, or first in motion and then at rest -- can't do that without changing."

"Of course not."

"And surely there's no time in which something can be simultaneously neither in motion nor at rest."

"There can't be."

"But it also can't change without changing."

"No."

"So when does it change? It can't change while at rest, or while in motion, or while in time."

"Right."

"Does this strange thing really exist -- this thing in which it is at the moment of changing?"

"What thing?"

"The instant. The instant seems to be something from which change takes place into one state or another. Change doesn't come from rest as such, or from motion as such. Rather, there's this peculiar nature -- the instant -- that lies between rest and motion, outside of time. And it's into this and out of this that what's in motion changes into rest, and what's at rest changes into motion."

"So it seems."

"Then the One, since it's both at rest and in motion, must change from one to the other -- because only in this way can it be in both states. And in changing, it changes in an instant. And at the moment of changing, it's in no time at all, and is therefore neither in motion nor at rest."

"Right."

"And the same applies to its other transitions: when it passes from being to ceasing-to-be, or from not-being to coming-into-being, it passes between certain states of motion and rest, and at that moment it neither is nor is not, neither comes into being nor is destroyed."

"It seems so."

"And by the same principle, in the passage from one to many and from many to one, it's neither one nor many, neither being separated nor being combined. In the passage from like to unlike and back, it's neither like nor unlike, neither becoming similar nor dissimilar. In the passage from small to great and equal and back again, it's neither small nor great nor equal -- neither growing, shrinking, nor being equalized."

"It seems so."

"All these, then, are the consequences for the One, if the One has being."

"Yes."


HYPOTHESIS I.aa: IF THE ONE IS -- What follows for the Others

"But shouldn't we also consider what happens to the others, if the One is?"

"We should."

"Then let's show what consequences follow for the things other than the One, if the One is."

"Let's do it."

"Since there are things other than the One, the others are not the One. If they were, they couldn't be other."

"Right."

"But the others are not entirely without the One either. In a way, they participate in it."

"How so?"

"The others are other than the One because they have parts. If they had no parts at all, they would just be one."

"Right."

"And parts, we agree, belong to a whole."

"Yes."

"And this whole must be one thing made up of many. The parts are parts of this one whole -- each part is a part not of the many, but of the whole."

"What do you mean?"

"If something were a part of many things, including itself, it would be a part of itself -- which is absurd. And it would be a part of each of the other parts, if it's a part of all. But if it's not a part of some particular one, then it would be a part of all the others except that one -- and so it wouldn't be a part of each one. And if not a part of each one, it can't be a part of any of the many. And if it's not a part of any of them, it can't be a part or anything else of all those things of which it's a part of none."

"That's clear."

"So a part isn't a part of many things or of all things, but of a single thing -- a single nature that we call a 'whole': one complete unity made up of all the parts. That is what the part is a part of."

"Exactly."

"So if the others have parts, they participate in the whole and in the One."

"Precisely."

"Then the things other than the One must be one complete whole with parts."

"They must."

"And the same argument applies to each individual part. Each part must participate in the One too. If each part is a part, 'each' means it's one thing, separate from the rest and existing on its own. Otherwise we couldn't say 'each.'"

"Right."

"But when we say the part participates in the One, it must be something other than the One. If it weren't, it wouldn't merely participate -- it would be the One. And nothing can be the One except the One itself."

"Nothing."

"So both the whole and each part must participate in the One. The whole is one whole whose parts are its parts. And each part is one part of the whole."

"Right."

"And won't the things that participate in the One be other than the One?"

"Of course."

"And the things other than the One must be many. If they were neither one nor more than one, they'd be nothing."

"True."

"But since the things that participate in 'the One as a part' and in 'the One as a whole' are more than one -- mustn't those very things be infinite in number?"

"How?"

"Look at it this way. When they come to participate in the One, they don't yet have the One, and they're not participating at the very moment they acquire it, right?"

"Right."

"So at that point, they're multitudes in which the One is not present."

"Yes."

"And if we were to abstract the very smallest fraction from them in thought -- that smallest fraction, if it doesn't participate in the One, must itself be a multitude and not one."

"It must."

"And if we keep looking at this other nature -- the nature of what's other than the Form -- considered purely in itself, then as far as we can see, it's unlimited in number."

"Yes."

"And yet, when each part becomes a part, the parts immediately have a limit in relation to the whole and to each other, and the whole has a limit in relation to the parts."

"Exactly."

"So the result for the others is this: their union with the One creates a new element in them -- something that gives them limitation in relation to one another. But in their own nature, they have no limit."

"That's clear."

"Then the things other than the One, both as wholes and as parts, are infinite -- and also share in limit."

"Yes."

"So they're both like and unlike one another and themselves."

"How?"

"Insofar as they're unlimited in their own nature, they're all affected in the same way."

"Yes."

"And insofar as they all share in limit, they're all affected in the same way."

"Right."

"But insofar as they're both limited and unlimited, they're affected in opposite ways."

"Yes."

"And opposites are the most unlike of things."

"True."

"So considered with respect to either property alone, they're like themselves and each other. Considered with respect to both properties together, they're maximally opposed and maximally unlike."

"It seems so."

"Then the others are both like and unlike themselves and one another."

"They are."

"And they're the same and different from one another, and in motion and at rest, and subject to every sort of opposite property. All of this can be proved without difficulty, since we've already shown they have the properties we described."

"That's right."


HYPOTHESIS I.bb: IF THE ONE IS (taken strictly) -- What follows for the Others

"Now let's set aside the further development of these points -- they're evident enough. Let's go back and consider: if the One is, is the opposite equally true of the others?"

"By all means."

"If the One is, what must happen to the others? Let's ask."

"Yes."

"The One must be distinct from the others, and the others from the One."

"Why?"

"Because there's nothing else besides them that is distinct from both. The phrase 'the One and the others' covers everything."

"Everything."

"So there's nothing outside them in which they could both exist."

"Nothing."

"Then the One and the others are never in the same thing."

"It seems not."

"So they're separated from each other."

"Yes."

"And we said that what is truly one has no parts."

"Of course."

"Then the One can't be in the others as a whole or as a part, if it's separated from them and has no parts."

"No."

"So there's no way the others can participate in the One -- if they can't share in it either as a whole or as a part."

"It seems not."

"Then there's no way the others are one, and they have no unity in them whatsoever."

"There isn't."

"And the others aren't many either. If they were many, each part of them would be a part of the whole. But the others, not participating in the One in any way, are neither one nor many, neither whole nor part."

"Right."

"So the others neither are nor contain two or three -- since they're entirely deprived of the One."

"Right."

"And the others are neither like nor unlike the One, and they don't contain likeness or unlikeness. If they were like and unlike, or contained likeness and unlikeness, they'd have two natures in them that are opposite to each other."

"That's clear."

"But it's impossible for something that participates in nothing to participate in two things."

"Impossible."

"So the others are neither like nor unlike, nor both. If they were like or unlike, they'd participate in one of those natures -- which would be one thing. If they were both, they'd participate in two opposites -- which would be two things. And we showed both are impossible."

"Right."

"Therefore they're neither the same nor different, neither in motion nor at rest, neither coming into being nor being destroyed, neither greater nor less nor equal. They don't experience anything of that sort. If they did, they'd be participating in one and two and three, in odd and even -- and we proved they can't participate in any of these, since they're completely and utterly devoid of the One."

"That's right."

"Therefore, if the One is, the One is everything and also nothing, both in relation to itself and to the others."

"Exactly."


HYPOTHESIS II.a: IF THE ONE IS NOT (taken as meaningful negation) -- What follows for the One

"Very well. Shouldn't we next consider what follows if the One is not?"

"Yes, we should."

"What does this hypothesis mean: 'if the One is not'? Is it different from saying 'if the not-One is not'?"

"Of course it's different."

"Just different? Or are the two expressions -- 'if the One is not' and 'if the not-One is not' -- actually complete opposites?"

"Complete opposites."

"Now, when someone says 'if Greatness is not' or 'if Smallness is not' or anything like that -- whenever they use such an expression, they clearly mean that what 'is not' is other than other things. Agreed?"

"Yes."

"So when someone says 'if the One is not,' they clearly mean that what 'is not' is other than everything else. We know what they mean."

"We do."

"When someone says 'the One,' they're referring to something known. And secondly, something other than everything else. It makes no difference whether they say the One exists or doesn't exist -- what 'does not exist' is still known to be something, and is still distinguished from other things."

"Necessarily."

"So let me start from the beginning. If the One is not, what follows? First, it seems, there must be knowledge of it -- otherwise, the phrase 'if the One is not' wouldn't even be meaningful."

"True."

"Second, the others must differ from it -- otherwise, it couldn't be described as different from the others."

"Right."

"So Difference belongs to it, as well as knowledge. When we speak of the One as different from the others, we're not talking about a difference in the others -- we're talking about a difference in the One."

"Clearly."

"Furthermore, the One-that-is-not is something. It participates in relation to 'that,' 'this,' 'these,' and the like. It's an attribute of 'this.' Because the One, and the things other than the One, couldn't have been spoken of at all -- nor could any attribute or relation of the One-that-is-not have been mentioned -- if the One didn't participate in 'some' and in these other relations."

"Right."

"Being can't be attributed to the One, since the One is not. But the One-that-is-not may -- indeed must -- participate in many things, if it and nothing else is what is not. If we were talking about something else not-being, not the One specifically, we couldn't say anything about it. But if it's specifically the One that is not, then it must participate in 'that' and in many other predicates."

"Certainly."

"And it will have unlikeness in relation to the others. The others, being different from the One, are of a different kind."

"Yes."

"And things of a different kind are other in kind?"

"Of course."

"And things other in kind are unlike?"

"They are."

"So if the others are unlike the One, then the One -- that which they're unlike -- must be unlike them."

"Clearly."

"Then the One has unlikeness, in respect of which the others are unlike it."

"It seems so."

"And if it has unlikeness to the others, mustn't it have likeness to itself?"

"How so?"

"If the One had unlikeness to the One, we wouldn't be talking about something with the nature of the One. Our hypothesis wouldn't be about the One at all -- it would be about something other than the One."

"Right."

"But that can't be."

"No."

"So the One must have likeness to itself."

"It must."

"Again: the One is not equal to the others. If it were equal, it would already exist and be like them by virtue of the equality. But if the One has no being, then it can neither exist nor be like."

"No, it can't."

"And since the One isn't equal to the others, the others can't be equal to it either."

"Certainly not."

"And things that are not equal are unequal?"

"Yes."

"And they're unequal to something unequal?"

"Yes."

"So the One participates in inequality -- and in respect of this inequality, the others are unequal to it."

"It does."

"And inequality implies Greatness and Smallness."

"It does."

"So the One, being of this nature, has both Greatness and Smallness."

"It appears so."

"And Greatness and Smallness always stand apart from each other."

"Yes."

"So there's always something between them."

"Yes."

"Can you think of anything between them other than Equality?"

"No -- it's Equality that lies between them."

"Then whatever has Greatness and Smallness also has Equality, which lies between them."

"That's clear."

"So the One-that-is-not, it seems, participates in Greatness, Smallness, and Equality."

"It seems so."

"And it must also, in some way, participate in being."

"How?"

"It must be the case that things are as we're saying. Because if they're not, then we're not speaking the truth when we say the One is not. But if we are speaking the truth, then clearly we're saying what is. Right?"

"Right."

"And since we affirm that we speak truly, we must be saying what is."

"We must."

"So the One-that-is-not has being -- in a sense. It has the being of not-being. If it were to let go of not-being, it would at once be. So it must hold onto the being of not-being in order to maintain its not-being."

"Exactly."

"Just as what-is must have the not-being of not-being in order to fully be, so what-is-not must have the being of not-being in order to fully not-be. The truest statement about being is that being participates in being -- not in the being of not-being. And the truest statement about not-being is that not-being participates in the being of not-being -- not in the not-being of not-being."

"Absolutely."

"So since what-is participates in not-being, and what-is-not participates in being, the One -- in order not to be -- must participate in being."

"It must."

"Then the One, if it is not, clearly has being."

"Clearly."

"And it has not-being too -- since it is not."

"Of course."

"Now, can something that's in one state pass to another state without changing?"

"Impossible."

"So everything that both is and is not in some state implies change."

"Yes."

"And change is motion."

"Yes."

"And the One was shown both to be and not to be."

"It was."

"So it both is and is not in the same state."

"Yes."

"Then the One-that-is-not has been shown to be in motion, since it changes from being to not-being."

"It seems so."

"But if it's nowhere among the things that are -- and it is nowhere, since it is not -- then it can't change from one place to another."

"Of course not."

"So it can't move by changing place."

"No."

"Nor can it spin in the same spot, because it nowhere touches 'the same.' 'The same' is something that is, and what is not can't be among the things that are."

"Right."

"So the One, if it is not, can't spin in that in which it is not."

"No."

"And the One, whether it is or is not, can't be altered into something other than itself. If it were altered and became different from itself, we'd no longer be talking about the One -- we'd be talking about something else."

"Right."

"But if it's not altered, and doesn't spin, and doesn't change place -- can it be in motion at all?"

"It seems not."

"And what's unmoved must be at rest. And what's at rest must stand still."

"It must."

"So the One-that-is-not both stands still and is in motion."

"It seems so."

"But if it's in motion, it must undergo alteration. Anything that moves, insofar as it moves, is no longer in the same state but in a different one."

"Right."

"So the One, being in motion, is altered."

"Yes."

"And if not in motion in any way, then not altered in any way."

"No."

"So insofar as the One-that-is-not is in motion, it's altered. But insofar as it's not in motion, it's not altered."

"Right."

"Then the One-that-is-not is both altered and not altered."

"That's clear."

"And mustn't what's altered become other than it previously was, and lose its former state -- which is destruction? And mustn't what's not altered neither come into being nor be destroyed?"

"Yes."

"So the One-that-is-not, being altered, both comes into being and is destroyed. And not being altered, it neither comes into being nor is destroyed. And so the One-that-is-not both comes into being and is destroyed -- and neither comes into being nor is destroyed."

"Right."


HYPOTHESIS II.b: IF THE ONE IS NOT (taken absolutely) -- What follows for the One

"Now let's go back to the beginning once more, and see whether the same conclusions follow or different ones."

"Let's do that."

"If the One is not, we're asking: what follows for the One?"

"Yes."

"When we say 'is not,' don't we mean a complete absence of being from whatever we apply those words to?"

"Completely."

"So when we say something 'is not,' we mean it is in no way and in no sense whatsoever. What is not has absolutely no participation in being."

"Absolutely."

"Then what is not can't be, and can't participate in being in any way."

"No."

"And didn't we define coming into being as the assumption of being, and destruction as the loss of being?"

"We did."

"Then something with no participation in being can neither assume nor lose it."

"No."

"So the One, since it in no way is, can't take on, lose, or have being in any way."

"Right."

"Then the One-that-is-not doesn't perish and doesn't come into being -- since it in no way participates in being."

"No."

"And it's not altered at all. If it were, it would be coming into being and being destroyed."

"True."

"And if it's not altered, it can't be in motion."

"It can't."

"And we can't say it stands still either, if it's nowhere. What stands still must always be in one and the same place."

"Of course."

"So the One-that-is-not never stands still and never moves."

"No."

"And nothing that exists can be attributed to it. If anything could, it would participate in being."

"That's clear."

"So neither Smallness, nor Greatness, nor Equality can be attributed to it."

"No."

"Nor Likeness, nor Difference -- either in relation to itself or to the others."

"Clearly not."

"And can the others be attributed to it? No -- because nothing at all can be attributed to it."

"Right."

"So the others can't be like it or unlike it, the same as it or different from it."

"They can't."

"And what is not can't be anything, or be this thing, or be related to this or that or another. It can't be past, present, or future. Knowledge, opinion, perception, language, a name -- nothing that exists has any connection with it."

"Nothing."

"Then the One-that-is-not has no condition of any kind."

"That seems to be the conclusion."


HYPOTHESIS II.aa: IF THE ONE IS NOT (taken as meaningful negation) -- What follows for the Others

"Once more: if the One is not, what happens to the others? Let's figure that out."

"Yes, let's."

"The others must exist in some way -- because if they didn't, we couldn't even be talking about them."

"True."

"And to speak of 'the others' implies difference. 'Other' and 'different' are synonymous."

"Yes."

"'Other' means other than something. 'Different' means different from something."

"Yes."

"So if there are others, there must be something they're other than."

"There must."

"And what could that be? Not the One -- since the One is not."

"Not the One."

"Then they're other than each other. That's the only remaining option -- unless they're other than nothing."

"Right."

"And they're each other than one another as groups or masses -- they can't be other as individual units, since the One is not. Rather, every mass of them is infinite in number. Even if you take what appears to be the smallest fraction, it instantly dissolves into many -- like something in a dream. What seemed one and smallest suddenly turns out to be vast, compared to the fragments it splits into."

"Right."

"And it's in such masses that the others are other than one another, if the others exist but the One does not."

"Exactly."

"And there will be many such masses, each appearing to be one but not actually being one -- since the One doesn't exist."

"Right."

"And it would seem that number can be applied to them -- since each one appears to be one, even though it's really many."

"Yes."

"And there will seem to be odd and even among them -- but none of this is real, if the One is not."

"No."

"And there will appear to be a smallest among them. But even this smallest appears large and manifold, compared to the many small fractions it contains."

"Right."

"And each mass will seem equal to the many small parts within it. It couldn't seem to pass from larger to smaller without appearing to pass through equality in between."

"That makes sense."

"And each mass will appear to have a limit in relation to the others and to itself -- even though it has neither beginning, middle, nor end."

"How?"

"Because whenever you think of any one of these as having a beginning, another beginning always appears before it. After every end, there's always another end remaining. And in every middle, there are truer middles within -- but smaller ones. You can't conceive of any of them as a unity, since the One doesn't exist."

"Right."

"So all being, whatever we think of, must shatter into fragments. Every particle has to be conceived without unity."

"Necessarily."

"And such being, when seen indistinctly and from a distance, appears to be one. But when seen up close and with a sharp mind, each individual thing appears to be infinite -- since it's deprived of the One, which doesn't exist."

"Nothing is more certain."

"Then each of the others must appear to be both infinite and finite, one and many -- if the One is not and the others exist."

"They must."

"And won't they appear to be like and unlike?"

"In what way?"

"Think of it like a painting. Things in a painting appear to be all one thing to someone standing at a distance -- they look like they're in the same condition, all alike."

"Yes."

"But when you get closer, they appear to be many and different. And because of the appearance of difference, they seem different in kind from one another -- unlike."

"Yes."

"So the masses must appear to be like and unlike themselves and each other."

"They must."

"And they'll appear to be the same and yet different from one another, in contact with themselves yet separated, having every kind of motion and every kind of rest, coming into being and being destroyed and in neither state. They'll have all such properties -- as we could easily spell out -- if the One is not and the many are."

"Very true."


HYPOTHESIS II.bb: IF THE ONE IS NOT (taken absolutely) -- What follows for the Others

"Now, one last time. Let's go back to the beginning and ask: if the One is not, and the others of the One are -- what follows?"

"Let's ask."

"First, the others will not be one."

"Obviously."

"And they won't be many either. Because if they were many, the One would be contained in them. And if none of them is one, they're all nothing -- and therefore not many."

"True."

"If there's no One in the others, the others are neither many nor one."

"They're not."

"And they don't even appear to be one or many."

"Why not?"

"Because the others have no connection of any kind with anything that is not. Nothing that is not can be associated with any of the others -- because what is not has no parts."

"True."

"So there's no appearance or impression of not-being in the others. Not-being is never in any way attributed to the others."

"No."

"Then if the One is not, none of the others can be conceived of as one or as many. Because you can't conceive of many without the One."

"You can't."

"So if the One is not, the others neither are nor can be conceived to be either one or many."

"It would seem not."

"Nor like or unlike."

"No."

"Nor the same or different. Not in contact or separated. Not in any of the states we just described as appearing to belong to them. The others neither are nor appear to be any of these things, if the One is not."

"True."

"Then may we not sum up the whole argument in a word and say truly: if the One is not, then nothing is?"

"We may indeed."

"Then let this much be said. And let us further affirm what seems to be the truth: whether the One is or is not, the One and the others, in relation to themselves and to one another -- all of them, in every way -- both are and are not, and both appear to be and appear not to be."

"Most true."
