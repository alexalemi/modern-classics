STATESMAN Part 1 — Translation Notes

OVERVIEW
This is the first half of the Statesman, a sequel to the Sophist. The Eleatic Stranger
continues to lead the discussion, now attempting to define the statesman using the
method of division. The respondent switches from Theaetetus to Young Socrates.
The dialogue covers: (1) the systematic division of knowledge to locate the
statesman, (2) the great myth of the reversed universe (Age of Cronus), (3) a
correction of the initial definition, and (4) the weaving analogy as a model for
understanding political art, plus a significant digression on the art of measurement.

KEY TRANSLATION DECISIONS

1. SPEAKER LABELS: "Stranger" maintained throughout for the Eleatic visitor,
   consistent with the Sophist translation. "Young Socrates" for the respondent,
   to clearly distinguish him from THE Socrates who appears briefly at the start.

2. THE METHOD OF DIVISION: The Stranger's systematic logical classifications
   ("divide here, then here, then here") can feel mechanical in Victorian prose.
   Modernized to feel more like a teacher walking a student through a thought
   process — conversational but rigorous. Preserved every step of the division
   chain: pure knowledge > directive > self-directing > living creatures > herds >
   walking > hornless > pure-bred > bipeds > featherless.

3. THE CRANE ANALOGY: One of Plato's most charming moments — imagining a crane
   that classifies "cranes" as one species and dumps humans in with everything else
   as "brutes." Made this vivid and pointed. It's a genuine insight about cognitive
   bias and arbitrary classification that feels remarkably modern.

4. THE MYTH OF THE REVERSED UNIVERSE (Age of Cronus): This is one of Plato's
   wildest mythological inventions. The cosmos periodically reverses direction;
   during the previous cycle, God directly shepherded humanity; people aged
   backward; the dead rose from the earth. Translated for maximum vividness
   while preserving the philosophical point: the myth distinguishes divine
   shepherding (Cronus era) from human governance (Zeus era), showing that
   our definition of "statesman" can't be modeled on the divine shepherd.

   Key choices in the myth:
   - "Pilot of the universe" for the divine figure who steers the cosmos
   - Made the aging-backward passage vivid and physical (white hair darkening,
     bearded cheeks growing smooth, etc.)
   - Preserved the philosophical crux: was the Age of Cronus actually happier?
     Only if people used their leisure for philosophy, not just eating and storytelling.
   - The decline after God lets go — forgetting, discord, evil growing — reads
     as a powerful metaphor for political entropy.

5. THE WEAVING ANALOGY: Extended and detailed. The Stranger walks through
   weaving as a model case of definition-by-division, to set up the political
   analogy in Part 2. Every step preserved: the separation of cooperative arts
   from causal arts, the distinction between tools and products, carding vs.
   weaving, warp vs. woof. Made the technical wool-working descriptions as
   clear as possible without losing accuracy.

6. THE ART OF MEASUREMENT DIGRESSION: An important philosophical passage
   distinguishing two kinds of measurement — relative (greater/lesser compared
   to each other) and normative (measured against a standard of due proportion).
   This is the theoretical foundation for the whole dialogue's methodology.
   Modernized the abstract language but preserved the argument's full structure.

7. META-COMMENTARY ON METHOD: The Stranger frequently pauses to discuss
   methodology — why they're taking the long route, why digressions are
   valuable, why examples matter. These passages can feel tedious in Victorian
   prose. Made them sound like a good teacher explaining why they're explaining,
   with enough energy to keep the reader engaged.

VOCABULARY CHOICES
- "Science" retained for episteme in contexts where "knowledge" is too vague
  and the systematic/expert quality matters
- "Art" for techne throughout
- "Herd management" and "collective care" for the various Greek terms for
  managing groups of animals
- "Due measure" / "due proportion" / "the right measure" for to metrion
- "Self-direction" for the king's distinctive kind of authority (giving one's
  own commands vs. relaying others')

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR PART 2
- Young Socrates is engaged and willing but occasionally rushed in his thinking;
  the Stranger gently corrects him
- The Stranger's tone is methodical and patient, with occasional humor (the
  crane joke, the "running alongside the bird-catcher" bit)
- The weaving analogy is set up here and will pay off in Part 2 when applied
  to politics
- The voluntary/compulsory distinction (king vs. tyrant) has been established
  and will be developed further
- The measurement digression sets up the principle that will govern the rest:
  not just comparison, but measuring against what's fitting and appropriate
