Stranger
But if the whole doesn't exist at all, then all the same difficulties remain -- plus a new one. Because besides having no being, being could never have come into being.

Theaetetus
Why not?

Stranger
Because whatever comes into being always comes into being as a whole. So anyone who doesn't count wholeness among the things that exist can't speak of either existence or coming-into-being at all.

Theaetetus
Yes, that certainly seems right.

Stranger
And here's another thing: how can something that isn't a whole have any specific quantity? Because whatever has a certain quantity must be the whole of that quantity.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
There will be countless other problems too, each generating endless headaches for anyone who says being is either one or two.

Theaetetus
The difficulties that are now coming into view prove that. Each objection links to the next, and they keep dragging everything that came before into greater and worse confusion.

Stranger
We're far from having exhausted the more rigorous thinkers who deal with being and not-being. But let's leave them and move on to those who speak less precisely. What we'll find, as a result of examining all of them, is that the nature of being is just as hard to grasp as that of not-being.

Theaetetus
Then let's go to the others.

Stranger
There seems to be a kind of War of Gods and Giants going on among them. They're fighting with each other about the nature of reality.

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
Some of them are dragging everything down from heaven and the unseen world to earth. They literally grasp rocks and trees in their hands. They hold onto these things and stubbornly insist that only what you can touch and handle has being or reality. They define being and body as one and the same. If anyone else says that something without a body exists, they utterly despise him and refuse to listen to anything but body.

Theaetetus
I've met men like that. They're a rough bunch.

Stranger
And that's exactly why their opponents defend themselves from above, from an unseen world. They argue fiercely that true reality consists of certain intelligible, incorporeal Forms. The bodies that the materialists claim are the ultimate truth, they break to pieces with their arguments and call them not real being, but just becoming -- a process of generation and motion. Between these two armies, Theaetetus, there's been an endless battle raging.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Let's ask each side in turn to give an account of what they mean by reality.

Theaetetus
How are we going to get it out of them?

Stranger
With the people who locate reality in the Forms, it'll be less difficult -- they're civilized enough. But getting an answer out of the ones who drag everything down to matter is going to be very hard -- maybe impossible. Here's what we should do.

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
If we could actually improve them, we would. But if that's impossible, let's at least imagine them as better and more reasonable than they are -- more willing to answer according to the rules of argument. After all, what better people agree to has more weight than what worse people agree to. And we're not interested in people -- we're seeking truth.

Theaetetus
Very good.

Stranger
Then let's address our improved materialists and ask them to state their views. You can interpret for them.

Theaetetus
Agreed.

Stranger
Would they admit that there's such a thing as a mortal animal?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And they'd acknowledge that a mortal animal is a body that has a soul?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Meaning the soul is something that exists?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And they'd say that one soul is just and another unjust, that one soul is wise and another foolish?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And that a soul becomes just and wise by possessing justice and wisdom, and the opposite by possessing their opposites?

Theaetetus
Yes, they'd say that.

Stranger
But surely they'd admit that anything which can be present or absent is something that exists?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
So they grant that justice, wisdom, the other virtues, and their opposites exist, and that a soul in which they reside exists. But would they say any of these things are visible and tangible, or are they all invisible?

Theaetetus
They'd say that hardly any of them are visible.

Stranger
And would they say they're corporeal -- that they have bodies?

Theaetetus
They wouldn't give a single answer to that. They'd say the soul does have a body. But as for the qualities themselves -- justice, wisdom, and the rest -- they wouldn't dare either deny that they exist or insist that they're all bodily things.

Stranger
Well, Theaetetus, I see genuine improvement in them! The real hard-liners -- the original earth-born types, children of the dragon's teeth -- wouldn't have shown any shame at all. They'd have stubbornly insisted that nothing exists that they can't squeeze in their hands.

Theaetetus
That's pretty much their attitude.

Stranger
Let's push the question further. If they'll admit that even the smallest fraction of being is incorporeal, that's enough. They then need to say what's the common nature shared by both corporeal and incorporeal things -- what they have in mind when they say both of them "are." They may be stuck. And if so, there's a possibility they'll accept a proposal of ours about the nature of being, since they don't have one of their own to offer.

Theaetetus
What's the proposal? Tell me, and we'll see.

Stranger
My proposal is this: anything whatsoever that has any capacity at all to affect something else, or to be affected by something else -- even for a single moment, even if the cause is trivial and the effect is slight -- that thing has real existence. I'm putting forward this definition: being is simply power.

Theaetetus
They accept your proposal, since they've got nothing better to offer.

Stranger
Good. Maybe we'll change our minds about this later -- and so might they. But for now, let's treat this as our working agreement with them.

Theaetetus
Agreed.

Stranger
Now let's turn to the Friends of the Forms. You'll interpret their views for us too.

Theaetetus
I will.

Stranger
We'll say to them: "You distinguish between true being and becoming, correct?"

Theaetetus
"Yes," they reply.

Stranger
"And you'd say that through the body and through perception we participate in becoming, but through the soul and through thought we participate in true being. And you'd say that being is always the same and unchanging, while becoming constantly varies?"

Theaetetus
Yes, that's what we'd say.

Stranger
"Well then, distinguished friends," we'll say, "what is this 'participation' you're talking about in both cases? Do you agree with the definition we just gave?"

Theaetetus
What definition?

Stranger
We said being is an active or passive capacity, arising from powers meeting one another. But, Theaetetus, maybe your ears won't catch their answer -- I know it because I'm used to hearing it.

Theaetetus
What do they say?

Stranger
They reject what we just told the materialists about existence.

Theaetetus
What did we tell them?

Stranger
That any capacity for acting or being acted upon, however slight, is a sufficient definition of being.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
They deny this. They say that the capacity for acting and being acted upon belongs to becoming, but that neither capacity applies to being.

Theaetetus
And isn't there some truth in what they say?

Stranger
Yes. But our reply will be that we need to learn more from them -- specifically, whether they also agree that the soul knows, and that being is known.

Theaetetus
They certainly say so.

Stranger
And is knowing an activity, or a passivity, or both? Or is one of them an activity and the other a passivity? Or does neither involve either acting or being acted upon?

Theaetetus
Clearly neither involves either -- otherwise they'd be contradicting themselves.

Stranger
I see your point. But here's what they'd have to accept: if knowing is active, then being known must be passive. And on that view, being -- insofar as it's known -- is acted upon by knowledge, and is therefore in motion. Because something at rest can't be acted upon.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And by heaven -- can we really be persuaded that motion, life, soul, and mind are absent from perfect being? Can we imagine that being just sits there, solemn and mindless, in awful frozen emptiness -- an everlasting fixture without thought?

Theaetetus
That would be a dreadful thing to accept, Stranger.

Stranger
But should we say being has mind and no life?

Theaetetus
How could that be?

Stranger
Or that it has both mind and life, but no soul to contain them?

Theaetetus
How else would it contain them?

Stranger
Or that it has mind, life, and soul, but -- though ensouled -- remains absolutely motionless?

Theaetetus
All three of those options seem irrational to me.

Stranger
Then we have to include motion, and things that move, within being.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
So, Theaetetus, our conclusion is this: if there's no motion, there's no mind anywhere, about anything, belonging to anyone.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
And yet this equally follows if we grant that everything is in constant motion -- on that view too, mind can't exist.

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
Do you think sameness -- of condition, manner, and subject -- could ever exist without some principle of stability?

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
And can you see how mind could exist, or ever come into existence, without stability?

Theaetetus
No.

Stranger
And surely we must fight with everything we have against anyone who would destroy knowledge, reason, and mind -- and yet still dares to speak confidently about anything.

Theaetetus
Yes, with all our might.

Stranger
Then the philosopher who truly reveres these things can't possibly accept the view of those who say everything is at rest -- whether as a unity or in many forms. And he'll be equally deaf to those who say everything is in constant motion. Like children begging, "Give us both!" the philosopher must include both motion and rest in his definition of being and reality.

Theaetetus
Absolutely true.

Stranger
Well then -- do we seem to have a good understanding of being now?

Theaetetus
Yes, I'd say we do.

Stranger
Oh no, Theaetetus. I think we're only just beginning to see the real difficulty of inquiring into being's nature.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
My friend, don't you see how deeply ignorant we still are, even though we feel like we're making progress?

Theaetetus
I certainly thought we were! And I have no idea how we ended up in this desperate position.

Stranger
Think about it. Now that we've made these admissions, can't we fairly be asked the same questions we were just asking the people who said everything is hot and cold?

Theaetetus
What questions? Remind me.

Stranger
I will -- by putting those same questions to you right now. That'll move us forward.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Wouldn't you say that rest and motion are completely opposed to each other?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And yet you'd say that both of them equally are -- that both exist?

Theaetetus
I would.

Stranger
And when you say both of them "are," do you mean both of them are in motion?

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
Or that they're both at rest?

Theaetetus
Of course not.

Stranger
Then you're conceiving of being as some third, distinct nature -- something under which both rest and motion are equally included. You're observing that they both participate in being, and on that basis declaring that they both exist.

Theaetetus
It does seem like we're glimpsing being as a third thing, when we say both rest and motion exist.

Stranger
So being isn't the combination of rest and motion. It's something different from both of them.

Theaetetus
So it would seem.

Stranger
Being, then, by its own nature, is neither in motion nor at rest.

Theaetetus
That does seem to be the truth.

Stranger
Then where's a person supposed to turn who wants to have any clear or settled idea of being?

Theaetetus
Where indeed?

Stranger
I'd say he can hardly turn anywhere. Because what isn't in motion must be at rest, and what isn't at rest must be in motion. But being has been placed outside both of these categories. Is that even possible?

Theaetetus
Utterly impossible.

Stranger
Here's something else we should keep in mind.

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
When we were asked what to attach the term "not-being" to, we were in the deepest confusion. Remember?

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And aren't we now in just as much confusion about being?

Theaetetus
If anything, Stranger, we're in even deeper trouble.

Stranger
Then let's acknowledge the difficulty. And since being and not-being are tangled up in the same confusion, there's hope: when one of them becomes clearer, the other will too. And if we can't make out either one, we may at least be able to steer between them without too much embarrassment.

Theaetetus
Good.

Stranger
Let's investigate how we come to apply many names to the same thing.

Theaetetus
Give me an example.

Stranger
Take a human being. We refer to the same person under many names -- we attribute colors, shapes, sizes, virtues, and vices to him. In all these cases, and ten thousand others, we don't just say he's a man, but also that he's good, and apply countless other attributes. The same goes for anything else: we start with something we treat as one thing, then describe it as many, under many names.

Theaetetus
That's true.

Stranger
And this gives beginners -- whether young or old -- a rich feast to argue about. There's nothing easier than insisting that the one can't be many, or the many one. They love denying that a man is good -- because, they say, man is man and good is good. You must have met people who are thrilled by these sorts of discoveries. They're often elderly folks whose limited intellects are blown away by such insights, which they think represent the height of wisdom.

Theaetetus
I certainly have.

Stranger
Then let's address our questions to these people too, along with everyone else who's ever speculated about being. Let's leave nobody out.

Theaetetus
What questions?

Stranger
Should we refuse to attach being to motion and rest, or anything to anything, treating everything as incapable of mixing with anything else? Or should we throw everything together into one class where everything connects with everything? Or should we say some things mix with some and others don't? Which of these will they prefer, Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
I can't answer for them. Why don't you take each option in turn and see what follows?

Stranger
Good idea. First, let's assume they say nothing can participate in anything else in any way. Then rest and motion can't participate in being at all.

Theaetetus
They can't.

Stranger
But would either of them exist if they don't participate in being?

Theaetetus
No.

Stranger
Well, that concession immediately demolishes everything: the doctrine that everything is in motion, the doctrine that everything is at rest, and the doctrine that being consists of unchanging, eternal Forms. Because all of these attach a notion of being -- claiming things "truly are" in motion, or "truly are" at rest.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
And the thinkers who sometimes combine things and sometimes separate them -- whether they create unity from plurality or infinity from unity, whether they produce finite elements and build compounds from them, whether they think creation happens in stages or continuously -- none of this talk makes any sense if there's no mixing at all.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And the most ridiculous of all would be the very people who insist on this no-mixing rule and yet won't let us call anything by another name because it participates in some quality from something else.

Theaetetus
Why's that?

Stranger
Because they're forced to use words like "is," "apart," "from others," "in itself" -- and ten thousand more. They can't give these up. These words are the connecting links of all speech. So they don't need anyone else to refute them. As the saying goes, the enemy lives inside their own house. They're constantly carrying an adversary around in their bellies, like that famous ventriloquist Eurycles, contradicting them out loud from within.

Theaetetus
That's exactly right -- a very accurate picture.

Stranger
Now, suppose we assume the opposite: that everything has the power to mix with everything.

Theaetetus
Even I can figure out what goes wrong there.

Stranger
How?

Theaetetus
Because motion itself would be at rest, and rest would be in motion -- if they could be attributed to each other.

Stranger
And that's utterly impossible.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
So only the third option remains.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Because one of these three must be the case: either everything mixes with everything, or nothing mixes with anything, or some things mix with some and others don't.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And the first two have been shown to be impossible.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
So anyone who wants to give a truthful answer will go with the third option: some things mix with some, and others don't.

Theaetetus
Exactly right.

Stranger
This selective mixing can be illustrated by letters. Some letters don't fit together, while others do.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And the vowels, especially, are a kind of bond running through all the other letters. Without a vowel, no consonant can be joined to another consonant.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
But does everyone know which letters combine with which? Or does that require a skill?

Theaetetus
It requires a skill.

Stranger
Which skill?

Theaetetus
Grammar.

Stranger
And isn't the same true of musical notes, high and low? The person who has the skill to know which sounds blend together is a musician, and the person who doesn't know isn't?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And we'll find this pattern holds everywhere -- in every case of having or lacking skill.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
So since we've agreed that categories of being -- like letters -- are sometimes capable and sometimes incapable of mixing, anyone who wants to correctly show which kinds combine and which don't must proceed through argument with the help of knowledge. And he'll need to ask whether there are universal connecting threads that run through everything, making mixing possible -- and again, in cases of division, whether there are other universal categories that make those divisions possible.

Theaetetus
He'll definitely need knowledge for that -- and if I'm not mistaken, the very greatest knowledge there is.

Stranger
And what should we call it? By Zeus -- have we stumbled upon the philosopher's own science without realizing it? Were we looking for the sophist and accidentally found the philosopher?

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
Shouldn't we say that the division according to classes -- which neither confuses what's different with what's the same, nor treats what's the same as if it were different -- is the business of the dialectical science?

Theaetetus
Yes, that's what we should say.

Stranger
Then the person who can do this rightly is able to perceive clearly: one Form pervading a scattered multitude, and many different Forms contained under one higher Form. And again: one Form knit together into a single whole but pervading many such wholes. And many Forms existing only in separation and isolation. This is the knowledge of classes -- knowing where they can combine with one another and where they can't.

Theaetetus
Exactly right.

Stranger
And you'd assign the art of dialectic only to the pure and genuine philosopher?

Theaetetus
Who else could be worthy of it?

Stranger
In this region we'll always find the philosopher, if we look for him. But like the sophist, he's not easy to see -- though for a different reason.

Theaetetus
What reason?

Stranger
The sophist runs away into the darkness of not-being, where he's learned to feel his way around by habit. He can't be found because of the darkness of the place.

Theaetetus
That seems right.

Stranger
But the philosopher, always conversing through reason with the Form of being, is also hard to see -- but because of excess of light. The souls of most people have no eyes that can endure the vision of the divine.

Theaetetus
Yes, that seems just as true as the other.

Stranger
We may come back to the philosopher later if we feel like it. But the sophist clearly can't be allowed to escape until we've gotten a good look at him.

Theaetetus
Agreed.

Stranger
Since we've established that some classes mix with each other and some don't, and that some mix with a few and others with many, and that there's no reason some shouldn't mix with everything -- let's pursue the inquiry as the argument suggests. We won't try to deal with all the Forms, or we'll get lost in the sheer number. Instead, let's select a few of the most important ones and examine their natures and their capacity for combining with each other. That way, even if we can't grasp being and not-being with perfect clarity, we may at least not fall short in our examination of them -- as far as this inquiry allows -- and perhaps we can assert the reality of not-being and escape unharmed.

Theaetetus
We must do that.

Stranger
The most important of all the categories are the ones we were just talking about: being, rest, and motion.

Theaetetus
By far.

Stranger
And we've said that two of them can't mix with each other.

Theaetetus
They absolutely can't.

Stranger
But being surely mixes with both, since both of them exist.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
So there are three.

Theaetetus
Right.

Stranger
And each of them is different from the other two, but the same as itself.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
But what do we actually mean by "same" and "different"? Are they two additional kinds beyond our three -- kinds that necessarily intermingle with the other three? If so, are we dealing with five kinds instead of three? Or when we say "same" and "different," are we unconsciously referring to one of the original three?

Theaetetus
We might be.

Stranger
But motion and rest are certainly neither "the same" nor "the different."

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
Whatever we apply to both motion and rest in common can't itself be either of them.

Theaetetus
Why not?

Stranger
Because motion would be at rest and rest would be in motion. Whichever one of them -- same or different -- gets attributed to both will force the other to change into its own opposite, since it would be participating in its opposite.

Theaetetus
Exactly right.

Stranger
And yet both motion and rest do partake of sameness and of difference?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
So we can't say that motion is the same, any more than we can say rest is the same, or that motion is the different.

Theaetetus
No, we can't.

Stranger
But what about being and the same -- could they be identical?

Theaetetus
Maybe.

Stranger
But if they were, then by saying motion and rest "have being," we'd also be saying they're "the same." And that clearly can't be right.

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
So being and the same can't be one thing.

Theaetetus
Hardly.

Stranger
Then we should treat "the same" as a fourth category, to be added to the other three.

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Stranger
And should we call "the different" a fifth? Or should we consider "being" and "the different" to be two names for one category?

Theaetetus
That's possible.

Stranger
But you'd agree that some things that exist are what they are in themselves, while others are always in relation to other things?

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
And "the different" is always relative -- always in relation to something else?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
But that wouldn't be the case if being and the different were the same thing. Because if "the different" were absolute as well as relative -- like being -- then there would be some instance of "the different" that isn't different from anything. But in fact, we find that whatever is different is necessarily what it is in relation to some other thing.

Theaetetus
That's exactly right.

Stranger
Then we have to accept "the different" as our fifth category among the ones we've selected.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And this fifth category pervades all the others. Because each of them is different from the rest -- not because of its own nature, but because it participates in the nature of "the different."

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
Now let's work through each of the five, one at a time.

Theaetetus
How?

Stranger
First, take motion. We affirm that it's absolutely different from rest. What else can we say?

Theaetetus
It is.

Stranger
And therefore it is not rest.

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
And yet it exists -- because it partakes of being.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And motion is different from the same?

Theaetetus
Basically, yes.

Stranger
So it's not the same.

Theaetetus
It's not.

Stranger
And yet motion is the same -- because everything partakes of the same.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
So we have to accept this and not object: motion is the same and is not the same. When we say these two things, we don't mean them in the same way. We call it "the same" in relation to itself, because it partakes of the same. And we call it "not the same" because, through its connection with "the different," it gets separated from the same -- it becomes not that, but other. So it's correctly called "not the same."

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And if motion itself somehow partook of rest, there'd be nothing wrong with calling motion "stationary."

Theaetetus
Right -- assuming some categories mix with each other and others don't.

Stranger
And we already proved that this kind of mixing among categories is natural, before we got to this part of the discussion.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Let's continue. Can we say motion is different from "the different"? After all, we've already shown it's different from "the same" and different from rest.

Theaetetus
That's certain.

Stranger
So motion is different and also not different?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
What's next? Should we say motion is different from three of the five but not the fourth? Because we agreed there are five categories we're examining.

Theaetetus
We surely can't reduce the number below what we found just now.

Stranger
Then we can assert without fear that motion is different from being?

Theaetetus
Without the slightest fear.

Stranger
So the clear result is this: motion, since it partakes of being, really is -- and also is not.

Theaetetus
Nothing could be plainer.

Stranger
Then not-being necessarily exists in the case of motion, and in every category. Because the nature of "the different" enters into all of them, making each of them different from being and therefore, in that sense, nonexistent. So we can truly say of all of them that they are not. And equally, because they partake of being, they are and they exist.

Theaetetus
That seems right.

Stranger
Every category, then, has plenty of being and an unlimited amount of not-being.

Theaetetus
So it seems.

Stranger
And being itself can be said to be different from the other categories.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Then we can conclude that being is not, insofar as there are other things. Because since it's not those other things -- which are unlimited in number -- it is itself one thing, and is not all the rest.

Theaetetus
That's close to the truth.

Stranger
We shouldn't quarrel with this result. It's in the nature of categories to mix with one another. And if anyone denies what we're saying now -- that being, in a sense, is not -- let him first refute our earlier conclusion about how categories interact, and then he can argue with what follows.

Theaetetus
Nothing could be fairer.

Stranger
Let me ask you to consider one more thing.

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
When we speak of not-being, we're not, I think, talking about something opposed to being. We're only talking about something different.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
When we say something is "not great," does that seem to imply "small" any more than it implies "equal"?

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
So when we use negative words -- "not" this or "non-" that -- they don't imply the opposite of the word that follows. They only imply something different from it, or more precisely, something different from the things those words represent.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
There's another point to consider, if you don't mind.

Theaetetus
What is it?

Stranger
The nature of "the different" seems to me to be divided into parts -- just like knowledge.

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
Knowledge is one thing, and yet its various branches each have their own particular name. That's why there are many arts and kinds of knowledge.

Theaetetus
Quite true.

Stranger
And isn't the same true of the parts of "the different," which is also one thing?

Theaetetus
Probably. But how?

Stranger
There's some part of "the different" that's set against the beautiful?

Theaetetus
There is.

Stranger
Does it have a name, or not?

Theaetetus
It does. Whatever we call "not-beautiful" is what's different from the beautiful -- not different from something else.

Stranger
Now tell me one more thing.

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
The not-beautiful -- isn't it simply a portion of existence that's been marked off from a certain kind of existence and then set against another existing thing?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
So the not-beautiful turns out to be a case of one being set against another being?

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
Then on this view, is the beautiful more real than the not-beautiful? Is the not-beautiful less real?

Theaetetus
Not at all.

Stranger
And "the not-great" exists just as fully as "the great"?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And in the same way, "the just" has to be put in the same class as "the not-just" -- neither one has any more existence than the other.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
The same applies to everything else. Since the nature of "the different" has real existence, its parts must be considered equally real.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
Then it seems that the contrast between a part of "the different" and a part of being is, if I may put it this way, just as real as being itself. It doesn't imply the opposite of being -- only what is different from being.

Theaetetus
Beyond question.

Stranger
Then what should we call it?

Theaetetus
Obviously, not-being. And this is exactly the nature of not-being that the sophist forced us to search for.

Stranger
And doesn't it have just as real an existence as any other category, as you were saying? Can I say with confidence that not-being has a secure existence and a nature of its own? Just as the great was found to be great and the beautiful beautiful, and the not-great not-great and the not-beautiful not-beautiful -- in the same way, not-being has been found to be and is not-being. It should be counted as one among the many categories of being. Do you still have any doubt about this, Theaetetus?

Theaetetus
None whatsoever.

Stranger
Do you see that our investigation has carried us beyond the limits Parmenides set?

Theaetetus
In what way?

Stranger
We've pushed further than he forbade us to go, and we've shown him more than he ever wanted investigated.

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
Because he says:

"Not-being never is, and do thou keep thy thoughts from this way of inquiry."

Theaetetus
Yes, that's what he says.

Stranger
But we've not only proved that things which are not, are -- we've shown what Form not-being actually takes. We've shown that the nature of "the different" exists and is distributed across all things in their relations to one another. And whatever part of "the different" is contrasted with being -- that is precisely what we've dared to call not-being.

Theaetetus
And I think we were absolutely right, Stranger.

Stranger
Then let no one say that we're asserting some sort of opposite of being and then claiming that opposite exists. We said goodbye to the question of whether being has an opposite long ago -- it may or may not, and it may or may not be definable. But as for our account of not-being: let someone either prove us wrong, or, as long as he can't, let him say what we're saying -- that categories mix with one another; that being and difference pervade everything and interpenetrate each other; that "the different" partakes of being and therefore is, yet is not the same as what it partakes of, but other; and being other than being, it's clearly and necessarily not-being.

And being itself, through partaking of "the different," becomes a category different from the rest. Being different from all of them, it is not each one of them, and is not all the rest. So undoubtedly, there are thousands upon thousands of cases in which being is not. And all other things, whether considered individually or collectively, in many respects are and in many respects are not.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And if anyone is skeptical about this seeming contradiction, he needs to come up with something better. Or if his idea of fun is dragging words this way and that, our argument proves he's not making worthy use of his talents. There's no charm in such puzzles, and there's no difficulty in spotting them. But we can point him toward something else -- something that's noble and also difficult.

Theaetetus
What's that?

Stranger
Something I've already mentioned. Forget these word games -- they're not hard. Instead, a person should be able to follow and critique every argument in detail. When someone says that "the same" is in some way "different," or that "the different" is in some way "the same," he should be able to understand and respond from the speaker's own point of view, in the very respect in which the claim is being made. But to show that somehow the same is different, the different is same, the great is small, or the like is unlike -- and to take delight in endlessly trotting out such contradictions -- that's no real refutation. It's the work of someone who's just beginning to grapple with the problem of being.

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Stranger
Because, my friend, trying to separate everything from everything else is crude and utterly unworthy of an educated or philosophical mind.

Theaetetus
Why?

Stranger
Total separation is the complete destruction of all reasoning. We can only achieve rational discourse through the weaving together of concepts with one another.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And notice that we were just in time in resisting those separatists and forcing them to admit that things do mix with one another.

Theaetetus
Why was the timing so important?

Stranger
So that we could establish discourse itself as a genuine kind of being. If we couldn't, the worst possible consequence would follow: we'd lose philosophy entirely. And the need to determine the nature of discourse is pressing right now. If we were completely deprived of it, we couldn't hold discourse at all. And we'd be deprived of it if we admitted that there's no mixing of categories whatsoever.

Theaetetus
That's true. But I don't see why we need to determine the nature of discourse right at this moment.

Stranger
Maybe this will help you see.

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
We've established that not-being is one category among many, distributed across all being.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And now the question is: does not-being mix with opinion and speech?

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
If not-being plays no part in statements, then everything we say must be true. But if not-being does play a part, then false opinion and false speech become possible. Because to think or say what is not -- that's what falsehood is. Falsehood arises in the domain of thought and speech.

Theaetetus
Exactly right.

Stranger
And where there's falsehood, there must be deception.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And if there's deception, then the world must be full of images, likenesses, and illusions.

Theaetetus
Of course.

Stranger
And we said the sophist had escaped into that territory and was denying the very possibility of falsehood. Nobody, he argued, either thinks or utters falsehood, since not-being has no part in being.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
But now we've shown that not-being does partake of being. So the sophist won't be able to hold that line anymore. But he'll probably shift his ground and say that some Forms partake of not-being and some don't, and that speech and opinion are among the ones that don't. And he'll keep fighting to the death against the image-making and appearance-making art -- the one we've placed him in -- because, he'll argue, if opinion and speech don't partake of not-being, there can be no such thing as falsehood.

So to counter this evasion, we need to start by investigating the nature of speech, opinion, and imagination. We need to find them, show they have a connection with not-being, and by proving that connection, prove that falsehood exists. Then we'll imprison the sophist there, if he deserves it -- or if not, we'll let him go and look for him in another category.

Theaetetus
What you said about the sophist at the beginning is certainly proving true, Stranger -- he's not easily caught. He seems to have an endless supply of defenses, and every single one has to be broken through before we can reach the man himself. We just barely got through his first defense -- the claim that not-being doesn't exist -- and now here's another: we still have to prove that falsehood exists in speech and opinion. And after that, there'll be another, and another, with no end in sight.

Stranger
Anyone who can make even a little progress should take heart, Theaetetus. What would someone who's discouraged by small gains do if he were making no progress at all -- or even losing ground? A faint heart, as the proverb says, will never take a city. But now that we've come this far, the citadel is ours. What remains is easier.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
Then, as I was saying, let's first work out a clear idea of speech and opinion. That will give us a solid basis for determining whether not-being has anything to do with them -- or whether they're always true and never false.

Theaetetus
Right.

Stranger
Now then -- let's talk about words, the way we were talking earlier about Forms and letters. That's the direction the answer lies.

Theaetetus
And what's the question about words?

Stranger
Whether all words can be connected with each other, or none, or only some.

Theaetetus
Clearly the last one.

Stranger
I think you mean that words which form a meaningful sequence can be connected, but words that have no meaning in sequence can't?

Theaetetus
What exactly are you saying?

Stranger
What I thought you were implying when you agreed. There are two basic kinds of vocal signs that indicate being.

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
One kind is called nouns, the other verbs.

Theaetetus
Describe them.

Stranger
The sign that indicates an action we call a verb.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And the sign that identifies the one performing the action we call a noun.

Theaetetus
Right.

Stranger
Now, a string of nouns by itself doesn't make a sentence. Neither does a string of verbs by itself.

Theaetetus
I don't follow.

Stranger
I see. When you agreed just now, you had something different in mind. But my point is simply that a bare sequence of nouns or verbs isn't discourse.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
I mean that words like "walks," "runs," "sleeps" -- or any other words indicating actions -- no matter how many you string together, they don't make a sentence.

Theaetetus
How could they?

Stranger
And again, when you say "lion," "stag," "horse," or any other words indicating agents -- you don't get a sentence this way either. In neither case do the sounds express any action or inaction, or the existence or nonexistence of anything. Not until verbs are combined with nouns. Then the words fit together, and the smallest such combination produces speech -- the simplest and shortest form of discourse.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
When someone says "A man learns" -- would you call that the simplest and shortest sentence?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
Because now he's conveying something about something that is, or is becoming, or has become, or will be. He's not just naming -- he's doing something by connecting a verb with a noun. That's why we say he's engaging in discourse, and we call this connection of words a statement.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And just as some things fit together and others don't, some vocal signs combine to form discourse and others don't.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
There's one more small point.

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
A sentence has to have a subject. It can't help it.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And it has to have a certain quality -- a character.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Now let's pay close attention to what we're doing.

Theaetetus
We must.

Stranger
I'll give you a sentence that combines a subject and an action, using a noun and a verb. You tell me who the sentence is about.

Theaetetus
I'll do my best.

Stranger
"Theaetetus sits." Not a very long sentence.

Theaetetus
Not very.

Stranger
Now tell me: who does the sentence speak about? Who's the subject?

Theaetetus
Me. I'm the subject.

Stranger
And how about this sentence --

Theaetetus
What sentence?

Stranger
"Theaetetus, with whom I'm now speaking, is flying."

Theaetetus
That's also a sentence everyone would agree speaks about me and applies to me.

Stranger
And we agreed that every sentence must have a certain quality.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
So what's the quality of each of these two sentences?

Theaetetus
I'd say one is false and the other is true.

Stranger
And the true one says what's true about you?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And the false one says what's different from the truth?

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
It speaks of things that are not as though they were?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
It says things are real about you that are not real. Because, as we were saying, for every person and thing, there's much that is and much that is not.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
Now, the second sentence about you was first of all an example of the shortest form of sentence we defined.

Theaetetus
Yes, we agreed on that.

Stranger
And second, it was about a subject.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
A subject that has to be you and can't be anyone else.

Theaetetus
Unquestionably.

Stranger
And it wouldn't be a sentence at all if it had no subject. We proved that a sentence without a subject is impossible.

Theaetetus
Right.

Stranger
So when "the different" is asserted of you as if it were "the same" -- when not-being is asserted as being -- that kind of combination of nouns and verbs is really and truly false discourse.

Theaetetus
Absolutely true.

Stranger
And from this it follows that thought, opinion, and imagination exist in our minds as both true and false.

Theaetetus
How so?

Stranger
You'll understand better if you first grasp what each of them is and how they differ from one another.

Theaetetus
Then explain it to me.

Stranger
Aren't thought and speech the same thing, with one difference: what we call thought is the soul's silent, internal conversation with itself?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
But when that stream of thought flows out through the lips and becomes audible, it's called speech?

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And we know that within speech there exists --

Theaetetus
What?

Stranger
Affirmation and denial.

Theaetetus
Yes, we know that.

Stranger
And when affirmation or denial happens silently, in the mind alone -- what do you call it besides opinion?

Theaetetus
That's the only name for it.

Stranger
And when opinion arises not on its own but through some form of perception -- would you call that anything other than imagination?

Theaetetus
Certainly not.

Stranger
So: since speech can be true or false, and since thought is the soul's conversation with itself, and opinion is the conclusion of thinking, and imagination is the fusion of perception with opinion -- it follows that some of these, being akin to speech, must contain an element of falsehood as well as truth.

Theaetetus
Certainly.

Stranger
Do you realize, then, that we've discovered false opinion and false speech sooner than we expected? Just a moment ago, it looked like we were taking on a task that would never be finished.

Theaetetus
I do realize it.

Stranger
Then let's not be discouraged about what's ahead. Now that we've made this breakthrough, let's go back to our earlier classification.

Theaetetus
Which classification?

Stranger
We divided image-making into two kinds: likeness-making and appearance-making -- the fantastic art.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And we said we weren't sure which one the sophist belonged to.

Theaetetus
We did.

Stranger
And our heads started spinning when it was claimed that there's no such thing as an image, a likeness, or an appearance, because falsehood can't exist in any way, at any time, in any place.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
But now we've shown that false speech and false opinion exist. And that means there can be imitations of real things, and from that condition of the mind an art of deception can arise.

Theaetetus
That's certainly possible now.

Stranger
And we already established that the sophist was lurking in one of the two divisions of image-making.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
Then let's make a fresh attempt. When we divide any class, let's always take the part on the right -- holding on to whatever contains the sophist -- until we've stripped away every general property he shares with others and exposed his specific difference, his unique nature. Then we can display him in his true form, first to ourselves, then to those with a kindred philosophical spirit.

Theaetetus
Very good.

Stranger
You may recall that all skill was originally divided into creative and acquisitive.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And the sophist kept showing up in the acquisitive class -- under hunting, competition, commerce, and so on.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
But now that the imitative art has enclosed him, it's clear we need to start by dividing the art of creation. Because imitation is a kind of creation -- the creation of images, not real things.

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
First, there are two kinds of creation.

Theaetetus
What are they?

Stranger
One is human, the other divine.

Theaetetus
I don't follow.

Stranger
Remember, we originally defined creative power as anything that brings into existence something that didn't exist before.

Theaetetus
I remember.

Stranger
Now look at the world -- all the animals and plants, the things that grow from seeds and roots, the inanimate substances formed within the earth, both those that melt and those that don't. Should we say these come into existence -- having not existed before -- through the creation of God? Or should we go along with the common opinion?

Theaetetus
Which opinion?

Stranger
That nature produces them from some spontaneous, mindless cause. Or should we say they're created by divine reason and knowledge that comes from God?

Theaetetus
I admit I probably waver on this because of my age. But when I look at you and see you're inclined to credit them to God, I'll defer to your judgment.

Stranger
Well said, Theaetetus. And if I thought you were the kind of person who'd later change his mind, I'd have argued with you gently and made you agree. But since I can see you'll arrive at that belief on your own -- without any argument from me -- toward the view that already attracts you, I won't try to rush what time will accomplish.

So let's suppose that things produced by nature are the work of divine craft, and things made by humans from natural materials are works of human craft. There are two kinds of making: human and divine.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Now subdivide each of those two sections once more.

Theaetetus
How do you mean?

Stranger
Just as we made a horizontal division of production into divine and human, now make a vertical one.

Theaetetus
Done.

Stranger
So there are four parts in all. Two relate to us and are human. Two relate to the gods and are divine.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And taking the other division: within each half, one part is the making of real things, while the other two parts are the making of likenesses or images. So creative skill is again divided into two.

Theaetetus
Walk me through the divisions again.

Stranger
We, other animals, and the elements from which things are made -- fire, water, and the like -- are all understood to be the creation and work of God.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
And then there are images of them -- things that are not the originals but correspond to them. These too are the creation of a wonderful skill.

Theaetetus
What kind of images?

Stranger
The appearances that arise spontaneously -- in sleep or by day. Things like shadows, which appear when darkness falls across a source of light. Or reflections, which are produced when light from a bright, smooth surface meets an external light and creates a perception that reverses our ordinary sight.

Theaetetus
Yes -- those images are just as much the work of a divine hand as the originals are.

Stranger
And what about human creation? Don't we build one house with the art of construction, and another with the art of drawing -- a sort of dream created by humans for people who are awake?

Theaetetus
Exactly.

Stranger
And all other products of human creation come in pairs the same way: the real thing, made by the art of making the thing itself, and the image, made by the art of imitation.

Theaetetus
Now I understand. And I'm ready to acknowledge that there are two kinds of production, each of them twofold. In the horizontal division, there's divine and human production. In the vertical division, there are real things and images of them.

Stranger
And let's not forget: within the imitative class, one part was supposed to be likeness-making and the other appearance-making -- if it could be shown that falsehood actually exists and belongs to the class of real things.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
And that has been shown. So now, without hesitation, we can count the two kinds as distinct.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Then let's divide the art of appearance-making once more.

Theaetetus
Where do we make the cut?

Stranger
There's one kind where the appearance is produced using a tool or instrument, and another where the creator of the appearance is himself the instrument.

Theaetetus
What do you mean?

Stranger
When someone makes himself look or sound like another person, that part of the appearance-making art is called mimicry.

Theaetetus
Yes.

Stranger
Let's call this branch the art of mimicry and assign it its territory. As for the other branch -- using tools -- we're tired, so let's leave it to someone else to classify and name.

Theaetetus
Fair enough. Let's give one its territory and leave the other.

Stranger
But there's one more distinction within mimicry, Theaetetus, that's worth our attention. I'll tell you why.

Theaetetus
Go ahead.

Stranger
Some imitators know what they're imitating. Others don't. And what greater line of distinction could there possibly be than the one between ignorance and knowledge?

Theaetetus
None.

Stranger
Now, the kind of imitation we were just talking about -- wasn't it the imitation of those who know? Someone who wants to imitate you would surely have to know you and what you look like.

Theaetetus
Naturally.

Stranger
But what about the Form of justice, or virtue in general? Aren't we well aware that many people, who have no real knowledge of these things but only a sort of opinion, do their best to make it seem like they genuinely hold these opinions -- expressing them as convincingly as they can in word and deed?

Theaetetus
Yes, that's very common.

Stranger
And do they always fail at appearing just when they're not? Or is the opposite true?

Theaetetus
The opposite. They often succeed.

Stranger
Then this kind of person should be called an imitator too -- but distinguished from the other kind, the way the ignorant are distinguished from the knowledgeable.

Theaetetus
True.

Stranger
Can we find a good name for each? This clearly isn't easy. The ancients had a certain confusion about dividing categories into species, so there aren't many names ready to hand. But for the sake of clarity, I'll be bold. Let's call imitation that coexists with mere opinion the imitation of appearances. And imitation that coexists with knowledge we'll call learned or informed imitation.

Theaetetus
Granted.

Stranger
The first kind is what we care about right now. Because the sophist was classified among imitators, but not among those with knowledge.

Theaetetus
Very true.

Stranger
Then let's examine our imitator-of-appearances and see if he rings solid like good iron, or if there's still a crack in him.

Theaetetus
Let's check.

Stranger
There's quite a substantial crack. Look: within the two classes of imitators, one is a simple creature who genuinely believes he knows what he only thinks he knows. The other type has been knocked around among arguments long enough to suspect and fear that he's actually ignorant of what he pretends to know in front of others.

Theaetetus
There are definitely both kinds, just as you describe.

Stranger
Should we call the first the sincere imitator, and the second the ironic or dissembling imitator?

Theaetetus
That sounds right.

Stranger
And should we say the dissembling type has one division or two?

Theaetetus
You tell me.

Stranger
On reflection, I see two. There's the dissembler who delivers long speeches to crowds in public. And there's the dissembler who works in private, using short questions and answers to force the person he's talking with into contradicting himself.

Theaetetus
That's absolutely right.

Stranger
And who's the one who makes the long speeches? The statesman, or the popular orator?

Theaetetus
The popular orator.

Stranger
And what should we call the other one? Is he the philosopher or the sophist?

Theaetetus
He can't be the philosopher -- we've established that he's ignorant. But since he imitates the wise, he'll have a name derived from "wise" -- sophos. What should we call him? I'm pretty confident I can't be wrong in calling him the true and genuine sophist.

Stranger
Then shall we tie up his name the way we did before -- tracing a chain from one end of his genealogy to the other?

Theaetetus
Absolutely.

Stranger
Here, then, is the pedigree of his art: he belongs to the conscious, dissembling section of the art of producing self-contradiction. He's an imitator of appearances -- separated out from the fantastic branch, which itself is a branch of image-making, which is part of creation. And his creation is human, not divine -- the juggling of words. Anyone who says the real sophist is of this blood and lineage will be speaking the absolute truth.

Theaetetus
Without a doubt.
