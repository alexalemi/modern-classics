CRATYLUS (Part 2)

Socrates
And yet, if you're allowed to add and remove whatever letters you want, names would be far too easy to make, and any name could be adapted to fit any object.

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
Yes, it is. So a wise dictator like yourself should observe the laws of moderation and probability.

Hermogenes
That's my goal.

Socrates
Mine too, Hermogenes. But don't be too much of a stickler, or "you'll unnerve me of my strength." Once you let me add "mechane" (contrivance) to "techne" (art), I'll be at the top of my game. I take "mechane" to be a sign of great accomplishment ("anein" -- to accomplish), since "mekos" means greatness, and "mekos" plus "anein" make up "mechane." (In the Greek, "mechane" is built from "megas" -- great -- and "anein" -- to accomplish.)

But as I was saying, now that I'm at my peak, I'd like to examine two key words: "arete" (virtue) and "kakia" (vice). I don't fully understand "arete" yet, but "kakia" is transparent and fits with everything we've been saying. Since all things are in flux ("ionton"), "kakia" means "kakos ion" -- "going badly." When this bad motion exists in the soul, it gets the general name "kakia" -- vice. (In the Greek, "kakia" comes from "kakos ienai" -- to go badly.)

The meaning of "kakos ienai" is further illustrated by "deilia" (cowardice), which should have come after "andreia" (courage) but got skipped. "Deilia" means the soul is bound by a strong chain ("desmos"), since "lian" means "strength." So "deilia" expresses the greatest and strongest bond on the soul. And "aporia" (difficulty, perplexity) is a related concept -- from "a" (not) and "poreuesthai" (to go) -- like anything that impedes motion and movement.

So "kakia" means going badly, limping and halting, which fills the soul with vice. And if "kakia" is the name for that sort of thing, "arete" (virtue) will be its opposite: first, ease of motion; second, the stream of the good soul flowing unimpeded, without obstruction. It's called "arete" -- or more correctly, "aeirheite" ("ever-flowing") -- and may at some point have had the form "hairete" ("eligible"), suggesting that nothing is more worth choosing than virtue. And this has been hammered into "arete." (In the Greek, "arete" can be derived from "aei rheite" -- ever-flowing -- or "hairete" -- worth choosing.)

I'm sure you'll think this is another one of my inventions. But if the previous derivation of "kakia" was right, then "arete" should be right too.

Hermogenes
But what about "kakon" (bad, evil), which played such a big role in your earlier discussion?

Socrates
That's a very strange word, and I can barely form an opinion about it. So I'll have to resort to my usual trick.

Hermogenes
What trick?

Socrates
Declaring it foreign. I'll give that word foreign origin status too.

Hermogenes
Fair enough. But suppose we leave those and try to work out the logic of "kalon" (beautiful) and "aischron" (ugly/shameful).

Socrates
The meaning of "aischron" is clear: it's "aei ischon rhoes" -- "always preventing flow." This matches everything we've been saying. The original name-giver was a fierce enemy of stagnation, so he gave the name "aeischoroun" to whatever hindered the flow. That's been compressed into "aischron." (In the Greek, "aischron" comes from "aei ischein rhoun" -- always holding back the stream.)

Hermogenes
But what do you say about "kalon" (beautiful)?

Socrates
That's more obscure, though the form has only been changed by converting "ou" into "o."

Hermogenes
What do you mean?

Socrates
This name seems to denote mind.

Hermogenes
How so?

Socrates
Let me ask you: what is the cause of anything having a name? Isn't the principle that assigns the name the cause?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
And that principle must be mind -- whether the mind of gods, humans, or both?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
And isn't mind what "called" ("kalesan") things by their names? And isn't mind the beautiful ("kalon")? (In the Greek, "kalon" -- beautiful -- is connected to "kalein" -- to call or name.)

Hermogenes
That's evident.

Socrates
And aren't the works of intelligence and mind worthy of praise, while other works deserve blame?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
Medicine does a physician's work, carpentry does a carpenter's work?

Hermogenes
Exactly.

Socrates
And the principle of beauty does the works of beauty?

Hermogenes
Of course.

Socrates
And we said that principle is mind?

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
Then mind is rightly called "beauty" because it does the works we recognize and celebrate as beautiful?

Hermogenes
That's evident.

Socrates
What other names are left?

Hermogenes
The words connected with "agathon" (good) and "kalon" (beautiful) -- like "sympheron" (expedient), "lysiteloun" (profitable), "ophelimon" (advantageous), "kerdaleon" (gainful), and their opposites.

Socrates
You can probably figure out "sympheron" (expedient) yourself from the previous examples. It's a sister word to "episteme" -- meaning the motion ("phora") of the soul accompanying the world. Things done on this principle are called "symphora" or "sympheronta" because they're carried along with the world. (In the Greek, "sympheron" comes from "sym-" -- together -- and "phora" -- motion.)

Hermogenes
That's probable.

Socrates
And "kerdaleon" (gainful) comes from "kerdos" (gain), but you have to change the delta to a nu to get at the meaning. This word also signifies good, but in a different way: the name-giver intended to express the power of mixing ("kerannumenon") and universal penetration in the good. But he inserted a delta instead of a nu and made "kerdos." (In the Greek, "kerdos" is connected to "kerannumenon" -- mixing.)

Hermogenes
Well, but what about "lysiteloun" (profitable)?

Socrates
I don't think people mean by "profitable" what pays ("lyei") the shopkeeper. They use it to mean "swift." You should think of the profitable as that which, being the swiftest thing in existence, allows no pause or standstill but always, whenever things start to stop, releases ("lyei") them again, making motion immortal and unceasing. In this sense, the good is rightly called "lysiteloun" -- "that which loosens ("lyon") the end ("telos") of motion." (In the Greek, "lysiteloun" comes from "lyein" -- to loosen -- and "telos" -- end.)

"Ophelimon" (advantageous) comes from "ophellein" -- to increase, to make grow. That's a common Homeric word and has a foreign flavor.

Hermogenes
And what about their opposites?

Socrates
The ones that are simple negatives hardly need discussion.

Hermogenes
Which ones?

Socrates
"Asympheron" (inexpedient), "anopheles" (unprofitable), "alysiteles" (unadvantageous), "akerdes" (ungainful).

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
I'd rather look at "blaberon" (harmful) and "zemiodes" (hurtful).

Hermogenes
Good.

Socrates
"Blaberon" (harmful) is what hinders or damages ("blaptein") the flow ("rhoun"). "Blapton" really means "boulomenon haptein" -- "wanting to hold or bind." And "haptein" (to bind) is the same as "dein" (to fasten), which is always a term of criticism. So "wanting to bind the flow" ("boulomenon haptein rhoun") would properly be "boulapteroun," which has been improved into "blaberon." (In the Greek, "blaberon" is compressed from "boulomenon haptein rhoun" -- wanting to bind the stream.)

Hermogenes
You produce the most curious results, Socrates! When I hear a word like "boulapteroun," I can't help imagining you're making your mouth into a flute and puffing away at some prelude to Athena.

Socrates
That's the fault of the name-makers, Hermogenes, not mine.

Hermogenes
Very true. But what's the derivation of "zemiodes" (hurtful)?

Socrates
What does "zemiodes" mean? Let me point out, Hermogenes, how right I was to say that great changes in meaning come from adding and removing letters. Even a very slight change can sometimes flip the meaning entirely. Take the word "deon" (obligation), which just occurred to me. It reminds me of something I was going to tell you: our fashionable modern language has twisted and disguised and completely altered the original meanings of both "deon" and "zemiodes," even though in the old language the meaning was clear.

Hermogenes
What do you mean?

Socrates
I'll explain. Our ancestors loved the sounds of iota and delta, especially the women, who are the most conservative with language. But now people change iota to eta or epsilon, and delta to zeta -- supposedly to sound grander.

Hermogenes
How do you mean?

Socrates
For example, in very ancient times they called the day either "himera" or "hemera." We call it "hemera."

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
Only the ancient form reveals the name-giver's intention. People longed for ("himeirousi") and loved the light that comes after darkness, so they called it "himera" -- from "himeros" (desire, longing). (In the Greek, the old form "himera" comes from "himeros" -- desire or longing.)

Hermogenes
Clearly.

Socrates
But now the name has been so altered that you can't tell the meaning. Though some people think "hemera" means it makes things "gentle" ("hemera").

Hermogenes
That's my own impression.

Socrates
And did you know the ancients said "duogon" instead of "zugon"?

Hermogenes
They did.

Socrates
"Zugon" (yoke) has no transparent meaning, but the old form "duogon" expresses the binding of two together ("duein agoge" -- "leading of two") for the purpose of pulling. This was changed to "zugon." And there are many other similar examples.

Hermogenes
There are.

Socrates
Following the same line of thought, the word "deon" (obligation) has a meaning opposite to all our other terms for good. "Deon" is a species of good, and yet it means "chain" ("desmos") or "hindrance of motion" -- making it the brother of "blaberon." (In the Greek, "deon" is connected to "desmos" -- a chain or bond.)

Hermogenes
Yes, Socrates, that's quite clear.

Socrates
Not if you restore the ancient form, which is probably the correct one. Read "dion" instead of "deon" -- convert the epsilon back to an iota, the old way. Then the word agrees with all the other good-words. "Dion," not "deon," signifies the good and is a term of praise. So the name-giver hasn't contradicted himself after all. In all these various words -- "deon" (obligatory), "ophelimon" (advantageous), "lysiteloun" (profitable), "kerdaleon" (gainful), "agathon" (good), "sympheron" (expedient), "euporon" (plenteous) -- the same concept is implied: the all-pervading, ordering principle is praised, and the restraining, binding principle is criticized.

And this helps explain "zemiodes" (hurtful): change the zeta back to a delta, as in the ancient language, and it becomes "demiodes." This name, as you'll see, refers to "that which binds motion" ("dounti ion"). (In the Greek, "zemiodes" is connected through its ancient form "demiodes" to "dein" -- to bind -- and "ion" -- going.)

Hermogenes
What do you say about "hedone" (pleasure), "lype" (pain), "epithymia" (desire), and words like those?

Socrates
I don't think they're particularly difficult. "Hedone" (pleasure) is "he onesis" -- the action that tends toward benefit. The original form was probably "heone," but a delta was inserted. (In the Greek, "hedone" comes from "onesis" -- benefit.)

"Lype" (pain) comes from the relaxation ("lyein") the body feels in sorrow.

"Ania" (trouble) is the hindrance of motion ("a" -- not -- and "ienai" -- to go).

"Algedon" (distress), if I'm not mistaken, is a foreign word, derived from "algeinos" (grievous).

"Odyne" (grief) is named from the "putting on" ("endysis") of sorrow.

"Achthedon" (vexation) -- "the word itself labors," as anyone can see.

"Chara" (joy) is the very expression of the soul's free flow and diffusion ("cheo" -- to pour).

"Terpsis" (delight) is so called from pleasure creeping ("herpon") through the soul, which can be likened to breath ("pnoe"). It was originally "herpnoun" but was changed over time to "terpnon."

"Euphrosyne" (cheerfulness) and "epithymia" (desire) explain themselves. "Euphrosyne" ought to be "eupherosyne" -- named from the soul moving ("pheresthai") in harmony with nature. It was changed to "euphrosyne."

"Epithymia" is really "he epi ton thymon iousa dynamis" -- "the power that enters into the passionate part of the soul." (In the Greek, "epithymia" comes from "epi" -- toward -- "thymos" -- spirit/passion -- and "iousa" -- going.)

"Thymos" (passion/spirit) itself comes from "thysis" -- the rushing and boiling of the soul.

"Himeros" (desire/yearning) denotes the stream ("rhous") that most powerfully draws the soul -- flowing with desire and expressing a violent attraction toward things. (In the Greek, "himeros" is connected to "hiemenos" -- rushing -- and "rhous" -- stream.)

"Pothos" (longing) expresses desire for what's not present but absent and "elsewhere" ("pou" -- somewhere). That's why "pothos" applies to absent things, while "himeros" applies to present ones.

"Eros" (love) is so called because it flows in ("esron") from outside. The stream isn't internal but is an influence introduced through the eyes. In ancient times, when they used omicron instead of omega, it was called "esros" (influx). Now, with omega substituted, it's "eros." (In the Greek, "eros" comes from "esrhein" -- to flow in.)

But why don't you give me another word?

Hermogenes
What do you think of "doxa" (opinion, belief) and that category of words?

Socrates
"Doxa" is either derived from "dioxis" (pursuit) -- expressing the soul's march in pursuit of knowledge -- or from the shooting of a bow ("toxon"). The latter seems more likely, and it's confirmed by "oiesis" (thinking), which is just "oisis" (motion) -- the movement of the soul toward the essential nature of each thing.

Similarly, "boule" (counsel, deliberation) has to do with shooting ("bole" -- a throw), and "boulesthai" (to wish or intend) combines aiming and deliberating. All these words follow "doxa" and involve the idea of shooting or aiming. And "aboulia" (lack of counsel) is, by contrast, a mishap -- missing the target, the aim, the goal.

Hermogenes
You're really picking up the pace now, Socrates.

Socrates
I'm almost at the finish line. But before I'm done, I want to explain "ananke" (necessity) and "hekousion" (the voluntary).

"Hekousion" (voluntary) is the yielding ("eikon") and unresisting -- yielding to motion that's in accordance with our will.

But "the necessary" is what resists our will, involving error and ignorance. The idea is taken from walking through a ravine ("anke") -- impassable, rugged, overgrown, impeding your progress. That's where "anankaion" gets its name: "an' anke ion" -- "going through a ravine." (In the Greek, "ananke" comes from "ana" -- through -- and "anke" -- ravine -- and "ion" -- going.)

While my strength holds out, let's press on. I hope you'll keep asking questions.

Hermogenes
Well then, let me ask about the greatest and noblest words: "aletheia" (truth), "pseudos" (falsehood), and "on" (being) -- and while we're at it, why the word "onoma" (name), which is the very subject of our whole discussion, has that name.

Socrates
Do you know the word "maïesthai" (to seek)?

Hermogenes
Yes -- it means the same as "zetein" (to inquire).

Socrates
"Onoma" (name) seems to be a compressed sentence meaning "on hou zetema" -- "being for which there is a search." This is even more obvious in "onomaston" (notable), which states in so many words that "real existence is that for which there is a seeking" ("on hou masma"). (In the Greek, "onoma" comes from "on" -- being -- and "masma/zetema" -- search or seeking.)

"Aletheia" (truth) is also a compressed phrase: "theia ale" -- "divine wandering" -- implying the divine motion of existence. (In the Greek, "aletheia" comes from "theia" -- divine -- and "ale" -- wandering.)

"Pseudos" (falsehood) is the opposite of motion -- another bad name from the legislator, given to stagnation and forced inactivity, which he compared to sleep ("eudein"). The original meaning is disguised by the addition of psi. (In the Greek, "pseudos" is connected to "eudein" -- to sleep -- with a psi added.)

"On" (being) and "ousia" (essence) are really "ion" with the iota broken off. This fits perfectly: being ("on") is also moving ("ion"). And the same goes for not-being: "ouk ion" -- not going. (In the Greek, "on" comes from "ion" -- going -- with the iota dropped.)

Hermogenes
You've really hammered those into shape. But suppose someone asked you: what about the words "ion" (going), "rheon" (flowing), and "doun" (binding)? Show me THEIR fitness.

Socrates
You mean, how would I answer that?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
One approach has already been suggested.

Hermogenes
Which one?

Socrates
Saying that words we can't explain are foreign in origin. And that's probably often true. But it's also possible that the original forms of words have been lost over the ages. Names have been so twisted in every direction that I wouldn't be surprised if the old language, compared to ours, sounded like a foreign tongue to us.

Hermogenes
Very likely.

Socrates
Yes, very likely. But the inquiry demands our full attention, and we can't back down. Because here's the thing: if someone keeps analyzing names into words, and then analyzing those words into their component elements, and keeps repeating this process, eventually the person answering has to give up in despair.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
And at what point should we give up? Surely when we reach the names that are the building blocks of all other names and sentences. These can't be assumed to be composed of still other names. Take "agathon" (good) -- we said it's a compound of "agaston" (admirable) and "thoos" (swift). And "thoos" is probably made up of other elements, and those of still others. But if we hit a word that can't be broken down any further, we're right to say we've reached a primary element that doesn't need further analysis.

Hermogenes
I think you're right.

Socrates
And if the names you're asking about turn out to be primary elements, we'll need a different method to examine their truth or correctness.

Hermogenes
Very likely.

Socrates
Exactly, Hermogenes. Everything we've discussed so far points to this conclusion. And if the conclusion is sound -- as I believe it is -- then come help me, so I don't say something absurd about the principle behind primary names.

Hermogenes
Let me hear it, and I'll do my best to help.

Socrates
I think you'll agree with me that one principle applies to all names, primary and secondary alike. Viewed simply as names, there's no difference between them.

Hermogenes
Certainly not.

Socrates
All the names we've been explaining were meant to indicate the nature of things.

Hermogenes
Of course.

Socrates
And that's equally true of primary names and secondary ones -- they're all names.

Hermogenes
Surely.

Socrates
But the secondary names, I think, derive their meaning from the primary ones.

Hermogenes
That's evident.

Socrates
Very good. But then how do the primary names -- the ones that can't be broken down further -- show the natures of things, to whatever extent they can? Here's a question: if we had no voice or tongue and wanted to communicate with each other, wouldn't we make signs with our hands and head and body, the way deaf people do?

Hermogenes
We'd have no other choice, Socrates.

Socrates
We'd imitate the nature of the thing. To express lightness and upwardness, we'd raise our hands toward the sky. For heaviness and downwardness, we'd let them drop to the ground. If we were describing a running horse or any other animal, we'd make our bodies and gestures resemble theirs as closely as possible.

Hermogenes
I don't see what else we could do.

Socrates
Right -- bodily imitation is the only way the body can express anything.

Hermogenes
Very true.

Socrates
And when we want to express ourselves using the voice, tongue, or mouth, our expression is simply an imitation of whatever we want to express.

Hermogenes
That must be right, I think.

Socrates
So a name is a vocal imitation of what the namer imitates?

Hermogenes
I think so.

Socrates
Actually, my friend, I don't think we've quite hit on the truth yet.

Hermogenes
Why not?

Socrates
Because if that were right, we'd have to say that people who imitate sheep, roosters, or other animals are naming them.

Hermogenes
That's true.

Socrates
So maybe my approach was wrong?

Hermogenes
It seems so. But I wish you'd tell me, Socrates: what kind of imitation IS a name?

Socrates
First of all, not a musical imitation, even though music is vocal. And not an imitation of what music imitates either. Those wouldn't be names, in my view. Let me put it this way: all objects have sound and shape, and many have color, right?

Hermogenes
Certainly.

Socrates
But the art of naming doesn't seem to be concerned with imitating those qualities. The arts that deal with sound and shape are music and painting.

Hermogenes
True.

Socrates
But isn't there an essence of each thing, just as there's a color and a sound? Isn't there an essence of color and sound themselves, and of anything else that can be said to have an essence?

Hermogenes
I'd think so.

Socrates
Well then, if someone could express the essence of each thing in letters and syllables, wouldn't they be expressing the nature of each thing?

Hermogenes
Absolutely.

Socrates
We called the musician and the painter the two kinds of imitators. What shall we call this imitator?

Hermogenes
I think, Socrates, he must be the namer -- the name-giver we've been looking for.

Socrates
If that's right, then we're in a position to examine the words "rhoe" (stream), "ienai" (to go), and "schesis" (retention) that you were asking about. We can see whether the namer grasped the nature of these things in letters and syllables well enough to imitate their essence.

Hermogenes
Very good.

Socrates
But are those the only primary names, or are there others?

Hermogenes
There must be others.

Socrates
I'd think so. But how do we analyze them further, and where does the imitator begin? Since the imitation of essence is made through syllables and letters, shouldn't we first separate the letters -- the way people studying rhythm first distinguish the basic sounds, then compound sounds, and only after that move on to rhythms?

Hermogenes
Yes.

Socrates
We should begin the same way with letters. First, separate the vowels into classes. Then the consonants and mutes, according to the standard scholarly distinctions. Then the semivowels, which are neither vowels nor mutes. And then classify the vowels themselves. When we've perfected this classification of sounds, we should give them names and see whether there are classes they can all be grouped into. That way we'll see their natures and whether they, like letters, fall into distinct categories. When we've thought all this through, we'll know how to match sounds to the things they resemble -- whether a single letter represents a single thing, or several need to be mixed together.

It's like painting. When a painter wants to depict something, sometimes he uses a single color -- just purple, say -- and sometimes he mixes several colors, as when painting skin tones. He uses his colors as the subject requires. And in the same way, we'll apply letters to the expression of things -- single letters where appropriate, or combinations, forming syllables, and from syllables making nouns and verbs, and from those building up entire language -- large, beautiful, and complete.

Just as the painter creates a figure, so we'll create speech through the art of naming, or rhetoric, or whatever we call it. Not that I mean WE literally do this -- I got carried away. I mean the ancients formed language this way. And what they put together, we need to take apart in the same way, if we want a scientific understanding of the whole subject. We need to check whether both the primary and secondary elements are correctly assigned. Because if they're not, the whole composition will be a sorry piece of work, built in the wrong direction.

Hermogenes
I can well believe that, Socrates.

Socrates
Well, do you think you'd be able to analyze them this way? I'm certain I couldn't.

Hermogenes
Then I'm even less likely to manage it.

Socrates
Shall we give up then? Or shall we do the best we can, saying -- as I said earlier about the gods -- that we know nothing of the truth and are only exploring human opinions? Let us say, before we proceed, that the higher method is the one we or anyone else should follow who wants to analyze language properly. But under the circumstances, as the saying goes, we must do as well as we can. What do you think?

Hermogenes
I very much approve.

Socrates
It may seem ridiculous, Hermogenes, that things should be imitated in letters and syllables. But we can't avoid it -- there's no better principle to look to for the truth of primary names. Without it, we'd have to resort to divine intervention, like the tragic poets who bring in gods on cranes whenever they're stuck. We could say "the gods gave the first names, so they must be right." That would be convenient. Or we could say the names come from some foreign people (since foreigners are older than us). Or we could say that the origins are lost in antiquity. But these are all just clever excuses for having no real explanation.

And yet, any ignorance about primary names means ignorance about secondary ones too, since secondary names can only be explained through primary ones. Clearly, anyone claiming expertise in language should be able to give a very clear account of primary names. Otherwise they'll just be talking nonsense about everything else. Don't you think that's true?

Hermogenes
Certainly, Socrates.

Socrates
My first thoughts about primary names are truly wild and ridiculous. But I'm happy to share them if you want, and I hope you'll share anything better you might have.

Hermogenes
Don't worry -- I'll do my best.

Socrates
First: the letter rho seems to me to be the universal instrument for expressing all motion ("kinesis"). But I haven't yet explained "kinesis" itself, which is really "iesis" (going). The ancients didn't use eta -- they used epsilon -- so the root is "kiein," a foreign form meaning the same as "ienai" (to go). The old word "kinesis" would be correctly given as "iesis" in modern letters. With the foreign root "kiein," allowing for the change of eta and insertion of nu, we get "kinesis" -- which should really be "kieinesis" or "eisis." And "stasis" (rest/standing still) is the negation of "ienai" (going), improved into "stasis."

Now, the letter rho struck the name-giver as an excellent instrument for expressing motion. He uses it constantly for this purpose: in "rhein" (to flow) and "rhoe" (stream), he represents motion with rho. Also in "tromos" (trembling), "trachys" (rough), and in "krouein" (to strike), "thrauein" (to crush), "ereikein" (to bruise), "thryptein" (to break), "kermatizein" (to crumble), "rhymbein" (to whirl). In all these kinds of movement, he generally uses the letter rho -- because, I imagine, he noticed that the tongue is most agitated and least at rest when pronouncing this letter. That's why he used it to express motion.

He uses the letter iota to express the subtle elements that pass through all things -- which is why he employs iota in imitating motion: "ienai" (to go), "hiesthai" (to rush).

Then there's another class of letters -- phi, psi, sigma, and zeta -- whose pronunciation involves a great expenditure of breath. These are used to imitate blowing and windy concepts: "psychron" (shivering cold), "zeon" (seething), "seïesthai" (to be shaken), "seismos" (earthquake). The name-giver always brings these in when he wants to imitate what is "physodes" (windy).

He seems to have thought that the closure and pressure of the tongue in pronouncing delta and tau expressed binding and rest -- stillness in a place. He noticed the liquid movement of lambda, where the tongue slips, and found in it the expression of smoothness, as in "leios" (smooth), "olisthanein" (to slip), "liparon" (sleek), "kollodes" (gluey), and similar words.

The heavier sound of gamma detained the slipping tongue, and the combination of gamma and lambda together gave the notion of something glutinous and sticky, as in "glischros" (sticky), "glykys" (sweet), "gloiodes" (gummy).

He noticed that nu is sounded from within and therefore has a quality of inwardness, so he used it in "endon" (inside) and "entos" (within).

Alpha he assigned to the expression of largeness, and eta to length, because they are big letters. Omicron was the sign of roundness, and so there's plenty of omicron in "gongylon" (round).

And so the original legislator, reducing everything to letters and syllables, impressing names and signs on things, and then building other signs out of them through imitation -- that's how he composed language. At least, that's my view, Hermogenes, of the truth about names.

But I'd like to hear what Cratylus has to say.

Hermogenes
But Socrates, as I was telling you before, Cratylus mystifies me. He says there's a natural fitness of names but never explains what it is, so I can't tell whether his vagueness is deliberate or not. Tell me now, Cratylus -- here in Socrates' presence -- do you agree with what Socrates has been saying about names, or do you have something better? If you do, share it. Either you'll learn from Socrates, or Socrates and I will learn from you.

Cratylus
Come now, Hermogenes -- you don't really think you can learn, or I can explain, any important subject in a moment, do you? Especially not a subject like language, which is perhaps the greatest of all.

Hermogenes
No, of course not. But as Hesiod says, and I agree: "to add a little to a little" is worthwhile. So if you can add anything at all, however small, to what we know, please make the effort. You'd be doing both Socrates and me a favor -- and you certainly owe us that.

Socrates
I'm by no means certain, Cratylus, about the views that Hermogenes and I have worked out. So don't hesitate to share your thoughts. If you've got something better than what I've said, I'll gladly accept it. And I wouldn't be at all surprised if you have. You've clearly been thinking about these things and have had teachers. So if you really do have a better theory about the truth of names, count me among your students.

Cratylus
You're right, Socrates, that I've studied these matters, and I might convert you into a student yet. But I'm afraid the opposite is more likely. I find myself moved to say to you what Achilles says to Ajax in the Prayers: "Illustrious Ajax, son of Telamon, lord of the people, you seem to have spoken entirely to my mind." You, Socrates, seem to me like an oracle, giving answers very much to my liking -- whether you're inspired by Euthyphro or some Muse has long been dwelling in your heart without your knowing it.

Socrates
My dear Cratylus, I've been wondering at my own wisdom for a while now. I don't trust myself. I think I need to pause and ask: "What am I actually saying?" Because there's nothing worse than self-deception -- when the deceiver is always at home and always with you. That's truly terrible. So I should often retrace my steps and try to "look fore and aft," as Homer says.

Now then, where are we? Haven't we been saying that a correct name indicates the nature of the thing? Has that been sufficiently established?

Cratylus
Yes, Socrates. I'm disposed to think that what you say is quite true.

Socrates
So names are given in order to teach?

Cratylus
Certainly.

Socrates
And naming is a craft, with skilled practitioners?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
And who are these practitioners?

Cratylus
The legislators you spoke of at the start.

Socrates
And does this craft, like other crafts, produce practitioners of varying skill? Some painters are better and some worse?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
The better painters produce better figures, and the worse produce worse ones. And among builders, the better ones build finer houses, the worse ones build worse ones.

Cratylus
True.

Socrates
And among legislators, some do their work better and some worse?

Cratylus
No -- there I disagree with you.

Socrates
So you don't think some laws are better and others worse?

Cratylus
No, I don't.

Socrates
Or that one name is better than another?

Cratylus
Certainly not.

Socrates
Then all names are correctly given?

Cratylus
Yes -- if they're names at all.

Socrates
Well, what about our friend Hermogenes' name, which we discussed earlier? If he has nothing of Hermes' nature in him, should we say this is a wrong name, or not his name at all?

Cratylus
I'd say it's not his name at all. It only appears to be. His real name belongs to whoever has the nature that corresponds to it.

Socrates
And if someone were to call him "Hermogenes," would that person even be speaking falsely? Because there may be a question of whether you CAN call him Hermogenes if he isn't one.

Cratylus
What do you mean?

Socrates
Are you saying that falsehood is impossible? Because if that's your position, I should point out that there have been plenty of liars throughout history.

Cratylus
But Socrates, how can you say what is not? How can you say something and yet say nothing? Isn't falsehood just saying what is not?

Socrates
That argument is a bit too subtle for a man my age. But let me ask: are you one of those philosophers who think falsehood can be spoken but not really said?

Cratylus
Neither spoken nor said.

Socrates
Not even uttered or addressed? For example, suppose someone met you in a foreign country, took your hand, and said, "Greetings, Athenian stranger, Hermogenes, son of Smicrion." Are you saying those words would have no application to you -- only to our friend Hermogenes here, or perhaps to nobody at all?

Cratylus
In my view, Socrates, the speaker would just be producing meaningless noise.

Socrates
Good enough for me. I just want to know: would the nonsense be true, false, or partly true and partly false?

Cratylus
I'd say the person would be putting himself in motion to no purpose. His words would be a meaningless sound -- like the noise of someone banging on a brass pot.

Socrates
Let's see if we can find common ground, Cratylus. You'd agree that a name is not the same as the thing it names?

Cratylus
I would.

Socrates
And you'd agree that a name is an imitation of the thing?

Cratylus
Certainly.

Socrates
And paintings are also imitations of things, just in a different way?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
I may not fully understand you, but let me ask: can't both kinds of imitation -- pictures and names -- be attributed and applied to the things they imitate?

Cratylus
They can.

Socrates
Now look at it this way: you can assign the portrait of a man to a man, and the portrait of a woman to a woman?

Cratylus
Certainly.

Socrates
And conversely, you can assign the portrait of a man to a woman, and the portrait of a woman to a man?

Cratylus
Very true -- you CAN.

Socrates
And are both ways of assigning them right, or only the first?

Cratylus
Only the first.

Socrates
That is, the assignment that gives each thing what belongs to it and is like it?

Cratylus
That's my view.

Socrates
Now, since you and I are friends and I want us to understand each other, let me state my position: the first kind of assignment, whether applied to pictures or names, I call right. And when applied to names specifically, I call it true as well as right. The other kind -- assigning a name that's unlike the thing -- I call wrong. And in the case of names, false as well as wrong.

Cratylus
That may be true for pictures, Socrates. They can be wrongly assigned. But not names -- names must always be right.

Socrates
Why? What's the difference? Can't I go up to a man and say "This is your portrait," showing him his own likeness, or perhaps the likeness of a woman? When I say "show," I mean bringing it before his eyes.

Cratylus
Certainly.

Socrates
And can't I also go up to him and say "This is your name"? A name, like a picture, is an imitation. So I could bring to his ears the imitation of himself, saying "This is a man," or the imitation of a female, saying "This is a woman." Isn't all that perfectly possible?

Cratylus
I'd like to agree with you, Socrates, so -- granted.

Socrates
That's generous of you, and I think I'm right, though we needn't settle it fully now. But if I can assign names as well as pictures to objects, then the right assignment is what we'd call truth, and the wrong assignment falsehood. And if there can be wrong assignment of names, there can also be wrong assignment of verbs. And if names and verbs can be wrongly assigned, then so can the sentences made from them. What do you say, Cratylus?

Cratylus
I agree. What you say is very true.

Socrates
Furthermore, primary nouns can be compared to pictures. In a picture, you can give all the right colors and shapes, or you might leave some out, or put in too many. Right?

Cratylus
Very true.

Socrates
And the person who gets everything right produces a perfect picture, while the person who adds or leaves something out produces a picture -- but not a good one.

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
In the same way, someone who imitates the nature of things in syllables and letters will produce a good image -- in other words, a good name -- if they get everything right. But if they subtract or perhaps add a little, they'll produce an image that's still an image -- just not a good one. From this I conclude that some names are well made and others poorly made.

Cratylus
That's true.

Socrates
Then the artist of names can sometimes be good and sometimes bad?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
And we called that artist the legislator?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
Then, like other artists, the legislator can be good or bad -- if our earlier reasoning holds?

Cratylus
Very true, Socrates. But with language, you see, it's different. When we use grammar to assign the letters alpha, beta, or any others to a particular name -- if we add, remove, or misplace a letter, the name isn't just written wrong, it's not really written at all. It becomes something other than that name entirely.

Socrates
But I'm not sure your view is correct, Cratylus.

Cratylus
How so?

Socrates
What you're saying might be true of numbers. A number must be exactly what it is, or it's a completely different number. Add or subtract a unit from ten and you've got a different number. But this doesn't apply to qualitative things or to representations. In fact, I'd go further: an image that reproduced every single aspect of the original would no longer be just an image.

Let me illustrate. Suppose there are two things: Cratylus, and an image of Cratylus. And suppose some god creates not just a representation of your outward form and color, like a painter would, but also replicates your inner organization -- the same warmth, softness, motion, soul, and mind -- copies all your qualities exactly and places this replica right next to you. Would you say that's Cratylus and an image of Cratylus, or that there are two Cratyluses?

Cratylus
I'd say there are two Cratyluses.

Socrates
Then you see, my friend, that we need to find a different standard of truth in images, and in names too. We can't insist that an image stops being an image just because something is added or left out. Don't you see that images are very far from being exact copies of what they represent?

Cratylus
Yes, I see.

Socrates
And wouldn't it be ridiculous if names were exactly the same as the things they name? Everything would be doubled, and nobody could tell which was the name and which was the reality.

Cratylus
Quite true.

Socrates
Then be brave and admit that one name can be well given and another poorly given. Don't insist that the name be exactly the same as the thing. Allow the occasional wrong letter. And if a wrong letter in a name, then a wrong noun in a sentence. And if a wrong noun in a sentence, then a sentence that doesn't perfectly fit its subject. The thing can still be named and described, as long as the general character of what you're describing is preserved. You'll remember that Hermogenes and I made this very point about the names of letters.

Cratylus
Yes, I remember.

Socrates
Good. When the general character is preserved, the thing is signified -- well, if all the right elements are there; not perfectly, if only some are. I think we'd better accept this, or we'll end up like travelers in Aegina who wander the streets late at night and get told by truth herself that we've arrived too late. Either accept this, or find some entirely new theory of the correctness of names. Because you can't keep saying that a name is the expression of a thing in letters and syllables while also saying one wrong letter destroys it completely -- that's contradictory.

Cratylus
I quite accept what you're saying, Socrates. That seems very reasonable.

Socrates
Good. Since we agree on that, let's ask whether a rightly given name should contain the proper letters.

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
And the proper letters are those that resemble the things?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
So names that are correctly given are mostly made up of appropriate, similar letters -- otherwise there'd be no resemblance at all. But there will also be some improper elements that spoil the beauty and completeness of the name. You'd agree?

Cratylus
I can't really quarrel with you, Socrates, but I'm not comfortable saying that an incorrectly given name is a name at all.

Socrates
Do you at least admit that a name is a representation of a thing?

Cratylus
Yes, I do.

Socrates
And don't you allow that some nouns are primary and others derived?

Cratylus
Yes, I do.

Socrates
Then if primary names are representations of things, is there any better way to create them than by making them as similar to their objects as possible? Or do you prefer the view of Hermogenes and many others that names are conventional -- meaningful to those who've agreed on them because they already know the things, and that convention is the only principle? Whether you stick with the current convention or make a new one where you call small "large" and large "small" -- it's all the same, they'd say, as long as there's agreement. Which view do you prefer?

Cratylus
Representation through likeness, Socrates, is infinitely better than representation through arbitrary signs.

Socrates
Very good. But if the name is to be like the thing, then the letters out of which primary names are composed must also be like things. Think of painting: how could you compose a picture that looks like anything at all if there were no pigments in nature that resembled the things being depicted?

Cratylus
That would be impossible.

Socrates
In the same way, names could never resemble real things unless the basic elements they're composed of bore some degree of resemblance to the objects the names imitate. And those elements are letters?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
Then let me invite you to reconsider what Hermogenes and I were saying about individual sounds. Do you agree that the letter rho expresses rapidity, motion, and hardness? Were we right?

Cratylus
I'd say you were right.

Socrates
And that lambda expresses smoothness and softness?

Cratylus
Right again.

Socrates
And yet, as you know, what we call "sklerotēs" (hardness) the Eretrians call "sklerotēr" -- with a rho at the end instead of a sigma.

Cratylus
Very true.

Socrates
So are rho and sigma equivalent? Do they both have the same significance -- rho at the end for them, sigma for us -- or does the word mean something to only one of us?

Cratylus
No, it surely means something to both of us.

Socrates
Insofar as rho and sigma are alike, or insofar as they're unlike?

Cratylus
Insofar as they're alike.

Socrates
Are they completely alike?

Cratylus
Well, at least for the purpose of expressing motion.

Socrates
But what about the lambda in the middle of "sklērotēs"? Lambda expresses softness, not hardness.

Cratylus
Well, maybe the lambda is wrongly inserted, Socrates, and should be changed to a rho -- as you were suggesting to Hermogenes when you talked about adding and removing letters as needed. You were right about that, in my opinion.

Socrates
Fine. But the word is still perfectly intelligible to both of us, isn't it? When I say "sklēron" (hard), you know what I mean.

Cratylus
Yes, my friend -- but that's just custom.

Socrates
And what is custom but convention? I make a sound, I understand what I mean by it, and you know what I mean. That's what you're saying, isn't it?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
And if when I speak you understand my meaning, I've communicated something to you?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
And this communication might proceed through unlike sounds just as easily as through similar ones -- as in the lambda of "sklērotēs." But if that's true, then you've made a convention with yourself, and the correctness of names turns out to depend on convention, since letters that are unlike the thing can communicate just as effectively as letters that are similar -- if they're backed by custom and convention.

Even if you draw the sharpest possible distinction between custom and convention, you still have to admit that the meaning of words comes from custom, not just from resemblance. Custom can work through unlike sounds just as well as through similar ones.

And since we're agreed on this, Cratylus, then convention must contribute to how we communicate our thoughts. How could you ever find names resembling every individual number, for instance, unless you allowed convention some authority in determining the correctness of names?

I quite agree with you that words should resemble things as much as possible. But I'm afraid this "resemblance" requirement, as Hermogenes might say, is a somewhat fragile thing, and it needs the mechanical support of convention to work properly. In an ideal world, we'd always use perfectly fitting resemblances, and that would be the most perfect language. The opposite -- arbitrary signs with no resemblance -- would be the most imperfect.

But let me ask: what is the purpose of names? What are they for?

Cratylus
The purpose of names, Socrates, I'd say, is to inform. The simple truth is: whoever knows the names knows the things they express.

Socrates
So you're saying, Cratylus, that since the name and the thing are similar, knowing the name means knowing the thing? Because similar things fall under the same art or branch of knowledge. So whoever knows names will also know things.

Cratylus
That's precisely what I mean.

Socrates
But let's think about what kind of information names give us. Is it the best kind? Or is there another, better kind?

Cratylus
I believe names give us the only and best kind of information. There can be no other.

Socrates
But do you also think that the person who discovers the names thereby discovers the things? Or is learning through names just one method, and there might be another way to investigate and discover?

Cratylus
I believe investigation and discovery work the same way as instruction -- through names.

Socrates
But don't you see, Cratylus, that whoever follows names in searching for things, analyzing their meanings, is in great danger of being deceived?

Cratylus
How so?

Socrates
Because clearly, whoever first gave names did so according to their own understanding of the things. Right?

Cratylus
True.

Socrates
And if that understanding was wrong -- if they named things based on a mistaken conception -- where does that leave us, their followers? Won't we be led astray?

Cratylus
But Socrates, surely the original name-giver must have known what he was doing? Otherwise his names wouldn't really be names at all. And you have clear proof that he didn't miss the truth -- the proof is his perfect consistency. Didn't you notice, when you were going through all those etymologies, that the words all shared a common character and purpose?

Socrates
That's no answer at all, my friend. If the name-giver made a mistake at the start, he could easily have forced everything else into agreement with that initial error. There'd be nothing strange about that -- it's no different from what happens in geometry, where a small, invisible flaw in the first step leads to consistently wrong conclusions all the way down. That's why every person should devote their greatest thought and attention to first principles -- are they rightly established or not? Once you've examined those thoroughly, everything else follows.

Now, I'd be amazed to find that names really are consistent. Let's revisit our earlier discussion. Weren't we saying that everything is in motion, in progress, in flux, and that names express this idea of motion? Isn't that what they seemed to mean?

Cratylus
Yes, absolutely. That's their meaning and the true meaning.

Socrates
Let's look again. Take "epistēmē" (knowledge) -- it's ambiguous. It seems to suggest "stopping the soul at things" rather than "going along with them." (In the Greek, "epi-histēmē" could suggest "epi" -- at -- and "histanai" -- to stop -- rather than "hepesthai" -- to follow.) So maybe we should keep the beginning as is but insert an iota instead of an epsilon.

Or take "bebaion" (certainty/firmness) -- that clearly expresses standing still and position, not motion.

"Historia" (inquiry) bears on its face the "stopping" ("histanai") of the stream.

"Piston" (trustworthy) certainly indicates cessation of motion.

"Mnēmē" (memory) obviously expresses rest in the soul, not motion.

And words like "hamartia" (error) and "symphora" (calamity), when you look at their etymologies, turn out to mean the same thing as "synesis" (understanding) and "epistēmē" (knowledge) and other positive words! (In the Greek, "hamartia" can be analyzed as "hama artein" -- "going along with" -- similar to "syn-ienai" and "hep-esthai.") The same applies to "amathia" (ignorance) and "akolasia" (lack of self-control): "amathia" can be explained as "hē hama theō iontos poreia" -- "the journey of one who travels with god" -- and "akolasia" as "hē akolouthia tois pragmasin" -- "following along with things."

So the names with the worst meanings turn out to be built on the same principle as those with the best. And I'm sure that anyone who took the trouble could find many more examples where the name-giver indicates not motion and progress but rest -- the exact opposite.

Cratylus
Yes, Socrates, but notice: the great majority express motion.

Socrates
So what, Cratylus? Are we counting names like votes? Do we decide correctness by majority rule? Whichever type has the most names wins?

Cratylus
No, that wouldn't be reasonable.

Socrates
Certainly not. But let's leave that question and move on. Were we not agreeing earlier that the first name-givers -- whether Greek or non-Greek -- were legislators, and that the art of giving names was the legislator's art?

Cratylus
Quite true.

Socrates
Then tell me: did the first legislators, who gave the first names, know the things they named, or didn't they?

Cratylus
They must have known, Socrates.

Socrates
Yes, friend Cratylus, they could hardly have been ignorant.

Cratylus
I'd say not.

Socrates
Let's return to the point we left. You were saying that the person who gave names must have known the things he named. Are you still of that opinion?

Cratylus
I am.

Socrates
And you'd say that the giver of the FIRST names also had knowledge of the things he named?

Cratylus
I would.

Socrates
But how could he have learned or discovered things from names if the primary names hadn't been given yet? We just agreed that the only way to learn and discover things is either by discovering the names yourself or learning them from someone else.

Cratylus
I think there's a good deal in what you say, Socrates.

Socrates
But if things can only be known through names, how can we suppose that the original name-givers had knowledge -- or were even legislators at all -- before any names existed, and therefore before they could have known anything through names?

Cratylus
I think, Socrates, the true explanation is this: a power greater than human gave things their first names, and the names given that way are necessarily the true ones.

Socrates
But then how did this inspired name-giver -- or god -- manage to contradict himself? We just showed that some names express motion and others express rest. Was that a mistake?

Cratylus
Well, I suppose one set of those aren't really names at all.

Socrates
And which set did the god make -- the ones expressing motion, or the ones expressing rest? Because, as I said, this can't be settled by counting.

Cratylus
No, not that way, Socrates.

Socrates
But if we have a battle of names -- some claiming to represent the truth and others making the same claim -- how are we to decide between them? We can't appeal to still other names -- we'd need some standard that, without using names at all, shows which set is right. And that standard would have to reveal the truth of things directly.

Cratylus
I agree.

Socrates
But if that's true, Cratylus, then it seems that things can be known without names?

Cratylus
Clearly.

Socrates
And how would you expect to know them? What other way is there except the true and natural way -- through their connections with each other, through their kinship, and through themselves? Because anything other than them, anything different from them, would point to something other and different.

Cratylus
What you're saying seems true, I think.

Socrates
Now hold on. Haven't we agreed several times that names, when correctly given, are likenesses and images of the things they name?

Cratylus
Yes.

Socrates
Then suppose you can learn things through names to whatever degree you like. And suppose you can also learn them from the things themselves. Which method is likely to be nobler and clearer: learning from the image whether the image and the truth it represents have been rightly conceived? Or learning from the truth itself whether the truth and its image have been properly executed?

Cratylus
We must learn from the truth, I'd say.

Socrates
How real existence is to be studied and discovered is a question that's probably beyond both you and me. But we can at least admit this much: knowledge of things is not to be derived from names. Things must be studied and investigated in themselves.

Cratylus
Clearly, Socrates.

Socrates
There's one more point. I wouldn't want us to be misled by the sheer number of names all trending in the same direction. I don't deny that the original name-givers really did create names under the assumption that everything is in motion and flux. That was their sincere belief -- but I think they were mistaken. Having fallen into a kind of whirlpool themselves, they're spinning around and trying to drag us in after them.

There's something, Cratylus, that I often think about, and I'd like your opinion: tell me, do you believe there exists an absolute beauty or good, or any other absolute reality?

Cratylus
Yes, Socrates, I do believe so.

Socrates
Then let's focus on that -- true beauty itself. Not whether a particular face is attractive or anything like that, since all particular things seem to be in flux. Let's ask: isn't true beauty always beautiful?

Cratylus
Certainly.

Socrates
And can we really speak of a beauty that's always passing away, that's one thing and then another? Wouldn't it have changed and vanished while the word was still in our mouths?

Cratylus
Undoubtedly.

Socrates
Then how can something be real if it's never in the same state? Things that remain the same can't change while they remain the same. And if they're always the same, always in the same condition, never departing from their original form, they can never change or be moved.

Cratylus
They certainly can't.

Socrates
And they can't be known by anyone either -- at least not if they're always changing. Because at the very moment the observer approaches, they'd become something different and other, so you could never get any further in knowing their nature or condition. You can't know what has no stable condition.

Cratylus
True.

Socrates
And we can't reasonably say there's knowledge at all if everything is in transition and nothing abides. Knowledge, too, would have to stop being knowledge if it's constantly changing. But if the very nature of knowledge changes, at the moment of change there'd be no knowledge. And if the transition never stops, there'd always be no knowledge -- no one to know and nothing to be known.

But if the knower and the known DO exist permanently, and beauty and goodness and every other thing also exist in this way, then they can't resemble a process or a flow, as we were just supposing. Whether there is this eternal nature in things, or whether Heraclitus and his many followers are right about universal flux, is a difficult question to settle. And no sensible person will put themselves or the education of their mind in the power of names. They won't trust names or name-givers to the point of confidently declaring that they have knowledge -- knowledge that condemns both themselves and everything else to an unhealthy unreality, where everything leaks like a cracked pot, or the world is like a person with a permanently runny nose.

This might be true, Cratylus. But it might also be very much untrue. So don't be too easily convinced of it. Think it over carefully, like a man, and don't accept such a doctrine without serious examination. You're young -- this is the age for learning. And when you've found the truth, come back and tell me.

Cratylus
I'll do as you say. But I can assure you, Socrates, that I've already been thinking about this for a long time. And after a great deal of reflection and effort, I lean toward Heraclitus.

Socrates
Well then, another day when you come back, you can teach me. But for now, go ahead to the country, as you're planning. And Hermogenes here will see you on your way.

Cratylus
Very good, Socrates. But I hope you'll keep thinking about these things yourself.
