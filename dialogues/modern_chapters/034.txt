PARMENIDES (Part 1)

Persons of the dialogue:
  Cephalus (narrator)
  Adeimantus
  Glaucon
  Antiphon
  Pythodorus
  Socrates
  Zeno
  Parmenides
  Aristoteles

---

We had traveled from our home in Clazomenae to Athens, and we ran into Adeimantus and Glaucon in the Agora.

"Welcome, Cephalus!" said Adeimantus, taking me by the hand. "Is there anything we can do for you here in Athens?"

"Actually, yes -- that's exactly why I'm here. I need to ask a favor."

"What is it?" he said.

"I'm trying to remember the name of your half-brother. He was just a child the last time I came here from Clazomenae, and that was a long time ago. His father's name was Pyrilampes, if I remember right?"

"Yes," he said. "And our brother's name is Antiphon. But why do you ask?"

"Let me introduce some countrymen of mine," I said. "They're lovers of philosophy, and they've heard that Antiphon was close with a man named Pythodorus, a friend of Zeno's. Apparently Pythodorus often recited to him a famous conversation that took place many years ago between Socrates, Zeno, and Parmenides. They'd like to hear it."

"That's quite true," he said.

"So could we hear it?" I asked.

"Nothing easier," he replied. "When Antiphon was young, he studied it very carefully. These days his interests have taken a different turn -- like his grandfather Antiphon, he's devoted to horses. But if that's what you want, let's go find him. He lives in Melita, which is nearby. He just left us to go home."

So we went to find him. He was at home, in the middle of giving a bridle to a metalworker to be fitted. When he was done with the smith, his brothers explained why we'd come. He greeted me as someone he remembered from my earlier visit, and we asked him to repeat the conversation. At first he wasn't eager -- he complained about how much work it would be -- but eventually he agreed.

He told us that Pythodorus had described the arrival of Parmenides and Zeno in Athens. They came for the great festival of the Panathenaea. Parmenides was about sixty-five at the time -- his hair was very white with age, but he was still a handsome man. Zeno was nearly forty, tall and good-looking. In his youth, it was said, he had been Parmenides' beloved.

They were staying with Pythodorus in the Ceramicus, just outside the city walls. Socrates -- who was then a very young man -- came to see them, along with many others. They wanted to hear Zeno's writings, which had been brought to Athens for the first time on this visit. Zeno read them aloud himself, while Parmenides was out. He was almost done when Pythodorus came in, bringing Parmenides and a man named Aristoteles -- who later became one of the Thirty Tyrants -- and they caught the tail end of the reading. Pythodorus had heard Zeno read these arguments before.


THE CHALLENGE TO ZENO

When the reading was finished, Socrates asked that the first thesis of the first argument be read again. After it was, he said:

"What exactly are you arguing here, Zeno? You're saying that if reality is many things, then those things must be both like and unlike -- but that's impossible, because what's like can't be unlike, and what's unlike can't be like. Is that your position?"

"Exactly," said Zeno.

"And if what's unlike can't be like, and what's like can't be unlike, then reality can't be many -- because that would lead to an impossibility. Isn't that the whole point? Every section of your treatise is meant to be a separate proof that plurality doesn't exist -- you've written as many proofs against plurality as you have arguments. Is that right, or am I missing something?"

"No," said Zeno, "you've understood my purpose exactly."

"I see, Parmenides," said Socrates, "that Zeno here isn't just your friend -- he's practically your literary alter ego. He's saying the same thing you say, just from a different angle, and trying to make us think he's telling us something new. You say in your poem that the All is One, and you give excellent proofs. He turns around and says there is no Many, and backs it up with overwhelming evidence. You affirm unity; he denies plurality. You're both saying the same thing from opposite directions -- and you've fooled the world into thinking you're making different arguments. That's a level of artistry most of us can't match."

"Well, Socrates," said Zeno with a smile, "you track an argument like a Spartan hound. But you haven't quite understood the real motive behind my work. It's not as artificial as you think. There was no grand scheme, no intention to deceive anyone. The truth is, I wrote these arguments to defend Parmenides against people who mocked him -- people who tried to show that his claim that reality is one leads to ridiculous and contradictory results. My answer was to hit back: I showed that their own hypothesis -- that reality is many -- leads to consequences even more ridiculous than the ones they complained about. It was youthful pugnacity, not the ambition of an older man. And actually, someone stole my copy before I meant to publish it, so I never even chose to make it public. But apart from that detail, Socrates, your analysis is perfectly right."


SOCRATES' THEORY OF FORMS

"Fair enough," said Socrates. "But let me ask you this, Zeno. Don't you think there's a Form of Likeness in itself, and another Form of Unlikeness -- the opposite of Likeness? And that you and I and everything else we call 'many' participate in these two Forms? Things that participate in Likeness become like to that degree; things that participate in Unlikeness become unlike to that degree. And some things participate in both, and so they're both like and unlike at the same time.

"What's so surprising about that? Now, if someone could prove that Likeness itself becomes unlike, or Unlikeness itself becomes like -- that would genuinely amaze me. But there's nothing extraordinary about showing that ordinary things, which merely participate in Likeness and Unlikeness, turn out to have both qualities.

"The same goes for the One and the Many. If someone could show me that the One itself -- the Form of Unity -- is many, or that the Many itself is one, I'd be truly astonished. But it's no big deal to prove that I am both many and one. When someone wants to show I'm many, they point out I have a right side and a left side, a front and a back, upper and lower halves -- I can't deny I participate in plurality. When they want to show I'm one, they point out that of the seven of us here, I'm one person who participates in unity. Both are true, and neither is paradoxical.

"Same thing with wood, stones, and everything else. If you show that such things are both one and many, fine -- you've shown that the one and the many coexist. But you haven't shown that the Many is one or the One is many. That would just be a truism, not a paradox.

"But here's what would really impress me: if someone could take the abstract Forms themselves -- Likeness and Unlikeness, One and Many, Rest and Motion -- the things grasped by pure reason -- and show that they can mix and separate among themselves. That, Zeno, you've handled with real energy. But as I said, I'd be far more amazed if someone could show that the same kind of puzzle and entanglement you've demonstrated in visible things also exists in the Forms themselves -- in the things we grasp through reason alone."


PARMENIDES' CRITIQUE

While Socrates was speaking, Pythodorus kept watching Parmenides and Zeno. They weren't entirely pleased with the direction the argument was heading, but they listened intently, glancing at each other from time to time with smiles that seemed to express admiration. When Socrates finished, Parmenides spoke up.

"Socrates," he said, "I admire your philosophical instincts. Tell me -- was this distinction your own idea? This separation between the Forms themselves and the things that participate in them? Do you believe there's a Form of Likeness that exists apart from whatever likeness we ourselves possess? A Form of the One, of the Many, and of everything else Zeno mentioned?"

"I do," said Socrates.

Parmenides continued: "And would you also say there are absolute Forms of Justice, Beauty, and Goodness -- that whole class of things?"

"Yes," he said. "I would."

"What about a Form of Human Being, apart from all of us actual humans? Or Forms of Fire and Water?"

"Those I'm often unsure about, Parmenides -- whether I should include them or not."

"And what about things that might seem laughable to mention -- things like hair, mud, dirt, or anything else that's cheap and trivial? Would you say each of these has a Form distinct from the actual objects we encounter?"

"Certainly not," said Socrates. "Those things just are what they appear to be. I'm afraid it would be absurd to suppose there's a Form of them. But I admit it bothers me sometimes. I start to wonder whether maybe everything has a Form -- but then I pull back, because I'm afraid of falling into a bottomless pit of nonsense. So I return to the kinds of things I was just talking about -- Justice, Beauty, Goodness -- and I focus my attention on those."

"That, Socrates," said Parmenides, "is because you're still young. The time will come -- if I'm not mistaken -- when philosophy will get a firmer grip on you, and you won't look down on even the lowliest things. Right now, you care too much about what people think. But tell me: you're saying there are certain Forms, and other things participate in them and get their names from that participation? Things become similar by participating in Similarity, great by participating in Greatness, just and beautiful by participating in Justice and Beauty?"

"Yes," said Socrates, "that's exactly what I mean."


THE WHOLE-OR-PART DILEMMA

"Then does each thing participate in the whole Form, or just a part of it? Is there any other way to participate?"

"I don't see how there could be," Socrates said.

"So do you think the whole Form -- which is one thing -- is present in its entirety in each of the many things that participate in it?"

"Why not, Parmenides?" said Socrates.

"Because one and the same thing would exist as a whole, simultaneously, in many separate individuals. It would be in a state of separation from itself."

"Not necessarily," said Socrates. "Think of it like daylight -- it's one and the same everywhere at once, and yet it doesn't split apart. Maybe each Form is like that: one and the same, present in all things simultaneously."

"That's a nice image, Socrates -- making one thing be in many places at once. It's as if you spread a sail over a group of people and said: there's one thing covering them all. Isn't that what you mean?"

"I suppose so."

"But would the whole sail be over each person? Or only a part of it -- a different part over each one?"

"Just a part."

"Then, Socrates, the Forms themselves would be divisible, and the things that participate in them would have only a portion of each Form -- not the whole thing."

"That seems to follow."

"So are you really prepared to say that a single Form is actually divided up, and yet remains one?"

"Certainly not," he said.

"Think about it. If you divide Greatness itself, and each great thing is great by virtue of a portion of Greatness that's less than absolute Greatness -- doesn't that seem absurd?"

"It does."

"Or take Equality. If something has a small portion of Equality -- less than Equality itself -- could that make it equal to something else?"

"Impossible."

"Or consider Smallness. Suppose one of us has a portion of Smallness. That portion is only a part of Smallness itself, so Smallness itself is greater than the portion. Now, if you add that portion of Smallness to something, should that thing become smaller or larger? It would have to become larger -- because you've added something to it -- but we're saying it's become smaller. That's absurd!"

"Completely absurd!"

"Then how, Socrates, can things participate in the Forms, if they can't participate as wholes or as parts?"

"That," said Socrates, "is not an easy question to answer."


THE THIRD MAN ARGUMENT

"Well," said Parmenides, "what about this problem?"

"What problem?"

"I imagine the way you arrive at a single Form for each kind goes something like this: you look at a number of great things, and when you see them all together, there seems to be one single nature common to them all. So you conclude that Greatness is one thing."

"That's right," said Socrates.

"But now consider: if you hold Greatness itself in your mind alongside the great things that participate in it, and compare them all together -- won't another Greatness appear? One that seems to be the source of the greatness they all share?"

"It would seem so."

"Then you'd have a new Form of Greatness, on top of the original Greatness and the particular great things. And then another one above all of those, in virtue of which they're all great. And so on -- each Form, instead of being one, multiplies infinitely."


THE FORMS AS THOUGHTS

"But wait," said Socrates. "Maybe the Forms are just thoughts -- they exist only in our minds, Parmenides. That way, each Form would still be one, and you'd avoid the infinite multiplication problem."

"So, can there be a thought that's a thought of nothing?"

"That's impossible," he said.

"It must be a thought of something?"

"Yes."

"Of something that exists, or something that doesn't?"

"Of something that exists."

"Isn't it a thought of some one thing -- something the thought recognizes as a single nature applying to all cases?"

"Yes."

"And won't that single something -- apprehended as one and the same across all instances -- be a Form?"

"There's no escaping that conclusion."

"Then," said Parmenides, "if you say everything else participates in the Forms, doesn't that mean either that everything is made of thoughts and everything thinks -- or that there are thoughts that exist but don't think at all?"

"Neither of those makes any sense," said Socrates. "Here's what I think makes more sense, Parmenides: the Forms are like patterns fixed in the nature of things. Other things resemble them -- they're copies of them. 'Participation' really just means resemblance."


THE RESEMBLANCE REGRESS

"But," said Parmenides, "if the copy resembles the Form, mustn't the Form also resemble the copy? Isn't resemblance a two-way street -- like can only be like what's like it?"

"It must be."

"And when two things are alike, don't they share in the same Form?"

"They must."

"And the thing that makes them alike -- that they both share -- won't that be the Form itself?"

"Exactly."

"Then the Form can't be like its copy, or the copy like the Form. Because if they're alike, some further Form of Likeness will always crop up. And if that new Form is like anything, yet another will appear. You'll get an endless chain of new Forms, one after another, if the Form resembles what participates in it."

"That's quite true."

"So participation by resemblance has to be abandoned. We need some other account of how things participate in Forms."

"It would seem so."

"Do you see, then, Socrates, how enormous the difficulty is, if you maintain that Forms exist as absolute, independent realities?"

"I do see that."


THE GREATEST DIFFICULTY

"And let me tell you," said Parmenides, "you've only begun to grasp how deep the problems go when you separate each Form off on its own."

"What do you mean?"

"There are many problems, but the greatest is this: if someone argues that these Forms, being what we say they must be, are fundamentally unknowable to us -- you couldn't prove them wrong. Not unless the challenger had extraordinary ability and was willing to follow a long and painstaking demonstration. Otherwise, they'd remain unconvinced and insist the Forms can't be known."

"What do you mean, Parmenides?" said Socrates.

"Here's the issue. I think you -- or anyone who holds that these absolute essences exist -- would agree that they can't exist in us."

"Of course not," said Socrates. "If they were in us, they wouldn't be absolute anymore."

"Exactly. So when the Forms relate to one another, their nature is determined by their relationships among themselves -- it has nothing to do with the likenesses or copies or whatever you want to call them that exist in our world. The things in our world that share names with the Forms are also defined only in relation to one another, not in relation to the Forms. They belong to our sphere, not to the sphere of the Forms."

"What do you mean?" said Socrates.

"Let me give you an example," said Parmenides. "Take mastery and slavery. A master has a slave, and there's nothing absolute about that relationship -- it's just one person related to another. But there's also a Form of Mastery in the abstract, which relates to the Form of Slavery in the abstract. These abstract natures have nothing to do with us, and we have nothing to do with them. They exist in their own sphere; we exist in ours. You see the point?"

"Yes," said Socrates, "I see."

"And absolute Knowledge would correspond to absolute Truth?"

"Yes."

"And each kind of absolute Knowledge would correspond to each kind of absolute Being?"

"Right."

"But our knowledge -- the knowledge we actually have -- corresponds to our truth, the truth within our sphere. And each kind of knowledge we have is knowledge of each kind of being we have."

"Necessarily."

"But we don't have the Forms themselves -- you've admitted that. We can't have them."

"No, we can't."

"And the absolute natures of things are known by the Form of Knowledge itself?"

"Yes."

"Which we don't have?"

"We don't."

"Then none of the Forms are known to us, since we don't have any share in absolute Knowledge?"

"It seems not."

"So the true nature of Beauty itself, of Goodness itself, and of all the other Forms we take to be absolute realities -- all of these are unknown to us?"

"It would seem so."

"But here's an even stranger consequence."

"What?"

"Would you agree that if absolute Knowledge exists, it must be far more exact than our knowledge? The same goes for absolute Beauty and everything else?"

"Yes."

"And if any being participates in absolute Knowledge, no one is more likely to have it than God?"

"Certainly."

"But then -- would God, having absolute Knowledge, be able to know human things?"

"Why wouldn't he?"

"Because, Socrates," said Parmenides, "we've established that the Forms don't apply to human things, and human things don't apply to the Forms. Each sphere's relationships are limited to its own domain."

"Yes, we did say that."

"So if God has this perfect authority and this perfect knowledge, his authority can't govern us, and his knowledge can't know us or any human thing. Just as our authority doesn't extend to the gods and our knowledge can't grasp anything divine, so too, by the very same reasoning, the gods -- being gods -- are neither our masters nor able to know human affairs."

"But surely," said Socrates, "to say that God lacks knowledge is monstrous."


THE WAY FORWARD

"These, Socrates," said Parmenides, "are just a few -- and only a few -- of the difficulties that arise if the Forms really exist and you insist each one is an absolute unity. Anyone who hears the case against them will deny that the Forms exist at all. And even if they do exist, he'll say they must be unknowable to human beings. He'll seem to have good reasons on his side, and he'll be extremely hard to convince. It would take a person of extraordinary ability to learn that everything has its own class and absolute essence. And even more remarkable would be the person who discovers all this on their own, works through the problems thoroughly, and can teach it all to others."

"I agree with you, Parmenides," said Socrates. "What you say is very much what I think too."

"And yet, Socrates," said Parmenides, "if someone, fixing their attention on these difficulties and others like them, does away with the Forms entirely -- refuses to admit that every individual thing has its own determinate Form, always one and the same -- they'll destroy the very power of reasoning. They'll have nothing for the mind to rest on. You seem to have noticed this yourself."

"That's very true," said Socrates.

"So what's to become of philosophy? Which way do we turn, if the Forms are unknowable?"

"I certainly don't see my way forward right now."

"That, Socrates," said Parmenides, "is because you're trying to define Beauty, Justice, Goodness, and the rest of the Forms without enough prior training. I noticed the same tendency when I heard you talking the other day with your friend Aristoteles. The impulse that draws you toward philosophy is noble and divine. But there's a method of inquiry that most people dismiss as idle chatter and think is useless. You need to train yourself in it while you're still young, or the truth will slip through your fingers."

"What kind of training do you mean, Parmenides?"

"The kind Zeno was practicing. Though I give you credit for telling him that you weren't interested in examining the puzzles as they apply to visible things -- only to objects of thought, to what we might call the Forms."

"Yes," said Socrates, "because it seems easy enough to show that visible things are both like and unlike, or experience any number of opposite qualities. There's no difficulty in that."

"True," said Parmenides. "But you need to go a step further. Don't just consider the consequences that follow from a given hypothesis. Consider also the consequences that follow from denying it. That will be much better training."

"What do you mean?"

"Take Zeno's hypothesis about the Many. You should investigate not only what follows for the Many in relation to themselves and to the One, and for the One in relation to itself and the Many -- assuming the Many exist. You should also investigate what follows for the One and the Many, in their relation to themselves and to each other, if the Many do not exist.

"Or again, take Likeness. If Likeness exists, or if it doesn't -- what follows in either case, both for the things in question and for everything else, in their relation to themselves and to each other?

"The same goes for Unlikeness, Motion and Rest, Coming-into-Being and Destruction, and even Being and Not-Being themselves. In a word: whenever you suppose something to be or not to be, or to be affected in any way, you must examine the consequences for the thing itself and for any other things you choose -- each one individually, several together, and all of them. And then you must look at those other things in relation to themselves and to whatever else you suppose to exist or not exist. If you want to train yourself perfectly and see the real truth, that's what you have to do."

"Parmenides!" said Socrates. "That's an enormous task. I'm not sure I fully understand it. Would you take some hypothesis and work through the steps? Then I'd grasp what you mean."

"That's a lot to ask of a man my age, Socrates."

"Then would you do it, Zeno?" said Socrates.

Zeno smiled. "Let's direct our request to Parmenides himself. He's quite right that you barely realize how much work you're asking for. And if there were a bigger audience I wouldn't press him -- these aren't subjects anyone, especially at his age, should speak about before a crowd. Most people don't realize that this roundabout journey through every possibility is the only way the mind can arrive at truth and wisdom. So I'll join your request, Parmenides -- I haven't heard you run through this exercise in a long time."

At this point -- according to what Antiphon told us Pythodorus had said -- Pythodorus himself, along with Aristoteles and the whole group, urged Parmenides to demonstrate the method.

"I can't refuse," said Parmenides. "Though I feel rather like old Ibycus -- the poet who fell in love in his old age, against his will, and compared himself to an old racehorse trembling at the starting gate, knowing the course all too well. That's how I feel right now, trembling at the thought of the ocean of arguments I have to wade through at my age. But I must oblige you, as Zeno says. And since we're among friends -- where shall I begin? What hypothesis shall we take on first, if I'm to attempt this exhausting exercise? Shall I begin with my own hypothesis -- the One itself? Shall we examine what follows from the assumption that the One exists, and also what follows if it doesn't?"

"By all means," said Zeno.

"And who will answer my questions?" said Parmenides. "I propose the youngest. He'll be the most agreeable, the most likely to say what he really thinks, and his answers will give me time to catch my breath."

"That's me, Parmenides," said Aristoteles. "I'm the youngest, and I'm at your service. Ask, and I'll answer."


HYPOTHESIS I.a: IF THE ONE IS (taken strictly) -- What follows for the One itself

"Very well," said Parmenides. "If the One is -- if it truly is one -- then it can't be many, can it?"

"No, it can't."

"Then it can't have parts, and it can't be a whole."

"Why not?"

"Because every part is part of a whole."

"Yes."

"And what's a whole? Isn't a whole something from which no part is missing?"

"Yes."

"Then either way -- whether as a whole made of parts, or as something with parts -- the One would be made up of parts."

"That's right."

"And either way, the One would be many, not one."

"True."

"But it's supposed to be one, not many."

"Yes."

"So if the One is to remain one, it won't be a whole and won't have parts."

"Right."

"And if it has no parts, it can't have a beginning, middle, or end -- because those would be parts of it."

"That follows."

"But a beginning and an end are the limits of everything."

"Yes."

"So the One, having neither beginning nor end, is unlimited."

"Unlimited, yes."

"And therefore shapeless. It can't be round or straight."

"Why not?"

"Because 'round' means all the outer points are equidistant from the center."

"Right."

"And 'straight' means the center lies between the two endpoints."

"Yes."

"But the One would need to have parts to be either round or straight. Since it has no parts, it's neither."

"That follows."

"And being like this, it can't be anywhere -- neither in something else nor in itself."

"Why?"

"If it were in something else, it would be surrounded by whatever contained it, and would be touched at many points and on many sides. But something that's one and indivisible and has no roundness can't be touched all around at many points."

"Certainly not."

"On the other hand, if the One were in itself, it would be contained by nothing other than itself -- which is what 'being in itself' would mean."

"Right."

"But then, the container would have to be different from the contained. The same thing can't do both at once. So the One would have to be two things."

"True."

"So the One can't be anywhere -- not in itself, not in anything else."

"It can't."

"Now consider: can something like this have motion or rest?"

"Why not?"

"Because motion is either change of place or change of nature."

"Right."

"And if the One changes its nature and becomes something different, it's no longer one."

"Clearly not."

"So it can't experience that kind of motion."

"No."

"What about moving through space?"

"If the One moves through space, it either spins in one spot or goes from one place to another."

"Yes."

"But to spin, it would need to rest on a center and have different parts at different distances from that center. Something with no center and no parts can't spin."

"Impossible."

"Well, does it travel from one place to another?"

"Maybe -- if it moves at all."

"But we already showed it can't be in anything."

"Right."

"So it's even more impossible for it to come to be in something."

"How so?"

"Because anything that's in the process of arriving somewhere is partly in and partly out of its destination. But something without parts can never be partly in and partly out of anything."

"True."

"And it's even more impossible for something without parts, something that isn't even a whole, to arrive somewhere -- since it can't arrive either as a part or as a whole."

"That follows."

"So the One doesn't change place by spinning, doesn't come into being somewhere new, and doesn't change its own nature."

"Right."

"Then with respect to every kind of motion, the One is immovable."

"Immovable."

"But we also said it can't be in anything."

"We did."

"So it's never in the same place."

"Why not?"

"Because 'the same place' would be something it's in, and we said it can't be in anything."

"Exactly."

"And it can't be in itself, and can't be in anything else."

"Right."

"So the One is never in the same place."

"Apparently not."

"But what's never in the same place is never at rest."

"Right."

"So the One is neither at rest nor in motion."

"That seems to follow."

"Furthermore, it can't be the same as anything else, or different from anything else. And it can't be the same as itself, or different from itself."

"How so?"

"If it were different from itself, it would be different from one -- and wouldn't be one."

"True."

"And if it were the same as something else, it would be that other thing, not itself. It wouldn't have the nature of one -- it would be other than one."

"Right."

"So it won't be the same as something else, or different from itself."

"It won't."

"And it can't be different from something else while remaining one. 'Different' isn't a property of one as such -- it belongs to 'the different' and nothing else."

"Right."

"So it's not by being one that it's different from anything."

"No."

"And if not by being one, then not by being itself. And if not by being itself, then it isn't itself different. And since it's not different at all, it won't be different from anything."

"Correct."

"Neither can the One be the same as itself."

"Why not?"

"Because the nature of 'the same' is not the nature of 'the one.'"

"How so?"

"When something becomes the same as something, it doesn't become one."

"What do you mean?"

"If something becomes the same as many things, it becomes many, not one."

"True."

"But if there were no difference between 'one' and 'the same,' then whenever something became 'the same,' it would also become 'one,' and vice versa."

"Yes."

"So if the One were the same as itself, it wouldn't be one with itself -- it would be one and also not one."

"That's impossible."

"Therefore the One can be neither different from something else nor the same as itself."

"Right."

"And so the One can be neither the same nor different, either in relation to itself or to anything else."

"That follows."

"Now: the One will be neither like nor unlike anything -- not itself, not anything else."

"Why?"

"Because likeness is sameness of properties."

"Yes."

"And we showed that sameness has a different nature from oneness."

"We did."

"And if the One had any property other than being one, that would make it more than one -- which is impossible."

"Yes."

"So the One can never have the same properties as anything else -- or as itself."

"Clearly not."

"Then it can't be like anything -- not another thing, not itself."

"No."

"And it can't be affected so as to be different either -- because that would make it more than one."

"Right."

"Something affected in a way different from itself or from another would be unlike itself or the other -- since sameness of properties is likeness."

"Yes."

"But the One is never affected differently, so it's never unlike."

"Never."

"So the One is neither like nor unlike -- not itself, not anything else."

"Plainly not."

"And being of this nature, it can be neither equal nor unequal -- not to itself, not to anything else."

"Why?"

"Because to be equal, it would need to have the same measures as what it's equal to."

"Yes."

"And to be greater or less than something commensurable, it would need more or fewer measures."

"Right."

"And for things not commensurable with it, it would be larger-measured than the smaller and smaller-measured than the larger."

"Yes."

"But something that doesn't share in sameness can't have the same measures, or the same anything."

"Impossible."

"And without the same measures, it can't be equal to itself or anything else."

"It seems not."

"But if it had more or fewer measures, it would have as many parts as measures -- and so the One would no longer be one but many."

"Right."

"And if it were of exactly one measure, it would be equal to that measure -- but we just showed it can't be equal to anything."

"We did."

"So the One has no share in one measure or many or few, no share in sameness at all. It's not equal to itself or anything else. It's not greater or less than itself or anything else."

"That all follows."

"Now: do we think the One can be older or younger than anything, or the same age?"

"Why couldn't it?"

"Because something the same age as itself or anything else would have to share in equality or likeness of time. And we said the One doesn't share in either."

"We did."

"And we said it doesn't share in inequality or unlikeness either."

"Yes."

"So how can the One, being like this, be older or younger than anything, or the same age?"

"It can't."

"Then the One can't be older, younger, or the same age as anything -- not itself, not anything else."

"Clearly not."

"But then the One can't be in time at all. Because anything in time must always be growing older than itself."

"Necessarily."

"And what's older is always older than something younger."

"Yes."

"So what becomes older than itself simultaneously becomes younger than itself -- since it has to have something younger to be older than."

"What do you mean?"

"Here's what I mean. Something doesn't need to become different from what's already different from it -- it already is different. If the difference has come about, it has become different. If it will come about, it will be different. But what's in the process of becoming different can only be becoming different from what's also becoming -- not from what has been or will be."

"That's necessarily true."

"But 'older' is a difference relative to 'younger' -- and nothing else."

"Yes."

"So what becomes older than itself must simultaneously become younger than itself."

"Right."

"But it also can't become for a longer or shorter time than itself -- it must become and exist and have existed for exactly the same time as itself."

"That's also necessary."

"So things that are in time and share in time must be the same age as themselves, and must also simultaneously become both older and younger than themselves."

"It seems so."

"But the One doesn't share in any of those properties."

"Not at all."

"So the One doesn't share in time and isn't in any time."

"That's what the argument shows."

"And 'was,' 'has become,' and 'was becoming' -- don't those words signify participation in past time?"

"Yes."

"And 'will be,' 'will become,' 'will have become' -- participation in future time?"

"Yes."

"And 'is' and 'becomes' -- participation in present time?"

"Certainly."

"So if the One has absolutely no participation in time, then it never came to be, never was becoming, never was. It hasn't now come to be, isn't becoming, isn't. It will not come to be, will not have come to be, will not be."

"That's absolutely right."

"But can anything participate in being in any way other than through these?"

"It can't."

"Then the One can't participate in being at all."

"It seems not."

"Then the One doesn't exist in any way."

"Apparently not."

"It doesn't even exist enough to be one. Because if it were one, it would already exist and participate in being. But as the argument shows, the One neither is nor is one."

"That seems right."

"And what doesn't exist can't have any attribute or relation."

"Of course not."

"Then there's no name for it, no description, no perception, no opinion, no knowledge of it."

"Clearly not."

"It isn't named, isn't spoken of, isn't the subject of any opinion, isn't known, and nothing that exists perceives it."

"That seems to follow."

"But can all this really be true about the One?"

"I don't think it can."


HYPOTHESIS I.b: IF THE ONE IS (taken as participating in being) -- What follows for the One itself

"Then let's go back to the beginning and start again. Let's see whether, on further review, things look different."

"I'm very happy to do that."

"We're asking: if the One is, what are all the consequences, whatever they may be?"

"Yes."

"Let's start at the very beginning. If the One is -- if it exists -- can it exist without participating in being?"

"No, it can't."

"Then the One has being. But its being isn't the same thing as the One. If it were, 'being' wouldn't be the being of the One, and the One wouldn't have participated in being -- saying 'the One is' would just mean 'the One is one.' But our hypothesis isn't 'if the One is one, what follows?' It's 'if the One is' -- right?"

"Exactly right."

"So 'is' means something different from 'one'?"

"Yes."

"When we say briefly 'the One is,' we're saying 'the One participates in being'?"

"Exactly."

"So let's ask again: if the One is, what follows? Doesn't this hypothesis imply that the One has parts?"

"How so?"

"Like this: if 'being' is predicated of the One -- the One is -- and 'one' is predicated of being -- being is one -- and if being and one aren't the same thing, then since the One that we've assumed does exist, it must be a whole whose parts are 'one' and 'being.'"

"Necessarily."

"And is each of these parts -- 'one' and 'being' -- simply a part, or is the word 'part' relative to the word 'whole'?"

"It's relative to the whole."

"So what exists as one is both a whole and has parts."

"Yes."

"Now, take each part of the One-that-is -- that is, the 'being' part and the 'one' part. Can either one lack the other? Can the one-part lack being, or the being-part lack oneness?"

"No, impossible."

"So each part also contains both 'one' and 'being' -- each part is itself made up of at least two parts. And the same principle keeps going forever: every part always has these two elements, because being always involves one and one always involves being. So the one is always disappearing into two."

"Exactly."

"Then the One, if it is, must be infinite in multiplicity?"

"It seems so."

"Let's try a different approach."

"Which one?"

"We said the One participates in being, and therefore it is."

"Yes."

"And in this way, the One turns out to be many."

"Right."

"Now, let's mentally separate the One from the being it participates in. Consider just the One alone, apart from its being. Will this abstract One be just one, or many?"

"Just one, I think."

"Let's see. The being of the One must be different from the One -- because the One is not being; rather, it's the One that participated in being."

"Yes."

"Now, if 'being' and 'the one' are two different things, it's not because the one is one that it differs from being, and it's not because being is being that it differs from the one. They differ from each other by virtue of Difference and Otherness."

"Yes."

"So Difference is not the same as either the One or Being."

"Clearly not."

"Now, if we pick any two of these three -- Being and Difference, or Being and the One, or the One and Difference -- in every case we've picked two things that can rightly be called 'both.'"

"What do you mean?"

"You can speak of Being?"

"Yes."

"And of the One?"

"Yes."

"So now we've spoken of each of them?"

"We have."

"And when I say 'Being and the One,' I speak of them both?"

"Yes."

"And 'Being and Difference,' or 'the One and Difference' -- in each case, I'm speaking of a 'both'?"

"Right."

"And what's correctly called 'both' must be two?"

"Undoubtedly."

"And of two things, how could either one not be one?"

"It couldn't."

"So if the items in each pair are two, each item must be one."

"Yes."

"And if each is one, then adding any one to any pair makes three."

"Yes."

"And three is odd, and two is even."

"Of course."

"And if there are two, there must be twice, and if there are three, there must be thrice -- since twice-one makes two, and thrice-one makes three."

"Necessarily."

"And if there are two and twice, there's twice-two; if three and thrice, there's thrice-three."

"Of course."

"And if there are three and twice, there's twice-three; and if two and thrice, there's thrice-two."

"Undoubtedly."

"So we have even-times-even, odd-times-odd, even-times-odd, and odd-times-even."

"Yes."

"And if that's so, is there any number that's left out? Any number that doesn't necessarily exist?"

"None at all."

"Then if the One is, number must also exist."

"Necessarily."

"But if number exists, there must be many things -- in fact, an infinite multiplicity of beings. Because number is infinite in multiplicity, and every number participates in being, doesn't it?"

"It certainly does."

"And if all number participates in being, then every part of number participates too?"

"Yes."

"So being is distributed across the entire multitude of things, and nothing that exists -- however small or however large -- is without it. And really, how could something that exists be without being? That's absurd on its face."

"Completely absurd."

"So being is divided up into things of every size -- the greatest and the smallest and everything in between -- and its divisions have no limit."

"Right."

"So being has the greatest number of parts?"

"The greatest."

"Is there any part that's a part of being yet somehow not a part at all?"

"Impossible."

"Because as long as it exists -- for as long as it is -- it must be one thing and can't be nothing?"

"Necessarily."

"So 'one' attaches to every single part of being -- it doesn't fail in any part, whether large or small or whatever its size."

"Right."

"But think: can the One, in its entirety, be in many places at the same time?"

"No -- I can see that's impossible."

"So if not in its entirety, then it must be divided. Because it can't be present with all the parts of being unless it's divided."

"Right."

"And what has parts will be as many as its parts."

"Necessarily."

"So we were wrong just now to say that being is divided into the greatest number of parts. Being isn't divided into more parts than the One. They're equal: the One is never absent from being, nor being from the One. The two run side by side, co-equal and co-extensive."

"That does seem right."

"So the One itself, broken up by being, is many and infinite."

"Apparently so."

"Not only does 'the One that has being' turn out to be many -- the One itself, distributed by being, must also be many."

"It must."

"Furthermore, since the parts are parts of a whole, the One as a whole must be limited. Aren't the parts contained by the whole?"

"They must be."

"And what contains is a limit."

"Of course."

"Then the One, if it has being, is both one and many, whole and parts -- limited and yet unlimited in number."

"Yes."

"And having limits, it must have extremes?"

"Necessarily."

"And if it's a whole, it must have a beginning, middle, and end. Can anything be a whole without those three? And if any of them is missing, is it still a whole?"

"No."

"Then the One has beginning, middle, and end."

"It does."

"And the middle is equidistant from the extremes, or it wouldn't be in the middle."

"Right."

"So the One will have some shape -- either straight, or round, or some combination."

"Yes."

"And if so, it will be both in itself and in something else."

"How?"

"Every part is in the whole, and none is outside it."

"Yes."

"And all the parts are contained by the whole."

"Yes."

"And the One is all its parts -- no more, no less."

"Right."

"And the One is the whole."

"Yes."

"So if all the parts are in the whole, and the One is both all the parts and the whole, and they're all contained by the whole -- then the One is contained by the One. And thus the One is in itself."

"That's true."

"But then again, the whole is not in the parts -- not in all of them, not in any single one. If it were in all of them, it would have to be in each one. But if there's even one part it's not in, then it can't be in all the parts. And that one part is one of 'all' -- so if the whole isn't in it, how can it be in all of them?"

"It can't."

"And the whole can't be in just some of the parts. If the whole were in some parts, the greater would be in the less -- which is impossible."

"Yes, impossible."

"And if the whole isn't in one part, or several, or all of the parts, it must be in something else -- or it's nowhere at all."

"Right."

"If it were nowhere, it would be nothing. But since it is a whole, and it's not in itself, it must be in something else."

"Exactly."

"So the One, considered as a whole, is in something else. But considered as all its parts, it's in itself. And therefore the One must be both in itself and in something else."

"It must."

"Given this nature, the One must be both at rest and in motion."

"How?"

"It's at rest because it's in itself. If it's in one thing and doesn't leave that thing, it stays in the same place -- namely, itself."

"Yes."

"And what's always in the same place must be at rest."

"Right."

"But on the other hand, what's always in something else can never be in the same place. And if it's never in the same place, it's never at rest. And if not at rest, then in motion."

"True."

"So the One, being always both in itself and in something else, must always be both at rest and in motion."

"It must."

"And it must be the same as itself, and different from itself; and likewise the same as the others, and different from the others. This follows from what we've already shown."

"How?"

"Everything, in relation to everything else, is either the same as it, or different from it. Or if neither the same nor different, then one is a part of the other, or one is a whole of which the other is a part."

"Clearly."

"Now, is the One a part of itself?"

"Certainly not."

"And since it's not a part of itself, it's not related to itself as whole to part."

"No."

"Is the One different from the One?"

"No."

"So it's not different from itself."

"Certainly not."

"If it's neither different from itself, nor a whole to its own part, nor a part of its own whole -- then it must be the same as itself."

"It must."

"But then again, a thing that is in a different place from 'itself' -- while 'itself' remains in the same place with itself -- must be different from 'itself,' mustn't it?"

"I think so."

"And we showed that the One is simultaneously in itself and in something else."

"We did."

"So, it seems, the One will be different from itself."

"It does seem so."

"Now, if anything is different from anything, won't it be different from what is different?"

"Necessarily."

"And everything that's not-one is different from the One, and the One is different from everything that's not-one?"

"Of course."

"So the One is different from the others."

"Different."

"But consider this: 'the same' and 'the different' are opposites of each other, aren't they?"

"Yes."

"Can the same ever be in the different, or the different in the same?"

"No."

"If the different is never in the same, then there's nothing in which the different resides for any span of time -- because during that time, however brief, the different would be in the same. Right?"

"Right."

"And since the different is never in the same, it can never be in anything that exists."

"True."

"So the different isn't in the not-one, and it isn't in the One."

"Certainly not."

"Then it's not by virtue of Difference that the One is different from the not-one, or the not-one different from the One."

"No."

"And they're not different from each other by virtue of themselves -- not if they don't share in Difference."

"How could they be?"

"So if they're not different by virtue of themselves or by virtue of Difference, they must completely escape being different from one another."

"They must."

"But the not-one can't share in the One either -- otherwise it wouldn't be not-one. It would be one in some way."

"True."

"And the not-one can't be a number -- because if it had number, it wouldn't be entirely not-one."

"It would not."

"And is the not-one a part of the One? But then it would share in the One."

"It would."

"So if the One and the not-one are utterly distinct from each other in every way -- the One isn't part or whole of the not-one, and the not-one isn't part or whole of the One..."

"Right."

"But we said that things that are neither parts nor wholes of each other, nor different from each other, will be the same as each other."

"We did."

"Then shall we say the One, standing in this relation to the not-one, is the same as it?"

"Let's say so."

"So the One is both the same as itself and the same as the others, and also different from itself and different from the others."

"That appears to be the conclusion."

"And it will be both like and unlike itself and the others."

"How?"

"Since the One was shown to be different from the others, the others are also different from the One."

"Yes."

"And the One is different from the others to the same degree that the others are different from it -- neither more nor less."

"Right."

"And if neither more nor less, then to a like degree."

"Yes."

"So insofar as the One is affected by being different from the others, and the others are affected in the same way relative to it, the One is affected in the same way as the others."

"What do you mean?"

"Think of it with names. You give a name to something?"

"Yes."

"And you can say the name once, or many times?"

"Yes."

"When you say it once, you name the thing the name belongs to. And when you say it many times, is it something different you're naming? Or is it always the same thing, whether you say the name once or a hundred times?"

"The same thing, of course."

"Now, 'different' is a name given to something?"

"Yes."

"So whenever you use the word 'different' -- once or many times -- you're naming the thing that word applies to, and nothing else."

"Necessarily."

"So when we say that the others are different from the One and the One is different from the others, we're using the word 'different' twice, but we're applying it to the same nature -- the nature of Difference. Right?"

"Yes."

"Then the One, being different from the others, and the others, being different from the One -- they share the same affection of 'being different.' And what shares the same affection is alike."

"Yes."

"So insofar as the One is different from the others, everything will be like everything else -- because everything is different from everything else."

"It seems so."

"But the like is the opposite of the unlike."

"Yes."

"And the different is the opposite of the same."

"Yes."

"And we also showed that the One is the same as the others."

"We did."

"And being the same as the others is the opposite of being different from them."

"Right."

"And insofar as it was different, it was shown to be like."

"Yes."

"But insofar as it's the same, it will be unlike -- because the affection of sameness is the opposite of the affection of difference, and it was the affection of difference that made it like."

"Yes."

"So the sameness makes it unlike. Otherwise sameness wouldn't be the opposite of difference."

"That follows."

"Then the One is both like and unlike the others -- like insofar as it's different, and unlike insofar as it's the same."

"That argument works."

"And there's another argument too."

"What?"

"Insofar as it's affected in the same way, it's not affected differently. And not being affected differently, it's not unlike. And not being unlike, it's like. But insofar as it's affected by being different, it's affected otherwise. And being affected otherwise, it's unlike."

"True."

"So because the One is both the same as the others and different from the others -- on either ground, or on both together -- it will be both like and unlike the others."

"Certainly."

"And in the same way, since it was shown to be both different from itself and the same as itself -- on either ground and on both -- it will be both like and unlike itself."

"Necessarily."

"And what about this: can the One touch or not touch itself and the others? Let's consider."

"Let's."

"We showed that the One is in itself as a whole."

"Yes."

"And also in other things."

"Yes."

"Insofar as it's in other things, it would touch them. Insofar as it's in itself, it would be prevented from touching them and would touch only itself."

"That follows."

"So it would touch both itself and the others."

"It would."
