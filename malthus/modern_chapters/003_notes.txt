CHAPTER 3 TRANSLATION NOTES

1. MAJOR CHANGES

- Chapter synopsis: Modernized language while preserving the structure. "The savage or
  hunter state" became "the hunter-gatherer stage of society"; "Northern Emigration"
  became "northern migration."

- "The passion between the sexes" rendered as "sexual desire" per vocabulary mappings.

- "Helots" (Spartan slaves) replaced with "slaves" since the reference is obscure to
  modern readers and the meaning is clear from context.

- "Hottentots" retained as the historical term used in the original, since Malthus is
  referencing a specific historical observation.

- "Corn, wine, and oil" -- "corn" changed to "grain" per vocabulary mappings;
  wine and oil kept as-is.

- "Zingis Khan" modernized to "Genghis Khan" (standard modern English spelling).

- "The world was all before them where to choose" -- this is a Milton quotation
  (Paradise Lost, Book XII). Kept verbatim as a recognized literary allusion.

- Long sentences broken into shorter ones throughout, but logical connectors preserved.
  Malthus's step-by-step reasoning is maintained.

- "shews" changed to "reveals" (per "shew/shewn" -> "show/shown" mapping, with
  slight variation for readability).

- "The commission of war is vice, and the effect of it misery" -- kept close to original
  as a key formulation, just slightly smoothed.

- British spellings converted: "favour" -> "favor", "labour" -> "labor" (where not
  referring to the specific phrase "to labour under").

- The barbarian migration passage -- Malthus's most vivid, almost poetic prose in this
  chapter. Kept the dramatic imagery ("clouds of barbarians," "blotted out the sun of
  Italy") while modernizing syntax.

2. NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER TRANSLATOR

- Chapter 3 does NOT yet introduce the formal terminology "preventive check" and
  "positive check." Despite the text analysis noting this chapter defines the two types
  of checks, the actual text only discusses misery and vice as general checks without
  using those specific technical terms. The formal terminology appears to be introduced
  in later chapters (likely Ch 4 for preventive, Ch 5 for positive). Do not
  retroactively insert these terms.

- The key conclusion established here: among both hunter-gatherer and pastoral peoples,
  population always grows when food supply increases, and is kept in check only by
  misery and vice. This is the empirical foundation for the theoretical framework
  that follows.

- Voice: Malthus is at his most vivid and almost literary in the barbarian migration
  passages. The next chapters shift to contemporary England (Ch 4-5) and the tone
  becomes more sociological and observational. Expect longer analytical passages and
  less dramatic narrative.

- "Subsistence" is used consistently throughout (not "survival" or alternatives).

- Numbers are spelled out when under 100 (e.g., "five, six, or more children").

- The phrase "misery and vice" as the twin checks on population is now established
  and should be used consistently going forward.
