CHAPTER 11 TRANSLATION NOTES

1. MAJOR CHANGES

- Chapter synopsis: Modernized throughout. "The passion between the sexes" rendered as "sexual desire" per vocabulary mappings. "Mr Godwin's" rendered as "Godwin's" per consistency rules. "Little apparent grounds" becomes "Very little evidence." "Passion of love not inconsistent either with reason or virtue" kept close to original but smoothed.

- "Mr Godwin" rendered as "Godwin" throughout, per consistency rules (already introduced in earlier chapters).

- "the passion between the sexes" rendered as "sexual desire" throughout, per vocabulary mappings. In the opening paragraph, the first instance uses "sexual desire" directly; subsequent references use "this passion" or "sexual desire" as appropriate to context.

- "criminal excesses" rendered as "destructive excess" -- "criminal" in the 18th-century sense meant morally wrong, not necessarily illegal. "Destructive" better captures the self-harming behavior Malthus describes.

- "corporeal debility and mental remorse" rendered as "physical weakness and mental regret" -- straightforward modernization.

- "pleasurable sensations" rendered as "pleasure" or "happiness" depending on context, to avoid the clinical feel.

- "the contemplation of the most improved reason" rendered as "the scrutiny of the sharpest reasoning" -- modernized while keeping Malthus's point that love withstands rational examination.

- Malthus's long sentence about looking back on love ("Perhaps there is scarcely a man...") kept as one long sentence. This is one of his most lyrical passages and the cumulative rhythm is essential to its effect. Minor modernization of syntax only.

- "sensual pleasures" rendered as "physical pleasures" in most instances, to avoid the modern connotation of "sensual" as specifically erotic. Malthus means all bodily pleasures. Exception: kept "sensual" in the Godwin quote about "commerce of the sexes" since the erotic connotation is appropriate there.

- "Archdeacon Paley" -- kept as is. William Paley was well known enough that no additional context is needed; the title "Archdeacon" gives enough signal about his role.

- Godwin's quoted passage ("Strip the commerce of the sexes...") preserved exactly as in the original, since it is a direct quotation. Citation kept.

- The tree analogy: preserved in full. This is one of Malthus's driest and most effective moments -- the comparison of a stripped-down sexual encounter to a bare pole is genuinely funny. Kept close to original.

- "a beautiful woman, and a map of Madagascar" -- preserved exactly. This is one of Malthus's most unexpectedly witty lines, and it needs no modernization.

- The long quoted description of feminine attractions ("the symmetry of person, the vivacity, the voluptuous softness of temper...") kept in quotation marks as Malthus is quoting Godwin's own language back at him.

- "the general interests of society" rendered as "society" in condensed form, but kept closer to original as "deeply harmful to society" to preserve Malthus's point about public vs. private harm.

- "philosophical probabilities" rendered as "scientific evidence" -- "philosophical" in the 18th-century sense meant empirical/scientific.

- "the brightest ornaments of the species" rendered as "the most brilliant individuals alive today" -- modernized a heavily ornamental phrase.

- "sensibly to affect population" rendered as "meaningfully affect population growth."

- Final paragraph: "the lower classes of people" rendered as "the working poor" per vocabulary mappings. "Free from want and labour" kept close to original but smoothed.

- British spellings converted: "labour" to "labor" in running text (kept in the final sentence as part of the phrase "want and labor").

- "notwithstanding" rendered as "despite" per vocabulary mappings.

- "shew" rendered as "demonstrate" per vocabulary mappings.

- "endeavoured" rendered as "tried" and Americanized.

2. NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER TRANSLATOR

- Chapter 12 is an extended critique of Godwin's system, focusing on self-love vs. benevolence. The tone continues the measured, respectful critique of Godwin established in Chapters 10-11.

- "Godwin" (not "Mr Godwin") is the established form of address. Use "Godwin" after any first mention.

- "Sexual desire" is the established rendering of "the passion between the sexes." If this phrase recurs in Chapter 12, maintain this rendering.

- "Physical pleasures" is the preferred rendering of "sensual pleasures" (to avoid modern erotic connotations of "sensual"). Exception: when the context is specifically sexual, "sensual" is fine.

- The preventive check and positive check are established terms requiring no redefinition.

- Malthus's dry humor has been prominent in this chapter (the tree/bare pole analogy, the Madagascar line). Chapter 12 may have similar moments -- let them breathe.

- Maintain American spellings: labor, favor, color, etc.

- "Philosophical" in Malthus's usage means "scientific" or "empirical" -- render accordingly.

- Paley has been introduced as "Archdeacon Paley" -- subsequent references can just use "Paley."

- This chapter ends on a note of pessimism about whether the working poor can ever achieve enough intellectual development to diminish sexual desire. Chapter 12 picks up the broader critique of Godwin. The pessimistic undertone should carry forward.
