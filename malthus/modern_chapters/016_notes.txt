CHAPTER 16 TRANSLATION NOTES

1. MAJOR CHANGES

- IMPORTANT NOTE ON CHAPTER CONTENT: The text analysis lists Chapter 16 as "Critique of Condorcet's Sketch," but the actual file (016.txt) contains the chapter about Adam Smith's error in equating national wealth with labor funds. Condorcet does not appear in this chapter at all. The chapter numbering in the text analysis appears to be off by one or two for this section of the book. This translation faithfully renders the actual content of the source file.

- Chapter synopsis: Fully modernized. "Dr Adam Smith" rendered as "Adam Smith" per consistency rules (after first mention in the synopsis, where it appears without "Dr" since previous chapters have already introduced him). "Revenue or stock of a society" rendered as "a nation's income or capital." "Funds for the maintenance of labour" rendered as "the money available to pay workers" in the synopsis and "the funds available to support labor" / "the real funds for supporting labor" in the body, following vocabulary mappings. "The labouring poor" rendered as "the working poor" per mappings. "China" section kept as-is since it's a proper noun.

- "Dr Adam Smith" rendered as "Adam Smith" throughout, per consistency rules (already introduced in earlier chapters). The first mention in the body text retains "Adam Smith" without the "Dr" prefix.

- "the funds destined for the maintenance of labour" -- this is a key recurring phrase in the chapter. Rendered variously as "the funds set aside for supporting labor," "the real funds for supporting labor," "the funds available to support labor," and "the money available to pay workers." Varied to avoid monotony while keeping the concept clear. The word "real" is added in several instances where Malthus distinguishes between nominal and effective labor funds.

- "the lower orders of society" / "the lower classes of the people" -- rendered as "the lower classes" per text analysis guidance. Malthus's class language preserved faithfully without softening.

- "the Revolution" -- this refers to the Glorious Revolution of 1688, not the French Revolution. Added "of 1688" on first mention to avoid confusion, since the French Revolution has been discussed extensively in earlier chapters.

- "corn" rendered as "grain" throughout per vocabulary mappings (three instances).

- "notwithstanding" rendered as contextual equivalents ("however," "still") per vocabulary mappings.

- "connexion" does not appear in this chapter.

- "expence" rendered as "expense" / "cost" per vocabulary mappings.

- "encreases" / "encreasing" do not appear (standard "increase" forms used in original).

- "provisions" rendered as "food" or "food supply" per vocabulary mappings ("the price of provisions" becomes "food prices" or "the price of food").

- "the exchangeable value in the market of Europe" -- kept close to original but slightly clarified as "the market value in Europe."

- Sentence structure: Malthus's long multi-clause sentences broken into shorter units throughout. The paragraph about cattle and meat prices was particularly dense and has been broken into more manageable sentences. The key argumentative chain (wealth increases -> but only in manufacturing -> no more food -> nominal wage rise only -> workers no better off) is made more explicit through clearer paragraph structure.

- "butchers' meat" -- rendered as "meat" in most cases. The term was standard 18th-century usage to distinguish from fish, poultry, etc. Modern readers will understand "meat" without the qualifier.

- "the caprice of fashion" -- rendered as "the whims of fashion."

- "the capricious taste of man" -- rendered as "the whims of fashion" in the first instance and "unpredictable forces" contextually.

- The muslin/silk and buckle/button examples -- preserved in full. These are vivid concrete details that illustrate manufacturing instability.

- "husbandry" rendered as "farming methods."

- "grating" (appears twice in original, clearly a typo/OCR error for "grazing") -- rendered as "grazing."

- "fatted beast" / "unproductive labourer" (French economists' term) -- preserved with the French economists reference. The analogy of a fattened animal as an "unproductive laborer" is one of the chapter's more striking images.

- "publick" does not appear in this chapter.

- British spellings converted: "labour" to "labor" throughout, "favour" to "favor," "colour" to "color," "unfavourable" to "unfavorable," "labourers" to "workers" in most cases.

- "poor rates" -- kept as-is since this term was established in earlier chapters (Ch 5, Ch 8, Ch 9) and is clear in context.

- "corporation and parish laws" -- kept as-is. These refer to the Settlement Acts and guild restrictions that limited labor mobility, and have been introduced in earlier chapters.

- Dr. Price and Mr. Howlett -- kept with their titles on first mention. Dr. Richard Price was a Welsh statistician who argued England's population had declined; Rev. John Howlett argued it had grown. The debate is presented fairly, as Malthus somewhat sides with Price.

- "Holland" -- kept as-is (historical name for the Netherlands in this context).

2. NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER TRANSLATOR

- Chapter 17 continues the economic argument about definitions of wealth. It opens with the question of whether Adam Smith's or the French economists' definition of wealth is more accurate. The established rendering of key terms should be maintained: "the real funds for supporting labor," "the working poor," "grain" (not "corn"), etc.

- "Adam Smith" (not "Dr Adam Smith") is the established form. However, the original chapter 17 opens with "Dr Adam Smith" -- convert to just "Adam Smith."

- Dr. Price appears prominently in Chapter 17 as well. He has been introduced with context in earlier chapters. The rendering "Dr. Price" is used here; maintain this.

- The French economists (Physiocrats) are referenced in this chapter and the next. No special annotation has been added -- the context makes their views clear enough. If Chapter 17 discusses them further, maintain the same approach.

- The distinction between "real" and "nominal" increases in wages/wealth is central to this chapter and carries forward. "Real" means backed by actual food production; "nominal" means prices go up but buying power doesn't. Keep this distinction sharp.

- "Grating" appears twice in the original text (a clear typo/OCR error for "grazing"). The translator of future chapters should watch for similar OCR artifacts.

- Maintain American spellings throughout: labor, favor, color, unfavorable, etc.

- The tone of this chapter is more economic and analytical than the Godwin critique chapters. It reads like a careful academic correction of Adam Smith rather than a philosophical argument. The next chapter continues this economic register before transitioning (eventually) to Condorcet and then to the theological closing chapters.

- The "Revolution" in this chapter means the Glorious Revolution of 1688. I added "of 1688" on first occurrence. Maintain this clarification if it comes up again, to avoid confusion with the French Revolution.

- The poor rates, corporation laws, and parish laws have all been introduced and contextualized in earlier chapters (especially Ch 5 and Ch 8-9). No re-introduction needed.
