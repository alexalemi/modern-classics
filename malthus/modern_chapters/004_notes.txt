CHAPTER 4 TRANSLATION NOTES

1. MAJOR CHANGES

- Chapter synopsis: Modernized language while preserving all topic points. "Criterions" corrected to "criteria" (the original uses a now-nonstandard plural). Phrasing tightened.

- Sentence structure: Malthus's characteristically long sentences with nested qualifications have been broken into shorter, clearer sentences throughout. The Hume digression (original paragraph 5) was especially tangled and required significant restructuring to make the logical flow clear, particularly the "if I find... I would infer" parallel structure.

- "the lower orders" / "lower classes" / "the lower Chinese": Mapped to "the lower classes," "the poorest Chinese people," and "the poorest members of society" as appropriate per the text analysis guidelines.

- "the passion between the sexes": Rendered as "sexual desire" and "this natural drive" -- this term was already introduced and defined in earlier chapters, so no fresh definition needed here.

- "the labouring poor": Rendered as "the laboring class" in context.

- "corn" does not appear in this chapter.

- "Dr Adam Smith" rendered as "Adam Smith" per consistency rules.

- "parish" kept as-is; context was already established in earlier chapters. The phrase "obliged to the parish for their support" rendered as "having to turn to the parish for support."

- "connexion" does not appear; "expence" does not appear; standard archaic spellings mapped per vocabulary list.

- British spellings converted: "labour" -> "labor," "labourer" -> "laborer," "vigour" -> "force," "colour" (not present), "favour" -> "favors."

- The cattle-from-Wales analogy preserved -- it's a vivid and effective image.

- The passage about China exposing children: "exposing children" modernized to "abandoning unwanted children" with the original meaning (infanticide/exposure) clear from context. Called it a "horrifying practice" to match Malthus's own "barbarous practice."

- "ale-house" rendered as "run-down tavern" for clarity to modern readers.

- "eighteen pence a day": Kept as-is. This is a historically specific detail.

- Preserved Malthus's rhetorical questions ("Can a man really bring himself...?" and "So why don't its effects show up...?").

- The final paragraph's reference to "vices" kept as-is per text analysis (technical term in Malthus's framework referring to prostitution and other consequences of delayed marriage).
