CHAPTER 15 TRANSLATION NOTES

1. MAJOR CHANGES

- Chapter synopsis: Modernized throughout. "Mr Godwin" rendered as "Godwin." "Avarice and Profusion" rendered as "Avarice and Extravagance" -- kept "Avarice" in the title since it is a direct reference to Godwin's essay title, but "Profusion" replaced with "Extravagance" for clarity. "The labourer" rendered as "workers." "Invectives against labour" rendered as "Attacking work itself."

- "Mr Godwin" rendered as "Godwin" throughout, per consistency rules.

- "Dr Adam Smith" rendered as "Adam Smith" throughout, per consistency rules.

- "parsimony" rendered as "frugality" throughout, per vocabulary mappings. Adam Smith's key insight -- that saving adds to capital and thus productive labor -- is rendered clearly.

- "profusion" rendered as "extravagance" throughout, per vocabulary mappings.

- "stock" rendered as "capital" throughout, per vocabulary mappings. The distinction between capital (productive wealth) and hoarded wealth (the miser's chest) is central to the chapter and has been made as clear as possible.

- "revenue" rendered as "income" throughout when referring to personal spending, per vocabulary mappings.

- "corn" rendered as "grain" per vocabulary mappings.

- "shew" rendered as "show" per vocabulary mappings (one instance near the end of the chapter, in the context of obtaining "patents which they may shew").

- "patents which they may shew in order to be awarded a proper share" -- rendered as "credentials they can show in order to be given a proper share." "Patents" in 18th-century usage meant certificates or licenses, not modern intellectual property patents. "Credentials" captures the idea of a document proving entitlement.

- "polar star" -- Malthus uses this metaphor three times in this chapter (and it appeared earlier). Rendered as "guiding star" to preserve the navigational metaphor while avoiding the more archaic "polar star." On the final occurrence, also used "compass" for variety.

- "self-love" rendered as "self-interest" in most instances. Malthus uses "self-love" in the 18th-century philosophical sense (rational self-interest), not in the modern sense of narcissism. However, in the passage about benevolence arising "slowly and gradually from self-love," the term "self-interest" is used to maintain consistency. The passage about benevolence being "generated, perhaps, slowly and gradually from self-love" -- rendered as "perhaps generated slowly and gradually from self-interest." This is a philosophically rich idea (benevolence evolving from self-interest) that connects to the larger argument.

- "the lower orders" rendered as "its poorest members" in the final section per text analysis guidance.

- "the labouring poor" / "the lower classes of society" -- kept close to original per text analysis guidance. Used "working classes" where natural.

- The extended metaphor comparing Godwin's ideal to a being who lives without food or sleep -- preserved in full. This is one of the chapter's most effective analogies.

- The navigation/shipwreck metaphor -- appears twice (middle and end of chapter). Preserved both instances. The second is especially effective as a closing image.

- "asperities" rendered as "harshness" -- the passage about benevolence softening the "partial deformities" and correcting the "asperities" of self-love is philosophically important. Rendered as "soften the rough edges, smooth out the harshness, and ease the wrinkles."

- "impolitic" rendered as "unwise" -- more immediately clear.

- "the French economists" -- refers to the Physiocrats. Kept as "the French economists" since Malthus does not name them specifically.

- British spellings converted: "labour" to "labor," "labourers" to "laborers," "colours" to "colors," "favour" to "favor," etc.

- Sentence structure: Many of Malthus's longer sentences broken into shorter units. The passage about the miser locking up wealth was a particularly dense series of nested arguments; broken into clearer steps while preserving all the logical moves.

The text analysis notes that Condorcet was a French philosopher and mathematician who wrote his optimistic vision while in hiding during the Terror -- brief context should be given on first mention if not already established.

- Adam Smith has been consistently rendered as "Adam Smith" (not "Dr Adam Smith") and should remain so.

- "Godwin" (not "Mr Godwin") is the established form throughout.

- The distinction between "capital" (productive investment) and hoarded wealth has been clearly established. If economic terminology recurs in Ch 16, use the same vocabulary: "capital" for "stock," "income" for "revenue" (personal), "frugality" for "parsimony."

- Maintain American spellings: labor, favor, color, etc.

- The tone at the end of this chapter becomes deferential toward Adam Smith -- Malthus is about to critique a specific passage from the Wealth of Nations. This deference carries over into the next chapter's opening (if the argument continues) or closes the Godwin section.

- "the principle of population" is Malthus's standard phrase for his core theory. It has been used consistently and should continue to be.

- "guiding star" has been used for "polar star" throughout this chapter. If the metaphor recurs, maintain consistency.
