CHAPTER 2 TRANSLATION NOTES

1. MAJOR CHANGES MADE

- Chapter synopsis modernized: "lower classes of society" -> "lower classes," "the different ratio" -> "the different rates," "amelioration" -> "improvement," etc. Kept close to original structure since the synopsis functions as a table-of-contents guide.

- "corn" does not appear in this chapter so no grain substitution was needed.

- Vocabulary mappings applied throughout:
  - "arithmetical" -> "arithmetic," "geometrical" -> "geometric"
  - "the labouring poor" / "the lower orders" -> "the working poor" / "the lower classes"
  - "amelioration" -> "improvement"
  - "provisions" -> "food" / "food prices"
  - "shew" -> "show"
  - "labour" -> "labor" throughout (American spelling)
  - "labourers" -> "workers"
  - "connexion" -> not present in this chapter
  - "favour" -> not present in chapter text but used in one passage -> "generosity"
  - "&c." -> not present

- Long multi-clause sentences broken into 2-3 shorter sentences throughout, especially:
  - The passage on what a "satisfactory history" would require (originally one sprawling sentence)
  - The oscillation description (originally a dense paragraph with many semicolons)
  - The passage about nominal vs. real wages

- The rhetorical questions in the "will he not lower his rank" passage preserved and made slightly more direct.

- "superabundant effects are repressed afterwards by want of room and nourishment" simplified to "the excess is kept in check afterward -- by a lack of space and food."

- "this Island" -> "this island of Britain" on first use for clarity (modern readers may not immediately know which island).

- "the dictate of nature and virtue seems to be an early attachment to one woman" -> "the natural and virtuous impulse seems to be an early, committed attachment to one person" (gender-neutral where possible without distorting Malthus's argument).

- "a powerful instinct to the increase of their species" -> "a powerful instinct to reproduce" (clearer modern phrasing).

- "the facility of combination among the rich, and its difficulty among the poor" -> "the ease with which the wealthy can coordinate among themselves compared to the difficulty the poor face in doing the same" (this is a striking proto-Marxist observation; made it crystal clear).

- Preserved all specific numbers: 7 million, 14 million, 21 million, 28 million, 56 million, 112 million, 35 million, 77 million, the 1-2-4-8-16... and 1-2-3-4-5... progressions, 512 to 10, 4096 to 13, one billion starting population.

- "pestilence" -> "epidemics" (more natural modern term).

- "clamouring" -> "crying" (American spelling and more natural).

2. NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER TRANSLATOR

- The "oscillation" concept is introduced and named in this chapter. Continue using "oscillation" and "backward-and-forward cycle" as the terms for this dynamic. Don't introduce new terminology for it.

- The three propositions are stated clearly at the end of this chapter. Chapter 3 and beyond will test them against evidence. Keep referencing them as "the three propositions" for consistency.

- "The preventive check" and "the positive check" are not yet formally named in this chapter, though the concepts are introduced (reason preventing marriage = preventive; misery and death = positive). They get their formal names in Chapter 3. Don't use the technical terms until Malthus himself introduces them.

- I used "geometric rate" / "arithmetic rate" (not "ratio") when describing the growth pattern in running text, but "geometric ratio" / "arithmetic ratio" when echoing Malthus's more formal statements. Keep this distinction.

- "Subsistence" is kept throughout as per the text analysis. Don't substitute "survival" or other synonyms.

- The passage about rich vs. poor coordination (the "facility of combination" passage) is proto-labor-economics. The next chapters continue this theme. I rendered "combination" as "coordination" / "collusion" depending on context. Stay consistent with this.

- British Poor Laws are mentioned but not yet discussed in detail. They're capitalized as "the Poor Laws" per the analysis. Chapter 3 will likely reference them more.

- America is established as the key empirical case for unchecked population growth (doubling every 25 years). This benchmark will recur throughout the essay.

- Tone: analytical and measured, with occasional passion breaking through (the rhetorical questions about watching children starve, the indictment of wealthy collusion). The chapter ends on a note of complete confidence in the three propositions. Maintain this confident-but-civil tone.
