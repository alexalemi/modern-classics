The Second Story: How an Unexpected Journey Changed Everything

In Paris, there lived two merchants who were the best of friends, despite belonging to different religious worlds. Jehannot, a Christian, had this crazy idea that he wanted to convince his Jewish friend Abraham to convert to Christianity. Not in a pushy way, but out of genuine care.

Jehannot would constantly tell Abraham, "Look, your faith seems to be fading, while Christianity is growing stronger and more vibrant. I'm worried about you." But Abraham was stubborn. He'd reply, "This is the faith I was born into, and it's the faith I'll stick with."

Most people would have given up, but not Jehannot. He kept talking, explaining Christianity with the clumsy passion of a merchant who's more used to selling silk than spiritual ideas. And something weird happened - Abraham started to listen.

Finally, Abraham made a surprising proposition. "Okay," he said, "I'll become a Christian, but first I need to go to Rome and see the Pope and all the church leaders. If their lives convince me that Christianity is real, I'll convert."

Jehannot's heart sank. He knew exactly what Abraham would find in Rome - and it wasn't going to be pretty.

"Are you serious?" Jehannot asked. "The journey is dangerous. The roads are full of risks for a wealthy merchant like you. Can't you just get baptized here?"

But Abraham was determined. He traveled to Rome and started observing the clergy. And wow, what he saw was not impressive. The church leaders were shockingly corrupt - obsessed with money, consumed by sexual scandals, and seemingly interested in everything except actual spirituality.

Most people would have run away from Christianity after seeing such hypocrisy. But Abraham was different. Instead of being disgusted, he became even more convinced that Christianity was genuinely miraculous.

"How is it possible," he thought, "that this religion survives and grows despite having such terrible representatives? There must be something truly powerful at work here."

When he returned to Paris, he told Jehannot, "I want to become a Christian. Your faith is incredible - it survives even when its own leaders try to destroy it."

Jehannot was stunned. The very corruption he feared would drive Abraham away had actually drawn him closer to Christianity.

They went to Notre Dame Cathedral, where Abraham was baptized and given the name Giovanni. He learned about the Christian faith, became deeply spiritual, and lived the rest of his life as a devoted, thoughtful believer.

Sometimes, the most unexpected journeys lead us exactly where we're meant to be.
