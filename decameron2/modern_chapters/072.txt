The Third Story: How a Crafty Monk Got Away with Everything

Let me tell you about Fra Rinaldo, a monk who was about as holy as a fox in a henhouse. 

In the city of Siena, there lived this young guy from a respectable family who had his eye on a very attractive married woman named Agnesa. Knowing she was pregnant, he came up with a brilliant plan: he'd become her child's godfather—her "gossip"—which would give him a perfect excuse to spend time with her.

Her husband, totally oblivious, happily accepted Rinaldo as godfather. This gave Rinaldo exactly the access he wanted. He started dropping hints about his romantic interest, but at first, the lady wasn't having it.

Then Rinaldo became a monk, which you'd think would cool his romantic ambitions. Ha! Instead, he became one of those monks who are more interested in looking good than being holy. He wore fancy clothes, wrote poetry, and strutted around like a peacock—which, he sarcastically notes, is pretty standard for monks these days.

Monks, he points out, love living comfortably. Their cells look more like luxury spas than religious quarters—packed with fancy wines, expensive oils, and gourmet treats. They claim to be spiritual, but they're really just enjoying the good life. They've convinced themselves that no one notices how plump and well-fed they are, despite supposedly living a life of strict discipline.

Rinaldo went back to pursuing Agnesa with renewed energy. When she hesitated, saying, "But you're a monk!", he had a smooth response: "This robe? I can take it off anytime." 

He even used some clever theological reasoning. "Who's more connected to your child," he asked, "Me, who held him at baptism, or your husband who actually made him?" When she agreed her husband was more connected, he used that logic to suggest they could, well, connect too.

Eventually, Agnesa gave in. They started meeting regularly, using the cover of his godfather status to avoid suspicion.

One day, while they were in her bedroom, her husband suddenly came home. Thinking quickly, Agnesa created an elaborate story about Rinaldo saving their child from mysterious "worms" through a special conjuring ritual. Her husband, completely fooled, was just grateful his son was "saved."

Rinaldo played along perfectly, talking about a miraculous healing and even suggesting they create a wax figure to commemorate the miracle at a local church.

The husband was so impressed that he treated Rinaldo and his monk companion to wine and treats, completely unaware he'd just been thoroughly played.

And that, my friends, is how one very clever—and very un-monastic—monk got exactly what he wanted.
