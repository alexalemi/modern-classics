Bernabo of Genoa Gets Totally Played: A Cautionary Tale of Trust, Lies, and Unexpected Revenge

After Elisa finished her heartbreaking story, Filomena—the queen of the storytelling group, who was strikingly beautiful and had the most charming smile—cleared her throat and said, "Okay, we made a deal with Dioneo, so since he and I are the last to tell our stories, I'll go first."

She began with a classic saying: "There's this old saying that the person who tries to trick someone always ends up getting caught. Now, I'm going to prove that this isn't just a nice-sounding proverb, but actual truth. Ladies, listen up—this story might just save you from some serious trouble."

One evening in Paris, a group of Italian merchants were hanging out, sharing stories over dinner. Somehow, their conversation turned to their wives back home, and things got interesting fast.

"When I'm away," one guy joked, "if I find a girl I like, I'm totally putting my wife out of my mind."

Another chimed in, "Same here. I figure my wife is probably doing her own thing anyway, so why should I care?"

Most of the guys agreed: their wives weren't sitting around waiting faithfully. But one merchant named Bernabo Lomellini disagreed. He bragged that his wife was basically the most perfect woman in all of Italy—beautiful, talented, and totally devoted to him.

"She's not just gorgeous," Bernabo said. "She can embroider like a pro, serve fancy dinners better than any waiter, ride horses, hunt with falcons, read, write, and do math like a trained accountant. And faithful? Completely. I could be gone for years, and she'd never even look at another man."

One young merchant named Ambrogiuolo thought this was hilarious. "Really?" he said. "Did the emperor give you some special wife-loyalty certificate?"
