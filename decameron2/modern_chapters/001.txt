INTRODUCTION

Let me tell you something important: showing compassion to people who are struggling is always a good thing. While everyone should try to be kind, it matters even more for those who've experienced hardship themselves and know what it's like to need help.

And trust me, I know all about needing help.

From my early youth until now, I've been consumed by an intense, passionate love – maybe more profound and complicated than you might expect from someone like me. Even though some wise people appreciated my feelings and thought more highly of me because of them, dealing with this love was incredibly challenging.

It wasn't because the person I loved was cruel, but because my own emotions were so overwhelming that I couldn't control them. This passion often brought me more pain than joy. During my darkest moments, a friend's conversation and incredible support kept me going. In fact, I'm pretty sure those conversations are the reason I survived at all.

As time passed, my all-consuming love gradually softened. What used to be a source of intense pain transformed into something more gentle – like remembering a powerful storm after it's passed.

I want to do something to pay forward the kindness I received. So I've decided to write a collection of stories – one hundred tales told over ten days by seven women and three young men. These stories emerged during a terrible plague, when people were seeking comfort and connection.

Why stories? Because I believe storytelling can be a form of healing.

Especially for women. Women experience love differently – more intensely and privately. Trapped by social expectations, often confined to their rooms, they navigate complex emotions with fewer outlets than men. While men can distract themselves with hunting, traveling, or business, women have fewer ways to escape overwhelming feelings.

So these stories are for them. Stories of love – both joyful and heartbreaking. Stories that might offer comfort, provide insight, and help them understand what to embrace and what to avoid in life.

If these stories bring even a moment of relief, then I'll feel I've honored the compassion shown to me during my own difficult times.
