The Second Story

Let me tell you about a classic case of a con artist getting exactly what he deserves.

People always say that someone who's seen as "good" can get away with just about anything. This story perfectly demonstrates how some so-called holy men are nothing more than master manipulators.

Take Fra Alberto, for example - a monk from Venice who was basically a professional fraud. Before becoming a friar, he was a thief, a pimp, and worse. But he discovered that by putting on a religious costume and crying on cue during mass, he could trick basically everyone in town.

Alberto became the go-to confessor and financial advisor for most of Venice. People trusted him completely, thinking he was this ultra-pious saint. Little did they know.

One day, he set his sights on Lisetta, a wealthy merchant's wife - a woman who was beautiful but, frankly, not too bright. During her confession, she bragged endlessly about her looks, claiming she was so gorgeous that she could have any lover she wanted.

Alberto saw his opportunity. He concocted an absolutely ridiculous scheme: he would convince her that the Angel Gabriel was in love with her.

"The angel was so impressed by your beauty," he told her, "that he wants to visit you personally." 

Lisetta, who was incredibly vain, was thrilled. She believed every single word.

Alberto's plan was genius in its absurdity. He would dress up as the Angel Gabriel and spend the night with her, convincing her that this was a divine encounter. And the best part? She bought it completely.

Night after night, he would sneak into her bedroom, dressed in white, promising her tales of heavenly glory - and delivering something rather more earthly.

But here's where things get complicated. Lisetta, unable to keep her big mouth shut, started bragging to her friends that the actual Angel Gabriel was her lover.

Word spread quickly. Soon, Lisetta's brothers-in-law found out and decided to catch this "angel" in the act.

Caught red-handed, Alberto did the only thing he could: he jumped out the window into the canal and ran for his life.

A local man found him, half-drowned and desperate. For a price, the man agreed to help Alberto escape - but not before publicly humiliating him first.

In front of the entire town of Venice, Alberto was paraded around dressed in a ridiculous costume, covered in honey and feathers, chained like a wild animal. Everyone who knew him got to see the "holy" man exposed as a complete fraud.

His fellow monks eventually dragged him away to prison, where he lived out the rest of his miserable life - a fitting punishment for a man who spent his life deceiving others.

The moral? Some people might think they're clever enough to fool everyone - but eventually, the truth always comes out.
