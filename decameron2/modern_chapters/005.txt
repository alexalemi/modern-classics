The Third Story

Neifile finished her story to everyone's praise, and Filomena, with the queen's approval, began her own tale:

"Neifile's story reminds me of an incredible situation that once happened to a Jewish merchant. Since we've already discussed matters of faith and truth, I think it's perfectly fine to explore how actual people navigate tricky moments. Listen carefully, because this story might just help you handle tough questions more cleverly.

Let me tell you something important: Sometimes stupidity can knock you right out of a good situation and dump you into total disaster. But smart thinking? That can rescue you from the most dangerous moments and lead you to safety and peace.

We see examples of foolishness destroying people's lives everywhere. But I want to show you how intelligence can be a lifeline – and I'll do that with a short story.

Saladin was an incredible leader – so remarkable that he'd risen from being a nobody to becoming the powerful ruler of Babylon, winning battles against both Muslim and Christian kings. After spending all his money in various wars and grand gestures, he suddenly needed cash fast. He remembered a wealthy Jewish merchant in Alexandria named Melchizedek who was known for lending money.

Saladin wanted to borrow from Melchizedek, but he knew the merchant was super tight with his money. He didn't want to just demand the funds, so he came up with a clever plan to approach him.

He invited Melchizedek to meet and asked him a seemingly simple but actually super complicated question: 'Which of the three religious laws do you believe is the true one – Jewish, Muslim, or Christian?'

Melchizedek immediately realized Saladin was trying to trap him. If he praised one religion over the others, he'd be walking into a dangerous political snare. So he responded with a brilliant story:

'Let me tell you about a wealthy man who had an incredible ring – a family treasure he wanted to pass down. He told his children that whoever possessed this ring after his death would be considered the primary heir, honored above all others.

This tradition continued through generations, until one father with three equally beloved sons found himself in a dilemma. Each son wanted the ring, and each believed he should inherit it. The father, not wanting to disappoint anyone, secretly had a master craftsman create two additional rings that were absolutely identical to the original.

When he was dying, he privately gave each son a ring. After his death, each son claimed to be the true heir, displaying his ring as proof. But since the rings were identical, nobody could determine which was the genuine article.

And that's exactly like the three religious laws,' Melchizedek explained. 'Each group believes they have the true inheritance from God. But just like with those rings, we can't definitively prove which one is absolutely correct.'"

Saladin was so impressed by this clever response that he immediately revealed his real reason for meeting: he needed a loan. Melchizedek not only provided the funds but also became a trusted advisor to Saladin, who rewarded his wisdom with honor and friendship.
