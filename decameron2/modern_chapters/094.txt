The Tenth Story: How a Clever Woman Outsmarted a Merchant

Let me tell you about one of the most brilliant con artists you'll ever hear about—a woman in Palermo who knew exactly how to separate a foolish merchant from his money.

Back then, merchants who arrived in port had to register their goods in a central warehouse, kind of like a medieval customs office. These warehouses kept detailed records of everything traders brought in, which made it easy for locals to figure out exactly how much money each merchant was carrying.

And in Palermo? The local women were masters at using this information. They weren't just beautiful—they were predators who could smell a potential target from a mile away.

Enter Niccolo da Cignano, nicknamed Salabaetto—a young Florentine trader with some nice woolen cloth worth about 500 gold coins. Blond, good-looking, and totally clueless, he was the perfect mark.

One local woman named Biancofiore set her sights on him. She was a professional—someone who could manipulate men with just a glance. Her first move? She sent a messenger to tell Salabaetto how desperately she was falling for him.

The guy never stood a chance.

She arranged a meetup at a bathhouse that was more like a five-star seduction suite. Silk sheets, scented waters, slave girls serving them—the works. Salabaetto thought he'd won the romance lottery. In reality, he was walking straight into a trap.

After several days of passionate encounters, Biancofiore pulled out her masterstroke. Suddenly, she was in "desperate" need of 500 gold coins to save her brother's life. And our lovesick merchant? He handed over the money without a second thought.

The moment the cash was in her hands, everything changed. Her passionate lover became mysteriously unavailable. No more romantic encounters, no repayment of the loan.

Salabaetto had been completely played—and he knew it. But what could he do? He'd been so eager to believe he was irresistible that he'd made himself the perfect victim.

Just another day in medieval Palermo.
