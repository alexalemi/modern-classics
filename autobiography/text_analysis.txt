TEXT ANALYSIS — The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin (1771–1790)
Source: Standard Ebooks edition (Frank Woodworth Pine's 1916 chapter
arrangement), prepared from the single-page XHTML; SE's editorial
endnotes stripped in prep. 19 chapters, one translation file each.

WHAT THIS IS

The founding document of the American self-made life: Franklin at 65
writing to his son ("Dear Son…"), then at 78 and 82 for the public,
telling how a chandler's tenth son ran away from Boston at 17 and
became printer, author, scientist, and public man. Unfinished — it
breaks off around 1760, before the Revolution. Written in four stints;
Pine's chapter titles (kept as our chapter headings) smooth the seams.

STYLE AND TONE

- First person throughout, and the voice is the whole game: shrewd,
  dry, self-amused, and disarmingly candid about his own vanity —
  he brags, then winks at the brag ("I cannot boast of much success in
  acquiring the reality of this virtue, but I had a good deal with
  regard to the appearance of it"). PRESERVE THE WINK. The modern
  voice should sound like the smartest, most practical person you
  know telling stories at dinner, never like a moralist.
- Chapter 1 opens as a letter — keep the "Dear Son" salutation and
  the letter frame; later chapters drop it naturally, as the source
  does.
- Franklin's signature metaphor: he calls his life's mistakes
  "errata" — a printer's term. KEEP "errata" (and "erratum")
  exactly; gloss once, lightly, at first use ("errata — a printer's
  word for typos"). It recurs; the ledger tracks each use.
- Anecdotes are the engine: the puffy rolls, the whistle-less voyage,
  the cod that smelled of fish dinners, Keimer's roast pig, the
  Braddock campaign. Render them as complete little stories with
  their punchlines intact.
- Franklin's practical schemes (the Junto, the library, the fire
  company, paving, the stove, the militia lottery) come with concrete
  operational detail — keep the how-to texture; it is the book's
  charm and its founders'-library relevance.

TRANSLATION CHALLENGES

- The prose is already plainer than most 18th-century writing;
  de-archaize (thee/thou in Quaker speech may stay in quoted dialogue
  for color — decide once, ledger it), untangle long sentences, keep
  ratio near 1.0 (verify bounds 0.8–1.2).
- Historical terms with quick appositive glosses (first use only):
  "the Junto — his mutual-improvement club", "vendue" → auction,
  "leather-apron men" keep with context. Currency (pistoles, pounds,
  shillings) stays as-is.
- Indented blocks in the source files (the 13 virtues with precepts,
  the daily schedule, verse, letters quoted whole) must stay indented
  blocks in the modern files — assemble.py renders them as set-off
  outlines. The virtue PRECEPTS are aphorisms: modernize only
  spelling-level archaisms, keep their epigram shape.
- Two inserted letters (Abel James, Benjamin Vaughan) urging him to
  continue appear mid-book; they are part of the text — translate
  them in their writers' voices, slightly more formal than Franklin's.

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Franklin mentions enslaved people matter-of-factly (household
  purchases, Braddock's officers' servants) — render plainly and
  historically, per project policy; never scrub, never expand.
- Religious content: his deism, tolerance, jabs at sects (and the
  Dunkers' lovely humility anecdote) are his views — keep the irony
  gentle, as he does. George Whitefield is treated with affectionate
  skepticism — preserve that exact balance.
- Native Americans appear in period frame (the treaty at Carlisle,
  the rum scene and its harsh closing remark) — translate faithfully
  in a neutral historical register; his words remain his.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- Ch. 1 salutation: "Dear Son"
- Ch. 3: the arrival scene keeps "three great puffy rolls"
- Ch. 4, the cod anecdote: "If you eat one another, I don't see why
  we can't eat you." followed by the moral kept near-verbatim: "So
  convenient a thing it is to be a reasonable creature, since it
  enables one to find or make a reason for everything one has a mind
  to do."
- Ch. 9: "the bold and arduous project of arriving at moral
  perfection"; the Temperance precept "Eat not to dullness; drink not
  to elevation."; Humility's precept "Imitate Jesus and Socrates.";
  and the punchline "a speckled ax was best".

FORMAT

19 files (000–018), one chapter each, no part markers. First line:
"Chapter N: Title" exactly as in the source file. Word ratio target
0.85–1.15.
