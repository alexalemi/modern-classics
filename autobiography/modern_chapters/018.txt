Chapter 19: Agent of Pennsylvania in London

Our new governor, Captain Denny, brought over with him the Royal Society medal I mentioned earlier, and presented it to me at a dinner the city gave in his honor. He handed it over with very polite expressions of his esteem, saying he had long been acquainted with my character. After dinner, when the company settled in to drink — as was the custom of the time — he took me aside into another room and confided that his friends in England had advised him to cultivate my friendship, as someone who could give him the best advice and do the most to make his administration go smoothly. He therefore wanted, above all things, a good understanding with me, and begged me to be assured that he stood ready, on every occasion, to do me any service in his power. He also had a great deal to say about the proprietor's good feelings toward the province, and how much it would benefit us all — and me in particular — if the long-running opposition to his measures were dropped and harmony restored between him and the people. Nobody, it was thought, could be more useful in bringing that about than myself, and I could count on adequate acknowledgments and rewards, et cetera, et cetera. The drinkers, noticing we hadn't come back to the table, sent us in a decanter of Madeira, which the governor made liberal use of, and in proportion as he drank he grew more lavish with his solicitations and promises.

My answer ran along these lines: that my circumstances, thank God, were such that proprietary favors were unnecessary to me, and that as a member of the Assembly I couldn't possibly accept any. That I had, however, no personal enmity toward the proprietary, and whenever the public measures he proposed genuinely appeared to be for the good of the people, no one would take them up and push them forward more zealously than I would — my past opposition having rested entirely on the fact that the measures urged on us were plainly designed to serve the proprietary interest at great cost to the people's. That I was much obliged to the governor for his professions of regard, and he could rely on me to do everything in my power to make his administration as easy as possible — hoping, at the same time, that he hadn't brought with him the same unfortunate instructions that had hampered his predecessor.

On that point he didn't explain himself just then. But when he later came to do business with the Assembly, the instructions appeared again, the disputes were renewed, and I was as active as ever in the opposition — the penman, first, of the request to see the instructions, and then of the remarks upon them, which can be found in the votes of the time and in the Historical Review I afterward published. Yet between the two of us personally, no ill will ever arose. We were often together; he was a man of letters, had seen much of the world, and was entertaining and pleasant company. He gave me my first news that my old friend Jas. Ralph was still alive — that he was considered one of the best political writers in England, had been employed in the dispute between Prince Frederic and the king, and had secured a pension of three hundred a year. His reputation as a poet, admittedly, was small — Pope had damned his poetry in the Dunciad — but his prose was thought as good as any man's.

The Assembly, finding at last that the proprietary obstinately insisted on shackling his deputies with instructions inconsistent not only with the privileges of the people but with the service of the crown, resolved to petition the king against them — and appointed me their agent, to go over to England to present and support the petition. The House had sent up a bill to the governor granting sixty thousand pounds for the king's use (ten thousand pounds of it subject to the orders of the general then in command, Lord Loudoun), which the governor, in compliance with his instructions, absolutely refused to pass.

I had booked my passage with Captain Morris of the packet at New York, and my stores were already on board, when Lord Loudoun arrived in Philadelphia — expressly, as he told me, to work out an accommodation between the governor and the Assembly, so that His Majesty's service wouldn't be obstructed by their quarrels. Accordingly, he asked the governor and me to meet with him, so he could hear what each side had to say. We met and talked the business through. On the Assembly's behalf, I urged all the various arguments that can be found in the public papers of the time — they were of my writing, and are printed with the minutes of the Assembly. The governor pleaded his instructions, the bond he had given to observe them, and his ruin if he disobeyed — yet he seemed not unwilling to risk it if Lord Loudoun would advise it. This his lordship chose not to do, though at one point I thought I had nearly persuaded him. In the end he preferred to press the Assembly to comply, and he begged me to use my influence with them toward that end, declaring that he would spare none of the king's troops for the defense of our frontiers, and that if we didn't keep providing for that defense ourselves, they would be left exposed to the enemy.

I told the House what had passed, and presented them with a set of resolutions I had drawn up, declaring our rights — stating that we did not give up our claim to those rights, but only suspended exercising them on this occasion under force, against which we protested. At length they agreed to drop the bill and frame another that conformed to the proprietary instructions. This, of course, the governor passed, and I was then free to proceed on my voyage. But in the meantime the packet had sailed with my sea-stores — some loss to me — and my only recompense was his lordship's thanks for my service, all the credit for the accommodation falling to his share.

He set out for New York ahead of me; and since the timing of the packet-boats was at his disposal, and there were two of them then waiting there, one of which, he said, was to sail very soon, I asked to know the precise time, so that I wouldn't miss her through any delay of my own. His answer was: "I have given out that she is to sail on Saturday next; but I may let you know, entre nous, that if you are there by Monday morning, you will be in time, but do not delay longer." Some accidental hold-up at a ferry made it Monday noon before I arrived, and I was much afraid she might have sailed, the wind being fair — but I was soon put at ease by the news that she was still in the harbor and wouldn't move till the next day. You would imagine I was now on the very point of departing for Europe. I thought so too; but I was not yet well acquainted with his lordship's character, of which indecision was one of the strongest features. Let me give some instances. It was about the beginning of April that I came to New York, and I think it was near the end of June before we sailed. There were two packet-boats then, which had been in port a long while, held for the general's letters — which were always going to be ready tomorrow. Another packet arrived; she was detained too; and before we sailed, a fourth was expected. Ours was first in line to be dispatched, having been there longest. Passengers were booked on all of them, some desperately impatient to be gone; the merchants were anxious about their letters and the orders they had given for insurance (it being wartime) on fall goods. But their anxiety accomplished nothing. His lordship's letters were not ready — and yet whoever waited on him found him always at his desk, pen in hand, and concluded he must be writing prodigiously.

Going one morning myself to pay my respects, I found in his antechamber a certain Innis, a messenger from Philadelphia who had come express with a packet from Governor Denny for the general. He handed me some letters from my friends there, which led me to ask when he was returning and where he lodged, so I could send some letters back with him. He told me he was ordered to call tomorrow at nine for the general's answer to the governor, and would set off immediately after. I put my letters into his hands that same day. Two weeks later I met him in the same place. "So, you are soon returned, Innis?" "Returned! No, I am not gone yet." "How so?" "I have called here by order every morning these two weeks past for his lordship's letter, and it is not yet ready." "Is it possible, when he is so great a writer? For I see him constantly at his desk." "Yes," says Innis, "but he is like St. George on the signs, always on horseback, and never rides on." The messenger's observation was, it seems, well founded; for when I was in England I learned that Mr. Pitt gave as one of his reasons for removing this general and sending out Generals Amherst and Wolfe that the minister never heard from him, and could not know what he was doing.

With this daily expectation of sailing, and all three packets gone down to Sandy Hook to join the fleet there, the passengers thought it best to be on board, in case a sudden order sent the ships off and left them behind. There, if I remember right, we sat about six weeks, eating up our sea-stores and obliged to buy more. At length the fleet sailed, the general and his whole army aboard, bound for Louisburg with the intent to besiege and take that fortress, all the packet-boats in company ordered to attend the general's ship, ready to receive his dispatches whenever they should be ready. We were out five days before we got a letter with leave to part, and then our ship quit the fleet and steered for England. The other two packets he kept — carried them with him to Halifax, where he stayed some time drilling his men in sham attacks on sham forts, then changed his mind about besieging Louisburg and returned to New York with all his troops, the two packets, and all their passengers! During his absence the French and their native allies had taken Fort George, on the frontier of that province, and after the surrender the garrison had been massacred in great numbers.

Later, in London, I saw Captain Bonnell, who commanded one of those packets. He told me that after being detained a month, he informed his lordship that his ship's bottom had grown so foul it must seriously slow her sailing — a point of real consequence for a packet-boat — and requested time to heave her down and clean her. He was asked how long that would take. He answered: three days. The general replied, "If you can do it in one day, I give leave; otherwise not; for you must certainly sail the day after tomorrow." So he never got leave — though he was detained afterward, day after day, for a full three months.

I also saw in London one of Bonnell's passengers, who was so enraged at his lordship for deceiving him and holding him so long at New York, then carrying him to Halifax and back again, that he swore he would sue him for damages. Whether he did or not, I never heard; but as he described the injury to his affairs, it was very considerable.

On the whole, I wondered greatly how such a man came to be entrusted with something as important as the conduct of a great army; but having since seen more of the great world — the means of obtaining offices, and the motives for giving them — my wonder is diminished. General Shirley, on whom command of the army fell at Braddock's death, would in my opinion, had he been kept in place, have made a far better campaign than Loudoun's of 1757, which was frivolous, expensive, and disgraceful to our nation beyond conception. For though Shirley was not a trained soldier, he was sensible and shrewd in himself, attentive to good advice from others, capable of forming judicious plans, and quick and active in carrying them out. Loudoun, instead of defending the colonies with his great army, left them totally exposed while he paraded idly at Halifax — by which means Fort George was lost. Besides that, he threw all our commercial operations into disorder and crippled our trade with a long embargo on the export of provisions, on the pretense of keeping supplies from the enemy, but really to beat down the price in favor of the contractors — in whose profits, it was said (perhaps only from suspicion), he had a share. And when the embargo was at last lifted, he neglected to send notice of it to Charlestown, so the Carolina fleet was held nearly three months longer, and their bottoms were so eaten by the worm that a great part of them foundered on the passage home.

Shirley, I believe, was sincerely glad to be relieved of a burden as heavy as the command of an army must be to a man with no military background. I was at the dinner the city of New York gave Lord Loudoun on his taking up the command. Shirley, though superseded by it, was there too. There was a great company of officers, citizens, and strangers, and some chairs had been borrowed from the neighborhood — among them one very low one, which fell to Mr. Shirley's lot. Noticing it, as I sat beside him, I said, "They have given you, sir, too low a seat." "No matter," says he, "Mr. Franklin, I find a low seat the easiest."

While I was, as I've said, stuck in New York, I received all the accounts of the provisions and so forth that I had furnished to Braddock — some of which couldn't be got any sooner from the various people I had employed to help in the business. I presented them to Lord Loudoun and asked to be paid the balance. He had them properly examined by the appropriate officer, who, after comparing every article with its voucher, certified them correct; and his lordship promised to give me an order on the paymaster for the balance due. This, however, was put off from time to time, and though I called for it often by appointment, I never got it. At length, just before my departure, he told me that on better consideration he had decided not to mix his accounts with those of his predecessors. "And you," says he, "when in England, have only to exhibit your accounts at the treasury, and you will be paid immediately."

I mentioned — without effect — the great and unexpected expense I'd been put to by the long detention at New York, as a reason for wanting to be paid promptly; and when I observed that it wasn't right I should face any further trouble or delay in recovering money I had advanced, since I charged no commission for my service, "O, sir," says he, "you must not think of persuading us that you are no gainer; we understand better those affairs, and know that everyone concerned in supplying the army finds means, in the doing it, to fill his own pockets." I assured him that was not my case, and that I had not pocketed a farthing; but he plainly didn't believe me — and indeed, I have since learned that immense fortunes are often made in such employments. As for my balance, I am not paid it to this day, of which more hereafter.

Our packet captain had boasted a great deal, before we sailed, of the swiftness of his ship. Unfortunately, once we got to sea she proved the dullest of ninety-six sail, to his considerable mortification. After many guesses at the cause, when we were near another ship almost as slow as ours — which nevertheless was gaining on us — the captain ordered all hands to come aft and stand as near the ensign staff as possible. We were, passengers included, about forty people. While we stood there the ship picked up her pace, and soon left her neighbor far behind — which proved what our captain suspected: she was loaded too heavily at the head. The water casks, it turned out, had all been placed forward; he ordered them moved further aft, whereupon the ship recovered her character and proved the best sailer in the fleet.

The captain said she had once gone at the rate of thirteen knots — which is reckoned thirteen miles an hour. We had on board as a passenger Captain Kennedy of the Navy, who insisted this was impossible, that no ship ever sailed so fast, and that there must have been some error in the division of the log-line or some mistake in heaving the log. A wager followed between the two captains, to be settled when there was wind enough. Kennedy examined the log-line rigorously beforehand and, satisfied with it, determined to throw the log himself. Accordingly, some days later, when the wind blew very fair and fresh and the packet's captain, Lutwidge, said he believed she was then making thirteen knots, Kennedy made the trial — and owned his wager lost.

I tell this story for the sake of the observation that follows. It has been remarked, as a flaw in the art of shipbuilding, that you can never know until she's tried whether a new ship will be a good sailer or not — the model of a fine sailing ship has been copied exactly in a new one that proved, on the contrary, remarkably slow. I suspect this may be partly caused by the differing opinions of seamen about how a ship should be loaded, rigged, and sailed. Each man has his system; and the same vessel, loaded by the judgment and orders of one captain, will sail better or worse than when loaded by the orders of another. Besides, it hardly ever happens that a ship is built, fitted for sea, and sailed by the same person. One man builds the hull, another rigs her, a third loads and sails her. None of them has the benefit of knowing all the ideas and experience of the others, and so none can draw sound conclusions from the whole picture combined.

Even in the simple business of sailing at sea, I have often noticed different judgments among the officers commanding successive watches, though the wind was the same. One would have the sails trimmed sharper or flatter than another, so that they seemed to have no fixed rule to steer by. Yet I think a set of experiments might be undertaken: first, to determine the best form of hull for swift sailing; next, the best dimensions and placement for the masts; then the shape and quantity of the sails and their position according to the wind; and lastly, the arrangement of the loading. This is an age of experiments, and a set of them, accurately made and combined, would I think be of great use. I am persuaded, therefore, that before long some ingenious philosopher will take it up — and I wish him success.

We were chased several times on our passage, but outsailed everything, and in thirty days had soundings. We had taken a good observation, and the captain judged himself so near our port, Falmouth, that if we made a good run in the night we might be off the mouth of the harbor by morning — and by running in the dark we might slip past the enemy privateers who often cruised near the entrance of the channel. Accordingly we set every sail we possibly could, and with the wind fresh and fair, we ran straight before it and made great way. After his observation, the captain shaped his course, as he thought, to pass well wide of the Scilly Isles; but it seems there is sometimes a strong indraught setting up St. George's Channel, which deceives seamen — it caused the loss of Sir Cloudesley Shovel's squadron. This indraught was probably the cause of what happened to us.

We had a watchman posted in the bow, to whom they often called, "Look well out before there," and he as often answered, "Ay, ay" — but perhaps with his eyes shut, half asleep, for they say men sometimes answer such calls mechanically. He did not see a light just ahead of us, which was hidden from the helmsman and the rest of the watch by the studding-sails; but an accidental yaw of the ship revealed it, and set off a great alarm, for we were very close — the light looked to me as big as a cartwheel. It was midnight, and our captain fast asleep; but Captain Kennedy, leaping up on deck and seeing the danger, ordered the ship to wear round, all sails standing — an operation dangerous to the masts, but it carried us clear, and we escaped shipwreck; for we had been running straight onto the rocks the lighthouse was built on. This deliverance impressed on me strongly the usefulness of lighthouses, and made me resolve to encourage the building of more of them in America, if I lived to return there.

In the morning the soundings and other signs showed we were near our port, but a thick fog hid the land from sight. About nine o'clock the fog began to rise, lifting off the water like the curtain at a playhouse, and discovering underneath the town of Falmouth, the vessels in its harbor, and the fields around it. It was a most pleasing spectacle to people who had so long had nothing to look at but the uniform view of a vacant ocean — and it gave us all the more pleasure because we were now free of the anxieties the state of war had caused us.

I set out at once with my son for London, and we stopped only briefly on the way to see Stonehenge on Salisbury Plain, and Lord Pembroke's house and gardens, with his very curious antiquities, at Wilton. We arrived in London the 27th of July, 1757.

As soon as I was settled in the lodging Mr. Charles had arranged for me, I went to visit Dr. Fothergill, to whom I had been strongly recommended and whose counsel on how to proceed I was advised to obtain. He was against an immediate complaint to the government; he thought the proprietaries should first be approached personally, since the interposition and persuasion of some private friends might possibly induce them to settle matters amicably. I then waited on my old friend and correspondent Mr. Peter Collinson, who told me that John Hanbury, the great Virginia merchant, had asked to be informed when I arrived, so that he might take me to Lord Granville's — then President of the Council, who wished to see me as soon as possible. I agreed to go with him the next morning. Accordingly Mr. Hanbury called for me and carried me in his carriage to that nobleman's house, where I was received with great civility; and after some questions about the present state of affairs in America, and some discussion of them, he said to me: "You Americans have wrong ideas of the nature of your constitution; you contend that the king's instructions to his governors are not laws, and think yourselves at liberty to regard or disregard them at your own discretion. But those instructions are not like the pocket instructions given to a minister going abroad, for regulating his conduct in some trifling point of ceremony. They are first drawn up by judges learned in the laws; they are then considered, debated, and perhaps amended in Council, after which they are signed by the king. They are then, so far as they relate to you, the law of the land, for the king is the Legislator of the Colonies." I told his lordship this was new doctrine to me. I had always understood from our charters that our laws were to be made by our Assemblies — to be presented to the king, certainly, for his royal assent, but that once given, the king could not repeal or alter them. And as the Assemblies could not make permanent laws without his assent, so neither could he make a law for them without theirs. He assured me I was totally mistaken. I did not think so, however; and his lordship's conversation having somewhat alarmed me as to what the court's sentiments about us might be, I wrote it down as soon as I got back to my lodgings. I recalled that about twenty years earlier, a clause in a bill brought into Parliament by the ministry had proposed making the king's instructions law in the colonies — but the clause was thrown out by the Commons, for which we adored them as our friends and the friends of liberty; until, by their conduct toward us in 1765, it appeared they had refused that point of sovereignty to the king only so they could reserve it for themselves.

After some days, Dr. Fothergill having spoken to the proprietaries, they agreed to meet me at Mr. T. Penn's house in Spring Garden. The conversation began with mutual declarations of willingness to make reasonable accommodations — though I suppose each side had its own ideas of what "reasonable" should mean. We then took up our several points of complaint, which I enumerated. The proprietaries justified their conduct as well as they could, and I the Assembly's. We now appeared very far apart — so wide in our opinions as to discourage all hope of agreement. Still, it was agreed that I should give them the heads of our complaints in writing, and they promised to consider them. I did so soon after; but they put the paper into the hands of their solicitor, Ferdinand John Paris, who managed all their law business in their great suit with the neighboring proprietary of Maryland, Lord Baltimore — a suit that had lasted seventy years — and who wrote all their papers and messages in their dispute with the Assembly. He was a proud, angry man; and since I had occasionally, in the Assembly's answers, treated his papers with some severity — they being genuinely weak in argument and haughty in expression — he had conceived a mortal enmity toward me, which showed itself whenever we met. So I declined the proprietaries' proposal that he and I discuss the heads of complaint between the two of us, and refused to treat with anyone but them. They then, on his advice, put the paper into the hands of the Attorney and Solicitor-General for their opinion and counsel — where it lay unanswered a year wanting eight days. All that time I made frequent demands of an answer from the proprietaries, and got nothing beyond word that they had not yet received the opinion of the Attorney and Solicitor-General. What that opinion was when they did receive it, I never learned, for they never communicated it to me; instead they sent a long message to the Assembly, drawn up and signed by Paris, reciting my paper, complaining of its want of formality as a rudeness on my part, and giving a flimsy justification of their conduct — adding that they would be willing to settle matters if the Assembly would send over some person of candour to treat with them, the implication being that I was not such a person.

The want of formality, or rudeness, was probably my not having addressed the paper to them with their assumed titles of True and Absolute Proprietaries of the Province of Pennsylvania — which I omitted as unnecessary in a paper whose whole purpose was simply to fix in writing what I had already delivered in conversation viva voce.

But during this delay, the Assembly prevailed on Governor Denny to pass an act taxing the proprietary estate along with the estates of the people — which was the grand point in dispute — and so they never bothered answering the message.

When this act came over, however, the proprietaries, counselled by Paris, determined to keep it from receiving the royal assent. Accordingly they petitioned the king in Council, and a hearing was appointed, at which two lawyers were employed by them against the act and two by me in support of it. They alleged the act was designed to load the proprietary estate in order to spare the people's, and that if it were allowed to stand — with the proprietaries, who were in odium with the people, left to their mercy in the apportioning of taxes — they would inevitably be ruined. We replied that the act had no such intention and would have no such effect: the assessors were honest and discreet men, under oath to assess fairly and equitably, and whatever advantage any one of them might gain by lessening his own tax through raising the proprietaries' was far too trifling to induce him to perjure himself. That is the substance of what I remember being urged on both sides — except that we also insisted strongly on the mischievous consequences a repeal must bring. For the money — £100,000, printed and given to the king's use, spent in his service, and now spread among the people — would be struck dead in their hands by a repeal, to the ruin of many and the total discouragement of future grants; and the selfishness of the proprietors in courting such a general catastrophe, merely from a groundless fear of their estate being taxed too highly, we pressed in the strongest terms. At this, Lord Mansfield, one of the counsel, rose, beckoned to me, and took me into the clerk's chamber while the lawyers were still pleading, and asked me whether I was really of the opinion that no injury would be done the proprietary estate in the execution of the act. I said certainly. "Then," says he, "you can have little objection to enter into an engagement to assure that point." I answered, "None at all." He then called in Paris, and after some discussion his lordship's proposition was accepted on both sides; a paper to that purpose was drawn up by the Clerk of the Council, which I signed together with Mr. Charles — who was also an Agent of the Province for its ordinary affairs — and then Lord Mansfield returned to the Council Chamber, where the law was finally allowed to pass. Some changes were, however, recommended, and we engaged that they should be made by a subsequent law; but the Assembly did not think them necessary. For one year's tax had already been levied under the act before the order of Council arrived, so they appointed a committee to examine the assessors' proceedings — and on that committee they placed several particular friends of the proprietaries. After a full enquiry, the committee unanimously signed a report that they found the tax had been assessed with perfect equity.

The Assembly regarded my entering into the first part of that engagement as an essential service to the Province, since it secured the credit of the paper money then spread all over the country, and they gave me their formal thanks when I returned. But the proprietaries were enraged at Governor Denny for having passed the act, and turned him out, with threats of suing him for breach of the instructions he had given bond to observe. He, however, having done it at the instance of the General, and for His Majesty's service, and having some powerful interest at court, despised the threats, and they were never put in execution. …
