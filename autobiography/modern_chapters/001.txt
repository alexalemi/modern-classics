Chapter 2: Beginning Life as a Printer

From the time I was a child I loved to read, and every bit of pocket money that came my way went straight into books. I started with Pilgrim's Progress, which delighted me, so my first collection was John Bunyan's works in cheap little separate volumes. Later I sold those to buy R. Burton's Historical Collections — small peddlers' books, cheap, forty or fifty of them in all. My father's little library was mostly books of religious argument, and I read most of them. I've often regretted since that, at a time when I was so hungry for knowledge, better books didn't fall into my hands — since by then it was settled that I wasn't going to be a clergyman. There was a Plutarch's Lives, though, which I read constantly, and I still count that time well spent. There was also a book of Defoe's called An Essay on Projects, and one by Dr. Mather called Essays to Do Good, and between them they may have given my thinking a turn that shaped some of the main events of my life.

This bookish streak finally convinced my father to make me a printer, even though he already had one son — James — in that trade. In 1717 my brother James came back from England with a press and a set of type to open his business in Boston. I liked printing much better than my father's trade, but I still had a hankering for the sea. To head off that danger, my father was impatient to bind me to my brother. I held out for a while, but eventually I gave in and signed the indentures when I was only twelve years old. I was to serve as an apprentice until I turned twenty-one, with journeyman's wages allowed only in the final year. In a short time I got very good at the work and became genuinely useful to my brother. And now I had access to better books. Getting to know the booksellers' apprentices meant I could sometimes borrow a small volume, which I was careful to return promptly and in good condition. Often I sat up in my room reading most of the night, when a book was borrowed in the evening and had to be back early the next morning before anyone missed it or wanted it.

After a while an ingenious tradesman named Mr. Matthew Adams, who had a nice collection of books and often stopped by our printing house, took notice of me, invited me to his library, and very kindly lent me whatever books I chose. Around then I took a fancy to poetry and wrote some little pieces. My brother, thinking there might be money in it, encouraged me and set me to composing topical ballads. One was called "The Lighthouse Tragedy," about the drowning of Captain Worthilake and his two daughters; the other was a sailor's song about the capture of Teach — Blackbeard — the pirate. They were wretched stuff, pure gutter-press ballad style, and when they were printed my brother sent me around town to sell them. The first one sold wonderfully, since the disaster was fresh and had made a great stir. This flattered my vanity. But my father threw cold water on the whole business by ridiculing my work and telling me that verse-makers were generally beggars. So I escaped being a poet — most likely a very bad one. But since writing prose has been of great use to me all my life, and was one of the main means of my advancement, let me tell you how, in my situation, I acquired what little skill I have at it.

There was another bookish boy in town, John Collins by name, and we were close friends. We sometimes argued, and we were very fond of arguing — each of us eager to prove the other wrong. That argumentative streak, by the way, can become a very bad habit. It makes people insufferable company, because you can't practice it without contradicting everyone, and beyond souring the conversation, it breeds resentment and sometimes real enmity with people whose friendship you may need. I had caught the habit from reading my father's books of religious controversy. Sensible people, I've noticed since, rarely fall into it — except lawyers, university men, and men of every sort who were educated at Edinburgh.

Somehow a question came up between Collins and me about whether it was proper to give girls an education, and whether they had the ability for study. He held that it was improper and that women were naturally unequal to it. I took the other side, perhaps partly for the sake of the argument. He was naturally the more eloquent, had words at the ready, and sometimes, it seemed to me, beat me more by fluency than by the strength of his reasons. We parted without settling the point, and since we weren't going to see each other for a while, I sat down and put my arguments in writing, made a clean copy, and sent it to him. He answered, I replied, and three or four letters a side had passed when my father happened to find my papers and read them. Without wading into the debate itself, he took the occasion to talk to me about my writing. He observed that though I had my opponent beat in spelling and punctuation (which I owed to the printing house), I fell far short of him in elegance of expression, in organization, and in clarity — and he convinced me with several examples. I saw the justice of his criticism, paid more attention to style from then on, and resolved to improve.

Around this time I came across an odd volume of the Spectator. It was the third; I had never seen any of them before. I bought it, read it over and over, and was delighted with it. I thought the writing was excellent and wanted, if possible, to imitate it. So here is what I did. I took some of the essays and made short notes of the point of each sentence. I set them aside for a few days, and then, without looking at the book, tried to reconstruct the essays — expanding each noted point back to full length, as completely as the original, in whatever suitable words came to hand. Then I compared my Spectator with the real one, found some of my faults, and corrected them. But I discovered I lacked a stock of words, or a readiness in calling them up — something I'd have acquired by now, I thought, if I'd kept on writing verse. Verse constantly demands words of the same meaning but different lengths to fit the meter, or different sounds for the rhyme; that would have forced me to keep hunting for variety, and the hunting would have fixed the variety in my mind and made me master of it. So I took some of the tales and turned them into verse, and after a while, when I had pretty well forgotten the prose, turned them back again. Sometimes I also jumbled my stacks of notes into confusion, and weeks later tried to sort them into the best order before writing out the full sentences and completing the essay. This was to teach me method in arranging my thoughts. Comparing my work with the original afterward, I found many faults and fixed them — but sometimes I had the pleasure of imagining that in certain small particulars I'd been lucky enough to improve on the original, which encouraged me to think I might in time become a tolerable English writer. And that I ambitiously wanted to be. My time for these exercises and for reading was at night after work, or in the morning before it started, or on Sundays, when I contrived to be alone in the printing house — dodging, as much as I could, the church attendance my father used to require of me when I lived under his roof. I still considered it a duty, truthfully; I just couldn't, it seemed to me, afford the time to practice it.

When I was about sixteen I happened on a book by one Tryon recommending a vegetable diet, and I decided to take it up. My brother, being still unmarried, didn't keep his own house; he and his apprentices boarded with another family. My refusal to eat meat was an inconvenience, and I was frequently scolded for my oddity. So I learned Tryon's methods of preparing some of his dishes — boiling potatoes or rice, making hasty pudding, and a few others — and then made my brother a proposal: if he'd give me, each week, half the money he paid for my board, I would board myself. He agreed instantly, and I soon found I could save half of what he gave me. That became an extra fund for buying books. But there was another advantage in it. When my brother and the rest went off from the printing house to their meals, I stayed behind alone, and having quickly dispatched my light meal — often no more than a biscuit or a slice of bread, a handful of raisins or a tart from the pastry shop, and a glass of water — I had the rest of the time until they came back for study. And I made all the better progress for that clearness of head and quickness of mind that usually come with temperance in eating and drinking.

It was around now that, having been embarrassed on some occasion by my ignorance of arithmetic — which I had twice failed to learn at school — I took up Cocker's book of Arithmetic and went through the whole thing on my own with great ease. I also read Seller's and Shermy's books on navigation and picked up the little geometry they contain, though I never went far in that science. And about this time I read Locke's Essay Concerning Human Understanding and The Art of Thinking by the gentlemen of Port Royal.

While I was intent on improving my language, I came across an English grammar (Greenwood's, I think) which ended with two little sketches of the arts of rhetoric and logic, the latter closing with a sample dispute in the Socratic method. Soon afterward I got hold of Xenophon's Memorable Things of Socrates, which is full of examples of the same method. I was charmed by it and adopted it. I dropped my blunt contradiction and head-on argument and put on the role of the humble inquirer and doubter. And since my reading of Shaftesbury and Collins had by then made me a genuine doubter on many points of our religious doctrine, I found this method the safest for me and very embarrassing for those I used it on. So I took a real delight in it and practiced it constantly, and I grew very artful at drawing people — even people who knew more than I did — into concessions whose consequences they didn't foresee, tangling them in difficulties they couldn't escape, and winning victories that neither I nor my cause always deserved. I kept up this method for a few years, then gradually dropped it, keeping only the habit of expressing myself with modest diffidence. Whenever I advance anything that might possibly be disputed, I never use the words "certainly," "undoubtedly," or anything else that gives an opinion an air of positiveness. Instead I say, I conceive or I take a thing to be so-and-so; it appears to me, or I should think it so for such and such reasons; or I imagine it to be so; or it is so, if I am not mistaken. This habit, I believe, has been a great advantage to me whenever I've had to press my opinions and persuade people into the projects I've been engaged in promoting from time to time. And since the chief ends of conversation are to inform or be informed, to please or to persuade, I wish well-meaning, sensible men would not weaken their power of doing good with a positive, overbearing manner — which almost never fails to disgust, tends to create opposition, and defeats every purpose speech was given to us for, namely giving or receiving information or pleasure. If you want to inform, a positive, dogmatic manner of stating your views may provoke contradiction and shut down a fair hearing. If you want information and improvement from what others know, yet at the same time present yourself as firmly fixed in your current opinions, then modest, sensible people, who don't love a fight, will probably leave you undisturbed in possession of your error. And with such a manner you can rarely hope to charm your listeners or win over the people whose agreement you want. Pope says it well:

  "Men should be taught as if you taught them not,
  And things unknown proposed as things forgot;"

going on to recommend that we

  "To speak, though sure, with seeming diffidence."

And he might have paired with this line one he paired — less aptly, I think — with another:

  "For want of modesty is want of sense."

If you ask, why less aptly? I have to quote the lines:

  "Immodest words admit of no defense,
  For want of modesty is want of sense."

Now, isn't a lack of sense (when a man is unfortunate enough to lack it) some excuse for his lack of modesty? And wouldn't the lines stand more justly this way?

  "Immodest words admit but this defense,
  That want of modesty is want of sense."

This, however, I submit to better judgments.

In 1720 or 1721 my brother began printing a newspaper. It was the second one to appear in America, and it was called the New England Courant. The only one before it was the Boston News-Letter. I remember some of his friends trying to talk him out of the venture as unlikely to succeed — one newspaper, in their judgment, being enough for America. As I write this (in 1771), there are no fewer than twenty-five. He went ahead anyway, and after I'd helped set the type and print off the sheets, my job was to carry the papers through the streets to the customers.

He had some clever men among his friends who amused themselves by writing little pieces for the paper, which raised its reputation and its sales, and these gentlemen often visited us. Hearing their conversations, and their accounts of how well their pieces were received, I itched to try my hand among them. But I was still a boy, and I suspected my brother would refuse to print anything in his paper that he knew was mine. So I contrived to disguise my handwriting, wrote an anonymous piece, and slipped it under the printing-house door at night. It was found in the morning and passed around to his writing friends when they called in as usual. They read it, discussed it within my hearing, and I had the exquisite pleasure of finding that it met with their approval — and that in their various guesses at the author, they named only men with some reputation among us for learning and cleverness. I suppose now that I was lucky in my judges, and that they were perhaps not quite as good as I thought them then.

Encouraged by this, I wrote several more pieces and delivered them to the press the same way, and they were equally approved. I kept my secret until my small stock of material for such performances was pretty well used up, and then I revealed it. After that my brother's acquaintances began to take a bit more notice of me — in a way that didn't entirely please him, since he thought, probably with reason, that it was making me too vain. This may have been one cause of the differences that started between us around this time. Though he was my brother, he considered himself my master and me his apprentice, and expected the same service from me as from anyone else, while I felt that some of his demands were degrading from a brother, from whom I expected more indulgence. Our disputes were often brought before our father, and I imagine I was either usually in the right or else the better pleader, because the judgment was usually in my favor. But my brother had a temper and had often beaten me, which I took extremely hard. Finding my apprenticeship tedious, I was constantly wishing for some way to shorten it — and at last a way presented itself, from an unexpected direction.

One of the pieces in our paper, on some political point I've now forgotten, gave offense to the Assembly. My brother was taken up, censured, and jailed for a month by the Speaker's warrant — because, I suppose, he wouldn't reveal his author. I was taken up and examined before the council too, but though I gave them no satisfaction, they settled for admonishing me and let me go — considering me, perhaps, as an apprentice bound to keep his master's secrets.

During my brother's confinement, which I resented a good deal despite our private differences, I ran the paper, and I made bold to give our rulers some jabs in it. My brother took this very kindly, while others began to see me in an unfavorable light — as a young genius with a turn for libel and satire. My brother's release came with an order of the House (a very odd one) that "James Franklin should no longer print the paper called the New England Courant."

His friends held a consultation in our printing house about what he should do. Some proposed dodging the order by changing the paper's name; but my brother saw inconveniences in that, and it was finally decided that the better way was to print the paper in the future under the name of Benjamin Franklin. And to escape the Assembly's censure — which might still fall on him for printing it through his apprentice — the contrivance was this: my old indenture would be returned to me with a full discharge written on the back, to be shown if anyone asked; but to secure my service to him anyway, I would sign new indentures for the rest of my term, which would be kept private. A very flimsy scheme it was. Still, it was carried out at once, and the paper ran under my name for several months.

At length a fresh quarrel arose between my brother and me, and I took it upon myself to assert my freedom, betting that he wouldn't dare produce the secret indentures. It wasn't fair of me to take that advantage, and so I count it one of the first errata of my life — errata, a printer's word for typos. But the unfairness of it weighed little with me against my resentment of the beatings his temper too often led him to give me — though he was otherwise not an ill-natured man. Perhaps I was too saucy and provoking.

When he found I meant to leave him, he made sure I couldn't get work at any other printing house in town, going around and speaking to every master, who accordingly refused to hire me. I then thought of going to New York, the nearest place with a printer. And I was rather inclined to leave Boston anyway when I considered that I'd already made myself a little obnoxious to the governing party; that, judging by the Assembly's arbitrary proceedings in my brother's case, I might soon land in trouble myself if I stayed; and further, that my careless arguments about religion had begun to make good people point at me with horror as an infidel or atheist. So I made up my mind. But my father was now siding with my brother, and I knew that if I tried to leave openly, steps would be taken to stop me. My friend Collins therefore undertook to manage a little scheme for me. He arranged my passage with the captain of a New York sloop, passing me off as a young acquaintance of his. I sold some of my books to raise a little money, was taken aboard privately, and with a fair wind, in three days I found myself in New York — nearly three hundred miles from home, a boy of just seventeen, without a single recommendation to anyone in the place, without knowing a soul there, and with very little money in my pocket.
