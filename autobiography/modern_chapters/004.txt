Chapter 5: Early Friends in Philadelphia

Keimer and I got along well enough on the surface, and agreed tolerably — he suspected nothing of my plans to set up on my own. He still carried a good deal of his old religious enthusiasm and loved an argument, so we had plenty of them. I used to work him over with my Socratic method: I'd snare him again and again with questions that seemed miles away from anything we were discussing, then lead him step by step into difficulties and contradictions, until at last he grew ridiculously cautious and would hardly answer the most ordinary question without first asking, "What do you intend to infer from that?" Still, all this gave him such a high opinion of my talents in the confuting line that he seriously proposed I join him in a project he had of founding a new religious sect. He would preach the doctrines; I would confound all opponents. But when he sat down to explain the doctrines to me, I found several puzzles I objected to — unless I could have my way a little too, and slip in some doctrines of my own.

Keimer wore his beard at full length, because somewhere in the Mosaic law it says, "Thou shalt not mar the corners of thy beard." He also kept Saturday as his Sabbath, and these two points were essentials with him. I disliked both, but agreed to accept them on condition that he adopt a doctrine of mine: no animal food. "I doubt," he said, "my constitution won't bear it." I assured him it would, and that he'd be the better for it. He was normally a tremendous glutton, and I promised myself some entertainment in half starving him. He agreed to try it if I'd keep him company, and we held to it for three months. A woman in the neighborhood cooked our meals and brought them to us regularly; she had from me a list of forty dishes to prepare for us at different times, with neither fish, flesh, nor fowl in any of them. The whim suited me all the better just then for its cheapness — it cost us no more than eighteenpence sterling a week apiece. I have since kept several Lents very strictly, dropping the ordinary diet for that one and switching back again abruptly, without the least inconvenience — so I put little stock in the advice that such changes must be made by easy stages. I went on pleasantly, but poor Keimer suffered grievously. He tired of the project, longed for the flesh-pots of Egypt, and ordered a roast pig. He invited me and two women friends to dine on it with him — but the pig came to the table a little too soon, and he couldn't resist the temptation. He ate the whole thing before we arrived.

During this time I had been doing some courting of Miss Read. I had great respect and affection for her, and some reason to believe she felt the same for me. But I was about to take a long voyage, and we were both very young — only a little past eighteen — so her mother thought it most prudent to keep things from going too far for the present. A marriage, if there was to be one, would be more convenient after my return, when I expected to be set up in my business. Perhaps, too, she thought my expectations weren't as well founded as I imagined them to be.

My chief friends at this time were Charles Osborne, Joseph Watson, and James Ralph — all lovers of reading. The first two were clerks to Charles Brockden, an eminent scrivener and conveyancer in town; the third was clerk to a merchant. Watson was a pious, sensible young man of great integrity; the other two were rather looser in their religious principles — particularly Ralph, who, like Collins, had been unsettled by me, and both of them made me pay for it. Osborne was sensible, candid, and frank, sincere and affectionate with his friends, but in literary matters too fond of criticizing. Ralph was clever, polished in his manners, and extremely eloquent; I don't think I ever knew a prettier talker. Both were great admirers of poetry and had begun trying their hands at little pieces. The four of us took many pleasant Sunday walks together in the woods near the Schuylkill, where we read to one another and talked over what we read.

Ralph was inclined to make poetry his pursuit. He had no doubt he could become eminent at it, and even make his fortune by it, arguing that the best poets, when they first began to write, must have made as many faults as he did. Osborne tried to talk him out of it — assured him he had no genius for poetry, and advised him to think of nothing beyond the business he'd been trained for: in the merchant's line, even with no capital of his own, his diligence and punctuality might recommend him to employment as an agent, and in time he could acquire enough to trade on his own account. My view was that amusing yourself with poetry now and then was fine, as far as it improved your command of language — but no farther.

Out of this came a proposal: at our next meeting, each of us would produce a piece of his own composing, so that we might improve by trading observations, criticisms, and corrections. Since language and expression were what we cared about, we ruled out any contest of invention by agreeing that the task would be a version of the eighteenth Psalm, which describes the descent of a Deity. As the meeting drew near, Ralph called on me first and told me his piece was ready. I told him I'd been busy and, having little inclination, had done nothing. He then showed me his piece for my opinion, and I approved it heartily — it seemed to me to have great merit. "Now," he said, "Osborne will never allow the least merit in anything of mine — he makes a thousand criticisms out of pure envy. He's not so jealous of you. So I'd like you to take this piece and present it as yours. I'll pretend I hadn't time, and produce nothing. Then we'll see what he says about it." It was agreed, and I copied it out immediately, so it would appear in my own handwriting.

We met. Watson's performance was read: some beauties in it, but many defects. Osborne's was read: much better. Ralph did it justice — pointed out some faults, but applauded the beauties. Ralph himself had nothing to produce. I hung back; seemed to want to be excused; hadn't had enough time to correct it, and so on. But no excuse would be accepted — produce I must. It was read, and read again. Watson and Osborne gave up the contest on the spot and joined in applauding it. Only Ralph made some criticisms and proposed some amendments — but I defended my text. Osborne sided against Ralph, told him he was no better a critic than he was a poet, and Ralph dropped the argument. As the two of them walked home together, Osborne praised what he took to be my production even more warmly — he'd restrained himself earlier, he said, in case I thought it flattery. "But who would have imagined," he said, "that Franklin was capable of such a performance — such painting, such force, such fire! He has even improved on the original. In ordinary conversation he seems to have no choice of words at all; he hesitates and blunders — and yet, good God, how he writes!" When we next met, Ralph revealed the trick we'd played on him, and Osborne got laughed at a little.

This episode fixed Ralph in his resolution to become a poet. I did everything I could to talk him out of it, but he kept scribbling verses until the poet Pope cured him. He became a pretty good prose writer, though. More of him later. As for the other two — since I may not have occasion to mention them again — I'll just note here that Watson died in my arms a few years later, much lamented; he was the best of our set. Osborne went to the West Indies, where he became an eminent lawyer and made money, but died young. He and I had made a serious agreement: whichever of us happened to die first would, if possible, pay a friendly visit to the other and report on how he found things in that separate state. But he never kept his promise.
