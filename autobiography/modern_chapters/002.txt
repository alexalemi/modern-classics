Chapter 3: Arrival in Philadelphia

By this point my old longing for the sea had worn itself out, or I might have indulged it now that I was free. But I had a trade, and I figured I was a pretty good workman, so instead I offered my services to the local printer, old Mr. William Bradford — the first printer Pennsylvania ever had, who'd left that colony over the quarrel with George Keith. He had no work for me; his business was small and he had all the help he needed. But he said, "My son in Philadelphia just lost his principal hand, Aquilla Rose — the man died. If you go down there, I believe he may take you on." Philadelphia was another hundred miles. I set out anyway, in a boat for Amboy, leaving my chest and things to come around after me by sea.

Crossing the bay, we hit a squall that tore our rotten sails to shreds, kept us from making the Kill, and drove us toward Long Island. Along the way a drunk Dutchman, another passenger, tumbled overboard. As he was going under I reached down through the water, grabbed him by his shaggy head, and hauled him up so we could pull him back in. The dunking sobered him a little, and he went to sleep — but first he fished a book out of his pocket and asked me to dry it for him. It turned out to be my old favorite author: Bunyan's Pilgrim's Progress, in Dutch, finely printed on good paper with copper engravings — a handsomer outfit than I'd ever seen it wear in its own language. I've since learned it has been translated into most of the languages of Europe, and I'd guess it has been read more widely than any other book except perhaps the Bible. Honest John was the first writer I know of to mix narration with dialogue — a very engaging way to write, because at the most interesting moments the reader feels himself brought right into the company, present at the conversation. Defoe imitated it successfully in his Crusoe, his Moll Flanders, Religious Courtship, Family Instructor, and other pieces; and Richardson did the same in his Pamela and the rest.

When we drew near the island, we found ourselves at a spot where landing was impossible — heavy surf pounding a stony beach. So we dropped anchor and swung around toward the shore. Some people came down to the water's edge and shouted to us, and we shouted back, but the wind was so high and the surf so loud that we couldn't make out a word either way. There were canoes on the shore, and we made signs and hollered for them to come fetch us; but either they didn't understand or they thought it couldn't be done, because they walked away. With night coming on, there was nothing to do but wait for the wind to die down. In the meantime the boatman and I decided to sleep if we could, so we crowded into the little hatch with the Dutchman, who was still soaked. The spray beating over the bow leaked through onto us, so that we were soon nearly as wet as he was. We lay like that all night with very little rest; but the wind eased the next day, and we managed to reach Amboy before dark — after thirty hours on the water with no food and nothing to drink but a bottle of filthy rum, the water we sailed on being salt.

By evening I was running a fever, and I went to bed; but having read somewhere that drinking plenty of cold water was good for a fever, I followed the prescription, sweated most of the night, and woke up cured. In the morning I crossed the ferry and continued my journey on foot, with fifty miles to go to Burlington, where I was told I'd find boats to carry me the rest of the way to Philadelphia.

It rained hard all day. I was soaked through and, by noon, thoroughly worn out, so I stopped at a shabby inn and stayed the night — beginning, now, to wish I had never left home. I made such a miserable sight, too, that I could tell from the questions people asked me that I was suspected of being a runaway servant, and in some danger of being arrested on that suspicion. Still, I pushed on the next day and by evening reached an inn within eight or ten miles of Burlington, kept by one Dr. Brown. He struck up a conversation while I had some refreshment, and, finding I'd done a little reading, became very sociable and friendly. Our acquaintance lasted as long as he lived. He had been, I imagine, a traveling doctor, for there was no town in England, nor any country in Europe, he couldn't describe in close detail. He had some learning and was clever, but he was a thoroughgoing unbeliever, and some years later he wickedly set about rewriting the Bible as comic doggerel verse, the way Cotton had done with Virgil. This cast many of the facts in a very ridiculous light and might have damaged weak minds if the thing had ever been published — but it never was.

I stayed at his house that night and reached Burlington the next morning, only to have the mortification of learning that the regular boats had left shortly before I arrived, and no other was expected until Tuesday — this being Saturday. So I went back to an old woman in town from whom I'd bought some gingerbread to eat on the water, and asked her advice. She invited me to lodge at her house until a boat should turn up, and being tired of traveling on foot, I accepted. When she learned I was a printer, she wanted me to stay in that town and set up shop — having no idea what stock it takes to get started. She was very hospitable, served me a dinner of ox-cheek with real good will, and would accept nothing in return but a pot of ale; and I considered myself settled until Tuesday. But walking along the river that evening, I saw a boat come by that turned out to be heading for Philadelphia with several people aboard. They took me in, and since there was no wind, we rowed the whole way. Around midnight, not having seen the city yet, some of the company were sure we must have passed it and refused to row another stroke; the others had no idea where we were. So we pulled toward the shore, worked into a creek, and landed near an old fence, whose rails we used for a fire — the night being cold, in October — and there we stayed until daylight. Then one of the company recognized the place as Cooper's Creek, a little above Philadelphia, which we saw the moment we got clear of the creek. We arrived about eight or nine o'clock on Sunday morning and landed at the Market Street wharf.

I've gone into such detail about this journey, and will do the same for my first entrance into that city, so that you can compare, in your mind, such unlikely beginnings with the figure I've since made there. I was in my working clothes, my good ones coming around by sea. I was dirty from the trip; my pockets bulged with shirts and stockings; I didn't know a soul, or where to look for lodging. I was exhausted from walking, rowing, and lack of sleep; I was very hungry; and my entire fortune consisted of one Dutch dollar and about a shilling in copper. The copper I gave to the people of the boat for my passage. At first they refused it, since I had rowed; but I insisted they take it. A man is sometimes more generous when he has little money than when he has plenty — perhaps for fear of being thought to have but little.

Then I walked up the street, gawking at everything, until near the market house I met a boy carrying bread. I had made many a meal on bread, and asking where he'd gotten it, I went straight to the baker he pointed out, on Second Street, and asked for biscuit, meaning the kind we had in Boston. It seems they didn't make that in Philadelphia. Then I asked for a threepenny loaf, and was told they had no such thing. So — not considering, or knowing, the difference in money, or how much cheaper things were here, or what his breads were called — I told him to give me threepence worth of any sort. He gave me, accordingly, three great puffy rolls. I was surprised at how much bread that bought, but I took it, and having no room in my pockets, walked off with a roll under each arm, eating the third. In this fashion I went up Market Street as far as Fourth Street, passing the door of Mr. Read, my future wife's father — and she, standing in the doorway, saw me, and thought I made, as I certainly did, a most awkward, ridiculous appearance. Then I turned down Chestnut Street and part of Walnut Street, eating my roll the whole way, and, coming around, found myself back at the Market Street wharf, near the boat I'd arrived in. I went to it for a drink of river water, and being filled up by that one roll, I gave the other two to a woman and her child who had come down the river with us and were waiting to travel on.

Refreshed, I walked up the street again, which by now had many clean-dressed people in it, all walking the same direction. I fell in with them and was carried along into the great Quaker meetinghouse near the market. I sat down among them, and after looking around a while and hearing nothing said — being very drowsy from the labor and lost sleep of the night before — I fell fast asleep, and stayed that way until the meeting broke up, when someone was kind enough to wake me. This, then, was the first house I was ever in, or slept in, in Philadelphia.

Walking back down toward the river, studying people's faces as I went, I met a young Quaker man whose look I liked, and stopping him, I asked if he could tell me where a stranger might find lodging. We were just then near the sign of the Three Mariners. "Here," he said, "is one place that takes in strangers, but it is not a reputable house; if thee wilt walk with me, I'll show thee a better." He brought me to the Crooked Billet on Water Street. There I got a dinner, and while I ate it, several sly questions were put to me — it seemed my youth and my appearance raised the suspicion, again, that I might be some runaway.

After dinner my sleepiness came back, and being shown to a bed, I lay down without undressing and slept until six in the evening; was called to supper; went back to bed very early; and slept soundly until morning. Then I made myself as presentable as I could and went to Andrew Bradford the printer's. In the shop I found the old man his father, whom I'd seen in New York — traveling on horseback, he had beaten me to Philadelphia. He introduced me to his son, who received me politely and gave me breakfast, but told me he didn't currently need a hand, having just hired one. However, there was another printer in town, newly set up, one Keimer, who might perhaps employ me; and if not, I was welcome to lodge at his house, and he'd give me a little work now and then until fuller business turned up.

The old gentleman offered to walk me over to the new printer himself. When we found him: "Neighbor," says Bradford, "I've brought you a young man of your own trade; perhaps you may have use for one." Keimer asked me a few questions, put a composing stick in my hand to see how I worked, and said he'd employ me soon, though just then he had nothing for me to do. Then — taking old Bradford, whom he'd never seen before, for some townsman who wished him well — he launched into a full account of his current venture and his prospects. Bradford, careful not to reveal that he was the father of the other printer, drew him on with artful questions and little planted doubts — on Keimer's boasting that he expected soon to have the greater part of the business in his own hands — until Keimer had explained everything: his plans, what backing he counted on, and exactly how he meant to proceed. I stood by and heard it all, and saw at once that one of them was a crafty old fox and the other a complete novice. Bradford left me with Keimer, who was thunderstruck when I told him who the old man was.

Keimer's printing house, I found, consisted of one battered old press and one small, worn-out font of English type, which he was then using himself, composing an elegy on Aquilla Rose — the man I mentioned earlier, a clever young fellow of excellent character, much respected in town, clerk of the Assembly, and a pretty fair poet. Keimer wrote verses too, but very poorly. In fact you couldn't really say he wrote them, because his method was to compose them directly in the type, straight out of his head. So with no written copy, only one pair of typecases, and the elegy likely to use up all the letters, nobody could possibly help him. I set about putting his press — which he hadn't yet used, and understood nothing about — into working order; and promising to come back and print off his elegy as soon as he had it ready, I returned to Bradford's, who gave me a small job for the time being, and there I had my room and board. A few days later Keimer sent for me to print the elegy. By then he'd gotten another pair of cases and a pamphlet to reprint, and he set me to work on it.

Both of these printers, I found, were poorly qualified for their business. Bradford hadn't been trained to it at all, and was barely literate; and Keimer, though something of a scholar, was purely a compositor and knew nothing of presswork. He had been one of the French Prophets and could act out their ecstatic convulsions on demand. At this time he professed no particular religion, but a little of every one as the occasion served; he was very ignorant of the world, and had, as I later discovered, a good deal of the knave in his makeup. He didn't like my lodging at Bradford's while working for him. He had a house of his own, true, but no furniture in it, so he couldn't put me up; instead he got me a room at Mr. Read's — the man I mentioned before, who owned Keimer's house. By then my chest and clothes had arrived, and I made a rather more respectable figure in the eyes of Miss Read than I had the day she first happened to see me eating my roll in the street.

I now began to make some acquaintances among the young people of the town who loved reading, and I spent my evenings with them very pleasantly; and earning money by hard work and frugality, I lived quite agreeably, forgetting Boston as much as I could, and not wanting anyone there to know where I was — except my friend Collins, who was in on my secret and kept it when I wrote to him. At length an incident occurred that sent me back much sooner than I'd intended. I had a brother-in-law, Robert Holmes, master of a sloop that traded between Boston and the Delaware. While at Newcastle, forty miles below Philadelphia, he heard about me and wrote me a letter, telling me how worried my friends in Boston were at my abrupt departure, assuring me of their good will, and promising that everything would be arranged to my satisfaction if I would come home — which he urged on me very earnestly. I wrote back, thanked him for his advice, and laid out my reasons for leaving Boston so fully, and in such a light, that I convinced him I was not as much in the wrong as he had supposed.
