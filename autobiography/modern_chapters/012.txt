Chapter 13: Public Services and Duties (1749–1753)

With the peace concluded and the militia association business at an end, I turned my thoughts back to the project of establishing an academy. My first step was to bring a number of active friends into the design — the Junto supplied a good share of them. The next was to write and publish a pamphlet called "Proposals Relating to the Education of Youth in Pennsylvania." I distributed it free among the town's principal citizens, and as soon as I could suppose their minds a little prepared by reading it, I set a subscription on foot for opening and supporting an academy. It was to be paid in yearly installments over five years; by dividing it that way, I judged the total would come in larger — and I believe it did, amounting, if I remember right, to no less than five thousand pounds.

In the introduction to these proposals I presented their publication not as my own act but as that of "some public-spirited gentlemen" — avoiding, as much as I could and according to my usual rule, presenting myself to the public as the author of any scheme for their benefit.

The subscribers, to carry the project into immediate execution, chose twenty-four trustees from among their number, and appointed Mr. Francis, then attorney general, and me to draw up a constitution for governing the academy. That being done and signed, a house was rented, teachers were hired, and the schools opened — in, I think, the same year, 1749.

The students multiplied fast, the house was soon found too small, and we were looking for a properly situated piece of ground to build on when Providence threw into our way a large house already built, which with a few alterations might serve our purpose well. This was the building I mentioned before, erected by Mr. Whitefield's hearers, and we obtained it in the following manner.

You should know that since the contributions to that building had come from people of many different sects, care had been taken, in naming the trustees who would hold the building and ground, that no single sect should predominate — lest in time that predominance become a means of appropriating the whole to that sect's use, contrary to the original intention. So one trustee of each sect was appointed: one Church of England man, one Presbyterian, one Baptist, one Moravian, and so on; and when one died, the vacancy was to be filled by election from among the contributors. The Moravian happened not to please his colleagues, and on his death they resolved to have no other of that sect. The difficulty then was how to avoid ending up with two of some other sect through the new choice.

Several people were named, and rejected for exactly that reason. At length someone mentioned me, with the observation that I was merely an honest man, and of no sect at all — which prevailed with them to choose me. The enthusiasm that existed when the house was built had long since cooled, and its trustees had not been able to raise fresh contributions to pay the ground rent and clear the other debts the building had run up, which embarrassed them greatly. Now that I was a member of both boards of trustees — the building's and the academy's — I was well placed to negotiate with both, and I finally brought them to an agreement: the building's trustees would cede it to the academy's, and the academy would pay off the debt, keep a large hall in the building forever open for occasional preachers (honoring the original intention), and maintain a free school for the instruction of poor children. The papers were drawn accordingly, and when the debts were paid, the academy's trustees took possession of the premises. By dividing the great, lofty hall into stories, with different rooms above and below for the several schools, and by purchasing some additional ground, the whole was soon made fit for our purpose, and the students moved into the building. The care and trouble of settling terms with the workmen, buying materials, and superintending the work fell on me, and I went through it the more cheerfully because it no longer interfered with my private business. The year before, I had taken a very able, industrious, and honest partner, Mr. David Hall, whose character I knew well — he had worked for me four years. He took the whole care of the printing office off my hands and paid me my share of the profits punctually. The partnership lasted eighteen years, successfully for us both.

After a while the academy's trustees were incorporated by a charter from the governor. Their funds grew through contributions in Britain and grants of land from the proprietaries, to which the Assembly has since made considerable additions — and thus was established the present University of Philadelphia. I have remained one of its trustees from the beginning, nearly forty years now, and have had the very great pleasure of seeing numbers of the young people educated there distinguished by their improved abilities, serviceable in public office, and ornaments to their country.

When I disengaged from private business, as I mentioned above, I flattered myself that with the sufficient though moderate fortune I had acquired, I had secured leisure for the rest of my life for scientific studies and amusements. I bought all of Dr. Spence's apparatus — he had come over from England to lecture here — and went at my electrical experiments with great eagerness. But the public, now considering me a man of leisure, laid hold of me for their own purposes. Every branch of our civil government, and almost all at once, imposed some duty on me: the governor put me into the commission of the peace; the city corporation chose me for the common council, and soon after made me an alderman; and the citizens at large elected me a burgess to represent them in the Assembly. This last post was the most agreeable to me. I was thoroughly tired by then of sitting in the House to hear debates in which, as clerk, I could take no part — debates often so unentertaining that I amused myself making magic squares or circles, anything to fend off the weariness — and I conceived that becoming a member would enlarge my power of doing good. I won't pretend, though, that my ambition wasn't flattered by all these promotions. It certainly was. Considering my low beginnings, they were great things to me — and the more pleasing for being so many spontaneous testimonies of the public's good opinion, entirely unsolicited by me.

The office of justice of the peace I tried a little, attending a few courts and sitting on the bench to hear cases; but finding that doing the job with credit required more knowledge of the common law than I possessed, I gradually withdrew from it, excusing myself by my obligation to attend to the higher duties of a legislator in the Assembly. My election to that trust was repeated every year for ten years, without my ever asking any voter for his vote, or signifying, directly or indirectly, any desire to be chosen. On my taking my seat in the House, my son was appointed their clerk.

The following year, a treaty was to be held with the Indians at Carlisle, and the governor sent a message to the House proposing that they nominate some of their members to be joined with some members of his council as commissioners for the purpose. The House named the speaker, Mr. Norris, and me; and being commissioned, we went to Carlisle and met the Indians accordingly.

Since those people are extremely apt to get drunk — and when drunk are very quarrelsome and disorderly — we strictly forbade the selling of any liquor to them. When they complained of this restriction, we told them that if they would stay sober during the treaty, we would give them plenty of rum when the business was over. They promised this, and they kept their promise — because they could get no liquor — and the treaty was conducted in very good order and concluded to mutual satisfaction. Then they claimed the rum, and received it. This was in the afternoon. They were nearly a hundred — men, women, and children — lodged in temporary cabins built in the form of a square just outside the town. In the evening, hearing a great noise among them, we commissioners walked out to see what was the matter. We found they had built a great bonfire in the middle of the square, and they were all drunk, men and women, quarreling and fighting. Their dark-colored bodies, half naked, seen only by the gloomy light of the bonfire, running after and beating one another with firebrands, accompanied by their horrid yelling, formed a scene as near our ideas of hell as could well be imagined. There was no appeasing the tumult, and we retired to our lodging. At midnight a number of them came thundering at our door, demanding more rum, of which we took no notice.

The next day, sensible that they had misbehaved in giving us that disturbance, they sent three of their old counselors to make their apology. The orator acknowledged the fault, but laid it upon the rum — and then endeavored to excuse the rum itself by saying, "The Great Spirit, who made all things, made everything for some use, and whatever use he designed anything for, to that use it should always be put. Now, when he made rum, he said, 'Let this be for the Indians to get drunk with,' and it must be so." And indeed, if it be the design of Providence to extirpate these savages in order to make room for cultivators of the earth, it seems not improbable that rum may be the appointed means. It has already annihilated all the tribes who formerly inhabited the seacoast.

In 1751, Dr. Thomas Bond, a particular friend of mine, conceived the idea of establishing a hospital in Philadelphia for the reception and cure of poor sick people, whether inhabitants of the province or strangers — a very beneficent design that has been ascribed to me, but was originally his. He was zealous and active in trying to procure subscriptions for it; but the proposal being a novelty in America, and at first not well understood, he met with small success.

At length he came to me with the compliment that he had found there was no such thing as carrying a public-spirited project through without my being concerned in it. "For," said he, "I am often asked by those I propose subscribing to, 'Have you consulted Franklin upon this business? And what does he think of it?' And when I tell them that I have not — supposing it rather out of your line — they do not subscribe, but say they will consider it." I inquired into the nature and probable usefulness of his scheme, and receiving from him a very satisfactory explanation, I not only subscribed to it myself but engaged heartily in the business of procuring subscriptions from others. First, however, before any soliciting, I endeavored to prepare people's minds by writing on the subject in the newspapers — my usual custom in such cases, but a step he had omitted.

The subscriptions afterward came more freely and generously; but as they began to flag, I saw they would be insufficient without some assistance from the Assembly, and therefore proposed to petition for it, which was done. The country members did not at first relish the project. They objected that it could only be serviceable to the city, and therefore the citizens alone should bear the expense of it; and they doubted whether the citizens themselves generally approved of it. My claim to the contrary — that the project met with such approval as to leave no doubt of our being able to raise two thousand pounds by voluntary donations — they considered a most extravagant supposition, and utterly impossible.

On this I formed my plan. I asked leave to bring in a bill for incorporating the contributors according to the prayer of their petition, and for granting them a blank sum of money — leave that was obtained chiefly on the consideration that the House could throw the bill out if they didn't like it. I then drew the bill so as to make the important clause a conditional one, namely: "And be it enacted, by the authority aforesaid, that when the said contributors shall have met and chosen their managers and treasurer, and shall have raised by their contributions a capital stock of ⸻ value (the yearly interest of which is to be applied to the accommodating of the sick poor in the said hospital, free of charge for diet, attendance, advice, and medicines), and shall make the same appear to the satisfaction of the speaker of the Assembly for the time being, that then it shall and may be lawful for the said speaker, and he is hereby required, to sign an order on the provincial treasurer for the payment of two thousand pounds, in two yearly payments, to the treasurer of the said hospital, to be applied to the founding, building, and finishing of the same."

This condition carried the bill through; for the members who had opposed the grant, and who now conceived they might have the credit of being charitable without the expense, agreed to its passage. And then, in soliciting subscriptions among the people, we urged the conditional promise of the law as an additional motive to give, since every man's donation would be doubled. Thus the clause worked both ways. The subscriptions soon exceeded the required sum; we claimed and received the public gift, which enabled us to carry the design into execution. A convenient and handsome building was soon erected; the institution has by constant experience been found useful, and flourishes to this day; and I do not remember any of my political maneuvers whose success gave me at the time more pleasure — or in which, after thinking it over, I more easily excused myself for having made some use of cunning.

It was about this time that another projector, the Rev. Gilbert Tennent, came to me with a request that I help him procure a subscription for building a new meetinghouse. It was to be for the use of a congregation he had gathered among the Presbyterians who were originally disciples of Mr. Whitefield. Unwilling to make myself disagreeable to my fellow citizens by soliciting their contributions too frequently, I absolutely refused. He then asked me to furnish him with a list of the names of people I knew by experience to be generous and public-spirited. I thought it would be unbecoming in me, after their kind compliance with my own solicitations, to mark them out to be worried by other beggars, so I refused that too. He then asked if I would at least give him my advice. "That I will readily do," I said. "In the first place, I advise you to apply to all those you know will give something; next, to those you are uncertain whether they will give anything or not, and show them the list of those who have given; and lastly, do not neglect those you are sure will give nothing — for in some of them you may be mistaken." He laughed, thanked me, and said he would take my advice. He did, for he asked everybody — and he obtained a much larger sum than he expected, with which he erected the capacious and very elegant meetinghouse that stands on Arch Street.

Our city, though laid out with a beautiful regularity — the streets wide, straight, and crossing each other at right angles — had the disgrace of letting those streets remain unpaved for years. In wet weather the wheels of heavy carriages plowed them into a quagmire, so that it was difficult to cross them; in dry weather the dust was offensive. I lived near what was called the Jersey Market, and saw with pain the inhabitants wading through mud while buying their provisions. At length a strip of ground down the middle of that market was paved with brick, so that once you were in the market you had firm footing — but you were often over your shoes in dirt getting there. By talking and writing on the subject, I was at length instrumental in getting the street between the market and the brick foot-pavement along the houses paved with stone. For some time this gave dry-shod access to the market; but the rest of the street being unpaved, whenever a carriage came out of the mud onto this pavement it shook off its dirt and left it there, and the pavement was soon covered with mire that nobody removed — the city as yet having no scavengers to clean its streets.

After some inquiry, I found a poor, industrious man who was willing to keep the pavement clean by sweeping it twice a week and carrying off the dirt from before all the neighbors' doors, for the sum of sixpence per month from each house. I then wrote and printed a paper setting out the advantages the neighborhood could have for this small expense: houses easier to keep clean, with so much less dirt carried in on people's feet; more custom for the shops, since buyers could get to them more easily; no dust blown in upon the goods in windy weather; and so on. I sent one of these papers to each house, and in a day or two went around to see who would sign an agreement to pay the sixpences. It was signed unanimously, and for a time well executed. All the inhabitants of the city were delighted with the cleanliness of the pavement surrounding the market, it being a convenience to everyone; and this raised a general desire to have all the streets paved, and made the people more willing to submit to a tax for that purpose.

After some time I drew a bill for paving the city and brought it into the Assembly. This was just before I went to England in 1757, and it did not pass until I was gone — and then with an alteration in the mode of assessment that I thought was not for the better, but with an additional provision for lighting the streets as well as paving them, which was a great improvement. It was a private person, the late Mr. John Clifton, who first impressed the people with the idea of lighting the whole city, by giving them a sample of the usefulness of lamps — he placed one at his own door. The honor of this public benefit has also been ascribed to me, but it belongs truly to that gentleman. I only followed his example, and can claim some merit only respecting the form of our lamps, which differed from the globe lamps we were at first supplied with from London. Those we found inconvenient in several respects. They admitted no air below, so the smoke did not readily go out above but circulated inside the globe, settled on its inner surface, and soon obstructed the very light the lamps were meant to give — besides costing the daily trouble of wiping them clean; and an accidental knock on one of them would demolish it and render it totally useless. I therefore suggested composing them of four flat panes, with a long funnel above to draw up the smoke, and crevices below to admit air and help the smoke rise. By this means they stayed clean and did not grow dark in a few hours, as the London lamps do, but continued bright till morning; and an accidental blow would generally break only a single pane, easily repaired.

I have sometimes wondered that the Londoners — seeing what the holes in the bottoms of the globe lamps used at Vauxhall do toward keeping them clean — never learned to put such holes in their street lamps. But those holes were made for another purpose: to carry flame quickly to the wick by a little flax hanging down through them. The other use, of letting in air, seems never to have been thought of; and so, after the lamps have been lit a few hours, the streets of London are very poorly illuminated.

The mention of these improvements puts me in mind of one I proposed, when in London, to Dr. Fothergill, who was among the best men I have known and a great promoter of useful projects. I had observed that the streets there, when dry, were never swept and the light dust carried away; it was suffered to accumulate until wet weather reduced it to mud, and then — after lying some days so deep on the pavement that there was no crossing except along paths kept clean by poor people with brooms — it was with great labor raked together and thrown up into carts open at the top, whose sides let some of the slush shake out and fall at every jolt on the pavement, sometimes to the annoyance of foot-passengers. The reason given for not sweeping the dusty streets was that the dust would fly into the windows of shops and houses.

An accidental occurrence had taught me how much sweeping could be done in a little time. One morning I found a poor woman at my door in Craven Street, sweeping my pavement with a birch broom. She looked very pale and feeble, as if just come out of a fit of sickness. I asked who employed her to sweep there. "Nobody," she said, "but I am very poor and in distress, and I sweeps before gentle-folkses doors, and hopes they will give me something." I told her to sweep the whole street clean and I would give her a shilling. This was at nine o'clock; at twelve she came for the shilling. From the slowness I had seen at first in her working, I could scarcely believe the work was done so soon, and I sent my servant to examine it. He reported that the whole street was swept perfectly clean, and all the dust placed in the gutter, which ran down the middle; and the next rain washed it quite away, so that the pavement and even the gutter were perfectly clean.

I judged then that if that feeble woman could sweep such a street in three hours, a strong, active man might have done it in half the time. And let me remark here on the convenience of having but one gutter in such a narrow street, running down its middle, instead of two, one on each side near the footway. Where all the rain that falls on a street runs from the sides and meets in the middle, it forms there a current strong enough to wash away all the mud it meets with; but when divided into two channels, it is often too weak to cleanse either, and only makes the mud it finds more fluid, so that the wheels of carriages and the feet of horses throw and dash it up onto the foot-pavement — which is thereby made foul and slippery — and sometimes splash it on the people walking there. My proposal, communicated to the good doctor, was as follows:

"For the more effectual cleaning and keeping clean the streets of London and Westminster, it is proposed that the several watchmen be contracted with to have the dust swept up in dry seasons, and the mud raked up at other times, each in the several streets and lanes of his round; that they be furnished with brooms and other proper instruments for these purposes, to be kept at their respective stands, ready to furnish the poor people they may employ in the service.

"That in the dry summer months the dust be all swept up into heaps at proper distances, before the shops and windows of houses are usually opened, when the scavengers, with close-covered carts, shall also carry it all away.

"That the mud, when raked up, be not left in heaps to be spread abroad again by the wheels of carriages and trampling of horses, but that the scavengers be provided with bodies of carts, not placed high upon wheels, but low upon sliders, with lattice bottoms, which, being covered with straw, will retain the mud thrown into them and permit the water to drain from it, whereby it will become much lighter, water making the greatest part of its weight; these bodies of carts to be placed at convenient distances, and the mud brought to them in wheelbarrows; they remaining where placed till the mud is drained, and then horses brought to draw them away."

I have since had my doubts about the practicability of the latter part of this proposal, on account of the narrowness of some streets and the difficulty of placing the draining-sleds so as not to encumber the passage too much; but I am still of the opinion that the former part — requiring the dust to be swept up and carried away before the shops open — is very practicable in the summer, when the days are long. For, walking through the Strand and Fleet Street one morning at seven o'clock, I observed there was not one shop open, though it had been daylight and the sun up above three hours — the inhabitants of London choosing voluntarily to live much by candlelight and sleep by sunshine, and yet often complaining, a little absurdly, of the duty on candles and the high price of tallow.

Some may think these trifling matters not worth minding or relating. But when they consider that though dust blown into the eyes of a single person, or into a single shop on a windy day, is of small importance, yet the great number of such instances in a populous city, and their frequent repetition, give the thing weight and consequence — perhaps they will not judge too severely those who give some attention to affairs of this seemingly low nature. Human felicity is produced not so much by great pieces of good fortune that seldom happen, as by little advantages that occur every day. Thus, if you teach a poor young man to shave himself and keep his razor in order, you may contribute more to the happiness of his life than by giving him a thousand guineas. The money may be soon spent, with only the regret remaining of having foolishly consumed it; but in the other case, he escapes the frequent vexation of waiting for barbers — and their sometimes dirty fingers, offensive breath, and dull razors — and shaves when it is most convenient to him, enjoying daily the pleasure of a job done with a good instrument. In this spirit I have hazarded the few preceding pages, hoping they may afford hints that some time or other may be useful to a city I love — having lived many years in it very happily — and perhaps to some of our towns in America.

Having been employed for some time by the postmaster-general of America as his comptroller, regulating several offices and bringing the officers to account, I was, upon his death in 1753, appointed jointly with Mr. William Hunter to succeed him, by a commission from the postmaster-general in England. The American office had never yet paid anything to that of Britain. We were to have six hundred pounds a year between us — if we could make that sum out of the profits of the office. To do this, a variety of improvements were necessary, some of them inevitably expensive at first, so that in the first four years the office ran more than nine hundred pounds into debt to us. But it soon after began to repay us; and before I was displaced by a freak of the ministers, of which I shall speak hereafter, we had brought it to yield three times as much clear revenue to the crown as the post office of Ireland. Since that imprudent transaction, they have received from it — not one farthing!

The business of the post office occasioned my taking a journey that year to New England, where the College of Cambridge, of their own motion, presented me with the degree of Master of Arts. Yale College, in Connecticut, had paid me a similar compliment before. Thus, without studying in any college, I came to partake of their honors. They were conferred in consideration of my improvements and discoveries in the electrical branch of natural philosophy.
