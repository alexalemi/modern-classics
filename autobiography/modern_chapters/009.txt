Chapter 10: Poor Richard’s Almanac and Other Activities

In 1732 I published my almanac for the first time, under the name "Richard Saunders." I kept it going for about twenty-five years, and people commonly called it Poor Richard's Almanac. I worked to make it both entertaining and useful, and it succeeded well enough that the demand grew and I made a considerable profit from it, selling nearly ten thousand copies a year. And since it was read everywhere — hardly a neighborhood in the province was without one — I came to see it as the perfect vehicle for carrying instruction to ordinary people, who bought scarcely any other books. So I filled all the little spaces between the notable days in the calendar with proverbs, chiefly ones that preached industry and frugality as the way to wealth, and wealth as a support to virtue — since it's harder for a man in want to always act honestly. As one of those very proverbs puts it, it is hard for an empty sack to stand upright.

These proverbs, which held the wisdom of many ages and nations, I eventually gathered up and wove into one connected speech, set at the front of the almanac for 1757 as the harangue of a wise old man to the crowd at an auction. Bringing all those scattered bits of counsel into a single focus made them strike with far more force. The piece was approved everywhere. It was copied into every newspaper on the continent; reprinted in Britain as a broadside to be pasted up on house walls; translated twice into French; and bought in great numbers by clergy and gentry to hand out free to their poor parishioners and tenants. In Pennsylvania, since it discouraged useless spending on foreign luxuries, some people thought it deserved a share of the credit for the growing plenty of money we saw for several years after it appeared.

I treated my newspaper, too, as another channel for instruction, and with that in view I often reprinted extracts from the Spectator and other moral writers, and sometimes published little pieces of my own that had first been composed for reading in our Junto. Among these were a Socratic dialogue arguing that a vicious man, whatever his talents and abilities, could never properly be called a man of sense; and a discourse on self-denial, showing that virtue isn't secure until its practice has become a habit, free from the tug of contrary inclinations. You can find these in the papers from around the beginning of 1735.

In running my newspaper I carefully shut out all libel and personal abuse, which in recent years has become such a disgrace to our country. Whenever someone pressed me to print something of that kind — pleading, as they generally did, the liberty of the press, and claiming a newspaper was like a stagecoach, where anyone willing to pay had a right to a seat — my answer was this: I would print the piece separately if they wished, and the author could have as many copies as he liked to distribute himself, but I would not take it upon myself to spread his slander. I had contracted with my subscribers to furnish them with what might be useful or entertaining, and I couldn't fill their papers with private quarrels that were none of their concern without doing them an obvious injustice. Nowadays many of our printers have no scruples about gratifying somebody's malice with false accusations against the finest characters among us, stoking animosity to the point of actual duels; and they are careless enough, on top of that, to print scurrilous jabs at the governments of neighboring states, and even at the conduct of our best national allies, which can carry the most dangerous consequences. I mention all this as a caution to young printers, and as encouragement not to pollute their presses and disgrace their profession with such shameful practices, but to refuse, steadily — since they can see from my example that this course of conduct will not, on the whole, hurt their business.

In 1733 I sent one of my journeymen to Charleston, South Carolina, where a printer was needed. I set him up with a press and type on a partnership agreement: I was to receive one-third of the profits and pay one-third of the expenses. He was a man of learning, and honest, but hopeless at accounts; and though he sometimes sent me remittances, I could never get a proper statement from him, or any satisfactory picture of our partnership, as long as he lived. After his death the business was carried on by his widow, who had been born and raised in Holland, where — as I've been told — bookkeeping is part of a girl's education. She not only sent me as clear a statement as she could reconstruct of the past transactions, but went on accounting with the greatest regularity and exactness every quarter afterward, and she managed the business so successfully that she raised a family of children respectably and, when the term of the partnership expired, was able to buy the printing house from me and set her son up in it.

I mention this affair chiefly to recommend that branch of education for our young women, as likely to be of more use to them and their children, in case of widowhood, than either music or dancing — it protects them from losses at the hands of crafty men, and lets them carry on, perhaps, a profitable business with its established connections until a son is grown up and fit to take it over, to the lasting advantage and enrichment of the family.

Around the year 1734 there arrived among us from Ireland a young Presbyterian preacher named Hemphill, who delivered — with a good voice, and apparently off the cuff — the most excellent sermons, which drew considerable crowds of every persuasion, all joined in admiring them. I became one of his regular hearers myself, for his sermons pleased me: they had little of the dogmatic about them, but pressed hard on the practice of virtue — or what in religious language is called good works. The members of our congregation who considered themselves orthodox Presbyterians, however, disapproved of his doctrine, and most of the older clergy joined them, hauling him before the synod on a charge of heterodoxy in order to silence him. I became his zealous partisan and did all I could to raise a party in his favor, and for a while we fought for him with some hope of success. There was a great deal of scribbling back and forth over the affair; and finding that, elegant preacher though he was, he was a poor writer, I lent him my pen and wrote two or three pamphlets for him, and one piece in the Gazette of April 1735. Those pamphlets, as is usually the case with controversial writing, were eagerly read at the time and soon out of fashion, and I doubt a single copy of them exists today.

During the contest an unlucky discovery hurt his cause badly. One of our adversaries, hearing him preach a much-admired sermon, thought he had read that sermon somewhere before — or at least part of it. On searching, he found the passage quoted at length in one of the British Reviews, from a discourse of Dr. Foster's. This detection disgusted many of our party, who promptly abandoned his cause, and it hastened our defeat in the synod. I stuck by him all the same, since I'd rather he gave us good sermons composed by others than bad ones of his own manufacture — though the latter, it must be said, was the practice of our regular ministers. He later admitted to me that none of the sermons he preached were his own, adding that his memory was such that he could retain and repeat any sermon after a single reading. After our defeat he left us to look for better fortune elsewhere, and I quit the congregation, never joining it again — though I kept up my subscription for the support of its ministers for many years.

In 1733 I had begun to study languages. I soon made myself enough of a master of French to read books in it with ease, and then took up Italian. An acquaintance who was also learning it kept tempting me to play chess with him. Finding this ate up too much of the time I had to spare for study, I finally refused to play anymore except on one condition: the winner of each game had the right to impose a task — a portion of grammar to be learned by heart, translations to be done, and so on — which the loser was bound on his honor to perform before our next meeting. Since we played about equally, we beat each other into that language. Afterward, with a little effort, I picked up enough Spanish to read their books as well.

I've already mentioned that I had only one year of instruction in a Latin school, and that when I was very young; after that I neglected the language entirely. But once I had a working acquaintance with French, Italian, and Spanish, I was surprised to find, looking over a Latin Testament, that I understood far more of that language than I had imagined — which encouraged me to take up the study of it again, and I met with more success this time, since those earlier languages had smoothed my way considerably.

From all this I've come to think there is some inconsistency in our usual way of teaching languages. We're told it's proper to begin with Latin, and that having got that, the modern languages derived from it will come more easily; and yet nobody proposes we begin with Greek in order to make the Latin easier. It's true that if you can clamber to the top of a staircase without using the steps, you'll manage them easily enough on the way down; but surely, if you begin at the bottom step, you'll climb to the top with far less trouble. So I would offer this for the consideration of those who superintend the education of our youth. Many who begin with Latin quit after spending years on it without any great proficiency, and what they have learned turns out nearly useless, so their time is simply lost. Wouldn't it have been better to begin with French, proceed to Italian, and so on? Even if, after the same investment of time, they quit the study of languages and never arrive at Latin, they would at least have acquired a tongue or two that, being in modern use, might actually serve them in everyday life.

After ten years' absence from Boston, and being now comfortable in my circumstances, I made a journey there to visit my relations — something I couldn't well have afforded any sooner. On the way home I stopped at Newport to see my brother, who was settled there with his printing house. Our old differences were forgotten, and our meeting was warm and affectionate. His health was failing fast, and he asked me, in case of his death — which he sensed was not far off — to take his son home with me, then just ten years old, and bring him up in the printing business. This I did, sending the boy to school for a few years before taking him into the shop. His mother carried on the business until he was grown, at which point I helped him with a set of new types, his father's being nearly worn out. And so I made my brother ample amends for the service I had deprived him of by leaving him so early.

In 1736 I lost one of my sons, a fine boy of four, to smallpox, caught in the common way. I bitterly regretted then, and regret still, that I had not given it to him by inoculation. I mention this for the sake of parents who skip that operation on the theory that they could never forgive themselves if a child died under it. My example shows the regret can be just the same either way — and therefore the safer course should be chosen.

Our club, the Junto, proved so useful, and gave its members such satisfaction, that several wanted to bring in their friends — which couldn't well be done without going beyond the number we had settled on as convenient, namely twelve. From the beginning we had made it a rule to keep the club a secret, and the rule was pretty well observed; the point was to avoid applications from unsuitable people whom we might find it awkward to refuse. I was among those against adding to our number. Instead, I proposed in writing that every member, separately, should try to form a subordinate club with the same rules about queries and so forth, without telling them of the connection to the Junto. The advantages I laid out were these: the improvement of that many more young citizens by the use of our methods; a better acquaintance with the general sentiments of the inhabitants on any occasion, since the Junto member could propose whatever queries we wished asked, and was to report back to the Junto what passed in his own club; the promotion of our particular business interests through wider recommendation; and the increase of our influence in public affairs, and of our power of doing good, by spreading the Junto's sentiments through the several clubs.

The project was approved, and every member undertook to form his club, but not all of them succeeded. Only five or six were completed, which went by various names — the "Vine," the "Union," the "Band," and so on. They were useful to their own members, gave us a good deal of amusement, information, and instruction, and answered, to a considerable degree, our aims of influencing public opinion on particular occasions — of which I'll give some instances in due course, as they happened.

My first promotion came in 1736, when I was chosen clerk of the General Assembly. That year the choice was made without opposition; but the following year, when I was proposed again (the choice, like that of the members themselves, being annual), a new member made a long speech against me in order to favor some other candidate. I was chosen anyway, which pleased me all the more, since besides the pay for the clerk's work itself, the place gave me a better opportunity of keeping up an interest among the members — which secured me the business of printing the votes, laws, paper money, and other occasional public jobs that were, on the whole, very profitable.

So I did not much like the opposition of this new member, a gentleman of fortune and education, with talents likely to give him, in time, great influence in the House — which indeed later happened. I didn't try to win his favor by paying him any servile respect, though. After a while I took another approach altogether. Having heard that he had in his library a certain very rare and curious book, I wrote him a note expressing my desire to read it and asking him to do me the favor of lending it for a few days. He sent it immediately, and I returned it in about a week with another note, warmly expressing my sense of the favor. The next time we met in the House, he spoke to me — which he had never done before — and with great civility; and from then on he showed a readiness to serve me on every occasion, so that we became great friends, and our friendship continued to his death. This is another instance of the truth of an old maxim I had learned, which says, "He that has once done you a kindness will be more ready to do you another, than he whom you yourself have obliged." And it shows how much more profitable it is to prudently remove hostility than to resent it, return it, and keep the feud going.

In 1737, Colonel Spotswood, formerly governor of Virginia and by then postmaster-general, grew dissatisfied with his deputy at Philadelphia over some negligence in rendering his accounts, and their inexactness, and took the commission from him and offered it to me. I accepted readily and found it a great advantage; for though the salary was small, the position eased the flow of correspondence, which improved my newspaper, increased the demand for it and the advertisements placed in it, and so came to bring me a considerable income. My old competitor's newspaper declined in proportion, and I was content not to retaliate for his refusal, back when he was postmaster, to let the post riders carry my papers. So he suffered greatly from his carelessness in accounting; and I mention it as a lesson to young men employed in managing affairs for others: always render your accounts, and make your remittances, with great clearness and punctuality. A reputation for doing so is the most powerful of all recommendations to new employment and more business.
