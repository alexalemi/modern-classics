Chapter 1: Ancestry and Early Youth in Boston

Twyford, at the Bishop of St. Asaph's, 1771.

Dear Son: I have always enjoyed collecting little stories about my ancestors. You may remember the questions I asked among the surviving relatives when you were with me in England, and the trip I made for exactly that purpose. It occurred to me that you might find it just as agreeable to know the circumstances of my own life — much of which is still unknown to you — and since I'm expecting a week of uninterrupted leisure here in my country retreat, I'm sitting down to write it all out for you. I have some other motives too. Having climbed from the poverty and obscurity I was born into, up to a comfortable fortune and some degree of reputation in the world, and having gotten this far through life with a considerable share of happiness, I thought my descendants might like to know the methods I used — which, with God's blessing, worked so well — since they may find some of them suited to their own situations, and worth imitating.

That happiness, when I've reflected on it, has sometimes led me to say that if it were offered, I'd have no objection to living the same life over again from the beginning — asking only for the advantage authors get with a second edition: the chance to correct some faults of the first. Along with correcting the faults, I'd change some of the unlucky accidents and events for more favorable ones. But even if that were refused, I'd still take the offer. And since no such repeat performance is on the table, the next best thing to living one's life over again seems to be recollecting that life — and making the recollection as durable as possible by writing it down.

In doing this I'll also be indulging the inclination, so natural in old men, to talk about themselves and their own past deeds. And I'll indulge it without boring anyone, since people who might otherwise feel obliged, out of respect for age, to sit and listen to me can read this or not, exactly as they please. And finally — I may as well confess it, since nobody would believe my denial anyway — I will probably be gratifying my own vanity a good deal. Honestly, I have hardly ever heard or read the opening words "Without vanity I may say..." without some vain thing following immediately after. Most people dislike vanity in others, whatever share of it they have themselves; but I give it fair treatment wherever I meet it, being convinced that it often does real good, both for the person who has it and for everyone within his reach. So in many cases it would not be entirely absurd for a man to thank God for his vanity, along with the other comforts of life.

And speaking of thanking God: I want to acknowledge, with all humility, that I owe the happiness of my past life to His kind providence, which led me to the methods I used and gave them their success. This belief makes me hope — though I must not presume — that the same goodness will continue to be shown to me, either in extending that happiness or in helping me bear some fatal reversal, which I may yet suffer as others have. What my future fortune looks like is known only to Him, who has the power to bless us even through our afflictions.

Some notes that one of my uncles once put into my hands — he had the same sort of curiosity about collecting family stories — supplied me with several details about our ancestors. From those notes I learned that the family had lived in the same village, Ecton, in Northamptonshire, for three hundred years, and he didn't know how much longer than that — perhaps from the time when "Franklin," which had been the name of a whole class of people, was taken as a surname, back when everyone across the kingdom was adopting surnames. They lived on a freehold of about thirty acres, supplementing it with the blacksmith's trade, which had stayed in the family down to his time, the eldest son always being raised to that business — a custom he and my father both followed with their own eldest sons. When I searched the parish registers at Ecton, I found a record of their births, marriages, and burials going back only to 1555, no registers having been kept there before that. From that register I discovered that I was the youngest son of the youngest son for five generations back. My grandfather Thomas, born in 1598, lived at Ecton until he grew too old to keep working, then went to live with his son John, a dyer at Banbury in Oxfordshire, with whom my father served his apprenticeship. My grandfather died there and is buried there; we saw his gravestone in 1758. His eldest son Thomas stayed in the house at Ecton and left it, with the land, to his only child, a daughter, who with her husband — a Fisher, of Wellingborough — sold it to Mr. Isted, now lord of the manor there. My grandfather had four sons who grew to adulthood: Thomas, John, Benjamin, and Josiah. I'll give you what account of them I can at this distance from my papers; if the papers haven't been lost while I've been away, you'll find many more details among them.

Thomas was trained as a smith under his father, but being clever, and encouraged in his studies (as all my father's brothers were) by an Esquire Palmer, then the leading gentleman of the parish, he qualified himself for the work of a scrivener — a legal copyist and clerk. He became a man of consequence in the county, a prime mover in nearly every public-spirited undertaking for the county, for the town of Northampton, and for his own village, where many stories were still told about him; and he was noticed and patronized by the Lord Halifax of that day. He died on January 6, 1702, old style — exactly four years, to the day, before I was born. The account of his life and character that we got from some old people at Ecton struck you, I remember, as something extraordinary, it was so similar to what you knew of mine. "Had he died on the same day," you said, "one might have supposed a transmigration."

John was trained as a dyer — of woolens, I believe. Benjamin was trained as a silk dyer, serving his apprenticeship in London. He was an ingenious man. I remember him well, because when I was a boy he came over to join my father in Boston and lived in the house with us for some years. He lived to a great age; his grandson, Samuel Franklin, lives in Boston now. He left behind two thick manuscript volumes of his own poetry — little occasional pieces addressed to his friends and relations, of which the following, sent to me, is a sample. He had invented a shorthand system of his own, which he taught me; but since I never practiced it, I have now forgotten it. I was named after this uncle, there being a particular affection between him and my father. He was very pious, a great attender of sermons by the best preachers, which he took down in his shorthand — he had many volumes of them. He was also quite the politician; too much so, perhaps, for a man of his station. Recently there fell into my hands, in London, a collection he had made of all the principal pamphlets on public affairs from 1641 to 1717. Many of the volumes are missing, as the numbering shows, but eight folio volumes still remain, and twenty-four in smaller sizes. A dealer in old books had come across them, and knowing me because I sometimes bought from him, he brought them to me. It seems my uncle must have left them behind when he went to America, about fifty years ago. His notes are in the margins throughout.

This obscure family of ours joined the Reformation early and stayed Protestant through the reign of Queen Mary, when their zeal against the Roman church sometimes put them in danger. They had gotten hold of an English Bible, and to hide it and keep it safe, they fastened it open with tapes under the seat of a joint-stool, inside the frame. When my great-great-grandfather read it to his family, he flipped the stool upside down on his knees and turned the pages under the tapes. One of the children stood at the door to give warning if he saw the apparitor coming — an officer of the church court. If he appeared, the stool was set back down on its feet, and the Bible lay hidden beneath it as before. I had this anecdote from my uncle Benjamin. The family remained in the Church of England until near the end of Charles the Second's reign, when some ministers who had been expelled for nonconformity began holding secret meetings in Northamptonshire. Benjamin and Josiah joined them, and stuck with them the rest of their lives; the rest of the family stayed with the Episcopal Church.

Josiah, my father, married young and took his wife and three children to New England around 1682. The dissenters' meetings had been outlawed and were frequently broken up, which persuaded some substantial men of his acquaintance to move to that country, and he was prevailed upon to go along, expecting that there they could practice their religion in freedom. By the same wife he had four more children born there, and by a second wife ten more — seventeen in all, of whom I can remember thirteen sitting at his table at one time, all of whom grew up, married, and made their way. I was the youngest son, and the youngest child but two, and was born in Boston, New England. My mother, the second wife, was Abiah Folger, daughter of Peter Folger, one of the first settlers of New England — honorably mentioned by Cotton Mather in his church history of the country, the Magnalia Christi Americana, as "a godly, learned Englishman," if I remember the words rightly. I have heard that he wrote various small occasional pieces, but only one was ever printed, and I saw it many years ago now. It was written in 1675, in the homespun verse of that time and place, and addressed to the men then running the government there. It argued for liberty of conscience, on behalf of the Baptists, the Quakers, and the other sects that had been persecuted, blaming the Indian wars and the country's other troubles on that persecution — so many judgments of God punishing so heinous an offense — and urging the repeal of those uncharitable laws. The whole thing struck me as written with a good deal of decent plainness and manly freedom. I still remember the six concluding lines, though I've forgotten the first two of the stanza; the gist of them was that his criticisms came from goodwill, and that he therefore wanted to be known as the author:

  "Because to be a libeller (says he)
  I hate it with my heart;
  From Sherburne town, where now I dwell
  My name I do put here;
  Without offense your real friend,
  It is Peter Folgier."

My older brothers were all apprenticed out to different trades. I was sent to grammar school at eight years old, my father intending to devote me, as the tithe of his sons, to the service of the Church. My early knack for reading — which must have come very early indeed, since I can't remember a time when I couldn't read — and the opinion of all his friends that I would surely make a good scholar, encouraged him in the plan. My uncle Benjamin approved too, and offered to give me all his shorthand volumes of sermons — as starting capital, I suppose — if I would learn his system. But I stayed at the grammar school not quite a year, even though in that time I had risen gradually from the middle of my class to the head of it, and had been moved up into the next class, so that I could pass with it into the third by year's end. My father, meanwhile, weighing the cost of a college education — which, with such a large family, he could not well afford — against the meager living so many educated men managed to earn afterward (reasons he gave to his friends within my hearing), changed his mind. He took me out of the grammar school and sent me to a school for writing and arithmetic, kept by a then-famous teacher, Mr. George Brownell, who was very successful in his profession, and successful by mild, encouraging methods. Under him I picked up a fair handwriting fairly quickly, but I failed at the arithmetic and made no progress in it at all. At ten years old I was brought home to help my father in his business, which was that of a tallow-chandler and soap-boiler — making candles and soap — a trade he had not been raised to, but had taken up on arriving in New England, once he found there was too little demand for his dyeing trade to support his family. So I was put to work cutting wick for the candles, filling the dipping mold and the molds for cast candles, minding the shop, running errands, and so on.

I disliked the trade, and had a strong pull toward the sea; but my father set himself firmly against it. Still, living near the water, I was in it and around it constantly. I learned early to swim well and to handle boats, and when I was out in a boat or canoe with other boys, I was usually the one allowed to steer, especially when things got difficult. On other occasions too I was generally the leader among the boys, and sometimes I led them into scrapes. I'll mention one instance, because it shows an early public spirit for projects — even if the spirit wasn't, at that point, honestly directed.

There was a salt marsh bordering part of the millpond, and at high water we used to stand on its edge to fish for minnows. With all our trampling we had turned it into a plain quagmire. My proposal was to build a wharf there fit to stand on, and I pointed out to my comrades a large heap of stones, intended for a new house near the marsh, that would suit our purpose very well. So in the evening, once the workmen had gone home, I gathered a number of my playmates, and working away diligently, like so many ants — sometimes two or three of us to one stone — we carried them all off and built our little wharf. The next morning the workmen were baffled to find the stones missing. They were discovered in our wharf; inquiries were made after the removers; we were found out and reported, and several of us got corrected by our fathers. And though I argued for the usefulness of the work, mine convinced me that nothing was useful which was not honest.

I think you may like to know something of his person and character. He had an excellent constitution, was of middle height but well built and very strong. He was clever with his hands, could draw nicely, knew a little music, and had a clear, pleasing voice — so that when he played psalm tunes on his violin and sang along, as he sometimes did in the evening after the day's work was done, it was a real pleasure to hear. He had a mechanical gift too, and on occasion was very handy with other tradesmen's tools. But his great strength lay in his sound understanding and solid judgment in practical matters, private and public alike. He never actually held public office — the large family he had to raise, and the tightness of his circumstances, kept him chained to his trade — but I well remember leading citizens coming often to consult him for his opinion on the affairs of the town, or of the church he belonged to, and showing considerable respect for his judgment and advice. Private people consulted him constantly about their affairs whenever some difficulty arose, and he was frequently chosen as arbitrator between quarreling parties. At his table he liked to have, as often as he could manage, some sensible friend or neighbor for conversation, and he always took care to start some clever or useful topic of discussion that might improve his children's minds. In this way he turned our attention to what was good, just, and prudent in the conduct of life; and little or no notice was ever taken of the food on the table — whether it was well or badly cooked, in season or out, well flavored or poorly, better or worse than some other dish of its kind. So I was brought up in such perfect inattention to these matters that I became entirely indifferent to what kind of food was set before me — and so unobservant of it that to this day, if you ask me a few hours after dinner, I can hardly tell you what I ate. This has been a convenience to me in traveling, where my companions have sometimes been quite miserable for want of proper gratification of their more delicate — because better trained — tastes and appetites.

My mother had an excellent constitution as well: she nursed all ten of her children herself. I never knew either my father or my mother to have any sickness except the one each died of — he at eighty-nine, she at eighty-five. They lie buried together in Boston, where some years ago I placed a marble stone over their grave with this inscription:

  Josiah Franklin,
  and
  Abiah his wife,
  lie here interred.
  They lived lovingly together in wedlock
  fifty-five years.
  Without an estate, or any gainful employment,
  By constant labor and industry,
  with God's blessing,
  They maintained a large family
  comfortably,
  and brought up thirteen children
  and seven grandchildren
  reputably.
  From this instance, reader,
  Be encouraged to diligence in thy calling,
  And distrust not Providence.
  He was a pious and prudent man;
  She, a discreet and virtuous woman.
  Their youngest son,
  In filial regard to their memory,
  Places this stone.
  J. F. born 1655, died 1744, Ætat 89.
  A. F. born 1667, died 1752, ⸻ 85.

By my rambling digressions I can tell that I'm growing old. I used to write more methodically. But one doesn't dress for private company the way one dresses for a public ball. Perhaps it's only negligence.

To return: I went on working in my father's business for two years, that is, until I was twelve. My brother John, who had been raised to the trade, had by then left my father, married, and set up for himself in Rhode Island, and every sign pointed to my being destined to fill his place and become a tallow-chandler. But my dislike of the trade continued, and my father grew worried that if he didn't find me a more agreeable one, I would break loose and go to sea, as his son Josiah had done — to his great vexation. So he sometimes took me on walks with him to watch joiners, bricklayers, wood-turners, brass-workers, and the rest at their work, hoping to spot where my inclination lay and pin it to some trade on land. It has been a pleasure to me ever since to watch good workmen handle their tools; and it has been useful too — I learned enough to do small jobs around my own house when a workman couldn't readily be found, and to build little machines for my experiments while the idea for the experiment was still fresh and warm in my mind. My father at last settled on the cutler's trade, and since my uncle Benjamin's son Samuel, who had trained in that business in London, had recently set up in Boston, I was sent to stay with him for a while on trial. But his expectation of a fee for taking me displeased my father, and I was brought home again.
