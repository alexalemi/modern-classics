Chapter 7: Beginning Business in Philadelphia

We sailed from Gravesend on July 23, 1726. For the incidents of the voyage I'll refer you to my journal, where you'll find them all set down in detail. Probably the most important thing in that journal is the plan I drew up at sea for regulating my future conduct in life. It's the more remarkable for having been formed when I was so young — and yet I stuck to it pretty faithfully all the way into old age.

We landed in Philadelphia on October 11, where I found a number of things changed. Keith was no longer governor; he'd been replaced by Major Gordon. I met him walking the streets as an ordinary citizen. He seemed a little ashamed to see me, but passed by without a word. I'd have been just as ashamed at seeing Miss Read, except that her friends — despairing of my return, with good reason, after they got my letter — had persuaded her to marry another man, one Rogers, a potter, which was done while I was away. She was never happy with him, though, and soon left him, refusing to live with him or use his name, since word had it by then that he had another wife. He was a worthless fellow, though an excellent workman — which was what had tempted her friends. He ran into debt, ran off in 1727 or 1728, went to the West Indies, and died there. As for Keimer, he had a better house now, a shop well stocked with stationery, plenty of new type, and a number of workmen — though none of them any good — and he seemed to be doing a great deal of business.

Mr. Denham took a store on Water Street, where we opened our goods. I attended to the business diligently, studied bookkeeping, and in a little while became expert at selling. We lodged and boarded together; he advised me like a father, having a sincere regard for me. I respected and loved him, and we might have gone on together very happily; but at the beginning of February, 1726/7, when I had just turned twenty-one, we both fell ill. My sickness was pleurisy, and it very nearly carried me off. I suffered a good deal, gave myself up for lost, and was actually rather disappointed when I found myself recovering — regretting, in some degree, that now I'd have to do all that disagreeable dying business over again someday. I forget what his illness was; it held him a long time and finally carried him off. He left me a small legacy in a nuncupative will — one spoken aloud, not written — as a token of his kindness to me, and left me, once more, to the wide world; for the store passed into the hands of his executors, and my employment under him was over.

My brother-in-law Holmes, who was now in Philadelphia, advised me to go back to my own trade; and Keimer tempted me with the offer of a large yearly salary to come and manage his printing house, so he could give more attention to his stationery shop. I'd heard a bad account of him in London from his wife and her friends, and wasn't eager to have anything more to do with him. I tried to find work as a merchant's clerk; but nothing turned up quickly, so I closed with Keimer again. In his shop I found this crew: Hugh Meredith, a Welsh Pennsylvanian, thirty years old, raised to farm work — honest, sensible, full of solid practical observation, something of a reader, but given to drink. Stephen Potts, a young countryman just come of age, raised to the same work, with uncommon natural gifts and great wit and humor, but a little lazy. Keimer had signed these two at extremely low weekly wages, to be raised a shilling every three months as they earned it by improving at the trade — and it was the expectation of those high wages, someday, that he'd used to reel them in. Meredith was to work the press, Potts to do bookbinding, and Keimer, by their agreement, was to teach them both — though he knew neither one nor the other. Then there was John, a wild Irishman brought up to no trade at all, whose service Keimer had bought for four years from a ship's captain; he too was to be made a pressman. George Webb, an Oxford scholar, whose time he had likewise bought for four years, intended for a typesetter — more about him shortly. And David Harry, a country boy he'd taken on as an apprentice.

I soon saw what Keimer was up to in hiring me at wages so much higher than he was used to paying: he wanted these raw, cheap hands trained up through me, and once I'd taught them — they all being bound to him by contract — he'd be able to do without me. I carried on cheerfully all the same, put his printing house in order (it had been in total confusion), and gradually got his men to mind their work and do it better.

It was an odd thing to find an Oxford scholar in the position of a bought servant. Webb was no more than eighteen, and gave me this account of himself: he was born in Gloucester and educated at the grammar school there, where he'd stood out among the students for a certain flair in acting when they put on plays; he belonged to the Witty Club there and had written some pieces in prose and verse that were printed in the Gloucester newspapers. From there he was sent to Oxford, where he stayed about a year, but was never satisfied — what he wanted above everything was to see London and become an actor. At last, when his quarterly allowance of fifteen guineas arrived, instead of paying his debts he walked out of town, hid his academic gown in a furze bush, and hiked to London. There, with no friend to advise him, he fell into bad company, soon ran through his guineas, found no way of getting introduced among the actors, grew desperate, pawned his clothes, and went hungry. Walking the street, starving and with no idea what to do with himself, he was handed a crimp's bill — a recruiting flyer offering immediate food and encouragement to anyone who would sign on to serve in America. He went straight in, signed the indenture papers, was put aboard ship, and came over — without ever writing a line to let his friends know what had become of him. He was lively, witty, good-natured, and pleasant company, but idle, thoughtless, and reckless to the last degree.

John the Irishman soon ran away. With the rest I began to get along very agreeably, for they all respected me the more once they found that Keimer was incapable of teaching them anything, while from me they learned something every day. We never worked Saturdays — that being Keimer's Sabbath — so I had two days a week for reading. My acquaintance among the clever, inquiring people of the town kept growing. Keimer himself treated me with great civility and apparent regard, and nothing troubled me now except my debt to Vernon, which I was still unable to pay, being as yet a poor manager of money. He, however, kindly never demanded it.

Our printing house often ran short of sorts — individual pieces of type — and there was no typefounder in America. I had seen type cast at James's foundry in London, though without paying much attention to how it was done; but I now contrived a mold, used the letters we had as punches, struck the matrices in lead, and in this way supplied all our shortages tolerably well. I also did occasional engraving; I made the ink; I was warehouseman and everything else — in short, a complete factotum.

But however useful I was, I found my services counting for less every day as the other hands improved at the trade; and when Keimer paid my second quarter's wages, he let me know he found them too heavy and thought I ought to take a cut. He grew less civil by degrees, played the master more, found fault constantly, picked at everything, and seemed to be building toward an explosion. I carried on anyway with a good deal of patience, figuring his tangled finances were partly to blame. At length a trifle snapped the connection. A great commotion broke out near the courthouse, and I stuck my head out the window to see what was going on. Keimer, who was down in the street, looked up, saw me, and shouted at me in a loud, angry voice to mind my business — adding some insulting words that stung all the more for being public, since every neighbor looking out at the same commotion witnessed how I was being treated. He came straight up into the printing house and kept the quarrel going; hot words passed on both sides; he gave me the quarter's notice we had agreed on, saying he wished he weren't obliged to give me so much warning. I told him his wish was unnecessary, because I'd leave him that instant — and so, taking my hat, I walked out the door, asking Meredith, whom I saw below, to look after a few things I'd left and bring them to my lodgings.

Meredith came by that evening, and we talked my situation over. He had developed a great regard for me and hated the idea of my leaving the shop while he was still stuck in it. He talked me out of returning to New England, which I'd begun to consider. He reminded me that Keimer was in debt for everything he owned; that his creditors were getting nervous; that he ran his shop miserably, often selling at no profit for ready cash and often extending credit without keeping accounts; that he must therefore fail — which would leave an opening I could profit from. I objected that I had no money. He then told me that his father had a high opinion of me and, from certain conversations they'd had, he was sure would advance money to set us up, if I would go into partnership with him. "My time with Keimer is up in the spring," he said. "By then we can have our press and type in from London. I know I'm no workman; if you agree, your skill in the business can be set against the stock I furnish, and we'll split the profits equally."

The proposal suited me, and I consented. His father was in town and approved it — all the more because he saw I had great influence with his son, had already prevailed on him to give up hard liquor for long stretches, and might, the father hoped, break him of that wretched habit entirely once we were so closely tied together. I gave the father an inventory of what we'd need, and he carried it to a merchant; the order was sent for, the secret was to be kept until it arrived, and in the meantime I was to find work, if I could, at the other printing house in town. But there was no opening there, so I sat idle for a few days — until Keimer, who had the prospect of a job printing paper money in New Jersey that would require engravings and a variety of type only I could supply, and who was afraid Bradford might hire me and snatch the job away, sent me a very civil message: that old friends shouldn't part over a few words spoken in a flash of temper, and that he wished me to come back. Meredith urged me to accept, since it would give him more chances to improve under my daily instruction; so I returned, and we went on more smoothly than we had for some time. He got the New Jersey job. For it I contrived a copperplate press — the first ever seen in the country — and I cut several ornaments and checks for the bills. We went together to Burlington, where I carried out the whole job to everyone's satisfaction; and he received so large a payment for the work that it kept his head above water a good while longer.

At Burlington I made the acquaintance of many of the leading people of the province. Several of them had been appointed by the Assembly as a committee to attend at the press and make sure no more bills were printed than the law allowed. So they took turns being constantly with us, and whoever's turn it was generally brought a friend or two along for company. My mind had been far better furnished by reading than Keimer's, and I suppose that's why my conversation seemed the more valued. They had me to their houses, introduced me to their friends, and showed me great civility, while he — though the master — was somewhat neglected. In truth he was an odd fish: ignorant of ordinary life, fond of rudely contradicting accepted opinions, slovenly to the point of outright dirtiness, fanatical on certain points of religion, and a bit of a rascal besides.

We stayed there nearly three months, and by that time I could count among my new friends Judge Allen; Samuel Bustill, the secretary of the province; Isaac Pearson; Joseph Cooper; several of the Smiths, members of the Assembly; and Isaac Decow, the surveyor-general. This last was a shrewd, sharp old man who told me he'd started out for himself, when young, wheeling clay for brickmakers; learned to write only after he came of age; carried the chain for surveyors, who taught him surveying; and had now, by his own industry, acquired a good estate. "I foresee," he said, "that you'll soon work this man out of his business and make a fortune at it in Philadelphia." He had, at that point, not the least hint of my intention to set up there or anywhere else. These friends were afterward of great use to me, as I occasionally was to some of them, and they all kept up their regard for me as long as they lived.

Before I come to my public start in business, it may be worth telling you the state of my mind at that time regarding principles and morals, so you can see how far they influenced the later events of my life. My parents had given me religious impressions early, and brought me up piously through childhood in the Dissenting way. But I was barely fifteen when, after doubting one point and then another as I found them disputed in the different books I read, I began to doubt Revelation itself. Some books against Deism fell into my hands — said to be the substance of sermons preached at Boyle's Lectures. They worked on me an effect precisely opposite to the one intended: the arguments of the Deists, quoted in order to be refuted, struck me as much stronger than the refutations. In short, I soon became a thorough Deist. My arguments corrupted some others — particularly Collins and Ralph; but each of them afterward wronged me badly without the least remorse, and when I recalled Keith's conduct toward me (another freethinker), and my own conduct toward Vernon and Miss Read, which at times troubled me greatly, I began to suspect that this doctrine, though it might be true, was not very useful. My London pamphlet — which had taken for its motto these lines of Dryden:

  "Whatever is, is right. Though purblind man
  Sees but a part o' the chain, the nearest link:
  His eyes not carrying to the equal beam,
  That poises all above;"

and which had concluded, from the attributes of God — his infinite wisdom, goodness, and power — that nothing could possibly be wrong in the world, and that vice and virtue were empty distinctions, no such things existing — now looked to me a much less clever performance than I'd once thought it. I began to doubt whether some error hadn't slipped unnoticed into my argument and infected everything that followed, as commonly happens in metaphysical reasoning.

I grew convinced that truth, sincerity, and integrity in dealings between one person and another were of the utmost importance to a happy life, and I wrote out resolutions — they still survive in my journal book — to practice them as long as I lived. Revelation, as such, carried no weight with me; but I came to hold the opinion that although certain actions might not be bad simply because Revelation forbade them, or good simply because it commanded them, yet probably those actions were forbidden because they were bad for us, or commanded because they were good for us, in their own natures, all things considered. And this conviction — along with the kind hand of Providence, or some guardian angel, or a run of lucky circumstances, or all of them together — carried me through that dangerous season of youth, and through the hazardous situations I sometimes found myself in among strangers, far from my father's eye and advice, without any willful gross immorality or injustice of the sort my lack of religion might have led you to expect. I say willful, because the instances I've already mentioned had something of necessity in them, arising from my youth, my inexperience, and the knavery of others. So I had a tolerably good character to begin the world with; I valued it properly, and was determined to keep it.

We hadn't been back in Philadelphia long before the new type arrived from London. We settled up with Keimer and left him, with his consent, before he heard anything of our plan. We found a house to rent near the market and took it. To lighten the rent — which was then only twenty-four pounds a year, though I've since known the same house to rent for seventy — we took in Thomas Godfrey, a glazier, and his family, who were to pay a considerable share of it, and we boarded with them. We had barely unpacked our type and set up our press when George House, an acquaintance of mine, brought in a countryman he'd met in the street asking for a printer. All our cash by then had gone into the endless particulars we'd had to buy, and this countryman's five shillings, our very first earnings, coming so exactly when needed, gave me more pleasure than any crown I've earned since; and the gratitude I felt toward House has made me, ever after, readier than I might otherwise have been to help young beginners.

There are croakers in every country — people forever prophesying its ruin. There was one such then living in Philadelphia, a person of note, an elderly man with a wise look and a very grave manner of speaking; his name was Samuel Mickle. This gentleman, a stranger to me, stopped one day at my door and asked whether I was the young man who had lately opened a new printing house. When I said I was, he told me he was sorry for me: it was an expensive undertaking, and the expense would be lost, for Philadelphia was a sinking place, its people already half bankrupt or close to it; all appearances to the contrary — new buildings, rising rents — were, to his certain knowledge, deceptive, being in fact among the very things that would soon ruin us all. And he gave me such a catalog of misfortunes, existing or shortly to exist, that he left me half melancholy. Had I known him before I went into the business, I probably never would have gone into it. This man went on living in that decaying place, and declaiming in the same strain, refusing for many years to buy a house there because everything was headed for destruction — and in the end I had the pleasure of seeing him pay five times as much for one as it would have cost him when he first began his croaking.

I should have mentioned before this that in the autumn of the previous year I had gathered most of my clever acquaintances into a club of mutual improvement, which we called the Junto; we met on Friday evenings. The rules I drew up required every member, in turn, to produce one or more questions on any point of morals, politics, or natural philosophy, to be discussed by the company; and once every three months to write and read an essay of his own, on any subject he pleased. Our debates were to be run by a president and conducted in the sincere spirit of inquiry after truth, without any appetite for dispute or desire for victory; and to keep tempers cool, all expressions of cocksureness in opinion, and all direct contradiction, were after a while made contraband, and banned under small cash fines.

The first members were: Joseph Breintnal, a copier of deeds for the scriveners — a good-natured, friendly, middle-aged man, a great lover of poetry, who read all of it he could get his hands on and wrote some that was tolerable; very clever at all sorts of little handicrafts, and sensible company.

Thomas Godfrey, a self-taught mathematician, great in his own line, and afterward the inventor of what is now called Hadley's Quadrant. But he knew little outside his line and wasn't pleasant company; like most great mathematicians I've met, he demanded universal precision in everything said, and was forever contradicting or quibbling over trifles, to the ruin of all conversation. He soon left us.

Nicholas Scull, a surveyor, afterward surveyor-general, who loved books and sometimes made a few verses.

William Parsons, bred a shoemaker, but who, loving to read, had picked up a considerable share of mathematics — which he first studied with an eye to astrology, and afterward laughed at himself for it. He also became surveyor-general.

William Maugridge, a joiner, a most exquisite craftsman, and a solid, sensible man.

Hugh Meredith, Stephen Potts, and George Webb I've described already.

Robert Grace, a young gentleman of some fortune — generous, lively, and witty; a lover of puns and of his friends.

And William Coleman, then a merchant's clerk, about my own age, who had the coolest, clearest head, the best heart, and the most exact morals of almost any man I ever met. He afterward became a merchant of great note and one of our provincial judges. Our friendship continued unbroken to his death, more than forty years; and the club lasted almost as long, and was the best school of philosophy, morality, and politics that then existed in the province. For our questions, which were read out a week before their discussion, set us reading with real attention on the various subjects, so that we could speak more to the point; and here, too, we picked up better habits of conversation, since everything in our rules was designed to keep us from disgusting one another. Hence the long life of the club — of which I'll have frequent occasion to say more later.

But my reason for describing it here is to show something of the pull I now had, since every one of these men exerted himself to steer business our way. Breintnal in particular got us, from the Quakers, the printing of forty sheets of their history, the rest being assigned to Keimer; and on this we worked exceedingly hard, for the price was low. It was a folio, pro patria size, set in pica with notes in the smaller long-primer type. I composed a sheet of it a day, and Meredith worked it off at press. It was often eleven at night, and sometimes later, before I'd finished distributing my type for the next day's work, because the little jobs our other friends sent in now and then set us back. But I was so determined to keep up my sheet a day of the folio that one night, when I'd imposed my forms and thought my day's work done, one of them was accidentally broken and two pages knocked into pi — a jumbled heap of type — and I immediately distributed it and set it all over again before I went to bed. This industry, visible to our neighbors, began to give us standing and credit. In particular, I was told that when the new printing office was mentioned at the merchants' Every-Night Club, the general opinion was that it must fail, there being two printers in town already, Keimer and Bradford; but Dr. Baird (whom you and I saw many years later at his birthplace, St. Andrews in Scotland) gave a contrary opinion: "The industry of that Franklin," he said, "is superior to anything of the kind I ever saw. I see him still at work when I go home from club, and he's at work again before his neighbors are out of bed." This struck the rest, and soon afterward one of them offered to supply us with stationery to sell; but we didn't yet choose to go into shopkeeping.

I dwell on this industry the more particularly, and the more freely — even though it sounds like singing my own praises — so that those of my descendants who read this may know the use of that virtue, when they see its effects working in my favor all through this account.

George Webb, who had found a lady friend willing to lend him the money to buy his remaining time from Keimer, now came to offer himself to us as a journeyman. We couldn't employ him then; but I foolishly let him in on a secret — that I soon intended to start a newspaper, and might have work for him at that point. My hopes of success, I told him, rested on this: the only newspaper then in town, printed by Bradford, was a paltry thing, wretchedly managed and not the least bit entertaining, yet it made him money; so I reckoned a good paper could hardly fail to do well. I asked Webb to keep it quiet; but he told Keimer, who immediately — to get ahead of me — published proposals for starting a paper himself, with Webb to be employed on it. This I resented; and since I couldn't yet launch our own paper, I set out to undercut them by writing several entertaining pieces for Bradford's paper, under the name of the "Busy-Body," which Breintnal kept up for some months after me. By this means the public's attention was fixed on Bradford's paper, and Keimer's proposals — which we burlesqued and ridiculed — were ignored. He started his paper anyway, and after dragging it along for three quarters of a year with at most ninety subscribers, he offered it to me for a trifle. I had been ready for some time to run a paper, so I took it over at once; and within a few years it proved extremely profitable to me.

I notice I keep saying "I," though our partnership still continued. The reason may be that, in fact, the whole management of the business fell on me. Meredith was no typesetter, a poor pressman, and seldom sober. My friends lamented my being tied to him, but I had to make the best of it.

Our first issues made an appearance quite different from anything seen before in the province — better type, better printing; and some spirited remarks of mine on the dispute then running between Governor Burnet and the Massachusetts Assembly struck the principal people, got the paper and its manager talked about everywhere, and within a few weeks brought them all in as our subscribers.

Many others followed their example, and our subscription list kept growing. This was one of the first good effects of my having learned, a little, to scribble. Another was that the leading men, seeing a newspaper now in the hands of someone who could also handle a pen, thought it worthwhile to oblige and encourage me. Bradford still printed the votes and laws and other public business. He had printed an address from the House to the governor in a coarse, blundering manner; we reprinted it elegantly and correctly, and sent a copy to every member. They felt the difference; it strengthened the hands of our friends in the House, and they voted us their printers for the year following.

Among my friends in the House I mustn't forget Mr. Hamilton, mentioned earlier, who had now returned from England and held a seat there. He took up my cause strongly in that instance, as he did in many others afterward, continuing his patronage until his death.

Mr. Vernon, about this time, reminded me of the debt I owed him, though he didn't press me. I wrote him a frank letter of acknowledgment, begged his patience a little longer, which he granted, and as soon as I was able I paid him the principal with interest, and many thanks besides — so that erratum was, in some degree, corrected.

But now another difficulty fell on me that I'd had not the least reason to expect. Mr. Meredith's father, who was to have paid for our printing house according to the expectations I'd been given, was able to advance only a hundred pounds currency, which had been paid; and a hundred more was still owed to the merchant, who grew impatient and sued us all. We posted bail, but we could see that if the money couldn't be raised in time, the suit would soon come to judgment and execution, and our hopeful prospects would be ruined along with us, since the press and type would have to be sold for payment — probably at half price.

In this distress, two true friends — whose kindness I have never forgotten, and never shall while I can remember anything — came to me separately, each without the other's knowledge and without any appeal from me, and each offered to advance me all the money necessary to take the whole business into my own hands, if that could be managed. They didn't like my continuing the partnership with Meredith, who, they said, was often seen drunk in the streets and playing cheap gambling games in alehouses, much to our discredit. These two friends were William Coleman and Robert Grace. I told them I couldn't propose a separation while any prospect remained of the Merediths keeping their part of our agreement, because I felt myself under great obligation to them for what they had done, and would do if they could; but if they finally failed to perform, and the partnership had to be dissolved, then I'd consider myself free to accept my friends' help.

So the matter rested for a while — until one day I said to my partner, "Perhaps your father is unhappy with the part you've taken in this affair of ours, and is unwilling to advance for the two of us what he would for you alone. If that's the case, tell me, and I'll turn the whole business over to you and go my way." "No," he said, "my father has really been disappointed, and really can't do it; and I'm unwilling to distress him further. I can see this is a business I'm not fit for. I was bred a farmer, and it was folly in me to come to town and apprentice myself, at thirty years old, to learn a new trade. Many of our Welsh people are moving to North Carolina, where land is cheap. I'm inclined to go with them and take up my old employment. You can find friends to help you. If you'll take the company's debts on yourself, return to my father the hundred pounds he advanced, pay my few personal debts, and give me thirty pounds and a new saddle, I'll give up the partnership and leave the whole business in your hands." I agreed to the proposal; it was drawn up in writing, signed, and sealed on the spot. I gave him what he asked, and he went off soon after to Carolina — from which, the next year, he sent me two long letters containing the best account yet given of that country, its climate, soil, farming, and so on, for in those matters he was very sound. I printed them in the papers, and the public was well pleased with them.

As soon as he was gone, I went back to my two friends; and because I wouldn't show an unkind preference for either, I took half of what each had offered — and half of what I needed from one, half from the other — paid off the company's debts, and carried on the business in my own name, advertising that the partnership was dissolved. I believe this was in or about the year 1729.
