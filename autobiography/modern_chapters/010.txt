Chapter 11: Interest in Public Affairs

I now began to turn my thoughts a little toward public affairs — starting, though, with small matters. The city watch was one of the first things I decided needed fixing. It was run by the constables of the various wards, each taking his turn; the constable would summon a number of householders to walk the rounds with him for the night. Anyone who preferred never to attend paid him six shillings a year to be excused. That money was supposedly for hiring substitutes, but it was really far more than substitutes cost, which made the constableship a profitable little office; and the constable, for a bit of drink, would often gather such ragamuffins around him as a watch that respectable householders wanted nothing to do with them. The rounds were often skipped, too, and most of the night spent tippling. So I wrote a paper to be read in the Junto, laying out these irregularities, and pressing hardest on the unfairness of that six-shilling tax, which took no account of anyone's circumstances: a poor widow keeping house, whose whole property under the watch's protection might not be worth fifty pounds, paid exactly as much as the wealthiest merchant with thousands of pounds' worth of goods in his stores.

On the whole, I proposed a more effective watch: hire proper men to serve at the business full time; and a fairer way to pay for it: levy a tax proportioned to property. The Junto approved the idea, and it was passed along to the other clubs — presented in each as if it had arisen there. The plan wasn't carried out right away, but by preparing people's minds for the change, it paved the way for the law we obtained a few years later, once the members of our clubs had grown into more influence.

Around this time I wrote a paper — first read in the Junto, afterward published — on the various accidents and careless habits by which houses were set on fire, with cautions against them and ways of avoiding them. It was much talked of as a useful piece, and it gave rise to a project that soon followed: forming a company for putting out fires more readily, and for helping one another remove and secure goods when they were in danger. Associates for the scheme turned up at once — thirty of them. Our articles of agreement required every member to keep, always in good order and fit for use, a certain number of leather buckets, along with strong bags and baskets for packing and hauling goods, all of which were to be brought to every fire; and we agreed to meet once a month and spend a social evening together, trading whatever ideas occurred to us on the subject of fires that might be useful when one actually broke out.

The usefulness of this institution soon showed itself, and when more men wanted in than we thought convenient for one company, we advised them to form another — which they did. And so it went, one new company after another, until they came to include most of the property owners in town. Now, as I write this, more than fifty years after its founding, that first company — the Union Fire Company — still survives and flourishes, though of the original members all are dead but myself and one man a year older than I am. The small fines members have paid for missing the monthly meetings have gone toward buying fire engines, ladders, fire hooks, and other useful equipment for each company; so I question whether there is a city in the world better provided with the means of stopping a fire at its beginning. In fact, since these institutions were established, the city has never lost more than one or two houses at a time to fire, and the flames have often been put out before the house they started in was half consumed.

In 1739 the Reverend Mr. Whitefield arrived among us from Ireland, where he had made a name for himself as a traveling preacher. At first he was permitted to preach in some of our churches; but the clergy took a dislike to him, soon refused him their pulpits, and he was obliged to preach in the fields. The crowds that came to his sermons, from every sect and denomination, were enormous; and I — being one of the number — found it a matter of curiosity to observe the extraordinary power of his oratory over his hearers, and how much they admired and respected him even while he was busy assuring them they were naturally half beasts and half devils. It was wonderful to see the change soon made in the manners of our townspeople. From being thoughtless or indifferent about religion, it seemed as if all the world were growing religious, so that you couldn't walk through town of an evening without hearing psalms sung in different families on every street.

Since assembling in the open air, at the mercy of the weather, proved inconvenient, the building of a house to meet in was no sooner proposed — and people appointed to receive contributions — than enough money came in to buy the ground and put up the building, one hundred feet long and seventy wide, about the size of Westminster Hall; and the work was carried on with such spirit that it was finished in far less time than anyone could have expected. Both house and ground were placed in the hands of trustees, expressly for the use of any preacher of any religious persuasion who might want to say something to the people of Philadelphia. The design in building it was to accommodate not any particular sect but the inhabitants in general — so that even if the Mufti of Constantinople were to send a missionary to preach Islam to us, he would find a pulpit at his service.

When Mr. Whitefield left us, he went preaching all the way through the colonies to Georgia. The settlement of that province had only just begun — but instead of being made with hardy, industrious farmers accustomed to labor, the only people fit for such an enterprise, it was made with families of bankrupt shopkeepers and other insolvent debtors, many of idle habits, taken out of the jails and set down in the woods. Unqualified for clearing land, and unable to endure the hardships of a new settlement, they perished in numbers, leaving many helpless children unprovided for. The sight of their miserable situation inspired Mr. Whitefield's benevolent heart with the idea of building an Orphan House there, where the children could be supported and educated. Returning northward, he preached up this charity and made large collections — for his eloquence had a wonderful power over the hearts and purses of his hearers, of which I myself was an instance.

I didn't disapprove of the plan itself; but Georgia then had neither materials nor workmen, and the proposal was to send both from Philadelphia at great expense. I thought it would have been better to build the house here and bring the children to it. I advised this; but he was set on his original project, rejected my counsel, and I therefore refused to contribute. Soon afterward I happened to attend one of his sermons, and as it went along I perceived he meant to finish with a collection. I silently resolved he should get nothing from me. I had in my pocket a handful of copper money, three or four silver dollars, and five pistoles in gold. As he proceeded I began to soften, and concluded to give the coppers. Another stroke of his oratory made me ashamed of that, and I determined to give the silver; and he finished so admirably that I emptied my pocket wholly into the collector's dish, gold and all. At the same sermon was another member of our club who shared my opinion about the building in Georgia, and who, suspecting a collection might be intended, had taken the precaution of emptying his pockets before he left home. Toward the end of the sermon, however, he felt a strong desire to give, and turned to a neighbor standing near him to borrow some money for the purpose. He had applied, unfortunately, to perhaps the only man in the whole company with the firmness not to be moved by the preacher. The answer came: "At any other time, Friend Hopkinson, I would lend to thee freely; but not now, for thee seems to be out of thy right senses."

Some of Mr. Whitefield's enemies liked to suggest that he would put these collections toward his own private profit; but I, who knew him intimately — I was the one who printed his sermons and journals — never had the least suspicion of his integrity, and I remain to this day firmly of the opinion that he was in all his conduct a perfectly honest man. And it seems to me my testimony in his favor ought to carry the more weight because we had no religious connection at all. He did, indeed, sometimes pray for my conversion, but he never had the satisfaction of believing his prayers were heard. Ours was a merely civil friendship, sincere on both sides, and it lasted to his death.

The following instance will show something of the terms we stood on. On one of his arrivals from England at Boston, he wrote to me that he would soon be coming to Philadelphia, but didn't know where he could lodge, since he understood his old friend and host, Mr. Benezet, had moved to Germantown. My answer was: "You know my house; if you can make shift with its scanty accommodations, you will be most heartily welcome." He replied that if I made that kind offer for Christ's sake, I should not miss my reward. And I wrote back: "Don't let me be mistaken; it was not for Christ's sake, but for your sake." One of our common acquaintances remarked, jokingly, that since it was the custom of the saints, whenever they received a favor, to shift the burden of the obligation off their own shoulders and lodge it in heaven, I had contrived to fix it on Earth.

The last time I saw Mr. Whitefield was in London, when he consulted me about his Orphan House and his plan of converting it into a college.

He had a loud, clear voice, and articulated his words and sentences so perfectly that he could be heard and understood at a great distance — especially since his audiences, however numerous, kept the most exact silence. He preached one evening from the top of the courthouse steps, which stand in the middle of Market Street where Second Street crosses it at right angles. Both streets were filled with his hearers to a considerable distance. Being among the hindmost in Market Street, I had the curiosity to learn how far his voice would carry, and so I walked backward down the street toward the river. I could hear him distinctly until I came near Front Street, where some noise in that street drowned him out. I then imagined a semicircle, with my distance as the radius, filled with listeners at two square feet apiece, and computed that he might well be heard by more than thirty thousand people. This reconciled me to the newspaper accounts of his having preached to twenty-five thousand in the fields, and to the ancient histories of generals haranguing whole armies — which I had sometimes doubted.

By hearing him often, I came to distinguish easily between sermons newly composed and those he had preached often in the course of his travels. His delivery of the latter was so improved by repetition that every accent, every emphasis, every modulation of his voice was so perfectly turned and so well placed that, without caring at all about the subject, you couldn't help being pleased with the discourse — a pleasure of much the same kind as you get from an excellent piece of music. This is an advantage traveling preachers have over settled ones: the settled ones can't polish a sermon with so many rehearsals.

His writing and printing, though, gave his enemies a standing advantage. Unguarded expressions, and even mistaken opinions, delivered in preaching, might afterward have been explained or qualified by supposing other remarks that accompanied them, or they might have been denied outright; but litera scripta manet — the written word remains. His critics attacked his writings violently, and with so much appearance of reason that they diminished the number of his followers and prevented its increase; so that I am of the opinion that if he had never written anything, he would have left behind him a much more numerous and important sect, and his reputation might in that case have gone on growing even after his death — for with nothing of his in writing on which to found a censure and give him a lower character, his disciples would have been at liberty to invent for him as great a variety of excellences as their enthusiastic admiration might wish him to have possessed.

My business, meanwhile, was continually growing, and my circumstances daily easier, my newspaper having become very profitable — for a time it was almost the only one in this and the neighboring provinces. I found out, too, the truth of the observation that "after getting the first hundred pound, it is more easy to get the second" — money itself being of a prolific nature.

The partnership at Carolina having succeeded, I was encouraged to enter into others, and to advance several of my workmen who had behaved well by setting them up with printing houses in different colonies, on the same terms as the one in Carolina. Most of them did well, and at the end of our six-year term were able to buy the types from me and carry on working for themselves — by which means several families were raised. Partnerships often end in quarrels; but mine, I'm happy to say, were all carried on and wound up amicably. I credit that in good part to one precaution: we settled very explicitly in our articles everything each partner was to do or could expect, so there was nothing left to dispute. I would recommend that precaution to anyone entering a partnership; for whatever esteem partners may have for each other, and whatever confidence, at the time of the contract, little jealousies and resentments can creep in — notions of inequality in the care and burden of the business, and so on — and these often end in broken friendships and broken connections, perhaps with lawsuits and other disagreeable consequences.
