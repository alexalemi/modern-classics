Chapter 8: Business Success and First Public Service

About this time there was a public outcry for more paper money. Only fifteen thousand pounds of it existed in the whole province, and even that was soon to be retired. The wealthy residents opposed printing any more — they were against paper currency on principle, afraid it would lose its value the way it had in New England, to the harm of everyone who was owed money. We had debated the question in our Junto, where I came down on the side of an addition, convinced that the first small issue struck in 1723 had done real good by increasing the trade, employment, and population of the province. I could see the proof: all the old houses were now occupied and many new ones were going up. Whereas I well remembered that when I first walked the streets of Philadelphia eating my roll, most of the houses on Walnut Street between Second and Front had signs on their doors reading "To be let," and plenty more stood empty on Chestnut Street and elsewhere — which made me think at the time that the people of the city were abandoning it one after another.

Our debates filled my head so completely with the subject that I wrote and printed an anonymous pamphlet on it, entitled "The Nature and Necessity of a Paper Currency." Ordinary people generally received it well; the rich men disliked it, since it swelled and strengthened the clamor for more money. But as they happened to have no writers among them capable of answering it, their opposition slackened, and the measure carried the House by a majority. My friends there, who considered that I had been of some service, saw fit to reward me with the job of printing the money — very profitable work and a great help to me. This was one more advantage gained by being able to write.

Time and experience made the usefulness of this currency so obvious that it was never much disputed afterward. It soon grew to fifty-five thousand pounds, by 1739 to eighty thousand, and during the war it rose to upward of three hundred and fifty thousand — trade, building, and population increasing all the while. Though I now think there are limits beyond which the quantity can do harm.

Soon afterward, through my friend Hamilton, I obtained the printing of the Newcastle paper money as well — another profitable job, as I thought then; small things look great to those in small circumstances, and to me these really were great advantages, because they were great encouragements. He also secured me the printing of that government's laws and votes, which stayed in my hands as long as I remained in the business.

I now opened a little stationer's shop. I stocked it with blanks — printed legal forms — of every sort, the most accurate that had ever appeared among us, with help in that from my friend Breintnal. I also carried paper, parchment, chapmen's books (the cheap little volumes peddlers sold), and so on. A compositor named Whitemash, whom I had known in London — an excellent workman — now came to me and worked with me steadily and diligently, and I took on an apprentice, the son of Aquilla Rose.

I was still in debt for the printing house. To secure my credit and reputation as a tradesman, I took care not only to be in reality industrious and frugal, but to avoid all appearances to the contrary. I dressed plainly. I was seen at no places of idle amusement. I never went out fishing or shooting; a book, I admit, did sometimes lure me from my work, but that was seldom, done in private, and caused no scandal. And to show that I was not above my business, I sometimes wheeled the paper I bought at the stores home through the streets on a wheelbarrow. Being thus regarded as an industrious, thriving young man who paid promptly for what he bought, I found the merchants who imported stationery soliciting my custom, others offering to supply me with books, and things went on swimmingly. Meanwhile Keimer's credit and business declined by the day, until at last he was forced to sell his printing house to satisfy his creditors. He went off to Barbados and lived there for some years in very poor circumstances.

His apprentice, David Harry — whom I had trained while I worked with him — bought his equipment and set up in his place in Philadelphia. At first I feared a powerful rival in Harry, since his friends were very capable and well connected, so I proposed a partnership to him — which he, luckily for me, rejected with scorn. He was very proud, dressed like a gentleman, lived expensively, spent much of his time out amusing himself, ran into debt, and neglected his business; whereupon all business left him. Finding nothing to do, he followed Keimer to Barbados, taking the printing house with him. There the apprentice employed his former master as a journeyman. They quarreled constantly; Harry fell steadily behind, and at last was forced to sell his types and return to his farm work in Pennsylvania. The man who bought them hired Keimer to run them, but within a few years Keimer died.

That left me no competitor in Philadelphia but the old one, Bradford — who was rich and comfortable, did a little printing now and then with whatever stray hands he could get, and was not very anxious about the business. Still, since he kept the post office, people assumed he had better opportunities for obtaining news; his paper was thought a better carrier of advertisements than mine, and so it got many more — profitable for him and a disadvantage to me. The truth is I did receive and send papers by post, but only by bribing the riders, who carried them privately, Bradford having been unkind enough to forbid it. That bred some resentment in me, and I thought so poorly of him for it that when I later came into his situation, I took care never to imitate it.

Up to this point I had continued to board with Godfrey, who lived with his wife and children in part of my house and had one side of the shop for his glazier's business — though he did little of it, being forever absorbed in his mathematics. Mrs. Godfrey set up a match for me with a relation's daughter and took every opportunity to bring us together, until a serious courtship on my part followed — the girl herself being thoroughly deserving. The old folks encouraged me with constant invitations to supper and a habit of leaving the two of us alone, until at length it was time to talk terms. Mrs. Godfrey managed our little negotiation. I let her know that I expected as much money with their daughter as would pay off what I still owed on the printing house — not above a hundred pounds, I believe, at the time. She brought back word that they had no such sum to spare. I said they could mortgage their house at the loan office. The answer, after some days, was that they did not approve the match: on inquiring of Bradford, they had been told that the printing business was not a profitable one; that the types would soon wear out and more be needed; that S. Keimer and D. Harry had failed one after the other and I would probably soon follow them; and accordingly I was forbidden the house and the daughter shut away.

Whether this was a genuine change of heart or only a tactic — on the theory that we were too far gone in affection to back out, and so would steal a marriage, leaving them free to give or withhold whatever they pleased — I don't know. But I suspected the tactic, resented it, and went there no more. Mrs. Godfrey afterward brought me more favorable reports of their disposition and would have drawn me on again, but I declared absolutely that I would have nothing more to do with that family. The Godfreys took offense; we fell out, and they moved away, leaving me the whole house — and I resolved to take no more lodgers.

But this affair had turned my thoughts toward marriage, and I looked around me and made overtures of acquaintance in other places. I soon found, though, that the printing business being generally considered a poor one, I could expect no money with a wife unless she was the sort I would not otherwise find agreeable. Meanwhile a friendly correspondence had continued between me and Mrs. Read's family, as neighbors and old acquaintances; they had all thought well of me from the time I first lodged in their house. I was often invited over and consulted in their affairs, where I was sometimes of use. I pitied poor Miss Read's unfortunate situation — she was generally dejected, rarely cheerful, and avoided company. I considered my giddiness and inconstancy in London to be in great measure the cause of her unhappiness, though her mother was good enough to think the fault more her own than mine, since she had prevented our marrying before I went there and had urged the other match in my absence. Our mutual affection revived — but there were now serious objections to a union. Her marriage was in fact regarded as invalid, a previous wife of her husband's being said to be living in England; but the distance made this hard to prove, and though there was a report of his death, that was not certain either. And even if it were true, he had left many debts, which his successor might be called on to pay. Yet we ventured over all these difficulties, and I took her to wife on September 1st, 1730. None of the troubles we had feared came to pass. She proved a good and faithful helpmate, and was a great help to me tending the shop; we thrived together, and have always tried to make each other happy. Thus I corrected that great erratum as well as I could.

About this time our club was meeting not at a tavern but in a little room of Mr. Grace's, set apart for the purpose. I made a proposal: since our books were so often cited in our discussions of the queries, it would be convenient to have them all together where we met, ready to consult when needed; and by clubbing our books into a common library, each of us — for as long as we chose to keep them together — would have the use of every other member's books, which would be nearly as good as owning the whole collection. Everyone liked the idea and agreed to it, and we filled one end of the room with such books as we could spare. There weren't as many as we expected; and though they proved very useful, some inconveniences arose from want of proper care of them, and after about a year the collection was broken up and each man took his books home again.

And now I set on foot my first project of a public nature: a subscription library. I drew up the proposals, had them put into proper form by our great scrivener, Brockden, and with the help of my friends in the Junto secured fifty subscribers at forty shillings each to begin with, plus ten shillings a year for fifty years — the term our company was to run. We afterward obtained a charter, and the company grew to a hundred members. This was the mother of all the North American subscription libraries, now so numerous. It has become a considerable thing in itself, and is continually growing. These libraries have improved the general conversation of Americans, made ordinary tradesmen and farmers as intelligent as most gentlemen from other countries, and perhaps contributed in some degree to the stand so widely made throughout the colonies in defense of their privileges.

It has been some time since I received the letters above, but I have been too busy until now to think of complying with their request. It might also be much better done if I were at home among my papers, which would aid my memory and help me pin down dates; but my return is uncertain, and having just now a little leisure, I will try to recollect and write what I can. If I live to get home, it can be corrected and improved there.

Having no copy here of what is already written, I don't know whether I have given an account of the means I used to establish the Philadelphia public library — which from a small beginning has now become so considerable — though I remember having gotten down to about the time of that transaction (1730). So I will begin here with an account of it, which can be struck out if it turns out to have been given already.

At the time I settled in Pennsylvania, there was not a good bookseller's shop in any of the colonies south of Boston. In New York and Philadelphia the printers were stationers, true, but they sold only paper and the like, almanacs, ballads, and a few common schoolbooks. Those who loved reading had to send to England for their books; the members of the Junto each had a few. We had left the alehouse where we first met and hired a room to hold our club in. I proposed that we all bring our books to that room, where they would not only be ready to consult in our conferences but become a common benefit, each of us free to borrow whichever he wished to read at home. This was done, and for some time it contented us.

Finding the advantage of this little collection, I proposed to make the benefit of books more widely available by starting a public subscription library. I sketched the plan and rules that would be necessary, and got a skillful conveyancer, Mr. Charles Brockden, to put the whole into formal articles of agreement to be signed, by which each subscriber committed to pay a certain sum down for the first purchase of books, and an annual contribution for adding to them. So few were the readers in Philadelphia at that time, and most of us so poor, that with all my industry I could not find more than fifty people — mostly young tradesmen — willing to pay down forty shillings each for the purpose, and ten shillings a year after that. On this little fund we began. The books were imported; the library was opened one day a week for lending to the subscribers, on their signed notes promising to pay double the value of any book not duly returned. The institution soon proved its usefulness, was imitated by other towns and in other provinces, and the libraries were enlarged by donations. Reading became fashionable; and our people, having no public amusements to divert them from study, grew better acquainted with books, and within a few years strangers were observing that we were better instructed and more intelligent than people of the same rank generally are in other countries.

When we were about to sign the articles — which were to bind us, our heirs, and so on for fifty years — Mr. Brockden, the scrivener, said to us, "You are young men, but it is scarcely probable that any of you will live to see the expiration of the term fixed in this instrument." A number of us, however, are still living; though the document itself was rendered void after a few years by a charter that incorporated the company and gave it perpetuity.

The objections and reluctance I met with in canvassing for subscriptions soon taught me how unwise it is to present yourself as the proposer of any useful project that might be supposed to raise your reputation even slightly above your neighbors', when you need their help to accomplish it. I therefore kept myself as much out of sight as I could, and presented it as a scheme of a number of friends, who had asked me to go around and propose it to those they thought lovers of reading. In this way my business went more smoothly, and I practiced the method ever after on such occasions; and from my frequent successes, I can heartily recommend it. The small present sacrifice of your vanity will be amply repaid later. If it remains uncertain for a while whom the merit belongs to, someone vainer than yourself will be encouraged to claim it — and then even envy will be disposed to do you justice, by plucking those assumed feathers and restoring them to their rightful owner.

This library gave me the means of improvement by constant study, for which I set aside an hour or two each day, and so repaired in some degree the loss of the learned education my father had once intended for me. Reading was the only amusement I allowed myself. I spent no time in taverns, at games, or at frolics of any kind; and my industry in my business continued as tireless as it was necessary. I was in debt for my printing house, I had a young family coming on to be educated, and I had to compete for business with two printers who had been established in the place before me. My circumstances, however, grew easier by the day. My old habits of frugality continued; and my father, among the lessons he gave me as a boy, had often repeated a proverb of Solomon — "Seest thou a man diligent in his calling, he shall stand before kings, he shall not stand before mean men" — so from then on I considered industry a means of obtaining wealth and distinction, which encouraged me. Not that I ever thought I would literally stand before kings — which, however, has since happened: I have stood before five of them, and even had the honor of sitting down to dinner with one, the King of Denmark.

We have an English proverb that says, "He that would thrive, must ask his wife." It was lucky for me that I had one as disposed to industry and frugality as I was. She helped me cheerfully in my business — folding and stitching pamphlets, tending shop, buying up old linen rags for the papermakers, and so on. We kept no idle servants; our table was plain and simple, our furniture the cheapest to be had. For instance, my breakfast was for a long time bread and milk (no tea), eaten out of a twopenny earthenware bowl with a pewter spoon. But mark how luxury will enter a family and make its progress in spite of principle: called to breakfast one morning, I found it in a china bowl, with a spoon of silver! They had been bought for me without my knowledge by my wife, at the enormous cost of three-and-twenty shillings — for which she had no other excuse or apology to make but that she thought her husband deserved a silver spoon and a china bowl as well as any of his neighbors. This was the first appearance of silver and china in our house; in the course of years, as our wealth increased, it gradually grew to several hundred pounds in value.

I had been religiously educated as a Presbyterian; and though some of that persuasion's doctrines — the eternal decrees of God, election, reprobation, and so on — appeared to me unintelligible, and others doubtful, and I early stopped attending the sect's public assemblies (Sunday being my study day), I was never without some religious principles. I never doubted, for instance, the existence of God; that he made the world and governed it by his Providence; that the most acceptable service of God was doing good to man; that our souls are immortal; and that all crime will be punished and virtue rewarded, either here or hereafter. These I regarded as the essentials of every religion; and finding them in all the religions we had in our country, I respected them all — though with different degrees of respect, according as I found them more or less mixed with other articles which, having no tendency to inspire, promote, or confirm morality, served mainly to divide us and make us unfriendly to one another. This respect for all of them, along with a belief that even the worst had some good effects, led me to avoid any talk that might weaken another man's good opinion of his own religion; and as our province filled with people and new places of worship were continually wanted — and generally built by voluntary contribution — my mite for that purpose, whatever the sect, was never refused.

Though I seldom attended any public worship, I still believed in its propriety, and in its usefulness when rightly conducted, and I regularly paid my annual subscription toward the support of the only Presbyterian minister or congregation we had in Philadelphia. He used to visit me sometimes as a friend and admonish me to attend his services, and now and then I let myself be persuaded — once for five Sundays running. Had he been, in my opinion, a good preacher, perhaps I might have continued, despite my need of Sunday's leisure for my course of study; but his sermons were chiefly either polemical arguments or explanations of the particular doctrines of our sect, and to me they were all very dry, uninteresting, and unedifying — not a single moral principle was taught or enforced, their aim seeming to be to make us Presbyterians rather than good citizens.

At length he took for his text that verse from the fourth chapter of Philippians: "Finally, brethren, whatsoever things are true, honest, just, pure, lovely, or of good report, if there be any virtue, or any praise, think on these things." In a sermon on such a text, I figured, we could not miss getting some morality. But he confined himself to five points only, as the apostle's meaning: 1. Keeping holy the Sabbath day. 2. Being diligent in reading the holy Scriptures. 3. Duly attending public worship. 4. Partaking of the Sacrament. 5. Paying due respect to God's ministers. These might all be good things; but as they were not the kind of good things I expected from that text, I despaired of ever finding any from any other, was disgusted, and attended his preaching no more. Some years before (in 1728) I had composed a little liturgy, or form of prayer, for my own private use, entitled Articles of Belief and Acts of Religion. I returned to the use of it, and went no more to the public assemblies. My conduct may have been blamable; but I leave it as it stands, without attempting further to excuse it — my present purpose being to relate facts, not to make apologies for them.
