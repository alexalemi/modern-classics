Letter 87

Letter 87: Some Arguments in Favor of the Simple Life

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

"I was shipwrecked before I ever got on board." I won't explain how that happened, or you'll file it away as one more Stoic paradox. But I will prove to you — whenever you're willing to listen, and even if you're not — that the words are neither false nor as surprising as they sound at first. In the meantime, here is what the journey showed me: how much we own that we don't need, and how easily we could decide to let go of things whose loss, whenever necessity does take them from us, we never even feel.

My friend Maximus and I have just spent two of the happiest days imaginable on the road, taking along very few slaves — one carriage-load — and no gear beyond what we wore on our backs. The mattress lies on the ground; I lie on the mattress. Of our two rugs, one goes under us and one over. Nothing could have been trimmed from our lunch: it took less than an hour to prepare, and we were nowhere without dried figs, never without writing tablets. If I have bread, the figs are a relish; if I don't, the figs are the bread. They make every day a New Year's feast for me, and I make the New Year happy and prosperous with good thoughts and greatness of soul — for the soul is never greater than when it has set aside everything that isn't its own and made its own peace by fearing nothing, its own riches by craving none.

The vehicle I'm riding in is a farmer's cart. The mules prove they're alive only by walking. The driver is barefoot — and not because it's summer, either. And yet I can barely force myself to want anyone to think this cart is mine. My false embarrassment about the truth is still holding out, you see. Whenever we meet a more elegant party on the road, I blush in spite of myself — proof that the conduct I approve and applaud has not yet found a firm, settled home in me. The man who blushes at riding in a rattletrap will brag when he rides in style.

So my progress is still too small. I don't yet have the courage to own my thrift in public. I still worry about what other travelers think of me. What I should have done instead was throw my opinion straight in the face of the whole human race: "You are mad. You are deceived. You pour your admiration on superfluous things. You value no man at what he's really worth. When property is at stake, you know exactly how to calculate. Before you lend a man money or favors — because by now you enter favors in your account-book too — you run the numbers with the most scrupulous care. 'His estates are wide, but his debts are heavy.' 'A fine house — built on borrowed capital.' 'No one can field a more brilliant retinue on short notice, but he can't cover what he owes.' 'If he paid off his creditors, he'd have nothing left.'" Well, you should do the same in every other case too: subtract, and find out what each man actually owns.

You call a man rich, I suppose, because his gold dinner service travels with him wherever he goes, because he farms land in every province, because the account-book he unrolls is a long one, because he owns estates so vast near the city that people would grudge him that much acreage even in the empty wastelands of Apulia. But when you've listed all of it — he is poor. Why? He's in debt. "For how much?" you ask. For everything he has. Or do you think it matters whether a man has borrowed from another man or from Fortune? What good are mules matched in identical livery? Or decorated chariots, and

  Horses draped in purple and embroidery,
  gold harness hanging at their necks,
  champing gilded bits, in trappings all of gold?

Neither master nor mule is improved by any of it.

Marcus Cato the Censor — whose life did the state as much good as Scipio's, for while Scipio made war on our enemies, Cato made war on our bad morals — used to ride a donkey; and a donkey, at that, loaded with saddlebags carrying the master's necessities. How I'd love to see him meet on the road today one of our fashionable dandies, with his outriders and his Numidian couriers and a great cloud of dust rolling ahead of him. Your dandy would look refined and well-attended next to Marcus Cato, no question — your dandy who, in the middle of all his luxurious equipment, is chiefly debating whether to hire himself out to the sword or to the hunting-knife. What a glory to his times: a general who had celebrated a triumph, a censor, and — most remarkable of all — a Cato, content with a single nag, and with less than a whole nag at that, since the baggage hanging down over both flanks had claimed part of the animal for itself. So wouldn't you rather have Cato's horse — that one horse, saddle-worn by Cato himself — than the dandy's whole string of plump ponies, Spanish cobs, and trotters? I can see there will be no end to this subject unless I make one myself. So I'll say no more about superfluous things — surely the man who first called them "hindrances" had a prophet's inkling that they would become exactly what they are now. Instead, let me deliver to you the syllogisms of our school — still a very small number — that bear on the question of virtue, which in our judgment is sufficient for the happy life.

"What is good makes men good. In the art of music, for example, what is good makes the musician. But chance events do not make a good man; therefore chance events are not goods." The Peripatetics answer that the premise is false: men are not in every case made good by what is good. In music there is something good — a flute, a harp, an organ tuned to accompany singing — but none of those instruments makes the musician. To which we reply: "You misunderstand what we mean by 'the good in music.' We don't mean what equips the musician; we mean what makes him. You are pointing at the tools of the art, not at the art itself. If anything in the art of music itself is good, that thing will make the musician — every time." Let me put it more clearly still. "Good in the art of music" can mean two things: what helps the musician's performance, and what helps his art. The instruments — flutes and organs and harps — belong to the performance; they have nothing to do with the art itself. A man is an artist without them; at most he lacks the means to exercise his art. But the good in a man is not double in that way, because the good of a man and the good of his life are one and the same.

"What can fall to the lot of the basest and most despised of men is not a good. But wealth falls to the lot of the pimp and the gladiator-trainer; therefore wealth is not a good." "Another false premise," they say, "for we see goods fall to very low sorts of men — in scholarship, and in the art of healing, and in the art of navigation too." But those arts make no claim to greatness of soul. They rise to no heights, and they don't frown on whatever Fortune brings. It is virtue that lifts a man up and sets him above everything mortals hold dear; virtue neither craves too much what people call good nor fears too much what people call bad. Chelidon, one of Cleopatra's eunuchs, held enormous wealth; and more recently Natalis — a man whose tongue was as shameless as it was filthy, a man whose mouth performed the vilest services — was heir to many men and made many men his heirs. Well? Was it the money that made him unclean, or did he dirty the money? Money tumbles into some men's hands the way a coin tumbles down a sewer. Virtue stands above all that. It is appraised in coin of its own minting, and it counts none of these random windfalls as good. But medicine and navigation don't forbid themselves or their practitioners to admire such things. A man who is not good can still be a doctor, a pilot, a scholar — yes, just as easily as he can be a cook. A man who comes to possess something that is not of a random sort cannot be called a random sort of man; a person is the same sort as what he possesses. A strongbox is worth exactly what it holds — or rather, it's a mere accessory of what it holds. Who ever prices a full purse at anything except the count of the money inside it? The same goes for the owners of great estates: they are accessories and afterthoughts to their own possessions.

Why, then, is the wise man great? Because he has a great soul. So it stands: what falls even to the most contemptible person is not a good. That's why I would never call inactivity a good — even the tree-frog and the flea have that. Nor would I call rest and freedom from bother a good — what has more leisure than a worm? Do you ask what produces the wise man? The same thing that produces a god. You must grant that there is something godlike in the wise man, something heavenly, something grand. The good doesn't come to everyone, and it doesn't tolerate just any owner. Look:

  See what each country bears, and what it won't:
  here grain grows richer, there the vine;
  elsewhere young trees and grasses, all unbidden,
  clothe themselves in green. See how Tmolus ships
  its saffron perfume out, how ivory comes
  from India; soft Sheba sends its incense,
  and the naked Chalybes their iron.

These products were parceled out to separate countries so that human beings would be forced to trade with one another, each needing something from his neighbor in turn. The Supreme Good has its own homeland too. It does not grow where ivory grows, or iron. Where does the Supreme Good live, then? In the soul. And unless the soul is pure and holy, there is no room in it for God.

"Good does not come from evil. But riches come from greed; therefore riches are not a good." "It isn't true," they say, "that good doesn't come from evil. Money comes from sacrilege and theft. Sacrilege and theft are evils, yes — but only because they work more evil than good. They bring profit; but the profit comes packaged with fear, anxiety, and torment of mind and body." Whoever says this has to admit that sacrilege, though an evil because it works much evil, is also partly good because it accomplishes a certain amount of good. What could be more monstrous than that? Though, to be honest, we have actually convinced the world that sacrilege, theft, and adultery belong among the goods. How many men feel no blush at theft; how many boast of their adulteries! Petty sacrilege gets punished; sacrilege on the grand scale rides in a triumphal procession. Besides, if sacrilege were wholly good in any respect, it would also be honorable and would count as right conduct — for it is conduct, and conduct concerns us. And no human being, thinking it over seriously, admits that.

So goods cannot spring from evil. For if sacrilege is an evil, as you object, only because it brings on much evil, then absolve sacrilege of its punishment, guarantee it immunity, and sacrilege becomes wholly good. And yet the worst punishment for a crime is inside the crime. You are mistaken, I insist, if you save your punishments for the hangman or the prison. The crime is punished the moment it is finished — no, the moment it is committed. Good does not spring from evil any more than figs grow on olive trees. What grows corresponds to its seed; goods cannot wander from their stock. Just as the honorable does not grow from the base, good does not grow from evil. For the honorable and the good are identical.

Some of our own school push back against this as follows: "Suppose money from any source whatsoever is a good. Then even when it is taken by an act of sacrilege, the money doesn't owe its origin to the sacrilege. Take this illustration. In one jar there is a piece of gold and there is a serpent. If you take the gold out of the jar, the jar doesn't yield me the gold because it contains the serpent too; it yields the gold in spite of containing the serpent. In the same way, gain results from sacrilege not because sacrilege is a base and cursed act, but because the act contains gain as well. In the jar, the serpent is the evil, not the gold lying beside it; in the act of sacrilege, the crime is the evil, not the profit." I disagree with these men, and here's why: the two cases are not alike at all. In the one, I can take the gold without the serpent; in the other, I cannot take the profit without committing the sacrilege. The gain in that case doesn't lie beside the crime; it is blended into the crime.

"What entangles us in many evils while we are trying to get it is not a good. But while we are trying to get riches, we are entangled in many evils; therefore riches are not a good." "Your first premise," they say, "carries two meanings. One is: we become entangled in many evils while trying to get riches. But we also become entangled in many evils while trying to get virtue — one man is shipwrecked traveling for his studies, another is captured by pirates. The second meaning is: that through which we become entangled in evils is not a good. And it won't logically follow from your proposition that it is through riches or through pleasure that we're entangled in evils. Otherwise, if riches are the route to many evils, riches are not merely not-a-good; they are a positive evil. You claim only that they are not a good. Besides," the objector adds, "you grant that riches have some use. You count them among the advantages. But on that same reasoning they can't even be an advantage, since the pursuit of them costs us so much disadvantage." Certain men answer the objection this way: "You are wrong to charge the disadvantages to riches. Riches injure no one. What harms a man is his own folly or his neighbor's wickedness — just as a sword by itself kills nobody; it is merely the tool in the killer's hand. Riches themselves don't harm you just because you come to harm on account of riches."

I think Posidonius reasons better. He holds that riches are a cause of evil not because they do any evil themselves, but because they goad men on until they are ready to do it. The efficient cause — the one that necessarily does harm directly — is one thing; the antecedent cause is another. It is the antecedent cause that riches carry inside them: they puff up the spirit and breed pride, they attract resentment, and they unsettle the mind so thoroughly that we delight in the mere reputation of being wealthy, even though that reputation is bound to hurt us. Goods, properly speaking, should be beyond all blame. They are pure; they don't corrupt the spirit; they don't tempt us. They lift and enlarge the spirit, yes — but without inflating it. Goods produce confidence; riches produce brazenness. Goods give us greatness of soul; riches give us arrogance. And arrogance is nothing but a false front of greatness.

"By that account," says the objector, "riches are not merely not-a-good; they are a positive evil." They would be an evil if they did harm by themselves — if, as I said, the efficient cause were what they carried. In fact what they carry is the antecedent cause — and a cause that doesn't merely rouse the spirit but drags it along by force. For riches shower on us a semblance of the good, so like the real thing that most people take it on trust. Virtue carries an antecedent cause too: the envy it draws — for many men are resented for their wisdom, and many for their justice. But that cause, though it comes with virtue, is not virtue's own product, and it is no mere semblance of reality. On the contrary: far closer to reality is that vision which virtue flashes into men's spirits, summoning them to love it and marvel at it.

Posidonius thinks the syllogism should be framed like this: "Things that give the soul no greatness, no confidence, no freedom from care are not goods. But riches and health and conditions like them do none of these things; therefore riches and health are not goods." Then he extends it further: "Things that give the soul no greatness, no confidence, no freedom from care — and that furthermore breed in it arrogance, vanity, and insolence — are evils. But the gifts of Fortune push us into those evil ways. Therefore the gifts of Fortune are not goods." "But by that reasoning," says the objector, "the gifts of Fortune won't even be advantages." No — advantages and goods stand on different footing. An advantage is what contains more usefulness than annoyance. But a good must be unmixed, with no harmfulness anywhere in it. A thing is not good if it does more benefit than injury — only if it does nothing but benefit. Besides, advantages can belong to animals, to imperfect men, to fools. So an advantage may have some disadvantage stirred into it; we call the mixture an "advantage" by judging its dominant element. The good can belong to the wise man alone, and it must be without alloy.

Cheer up: there's only one knot left for you to untangle, though it's a knot fit for a Hercules. "Good does not come from evil. But riches come from many cases of poverty; therefore riches are not a good." Our school doesn't recognize this syllogism; the Peripatetics both concoct it and solve it. But Posidonius reports that this fallacy, batted around every school of logic there is, was refuted by Antipater as follows: "The word 'poverty' denotes not the possession of something but the non-possession of it — or, as the ancients put it, deprivation (the Greeks say 'by deprivation,' meaning negatively). 'Poverty' states not what a man has, but what he does not have. So no fullness can come from a multitude of empties; riches are made up of many positive possessions, not many deficits. You have," he says, "the wrong notion of what poverty means. Poverty is not the possession of little; it is the non-possession of much. The word applies not to what a man has, but to what he lacks." I could say all this more easily if Latin had a word for the Greek term meaning "not-possessing." Antipater assigns that quality to poverty; for my own part, I can't see what poverty is except the possession of little. If we ever find ourselves with plenty of leisure, we can investigate what the essence of riches is and what the essence of poverty is. But when that day comes, let's also consider whether it might be better to soften poverty, and to take the arrogance out of wealth, than to quibble over the words as if the question of the things were already settled.

Imagine we've been summoned to an assembly, and a bill to abolish riches is on the floor. Is this how we'd argue for it or against it — with these syllogisms? Will syllogisms make the Roman people demand poverty and praise it — poverty, the foundation and the cause of their empire — and shrink in fear from their own wealth instead? Will they make the people reflect that they found that wealth among the peoples they conquered; that wealth is the source from which the vote-hunting and the bribery and the disorder burst into a city once famous for scruple and sobriety; that the spoils of conquered nations are put on display far too lavishly; and that whatever one people has snatched away from all the rest can be snatched even more easily by all the rest from one? Better to vote for that law with our conduct, and to take our desires by direct assault, than to outflank them with logic. If we can, let us speak more bravely; if not, let us speak more frankly.

Farewell.
