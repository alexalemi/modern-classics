Letter 77

Letter 77: On Taking One’s Own Life

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Today, without warning, the “Alexandrian” ships came into view — I mean the ones that are sent ahead to announce the fleet is coming; people call them mail-boats. All Campania is glad to see them. The whole crowd of Puteoli stands packed on the docks, and no matter how many vessels are out there, they can pick out the Alexandrian boats by the very set of their sails. Those ships alone are allowed to keep their topsails spread — the upper canvas that every ship flies on the open sea, because nothing drives a ship along like her topsails; that is where most of the speed comes from. So when the breeze stiffens and grows stronger than is comfortable, crews lower their yards, since the wind has less force close to the water. But once the ships have made Capri and the headland where

  Tall Pallas keeps her watch upon the stormy peak,

every other vessel is ordered to make do with her mainsail — and the topsail stands out, unmistakable, on the Alexandrian mail-boats.

While everyone else was bustling and hurrying down to the waterfront, I took great pleasure in my own laziness. I would soon have letters from my friends in hand, but I felt no rush to learn how my affairs were doing overseas, or what news the letters carried. For some time now I have had no losses — and no gains either. Even if I were not an old man, that thought would have pleased me; as things stand, it pleased me all the more. However little I might own, I would still have more travel-money left than road left to travel — especially since the road we are on is one we need not follow to the very end. A journey is incomplete if you stop halfway, or anywhere short of your destination; but a life is not incomplete if it is honorable. Wherever you stop living, if you stop nobly, your life is whole. And often a man must stop bravely, and not necessarily for momentous reasons — after all, the reasons that keep us here are not momentous either.

Tullius Marcellinus — a man you knew very well, quiet in his youth and old before his time — fell ill with a disease that was by no means hopeless, but long, troublesome, and demanding, and so he began to think about dying. He called many of his friends together, and each gave him advice. The timid friend urged him to do whatever he had already made up his mind to do. The flattering, wheedling friend offered the counsel he guessed Marcellinus would find most pleasing when he thought it over later. But our Stoic friend — a rare man, and, to give him the praise he deserves, a man of courage and vigor — advised him best of all, it seems to me. He began like this: “Don’t torment yourself, my dear Marcellinus, as if the question you are weighing were a great matter. Living is not a great matter; all your slaves live, and so do all the animals. What is a great matter is to die honorably, sensibly, bravely. Think how long you have been doing the same things: food, sleep, desire — around and around, the same daily circle. The wish to die can be felt not only by the sensible man, or the brave man, or the unhappy man, but even by the man who is simply full.”

Marcellinus did not need someone to urge him so much as someone to help him: his slaves refused to do what he asked. So the Stoic first took away their fear, showing them the household ran a risk only when it was uncertain whether the master’s death was his own choice — and that besides, it was as bad a practice to kill one’s master as to stop him by force from ending his own life. Then he suggested to Marcellinus himself that it would be a kindness, when life was finished, to hand out gifts to those who had attended him through the whole of it — just as, when a banquet ends, what remains is shared among the attendants standing around the table. Marcellinus was an easygoing and generous soul, even where his own property was concerned; so he distributed small sums among his grieving slaves, and comforted them too. He needed no sword and no bloodshed. For three days he fasted, and had a tent set up in his own bedroom. Then a tub was brought in; he lay in it a long while, and as the hot water was poured over him continuously, he slipped away little by little — not without a certain pleasure, as he himself remarked, the kind of pleasure a slow fading-out tends to bring. Those of us who have ever fainted know from experience what that feeling is.

I have wandered into this little story, but you will not be sorry to hear it, for you will see that your friend’s departure was neither difficult nor painful. Though he brought about his own death, he made the gentlest of exits — he simply glided out of life. And the story may be of some use, too; a crisis often calls for exactly such examples. There are times when we ought to die and are unwilling; and times when we are dying, and are unwilling still. No one is so ignorant as not to know that he must die someday — and yet when death comes near, a man turns to run, trembles, weeps. Wouldn’t you call him an utter fool if he cried because he was not alive a thousand years ago? Then he is just as much a fool if he cries because he will not be alive a thousand years from now. It is all the same: you will not be, and you were not. Neither stretch of time belongs to you. You have been set down on this single point of time — and if you could stretch it, how far would you stretch it? Why weep? Why pray? You are taking pains for nothing.

  Give up the hope that prayers of yours can bend
  what the gods have decreed from its appointed end.

Those decrees are fixed and unalterable, driven by a mighty, everlasting necessity. You will go where all things go. What is strange about that? You were born under this law. It happened to your father, your mother, your ancestors, everyone before you; it will happen to everyone after you. An unbreakable chain that no power can alter binds all things together and draws all things along. Think of the multitudes bound for death who will come after you; think of the multitudes who will go with you! You would die more bravely, I suppose, in the company of many thousands — well, there are many thousands, men and animals both, drawing their last breath at this very moment, in all their different ways, while you stand there hesitating. Did you really believe you would never arrive at the destination you have been traveling toward your whole life? There is no journey without an end.

You expect me, I suppose, to bring out examples of great men at this point. No — I will cite the case of a boy. The story of the Spartan lad has come down to us: taken captive while still very young, he kept shouting in his Doric dialect, “I will not be a slave!” — and he was as good as his word. The very first time he was ordered to do menial, degrading work — the command was to fetch a chamber-pot — he dashed his head against the wall and died. Freedom is that close at hand — and is anyone still a slave? Wouldn’t you rather your own son died like that than grew old by giving in weakly? Why be distressed, then, when even a boy can die so bravely? Refuse to follow, and you will be led. Take into your own hands what is now in the hands of another. Won’t you borrow that boy’s courage and say, “I am no slave”? Poor fellow — you are a slave to men, a slave to your business, a slave to life itself. For life, if the courage to die is missing, is slavery.

And what exactly are you waiting for? The very pleasures that make you linger, that hold you back — you have used them all up. Not one of them is new to you; not one of them hasn’t already turned stale from sheer excess. You know the taste of wine and of cordials. It makes no difference whether a hundred measures pass through your bladder or a thousand: you are nothing but a wine-strainer. You are a connoisseur of the oyster and the mullet; your luxury has left nothing untasted for the years ahead — and yet these are the things you are torn from unwillingly. What else is there you would hate to lose? Friends? Who can be a friend to a man like you? Country? Do you care enough about your country to be late for dinner? The light of the sun? You would blow it out if you could — for what have you ever done that could bear to be seen in the light? Admit the truth: it is not the senate chamber or the forum you are reluctant to leave, not even the natural world — you just can’t bear to leave the fish-market, though you have emptied its stalls.

You are afraid of death — but how heroically you scorn it over a plate of mushrooms! You want to live: do you even know how? You are afraid to die. But tell me: is this life of yours anything but death? Gaius Caesar was passing along the Via Latina when a man stepped out from a column of prisoners, his gray beard hanging to his chest, and begged to be put to death. “What!” said Caesar. “You call this being alive?” That is the answer for men to whom death would come as a relief. “You’re afraid to die? You call this being alive?” “But,” someone says, “I want to live — I am doing so much honorable work. I am reluctant to leave life’s duties; I am carrying them out loyally and tirelessly.” Well — don’t you know that dying is one of life’s duties too? You are deserting no duty, for there is no fixed quota you are obliged to complete.

There is no life that is not short. Measured against the universe, even Nestor’s life was short — even Sattia’s, the woman who had it carved on her tombstone that she had lived ninety-nine years. Some people, you see, boast about a long old age; but who could have stood that old lady if she’d had the luck to round out her hundredth year? Life is like a play: what matters is not how long the acting runs, but how good it is. It makes no difference where you stop. Stop wherever you like — only make sure the ending is well turned.

Farewell.
