Letter 91: On the Lesson to Be Drawn from the Burning of Lyons

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Our friend Liberalis is heartbroken. He has just gotten the news of the fire that wiped out the colony of Lyons. A disaster like that would shake anyone, let alone a man who loves his home city as dearly as he does. And the blow has forced him to ask how strong his own character really is — a character he trained, I suppose, against the troubles he thought he had reason to fear. But this trouble was so unexpected, so nearly unheard of, that I'm not surprised it caught him without his guard up. There was no precedent for it. Fire has damaged plenty of cities; it has annihilated none. Even when an enemy hurls fire against the walls, the flames gutter out in a hundred places, and however often they are rekindled, they rarely devour so completely that nothing is left for the sword to finish. Even earthquakes have hardly ever been violent enough to flatten whole cities. And no blaze anywhere has ever raged so fiercely that a second fire would have found nothing left to burn. Yet here, so many beautiful buildings — any one of which would have made a town famous by itself — came down in a single night. In the deepest peace, something happened worse than anyone fears in wartime. Who can believe it? With weapons at rest everywhere, with the whole world at peace, Lyons — the pride of Gaul — is missing.

Fortune has usually given people warning when she meant to strike them all at once; she has let them feel the disaster coming. Every great thing has been granted some grace period before its fall. But here, a single night lay between a very great city and none. In short: it takes me longer to tell you the city perished than it took the city to perish.

All of this has bent even Liberalis' will, which stands so straight and steady against his own troubles. And there is a reason he was shaken: it is the unexpected that lands with the most weight. Strangeness adds heaviness to disaster; every mortal hurts more from a blow that also surprises him.

So nothing ought to be unexpected by us. Our minds should be sent out ahead to meet every problem, and we should consider not what usually happens, but what can happen. For what is there that Fortune, when she chooses, cannot drag down from the very peak of its prosperity? What is there that she doesn't attack all the more violently the more brilliantly it shines? What is hard or laborious for her? She doesn't always come at us the same way, or even at full strength. Sometimes she turns our own hands against us; sometimes, content with her own powers, she invents dangers that need no human agent. No moment is exempt. In the middle of our pleasures, the causes of our suffering are already sprouting. War breaks out in the middle of peace, and the very things we counted on to protect us become the things we fear; the friend turns enemy, the ally turns hostile. The summer calm whips itself into sudden storms wilder than winter's. With no enemy in sight, we suffer what enemies inflict — and if every other cause of disaster fails, too much good fortune will invent one on its own. Illness attacks the most temperate, wasting disease the strongest, punishment the most innocent, and the noisy mob the most secluded.

Fortune picks out some new weapon to remind us of her strength whenever she thinks we've forgotten her. Whatever has been built up by a long line of years, at the cost of enormous labor and by the great kindness of the gods, one single day scatters and destroys. No — whoever said "a day" has granted disaster too generous a schedule. An hour, an instant, is enough to overthrow an empire. It would be some consolation for our frailty, and the frailty of everything we make, if things perished as slowly as they came into being. But as it is, growth is sluggish and ruin is quick. Nothing is stable, public or private. The destinies of men, and of cities too, are spinning. In the middle of the greatest calm, terror rises; with nothing outside to stir up trouble, disasters burst out of the places we least expected. Thrones that stood firm through civil wars and foreign wars crash down with no one pushing them. How few states have carried their good fortune all the way to the end!

We should therefore think through every possibility, and fortify our minds against whatever evils may come. Exile, the torture of disease, war, shipwreck — rehearse them all. Fortune may tear you from your country, or your country from you; she may banish you to a wasteland — and this very place, where the crowds are suffocating, may become a wasteland itself. Let us set the whole of the human lot before our eyes. If we don't want to be overwhelmed — or even stunned — by unusual disasters, as though they were something new, then let us picture in advance not just the worst that commonly happens, but the very worst that can happen. We must think Fortune through fully and completely.

How often have cities in Asia, how often in Achaia, been leveled by a single shock of earthquake! How many towns in Syria, how many in Macedonia, have been swallowed whole! How often has that kind of devastation left Cyprus in ruins! How often has Paphos collapsed! Word reaches us constantly of entire cities utterly destroyed — and how small a part of the world are we, to whom such news comes so often!

So let us stand up to face whatever Fortune does, and whatever happens, let us be sure of this: it is never as great as rumor advertises. A rich city has been burned to ashes — the jewel of the provinces, counted among them and yet standing apart from them; but rich as it was, it sat on a single hill, and not a very large one. And of all those cities whose magnificence and grandeur you hear about today, time will erase even the traces. Don't you see how, in Achaia, the foundations of the most famous cities have already crumbled away, until nothing is left to show they ever existed? It's not only what human hands have made that totters to the ground; not only what human art and effort set in place that the passing days overthrow. Mountain peaks dissolve. Whole regions have subsided. Places that once stood far out of sight of the sea now lie under the waves. Fire's enormous power has eaten away the hills it once glowed through, and gnawed down to nothing the loftiest summits — once the sailor's comfort and his beacon. The works of nature herself take this battering; so we ought to bear the destruction of cities with untroubled minds. They stand only to fall. That end waits for every one of them. Maybe some force pent up inside, some blast of air made tremendous because its way is blocked, will throw off the weight that holds it down; maybe a whirlpool of raging currents, mightier for being hidden in the depths of the earth, will smash through whatever resists it; maybe the violence of fire will burst the framework of the earth's crust apart; maybe time, from which nothing is safe, will wear them down little by little; maybe an unhealthy climate will drive the inhabitants out and mold will eat the deserted walls. It would be tedious to count all the roads fate can take. But this one thing I know: everything mortals make is doomed to be mortal — and we live in the middle of things that are destined to die.

These are the thoughts, and thoughts like them, that I am offering as consolation to our friend Liberalis, who burns with an almost unbelievable love for his city. Perhaps it was destroyed only so it could be raised again to something better. Often a setback has simply cleared the ground for greater prosperity. Many buildings have fallen only to rise higher. Timagenes, who held a grudge against Rome and her success, used to say the only thing that grieved him about fires in Rome was knowing that better buildings would rise than the ones the flames had taken. In Lyons, too, most likely, every citizen will work to rebuild everything larger and safer than what was lost. May it be built to last — built, under happier omens, for a longer life! This colony is only in its hundredth year — not even the limit of a single human lifetime. Planted by Plancus, its founder, and favored by its natural site, it grew strong and swelled to the population it holds today. And yet think how many disasters, and how severe, it has endured within the span of one old man's life!

So let the mind be trained to understand its lot and to endure it. Let it know that there is nothing Fortune does not dare — that she has the same jurisdiction over empires as over emperors, the same power over cities as over the citizens who live in them. We must not cry out against any of this. We have entered into such a world; these are the laws we live under. If the terms suit you, obey; if not, leave by whatever door you please. Cry out in anger if some unfair rule is aimed at you personally; but if this inescapable law binds the highest and the lowest alike, then make your peace with fate, which dissolves all things. You should not measure us by our burial mounds, or by the monuments of unequal size that line the road; their ashes level all men. We are unequal at birth, but we are equal in death. And what I say about cities I say about the people in them: Ardea, the little town, was captured — and so was Rome. The great founder of human law made no distinctions among us for high lineage or famous names, except while we live. When we come to the end that waits for all mortals, he says: "Away with ambition! Let one and the same law apply to every creature that burdens the earth." For endurance, we are all equal: no one is more fragile than anyone else, and no one is more certain of being alive tomorrow.

Alexander, king of Macedon, took up the study of geometry. Unhappy man — because it would teach him how tiny the earth really was, this earth of which he had seized only a fraction. Unhappy man, I say again, because he was bound to discover that his title was a lie. Who can be "the Great" in something so small? The lessons were intricate and could only be mastered by careful attention — not the sort of thing a madman could grasp, a man whose thoughts went ranging out beyond the ocean. "Teach me something easy!" he cries. And his teacher answers: "These things are the same for everyone — just as hard for one as for another." Imagine that nature is saying the same to us: "The things you complain of are the same for all. I can make nothing easier for any man. But whoever wishes can make things easier for himself." How? Through an even mind. You will have to feel pain, and thirst, and hunger, and old age too — if a longer stay among men is granted you. You will have to be sick, and lose things, and die. But don't believe the noisy crowd around you: none of these things is an evil, none is beyond your strength to bear, none is truly heavy. It's only common opinion that makes them frightening. Fearing death, then, is like fearing gossip — and what is more foolish than a man afraid of words? Our friend Demetrius puts it cleverly: "The talk of ignorant men is like the rumblings of the belly. What difference does it make to me," he says, "whether the noise comes from above or below?" What madness, to fear a bad reputation among people whose reputations are bad! Just as you had no cause to be terrified of what people say, you have no cause now to fear these things — things you would never have feared if their talk hadn't forced the fear on you. Does it harm a good man to be spattered with unjust gossip? Then don't let gossip damage death in our estimation either — for death has a bad reputation too. Yet not one of the people who slander death has ever tried it.

Meanwhile it is reckless to condemn what you know nothing about. This much, though, you do know: death is a help to many people. It sets many free from torture, from want, from sickness, from suffering, from weariness. Nothing has any power over us, once we have death in our own power.

Farewell.
