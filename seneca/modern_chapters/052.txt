Letters 120–121

Letter 120: More About Virtue

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Your letter wandered through several small problems but finally settled on one and asked me to explain it: how do we come to know the good and the honorable? Other schools treat these as two separate things; our school only distinguishes them. Here is what I mean. Some people believe the good is whatever is useful — so they hand the title to riches, horses, wine, even shoes. That is how cheap the good becomes for them, how low they let it stoop. The honorable, for them, is whatever squares with right conduct: caring dutifully for an aging father, helping a friend out of poverty, showing courage on campaign, giving prudent and balanced advice. We make the good and the honorable two things, but out of one substance: only the honorable can be good, and the honorable is always, necessarily, good. It would be superfluous to add the fine distinction between the two — I have drawn it so many times. But I will say this one thing: we count nothing as good that can be put to bad use by anybody. And you can see for yourself what bad uses many men make of their riches, their high positions, and their muscles.

Back, then, to your question: how did we first acquire knowledge of the good and the honorable? Nature could not teach us this directly. She gave us the seeds of knowledge, not knowledge itself. Some say we simply stumbled on it — but it is unbelievable that a vision of virtue ever presented itself to anyone by accident. Our view is that it came from inference and observation, from comparing acts that occurred again and again; our school holds that the good and the honorable were grasped by analogy. And since Latin scholars have granted the word "analogy" citizenship, I don't think it should be deported — I think it should be welcomed into the full citizenship it can justly claim. So I'll use the word, not as a tolerated resident but as an established one.

What is this analogy? Let me explain. We understood what health of the body was, and from that we deduced that there must also be a health of the mind. We knew bodily strength, and from that we inferred a sturdiness of soul. Certain kind acts, humane acts, brave acts had amazed us, and we began to admire them as if they were perfect. Underneath lay plenty of faults, hidden by the shine of a few conspicuous deeds — and to those we shut our eyes. Nature tells us to magnify whatever deserves praise, and everyone polishes fame beyond the truth. So from deeds like these we drew our picture of some enormous good. Fabricius refused King Pyrrhus's gold, judging it greater than a crown to be able to scorn a king's money. And the same Fabricius, when Pyrrhus's own physician promised to poison his master, warned Pyrrhus to watch out for the plot. One and the same man had the strength of will neither to be bought by gold nor to win by poison. So we admired him — a hero who could not be moved by the king's promises, or by promises made against the king; a man who held fast to his ideal; a man who (is anything harder?) waged war without sin, for he believed there were wrongs one could commit even against an enemy; a man who, in the extreme poverty he had made his glory, recoiled from taking riches just as he recoiled from using poison. "Live, Pyrrhus, thanks to me," he cried, "and be glad, for once, of the very thing you've been grieving over — that Fabricius cannot be bribed!"

Horatius Cocles blocked the narrow bridge alone and ordered his own retreat cut off, so that the enemy's way in would be destroyed with it; then he held off his attackers until the crash of the collapsing beams rang in his ears. When he looked back and saw that his country, through his own danger, was out of danger, he shouted: "Whoever wants to chase me, come this way!" — and plunged in headlong, taking as much care to come out of that churning channel with his weapons as to come out unhurt. He returned with the glory of his conquering arms intact, as safely as if he had walked back across the bridge.

These deeds and others like them showed us the face of virtue. Now let me add something that may surprise you: evil things have sometimes worn the look of what is honorable, and the best has shone out through its opposite. For there are, as you know, vices that live next door to virtues; even what is lost and degraded can resemble what is upright. The spendthrift passes himself off as a generous man — though it matters a great deal whether a man knows how to give or simply doesn't know how to keep. Believe me, my dear Lucilius, there are many men who don't give their money away; they throw it away. I don't call a man generous for being on bad terms with his own cash. Carelessness looks like ease, and recklessness looks like courage. This resemblance is what forced us to watch closely, and to tell apart things that look alike on the surface but are at war underneath. And in watching men whom some noble effort had made famous, we began to notice which of them had done a deed with high spirit and lofty impulse — and had done it exactly once. We saw one man brave in war and a coward in politics, bearing poverty with courage and disgrace with a hangdog face; we praised the deed, and we despised the man. We saw another man kind to his friends and restrained toward his enemies, running his public and private affairs with scrupulous devotion, patient wherever something had to be endured and prudent wherever something had to be done. We saw him giving with an open hand when payment was owed, and, when hard work was called for, digging in resolutely and lifting his body's weariness by sheer will. More than that: he was always the same man, consistent in everything he did, right not only by judgment but by habit so ingrained that he not only could act rightly but could not help acting rightly. In such a man we formed our conception: here perfect virtue exists.

We divided this perfect virtue into its parts. Desires had to be reined in, fears suppressed, right actions put in order, debts paid where owed — so we marked off self-control, courage, prudence, and justice, and assigned each its special job. Then how did we form our idea of virtue itself? From that man's order, his propriety, his steadfastness, the complete harmony of all his actions, and a greatness of soul that rose above everything. From there we derived our conception of the happy life — the life that flows along with a steady current, entirely under its own control. And how did we discover that? I'll tell you. That perfect man, the man who had attained virtue, never cursed his luck, never met what chance brought with a fallen face. Believing himself citizen and soldier of the universe, he accepted his labors as if they were orders. Whatever happened, he did not spurn it as some evil blown in on him by accident; he took it as an assignment. "Whatever this is," he said, "it is my lot. It's rough and it's hard — so let's get to work on it."

Necessarily, then, he showed himself a great man — he never groaned in evil days, never complained about his destiny. He gave many people a clear picture of what he was; he shone like a light in the dark and turned everyone's thoughts toward himself, because he was gentle and calm, and equally ready to obey the orders of men and of God. He had a perfect soul, developed to its utmost height — a soul that nothing outranks except the mind of God, from which a portion flows down even into this mortal chest of ours. And the chest is never more divine than when it reflects on its own mortality — when it understands that a man was born to finish his life, and that the body is not a permanent home but the soul's inn — and a short stay at that — to be left behind the moment you see you have become a burden to your host. The greatest proof, my dear Lucilius, that the soul comes down from somewhere higher is this: it finds its present quarters cramped and low, and it is not afraid to leave. For the man who remembers where he came from knows where he is going. Don't we see how many discomforts plague us, how badly this flesh of ours fits? We complain now about our heads, now about our digestion, now about our chests and throats. One day it's the nerves, another day the feet; then diarrhea, then a cold; sometimes too much blood, sometimes too little. Now this troubles us, now that, and we're told to move along — which is exactly what happens to people living in someone else's house.

And yet we, who were allotted these decaying bodies, still set eternity before our eyes. We reach in our hopes for the furthest stretch a human life can possibly be spun out to, satisfied with no income and no influence. Could anything be more shameless, more foolish? Nothing is enough for us — for us, who must die someday; who, in fact, are dying already. Every day we stand nearer the edge, and every hour shoves us toward the drop we must go over. Look how blind our minds are! What I keep calling the future is happening at this very minute — and a large part of it has already happened, for it consists of all the life we've lived. We're wrong to fear our last day: every day contributes just as much to death's account. The stumbling step doesn't cause the exhaustion; it announces it. The final hour reaches death; every hour approaches it. Death wears us away; it does not sweep us away.

For this reason the noble soul, conscious of its better nature, takes care to behave honorably and diligently at the post where it is stationed — yet it counts none of these surrounding things as its own. It uses them as a loan, the way a traveler passing through uses what he finds, hurrying on. When we see steadfastness like that in a man, how can we help sensing the image of an extraordinary nature — especially if, as I said, its consistency proved the greatness genuine? For it is consistency that lasts; what is false doesn't hold up. Some men are a Vatinius one day and a Cato the next. One moment Curius isn't stern enough for them, Fabricius isn't poor enough, Tubero isn't frugal enough or content with plain enough things; the next moment they're rivaling Licinus in wealth, Apicius at the table, Maecenas in soft living. The surest proof of a sick mind is wavering — this endless seesaw between pretending to virtue and loving vice.

  He'd keep two hundred slaves on hand — then ten.
  One hour his talk was kings and potentates
  and nothing short of greatness; the next hour:
  "A three-legged table, a dish of clean salt,
  and a coarse-woven gown to keep the cold out —
  that's all I ask." Yet hand him a cool million,
  that sparing, that contented man, and in
  five short days there'd be nothing in his purse.

The men I'm describing are of the stamp Horace sketches there — never the same man twice, never even resembling himself, so far does he swing between opposites. Did I say many men are like this? Almost all of them are. Everyone changes his plans and prayers by the day. Now he wants a wife, now a mistress; now he wants to reign, now he grovels lower than any slave; now he puffs himself up until everyone hates him, now he shrinks and flattens himself below the genuinely humble; now he flings money about, now he steals it. That is how a foolish mind gives itself away most clearly: it appears in one shape after another and is never like itself — and nothing, to my mind, is more shameful. Believe me, it is a great role — playing one man. Nobody but the wise man can play a single person; the rest of us keep changing masks. No man can wear one face in private and another in public for long. One moment you'll think us thrifty and serious; the next, wasteful and idle. We keep swapping characters, taking up a part opposite to the one we just threw off. So demand this of yourself: the character you assumed at the beginning, keep it to the final scene of life's drama. Make it possible for people to praise you — or, failing that, at least to recognize you. As things stand, a man can fairly ask about someone you saw only yesterday: "Who is this?" That is how great the change has been.

Farewell.

Letter 121: On Instinct in Animals

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You'll take me to court, I'm sure of it, when I lay out today's little problem — one we've already fumbled with long enough. You'll shout, yet again: "What does this have to do with character?" Shout away — but first let me name you some other defendants for your lawsuit, Posidonius and Archidemus; let them stand trial. Then I'll add this: whatever deals with character doesn't necessarily produce good character. A man needs one thing for his food, another for his exercise, another for his clothing, another for his instruction, another for his pleasure. All of it relates to his needs; not all of it makes him better. Character, likewise, is served by different things in different ways: some things correct it and keep it in line; others investigate its nature and where it comes from. And when I ask why Nature produced man, and why she set him above the other animals, do you suppose I've left the study of character behind me? Wrong. How are you to know what character is worth wanting unless you've discovered what suits man best — unless you've studied his nature? You can find out what you should do and what you should avoid only when you've learned what you owe to your own nature.

"What I want," you say, "is to learn how to desire less and fear less. Strip me of my unreasoning beliefs. Show me that this thing people call happiness is fickle and hollow — a word just one prefix away from unhappiness." I'll give you what you want: I'll cheer on your virtues and flog your vices. People may decide I'm overzealous and reckless on this point, but I will never stop hounding wickedness, bridling the wildest of the emotions, blunting the pleasures that end in pain, and shouting down men's prayers. Of course I will — for the greatest evils are exactly the things we've prayed for, and everything we've ever given thanks for has turned into something that needs consoling.

Meanwhile, allow me to chew thoroughly on some points that may seem, for the moment, rather far from the question at hand. We were once debating whether all animals have a feel for their own constitution — their basic makeup. The best proof that they do is the fitness and nimbleness of their movements: they handle themselves as though they'd been trained for it. Every creature is expert in its own line. The skilled workman handles his tools with an ease born of experience; the pilot knows how to steer his ship; the painter, quick of eye and hand, moves among the countless colors he has laid out for the portrait, back and forth between palette and canvas. In just the same way, an animal is agile in everything that involves the use of its body. We tend to marvel at skilled dancers, whose gestures fit every meaning and emotion of the piece, whose movements keep pace with the speed of the words. But what art gives the performer, nature gives the animal. No animal fumbles with its own limbs; none is at a loss for how to use its body. They do this from the moment of birth. They arrive in the world with the knowledge; they are born fully trained.

"But," people answer, "the reason animals use their limbs so deftly is that moving them wrongly would hurt. On your own school's theory, they're compelled — it's fear, not will, that steers them right." Wrong. Bodies driven by compulsion move slowly; motion that comes from within has snap to it. And the proof that fear of pain isn't the prompt is this: even when pain checks them, they keep struggling toward their natural movement. So the child who is working on standing, getting used to carrying his own weight, no sooner starts testing his strength than he falls — and gets up, in tears, again and again, until through painful practice he has drilled himself into what nature demands. And certain animals with hard shells, flipped onto their backs, twist and grope with their feet and rock from side to side until they right themselves. The tortoise on his back feels no pain; but he is restless for his natural position, and he does not stop heaving himself about until he stands on his feet again.

So all these animals have a consciousness of their own constitution, and that is why they manage their limbs so readily; and we have no better proof that they're born equipped with this knowledge than the fact that no animal is clumsy at using its own body. But here comes an objection: "According to you, a constitution is 'the ruling power of the soul standing in a certain relation to the body.' How is a baby supposed to comprehend anything that intricate and subtle — something I can barely explain even to you? Every living creature would have to be born a logician, just to understand a definition that's over the heads of most Roman citizens." A fair complaint — if I had said that animals understand the definition of a constitution, rather than their own constitution. Nature is easier to understand than to explain. That baby doesn't know what "constitution" is; he knows his own constitution. He doesn't know what "a living creature" is; he feels that he is one. And even that constitution of his he grasps only confusedly, sketchily, in the dark. We, too, know that we have souls; but what the soul is, where it sits, what it's like, where it comes from — that we do not know. And just as we are conscious of our own souls while knowing nothing of their nature or their address, so all animals are conscious of their own constitutions. They cannot help feeling it, because the very faculty they feel everything else with is the same one; they cannot help having a feel for the thing they obey, the thing that governs them. Every one of us understands that there is something that stirs his impulses; what it is, he doesn't know. He knows he has a drive in him; what it is or where it comes from, he doesn't know. In the same way, even children and animals are conscious of their ruling part — just not clearly, not in sharp outline.

"So you're claiming," says the objector, "that every living thing starts out adapted to its own constitution, and that man's constitution is a rational one — so man is attached to himself not merely as a living being but as a reasoning being? For man is dear to himself precisely in the part that makes him a man. Then how can a baby, who doesn't yet have reason, be adapted to a rational constitution?" But each age has its own constitution — one for the infant, another for the boy, another for the old man; each is adapted to the constitution it currently finds itself in. The infant is toothless: he is fitted to that condition. Then his teeth come in: he is fitted to that condition too. The plant that will become grain and fruit has one constitution when it is young and barely peeking over the furrow, another when it has gathered strength and stands on a stalk soft but sturdy enough to carry its weight, another still when it turns yellow, hardens in the ear, and promises the threshing floor — whatever constitution it comes into, it keeps it and conforms to it. Infancy, boyhood, youth, and old age are different stages; but I, who have been infant and boy and youth, am still the same I. So although each thing has a different constitution at different times, its adaptation to its constitution is the same. Nature doesn't assign boyhood or youth or old age to me; she assigns me to them. So the child is adapted to the constitution he has right now, in childhood — not to the one he'll have as a young man. Even if there is some higher phase waiting for him to grow into, the state he's born in is according to nature too. First and foremost, the living thing is adapted to itself — for there has to be a standard that everything else gets referred back to. I seek pleasure. For whom? Myself. So I am looking out for myself. I shrink from pain. On whose behalf? My own. So I am looking out for myself. If I gauge everything I do by my own welfare, then my own welfare comes before everything else. This exists in every living being — not grafted on, but inborn.

Nature raises her own offspring; she does not cast them off. And because the most dependable protection is the protection closest at hand, each of us has been entrusted to himself. That is why, as I've said in earlier letters, even young animals, fresh from the womb or the egg, know at once and on their own what will hurt them, and steer clear of what deals death. They even flinch at the shadow of a hawk gliding overhead.

No animal enters life free from the fear of death. "How," people ask, "can an animal at birth understand what is wholesome and what is deadly?" The first question is whether it understands, not how. And that it does understand is clear from this: even if you added understanding, the animals would act no more competently than they already do. Why doesn't the hen fear the peacock or the goose, yet run from the hawk — a far smaller bird, and one she has never even met? Why do chicks fear a cat and not a dog? Clearly these birds carry a foreknowledge of harm that rests on no experiments — they avoid the thing before they could possibly have experience of it. And so you won't put it down to chance: they don't shrink from other things you'd expect them to fear; they never once forget their watchfulness; and they all possess, equally, the knack of avoiding what would destroy them. Besides, their fear doesn't grow as their lives go on.

From this it's plain enough that they didn't reach this condition through experience; it comes from an inborn drive for self-preservation. What experience teaches is slow and uneven; what Nature hands down comes to everyone alike, and comes at once. But if you insist on an explanation, shall I tell you how it is that every living thing tries to understand what would harm it? It feels that it is made of flesh — and so it perceives what flesh can suffer: cutting, burning, crushing — and which animals are armed to inflict the damage. From these it forms its idea of the harmful and the hostile. The two tendencies are joined: every creature at one and the same time looks out for its own safety, reaching for what helps it and recoiling from what would hurt it. Impulse toward the useful and revulsion from its opposite are according to nature; without any reflection to prompt them, without any instruction, whatever Nature has prescribed gets done.

Don't you see how skillful bees are at building their cells — how completely harmonious in sharing and enduring the work? Don't you see the spider's web, too fine for any human hand to copy — what a task it is to arrange those threads, some running straight to the center to make the web solid, others circling round and thinning out, spread to tangle and net the smaller insects for whose ruin the web is hung? This art is born, not taught — and that is why no animal is more skilled than another. Look: every spiderweb is equally fine; every opening in a honeycomb is identical in shape. What art passes along is uncertain and uneven; what Nature deals out is always uniform. She has handed down nothing except the duty of self-care and the skill to carry it out — which is why living and learning begin at the same time. No wonder creatures are born with the one gift whose absence would make being born pointless. This is the first equipment Nature issued them for survival: attachment to themselves, and love of themselves. They could not have survived without wanting to. The wanting alone wouldn't have saved them; but without it, nothing could have. In no animal will you find contempt for itself, or even neglect of itself. Dumb beasts, sluggish at everything else, are clever at living. So you will see that creatures useless to everyone else never fail themselves.

Farewell.
