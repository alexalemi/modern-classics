Letters 23–25

Letter 23: On the True Joy Which Comes from Philosophy

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Do you expect me to write you about how gently winter treated us — a short season, and a mild one — or what a nasty spring we're having, with cold weather out of season, and all the other small talk people fall back on when they can't think of anything to say? No. I'll write you something that might actually help us both. And what could that be, if not a push toward soundness of mind? You ask what the foundation of a sound mind is? This: do not find your joy in worthless things. Foundation, did I say? It's the summit. You have reached the top when you know what it is that makes you glad, and when you have not handed your happiness over to things outside yourself. A man who is tugged forward by hope of anything — even something within reach, even something easy to get, even if his hopes have never once cheated him — is anxious and unsure of himself. Above all, my dear Lucilius, make this your business: learn how to feel joy.

Do you think I'm robbing you of pleasures when I clear away Fortune's gifts, when I advise you to steer wide of hope, the sweetest thing that warms our hearts? Just the opposite: I don't want you ever to be without gladness. I want it born in your own house — and it is born there, if only it is born inside you. Every other kind of cheer fails to fill the heart; it only smooths the forehead, and it never lasts. Unless, of course, you think a man has joy just because he's laughing. The soul itself must be happy and confident, lifted above everything that can happen to it.

Real joy, believe me, is a serious business. Do you imagine anyone can look death in the face with a careless expression — "bright and breezy," as our young dandies like to put it? Or throw the door open to poverty that way, or keep the reins on his pleasures, or rehearse the endurance of pain? The man who turns these things over in his heart is full of joy — but not a giggling joy. That is exactly the joy I want you to own, because once you have found its source, it will never run dry. Shallow mines give up their yield right at the surface; the truly rich ones hide their veins deep, and they pay out more and more to the miner who keeps digging. The trinkets that delight the crowd give only a thin pleasure, a coat of shine — and any joy that is merely plated has nothing underneath. The joy I'm talking about, the joy I'm trying to lead you to, is solid all the way through, and it opens up wider the deeper you go into it. So do the one thing, dearest Lucilius, that can make you truly happy: throw away and trample underfoot everything that merely glitters, everything dangled in front of you by someone else or obtainable from someone else. Look toward the true good, and take joy only in what comes from your own store. And what do I mean by "your own store"? I mean your very self — the best part of you. Even the frail body, though we can do nothing without it, should be treated as necessary rather than important. It drags us into empty pleasures — brief, soon regretted, and, unless they are reined in by strict self-control, ready to flip into their opposites. I mean exactly this: pleasure, unless it is kept within bounds, goes plunging straight over the edge into sorrow.

But keeping within bounds is hard with anything you believe to be good. Only the real good can be craved safely. What is this real good, you ask, and where does it come from? I'll tell you: it comes from a good conscience, from honorable intentions, from right actions, from contempt for Fortune's gifts, from a calm and even way of living that walks a single road. People who leap from one plan to another — or who don't even leap, but get carried across by sheer accident — how can such wavering, drifting people hold on to any good that is fixed and lasting? Only a few steer themselves and their affairs by a guiding purpose. The rest don't move forward at all; they are simply swept along, like debris floating in a river. Some are caught by slack water and carried gently; some are torn along by a stronger current; some are stranded near the bank as the flow gives out; and some are flushed out to sea by the rush of the stream. So: decide what you want, and stand by the decision.

Now it's time to pay my debt. Here is a saying of your friend Epicurus that will clear this letter's account: "It is a nuisance to be always beginning life." Or another, which may catch the meaning better: "They live badly who are always beginning to live." Why? — you're right to ask; the line needs a commentary. Because for such people life is always unfinished. A man cannot stand ready for death if he has only just started to live. We should aim to have already lived enough — and no one thinks he has, if he's still drawing up the blueprints for his life. Don't imagine such people are rare; practically everyone is like this. Some men, in fact, only begin to live when it's time to stop. And if that surprises you, here is something that will surprise you more: some men have stopped living before they ever began.

Farewell.

Letter 24: On Despising Death

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You write that you're anxious about the outcome of a lawsuit an angry opponent is holding over you, and you expect me to advise you to picture a happier ending and rest in the comforts of hope. After all, why summon trouble early — it must be endured soon enough once it arrives — why ruin the present out of fear of the future? It is certainly foolish to be miserable now because you may be miserable later. But I'm going to lead you to peace of mind by a different road: if you want to be rid of all worry, assume that whatever you're afraid might happen is certainly going to happen. Whatever the trouble is, take its measure in your own mind and size up your fear. You will soon see that what you dread is either small or short. And you won't need long to gather examples to steady you; every age has produced them. Turn your thoughts to any period of Roman or foreign history, and cases of great achievement and great endurance will come crowding in on you.

If you lose this case, can anything worse happen to you than exile or prison? Is there any fate a man can dread beyond being burned or being killed? Name those penalties one at a time, and then name the men who shrugged them off — you won't have to hunt for them; the only problem is choosing. Rutilius bore his conviction as if the one thing that bothered him was the injustice of the verdict. Metellus endured exile with courage; Rutilius endured it gladly — the first agreed to come back only because his country called him; the second refused to return when Sulla summoned him. And nobody, in those days, said no to Sulla. Socrates went on holding his conversations in prison, and when certain people offered him the chance to escape, he declined; he stayed, in order to free mankind from its two heaviest fears — death and imprisonment. Mucius put his own hand into the fire. Being burned is painful; how much more painful when you are the one doing it to yourself! Here was a man with no learning, not braced for death and pain by any words of wisdom, equipped with nothing but a soldier's courage, punishing himself for the failure of his daring. He stood and watched his own right hand coming apart on the enemy's brazier, and he did not pull back the dissolving limb, its bones laid bare, until his enemy took the fire away. He could have done something more successful in that camp; he could never have done anything braver. See how much keener a brave man is to seize danger than a cruel man is to inflict it: Porsenna was readier to pardon Mucius for trying to kill him than Mucius was to pardon himself for failing.

"Oh," you say, "those stories have been recited to death in every school. The minute you reach the topic 'On Despising Death,' you'll be telling me about Cato." And why shouldn't I tell you about Cato — how on that last glorious night he read Plato's book with a sword laid at his pillow? He had arranged for the two things he needed at the end: the first, the will to die; the second, the means. So he put his affairs in order — as far as anything ruined and near its end can be put in order — and decided he had to make sure that no one would have the power to kill Cato, and no one the luck to save him. Drawing the sword — which he had kept clean of blood until that final day — he cried: "Fortune, you have gained nothing by blocking everything I attempted. Until now I have fought for my country's freedom, not my own. I did not labor so stubbornly to be free myself, but to live among free men. Now, since the cause of mankind is past hope, let Cato be moved to safety." Then he dealt his body the fatal wound. The doctors bound it up; Cato had less blood and less strength, but no less courage. Angry now not only at Caesar but at himself, he set his bare hands against the wound — and that noble soul, which had defied every power on earth, he did not so much release as expel.

I am not piling up these stories to exercise my wit; I'm doing it to brace you against the thing that is thought most terrible. And the job will be all the easier when I show you that it isn't only heroes who have despised the moment when the soul breathes its last; some men who were cowards in everything else have matched the bravest here. Take Scipio, the father-in-law of Gnaeus Pompeius: driven back onto the African coast by a headwind, he saw his ship in enemy hands. He ran himself through with his sword, and when they asked where the commander was, he answered: "All is well with the commander." Those words lifted him to the level of his ancestors, and kept unbroken the glory that fate had granted the Scipios in Africa. It was a great thing to conquer Carthage; a greater thing to conquer death. "All is well with the commander!" Should a general die any other way — above all, one of Cato's generals? I won't send you back through history, collecting all the men across the ages who have despised death; there are too many. Look at our own times — the softness and over-refinement we're always complaining about — and you will still find people of every rank, every station, every age, who cut their misfortunes short by death.

Believe me, Lucilius: death is so far from being fearful that, thanks to it, nothing is fearful. So when your enemy threatens, listen calmly. Your conscience gives you confidence — but since many things outside the merits of a case carry weight, hope for perfect justice, and prepare yourself for perfect injustice. Above all, remember to strip things of the noise and confusion that surround them, and look at what each thing is at bottom. You will find that there is nothing frightening in them except the fear itself. What you see happening with little boys happens to us too — we are only slightly bigger boys. When the people they love, the people they live with and play with, put on masks, the boys are scared out of their wits. It's the mask we need to strip off — not just from people, but from things — and give each thing back its own face.

"Why do you hold up before my eyes swords and fires and a mob of executioners roaring around you? Drop the parade you hide behind to frighten fools! You are nothing but Death — the same Death that a manservant of mine and a maidservant despised only yesterday. Why do you spread out the whips and the rack again in all that grand display? Why the engines built to torture each joint of the body one by one, and the thousand other machines for taking a man apart piecemeal? Put away the stage props that numb us with terror. And you — silence the groans, the cries, the bitter shrieks torn out of a man as he's stretched on the rack! You are nothing but Pain — the pain that gout-ridden man over there scorns, that the invalid endures in the middle of his dinner parties, that a girl bears in childbirth. If I can bear you, you are slight; if I cannot bear you, you are short."

Turn these words over — you have heard them often and said them often. But now prove by the outcome that what you have heard and said is true. For there is an ugly charge that keeps getting leveled at our school: that we deal in the words of philosophy, not its deeds.

What — have you only just now learned that death hangs over your head, that exile does, that grief does? You were born to these risks. Let us treat everything that can happen as something that will happen. I know you have already done what I'm advising; now I add a warning: don't drown your soul in these petty anxieties of yours. If you do, it will go dull, and it will have too little strength left when the moment comes for it to rise. Turn your mind from your own case to the case of men in general. Tell yourself that our little bodies are mortal and fragile; pain can reach them from other directions than an enemy's wrong or a stronger man's power. Our pleasures themselves turn into torments: banquets bring indigestion; drinking bouts bring numbness and trembling; the habits of lust cripple the feet, the hands, every joint of the body.

I may become poor: then I'll be one among many. I may be exiled: then I'll count myself a native of wherever they send me. They may put me in chains. So what? Am I unchained now? Look at this dragging weight of a body that nature has shackled me to. "I shall die," you say. What you mean is: I shall stop being able to fall sick; I shall stop being able to be imprisoned; I shall stop being able to die. I'm not foolish enough to run through the old Epicurean refrain at this point — that the terrors of the underworld are empty, that Ixion is not spinning on his wheel, that Sisyphus is not shouldering his boulder uphill, that no man's entrails can grow back every day to be devoured again. Nobody is childish enough to fear Cerberus, or the shadows, or the ghost-costume of the dead, held together by nothing but bare bones. Death either annihilates us or strips us bare. If we are released, the better part of us remains, its burden lifted; if we are annihilated, nothing remains — good and bad alike are carried off.

Allow me to quote a verse of your own here — first reminding you that when you wrote it, you wrote it for yourself as much as for anyone else. It is shameful to say one thing and mean another; how much more shameful to write one thing and mean another! I remember the day you were handling that familiar theme: that we don't suddenly fall into death, but move toward it by inches. We die every day. For every day a little of our life is taken from us; even while we are growing, our life is shrinking. We lose infancy, then childhood, then youth. Count yesterday too: all the time that has passed is time lost; this very day we're spending, we split with death. It is not the last drop that empties the water-clock, but all the water that flowed out before. In the same way, the final hour, when we cease to exist, does not produce death all by itself; it only completes it. That is the moment we arrive — but we have been on the road a long time. When you had laid all this out, you said, in your usual style (you're always impressive, but never sharper than when you fit the truth with the right words):

  The death that comes for us is not one death:
  the death that takes us off is just the last.

I'd rather you read your own lines than my letter; they will make it plain to you that this death we fear is the last death, not the only one.

I can see what you're looking for: you're asking what I've packed into this letter — what bracing saying from some master mind, what useful rule. Since the subject at hand is the one we've been discussing, I'll send you something on that very theme. Epicurus scolds those who crave death as much as those who dread it: "It is absurd," he says, "to run toward death because you are tired of life, when it is your way of life that has made you run toward death." And in another passage: "What is so absurd as to seek death, when it is fear of death that has robbed your life of peace?" And you may add a third of the same stamp: "So great is men's thoughtlessness — their madness — that some are driven to death by the very fear of it."

Ponder any of these, and you will strengthen your mind to endure both death and life. For we need warning and toughening on both fronts — against loving life too much, and against hating it too much. Even when reason advises ending it, the impulse should not be obeyed rashly or at a run. A brave and wise man should not flee from life; he should make a proper exit. And above all he should avoid the weakness that has taken hold of so many: a lust for dying. For the mind has its unreasoning drifts, my dear Lucilius, and one of them tilts toward death. It seizes the noblest and most spirited men, and it seizes the abject and cowardly too: the first kind despise life; the second find it a burden.

Others slide into it out of sheer over-fullness — from doing and seeing the same things until it isn't that they hate life; they are simply glutted with it. We slip toward this mood with philosophy itself nudging us along, and we say: "How long the same things? I will wake and sleep, get hungry and get full, shiver and sweat. Nothing ends; everything is linked in a kind of circle — things flee and are chased. Night presses at the heels of day, day at the heels of night; summer ends in autumn, winter crowds hard behind autumn, winter softens into spring. Nothing in nature passes except to come back. I do nothing new; I see nothing new. Sooner or later a man gets sick even of this." There are many who feel that living is not painful — merely pointless.

Farewell.

Letter 25: On Reformation

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

As for our two friends, we'll have to proceed along different lines: the faults of the one are to be corrected; the faults of the other, stamped out. I intend to take every liberty — I don't love a man if I'm unwilling to hurt his feelings. "What," you say, "do you expect to keep a forty-year-old under your tutelage? Consider his age — hardened by now, past kneading. You can't reshape a man like that; only young minds take the mold." I don't know whether I'll make progress; but I would rather fall short of success than fall short of faith. Don't give up on curing the sick even when the disease is chronic — provided you stand firm against their excesses and make them do, and put up with, plenty of things against their will. As for our other friend, I'm not especially confident either — except for one thing: he still has enough shame left to blush at his sins. That modesty should be nursed along; as long as it lives in his soul, there is room for hope. But this veteran of yours, I think, wants more careful handling, so that he doesn't give up on himself altogether. And there is no better moment to approach him than now, while he's in a quiet stretch and looks like a man who has mended his ways. That lull of virtue has fooled others; it doesn't fool me. I fully expect his faults to come back — with compound interest. For the moment, I'm certain, they are suspended, not gone. I'll give the matter some time, and see whether or not something can be done.

But you — show me, as in fact you are doing, that you have a stout heart. Lighten your baggage for the march. Nothing we own is essential. Let us return to nature's law, and riches lie ready for us there. The things we actually need are free for everyone, or cheap: nature craves only bread and water. Nobody is poor by that standard — and the man who has fenced his desires within those limits can go head-to-head with Jupiter himself for happiness, as Epicurus says. And I must fold one or two more of his sayings into this letter: "Do everything as if Epicurus were watching you." There's no question that it does you good to have posted a guardian over yourself — to have someone you can look up to, someone you count as a witness to your very thoughts. It is nobler still, of course, to live as if some good man were always at your side, watching everything you do; but I'll settle for this much: whatever you do, act as if anyone at all were looking on. Solitude is what talks us into every kind of wrong. When you have come far enough to have some respect for yourself as well, you may dismiss your attendant. Until then, set some man's authority as a guard over you — the great Cato, or Scipio, or Laelius — anyone whose presence would make even hopeless wrecks swallow their bad impulses — while you go on making yourself into the kind of man in whose company you wouldn't dare to sin. When that is accomplished, and you begin to hold yourself in some esteem, I'll gradually allow you what Epicurus suggests in another passage: "The time to withdraw into yourself is exactly when you are forced into a crowd."

You need to make yourself different from the multitude. But until it is safe for you to be alone, pick out particular individuals; everyone does better in somebody's company — anybody's — than alone in his own. "The time to withdraw into yourself is exactly when you are forced into a crowd." Yes — provided you are a good man, a calm man, a self-restrained one. Otherwise, you had better withdraw into a crowd to get away from yourself. Alone, you are keeping bad company.

Farewell.
