Letters 34–40

Letter 34: On a Promising Pupil

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I swell with pride, I jump for joy, I shake off my years and my blood runs warm again, every time your actions and your letters show me how far you have outdone yourself — the ordinary man you left behind long ago. If a farmer is delighted when his tree finally bears fruit, if a shepherd takes pleasure in the new lambs of his flock, if every teacher looks at his student and sees his own youth again — then how do you think it feels to have trained a mind, to have shaped it while it was still young, and suddenly to see it grown up?

I claim you as my own. You are my handiwork. When I first saw what you were capable of, I laid my hand on you. I urged you, I used the goad, I never let you slow to a stroll — I kept prodding you awake. I am still doing it; only now I am cheering on a runner who is already in the race, and who cheers me on in return.

"What more do you want from me, then?" you ask. "The will is still my own." Well, the will in this case is nearly everything — not merely half, as in the proverb "Well begun is half done." It is more than half, because this whole business is decided in the soul. So the greater part of goodness is the will to become good. Do you know what I mean by a good man? One who is complete, finished — a man no pressure and no need can make bad. I see that man in you, if you keep on steadily, bend to the work, and see to it that everything you do and everything you say match each other, answer to each other, and come stamped from the same mold. If a man's acts are out of harmony, his soul is crooked.
Farewell.

Letter 35: On the Friendship of Kindred Minds

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

When I push you so hard toward your studies, I am serving my own interest: I want your friendship, and I cannot have it unless you keep developing yourself the way you have begun. Right now you love me, but you are not yet my friend. "Wait," you say, "are those two different things?" Different? They are not even alike. A friend loves you, of course; but someone who loves you is not automatically your friend. Friendship always helps; love sometimes does harm. If for no other reason, then, perfect yourself for this one: so that you may learn how to love.

Hurry, then — so that while you are perfecting yourself for my benefit, you don't end up having learned perfection for somebody else's. True, I am already collecting some of the profit just by imagining that we two will be of one mind, and that whatever strength age has drained from me will flow back to me out of yours — though there really isn't so much difference in our ages. But I want to enjoy the finished fact. We feel a certain joy in the people we love even when they are far away, but that kind of joy is thin and fleeting. Seeing a man, being with him, talking with him — that is living pleasure; above all when you not only see the man you want, but the kind of man you want. So give yourself to me as a gift of great price. And to spur yourself on, remember that you are mortal and that I am old. Hurry to me — but hurry to yourself first. Make progress, and above all work at being consistent with yourself. Whenever you want to test whether you have accomplished anything, check whether you want the same things today that you wanted yesterday. A change of wishes shows a mind at sea, blown here and there, wherever the wind sets. What is settled and solid does not wander from its place. That is the blessed condition of the completely wise man — and, up to a point, of the man who is making progress too. What is the difference between them? The one moves but never changes position: he bobs at anchor where he is. The other does not move at all.
Farewell.

Letter 36: On the Value of Retirement

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Encourage your friend to look down, with a stout heart, on the people who criticize him for choosing the shade of retirement — for walking away from a career of public honors and preferring peace to everything he might still have won. Let him show these critics, every day, how wisely he has looked out for his own interests. The men everyone envies will keep marching past him; some will be shoved out of the ranks, and others will fall. Prosperity is a turbulent thing; it torments itself. It rattles the brain in more ways than one, goading men toward this aim and that — power for some, high living for others. Some it puffs up; others it slackens until they go completely soft.

"But," comes the retort, "so-and-so carries his prosperity well." Yes — the way he carries his liquor. So don't let that crowd persuade you that a man besieged by admirers is happy. They run to him the way crowds rush a pool of water, muddying it as they drain it. "But," you say, "men call our friend an idler and a slacker." There are people, you know, whose speech comes out backwards — they use words to mean their opposites. They used to call him happy; so what? Was he? It doesn't even bother me that some find him rough and gloomy. Aristo used to say he preferred a stern young man to a cheerful one who pleased the crowd. "Wine that tastes harsh and sour when it is new," he said, "ages into good wine; the wine that tastes fine at the vintage can't survive the years." So let them call him stern, an enemy of his own advancement. That very sternness will age well — provided he keeps cherishing virtue and keeps soaking in the studies that genuinely cultivate a man: not the kind you sprinkle over yourself, but the kind the mind must be steeped in. Now is his time to learn. "What? Is there any time when a man should not learn?" None at all. But while studying is creditable at every age, being schooled is not. An old man learning his ABC's is a disgraceful and ridiculous sight; the young man must store up, the old man must draw out and use. So you will be doing yourself an enormous favor by making this friend of yours as good a man as possible. The kindnesses worth seeking and worth giving, they tell us, are the ones that benefit the giver as much as the receiver — and those are unquestionably the best kind.

Besides, he no longer has any choice in the matter: he has given his word. And it is less disgraceful to settle short with a creditor than to settle short with a promising future. To pay his debt of money, the merchant needs a lucky voyage, the farmer needs fertile fields and kindly weather; the debt your friend owes can be paid in full with nothing but good will. Fortune has no jurisdiction over character. Let him govern his character so that, in perfect peace, he can bring to perfection the spirit within him that feels neither loss nor gain, but stays in the same attitude no matter how things fall out. Heap worldly goods on a spirit like that, and it rises above its wealth; let chance strip away part of that wealth, or all of it, and the spirit is not diminished.

If your friend had been born in Parthia, he would have begun bending the bow as a child; in Germany, he would have been hurling his slender spear from boyhood; in our great-grandfathers' day, he would have learned to ride a horse and strike an enemy hand to hand. That is what each nation's way of life recommends to a man — no, prescribes for him. So what should this friend of yours train at? The one skill that works against every weapon and every kind of enemy: contempt of death. Nobody doubts that death carries something terrifying in it; it shocks even our souls, which nature has shaped to love their own existence. Otherwise there would be no need to prepare ourselves and whet our courage for something we would walk toward by a kind of instinct, the way every creature moves to preserve itself. No man trains so that he can lie down calmly on a bed of roses if the need ever arises. He hardens his courage for this: so that torture cannot make him break his sworn word, and so that, if it comes to it, he can stand his watch in the trenches, wounded, without even leaning on his spear — because sleep steals over a man who props himself up on anything at all.

In death there is nothing harmful — for there would have to be someone left for it to harm. And if you are gripped by such a craving for a longer life, consider this: nothing that vanishes from our sight is annihilated. It is reabsorbed into the world of things it came from — and will soon come from again. Things end their course; they do not perish. And death, which we fear and flinch from, interrupts life; it does not steal it. The day will come when we are returned to the light — though many men would object to that, if they were not brought back with their memories wiped clean.

Later I mean to show you, more carefully, that everything which seems to perish is merely changed. Since you are destined to return, you ought to leave with a calm mind. Watch how the universe circles back on itself: you will see that no star in our sky is snuffed out; they all set and rise in their turns. Summer has gone, but another year will bring it back; winter lies low, but its own proper months will restore it; night has swallowed the sun, but day will soon chase night away again. The wandering stars retrace their old paths; one part of the sky is forever rising, another forever sinking. One thing more, and then I'll stop: infants, and small boys, and the insane have no fear of death — and it is deeply shameful if reason cannot give us the peace of mind their foolishness has given them.
Farewell.

Letter 37: On Allegiance to Virtue

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You have promised to be a good man; you have enlisted under oath — and that oath is the strongest chain that can bind you to a sound mind. Anyone who tells you this is a soft, easy kind of soldiering is mocking you, and I won't have you deceived. The words of this most honorable contract are the same as the words of the most disgraceful one — the gladiator's: "to be burned, to be bound, to be killed by the sword." From the men who rent their strength to the arena, who eat and drink what they must pay for in blood, a bond is taken that they will endure all this even against their will. From you the bond is that you will endure it willingly, even gladly. The gladiator may lower his weapon and appeal to the crowd for mercy; you will neither lower your weapon nor beg for your life. You must die on your feet, unbeaten. And anyway, what would you gain — a few days, a few years? From the moment we are born, there is no discharge.

"Then how do I get free?" you ask. You cannot escape necessity, but you can conquer it.

  Force makes a way.

And philosophy will give you that force. Turn to philosophy, then, if you want to be safe, untroubled, happy — in short, and this matters most, free. There is no other road to it. Folly is low, abject, mean, slavish — thrown open to a whole pack of the cruelest passions. And those passions, savage taskmasters that rule you sometimes by turns and sometimes all at once, can be driven out by wisdom, which is the only real freedom. There is just one path there, and it runs straight; you will not lose your way. Walk with a steady step. If you want everything under your control, put yourself under the control of reason; if reason rules you, you will rule much else. From reason you will learn what to attempt and how to go about it; you will stop blundering into things. Show me one man who can tell you how he began to crave what he craves. Nobody arrived there by planning; he was shoved there by impulse. Fortune attacks us as often as we attack Fortune. It is disgraceful to be carried along instead of walking — to find yourself suddenly in the middle of a whirlpool of events, asking, dazed: "How did I get here?"
Farewell.

Letter 38: On Quiet Conversation

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You are right to insist that we step up our exchange of letters. But the greatest benefit comes from conversation, because it creeps into the soul a little at a time. Prepared lectures, declaimed in front of a crowd, make more noise and less intimacy. Philosophy is good advice, and no one gives advice at the top of his lungs. Sometimes, granted, we need those harangues — if I may call them that — when a doubter needs a shove. But when the aim is not to make a man want to learn but actually to make him learn, we must come down to the low tones of conversation. Quiet words enter more easily and stay in the memory. We do not need many words; we need effective ones.

Words should be scattered like seed. However small a seed may be, once it finds favorable ground it unfolds its strength, and from almost nothing it spreads to its fullest growth. Reason works the same way: it does not look large from the outside, but it grows as it works. Few words are spoken; but if the mind truly catches them, they gather strength and shoot up. Yes, precepts are like seeds: they produce much, and they are slight things. Only — as I said — a favorable mind must receive them and take them in. Then the mind will produce abundantly in its turn, giving back more than it received.
Farewell.

Letter 39: On Noble Aspirations

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I will indeed put together the notes you ask for, carefully ordered and tightly packed. But consider whether the usual course might not help you more than what people now call a "breviary" — in the good old days, when we still spoke real Latin, it was called a "summary." The full course is what a learner needs; the digest is for the man who already knows. One teaches; the other jogs the memory. But I'll give you plenty of both. And a man like you should not be asking me for this authority or that; the man who has to produce a voucher for every statement admits he is a nobody. So I will write exactly what you want, but in my own way; until then, you have plenty of authors whose works will keep your thinking in order well enough. Pick up the list of the philosophers. That act alone will wake you up, once you see how many men have been working for your benefit. You will burn to be one of them yourself. For this is the finest quality the noble soul carries within it: it can be roused toward honorable things.

No man of high gifts is content with what is low and mean; the vision of great achievement calls him and lifts him up. Just as flame leaps straight into the air and can no more be boxed in or held down than it can hold still, so our soul is always in motion — and the more fire it has, the livelier its motion. But happy is the man who has aimed that momentum at better things! He puts himself beyond the jurisdiction of chance. He will handle prosperity wisely, shrink adversity, and look down on what other men gawk at. It is the mark of a great soul to scorn great things and prefer the moderate to the excessive; the moderate is useful and life-giving, while the excessive does harm precisely by being too much. Too rich a soil lays the grain flat; branches snap under too heavy a load; an over-bearing tree never ripens its fruit. The same happens to souls: unchecked prosperity wrecks them, and they use it to injure not only others but themselves. What enemy was ever as insolent to a foe as some men's pleasures are to them? The only excuse we can allow for their lack of self-control, their mad lusts, is that they suffer the very evils they inflicted on others. And the madness torments them justly, because desire that oversteps nature's mean must run off into limitless space. Nature's mean has boundaries; waywardness, and the acts born of willful lust, have none. Utility measures what you need — but by what yardstick do you measure the superfluous? So men sink into their pleasures, and once accustomed to them they cannot do without them; and they are most wretched of all for this reason: they have reached the point where what was once superfluous has become indispensable. They are the slaves of their pleasures, not the enjoyers of them. They even love their own ills — and that is the worst ill of all. Unhappiness hits bottom when shameful things not only attract a man but delight him, and there is no room left for a cure, because what used to be vices have become habits.
Farewell.

Letter 40: On the Proper Style for a Philosopher's Discourse

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Thank you for writing to me so often; you are showing me your real self in the only way you can. I never receive a letter from you without instantly being in your company. If portraits of absent friends give us pleasure — though they only refresh the memory and offer a false, hollow comfort for our missing them — how much more pleasant is a letter, which carries real traces, real marks, of an absent friend! The sweetest thing about meeting face to face is there in the print of a friend's hand on his letter: recognition.

You write that you heard the philosopher Serapio lecture when his ship put in at your town. "He likes to haul his words up in one great rush," you say. "He doesn't let them flow one at a time; he drives them on so they crowd and trample each other. More words come than any single voice can carry." I do not approve of this in a philosopher. A philosopher's speech, like his life, should be composed — and nothing that rushes headlong in a hurry is well ordered. That is why in Homer the rapid style, sweeping down without a break like a snow-squall, is given to the younger speaker, while from the old man eloquence flows gently, sweeter than honey.

So mark my words: that torrential manner of speaking, fast and copious, suits a sidewalk showman better than a man discussing and teaching a serious, important subject. But I object just as strongly to dripping the words out as to racing them: a speaker should neither keep the ear straining nor bury it. The starved, thin-spun style also loses the audience, worn out by its stammering slowness — though it is true that a word we have waited for sinks in more easily than a word that flits past on the wing. Besides, people speak of "handing down" precepts to a pupil; but you are not handing down what will not stay in the hand. And there is this: speech that serves the truth should be plain and unadorned. This crowd-pleasing style has nothing to do with truth. It wants to stampede the mob, to sweep away careless ears by sheer momentum; it doesn't offer itself for examination — it snatches itself out of reach. How can speech govern others when it cannot govern itself? And may I add that speech meant to heal our minds has to sink down into us? Remedies do no good unless they stay in the system.

Anyway, this kind of speech is mostly hollow: more sound than power. My terrors need to be calmed, my irritations soothed, my illusions shaken off, my indulgences reined in, my greed rebuked. Which of those cures can be done in a hurry? What doctor heals his patient on a flying visit? Can I even say that this roaring jumble of ill-chosen words gives pleasure? No. Just as with tricks you would have sworn were impossible — finding out once how they're done is enough — so with these word-acrobats: to have heard them once is plenty. What would anyone want to learn or imitate there? What should you conclude about a man's soul when his speech charges out in total disorder and cannot be reined in? When you run downhill, you cannot stop where you meant to stop; your body's momentum carries your steps past the place you chose. It is the same here: this speed of speech has no control over itself, and it is unworthy of philosophy — for philosophy should place her words carefully, not fling them, and should move one step at a time.

"What then?" you say. "Shouldn't philosophy sometimes raise her voice?" Of course she should — but let her keep her dignity of character, and that is exactly what violent, excessive force strips away. Let philosophy have great strength, but strength held well under control; let her be a stream that never stops flowing — never a torrent. I would hardly allow even a courtroom orator a speed like this, which cannot be called back and plows ahead lawlessly. How could the jurors follow it — men who are often inexperienced and untrained? Even when an orator is carried away by the urge to show off, or by real emotion, even then he should not quicken his pace and pile up words beyond what the ear can bear.

You will be acting rightly, then, if you pay no attention to the men who care how much they can say rather than how well — and if, forced to choose, you choose to speak like Publius Vinicius, the stammerer. When Asellius was asked how Vinicius spoke, he answered: "Gradually." (Geminus Varius put it this way: "I don't see how you can call that man eloquent — he can't get three words out in a row.") Well, why shouldn't you choose to speak the way Vinicius does? Some joker may cross your path, of course, like the man who — while Vinicius was hauling his words out one at a time, as if he were dictating rather than speaking — called out: "Say, haven't you got anything to say?" And yet that is the better fault to have; for the rush of Quintus Haterius, the most famous orator of his age, is exactly what a sensible man should avoid. Haterius never hesitated, never paused; he started once, and he stopped once.

I suppose, too, that some styles suit some nations more, some less. In a Greek you can put up with the unbridled style; we Romans have gotten into the habit of spacing out our words even when we write. And our own Cicero, the man with whom Roman oratory sprang into prominence, was a slow pacer too. The Roman tongue is more inclined to take stock of itself — to weigh, and to offer something worth weighing. Fabianus, a man notable for his life, for his learning, and — third and least — for his eloquence, used to discuss a subject with dispatch rather than haste; you would call it ease rather than speed. That quality I approve in the wise man, though I do not demand it: let his speech simply come out unhampered — though I would rather it were delivered deliberately than spouted.

But here is one more reason to frighten you away from the disease of speed: you could only master that style by losing your sense of shame. You would have to rub all the modesty off your face and refuse to hear yourself speak. That heedless flood will carry along plenty of expressions you would wish you could call back; and, I repeat, you cannot reach that pace and keep your self-respect. Besides, you would need to practice every day and shift your attention from matter to words. And even if the words came readily and flowed without any effort of yours, they would still have to be kept on the leash. A modest walk suits a philosopher; so does restrained speech, far removed from boldness. So when everything is boiled down, the kernel of my advice is this: I bid you be slow of speech.
Farewell.
