Letters 103–105

Letter 103: On the Dangers of Association with Our Fellow-men

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Why are you scanning the horizon for troubles that might come your way — and might just as easily never come at all? I mean fires, collapsing buildings, the kind of accidents that simply happen to us rather than plot against us. Watch out instead for the troubles that dog our steps and reach out to grab us. Accidents can be serious, but they're rare — a shipwreck, a carriage overturning. It is from man that man's daily peril comes. Arm yourself against that; keep your eye fixed on that. No evil is more common, none more persistent, none better at slipping past your guard. Even a storm gives warning before it breaks; houses creak before they crash; smoke announces the fire. But damage from a human being is instantaneous — and the closer it comes, the more carefully it is hidden.

You're wrong to trust the faces of the people you meet. They have the look of men and the souls of wild animals — except that with animals, only the first encounter is dangerous; they don't chase what they've already passed by. Nothing drives them to hurt you except necessity: hunger or fear forces them into a fight. But man delights in ruining man.

Still, think about the danger you face from man in order to work out the duty you owe as a man. Deal with others so as to do no harm, and you're less likely to be harmed. Share in everyone's joys, feel for them in their troubles, and remember what you should offer and what you should hold back. What will you gain by living this way? Not necessarily safety from their hands — but at least safety from their deceit. And as far as you can, take refuge in philosophy. She will hold you close, and in her sanctuary you will be safe — or at any rate safer than before. People only collide when they're traveling the same road. But you must never flaunt this philosophy of yours; worn with insolence and arrogance, it has been the ruin of many. Let philosophy strip away your faults, not sharpen your tongue for the faults of others. Don't let her stand aloof from the ordinary customs of mankind, or make it her business to condemn everything she herself doesn't do. A man can be wise without parading it, and without making enemies.

Farewell.

Letter 104: On Care of Health and Peace of Mind

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I've run off to my villa at Nomentum — fleeing what, do you suppose? The city? No: a fever that was quietly working its way into my system. It already had a grip on me. My doctor kept insisting that when the pulse is upset and irregular, throwing off its natural rhythm, the disease is under way. So I ordered the carriage brought around at once and insisted on leaving, over the protests of my wife Paulina, who tried to stop me. I remembered what my master Gallio said when a fever came over him in Achaia and he boarded a ship immediately: the disease, he insisted, was in the place, not in his body. That's what I told my dear Paulina, who is always urging me to take care of my health. I know her very life-breath comes and goes with mine, and so, in caring for her, I am beginning to care for myself. Old age has made me braver about enduring many things, but here I'm gradually losing that gift of age. For it occurs to me that inside this old man there is a young person too — and youth needs tenderness. Since I cannot persuade her to love me more heroically, she persuades me to cherish myself more carefully. And genuine feelings deserve to be indulged. Sometimes, even against weighty reasons, the breath of life must be called back and held at our very lips, at the cost of real suffering, for the sake of those we love — because a good man should live not as long as it pleases him, but as long as he ought. The man who doesn't value his wife or his friend enough to linger a little longer in life, who stubbornly insists on dying, is not a hero. He is self-indulgent.

The soul should give itself this order whenever the needs of loved ones require it: it should pause and humor those near and dear, not only when it merely wants to die, but even when it has already begun. It shows a great heart to come back to life for the sake of others, and noble men have done it more than once. But I think this, too, is kindness of the highest order: the greatest privilege of old age is the license to be careless about self-preservation and to spend life more daringly — yet you should guard your old age all the more carefully if you know that doing so is pleasing, useful, or dear to someone you love. There's no small joy and profit in it, either. What is sweeter than to be so valued by your wife that you become more valuable to yourself because of it? So my dear Paulina manages to make me answerable not only for her fears, but for my own.

You're curious how the travel prescription turned out? The moment I escaped the oppressive air of the city — and that dreadful reek of smoking kitchens which, once fired up, pour out a ruinous mess of steam and soot — I felt my health mending at once. And how much stronger do you think I felt when I reached my vineyards! Turned out to pasture, so to speak, I positively charged at my meals. I'm my old self again: no wavering weakness in my body, no fog in my brain. I'm getting back to work with all my energy.

But the place itself contributes little, unless the mind is fully its own master and can find seclusion at will even in the thick of business. The man who is forever choosing resorts and hunting for leisure will find something to distract him in every one of them. Socrates is said to have answered a man who complained that his travels had done him no good: "It serves you right — you traveled in your own company!" What a blessing it would be for some people, if they could only wander away from themselves! As it is, they are their own source of worry, vexation, demoralization, and fear. What's the profit in crossing the sea, in moving from city to city? If you want to escape your troubles, what you need is not a different place but a different self. Say you've reached Athens, or Rhodes; pick any city you fancy — what does its character matter? You'll be bringing your own.

Suppose you believe wealth is a good: then poverty will torture you, and — most pitiful of all — an imaginary poverty. You may be rich; but because your neighbor is richer, you'll consider yourself poor by exactly the amount you fall short of him. Suppose you believe public office is a good: you'll be galled every time another man is made consul, or made consul again; you'll burn with envy whenever you see a certain name repeated in the public records. Your ambition will be so frantic that if anyone at all is ahead of you, you'll feel you're running last. Or suppose you rate death as the worst of evils — when there is really nothing evil in it except what comes before it: fear. You'll be scared out of your wits by dangers imagined as much as real, tossed forever on a sea of illusion. What good will it do you to have

  threaded your way through all the towns of Argolis,
  a fugitive slipping through the thick of your enemies?

Peace itself will supply you with fresh alarms. Once the mind has taken a shock, it loses confidence even in the midst of safety; once it acquires the habit of blind panic, it can no longer provide even for its own preservation. It doesn't avoid danger — it runs from it. And we are more exposed to danger with our backs turned.

You may judge the loss of someone you love to be the heaviest of blows; yet that would be as foolish as weeping because the trees that delight your eye and beautify your home drop their leaves. Look on everything that pleases you as a plant in flower: enjoy it while it's in leaf, for one plant after another must fall and die, each in its season. But just as losing leaves is a light matter, because they grow again, so it is with losing those you love, the delights of your life: they can be replaced, even if they can't be reborn. "But the new friends won't be the same." No — and neither will you. You change with every day and every hour. In other people you see readily enough what time steals; in yourself the theft is hidden, because it doesn't happen in plain view. Others are snatched from our sight; we are being secretly stolen from ourselves. You won't consider any of this, won't put any salve on these wounds; instead you'll sow yourself a crop of worries by hoping one moment and despairing the next. If you're wise, mix the two: never hope without a dose of despair, never despair without a dose of hope.

What has travel, by itself, ever done for anyone? It has never restrained pleasure, bridled desire, checked bad temper, or crushed the wild assaults of passion; it has never rid the soul of any evil. It doesn't give us judgment, doesn't shake off our errors; it merely occupies our attention for a moment with novelty, the way children stop to gawk at something unfamiliar. What's more, it aggravates the instability of a mind that's already sick; the very motion makes it more restless and jittery. That's why the places we sought most eagerly, we abandon more eagerly still — like birds that flit off as soon as they've landed. What travel will give you is acquaintance with other nations. It will show you mountains of strange shape, unfamiliar stretches of plain, valleys watered by everlasting springs, the peculiarities of some notable river — how the Nile rises and swells in summer, how the Tigris vanishes underground, runs through hidden channels, and reappears at full force, or how the Maeander, that well-worn theme and plaything of the poets, coils in loop after loop, winding so close to its own channel before it flows on. But none of this information will make us better men, or sounder ones.

We should be spending our time in study instead, and with the masters of wisdom, learning what has been investigated but not yet settled. That is how the mind is rescued from the most wretched slavery and won over to freedom. As long as you don't know what to avoid and what to seek, what is necessary and what is superfluous, what is right and what is wrong, you are not traveling — you are wandering. All this rushing around will do you no good, for you travel in the company of your emotions, and your afflictions follow you. If only they were following you! They'd be farther away. As it is, you're not leading them — you're carrying them. So they press in on you from every side, chafing and galling. It's medicine the sick man should be shopping for, not scenery. Suppose someone has broken a leg or dislocated a joint: he doesn't board a carriage or a ship for foreign parts; he calls in a doctor to set the fracture or work the joint back into its socket. Well then — when the spirit is broken or wrenched in so many places, do you think a change of address can heal it? The trouble sits too deep to be cured by a journey. Travel makes no one a doctor or an orator; no art is learned simply by living somewhere.

Where does that leave us? Can wisdom, the greatest art of all, be picked up along the road? Believe me: travel as far as you like, and you will never get beyond the reach of desire, beyond the reach of temper, beyond the reach of fear. If you could, the whole human race would have set out on pilgrimage to the spot long ago. As long as you carry the causes of these ills around with you, they will weigh you down and wear you to skin and bone across every land and sea. Are you surprised that running away doesn't help? What you're running from is inside you. So fix yourself; lift the load off your own shoulders; and hold within safe limits the cravings that ought to be removed altogether. Scrub every trace of wickedness from your soul. If you want to enjoy your travels, cure your traveling companion. As long as that companion is greedy and grasping, greed will stick to you; keep company with an arrogant man, and your own swagger sticks too. Live with an executioner and you'll never shed your cruelty; make an adulterer your clubmate and he'll fan your basest appetites. If you want to be stripped of your faults, get far away from the models of those faults. The miser, the swindler, the bully, the cheat — the ones who would damage you just by standing near you — are inside you.

Move to better company, then: live with the Catos, with Laelius, with Tubero. And if you like Greek company too, spend your time with Socrates and Zeno — the one will teach you how to die if it becomes necessary, the other how to die before it becomes necessary. Live with Chrysippus, with Posidonius. They will introduce you to things earthly and things heavenly. They will order you to work at something more than neat turns of phrase mouthed for the entertainment of an audience; they will order you to harden your heart and rise above threats. The only harbor safe from this life's seething storms is this: contempt for the future, a firm stance, and a readiness to take Fortune's missiles full in the chest — not skulking, not turning your back. Nature brought us forth with brave spirits. Just as she planted ferocity in some animals, cunning in others, timidity in others, so she gave us a soaring, aspiring spirit that seeks the most honorable life, not the safest one — a spirit most like the soul of the universe, which it follows and imitates as far as mortal steps allow. This spirit pushes itself forward, sure of winning praise and esteem. It stands above everything, master of all it surveys, so it should bow to nothing: no task too heavy for it, nothing strong enough to weigh down a man's shoulders.

  Shapes dread to look upon, of toil and death

are not dreadful in the least to anyone who can face them with an unflinching gaze and pierce through the shadows. Many a thing held up as a terror by night is turned to ridicule by day. "Shapes dread to look upon, of toil and death": our Vergil put it perfectly. These shapes are dreadful not in reality but only "to look upon" — that is, they seem terrible; they are not. What is there in these apparitions, I ask you, half as fearsome as rumor makes out? Why on earth, my dear Lucilius, should a man fear toil, or a mortal fear death? I can think of countless men who assume that whatever they themselves cannot do is impossible, who insist that we Stoics talk bigger than human nature can deliver. But how much more highly I think of these men than they do! They can do these things — they simply decline to. Ask anyone who ever actually tried: whom have these tasks ever failed? Who didn't find them easier in the doing? It is not the difficulty that drains our confidence; it is our lack of confidence that creates the difficulty.

If you want a model, take Socrates: a long-suffering old man, storm-tossed through every hardship, yet unconquered by poverty (made heavier by burdens at home) and unconquered by toil, including the drudgery of military service. He was sorely tried at home too — think of his wife, with her rough manners and shrewish tongue, or his children, whose unruliness proved them their mother's sons more than their father's. And consider the times he lived in: war, or tyranny, or a democracy crueler than wars and tyrants. The war lasted twenty-seven years; then the state fell prey to the Thirty Tyrants, many of them his personal enemies. Last came that final condemnation on the gravest of charges: they accused him of corrupting the state religion and corrupting the young — of teaching the youth, they said, to defy the gods, defy the council, defy the state itself. After that, the prison, and the cup of poison. And all of it changed the soul of Socrates so little that it didn't even change his face. What a marvelous, rare distinction! He held that bearing to the very end: no one ever saw Socrates too elated or too downcast. Amid all the turbulence of Fortune, he was untroubled.

Do you want another case? Take the younger Marcus Cato, whom Fortune handled with even more hostility and more persistence. He withstood her at every turn, and at the end, at the point of death, he showed that a brave man can live in spite of Fortune and die in spite of her. His whole life was spent either in civil war or under a regime that was breeding one. And you may say that he, no less than Socrates, pledged himself to liberty in the middle of slavery — unless you happen to think Pompey, Caesar, and Crassus were liberty's allies! No one ever saw Cato change, however often the state changed. He kept himself the same in every circumstance: as praetor, in defeat, under accusation, in his province, on the speaker's platform, in the army, in death. In the republic's hour of terror — Caesar on one side with ten battle-ready legions and whole foreign nations at his call, Pompey on the other, content to stand alone against all comers, and the citizens leaning toward one or the other — Cato alone founded a party for the Republic. If you want a mental picture of that moment, imagine on one side the people and the whole mob, hungry for revolution; on the other, the senators and knights, the chosen and honored men of the state; and left standing between them, just these two: the Republic, and Cato.

I promise you, you will marvel when you see

  Atreus' son, and Priam, and Achilles, angry at them both.

Like Achilles, Cato scorns both factions and disarms them. And this is the vote he casts on the pair of them: "If Caesar wins, I kill myself; if Pompey wins, I go into exile." What was left to fear for a man who, in victory or defeat, had already sentenced himself as harshly as his most furious enemies could have? So he died by his own decision.

You see that toil can be endured: Cato led an army on foot through the African desert. You see that thirst can be endured: he marched over sun-scorched hills, dragging the remnants of a beaten army with no supply train, going without water, wearing a full weight of armor — and always the last to drink at the few springs they happened to find. You see that honor can be despised, and dishonor too: on the very day Cato lost the election, they say, he played a game of ball. You see that a man can be free of fear of the powerful: Cato attacked Caesar and Pompey at the same time, in an age when no one dared cross the one without currying favor with the other. You see that death can be scorned as easily as exile: Cato sentenced himself to exile, and finally to death — and to war the whole time.

And so, if only we're willing to pull our necks out of the yoke, we can keep just as stout a heart against terrors like these. But first and foremost we must refuse pleasures; they make us soft and feeble, they demand a great deal from us, and they drive us, in turn, to demand a great deal from Fortune. Second, we must spurn wealth: wealth is the badge of slavery. Give up gold and silver and whatever else burdens our richly furnished houses. Liberty cannot be had for free. If you set a high price on liberty, you must set a low price on everything else.

Farewell.

Letter 105: On Facing the World with Confidence

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I'm going to tell you some things to keep in mind if you want to live more safely. And I suggest you listen to these precepts exactly as if I were advising you how to keep your health at your country place in Ardea.

Consider what it is that goads man into destroying man: you'll find it is hope, envy, hatred, fear, and contempt. Of all these, contempt is the least harmful — so much so that many people have actually hidden behind it as a kind of cure. The man who despises you does you an injury, true, but then he moves on; no one goes on hurting, deliberately and persistently, a person he despises. Even on the battlefield, soldiers lying on the ground are passed over; men fight with those still on their feet. As for the greedy hopes of the wicked, you can avoid those as long as you own nothing that stirs another man's appetite, nothing that stands out. People will covet even small things, if they catch the eye or come along rarely.

You'll escape envy if you don't push yourself into public view, if you don't boast about what you have, if you know how to enjoy things privately. Hatred comes either from giving offense — and you avoid that by provoking no one — or it comes unprovoked, and there common sense will keep you safe. Yet unprovoked hatred has been dangerous to many; some people have been hated without ever having made an enemy. As for not being feared: a moderate fortune and an easygoing nature will see to that. Let people know you're the kind of man who can be wronged without danger, and that reconciling with you is easy and sure. Being feared is as much trouble at home as it is out in the world — as bad to be feared by a slave as by a free man, for everyone has strength enough to do you some harm. Besides, whoever is feared, fears: no one has ever managed to inspire terror and live with a peaceful mind.

That leaves contempt. The man who has made contempt part of his own equipment — who is despised because he chooses to be, not because he has to be — holds its measure in his own hands. Any inconveniences on that score can be dispelled by honorable pursuits, and by friendships with men who have the ear of a man of influence. Engage with such men, but don't get entangled — or the cure may cost you more than the risk. Nothing, though, will help you so much as keeping quiet: talk very little with others, and as much as possible with yourself. There is a certain charm in conversation, something subtle and coaxing, which — like wine or love — draws our secrets out of us. And no one keeps to himself what he hears. No one repeats only as much as he was told. The man who tells the tale will tell the name too. Everyone has someone he trusts with exactly what was entrusted to him. Even if each one curbs his chattiness and settles for a single listener, he'll end up with a nation — and what was a secret a moment ago becomes common talk.

The greatest contribution to peace of mind is this: never do wrong. People without self-control lead disturbed and stormy lives; their crimes are matched by their fears, and they are never at rest. After the deed they tremble; they are trapped. Their consciences won't let them attend to anything else, and keep hauling them back to answer for themselves. Whoever expects punishment receives it — and whoever deserves it expects it. A guilty conscience may find safety in something, but it finds ease in nothing; such a man assumes that even if he isn't caught yet, he may be caught soon. His sleep is troubled. Whenever he mentions another man's crime, he thinks of his own — never sufficiently blotted out, he feels, never sufficiently hidden. A wrongdoer sometimes has the luck to escape notice — never the assurance of it.

Farewell.
