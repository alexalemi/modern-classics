Letter 86: On Scipio’s Villa

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I am writing to you from the country house that once belonged to Scipio Africanus himself. I have been resting here, and before I sat down to write I paid my respects to his spirit, and to an altar which, I suspect, is actually the great soldier's tomb. That his soul went back to the sky it came from, I have no doubt — and not because he commanded mighty armies. Cambyses had mighty armies too, and Cambyses was a madman who simply happened to use his madness successfully. No, I believe it because Scipio showed moderation and a sense of duty to a degree that is hard to believe. And I admire those qualities more in the moment he walked away from his country than in all the years he defended her. Because by then it had come down to this: either Scipio stayed in Rome, or Rome stayed free. "I want nothing," he said, "that infringes our laws or our customs by so much as an inch. Let every Roman citizen have equal rights. My country, enjoy the good I have done you — but enjoy it without me. I have been the cause of your freedom; now I will be the proof of it. If it is true that I have grown too big for your good, then I go into exile."

How can I not admire that greatness of spirit — choosing voluntary exile just to take the weight off the state? Matters had reached the point where either liberty had to do harm to Scipio, or Scipio had to do harm to liberty. Either one would have been an offense against heaven. So he yielded to the laws and withdrew here to Liternum, putting the state in his debt for his own exile just as surely as for Hannibal's.

I have gone through the whole house. It is built of squared stone. A wall runs around an entire forest. There are towers buttressed out on both sides to defend the house, and a well hidden among the buildings and shrubbery — big enough to supply an army. And then there is the bath: tiny, and buried in darkness, in the old style, because our ancestors did not believe a bath could be hot unless it was dark. It gave me real pleasure to stand there and compare Scipio's ways with ours. Think of it. In this cramped little recess, the "terror of Carthage" — the man Rome must thank for being captured only once — used to wash a body worn out by work in the fields. For he kept himself busy, and he tilled the soil with his own hands, the way the good old Romans did. Under this dingy roof he stood; this floor, cheap as it is, bore his weight.

But who could bear to bathe like that now? A man thinks himself poor and shabby these days unless his walls gleam with huge, expensive mirrors; unless his Alexandrian marbles are set off with mosaics of Numidian stone, their borders worked over on every side with elaborate patterns in as many colors as a painting; unless the vaulted ceiling is buried in glass; unless the swimming pools are lined with Thasian marble — a sight that was once rare and wonderful in any temple — pools into which we lower bodies already drained weak by heavy sweating; and unless, to finish it off, the water pours out of silver spigots. And so far I have only been describing the ordinary bathhouses. What am I supposed to say when I get to the baths of the freedmen? The sheer crowds of statues! The columns that hold up nothing at all, installed purely for decoration, purely for the sake of spending money! The masses of water crashing noisily down from level to level! We have grown so luxurious that we refuse to walk on anything but precious stones.

In this bath of Scipio's there are little chinks — you cannot really call them windows — cut through the stone wall in a way that lets in light without weakening the defenses. Nowadays, though, people say a bath is fit only for moths unless it has been designed to catch the sun all day long through the widest possible windows, unless a man can bathe and get a tan at the same time, unless he can lie in his tub and gaze out over stretches of land and sea. And so it goes: the establishments that drew crowds and won gasps of admiration when they first opened get deserted and filed away with the venerable antiques the moment luxury works out some new trick — luxury busily inventing her own undoing. In the early days, though, there were few baths, and nobody dressed them up at all. Why should anyone lavish decoration on a thing that costs a penny to enter and was invented for use, not for delight? The bathers of those days did not have water poured over them, and it did not run endlessly fresh as if from a hot spring; they did not think it mattered in the least how perfectly clear the water was that they were about to leave their dirt in. Good gods, what a pleasure it is to step into that dark bath, under its plain common roof, and know that your hero Cato, as aedile — or Fabius Maximus, or one of the Cornelii — warmed its water with his own hands! For even that used to be part of the noblest aediles' job: to walk into the places where ordinary people gathered and demand that they be kept clean and heated to a temperature fixed by use and health — not the temperature that has lately become fashionable, as hot as a house fire, so hot that a slave convicted of some crime could now simply be sentenced to be bathed alive. As far as I can tell, there is no longer any difference between "the bath is warm" and "the bath is on fire."

And how people today sneer at Scipio as a country bumpkin — because he did not let daylight into his sweating-room through wide windows, because he did not roast himself in the blazing sun and dawdle around until he could stew in the hot water! "Poor fool," they say, "he didn't know how to live! His water wasn't even filtered. It was often cloudy, and after a heavy rain it was practically mud!" But it did not matter much to Scipio if his bathwater looked like that. He came there to wash off sweat, not ointment. And how do you suppose certain people will answer me? They will say: "I don't envy Scipio. A bath like that — now that was truly an exile's life." Friend, if you were wiser, you would know that Scipio did not even bathe every day. The writers who have handed down the old Roman ways tell us that the Romans washed their arms and legs daily — those being the parts that picked up dirt in a day's work — and bathed all over just once a week. At this point somebody will snort: "Well! They must have been filthy fellows. Imagine what they smelled like!" They smelled of the camp, the farm, and heroism. Now that spotless, glittering bathhouses have been invented, men are actually fouler than they used to be. When Horace wants to describe a scoundrel, a man notorious for over-the-top luxury, what does he say? "Buccillus smells of perfume." Show me a Buccillus today: his perfume would rank as pure goat-stink, and he would have to take the place of Gargonius, the smelly fellow Horace set opposite him in the same passage. Nowadays it is not enough merely to wear scent; you have to slap on a fresh coat two or three times a day so it will not evaporate off your body. And why on earth does a man preen over that perfume as if the smell were his own?

If all this strikes you as too gloomy, charge it to Scipio's villa — where I have also learned a lesson from Aegialus, the most careful of farmers and the current owner of this estate. He has taught me that a tree can be transplanted no matter how far along in years it is. We old men need to learn that lesson, since there is not one of us who is not planting an olive orchard for his successor. I have seen those orchards bearing fruit in due season after three or four unproductive years. And you too, one day, will be shaded by the tree that

  is slow to grow, but will bring shade to cheer
  your grandsons in the far-off years,

  Sow beans in the spring; then you too, clover,
  are welcome in the crumbling furrows; and
  the millet calls for its yearly care.

You can judge from a little incident whether those crops should really be planted at the same time, or whether both belong to spring. It is June as I write this — we are well along toward July — and this very day I have watched farmers harvesting beans and sowing millet.

But back to our olive orchard. I saw it planted in two ways. If the trees were large, Aegialus took the trunks and cut the branches back to a foot each; then he transplanted the tree with its root-ball, first trimming off the roots and leaving only the thick knob they hang from. He smeared this with manure, set it into the hole, and then not only heaped the earth up around it but stamped and pressed it down hard. Nothing, he says, works better than this packing. It keeps out the cold and the wind; and besides, the trunk is not shaken about so much, which gives the young roots a chance to come out and take hold in the soil. Roots that new are necessarily still soft; they have only a slight grip, and a very little shaking tears them loose. The root-ball itself he shaves clean before he covers it up, because, he maintains, new roots spring from every part that has been shorn. And the trunk should stand no more than three or four feet out of the ground. That way it greens over thickly right from the base, and there is none of that big, dry, withered stump you see in old olive orchards. The second way of planting was this: he set out, in the same fashion, branches that were strong and soft-barked, the way young saplings' branches usually are. These grow a little more slowly, but since they spring from what is practically a cutting, there is nothing rough or ugly about them.

One more thing I have seen recently: an aged vine transplanted out of its own vineyard. With a vine, the little fibers should be gathered up too, if possible, and then you should cover the stem more generously with earth, so that roots will sprout even from the stock itself. I have seen such plantings made not only in February but at the very end of March — and the vines take hold of elms that are strangers to them, and embrace them. All the trees that are, so to speak, "thick-stemmed," Aegialus declares, should be helped along with water from the tank. If we have that help, we are our own rain-makers.

I do not intend to pass along any more of these secrets — otherwise I will do to you what Aegialus did to me, and train up my own competitor.

Farewell.
