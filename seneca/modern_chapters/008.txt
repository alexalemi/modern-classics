Letters 30–33

Letter 30: On Conquering the Conqueror

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I have just seen Aufidius Bassus — that good and noble man — broken in health and wrestling with his years. But the years now press on him too heavily for him to get back up; old age has settled onto him with its great weight — no, with its entire weight. You know his body was always frail and dry. For a long time he kept it in hand — kept it together, to put it more accurately — and then all at once it gave way. When a ship springs a leak, you can always plug the first crack, and the second; but when many seams begin to open at once and let in the sea, there is no saving the gaping hull. It is the same with an old man's body: up to a certain point you can prop its weakness and hold it up. But when it starts to resemble a condemned building — when every joint is spreading, and while you repair one, another fails — then it is time for a man to look around and consider how to make his exit.

But the mind of our friend Bassus is fully awake. That is the gift philosophy gives us: joy in the very sight of death; strength and courage no matter what state the body is in; cheerfulness that does not fail even when the body is failing. A great pilot can sail with his canvas in shreds; if his ship is stripped to the hull, he rigs what is left of her and holds her on course. That is exactly what our friend Bassus is doing. He contemplates his own end with a courage and a calm face that you would call excessive indifference in a man contemplating someone else's.

This is a great accomplishment, Lucilius, and one that takes long practice to learn: to depart calmly when the unavoidable hour arrives. Other kinds of death carry an ingredient of hope. A disease runs its course; a fire gets put out; houses that seemed certain to crush the people inside have set them down unhurt; the sea has thrown swimmers back on shore alive by the same force that dragged them under; the soldier has drawn back his sword from the very neck of the doomed man. But those whom old age is leading off to death have nothing left to hope for. Old age alone grants no reprieve. No ending is more painless, true; but none takes longer.

Bassus seemed to me to be attending his own funeral — laying out his own body for burial, living almost like a man who had outlived his own death, bearing his grief at his own departure the way a wise man should. Because he talks freely about death, and works hard to persuade us that if this process contains any discomfort or fear, the fault belongs to the dying person and not to death itself — and that there is no more trouble in the actual moment than there is after it is over. "And it is just as insane," he adds, "to fear what will not happen to you as to fear what you will not feel if it does happen." Can anyone really believe that the very thing that removes all feeling can itself be felt? "Therefore," says Bassus, "death stands so far beyond every evil that it is beyond every fear of evil."

I know all this has been said often and needs to be said often. But such precepts never worked on me the same way when I read them in books, or heard them from men declaring — from a safe distance — that the thing was nothing to fear. This old man carried the greatest weight with me because he discussed death with death standing near. I will tell you what I actually think: I believe a man is braver at the very moment of death than on the approach to it. When death is right in front of us, it gives even the inexperienced the courage to stop dodging what cannot be dodged. That is how a gladiator who was faint-hearted through the entire fight ends it by offering his throat to his opponent and guiding the wavering blade to the spot himself. But an end that is close at hand and certain to come, and slow in coming — that demands a tough, tenacious courage of soul. It is a rarer thing, and only the wise man can supply it.

So I listened to Bassus with the deepest pleasure. He was casting his vote on death — pointing out what sort of thing it is when observed, so to speak, at close range. I suppose a man would command more of your confidence, and carry more weight, if he had come back to life and could report from experience that there is no evil in death. Short of that, the people best qualified to tell you what disquiet the approach of death brings are the ones who have stood in its path, watched it coming, and welcomed it. Count Bassus among them — and he had no wish to deceive us. He says it is as foolish to fear death as to fear old age; for death follows old age exactly as old age follows youth. Whoever does not want to die never really wanted to live. Life is given to us with death as the condition; death is where the whole road leads. How foolish to fear it, then — a man simply awaits what is certain; what he fears is the uncertain. Death has its fixed rule, fair and unavoidable. Who can complain about being governed by terms that include everyone? The chief part of fairness, after all, is that it falls on all alike.

But there is no need to plead Nature's case at the moment. She wants our law to be identical with her own: she simply takes apart what she has put together, and puts together again what she has taken apart. And if any man has the luck to be set gently adrift by old age — not torn suddenly out of life but withdrawn from it bit by bit — he ought to thank the gods, every one of them, because after he has had his fill he is carried off to the rest that is appointed for mankind, the rest a tired man welcomes. You can find certain people who crave death more eagerly than others beg for life. And I honestly do not know which ones give us more courage — those who call for death, or those who meet it calm and cheerful — because the first attitude sometimes springs from madness or a burst of rage, while the second is the calm that comes from settled judgment. Men have gone charging to meet death in a fit of anger before now. But when death comes to meet him, no one welcomes it cheerfully except the man who composed himself for it long ago.

So I admit it: I visited this dear friend of mine again and again on various pretexts, but really to learn whether I would find him the same each time — whether his strength of mind was fading along with his strength of body. It was growing, the way a charioteer's joy shows more and more clearly as he rounds the seventh lap and closes on the prize. In fact, following the counsels of Epicurus, he often said: "First of all, I hope there is no pain at the moment a man breathes his last. But if there is, its very shortness carries a certain comfort — no great pain lasts long. And in the worst case, even if soul and body are being torn apart with excruciating pain, a man finds relief at that very moment in the thought that after this pain he can never feel pain again. As for me, I am sure an old man's soul is sitting on his very lips; it takes only a little force to detach it from the body. A fire that has caught hold of solid fuel needs water to put it out — sometimes the collapse of the whole building. But a fire with nothing left to feed on dies down by itself."

I am glad to hear such words, my dear Lucilius — not because they are new to me, but because they lead me into the presence of the actual fact. And what then? Have I not watched many men break the thread of life? I have indeed. But the ones who carry weight with me are those who come to death without any loathing for life — who let death in, so to speak, instead of pulling it toward them. Bassus kept saying: "This torture is our own fault, because we only shrink from dying when we believe the end is close at hand." But who is not close to it? Death is ready for us in every place and at every hour. "Consider," he went on, "whenever some cause of death seems imminent, how much nearer other kinds of dying stand — kinds we do not fear at all." A man is threatened with death by an enemy; a bout of indigestion beats the enemy to it. If we are willing to examine our fears honestly, we will find that some are real and others only seem to be. We do not fear death; we fear the thought of death. From death itself we are always the same distance. So if death is to be feared at all, it is to be feared always — for what season of life is exempt from it?

But what I really ought to fear is that you will hate a letter this long worse than death itself. So I will stop. As for you: think on death always, so that you never fear it.

Farewell.
Letter 31: On Siren Songs

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Now I recognize my Lucilius! He is beginning to show the character he promised. Follow up that impulse that sent you striding after everything that is best, trampling underfoot whatever the crowd approves. I would not have you greater or better than you planned — in your case the foundations alone already cover a great deal of ground; just finish everything you laid out, and take in hand the plans you have been carrying in your head. In short: you will be a wise man if you stop up your ears. And wax is not enough — you need a denser plug than the one they say Ulysses used on his crew. The song he feared was seductive, but it came from only one direction; the song you have to fear echoes around you not from a single headland but from every quarter of the world. So do not sail past one stretch of coast you distrust for its treacherous pleasures — sail past every city. Be deaf even to the people who love you most: they pray for bad things with good intentions. And if you want to be happy, beg the gods to grant none of their fond wishes for you. The things they want heaped on you are not really good things. There is only one good — the cause and support of a happy life: trust in yourself. And you cannot reach it until you have learned to despise hard work, and to count it among the things that are neither good nor bad. Because a single thing cannot be bad at one time and good at another — light and bearable now, a cause of dread later. Work is not a good. Then what is a good? Contempt for work. So I fault the men who toil for nothing. But when a man is straining toward honorable things, then the harder he pushes, the less he lets himself be beaten or brought to a halt, the louder I will applaud him and shout my encouragement: "So much the better! Up — take a fresh breath — take that hill in a single push if you can!"

Work is the food of noble minds. So there is no reason why, following that old vow of your parents, you should pick and choose which fortune to want or which to pray for — and anyway it is beneath a man who has already made the full circuit of the highest honors to keep pestering the gods. Why pray at all? Make yourself happy through your own effort. You can do it, once you understand this: whatever is blended with virtue is good; whatever is joined to vice is bad. Just as nothing gleams without light mixed into it, and nothing is black unless it contains darkness or draws some dimness into itself, and nothing is hot without the help of fire or cold without air — in the same way it is the presence of virtue or vice that makes things honorable or shameful.

Then what is good? Knowledge. What is evil? Ignorance. Your wise man — who is also a craftsman — will reject or select in each case as the occasion demands; but he does not fear what he rejects or admire what he selects, so long as his soul is stout and unconquerable. I forbid you to be discouraged or cast down. It is not enough not to shrink from work: ask for it. "But," you say, "isn't trivial, pointless work — work sprung from shabby motives — a bad kind of work?" No more than the work spent on noble efforts. Because the very quality that endures toil and drives itself up the hard uphill road belongs to the spirit, which says: "Why are you slacking? It is not a man's part to fear sweat." And beyond this, for virtue to be complete there must be an even temperament and a scheme of life consistent with itself all the way through — and that result cannot be reached without knowledge, and without the art that lets us understand things human and things divine. That is the greatest good. Seize it, and you begin to be the associate of the gods, not their beggar.

"But how," you ask, "do you get there?" Not by crossing the Pennine or Graian passes, or trekking the wastes of Candavia. You do not have to face the Syrtes, or Scylla, or Charybdis — though you traveled through every one of those places for the bribe of a petty governorship. The journey nature has equipped you for is safe and pleasant. She has given you gifts that will lift you level with God — if you do not desert them. Your money will not put you on a level with God: God owns no property. Your bordered robe will not do it: God wears no clothes. Your reputation will not do it, nor showing yourself off, nor having your name known across the world: no one has knowledge of God, and many think poorly of him without paying any price for it. The mob of slaves carrying your litter through the city streets and down foreign roads will not help you: this God I am speaking of, though the highest and most powerful of beings, carries everything on his own shoulders. Even beauty and strength cannot make you blessed; neither of them survives old age.

What we have to look for, then, is something that does not pass, day after day, further under the control of a power that cannot be resisted. And what is that? The soul — but the soul that is upright, good, and great. What else could you call such a soul but a god dwelling as a guest in a human body? A soul like that can descend into a Roman knight just as easily as into a freedman's son, or a slave. For what is a Roman knight, or a freedman's son, or a slave? Titles — born of ambition or of injustice. You can leap to heaven from the very slums. Only rise

  and shape yourself into kinship with your God.

You will not do that shaping in gold or silver; an image in the likeness of God cannot be cast from materials like those. Remember that back when the gods were kind to men, they were shaped from clay.

Farewell.
Letter 32: On Progress

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I have been asking about you — questioning everyone who comes from your part of the country: what you are doing, where you are spending your time, and with whom. You cannot deceive me; I am with you. Live exactly as if I were certain to get news of everything you do — no, as if I were certain to see it. And if you wonder what pleases me most in what I hear about you, it is that I hear nothing: most of the people I ask do not know what you are doing.

That is sound practice — keeping clear of people of a different stamp with different aims. And I am confident enough that you cannot be bent, that you will hold to your purpose even if the crowd surrounds you and tries to pull you off it. Then what is on my mind? I am not afraid they will change you; I am afraid they will slow you down. Even a man who merely holds you back does real damage — especially since life is so short, and we make it shorter still with our unsteadiness, forever making fresh starts at living, first one beginning and then immediately another. We break life into little pieces, and fritter it away. So hurry ahead, dearest Lucilius, and think how you would quicken your pace if you had an enemy at your back — if you suspected cavalry closing in, pressing hard on your tracks as you fled. It is true: the enemy is pressing. So pick up speed, get away, reach safe ground — and keep remembering what a noble thing it is to round off your life before death comes, and then wait in peace for whatever portion of time remains, claiming nothing more for yourself, because you are already in possession of the happy life — and a happy life is not made happier by being longer. Oh, when will you see the day when you know that time means nothing to you — when you are peaceful and calm, careless of tomorrow, because you are enjoying your life to the full?

Do you want to know what makes men greedy for the future? No one has yet found himself. Your parents, to be sure, prayed for other blessings for you; but I pray instead that you will despise all the things they wished for you in abundance. Their prayers plunder plenty of other people just so that you can be enriched; whatever is handed over to you has to be taken from someone else. What I pray is this: that you gain such command over yourself that your mind, shaken now by wandering thoughts, finally comes to rest and holds steady; that it becomes content with itself; and that once it understands what things are truly good — and they are ours the moment we understand them — it has no need of added years. He has finally passed beyond every necessity — he has won his honorable discharge and is free — who is still alive after his life is complete.

Farewell.
Letter 33: On the Futility of Learning Maxims

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You want me to close these letters, as I closed the earlier ones, with sayings drawn from the masters of our school. But they never dealt in choice extracts; the whole weave of their work is strong. There is unevenness, you know, when some objects stand out above the rest. A single tree is nothing remarkable when the whole forest rises to the same height. Poetry is crammed with sayings of this kind, and so is history. So do not think of those lines as belonging to Epicurus: they are common property — really, they are ours most of all. They simply get noticed more in Epicurus, because they come at rare intervals, when you are not expecting them, and because it is surprising to hear brave words from a man who made a practice of going soft. That, at least, is what most people say of him. In my own opinion Epicurus is genuinely brave, long sleeves and all. Courage, energy, and readiness for battle can be found among the Persians just as surely as among peoples who dress girded for a fight.

So stop calling on me for extracts and quotations. The kind of thought you pick out here and there from other philosophers runs unbroken through the whole body of our writings. We keep no window display; we do not trick the customer into stepping inside the shop only to find nothing there but what was hanging in the window. We let the customers take their samples from wherever they please. Suppose we did want to sort individual mottoes out of the common stock — to whom would we credit them? Zeno, Cleanthes, Chrysippus, Panaetius, or Posidonius? We Stoics are not subjects of a king; each of us claims his own freedom. Among the others, whatever Hermarchus says, whatever Metrodorus says, gets credited to one source; everything anyone in that brotherhood utters is spoken under the leadership and commanding authority of one man alone. But we, I insist, cannot pick out any single thing — try as we may — from so vast a supply of things equally good.

  Only the poor man counts his flock.

So give up hoping that you can skim the wisdom of great men out of summaries. You have to look at their wisdom whole; study it whole. They are working out a design, weaving a masterpiece line upon line, from which nothing can be removed without injury to the whole. Examine the separate parts if you like — as long as you examine them as parts of the man himself. A woman is not beautiful because her ankle or her arm draws compliments; she is beautiful when the whole of her makes you forget to admire the parts.

Still, if you insist, I will not be stingy with you — I will be lavish. There is a huge crowd of these passages, scattered about everywhere in profusion; they do not need to be gathered, only picked up. They do not drip out a drop at a time; they flow continuously, unbroken, all of a piece. And no doubt they do real good for beginners — the ones still worshipping outside the shrine. Single maxims sink in more easily when they are framed and fenced off like a line of verse. That is why we give children a proverb to learn by heart — what the Greeks call a chria — because a young mind can hold that much and no more. But for a man whose progress is real to go chasing choice extracts, propping his weakness on the best-known and briefest sayings, leaning on his memory — that is disgraceful. It is time for him to lean on himself. He should be making such maxims, not memorizing them. It is shameful for an old man, or a man in sight of old age, to have nothing but notebook wisdom. "This is what Zeno said." And what have you said? "This is the opinion of Cleanthes." And what is your opinion? How long will you march under another man's orders? Take command. Say something posterity will remember. Put out something from your own stock. This is why I hold that there is nothing distinguished in any of these men who never create anything themselves but lurk forever in someone else's shadow — playing the interpreter, never once daring to put into practice what they have spent so long learning. They have exercised their memories on other men's material. But remembering is one thing; knowing is another. Remembering is guarding something left in your memory's keeping; knowing means making everything your own — not depending on the copy, not forever glancing back at the master. "Thus said Zeno, thus said Cleanthes," indeed! Let there be some daylight between you and your book! How long will you be a student? From now on, be a teacher as well. "But why," someone asks, "should I keep going to lectures on things I can read?" "The living voice," comes the answer, "is a great help." Perhaps — but not the voice that only mouths another man's words and does the work of a stenographer.

Consider this fact, too. Those who never reach independence of mind begin by following their leader in cases where everyone else has abandoned him; then they follow him into questions where the truth is still being worked out. And the truth will never be discovered if we sit content with the discoveries already made. Besides, a man who follows another discovers nothing — he is not even searching. What then? Shall I not walk in the footsteps of the men who went before me? I will indeed use the old road; but if I find a shorter cut that is smoother to travel, I will open the new road. The men who made these discoveries before us are not our masters; they are our guides. Truth lies open to everyone. No one has a monopoly on it yet — and there is plenty of it left for those still to come.

Farewell.
