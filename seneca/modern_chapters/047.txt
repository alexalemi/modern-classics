Letters 109–111

Letter 109: On the Fellowship of Wise Men

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You wanted to know whether one wise man can help another. After all, we say the wise man is fully stocked with every good — he has reached perfection. So how could anyone help a man who already possesses the Supreme Good?

Good men are good for each other. Each gives the other's virtues a workout and keeps his wisdom in fighting trim. Each needs someone to compare notes with, someone to investigate things alongside. Skilled wrestlers stay sharp by sparring; a musician is spurred on by a player of equal skill. The wise man, too, needs his virtues kept in motion — and just as he prods himself into action, he can be prodded by another wise man. What does one wise man actually do for another? He quickens his impulses. He points out openings for honorable action. Beyond that, he can work out some of his own ideas in company and share what he has discovered. For even the wise man will always have something left to discover, some territory where his mind can push into new ground.

Bad men harm bad men. Each drags the other lower — stoking his anger, applauding his rudeness, praising his pleasures. Bad men hit bottom when their faults have thoroughly blended, when their wickedness has been pooled, so to speak, into a partnership. Flip that over, and you see how a good man helps a good man. "How?" you ask. He brings the other joy. He strengthens his confidence. Each looks at the other's calm, and the delight of both grows. Besides, they teach each other things they know — because the wise man is not all-knowing. And even if he were, someone might still devise shortcuts and point them out, ways of getting the whole business across more easily. The wise man helps the wise man not by his own strength alone, mind you, but by the strength of the man he's helping. The other could, it's true, develop his own gifts by himself — but even the swiftest runner is helped by a pacesetter.

"The wise man doesn't really help the wise man; he helps himself. Here's the proof: strip the second man of his own powers, and the first accomplishes nothing." On that logic you might as well say sweetness isn't in the honey — since it's the eater whose tongue and palate must be built for that kind of food, so that the flavor delights him instead of putting him off. There are people so altered by disease that honey tastes bitter to them. Both men need to be in good health: one to give the help, the other to be fit to receive it.

Again they say: "Once a thing has reached its highest heat, adding more heat is pointless. Once a man has reached the Supreme Good, a helper is pointless. Does a fully stocked farmer beg supplies from his neighbors? Does a soldier armed head to toe for battle need more weapons? Neither does the wise man — he is equipped and armed for life." My answer: even a thing heated to the highest degree needs continued heat to stay there. And if you object that heat maintains itself, I say you're comparing very different things. Heat is one single thing; helpfulness comes in many kinds. And heat doesn't need more heat added in order to be hot — but the wise man cannot hold his mind at its own high level without the company of friends of his own kind, people to share his goodness with. What's more, there is a kind of mutual friendship among all the virtues. So the man who loves the virtues of his peers, and shows his own to be loved in return, is being helpful. Like delights in like — especially when the likeness is honorable and the approval runs both ways. And besides, only a wise man can prompt another wise man's soul intelligently, just as only a human being can prompt a human being rationally. As reason is needed to prompt reason, so perfect reason is needed to prompt perfect reason.

Some say we're also "helped" by people who hand us the so-called indifferent things — money, influence, security, and all the other prized or essential props of living. Argue that way, and the biggest fool in town will count as a helper of the wise. But helping really means prompting the soul in accordance with Nature — by the excellence of the prompter and the excellence of the prompted alike. And this can't happen without profit to the helper too, for in training another man's excellence you necessarily train your own. But set aside the supreme goods and their causes; wise men can still be useful to each other. For a sage merely to discover another sage is itself worth wanting, since everything good is naturally dear to the good man. One feels at home with a good man the way one feels at home with oneself.

To prove the point, I have to move from this question to a neighboring one. People ask whether the wise man will weigh his opinions alone, or turn to others for advice. He is forced to do the latter whenever he takes up public or household business — everything, so to speak, that is mortal. In such matters he needs outside advice, just like the doctor, the pilot, the attorney, the courtroom pleader. So the wise will sometimes help the wise; they will persuade each other. But even in the great matters — the divine matters, as I've been calling them — the wise man can be useful too: by discussing honorable things in common, by contributing his thoughts and ideas. Besides, it accords with Nature to show affection for our friends and to rejoice in their progress as if it were absolutely our own. If we don't do that, even virtue — which stays strong only by exercising our perceptions — will not stay with us. Now virtue tells us to arrange the present well, to think ahead about the future, to deliberate and apply the mind. And a man who brings a friend into the council can apply his mind and think a problem through more easily.

So the wise man will seek out either the perfect sage or someone who has progressed to the very border of perfection. And the perfect sage will help us further if he backs up our deliberations with ordinary good sense. They say men see farther in other people's affairs than in their own. In ordinary people that's a defect of character: self-love blinds them, and fear in the hour of danger robs them of a clear view of what's useful — a man only begins to be wise when he is at ease and free of fear. But even wise men see certain things more clearly in another's case than in their own. Moreover, the wise man, together with his fellow sage, will make good on that sweetest and most honorable of proverbs — always desiring the same things, and always refusing the same things. It is a noble outcome when two draw the load with an equal yoke.

There — I've answered your demand, though it really belongs in my volumes On Moral Philosophy. Reflect, as I keep telling you, that there's nothing in topics like these for us except mental gymnastics. For I come back again and again to the same thought: "What good does this do me? Make me braver now, more just, more restrained! I haven't yet had the chance to use my training; I still need the physician. Why ask me to carry useless knowledge? You promised great things — test me, watch me! You assured me I'd be unterrified though swords flashed around me, though the blade's point grazed my throat. You assured me I'd be at ease though fires blazed around me, though a sudden whirlwind snatched up my ship and carried it across the whole sea. Give me the course of treatment that lets me despise pleasure and glory. Afterward you can teach me to untangle knotty problems, settle doubtful points, see through what's obscure. For now, teach me what I need to know!"

Farewell.

Letter 110: On True and False Riches

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

From my villa at Nomentum, I send you greetings and this instruction: keep a sound spirit within you — in other words, win the blessing of all the gods. For the man who has become a blessing to himself is assured of their grace and favor. Set aside, for now, the belief certain people hold that each of us has a god assigned as a kind of attendant — not a god of full rank, but one of the lower grade, one of those Ovid calls "plebeian gods." But while you set the belief aside, remember this: our ancestors who held it were being Stoics without knowing it, for they assigned a Genius or a Juno to every individual. Later on we'll investigate whether the gods have time on their hands to manage the affairs of private individuals. In the meantime, know this: whether we're allotted special guardians or abandoned to Fortune, you can lay no heavier curse on a man than to pray that he be at war with himself.

Not that there's any reason to ask the gods to turn hostile toward someone you think deserves punishment. They are hostile to such a man already, I maintain — even when he seems to be rising by their favor. Look carefully at how our affairs actually stand, not at what people say about them, and you'll understand that our so-called evils are more likely to help us than to hurt us. How often has "misfortune" been the source and beginning of happiness! How often have privileges we received with deep thanksgiving built themselves into a staircase up the side of a cliff — lifting still higher men who were already eminent, as if they had previously stood somewhere they could fall from in safety! Yet even that fall holds nothing evil, if you look at the end of it, beyond which nature lays no man lower. The universal limit is close; yes, close by is the point where the prosperous man is toppled — and the point where the unfortunate man is set free. It is we who stretch both distances out, lengthening them with our hopes and our fears.

If you're wise, though, measure everything by the human condition: keep your joys and your fears alike on a short leash. In fact, it's worth not rejoicing over anything for long, so that you won't fear anything for long. But why am I narrowing the scope of this evil? There is no reason to think anything is to be feared. Everything that rattles us and keeps us aflutter is empty. Not one of us has sifted out the truth; we've simply handed our fear along to one another. No one has dared to walk up to the thing that frightened him and get to know his fear — even the good hidden inside it. That's why falsehood and vanity keep their credit: nobody refutes them. Let's decide it's worth looking closely. Then it will become clear how fleeting, how unsure, how harmless the things we fear really are. The confusion in our spirits is just what Lucretius diagnosed:

  Like boys who cower, frightened, in the dark,
  we grown-ups fear things in the light of day.

Well then — aren't we more foolish than any child, we who are afraid in daylight? Except you were wrong, Lucretius: we are not afraid in the daylight. We have turned everything into darkness. We see neither what harms us nor what helps us; all our lives we blunder ahead, and we neither stop nor step more carefully for that. You see what madness it is to charge forward in the dark. And yet we're bent on it — on making ourselves harder to call back, from farther away. We don't know where we're going, but we rush at full speed in the direction we're straining toward.

Yet the light can begin to dawn, if we're willing. Only one thing can bring it about: acquiring, through knowledge, a familiarity with things divine and human — not just splashing in it but steeping ourselves in it; reviewing the same principles over and over, even when we understand them, and applying them again and again to ourselves; investigating what is good, what is evil, and what has been falsely labeled one or the other; investigating, finally, honor and baseness, and Providence. And the range of human intelligence isn't fenced in even there. It may explore beyond the universe itself — where it is headed, where it arose, and the ruin toward which all nature is hurrying so fast. We have dragged the soul away from that divine contemplation and down into mean, low work: to slave for greed; to abandon the universe and its frontiers and, under the orders of masters who will try anything, to pry beneath the earth and dig up whatever evil it can find there — unsatisfied with what was laid out in front of it for free.

God, the Father of us all, placed whatever was meant for our good ready at our hands. He didn't wait for us to search; he gave it voluntarily. What would harm us, he buried deep in the earth. We have no one to complain about but ourselves: against the will of Nature, who hid them, we hauled up into the light the materials of our own destruction. We have signed our souls over to pleasure — and serving pleasure is the beginning of every evil. We have surrendered ourselves to ambition and reputation, and to other aims equally idle and equally useless.

So what am I urging you to do? Nothing new — we aren't hunting cures for new diseases — but first of all this: figure out for yourself what is necessary and what is superfluous. What is necessary will meet you everywhere; what is superfluous always has to be hunted down, and hunted hard. But don't congratulate yourself too much for despising gilded couches and jeweled furniture. What virtue is there in despising useless things? Admire yourself when you've come to despise the necessities. It's no great feat to live without royal pomp, to feel no craving for thousand-pound boars or flamingo tongues or the other absurdities of a luxury so jaded with whole roasted game that it picks different bits from different animals. I'll admire you when you've learned to scorn even plain bread; when you've convinced yourself that grass grows for the needs of men as well as cattle; when you've discovered that the treetop can fill the belly — that belly into which we cram precious things as if it could keep what it receives. We should feed the stomach without being fussy about it. What does it matter what the stomach takes in, when it must lose everything it takes? You delight in the carefully arranged delicacies caught on land and sea — some prized when they reach the table fresh, others when they've been fed and fattened so long they nearly melt and can barely hold their own grease. You like their subtly engineered flavors. But I promise you: once those choice, elaborately seasoned dishes have gone down, one and the same corruption overtakes them all. Do you want to despise the pleasures of eating? Then look at where eating ends. I remember some words of Attalus that drew applause from the whole room:

"Riches deceived me for a long time. I would catch some gleam of them here and there and stand dazed. I assumed their hidden power matched their visible shine. Then, at a certain elaborate entertainment, I saw embossed work in silver and gold equal to the wealth of an entire city; colors and tapestries contrived to match objects worth more than gold or silver, brought not only from beyond our borders but from beyond our enemies' borders; here files of slave-boys picked for their training and their beauty, there throngs of slave-women, and everything else a prosperous and mighty empire could put forward after taking inventory of its possessions. What is this, I said to myself, but a deliberate stirring-up of human cravings that are inflamed enough already? What is the point of this whole parade of money? Did we gather here to learn what greed is? For my part, I walked out with less craving than I walked in with. I came to despise riches — not because they are useless, but because they are petty. Did you notice how that whole program, slow-moving and carefully staged as it was, played itself out inside a few hours? Has a business filled up this entire life of ours that could not fill up one whole day?

"Another thought struck me too: the riches seemed as useless to the men who owned them as to the men who watched. So now, whenever some spectacle of that sort dazzles my eyes — whenever I see a splendid palace, a polished corps of attendants, handsome bearers carrying a litter — I say to myself: Why wonder? Why gape? It's all show. These things are shown to you, not given to you — and even while they please, they are passing away. Turn instead to the true riches. Learn to be content with little, and call out with courage and greatness of soul: 'We have water, we have porridge — let us compete in happiness with Jupiter himself.' And why not, I ask you, make that challenge even without the porridge and the water? To hang the happy life on silver and gold is disgraceful — and to hang it on water and porridge is just as disgraceful. 'But what would I do without them?' someone says. You want the cure for want? Hunger ends hunger. Otherwise, what real difference is there between being enslaved by big things and being enslaved by small ones? What does it matter how little it is that Fortune can refuse you? Even your porridge and water can fall under someone else's control. Freedom belongs not to the man over whom Fortune has little power, but to the man over whom she has none at all. Here is what I mean: you must crave nothing, if you would go head to head with Jupiter — for Jupiter craves nothing."

That is what Attalus told us. If you're willing to think on these things often, you will work not to seem happy but to be happy — and to seem happy to yourself, rather than to others.

Farewell.

Letter 111: On the Vanity of Mental Gymnastics

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You asked me for a Latin word for the Greek sophismata. Many people have tried to pin a term on them, but no name has stuck — naturally enough, since the thing itself never won general acceptance among us, and so the name met resistance too. The word Cicero used seems the best fit to me: he called them cavillationes — quibbles. A man who surrenders himself to them weaves plenty of clever little traps, but he makes no progress toward actual living. He doesn't come out braver, or more restrained, or loftier in spirit.

But the man who has practiced philosophy as his own cure becomes great-souled, full of confidence, unbeatable — and bigger the closer you get to him. It's the same with high mountains: from far off they look lower than they are, and only when you approach do you see plainly how tall the peaks stand. That, my dear Lucilius, is the true philosopher — true in deeds, not in tricks. He stands on high ground: admirable, lofty, genuinely great. He doesn't stretch himself or walk on tiptoe like people who cheat their height, wanting to look taller than they are; he is content with his own size. And why shouldn't he be content, when he has grown to a height Fortune's hands can't reach? He is above earthly things and equal to himself in every situation — whether the current of life runs smooth, or he is tossed on rough and desperate seas. That kind of steadiness can never be won by the hair-splitting I just mentioned. The mind plays with quibbles and profits nothing; all it does is drag philosophy down from her heights onto the flat ground. The real philosopher climbs; the quibbler just walks in circles at sea level.

I wouldn't forbid you to practice such exercises now and then — but save them for a time when you want to do nothing. Their worst feature is this: they generate a charm of their own, gripping and holding the soul with a show of subtlety — while matters of real weight are calling for us, and a whole lifetime is barely enough to learn one single principle: to despise life. "What? Surely you meant 'control,' not 'despise'?" No. Controlling life is the second task. No one has ever controlled his life rightly who has not first learned to despise it.

Farewell.
