Letter 85

Letter 85: On Some Vain Syllogisms

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I had meant to spare you. I was leaving out the knotty problems that still remained, satisfied to give you just a taste of our school's arguments — the ones designed to prove that virtue, all by itself, is enough to complete the happy life. But now you order me to bring in the whole arsenal: our syllogisms, and the ones rival schools have devised to belittle us. If I'm willing to do that, the result won't be a letter; it will be a book. And I say it again, for the record: I take no pleasure in this kind of quibbling. I'm ashamed to march into the arena and do battle on behalf of gods and men armed with nothing but an awl.

Still, you asked. Here is the chain: "Whoever has prudence also has self-restraint; whoever has self-restraint is steadfast; whoever is steadfast is unperturbed; whoever is unperturbed is free from sadness; whoever is free from sadness is happy. Therefore the prudent man is happy, and prudence is enough for the happy life."

Certain Peripatetics — Aristotle's school — answer this by redefining the terms. "Unperturbed," they say, means a man who is rarely perturbed, and only moderately — not a man who is never perturbed at all. Likewise a man is called "free from sadness" if he isn't prone to sadness, if he falls into that unfortunate state seldom and not too deeply. Human nature being what it is, they argue, no one's spirit can be exempt from sadness; the wise man isn't overwhelmed by grief, but he is touched by it — and so on, all according to the teachings of their school. They don't abolish the passions this way; they only moderate them. But what a petty superiority we're granting the wise man, if he's merely braver than the biggest coward, happier than the most dejected, more self-controlled than the most out-of-control, and greater than the lowest of the low! Would Ladas, the legendary runner, brag about his speed by comparing himself with the lame and the feeble?

  She could skim over the tips of the standing grain
  and never bruise the tender ears as she ran,
  or dart across open sea, poised above the swell,
  and never wet her flying feet in the water.

If the wise man, on your definition, has any passions at all, his reason will be no match for them; it will be swept along as if on a rushing current — especially if you assign him not one passion to wrestle with, but all of them. And a crowd of passions, even moderate ones, can do more to him than the violence of one powerful passion. He craves money, but moderately. He has ambition, but it isn't fully roused yet. He has a temper, but it can be talked down. He's fickle, but not wildly or restlessly so. He has lust, but not the violent kind. We'd have an easier time with a man who had one full-blown vice than with a man who has all the vices, none of them in extreme form.

Besides, the size of a passion makes no difference. Whatever its size, it doesn't know how to obey, and it doesn't take advice. No animal obeys reason — not the wild ones, not the tame and gentle ones either; nature made them deaf to counsel. In the same way the passions don't follow and don't listen, however slight they are. Tigers and lions never put off their wildness. They set it aside sometimes, and then, just when you're least prepared, that softened ferocity flares back into savagery. Vices are never genuinely tamed.

Again: if reason prevails, the passions never even get started. But if they get moving against reason's will, they'll keep moving against reason's will. It's easier to stop them at the start than to steer them once they've gathered force. So this halfway position is misleading and useless. Treat it exactly the way you'd treat the claim that we ought to be "moderately" insane, or "moderately" sick. Virtue alone possesses moderation; the evils that afflict the mind don't come in moderate sizes. You can remove them more easily than you can manage them. Can anyone doubt that the vices of the human mind, once they've become chronic and hardened — at that point we call them "diseases" — are beyond control? Greed, cruelty, brutality: uncontrollable. Then the passions are beyond control too, because the passions are the road we take to reach the vices.

Again: grant any allowance to sadness, fear, desire, and the rest of the wrong impulses, and they'll slip out of our jurisdiction entirely. Why? Because the triggers that rouse them lie outside our power. So they'll grow in proportion as their causes are greater or smaller. Fear will swell as the terrifying thing looks bigger or comes closer; desire will sharpen as the hope of a bigger prize calls it into action. If the existence of the passions isn't in our control, neither is their strength; once you let them get started, they'll grow right along with their causes, and they'll end up as big as they're going to grow. Besides, however small these evils are, they get bigger. Whatever is harmful never keeps within bounds. However trifling a disease is at the beginning, it creeps forward — and sometimes the slightest worsening of an illness is what finishes off a weakened body.

But what folly it is, when the beginnings of a thing lie outside our control, to believe that its endings lie within it! How can I have the power to shut something down when I didn't have the power to keep it out? It's easier to bar the door than to subdue the intruder once you've let him in.

Some have drawn this distinction: "The self-controlled and wise man is at peace in the settled attitude and habit of his mind, but not in what actually befalls him. As far as his habit of mind goes, he isn't perturbed or saddened or afraid; but plenty of outside causes strike him and bring disturbance with them." What they mean is this: "He's not a man of angry temperament, but he still sometimes gives way to anger," and "He's not a fearful man, but he still sometimes feels fear." He's free of the fault, in other words, but not free of the feeling. But let fear in once, and through repeated use it turns into a vice; let anger into the mind, and it corrupts what was formerly the habit of a mind without anger. Besides, if the wise man doesn't despise everything that comes at him from outside — if he ever fears anything at all — then when the moment arrives to march bravely against the spear or the flames for his country, his laws, and his liberty, he'll go out reluctantly, with his spirit dragging. And that kind of inner contradiction doesn't suit the wise man.

Then there's another point: we should be careful not to tangle together two principles that ought to be tested separately. It's one conclusion, reached on its own, that the honorable is the only good; and a separate conclusion that virtue is sufficient for the happy life. If the honorable is the only good, everyone agrees that virtue is enough for living happily. But it doesn't run backward: if virtue alone makes men happy, no one will concede on that basis that the honorable is the only good. Xenocrates and Speusippus, Plato's successors, hold that a man can become happy by virtue alone without holding that the honorable is the only good. Epicurus, too, judges that the man who has virtue is happy — but that virtue by itself isn't sufficient for the happy life, because what makes him happy is the pleasure that results from virtue, not the virtue itself. A useless distinction. For the same philosopher declares that virtue never exists without pleasure; so if virtue is always joined to pleasure, always inseparable from it, then virtue is sufficient all by itself. It keeps pleasure in its company; even alone, it is never without it. And it's absurd to say that a man will be happy by virtue alone, and yet not completely happy. I can't discover how that would even work. The happy life contains within itself a perfect good, a good that can't be surpassed; and if a man has that good, his life is completely happy.

Now, if the life of the gods contains nothing greater or better, and the happy life is divine, then there is no further height a man can be raised to. Moreover, if the happy life lacks nothing, then every happy life is perfect: it is happy and supremely happy at the same time. Do you have any doubt that the happy life is the Supreme Good? Then if it possesses the Supreme Good, it is supremely happy. Just as the Supreme Good doesn't admit of increase — what could stand above the supreme? — so the happy life can't be increased either, since it doesn't exist without the Supreme Good. Bring in one man who is "happier" than another, and you'll soon bring in one who is "much happier"; you'll end up slicing the Supreme Good into countless gradations — when, as I understand it, the Supreme Good is precisely the good that has no degree above it. If one man is less happy than another, it follows that he'd rather have the happier man's life than his own. But the happy man prefers no one's life to his own. Either alternative is unbelievable: that there should be something left for a happy man to wish for beyond what is, or that he should fail to prefer what's better than what he already has. Certainly the more prudent he is, the more he'll strive for the best and want to reach it by every possible means. But how can a man be happy who is still able — no, still bound — to crave something more?

I'll tell you where this error comes from: people don't understand that the happy life is a single whole. It's the quality of the life, not its size, that sets it on the highest plane. That's why there is complete equality between the long life and the short one, between the life spread wide and the life kept narrow, between the life whose influence is felt in many places and many directions and the life confined to a single pursuit. Whoever measures life by number, by size, or by parts strips it of what makes it distinctive. And in a happy life, what is the distinctive thing? Its fullness. Satiety, I take it, is the limit of our eating and drinking. A eats more and B eats less — what difference does it make? Each is now full. A drinks more and B drinks less — what difference? Neither is thirsty anymore. A lives many years and B lives few: no matter, so long as A's many years hold as much happiness as B's few. The man you insist is "less happy" isn't happy at all; the word doesn't come in smaller sizes.

"Whoever is brave is fearless; whoever is fearless is free from sadness; whoever is free from sadness is happy." This syllogism is our own school's work. The attempted refutation goes like this: we Stoics are treating as settled a premise that is false and hotly disputed — namely, that the brave man is fearless. "What!" they say. "The brave man won't fear the evils hanging over his head? That's the condition of a madman, a lunatic — not a brave man. The brave man feels fear, certainly, only very slightly; he isn't absolutely free of it." But people who say this are doubling back to their old move: treating smaller vices as the equivalent of virtues. The man who feels fear rarely and slightly isn't free of the evil; he just suffers a milder case of it. "Not so," comes the reply. "I say a man is mad if he doesn't fear the evils hanging over his head." What you say is perfectly true — if the things threatening him really are evils. But if he knows they aren't evils, if he believes the only evil is disgrace, then he'll be bound to face dangers without anxiety and to despise the things other men can't help fearing. Or, if not fearing evils is the mark of a fool and a madman, then it follows that the wiser a man is, the more afraid he ought to be!

"So it's Stoic doctrine, then, that the brave man will expose himself to dangers." Not at all. He won't fear them — but he will avoid them. Caution suits him; fear does not. "What then? Won't he fear death, prison, burning, and all the other missiles Fortune throws?" Not at all, because he knows they aren't evils; they only look like evils. He counts all these things among the bogeymen of human existence. Paint him the whole picture: slavery, the lash, chains, want, limbs mangled by disease or by torture — add whatever else you like. He'll file every one of them under panics of a deranged mind. These things are fearful only to the fearful. Or do you call that an evil which, some day, we may be driven to choose freely, of our own accord?

Then what is an evil, you ask? Giving in to the things that are called evils — surrendering your liberty into their hands, when you ought to suffer anything to preserve that liberty. Liberty is lost unless we despise the things that put the yoke on our necks. If people knew what bravery really was, they'd have no doubts about how a brave man should act. Bravery isn't thoughtless recklessness, or a love of danger, or an appetite for frightening situations. It is the knowledge that lets us tell what is an evil from what is not. Bravery takes the most attentive care of itself — and, at the same time, endures with the greatest patience everything that wears the false mask of evil. "What then?" comes the question. "If the sword is brandished over your brave man's neck, if he's run through in one place and then another, if he watches his own entrails spill into his lap, if the torture is paused and then renewed so he'll feel it more keenly, if the blood runs fresh from wounds that had only just stopped bleeding — is he not afraid? Will you tell me he hasn't felt pain, either?" He has felt pain; no human virtue can strip away feeling. But he has no fear. Unconquered, he looks down on his sufferings from a great height. Do you ask what spirit is in him at such a moment? The spirit of a man comforting a sick friend.

"What is evil does harm; what does harm makes a man worse; but pain and poverty don't make a man worse; therefore they are not evils." "Your premise is wrong," says the objector, "because what harms a man doesn't necessarily make him worse. The storm and the squall do harm to the pilot, but they don't make him a worse pilot for all that." Some of our own Stoics answer this as follows: "The pilot is made a worse pilot by storms and squalls, in the sense that he can't carry out his intention and hold his course. In his art he becomes no worse a pilot; in his work, he does become worse." To which the Peripatetics retort: "Then poverty will make even the wise man worse, and so will pain, and so will everything of that sort. Those things won't strip him of his virtue, but they will hinder virtue's work."

That would be a fair point, except that the pilot and the wise man are two different kinds of person. The wise man's aim in conducting his life is not to accomplish whatever he attempts at any cost, but to do everything rightly. The pilot's aim is to bring his ship into port at any cost. The arts are servants: they must deliver what they promise. Wisdom is mistress and ruler. The arts render a slave's service to life; wisdom gives the orders.

For my own part, I'd give a different answer altogether: the pilot's art is never made worse by the storm — and neither is his practice of it. The pilot never promised you a prosperous voyage; he promised a competent performance of his job — that is, expert knowledge of steering a ship. And the harder Fortune presses him, the more that expert knowledge shows. The man who can say, "Neptune, you will never sink this ship except on an even keel," has met the full requirement of his art. The storm doesn't hinder the pilot's work — only his success. "What," you say, "isn't a pilot harmed by whatever keeps him from making port, wrecks all his efforts, drags him out to sea, holds his ship pinned, or strips her masts?" No — it harms him as a voyager, not as a pilot; as a pilot, he's untouched. So far is the storm from hindering the pilot's art that it actually puts the art on display: anyone, as the proverb says, is a pilot on a calm sea. These disasters obstruct the voyage, not the steersman as steersman. A pilot plays a double role. One he shares with everyone on board, because he is a passenger too. The other is his alone, because he is the pilot. The storm harms him as a passenger, not as a pilot.

And there's more. The pilot's art is a good that belongs to other people: it concerns his passengers, the way a doctor's art concerns his patients. But the wise man's good is a common good — it belongs both to the people he lives among and to himself. So perhaps the pilot really is harmed, since the services he promised to others are blocked by the storm. But the wise man is not harmed by poverty, or by pain, or by any of life's other storms. Not all of his functions are shut down — only the ones directed at other people. He himself is always in action, and he is greatest in performance at the very moment Fortune has blocked his road, because that is when he's doing the business of wisdom itself — and wisdom, as I've already said, is both a good for others and his own good too. Nor is he barred from helping others even when hard circumstances are pressing him down. His poverty prevents him from showing how the state should be managed; but he teaches, all the same, how poverty should be managed. His work goes on through the whole of his life.

So no stroke of Fortune, no external circumstance, can shut the wise man off from action. The very thing that claims his attention is what keeps him from attending to other things. He stands ready for either outcome: if goods come, he governs them; if evils come, he conquers them. He has trained himself this thoroughly, I mean: to make his virtue visible in prosperity and in adversity alike, keeping his eyes on virtue itself, not on the materials virtue happens to be working with. So neither poverty nor pain nor anything else that turns the inexperienced aside and sends them stampeding will pull him off his course. Do you suppose he's weighed down by evils? He makes use of them. Phidias, the great sculptor, didn't know how to make statues only out of ivory; he made them out of bronze too. If you'd handed him marble, or some still cheaper material, he'd have carved the best statue that material allowed. In the same way, the wise man will develop virtue amid wealth if he can, and if not, in poverty; in his own country if possible, and if not, in exile; as a commander if possible, and if not, as a common soldier; in sound health if possible, and if not, enfeebled. Whatever Fortune hands him, he will make something remarkable out of it.

Animal-tamers never miss. They take the most savage beasts — creatures that would terrify anyone who stumbled on them — and bend them to man's will; and not content with driving out the ferocity, they gentle them until beast and man live under the same roof. The trainer puts his hand in the lion's mouth; the keeper kisses his tiger; a small Ethiopian boy orders the elephant down onto its knees, or has it walk the rope. Just so, the wise man is an expert at taming evils. Pain, want, disgrace, prison, exile — dreaded everywhere — when they come up against the wise man, they are tamed.

Farewell.
