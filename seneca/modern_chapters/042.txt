Letter 99: On Consolation to the Bereaved

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I'm enclosing a copy of the letter I wrote to Marullus when he had lost his little son and was reported to be grieving with less self-command than a man should. In it I did not follow the usual rules of condolence, because I didn't think he should be handled gently — in my judgment he deserved criticism more than comfort. When a man has just been struck and is struggling under a fresh, heavy wound, you have to humor him for a while: let him grieve it out, or at least discharge the first shock. But people who have settled into grief as an indulgence should be called out at once, and taught that there is such a thing as foolishness even in tears.

"You're looking for comfort? Let me give you a scolding instead. You are taking your son's death the way a woman would — what would you do if you had lost a close friend? A son died: a little child, his promise still unknown; a small piece of time is gone. We go hunting for reasons to grieve; we'd even file unfair complaints against Fortune, as if Fortune would never hand us a just one! I honestly believed you had spirit enough to face solid troubles, let alone these shadowy ones that men moan over out of sheer habit. If you had lost a friend — and that is the greatest blow of all — your task would have been to work at rejoicing that you had him, rather than mourning that you lost him.

"But most men never add up their gains, never count their joys. Grief like yours has this evil among its others: it is not only useless, it is ungrateful. Was it all for nothing, then, that you had such a friend? So many years, such close companionship, such intimate sharing of everything that mattered — did all of it accomplish nothing? Do you bury the friendship along with the friend? And why lament losing him, if having him did you no good? Believe me, a great part of those we have loved stays with us, even after chance has taken their persons away. The past is ours, and nothing we hold is more secure than what has already been. We are ungrateful for what we've received because we are forever hoping for the future — as if the future, supposing any future is ours at all, won't quickly blend into the past. People who take pleasure only in the present put narrow limits on their enjoyment; the future and the past both serve our delight — the one with anticipation, the other with memory — but the one is uncertain and may never arrive, while the other cannot un-happen.

"What madness, then, to lose our grip on the surest thing we own! Let us rest content with the pleasures we have already drunk down — so long as, while we drank them, the soul wasn't pierced like a sieve, losing whatever it received. There are countless cases of men who have buried sons in the prime of manhood without a tear — men who walked back from the funeral pyre to the Senate chamber, or to whatever public duty, and got straight to work on something else. And rightly. First, grieving is pointless if grief brings you no help. Second, it is unfair to complain about what has happened to one man when it lies in store for all. Third, it is foolish to mourn a loss when the gap between the lost and the mourner is so slight. We should take these things with a calmer spirit, because we follow close behind the ones we have lost.

"Look at the speed of time — the swiftest thing there is. Consider the shortness of the course we run at full tilt. Watch this whole throng of humanity straining toward the same finish line, separated by the briefest intervals even when they seem longest: the one you count as lost has simply been sent ahead. And what is more irrational than to weep for the man ahead of you on a road you yourself must travel? Does a man weep over an event he knew would happen? If he never thought of death as part of the human bargain, he was only cheating himself. Does a man weep over an event he has been admitting all along is unavoidable? Whoever complains that someone died is complaining that he was human. We are all bound by the same terms: whoever is granted birth is scheduled for death. Stretches of time separate us; death levels us. The span between our first day and our last is shifting and uncertain: measure it by its troubles and it is long even for a boy; measure it by its speed and it is scanty even for an old man. Everything is slippery, treacherous, more changeable than any weather. Everything is tossed about and turns into its opposite at Fortune's whim, and in all this churning of mortal affairs only one thing is guaranteed to anyone — death. Yet everybody complains about the one thing in which no one is ever deceived. 'But he died a boy.' I'm not yet prepared to argue that whoever finishes life quickly has gotten the better bargain; so consider instead the man who has grown old. How little has he gained over the child! Set before your mind's eye the vast abyss of time; take in the whole universe; then compare what we call a human life with infinity — and you will see how tiny a thing we pray for, how little it is we strain to stretch. How much of that time is taken up with weeping, how much with worry! How much with praying for death before death arrives, how much with our health, how much with our fears! How much goes to the years when we were too green to use, or used badly! And half of the whole is spent asleep. Add our labors, our griefs, our dangers — and you will grasp that even in the longest life, actual living is the smallest part. And yet who will admit that a man is not better off for being allowed to go home early, his journey finished before it wore him out? Life is neither a good nor an evil; it is simply the place where goods and evils happen. So this little boy has lost nothing except a gamble in which loss was more certain than gain. He might have turned out temperate and sensible; with your care he might have been shaped into something better; but — and this fear is the more reasonable one — he might have turned out just like the many. Look at the young men of the noblest houses, whose extravagance has flung them into the arena; look at the men who serve their own lusts and other people's, whose days never pass without a drunken bout or some spectacular disgrace — and it will be clear to you that there was more to fear than to hope for.

"So you should not go inviting excuses for grief, or swell small burdens with indignation. I'm not urging you to strain and rise to some great height; I don't think so little of you as to believe you need to summon your whole stock of virtue for this trouble. This is not pain; it is a sting — and it is you yourself who are turning it into pain.

"Philosophy has certainly done you good service if you can bear bravely the loss of a boy still better known to his nurse than to his father! What then — am I now advising you to be hard-hearted? Do I want your face unmoved at the funeral itself, your soul not permitted even the pinch of pain? Not at all. That would be lack of feeling, not virtue — to watch the burial of your own dear ones with the same expression you wore when they were alive, and to feel nothing at the first bereavement in your family. But suppose I did forbid you to feel: certain feelings claim their own rights. Tears fall no matter how we fight to check them, and in falling they ease the soul. So what shall we do? Let them fall — but let us not command them to. Let us weep as much as emotion floods our eyes, not as much as imitation demands. Let us add nothing to natural grief, and not swell it to match other people's example. The performance of grief demands more than grief itself: how few men are sad in their own company! They wail the louder for being heard; people who are quiet and contained when alone break into fresh fits of tears the moment they see others near them. That is when they lay violent hands on themselves — which they could have done more easily with no one there to stop them; that is when they pray for death; that is when they throw themselves off their couches. Their grief slackens the moment the audience leaves. Here, as everywhere, we are stuck in the same fault: conforming to the pattern of the many, looking to convention instead of duty. We abandon nature and hand ourselves over to the mob — never good advisers in anything, and in this matter, as in all others, utterly inconsistent. People see a man bearing his grief bravely: they call him unloving and cold-hearted. They see a man collapse and cling to his dead: they call him womanish and weak. Everything, therefore, should be brought back to reason. And nothing is more foolish than to court a reputation for sorrow and give tears your official sanction; for I hold that in a wise man some tears fall by consent, and others by their own force.

"Here is the difference. When the first news of a bitter loss strikes us — when we hold the body that will soon pass from our arms to the funeral flames — then tears are wrung from us by nature's own necessity: the life-force, struck by grief's blow, shakes the whole body, and the eyes with it, pressing out the moisture that lies within. Tears like these are forced out, against our will. Different are the tears we allow to escape when we linger in memory over those we have lost. There is a certain sweet sadness in them — a pleasant voice remembered, a warm conversation, the busy devotion of the old days — and then the eyes loosen, as it were, with joy. This kind of weeping we permit; the other kind overpowers us.

"So there is no reason why, just because a group of people is standing in front of you or sitting at your side, you should either dam up your tears or pour them out. Held back or let flow, they are never so disgraceful as when they are faked. Let them come naturally. Tears can fall from eyes that are calm and at peace; they often flow without touching the wise man's authority — with such measure that they show no lack of feeling and no lack of self-respect. We can, I assure you, obey nature and keep our dignity. I have seen men worthy of reverence at the burial of their own dear ones — love written plainly on their faces even with the whole apparatus of mourning stripped away — with nothing in their bearing beyond what genuine emotion allowed. There is a gracefulness even in grief. The wise man should cultivate it: in tears, as in everything else, there is such a thing as enough. It is the unwise whose sorrows, like their joys, overflow.

"Accept what is inevitable with an unruffled spirit. What can happen that is past believing? What that is new? How many men at this very moment are arranging funerals! How many are buying grave-clothes! How many are mourning after you yourself have finished mourning! Every time you reflect that your boy has ceased to be, reflect also on man — who holds no sure promise of anything, whom Fortune does not necessarily escort all the way to old age, but lets go at whatever point she pleases. Do speak of the departed often, and cherish his memory as much as you can. The memory will come back to you all the more often if it comes without bitterness; no one enjoys spending time with a sorrowful companion, much less with sorrow itself. And whatever things he said, whatever little jokes — child as he was — gave you pleasure to hear, recall them again and again; tell yourself with confidence that he could have made good on the hopes you, his father, had for him. To forget the beloved dead, to bury their memory along with their bodies, to weep for them extravagantly and afterward scarcely think of them — that is the mark of a soul beneath a man's. That is how birds and beasts love their young: the affection flares up hot, almost to madness, and cools completely when its object dies. That does not suit a man of sense. Keep remembering; stop mourning. And I do not in the least approve of Metrodorus's remark — that there is a certain pleasure akin to sadness, and that at times like these one ought to hunt it down. Those are Metrodorus's actual words. I have no doubt how you will feel about them; for what is baser than to chase pleasure in the very middle of mourning — no, by means of mourning — and to hunt among your own tears for something that feels good? And these are the men who accuse us of too much strictness, who slander our teaching as harsh — because we say grief should either not be admitted into the soul at all, or should be shown the door at once. But which is harder to believe, which more inhuman: to feel no grief at the loss of a friend, or to go hawking after pleasure in the middle of grief? What we Stoics advise is honorable: when emotion has drawn a moderate flow of tears and has, so to speak, ceased to boil, the soul is not to be surrendered to grief. But what do you mean, Metrodorus — that grief should come with a blending of pleasure? That is the candy method of pacifying children; that is how we quiet a crying infant, by pouring milk down its throat.

"Even at the moment your son's body is on the pyre, or your friend is drawing his last breath, you will not let your pleasure pause — you would rather tickle your grief with it? Which is more honorable: to remove grief from your soul, or to invite pleasure in to keep grief company? Invite, did I say? No — chase after it, and out of grief's own hands. 'There is a certain pleasure related to sadness,' he says. We Stoics may say that; you may not. The only good you recognize is pleasure, and the only evil, pain — and what kinship can there be between a good and an evil? But suppose such kinship exists: is now, of all times, the moment to dig it up? Shall we ransack grief itself, to see what delight and pleasure might be found around it? Some remedies, beneficial for one part of the body, cannot be applied to another, because there they would be offensive and unfit; what would do good in one case at no cost to decency becomes shameful because of where the wound sits. Aren't you likewise ashamed to cure sorrow with pleasure? No — this sore spot needs harsher treatment. Say this instead: no sensation of evil can reach a man who is dead — for if it can reach him, he is not dead. Nothing, I say, can hurt a man who is nothing; if he can be hurt, he is alive. Do you think him badly off because he no longer exists, or because he still exists as somebody? No torment can come to him from not existing — for what can be felt by one who is not there? — and none from existing, since he has escaped the greatest disadvantage death carries: not existing at all.

"And let us say this, too, to the man who mourns and misses one taken young: measured against eternity, young or old, we all live equally short lives. Of all time, less comes to us than anyone could call least — for 'least' is at any rate some fraction, but this life of ours is next to nothing. And yet — fools that we are — we spread it out as if it were broad and grand!

"I have written you all this not because you should expect a cure from me this late — it is clear to me that you have already told yourself everything you will read here — but to rebuke you even for the brief delay in which you slipped away from your true self, and to brace you for what comes next: rouse your spirit against Fortune, and keep watch for her missiles — not as if they might come, but as things that are bound to come."

Farewell.

Letter 100: On the Writings of Fabianus

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You write that you read Fabianus Papirius's book, The Duties of a Citizen, with the greatest eagerness, that it fell short of your expectations — and then, forgetting you are dealing with a philosopher, you start criticizing his prose.

Suppose your claim is true — that he pours his words out rather than placing them. Let me tell you at the start that this trait has a charm of its own, a grace that belongs to a smoothly gliding style. It matters a great deal, I maintain, whether prose tumbles out or flows along. And there is a further difference here, which I'll make clear: Fabianus's words do not spill, to my mind — his words flow like a river, abundant without confusion, yet never slow. His style openly declares and announces that he has not spent long hours kneading his material and twisting it into shape. But even granting the facts as you'd have them: the man was building character, not sentences, and he wrote his words for the mind, not for the ear. Besides, had he been delivering them in person, you would have had no time to inspect the details — the whole would have swept you along. And as a rule, what pleases by its swiftness loses value when you take it in hand to read.

Even so, that very power of catching you at first sight is a great advantage, whether or not careful inspection turns up something to criticize. If you ask me, the writer who forces approval is greater than the one who earns it — though I know the second kind is safer; I know he can post surer guarantees for the future. A fussy, meticulous way of writing does not suit the philosopher. If he is timid about words, when will he ever be brave and steady? When will he ever show what he is made of? Fabianus's style was not careless; it was confident. That is why you will find nothing shoddy in his work: the words are well chosen but not hunted down; not wrenched out of order and inverted after the current fashion; and they carry distinction even though they come from ordinary speech. There you have honorable, splendid thoughts, not shackled into aphorisms, but spoken with room to breathe. Of course we will notice passages not pruned enough, not built with enough care, missing the polish that is in vogue nowadays; but survey the whole, and you will find no clever subtleties spun about nothing. There may be no variety of marbles, no water piped from room to room, no "poor man's rooms," none of the other devices luxury piles up when plain charm is not enough — but, as the common phrase puts it, it is a good house to live in.

Besides, opinions differ about style itself. Some want it filed smooth of every roughness; some take such pleasure in abruptness that wherever a passage happens to spread out smoothly, they would deliberately break it up, scattering the closing words so the sentences land where you least expect. Read Cicero: his style is all of a piece; it moves at a measured pace, gentle without going soft. Asinius Pollio's, by contrast, is bumpy, jerky, quitting where you least expect it. Cicero always brings things to rest gradually; Pollio drops them mid-stride — except in the very few places where he holds to a fixed rhythm and a single pattern.

You say next that everything in Fabianus strikes you as commonplace, lacking lift; I hold that he is free of any such fault. His style is not commonplace — it is calm, fitted to his peaceful and well-ordered mind: not low, but level. It lacks the orator's punch and spur, which is what you are looking for, and the sudden jolt of epigrams. But look, please, at the whole work — how well ordered it is. There is a distinction in it. His style does not possess dignity; it suggests it.

Name someone you would rank above Fabianus. Cicero, say — whose books on philosophy are nearly as numerous as Fabianus's own. Granted; but it is no small thing to be less than the greatest. Or Asinius Pollio, say. Granted again; I will content myself with answering: it is a distinction to be third in a field this great. Add Livy too, if you like — for Livy wrote dialogues that belong to history as much as to philosophy, and works that deal with philosophy outright. I will yield on Livy as well. But consider how many writers Fabianus outranks, if only three surpass him — and those three the greatest masters of eloquence.

But, you may say, he does not offer everything. His style is elevated but not muscular; it flows abundantly but lacks force and sweep; it is not crystal, though it is clear. "You would look in vain," you object, "for any savage denunciation of vice, any brave words in the face of danger, any proud defiance of Fortune, any scornful jab at ambition. I want to see luxury rebuked, lust condemned, willfulness broken. Give me the orator's edge, the tragedian's height, the comedian's fine touch." You want him to lean on the smallest thing of all — phrasing; but he has sworn allegiance to the greatness of his subject, and eloquence follows him like a shadow, without his aiming at it.

Our author will not, I grant, examine every detail, or drill into it, or weigh and press each separate word. I admit it. Plenty of phrases will fall short or fail to strike home, and at times the style will slide along lazily. But there will be light everywhere throughout the work; there will be long stretches that never tire the reader. And finally, he offers you this: he makes it plain that he meant what he wrote. You will understand that his aim was for you to know what pleased him — not for him to please you. All of it works toward progress and toward sanity, with no fishing for applause.

I do not doubt that his writings are just as I have described them, though I am reaching back for him rather than holding a firm memory; the general flavor of his work stays with me — not from a careful recent reading, but in outline, the way things do after an acquaintance of long ago. But certainly, whenever I heard him lecture, this is how his work struck me: not dense, but full — the kind that would fire young men of promise and rouse their ambition to become like him, without making them despair of surpassing him. And that seems to me the most helpful encouragement of all; for it is disheartening to awaken in a man the desire to emulate while taking away the hope of it. At any rate, his language flowed freely, and though you might not approve of every detail, the whole was noble.

Farewell.
