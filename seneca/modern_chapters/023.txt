Letter 75: On the Diseases of the Soul

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You've been complaining that my letters are carelessly written. Well, who talks carefully unless he wants to sound affected? I want my letters to be exactly what my conversation would be if we were sitting together, or out taking a walk — spontaneous and easy. There is nothing strained or artificial in them. If I could, I would rather show you what I feel than tell you. Even if I were arguing a point, I wouldn't stamp my foot or wave my arms or raise my voice; I'd leave all that to the orators, and be content to have gotten my feelings across to you without dressing them up or dragging them down. The one thing I'd like to convince you of completely is this: I feel everything I say — and I don't just feel it, I'm wedded to it. A man kisses his mistress one way and his children another; but in the father's embrace too, holy and restrained as it is, there is plenty of love on display.

Not that I want our conversation about matters this important to be thin and dry — even philosophy doesn't refuse the company of wit. But you shouldn't spend much attention on mere words. Here is the kernel of my idea: let us say what we feel, and feel what we say; let speech harmonize with life. A man has kept his promise when he is the same person whether you see him or hear him. We will see exactly what sort of man he is, and how large a man he is — provided he is one and the same throughout. Let our words aim not to please, but to help. Of course, if eloquence comes to you without labor — if you're naturally gifted, or can pick it up at little cost — then take it and put it to the noblest possible use. But let it be the kind of eloquence that displays the facts rather than itself. Eloquence and the other arts are entirely a matter of cleverness; our business here is the soul.

A sick man doesn't call in a doctor for his speaking voice. If the doctor who can cure him also happens to talk elegantly about the treatment, fine — the patient will take it in good part. But he'll find no reason to congratulate himself on landing an eloquent physician. It's no different from discovering that a skilled ship's pilot is also handsome. Why are you tickling my ears? Why are you entertaining me? That is not the business at hand. I am here to be cauterized, operated on, put on a diet. That's what you were called in to do.

You are being asked to cure a disease that is chronic and serious — one that infects the whole human race. You have as big a job on your hands as a doctor during a plague. And you're worried about words? Be glad right now if you can cope with things. When will you learn everything there is to learn? When will you plant what you've learned so firmly in your mind that it cannot escape? When will you put it all into practice? Because these things can't simply be committed to memory, like other subjects; they have to be tested in action. The happy man is not the one who knows them but the one who does them.

"What?" you say. "Are there no degrees of happiness below your 'happy' man? Is it a sheer cliff just beneath wisdom?" I don't think so. The man who is making progress still counts, technically, among the fools — but a long interval separates him from them. And among the people making progress themselves there are wide gaps. They fall into three classes, as certain philosophers hold.

First come those who don't yet possess wisdom but have already taken up a position nearby — though even "nearby" is still outside. Who are they? If you ask me, they are the men who have already laid aside all their passions and vices, who have learned what to embrace — but whose assurance is still untested. They haven't yet put their good to use. Still, from now on they can't fall back into the faults they escaped. They have already reached a point from which there is no slipping backward — but they don't know it yet. As I remember writing in another letter, "They don't know that they know." It is now given to them to enjoy their good, but not yet to be sure of it. Some define this class — this class of men making progress — as people who have escaped the diseases of the mind but not yet its passions, and who are still standing on slippery ground; because no one is beyond the reach of evil unless he has cleared it away entirely, and no one has cleared it away entirely except the man who has put wisdom in its place.

I've explained before, more than once, the difference between the diseases of the mind and its passions. But let me remind you. The diseases are hardened, chronic vices, like greed and ambition; they have wrapped the mind in too tight a grip and started to become its permanent afflictions. A brief definition: a disease is a persistent distortion of judgment, which makes things that are only mildly worth wanting look intensely worth wanting. Or if you prefer, put it this way: it is straining too hard after things that deserve only mild pursuit or none at all, or prizing highly things that deserve little value or none. The passions are objectionable impulses of the spirit, sudden and violent; when they come often and go unattended, they settle into a state of disease. Think of a cold: one attack, not yet habitual, produces a cough; let it become regular and chronic, and it produces consumption. So we can say that the people who have made the most progress are beyond the reach of the diseases — but they still feel the passions, even when they are very near perfection.

The second class is made up of those who have laid aside both the greatest ills of the mind and its passions, but who don't yet hold their immunity securely. They can still slip back into their former state.

The third class are past many vices — the great vices especially — but not past all of them. They've escaped greed, say, but still feel anger; lust no longer troubles them, but ambition still does; they no longer desire, but they still fear. And because they fear, they stand firm against some things and give way to others: they scorn death and are terrified of pain.

Let's dwell on that for a moment. It will go well with us if we're admitted even to this third class. The second stage takes great good luck in your natural gifts, plus great and unceasing devotion to study. But even the third rank is nothing to sneer at. Think of the mass of evils you see all around you — look how there is no crime without its example, how far wickedness advances every single day, how much goes wrong in the home and in public life. You'll see we're making a considerable gain if we're not counted among the worst.

"But as for me," you say, "I hope I have it in me to rise higher than that!" I would pray for this rather than promise it — we've been beaten to the start. We hurry toward virtue with our vices hanging on us. I'm ashamed to say it, but we honor what is honorable only in our spare time. And yet what a rich reward is waiting if we would only break off the business that keeps cutting ahead of us and the evils that cling to us with such stubborn tenacity! Then neither desire nor fear will rout us. Undisturbed by fears, unspoiled by pleasures, we will dread neither death nor the gods; we will know that death is no evil and that the gods are not powers of evil. What harms is no stronger than what is harmed, and what is utterly good has no power to harm at all. If we ever escape from these low dregs to that high and lofty place, what awaits us is peace of mind and — once every error has been driven out — perfect freedom. You ask what that freedom is? Fearing no man and no god; wanting nothing shameful and nothing in excess; holding supreme power over yourself. And it is a priceless good to be master of yourself.

Farewell.
