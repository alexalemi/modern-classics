Letter 94

Letter 94: On the Value of Advice

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

There is a branch of philosophy that deals in precepts — advice fitted to the individual case rather than framed for the whole human race. It tells a husband how to behave toward his wife, a father how to bring up his children, a master how to govern his slaves. Some philosophers accept this branch as the only one worth having and throw out all the rest, on the grounds that the rest wanders off beyond anything we practically need — as if anyone could give advice about one portion of life without first gaining a knowledge of life as a whole!

Aristo the Stoic takes the opposite view. He thinks this branch counts for very little. It doesn't sink into the mind, he says; it holds nothing but old wives' precepts. The greatest benefit comes from philosophy's actual doctrines, from the definition of the Supreme Good. Once a man has completely understood that definition and thoroughly learned it, he can frame his own precept for whatever the situation demands. A student of javelin-throwing keeps aiming at one fixed target, training his hand to guide the throw; and once instruction and practice have given him the skill, he can use it against any target he pleases — he has learned to hit not some random object, but precisely the thing he aims at. In the same way, the man who has equipped himself for the whole of life needs no advising on each separate item, because he is now trained to meet his problem as a whole. He doesn't merely know how to live with his wife or his son; he knows how to live rightly — and how to live with wife and children is included in that knowledge.

Cleanthes takes a middle line: this branch of wisdom is useful, yes, but a feeble thing unless it flows from general principles — unless it rests on philosophy's actual doctrines and main headings. So the subject splits into two separate questions. First: is precept-giving useful or useless? And second: can it by itself produce a good man? In other words, is it superfluous — or does it make everything else superfluous?

Those who call this branch superfluous argue like this: "If an object held in front of the eyes blocks the vision, it has to be removed. As long as it's in the way, it is a waste of time to hand out precepts like 'Walk this way; stretch your hand out in that direction.' In the same way, when something blinds a man's soul and keeps him from seeing his line of duty clearly, there is no use in advising him: 'Live this way with your father, that way with your wife.' Precepts will do no good while the mind is clouded with error; only when the cloud is scattered will it become clear what his duty is in each case. Otherwise you are merely showing a sick man what he ought to do if he were well, instead of making him well. Suppose you're trying to teach a poor man the art of acting rich — how can that be managed while his poverty stays put? You are trying to explain to a starving man how to play the part of a man with a full stomach, when what he needs first is relief from the hunger gripping his gut.

"The same thing, I assure you, holds for every fault. The faults themselves must be removed, and no precepts should be given that cannot possibly be carried out while the faults remain. Unless you drive out the false opinions we suffer under, the miser will never take in a lesson on the proper use of money, nor the coward a lesson on scorning danger. You must make the miser see that money is neither a good nor an evil — show him rich men who are miserable to the last degree. You must make the coward see that the things that scare us out of our wits are less fearsome than rumor advertises them, whether the object of fear is suffering or death; that when death comes — the sentence law has fixed for every one of us — it is often a great comfort to reflect that it can never come again; that in the middle of suffering, a resolute soul will be as good as a cure, for the soul lightens any burden it endures with stubborn defiance. Remember that pain has this most excellent quality: if it is prolonged, it cannot be severe, and if it is severe, it cannot be prolonged. And we should bravely accept whatever commands the unbreakable laws of the universe lay upon us.

"Once doctrines like these have brought the erring man to a sense of his own condition — once he has learned that the happy life is not the life that conforms to pleasure but the life that conforms to Nature; once he has fallen deeply in love with virtue as man's only good and recoiled from baseness as man's only evil, and knows that everything else — riches, office, health, strength, power — falls in between and is not to be counted among goods or among evils — then he will not need a monitor at his elbow for every separate action, saying: 'Walk this way, eat that way. This is the conduct proper for a man and that for a woman; this for a married man and that for a bachelor.' Indeed, the people who take the greatest pains to hand out such advice are the very ones who can't practice it. It is the tutor lecturing the boy, the grandmother lecturing her grandson; it is the hottest-tempered schoolmaster who insists that one must never lose one's temper. Walk into any elementary school and you will find that these very pronouncements, straight from the high-browed philosophers, are sitting in the children's lesson-book!

"And will you offer precepts that are clear, or precepts that are doubtful? The clear ones need no counsellor, and the doubtful ones win no belief — so the giving of precepts is superfluous. Here's the proof. If you are counselling someone on a matter that is unclear or ambiguous in meaning, you will have to back your precepts with proofs; and if you have to fall back on proofs, then it is the proofs that do the work, and do it better on their own. 'This is how you must treat your friend, this your fellow citizen, this your associate.' And why? 'Because it is just.' But I can find all of that material under the heading of Justice. There I find that fair dealing is desirable for its own sake, that we are not forced into it by fear or hired into it for pay, and that no man is just who is drawn to anything in this virtue except the virtue itself. Once I have convinced myself of that view and thoroughly absorbed it, what good can I get from precepts that only teach a man who is already trained? To one who knows, giving precepts is superfluous; to one who does not know, it is insufficient — for he must be told not only what he is being instructed to do, but why. I ask again: are such precepts useful to the man with correct ideas about good and evil, or to the man without them? The man without them will get no benefit from you: some notion that clashes with your counsel has already monopolized his attention. And the man who has carefully decided what to seek and what to avoid knows what he ought to do without a single word from you. So that whole branch of philosophy can be abolished.

"There are two reasons we go astray: either the soul carries an evil quality planted in it by wrong opinions, or — even if not yet possessed by false ideas — it leans toward falsehood and is quickly corrupted by some outward appearance that pulls it the wrong way. So our duty is either to doctor the diseased mind carefully and free it from its faults, or to take possession of the mind while it is still unoccupied, though tilted toward evil. Philosophy's main doctrines accomplish both; therefore this giving of precepts accomplishes nothing. Besides, if we are to hand out precepts to each individual, the task is staggering. One class of advice for the financier, another for the farmer, another for the businessman, another for the man cultivating a king's good graces, another for the man seeking the friendship of his equals, another for the man courting those below him. In marriage, you will advise one man how to conduct himself with a wife who came to him a maiden, and another how to behave with a woman who was married to someone else before; how the husband of a rich woman should act, and how the husband of a dowerless one. Or do you think there is no difference between a childless wife and a mother of children, between a woman advanced in years and a mere girl, between a mother and a stepmother? We cannot cover all the types, and yet each type demands its own treatment. The laws of philosophy, by contrast, are concise, and they bind in every case. What's more, the precepts of wisdom ought to be definite and certain; where things cannot be defined, they lie outside wisdom's sphere — for wisdom knows the proper limits of things.

"We should therefore do away with this whole branch of precepts, because it cannot give to everyone what it promises only to a few — while wisdom embraces everyone. Between the madness of people in general and the madness that goes to a doctor there is no difference except this: the one suffers from disease, the other from false opinions. In the one case the symptoms of raving can be traced to ill health; the other is the ill health of the mind itself. If a man offered precepts to a lunatic — how he ought to speak, how he ought to walk, how to conduct himself in public and in private — he would be more of a lunatic than his patient. What is really needed is to treat the black bile and remove the root cause of the madness. And that is what must be done in the other case too, the case of the diseased mind. The madness itself must be shaken off; otherwise your words of advice will vanish into thin air."

That is what Aristo says, and I will answer his arguments one by one. First, his point about the obligation to remove whatever blocks the eye and hinders the vision. I admit that such a person needs no precepts in order to see; what he needs is treatment — a cure for his eyesight, the removal of the obstacle handicapping him. For it is Nature that gives us our eyesight, and whoever removes the obstruction simply restores to Nature her proper function. But Nature does not teach us our duty in every case. Again, a man whose cataract is cured cannot, the moment he recovers, give back their eyesight to other men as well; but when we are freed from evil, we can free others too. The eye needs no encouragement — no counsel, even — to distinguish different colors; black and white can be told apart without prompting from anyone. The mind, on the other hand, needs many precepts before it can see what to do in life. And notice that even in eye-treatment the physician doesn't just accomplish the cure — he throws in advice into the bargain. He says: "There's no reason to expose your weak vision to dangerous glare right away. Begin with darkness, then move into half-light, and only then grow bolder, accustoming yourself gradually to the bright light of day. There's no reason to read directly after eating; no reason to impose hard tasks on your eyes while they are swollen and inflamed. Keep winds and strong blasts of cold air off your face" — and other suggestions of the same sort, which are worth just as much as the drugs themselves. The physician's art supplements remedies with advice.

"But error is the source of sin," comes the reply; "precepts do not remove error, nor do they rout our false opinions about Good and Evil." I admit that precepts alone are not enough to overthrow the mind's mistaken beliefs; but that does not make them useless when they accompany other measures. In the first place, they refresh the memory. In the second place, matters that looked like a jumbled mass when viewed as a whole can, once sorted into their proper classes, be examined with far greater care. By our opponents' logic, you might as well call consolation and exhortation superfluous. Yet they are not superfluous; neither, therefore, is counsel.

"But it is folly," they retort, "to prescribe what a sick man ought to do as if he were well, when you should really be restoring his health; without health, precepts aren't worth a straw." But don't sick men and sound men have certain things in common, about which they need continual advice — not to grab greedily at food, for instance, and to avoid getting overtired? Poor and rich alike have certain precepts that fit them both. "Cure their greed, then," people say, "and you won't need to lecture either the poor man or the rich one, once the craving in each of them has subsided." But isn't it one thing to be free of the lust for money, and quite another to know how to use money? Misers don't know the proper limits in money matters — but even men who are not misers fail to understand its use. Then comes the reply: "Do away with error, and your precepts become unnecessary." Wrong. Suppose greed is slackened, luxury confined, rashness reined in, laziness pricked with the spur — even after the vices are removed, we must still go on learning what we ought to do, and how we ought to do it.

"Nothing will be accomplished," it is said, "by applying advice to the more serious faults." No — and medicine cannot master incurable diseases either; yet it is still used, in some cases as a cure, in others as a relief. Not even the power of philosophy entire, summoning all its strength for the purpose, will pull out of the soul a disease that has become stubborn and chronic. But Wisdom is not incapable of curing anything just because she cannot cure everything. People say: "What good does it do to point out the obvious?" A great deal of good — for we sometimes know facts without paying attention to them. Advice is not teaching; it engages the attention, rouses us, concentrates the memory and keeps it from losing its grip. We miss much that sits right before our eyes. Advice is really a kind of exhortation. The mind often works hard at not noticing even what lies in plain sight; so we must force upon it the knowledge of things it knows perfectly well. Think of what Calvus said at Vatinius' trial: "You all know that bribery has been going on — and everyone knows that you know it." You know friendship should be scrupulously honored, and you do not honor it. You know a man does wrong to demand chastity of his wife while carrying on with other men's wives; you know that just as she must have no dealings with a lover, you must have none with a mistress — and you do not act on it. That is why you must be brought back to these facts again and again: they should not sit in storage, but be ready for use. Whatever is wholesome should be discussed often and held before the mind often, so that it is not merely familiar to us but ready to hand. And remember, too, that in this way what is already clear often becomes clearer.

"But if your precepts are not obvious," comes the answer, "you will be bound to add proofs — so it is the proofs, not the precepts, that will be helpful." But can't the adviser's authority carry weight even without proofs? — the way a legal expert's opinions hold good even when he doesn't deliver the reasons behind them. Besides, the precepts themselves carry great weight of their own, whether woven into the fabric of verse or condensed into prose proverbs, like the famous wisdom of Cato: "Buy not what you need, but what you must have; what you do not need is dear at a penny." Or those oracles, and sayings like oracles: "Be thrifty with time!" "Know thyself!" Will you need somebody to explain the meaning when a person quotes you lines like these?

  Forgetting trouble is the way to cure it.

  Fortune favors the brave; the coward is defeated by his own faint heart.

Maxims like these need no special pleader. They go straight to our emotions, and they help us simply because Nature is exercising her proper function. The soul carries within itself the seed of everything honorable, and advice stirs that seed to growth, the way a spark fanned by a gentle breeze unfolds its natural fire. Virtue is roused by a touch, a jolt. Moreover, there are certain things that live in the mind and yet are not ready to hand; they begin to work easily only once they are put into words. Other things lie scattered about in various places, and the unpracticed mind cannot arrange them in order; so we should bring them into unity and join them together, to make them more powerful and more of a lift to the soul. Or, if precepts accomplish nothing at all, then every method of instruction should be abolished, and we should content ourselves with Nature alone.

The people who hold that view forget that one man is lively and quick of wit, another sluggish and dull — that some men simply have more intelligence than others. The strength of the mind is nourished and kept growing by precepts; they add new points of view to the inborn ones and straighten out warped ideas. "But suppose," people retort, "that a man does not possess sound doctrines — how can advice help him when he is chained down by vicious ones?" In this way, plainly: it frees him from them. His natural disposition has not been crushed, only overshadowed and held down. Even so it keeps trying to rise, struggling against the influences that push toward evil; and when it gets support, when the aid of precepts arrives, it grows stronger — provided the chronic trouble has not corrupted or annihilated the natural man. In that case, not even the training philosophy gives, striving with all its might, will restore him. And what is the difference, really, between the doctrines of philosophy and precepts, except that doctrines are general precepts and precepts are special ones? Both give advice — the one through the universal, the other through the particular.

Some say: "If a man is familiar with upright and honorable doctrines, advising him is superfluous." By no means. This man has indeed learned to do what he ought to do; but he does not see clearly enough what those things are. For it is not only our emotions that keep us from praiseworthy deeds; it is also lack of practice in discovering what a particular situation demands. Our minds are often under good control and yet, at the same time, idle — untrained in finding the path of duty. Advice makes that path plain. Again, it is written: "Cast out all false opinions about Good and Evil, replace them with true ones, and advice will have nothing left to do." Order in the soul can certainly be established that way — but that is not the only way. Even if proofs establish just what Good and Evil are, precepts still have their proper role. Prudence and justice consist of certain duties, and duties are set in order by precepts. What's more, our very judgment of Good and Evil is strengthened by following up our duties, and precepts lead us to that end. The two agree with each other; nor can precepts take the lead unless the duties follow. They keep their natural order — so precepts clearly come first.

"Precepts are numberless," it is said. Wrong again! On the important, essential things they are not numberless. Of course there are slight variations of time, place, and person; but even for these, precepts of general application are given. "But no one cures madness by precepts," it is said, "and therefore no one cures wickedness by them either." There is a difference. If you rid a man of insanity, his sanity returns on its own; but if we remove false opinions, insight into practical conduct does not immediately follow. And even if it did follow, counsel would still confirm his right opinion about Good and Evil. It is also wrong to believe that precepts are of no use to the mad. On their own they accomplish nothing, but they help the cure along: both scolding and correction rein a lunatic in. (I mean here lunatics whose wits are shaken, not hopelessly gone.)

"Still," it is objected, "laws do not always make us do what we ought — and what are laws but precepts with threats mixed in?" First of all: if laws fail to persuade, it is precisely because they threaten; precepts do not coerce — they correct men by pleading. Again, laws frighten a man away from crime, while precepts urge him toward his duty. And besides, laws too are a help toward good conduct — at least when they instruct as well as command. On this point I disagree with Posidonius, who says: "I do not think the preambles should have been added to Plato's Laws. A law should be brief, so that the uninstructed can grasp it the more easily. It should be like a voice sent down from heaven: it should command, not discuss. Nothing seems to me duller or more foolish than a law with a preamble. Warn me, tell me what you want me to do — I am not learning; I am obeying." No: laws framed with preambles are helpful — and notice that a state with defective laws will have defective morals. "But they do not work in every case." Well, neither does philosophy; and philosophy is not therefore ineffectual and useless in the training of the soul. And besides — what is philosophy but the Law of Life? But grant, if we like, that laws do not help; it does not follow that advice does not help either. On that ground you would have to say that consolation does not help, or warning, or exhortation, or scolding, or praise — for these are all varieties of advice, and it is by such means that we arrive at a perfected state of mind. Nothing succeeds better at bringing honorable influences to bear on the mind, or at straightening a wavering spirit inclined to evil, than the company of good men. Seen often and heard often, they sink little by little into the heart and take on the force of precepts.

The mere meeting of wise men lifts us up; a great man can help you even when he is silent. I could not easily tell you how it helps — I only know for certain that I have been helped that way. Phaedo says: "Certain tiny creatures leave no pain when they sting us; so subtle is their power, so deceptive for purposes of harm. Only a swelling reveals the bite, and even in the swelling there is no visible wound." That will be your experience in dealing with wise men: you will not discover how or when the benefit reaches you, but you will discover that you have received it. "And what is the point of this remark?" you ask. The point is that good precepts, welcomed within you often, will do you just as much good as good examples. Pythagoras declares that our souls undergo a change when we enter a temple and behold the images of the gods face to face and stand waiting for the word of an oracle. And who can deny that certain precepts strike even the rawest beginner with real force? Brief sayings, but weighty ones: "Nothing in excess." "The greedy mind no gain can satisfy." "Expect from others the treatment you have given them." We feel a kind of shock when we hear such sayings; no one ever thinks of doubting them or asking "Why?" — so strongly does plain truth attract us, even with no reason attached. If reverence reins in the soul and checks vice, why can't counsel do the same? If a rebuke can shame a man, why shouldn't counsel have the same power, even using bare precepts? And the counsel that supports its suggestion with reason — that adds why a thing should be done, and what reward waits for the man who does it and obeys — works better still and settles deeper in the heart. If commands are helpful, so is advice. But commands are helpful; therefore advice is helpful too.

Virtue divides into two parts: the contemplation of truth, and conduct. Training teaches contemplation; admonition teaches conduct. And right conduct both exercises virtue and reveals it. If a man about to act is helped by advice, he is helped by admonition too. Therefore, if right conduct is necessary to virtue, and admonition makes right conduct clear, then admonition is an indispensable thing. The soul has two strong supports — trust in the truth, and confidence — and both come from admonition. For men believe it; and once belief is established, the soul receives great inspiration and fills with confidence. Therefore admonition is not superfluous.

Marcus Agrippa — a great-souled man, and the only one of all those the civil wars raised to fame and power whose success was good for the state — used to say that he owed a great debt to the proverb: "Harmony makes small things grow; discord makes great things fall apart." He held that this saying had made him the best of brothers and the best of friends. And if proverbs of this kind, welcomed intimately into the soul, can mold the very soul, why shouldn't the branch of philosophy that consists of such proverbs carry equal influence? Virtue depends partly on training and partly on practice: you must first learn, and then strengthen what you have learned by action. If that is true, then it is not only the doctrines of wisdom that help us but the precepts too, which check and banish our passions like a kind of official decree.

It is said: "Philosophy divides into two things — knowledge, and a state of mind. For a man who has learned and understood what he should do and avoid is not yet a wise man until his mind has been remolded into the shape of what he has learned. This third department — precepts — is compounded out of the other two, from doctrine and from state of mind; so it is superfluous for the perfecting of virtue, since the other two are enough for the purpose." At that rate consolation would be superfluous too, since it is also a compound of the other two — and so would exhortation, and persuasion, and even proof itself, for proof also springs from a well-ordered and steady state of mind. But though these things flow from a sound state of mind, the sound state of mind also flows from them: it both creates them and results from them. Furthermore, what you describe is the mark of a man already perfect, one who has reached the summit of human happiness. The approach to that summit is slow; and in the meantime, in practical affairs, the path should be pointed out for the man who is still short of perfection but making progress. Wisdom may perhaps show herself this path without any prompting, once she has brought the soul to the point where it can only be moved in the right direction. Weaker characters, however, need someone to walk ahead of them and say: "Avoid this," or "Do that." Besides, if a man waits until he can know on his own what the best course of action is, he will sometimes go astray in the meantime, and going astray will keep him from ever arriving at the point where he can be content with himself. The soul must be guided at the very moment it is becoming able to guide itself. Schoolboys learn by direction: their fingers are held and guided by another hand so that they can trace the outlines of the letters; then they are told to imitate a model and form their handwriting on it. In the same way the mind is helped when it is taught by direction. Facts like these prove that this branch of philosophy is not superfluous.

The next question is whether this part alone is enough to make men wise. That problem I will take up at the proper time; but for now, setting all argument aside, isn't it obvious that we need someone to serve as our preceptor against the precepts of the world at large? No word reaches our ears without doing us harm. We are injured by good wishes and by curses alike. Our enemies' angry prayers plant false fears in us; and our friends' affection spoils us with its kindly wishes — for that affection sends us groping after goods that are far away, uncertain, and unsteady, when we could be opening the storehouse of happiness at home. We are not allowed, I tell you, to travel a straight road. Our parents draw us into wrong; our slaves draw us into wrong. Nobody confines his mistakes to himself: people sprinkle folly on their neighbors, and take it back from them in turn. That is why you find a nation's vices in each individual — because the nation gave them to him. Each man, in corrupting others, corrupts himself; he drinks in badness, and then pours it out again — and the result is one vast mass of wickedness, the worst of every separate person concentrated in a single heap.

We should therefore have a guardian, so to speak, to keep plucking us by the ear — to bat away the rumors and protest against the crowd's enthusiasms. For you are mistaken if you suppose our faults are born in us: they came from outside; they were heaped upon us. So by frequent admonitions we can push back the opinions that din around our ears. Nature allies us with no vice. She produced us whole and free. She set nothing before our eyes that might stir up the itch of greed. She put gold and silver beneath our feet, and gave those feet the job of stamping down and crushing everything that causes us to be stamped down and crushed. She lifted our gaze toward the sky, and willed that we look upward at her glorious and wonderful works: the rising and the setting sun; the whirling course of the onrushing world, which discloses the things of earth by day and the heavenly bodies by night; the movements of the stars — slow, if you compare them with the universe, yet enormously swift if you consider the size of the orbits they trace at unslackening speed; the eclipses of sun and moon, each in its turn; and all the other wonders, wonderful whether they arrive on schedule or leap suddenly into view — trails of fire by night, flashes across an open sky with no stroke or rumble of thunder, columns and beams and the varied shapes of flame. All these she ordained to travel above our heads. But gold and silver — and the iron which, because of the gold and silver, never brings peace — she hid away, as if they were dangerous things to trust to our keeping. It is we ourselves who dragged them into the light of day, so that we could fight over them. It is we ourselves who tore away the earth that covered them and dug up the causes and instruments of our own destruction. It is we ourselves who then blamed our misdeeds on Fortune, and are not ashamed to hold up as the loftiest of objects things that lately lay in the depths of the earth. Do you want to know how false the gleam is that has deceived your eyes? There is really nothing fouler, nothing more sunk in darkness, than this stuff of earth, buried and covered so long in the mud where it belongs. Of course it is foul — it was hauled up through a long, murky mine-shaft. There is nothing uglier than these metals while they are being refined and separated from the ore. And watch the workmen themselves, the ones who must handle and sift the barren grade of dirt that comes up from the bottom — see how caked with soot they are! Yet the stuff they handle dirties the soul more than the body; and there is more foulness in the owner than in the workman.

It is indispensable, then, that we be admonished — that we have some advocate of upright mind, and amid all the uproar and jangle of falsehood hear one voice only. But what voice shall it be? Surely a voice that, amid all the tumult of self-seeking, whispers wholesome words into the deafened ear, saying: "You have no need to envy the men the people call great and fortunate. Applause need not shake your composure or your sanity of mind. You need not grow disgusted with your own calm spirit at the sight of a great man in purple, ringed by the famous symbols of power. You need not judge the magistrate for whom the road is cleared to be any happier than yourself, whom his officer shoves off the road. If you would wield a command that profits you and injures nobody, clear your own faults out of the way. There are plenty of men who set fire to cities, who storm garrisons that stood impregnable for generations and safe through countless ages, who raise siege-mounds as high as the walls they are attacking, who shatter towers of wondrous height with battering-rams and engines. There are plenty who can send their columns ahead and press murderously on the enemy's rear, who can reach the Great Sea dripping with the blood of nations. But even these men, before they could conquer their enemy, were conquered by their own greed. No one withstood their attack — and they themselves could not withstand their own desire for power and impulse to cruelty. At the very moment they seemed to be hounding others, they were being hounded themselves. A mad desire to lay waste other men's territory hounded Alexander into misfortune and dispatched him to unknown countries. Do you call that man sane, who began by devastating Greece — the land where he was educated? Who snatched from each nation its dearest possession, ordering Spartans to be slaves and Athenians to hold their tongues? Not content with the ruin of all the states Philip had conquered or bribed into bondage, he knocked down commonwealth after commonwealth in place after place and carried his weapons across the whole world. His cruelty grew tired, but it never stopped — like a wild beast that tears to pieces more than its hunger demands. Already he has fused many kingdoms into one kingdom; already Greeks and Persians fear the same master; already nations Darius left free are bending under the yoke. Yet on he presses, past the Ocean, past the Sun, thinking it shame to turn his victorious course aside from the tracks of Hercules and Bacchus; he is ready to do violence to Nature herself. It is not that he wants to keep going; it is that he cannot stop — like a falling weight, whose plunge ends only when it lies motionless.

"It was not virtue or reason that persuaded Gnaeus Pompeius into foreign and civil wars; it was his mad craving for unreal glory. Now he attacked Spain and the faction of Sertorius; now he sailed off to put the pirates in chains and subdue the seas — excuses, all of it, pretexts for extending his power. What dragged him into Africa, into the North, against Mithridates, into Armenia and every corner of Asia? Nothing but a bottomless desire to grow bigger — for only in his own eyes was he not great enough. And what drove Gaius Caesar to the combined ruin of himself and the state? Glory, self-seeking, and a refusal to set any limit to his pre-eminence over all other men. He could not bear that even a single person outrank him — though the state was content to have two men standing at its head. And do you imagine that Gaius Marius — who held the consulship once by election and stole it every other time — was inspired by virtue as he slaughtered the Teutons and the Cimbri and chased Jugurtha through the wilds of Africa? Marius led armies, and ambition led Marius."

Men like these, while they were shaking the world, were shaken themselves — like cyclones, which whirl up everything they seize only because they are whirled first, and rush on with all the greater force because they have no control over themselves; and so, after wrecking so much for others, they come to feel in their own bodies the ruinous force that let them wreak havoc on so many. You must never believe that a man can become happy through the unhappiness of another. We must untangle all these examples that are forced before our eyes and crammed into our ears; we must clean out our hearts, for they are stuffed full of evil talk. Into the ground those opinions have seized, virtue must be led — the kind of virtue that roots out falsehood and every doctrine that contradicts the truth; that separates us from the crowd we trust far too much, and restores us to sound opinions. For this is wisdom: a return to Nature, a restoration to the condition from which our common errors have driven us. It is a great part of health to have deserted the counsellors of madness — to have fled far from a companionship in which each party poisons the other.

If you want proof of what I'm saying, look how differently each man lives in public and in private. A quiet home does not by itself give lessons in upright conduct; the countryside does not by itself teach plain living. But when the witnesses and the onlookers are removed, the faults that ripen on being seen and displayed sink into the background. Who puts on the purple robe with no one there to see it? Who dines off gold plate alone? Who, flinging himself down in the shade of some country tree, unpacks the splendor of his luxury in solitude? Nobody dresses up for his own eyes only, or even for the admiration of a few friends and relatives; a man spreads out his well-appointed vices in proportion to the size of the crowd admiring them. That is how it is: the claque and the witnesses are the irritants of all our mad cravings. Make us stop displaying, and you will make us stop craving. Ambition, luxury, and waywardness need a stage to perform on; you will cure them all if you keep out of sight.

Therefore, if our home must stand amid the din of a city, let there be an adviser standing at our side. When men praise great incomes, let him praise the man who can be rich on a small estate and measures his wealth by the use he makes of it. When men glorify influence and power, let him recommend, unprompted, a leisure devoted to study — a soul that has turned away from external things and found itself. Let him point out men who are happy by the crowd's reckoning but who totter on their envied heights of power — dismayed, holding an opinion of themselves very different from what everyone else holds. What others see as an eminence, they experience as a sheer drop. So their hearts sink and they flutter with fear whenever they look down the steep face of their own greatness; for they reflect that there are many ways of falling, and that the topmost point is the most slippery footing of all. Then they dread the very thing they fought for, and the good fortune that made them weighty in other men's eyes weighs still more heavily on themselves. Then they praise easy leisure and independence; they hate the glitter and look for an escape while their fortunes still stand. Then, at last, you may watch them turning to philosophy out of fear, and hunting for sound advice as their fortunes run aground. For good fortune and good sense stand, as it were, at opposite poles — that is why we are wiser in the midst of adversity. It is prosperity that takes away right judgment.

Farewell.
