Letters 54–57

Letter 54: On Asthma and Death

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

My bad health had granted me a long furlough — and then, without warning, it attacked again. "What kind of bad health?" you ask. A fair question, since there's no kind I haven't met. But one particular ailment has been assigned to me, so to speak, as my very own. I don't see why I should call it by its Greek name; "shortness of breath" describes it well enough. The attack is very brief, like a squall at sea; it's usually over within an hour. And no wonder — who can spend a long time breathing his last? Every misery and hazard the body has to offer has passed through me, and none seems worse than this one. Naturally so. Anything else is merely being sick; this is gasping out your life. That's why the doctors call it "rehearsing death." One of these days the breath will finally do what it has practiced so often.

Do you think I'm writing this letter in high spirits just because I escaped? Celebrating this so-called recovery would be as absurd as a defendant thinking he's won his case because he's managed to postpone the trial. But even while I was fighting for air, I never stopped resting easy in cheerful, brave thoughts.

"What's this?" I said to myself. "Does death keep testing me? Let it. I tested death a long time ago." "When?" you ask. Before I was born. Death is simply not existing, and I already know what that's like. What was before me will be after me. If there's any suffering in that state, there must have been suffering back then too, before we came into the light of day — and we felt no discomfort then. I ask you: wouldn't you call a man an utter fool for believing that a lamp is worse off after it's put out than before it was lit? We too are lit and put out. In between comes a stretch of suffering; on either side lies deep peace. Unless I'm badly mistaken, my dear Lucilius, this is where we go wrong: we think death lies only ahead of us, when in fact it lies both behind us and ahead. Whatever was before our birth was death. And what difference does it make whether you never begin or you leave off, when the result of either is the same — you do not exist?

I kept up these encouragements to myself — silently, of course, since I didn't have breath to spare for talking. Then little by little the shortness of breath, already dwindled to a sort of panting, came at longer intervals, then slowed, and finally leveled off. Even now, though the gasping has stopped, my breath doesn't come and go naturally; I feel a catch in it, a hesitation. Let it do as it pleases, so long as the sighing doesn't come from my soul. Take this assurance from me: I will not tremble at the final hour. I'm already prepared; I make no plans for a whole day ahead. But the man you should praise — and imitate — is the one who enjoys living and yet doesn't object to dying. Where's the virtue in leaving when you're being thrown out? And yet there's a virtue here too: I am being thrown out, yes, but as if I were choosing to leave. That's why the wise man can never be thrown out — being thrown out means being removed from a place you're unwilling to leave, and the wise man does nothing unwillingly. He escapes necessity because he wills what necessity is going to force on him anyway.

Farewell.

Letter 55: On Vatia’s Villa

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I've just come back from a ride in my litter, and I'm as worn out as if I'd walked the whole distance instead of being carried sitting down. Even being carried is hard work if it goes on long enough — maybe all the more so because it's an unnatural kind of exercise. Nature gave us legs to do our own walking and eyes to do our own seeing. Our luxuries have sentenced us to weakness: what we long refused to do, we've finally lost the power to do. Still, I needed to give my body a shaking — either to jolt loose the bile that had gathered in my throat, if that was my trouble, or, if the very breath in me had somehow thickened, to let the jolting thin it out; I could feel it was doing me good. So I insisted on riding longer than usual, along an attractive stretch of beach that curves between Cumae and Servilius Vatia's country house, pinned like a narrow path between the sea on one side and the lake on the other. A recent storm had packed the sand firm underfoot. As you know, when the waves beat down hard and fast they level a beach, while a long spell of fair weather loosens it, because the sand loses the moisture that binds it together.

As my habit is, I began looking around for something I could use, and my eyes landed on the villa that had once belonged to Vatia. So this was where that famous praetorian millionaire spent his old age! He was famous for nothing but his leisure, and for that alone people counted him lucky. Whenever men were ruined by their friendship with Asinius Gallus, or ruined by hating Sejanus — and later by being close to him, since offending him was no more dangerous than loving him — people would cry out: "O Vatia, you alone know how to live!" But what Vatia knew was how to hide, not how to live. And there is a world of difference between a life of leisure and a life of idleness. While Vatia was alive, I never drove past that place without saying to myself: "Here lies Vatia."

But philosophy, my dear Lucilius, is such a holy thing — so deserving of reverence — that even its counterfeit pleases people. The crowd assumes that anyone who has withdrawn from society is a man of leisure: carefree, self-sufficient, living for himself. In fact those rewards belong only to the wise man. Does a man gnawed by anxiety know how to live for himself? Does he even know — and this comes first — how to live at all? The man who has run away from affairs and from other people, who has been driven into seclusion by the wreckage his own desires made, who can't bear to see his neighbor happier than he is, who has gone to ground in fear like some timid, sluggish animal — that man is not living for himself. He is living for his belly, his sleep, and his lust, and that is the most shameful thing of all. Living for no one doesn't automatically mean living for yourself. Still, steadiness and sticking to a purpose count for so much that even sheer laziness, stubbornly kept up, manages to command a certain respect.

I can't describe the villa itself with any accuracy. I know only the front of the house and the parts on public display, the parts any passerby can see. There are two grottoes, cut by hand at enormous labor, each as big as the most spacious hall: one never admits the sun; the other holds it until sunset. A stream runs through a grove of plane-trees, drawing its supply from both the sea and Lake Acheron; it cuts through the grove like a race-course, and it's big enough to stock fish, though its water is constantly being drawn off. When the sea is calm they leave it alone; only when storms give the fishermen a forced holiday do they dip into that well-stocked reserve. But the most convenient thing about the villa is this: Baiae is right next door. The villa is spared all the annoyances of that resort and enjoys all its pleasures. I appreciate these attractions myself, and I believe it's a villa for every season of the year. It faces the west wind — intercepts it, in fact, so completely that Baiae never gets it. Vatia was no fool, clearly, when he picked this spot to park his leisure in, though by then his leisure was already barren and decrepit.

But the place where you live contributes very little to your peace of mind. It's the mind that must make everything agreeable to itself. I have seen men gloomy in a cheerful, lovely villa, and I have seen men in the middle of total solitude who looked for all the world like men buried in business. So don't conclude that your life is badly placed just because you aren't here in Campania. Anyway, why aren't you? Just send your thoughts this way. You can converse with absent friends as often as you like, and for as long as you like. In fact we enjoy this pleasure — the greatest pleasure there is — all the more when we're apart. Presence makes us spoiled: because we can talk or sit together whenever we please, once we've parted we give no thought at all to the people we just saw. And we should bear absence cheerfully for another reason: everyone is constantly absent from his friends, even when they're present. Count it up — first the nights spent apart, then each friend's separate engagements, then each one's private studies and trips to the country — and you'll see that foreign travel doesn't steal much from us. A friend should be held in the spirit, and the spirit never loses him; the spirit can see whomever it wants to see, every single day.

So share your studies with me, and your meals, and your walks. Life would be lived within cramped walls indeed if anything were closed off to our thoughts. I see you, my dear Lucilius; I hear you at this very moment. I am so completely with you that I wonder whether I should stop writing you letters and start sending you notes.

Farewell.

Letter 56: On Quiet and Study

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

May I die if silence isn't the first requirement for a man who has shut himself away to study! And now imagine the assortment of noises booming around my ears. I have lodgings directly over a public bathhouse. Picture every variety of sound that can make a man hate his own ears. When the strongmen are working out, swinging their lead weights around, straining hard — or pretending to strain hard — I hear the grunting; and every time they let out the breath they've been holding, I hear the whistling, high-pitched panting. When some lazy customer settles for a cheap rubdown, I hear the smack of the masseur's hand on his shoulders — a different note depending on whether the palm lands flat or cupped. Then a professional ball-player shows up and starts shouting the score: that's the finishing touch. Add the occasional rowdy being hauled off, or a pickpocket caught in the act; add the man who loves the sound of his own voice in the bath, and the enthusiasts who hit the swimming pool with a tremendous smack and splash. Besides all these — people whose voices, if nothing else, are at least normal — imagine the hair-plucker, forcing out his thin, shrill cry over and over to advertise himself, never quiet except when he's plucking someone's armpits and making the customer shriek instead of him. Then the cake-seller with his whole repertoire of cries, and the sausage-man, and the sweets-vendor, and every hawker of food crying his wares, each with his own distinctive tune.

"You must have nerves of iron," you say, "or be deaf as a post, if your mind holds together in all that assorted, clashing racket — when our friend Chrysippus is driven half to death by the steady stream of people telling him good morning!" But I swear to you this uproar means no more to me than waves or falling water — even though I'm told a certain tribe once moved their city for the sole reason that they couldn't stand the thunder of a Nile cataract. Words distract me more than noise does. Words demand attention; noise just fills the ears and beats on them. In the category of sounds that surround me without distracting me, I count passing carriages, the machinist on my block, a saw-sharpener nearby, and the fellow demonstrating little pipes and flutes at the Trickling Fountain — who doesn't so much sing as shout.

An intermittent noise, I'll admit, bothers me more than a steady one. But by now I've toughened myself against the whole lot; I could endure even a boatswain screeching out the stroke to his rowers. I force my mind to pay attention to itself and not be lured outside. Let all outdoors be bedlam, so long as there's no riot within — so long as fear and desire aren't brawling in my chest, so long as stinginess and extravagance aren't at each other's throats. What good is a quiet neighborhood if your emotions are in an uproar?

  It was night, and all the world lay lulled to rest.

Not true. There is no real rest unless reason has done the lulling. Night doesn't banish our troubles; it brings them out where we can see them. It merely trades one set of worries for another. Even in bed, the sleepless hours are as harassing as the day was. Real tranquility is what an uncorrupted mind enjoys when it relaxes. Look at the poor wretch courting sleep in his hushed mansion: so that no sound may disturb his ear, his whole troop of slaves is ordered silent, and whoever approaches him must come on tiptoe — and there he tosses from side to side, snatching at scraps of sleep between complaints, insisting he heard sounds he never heard at all. And why? His soul is making the noise. The soul is what must be quieted; that's where the mutiny needs putting down. Don't assume a soul is at peace just because the body lies still. Sometimes quiet means disquiet.

So whenever that ungovernable sluggishness gets a grip on us, we should rouse ourselves to action and keep busy with good pursuits. Great generals, when they see their troops turning mutinous, put them to work or keep them occupied with small raids. A busy man has no time for mischief, and nothing is more certain than this: the vices of leisure are worked off by work. People have often supposed I retired from public life out of disgust with politics, out of regret at my luckless, thankless position. Yet here in this retreat — where fear and weariness drove me — my ambition sometimes sprouts up fresh. It never quieted down because it was rooted out; it quieted down because it got tired, or maybe just annoyed at how its plans were going. It's the same with luxury. It seems to have left for good; then, once we've made our public profession of frugality, it starts nagging at us, and in the middle of our economies it goes hunting for the pleasures we merely walked away from but never condemned. And the more stealthily it comes back, the harder it hits. Vices out in the open are milder; even a disease is further along the road to a cure once it breaks out of hiding and shows its strength. Greed, ambition, and the other diseases of the mind — you can be sure they do their worst harm when they hide behind a show of health.

We look retired. We are not. If we had retired sincerely — if we had truly sounded the signal for retreat and turned our backs on the world's glitter — then, as I said above, nothing outside us could distract us. No chorus of men, no chorus of birds, could break in on good thoughts once those thoughts had grown steadfast and sure. A mind that startles at voices and chance sounds is unstable; it hasn't yet withdrawn into itself. It carries a residue of anxiety inside, a rooted fear that makes it prey to worry — as our Vergil says:

  I, whom no hurled spear could once make flee,
  nor Greeks advancing in their massed array,
  now startle at each sound and fear each breeze,
  afraid both for my child and for the load I bear.

The first man is the wise man: he doesn't flinch at the whistle of spears, or the clashing armor of the massed enemy, or the roar of a city falling around him. The second man knows nothing; he fears for his belongings, pales at every sound; any cry at all knocks him flat, because he takes it for the battle-shout; the slightest stir leaves him breathless with fright. His load is what makes him afraid. Pick anyone you please from among Fortune's favorites, dragging their many responsibilities, hauling their many burdens, and you'll see Vergil's man — "afraid both for his child and for the load he bears."

You may be sure you're at peace with yourself, then, when no noise reaches you, when no voice can shake you out of yourself — not a flattering voice, not a threatening one, not just an empty racket buzzing meaninglessly around you. "Fine," you say, "but isn't it sometimes simpler just to get away from the uproar?" Granted. And I'm going to do exactly that: I'll move out of this place. I only wanted to test myself and get some practice. Why should I go on being tormented, when Ulysses found so simple a remedy for his crew against even the Sirens?

Farewell.

Letter 57: On the Trials of Travel

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

When the time came to return to Naples from Baiae, I had no trouble convincing myself that a storm was brewing — anything to avoid another trip by sea. But the road was so deep in mud the whole way that you might say I made a voyage after all. That day I went through the athlete's complete routine: first the anointing — with mud, in our case — and then the dusting with sand, courtesy of the Naples tunnel. No place could feel longer than that prison. Nothing could be dimmer than those torches, which didn't help us see through the darkness so much as see the darkness itself. And even if the place had any light, the dust would smother it — dust that is oppressive and unpleasant even in the open air. Imagine it in there, where it rolls back on itself and, penned in with no ventilation, blows back into the faces of the very people who stirred it up. So we suffered two opposite plagues at once: on the same road, on the same day, we fought both mud and dust.

Still, the gloom gave me something to think about. I felt a certain jolt in my mind — a change, though not fear — caused by the novelty and the nastiness of an unusual situation. I'm not talking about myself here, of course; I'm nowhere near being a perfect man, or even a middling one. I mean the man over whom Fortune has lost her power. Even his mind will feel the jolt; even his color will change. There are certain feelings, my dear Lucilius, that no courage can escape; nature keeps reminding courage what perishable stuff it is. So even the brave man will knit his brow at grim news, and shudder at a sudden apparition, and go dizzy standing at the rim of a cliff and looking down. That isn't fear. It's a natural reaction that reason cannot argue away. That's why some brave men, perfectly willing to shed their own blood, can't bear to watch someone else's being shed. Some people go weak and faint at the sight of a fresh wound; others react the same way to handling — or even looking at — an old, festering one. And some men will take a sword-stroke more readily than they can watch one being struck.

What I felt, then, as I said, was a change, not a real disturbance. At the first glimpse of daylight my good spirits came back — unbidden, uncommanded. And I began musing to myself on what fools we are to fear some things more than others, when every one of them ends the same way. What difference does it make whether a watchtower falls on you or a mountain? None — you'll find none at all. Yet some men fear the mountain more, though both are equally deadly. That's how blind fear is: it looks not at the outcome but at the machinery of the outcome.

Now perhaps you think I'm talking about the Stoics, who hold that the soul of a man crushed under a great weight can't survive, but is scattered on the spot because it had no free way out. I am not — and the people who say that are, in my opinion, wrong. Just as a flame cannot be crushed out, because it slips around the edges of whatever presses down on it; just as air cannot be damaged by whips and blows, or even sliced apart, but flows back around whatever it yields to — in the same way the soul, which is made of the finest particles there are, cannot be trapped or destroyed inside the body. Because of its very fineness, it slips through the very thing that is crushing it. As a lightning-bolt, however widely its strike flashes, makes its return through a narrow crack, so the soul, subtler even than fire, has a way of escape through any part of the body. So the only real question is this: can the soul be immortal? But of one thing be sure: if the soul does survive the body's crushing, then the soul cannot possibly be crushed out — precisely because it does not perish. The rule of immortality admits no exceptions, and nothing can harm what is everlasting.

Farewell.
