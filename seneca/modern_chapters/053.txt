Letters 122–123

Letter 122: On Darkness as a Veil for Wickedness

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

The days have started to shrink. They've lost a good deal of light already, but there's still plenty of time in them — if you get up when the day itself does. The man who's awake to meet the dawn is more productive and, frankly, a better man. But the one who lies snoring while the sun stands high overhead, who only comes to at noon — and for plenty of people even noon feels like the middle of the night — that man is a disgrace to himself.

Some people have gone further and swapped the jobs of light and darkness entirely. Their eyes, still swollen from yesterday's binge, don't open until night is coming on. They're like those peoples Vergil describes, whom Nature has tucked away on the far side of the world, directly beneath our feet:

  When Dawn with her panting horses breathes down on our faces,
  for them the red evening is just lighting its late lamps.

Except with these men it isn't their country that's on the opposite side of the world — it's their life. There are Antipodes living right here in our own city who, as Cato put it, "have never seen the sun rise or set." Do you think people like this know how to live, when they don't even know when to live? Do they fear death — these men who have already buried themselves alive? They're as unnatural as owls and night-birds. They may pass their dark hours surrounded by wine and perfume; they may spend the whole of their backwards waking life eating dinners of course after course, each cooked separately; but that isn't a banquet. It's a funeral service they're holding for themselves. And at least the dead get their banquets in the daytime.

To a man who's actually doing something, no day is long. So let's lengthen our lives — because the duty of life, and the proof of it, is action. Trim the night short; carry some of it over into the day's work. Birds being fattened for the table are kept in the dark, so that they'll plump up without moving. It's the same with men who vegetate: no exercise, and the idle body drowns in its own flesh; in their smug retirement, the fat of laziness settles on them. That's why the bodies of these sworn servants of darkness are so repulsive to look at. Their color is more alarming than an invalid's — pale, limp, bloated, walking corpses while they're still alive. And that, believe me, is the least of their troubles. How much deeper the darkness is inside them! Such a man is dazed at the core; his inner sight has gone dim; he could envy the blind. Whoever had eyes so that he could see in the dark?

You ask how the soul acquires this perversion — this habit of turning its back on daylight and handing its whole existence over to the night. All vices rebel against Nature; every one of them deserts the appointed order. Luxury's motto is: enjoy the unusual. It isn't enough to stray from the right path — luxury wants to get as far from it as possible, and finally to plant its flag on the exact opposite side. Tell me: don't men live against Nature when they drink on an empty stomach, pouring wine into empty veins and arriving at their food already drunk? Yet that's a favorite trick of the young set — building up their capacity so they can start drinking practically in the doorway of the bathhouse, among the naked bathers, and then swig wine and immediately scrape off the sweat they've worked up with round after round of hot drinks. To them, a glass after lunch or dinner is hopelessly middle-class — that's what country squires do, men who don't understand pleasure. What delights them is the neat wine with no food to cushion it, the way it goes straight to the muscles; the whole joy of the binge is the empty stomach.

Don't men live against Nature when they trade clothes with women? Don't men live against Nature when they chase a fresh, boyish look at an age when nothing about them is boyish? What could be crueler, or more pitiful? Will he never be allowed to grow up — held forever in an artificial boyhood? Don't men live against Nature when they crave roses in winter, and force a spring lily to bloom in the cold with hot-water pipes and rigged temperatures? Don't men live against Nature when they plant orchards on top of walls? When whole forests wave on the roofs and towers of their houses, the roots starting at a height the treetops would be showing off to reach? Don't men live against Nature when they sink the foundations of their bathhouses into the sea — and can't enjoy a swim unless the heated pool is being slapped by storm waves?

Once men have decided to want everything the wrong way around, they end by abandoning Nature's way altogether. "It's daylight — time for bed! Everyone's resting — now for our workout, our drive, our lunch! Look, dawn's coming — dinner time! We can't do what ordinary people do. Living the usual, conventional way is cheap and common. Let's give up the standard-issue day. Let's have a morning that's ours alone, custom-made!" Men like this are as good as dead. Living by torches and lamps — aren't they attending their own funeral, and ahead of schedule?

I remember when this way of life was actually fashionable. Take Acilius Buta, a man of praetorian rank, who burned through an enormous fortune; when he confessed to Tiberius that he was broke, the emperor answered: "You've woken up too late!" Or take the day Julius Montanus was giving a public reading of his poetry — a middling poet, famous for being Tiberius's friend and then for losing that friendship. Montanus loved to stuff his poems with sunrises and sunsets. So when somebody complained that his reading had gone on all day and swore no one should ever attend one again, Natta Pinarius said: "I'll make him the fairest offer he'll ever get: I'm willing to listen to him from sunrise to sunset!" Montanus read on and reached these lines:

  Now the bright morning begins to spread its clear-burning flames;
  the red dawn scatters its light, and the sad-eyed swallow returns
  to her nestlings, carrying food to the chatterers, sharing it out,
  serving them with her sweet bill.

At that, Varus — a Roman knight who trailed around after Marcus Vinicius, a sponger who earned his seat at elegant dinners with his nasty wit — shouted: "Bedtime for Buta!" Later, when Montanus declaimed:

  Look, now the shepherds have folded their flocks, and the slow-moving
  darkness begins to spread silence over the drowsy, sleep-lulled land,

the same Varus called out: "What, night already? I'll go pay my morning call on Buta!" Nothing, you see, was more notorious than Buta's upside-down life. And as I said, for a while that life was the fashion.

Why do some men live this way? Not because they think night itself has any special charm, but because what's normal gives them no pleasure. Light is a bitter enemy to a bad conscience. And to a man who prices every desire and every contempt by what it costs him, illumination that comes free is beneath notice. There's more: the luxurious man wants to be talked about his whole life long. If people are silent about him, he feels he's wasting his time. It actually pains him whenever one of his stunts goes unnoticed.

Plenty of men devour their fortunes; plenty keep mistresses. To make a name in that crowd, you need a program not just of luxury but of notoriety — in a city this busy, garden-variety scandal doesn't even register. I once heard Pedo Albinovanus — the most delightful storyteller — describing life in his apartment above the townhouse of Sextus Papinius. Papinius belonged to the tribe that shuns the light. "Around nine at night I hear whips cracking. I ask what's happening: Papinius is going over his accounts. Around midnight, furious shouting. I ask what's wrong: he's doing his voice exercises. Around two in the morning, the sound of wheels — what's that? He's going out for his drive. And at dawn, total uproar — slaves being summoned, butlers running, chaos among the cooks. I ask about that too: he's back from the bath and has called for his cordial and his appetizer. 'Did his dinner run past the day?' Certainly not," said Pedo. "He lived very frugally — the night was the only thing he spent freely. So if you believe the people who call him a tightwad and a miser, go ahead and call him a slave of the lamp as well."

Don't be surprised to find so many varieties of vice. Vices are endless; they come in countless shapes; no catalogue could hold them all. Doing right is simple; doing wrong is complicated, with infinite room to swerve. The same goes for character. Follow Nature, and character is easy to manage, free, and varies only in small shades. But the sort of men I've described are warped through and through — out of tune with everything, including themselves. The main cause of this disease, I think, is a fastidious disgust with ordinary life. Just as these men set themselves apart by their dress, by the theatrical staging of their dinners, by the elegance of their carriages, so they want to be special in the way they carve up the hours of their day. They refuse to be wicked in the conventional way, because the whole payoff of their wickedness is notoriety. Notoriety is what every such man is after — every man who is, quite literally, living backwards.

So, Lucilius, let's keep to the road Nature has mapped out for us, and never leave it. Follow Nature, and everything is easy and open; fight her, and life is nothing but rowing against the current.

Farewell.

Letter 123: On the Conflict Between Pleasure and Virtue

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Worn out — more by the discomfort of the trip than by its length — I reached my villa at Alba late at night and found nothing ready except myself. So I'm shaking off my fatigue at the writing table, and getting some good out of my cook's and my baker's slowness. In fact, I'm talking the matter over with myself, and the topic is exactly this: nothing is heavy if you pick it up lightly, and nothing needs to make you angry — provided you don't add anger to your pile of troubles. My baker has no bread; but the overseer has some, or the steward, or one of my tenants. "Bad bread!" you say. Just wait: it will turn good. Hunger will make even that loaf soft and fine as bakery's best. The trick is not to eat until hunger gives the order. So I'll wait, and I won't touch food until I either get good bread or stop being fussy about bad. You have to train yourself to a slim table. Time and place throw up obstacles everywhere — even the rich man, fully rigged for pleasure, will run into them and be pulled up short. To have whatever he wants is in no man's power. What is in his power is this: not to wish for what he doesn't have, and to make cheerful use of whatever shows up. A good-humored stomach — one willing to take rough handling — is a long stride toward freedom.

You can't imagine how much pleasure I'm taking in the way my tiredness is settling down all by itself. No masseurs, no bath, no cure of any kind — except time. What work has piled up, rest carries away. This supper, whatever it turns out to be, will give me more pleasure than a banquet celebrating some new consul. I've put my spirit to a surprise test — and that's the simpler and truer kind. When a man has made his preparations and formally ordered himself to be patient, you can't tell how much strength he really has. The surest proofs are the ones given on the spot: looking at your own troubles not just fairly but calmly, without tantrums or arguments; making up your own shortfall by simply not craving the thing you were owed; and reminding yourself that a habit of ours may go unsatisfied — we ourselves never truly lack anything. How many things are superfluous we never realize until they run out. We were using them not because we needed them but because we had them. And how much do we buy only because our neighbors bought it, or because most people own one! Half our troubles come from living by the pattern — steering not by reason but by convention.

If only a few people did a thing, we'd refuse to copy it; but let the majority start, and we fall in line — as if being common made a thing more honorable! Once a wrong idea becomes widespread, it passes for the standard of what's right. Everyone now travels with Numidian outriders in front and a squad of runners to clear the road; it's a disgrace to have no attendants elbowing crowds out of your way, no great cloud of dust announcing that Somebody Important is coming. Everyone now has mules loaded with crystal cups and myrrhine vases carved by famous artists; it's a disgrace if your baggage is the kind that can rattle down the road without danger. Everyone's page-boys ride with their faces slathered in ointment so heat and cold won't trouble their tender complexions; it's a disgrace if a single boy in your entourage shows a healthy cheek not covered in cosmetics.

Avoid conversation with all such people. These are the men who pass their bad habits from hand to hand, grafting them onto everyone they meet. We used to think the worst of the breed were the ones who paraded their fine words; but there are men who parade their vices. Their talk does real damage. Even when it doesn't convince you on the spot, it leaves seeds of trouble in the soul, and the evil follows us around after we've left them, sure to spring up later with fresh strength. People who've heard a concert carry the melodies home in their heads, and the sweetness of the tunes keeps intruding, making serious thought impossible. In just the same way, the talk of flatterers and cheerleaders for vice sticks with us long after we've heard it. A catchy tune is hard to evict from the memory — it stays, it lasts, it comes back when you least expect it. So shut your ears against evil talk, and do it at the first note. Once that talk has gotten inside — once the words are admitted and settled in the mind — they grow bolder. And then we begin to talk like this: "Virtue, Philosophy, Justice — a rattle of empty words. The only happiness is treating yourself well. Eating, drinking, spending — that's real living; that's how you remember you're mortal. The days are running out; life is racing away and can't be refunded. Why hesitate to wise up? Life won't take pleasures forever; meanwhile, while it can, while it's begging for them, what's the point of forcing frugality on it? Get ahead of death — whatever death is going to steal from you, spend on yourself now. You have no mistress, no favorite slave-boy to make a mistress jealous; you show up in public sober every day; you dine as though Father were going to audit your account-book. That isn't living — that's minding someone else's life. And what madness, to look out for your heir's interests and deny yourself everything, until the sheer size of the inheritance turns your friends into enemies! The more your heir stands to gain, the harder he'll celebrate your exit. As for those sour types who set themselves up as schoolmasters to the world, judging everyone else's life while making an enemy of their own — don't give them a second thought, and don't hesitate to choose good living over a good name."

These are the voices you must flee the way Ulysses fled the Sirens — he wouldn't sail past until he was lashed to the mast. These voices are every bit as powerful. They lure men away from country, parents, friends, and every honest habit; they wreck them, with hopes that are shameful — or at best doomed — on a shameful life. How much better to hold a straight course and steer for the harbor where "pleasant" and "honorable" finally mean the same thing! We can get there once we understand that there are two classes of things: those that attract us and those that repel us. Riches, pleasures, beauty, ambition — everything seductive and smooth — pulls us in; toil, death, pain, disgrace, a stricter table — these push us away. So we must train ourselves out of fearing the one and desiring the other. Fight the battle in reverse: retreat from what entices, and charge what attacks.

Notice how differently men walk downhill and uphill? Going down a slope, you lean back; climbing a steep grade, you lean forward. To throw your weight forward on the way down, Lucilius, or backward on the way up — that is to side with vice. The pleasures run downhill; the road to what is rough and hard runs up. Downhill, hold the body back on the rein; uphill, throw it forward.

Do you think I'm saying the only men who ruin our ears are the ones who preach pleasure and teach us to dread pain — pain, which is frightening enough on its own? I think we're also injured by men who dress up in the Stoic uniform and coach us into vice all the same. They boast that only the wise and learned man knows how to be a lover: "He alone has the science of this art; the wise man is also the best-trained at drinking and feasting. The one question worth studying is: up to what age does the bloom of love last?" Let all that be written off as a concession to Greek ways. We should be turning our attention to words like these instead: "No man is good by accident. Virtue has to be learned. Pleasure is a low, small thing, worth nothing — we share it with the dumb animals; the tiniest and meanest of them go fluttering after it. Glory is empty and fleeting, lighter than air. Poverty is an evil to no one — unless he kicks against it. Death is not an evil. What is it, you ask? The one law of mankind that plays no favorites. Superstition is a lunatic's error: it fears the very beings it should love, and insults the very beings it worships. What difference is there between denying the gods and defaming them?"

Principles like these you should learn — no, learn by heart. Philosophy has no business writing excuses for vice. When a doctor tells a sick man to live recklessly, the patient is past saving.

Farewell.
