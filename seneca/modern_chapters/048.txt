Letters 112–113

Letter 112: On Reforming Hardened Sinners

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Believe me, I want your friend shaped and trained just as much as you do. But he has come to us in a very hardened state — or rather, and this is the harder problem, in a very soft state, broken down by bad habits of long standing.

Let me give you an illustration from my own hobby. Not every vine will take a graft. If the vine is old and decayed, or thin and feeble, it will either refuse the cutting outright or fail to feed it; it won't take the graft into itself and make it part of its own body, won't adapt itself to the new wood's quality and nature. So what we usually do is cut the vine off above the ground, so that if nothing comes of the first attempt, we can try our luck a second time and graft below the ground.

Now, this man you've written to me about has no strength. He has pampered his vices. He has managed to go flabby and hard at the same time. He cannot receive reason, and he cannot nourish it. "But," you say, "he wants it. He asks for it of his own free will." Don't believe him. I don't mean he's lying to you — he genuinely thinks he wants it. Luxury has upset his stomach, that's all; give it a little while and the two of them will be reconciled. "But he says he's disgusted with his old way of living." Very likely. Who isn't? People love their vices and hate them at the same time. The right season to pass judgment on him will come when he has given us a guarantee that he really hates luxury. As things stand now, he and luxury are merely not on speaking terms.

Farewell.

Letter 113: On the Vitality of the Soul and Its Attributes

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You want my written opinion on a question our school likes to kick around: whether justice, courage, foresight, and the other virtues are living things. It's quibbles like this, my dear Lucilius, that have convinced the public we sharpen our wits on useless targets and fritter away our free time on debates that will profit no one. Still, you asked, so I'll lay out the question as our school sees it. For myself, I confess to a different creed: I think some topics are best left to the sort of man who goes around in white shoes and a Greek cloak. But the arguments that stirred the old masters — or that the old masters stirred up — I will explain to you.

The soul, everyone agrees, is a living thing, because it is what makes us living things, and because "living things" take their very name from it. Now, virtue is nothing but the soul in a certain condition; therefore virtue is a living thing. Again: virtue acts, and no action happens without impulse; and whatever has impulse must be a living thing, because nothing except a living thing has impulse. The comeback to this is: "If virtue is a living thing, then virtue itself possesses virtue." Of course it does — it possesses its own self! Just as the wise man does everything by means of virtue, virtue does everything by means of itself. "In that case," they say, "all the arts are living things too, and all our thoughts, and everything the mind grasps. It follows that many thousands of living things dwell in the cramped quarters of a man's heart, and that each of us is — or at least contains — a whole crowd of living creatures."

Stuck for an answer? Here it is: each of these will be a living thing, but they will not be many separate living things. Why not? I'll explain, if you'll lend my words your subtlety and your concentration. Each separate living thing must have a substance of its own. But all the things I just listed share a single soul; so each can be a living thing without their being many. I myself am a living thing and a man, but you can't say there are two of me on that account. Why? Because to be two, they would have to be two separate existences — one would have to be split off from the other to make a pair. Whatever is manifold inside a single whole falls under a single nature, and so it is single.

My soul is a living thing, and so am I, but we are not two persons. Why? Because the soul is a part of me. A thing will only be counted as something in its own right when it stands by itself. As long as it is part of something else, it cannot be regarded as separate. Why? I'll tell you: because what is separate must be its own thing — personal, whole, and complete in itself.

I've put myself on record as holding a different opinion. For if you accept this doctrine, then not only the virtues will be living things, but so will their opposite vices, and the emotions too — anger, fear, grief, suspicion. Push the argument further, and every opinion and every thought becomes a living thing. That is simply not admissible; for not everything a man does is the man himself.

"What is Justice, then?" people say. Justice is a soul holding itself in a certain attitude. "So if the soul is a living thing, Justice is one too." Not at all. Justice is a state of the soul — a kind of power the soul has — and that same soul shifts into various likenesses without becoming a different kind of animal every time it acts differently. Nor is what the soul produces a living thing.

If Justice, Courage, and the other virtues are actually alive, do they stop being living things and then start life over again, or are they living things forever? Virtues cannot cease to exist. Then there are many — no, countless — living things lodging in this one soul. "Not many," comes the answer, "because they are all attached to the one soul, parts and limbs of a single whole." Then the picture we're painting of the soul is a many-headed hydra, each head fighting and destroying on its own. And yet no head is a separate living thing; each is the head of a living thing, and the hydra itself is one single animal. Nobody ever believed the Chimaera contained a living lion or a living serpent; those were parts of the whole Chimaera, and parts are not living things. So how do you conclude that Justice is a living thing? "Justice is active and helpful," they reply. "What acts and helps has impulse, and what has impulse is a living thing." True — if the impulse is its own. But Justice's impulse is not its own; it comes from the soul.

Every living thing remains what it began as, until death. A man is a man until he dies, a horse a horse, a dog a dog; they cannot change into anything else. Now let's grant that Justice — defined as "a soul in a certain attitude" — is a living thing. Suppose it so. Then Courage is alive too, being "a soul in a certain attitude." But which soul? The one we just defined as Justice? The soul is kept inside the first creature and cannot cross over into another; it must live out its existence in the being where it began. Besides, one soul cannot belong to two living things, much less to many. And if Justice, Courage, Restraint, and all the other virtues are living things, how will they share one soul? They must have separate souls, or else they are not living things. Several living things cannot have one body — our very opponents admit that. Now, what is the "body" of Justice? "The soul," they concede. And of Courage? "The soul again." And yet there cannot be one body for two living things. "But the same soul," they answer, "puts on the guise of Justice, or Courage, or Restraint." That would work if Courage were absent while Justice was present, and Restraint absent while Courage was present. As it stands, all the virtues are there at the same time. So how can the individual virtues be living things, if you grant there is one single soul — and one soul cannot make more than one single living thing?

Again: no living thing is part of another living thing. But Justice is part of the soul; therefore Justice is not a living thing. By now I seem to be wasting effort on an acknowledged fact — this is a topic to laugh out of the room, not to debate. And no two living things are equal. Look at the bodies of all creatures: every one has its particular color, shape, and size. Among all the reasons to marvel at the genius of the divine Creator, I count this one: in all that abundance, nothing repeats. Even things that look alike turn out, on comparison, to be different. God has made the whole multitude of leaves we see, and each is stamped with its own pattern; all the many animals, and no two the same size — always some difference. The Creator has set himself the task of making things distinct — unlike and unequal. But the virtues, by your own argument, are all equal. Therefore they are not living things.

Every living thing acts on its own; but virtue does nothing on its own — it must act in partnership with a man. All living things are either rational, like men and gods, or irrational, like beasts and cattle. The virtues are certainly rational; yet they are neither men nor gods; therefore they are not living things. Every rational living thing sits inactive until some external impression stirs it; then impulse comes, and finally assent confirms the impulse. Let me explain what assent is. Suppose I ought to take a walk: I do walk, but only after giving myself the order and approving my own opinion. Or suppose I ought to sit down: I do sit, but only after the same process. Now, this assent is no part of virtue. Say the virtue in question is Prudence: how is Prudence going to assent to the opinion "I must take a walk"? Nature doesn't allow it. Prudence looks after the interests of its possessor, not its own; Prudence can neither walk nor sit down. So it has no power of assent, and it is not a rational living thing. And if virtue is a living thing, it must be rational; but it is not rational; therefore it is not a living thing.

If virtue is a living thing, and virtue is a Good — then isn't every Good a living thing? It is; our school professes exactly that. Now, saving your father's life is a Good; giving a judicious opinion in the senate is a Good; handing down just verdicts is a Good. Therefore the act of saving your father is a living thing, and so is the act of giving sound opinions. We have pushed this absurdity so far that you can't keep from laughing out loud: wise silence is a Good, and so is a frugal dinner; therefore silence is a living thing, and so is dining. And I will never stop tickling my own mind and making sport for myself with this dainty nonsense. Justice and Courage, if they are living things, are certainly earthly ones. And every earthly living thing gets cold and hungry and thirsty. Therefore Justice shivers, Courage goes hungry, and Kindness could use a drink!

And why stop there? Shouldn't I ask our honorable opponents what shape these creatures have? A man's? A horse's? A wild beast's? If they give them a round shape, like a god's, I shall ask whether greed and luxury and madness are equally round — for these, too, are "living things." If I find they've rounded those off as well, I'll go on to ask whether a modest walk is a living thing. By their own argument they'll have to admit it — and say that a gait is a living thing, and a round one at that!

Now, don't imagine I'm the first man in our school to speak from his own judgment instead of the rulebook. Cleanthes and his pupil Chrysippus could not agree on what walking is. Cleanthes held it was spirit transmitted from the ruling essence to the feet; Chrysippus, that it was the ruling essence itself. So why, following Chrysippus's own example, shouldn't every man claim his freedom and laugh down all these "living things" — so numerous that the universe itself can't hold them?

Someone might say: "The virtues are not many living things, and yet they are living things. Just as one individual can be both poet and orator without being two men, so these virtues are living things without being many. The soul is one and the same; it can be at once just and prudent and brave, holding itself in a certain attitude toward each virtue." The dispute is settled; we're agreed. For the moment I'll grant that the soul is a living thing — reserving my final vote for later — but I deny that the soul's acts are living creatures. Otherwise all words and all verses would be alive. If prudent speech is a Good, and every Good is a living thing, then speech is a living thing. A prudent line of poetry is a Good; every Good is a living thing; therefore the line of poetry is a living thing. And so "Arms and the man I sing" is a living thing — though they can hardly call it round, since it has six feet!

"This whole business we're discussing," you say, "is a tangled web." I split with laughter whenever I reflect that solecisms and barbarisms and syllogisms are living things, and, like a portrait painter, I give each one a suitable face. Is this what we discuss with knitted brow and wrinkled forehead? After this I can't say, with Caelius, "What melancholy trifling!" It's more than trifling; it's absurd. What all this buys you, I can't see. Why don't we discuss something useful and wholesome instead — how we may attain the virtues, and what road will take us to them?

Teach me, not whether Courage is a living thing, but that no living thing is happy without courage — without the strength, that is, to stand against hazards and to master every stroke of Fortune by rehearsing and anticipating her attack. And what is Courage? It is the impregnable fortress of our mortal weakness. The man who has walled himself inside it holds out, free of anxiety, through life's whole siege, because he is using his own strength and his own weapons. Here let me give you a saying of our philosopher Posidonius: "Never think yourself safe because you carry the weapons of Fortune. Fight with your own! Fortune supplies no arms against herself; men equipped against their enemies stand unarmed against Fortune."

Alexander, to be sure, harried and routed the Persians, the Hyrcanians, the Indians, and every other nation the East spreads out to the edge of the Ocean; but he himself, as he killed one friend and lost another, would lie in the dark mourning now his crime, now his loss. The conqueror of so many kings and nations was laid low by anger and grief! For he had made it his aim to win control of everything except his emotions. What enormous mistakes possess men who long to push the borders of empire beyond the seas, who count themselves most prosperous when their armies occupy many provinces and they can stitch new territory onto the old — and who know nothing of the kingdom that stands equal to heaven itself in greatness! The greatest command is command of yourself.

Let self-command teach me what a holy thing Justice is — the virtue that always looks to another's good and asks nothing for itself except the chance to be exercised. It should have nothing to do with ambition and reputation; it should be its own satisfaction. And before everything else, let each man convince himself of this: "I must be just without reward." And that's not enough; let him convince himself of this as well: "May I take pleasure in devoting myself, of my own free will, to this noblest of virtues." Let all his thoughts turn as far as possible from personal advantage. Don't look around for the reward of a just deed; a just deed in itself pays a still greater one. Drive deep into your mind what I said a moment ago: it makes no difference how many people know of your integrity. Those who want their virtue advertised are working for fame, not virtue. Aren't you willing to be just without being famous? In fact, you will often have to be just and disgraced at the same time. And then, if you're wise, let a bad reputation, well earned, be a delight to you.

Farewell.
