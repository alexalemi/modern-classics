Letters 20–22

Letter 20: On Practising What You Preach

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

If you're healthy — and if you think yourself worthy of finally becoming your own master — I'm glad. The credit will be mine, if I can pull you out of the flood you've been thrashing in with no hope of reaching shore. But here is what I ask of you, my dear Lucilius — what I beg of you: let wisdom sink all the way into your soul, and measure your progress not by what you say or write, but by the steadiness of your heart and the shrinking of your desires. Prove your words by your deeds.

The speechmakers chasing the approval of a packed hall have one aim; the men who charm the ears of the young and the idle with clever, fluent argument have another. Philosophy teaches us to act, not to speak. It demands that every man live by his own standard — that his life not clash with his words, and that his inner life be all one color, in harmony with everything he does. This is the highest duty of wisdom, and its highest proof: that deed and word agree, that a man be equal to himself in every situation — always the one same self.

"But who can hold that standard?" you ask. Very few, granted; but some can. I won't pretend it's easy, and I don't say the philosopher can always keep the same pace — only that he can always keep the same road. So observe yourself. See whether your clothes and your house disagree with each other; whether you're lavish with yourself and stingy with your household; whether you eat frugal dinners and build luxurious houses. Settle, once and for all, on a single rule to live by, and square your whole life to it. Some people pinch pennies at home and strut in public; that kind of discord is a flaw — the mark of a wobbling mind that can't yet hold its balance. And I can tell you where the wobble comes from, this mismatch between actions and intentions: no one decides what he actually wants, and even if he does decide, he doesn't stay decided — he jumps the track. And he doesn't just change; he circles back and slides into the very conduct he'd renounced and sworn off. So let me skip the ancient definitions of wisdom and sum up the whole business of human life in one: What is wisdom? Always desiring the same things, and always refusing the same things. You can even skip the fine print — that what you desire should be right — because nothing but the right thing can satisfy a person the same way every day.

That's why people don't know what they want except in the moment of wanting it. No one ever decides, once and for all, what to desire and what to refuse. Judgment shifts from day to day, flips to its opposite, and turns many a man's life into a kind of game. So press on as you've begun. Perhaps you'll be led all the way to perfection — or at least to a point that you alone will recognize as still short of it.

"But what becomes of my crowded household," you say, "without a household income?" Once you stop feeding that crowd, it will feed itself. Or else poverty will teach you, as a favor, what your own favors never could. Poverty keeps your true and tested friends for you; it clears out everyone who came not for you but for what you have. Shouldn't you love poverty for this one service alone — that it shows you who really loves you? When will the day come when no one lies to you by way of compliment? So point your thoughts, your efforts, your desires at this: being content with yourself and with the goods that grow out of yourself. Every other prayer, leave in God's keeping. What happiness could be nearer at hand? Bring yourself down to a modest level, one you cannot be thrown down from — and to help you do it more willingly, this letter's little wage will speak to exactly that subject. I'll pay it right now.

Glare at me if you like, but Epicurus will once again be glad to settle my debt: "Believe me, your words will carry more weight if you sleep on a cot and wear rags. Then you won't just be saying them; you'll be proving them." I certainly listen in a different spirit to our friend Demetrius, now that I've seen him sleeping without so much as a cloak over him — without even a mat beneath him. He is not just a teacher of the truth; he is a witness to it. "But can't a man despise wealth while it's sitting right in his pocket?" Of course he can. He too is a great soul, the man who sees riches heaped around him, wonders long and deeply that they've come to him, and smiles — and hears rather than feels that they're his. It's no small thing not to be spoiled by living with wealth; the truly great man is the one who is poor in the midst of riches. "Yes, but I don't know," you say, "how that man will endure poverty if he falls into it suddenly." Nor do I know, Epicurus, whether your poor man will despise riches if he suddenly falls into those. So in both cases it's the mind that must be appraised: we have to ask whether your man is pleased with his poverty, and whether my man is displeased with his riches. Otherwise the cot and the rags prove very little about his good intentions — unless it's clear he endures them by preference, not necessity.

The mark of a noble spirit, though, is not to fling yourself into such things because they're better, but to rehearse them because they're easy to endure. And they are easy, Lucilius; come to them after long rehearsal, and they're even pleasant, because they carry a sense of freedom from worry — and without that, nothing is pleasant. So I hold it essential to do what I told you in an earlier letter great men have often done: set aside a few days to prepare for real poverty by practicing pretend poverty. We need this all the more because we've soaked so long in luxury that every duty looks hard and heavy. Better to shake the soul out of its sleep, prod it, and remind it how little nature has actually prescribed for us. No man is born rich. Every one of us, on first seeing daylight, is ordered to be content with milk and a scrap of cloth. That is how we start out — and then whole kingdoms are too small for us.

Farewell.

Letter 21: On the Renown Which My Writings Will Bring You

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Do you really conclude that your trouble is with those men you wrote to me about? Your greatest trouble is with yourself: you are your own stumbling block. You don't know what you want. You're better at approving the right course than at following it. You see where true happiness lies, but you don't have the courage to go get it. Since you can't seem to see what's holding you back, let me tell you.

You think the life you're supposed to leave behind is an important one; and just as you've resolved on that calm state you hope to pass into, the glitter of your present life holds you back — as if leaving it meant stepping down into filth and darkness. That's a mistake, Lucilius. Going from your life into the other one is a promotion. The difference between the two is the difference between shine and light. Light has a definite source of its own inside it; shine is borrowed. Shine gleams by someone else's illumination — and anyone who steps between the source and the object throws the object into dense shadow. Light glows from within.

It's your own studies that will make you shine — that will make you eminent. Let me give you Epicurus's example. He was writing to Idomeneus, trying to call him back from a showy career to a sure and steadfast kind of fame. Idomeneus at the time was a minister of state, a man wielding real authority with important affairs in hand. "If it's fame that attracts you," Epicurus told him, "my letters will make you more renowned than all the things you cherish — all the things that make you cherished." Was he wrong? Who would ever have heard of Idomeneus if the philosopher hadn't engraved his name into those letters? All the grandees and satraps — even the king Idomeneus petitioned for his title — are sunk in deep oblivion. Cicero's letters are what keep the name of Atticus from perishing. It would have done Atticus no good to have Agrippa for a son-in-law, Tiberius for his granddaughter's husband, Drusus Caesar for a great-grandson. Among names that mighty, his would never be spoken at all if Cicero hadn't bound Atticus to himself. The deep flood of time will roll over us. A few great men will lift their heads above it, and though they too must sink at last into the same silence, they will battle oblivion and hold their ground a long while.

What Epicurus could promise his friend, I promise you, Lucilius. I will find favor with later generations; I can take names with me that will endure as long as my own. Our poet Vergil promised two heroes an everlasting name, and he is keeping his promise:

  Fortunate pair! If my songs have any power,
  no day will ever erase you from the memory of time —
  not while Aeneas' line still keeps the Capitol,
  that rock immovable, and a Roman father holds the empire.

Whenever men are pushed forward by fortune — whenever they've become part and parcel of someone else's influence — they enjoy abundant favor and a crowded doorstep exactly as long as they keep their position. The moment they leave it, they slip out of everyone's memory. But the respect paid to native genius keeps growing; and the honor doesn't stop with the man himself — it attaches to whatever his memory touches, and gets passed along from hand to hand.

Now, Idomeneus shouldn't get into my letter free of charge, so he can pay the debt from his own account. It was to him that Epicurus addressed the famous saying urging him to make Pythocles rich — but not rich in the vulgar, double-edged way. "If you wish to make Pythocles rich," he said, "do not add to his money; subtract from his desires." The idea is too clear to need explanation and too well put to need reinforcement. I'll add only one warning: don't assume it applies to riches alone. Apply it anywhere you like; it holds the same value. "If you wish to make Pythocles honorable, do not add to his honors; subtract from his desires." "If you wish Pythocles to have pleasure that lasts, do not add to his pleasures; subtract from his desires." "If you wish to make Pythocles an old man whose life is filled to the full, do not add to his years; subtract from his desires." There's no reason to treat these words as Epicurus's private property; they are public property. I think we should do in philosophy what they do in the Senate: when someone makes a motion I partly approve of, I ask him to divide the motion, and I vote for the part I like. So I'm all the more glad to repeat Epicurus's distinguished words — because I want to prove something to the people who run to him with bad motives, thinking they'll find in him a screen for their own vices: whatever school you follow, you must live honorably.

Go to his Garden and read the motto carved there: "Stranger, here you will do well to linger; here our highest good is pleasure." The caretaker of that place, a kindly host, will be ready for you. He'll welcome you with barley meal and pour you water in abundance, and ask: "Haven't you been well entertained?" "This garden," he says, "does not whet your appetite; it quenches it. It doesn't make you thirstier with every drink; it slakes thirst with a natural cure — a cure that charges no fee. This is the 'pleasure' I have grown old in."

With you, though, I'm speaking of the desires that refuse all soothing — the ones that must be bribed to stop. Because as for the exceptional desires — the kind that can be postponed, disciplined, checked — I have just one thought to share: pleasure of that sort is in line with our nature, but not with our needs. You owe it nothing; whatever you spend on it is a free gift. The belly won't listen to advice: it demands, it pesters. Yet it isn't an expensive creditor. You can send it away at small cost — as long as you give it what you owe, not everything you're able to give.

Farewell.

Letter 22: On the Futility of Half-way Measures

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

By now you understand that you must pull yourself out of these showy and corrupting pursuits; what you still want to know is how. Some things can only be pointed out by someone on the spot. A doctor can't prescribe by letter the right hour for eating or bathing; he has to feel the pulse. There's an old saying about gladiators: they plan the fight in the ring. Something in the opponent's glance, some movement of his hand, even a slight lean of his body — the watchful eye reads the warning there. What is usually done, or ought to be done — that we can put into general rules and write down; advice like that can be given not only to absent friends but to future generations. But the second question — when to make your move, and how — no one can settle at long range. You must take counsel with the situation itself, on the spot. And you must be not only present in body but alert in mind if you're going to catch the opportunity as it flies. So watch for it; and when you see it, seize it, and throw everything — all your energy, all your strength — into this one task: getting yourself free of those business duties.

Now listen carefully to the opinion I'm about to give you. It is this: withdraw from that kind of existence, or withdraw from existence altogether. But I also say: take the gentle path. Loosen the knot you've tied so badly rather than cut it — with this proviso, that if there turns out to be no other way to loosen it, you cut it after all. No man is such a coward that he'd rather hang in suspense forever than fall once and be done with it. In the meantime — and this comes first — don't tie yourself up any further. Be content with the business you've already lowered yourself into — or, as you'd rather have people think, tumbled into. Don't keep struggling on toward something bigger; if you do, you'll lose your whole excuse, and everyone will see it was no tumble. The usual defense men offer is a lie: "I was compelled to do it. Suppose it was against my will — I had to." No one is compelled to chase prosperity at a dead run. Even if you don't fight the current, it counts for something just to call a halt — to stand still instead of pressing eagerly after Fortune's favors.

And will you be put out with me if I don't just come to advise you myself, but call in others — wiser heads than mine, the men I lay every problem before when I'm pondering one? Read Epicurus's letter on this very question; it's addressed to Idomeneus. He begs him to escape as fast as he can, and beat his retreat before some stronger influence steps in and takes away his freedom to withdraw. But he adds: attempt nothing except at the moment when it can be attempted suitably and in season. Then, when the long-sought occasion comes — up and out. He forbids so much as a doze in anyone who is plotting escape; and he holds out hope of a safe release from even the hardest positions, provided we're neither too hasty before the time nor too sluggish when the time arrives.

Now, I suppose, you want the Stoic motto too. Nobody has any business slandering our school to you for recklessness; the truth is, its caution is greater than its courage. Perhaps you're expecting it to say something like this: "It is shameful to flinch under a burden. Wrestle with the duties you have once taken on. No man is brave and earnest if he runs from danger, if his spirit doesn't grow with the very difficulty of his task." Words like those will indeed be said to you — if your perseverance has an object that's worth the price, if you won't have to do or endure anything unworthy of a good man. Besides, a good man doesn't waste himself on shabby, discreditable work, or stay busy merely for the sake of being busy. Nor will he do what you imagine — get so tangled in ambitious schemes that he has to endure their ebb and flow forever. No: when he sees the dangers, the uncertainties, the hazards he used to be tossed around in, he will withdraw — not turning his back to the enemy, but falling back step by step to a safe position. And business, my dear Lucilius, is easy to escape, if only you will despise its rewards. What holds us back, what keeps us from escaping, is thinking like this: "What? Walk away from such great prospects? Leave right at harvest time? No slaves at my side? No retinue for my litter? No crowd in my reception hall?"

So men leave these advantages reluctantly; they love the reward of their hardships and curse the hardships themselves. They complain about their ambitions the way they complain about their mistresses — which is to say, get at their real feelings, and you'll find not hatred but a lovers' quarrel. Examine the minds of those who cry down what they craved, who talk about escaping the things they can't do without, and you'll see it: they are lingering by their own free will in a life they declare hard and wretched to endure. It is so, my dear Lucilius — slavery holds a few men fast, but many more hold fast to their slavery.

But if you intend to be rid of that slavery, if freedom is genuinely pleasing in your eyes, and if you're asking for advice for this one purpose — to accomplish it without perpetual torment — then how could the whole company of Stoics fail to approve? Zeno, Chrysippus, and all their kind will give you counsel that's temperate, honorable, and fitted to your case. But if you keep twisting around to look behind you — how much can I carry away with me? how much money will I need to outfit my life of leisure? — you'll never find the way out. No man can swim ashore and take his baggage with him. Rise to the higher life, and may the gods favor you — but not the way they favor certain men when, with kind and smiling faces, they hand out magnificent evils, excused by the single fact that these things which burn and torture were granted in answer to prayer.

I was already putting the seal on this letter, but it has to be broken again, so it can come to you with its customary contribution and carry some noble saying along with it. And here is one that comes to mind — I honestly can't tell you whether its truth or its fine expression is the greater. "Said by whom?" you ask. By Epicurus — I am still appropriating other men's property. "Everyone goes out of life just as if he had only just entered it." Take anyone off guard — young, old, middle-aged — and you'll find them all equally afraid of death and equally ignorant of life. No one has anything finished, because we keep pushing everything we undertake into the future. Nothing in that saying pleases me more than the way it taunts old men with being infants. "No one," he says, "leaves the world any differently than he came into it." False! We are worse when we die than when we were born. And the fault is ours, not Nature's. Nature has a complaint to file against us: "What is this? I brought you into the world without desires, without fears, free of superstition, treachery, and the rest of the curses. Go out the way you came in!"

A man has caught the message of wisdom if he can die as free from care as he was at birth. But as it is, we all tremble when the dreaded moment approaches: courage fails, cheeks go white, useless tears fall. And what is more shameful than fretting at the very threshold of peace? The reason is that we arrive stripped of all our goods: we have jettisoned life's whole cargo and we're in distress, because none of it was stowed in the hold — all of it was heaved overboard along the way and has drifted off. Men care nothing about living nobly, only about living long — although living nobly is within everyone's reach, and living long is within no one's power.

Farewell.
