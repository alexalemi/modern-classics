Letters 114–115

Letter 114: On Style as a Mirror of Character

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You've been asking me why certain periods produce a rotten style of speaking — how a whole generation's talent can slide into the same vices, so that in one era the fashion is prose all puffed up and straining for power, and in another it's prose that minces along, modulated like a piece of concert music. You wonder why one age applauds ideas so bold you can hardly believe anyone dared them, while another goes in for choppy, insinuating phrases where you have to read in more meaning than actually reaches your ear. And why some epochs claimed the right to use metaphor shamelessly, as if there were no such thing as going too far.

Here is the answer, and you already know it — it's a saying you hear on the street, one the Greeks turned into a proverb: men's speech matches their lives. Just as each individual's way of acting speaks for him, so a period's way of speaking reproduces the general character of the time, whenever public morals have slackened and the public has given itself over to softness. Decadence in speech is proof of decadence in the city — provided it's popular and fashionable, not just the quirk of one or two individuals.

A man's talent cannot be one thing and his soul another. If the soul is healthy, well ordered, serious, and restrained, the talent is likewise sound and sober. When the soul goes bad, the talent is infected too. Haven't you noticed that when a man's soul has gone sluggish, his limbs drag and his feet shuffle? That if the soul is soft, you can spot the softness in the very walk? That a keen, confident soul quickens the step? That madness in the soul — or anger, which is madness's near relation — speeds the body's movements from walking to rushing?

And how much more must all this affect one's talent, which is woven through and through with the soul — shaped by it, taking its orders from it, receiving its laws from it.

How Maecenas lived is too well known to need retelling here: how he walked, how soft he was, how he loved putting himself on display — how determined he was that his vices should not go unnoticed. Well then: doesn't the looseness of his prose match his loose, ungirt clothing? Are his habits, his entourage, his house, his wife, any less on display than his sentences? He could have been a man of great powers, if he had gone at his work by the straight road — if he hadn't shrunk from being understood, if he hadn't been just as slack in his style as in everything else. What you find in his eloquence, you will see, is a drunk man's talk: winding, wandering, sprawling without limit.

Is there anything more unbecoming than this: "a stream and a bank all shaggy with long-tressed woods"? Or look how "men plow the channel with boats and, churning up the shallows, leave gardens in their wake." Or this: "he crimps his lady-locks, and bills and coos, and falls a-sighing, like a lord of the forest who offers prayer with drooping neck." Or: "an unredeemed crew, they hunt out folk at feasts, and lay siege to households with the wine-cup, and by hope exact death." Or: "a Genius could scarcely stand witness to his own festival"; or "threads of slender tapers and crackling meal"; or "mothers or wives robing the hearth."

Reading through words like these, can't you instantly picture him — the man who was forever parading through the city with his tunic hanging loose? Even when he was standing in for the absent emperor, he was in undress when they came to him for the day's password. The man who sat as judge on the bench, or spoke in public, or appeared at any official function with his cloak pulled up over his head, only the ears sticking out, like the millionaire's runaway slaves in the farce. The man who, at the very moment the state was locked in civil war — the city in crisis, under martial law — went about in public attended by two eunuchs, both of them more man than he was. The man who had only one wife and yet was married a thousand times.

Those sentences of his — so faultily joined, so carelessly tossed off, arranged in such deliberate defiance of common practice — announce that their author's character was equally strange, equally unsound, equally off the track. He does get high praise, and deserves it, for his humanity: he was sparing with the sword, he shrank from bloodshed, and the only field where he showed what his power could do was his self-indulgence. But he spoiled that genuine credit with these ridiculous fussy affectations of style. Look closely and you see he was not really gentle, but soft — the proof is in his twisted word order, his upside-down expressions, and those startling thoughts of his, which often contain something great but go nerveless in the telling. Too much success, you would say, had turned his head.

Sometimes the fault lies in the man, sometimes in his times. When prosperity has spread luxury far and wide, people begin with more careful attention to how they look. Next they go crazy over furniture. Then it's the houses: how to spread them over more ground, as if they were country estates; how to make the walls gleam with marble shipped across the seas; how to gild the ceiling until it matches the shine of the inlaid floors. From there the exquisite taste moves to the dinner table, where the game is to win applause by novelty and by scrambling the customary order of the courses — serving first what used to come last, so that departing guests get the dishes that once greeted them when they arrived.

Once the mind has gotten into the habit of despising the usual, of finding the customary cheap, it starts hunting for novelty in speech as well. Now it dredges up obsolete, antique words and shows them off; now it coins words nobody has heard, or mangles existing ones; now a bold, incessant use of metaphor becomes the signature of style, according to whatever fashion has just come in. Some writers chop their thoughts short, hoping to impress by leaving the meaning in doubt and making the listener suspect his own dullness. Some dwell on a thought and stretch it out. Others go right up to the edge of a fault — which a writer really must do, if he wants grandeur — but these actually fall in love with the fault itself. In short, wherever you see a corrupt style winning applause, you can be sure the character of the age has also strayed from the true standard.

Just as extravagant banquets and elaborate clothes are symptoms of a sick state, so a slack style — if it's popular — shows that the mind, which is where words come from, has lost its balance. And don't be surprised that corrupt speech is welcomed not only by the grubby crowd but by our cultivated set as well; the two differ in their clothes, not in their judgment. What you may more fairly wonder at is that approval goes not just to the products of vice, but to the vices themselves. It has always been so: no talent has ever won acceptance without something being pardoned. Name me any famous man you like; I can tell you what his age forgave in him, what his age deliberately overlooked. I can show you many men whose vices never hurt them, and more than a few who were actually helped by them. I can show you men of the highest reputation, held up as models for our admiration — and yet if you set out to correct their faults, you erase them, because the vices are so entangled with the virtues that they drag the virtues down with them.

Besides, style has no fixed laws. It is changed by the usage of the crowd, and never stays the same for long. Many orators go raiding earlier centuries for their vocabulary and talk in the language of the Twelve Tables — Rome's oldest law code. To them, Gracchus, Crassus, and Curio are too polished and too modern; so it's back to Appius and Coruncanius! Others, determined to use nothing but worn, everyday expressions, sink into flatness. Both classes are degenerate, each in its own way — and it's every bit as degenerate to use only words that are showy, high-flown, and poetical, avoiding whatever is familiar and in common use. The one fault, I'd say, is as bad as the other: the first crowd grooms itself beyond all reason, the second neglects itself beyond all reason; the former shave their legs, the latter won't even shave their armpits.

Now let's turn to the arrangement of words. Here — what an endless catalogue of faults I could show you! Some writers are all for abruptness and rough edges, deliberately jumbling anything that threatens to flow smoothly. They want a jolt at every transition; whatever lands unevenly on the ear they call strong and manly. With others it isn't so much an arrangement of words as a setting of them to music, so coaxing and soft is the glide of their style. And what shall I say of the arrangement where words are held back and back, until, long awaited, they barely slip in at the sentence's end? Or of that gently closing style, Cicero-fashion, easing down its slope with the same poised descent every time, and always to the same familiar rhythm? Nor do the faults stop at the shape of the sentences — thoughts too can be petty and childish, or cheap, or more daring than decency allows, or flowery and cloying, or built to end in nothing, delivering sound and nothing more.

Some one individual makes these vices fashionable — whoever rules the eloquence of the day — and the rest follow his lead and pass the habit around. So when Sallust was at his height, phrases were lopped short, words stopped where you least expected, and obscure brevity passed for elegance. Lucius Arruntius, a man of rare plainness of character, who wrote a history of the Punic War, was a card-carrying member of the Sallust school. Sallust has the phrase exercitum argento fecit — literally "he made an army with silver," meaning he raised troops with money. Arruntius fell in love with this and planted the verb "make" all through his book. In one place he "makes a rout" of our forces; in another Hiero, king of Syracuse, "makes a war"; in another the news "makes" the people of Panormus surrender to Rome. I only want to give you a taste — the entire book is stitched together out of stuff like this. What Sallust kept for occasional use, Arruntius made a constant, almost continuous habit — and no wonder: Sallust's phrases came to him; Arruntius went out hunting for them. That is what comes of copying another man's vices. Again, Sallust wrote of "wintering waters." Arruntius, in the first book of his Punic War, has a storm that "suddenly wintered"; elsewhere, wanting to say a year was unusually cold, he says "the whole year wintered"; elsewhere he sends off sixty light transports with only soldiers and essential crew aboard "while the north wind wintered" — and he keeps propping up passage after passage with the same metaphor. Somewhere Sallust says that amid civil war a man "sought fame for fairness and goodness"; Arruntius cannot hold himself back from announcing at once, in book one, that there were vast "remindings" of Regulus.

Faults like these, stamped into a style by imitation, are not necessarily signs of slack standards or a debased mind. A man's true faults are personal, homegrown, and you can read his temperament from them: an angry man talks angrily, an excitable man in a flutter, a soft man in a style with no push-back in it. You see the same tendency in the men who pluck or thin their beards, who shave the upper lip close while carefully cultivating the rest, who wear cloaks in outrageous colors and see-through togas, and who will not lift a finger to do anything that might escape notice. They want to provoke attention, to catch the eye; they'll accept even disapproval, so long as they get looked at. That is Maecenas's style, and the style of everyone else who strays off the road not by accident but knowingly and on purpose.

This comes from a great sickness in the soul. Just as with wine, the tongue doesn't stumble until the mind buckles under the load and gives way or betrays itself — so with this drunkenness of style (what else can I call it?): it troubles no one unless the soul has started to totter. So take care of the soul. From the soul come our thoughts, from the soul our words, from the soul our bearing, our expression, our very walk. When the soul is sound and strong, the style is vigorous, energetic, manly; when the soul falls, everything else comes down in the ruins.

  Keep the king safe, and one mind rules the swarm;
  lose him, and the bees break faith.

The soul is our king. While it is safe, all the other functions stay at their posts and obey orders; let the soul wobble even slightly, and they wobble with it. And once the soul has surrendered to pleasure, its powers and its actions go slack, and everything it undertakes comes from a source that is limp and unsteady.

To keep on with my comparison: the soul is sometimes a king and sometimes a tyrant. A king, when it honors what is honorable, watches over the welfare of the body in its charge, and gives that body no shameful, no degrading orders. But a soul that is unrestrained, ruled by desire, gone soft, turns kingship into that most hateful and dreaded thing — tyranny. Then it falls prey to the passions, which dog its steps: exhilarated at first, like a mob gorged on a public handout that will be its ruin, pawing at what it cannot consume. But when the disease has gradually eaten the strength away, when luxury has worked into marrow and sinew, such a soul gloats over the very limbs its overindulgence has made useless. Barred from its own pleasures, it watches other people's instead; it becomes the procurer and the spectator of the appetites that its own excesses have left it unable to feel. Its heap of delights is less sweet to it than bitter, because it can no longer push all the old delicacies down its overworked throat and stomach, because it can no longer whirl through its maze of eunuchs and mistresses — and it grieves that so large a share of its happiness is shut off by the limits of the body.

Isn't it madness, Lucilius, plain madness, that not one of us stops to reflect that he is mortal? That he is fragile? That he is, after all, one single person? Look at our kitchens — the cooks scurrying among all those fires. Is it for one belly, do you think, that all this bustle of food preparation goes on? Look at our vintage wines and the storehouses stacked with vintages of many generations. Is it for one belly, do you think, that we lay down wine sealed with the names of so many consuls, gathered from so many vineyards? Look how many regions of the earth are under the plow, how many thousands of farmers are tilling and digging. Is it for one belly, do you think, that crops are sown in Sicily and Africa? We would be sane, and our wants would shrink to reason, if each of us would take stock of himself — measure his body's needs while he's at it, and grasp how little he can actually consume, and for how short a time. But nothing will help you toward moderation so much as this frequent thought: that life down here is short and uncertain. Whatever you are doing, keep death in view.

Farewell.

Letter 115: On the Superficial Blessings

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I wish, my dear Lucilius, that you would stop fussing so much over words and their arrangement; I have bigger things to commend to your care. Ask what to write, not how to write it — and ask even that not for the sake of writing it but of feeling it, so that you can press what you have felt deeper into yourself and, so to speak, set your seal on it. Wherever you notice a style that is too careful, too polished, you can be sure the mind behind it is equally absorbed in petty things. The truly great man speaks casually and easily; whatever he says, he says with assurance rather than with effort.

You know the type — the young dandies, beards and hair just so, fresh out of the bandbox. From them you will never get anything strong, anything solid. Style is the clothing of thought: if it has been trimmed and dyed and worked over, it tells you the mind has its flaws and blemishes too. Elaborate elegance is not the dress of a man.

If we were granted the privilege of looking into a good man's soul — oh, what a face we would see: how fair, how holy, how magnificent, how gracious and shining! Radiant here with justice and self-control, there with courage and wisdom. And besides these, thrift, moderation, endurance, refinement, kindness, and — hard as it is to believe — love of one's fellow men, that Good so rare among men: all shedding their glory over that soul. And forethought joined with fine taste, and rising out of them all, greatness of soul, the noblest virtue of the lot — heavens, what charm it would add, what authority and dignity! What a marriage of sweetness and power! No one could call such a face lovable without also calling it worthy of worship. If anyone ever did behold such a face, more exalted and more radiant than mortal eyes are used to, wouldn't he stop dead, as if struck by a visitation from above, and breathe a silent prayer — "May I be forgiven for having looked on this!"? And then, drawn on by the encouraging kindness of its expression, wouldn't we bow down and worship? After long gazing at a countenance so far above us, so far beyond anything we're used to seeing — gentle-eyed, yet flashing with living fire — wouldn't we then, in reverence and awe, come out with those famous lines of our poet Virgil:

  O maiden, what shall I call you? For your face
  is more than mortal, and your voice rings sweeter
  than any human tongue...
  Be gracious, whoever you are, and lighten
  this load we carry.

And such a vision will indeed be a present help and a relief to us, if we are willing to worship it. And that worship does not mean slaughtering fattened bulls, or hanging up offerings of gold and silver, or pouring coins into a temple treasury. It means a will that is reverent and upright.

There is not one of us, I tell you, who would not burn with love for this vision of virtue, if only he were allowed to see it. As it is, too much stands in the way — our sight is either pierced by excess glare or clogged with darkness. But if, just as certain drugs are used to sharpen and clear the eyesight, we are willing to clear the eye of the mind of what obstructs it, then we will be able to make out virtue even when it is buried in a body — even with poverty standing in front of it, even with lowliness and disgrace blocking the road. We will see that beauty, I say, though it be smothered in rags. And by the same token we will see straight through to evil and the deadness of a sorrow-laden soul, despite the broad glitter of riches flashing all around it, despite the false light of high office on one side and great power on the other beating hard against the eye.

Then we will finally be able to understand how contemptible the things we admire really are. We are like children, who count every toy a treasure, who hold necklaces bought for a penny dearer than their parents or their brothers. And what is the difference, as Aristo says, between us and the children — except that we grown-ups go crazy over paintings and sculpture, and our folly comes at a higher price? Children delight in the smooth speckled pebbles they pick up on the beach; we delight in tall columns of veined marble, hauled in from the sands of Egypt or the deserts of Africa to hold up some colonnade or a dining hall big enough to seat a city. We admire walls veneered with a thin skin of marble — knowing perfectly well what defects the marble is hiding. We cheat our own eyes; and when we have plated our ceilings with gold, what are we delighting in but a lie? For we know that under all that gilding lurks some ugly wood.

And this kind of surface decoration isn't confined to walls and ceilings. All those famous men you see strutting about with their heads in the air — their prosperity is gold leaf too. Look underneath, and you'll learn how much evil sits beneath that thin coating of titles.

Take the commodity that keeps so many magistrates and judges busy — that makes magistrates and judges: money. Ever since money came to be honored, the true honor of things has been in ruins. We become, by turns, merchants and merchandise; we ask not what a thing is but what it costs. We do our duty if it pays, and neglect it if it pays; we hold an honorable course as long as it feeds our expectations, ready to swing to the opposite tack the moment crooked conduct promises more. Our parents drilled the admiration of gold and silver into us; the craving, planted young, settled deep and grew as we grew. Then too the whole nation, at odds on everything else, agrees on this: this is what they respect, this is what they beg for their children, this is what they dedicate to the gods when they want to show gratitude — as if it were the greatest thing a human being can possess. And finally public opinion has sunk to this: poverty is a hissing and a byword, despised by the rich and hated by the poor.

Add to the account the verses of the poets, throwing fuel on our passions — verses in which wealth is praised as if it were the one credit and glory of mortal life. The immortal gods themselves, to hear the poets tell it, can neither give anything better than wealth, nor own anything better:

  The Sun-god's palace rose on towering columns,
  ablaze with gold.

Or look at the Sun's own chariot:

  Gold was the axle, gold the pole,
  gold the rims around the circling wheels,
  and every spoke was silver.

And finally, when they want to praise an age as the best of all, they call it the Golden Age. Even in the Greek tragic poets there are characters who trade innocence, health, and good name for profit:

  Call me a scoundrel — only call me rich.

  We all ask how much wealth he has; no one asks
  whether he's good.

  Nobody asks where your fortune came from, or how —
  only how big it is.

  Every man is worth exactly what he owns.

  What is shameful for us to own? Nothing.

  Rich, I choose to live; poor, I'd rather die.

  A man dies well who dies while making money.

  Money — that colossal blessing to mankind! —
  no mother's love can match it, no small child's
  lisp, no father's claim to honor. And if Venus's
  face has half its sparkle, no wonder Love sets
  gods and men on fire.

When these last lines were spoken at a performance of one of Euripides' tragedies, the entire audience rose as one man to hiss the actor and the play off the stage. But Euripides jumped up, claimed a hearing, and asked them to wait and see what end was in store for this admirer of gold. Bellerophon, in that drama, was to pay the penalty — the penalty that every man pays in the drama of life. For greed never goes unpunished; though greed is, all by itself, punishment enough. What tears and toil money wrings out of us! Greed is miserable in what it craves, and miserable in what it gains. Think, too, of the daily worry, which torments every owner in proportion to what he owns. Having money is a greater agony than getting it. And how we grieve over losses — which fall heavy, and seem heavier still. And finally, even if Fortune takes nothing away, whatever we fail to gain on top counts as sheer loss.

"But," you'll say, "people call that man over there happy and rich; they pray to someday own as much as he does." True enough. And so? Can you imagine a more pitiable condition than owning misery and hatred into the bargain? If only those doomed to crave wealth could compare notes with the rich man! If only those doomed to chase office could confer with the ambitious men who have reached the top of the honors ladder! They would soon change their prayers — because these great men are forever gaping after some new gain while cursing everything already behind them. There is no one in the world satisfied with his own prosperity, even when it comes at a run. Men complain of their plans and of how their plans turned out; what they failed to get always looks better than what they got.

So philosophy can settle this problem for you, and give you what I count the greatest gift there is: never having to regret your own conduct. That is a happiness that holds; no storm can shake it. And no finely woven words, no smoothly flowing language, will steer you into that harbor. Let words go as they please — provided only that the soul keeps its own sure order; provided the soul is great, and holds steadily to its convictions, pleased with itself precisely for the things that displease everyone else; a soul that measures its progress by how it lives, and reckons that it knows exactly as much as it neither desires nor fears.

Farewell.
