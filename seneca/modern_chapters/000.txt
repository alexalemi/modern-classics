Letters 1–6

Letter 1: On Saving Time

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Keep doing what you're doing, my dear Lucilius: claim yourself for yourself. Gather up and guard your time — the time that until now was being taken from you, or stolen outright, or simply slipping through your fingers. Believe me when I tell you how it goes: some moments are torn from us, some are quietly lifted, and some just drift out of reach. But the most shameful loss of all is the loss that comes from carelessness. Look closely and you'll see that the largest part of life slips away from people while they're doing wrong, a good share while they're doing nothing, and the whole of it while they're doing what doesn't matter.

Show me the man who puts a real price on his time, who values each day, who understands that he is dying daily. Because that's our mistake — we see death as somewhere up ahead, when most of it is already behind us. Whatever years lie behind us are in death's hands.

So do exactly what you tell me you're doing: hold every hour in your grasp. Get a grip on today and you'll depend that much less on tomorrow. While we are postponing, life speeds by. Nothing, Lucilius, is ours, except time. Nature put us in possession of this one thing — this one fleeting, slippery thing — and anyone who feels like it can push us out of it. And how foolish people are! They'll let the cheapest trifles, things easily replaced, get charged to their account once received; but when they've been given someone's time, the most precious commodity there is, they never consider themselves in debt at all. And yet time is the one loan that even a grateful borrower can never repay.

You may want to know how I'm doing myself — I, who hand out this advice so freely. I'll confess frankly: my books balance the way you'd expect from a man who spends freely but keeps careful accounts. I can't claim I waste nothing. But I can tell you what I'm wasting, and how, and why; I can give you the full accounting of my poverty. My situation is like that of many people who've been reduced to little through no fault of their own: everyone forgives them, and no one comes to their rescue.

So where does that leave us? Here: I don't consider a man poor if the little he has left is enough for him. But you — hold on to what's really yours, and you can't start too early. As our ancestors used to say, it's too late to start saving when you're down to the dregs of the cask. What's left at the bottom isn't just the smallest part — it's the worst. Farewell.

Letter 2: On Discursiveness in Reading

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

From what you write, and from what I hear, I'm forming a good opinion of your future. You don't run around from place to place, unsettling yourself with every change of address — that kind of thrashing about is the sign of a disordered spirit. The first proof of a well-ordered mind, in my view, is the ability to stand still and keep your own company.

But be careful that all this reading — many authors, every kind of book — doesn't leave you scattered and unsteady. If you want ideas that will take root and hold, you have to stay awhile with a few master thinkers and digest their work. To be everywhere is to be nowhere. People who spend their lives traveling end up with many acquaintances and no friends — and the same goes for anyone who never gets close to a single great author but skims them all at a run. Food does no good, and never becomes part of you, if it leaves the stomach as soon as it's eaten. Nothing blocks healing like constantly switching medicines. No wound closes while you're trying one salve after another. A plant that keeps getting moved never grows strong. Nothing is so useful that it can help you while it's in transit. And a crowd of books is a distraction.

So, since you can't read all the books you might own, it's enough to own only as many books as you can read. "But," you say, "I want to dip into this one first, then that one." Tasting a little from many dishes is the sign of a fussy appetite: when they're many and varied, they cloy but don't nourish. Read the proven authors, and when you crave a change, go back to the ones you read before. Every day, acquire something that will arm you against poverty, against death, against every other misfortune as well; and after ranging over many thoughts, pick out one to digest thoroughly that day.

That's my own practice: from the many things I've read, I claim some one part for myself. Today's comes from Epicurus — yes, I make a habit of crossing over into the enemy's camp. Not as a deserter, mind you; as a scout. He says: "Contented poverty is an honorable thing." Though really, if it's contented, it isn't poverty at all. It isn't the man who has too little who is poor — it's the man who craves more. What does it matter how much sits in a man's safe or his warehouses, how large his herds are or how fat his interest payments, if he's eyeing his neighbor's property and counting up not what he's gained but what he hopes to gain? You ask what the proper limit to wealth is? First, having what is necessary. Second, having what is enough. Farewell.

Letter 3: On True and False Friendship

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You sent your letter by the hand of a "friend" — your word for him. And in your very next sentence you warn me not to discuss your affairs with him, since you don't make a habit of it yourself. In one and the same letter you've told me he is your friend and that he isn't. Now, if you were using the word loosely — the way we call every candidate for office "the honorable gentleman," or greet a man whose name escapes us as "my dear sir" — fine, so be it. But if you consider anyone a friend whom you don't trust as much as you trust yourself, you are badly mistaken, and you don't understand what real friendship means.

Talk everything over with a friend, by all means — but first, talk over the friend himself. Once friendship is settled, you must trust; before it's formed, you must judge. The people who get this backwards — who, breaking Theophrastus's rule, judge a man after making him a friend instead of making him a friend after judging him — have their duties exactly reversed. Take a long time deciding whether to admit someone into your friendship. But once you've decided to admit him, welcome him with your whole heart and soul, and speak with him as boldly as with yourself.

As for you yourself: live in such a way that you'd trust yourself with nothing you couldn't entrust even to your enemy. Still, since certain things are kept private by custom, share with your friend at least all your worries and all your plans. Treat him as loyal, and you will make him loyal. Some people, by fearing to be deceived, have taught men to deceive — their suspicion handed their friend the right to do wrong. Why should I hold back any word in my friend's presence? Why shouldn't I feel as alone in his company as I do by myself?

There's a class of people who will tell whoever happens to be nearest what should be told only to friends, unloading whatever's bothering them into any available ear. Others are afraid to confide even in those closest to them; if they could, they wouldn't trust even themselves, and they push every secret down deep inside. Do neither. It's equally a fault to trust everyone and to trust no one — though I'd say the first fault is the more innocent, the second the safer.

In the same way, there are two kinds of people who deserve a scolding: the ones who can never rest, and the ones who never do anything else. Because a love of bustle isn't industry — it's the restlessness of a hunted mind. And true rest doesn't mean writing off all motion as a nuisance; that kind of rest is slackness and inertia. So keep in mind a line I came across in Pomponius: "Some men shrink so far into dark corners that daylight itself looks dim to them." Mix the two: the resting man should act, and the acting man should rest. Take it up with Nature — she'll tell you she made both day and night. Farewell.

Letter 4: On the Terrors of Death

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Keep on as you've begun, and hurry all you can — the sooner you finish the work, the longer you'll get to enjoy a mind that is improved and at peace with itself. There's enjoyment in the improving too, of course; but the pleasure of contemplating a mind cleansed of every stain, a mind that shines, is something else entirely. You remember, surely, the joy you felt when you put away the clothes of boyhood, put on a man's toga, and were escorted down to the forum. Expect a greater joy still when you put away the boy's mind, and wisdom enrolls you among men. Because it isn't boyhood that stays with us — it's something worse: boyishness. And it's all the more serious because we carry the authority of age together with the follies of boyhood — of infancy, even. Boys fear trifles, small children fear shadows; we fear both.

All you need to do is keep advancing, and you'll come to understand that some things are less to be dreaded precisely because they frighten us so much. No evil is great if it is the last evil of all. Death arrives; it would be worth dreading if it could stay with you. But death must either never come at all, or come and be gone.

"It's hard, though," you say, "to bring the mind to the point where it can look down on life." But look at the trivial reasons that drive people to throw life away. One man hangs himself outside his mistress's door. Another throws himself off the roof to escape a bad-tempered master's taunts. A runaway drives a sword into his own belly rather than be dragged back. Don't you think virtue can manage what runaway terror manages so easily? No one can have a peaceful life who spends it scheming to prolong it, or who counts a long string of consulships among the great blessings. Rehearse this thought every day, so that you can leave life contentedly when the time comes. Plenty of men clutch and cling to life the way people swept down rapids clutch and cling at thorns and jagged rocks.

Most men toss miserably between the fear of death and the hardships of life: they don't want to live, and they don't know how to die. So make your whole life agreeable to yourself by giving up all anxiety about keeping it. No good thing makes its owner happy unless his mind is prepared for its loss — and nothing is lost more painlessly than the thing that can't be missed once it's gone. So encourage yourself, toughen yourself, against the blows that fall even on the most powerful. Pompey's fate was decided by a boy and a eunuch; Crassus's by a cruel and insolent Parthian. Gaius Caesar ordered Lepidus to bare his neck to the tribune Dexter's axe — and then offered his own throat to Chaerea. Fortune has never raised any man so high that she didn't threaten him with everything she had granted him. Don't trust her calm; in a moment the sea is churned to its depths. The very day the ships make their brave show at the games, they're swallowed.

Consider that a highwayman or an enemy could cut your throat at any time. And short of that, every slave holds the power of life and death over you, master or no master. I'll say it plainly: whoever holds his own life cheap is master of yours. Run through the men who have died by plots inside their own houses, killed openly or by trickery, and you'll see that slaves' anger has cut down as many as kings' anger has. So what does it matter how powerful the man you fear may be, when the thing you're afraid of is a power everyone has?

"But if you fall into enemy hands," you say, "the conqueror will order you led away —" yes, led to where you were already going. Why fool yourself, and act as if you've only just now been told the fate you've been under all along? Take my word for it: since the day you were born, you have been being led there. We must turn these thoughts, and thoughts like them, over in our minds if we want to be calm as we wait for that final hour — the one whose fear poisons all the hours before it.

But I need to end this letter. Here's the saying that pleased me today — picked, once again, from another man's Garden, Epicurus's: "Poverty brought into line with the law of nature is great wealth." Do you know what limits that law of nature sets for us? To keep off hunger, thirst, and cold — that's all. To banish hunger and thirst, you don't have to wait on the doorsteps of the purse-proud, or endure the hard frown, or the kindness that humiliates; you don't have to scour the seas or follow an army. What nature demands is close at hand and easy to get. It's the extras men sweat for — the extras that wear our togas threadbare, that keep us growing old in army camps, that fling us onto foreign shores. What is enough is within reach. Whoever has made a fair peace with poverty is rich. Farewell.

Letter 5: The Philosopher's Mean

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I applaud you, and I'm delighted that you're sticking to your studies and — setting everything else aside — working at one thing every day: becoming a better man. I don't just urge you to keep it up; I'm begging you to. But let me give you a warning: don't act like the people who want to be conspicuous rather than to improve, doing things with their dress or their way of living that are designed to draw stares. Rough clothes, unkempt hair, a slovenly beard, loud contempt for silverware, a bed on the bare ground — avoid that whole perverse wardrobe of self-display. The very name "philosophy" attracts enough sneers even when it's pursued quietly; what happens if we start seceding from ordinary human customs altogether? Inside, let us be different in every respect; outside, let us fit in with everyone else. Don't wear too fine a toga — and don't wear a shabby one either. We don't need silver plate crusted with solid gold; but let's not imagine that doing without silver and gold proves the simple life. Let's aim at a way of life better than the crowd's, not opposed to it — otherwise we frighten off the very people we're trying to improve. We also make them unwilling to imitate us in anything, for fear they'd have to imitate us in everything.

The first thing philosophy promises is fellow-feeling with all mankind — sympathy, sociability. Being unlike other people breaks that promise. We have to make sure the things we do to win admiration aren't simply ridiculous and repellent. Our motto, as you know, is "live according to Nature" — and it is flatly against nature to torture your body, to hate easy elegance, to be dirty on purpose, to eat food that isn't just plain but foul and revolting. Hunting for delicacies is decadence; avoiding ordinary things you can buy for next to nothing is madness. Philosophy calls for plain living, not for penance — and plain can perfectly well be neat. This is the middle course I approve of: a life that strikes a happy balance between the ways of the wise man and the ways of the world; everyone should admire it, but everyone should recognize it too.

"So then, shall we do just as everyone else does? Shall there be no difference between us and them?" A very great difference — but one that people discover when they look closely. A visitor to our house should admire us, not our furnishings. He's a great man who uses clay dishes as if they were silver — and just as great a man who uses silver as if it were clay. Not being able to endure wealth is the mark of an unsteady mind.

But let me share today's profit with you as well. In our own Hecato — no raid on a rival camp needed this time — I find that limiting your desires helps cure your fears too. "Cease to hope," he says, "and you will cease to fear." "But how," you'll ask, "can two such different things travel together?" It's true, my dear Lucilius: they look opposed, but they're linked. The same chain binds the prisoner and the soldier who guards him — and hope and fear, unlike as they are, keep step together. Fear follows hope. And I'm not surprised they move that way. Both belong to a mind in suspense — a mind fretting over what comes next. The chief cause of both is that we don't fit ourselves to the present but send our thoughts far ahead. And so foresight, the noblest gift the human race has, gets twisted into a curse. Wild animals run from the dangers they can see, and once they've escaped, they're at ease; we torment ourselves over what's coming and what's past alike. Many of our blessings do us harm: memory replays the agony of fear, and foresight previews it. Nobody is made wretched by the present alone. Farewell.

Letter 6: On Sharing Knowledge

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I feel, my dear Lucilius, that I am being not merely reformed but transformed. Not that I promise myself — or even let myself hope — that there's nothing left in me that needs changing. Of course there's plenty that should be tightened up, or thinned down, or built out. But this very thing is proof that my spirit has changed for the better: it can now see faults in itself it never noticed before. With some patients, it's cause for congratulation when they realize they're sick.

I wanted to share this sudden change in myself with you, because then I could begin to trust our friendship more surely — the true friendship that hope and fear and self-interest cannot break, the friendship in which, and for the sake of which, men are willing to die. I can show you plenty of men who have lacked not a friend but a friendship. That can never happen when two souls are drawn together into partnership by a shared desire for what is honorable. And why not? Because such men know they hold everything in common — especially their troubles.

You can't imagine how much progress I see each day bringing me. "Then send me a share of these gifts you've found so helpful," you'll say. Believe me, I want to pour all of it into you; part of my joy in learning anything is that I get to teach it. Nothing will ever please me — however excellent, however beneficial — if I have to keep the knowledge of it to myself. If wisdom were offered to me on one condition, that I keep it shut away and never speak of it, I would turn it down. No good thing is any pleasure to own without a friend to share it.

So I'll send you the books themselves — and, so you won't waste time hunting through them for the profitable parts, I'll mark the passages, so you can turn straight to what I approve and admire. But the living voice, and a life lived side by side, will help you more than any written word. You have to come and see for yourself — first, because people trust their eyes more than their ears, and second, because the road is long through precepts, but short and sure through examples. Cleanthes could not have become the very image of Zeno by merely hearing his lectures: he shared his life, saw into his private purposes, watched to see whether the man lived by his own rules. Plato and Aristotle and that whole crowd of sages who would each go his own way got more from Socrates's character than from his words. It wasn't Epicurus's classroom that made Metrodorus, Hermarchus, and Polyaenus into great men — it was living under the same roof with him. So I'm calling you here not only to gain something, but to add something; we can do each other a great deal of good.

Meanwhile, I owe you your little daily wage. Here's what pleased me today in Hecato: "You ask what progress I've made? I have begun to be a friend to myself." Real progress — a man like that will never be alone. And you may be sure of this: such a man is a friend to the whole human race. Farewell.
