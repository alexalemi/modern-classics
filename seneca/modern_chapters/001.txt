Letters 7–9

Letter 7: On Crowds

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You ask what you should avoid above all else? Crowds. You can't yet trust yourself in one safely. I'll confess my own weakness on this point: I never bring back home the same character that I took abroad with me. Something of the calm I've forced on myself gets shaken loose; some enemy I had driven off slips back in. Like an invalid who has been weak so long he can't be carried outdoors without a relapse — that's us, souls convalescing from a long illness. Mixing with the crowd does damage. Every single person in it will recommend some vice to us, or stamp one on us, or smear us with one before we've even noticed. And the bigger the mob we mingle with, the bigger the danger.

Nothing is as ruinous to good character as killing time at the games — that's where vice sneaks in through the open door of pleasure. What do I mean? I mean I come home greedier, more ambitious, more self-indulgent — crueler, in fact, and less human — because I've been among human beings. The other day I dropped in on the midday show, expecting some fun, some wit, a breather — the slot where the audience's eyes get a rest from human slaughter. It was the opposite. The morning's fights had been mercy itself by comparison. Now the fooling around is over, and it's butchery pure and simple. The men have nothing to defend themselves with. Every inch of them is open to the blow, and no blow ever misses. Plenty of people prefer this to the regular pairings and the special-request bouts. Of course they do — there's no helmet or shield to turn the blade. Why bother with armor? Why bother with skill? All that just delays death. In the morning men are thrown to the lions and the bears; at noon they're thrown to the spectators. The crowd orders each killer sent against the man who will kill him next, and keeps the latest winner in stock for another slaughter. The exit for every fighter is death, and fire and sword keep the show moving. And all this goes on while the arena stands practically empty.

"But he was a highway robber," you may say. "He killed a man!" So what? Granted he was a murderer and deserved the punishment — what crime did you commit, poor fellow, that you deserve to sit and watch it? All morning they scream: "Kill him! Whip him! Burn him! Why does he meet the sword like such a coward? Why does he strike so feebly? Why won't he die game? Lash him into his wounds! Make them trade blow for blow, chests bare, right into the stroke!" And when the show pauses for intermission, the announcement comes: "A little throat-cutting in the meantime, so there's still something going on!"

Come now — can't you see even this much, that a bad example comes back on the one who sets it? Thank the immortal gods that the man you're teaching cruelty to is one who can't learn it. A young character that can't yet keep its grip on the right has to be pulled out of the mob; it's too easy to go over to the majority. Even a Socrates, a Cato, a Laelius could have had their principles shaken by a crowd unlike them — that's how true it is that none of us, however hard we polish our nature, can stand the shock of vices marching in with a procession that size behind them. A single example of extravagance or greed does plenty of damage. A luxurious housemate softens and weakens us little by little; a rich neighbor keeps our envy stirred up; a spiteful companion rubs his rust off on us no matter how clean and honest we are. So what do you think happens to a character the whole world is assaulting? You will end up either imitating the world or loathing it.

Avoid both. Don't copy the bad because they're many, and don't hate the many because they're unlike you. Withdraw into yourself as far as you can. Associate with those who will make a better man of you. Welcome those you can make better yourself. It works both ways: men learn while they teach.

And don't let pride in advertising your talents lure you out before the public, reciting and lecturing. I'd tell you to go ahead if you had merchandise this mob wanted; as it is, not one of them can understand you. Maybe one or two individuals will drift your way, and even those you'll have to shape and train up to the point of understanding you. "Then who did I learn all this for?" Don't worry — nothing was wasted. You learned it for yourself.

But so that I won't have learned only for myself today, I'll share three excellent sayings that came my way, all circling the same point. One of them pays this letter's debt; the other two, accept as an advance. Democritus says: "One man means as much to me as a multitude, and a multitude as much as one man." And this was nobly said too, whoever said it (the author is disputed) — asked why he worked so hard at an art that would reach almost no one, he answered: "A few are enough for me; one is enough; none is enough." The third, and a fine one, is Epicurus, writing to a partner in his studies: "I write this not for the many but for you; each of us is audience enough for the other."

Take these to heart, Lucilius, and you'll learn to scorn the pleasure that comes from the applause of the majority. Many people praise you — but is that any reason to be pleased with yourself, if you're someone the many can understand? Let your good qualities face inward.

Farewell.

Letter 8: On the Philosopher’s Seclusion

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

"So you're telling me," you say, "to shun the crowd, withdraw from society, and settle for a clear conscience? What happened to your school's marching orders — die in the middle of active work?" Listen: the retreat I keep urging on you — my purpose in shutting myself away and locking the door is to help more people. I never spend a day in idleness; I claim part of the night for study too. I don't budget time for sleep — I surrender to it when I must, and when my eyes are worn out with waking and ready to fall shut, I hold them to the task. I've withdrawn not only from people but from business, and from my own business most of all. The work I'm doing is for later generations: writing down things that might help them. There are certain healthy counsels — think of them as recipes for useful medicines — that I'm putting into writing, because I've found them effective on my own sores, which may not be fully healed but have at least stopped spreading.

I point other men to the right road — the one I found late, worn out from wandering. I shout to them: "Avoid whatever the crowd loves, whatever Fortune tosses your way! Stop in doubt and fear before every windfall she brings — it's the dumb animals and the fish that get taken in by baited hopes. You call these things Fortune's gifts? They're traps. Anyone who wants a life of safety will steer as wide as he can of these sticky snares of her favor, which fool us — wretched in this too — into thinking we hold them, when they hold us. A career like that leads along cliff edges, and life on those heights ends in a fall. Once prosperity starts driving us off course, we can't even stand against her, or go down 'with the ship at least on her course,' or go down once and for all. Fortune doesn't capsize us — she drives the bow under and pounds us on the rocks.

"So hold fast to this sound and wholesome rule of life: indulge the body only as far as good health requires. Handle it a bit roughly, or it will stop obeying the mind. Eat to ease your hunger, drink to quench your thirst, dress to keep out the cold, and house yourself as shelter from discomfort — nothing more. Whether that house is built of turf or of colorful imported marble matters very little: understand that thatch covers a man just as well as a roof of gold. Despise everything that useless labor sets up as ornament and decoration. Keep in mind that nothing deserves wonder except the soul — and to a great soul, nothing else is great."

When I talk in these terms with myself and with future generations, don't you think I'm doing more good than when I show up as counsel in court, or press my seal into a will, or lend my voice and my hand in the senate to some candidate? Believe me, the people who seem to be doing nothing are doing the greater things: they're handling the mortal and the immortal at the same time.

But I must stop and pay the customary contribution that balances this letter. It won't come out of my own pocket — I'm still working through Epicurus. Today I read this sentence in him: "To enjoy real freedom, you must be the slave of philosophy." The man who submits and surrenders himself to her isn't kept waiting; he's freed on the spot. For the very service of philosophy is freedom.

Now, you'll probably ask why I quote so many of Epicurus's fine sayings instead of our own school's. But why should you count them as Epicurus's property rather than common property? Think how many poets say things philosophers have said — or should say. I won't even touch the tragedians or our national drama (which has its serious side too, standing halfway between comedy and tragedy). What a stock of shrewd verse lies buried in the mime! How many of Publilius's lines deserve delivery from tragic actors, not just comedians in slippers! I'll quote one verse of his that touches philosophy — the very part of it we were just discussing — where he says that Fortune's gifts should never be counted as our own:

  Whatever you have gained by coveting
  is still not yours.

I remember you put the same idea much better and tighter yourself:

  What Fortune makes yours is not really yours.

And a third of yours, better still, shouldn't be left out:

  The good that can be given can be taken away.

I won't charge these to the expense account — you got them from your own stock.

Farewell.

Letter 9: On Philosophy and Friendship

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You want to know whether Epicurus is right when, in one of his letters, he criticizes those who say the wise man is self-sufficient and therefore has no need of friendship. That's the charge he brings against Stilbo and the others who hold that the supreme good is a soul beyond feeling.

We run straight into ambiguity if we try to compress the Greek term for "no feeling" into one quick word — it can be read as the opposite of what we mean. What we mean is a soul that refuses to register any evil; what people will hear is a soul that can't endure any. Consider whether it isn't better to say "a soul that cannot be harmed," or "a soul entirely beyond the reach of suffering." Here's the difference between us and that other school: our wise man feels his troubles but overcomes them; theirs doesn't even feel them. What we share with them is this: the wise man is self-sufficient. And yet, sufficient as he is unto himself, he still wants friends, neighbors, associates.

Notice just how self-sufficient he is: at need, he can be content with a part of himself. If disease or war costs him a hand, if some accident takes one eye or both, what's left will satisfy him; maimed and diminished, he'll be as glad in his body as he was when it was whole. But not mourning what's missing doesn't mean not preferring it intact. The wise man is self-sufficient in this sense: he can do without friends — not that he wants to. And by "can" I mean this: he bears the loss of a friend with a level spirit.

He'll never actually be short of friends, though, because it lies in his own power how quickly he replaces a loss. If Phidias loses a statue, he can carve another on the spot; just so, this master of the art of making friendships can fill the place of a friend he has lost. How do you make a friend quickly? I'll tell you — provided we agree that I may pay my debt right now and square this letter's account. Hecato says: "I can show you a love potion made without drugs, without herbs, without any witch's incantation: if you want to be loved, love." And there's great pleasure not only in keeping up old, established friendships but in starting and winning new ones. The difference between winning a friend and having won one is the difference between the farmer sowing and the farmer reaping. The philosopher Attalus used to say: "It's more pleasant to make a friend than to have one, just as it's more pleasant for the artist to paint than to have painted." While the work has you absorbed, the absorption itself is a deep delight; the pleasure dulls once you lift your hand from the finished masterpiece. From then on he enjoys the fruits of his art; while he was painting, he enjoyed the art itself. With our children, their grown years bear the more abundant fruit, but their infancy was sweeter.

Now back to the question. The wise man, I say, self-sufficient though he is, still wants friends — if for no other reason, then so that he may practice friendship, so that a virtue that size doesn't lie idle. Not for the purpose Epicurus names in that letter — "so there's someone to sit by his bed when he's sick, someone to help when he's imprisoned or poor" — but so that he may have someone whose sickbed he can sit beside, someone held prisoner by an enemy whom he can set free. Whoever looks only to himself and comes to friendship for that reason has it backwards. And the end will match the beginning: he made a friend to help him out of chains; at the first rattle of the chain, that friend is gone. These are the fair-weather friendships, as people call them. Choose a friend for his usefulness and he'll please you exactly as long as he's useful. That's why prosperous men are mobbed by troops of friends while ruined men stand in a vast emptiness, their friends fleeing the very crisis that would have tested their worth. That's why we see so many shameful desertions, so many betrayals out of fear. Beginnings and endings have to agree. The man who starts being your friend because it pays will stop because it pays. If anything in a friendship attracts him besides the friendship itself, some other payoff will attract him away.

"Then what do I make a friend for?" To have someone I can die for. Someone I can follow into exile, someone whose life I'll stake my own against — and pay up, too. What you're describing is a business deal, not a friendship: it courts advantage and looks to the return. No question, the feeling of a lover has something in it akin to friendship — you might call love a friendship gone mad. So then: does anyone fall in love for profit? For advancement? For fame? Love on its own, careless of everything else, sets the soul on fire with desire for the beautiful — not without hope, yes, of affection returned. What follows? Can a cause that is more honorable produce a passion that is corrupt?

"But we're not discussing whether friendship is worth pursuing for its own sake." On the contrary — that's exactly what most needs proving. Because if it is to be sought for its own sake, then the man who is enough for himself may still seek it. "And how does he seek it?" The way you seek any thing of great beauty: not pulled toward it by hope of profit, not scared off by Fortune's mood swings. Whoever seeks friendship for its convenient occasions strips it of all its nobility.

"The wise man is self-sufficient." Many people get this sentence wrong, my dear Lucilius: they banish the wise man from the world and shut him up inside his own skin. But we have to mark carefully what it means and how far it goes. The wise man is sufficient unto himself for living happily — not for merely living. For mere living he needs a great many supports; for living happily he needs only a sound and upright soul, one that looks down on Fortune.

I'd like to show you a distinction of Chrysippus's as well. He says the wise man lacks nothing, yet needs many things; "the fool, on the other hand, needs nothing — he doesn't know how to use anything — but he lacks everything." The wise man needs hands, eyes, and countless things for daily use, but he lacks nothing. Lack implies necessity, and nothing is necessary to the wise man. So although he's self-sufficient, he still has need of friends — and wants as many as possible — but not so that he may live happily. He'll live happily even without them. The supreme good never sends out for equipment: it's raised at home, and the whole of it grows from itself. Let the good beg any part of itself from outside, and it has started taking orders from Fortune.

"But what kind of existence will the wise man have if he's left friendless in prison, or stranded among strangers, or held up on a long voyage, or cast up on an empty shore?" The kind Jupiter has when the world dissolves — the gods run together into one, Nature pauses her work for a while, and he settles into himself, given over to his own thoughts. That's more or less what the sage does: he retreats into himself; he is his own company. Still, as long as he's free to arrange his affairs by his own judgment, he is self-sufficient — and marries a wife. Self-sufficient — and raises children. Self-sufficient — and yet could not live at all if it meant living with no other human being. What draws him into friendship is no personal advantage but a natural pull. Friendship has a built-in attraction for us, the way other things do. Just as we hate solitude and reach for company, just as nature bonds man to man, so here too there's a prod that makes us hungry for friendship. Even so — though he loves his friends dearly, ranking them with himself and often above himself — the sage will keep all his good within his own borders, and he'll say what Stilbo said, the very Stilbo Epicurus's letter attacks. Stilbo's city had been captured, his children lost, his wife lost; he walked out of the general wreckage alone — and yet happy. Demetrius, whose destructions had earned him the name Sacker of Cities, asked whether he had lost anything. "I have all my goods with me," Stilbo said. There's a brave, tough man for you! The enemy conquered his city; Stilbo conquered his conqueror. "I have lost nothing" — he forced Demetrius to wonder whether he'd really won at all. "My goods are all with me": in other words, nothing that can be taken away counts as a good.

We marvel at certain animals because they can pass through fire without harm. How much more marvelous is this man, who marched out through fire and sword and rubble unhurt and unrobbed! You see now how much easier it is to conquer a whole nation than to conquer one man? Stilbo's saying is common ground with Stoicism: the Stoic, too, carries his goods intact through cities burned to ash, for he is self-sufficient. That is the boundary he draws around his own happiness.

And don't imagine that our school alone produces noble words. Epicurus himself, Stilbo's scold, said something much the same — take it as a credit to me, though I've already cleared today's account. "Whoever does not think that what he has is ample wealth is unhappy, though he be master of the whole world." Or, if this strikes you as better put (since we should aim at the meaning, not the words): "A man may rule the world and still be miserable, if he doesn't feel supremely happy." And to show you that these feelings are universal — Nature herself prompts them — you'll find this line in one of the comic poets:

  Unhappy is the man who thinks himself unhappy.

What does your condition matter, if it looks bad in your own eyes? "So then," you say, "take that man over there, rich by shameful means, or that one, master of many and slave of more — if they call themselves happy, does their own opinion make them happy?" What matters isn't what a man says but what he feels — and not what he feels on one particular day, but what he feels steadily. No need to fear this great prize falling into unworthy hands, though: only the wise man is pleased with what is his own. Folly is forever sick of itself.

Farewell.
