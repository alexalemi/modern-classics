Letters 83–84

Letter 83: On Drunkenness

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You ask me for an account of each of my days — the whole day, start to finish. You must think well of me if you assume there's nothing in them to hide. And in fact that is how we ought to live: as if we lived in plain sight of everyone. That is how we ought to think, too: as if someone could look into our inmost hearts — because someone can. What good does it do to hide something from people? Nothing is shut away from the sight of God. He is the witness of our souls; he stands in the very middle of our thoughts — and I say "stands in" as one who may, at any moment, walk out. So I'll do as you ask, and gladly report by letter what I'm doing and in what order. I'm going to keep watch on myself continually and — a most useful habit — review each day when it's over. This is exactly what makes us bad: none of us looks back over his own life. We think only about what we're going to do next. And yet our plans for the future always hang on the past.

Today has come to me in one piece; no one has stolen the smallest scrap of it. The whole of it has been divided between rest and reading. A small slice went to physical exercise, and on that score I can thank old age: my exercise costs very little effort. The moment I move, I'm tired — and tiredness is the whole aim and end of exercise, no matter how strong you are. You want to know who my training partners are? One is enough for me: the slave Pharius, a pleasant fellow, as you know — though I'll be trading him for another. At my time of life I need someone of even tenderer years. Pharius, for his part, insists that he and I are at the same stage of life, since we're both losing our teeth. Even so, I can barely match his pace when he runs, and before long I won't be able to match it at all — see what daily exercise does for you! A wide gap opens fast between two people traveling in opposite directions. He is climbing at the very moment I'm coming down, and you know how much quicker coming down is. No — I put that wrong. My life isn't coming down anymore; it's dropping straight off. Still, you ask, how did today's race come out? A dead heat — something that rarely happens in a footrace.

After tiring myself out that way (I can't call it exercise), I took a cold bath — which, at my house, means just short of hot. I, the old cold-water fanatic, who used to salute the new year with a plunge into the canal, who used to open every first of January with a dive into the Aqua Virgo as naturally as I'd sit down to read or write or draft a speech — I have switched my allegiance, first to the Tiber, and then to my own tank, which is warmed by nothing but the sun, and only on days when I'm at my strongest and my body has no complaints. There's not much bathing left in me. After the bath, some stale bread, and breakfast without a table — no need to wash your hands after a meal like that. Then a very short nap. You know my habit: I take the barest scrap of sleep — just unharnessing, so to speak. It's enough for me to simply stop being awake. Sometimes I know I've slept; other times I merely suspect it.

Listen — there's the roar of the Races all around me now! A sudden, unanimous cheer slams against my ears. But it doesn't shake my thoughts or even break their thread. I can put up with an uproar with complete patience. All those voices blended into a single note sound to me like waves breaking, or wind lashing the treetops, or any other noise that carries no meaning.

What, then, have I been giving my attention to? I'll tell you. A thought has been stuck in my head since yesterday: what did the shrewdest men in the world mean when they offered flimsy, tangled little proofs for questions of the greatest importance — proofs that may be true, and yet look exactly like tricks? Zeno, that greatest of men, the revered founder of our brave and holy school of philosophy, wants to talk us out of drunkenness. So listen to his argument that the good man won't get drunk: "No one entrusts a secret to a drunken man; but people entrust secrets to a good man; therefore, the good man will not get drunk." Now watch how ridiculous Zeno looks when we set a matching syllogism beside his. There are many to choose from, but one will do: "No one entrusts a secret to a man who's asleep; but people entrust secrets to a good man; therefore, the good man does not sleep."

Posidonius defends our master Zeno in the only way possible — and even that way, I hold, won't hold up. Posidonius says the word "drunken" is used in two senses: of a man who is loaded with wine and has lost control of himself, and of a man who habitually gets drunk — a slave to the habit. Zeno, he says, meant the second: the man in the habit of getting drunk, not the man who is drunk right now; and nobody would trust that man with a secret, because he might blurt it out over his cups. That's a dodge. The syllogism is about the man who is actually drunk, not the man who is going to get drunk. And you'll surely grant there's a big difference between a man who's drunk and a drunkard. The man who's drunk right now may be drunk for the first time in his life, with no habit at all; and the drunkard is often stone sober. So I take the word in its ordinary meaning — especially since the syllogism comes from a man who makes a business of precision, who weighs every word he uses. Besides, if that's what Zeno meant, and what he wanted it to mean to us, then he was exploiting an ambiguous word to smuggle in a fallacy — and no one should do that when the object is truth.

But fine — let's grant he meant what Posidonius says. Even then the conclusion is false: that secrets are not entrusted to a habitual drinker. Think how many soldiers — hardly models of sobriety — have been trusted by a general or a captain or a centurion with messages that had to stay secret! Take the famous plot to kill Gaius Caesar — I mean the Caesar who defeated Pompey and got control of the state. Tillius Cimber was trusted with it every bit as much as Gaius Cassius. Cassius drank water his whole life; Cimber was a sot and a brawler besides. Cimber even joked about it himself: "Me, carry a master? I can't even carry my liquor!" So let each of us call to mind the men we know who can't be trusted with wine but can be trusted with a secret. One case comes to my mind that I'll pass along, so it doesn't slip into oblivion — life should be stocked with vivid examples, and we shouldn't always be reaching back into the dim past.

Lucius Piso, the man in charge of keeping order in Rome, was drunk from the very moment of his appointment. He spent the better part of every night at banquets and slept until noon — that was his morning. Yet he applied himself most diligently to his official duties, which included the safekeeping of the city. Even the sainted Augustus trusted him with secret orders when he gave him command of Thrace — which Piso conquered. Tiberius trusted him too, when he went off on his Campanian holiday, leaving behind in Rome plenty of unfinished business that stirred up both suspicion and hatred. And I suspect it was because Piso's drunkenness had worked out so well for the emperor that he later named Cossus city prefect — a man of weight and balance, but so soaked, so pickled in drink, that once, arriving at a Senate meeting straight from a banquet, he sank into a sleep nobody could rouse him from and had to be carried home. This is the man to whom Tiberius sent many orders written in his own hand — orders he decided he couldn't trust even to the staff of his own household. Cossus never let a single secret slip, personal or public.

So let's retire all the set speeches like this one: "The man in the grip of drink is not master of his own soul. As new wine bursts the very vats, and the strength of the fermentation heaves the dregs up from the bottom, so when the wine froths inside a man, whatever lies hidden in him is carried up and brought into the open. A man overloaded with wine can't keep down his food — and he can't keep down a secret either. He pours out his own and other people's alike." That does commonly happen — but so does this: we take counsel on serious matters with men we know to be steady drinkers. So the claim laid down in defense of Zeno's syllogism — that a secret is never entrusted to the habitual drinker — is false.

How much better to indict drunkenness openly and expose its vices! Even the middling good man avoids them — never mind the perfect sage, who is satisfied once his thirst is quenched, and who, even if a friend's celebration occasionally carries him a little far, always stops short of drunk. Whether too much wine can scramble even the sage's mind and push him into a fool's behavior, we can look into later. Meanwhile, if you want to prove that a good man shouldn't get drunk, why work it out by logic? Say how disgraceful it is to pour down more than you can carry, without knowing the capacity of your own stomach. Say how many things drunk men do that make them blush when they're sober. Say plainly that drunkenness is nothing but voluntary madness. Stretch the drunkard's condition out over several days — would you have any doubt then that he's insane? As it is, the madness is no smaller; it just doesn't last as long. Think of Alexander of Macedon, who stabbed Clitus, his dearest and most loyal friend, at a banquet; when Alexander understood what he had done, he wanted to die — and he certainly should have.

Drunkenness ignites every vice and puts it on display, and it strips away the shame that veils our worst undertakings. For more men hold back from what's forbidden because they're ashamed of sinning than because their desires are good. When the wine grows too strong and takes command of the mind, every evil that was lurking comes out of its hiding place. Drunkenness doesn't create vice; it merely brings it into view. That's when the lustful man doesn't even wait for the privacy of a bedroom, but grants his appetites everything on the spot; that's when the shameless man announces and advertises his disease; that's when the surly man restrains neither his tongue nor his fist. The arrogant man grows more arrogant, the ruthless man more cruel, the slanderer more spiteful. Every vice is off its leash and out in front. Add to this that we forget who we are; the words come halting and slurred; the eyes swim; the step falters; the head spins; the very ceiling starts to wheel around as if a cyclone had hold of the whole house; and the stomach is in torment as the wine turns to gas and bloats our very insides. All of that can be endured, at least, while the man still has his strength. But what is he to do when sleep has sapped it, and what was drunkenness has become indigestion?

Think what calamities drunkenness has brought down on whole nations! This is the evil that has betrayed the most spirited and warlike peoples to their enemies; that has opened breaches in walls defended through years of stubborn fighting; that has forced under a foreign yoke peoples who were utterly unyielding and refused all mastery; that has conquered with the wine-cup men who were unconquerable in the field. Alexander — I just mentioned him — came safely through all his marches, all his battles, all his winter campaigns fought through every disadvantage of season and terrain, all the rivers flowing down from unknown sources, all the seas. It was overdrinking that laid him low — that, and the notorious death-dealing "bowl of Hercules."

Where's the glory in holding a lot of liquor? When you've won the prize; when the other guests, sprawled out snoring or vomiting, have declined your challenge to yet another round; when you're the last man standing at the party; when you've vanquished everyone with your magnificent show of prowess and no one has a capacity to match yours — you have been vanquished by the cask. Mark Antony was a great man, a man of distinguished ability; and what ruined him, what drove him into foreign habits and un-Roman vices, if not drunkenness — and, no less potent than wine, his love of Cleopatra? Drink made him an enemy of the state; drink left him no match for his enemies; drink made him cruel — for he would sit at dinner while the heads of the leading men of Rome were carried in; amid the most elaborate feasts and royal luxury he would study the faces and hands of the men he had proscribed; and heavy with wine, he still thirsted for blood. That he was getting drunk while he did such things was intolerable; that he did such things while actually drunk — how much worse! Cruelty usually follows hard drinking, because a man's soundness of mind gets corrupted and turns savage. Just as a lingering illness makes men whiny and irritable, wild at the least crossing of their wishes, so continual bouts of drunkenness brutalize the soul. When men are often beside themselves, the habit of madness settles in, and the vices the liquor bred keep their strength even when the liquor is gone.

So state the reasons why the wise man ought not to get drunk. Show the ugliness of the thing, and the evils that haunt it, by facts and not by words. Do the easiest thing of all: demonstrate that what people call pleasures turn into punishments the moment they pass their proper limits. Because if you set out to prove that the wise man can soak himself in any amount of wine and still hold his course, still steady even in his cups, then you may as well go on to conclude, syllogism by syllogism, that he won't die if he swallows poison, won't sleep if he takes a sleeping draught, and won't vomit up what's clogging his stomach when you dose him with hellebore. But when a man's feet are tottering and his tongue is tripping, what reason do you have to call him half sober rather than half drunk?

Farewell.

Letter 84: On Gathering Ideas

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

These journeys you mention — the kind that shake the laziness out of my system — I count as good both for my health and for my studies. Why they help my health, you can see: since my passion for books makes me lazy and careless about my body, traveling lets me exercise by proxy. Why they help my studies, I'll show you: I haven't cut back on my reading in the slightest. And reading, I hold, is indispensable — first, so that I'm not satisfied with myself alone; and second, so that once I've learned what others have found out, I can judge their discoveries and think about the discoveries still waiting to be made. Reading feeds the mind, and refreshes it when it's worn out with study — though the refreshment isn't had without study either. We shouldn't confine ourselves to writing only, or to reading only: endless writing will darken and drain your strength; endless reading will make it soft and watery. Better to turn from one to the other, blending them, so that the pen works the gatherings of your reading into something solid.

We ought to imitate the bees, as people say: they wander about and raid the flowers best suited for making honey, then arrange what they've brought home and sort it through the combs, and — as our Virgil puts it —

  pack close the flowing honey,
  and swell their cells with nectar sweet.

It isn't certain whether the juice they draw from the flowers is honey the moment they gather it, or whether they change what they've collected into that delicious thing by blending something with it — some property of their own breath. Some authorities believe bees have no art of making honey, only of gathering it; they say that in India honey has been found on the leaves of certain reeds, produced by a dew peculiar to that climate, or by the juice of the reed itself, which has an unusual sweetness and richness. Our own grasses, they say, carry the same quality, fainter and less obvious, and a creature born for exactly this work hunts it out and collects it. Others hold that what the bees have culled from the tenderest of the blooming, flowering plants is transformed into this new substance by being packed away and carefully stored, helped along by something like fermentation, which fuses the separate elements into one.

But I mustn't get pulled off into another subject. The point is: we too ought to copy these bees. We should sort out whatever we've gathered from our varied reading — such things keep better when kept separate — and then, applying the supervising care our nature has given us (our own talent, in plain terms), blend all those separate flavors into one delicious compound, so that even though you can see where it came from, it is clearly a different thing from its source. This is what we watch nature do in our own bodies with no effort on our part: the food we eat is a burden only as long as it keeps its original form and sits whole in the stomach; only when it has been changed from what it was does it pass into muscle and blood. Let's do the same with the food that feeds our higher nature: whatever we take in, let's not allow it to sit inside us unchanged and foreign — then it would never be part of us. It has to be digested. Otherwise it goes into the memory and not into the reasoning power. Let's welcome these ideas wholeheartedly and make them our own, so that out of many things one thing is formed — the way one number is formed out of many smaller sums when a single reckoning gathers up the different amounts. This is what our mind should do: let the mind hide all the sources; show only the result. And even if a resemblance does show through — to some writer your admiration has stamped deep into you — I want you to resemble him the way a child resembles his father, not the way a portrait resembles its subject. A portrait is a dead thing.

"What," you say, "won't people be able to tell whose style you're imitating, whose way of arguing, whose sharp sayings?" Sometimes, I think, they can't tell at all — not if the copy is a true one; for a true copy stamps its own form on everything it takes from its model, so that it all fuses into a unity. Haven't you noticed how many voices there are in a chorus? Yet out of them all comes one voice. One takes the high part, another the low, another the middle; there are women's voices in among the men's, and flutes woven between them. The individual voices are hidden; what you hear belongs to all of them together. (I mean the chorus the old philosophers knew. At our modern spectacles there are more singers on stage than there used to be spectators in the old theaters: the aisles are packed with rows of them, brass rings the auditorium, the stage throbs with flutes and instruments of every kind — and out of all those clashing sounds comes a harmony.)

That's the kind of mind I want mine to be: stocked with many skills, many precepts, and patterns of conduct drawn from many ages of history — but all of it blended into harmony, into one. "How is that done?" you ask. By constant attention — by doing nothing without the approval of reason. And if you're willing to hear her voice, reason will tell you: "Abandon the pursuits that have kept you running here and there. Abandon riches; they are either a danger or a burden to whoever holds them. Abandon the pleasures of body and mind; they only soften and weaken you. Abandon the chase for office; it is a swollen, idle, empty thing with no finish line, as anxious that no one gets ahead of it as that no one comes up behind it. It is sick with envy — a double envy, in fact; and you see how wretched a man is when he is envied and envies too."

Do you see those mansions of the great, those doorways loud with the shoving of morning callers? They have plenty of insults for you on your way in the door, and more once you're inside. Walk past the staircases of the rich and the porches made hazardous by the crush — there you'd be standing not just on the edge of a cliff but on slippery ground. Set your course instead toward wisdom; seek her ways, which are ways of surpassing peace and plenty. Whatever looks prominent in human affairs — however small it really is, and prominent only next to what's lowest — is still reached by a hard, grinding climb. It is a rough road that leads to the heights of greatness. But if you choose to scale this peak, which stands far above Fortune's whole range, you will look down from there on everything men count loftiest — and yet you can reach the top over level ground.

Farewell.
