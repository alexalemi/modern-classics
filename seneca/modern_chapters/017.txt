Letter 66

Letter 66: On Various Aspects of Virtue

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I have just seen Claranus, an old schoolmate of mine, for the first time in many years. You don't need me to add that he is an old man now; but I can tell you that I found him vigorous in spirit and sturdy, even though he is wrestling with a frail and feeble body. Nature was unfair to him — she gave a rare soul a poor house to live in. Or maybe she did it deliberately, to prove to us that an absolutely strong and happy mind can hide under any exterior at all. Either way, Claranus overcomes every one of his handicaps: he began by despising his own body, and from there he has worked his way up to despising everything else that doesn't matter. The poet who sang

  Virtue shows more pleasing in a form that's fair,

was mistaken, in my opinion. Virtue needs nothing to set it off; it is its own great glory, and it makes holy whatever body it lives in. At any rate, I have started to see Claranus in a different light. To me he looks handsome — as straight and well-built in body as he is in mind. A great man can come out of a shack; just so, a great and beautiful soul can come out of an ugly, insignificant body. I think Nature breeds certain men of this stamp on purpose, to prove that virtue can be born absolutely anywhere. If she could have produced souls by themselves, naked, she would have done it. As it is, she does something even greater: she produces men whose bodies are obstacles — and who break through the obstruction anyway. Claranus, I believe, was produced as a demonstration, so that we could understand that a soul is not disfigured by an ugly body; rather, it is the body that is beautified by the loveliness of the soul.

Now, though Claranus and I spent only a few days together, we had many conversations, and I'll pour them out to you here just as they came. The first day we dug into this problem: how can all goods be equal if they come in three kinds? According to our school's teaching, certain goods are primary — joy, peace, the welfare of one's country. Others are of the second order, molded out of unhappy material — endurance under suffering, self-control during a severe illness. For the first kind we will pray outright; for the second, only if the need arises. And there is a third variety besides: a modest walk, a calm and honest face, a bearing that suits the wise man. Now how can these things be equal to one another, when we ought to pray for some of them and avoid others?

If we want to sort them out, we had better go back to the First Good and ask what its nature really is. It is this: the soul that gazes straight at truth; that is skilled in what should be sought and what should be avoided; that sets its values not by opinion but by nature; that ranges through the whole universe and turns its contemplating gaze on everything in it; that keeps strict watch over its own thoughts and actions, equally great and equally forceful in both; that stands above hardships and above flattery alike, surrendering to neither extreme of fortune; that rises above everything that comes, blessings and disasters both — a soul absolutely beautiful, perfectly furnished with grace and with strength together, healthy and lean, unruffled, undismayed, a soul no violence can shatter, a soul that no stroke of luck can puff up or beat down. A soul like that is virtue itself. That's what virtue would look like if it ever came into a single view and showed itself all at once, complete. But it has many aspects. They unfold as life varies and as actions differ; yet virtue itself never becomes greater or smaller. The Supreme Good cannot shrink, and virtue cannot slide backward; instead, it transforms itself — now into this quality, now into that — shaping itself to the role it has to play. Whatever it touches, it makes like itself; it dyes everything its own color. It beautifies our actions, our friendships, and sometimes whole households that it has entered and set in order. Whatever it has handled it makes, then and there, lovable, remarkable, admirable.

So the power and greatness of virtue cannot rise any higher, because what is already greatest has no room to increase. You will find nothing straighter than straight, nothing truer than true, nothing more temperate than perfect self-control. Every virtue is limitless — for limits belong to things with fixed measurements. Constancy cannot advance any further; neither can faithfulness, or truthfulness, or loyalty. What can be added to what is perfect? Nothing — otherwise the thing you added to wasn't perfect. And nothing can be added to virtue either; if anything could be added, something must have been missing. Honor, likewise, permits no additions: it is honorable precisely because of the qualities I have named. And what else? Don't you think propriety, justice, and lawfulness belong to the same type — held within fixed bounds? Being able to grow is proof that a thing is still imperfect.

The good, in every case, obeys these same laws. The advantage of the state and the advantage of the individual are yoked together; you can no more separate them than you can separate the praiseworthy from the desirable. Therefore the virtues are all equal to one another; and so are the works of virtue, and so are all the men lucky enough to possess them. The virtues of plants and animals, on the other hand, are perishable — frail, fleeting, uncertain. They spring up and they sink back down, and for that reason they aren't rated at the same value. But human virtues fall under one rule only, because right reason is single and of one kind only. Nothing is more divine than the divine, or more heavenly than the heavenly. Mortal things decay, fall, wear out, grow, drain away, refill; because their lot is uncertain, there is inequality among them. But the nature of divine things is one. And reason is nothing else than a piece of the divine spirit set inside a human body. If reason is divine, and no good is without reason, then every good is divine. Furthermore, there is no distinction between things divine — so there is none between goods, either. Therefore it follows that joy, and a brave unyielding endurance of torture, are equal goods; in both there is the same greatness of soul — relaxed and cheerful in the one case, combative and braced for battle in the other.

What — don't you think the courage of the man who bravely storms the enemy's stronghold equals the courage of the man who endures a siege with total patience? Scipio is great when he rings Numantia with his siege-works and squeezes it, driving an enemy he could not conquer to turn their own hands against themselves. But great, too, are the souls of the defenders — men who know that as long as the road to death lies open, no blockade is complete; men who breathe their last in the arms of liberty. In the same way all the other virtues are equal to one another: tranquility, simplicity, generosity, constancy, even-temperedness, endurance. For underneath them all lies a single virtue — the one that keeps the soul straight and unswerving.

"What then," you say, "is there no difference between joy and the unyielding endurance of pain?" None at all, as far as the virtues themselves are concerned; a very great one, however, in the circumstances in which each virtue gets displayed. In the one case there is a natural relaxing and loosening of the soul; in the other, an unnatural pain. So the circumstances, between which a great gulf can be drawn, belong to the category of indifferent things — but the virtue shown in each case is equal. Virtue is not changed by the material it works on. Hard, stubborn material does not make it worse; pleasant, joyful material does not make it better. So virtue necessarily stays equal. In each case, what is done is done with equal rightness, equal wisdom, and equal honor. The goodness on both sides is equal, and neither man can rise above it — not the one in his joy, not the other in his suffering; each is doing as well as a human being can do. And two goods, neither of which can possibly be better, are equal.

For if things outside virtue could shrink it or swell it, then the honorable would stop being the only good. Grant that, and honor has perished altogether. Why? Let me tell you: because no act is honorable that is done unwillingly, under compulsion. Every honorable act is voluntary. Mix in reluctance, complaints, cowardice, or fear, and it loses its best feature — its own approval of itself. What is not free cannot be honorable, for fear means slavery. The honorable is entirely free of anxiety; it is calm. If it objects, if it laments, if it counts anything as an evil, it falls into turmoil and starts floundering in deep confusion — on one side the appearance of right calls it forward; on the other, the suspicion of evil drags it back. So when a man is about to do something honorable, he should not count any obstacle as an evil — inconvenient, maybe, but not evil — and he should will the deed and do it willingly. Every honorable act is done without orders and without compulsion; it is pure, and mixed with no evil at all.

I know what you may say to me at this point: "Are you trying to make us believe it doesn't matter whether a man feels joy or lies on the rack wearing out his torturer?" I could answer: "Epicurus also maintains that the wise man, even while he is being roasted inside the bronze bull of Phalaris, will cry out, 'How pleasant! This doesn't touch me at all.'" Why should you be surprised that I call the man reclining at a banquet and the victim standing firm under torture possessors of equal goods, when Epicurus maintains something far harder to believe — that it is pleasant to be roasted? But the answer I actually give is this: there is a great difference between joy and pain. If I'm asked to choose, I will seek the one and avoid the other. The first is according to nature; the second is against it. Rated by that standard, a great gulf stands between them. But when it comes to the virtue involved, the virtue is the same in each case, whether it walks in through joy or through sorrow. Vexation and pain and the other inconveniences count for nothing, because virtue overpowers them. Just as the brightness of the sun dims every lesser light, so virtue, by its sheer greatness, shatters and overwhelms all pains, annoyances, and wrongs; wherever its radiance reaches, every light that shines without virtue's help goes out. Inconveniences, when they run into virtue, matter no more than a storm-cloud matters to the sea.

Here is the proof: the good man will rush without hesitation toward any noble deed. Put the executioner in his path, the torturer, the stake — he will keep going, looking not at what he must suffer but at what he must do. He will entrust himself to an honorable deed as readily as he would to a good man; he will count it profitable to himself, safe, lucky. And he will hold the same opinion of an honorable deed loaded with sorrow and hardship that he holds of a good man who is poor, or wasting away in exile. Come now, set a good man rolling in wealth beside a man who has nothing — except that in himself he has everything. They will be equally good, though their fortunes are utterly unequal. The same standard, as I said, applies to things as well as to men: virtue is just as praiseworthy living in a sound and free body as in one that is sick or in chains. So you will not praise your own virtue any more if Fortune has favored you with a sound body than if she has given you a body crippled in some limb — that would be like rating a master low because he is dressed like a slave. Everything Fortune rules over is mere property: money, the body, position — weak things, shifting, quick to perish, held on uncertain terms. But the works of virtue are free and unconquered — no more worth seeking when Fortune treats them kindly, and no less when adversity is weighing them down.

Now, friendship among men corresponds to desirability among things. You would not, I imagine, love a good man more for being rich than for being poor, or love a strong, muscular man more than a slender, delicate one. Then neither will you seek or love a good thing that is cheerful and peaceful more than one that is full of struggle and sweat. Or if you do, then of two equally good men you will care more for the neat, well-groomed one than for the dusty, unkempt one. Next you will go so far as to prefer a good man sound in all his limbs and without a blemish to one who is weak or half-blind; and little by little your pickiness will reach the point where, of two equally just and sensible men, you will choose the one with the long curling hair! Whenever the virtue in two men is equal, the inequality in everything else about them disappears. All those other things are not parts of them; they are accessories. Would any father judge his own children so unfairly as to love a healthy son more than a sickly one, or a tall child of striking stature more than a short or middle-sized one? Wild beasts play no favorites among their young; they lie down and suckle them all alike. Birds share out the food fairly. Ulysses hurries back to the rocks of his Ithaca as eagerly as Agamemnon speeds to the kingly walls of Mycenae. No man loves his homeland because it is great; he loves it because it is his own.

And what is the point of all this? For you to know that virtue looks on all her works with the same eyes, as if they were her children — equally kind to all of them, and more deeply kind to the ones that meet hardship. Even parents lean with extra affection toward the children they pity. Virtue does not exactly love her troubled, heavily burdened works more than the rest; but like a good parent, she gives them more of her care.

Why is no good greater than any other good? Because nothing is more fitting than what fits, and nothing more level than what is level. You cannot say that one thing is "more equal" to a given object than another thing is; and so nothing is more honorable than what is honorable. Accordingly, if all the virtues are equal by nature, then the three varieties of goods are equal too. This is what I mean: feeling joy with self-control and suffering pain with self-control are equal. The joy in the one case does not outrank the steadfastness in the other — the steadfastness that swallows the groan while the victim is in the torturer's grip. Goods of the first kind are desirable; goods of the second are worthy of admiration; and they are equal all the same, because whatever hardship comes attached to the second kind is outweighed by the good itself, which is so much greater. Anyone who believes them unequal has turned his gaze away from the virtues themselves and is inspecting the packaging. True goods have the same weight and the same measure; the counterfeit kind are full of empty space, so that when they are put on the scale they come up short, however imposing and grand they look to the eye.

Yes, my dear Lucilius: the good that true reason approves is solid and everlasting. It strengthens the spirit and lifts it up so that it stays on the heights forever. The things the mob thoughtlessly praises as goods merely puff us up with empty joy; and the things feared as if they were evils merely strike panic into men's minds — for the mind is spooked by the mere appearance of danger, the same way animals are. Both of them distract and sting the spirit for no good reason: the one does not deserve joy, and the other does not deserve fear. Reason alone is unchangeable; reason alone holds fast to its decisions. For reason is not the slave of the senses but their ruler. Reason is equal to reason, as one straight line is equal to another; therefore virtue is equal to virtue, since virtue is nothing else than right reason. All the virtues are reasons. Reasons are reasons if they are right reasons; and if they are right, they are equal. As reason is, so are its actions; therefore all actions are equal too — for since they resemble reason, they resemble one another. And I hold that actions are equal to each other exactly insofar as they are honorable and right. There will be great differences, of course, as the material varies — now broader, now narrower; now glorious, now humble; now wide-ranging, now confined. But the best part in every case is equal: they are all honorable. In the same way, all good men, precisely insofar as they are good, are equal. There are differences of age — one is older, another younger; of body — one is handsome, another ugly; of fortune — this man is rich, that one poor; this one is influential, powerful, known to whole cities and peoples, that one is unknown to almost everybody and obscure. But in the one respect in which they are good, they are equal.

The senses cannot judge what is good and what is evil. They do not know what is useful and what is not. They cannot file an opinion unless the thing is right in front of them; they can neither see into the future nor remember the past; and they do not know what follows from what. Yet it is exactly from that kind of knowledge that a coherent sequence and succession of actions is woven, and a unity of life is created — a unity that will run on a straight course. Reason, then, is the judge of good and evil. Whatever is foreign and external she counts as dross; whatever is neither good nor evil she rates as a mere accessory, insignificant and trivial. For all her good resides in the soul.

But there are certain goods that reason counts as primary and goes after on purpose — victory, for example, good children, the welfare of one's country. Certain others she counts as secondary; these only show themselves in adversity — for example, an even mind in enduring a severe illness or exile. And certain goods are indifferent, no more according to nature than against it — a discreet walk, say, or a composed way of sitting in a chair. For sitting is no less natural an act than standing or walking. The two higher kinds of goods differ from each other. The primary ones are according to nature — such as taking joy in the devotion of your children and in the well-being of your country. The secondary ones are contrary to nature — such as courage under torture, or endurance of thirst when illness sets fever burning through a man's vitals. "What," you say, "can anything contrary to nature be a good?" Of course not. But the thing this good springs from is sometimes contrary to nature. Being wounded, wasting away over a fire, being broken down by disease — those things are contrary to nature. But to keep an unconquerable soul in the middle of such distresses — that is fully in accordance with nature. To put my point briefly: the material a good is concerned with is sometimes against nature, but a good itself never is — because no good exists without reason, and reason follows nature.

"Then what is reason?" you ask. It is copying nature. "And what is the greatest good a man can possess?" It is to conduct yourself according to what nature wills.

"There is no doubt," says the objector, "that peace which was never attacked brings more happiness than peace won back at the cost of great slaughter. There is no doubt, either, that unbroken health brings more happiness than health forced back to soundness, as it were, through endurance and suffering, after an illness that threatened life itself. And in the same way, there will be no doubt that joy is a greater good than a soul grinding to the bitter end through the torment of wounds or the stake." Not at all. Things that come by chance admit of enormous differences, because they are rated by their usefulness in the eyes of the people who experience them. But with goods, the only point to consider is that they agree with nature — and this is equal in all of them. When we vote for someone's motion in the Senate, it cannot be said that one senator is "more in accord with the motion" than another; they all vote for the same motion alike. I say the same of the virtues: they all accord with nature. And I say it of goods too: they all accord with nature. One man dies young, another in old age, still another in infancy, having caught nothing more than a first glimpse out into life. All of them were equally subject to death, even if death let one of them travel further along the road of life, cut off the second in his flower, and broke off the third at the very beginning. One man gets his release at the dinner table. Another's sleep slides straight over into the sleep of death. Another is snuffed out in the middle of his pleasures. Now set beside these the people who have been run through by the sword, or bitten to death by snakes, or crushed under falling buildings, or twisted piece by piece out of existence on the rack. Some of these exits can be counted better, some worse; but the dying itself is equal in all of them. The roads to the end are different; the end is one and the same. Death has no degrees of greater or less. It has the same limit in every case: the closing of a life.

The same is true of goods, I assure you. You will find one good in the middle of pure pleasure, another in the middle of sorrow and bitterness. One manages Fortune's favors; the other overpowers her assaults. Each is equally a good, though one travels a level, easy road and the other a rough one. And the end of them all is the same: they are goods, they deserve praise, they walk in company with virtue and reason. Virtue makes everything it acknowledges equal to everything else.

You needn't be surprised that this is one of our principles. In the writings of Epicurus you will find two goods that make up his Supreme Good, his blessedness: a body free from pain and a soul free from disturbance. These goods, once complete, do not increase — for how can what is complete increase? Suppose the body is free from pain: what can be added to the absence of pain? Suppose the soul is composed and calm: what can be added to that tranquility? Perfectly fair weather, polished to its purest brilliance, does not admit of becoming any clearer. Just so, when a man takes care of his body and of his soul, weaving his good out of both, his condition is perfect; he has found the complete answer to his prayers, so long as there is no storm in his soul and no pain in his body. Whatever extra delights fall to him beyond these two things do not increase his Supreme Good; they merely season it, so to speak — a little added spice. For the absolute good of human nature is satisfied with peace in the body and peace in the soul.

I can show you, right now, a graded list of goods in Epicurus's own writings, very like the one in our own school. There are some things, he declares, that he would prefer to have happen to him — bodily rest free from every discomfort, and a soul at leisure, delighting in the contemplation of its own goods. And there are other things which he would prefer not to happen, but which he praises and approves all the same — for example, the kind of resignation in the face of failing health and terrible suffering that I mentioned a moment ago, and that Epicurus himself showed on the last and most blessed day of his life. He tells us that he had to endure excruciating agony from a diseased bladder and an ulcerated stomach — pain so acute that it could not possibly get worse — "and yet," he says, "that day was none the less happy." And no man can spend such a day in happiness unless he possesses the Supreme Good.

So even in Epicurus we find goods that one would prefer not to experience — goods which, when circumstances decide the matter, must nevertheless be welcomed, approved, and ranked with the highest. We cannot say that the good which rounded out a happy life — the good for which Epicurus gave thanks with the last words he ever spoke — is unequal to the greatest goods there are.

Allow me, my excellent Lucilius, one bolder statement still: if any goods could be greater than others, I would choose the ones that look harsh over the mild and alluring ones — and I would pronounce them greater. Breaking your way through difficulties is a bigger accomplishment than keeping your joy within bounds. It takes the same reason, I know perfectly well, for a man to bear prosperity honorably and to bear misfortune bravely. The soldier who sleeps calmly in front of the ramparts when no enemy is attacking the camp may be every bit as brave as the man who, with the tendons of his legs cut through, props himself up on his knees and does not let his weapons fall. But it is the blood-stained soldier coming back from the front who hears the shout: "Well done, hero!" And so I would give the greater praise to the goods that have stood trial, that show courage, that have fought it out with Fortune. Would I hesitate whether to praise the maimed and shriveled hand of Mucius above the uninjured hand of the bravest man in the world? There Mucius stood, despising the enemy and despising the fire, watching his own hand drip blood over the flames on his enemy's altar — until Porsenna, the very king who had been demanding his punishment, grew envious of the hero's glory and ordered the fire carried away against the victim's will.

Why shouldn't I count this good among the primary goods — and count it greater, in this one respect, than those safe, untested goods that have never had a run-in with Fortune — exactly as it is a rarer thing to have beaten an enemy with a hand lost than with a hand armed? "What," you say, "will you desire that good for yourself?" Of course I will. This is a thing a man cannot achieve unless he can also desire it. Should I desire instead to stretch out my limbs for my slaves to knead — or to have a woman, or a man made over into the likeness of a woman, pull my finger-joints for me? I can't help believing Mucius was the luckier man, because he handled the flames as calmly as if he were holding out his hand to the masseur. He burned away all his old mistakes. He finished the war unarmed and maimed; and with that stump of a hand, he conquered two kings.

Farewell.
