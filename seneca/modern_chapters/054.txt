Letter 124

Letter 124: On the True Good as Attained by Reason

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

  Many an old-time precept I could hand you,
  if you did not flinch, ashamed to study
  lessons that look so lowly.

But you do not flinch, and no fine point of study scares you off. Your mind is too well trained to take up serious questions in a casual, easygoing way. I like your method: everything has to count toward some real measure of progress, and the only thing that annoys you is when the utmost subtlety accomplishes nothing at all. I am going to take some pains to show that this is not one of those cases. Our question is this: is the Good grasped by the senses, or by the understanding? And a corollary comes with it: the Good does not exist in animals or in small children.

Everyone who sets pleasure on the throne as the supreme ideal holds that the Good is a matter of the senses. We Stoics say it is a matter of the understanding, and we assign it to the mind. If the senses were the judges of what is good, we would never turn down a single pleasure — there is no pleasure that does not attract, none that does not please. And by the same token we would never voluntarily accept any pain, because there is no pain that does not grate against the senses. What's more, the people who are too fond of pleasure, and the people who fear pain above everything, would deserve no blame at all. Yet in fact we condemn men who are slaves to their appetites and their lusts, and we despise men who are too afraid of pain to dare anything worthy of a man. But what wrong would such men be doing, if they were simply consulting the senses — the very judges to whom you and your school have entrusted the whole test of what to seek and what to avoid?

Surely reason is the governing power in a question like this. Reason made the ruling about the happy life, about virtue, about honor; reason has made the ruling about good and evil too. With our opponents, the vilest part of us is allowed to pass sentence on the better part, so that the senses — dense, dull things, and more sluggish in human beings than in the other animals — get to pronounce judgment on the Good. Imagine someone trying to make out tiny objects by touch instead of by eyesight! To tell good from evil we would need the subtlest, sharpest faculty we possess — nothing cruder than the eye could even attempt it. So you can see in what ignorance of truth a man spends his days, and how flat he has knocked down the lofty and the divine, if he thinks the sense of touch can pass judgment on the nature of the Supreme Good and the Supreme Evil!

"Just as every science and every art," he says, "must have some element that is tangible, something the senses can grasp — that is where sciences start and where they grow from — so the happy life takes its foundation and its beginnings from tangible things, from what falls within the reach of the senses. Surely you admit that the happy life starts from things the senses can touch."

But what we call "happy" is what accords with Nature; and what accords with Nature is obvious, plain to see at once — just as obvious as what is complete. What accords with Nature, what is handed to us as a gift the moment we are born, is not the Good, I maintain, but the beginning of the Good. You, on the other hand, hand the Supreme Good, pleasure, to newborn babies — so that the child starts out at the very point the perfected man arrives at. You are putting the treetop where the root belongs. If somebody claimed that the child still hidden in its mother's womb — its sex unknown, delicate, unformed, unfinished — was already in possession of goodness, he would obviously be talking nonsense. And yet how little difference is there between the child who has just received the gift of life and the one who is still a hidden burden inside its mother! As far as understanding good and evil goes, they are equally developed; a baby is no more capable of grasping the Good than a tree is, or some animal.

And why is the Good absent from a tree or an animal? Because reason is absent. For the same cause it is absent from a child: the child has no reason yet either, and will reach the Good only when it reaches reason. There are creatures without reason; there are creatures not yet endowed with reason; and there are creatures who have reason, but only incompletely. In none of these does the Good exist, because the Good arrives only in reason's company. What separates the three classes I just listed? In the one that will never possess reason, the Good will never exist. In the one not yet endowed with reason, the Good cannot exist at that time. In the one that has reason but only incompletely, the Good is capable of existing — but does not exist yet. This is my point, Lucilius: the Good is not to be found in just any body, or at just any age. It is as far removed from infancy as last is from first, as far as the finished thing is from the thing that has only just sprung into being. So it cannot exist in a tender little body whose frame has only begun to knit itself together. Of course not — no more than in the seed.

Grant all this, and here is how to understand it. There is a certain good of a tree or a plant — but not when the first shoot is just breaking out of the ground. There is a certain good of wheat — but it does not yet exist in the swelling stalk, or when the soft ear is pushing its way out of the husk; only when summer days and the wheat's appointed ripeness have matured it. Just as Nature everywhere does not deliver her good until she is brought to completion, so man's Good does not exist in man until both reason and the man are complete. And what is this Good? I'll tell you: a mind that is free and upright, that puts other things beneath itself and puts itself beneath nothing. Infancy is so far from admitting this Good that boyhood cannot even hope for it; young manhood hopes for it without any right to; and even old age is very lucky if it has arrived there after long and concentrated study. If this is the Good, then the Good is a matter of the understanding.

"But," comes the retort, "you admitted a certain good of trees and of grass — so surely there can be a certain good of a child too." The true Good, though, is not found in trees or in animals; what exists in them is called "good" only as a courtesy. "Then what is it?" you ask. Simply whatever accords with the nature of each. The real Good cannot possibly find a home in an animal; its nature is more blessed, of a higher order altogether. Where there is no room for reason, the Good does not exist.

There are four natures to consider here: tree, animal, man, and God. The last two, because they have the power of reason, are of the same nature — distinguished only in that one is immortal and the other dies. The Good of one of them, God, is perfected by Nature herself; the Good of the other, man, is perfected by effort and study. The rest are perfect only after their own kind — not truly perfect, since they lack reason. For, to put the whole thing in a sentence, only that is truly perfect which is perfect according to nature as a whole, and nature as a whole possesses reason. Everything else can be perfect merely in its kind.

Whatever cannot contain the happy life cannot contain what produces the happy life; and the happy life is produced by Goods alone. In animals there is no trace of the happy life, and no trace of what produces it: in animals the Good does not exist. The animal takes in the world around it through its senses alone. It remembers the past only when it bumps into something that jogs those senses — a horse, for instance, remembers the right road only when it is standing at the start of it. In its stall it has no memory of the road at all, however many times it has trodden it. And the third division of time — the future — lies entirely beyond an animal's reach.

How, then, can we call their nature perfect when they have no experience of time in its completeness? Time is threefold: past, present, and future. Animals are given only the slice that matters most to them in their brief coming and going — the present. Rarely do they recall the past, and then only when some present reminder confronts them. So the Good of a perfect nature cannot exist in an imperfect nature; if a nature of that sort could possess the Good, then so could a vegetable. I don't deny that animals have strong and swift impulses toward the actions that seem natural to them — but those impulses are confused and disordered, and the Good is never confused or disordered.

"What!" you say. "Animals move in a disturbed and disorderly fashion?" I would say they moved in a disturbed and disorderly fashion if their nature admitted of order; as it is, they move in accordance with their nature. A thing can only be called "disturbed" if it could at some other time be undisturbed; a thing is only "in trouble" if it could be at peace. No man is vicious except one who has the capacity for virtue; in animals, their motion is simply what their nature produces. But — not to wear you out — there is a certain sort of good in an animal, a certain sort of virtue, a certain sort of perfection; only not the Good, or virtue, or perfection in the absolute sense. That privilege belongs to reasoning beings alone, the ones who are permitted to know the why, the how far, and the how. So: good can exist only in that which possesses reason.

Do you ask where this whole argument is heading, and what use it will be to your mind? I'll tell you. It exercises the mind and sharpens it, and by keeping it honorably occupied, guarantees that it will accomplish some good. And even this is a benefit — anything that detains men when they are hurrying toward wickedness. But I will say something more: I can do you no greater service than by showing you the Good that is rightfully yours — by lifting you out of the class of the animals and setting you on a level with God.

Why, tell me, do you nurse and train your physical strength? Nature has dealt out more of it to cattle and to wild beasts. Why do you cultivate your looks? When you have done everything you can, the animals still surpass you in beauty. Why the endless fussing over your hair? Let it hang loose in the Parthian style, tie it up like the Germans, let it run wild like a Scythian — you will still see a thicker mane tossing on any horse you please, and a handsomer one bristling on the neck of any lion. Even after training yourself for speed, you will be no match for a hare. Aren't you willing to walk away from all these contests — where you are bound to lose, since you are straining after something that was never yours — and come back to the Good that really belongs to you?

And what is that Good? A clear and flawless mind — a rival to God's — raised above mortal concerns, counting nothing of its own as lying outside itself. You are a reasoning animal. What Good, then, lies inside you? Perfect reason. Will you develop it to its farthest limit, to its fullest growth? Count yourself happy only when all your joys are born of reason — when, having surveyed everything men grab at, pray for, and stand guard over, you find nothing left that you desire; I do not say nothing you would prefer. Here is a short rule to measure yourself by, a test to tell you when you have reached perfection: "You will come into your own when you understand that the people the world calls fortunate are really the most unfortunate of all."

Farewell.
