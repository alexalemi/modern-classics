Letter 71

Letter 71: On the Supreme Good

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You keep sending me your particular problems, one after another, forgetting that a whole stretch of sea lies between us. Advice is worth most at the moment it's given, so by the time my opinion on some question reaches you, the opposite opinion may be the better one. Advice has to fit circumstances, and our circumstances are not just moving along — they're being whirled along. So advice should be produced on the spot; and even that is too slow. It should "grow as we work," as the saying goes. What I want to show you instead is the method for finding it yourself.

Whenever you want to know what to avoid or what to pursue, look at how it relates to the Supreme Good — to the purpose of your whole life. Everything we do ought to be in harmony with that. No one can put the details of his life in order until he has set the overall aim of his life in front of him. A painter may have all his colors mixed and ready, but he can't produce a likeness until he has decided what he wants to paint. The reason we go wrong is that we all think about the pieces of life, but never about life as a whole. An archer has to know what he's trying to hit; only then can he aim and guide the arrow with his skill. Our plans miscarry because they have no target. If you don't know which harbor you're sailing for, no wind is the right wind. No wonder Fortune has such power over our lives: we live by chance. And with certain people there's an odd twist — they don't know that they know things. Just as we sometimes hunt everywhere for the person standing right beside us, we tend to forget that the goal, the Supreme Good, is close at hand.

To grasp the nature of this Supreme Good, you don't need many words or any roundabout discussion. You should be able to point at it with one finger, so to speak, not scatter it into pieces. What's the use of chopping it into little bits when you can simply say: the Supreme Good is what is honorable? Better yet — and this may surprise you even more — what is honorable is the only good; every other so-called good is alloyed and debased metal. Once you convince yourself of this, and once you come to love virtue devotedly (merely loving it isn't enough), then everything virtue has touched will turn out blessed and prosperous for you, no matter how it looks to anyone else. Torture — if you lie there calmer in mind than the man torturing you. Illness — if you don't curse Fortune and don't surrender to the disease. In short, everything other people call an evil becomes manageable, and ends in good, if you rise above it.

Let this one thing be settled — that nothing is good except what is honorable — and every hardship will have a fair claim to the name of "good," once virtue has made it honorable. Many people think we Stoics are promising more than the human lot allows, and they have a right to think so: they're looking only at the body. Let them turn back to the soul, and they'll soon be measuring man by the standard of God. Rouse yourself, my excellent Lucilius, and drop the philosophers' word-games — the ones that shrink the most glorious subject in the world down to a matter of syllables, that lower and wear out the soul by teaching it in scraps. Then you'll become like the men who discovered these teachings, not like the men whose teaching does its best to make philosophy look difficult rather than great.

Socrates — who called all of philosophy back to conduct, and said the highest wisdom is telling good from evil — put it this way: "Follow these rules, if my words carry any weight with you, so that you may be happy — and let some people think you a fool. Let anyone who wants to insult you and do you wrong; if virtue lives with you, you will suffer nothing. If you wish to be happy, if you want to be, in good faith, a good man, then let this person or that person look down on you." No one can manage this until he has come to regard all goods as equal — because no good exists without the honorable, and the honorable is in every case equal.

"What?" you say. "Is there no difference between Cato winning the praetorship and Cato losing the election? No difference whether Cato is conquered or conqueror on the battle line at Pharsalia? When his party was defeated but he could not be defeated, was that goodness really equal to what he'd have had if he'd returned home victorious and arranged a peace?" Of course it was. It is the same virtue that overcomes bad fortune and keeps good fortune under control; and virtue cannot be increased or decreased. It stands at one height.

"But," you'll object, "Gnaeus Pompey will lose his army! The patricians, the noblest patterns the state ever produced, the front rank of Pompey's cause — a senate under arms — will be routed in a single battle. The wreckage of that great aristocracy will be scattered across the whole world: one part will fall in Egypt, another in Africa, another in Spain. And the poor state won't even be granted the privilege of being ruined once and for all!" Yes, all of it may happen. Juba's knowledge of every position in his own kingdom may do him no good, and no good the stubborn bravery of his people fighting for their king. Even the men of Utica, crushed by their troubles, may waver in their loyalty; and the good fortune that always followed the name of Scipio may desert Scipio in Africa. But long ago destiny "saw to it that Cato should come to no harm."

"He was conquered all the same!" Fine — put that down among Cato's "failures." Cato will bear whatever cheats him of victory with the same stout heart he bore what cheated him of the praetorship. The day he lost the election, he spent playing games; the night he intended to die, he spent reading. To him, losing the praetorship and losing his life belonged in the same category: he had convinced himself that a man must endure whatever can happen. And why shouldn't he suffer a revolution in the state bravely and calmly? What is free from the risk of change? Not the earth, not the sky, not the whole fabric of the universe — even though God's hand steers it. It will not keep its present order forever; some day will throw it from its course. Everything moves on its appointed schedule; all things are destined to be born, to grow, and to be destroyed. The stars you see wheeling above us, and this seemingly immovable earth we cling to and stand on — they will be consumed, and they will cease to exist. Everything has its old age; Nature simply sends all things toward the same finish line at unequal intervals. Whatever is will cease to be — and yet it will not perish; it will be dissolved back into its elements. To our minds, dissolving means perishing, because we see only what is nearest; the sluggish mind, sworn into the service of the body, doesn't see past its own borders. Otherwise it would bear its own ending, and the ending of everything it owns, with more courage — if it could hope that life and death take their turns like everything else in the universe: that whatever is put together is broken apart, whatever is broken apart is put together again, and that the eternal craftsmanship of God, who governs all things, is at work on exactly this task.

So the wise man will say just what a Marcus Cato would say, looking back over his life: "The whole human race, all who are and all who will be, stands condemned to die. Of all the cities that have ever ruled the world, and all that have been the shining ornaments of empires not their own, people will one day ask where they were — and they will have been swept away by destructions of every kind. Some will be ruined by wars; others will be eaten away by idleness, by the kind of peace that ends in sloth, and by the vice that brings down even mighty dynasties — luxury. All these fertile plains will be buried out of sight by a sudden flood of the sea, or the ground will settle and slip and drag them down into a yawning chasm. Why then should I be angry, or grieve, if I go a tiny interval of time ahead of the universal destruction?" Let great souls comply with God's wishes, and accept without hesitating whatever fate the law of the universe ordains. At death the soul is either sent out into a better life, to dwell with the divine in greater radiance and calm — or, at the very least, it suffers no harm: it is mingled back into nature and returns to the universe.

So Cato's honorable death was no less a good than his honorable life, because virtue does not stretch. Socrates used to say that truth and virtue are the same thing. Just as truth does not grow, virtue does not grow either; it has its full proportions; it is complete. So don't be surprised that goods are equal — both the ones we choose deliberately and the ones circumstances impose on us. Because if you once accept that they're unequal — if you rank the brave endurance of torture among the lesser goods — you'll end up ranking it among the evils too. You'll call Socrates unhappy in his prison; you'll call Cato unhappy as he tears his wound open again with his bare hands, more courageously than he inflicted it; you'll call Regulus the unluckiest man of all because he paid the penalty for keeping his word — even to his enemies. And yet no one, not even the softest creature alive, has ever dared to say that. People deny that a man like Regulus is happy; but for all that, they also deny that he is wretched. The earlier Academics do admit that a man is happy even under such tortures — but not completely, not fully happy. With that we cannot agree at all. Unless a man is happy, he has not attained the Supreme Good; and the good which is supreme has no higher degree above it — provided virtue exists in the man, provided adversity does not impair it, provided the virtue stands unharmed even when the body is injured. And it does stand. For as I understand virtue, it is high-spirited and exalted; whatever harasses it only rouses it. That spirit that young men of noble character sometimes catch — when the beauty of some honorable thing strikes them so deeply that they despise all the gifts of Fortune — wisdom pours that spirit into us and makes it ours for good. Wisdom brings the conviction that there is only one good, the honorable; that it can be neither shortened nor extended, any more than a carpenter's rule — the tool we test straight lines with — can be bent. Bend the rule at all, and you spoil the straight line. Apply the same figure to virtue, and we say: virtue too is straight, and it does not bend. Can you tighten what is already rigid? Virtue passes judgment on everything, and nothing passes judgment on virtue. And if the rule itself cannot be made any straighter, then the things virtue produces cannot be straighter in one case and less straight in another. They must correspond to virtue; therefore they are equal.

"What!" you say. "You're calling it equally good to recline at a banquet and to be stretched on the rack?" Does that shock you? Then here is something more shocking: reclining at a banquet can be an evil, and reclining on the rack can be a good — if the first is done shamefully and the second honorably. It isn't the material that makes an act good or bad; it's the virtue. Every act in which virtue shows itself has the same measure and the same worth. Right now the man who measures every soul by his own is shaking his fist in my face, because I hold that there is parity between the goods of the man who passes sentence honorably and the man who suffers sentence honorably; because I hold there is parity between the man celebrating a triumph and the man — unconquered in spirit — being carted along in front of the victor's chariot. Critics like that assume that whatever they can't do, can't be done; they pass judgment on virtue in the light of their own weaknesses. Why marvel that it can help a man — sometimes even please him — to be burned, wounded, killed, or chained in prison? To a man of luxury, a simple life is a penalty; to a lazy man, work is punishment; the dandy pities the hard worker; to the idle, study is torture. In the same way, we call anything we're all too weak for "hard" and "beyond endurance" — forgetting what a torment it is for plenty of men to go without wine, or to be rolled out of bed at dawn. None of these things is difficult by nature. We ourselves are soft and flabby. Great matters must be judged with greatness of soul; otherwise we'll blame them for what is really our own fault. It's like perfectly straight objects that look bent or broken when you sink them in water. It matters not only what you see, but with what eyes you see it: our souls are too dim-sighted to make out the truth. But give me an unspoiled, sturdy-minded young man, and he'll tell you the more fortunate person is the one who carries the whole weight of adversity on unbending shoulders — the one who stands taller than Fortune. There's nothing remarkable about not being tossed around when the sea is calm. Save your wonder for the man who is lifted up when everyone else sinks, who stays on his feet when everyone else is flat on the ground.

What element of evil is there in torture, or in the other things we call hardships? As far as I can see, the evil is this: that the mind sags, and bends, and collapses. None of that can happen to the wise man. He stands upright under any load. Nothing subdues him; nothing he must endure annoys him. He doesn't complain that he has been struck by something that can strike any man. He knows his own strength; he knows he was born to carry burdens. I don't remove the wise man from the human race, and I don't deny him the sense of pain, as if he were a rock that feels nothing. I remember that he is made of two parts. One part is irrational — that's the part that can be bitten, burned, hurt. The other part is rational — that's the part that holds firmly to its convictions, that is courageous and unconquerable. That is where man's Supreme Good lives. Until it is fully attained, the mind wavers in uncertainty; only when it is fully achieved does the mind become fixed and steady. So the man who has just begun — the man climbing toward the heights and cultivating virtue, even the man drawing near the perfect good who hasn't yet put the finishing touch on it — will slip back at times, will feel his mental effort slacken. He hasn't crossed the uncertain ground yet; he's still standing in a slippery place. But the happy man, whose virtue is complete, loves himself most of all when his bravery has passed the severest test — when what everyone else dreads he not only endures but welcomes, if that is the price of a duty honor imposes. He would far rather have people say of him "How much nobler!" than "How much luckier!"

And now I've reached the point your patient waiting has been calling me toward. Don't imagine that our human virtue rises above nature. The wise man will tremble, will feel pain, will turn pale — those are all sensations of the body. Then where does real distress live — the thing that is truly an evil? In the other part of us, no doubt: but only if these trials drag the mind down, force it to confess itself a slave, and make it sorry it exists. The wise man conquers Fortune by his virtue; yet plenty of men who profess wisdom are frightened by the flimsiest threats. And at this stage it's our mistake to demand the same from the learner as from the wise man. I'm still urging myself to do what I recommend, and my urging isn't followed yet. Even if it were, I wouldn't have these principles so ready, so well drilled, that they would rush to my defense in every crisis. Wool takes some dyes the moment it touches them; others it won't absorb unless it is soaked and steeped again and again. It's the same with doctrines: some the mind can apply the moment it accepts them. But this one — unless it has gone deep and soaked in for a long time, unless it has not merely tinted the soul but thoroughly saturated it — delivers on none of its promises. The teaching itself can be handed over quickly, in very few words: virtue is the only good; at any rate, there is no good without virtue; and virtue itself is located in our nobler part — the rational part. And what will this virtue be? A true and never-swerving judgment. From it every impulse of the mind will spring, and by it every outward appearance that stirs an impulse will be brought into clear focus. And it follows from that judgment to count everything virtue has colored as good — and as equally good.

Bodily goods are, to be sure, good for the body; but they are not good, full stop. They will have some value, but no genuine worth, and they will differ widely — some smaller, some greater. And we have to admit there are great differences among the followers of wisdom themselves. One man has already made enough progress to dare to lift his eyes and look Fortune in the face — but not for long; her overwhelming glare dazzles him, and his eyes drop. Another has come far enough to hold her gaze — unless, that is, he has reached the summit and stands full of confidence. Whatever is short of perfection is bound to be unsteady: advancing at one moment, slipping or faltering at the next. And it will surely slip back unless it keeps fighting forward — slacken your zeal and your faithful effort at all, and you go backward. No one can resume his progress at the exact point where he dropped it. So let us press on and persevere. More of the road remains than we have put behind us — but wanting to go forward is the greater part of going forward.

Of this task I am fully aware. I want it, and I want it with my whole heart. I can see you've been roused too, racing with tremendous energy toward infinite beauty. So let us race. Only on those terms is life a blessing to us. Otherwise it is delay — and disgraceful delay at that, while we busy ourselves with things that should disgust us. Let's see to it that all time belongs to us. And it cannot, until we ourselves first belong to us. When will we get to despise both kinds of fortune? When will we get to say — every passion subdued and brought under our own control — the words "I have conquered"? Whom have I conquered, you ask? Not the Persians, not the far-off Medes, not any warlike tribe beyond the Dahae. I have conquered greed, ambition, and the fear of death — the fear that has conquered the conquerors of the world.
Farewell.
