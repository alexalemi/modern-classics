Letter 92: On the Happy Life

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You and I will agree, I think, on the following: that outward things are sought for the body's sake, that the body is cared for out of regard for the soul, and that the soul itself has service departments — the parts that let us move and stay alive — which exist only for the sake of the ruling part. That ruling part contains something irrational and something rational. The irrational part obeys; the rational part is the one thing that takes orders from nothing else and refers everything else to itself. Divine reason, after all, is set in supreme command over the universe and answers to nothing; and the reason we carry around is the same stuff, because it is derived from the divine.

If we agree on that, we must agree on what follows: the happy life depends on one thing and one thing only — our attainment of perfect reason. Reason alone keeps the soul from bowing down; reason alone stands its ground against Fortune. Whatever state a man's affairs are in, it keeps him untroubled. And the only real good is the one that can never be damaged. The happy man, I declare, is the one whom nothing can make less than he is. He holds the heights leaning on no one but himself — for whoever props himself up on anything can fall. If it's otherwise, then things that are not us begin to hold great power over us. And who wants Fortune holding the upper hand? What sensible man preens himself on what isn't his own?

What is the happy life? Peace of mind, and a tranquility that is unbroken and continuous. You will have it if you have greatness of soul; you will have it if you have the steadiness to hold onto a good judgment once you have reached it. How does a man get there? By seeing the truth whole; by keeping, in everything he does, order, measure, and fitness; by having a will that is kindly and gives no offense, that stays fixed on reason and never leaves it, that earns love and admiration at the same time. To give you the whole principle in one sentence: the wise man's soul should be the sort of soul that would suit a god.

What more can a man want who already has everything honorable? For if dishonorable things can contribute to the best condition, then a happy life becomes possible without an honorable one. And what is more debased, or more foolish, than tying the good of a rational soul to irrational things? Yet certain philosophers hold that the Supreme Good can be increased, on the grounds that it is hardly complete when the gifts of Fortune run against it. Even Antipater, one of the great leaders of that school, admits he gives some weight to externals — though only a very little. But you see the absurdity: it is adding a lamp to broad daylight. What can a spark matter in the blaze of the sun?

If honor alone doesn't content you, then you must want to add either the calm the Greeks call "undisturbedness," or else pleasure. The first can be had in any case: the mind is free of disturbance when it is fully free to contemplate the universe, and nothing pulls it away from the study of nature. The second, pleasure, is simply the good of cattle. To honor it is to bolt the irrational onto the rational, the shameful onto the honorable. A pleasant tingle in the body affects this life of ours — so why hesitate to say that all is well with a man because all is well with his appetite? And would you count — I won't say among heroes, but among men — a person whose Supreme Good is a matter of flavors and colors and sounds? Let him fall out of the ranks of the noblest class of living beings, second only to the gods. Let him go herd with the dumb beasts — an animal whose delight is fodder.

The irrational part of the soul is itself double. One part is spirited, ambitious, headstrong; it lives in the passions. The other is lowly, sluggish, and sold out to pleasure. Philosophers have neglected the first — which, unbridled as it is, is still the better of the two, certainly braver and more worthy of a man — and have declared the second, the nerveless and ignoble one, indispensable to the happy life. They have ordered reason to wait on it. They have made the Supreme Good of the noblest of living beings a mean, abject thing — and a monster besides, a hybrid stitched together from parts that don't match. As our Virgil says, describing Scylla:

  Above, a human face and a maiden's breast —
  a lovely breast — below, a shapeless monster,
  huge in bulk, a dolphin's tail
  joined to a belly like a wolf's.

And yet Scylla is at least assembled from wild animals — dreadful ones, swift ones. From what grotesque shapes have these wiseacres pieced together wisdom! Man's primary art is virtue itself; joined to it is the useless, short-lived flesh, fit only — as Posidonius remarks — for taking in food. That divine virtue ends in filth; onto its higher parts, which are holy and heavenly, they fasten a sluggish, flabby animal. As for the second item on the wish list — quiet — although by itself it would do the soul no actual good, it would at least clear hindrances out of the soul's way. Pleasure, on the other hand, actively destroys the soul and softens all its strength. Where will you find elements so mismatched yoked together? To the most vigorous thing is joined the most sluggish; to the austere, the frivolous; to the most holy, the unrestrained — unrestrained to the point of impurity.

"What, then," comes the retort, "if good health, rest, and freedom from pain won't hinder virtue — shouldn't you seek them?" Of course I'll seek them. Not because they are goods, but because they are according to nature, and because I will acquire them through the exercise of good judgment on my part. What will be good in them, then? Only this: that choosing them well is a good thing. When I put on suitable clothes, or walk as I should, or dine as I ought to dine, the dinner and the walk and the clothes are not goods; the good is the deliberate choice I show in them, keeping in each thing I do a measure that squares with reason. Let me add this too: choosing clean clothing is a fitting aim for a man's efforts, because man is by nature a clean and well-groomed animal. So the choice of neat clothes is a good, not the neat clothes themselves — the good is not in the thing selected, but in the quality of the selecting. It's our actions that are honorable, not the objects they handle.

And what I've said about clothing, you may apply to the body. Nature has wrapped the soul in the body as in a kind of garment; the body is the soul's cloak. But who ever priced clothes by the wardrobe that held them? A scabbard makes a sword neither good nor bad. So about the body I give you the same answer: if I have the choice, I'll choose health and strength — but the good involved will be my judgment about them, not the things themselves.

Another retort: "Granted, the wise man is happy. Still, he doesn't attain the Supreme Good as we've defined it unless the means nature provides for reaching it are at his disposal. A man who has virtue can't be unhappy; but he can't be perfectly happy either if he lacks natural gifts like health or sound limbs." In saying this, you grant the claim that seems harder to believe — that a man in unremitting, extreme pain is not wretched, is even happy — and you deny the far less serious one, that he is completely happy. But if virtue can keep a man from being wretched, it will find it easier still to make him completely happy; for the gap between happy and completely happy is smaller than the gap between wretched and happy. Can a power strong enough to snatch a man out of disaster and set him among the happy fail to finish the job and make him supremely happy? Does its strength give out at the very top of the climb?

Life contains advantages and disadvantages — both beyond our control. If a good man is not wretched though weighed down with every kind of disadvantage, how is he not supremely happy just because he lacks a few advantages? As the load of disadvantages does not drag him down to wretchedness, so the lack of advantages does not pull him back from supreme happiness. He is just as supremely happy without the advantages as he is free of wretchedness under the load of disadvantages. Otherwise, if his good can be damaged, it can be taken from him altogether.

A little way back I said that a tiny fire adds nothing to the sun's light. The sun's brightness blots out any light that shines on its own beside it. "But," someone says, "certain objects block even sunlight." The sun is unimpaired even among obstacles: an object may come between and cut off our view of it, but the sun sticks to its work and keeps its course. Whenever it shines out from behind the clouds, it is no smaller — and no less punctual either — than when the sky is clear; because there is a great difference between something merely standing in the way of its light and something interfering with its shining. In the same way, obstacles take nothing away from virtue. It is no smaller; it merely shines with less brilliance. To our eyes it may be dimmer and harder to see; in itself it is the same and, like the sun in eclipse, is still exerting its power in secret. Disasters, losses, and wrongs have only as much power over virtue as a cloud has over the sun.

Then we meet someone who maintains that a wise man who has suffered bodily misfortune is neither wretched nor happy. He too is wrong: he is putting the results of chance on a level with the virtues, and giving the honorable no more weight than things with no honor in them. And what is more detestable, more unworthy, than filing contemptible things in the same class as things that deserve reverence? Reverence belongs to justice, duty, loyalty, courage, and prudence. The other list is worthless — a sturdy leg, strong shoulders, good teeth, firm healthy muscles — attributes the most worthless men are often blessed with in fuller measure. Again, if the wise man whose body torments him is to be rated neither wretched nor happy, but parked in some halfway position, then his life will also be neither desirable nor undesirable. But what is as foolish as calling the wise man's life not desirable? And what is further past believing than a life that is neither desirable nor undesirable? Again: if bodily ills don't make a man wretched, then they leave him free to be happy; for things that have no power to change his condition for the worse have no power to disturb that condition at its best either.

"But," someone will say, "we know what cold is and what hot is; lukewarm lies between. In the same way, A is happy, B is wretched, and C is neither happy nor wretched." Let's examine this figure they deploy against us. If I add more cold water to your lukewarm water, it ends up cold; if I pour in more hot, it finally turns hot. But with your man who is neither wretched nor happy, no matter how many troubles I add, by your own argument he does not become unhappy — so your figure offers no analogy. Now, set before me this man of yours, neither miserable nor happy. I add blindness to his misfortunes: he is not made unhappy. I cripple him: not made unhappy. I pile on afflictions, severe and unceasing: not made unhappy. Well then — a man whose life all these ills cannot turn to misery cannot be dragged by them out of a happy life either. And if, as you admit, the wise man cannot fall from happiness into wretchedness, he cannot fall into non-happiness at all. Once you've started to slip, where exactly do you stop? Whatever keeps a man from rolling to the bottom keeps him at the summit. Why, you ask, can't the happy life possibly be destroyed? It can't even be loosened at the joints. And that is why virtue, all by itself, is sufficient for the happy life.

"But isn't the wise man happier if he has lived longer, with no pain to distract him, than one who has had to wrestle bad Fortune all his days?" Answer me this: is he any better, any more honorable? If not, then he isn't happier either. To live more happily, he must live more rightly; if he can't do that, he can't live more happily either. Virtue cannot be stretched any tighter, and so neither can the happy life, which depends on virtue. Virtue is so great a good that it doesn't feel petty assaults like shortness of life, pain, and the body's assorted complaints. As for pleasure — it doesn't deserve so much as a glance from virtue.

Now, what is the chief thing in virtue? That it needs not a single day beyond the present, and keeps no running count of the days that are ours. In the smallest sliver of time, virtue completes an eternity of good. These goods sound incredible to us, beyond human nature — because we measure virtue's grandeur by the yardstick of our own weakness, and we give our vices the name of virtue. But doesn't it sound just as incredible that a man in extreme suffering should say, "I am happy"? Yet that very sentence was heard in the factory of pleasure itself. "Today, and one other day," said Epicurus, "have been the happiest of my life" — and this though on the one day he was tortured by a blocked bladder, and on the other by the incurable pain of an ulcerated stomach; and yet, that day was none the less happy. Why, then, should the goods that virtue bestows be incredible to us, who cultivate virtue, when they turn up even among people who take pleasure for their mistress? They too, base and small-minded as they are, declare that even in extreme pain and misfortune the wise man will be neither wretched nor happy. But that is incredible too — more incredible, in fact, than the other claim. I cannot see how virtue, once knocked from her heights, can help being flung all the way to the bottom. Either she must keep a man happy, or, once driven from that post, she cannot stop him from becoming unhappy. As long as virtue stands her ground, she cannot be driven from the field. She must either conquer or be conquered.

But some say: "Virtue and the happy life belong to the immortal gods alone; we can grasp only the shadow, so to speak, and semblance of goods like theirs. We approach them; we never arrive." Yet reason is common to gods and men. In them it is already perfected; in us it is capable of being perfected. What drives us to despair is our vices. Man, the second class of rational being, is of a lower order — a guardian, so to speak, too unstable to hold fast to what is best, his judgment still wavering and unsure. He may need eyes and ears, good health, an outward appearance that isn't repellent, and, beyond that, a longer span of days with his constitution intact. Through reason he can lead a life he won't regret; yet there also lives in this imperfect creature, man, a certain force that makes for badness, because he has a mind easily tipped toward the perverse. Suppose the badness that is out in the open, already stirred into action, were removed — the man is still not a good man, but he is being molded toward goodness. And whoever is missing any quality that makes for goodness is bad.

But the man

  in whose body virtue dwells, and spirit
  ever present,

is the equal of the gods. Mindful of where he came from, he strives to return there. No man does wrong in attempting to climb back to the heights he once came down from. And why shouldn't you believe that something divine exists in one who is a part of God? This whole universe that surrounds us is one thing, and it is God; we are God's associates, God's limbs. Our soul has the capacity, and it is carried up to that place, if vices do not hold it down. Just as our bodies are built by nature to stand erect and look up at the sky, so the soul — which can reach out as far as it will — was framed by nature for this very end: to desire equality with the gods. And if it uses its powers and stretches upward into its own proper region, it is by no foreign road that it struggles toward the heights. It would be a great labor to journey to heaven; the soul merely returns there. Once it has found the road, it marches on boldly, scornful of everything else. It casts no backward glance at wealth. Gold and silver — things fully worthy of the darkness they once lay in — it values not by the sheen that dazzles ignorant eyes, but by the muck of the old days, out of which our greed first pried and dug them.

The soul, I insist, knows that riches are stored somewhere other than in men's heaped-up treasure-houses; it is the soul, not the strong-box, that should be filled. It is the soul that men may set in dominion over all things and install as owner of the universe, so that its wealth has no boundary short of East and West; so that, like the gods, it possesses all things; so that, with vast resources of its own, it looks down from on high upon the wealthy — not one of whom enjoys his own money as much as he resents another's. When the soul has lifted itself to this lofty height, it treats the body too not as a thing to love but as a thing to supervise — a burden that must be carried; it will not serve what it was set over as master. No man is free who is a slave to his body. Pass over all the other masters that come from fussing too much about the body: the rule the body itself exercises is fussy and fastidious enough.

From this body the soul goes forth — sometimes calm, sometimes exultant — and once it has gone, it does not ask what will become of the deserted clay. Just as we take no thought for the clippings of our hair and beard, so that divine soul, at the point of leaving the mortal man, cares no more where its earthly container ends up — burned by fire, sealed under stone, buried in earth, torn by wild beasts — than a newborn child cares about the afterbirth. Whether this body is cast out for the birds to pick at, or devoured when

  thrown to the sea-dogs as prey,

what is that to him who is nothing? Even while it is among the living, the soul fears nothing that may happen to the body after death; such things may have been brandished as threats, but they were never enough to terrify the soul before the moment of dying. It says: "The executioner's hook does not frighten me, nor the ugly mutilation of a corpse exposed to the sneers of spectators. I ask no one to perform the last rites for me; I entrust my remains to no one's keeping. Nature has made provision that none shall go unburied. Time will lay away whatever cruelty has cast out." Maecenas put it eloquently:

  I want no tomb: for Nature gives
  outcast bodies burial.

You would think that was said by a man of strict principles. He did indeed have a noble and vigorous nature — but in prosperity he let it go slack, and it spoiled.
Farewell.
