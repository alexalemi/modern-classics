Letter 95

Letter 95: On the Usefulness of Basic Principles

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You keep asking me to take up, right now and without postponement, a topic I once said should wait for its proper time: whether the department of philosophy the Greeks call "paraenetic" and we Romans call "preceptorial" — the giving of practical advice — is enough by itself to make us perfectly wise. Now, I know you would take it in good part if I refused. But that is exactly why I accept your request all the more willingly, and I refuse to let the old saying lose its point:

  Don't ask for what you'll wish you hadn't got.

For sometimes we work hard to get things we would turn down if they were freely offered. Call that fickleness or call it childishness — either way, the cure for the habit is prompt compliance. There are plenty of things we want people to think we want, when we really don't. A lecturer sometimes hauls onto the platform an enormous work of research, written in the tiniest handwriting and rolled up tight; after reading off a good chunk of it he says, "I'll stop, if you like" — and up goes the shout, "Read on! Read on!" from the very people who are praying he'll shut his mouth then and there. We often want one thing and pray for another, and we don't tell the truth even to the gods — while the gods either don't listen, or take pity on us. Well, I shall be entirely without pity, and take my revenge by loading a huge letter onto your shoulders. If you find yourself reading it with reluctance, you may say, "I brought this on myself," and count yourself among the men driven frantic by the ambitious wives they schemed to marry, or the men tormented by riches they sweated blood to earn, or the men tortured by the titles they chased with every trick and every ounce of effort they had — and all the others who are the authors of their own misfortunes.

But enough preamble; let me come to the question. People argue like this: "The happy life consists in upright conduct; precepts guide us to upright conduct; therefore precepts are sufficient for the happy life." But precepts do not always guide us to upright conduct. They work only when the will is receptive; sometimes they are applied entirely in vain, when wrong opinions have the soul in their grip. Besides, a man may act rightly without knowing that he is acting rightly. Nobody, unless he has been trained from the start and equipped with complete reason, can develop to full proportions — understanding when he should do a given thing, and how far, and with whom, and how, and why. Without that training a man cannot strive wholeheartedly after what is honorable, or even steadily or gladly; he will forever be glancing back over his shoulder and wavering.

Then it is argued: "If honorable conduct results from precepts, then precepts are amply sufficient for the happy life; the first statement is true; therefore so is the second." Our reply: honorable conduct is indeed brought about by precepts — but not by precepts alone. "Well then," comes the answer, "if the other arts get along on precepts, wisdom can get along on them too; for wisdom is itself an art — the art of living. A pilot is made by precepts that tell him to turn the tiller this way and that, set his sails, use a fair wind, tack, make the best of shifting and contrary breezes — all in the proper manner. Other craftsmen are drilled by precepts too; so precepts can do the same job for our craftsman in the art of living." But notice: all those arts are concerned with the tools of life, not with life as a whole. That is why so much can clog them from the outside and complicate them — hope, greed, fear. But the art that professes to teach the art of living cannot be blocked by any circumstance from doing its work; it shakes off the tangles and drives straight through the obstacles. Would you like to know how different its standing is from that of the other arts? In their case, it is more forgivable to go wrong on purpose than by accident; in the case of wisdom, the worst fault of all is to sin deliberately. Here is what I mean. A scholar will blush for shame if he makes a grammatical blunder by accident — not if he makes one on purpose. A doctor who fails to notice that his patient is sinking is a far worse practitioner than one who notices it and hides what he knows. But in this art of living, it is the deliberate mistake that is the more shameful.

Furthermore, many arts — and the most liberal of them at that — have their own bodies of doctrine, not mere precepts of advice. Medicine, for example: hence the different schools of Hippocrates, of Asclepiades, of Themison. And no art with a theoretical side can exist without its own doctrines — the Greeks call them dogmas, while we Romans may say "doctrines" or "tenets" or "adopted principles," the sort of thing you find in geometry or astronomy. But philosophy is both theoretical and practical; it contemplates and acts at the same time. You are quite mistaken if you think philosophy offers you nothing but everyday assistance; her ambitions run far higher than that. "I investigate the whole universe," she cries. "I do not stay shut up in a mortal dwelling, content to hand you advice for or against. Great matters summon me — matters set far above your head." As Lucretius puts it:

  To you I shall reveal the ways of heaven
  And of the gods, and spread before your eyes
  The atoms — out of which all things are born,
  Grow, and are fed by nature's building power,
  And into which she breaks them at the end.

Philosophy, then, being theoretical, must have her doctrines. And why? Because no one can perform right actions properly except the man who has been entrusted with reason — reason that will let him, in every case, fulfill every category of duty. And he cannot honor those categories unless he receives precepts for every occasion, not just the present one. Precepts by themselves are weak — rootless, so to speak — if they are attached to the parts of life and not to the whole. It is the doctrines that fortify us, that keep us safe and calm, that take in at one sweep the whole of life and the whole of the universe. The difference between philosophical doctrines and precepts is the difference between elements and limbs: the limbs depend on the elements, while the elements are the source of the limbs and of everything else.

"But the old-style wisdom," people say, "did nothing but tell you what to do and what to avoid — and the men of those days were far better men. Ever since the scholars showed up, the sages have grown scarce. That frank, simple virtue has turned into hidden and crafty cleverness; we are taught how to argue, not how to live." No doubt the old wisdom, especially in its beginnings, was crude — just as the other arts were, which grew refined as they went along. But in those days there was not yet any need for carefully engineered remedies. Wickedness had not yet risen so high, or scattered itself so widely. Plain vices could be treated by plain cures. Now we need defenses built with all the more care, because the forces attacking us are so much stronger. Medicine was once the knowledge of a few herbs — to stop bleeding, to heal wounds; by degrees it reached its present tangled variety. And no wonder medicine had less to do in the early days! Men's bodies were still sound and strong; their food was light, unspoiled by art and luxury. But once they began to seek out dishes not to satisfy appetite but to arouse it, and devised countless sauces to whip up their gluttony, then what had been nourishment to a hungry man became a burden to a stuffed stomach. From that come pallor, and the trembling of wine-soaked muscles, and a wasted thinness that owes more to indigestion than to hunger. From that come weak, tottering steps, and a stagger like a drunkard's walk. From that comes dropsy, spreading under the whole skin, and the belly swelling into a paunch from the standing habit of taking in more than it can hold. From that come yellow jaundice, discolored faces, bodies rotting from within, fingers going knotty as the joints stiffen, muscles numbed past all feeling, and the heart pounding away without pause. Why should I mention the dizzy spells? Or the pain of the eye and the ear, the itching and throbbing of a fevered brain, and ulcers all through the digestive tract? Beyond these there are countless kinds of fever — some savagely acute, some creeping up on us with quiet damage, others arriving with chills and violent shaking. Why go through the other numberless diseases — the tortures that high living hands out?

Men used to be free of such ills, because they had not yet gone soft with indulgence, because they still governed themselves and supplied their own needs. They toughened their bodies with work and genuine toil, wearing themselves out with running or hunting or working the soil. They came home to food that only a hungry man could enjoy. Hence there was no need for our huge medical apparatus, all the instruments and the pill-boxes. Their health was plain for a plain reason: it takes elaborate courses to produce elaborate diseases. Just count the number of things — all destined for a single throat — that luxury mixes together after ransacking land and sea. So many different foods are bound to disagree with each other; they go down with difficulty and are digested with difficulty, each one jostling against the rest. No wonder the diseases bred by ill-assorted food are shifting and manifold; when so many unnatural combinations are dumped in together, something has to overflow. That is why there are as many ways of being sick as there are ways of living. The illustrious founder of the medical profession remarked that women never lose their hair or suffer pain in the feet. Yet nowadays their hair falls out and their feet ache with gout. Woman's physical nature has not changed; it has been conquered. By matching men's indulgences they have also matched men's diseases. They stay up just as late, they drink just as hard; they challenge the men at wrestling and at carousing; they match them in vomiting up from strained stomachs all the wine they poured in — drinking to vomit, and vomiting to drink again; they match them, too, in chewing ice to soothe a fevered digestion. Even in their lusts they yield nothing to the males — although they were born to the passive role (may the gods and goddesses confound them!) — devising the most contorted varieties of unchastity, and playing the man's part in men's company. Why be surprised, then, that we can catch out the greatest and most skilled of physicians in a false statement, when so many women are gouty and bald? By their vices they have forfeited the privileges of their sex; having put off their womanhood, they are condemned to suffer the diseases of men.

The doctors of the old days knew nothing of prescribing frequent feedings and propping up a feeble pulse with wine; they did not understand the drawing of blood, or easing chronic conditions with sweat-baths; they did not know how, by binding the ankles and arms, to draw back to the surface the hidden strength that had retreated to the core. They were not forced to hunt up so many varieties of relief, because the varieties of suffering were so few. But now — look how far the evils of ill-health have advanced! This is the interest we pay on the pleasures we coveted past all reason and all decency. Are you surprised that diseases are beyond counting? Count the cooks. All serious study is at a standstill; teachers of the liberal arts lecture to empty rooms in out-of-the-way corners. The halls of the professors and the philosophers stand deserted — but look at the crowd in the cookshops! Look how many young men besiege the kitchens of their gluttonous patrons! I pass over the troops of unhappy boys who, when the banquet is finally over, must endure other humiliations in the bedroom. I pass over the regiments of slave-boys kept for lust, sorted by nation and by color — all required to have the same smooth skin, the same first down on the cheek, the same style of hair, so that no straight-haired boy gets mixed in among the curly-heads. I pass over the swarm of bakers, and the mob of waiters who scurry in with the courses at a signal. Ye gods — how many men kept busy in the service of a single belly! What — do you imagine those mushrooms, the epicure's poison, are doing no secret damage, even if they haven't struck yet? Do you suppose your snow in summer doesn't harden the tissue of the liver? Do you suppose those oysters — sluggish meat fattened on slime — don't load a man down with their mud-bred heaviness? Do you suppose the so-called "Sauce of the Provinces," that costly extract of rotten fish, doesn't scald the stomach with its salted putrefaction? Do you suppose those festering delicacies, swallowed practically flaming from the kitchen fire, are quenched in the digestive tract without doing any harm? Then how foul and how unhealthy their belches are — and how men disgust themselves, breathing out the fumes of yesterday's debauch! You may be certain their food is not being digested. It is rotting.

I remember once hearing gossip about a notorious dish into which a cookshop, on its way to a well-earned bankruptcy, had piled together everything the epicures love to linger over: two kinds of mussels, and oysters trimmed round to the edible line, set off at intervals by sea-urchins, the whole thing flanked with mullets, filleted and served boneless. These days we are ashamed of foods served one at a time; people mash many flavors into one. The dinner table now does the work the stomach ought to do. I fully expect, next, to see food served pre-chewed — and how far off is that, really, when the shells and bones are picked out for us and the cook performs the office of the teeth?

"It's too much trouble," they say, "to take our luxuries one at a time. Let's have everything served at once, blended into a single flavor. Why should I reach for one dish at a time? Let many arrive together; let the dainties of the separate courses be combined and confounded. Those who used to claim this was all done for show and vanity should understand: it is not for display — it is a solemn offering to our sense of duty! Let the dishes usually served in sequence come drenched together in one sauce. Let there be no distinctions: let oysters, sea-urchins, shellfish, and mullets be mixed up and cooked in the same pan." Vomited food could not be jumbled together more thoroughly. And as the food is confounded, so the resulting diseases are complex, unaccountable, manifold, variegated — and medicine has had to arm itself against them with many weapons and many rules of treatment.

Now I tell you the same holds for philosophy. It was once simpler, because men's sins were on a smaller scale and could be cured with modest effort; but in the face of this moral upheaval, every remedy must be tried. And if only the plague could be beaten even so! We are mad — not just individually, but nationally. We check manslaughter and the murder of individuals; but what about war, and the celebrated glory of slaughtering whole peoples? Our greed knows no limits; neither does our cruelty. And as long as such crimes are committed quietly, by single men, they are less harmful and less monstrous; but now cruelty is practiced by decree of senate and popular assembly, and the state orders publicly what it forbids privately. Deeds that would cost a man his life if done in secret, we applaud because generals in uniform carried them out. Man, the gentlest species of all by nature, is not ashamed to revel in one another's blood, to wage war, and to hand war down to his sons — when even the dumb beasts, even the wild ones, keep peace with their own kind. Against this overpowering, epidemic madness, philosophy has become a heavier labor; she has had to gain strength in proportion to the strength of what she fights.

It used to be easy to scold the men who were slaves to the bottle and hunted after fancier food; it took no great effort to lead the spirit back to a simplicity it had strayed from only a little way. But now

  One needs the rapid hand, the master's craft.

Men hunt pleasure from every source. No vice stays within its own limits; luxury topples over into greed. Honor has fallen into oblivion. Nothing is shameful anymore, if it fetches a good price. Man — a sacred thing to man — is slaughtered for sport; men whom it was once a sacrilege even to train for the giving and receiving of wounds are now driven into the arena naked and unarmed, and a human being turned into a corpse is judged a satisfying show.

Amid this wreckage of morals, something stronger than the usual is needed — something to shake off ills grown chronic. To root out beliefs in error that have sunk this deep, conduct must be governed by doctrines. Only when we add precepts, consolation, and encouragement to those doctrines can they prevail; by themselves, precepts are ineffective. If we want to hold men fast and tear them free of the ills that grip them, they must learn what is evil and what is good. They must know that everything except virtue changes its name, turning now good and now bad. Just as the soldier's first bond is his oath of allegiance — his love for the standard and his horror of desertion — and just as, once the oath has been sworn, every other duty can easily be demanded of him and every trust placed in him: so it is with those you would lead to the happy life. The foundations must be laid first, and virtue worked deep into the man. Let men be held by something like a religious devotion to virtue; let them love her; let them long to live with her, and refuse to live without her.

"But wait," people say. "Haven't certain men reached excellence without any elaborate training? Haven't they made great progress obeying bare precepts alone?" Quite true — but they had lucky temperaments; they snatched salvation in passing, as it were. For just as the immortal gods never learned virtue — being born with virtue complete, goodness built into their very nature — so certain men are fitted with extraordinary gifts and arrive without a long apprenticeship at what is ordinarily a matter of teaching, embracing what is honorable the moment they hear it. Hence the choice minds that seize on virtue at once, or produce it from within themselves. But your dull, sluggish fellow, hobbled by bad habits, must have this rust scoured off his soul again and again. Now, since those inclined toward the good can be lifted to the heights more quickly, the weaker spirits, too, will be helped and freed from their false opinions if we entrust to them the settled principles of philosophy — and you may see how essential those principles are in the following way. Certain things sink into us and make us sluggish in some directions and headlong in others. These two faults — recklessness and sloth — cannot be checked or roused until we remove their causes, which are mistaken admiration and mistaken fear. So long as those feelings possess us, you may say to us, "You owe this duty to your father, this to your children, this to your friends, this to your guests" — and greed will hold us back, try as we may. A man may know he should fight for his country; fear talks him out of it. A man may know he should sweat out his last drop of strength for his friends; soft living forbids it. A man may know that keeping a mistress is the deepest insult to his wife; lust drives him the other way. So it is useless to hand out precepts unless you first clear away what will stand in the precepts' path; it does no more good than laying weapons beside a man and marching him up to the enemy with his hands tied. To deal with the precepts we offer, the soul must first be set free. Suppose a man is doing just what he ought: he cannot keep it up continuously or consistently, because he will not know why he is doing it. Some of his conduct will come out right through luck or through practice, but he will hold in his hand no rule to measure his actions by — nothing he can trust to tell him whether what he has done is right. A man who is good by accident gives no guarantee of staying good forever. Furthermore, precepts may perhaps help you to do what ought to be done; they will not help you do it in the right way — and if they don't get you that far, they don't lead you to virtue. I grant that a man who is warned will do what he should. But that isn't enough, because the credit lies not in the deed itself but in the way it is done. What is more shameful than a lavish dinner that eats through a knight's whole income? What deserves the censor's condemnation more than a man forever indulging himself and — as the gluttons say — his "inner man"? And yet many a careful man has spent a cool million on his inaugural dinner! The very sum called disgraceful when spent on appetite is beyond reproach when spent for official purposes. For then it isn't luxury — it's an expense sanctioned by custom.

A mullet of monstrous size was once presented to the Emperor Tiberius. They say it weighed four and a half pounds — and why shouldn't I tickle certain epicures' palates by naming its weight? Tiberius ordered it sent to the fish-market and put up for sale, remarking, "My friends, I shall be very much surprised if either Apicius or Publius Octavius doesn't buy that mullet." The guess came truer than he expected: the two men bid against each other, and Octavius won — earning a tremendous reputation among his set because he had paid five thousand sesterces for a fish the Emperor had sold and even Apicius had failed to buy. Paying such a price was disgraceful for Octavius, but not for the man who first bought the fish to present it to Tiberius — though I would blame him as well; still, at least he admired it as a gift he thought worthy of Caesar.

When people sit at the bedsides of sick friends, we honor them for it. But when people do it to angle for a legacy, they are vultures waiting for the carcass. The same act can be shameful or honorable: the purpose and the manner make all the difference. Now, every act of ours will be honorable if we pledge our allegiance to honor and judge honor, and what flows from it, to be the only good that can fall to a man — everything else being good only for a day. So I hold that there must be planted deep in us a settled conviction that applies to life as a whole: this is what I call a doctrine. As that conviction is, so will our acts and thoughts be; and as our acts and thoughts are, so will our lives be. When you are ordering an entire existence, advice about the details is not enough. Marcus Brutus, in the book he entitled On Duty, gives many precepts to parents, to children, to brothers; but no one will do his duty as he ought unless he has some principle to refer his conduct to. We must set before our eyes the goal of the Supreme Good — something to strive toward, something every act and every word can look to for its bearings — just as sailors must steer by a fixed star. Life without an ideal is aimless drifting; and the moment an ideal has to be set up, doctrines become necessary. You will admit, I am sure, that nothing is more shameful than hesitant, wavering conduct — than the habit of timidly backing out. And that will be our experience in everything, until we remove whatever checks the spirit, whatever clogs it and keeps it from committing itself wholly to the attempt.

Precepts are commonly given on how the gods should be worshipped. But let us forbid the lighting of lamps on the Sabbath: the gods have no need of light, and even men take no pleasure in soot. Let us forbid the morning levee and the crowding of temple doorways: those ceremonies flatter human ambition — God is worshipped by those who know him. Let us forbid the carrying of towels and body-scrapers to Jupiter, and the holding of mirrors for Juno: God seeks no servants. Of course not — he himself is mankind's servant; everywhere, and for everyone, he stands ready to help. A man may hear all about the proper limits of sacrifice, and how far to keep clear of burdensome superstition; but he will never make real progress until he has formed a right conception of God — as the one who possesses all things and allots all things, and gives them freely, without price. And what reason do the gods have for their kindnesses? Their nature. Anyone who thinks they are merely unwilling to do harm is mistaken: they cannot do harm. They can neither suffer injury nor inflict it — for to harm and to be harmed belong to the same order of things. Universal nature, glorious and beautiful in all her parts, has made those she lifted beyond the danger of evil incapable of dealing it out.

The first way to worship the gods is to believe in the gods. The next is to acknowledge their majesty — and their goodness, without which there is no majesty; to know that they are the powers presiding over the universe, ruling all things by their strength and standing guard over the human race, even though they sometimes take no notice of the individual man. They neither have evil nor give it; but they do chasten certain men, and restrain them, and impose penalties — and sometimes their punishment comes disguised as a blessing. Would you win the gods' favor? Be a good man. Whoever imitates them has worshipped them enough. Then comes the second question: how to deal with men. What is our aim? What precepts do we give? To abstain from bloodshed? What a small thing it is not to harm the very person you ought to help! High praise indeed, that a human being should be gentle to a human being! Shall we advise stretching out a hand to the shipwrecked sailor, pointing the road to the lost traveler, sharing bread with the starving? Yes — when I can first list everything that ought to be given or withheld. In the meantime, I can lay down for mankind one short rule covering all our duties to one another: everything you see, everything that contains both the divine and the human, is one. We are all limbs of one great body. Nature made us kin, creating us from the same materials and for the same ends. She planted in us love for one another and made us for fellowship. She established fairness and justice; and by her decree, it is more wretched to do harm than to suffer it. By her command, let our hands be ready wherever help is needed. Keep this verse in your heart and on your lips:

  I am a man; nothing that touches man
  Do I count foreign to me.

Let us hold things in common — for our birth is in common. Human society is like a stone arch: it would fall if the stones did not brace one another — and that mutual pressure is exactly what holds it up.

Next, after gods and men, let us consider how to make use of things. It is pointless to have rattled off precepts unless we begin by settling what opinion we ought to hold of each thing — of poverty, riches, fame, disgrace, one's country, exile. Let us shut rumor out and set a value on each thing for ourselves, asking what it is rather than what it is called.

Now to the virtues. Some will advise us to rate prudence very high, to cherish courage, and to cling to justice, if possible, more tightly than to any other quality. But this will do us no good if we do not know what virtue is — whether it is simple or compound; whether it is one or many; whether its parts stand separate or are woven together; whether the man who has one virtue has them all; and how, exactly, they differ from one another. A carpenter has no need to investigate his craft in terms of its origin or its purpose, any more than a pantomime dancer must investigate the art of dance; those arts understand themselves, and that is enough — they do not refer to life as a whole. But virtue is knowledge of other things as well as of herself: to learn virtue, we must learn everything about virtue. Conduct will not be right unless the will behind it is right, for the will is the source of conduct. And the will cannot be right without a right attitude of mind, for the mind is the source of the will. And that attitude of mind will not be found even in the best of men unless he has learned the laws of life as a whole, worked out a sound judgment on everything, and measured facts against the standard of truth. Peace of mind belongs only to those who have reached a fixed and unchanging standard of judgment; everyone else ebbs and flows in their decisions, floating between rejecting things and chasing them. And what causes all this tossing back and forth? Nothing is clear to them, because they consult the least reliable of all authorities — rumor. If you would always desire the same things, you must desire the truth. And no one reaches the truth without doctrines, for doctrines take in the whole of life. Good and evil, honorable and disgraceful, just and unjust, dutiful and undutiful; the virtues and their exercise; the possession of comforts; reputation and standing; health, strength, beauty, sharpness of the senses — all of these call for someone able to appraise them. You should be permitted to know what each item on the list is really worth; for you are often deceived into thinking things are worth more than they are — so badly deceived that what men rate highest of all — riches, influence, power — should really be marked down to a penny.

You will never understand this unless you have examined the actual standard by which such things are measured against each other. Just as leaves cannot flourish on their own — they need a branch to hold to and draw sap from — so your precepts, taken alone, wither away; they must be grafted onto a school of philosophy. Besides, those who abolish doctrines don't see that the very arguments they use to disprove doctrines establish them. For what are they saying? That precepts are sufficient to equip a life, and that the doctrines of wisdom — the dogmas — are superfluous. But that very claim is a doctrine — just as it would be if I now declared that precepts must be dropped as superfluous, that doctrines alone should be used, and that our studies should aim at doctrines only: by the very act of declaring that precepts should not be taken seriously, I would be uttering a precept. Some matters in philosophy call for admonition; others call for proof — and a great deal of proof, because they are complicated and can barely be untangled with the utmost care and the utmost skill in argument. If proofs are necessary, so are doctrines, for doctrines deduce the truth by reasoning. Some things are plain, some are obscure: plain, whatever the senses and the memory can take hold of; obscure, whatever lies beyond their reach.

But reason is not satisfied with the obvious. Its higher and nobler business is with hidden things. Hidden things require proof; proof cannot come without doctrines; therefore doctrines are necessary. What produces settled conviction — and perfected conviction too — is assured belief in certain facts; and if, for lack of that assurance, everything drifts loose in our minds, then doctrines are indispensable, for they give the mind the means of unswerving judgment. When we advise a man to value his friends as highly as himself, to remember that an enemy can become a friend, to cultivate love in the one and soften hatred in the other, we add: "This is just and honorable." But the just and honorable element in our doctrines is grasped by reason; so reason is necessary, for without it the doctrines cannot exist either. But let us join the two together. Branches are useless without their roots — and the roots themselves are strengthened by the growth they have produced. Everyone can see how useful the hands are; their help is out in the open. But the heart, which gives the hands their growth and power and motion, is hidden. I can say the same of precepts: they are out in the open, while the doctrines of wisdom lie concealed. And just as only the initiated know the innermost portion of the rites, so in philosophy the hidden truths are revealed only to those who have been received and admitted into her mysteries — while precepts and the like are familiar even to outsiders.

Posidonius holds that not only precept-giving (nothing stops me from using the word) but even persuasion, consolation, and encouragement are necessary. To these he adds the investigation of causes — and I don't see why I shouldn't dare to call it "aetiology," since the scholars who mount guard over the Latin language use the term as if they had every right to. He remarks that it will also be useful to give a portrait of each particular virtue; this science Posidonius calls "ethology," while others call it "characterization." It sets out the signs and marks belonging to each virtue and each vice, so that like can be told from like. Its function is the same as precept-giving. The man who gives precepts says, "If you would have self-control, do this and this." The man who draws the portrait says, "The man who does this and this, and refrains from that, is the man who has self-control." What's the difference, you ask? One gives the precepts of virtue, the other gives virtue in the flesh. These portraits — these samples, to borrow the merchant's word — do have their use, I admit; just put them on display with a good recommendation, and you will find men to copy them. Wouldn't you consider it useful to be given the marks by which to recognize a thoroughbred horse, so that you aren't cheated in your purchase or left wasting your time on a nag? Then how much more useful to know the marks of a surpassingly fine soul — marks one man may take over from another for himself!

  Straightway the foal of the noble herd, brought up in the pastures,
  Marches with spirited step, and sets down his feet with a light touch;
  First on the dangerous path he goes, and into the threatening river,
  Trusting himself to the unknown bridge, unafraid at its creakings;
  Neck carried high in the air, and a clean-cut head, and a belly
  Spare, and a rounded back, and a breast abounding in courage
  And muscle. And when the far-off clash of weapons resounds, he
  Cannot stand still: he pricks up his ears, and his limbs are all trembling,
  And out he pours the pent-up fire held close in his nostrils.

Vergil's description, though it refers to something else, might perfectly well be the portrait of a brave man; at any rate, I would choose no other image for a hero. If I had to describe Cato — unterrified amid the din of civil war, first to attack the armies already marching on the Alps, throwing himself face-forward into the civil conflict — this is exactly the bearing and expression I would give him. Surely no one ever "marched with more spirited step" than the man who rose against Caesar and Pompey at the same time and, while some backed Caesar's party and others Pompey's, threw down his challenge to both — showing that the Republic, too, still had someone on its side. For it is not enough to say of Cato "unafraid at its creakings." Of course he is not afraid! He does not flinch at real and present thunder; in the face of ten legions, Gallic auxiliaries, and a motley host of citizens and foreigners, he speaks the language of freedom, urging the Republic not to give up the fight for liberty but to hazard everything — declaring that it is more honorable to fall into servitude than to fall in step with it. What force, what energy is his! What confidence, in the midst of the general panic! He knows he is the one man whose standing is not in question — that men do not ask whether Cato is free, but whether he is still among the free. Hence his contempt for danger and for the sword. What a pleasure it is to say, in admiration of a hero's unflinching steadiness while the whole state fell in ruins around him:

  A breast abounding in courage and muscle!

It will help not only to describe the usual character of good men and sketch their figure and features, but to relate and retell the actual men there have been of this kind. We might picture Cato's last and bravest wound, through which Freedom breathed her last; or the wise Laelius, and the harmony of his life with his friend Scipio; or the noble deeds of the Elder Cato at home and abroad; or the wooden couches of Tubero, spread at a public feast — goatskins in place of tapestry, and earthenware vessels set out for the banquet before the very shrine of Jupiter. What was that but consecrating poverty on the Capitol? Even if I knew no other deed of his to rank him with the Catos, is that one not enough? It was a censorship, not a banquet. How pitifully the men who covet glory misunderstand what glory is, and how it should be sought! On that day the Roman people looked over the furniture of many hosts, and marveled at one man's only. The gold and silver of all the rest has been broken up and melted down a hundred times over; but Tubero's earthenware will outlast eternity.

Farewell.
