Letters 59–63

Letter 59: On Pleasure and Joy

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Your letter gave me great pleasure — and kindly allow me to use that word in its everyday sense, without insisting on its strict Stoic meaning. For we Stoics hold that pleasure is a vice. Very likely it is; but it's the word we all reach for when we want to name a happy state of mind. I'm well aware that if we test words against our formula, even pleasure has a bad reputation, and joy is something only the wise man attains. For "joy" is an elevation of the spirit — of a spirit that trusts in the goodness and truth of what it possesses. In common speech, though, we say we take great "joy" in a friend's election to the consulship, or his wedding, or the birth of his child — events which, far from being joys, are more often the openings of sorrows to come. No: the mark of real joy is that it never stops, and never turns into its opposite.

So when our Virgil speaks of

  The evil joys of the mind,

the phrase is eloquent, but not strictly accurate. No joy can be evil. He gave the name "joy" to pleasures, and in doing so he said what he meant: that people take delight in their own harm.

Still, I wasn't wrong to say I received great "pleasure" from your letter. Even when an ignorant man's delight has an honorable cause, his emotion is unruly, ready at any moment to swing off in another direction. So I call it "pleasure": it starts from an opinion about a false good; it escapes control; it runs to excess.

But back to the point — let me tell you what delighted me in your letter. You have your words under control. You aren't swept away by your own language, or carried past the limits you set for yourself. Many writers get lured by the charm of some attractive phrase into topics they never meant to discuss. Not you: everything is compact and fitted to the subject. You say all you want to say, and you mean even more than you say. That is proof of the weight of your material — it shows that your mind, like your words, contains nothing superfluous, nothing inflated.

I do find metaphors — not reckless ones, but the kind that have earned their keep through use. I find similes too; and if anyone forbids similes, insisting they belong to poets alone, then he clearly hasn't read our older prose writers, who had not yet learned to strike poses for applause. Those writers, whose eloquence was plain and aimed only at proving the case, are full of comparisons; and I think comparisons are necessary — not for the reasons poets need them, but as props for our weakness, to bring speaker and listener alike face to face with the subject.

As it happens, I'm reading Sextius at this very moment — a sharp man, a philosopher who writes in Greek but holds to the Roman standard of ethics. One of his similes struck me especially: an army marching in hollow square, through country where the enemy might appear from any quarter, ready for battle. "That," he says, "is exactly what the wise man should do: keep his fighting qualities deployed on every side, so that wherever attack threatens, his defenses are ready at hand and answer the commander's signal without confusion." We see this in armies serving under great generals: every unit understands the general's order at once, because the troops are arranged so that a signal from one man reaches cavalry and infantry at the same moment. And this, he says, is even more necessary for people like us. Soldiers have often feared an enemy for no reason at all, and the stretch of road they thought most dangerous has turned out safest. But folly gets no such rest. Fear haunts it at the front of the column and at the rear; both flanks are in a panic. Peril chases folly and meets it head-on. It flinches at everything; it is never prepared; it takes fright even at its own reinforcements. But the wise man is fortified against every assault. He is alert. Let poverty attack, or sorrow, or disgrace, or pain — he will not retreat. He will walk undaunted straight at them, and straight through the middle of them.

We human beings are chained and weakened by many vices. We've wallowed in them so long that getting clean is hard: we aren't merely stained by them — we are dyed. But rather than hop from one figure to the next, let me raise a question I often turn over in my own heart: why does folly grip us so stubbornly? First, because we don't fight it hard enough, don't struggle toward salvation with everything we have. Second, because we don't put enough trust in what the wise have discovered — we don't drink in their words with open hearts; we come at this enormous problem in far too casual a spirit. And how could anyone learn enough in the fight against his vices, if the time he gives to learning is only the time left over from his vices? None of us goes deep. We skim the surface, and we consider a few stray minutes spent in the search for wisdom plenty — more than plenty — for a busy man.

What hinders us most of all is that we are too easily satisfied with ourselves. If we meet someone who calls us good men, or sensible men, or holy men, we recognize ourselves in the portrait. Not content with moderate praise, we accept whatever shameless flattery heaps on us, as if it were owed to us. We agree with the people who declare us the best and wisest of men, even though we know they lie constantly. We're so pleased with ourselves that we want to be praised for a virtue at the very moment we're indulging its opposite. A man hears himself called "most gentle" while he's inflicting torture, "most generous" while he's looting, "most sober" in the middle of drunkenness and lust. It follows that we don't want to be reformed — precisely because we believe we're already the best of men.

Alexander was ranging as far as India, laying waste to tribes barely known even to their own neighbors. During the siege of one city, while he was riding around the walls hunting for the weak spot in the fortifications, an arrow struck him. He kept up the siege for a long time anyway, determined to finish what he had begun. But as the wound dried and the bleeding stopped, the pain grew; his leg went slowly numb against his horse's flank; and when at last he was forced to withdraw, he said: "Everyone swears I am the son of Jupiter — but this wound cries out that I am a man." Let's do the same. Flattery makes a fool of each of us according to his station. Let's answer the flatterer: "You call me a man of sense. But I know how many of the things I crave are useless, and how many of the things I want will do me harm. I don't even have the knowledge a full stomach teaches the animals — the right measure of my own food and drink. I still don't know how much I can hold."

Now I'll show you how to know that you are not wise. The wise man is joyful, cheerful and calm, unshaken; he lives on the same plane as the gods. Now go and examine yourself. If you are never downcast; if no anxiety harasses your mind with worry about what's coming; if day and night your soul keeps its even, unswerving course, upright and content with itself — then you have reached the greatest good a mortal can possess. But if you go seeking pleasures of every kind, in every direction, then know this: you are as far short of wisdom as you are short of joy. Joy is the destination you're aiming for, but you have lost the road if you expect to arrive in the middle of riches and official titles — in other words, if you go looking for joy surrounded by worries. Those things you chase so eagerly, as if they held happiness and pleasure, are causes of grief.

All people of this kind, I insist, are chasing joy; they simply don't know where a joy that is both great and lasting can be found. One hunts for it in dinner parties and self-indulgence; another in campaigning for office and a crowd of clients around him; another in a mistress; another in the idle display of culture, in literature with no power to heal. All of them are led astray by delights that are deceptive and short-lived — like drunkenness, which pays for a single hour of hilarious madness with days of sickness; like applause and the roar of an enthusiastic crowd, which are won, and paid for, at the cost of enormous disquiet.

So reflect on this: the effect of wisdom is a joy that is unbroken and continuous. The wise man's mind is like the sky above the moon: eternal calm fills that region. Here, then, is your reason for wanting to be wise — the wise man is never without joy. And that joy springs from one source only: the knowledge that you possess the virtues. Only the brave, the just, the self-controlled can rejoice. "What," you ask, "don't the foolish and the wicked rejoice too?" No more than lions rejoice over a kill. When men have worn themselves out with wine and lust, when the night gives out before the debauch does, when the pleasures they've heaped on a body too small to hold them begin to fester — then, in their misery, they groan out Virgil's lines:

  You know how we passed that last of nights
  among false-glittering joys.

Pleasure-lovers spend every night among false-glittering joys, and every night as if it were their last. But the joy that comes to the gods, and to those who imitate the gods, is never interrupted, and never ends. It would end, if it were borrowed from outside. Because it is not in anyone else's power to give, it is not subject to anyone else's whim. What Fortune has not given, Fortune cannot take away.

Farewell.

Letter 60: On Harmful Prayers

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I file a complaint, I bring a lawsuit, I am angry. Do you still want what your nurse, your guardian, your mother prayed you would get? Don't you see yet what evil they were praying for? How hostile to us are the wishes of our own families! And the more completely those wishes come true, the more hostile they are. It no longer surprises me, at my age, that trouble has dogged us from earliest youth: we grew up amid our parents' curses. May the gods hear our own prayer too — one that asks for no favors!

How long shall we go on asking the gods for things, as if we still couldn't support ourselves? How long will we keep filling the marketplaces of our great cities with grain? How long must whole populations gather the harvest for us? How long will fleets of ships, drawing on more than one sea, deliver the makings of a single meal? A bull is filled by grazing a few acres; one forest is enough for a herd of elephants. Man alone forages both the earth and the sea. What then — did nature give us such insatiable bellies, along with such puny bodies, that we should outdo the biggest and hungriest animals in sheer greed? Not at all. How little it takes to satisfy nature! A very little sends her away content. It isn't the natural hunger of our bellies that costs us dear; it's our restless craving. So the people who, as Sallust puts it, "listen to their bellies" should be counted among the animals, not among men — and some of them not even among the animals, but among the dead. He truly lives who is useful to many; he truly lives who makes use of himself. But those who creep into a hole and go torpid are no better off in their houses than they would be in their tombs. You could carve such a man's name right there on the marble over his door — for he has died before he is dead.

Farewell.

Letter 61: On Meeting Death Cheerfully

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Let us stop wanting what we have always wanted. I, at least, am doing it: in my old age I have stopped wanting what I wanted as a boy. My days and my nights go to this one task; this is my work, this is what I think about — putting an end to my old faults. I am trying to live every day as if it were a complete life. I don't snatch at it as if it were my last; I simply regard it as though it could be. This very letter is written in that spirit — as if death might call me away in the act of writing it. I am ready to depart; and I will enjoy life precisely because I am not over-anxious about the date of my departure.

Before I grew old, I tried to live well. Now that I am old, I shall try to die well — and dying well means dying gladly. See to it that you never do anything unwillingly. What is bound to be necessity if you resist it is not necessity if you want it. I mean this: the man who takes his orders gladly escapes the bitterest part of slavery — doing what he does not want to do. The unhappy man is not the one who acts under orders; it's the one who acts against his will. So let us set our minds to want whatever our circumstances demand — and above all, to think about our own end without sadness.

We must prepare for death before we prepare for life. Life is well enough furnished, but we are greedy about the furnishings: something always seems to be missing, and always will. Having lived long enough depends neither on our years nor on our days, but on our minds. I have lived long enough, my dear Lucilius. I have had my fill. I await death.

Farewell.

Letter 62: On Good Company

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

People deceive us when they claim that a crowd of obligations keeps them from their liberal studies. They fake their engagements, and multiply them — when their only real engagement is with themselves. As for me, Lucilius, my time is free. It really is free: wherever I am, I am my own master. I don't hand myself over to my affairs; I lend myself to them, and I don't go hunting for excuses to waste time. Wherever I find myself, I carry on my own reflections and turn over some healthy thought in my mind. When I give myself to my friends, I don't withdraw from my own company; and I don't linger with the people some occasion, or some case arising from my official position, has attached to me. Instead, I spend my time with the best men of all: whatever land they lived in, whatever age, I let my thoughts fly to them. Demetrius, the best of men, I take everywhere with me; I leave the wearers of purple and fine linen and talk with him, half-naked as he is, and I hold him in the highest esteem. And why shouldn't I? I have found that he lacks nothing. Anyone at all can despise all things; no one can possess all things. The shortest way to riches is to despise riches. But our friend Demetrius lives not merely as if he had learned to despise everything, but as if he had handed everything over for other people to own.

Farewell.

Letter 63: On Grief for Lost Friends

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I am sorry to hear that your friend Flaccus has died — but I would not have you grieve more than is fitting. That you should not grieve at all, I hardly dare demand; and yet I know it would be the better way. But where is the man blessed with the steadiness of soul to manage it, unless he has already risen far above Fortune's reach? Even such a man would feel the sting of a loss like this — but only a sting. As for the rest of us, our tears can be forgiven, provided they don't run to excess, and provided we have checked them ourselves. When we lose a friend, our eyes should be neither dry nor streaming. Let your tears flow, but let them also cease. We may weep; we must not wail.

Does the rule I'm laying down for you seem harsh? Then consider that the greatest of Greek poets granted the right of weeping for one day only — he tells us that even Niobe remembered to eat. Do you want to know where all the lamentation and extravagant weeping comes from? Through our tears we are hunting for proof that we feel the loss. We aren't surrendering to sorrow — we're parading it. Nobody goes into mourning for an audience of himself alone. Shame on our miserable folly: there is a streak of vanity even in our grief!

"What," you say, "am I to forget my friend?" It's a short-lived memory you're promising him, if it lasts only as long as your grief. Soon enough some passing thing, however trivial, will smooth that brow of yours into a laugh. And I don't put off to any longer date than that the softening of every regret, the quieting of even the bitterest grief. The moment you stop watching yourself, this picture of sorrow you've been contemplating will fade. Right now you are standing guard over your own suffering — but even while you stand guard, it slips away, and the sharper it is, the sooner it ends.

Let's work at making the memory of those we've lost a pleasant one. Nobody willingly returns to a subject he can't think about without pain. It's inevitable that the names of those we loved and lost come back to us with a certain sting — but there is a pleasure even in that sting. As my friend Attalus used to say: "The memory of dead friends is pleasant the way certain fruits are pleasantly tart, or the way the very bitterness of an extremely old wine is what delights us. Give it enough time, and everything that hurt is extinguished, and the pleasure reaches us pure." If we take his word for it, "thinking of friends alive and well is like enjoying cakes and honey; remembering friends who have passed away gives a pleasure that carries a touch of bitterness. Yet who will deny that even bitter, sharp-edged things can whet the appetite?" There, for my part, I disagree with him. To me, the thought of my dead friends is sweet — even comforting. For I had them as if I would one day lose them; I have lost them as if I have them still.

So, Lucilius, act in keeping with your usual serenity of mind, and stop putting the wrong interpretation on Fortune's gifts. Fortune has taken away — but Fortune gave. Let us enjoy our friends greedily, because we don't know how long the privilege will be ours. Think how often we leave them behind when we set out on some long journey; think how often we fail to see them even while we're staying in the same place. We'll soon realize that we lost too much of their time while they were alive. And will you put up with people who neglect their friends utterly, and then mourn them abjectly — who love no one until they have lost him? The reason they grieve so extravagantly at such times is that they're afraid people will doubt whether they ever loved at all; too late, they go looking for proofs of their feeling. If we have other friends, we treat them shabbily and rate them cheaply if they count for so little that they can't console us for the loss of one. If we have no other friends, then we have done ourselves a worse injury than Fortune has done us: Fortune robbed us of one friend, but we robbed ourselves of every friend we failed to make. Besides, a man who couldn't love more than one never had much love even for that one. If a thief made off with a man's only tunic, and the man chose to stand there wailing over his loss rather than look around for some way to escape the cold, for something to throw over his shoulders — wouldn't you call him an utter fool?

You have buried someone you loved; now look for someone to love. It is better to replace a friend than to weep for him. What I'm about to add is, I know, a worn old commonplace, but I won't leave it out just because everyone says it: time ends a man's grief even when he never chose to end it himself. And for a sensible man, the most shameful cure for sorrow is simply growing tired of sorrowing. I would rather you abandon grief than have grief abandon you. Stop grieving as soon as you can — because even if you wanted to keep it up, you couldn't keep it up for long. Our ancestors fixed one year as the limit of mourning for women — not because women needed to mourn that long, but so that they would mourn no longer. For men, no term is set, because for a man no term of mourning is honorable. Even so, show me one woman — out of all the pitiful creatures who had to be dragged from the funeral pyre or pried off the corpse — whose tears lasted a whole month. Nothing turns offensive as quickly as grief. While it's fresh, it finds consolers and draws people to its side; once it goes chronic, it gets laughed at — and rightly, because by then it is either put on or foolish.

And the man writing you all this is the very one who wept so uncontrollably for his dear friend Annaeus Serenus that I must be counted — much against my will — among the examples of men defeated by grief. Today I condemn what I did, and I understand the main reason I mourned so wildly: I had never imagined that his death could come before mine. The only thought that ever occurred to me was that he was the younger — much younger, at that. As if the Fates called us up in order of age!

So let us think constantly about our own mortality, just as much as about the mortality of all those we love. Back then I should have said: "My friend Serenus is younger than I am — but what does that matter? He ought to die after me, but he may die before me." Because I never said it, Fortune caught me unprepared with one sudden blow. Now is the time for you to reflect, not only that all things are mortal, but that their mortality follows no fixed schedule. Whatever can happen at any time can happen today. So let us reflect, my beloved Lucilius, that we shall soon arrive where this friend — to our sorrow — has arrived already. And perhaps, if the tale the wise men tell is true and some place stands ready to welcome us, then the friend we think we have lost has only been sent on ahead.

Farewell.
