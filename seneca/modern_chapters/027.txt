Letters 80–81

Letter 80: On Worldly Deceptions

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Today I have some free time — thanks less to my own efforts than to the games, which have drawn off every bore in town to the boxing match. No one will barge in on me, no one will derail my train of thought, which moves ahead all the more boldly for that very confidence. The door hasn't been creaking on its hinges every five minutes; no one will be twitching my curtain aside; my thoughts can march on in safety. And that matters most for a man who travels under his own power and cuts his own path. Do I follow no predecessors, then? I do — but I give myself permission to discover something new, to change things, to throw things out. I am not their slave; I simply give them my vote.

And yet that was a bold thing I just said, promising myself quiet and uninterrupted seclusion. Because listen: a huge roar has just come up from the stadium. It doesn't scatter my thoughts, but it does shift them to a comparison the noise itself suggests. How many men train their bodies, I think to myself, and how few train their minds! What crowds pour into a show that is pure sham, staged for nothing but amusement — and what a desert reigns wherever the good arts are taught! How empty-headed are the athletes whose muscles and shoulders we all admire! Here is the question I keep chewing on: if the body can be drilled to such endurance that it stands up to the punches and kicks of several opponents at once, that a man can last out the whole day under a scorching sun in the burning dust, soaked the entire time in his own blood — if the body can do that, how much more easily could the mind be toughened to take Fortune's blows without going down for good, to struggle back to its feet after it has been flattened, after it has been trampled? The body needs a long list of supplies to be strong; the mind grows from within, feeding and exercising itself. Those athletes need mountains of food, rivers of drink, quantities of oil, and years of training on top of it all. Virtue comes to you without equipment and without expense. Everything it takes to make you a good man is already inside you.

And what do you need in order to become good? To want it. But what better thing could you possibly want than to break out of this slavery — a slavery that weighs on every one of us, and that even slaves of the lowest rank, born into that degradation, fight to strip off by every means they have? To buy their freedom they pay out savings scraped together by cheating their own stomachs. And you won't leap at liberty at any price — you, who claim you were born to it? Stop glancing at your strong-box. Freedom cannot be bought. There is no point entering "freedom" as an item in your ledger; freedom belongs neither to the men who have bought it nor to the men who have sold it. This good you must give to yourself; from yourself you must ask it.

Free yourself first from the fear of death — that is what sets the yoke on our necks — and then from the fear of poverty. If you want to know how little evil there is in poverty, compare the faces of the poor with the faces of the rich. The poor man smiles more often, and more honestly. His troubles don't go down deep; even when some anxiety hits him, it passes like a scrap of cloud. But the good cheer of the people we call happy is an act, while their sadness is heavy and festering — all the heavier because they are not allowed to show it. They must play the part of happiness while sorrows eat out their hearts. I find myself reaching for the same illustration again and again, because nothing captures so well this drama of human life, in which we are handed parts we then play so badly. There stands the actor, strutting the stage with his chest thrown out and his head thrown back, declaiming:

  Behold, I am the man whom Argos hails as lord,
  the heir of Pelops, master of the lands
  that run from the Hellespont and the Ionian sea
  down to the straits of the Isthmus.

And who is this fellow? A slave. His wages: five measures of grain and five denarii. And that other one — proud, imperious, swollen with confidence in his own power — who thunders:

  Silence, Menelaus, or this hand strikes you dead!

He earns a daily pittance and sleeps on rags. You can say the same of all those fine gentlemen you see riding in litters above the heads of the crowd: in every case their happiness is a costume, put on like an actor's mask. Tear it off, and you will despise them.

When you buy a horse, you order the blanket removed. When slaves are put up for sale, you have their clothes pulled off, so that no bodily flaw can hide from you. And when you judge a man, you judge him with the disguise still on? Slave-dealers use a bit of finery to cover any defect that might put buyers off, which is exactly why the finery itself makes a buyer suspicious. If you spotted a leg or an arm wrapped in bandages, you would demand the wrappings off and the limb itself shown to you. Do you see that Scythian or Sarmatian king over there, his head decorated with the badge of his office? If you want to appraise him — to know his full worth — take off the diadem. A great deal of evil hides underneath it. But why am I talking about other people? If you want to set a value on yourself, put away your money, your estates, your titles, and look inside — into your own soul. Right now, you are taking other people's word for what you are.

Farewell.

Letter 81: On Benefits

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You complain that you have run into an ungrateful man. If this is your first time, thank either your luck or your caution. Though in this case, caution can accomplish nothing except to make you stingy. If you want to dodge this particular danger, you will simply stop doing people favors — and so, to keep your benefits from being wasted on someone else, you will waste them yourself.

Better to get no return than to give no benefits. Even after a bad harvest, a man sows again; the losses of a stubborn, barren soil are often made good by one fertile year. To find one grateful person, it is worth testing many ungrateful ones. No one's hand is so sure in giving that he isn't frequently fooled — and it is good for the traveler to lose the road sometimes, so that he learns to hold to it. After a shipwreck, sailors go back to sea. The banker is not scared out of the forum by one swindler. If we had to drop everything that ever gave us trouble, life would soon go slack and dull with idleness. In your case, let this very setback make you more generous: when the outcome of an undertaking is uncertain, you have to try again and again to succeed in the end. But I have discussed all this at length in the books I wrote called On Benefits.

The question that really needs examining — one I don't think has ever been made clear enough — is this: whether a man who once helped us has squared the account and released us from our debt if he harms us later. Add this one too, if you like: what if the later harm outweighs the earlier help? If you want the formal verdict of a strict judge, you will find he offsets one act against the other and rules: "Though the injuries outweigh the benefits, still, whatever of the benefit survives the injury should be credited to the benefit's side." The harm was greater, yes — but the help came first, so time must be weighed as well. Other points are too obvious to need spelling out: How gladly was the help given? How reluctantly was the harm done? Benefits and injuries alike are matters of intent. "I didn't want to do him the favor, but respect for the man won me over" — or his pestering did, or hope of something in return. What we owe in every case depends on the spirit in which the gift was given: we weigh not the size of the gift but the quality of the goodwill behind it. So away with guesswork. The earlier act was a benefit; the later act, which outweighed it, was an injury. The good man arranges the two columns of his ledger so as to cheat himself on purpose — adding to the benefit, subtracting from the injury.

The more lenient judge, though — and I would rather be that judge — will order us to forget the injury and remember the favor. "But surely," you say, "justice means rendering each man his due: thanks for a benefit, and retribution, or at the very least resentment, for an injury!" That holds, I answer, when one man inflicted the injury and a different man conferred the benefit. When it is the same man, the benefit cancels the injury's force. A man who deserves pardon even with no good deeds on his record deserves something better than mere pardon when he does wrong with a benefit standing to his credit. I do not price benefits and injuries at the same rate; I rate a benefit higher. Not every grateful person understands what it means to be in debt for a benefit. Even a thoughtless, crude fellow out of the common herd may know it, especially just after the gift arrives; what he does not know is how deep the debt goes. Only the wise man knows the exact value of everything. The fool I just mentioned, however good his intentions, either pays less than he owes, or pays at the wrong time or in the wrong place; what he ought to return, he fumbles and loses.

There is a wonderfully precise vocabulary for certain things — a long-established terminology that captures whole duties in single expressions. We say, as you know: "So-and-so has made a return for the favor." Making a return means handing over what you owe of your own free will. We do not say he has "paid back" the favor — "pay back" is for a man who has been dunned, for men who pay unwillingly, or under any circumstances at all, or through a third party. We do not say he has "restored" the benefit, or "settled" it; we have never been content with a word that properly belongs to a money debt. Making a return means offering something to the man from whom you received something. The phrase implies a voluntary act: the man who has made a return has served the summons on himself.

The wise man will run through every circumstance in his own mind: how much he received, from whom, when, where, how. And so we declare that no one but the wise man knows how to return a favor — and, for that matter, no one but the wise man knows how to confer a benefit: I mean the man who enjoys the giving more than the receiver enjoys the getting. Someone will file this remark among the startling statements we Stoics are famous for, the ones the Greeks call "paradoxes," and will say: "So you maintain that only the wise man knows how to return a favor? Then no one else knows how to repay a creditor, or pay a seller the full price of his goods?" To keep the resentment off my own head, let me point out that Epicurus says the very same thing; Metrodorus, at any rate, remarks that only the wise man knows how to return a favor. Our objector is also astonished when we say, "Only the wise man knows how to love; only the wise man is a real friend." Yet returning favors is a part of love and of friendship — an ordinary part, in fact, more common than real friendship itself. He is astonished, too, at our saying, "There is no loyalty except in the wise man" — as if he didn't say the same thing himself! Or do you suppose there is loyalty in a man who doesn't know how to return a favor? So let these people stop accusing us of impossible boasting and understand this: the real substance of honor lives in the wise man; what circulates in the crowd is honor's ghost and imitation. None but the wise man knows how to return a favor. Even a fool can return one, up to the limit of his knowledge and his power; what he lacks would be understanding, not willingness. Wanting to is not something that can be taught.

The wise man will compare everything against everything else, for the very same object grows larger or smaller with the time, the place, and the reason. Often all the riches lavished on a palace accomplish less than a thousand denarii given at the right moment. It matters a great deal whether you hand a man a gift or come to his rescue — whether your generosity saves him or sets him up in life. Often the gift is small and its consequences huge. And what a gulf there is between taking something you lacked — something that was offered — and accepting a benefit precisely so you can confer one in return.

But let's not slide back into ground we have already covered. In this weighing of benefits against injuries, the good man will judge with perfect fairness, yes — but he will lean toward the benefit's side; that is the direction he turns more readily. And in matters of this kind, the person involved usually counts for a great deal. "You did me a favor in that business with my slave, but you injured me in my father's case." "You saved my son, but you robbed me of my father." So he will work through every point where comparison is possible, and if the difference is slight, he will pretend not to see it. Even if the difference is large, he will let it pass — provided the concession costs nothing in duty or loyalty, and provided the injury falls on the good man alone. The sum of it is this: the good man will be easygoing in striking the balance; he will let too much be charged against his own account. He will refuse to pay off a benefit by cancelling it against an injury. The side he leans toward, the verdict he wants to reach, is this: to be in debt for the favor, and to make a return for it. Anyone who receives a benefit more gladly than he repays it has it wrong. A man paying off a loan is more lighthearted than a man taking one out; by that same measure, a man unburdening himself of the greatest of all debts — a benefit received — should be more joyful than a man signing for the biggest of all obligations. Ingrates go wrong here too: they pay their creditors principal plus interest, but treat benefits as money they can use interest-free. The debt grows with every postponement, and the longer the payment is put off, the more remains to pay. A man is an ingrate if he repays a favor without interest. So count the interest too, when you compare what you have taken in against what you have paid out. We should try by every means to be as grateful as we can.

For gratitude is a good we do ourselves — unlike justice, which is commonly supposed to concern other people. Gratitude flows back, in large measure, to its source. There is no man who, in helping his neighbor, has not helped himself — and I don't mean because the man you aided will want to aid you, or the man you defended will want to protect you, or because a good example circles back to bless its author the way bad examples recoil on theirs (people get no pity when they suffer wrongs they have taught the world how to commit). I mean that the reward of all the virtues lies in the virtues themselves. They are not practiced for pay: the wage of a good deed is to have done it. I am grateful not so that my neighbor, encouraged by my earlier thanks, will be quicker to help me next time, but simply so that I may do a supremely pleasant and beautiful thing. I feel gratitude not because it profits me, but because it delights me. And to prove it: if I could be grateful only by appearing ungrateful — if I could return a benefit only by an act that looked like an injury — even then I would go calmly ahead toward what honor demands, straight through the disgrace. No one, I think, holds virtue dearer or is more devoted to it than the man who has thrown away his reputation for goodness rather than throw away the approval of his own conscience. So, as I said, your gratitude does more good to you than to your neighbor. He has had a common, everyday experience — getting back what he gave. You have had a great one, the fruit of a soul in its happiest condition: you have felt gratitude. If wickedness makes people miserable and virtue makes them blessed, and if being grateful is a virtue, then what you handed back was merely the customary payment, but what you gained is beyond price — the consciousness of gratitude, which visits only a soul that is godlike and blessed. And the opposite feeling is punished at once, and punished to the limit. No ungrateful man will be unhappy someday. I grant him no grace period. He is unhappy now.

So let us avoid ingratitude — not for other people's sake, but for our own. When we do wrong, only the smallest and lightest part of it splashes onto our neighbor; the worst part — the densest, if I may put it that way — stays home and torments the owner. My master Attalus used to say: "Evil herself drinks the largest portion of her own poison." Snake venom is not like this poison: what the serpent carries for the destruction of others it stores without harm to itself, but this kind ruins its possessor. The ungrateful man racks and torments himself. He hates the gifts he has accepted, because he will have to return them, so he talks their value down — while the injuries done to him he inflates and exaggerates. And what is more wretched than a man who forgets his benefits and clings to his injuries?

Wisdom does the opposite: she lends grace to every benefit, presses it warmly on her own attention, and delights her soul by remembering it again and again. Bad men get one pleasure out of a benefit, and a short-lived pleasure at that: it lasts only while they are receiving it. The wise man draws from it a steady, permanent joy, for his delight is not so much in receiving the gift as in having received it — and that joy never dies; it stays with him always. The wrongs done to him he despises; he forgets them — not by accident, but on purpose. He does not put the worst construction on everything, or go hunting for someone to blame for every mishap; he charges even men's sins to chance. He will not read malice into a word or a look; whatever goes wrong, he softens it by interpreting it generously. He does not remember an injury sooner than a service. As far as he can, he lets his memory settle on the earlier and better deed, and he never changes his attitude toward those who have deserved well of him — except when the bad deeds far outrun the good, and the gap is obvious even to a man trying to shut his eyes to it. And even then he goes only this far: after the injury has tipped the scale, he tries to return to the attitude he held before the benefit. For when the injury merely equals the benefit, some kindly feeling is still left over. Just as a defendant is acquitted when the votes are tied, and just as kindness always bends a doubtful case toward the better reading, so the wise man's mind, when another man's merits merely equal his misdeeds, will stop feeling the obligation — but not stop wanting to feel it. He acts exactly like the man who goes on paying his debts after the court has cancelled them.

But no one can be grateful who has not learned to scorn the things that drive the common herd out of its mind. If you want to make return for a favor, you must be willing to go into exile, to pour out your blood, to accept poverty — even, and this happens often, to let your very innocence be spattered and dragged through shameful slanders. Gratitude does not come cheap. We hold nothing dearer than a benefit while we are chasing it, and nothing cheaper once we have caught it. Do you ask what makes us forget the benefits we have received? Our hunger for the next ones. We think not about what we have obtained but about what we are going to pursue. We are pulled off course by riches, titles, power — everything that is precious in our opinion and worthless at its true price. We do not know how to weigh things. We should take our counsel about them not from their reputation but from their nature. Those glittering things have no grandeur to enchant our minds beyond the fact that we have gotten used to marveling at them. They are not praised because they deserve to be desired; they are desired because they have been praised — and once the error of individuals has created a public error, the public error turns around and creates the errors of individuals.

But just as we take those valuations on faith, let us also take this truth on the faith of the people: nothing is more honorable than a grateful heart. Every city will echo the sentence, every race, even the ones from savage countries. On this point good men and bad will agree. Some praise pleasure, some prefer hard work; some call pain the greatest of evils, some call it no evil at all; some will count riches into the supreme good, others will say their discovery was a disaster for the human race, and that no man is richer than the one for whom Fortune has found nothing left to give. Amid all this squabbling of opinions, every voter, as the saying goes, will shout "aye" to one proposition: that thanks should be returned to those who have deserved well of us. On this question the whole quarrelsome herd agrees — and meanwhile we keep paying back injuries instead of benefits, and the primary reason a man turns ungrateful is that he found it impossible to be grateful enough. Our madness has gone so far that it is now downright dangerous to do a man a great favor: because he thinks it shameful not to repay it, he would prefer his benefactor out of the way entirely. "Keep what you have received. I am not asking for it back; I am not demanding anything. Just let it be safe to have done you a favor." No hatred is deadlier than the hatred born of shame over a benefit betrayed.

Farewell.
