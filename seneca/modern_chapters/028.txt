Letter 82

Letter 82: On the Natural Fear of Death

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I have stopped worrying about you. "And which of the gods," you ask, "did you get to guarantee that?" One that deceives no one, I assure you: a soul in love with what is upright and good. The better part of you is on safe ground. Fortune can do you injury; what matters more is that I have no fear of you doing injury to yourself. Go on the way you have begun, and settle yourself into this way of living — not luxuriously, but calmly. I would rather be in trouble than in luxury; and take "in trouble" here the way people usually mean it: living a hard, rough, toilsome life. We often hear a man's life praised with a little envy in the voice — "So-and-so lives luxuriously" — and what the words really mean is: "He has gone soft." For the soul goes soft by degrees, weakening until it matches the ease and laziness it lies in. Tell me — isn't it better for a real man to end up hard instead? But then these same pampered fellows are terrified of the very thing they have made their lives resemble. There is a great deal of difference between lying idle and lying buried! "But," you say, "isn't it better to lie idle than to be whirled around in these eddies of business?" Both extremes deserve blame — the strain and the sluggishness alike. I hold that the man stretched out on a perfumed couch is no less dead than the man dragged off by the executioner's hook.

Leisure without study is death — a tomb for a living man. What is the advantage of retirement, in the end? As if the real causes of our anxiety didn't follow us across the sea! What hiding place is there that the fear of death cannot enter? What retreat is so fortified, so far withdrawn, that pain doesn't fill it with dread? Wherever you hide yourself, human ills will raise their racket all around you. Plenty of external things circle us, to deceive us or to crush us; and plenty of internal ones boil and fret even in perfect solitude.

So wrap philosophy around yourself — an impregnable wall. Fortune can batter it with every siege engine she has and never find a way in. The soul that has let go of external things stands on unassailable ground; it defends itself in its own fortress; every weapon hurled at it falls short. Fortune does not have the long reach we give her credit for: she can seize no one who is not clinging to her. So let us spring back from her as far as we can — and the only thing that makes that possible is knowledge of ourselves and of nature. The soul should know where it is going and where it came from, what is good for it and what is evil, what it seeks and what it avoids, and what that Reason is which sorts the desirable from the undesirable — the Reason that tames the madness of our desires and calms the violence of our fears.

Some men flatter themselves that they have beaten these evils on their own, no philosophy required; then some accident catches them off guard, and the confession of error is wrung out of them — too late. The boastful words die on their lips the moment the torturer orders them to stretch out their hands, the moment death draws closer. You could say to such a man: "It was easy for you to challenge evils that were nowhere in sight. But here comes pain — the pain you declared you could endure. Here comes death, the thing you hurled so many brave boasts against. The whip cracks, the sword flashes:

  Now, Aeneas, you will need your courage,
  now you will need a sturdy heart!"

That sturdy heart comes from constant study — provided you practice with your soul and not just your tongue, and provided you prepare yourself to meet death. And for that preparation, expect no encouragement, no cheer, from the men who try to argue you into believing, by hair-splitting logic, that death is no evil. Because I do take pleasure, my excellent Lucilius, in poking fun at the absurdities of the Greeks — absurdities I am continually surprised to find I haven't shaken off myself. Our own master Zeno uses a syllogism like this: "No evil is glorious; but death is glorious; therefore death is no evil." There's a cure, Zeno! Consider me freed of fear — after that, I won't hesitate to stretch my neck out on the block. You're quibbling a man into scorning death? Couldn't you find sterner words, instead of making a dying man laugh? And honestly, Lucilius, I couldn't easily tell you which of the two was the bigger fool: the man who thought this syllogism would quench the fear of death, or the man who took the trouble to refute it — as if any of it had anything to do with the matter. For the refuter set up a counter-syllogism of his own, built on our classing death as "indifferent" — one of the things the Greeks call adiaphora. "Nothing indifferent," he says, "can be glorious; death is glorious; therefore death is not indifferent." You can see the trick sliding through that one: mere death is not, in fact, glorious; a brave death is glorious. And when you say "nothing indifferent is glorious," I'll grant it — with this addition: nothing is glorious except in its dealings with indifferent things. By "indifferent" I mean neither good nor evil: sickness, pain, poverty, exile, death. None of these is glorious in itself; but nothing can be glorious without them. For it is never poverty we praise — it is the man poverty cannot humble or bend. It is never exile we praise — it is the man who walks into exile in the same spirit in which he would have sent another man there. We do not praise pain; we praise the man pain never coerced. No one praises death; we praise the man whose soul death carried off before it could shake it. All these things are, in themselves, neither honorable nor glorious; but let virtue visit and touch any one of them, and virtue makes it honorable and glorious. They simply lie in the middle, and the deciding question is only this: does wickedness lay hold of them, or does virtue? The death that in Cato's case is glorious becomes, in the case of Brutus, instantly base and disgraceful. This is the Brutus who, condemned to death, kept angling for a postponement — who stepped aside a moment to relieve himself, and then, summoned to die and ordered to bare his throat, cried out: "I will bare my throat, if only I may live!" What madness, to run away when there is no turning back! "I will bare my throat, if only I may live!" He came very close to adding: "even under Antony!" There was a man who deserved to be sentenced to life!

But, as I was saying: you can see that death in itself is neither an evil nor a good. Cato used it most honorably; Brutus most shamefully. Add virtue to a thing and it takes on a glory it never had before. We call a room sunny even though the same room is pitch-black at night. The day fills it with light; the night steals the light away. So it is with the things we call indifferent — the "middle" things: riches, strength, beauty, titles, kingship, and their opposites — death, exile, sickness, pain, and all the other evils that frighten us to one degree or another. It is the wickedness or the virtue that hands out the names "good" and "evil." An object is not hot or cold by its own essence: throw it into the furnace and it heats; drop it into water and it chills. Death is honorable when it is joined to something honorable — I mean virtue, and a soul that despises the worst that can happen.

And there are vast distinctions among these "middle" things. Death is not as indifferent as, say, the question of whether your hair is trimmed evenly or unevenly. Death belongs to the class of things that are not evils, but still wear the look of evil. For we are born with love of self, with a desire to exist and preserve ourselves, and with a horror of dissolution — death seems to strip us of so many goods, to pull us away from the abundance we have grown used to. And one more thing estranges us from death: the present we already know, but the future we are crossing into is unknown, and we shrink from the unknown. It is natural, too, to fear the world of shades, where death is supposed to lead. So although death is an indifferent, it is not the kind of thing you can shrug off easily. The soul must be hardened by long practice before it can bear the sight of death and the approach of it.

Death deserves far more scorn than it usually gets — because we believe too many of the stories about it. Plenty of great minds have worked hard to blacken its reputation: they have painted the prison of the world below, the land drowned in everlasting night, where

  the huge warden of Hell,
  sprawled in his blood-stained cave on half-gnawed bones,
  keeps the bloodless ghosts in terror
  with a bark that never ends.

Even if you win that argument — even if you prove these are only stories, and the dead have nothing left to fear — a second fear comes creeping in. For the fear of going to the underworld is matched, exactly, by the fear of going nowhere.

With all these notions dinned into our ears by long tradition, how can the brave endurance of death be anything but glorious — anything but one of the greatest achievements of the human mind? The mind will never climb to virtue while it believes death is an evil; it will climb once it holds death to be a matter of indifference. Nature does not build men to march with a great heart toward what they believe is evil: a man will shuffle toward it, dragging his feet. And nothing glorious ever comes of reluctance and cowardice. Virtue does nothing under compulsion. Besides, no deed is honorable unless the whole heart is in it — unless the doer has given himself to it completely, with no part of him in revolt. But when a man goes to face an evil because he fears something worse, or because he hopes for goods that matter enough to make the one evil worth swallowing, then his judgment is pulled in two directions. On one side is the motive urging him to carry out his plan; on the other, the motive holding him back, making him flee from what looks suspicious or dangerous. He is torn both ways — and the moment that happens, the glory of the act is gone. For virtue carries out its plans only when the spirit is in harmony with itself. There is no fear in anything virtue does.

  Do not yield to evils. Meet them head-on, bolder still,
  wherever your fortune allows you a way.

You cannot meet them "bolder still" if you are convinced they are real evils. Dig that idea out of your soul by the roots; otherwise your fear will hang there, undecided, and choke the impulse to act. You will be shoved toward the thing you ought to charge at like a soldier.

Those of our school, it is true, would have you think that Zeno's syllogism is correct, and that the second one — the counter — is deceptive and false. For my part, I refuse to reduce such questions to the rules of logic, to the quibbles of an utterly worn-out system. Away with the whole business, I say — the kind of question that makes a man feel cornered the moment it is put to him, that forces him to concede a premise and then to say one thing in his answer while believing another. When truth is at stake, we must deal more frankly; when fear is to be fought, more bravely. These questions the dialecticians tangle up in subtleties, I would rather untangle and weigh in plain reason — aiming to win conviction, not to trap the judgment.

A general is about to lead an army into action — men prepared to die for their wives and children. How does he fire them for battle? I give you the Fabii, one clan taking a whole state's war onto its shoulders. I point you to the Spartans posted in the very pass of Thermopylae. They have no hope of victory, no hope of coming home. The ground they stand on is to be their tomb. In what language do you urge them to make a barricade of their bodies, to take the ruin of their whole people onto themselves, to retreat from life sooner than from their post? Will you say: "What is evil is not glorious; but death is glorious; therefore death is not an evil"? What a stirring speech! After words like those, who could hesitate to throw himself onto the massed spears of the enemy and die where he stood? But take Leonidas — how bravely he spoke to his men! "Fellow soldiers," he said, "eat your breakfast as men who will dine in the underworld!" And the food did not turn to lumps in their mouths, or stick in their throats, or slip from their fingers: they accepted the invitation cheerfully — to breakfast and to dinner both. And think of the famous Roman general who sent his soldiers to seize a position straight through an enormous enemy army, and addressed them so: "Men, you must go to that place. There is no must about your coming back."

You see how straightforward virtue is, how commanding. But what man on earth will your logical trickery make braver or more upright? It breaks the spirit instead — and there is no worse time to cramp the spirit and pin it to petty, thorny puzzles than when some great work is being planned. It is not the Three Hundred who need to be relieved of the fear of death — it is all mankind. And how will you prove to all of them that death is no evil? How will you overcome the notions of a whole lifetime, dyed into us from infancy? What rescue will you find for human helplessness? What will you say that sends men burning with zeal into the middle of danger? What speech will talk the whole race out of this universal fear — what feat of cleverness will overturn the settled conviction of the human race standing squarely against you? Are you going to build me catchwords, and string together petty little syllogisms? It takes great weapons to bring down great monsters. You remember the monstrous serpent in Africa — more terrifying to the Roman legions than the war itself — which arrows and slings attacked in vain. Even "Pythius," their great siege engine, could not wound it: its sheer size, and a hide as tough as its bulk, made spears and every weapon thrown by a human hand glance off. It was finally crushed by rocks the size of millstones. And you — is that the kind of toy weapon you are hurling at death? Will you stop a lion's charge with an awl? Your arguments are sharp, I grant you. So is a stalk of grain — nothing is sharper. And some arguments are made useless and unavailing by their very subtlety.

Farewell.
