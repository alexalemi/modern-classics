Letters 41–45

Letter 41: On the God Within Us

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You are doing something excellent — something that will do you real good — if, as your letter says, you are pressing on toward sound understanding. It is foolish to pray for what you can get from yourself. We don't need to lift our hands toward heaven, or beg some temple keeper to let us up close to the idol's ear, as if a prayer whispered there carried better. God is near you, he is with you, he is within you. I mean that, Lucilius: a holy spirit lives inside us, keeping watch over our good deeds and our bad ones — our guardian. As we treat this spirit, so it treats us. In fact, no one is good without God's help. Can anyone rise above Fortune unless God helps him rise? He is the one who supplies noble and upright counsel. In every good man,

  A god dwells — which god, we do not know.

If you have ever come into a grove of ancient trees grown far past their usual height, their crossed and woven branches curtaining off the sky, then the sheer height of the forest, the seclusion of the spot, and your wonder at that thick unbroken shade in the middle of open country will convince you a divine presence is there. Or if a cave, eaten deep into crumbling rock, holds up a mountain on its arch — a space no hands built, hollowed out to that vastness by nature alone — your soul will be shaken by a certain intimation that God exists. We worship the sources of mighty rivers; we set up altars where a great stream bursts suddenly out of hiding; we honor hot springs as divine; we consecrate certain pools for their dark water or their bottomless depth. So when you see a man unterrified in the middle of dangers, untouched by cravings, happy in adversity, calm in the storm — a man who looks at other men from a higher level and at the gods as equals — won't a feeling of reverence for him steal over you? Won't you say, "This thing is too great and too lofty to be of the same stuff as the little body it lives in. A divine power has come down into that man"? When a soul rises above other souls — steady, under control, passing through every experience as if it were a small matter, smiling at the things we fear and the things we pray for — a force from heaven is moving it. A thing like that cannot stand upright unless the divine is propping it up. So the greater part of it stays in the place it came down from. Just as the sun's rays touch the earth but remain rooted in the source they are sent from, so the great and holy soul — sent down so that we could know the divine at closer range — keeps company with us but stays fastened to its origin. It hangs from that source; it turns its gaze there and strains to return; and it takes part in our affairs the way a superior does, not an equal.

What, then, is such a soul? One that shines with no borrowed good — only its own. What is more foolish than to praise a man for things that aren't his? What is more insane than to marvel at things that can pass to somebody else in the next instant? A golden bit does not make a better horse. The lion with the gilded mane — handled, tamed, worn down until he puts up with the decoration — goes into the arena a very different creature from the wild lion whose spirit is unbroken. The wild one, bold in his attack, exactly as nature meant him to be, magnificent in his shaggy fierceness — his glory is that no one can look at him without fear — stands above that other one, the listless gilded brute.

No one should glory in anything but his own. We praise a vine if it loads its shoots with grapes, if its weight bends the very poles that hold its fruit down to the ground. Would anyone prefer to that vine one with golden grapes and golden leaves hanging on it? Fertility is the virtue that belongs to a vine. In a man, too, praise what belongs to the man. He has a household of handsome slaves, a beautiful house; he plants wide acres and collects a wide income? None of that is in the man himself; it is all around him. Praise in him what can neither be given nor snatched away — what is peculiarly a man's own. You ask what that is? The soul, and reason brought to perfection in the soul. For man is a reasoning animal. So man's highest good is reached when he has fulfilled the purpose nature designed him for at birth. And what does this reason demand of him? The easiest thing in the world — to live according to his own nature. It is the general madness of mankind that makes this hard: we keep shoving one another into vice. And how can a man be called back to safety when no one is holding him back and the whole crowd is pushing him on?

Farewell.

Letter 42: On Values

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

So that friend of yours has already convinced you he is a good man? And yet no one can become good — or be known to be good — in so short a time. Do you know whom I mean now when I say "a good man"? One of the second grade, like your friend. The first-class kind is born perhaps once every five hundred years, like the phoenix. And no wonder greatness comes only at long intervals. Fortune turns out ordinary talents by the batch, made to please the mob; but what she wants us to admire, she advertises by the very fact that she makes it rare.

This man you mention, though, is still a long way from the state he claims to have reached. If he knew what "a good man" meant, he wouldn't believe himself one yet; he might even despair of ever becoming one. "But he thinks badly of bad men," you say. So do bad men themselves; there is no heavier penalty for vice than that it disgusts itself and everything like it. "But he hates men who grab great power suddenly and use it without restraint." And he will do exactly the same the moment he gets the same power. With many men, the vices go unnoticed only because they are powerless; the instant these men feel sure of their own strength, their vices will be every bit as daring as the ones prosperity has already put on display. All they lack is the means to unfold their wickedness. You can handle even a poisonous snake while it is stiff with cold; the venom isn't missing — it is only numbed. With many men, the cruelty, the ambition, the self-indulgence want nothing but Fortune's favor to dare crimes that would match the worst on record. That the wish is there you can discover in a moment: give them power equal to their wishes.

Do you remember? You once declared a certain man was under your influence, and I called him fickle, a bird of passage, and said you were holding him not by the foot but by a wing. Was I wrong? You had him by a single feather; he left it in your hand and escaped. You know what a spectacle he made of himself in front of you afterward, and how many of his schemes were poised to come down on his own head. He did not see that in endangering others he was tottering toward his own fall. He did not stop to think how heavy the things he was chasing were — even the ones that weren't superfluous.

So here is the truth to note about the things we pursue, the things we sweat and strain for: either there is nothing desirable in them, or the undesirable outweighs the rest. Some are superfluous; others are not worth their price. But we don't see this clearly. We treat things as free gifts when in fact they cost us dearly. Here is the proof of our stupidity: we think "buying" applies only to the things we pay cash for, and we call free the things we pay for with our very selves. Things we would refuse to buy if the price were our house or some pleasant, profitable estate — those we are eager to get at the cost of anxiety, of danger, of lost honor, lost freedom, lost time. So true it is that every man holds nothing cheaper than himself.

In all our plans and dealings, then, let's act the way we act at a huckster's stall: let's see what the thing we crave is going to cost. Very often what costs nothing costs us the most. I can show you plenty of prizes whose pursuit and capture have wrenched our freedom right out of our hands. We would belong to ourselves, if only these things did not belong to us.

I would have you run the same calculation on losses, too, not just gains. "This thing is bound to perish." It was an extra; you will live without it as easily as you lived before it. If you had it a long time, you lose it after you've had your fill; if only a short time, you lose it before you've grown attached. "You will have less money." Yes — and less trouble. "Less influence." Yes — and less envy. Look around at the things that drive us mad, the things we lose with floods of tears, and you will see: it is not the loss that hurts us, it is the notion of loss. No one feels that these things are gone; he only thinks they are. The man who owns himself has lost nothing. But how few men are blessed with ownership of themselves!

Farewell.

Letter 43: On the Relativity of Fame

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You want to know how the news reached me — who told me you were entertaining this idea you hadn't breathed to a single soul? The best-informed character there is: gossip. "What," you say, "am I such a great personage that I can set gossip going?" There is no reason to measure yourself against this part of the world; measure yourself against the place where you live. Whatever rises above its surroundings is great — right at the spot where it rises. Greatness is not absolute; comparison swells it or shrinks it. A ship that looms large on a river looks tiny on the ocean. A rudder that is big for one vessel is small for another.

So in your province you really do matter, however little you think of yourself. People are asking what you do, how you dine, how you sleep — and they are finding out, too. All the more reason to live carefully. But do not count yourself truly happy until you can live in full view — until your walls protect you instead of hiding you. We like to believe we build walls around ourselves for safety; the truth is, we build them so we can sin in more privacy. Here is a fact you can weigh a man's character by: you will hardly find anyone who can live with his door wide open. It was conscience, not pride, that stationed doorkeepers at our doors; we live in such a way that being suddenly seen is the same as being caught in the act. But what good does it do to hide, to dodge every eye and ear? A good conscience welcomes the crowd; a bad one is anxious and troubled even when it is alone. If your deeds are honorable, let everyone know them. If they are shameful, what does it matter that no one else knows, so long as you know? How wretched you are, if you hold that witness cheap!

Farewell.

Letter 44: On Philosophy and Pedigrees

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You are at it again — telling me you are a nobody, that nature treated you shabbily first and Fortune second — when all the while you have it in your power to step out of the crowd and rise to the highest human happiness. If there is one good thing about philosophy, it is this: it never looks into pedigrees. Trace anyone back to his first source and everyone descends from the gods. You are a Roman knight, and your own steady work carried you into that class; but plenty of men find the fourteen rows barred to them; the senate house does not open to everyone; even the army is choosy about whom it admits to its toil and danger. A noble mind, though, is open to all men. By that test we can all achieve distinction. Philosophy neither rejects nor selects anyone; its light shines for all. Socrates was no aristocrat. Cleanthes hauled water and hired himself out to keep a garden. Philosophy did not find Plato a nobleman; it made him one. Why despair of ranking with men like these? They are all your ancestors — if you conduct yourself in a way that is worthy of them. And you will, if you convince yourself right now that no man outranks you in real nobility. We have all had the same number of forefathers; there is no man whose beginnings do not lie beyond memory. Plato says: "Every king springs from a race of slaves, and every slave has had kings among his ancestors." The long flight of time, with all its turns, has shuffled these things together, and Fortune has turned them upside down. Then who is well-born? The man whose nature is well fitted for virtue. That is the only point worth looking at. Otherwise, if you go hunting through antiquity, everyone traces back to a date before which there is nothing. From the first beginnings of the universe down to today, we have come along a line that runs bright, then shabby, then bright again. A hall packed with smoke-blackened busts does not make the nobleman. No one in the past lived his life to lend us glory; what came before us is not ours. It is the soul alone that makes us noble — and the soul may rise above Fortune from any condition at all, no matter what that condition used to be.

Suppose, then, you were not a Roman knight but a freedman. Even so, you could by your own effort come to be the one free man in a crowd of gentlemen. "How?" you ask. By marking off good things from bad without taking the crowd's word for it. Look not at where things come from but at where they lead. If anything can make life happy, it is good on its own merits — because it cannot be twisted into evil. Then where do we all go wrong, since everyone craves the happy life? We mistake the equipment of happiness for happiness itself, and the harder we chase it, the farther we run from it. The sum and substance of the happy life is unshakable peace of mind, and the secret of that peace is unshaken confidence; yet men keep gathering up the causes of worry, and while they haul their baggage down life's treacherous road, they don't just carry burdens — they pick up more along the way. So they fall farther and farther from what they are seeking, and the more effort they spend, the more they get in their own way and are driven back. That is what happens when you hurry through a maze: the faster you go, the worse you are tangled.

Farewell.

Letter 45: On Sophistical Argumentation

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You complain that books are scarce where you are. But it is quality, not quantity, that matters. Reading from a short list does you good; reading from a big assortment only entertains you. A man who wants to reach his destination follows one road; he does not wander down many. What you're describing is not traveling; it is drifting.

"But," you say, "I would rather have your advice than your books." Even so, I am ready to send you every book I own — to ransack the whole storehouse. If I could, I would come myself; and if I weren't hoping you will finish your term of office soon, I would have imposed this old man's journey on myself. No Scylla, no Charybdis, none of their storied straits could have scared me off. I would not just have crossed those waters — I would have swum them, for the chance to greet you and judge with my own eyes how much you have grown in spirit.

As for your asking me to send my own writings — that does not make me think myself learned, any more than a request for my portrait would flatter my looks. I know it comes from your affection rather than your judgment; and if judgment is in it anywhere, affection forced the judgment on you. But whatever my books are worth, read them as the work of a man still searching for the truth — not in possession of it, and searching stubbornly, too. I have sold myself to no man; I carry no master's name. I give great weight to the judgment of great men, but I claim something for my own as well. Those men left us not finished discoveries but open problems still waiting to be solved. They might have discovered the essentials, if they hadn't gone hunting the superfluous too. They lost a great deal of time quibbling over words and building tricky arguments — the sort of thing that exercises the wit to no purpose. We tie knots in words, wrap them in double meanings, and then try to untie them.

Do we have that much spare time? Do we already know how to live — how to die? Our whole soul should be pressing toward the point where the real duty lies: seeing to it that things do not deceive us, not just words. Why draw fine distinctions between look-alike words, when no one is ever fooled by them except in the middle of the debate? It is things that lead us astray; it is between things that you must learn to distinguish. We embrace evil instead of good; we pray for the opposite of what we prayed for yesterday. Our prayers clash with our prayers, our plans with our plans. How closely flattery resembles friendship! It doesn't just imitate friendship — it outdoes it, passes it in the race. It finds ears wide open and indulgent, sinks straight to the bottom of the heart, and pleases precisely where it does harm. Show me how to see through that resemblance! An enemy comes to me full of compliments, wearing a friend's face. Vices creep into us under the names of virtues: recklessness hides under the label of courage, moderation gets called laziness, the coward passes for prudent. And here the danger of going astray is real. So stamp these things with clear labels.

Then again, the man who is asked whether he has horns on his head is not such a fool that he feels his forehead to check — and not so silly or slow, either, that any argument, however subtle, can persuade him he doesn't know the facts. Quibbles like that deceive as harmlessly as the juggler's cups and dice, where the trickery itself is the pleasure. Show me how the trick is done, and I lose all interest. I say the same about these word games — for what else should we call such sophistries? Not knowing them does no harm; mastering them does no good. If you really want to sort out slippery meanings, then teach us this instead: that the happy man is not the one the crowd calls happy — the man whose coffers the money pours into — but the man whose goods are all in his soul. A man who stands upright and exalted, who spurns inconstancy; who sees no one he would trade places with; who values men only at their worth as men; who takes Nature for his teacher, conforms to her laws, and lives as she commands; whom no violence can strip of what he owns; who turns evil into good; who is sure in judgment, unshaken, unafraid — a man whom force may move but never bewilder. When Fortune hurls her deadliest missile at him with everything she has, she may graze him — rarely — but never wound him. Her other missiles, the ones she flattens the rest of mankind with, bounce off him like hail rattling on a roof: it makes noise, harms no one inside, and melts away.

Why do you bore me with the famous "liar" fallacy, the one so many books have been written about? Look — suppose my whole life is a lie. Refute that. If you are sharp enough, bring my life back to the truth. As it stands, my life counts things essential that are mostly superfluous; and even the part that is not superfluous has no power to make a man fortunate and blessed. Just because a thing is necessary, it does not follow that it is good. Otherwise we degrade the word "good" by handing it to bread and barley porridge and the other bare necessities of staying alive. The good is always necessary; the necessary is not always good, since some downright paltry things are necessary too. No one so misses the noble meaning of the word "good" as to sink it to the level of these humdrum utilities.

Well then — wouldn't your effort be better spent proving to everyone that the hunt for the superfluous costs a great deal of time, and that many people go through life merely collecting the tools of living? Examine individuals, survey mankind in general: there is no one whose life does not face toward tomorrow. "What's the harm in that?" you ask. Infinite harm. Such people are not living; they are getting ready to live. They postpone everything. Even if we paid strict attention, life would outrun us; as it is, life finds us dawdling and passes us by as if it belonged to someone else — and though it ends on the final day, it perishes every day.

But I must not run past the bounds of a letter, which ought not to overfill the reader's left hand. So I will put off to another day our case against the hair-splitters — those over-subtle fellows who make argument the master when it should be the servant.

Farewell.
