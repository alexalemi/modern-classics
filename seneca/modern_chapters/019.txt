Letter 70: On the Proper Time to Slip the Cable

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

After a long absence I have seen your beloved Pompeii again, and it brought me face to face with my own youth. Everything I did there as a young man, it seemed to me I could still do — had done, in fact, only a little while ago. We have sailed past life, Lucilius, the way sailors sail past a coastline. At sea, as our poet Virgil puts it,

  Lands and towns drop away astern,

and just so, on this voyage where time runs at full speed, we put below the horizon first our boyhood, then our youth, then those years that lie between young manhood and middle age and border on both, and then the best years of old age itself. Last of all we begin to sight the one landfall appointed for the whole human race. Fools that we are, we take it for a dangerous reef. It is the harbor. Some day we must put in there; we are never entitled to refuse it; and if a man reaches that harbor in his early years, he has no more right to complain than a sailor who has made a fast crossing. Some sailors, as you know, are teased and held back by sluggish winds until they grow weary and sick of the flat calm; others are carried home by a steady gale.

Assume the same thing happens to us. Life has rushed some men to the harbor — the harbor they were bound to reach anyway, even dawdling — while others it has chafed and battered the whole way. And to a life like that, as you know, one should not always cling. Merely living is not the good; living well is. So the wise man will live as long as he ought, not as long as he can. He will note where he is to live, and with whom, and how, and what he is to be doing. He thinks always about the quality of his life, never the quantity. If life presents him with too many things that torment him and wreck his peace of mind, he sets himself free. And this right is his not only in the final emergency, but the moment Fortune starts to look untrustworthy: then he surveys the ground carefully and considers whether this is the place to stop. It makes no difference to him, he holds, whether he makes his end or merely meets it, whether it comes late or early. He does not fear it as some great loss: no one can lose much when only a trickle is left. It is not a question of dying earlier or later, but of dying well or badly. And dying well means escaping the danger of living badly.

That is why I find the words of the famous Rhodian utterly spineless. This man had been thrown into a cage by his tyrant and was fed there like a wild animal, and when someone advised him to end it by starving himself, he answered: "While a man has life, he may hope for anything." Perhaps so; but life is not worth buying at every price. Let the prizes be as great and as guaranteed as you please: I will not crawl to them through a shameful confession of weakness. Shall I dwell on the fact that Fortune holds every power over a man while he lives — rather than on the fact that she holds none over a man who knows how to die?

And yet there are times when a man, though death is certain and he knows torture is scheduled for him, will refuse to lend a hand to his own punishment. To himself he would lend it. It is folly to die for fear of dying. The executioner is coming: wait for him. Why get there first? Why volunteer to manage a piece of cruelty that belongs to someone else? Do you begrudge your executioner his job, or are you just sparing him the trouble? Socrates could have ended his life by fasting — died of hunger rather than hemlock. Instead he spent thirty days in prison waiting for death, not with the thought "anything might happen yet" or "so long a stretch has room for plenty of hopes," but so that he might show himself obedient to the laws, and so that his friends might have the last hours of Socrates to live on. What would have been more foolish than to despise death, yet be afraid of poison?

Scribonia, a woman of the stern old school, was the aunt of Drusus Libo, a young man as stupid as he was well-born, with ambitions higher than anyone could afford in that age — or a man like him in any age. He had been carried out of the senate-house sick, in his litter, with a very thin escort — for all his relatives had undutifully abandoned him once he was no longer a defendant but as good as a corpse — and he began to debate whether to kill himself or wait for death. Scribonia said to him: "What pleasure do you find in doing another man's work?" He did not take her advice; he laid hands on himself. And he was right after all. When your enemy has scheduled your death for two or three days out, staying alive really is doing another man's work.

So there is no blanket answer to the question of whether, when a power beyond our control decrees our death, we should go to meet it or wait for it. Plenty of arguments pull in each direction. If one death comes with torture and the other is simple and easy, why not grab the easy one? Just as I will choose my ship when I am going on a voyage, and my house when I am moving in, so I will choose my death when I am leaving life. What's more, a longer life is not automatically a better one, but a longer death is automatically a worse one. In nothing should we humor the soul more than in dying. Let it leave by whatever way it feels the pull: whether it reaches for the sword, or the rope, or a drug that empties the veins, let it go, and snap the chains of its slavery. A man should make his life acceptable to others as well as himself; his death, to himself alone. The best death is the one we like. It is foolish to sit thinking: "One person will say I wasn't brave enough; another, that I was too rash; a third, that some other death would have shown more spirit." What you should think instead is: the decision before me is one that public opinion holds no share in. Your single aim should be to get clear of Fortune as fast as you can; otherwise, there will never be a shortage of people who think badly of what you did.

You can even find men who profess philosophy and yet insist that no violence should be done to one's own life, and who call it an abomination for a man to be his own destroyer: we should wait, they say, for the end nature has decreed. A man who says this does not see that he is closing the road to freedom. Of everything the eternal law ever arranged, the best is this: it gave us one entrance into life, but many exits. Should I wait on the cruelty of disease, or the cruelty of men, when I can walk out through the middle of my tortures and shake off my troubles? This is the one count on which we cannot complain of life: it holds no one against his will. Humanity is well situated: no one is unhappy except by his own fault. If you like life, live; if not, you may go back where you came from. You have often been cupped to relieve a headache; you have had veins opened to bring your weight down. To pierce the heart, no gaping wound is required: a lancet opens the way to that great freedom, and tranquility costs a pin-prick.

What is it, then, that makes us sluggish and slow? None of us reflects that some day he must leave this house of life. It is just the way old tenants are: fondness for the place and force of habit keep them on, even when the place mistreats them. Do you want to be free with respect to your body? Then live in it as a tenant about to move out. Keep it before your mind that some day this lease will end; then you will be braver when moving day becomes necessary. But how will a man ever think about his own end if he craves everything without end? And yet there is nothing we need to rehearse more. Our other training may never be called on. Our souls stand ready for poverty — but our wealth has held out. We have armed ourselves to despise pain — but the good luck of a sound and healthy body has never asked us to prove it. We have taught ourselves to bear bravely the loss of those we love — but Fortune has kept alive everyone we loved. This is the one drill where the day of the test is guaranteed to come.

Do not imagine that only great men have had the strength to burst the bars of human servitude; do not believe this is something only a Cato can do — Cato, who with his bare hand dragged out the soul his sword had failed to set free. Men of the lowliest station have flung themselves to safety by one mighty effort, and when they were not allowed to die at their own convenience, or to pick and choose the instrument of death, they have snatched up whatever lay at hand and by sheer force turned objects harmless by nature into weapons of their own. Recently, in a training-school for beast-fighters, there was a German who was getting ready for the morning show. He withdrew to relieve himself — the only thing he was allowed to do in private, without a guard. There he seized the stick of wood tipped with a sponge that is kept for the filthiest of uses, and rammed the whole thing down his throat, blocking his windpipe and choking the breath out of his body. That was throwing an insult in death's face. Exactly so — not an elegant or a decent way to die; but what is more foolish than being fastidious about dying? What a brave man! He deserved to be given a choice of fate. How boldly he would have used a sword! With what spirit he would have hurled himself into the depths of the sea, or off a cliff! Cut off from every resource, he still found a way to furnish himself with a death, and with a weapon for it — proof that nothing but the will stands between a man and his dying. Let each of us judge this fierce fellow's act as he pleases, so long as we agree on this much: the foulest death is preferable to the cleanest slavery.

Since I began with humble examples, I will keep on with them. Men will demand more of themselves when they see that even the most despised class of men can despise death. We treat the Catos and the Scipios and the other names we are trained to admire as beyond imitation; but I will now show you that the virtue I am speaking of turns up as often in the gladiators' barracks as among the commanders of a civil war. Recently a gladiator, sent off to the morning show, was riding in a cart with the other prisoners. Nodding as if he could not fight off sleep, he let his head droop lower and lower until it slipped between the spokes; then he held his body rigid just long enough for the turning wheel to break his neck. The very wagon that was carrying him to his punishment carried him out of it.

When a man wants to break out and be gone, nothing stands in his way. Nature keeps us in an open enclosure; the way out is unbarred. When our situation permits it, we may look around for an easy exit. If many means of freedom lie ready to hand, we can take our pick and consider the best way out; but if chances are hard to come by, seize the nearest as if it were the best, however unheard-of, however new. A man who does not lack the courage to die will not lack the ingenuity. Look how even the lowest of slaves, when suffering spurs him, rouses himself and outwits the most watchful guards! The truly great man is not just the one who has given himself the order to die, but the one who has found the means.

I promised you a few more illustrations from the same games. During the second event of a mock sea-battle, one of the barbarians sank the spear he had been issued against his enemy deep into his own throat. "Why, oh why," he said, "did I not escape long ago from all this torture and all this mockery? Why do I stand here armed, and wait for death to come?" The show was all the more striking for the lesson it taught: that dying is more honorable than killing.

Well then: if desperate and dangerous men can possess such a spirit, shall it be missing in men who have prepared for these emergencies by long rehearsal — and by reason, the mistress of everything? Reason teaches us that fate has many roads in, but one destination, and that it makes no difference where the inevitable begins. Reason also gives this advice: die as you please, if you can; if you cannot, die as you are able, and seize whatever offers itself for doing yourself that violence. To live by robbery is a crime; but to die by robbery — that is the noblest act of all.

Farewell.
