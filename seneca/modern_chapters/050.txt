Letters 116–117

Letter 116: On Self-control

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

People have often debated the question: is it better to have moderate passions, or none at all? Our Stoic school throws the passions out; the Peripatetics keep them on a leash. I, for my part, don't see how a disease in moderation can be healthy or useful. But don't be afraid — I'm not going to confiscate anything you'd hate to give up. I'll be kind and easygoing about the things you chase — the ones you consider necessary for life, or useful, or pleasant. What I'll strip away is the vice in the chasing. After I've put desire itself under a ban, I'll still let you want those same things — but fearlessly, and with sharper judgment; you'll actually feel the pleasures more than you do now. Why wouldn't they come more readily when you call, once you're their master instead of their slave?

"But," you object, "it's natural to suffer when I lose a friend. Grant a few tears their right to fall! It's also natural to care what people think, and to feel low when their opinion of me is unfavorable. Why won't you allow me such an honorable dread of a bad reputation?"

There is no vice that can't produce a character reference. There is no vice that isn't modest and reasonable at the start; the trouble spreads from there. If you allow it to begin, you cannot guarantee it will stop. Every passion is weak at first. Then it wakes itself up and gathers strength as it goes: it is easier to keep it out than to throw it out. Who denies that all the passions flow from a source that is, in a sense, natural? Nature gave us a concern for our own well-being; but that very concern, overindulged, becomes a vice. Nature mixed pleasure into the necessities of life — not so that we would chase the pleasure, but so that the extra sweetness would make the indispensable things attractive to us. Let pleasure start claiming rights of its own, and its name is luxury.

So let us resist these faults while they are standing at the door, because, as I said, it is easier to refuse them entry than to show them out. And if you protest, "A person should be allowed a certain amount of grief, a certain amount of fear" — I answer that the "certain amount" has a way of stretching on, and it will not stop on your schedule. The wise man can safely be his own supervisor: he can halt his tears, and his pleasures, at will. We can't retrace our steps so easily, so for us the best plan is not to take the first step at all. I think Panaetius gave a very neat answer to a young man who asked him whether the wise man should fall in love. "As for the wise man, we shall see," he said. "But you and I, who are still a long way from wisdom, had better not trust ourselves to a state that is disordered, out of control, enslaved to another person, and worthless in its own eyes. If our love is returned, the kindness inflames us; if it is scorned, our pride does. An easy love hurts us as much as a difficult one: we're captured by the one that yields, and we batter ourselves against the one that resists. So, knowing how weak we are, let's stay quiet. Let's not expose this unstable spirit to drink, or beauty, or flattery, or anything else that coaxes and pulls."

What Panaetius said about love applies, I believe, to all the passions. As far as we can, let's step back from the slippery ground; even on dry footing it's hard enough to stand firm. At this point I know you'll hit me with the standard complaint against the Stoics: "You promise too much and demand too much. We are mere miniatures of men; we can't deny ourselves everything. We'll grieve, but not too much; we'll desire, but in moderation; we'll get angry, and then we'll be talked down." Do you know why we can't reach the Stoic ideal? Because we refuse to believe we can. No — the truth is, something else is at work too: we are in love with our vices. We stand up for them, and we would rather excuse them than evict them. Nature gave us strength enough — if only we would use it, if only we would gather our powers and make them all fight for us, or at least not against us. Unwillingness is the reason; inability is the excuse.

Farewell.

Letter 117: On Real Ethics as Superior to Syllogistic Subtleties

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You are going to manufacture a lot of trouble for me — and drag me, without meaning to, into a long argument and considerable bother — if you keep sending me these petty little questions. To settle them, I can't disagree with my fellow Stoics without hurting my standing among them, and I can't sign on without hurting my conscience. Your question is whether the Stoic doctrine is true: that wisdom is a Good, but that being wise is not. I'll lay out the Stoic position first, and then I'll be bold enough to give you my own.

We Stoics hold that the Good is corporeal — a body — because the Good is active, and whatever acts is a body. What is good, helps. But to help, a thing must act; and if it acts, it is a body. The Stoics declare that wisdom is a Good; it follows that they must call wisdom corporeal too. But being wise, they think, doesn't rate the same treatment. It is incorporeal — a mere attendant on something else, namely wisdom — and so it doesn't act or help in any way.

"What!" comes the reply. "Then why do we say that being wise is a Good?" We do say so — but only by referring it back to the thing it depends on, wisdom itself. Before I build my own creed and plant myself entirely on the other side, let me tell you how other philosophers answer this. "By that logic," they say, "not even living happily is a Good. Like it or not, your school has to answer that the happy life is a Good, but that living happily is not." And this objection gets thrown at our school as well: "You want to be wise. Therefore being wise is something to be desired. And if it is to be desired, it is a Good." So our philosophers are forced to twist their terms — to wedge an extra syllable into the word "desired" that our language does not normally allow. With your permission, I'll wedge it in anyway. "What is good," they say, "is a thing to be desired; the desirable extra is what falls to us after we have attained the Good. It is not sought as a Good itself; it is a bonus added on once the Good has been won."

I don't share this view. I judge that our philosophers have sunk to this argument because they are already shackled by the first link in the chain, and so they aren't free to change their definition. We usually give great weight to what all men take for granted; for us, universal agreement is evidence of truth. For instance, we infer that the gods exist partly from this: every human being has some idea of deity planted in him, and there is no tribe anywhere so far beyond law and custom that it doesn't believe in gods of some sort. When we discuss the immortality of the soul, the general opinion of mankind — who fear, or worship, the spirits of the world below — carries real weight with us. Well, I'll cash in that same universal belief: you will find no one who doesn't hold that wisdom is a Good, and being wise a Good too. But I won't appeal to the crowd like a beaten gladiator throwing himself on the audience's mercy. Let's fight at close quarters, with our own weapons.

When something affects an object, is it inside the object it affects, or outside? If inside, it is just as much a body as the thing it affects — for nothing can affect another thing without touching it, and what touches is a body. If outside, then it withdrew after doing its work; withdrawal is motion, and what moves is a body. You expect me, I suppose, to deny that "a race" differs from "running," that "heat" differs from "being hot," that "light" differs from "giving light." I grant that each pair differs — but not that the two fall into separate classes. If health is an indifferent thing, so is being healthy; if beauty is indifferent, so is being beautiful. If justice is a Good, so is being just. If baseness is an evil, then being base is an evil — exactly as, if sore eyes are an evil, having sore eyes is an evil too. Neither member of the pair, you can be sure, can exist without the other. Whoever is wise is a man of wisdom; whoever is a man of wisdom is wise. The one so exactly matches the other that some people regard them as one and the same thing.

But here is a question I'd love to put: granted that all things are good, bad, or indifferent — into which class does being wise fall? People deny it's a Good; it obviously isn't an evil; so it must be one of the "middle" things. But by the "middle" or "indifferent" we mean what can fall to the bad man as easily as to the good — money, beauty, high social position. Being wise can fall to the good man alone; therefore it is not indifferent. It isn't an evil either, because it cannot fall to the bad man at all. Therefore, it is a Good. What only the good man can possess is a Good; being wise belongs only to the good man; therefore it is a Good. "It's merely an accessory of wisdom," says the objector. Fine, I answer: this thing you call being wise — does it actively produce wisdom, or does it passively tag along with wisdom? Either way it's a body: for what is acted upon and what acts are both bodies, and if it's a body, it's a Good. The only thing that could disqualify it would be being incorporeal — and it isn't.

The Peripatetics hold that there is no distinction at all between wisdom and being wise, since each implies the other. Can you imagine anyone being wise without possessing wisdom? Or anyone possessing wisdom without being wise? But the old masters of logic do distinguish the two, and from them the classification has come all the way down to the Stoics. What kind of classification, I'll explain. A field is one thing; possessing the field is another — obviously, since "possessing the field" refers to the possessor rather than to the field itself. In the same way, wisdom is one thing and being wise another. You'll grant, I suppose, that these are two separate ideas — the possessed and the possessor: wisdom the thing possessed, the wise man its possessor. Now, wisdom is Mind perfected — developed to its highest and best; it is the art of living. And what is being wise? I can't call it "Mind perfected"; rather, it is what falls to the man who has a perfected mind. So a good mind is one thing, and having a good mind, so to speak, is another.

"There are," the argument runs, "certain natural classes of bodies: we say 'this is a man,' 'this is a horse.' Attending on these bodily natures are certain movements of the mind that declare something about the bodies. And these declarations have an essential quality all their own, cut off from body. For example: I see Cato walking. The senses report it; the mind believes it. What I see is a body, and it is on a body that I fix my eyes and my mind. But then I say, 'Cato walks.' What I say" — so they continue — "is not a body; it is a declarative statement about a body — call it an utterance, a declaration, a proposition. So when we say 'wisdom,' we mean something bodily; when we say 'he is wise,' we are making a statement about a body. And it makes a real difference whether you name the person directly or make a statement about him."

Suppose for the moment that these really are two separate conceptions (I'm not ready to give my own verdict yet): what prevents there being a third thing — which is nonetheless a Good? I said a moment ago that a "field" is one thing and "possession of a field" another; naturally, because possessor and possessed are of different natures — one is land, the other is the man who owns the land. But in the question before us, both are of the same nature: the possessor of wisdom, and wisdom itself. Moreover, with the field, the thing possessed is one thing and the possessor another; here, possessed and possessor come under the same heading. The field is owned by legal right; wisdom is owned by nature. The field can change hands and pass to a new owner; wisdom never leaves its owner. So there's no point trying to compare things so unlike.

I had started to say that these can be two separate conceptions and yet both be Goods. Wisdom and the wise man, for instance, are two separate things, and you grant that both are good. And just as there is no objection to counting both wisdom and the possessor of wisdom as Goods, there is no objection to counting as a Good both wisdom and the possession of wisdom — in other words, being wise. The only reason I want to be a wise man is in order to be wise. And really — isn't a thing a Good when, without it, a certain other thing can't be a Good? You'll surely admit that wisdom offered without the right to use it is not worth accepting. And what is the use of wisdom? Being wise. That is its whole value; take it away, and wisdom is useless. If torture is an evil, then being tortured is an evil — with this proviso, in fact: strip away the consequence, and the instruments aren't evil at all. Wisdom is the condition of a perfected mind; being wise is the employment of a perfected mind. How can the employment of a thing not be a Good, when without employment the thing itself is not a Good? If I ask you whether wisdom is desirable, you admit it. If I ask whether the employment of wisdom is desirable, you admit that too — since you say you wouldn't accept wisdom if you weren't allowed to employ it. Now, what is desirable is a Good. Being wise is the employment of wisdom — just as making a speech is the employment of eloquence, and seeing is the employment of the eyes. Being wise is the employment of wisdom; the employment of wisdom is desirable; therefore being wise is desirable; and if it is desirable, it is a Good.

For years now I have been condemning myself for imitating these men in the very act of prosecuting them — for wasting words on a point that's perfectly obvious. Who can doubt that if heat is an evil, being hot is an evil too? Or that if cold is an evil, being cold is an evil? Or that if life is a Good, so is being alive? All these questions live on the outskirts of wisdom, not in wisdom itself — and wisdom itself is where we should make our home. Even if the mind fancies a ramble, wisdom has large and spacious grounds of its own: we may investigate the nature of the gods, the fuel that feeds the stars, all the varied courses of the heavenly bodies; we may ask whether our affairs move in step with the stars, whether the impulse to motion flows from there into every mind and body, and whether even the events we call chance are bound by strict laws — whether nothing in this universe is unforeseen or off schedule in its rounds. Such subjects have been dropped from moral instruction these days, but they lift the mind and raise it to the scale of what it studies. The questions I was discussing a moment ago do the opposite — they grind the mind down and wear it thin, not whetting it, as you and your friends maintain, but weakening it. And I ask you: are we really going to squander the study we owe to greater and better themes on a question that may well be wrong and is certainly worthless? What will it profit me to know whether wisdom is one thing and being wise another? What will it profit me to know that the one is a Good and the other isn't? Let me take a gamble and put up this prayer: "Wisdom for you, and being wise for me!" We'll come out even.

Show me instead the road to those greater ends. Tell me what to avoid and what to seek; by what studies I can shore up my tottering mind; how I can beat back the waves that hit me broadside and drive me off course; how I can face down all my evils, and how I can be rid of the calamities that have crashed in on me and the ones I have crashed into myself. Teach me how to carry sorrow without a groan, and prosperity without making anyone else groan. Teach me not to sit waiting for the final, inevitable end, but to make my own retreat, of my own free will, when it seems right to me. Nothing seems to me more shameful than praying for death. If you want to live, why pray for death? And if you don't want to live, why ask the gods for what they gave you at birth? That you must die some day has been settled, whether you like it or not; when you shall wish to die is in your own hands. The one is a necessity for you; the other is a privilege.

I recently read a most disgraceful sentiment from — the more shame to him — a learned gentleman: "May I die as soon as possible!" You fool, you are praying for what is already yours. "May I die as soon as possible!" Perhaps you grew old in the act of saying it! In any case, what's stopping you? No one is holding you; leave by whatever road you please. Pick any part of Nature and tell it to supply the exit. The elements by which the world does its work — water, earth, air — are no more the causes of life than they are the roads to death. "May I die as soon as possible!" And what exactly do you mean by "as soon as possible"? What day are you setting for the event? It may come sooner than your prayer asks. Talk like this comes from a weak mind, one fishing for pity with its curses; the man who prays for death does not want to die. Ask the gods for life and health. If you have resolved to die, death's reward is this: you are done with praying.

These, my dear Lucilius, are the problems we should be dealing with — these are the problems on which we should be shaping our minds. This is wisdom; this is what being wise means: not batting empty subtleties around in idle, petty debates. Fortune has set you so many problems that you haven't solved yet — and you're still splitting hairs? How foolish, to practice fancy exhibition strokes after the signal for battle has sounded! Throw away the practice weapons; you need armor for a fight to the finish. Tell me how to keep sadness and fear from shaking my soul; tell me how to shrug off this load of hidden cravings. Do something! "Wisdom is a Good, but being wise is not a Good" — the payoff of talk like that is a verdict that we aren't wise, and a whole field of study made into a joke for wasting its effort on useless things. Would you believe this question is debated too: whether future wisdom is a Good? As if anyone could doubt it! Do barns feel the weight of the harvest that is still to come? Does boyhood sense approaching manhood in its muscle and force? The sick man, in the meantime, gets no help from the health that is on its way — no more than a runner or a wrestler is refreshed by the rest period that comes months after the match. Who doesn't know that what is yet to be is not a Good, for the simple reason that it is yet to be? What is good necessarily helps; only things that exist now can help; what doesn't help isn't a Good; and if it helps, it already is. I shall be wise some day, and that Good will be mine when I am — but in the meantime it doesn't exist. A thing must first be, and only then be of some particular kind. How, I ask you, can what is still nothing already be a Good? What better proof could you want that a thing does not exist than to say, "It is yet to be"? What's still on the road obviously hasn't arrived. "Spring will follow" — so I know it is winter now. "Summer will follow" — so I know it is not summer. The best evidence that a thing isn't here yet is that it's still coming. I hope to be wise some day, but in the meantime I am not wise; for if I had that Good, I'd already be free of this evil. Some day I shall be wise — and from that very sentence you can tell that I am not wise yet. I cannot live in that Good and in this evil at once; the two don't mix, and good and evil don't share the same person.

So let's rush past all this clever nonsense and hurry on to what will actually help us. No man running frantically for a midwife while his daughter is in labor stops to read the praetor's edict or the schedule of the games. No one racing to his burning house pauses over a checkerboard to work out how the trapped piece might be freed. But good heavens — the news is pouring in on you from every side: your house on fire, your children in danger, your country under siege, your property plundered. Add shipwreck, earthquakes, and everything else there is to dread. Battered by all this, you're setting aside time for intellectual entertainment? You're asking the difference between wisdom and being wise? Tying and untying knots while that ruin hangs over your head? Nature has not been so generous and open-handed with our time that we can afford to waste any of it. And notice how much gets lost even by the careful: one man's time is stolen by his own poor health, another's by illness in the family; now private business swallows the hours, now public business; and all the while sleep splits our lives with us, half and half.

Out of this time — so short, so swift, carrying us off in its current — what is the sense of spending the greater part on useless things? Besides, our minds have gotten into the habit of entertaining themselves instead of curing themselves — of making philosophy an aesthetic pleasure, when philosophy should really be a remedy. What the difference is between wisdom and being wise, I do not know; but I do know this: it makes no difference to me whether I know it or not. Tell me — when I have found out the difference between wisdom and being wise, will I be wise?

Why, then, do you keep me busy with the words of wisdom rather than its works? Make me braver. Make me calmer. Make me Fortune's equal — make me her superior. And I can be her superior, if I turn everything I learn to that end.

Farewell.
