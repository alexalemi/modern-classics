Letters 72–73

Letter 72: On Business as the Enemy of Philosophy

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

The question you're asking me about was once perfectly clear in my mind. I had mastered it so thoroughly that I didn't even need to think. But I haven't tested my memory of it in a while, and it isn't coming back easily. I feel like a book whose rolls have stuck together from sitting unread. My mind needs to be unrolled; whatever has been stored away in there ought to be taken out and inspected from time to time, so it's ready for use the moment it's needed. So let's set that question aside for now — it demands real labor and real care. As soon as I can expect to stay put in one place for a while, I'll take it up properly. Some subjects you can write about while bouncing along in a carriage; others need a desk, quiet, and a closed door. Still, I ought to get something done even on days like these — days that are booked solid from morning to night. There is never a moment when fresh business won't come along. We sow it ourselves, and that's why one task always sprouts into several. Then we keep granting ourselves postponements: "As soon as I finish this, I'll settle down to serious work," or, "Once I get this annoying mess sorted out, I'll devote myself to study."

But philosophy is not something to put off until you have free time. Everything else should be neglected so that we can attend to philosophy, because no amount of time is long enough for it — not even if our lives stretched from boyhood to the furthest limit granted to man. And it makes little difference whether you abandon philosophy entirely or study it in fits and starts; it doesn't stay where you left it. Break its continuity and it snaps back to the beginning, like a taut cord let go. We have to resist the affairs that eat our time. Don't try to untangle them — shove them out of the way. There is no time that's actually unsuitable for useful study; and yet plenty of men fail to study in the very circumstances that make study necessary. "Something will come up to stop me," he says. Not for the man whose spirit is happy and alert no matter what business he's in. It's the people still short of perfection whose happiness can be interrupted. The joy of the wise man is unbroken and continuous — a fabric woven in one piece, torn by no accident and no turn of Fortune. At all times and in all places, he is at peace. His joy depends on nothing external and waits on no favor from man or Fortune. His happiness is homegrown: it would leave his soul if it had entered from outside, but it was born there. Now and then some event reminds him that he's mortal, but it's a light blow — it only grazes the skin. Some trouble may brush him, I admit, like a puff of wind; but that Supreme Good of his stands unshaken. What I mean is this: there are external nuisances, like pimples and boils breaking out on a body that is basically strong and sound — but there's no disease down deep. The difference between the man of perfect wisdom and the man still making progress is the difference between a healthy man and someone convalescing from a long, hard illness — a man for whom "health" just means a milder attack of his disease. If the convalescent isn't careful, he relapses at once, straight back into the old trouble. But the wise man can't slip back — can't slip into any sickness at all. Bodily health is a temporary arrangement; the doctor can't guarantee it even after he's restored it. Often enough he's rousted out of bed to see the very patient who called him before. The mind, though, once healed, is healed for good.

Let me tell you what I mean by a healthy mind: one that is content with itself; that trusts itself; that understands that everything people pray for, all the advantages men give and chase, count for nothing in a happy life. That mind is sound. Because anything that can be added to is incomplete; anything that can suffer loss won't last. The man who wants his happiness to last should rejoice in what is genuinely his own — and everything the crowd gapes at ebbs and flows. Fortune gives us nothing we can actually own. Even Fortune's gifts only please us once reason has tempered and blended them to our taste; it's reason that makes even external goods agreeable — gulp them down greedily and they turn sour. Attalus, my old teacher, used to use this picture: "Have you ever watched a dog snapping, jaws wide, at scraps of bread or meat his master tosses him? Whatever he catches he swallows whole on the spot, and instantly gapes again in hope of more. That's us. Whatever Fortune throws, we bolt it down without tasting it, and stand there tense and frantic for the next thing to snatch." Not the wise man. He is satisfied. If something does fall his way, he takes it casually and sets it aside. The happiness he enjoys is supremely great, it lasts, and it is his. Now take a man with good intentions who has made progress but is still far below the summit: his life is a series of ups and downs — one moment lifted to heaven, the next brought down to earth. For the untrained and inexperienced, there's no bottom to the fall; they drop into Epicurus's Chaos — empty and boundless. And there's a third class: the men who are close to wisdom. They haven't grasped it, but they have it in view — within striking distance, you might say. They aren't tossed around, and they aren't drifting backward either. They're not yet on dry land, but they're already in the harbor.

So, given the enormous gap between those on the heights and those in the depths, and given that even the men in the middle are chased by their own ebb and flow — and by the very real risk of sliding back into their old degenerate ways — we must not surrender ourselves to busyness. Shut it out. Once it gets a foot in the door, it will bring in replacements for itself. Let's resist it at the start. Better that it never begin than that we have to make it stop.

Farewell.

Letter 73: On Philosophers and Kings

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

It seems to me a mistake to believe that people loyally devoted to philosophy are stubborn and rebellious — scorners of magistrates or kings or anyone who runs public affairs. Just the opposite: no class of men appreciates a ruler more than the philosopher does, and rightly so, because rulers give no one a greater gift than the people they allow to enjoy peace and leisure. So those whose whole purpose — living rightly — profits from the security of the state are bound to cherish the author of that good like a father. Far more, at any rate, than the restless types who live in the public eye, who owe the ruler much but expect from him even more, and who are never so loaded with favors that their cravings — which grow with every feeding — are ever satisfied. A man whose mind is on favors to come has already forgotten the favors received; and there is no worse thing in greed than its ingratitude. Besides, no man in public life thinks of the many he has passed; he thinks only of those still ahead of him. And these men take less pleasure in seeing a crowd behind them than they take offense at seeing anyone in front. That's the disease of every kind of ambition: it doesn't look back. And ambition isn't the only restless thing — every craving is, because every craving begins where it ought to end.

But that other man — upright, clean, the one who has left the senate and the courts and all affairs of state to withdraw to nobler business — cherishes the people who make it possible for him to do so in safety. He alone thanks them unprompted; he alone owes them a great debt they know nothing about. Just as a man honors and reveres the teachers who freed him from his early wanderings, so the wise man honors these men too, under whose protection he can put his good theories into practice. "But other people are also protected by a king's power," you say. Perfectly true. But out of everyone who profits from the same stretch of calm weather, the man who reckons his debt to Neptune largest is the one whose cargo on that voyage was biggest and most valuable. The merchant pays his vow with more feeling than the passenger. And among the merchants themselves, heartier thanks come from the dealer in spices, purple cloth, and goods worth their weight in gold than from the man hauling cheap stuff that's little better than ballast. In the same way, the benefits of this peace, which reaches everyone, are felt most deeply by those who use it well.

For there are plenty of our toga-wearing citizens to whom peace brings more trouble than war. Do you really think the men who spend their peace on drunkenness, or lust, or other vices it would be worth even a war to interrupt, owe as much for it as we do? Not unless you imagine the wise man is so unfair that he believes he owes nothing personally for benefits he enjoys along with everyone else. I owe a great debt to the sun and the moon — and yet they don't rise for me alone. I am personally obliged to the seasons and to the god who governs them, though none of it was portioned out for my benefit. The foolish greed of mortals draws a line between possessing and owning, and counts nothing its own that the public has a share in. But our philosopher considers nothing more truly his own than what he shares in partnership with the whole human race. These things wouldn't be common property — which they are — unless every individual held his portion; even the slightest share in a thing makes you a partner. And here's the further point: the great and true goods are not divided up so that each person gets a sliver. They belong entire to each individual. At a grain distribution, men receive only the amount promised to each; a banquet, a meat-dole, anything a man can carry off in his hands, gets split into portions. But these goods are indivisible — I mean peace and liberty — and they belong whole to all men and whole to every man.

So the philosopher thinks about the person who makes it possible for him to use and enjoy these things — the person who exempts him when the state's dire need calls men to arms, to sentry duty, to defending the walls, and to all the endless demands of war — and he gives thanks to the helmsman of his state. This is what philosophy teaches above all: to acknowledge the debt of benefits received honorably, and to pay it honorably. Though sometimes the acknowledgment itself is the payment. Our philosopher will freely admit, then, that he owes a large debt to the ruler whose management and foresight let him enjoy rich leisure, control of his own time, and a quiet uninterrupted by public duties.

  Shepherd, a god gave me this peaceful ease,
  for he shall be a god to me forever.

And if even leisure like our poet's owes a great debt to the man who gave it — leisure whose greatest boon is only this:

  He let me turn my cattle out to graze, as you can see,
  and play whatever tune I pleased on my rustic reed —

then how highly should we value the philosopher's leisure, which is spent among the gods, and makes us gods? Yes, that is exactly what I mean, Lucilius; and I am inviting you to heaven by a shortcut.

Sextius used to say that Jupiter has no more power than the good man. Jupiter, of course, has more gifts to hand out to mankind. But when you're choosing between two good men, the richer one isn't the better one — any more than, between two pilots of equal skill at the tiller, you'd call the one with the bigger, showier ship the better sailor. In what way is Jupiter superior to our good man? His goodness lasts longer. But the wise man doesn't rate himself one bit lower just because his virtues run on a shorter clock. Take two wise men: the one who dies at a greater age is not happier than the one whose virtue was confined to fewer years. In the same way, a god has no advantage over a wise man in happiness, even though he has one in years. Virtue is not greater for lasting longer. Jupiter possesses everything — but he has handed the possession of it all over to others. The only use of it he keeps for himself is this: he is the cause of everyone else's use. The wise man surveys other people's possessions, and disdains them, as calmly as Jupiter does — and he thinks the better of himself for it, because Jupiter cannot make use of them, and the wise man does not want to. So let's believe Sextius when he points out the path of perfect beauty and cries: "This is 'the way to the stars' — the way of thrift, self-restraint, and courage!"

The gods are not snobbish or jealous. They open the door; they lend a hand as you climb. Are you amazed that a man goes to the gods? God comes to men — no, closer than that: he comes into men. No mind is good without God. Divine seeds are scattered through our mortal bodies. If a good farmer receives them, they spring up true to their source, the equals of the stock they came from. But if the farmer is bad — barren soil, or swampland — he kills the seeds and raises weeds instead of wheat.

Farewell.
