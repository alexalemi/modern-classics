Letters 106–107

Letter 106: On the Corporeality of Virtue

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

My slowness in answering your letter was not because I was busy. Don't accept that excuse — not from me, not from anyone. I am free, and so is everyone who wants to be. No man is at the mercy of his affairs. He tangles himself up in them by his own choice, and then flatters himself that being busy is proof of a full and happy life. So why didn't I answer sooner? The question you asked me was being woven into the fabric of my book. You know I'm planning to cover the whole of moral philosophy and settle every problem that touches it. So I hesitated: should I make you wait until the subject came up in its proper place, or hand down a ruling out of order? In the end it seemed kinder not to keep someone waiting who has come from so far away. So I'm going to pull this question out of its sequence — and I'll also send you, without waiting to be asked, whatever belongs to the same family of problems.

What family is that? Questions where the knowing is pleasant rather than useful. Your question, for instance: is the good corporeal — a physical body? Well, the good acts, because it does us good; and what acts is a body. The good stirs the mind and, in a way, molds and shapes what is essential to it. The goods of the body are bodily; so the goods of the soul must be too, because the soul itself is a body. And so man's good must be a body, since man himself is one. I am badly lost if the things that sustain a man, that preserve or restore his health, are not physical — and therefore his good is a body. You won't doubt, I'm sure, that the emotions are bodies (let me wedge in one more topic that wasn't on the agenda) — anger, love, sternness — unless you doubt that they change our faces, knot our foreheads, relax our features, spread a blush, or drain the blood away. Well then? Do you think marks that show so plainly on the body are stamped there by anything but a body? And if the emotions are bodies, so are the diseases of the spirit — greed, cruelty, and all the faults that harden in our souls until they pass the point of cure. So evil is a body too, with all its branches — spite, hatred, pride. And so are the goods: first because they are the opposite poles of the bad, and second because they show you the same physical symptoms. Don't you see how courage makes the eye flash? How prudence pulls the face into concentration? How reverence produces a settled calm, joy a serenity, sternness a stiffness, gentleness an easing of the features? These qualities are bodies, then — they change the tone and shape of physical stuff, each exercising its power in its own kingdom.

Now, all the virtues I've named are goods, and so is everything they produce. And can you doubt that whatever can touch is a body?

  Nothing but body can touch or be touched,

as Lucretius says. The changes I've described could not affect the body without touching it; therefore they are bodies. Furthermore, anything with the power to move, force, restrain, or control is a body. Well? Doesn't fear hold us back? Doesn't boldness push us forward? Courage spur us on and give us momentum? Restraint rein us in and call us home? Joy lift our spirits, sadness drag them down? In short, everything we do, we do at the command of vice or virtue. And only a body can control or force another body. The good of the body is a body; a man's good is bound up with his bodily good; therefore it is a body.

Now that I've humored you, I'll say your line before you can: "What a board game we're playing!" We dull our fine edge on superfluous puzzles like these; they make men clever, not good. Wisdom is a plainer thing than that. In fact, it is clearly better to use our learning to improve the mind — instead of squandering philosophy itself the way we squander everything else, on things we don't need. We suffer from excess in everything, and we suffer from excess in learning too: we learn not for life but for the lecture hall.

Farewell.

Letter 107: On Obedience to the Universal Will

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Where is that good sense of yours? Where is that skill of yours at sizing things up? That greatness of soul? Have you really been reduced to torment by a trifle? Your slaves saw that you were absorbed in business and took it as their chance to run away. Fine. If your friends had deceived you — and by all means let them keep the name we wrongly gave them, so that their shame is the greater for failing to live up to it — if your friends, I say, had deceived you, all your affairs would be missing something. As it is, you have merely lost men who were damaging your own work and making you a burden to your neighbors. Nothing in this is unusual or unexpected. To be upset by such things is as absurd as complaining that you got splashed in the street or spattered with mud. The program of life is the program of a bathhouse, a crowd, a journey: some things will be thrown at you, and some will hit you by accident. Life is not a dainty business. You have set out on a long road: you are bound to slip, collide, fall, wear out, and cry "I want to die!" — which is to say, you are bound to lie. At one stage you will leave a comrade behind; at another you will bury one; at another you will be afraid for one. It is across stumbling ground like this that the rugged journey has to be traveled.

Does someone wish for death? Let the mind be prepared to meet everything. Let it know it has arrived at the heights where the thunder plays; let it know it has come to the place where

  Grief and avenging Cares have made their beds,
  where pale Disease lives, and gloomy Old Age.

These are the housemates you must spend your days with. Avoid them you cannot; despise them you can. And you will despise them, if you think ahead often and rehearse the future. Everyone walks bravely toward a danger he prepared for long in advance, and stands up even under hardship if he has practiced meeting it. It's the unprepared who panic at the smallest things. We must see to it that nothing takes us by surprise. And since everything is heavier when it is unfamiliar, constant reflection will guarantee that whatever the trouble is, you won't face it like a raw schoolboy.

"My slaves have run away from me!" Yes — and other men have been robbed, blackmailed, murdered, betrayed, trampled underfoot, attacked with poison or with slander. Name any trouble you like; it has happened to many. Beyond that, there are missiles of every kind hurled at us. Some are already planted in us; some are in the air and on their way at this very moment; some that were aimed at other men graze us instead. We should not be surprised at any of the conditions we were born into — conditions no one should complain about, because they are ordained equally for all. Yes, I say equally: for a man might have suffered even the thing he escaped. An equal law is not one that everyone has felt, but one that is laid down for everyone. Prescribe this sense of fairness for your own mind: let us pay the tax of our mortality without complaint.

Winter brings the cold: we must shiver. Summer comes back with its heat: we must sweat. Freak weather shakes our health: we must fall ill. In some places we will meet wild beasts — or men more destructive than any beast. Flood will cost us something, or fire will. We cannot change this order of things. What we can do is acquire stout hearts worthy of good men — hearts that endure chance bravely and fall into step with Nature. And Nature steers this world-kingdom you see around you by means of change: clear skies follow cloudy ones; after a calm comes the storm; the winds blow by turns; day follows night; part of the sky is rising while part is setting. Eternity is made of opposites.

It is to this law that our souls must adjust themselves; this they should follow, this they should obey. Whatever happens, assume it was bound to happen, and do not volunteer to rail at Nature. What you cannot fix, it is best to endure — and to attend, without complaining, on the God under whose guidance everything moves forward. It is a bad soldier who grumbles while following his commander. So let us take our orders briskly and cheerfully, and never desert the march of this most beautiful universe, into which everything we will ever suffer is already woven.

Let us address Jupiter, the pilot of this world-mass, in the words of our great Cleanthes — those most eloquent lines, which I will allow myself to render in Latin, following the example of the eloquent Cicero. If you like them, make the most of them; if they displease you, you'll know I was only doing what Cicero did:

  Lead me, Zeus, and you too, Destiny,
  wherever you have decreed that I shall go.
  I will follow without flinching. And if I falter
  and turn unwilling, I will follow anyway —
  groaning, enduring in vice what I might
  have done in virtue. Fate leads the willing soul,
  and drags the unwilling along.

Let us live that way, and speak that way: let Fate find us ready and eager. That is the great soul — the man who has handed himself over to Fate. And the weakling, the degenerate, is the man who struggles, who bad-mouths the order of the universe, and who would rather reform the gods than reform himself.

Farewell.
