Letters 17–19

Letter 17: On Philosophy and Riches

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Toss all of that aside if you're wise — no, toss it aside so that you can be wise. Race toward a sound mind at top speed and with everything you've got, and if any tie holds you back, loosen it or cut it. "But," you say, "my estate is holding me up. I want to arrange things so that it can support me when I've retired from work — so that poverty won't be a burden to me, and I won't be a burden to anyone else." When you talk like that, you don't seem to know the strength and power of the good you're considering. You do grasp the big thing — the great benefit philosophy confers — but you don't yet see its workings up close; you don't yet know how much help it gives us in everything, everywhere; how (to borrow Cicero's phrase) it doesn't just come to our rescue in the greatest matters but reaches down into the smallest. Take my advice: call wisdom in for a consultation. She'll advise you not to sit forever over your ledger. Obviously what you're after, with all this postponing of your studies, is to make sure you'll never have to fear poverty. But what if poverty is something to desire? Riches have blocked many a man's road to wisdom; poverty travels light and carefree. When the trumpet sounds, the poor man knows the attack isn't aimed at him. When someone shouts "Fire!" he looks for the way out, not for what he can carry. If he has to take a sea voyage, the harbor doesn't roar and the docks don't churn with one man's entourage. No mob of slaves surrounds him — slaves with mouths that force their master to covet the harvests of lands across the sea. It's easy to fill a few stomachs when they're well trained and want nothing except to be filled. Hunger is cheap; a picky appetite is expensive. Poverty is content once its pressing needs are met.

Why, then, should you refuse Philosophy as a companion? Even the rich man imitates her ways when he's in his right mind. If you want leisure for your mind, either be poor or live like a poor man. Study does no good unless you take the trouble to live simply — and living simply is voluntary poverty. So away with all the excuses: "I don't have enough yet; once I reach the amount I've set, then I'll give myself over to philosophy completely." And yet this very thing you keep postponing and placing behind everything else is what should come first — you should begin with it. "I just want enough to live on," you answer. Fine — but learn while you're earning it. If anything forbids you to live nobly, nothing forbids you to die nobly. There is no reason poverty should call us away from philosophy — no, not even outright want. When we're hurrying after wisdom, we must be willing to endure even hunger. Men have endured hunger in besieged towns, and what did all that endurance buy them except not falling into the conqueror's power? How much greater is what's promised here: lasting freedom, and the assurance that we need fear neither God nor man. Even starving, we must reach that goal. Armies have put up with every kind of want, have lived on roots, have fought off hunger with food too revolting to name — and all of it to win a kingdom, and (more astonishing still) a kingdom that would belong to somebody else. Will any man hesitate to endure poverty, if that's what it takes to free his mind from madness?

So there's no need to pile up riches first. You can make the journey to philosophy without travel money. Is that really the plan? After you've acquired everything else, then you'll want wisdom too — philosophy as life's last requirement, a sort of supplement? No. Be a philosopher now, whether you have anything or not — because if you have anything, how do you know you don't have too much already? — and if you have nothing, then seek understanding first, before you seek anything else. "But I'll lack the necessities of life," you say. In the first place, you can't lack them, because nature demands very little, and the wise man fits his needs to nature. But if the absolute pinch of need arrives, he'll simply take his leave of life and stop being a trouble to himself. And if his means are thin and scanty, he'll make the best of them, without fretting or worrying about anything beyond the bare essentials. He'll give his belly and his shoulders their due, and with a free and happy spirit he'll laugh at the scrambling of rich men and the panic of those chasing wealth, and say: "Why postpone your real life to some far-off date? Are you waiting for interest to come due, for a return on your merchandise, for a place in some rich old man's will — when you can be rich here and now? Wisdom pays out wealth in ready cash, and she pays it to exactly those people she has taught to find wealth unnecessary." But all that applies to other men; you stand nearer the rich class. Move yourself to a different age, and you have too much. And in every age, what is enough remains the same.

I might have closed the letter at this point, if I hadn't gotten you into bad habits. No one greets Parthian royalty without bringing a gift, and I can't say goodbye to you without paying up. So be it — I'll borrow from Epicurus: "For many men, the getting of riches has not been an end of troubles but a change of troubles." I'm not surprised. The fault was never in the wealth; it's in the mind itself. The same thing that made poverty heavy for us makes riches heavy too. It matters little whether you lay a sick man on a wooden bed or a golden one — wherever you move him, he takes his disease along. In just the same way, it matters little whether a diseased mind is set down in riches or in poverty. The sickness follows the man. Farewell.

Letter 18: On Festivals and Fasting

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

It's December, and the whole city is in a sweat over it. Merrymaking has been given official license. Everything echoes with enormous preparations — as if the Saturnalia were any different from an ordinary business day! The difference has shrunk so close to nothing that I think the man had it right who said: "Once December was a month; now it's a year."

If you were here with me, I'd love to talk it over and hear what you think we should do — keep our daily routine unchanged, or, so as not to seem out of step with everyone around us, dine more festively and shed the toga. As things stand, we Romans now change our dress for pleasure and holiday-making, though in the old days that happened only when the state was in trouble and the times were dark. If I know you, you'd have played the umpire and ruled that we should be neither exactly like the holiday crowd in their liberty-caps nor entirely unlike them — unless, that is, this is the very season when the soul should be given its orders: told to stand alone in abstaining from pleasure at the precise moment the whole mob has thrown itself into pleasure. Because that is the surest proof a man can get of his own steadiness — neither seeking out the things that seduce him and lure him toward indulgence, nor being dragged into them. It takes real courage to stay dry and sober when the mob is drunk and vomiting; but it takes greater self-control not to withdraw at all — to do what the crowd does, only differently: neither making yourself conspicuous nor melting into the crowd. You can keep a holiday without going on a binge.

But I'm so determined to test the steadiness of your mind that I'm going to give you a lesson drawn from the teaching of great men: set aside a certain number of days during which you will be content with the scantiest and cheapest fare, with coarse and rough clothes, saying to yourself the whole time: "Is this the condition that I feared?" It's exactly when the soul is free of care that it should be toughening itself in advance for the times of stress; it's while Fortune is being kind that it should be fortifying itself against her violence. In peacetime the soldier runs maneuvers, throws up earthworks with no enemy in sight, and wears himself out with unnecessary labor so that he'll be equal to the necessary kind. If you don't want a man to flinch in the crisis, train him before it comes. That's the path followed by those men who, in their rehearsal of poverty, have come nearly to the point of actual want every month — so that they would never recoil from what they had practiced so often.

Don't imagine I mean "Timon's dinners," or "paupers' huts," or any of the other games bored millionaires play to kill the tedium of their lives. Let the cot be a real cot, the cloak a genuinely coarse one, the bread hard and grimy. Endure it for three or four days at a stretch, sometimes longer, so that it's a real test of yourself and not a hobby. Then, I promise you, my dear Lucilius, you will leap for joy when you're full on a penny's worth of food — and you'll understand that a man's peace of mind does not depend on Fortune. Even when she's angry, she grants enough for our needs.

Not that you should think you're doing anything great. You'll only be doing what thousands of slaves and thousands of poor men do every day of their lives. But you can credit yourself with this much: you'll be doing it freely, and it will be as easy for you to endure it forever as to try it now and then. Let's practice our strokes on the dummy. Let's get on intimate terms with poverty, so that Fortune can never catch us off guard. We'll be rich with that much less worry once we've learned how far poverty is from being a burden.

Even Epicurus, the teacher of pleasure, kept certain set days on which he fed his hunger grudgingly — he wanted to see whether that left him short of full and complete happiness, and if so, by how much, and whether the difference was worth buying at the price of great effort. At least, that's what he says in the famous letter to Polyaenus, written in the year Charinus held office. He actually brags that he fed himself for less than a penny, while Metrodorus, who hadn't come as far yet, needed a whole one. Do you think a man can be full on fare like that? Yes — and he can have pleasure too. Not that shifty, fleeting pleasure that needs a fresh jolt every so often, but a pleasure steady and sure. Water, barley-meal, and a crust of barley bread are not a cheerful diet, but it is the highest kind of pleasure to be able to take pleasure even in that — to have pared your needs down to the little that no unkindness of Fortune can snatch away. Prison rations are more generous than this; men held for execution aren't fed so meanly by the man who is going to execute them. What a great soul it takes, then, to descend of your own free will to a diet that even men under a death sentence have no reason to fear! That is forestalling Fortune's spear-thrusts.

So begin, my dear Lucilius, to follow these men's custom: set apart certain days on which you withdraw from your business and make yourself at home with the scantiest fare. Open an account with poverty.

  Dare, my friend, to scorn the sight of wealth;
  shape yourself to kinship with your God.

For no one is in kinship with God except the man who has scorned wealth. I'm not forbidding you to own it, of course — I want to bring you to the point where you own it fearlessly. And there's only one way to get there: convince yourself that you can live happily without it as well as with it, and always look at riches as if they were about to fly away.

But now it's time to start folding up this letter. "Settle your debts first," you cry. Very well — here's a draft on Epicurus; he'll pay out the sum: "Ungoverned anger begets madness." You can't help knowing how true that is, since you've had not only slaves but enemies. This passion flares up against every kind of person; it springs from love as much as from hate, and it shows itself in the middle of games and jokes no less than in serious business. What matters isn't how big the provocation is, but what kind of mind it lands in. It's the same with fire: what counts isn't the size of the flame but what it falls on. Solid timber has withstood a raging blaze, while dry, combustible stuff nurses the smallest spark into a conflagration. So it is with anger, my dear Lucilius: the outcome of a mighty anger is madness. Avoid anger, then, not merely to escape excess but to keep a healthy mind. Farewell.

Letter 19: On Worldliness and Retirement

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I leap for joy whenever a letter arrives from you. Your letters fill me with hope — they're no longer mere promises about you, but guarantees. And I beg and pray you: keep on this course. What better thing could I ask of a friend than something asked for his own sake? If you can, pull yourself out of all the business you describe; if you can't, tear yourself out. We have squandered enough of our time already — let's begin, in old age, to pack our bags. Surely no one can begrudge us that. We have spent our lives on the high seas; let us die in harbor. Not that I'd have you make a name for yourself by your retirement; retirement is nothing to parade, and nothing to hide either. Nothing to hide, I say — I won't push you so far toward condemning all mankind as mad that I'd have you dig yourself a hole and vanish into oblivion. Just manage it so that your retirement is never conspicuous, though it should be plain to see. Second point: men whose choices are still open from the start can deliberate about whether they'd like to pass their lives in obscurity; you no longer have a free choice. Your ability and energy have thrust you into the world's work — that, and the charm of your writing, and your friendships with famous and distinguished men. Renown has already taken you by storm. You could sink into the deepest obscurity and bury yourself completely, and your earlier deeds would still point you out. You cannot keep lurking in the dark; wherever you run, much of the old gleam will follow you.

Peace, though, you can claim without anyone resenting you, without any sense of loss, without a single pang. What will you be leaving behind that you can imagine being sorry to leave? Your clients? None of those men courts you for yourself; they court something from you. Once men hunted friends; now they hunt loot. Let a lonely old man change his will, and by morning his caller has moved to another doorstep. Great things don't come cheap — so do the arithmetic: is it better to walk away from your own true self, or merely from some of your property? If only you'd had the luck to grow old in the modest circumstances you were born to, and fortune had never carried you so high! Your quick rise to prosperity swept you far out of sight of wholesome living — the province, the procuratorship, and everything such things promise. Next you'll take on greater duties, and after those, greater still. And where does it end? Why wait until there's nothing left for you to crave? That moment will never come. We Stoics say there's a chain of causes out of which fate is woven; believe me, there's a chain of desires too — each one begins where the last one ends. You have been dropped into a life that will never, on its own, put an end to your misery and your servitude. Pull your chafed neck out of the yoke; better that it be cut through once than galled forever. If you retreat into private life, everything will be on a smaller scale, but you will be satisfied to the full; as it is, plenty is heaped on you from every side, and none of it satisfies. Which would you rather be — poor and full, or rich and hungry? Prosperity is greedy itself, and it lies exposed to the greed of others. And as long as nothing is enough for you, you will never be enough for anyone else.

"But how can I take my leave?" you ask. Any way you can. Think of all the risks you've run for money, all the labor you've put in for a title — you have to dare something for leisure, too. Otherwise you'll grow old in the churn of procuratorships abroad and civil duties at home, in turmoil and ever-fresh floods of responsibility, which no man has ever escaped by keeping his head down or living quietly. What does your own wish for a secluded life have to do with it? Your position in the world wishes the opposite! And what if you let that position grow even greater now? Everything added to your success will be added to your fears. Here I want to quote you Maecenas, who told the truth from the very summit: "There's thunder even on the loftiest peaks." If you ask what book it's from: the one called Prometheus. He meant simply that the high peaks have their tops wrapped in thunderstorms. But is any power worth a price so high that you — a man like you — would ever adopt a style as debauched as his to get it? Maecenas was a gifted man, and he would have left a great model for Roman oratory to follow if success hadn't gone soft on him — hadn't, in fact, gutted the man in him. That end is waiting for you too, unless you shorten sail right now and, as Maecenas wouldn't do until it was too late, hug the shore.

That saying of Maecenas's might have squared my account with you — but knowing you, you'll file an injunction against me and refuse to accept payment of my debt in such crude, debased coin. However that stands, I'll draw on the account of Epicurus. He says: "Before you consider what you're going to eat and drink, consider whom you're going to eat and drink with — for a feast of meat without a friend is the life of a lion or a wolf." You won't have that privilege unless you withdraw from the world; until then, your dinner guests will be whoever your secretary sorts out of the crowd of morning callers. But it's a mistake to pick out a friend in the reception hall, or to test him at the dinner table: a friend at your table is better made than found there. The worst misfortune of a busy man buried in his possessions is this — he believes men are his friends when he is no friend to them, and he assumes his favors are winning people over, when in fact, with certain men, the more they owe, the more they hate. A small loan makes a man your debtor; a large one makes him your enemy. "What," you say, "don't kindnesses create friendships?" They do — if you've had the freedom to choose who receives them, and if they're placed with judgment instead of scattered broadcast.

So while you're beginning to call your mind your own, apply in the meantime this maxim of the wise: what matters is not what a man receives, but who it is that receives it. Farewell.
