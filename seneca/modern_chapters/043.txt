Letters 101–102

Letter 101: On the Futility of Planning Ahead

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Every day and every hour show us what nothings we are. Some fresh piece of evidence reminds us of the frailty we keep forgetting — and forces us, right in the middle of planning for eternity, to glance over our shoulder at death.

You ask what this gloomy opening means? It means Cornelius Senecio, a distinguished and capable Roman knight — you knew him. He had climbed from small beginnings to real money, and the rest of the road ran downhill in front of him. Rank grows more easily than it gets started, and money comes slowest while it still lives next door to poverty; it limps along until it has crawled clear. Senecio was right on the border of wealth, carried there by two very powerful assets, either of which could have made him rich by itself: he knew how to make money, and he knew how to keep it.

He was a man who lived very simply and took care of his health and his fortune with equal attention. He called on me early in the morning, as usual. Then he spent the whole day, right up to nightfall, at the bedside of a friend who was seriously and hopelessly ill. He ate a pleasant dinner. Then a sudden, violent attack of quinsy seized him, and with the breath squeezing through his swollen throat, he barely lasted until daybreak. Within a very few hours of performing every duty of a sound and healthy man, he was gone. A man who was venturing money by land and by sea, who had entered public life and left no kind of business untried, was snatched from the world at the very peak of his success, at the very moment the money was pouring into his coffers.

  Graft your pear trees now, Meliboeus — set out your vines in their rows!

How foolish it is to lay out plans for a life when you don't even own tomorrow! What madness, to build long-range hopes! "I'll buy and build, lend money and call it in, collect offices and honors — and then, old and full of years, I'll retire to a life of ease." Believe me: everything is doubtful, even for the fortunate. No one has any right to promise himself anything out of the future. The very thing we're holding slips through our fingers, and Fortune cuts into the very hour we're cramming so full. Time does roll on by a fixed law — but it rolls in the dark. What good is it to me that nature's course is certain, when my own is not?

We plan long voyages, and homecomings postponed until we've wandered every foreign shore. We plan for military service, and for the slow rewards of hard campaigning. We angle for a governorship, then for the office after that — and the whole time, death is standing right beside us. But since we never think of it except as something that happens to the neighbors, the examples of mortality press in on us daily and stay in our minds only as long as they surprise us.

Yet what is more foolish than being surprised that something which can happen every day has happened on some particular day? There is indeed a limit set for each of us, fixed exactly where the unbending law of fate has fixed it — but none of us knows how close he stands to that limit. So let us set our minds in order as though we had come to the very end. Let us postpone nothing; let us balance life's books each day. The greatest flaw in life is that it is always unfinished, always with some part of it put off. The man who puts the finishing touches on his life every day is never short of time. And yet out of that shortage come fear, and a craving for the future that eats away at the mind. Nothing is more wretched than dangling over the question of how things will turn out. How much is left? What kind will it be? — and the troubled mind flutters with a fear it can't even account for.

How do we escape this churning? One way only: if our life stops straining forward and gathers itself into itself. The only man anxious about the future is the man whose present is doing him no good. But when I have paid my soul what I owe it, when a well-balanced mind knows that a day is no different from eternity — then, whatever days and whatever problems are still to come, the soul looks out from a great height and laughs heartily to itself at the endless parade of the ages. What disturbance can the shifts and instability of Fortune cause, if you stand certain in the face of everything uncertain?

So begin at once, my dear Lucilius, to live — and count each separate day as a separate life. The man who has prepared himself this way, whose daily life has been a rounded whole, is easy in his mind. But those who live on hope alone watch the immediate future slip out of their grasp again and again, while greed creeps in to take its place, along with the fear of death — a curse that lays a curse on everything else. Out of that fear came the most degraded prayer on record, in which Maecenas accepts weakness, deformity, and finally the torture of the cross, so long as his life is stretched out through all of it:

  Cripple my hand, cripple my foot,
  pile a hump on my crooked back,
  shake my teeth loose in my head —
  all is well, if my life remains.
  Keep it for me, I beg you,
  even if I sit on the sharp cross itself!

There he is, praying for the thing that would be the most pitiable fate in the world if it actually happened — and begging for an extension of his torment as though he were begging for life! I would consider him utterly contemptible if he'd been willing to live right up to the moment of crucifixion. "No," he cries, "break my body if you must, so long as the breath stays in the battered, useless wreck. Maim me — just give me a little more time in the world, misshapen and deformed as I may be. Nail me up, if it comes to that, and set my seat on the sharp cross." Is it worth pressing down on your own wound, worth hanging stretched out on a gibbet, just to postpone the one thing that is the cure for troubles and the end of all punishment? Is it worth weakening yourself to that point just to keep breathing — breath you'll only have to give up anyway? What could you wish for Maecenas except that heaven show him mercy? What is the meaning of such spineless, disgraceful verse? What is the meaning of cutting deals with panic? Of begging for life so abjectly? He can't ever have heard Virgil read the line:

  Tell me — is death really such a misery?

He asks for the extremity of suffering and — harder still to bear — for its prolongation, its extension. And what does he gain by it? Merely a longer existence. But what kind of life is a drawn-out death? Can you find anyone who would rather waste away in pain, dying one limb at a time, letting his life out drop by drop, than die once and be done with it? Can you find any man willing to be fastened to the accursed tree — already sick, already deformed, chest and shoulders swollen with ugly tumors — and draw breath after breath through long-drawn-out agony? He would have had plenty of reasons to die before he ever mounted the cross!

Deny it now, if you can: nature is very generous in making death unavoidable. Many men have stood ready to strike bargains even more shameful — to betray their friends so they could live a little longer, or to hand over their own children to disgrace so they could keep enjoying the daylight that had witnessed all their sins. We must shake off this greed for life, and learn that it makes no difference when your suffering comes, because sooner or later you are bound to suffer. The point is not how long you live, but how nobly. And often, living nobly means you cannot live long.

Farewell.

Letter 102: On the Intimations of Our Immortality

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

A man is annoying when he shakes you out of a pleasant dream — he's spoiling a pleasure that may be unreal but looks and feels exactly like the real thing. Your letter has done me the same injury. It pulled me back abruptly from a meditation I was absorbed in and ready to carry further, if I'd been allowed. I was taking pleasure in investigating the immortality of souls — more than that, in believing it. I was lending a ready ear to the great authors, who not only approve that most welcome doctrine but promise it. I was giving myself over to a noble hope. I was already weary of myself, already starting to look down on the broken fragments of this existence, feeling that I was bound for that infinity of time, that inheritance of eternity — when your letter arrived and woke me, and I lost my lovely dream. But once I've settled accounts with you, I'll go looking for it and get it back.

At the beginning of your letter you remarked that I hadn't worked through the whole problem — the one where I was trying to prove a doctrine of our school: that the renown which comes to a man after death is a good. You said I hadn't answered the standard objection: "No good can be made up of things that are separate and distinct; yet renown is made up of exactly such things." What you're asking about, my dear Lucilius, belongs to another chapter of the same subject, and that's why I had set those arguments aside — them, and others covering the same ground. As you know, logical questions get tangled up with ethical ones. So I handled the essential part, the part that bears on conduct: whether it's foolish and useless to concern ourselves with what lies beyond our last day; whether our goods die with us and nothing is left of the man who is no more; whether any profit can be gained, or pursued in advance, from something we won't be able to feel when it comes.

All of that bears on conduct, so it went in under its proper topic. But what the logic-choppers say against the idea had to be sifted out separately, and so it was set aside. Now that you demand answers to all of it, I'll examine their claims and refute them one by one. But my rebuttals will be impossible to follow without one preliminary remark. And what is it? Just this: some bodies are continuous, like a man. Some are composite — a ship, a house, anything that results from joining separate parts into a single whole. And some are made up of members that remain distinct, each one standing separate — like an army, a crowd, or a senate. The people who make up such bodies are united by law or by function, but by their nature they are distinct individuals. What else do I need to lay down beforehand? Only this: we hold that nothing is a good if it is composed of separate things. A single good must be held together and governed by a single soul; the essential quality of each single good must itself be single. This can be proved on its own whenever you like; for now it simply had to be stated, because our own weapons are being hurled back at us.

The opponents argue like this: "You say no good can be made up of separate things? Yet this renown of yours is nothing but the favorable opinion of good men. Reputation doesn't consist of one person's remarks, and disgrace doesn't consist of one person's disapproval; in the same way, renown doesn't mean we've merely pleased one good man. To constitute renown, the agreement of many distinguished and praiseworthy men is required. But that agreement is the verdict of a number of people — that is, of persons who are distinct. Therefore it is not a good.

"You say, again, that renown is the praise given to a good man by good men. Praise means speech; speech is utterance with a particular meaning; and utterance, even from the lips of good men, is not in itself a good. Not everything a good man does is a good. He shouts applause, he hisses disapproval — but no one calls the shouting or the hissing a good, even if his conduct as a whole is admired and praised, any more than anyone would applaud a sneeze or a cough. Therefore renown is not a good.

"Finally, tell us this: does the good belong to the one who praises, or to the one who is praised? If you say it belongs to the one who is praised, you're on as foolish an errand as claiming that my neighbor's good health is my own. But praising worthy men is an honorable action; so the good belongs entirely to the man doing the praising — the man performing the action — and not to us, the ones being praised. And that was the whole question under discussion."

Now let me answer these objections one at a time, and quickly. The first question is still whether any good can be made up of separate things — and there are votes on both sides. But does renown really need many votes? It can be satisfied with the verdict of one good man: it is one good man who decides that we are good. "What!" comes the retort. "Would you define reputation as the esteem of one individual, and disgrace as one man's spiteful chatter? Glory, as we understand it, is something broader — it demands the agreement of many." But "the many" and "the one" stand on different footing here. Why? Because if the good man thinks well of me, that amounts in practice to being thought well of by all good men — for they would all think the same, if they knew me. Their judgment is one and the same; truth stamps it identically. They cannot disagree, which means they would all hold the same view, being unable to hold different ones. "One man's opinion," you say, "isn't enough to create glory or reputation." In the first case, one judgment is a universal judgment, because all of them, if asked, would judge alike. In the other case, people of dissimilar character hand down divergent verdicts. You'll find tangled emotions there — everything doubtful, shifting, untrustworthy. Do you suppose the many could ever hold one opinion? Even one man among them doesn't hold one opinion. With the good man, it is truth that produces conviction, and truth has one face and one function; the crowd agrees only on unsound ideas, and the false are never steady — they are irregular and clashing.

"But praise," says the objector, "is nothing but an utterance, and an utterance is not a good." When they define renown as praise bestowed on the good by the good, what they're referring to is not an utterance but a judgment. A good man may say nothing at all; but if he decides that a certain person deserves praise, that person is praised. Besides, praise is one thing and praise-giving another; the giving requires the voice as well. That's why no one speaks of a "funeral praise" but of a eulogy — a giving of praise — because its whole function depends on speech. When we say a man is worthy of praise, we are promising him men's goodwill not in words but in judgment. So even the good opinion of a man who silently approves of someone good is praise.

Praise, as I've said, is a matter of the mind rather than of speech; speech merely brings out the praise the mind has already formed and publishes it to the crowd. To judge a man worthy of praise is to praise him. When our tragic poet sings that it is a wonderful thing "to be praised by a well-praised hero," he means a hero worthy of praise. And when an equally venerable poet says "praise nurtures the arts," he doesn't mean praise-giving — that ruins the arts. Nothing has corrupted oratory, and every other art that plays to an audience, so much as popular approval. Reputation cannot exist without words; but renown can be content with men's judgments and complete without a syllable being spoken. It is satisfied not only with silent approval, but even in the face of open protest. Here, in my opinion, is the difference between renown and glory: glory rests on the judgments of the many; renown, on the judgments of the good.

"But whose good is it," comes the reply, "this renown, this praise given to a good man by good men? Does it belong to the one praised, or to the one who praises?" To both, I say. It is my good, as the one being praised, because nature made me to love all men, and I rejoice in having done good deeds, and I congratulate myself on finding men who express their sense of my virtues with gratitude. That they are grateful is a good for the many — but it's a good for me too. My spirit is arranged so that I can count other men's good as my own — above all the good that I myself have caused. And this good belongs also to the ones who give the praise, for it is exercised through virtue, and every act of virtue is a good. My friends could not have gained this blessing if I hadn't been the right sort of man. So being praised when you deserve it is a good belonging to both sides — just as a just decision is a good both for the one who renders it and for the one in whose favor it is rendered. Do you doubt that justice is a blessing to its possessor as well as to the man who receives his due? To praise the deserving is justice; therefore the good belongs to both.

That's answer enough for these dealers in fine print. But our purpose shouldn't be to argue cleverly, dragging philosophy down from her majesty into petty quibbles like these. How much better to follow the open, straight road than to lay out a winding course for yourself that you'll have to retrace with endless trouble! Argumentation of that kind is nothing but experts juggling with each other.

Tell me instead how much in keeping with nature it is to let the mind reach out into the boundless universe. The human soul is a great and noble thing; it accepts no limits except the ones it shares with the gods themselves. First of all, it will not accept a lowly birthplace — Ephesus, or Alexandria, or any spot still more crowded with people and thickly packed with houses. The soul's homeland is the whole sweep that circles the height and breadth of the sky — the entire rounded dome under which lie land and sea, under which the upper air divides the human from the divine and joins them too, and where all the sentinel stars stand their turns on watch. And the soul will not put up with a narrow allowance of time. "All the years are mine," it says. "No age is closed to great minds; all time lies open to the progress of thought. When the day comes to separate the heavenly from its earthly mixture, I will leave the body here, where I found it, and take myself back to the gods of my own accord. I am not cut off from them even now — only held back, in a heavy earthly prison."

These delays of mortal existence are the prelude to a longer and better life. The mother's womb holds us for ten months, preparing us not for the womb itself but for the open world we are delivered into once we're finally fit to draw breath and survive in the open air. In just the same way, through all the years that stretch from infancy to old age, we are ripening toward another birth. A different beginning waits for us, a different state of things. As yet we can bear the light of heaven only in rare glimpses. So look ahead without fear to that appointed hour — the last hour of the body, but not of the soul. Look around at everything lying about you as if it were luggage in a roadside inn: you must travel on. Nature searches you as bare on the way out as on the way in. You cannot take away more than you brought — in fact, you must throw away most of what you carried into life. You will be stripped of this outer skin, your last protection; stripped of the flesh; drained of the blood that runs and circulates through your body; stripped of the bones and sinews, the framework of these frail and temporary parts.

This day, which you fear as your last, is your birthday into eternity. Lay down your burden — why hang back? — as if you hadn't once before left a body that was hiding you. You cling to it, you struggle; well, at your first birth too, it took your mother's great labor to set you free. You weep and wail — and that very weeping happens at birth as well, though then it was forgivable: you came into the world ignorant of everything and utterly unpracticed. When you left the warm, sheltering protection of your mother's womb, a freer air breathed on your face; then you flinched at the touch of a rough hand, and you stared in amazement at unfamiliar things, still soft and knowing nothing.

But now it is nothing new for you to be separated from what you were once a part of. Let these limbs go calmly — they're of no more use — and lay aside the body you have lived in so long. It will be torn apart, buried out of sight, and worn away. Why be downcast? This is what always happens: when we are born, the afterbirth perishes. Why love such a thing as if it were your own possession? It was only your covering. The day will come that tears you out and leads you away from this foul, reeking womb of a world. Withdraw from it now, as much as you can. Withdraw from pleasure, except what is bound up with the essential and important things of life; grow a stranger to it even now, and turn your thoughts to something nobler and higher. One day the secrets of nature will be laid open to you, the haze will be shaken from your eyes, and bright light will stream in on you from every side.

Picture the blaze when all the stars mingle their fires. No shadow will trouble that clear sky; the whole expanse of heaven will shine evenly — day and night take turns only in the lowest layer of the air. Then you will say that you lived your whole life in the dark, once you have seen the perfect light with your whole, perfected being — the light you now see only darkly, through vision cramped to its narrowest. And yet, far off as it is, you already gaze at it in wonder. What do you think that heavenly light will be, when you have seen it in its own home?

Thoughts like these let nothing mean settle in the soul — nothing low, nothing cruel. They insist that the gods are witnesses of everything. They command us to seek the gods' approval, to prepare ourselves to join them some day, and to plan for immortality. The man who has grasped this idea shrinks from no attacking army, isn't rattled by the trumpet-blast, isn't cowed by any threat. Why should a man feel fear, when he looks forward to death? Even the man who believes the soul lasts only as long as it is chained to the body, and scatters the moment it is released, still arranges his life so that he'll be useful even after death. For though he is taken from our sight, still

  again and again our thoughts run back to the hero,
  and the glory his line has won returns to the mind.

Consider how much good examples help us, and you will understand this: the memory of a great man is no less useful than his presence.

Farewell.
