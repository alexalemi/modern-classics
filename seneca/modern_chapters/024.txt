Letter 76

Letter 76: On Learning Wisdom in Old Age

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You have been threatening to become my enemy if I don't keep you posted on everything I do, day by day. See how frankly you and I live with each other: I am going to confide even this to you. I have been attending a philosopher's lectures. Four days now I have been going to his school and listening to the talk that starts at two in the afternoon. "A fine age for that!" you say. Fine indeed — and what could be sillier than refusing to learn a thing just because you've gone a long time without learning it? "So what are you telling me? Should I fall in line with the dandies and the schoolboys?" Well, if this is the only thing that embarrasses my old age, I'm doing pretty well. The classroom admits men of every age. "Is that why we grow old — so we can trail after the youngsters?" But if I, an old man, can go to the theatre, and have myself carried to the races, and can't bear to let a single fight in the arena be settled without me there to watch, shall I blush to show up at a philosopher's lecture?

You should keep learning as long as there is anything you don't know — which means, if the proverb has it right, as long as you live. And no proverb was ever better suited to its occasion: As long as you live, keep learning how to live. Mind you, I do some teaching in that school as well. What, you ask, could I possibly teach there? This: that even an old man should keep learning. But I am ashamed of the human race every time I walk into the lecture-hall. To get to Metronax's house, as you know, I have to pass right by the Theatre of Naples. The building is packed. Inside, men are deciding, with tremendous passion, who deserves to be called a good flute-player; even the Greek piper and the announcer draw a crowd. But in the other place — where the question on the table is "What is a good man?" and the lesson being taught is how to be one — the seats are nearly empty, and most people think the few who do sit in them are wasting their time. Empty-headed idlers, they get called. I hope I'm blessed with mockery like that. The jeers of the ignorant should be heard with an untroubled mind; when a man is marching toward the goal of honor, he should scorn scorn itself.

So push on, Lucilius, and hurry, or you'll end up like me — learning in your old age. Hurry all the more, in fact, because you have put the subject off for so long, and it is one you will barely master even as an old man. "How much progress will I make?" you ask. As much as you try to make. What are you waiting for? Wisdom doesn't fall to any man by luck. Money will come on its own; offices will be handed to you; influence and authority may well be forced on you; but virtue will not drop into your lap. And the knowledge of it is not won with light effort or a little sweat — though the sweating is worth it, when you are about to win every good there is at a single stroke. For there is only one good: the honorable. In all those other things that public opinion smiles on, you will find nothing true, nothing certain. Why there is only one good, and why it is the honorable, I will now explain — since you have ruled that my earlier letter didn't carry the argument far enough, and that the doctrine was recommended to you rather than proved. I'll also compress what other authors have said into a small space.

Everything is judged by the standard of its own particular good. We value a vine for its yield and the flavor of its wine, a stag for its speed. With pack animals we ask how strong their backs are, since carrying loads is their whole use. In a dog, if his job is to find a wild animal's trail, the first thing we want is a keen nose; if his job is to run the quarry down, fast legs; if to attack and grapple with it, courage. In each thing, the best quality should be the one it was born for — the one it is judged by. And what is the best quality in a human being? Reason. By reason man surpasses the animals and is surpassed only by the gods. Perfect reason, then, is the good that belongs peculiarly to man; everything else he shares, to some degree, with animals and plants. Man is strong: so is the lion. Man is handsome: so is the peacock. Man is swift: so is the horse. I am not saying he is outdone in all these categories. I am not looking for what is greatest in him, but for what is distinctly his. Man has a body: so do trees. Man can act deliberately and move at will: so can beasts, so can worms. Man has a voice — but how much louder is the dog's, how much more piercing the eagle's, how much deeper the bull's, how much sweeter and more musical the nightingale's! What, then, is peculiar to man? Reason. When reason is right and has been brought to perfection, a man's happiness is complete. So if everything is praiseworthy — has reached the goal its nature intended — when it has perfected its own particular good, and if man's particular good is reason, then a man who has perfected his reason is praiseworthy and has reached the goal his nature set for him. This perfect reason has a name: virtue. And virtue and the honorable are one and the same.

It follows that the only good in man is the good that belongs only to man. We are not asking, right now, what is a good; we are asking what is man's good. And if nothing belongs peculiarly to man except reason, then reason will be his one distinctive good — but a good worth all the others put together. If a man is bad, I suppose you will think ill of him; if good, well of him. So the quality in a man by which he earns approval or disapproval is his chief good — his only good. You don't doubt that it is a good; you only doubt that it is the sole one. Suppose a man has everything else — health, money, a long pedigree, a reception-hall full of callers — but is admittedly bad: you will disapprove of him. Suppose another man has none of what I've listed — no money, no train of clients, no rank, no line of grandfathers and great-grandfathers — but is admittedly good: you will approve of him. So this is man's one distinctive good. The man who has it deserves praise even if he lacks everything else; the man who lacks it stands condemned and rejected, though he has everything else in heaps. What holds for men holds for things. We call a ship good not when she is painted in expensive colors, not when her prow is plated with silver or gold or her figurehead carved in ivory, not when she is loaded with the imperial treasury or the wealth of kings — but when she is steady and sound and tight, with seams that keep the water out, sturdy enough to take the pounding of the waves, obedient to her helm, fast, and indifferent to the wind. You will call a sword good not because its belt is gold or its scabbard studded with gems, but because its edge cuts clean and its point will go through any armor. Of a carpenter's rule we do not ask how beautiful it is, but how straight it is. Each thing is praised by the standard it exists to meet — by the quality that is its own.

So in the case of a man, too, the relevant question is not how many acres he plows, how much money he has out at interest, how many callers crowd his morning receptions, how costly the couch he lies on or how transparent the cups he drinks from — but how good he is. And he is good if his reason is well-ordered and right and fitted to what his nature intended. That is what we call virtue; that is what we mean by "honorable"; and it is man's unique good. For since reason alone perfects a man, reason alone, perfected, makes him happy — and man's only good is the one thing that makes him happy. We do say, it's true, that the things virtue produces and gathers around itself — all the works of virtue, that is — are also goods. But virtue itself is the only good for this reason: there is no good without it. If every good resides in the soul, then whatever strengthens the soul, lifts it, enlarges it, is a good; and virtue is what makes the soul stronger, higher, larger. Everything else — all the things that set our desires itching — drags the soul down and weakens it. When we think those things are lifting the soul up, they are merely puffing it up, cheating it with so much air.

The whole of life's conduct is governed by one pair of considerations: the honorable and the base. It is with reference to those two that our reason decides to do a thing or leave it undone. Let me spell out what I mean. A good man will do what he believes it is honorable to do, even if it costs him labor; he will do it even if it costs him harm; he will do it even if it costs him danger. And he will not do what is base, even if it brings him money, or pleasure, or power. Nothing will scare him away from the honorable; nothing will tempt him into the base. So if he is resolved to follow the honorable without exception, to avoid the base without exception, and in every act of his life to keep his eye on those two things — counting nothing good but the honorable, nothing bad but the base — if virtue in him is the one thing uncorrupted, holding its steady course on its own, then virtue is that man's only good, and nothing can happen to it now that would make it anything else. It is past all risk of change. Folly can creep upward toward wisdom; wisdom never slips back into folly.

You may remember my telling you that the things people commonly desire and dread have been trampled underfoot by many a man in a moment of blind passion. Men have been found who would thrust their hands into the flames, whose smiles the torturer could not wipe away, who shed not one tear at their children's funerals, who walked to their deaths without flinching. Love, anger, lust — these have dared every danger. Now if a moment's stubbornness, spurred by some goad pricking the spirit, can manage all that, how much more can virtue manage — virtue, which acts not on impulse or on a sudden, but evenly, with a strength that lasts? It follows that the things which men in a fit of passion often despise, and which the wise despise always, are neither goods nor evils. Virtue itself, then, is the only good. She walks proudly between the two extremes of fortune, with a splendid contempt for both.

But once you accept the view that anything besides the honorable is good, every virtue suffers for it. No virtue can ever be won and held if it has to keep one eye on anything outside itself. And if any such thing exists, it stands in conflict with reason, from which the virtues spring — and with truth, which cannot exist without reason. And any opinion in conflict with truth is false. A good man, you'll grant, must show the utmost devotion to the gods. Therefore he will bear whatever happens to him with an untroubled spirit, knowing it has come about through the divine law by which the whole universe moves. Granted that, there will be one good for him and only one — the honorable — since the honorable itself commands us to obey the gods, not to flare up at sudden misfortune or bewail our lot, but to accept fate patiently and do what it orders. If anything besides the honorable is good, then we will be hounded by a greed for life, and a greed for all the furniture of life — an intolerable condition, endless, never settled. The only good, then, is the honorable: the good that knows its limits.

I have argued before that man's life would be more blessed than the gods' if things the gods have no use for — money, high office — were goods. Consider this too: if our souls survive when they are released from the body, a happier state awaits them than they knew while housed in it. But if the things we use for the body's sake are goods, then our souls will be worse off once set free — and that runs against everything we believe, that the soul is happier shut up and confined than free and away into the universe. I also said that if the things dumb animals possess as fully as man are goods, then dumb animals will lead happy lives — which is of course impossible. And one more point: for the sake of the honorable a man must be willing to endure anything — which would not be necessary if there existed any other good besides it.

I treated all this at more length in an earlier letter; here I have compressed it and run through the argument briefly. But a doctrine like this will never ring true to you until you lift your mind higher and put the question to yourself: if duty called, would you be willing to die for your country — to buy the safety of all your fellow-citizens at the price of your own — offering your neck not merely with patience but with gladness? If you would, then in your eyes there is no other good; for you are giving up everything else to get this one. See what power the honorable has: you will die for your country at a moment's notice, the moment you know you ought to. Sometimes a noble act yields great joy even in the shortest, most fleeting span of time; and though none of the fruits of the finished deed will reach its doer once he is dead and gone from human affairs, still the mere contemplation of the deed to come is a delight. The brave and upright man, picturing the rewards of his death — his country free, everyone he is paying out his life for delivered — feels the keenest pleasure and enjoys the fruit of his own peril. But even the man denied that joy, the joy of contemplating his last great effort, will leap to his death without a heartbeat's hesitation, content to be acting rightly and dutifully. Pile discouragements on him if you like. Tell him, "Your deed will be quickly forgotten," or "Your fellow-citizens will hardly thank you." He will answer: "All that lies outside my task. My thoughts are on the deed itself. I know it is honorable. So wherever honor leads and summons me, I go."

This, then, is the only good — and not only every soul that has reached perfection knows it, but every soul that is noble by nature and rightly inclined. All other goods are cheap and changeable. That is why owning them is such an anxious business. Even when Fortune's kindness has piled them high, they sit heavy on their owners, always pressing down, sometimes crushing. Not one of the men you see dressed in purple is happy — no more than the actors on whom the play bestows a scepter and a royal cloak for the length of the performance. They strut their hour before a packed house, chests out, on their high stage boots; the moment they exit, the boots come off and they shrink back to their real height. Not one of the men whom riches and honors have set on high is actually great. Then why does he look great to you? Because you are measuring the pedestal along with the man. A dwarf is not tall because he stands on a mountain-top; a colossus keeps its height even standing in a well. This is the mistake we labor under, the trick that keeps fooling us: we never price a man at what he is; we add on the trappings he comes dressed in. But when you want to know a man's true worth — what kind of man he really is — appraise him naked. Have him set aside his inherited estate, his titles, and all Fortune's other frauds; have him strip off his very body. Look at his soul: its quality, its stature. Then you will know whether his greatness is his own or borrowed.

If a man can watch the flash of a sword with steady eyes, if he knows it makes no difference to him whether his soul leaves through his mouth or through a wound in his throat, call him happy. Call him happy too if, when bodily torture is threatened — by accident or by the strength of the stronger — he can hear talk of chains and exile and all the empty terrors that rattle men's minds, and remain unconcerned, and say:

  Maiden, no shape of trouble rises here
  that is new to me, or sudden; I have gone
  before it all — faced and surveyed it, long ago,
  within my soul.

"Today it is you who threaten me with these horrors; but I have always threatened myself with them, and prepared myself, a man, for a man's destiny." An evil pondered in advance lands as a soft blow. To the fool, and to anyone who trusts in Fortune, each event arrives as a shape "new and sudden"; for the inexperienced, a large part of any evil is its novelty. The proof: things men first counted as hardships they bear more bravely once they have grown used to them. That is why the wise man accustoms himself to troubles before they come — lightening by long reflection what others lighten only by long endurance. We sometimes hear the inexperienced say, "I knew this was coming to me." The wise man knows that everything is coming to him. Whatever happens, he says: "I knew it."

Farewell.
