Letter 89

Letter 89: On the Parts of Philosophy

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

What you want to know is genuinely useful — essential, in fact, for anyone in a hurry to reach wisdom: the parts of philosophy, and how that enormous body divides into its separate limbs. Study the parts, and the whole comes easier. I only wish philosophy could appear before our eyes entire, the way the whole expanse of the sky spreads out for us to gaze at — it would be a sight very much like the sky. Every mortal who saw her would fall helplessly in love, and we would abandon all the things we now believe to be great because we don't know what greatness is. But since that view isn't granted to us, we have to study philosophy the way we study the secrets of the heavens: one piece at a time.

The wise man's mind, it's true, takes in the whole structure of philosophy at a glance, sweeping over it as quickly as our eyes sweep the sky. But the rest of us are still breaking through fog; our vision blurs even for what's right in front of us. For people like us, it's easier to be shown each object separately while the universe as a whole is still beyond our grasp. So I'll do what you ask and divide philosophy into parts — but not into scraps. Dividing it is useful; mincing it is not. What is impossibly large is hard to take in, and so is what is impossibly small. A people is divided into tribes, an army into companies; anything that has grown large becomes easier to see once it's broken up — but the pieces, as I say, must not be countless and tiny. Over-analysis is faulty in exactly the same way as no analysis at all: grind anything down to dust and you might as well have left it a lump.

First, then, if you agree, let me draw the line between wisdom and philosophy. Wisdom is the perfect good of the human mind; philosophy is the love of wisdom, and the striving to reach it. Philosophy aims at the goal wisdom has already reached. And it's obvious why philosophy got its name: the name itself confesses what it loves. Some people have defined wisdom as the knowledge of things divine and things human. Others add: "and their causes as well." The added phrase seems unnecessary to me, since the causes of things divine and human are themselves part of the divine order. Philosophy, too, has been defined in all sorts of ways. Some have called it "the study of virtue," others "the study of straightening out the mind," others "the pursuit of right reason." But one thing is as good as settled: philosophy and wisdom are not the same. The seeker and the thing sought cannot be identical. Greed and money are different things — one does the craving, the other is what's craved — and philosophy and wisdom differ in the same way: one is the result and reward of the other. Philosophy does the traveling; wisdom is the destination.

Wisdom is what the Greeks call sophia. The Romans used that word too, in the old days, in the same sense in which they now also say "philosophy." Our old national plays will prove it to you, and so will the epitaph carved on the tomb of Dossennus:

  Pause, stranger, and read the wisdom of Dossennus.

Some of our own school, though — even though philosophy meant to them "the study of virtue," with virtue the thing sought and philosophy the seeker — have insisted that the two cannot be pulled apart. Philosophy cannot exist without virtue, nor virtue without philosophy. Philosophy is the study of virtue, but a study carried on by means of virtue itself; and there can be no virtue without the study of virtue, and no study of virtue without virtue. This isn't like shooting at a target far off, where the archer stands in one place and the mark in another. The approaches to virtue don't lie outside her the way roads into a city lie outside the city; the path that leads to virtue leads through virtue herself. Philosophy and virtue hold on to each other.

The greatest authors — and the greatest number of them — have held that philosophy has three divisions: moral, natural, and rational. The first keeps the soul in order. The second investigates the universe. The third works out what words really mean, how they combine, and the proofs that keep falsehood from creeping in and shoving truth aside. But there have also been those who carved philosophy into fewer parts, and those who carved it into more. Certain Peripatetics, Aristotle's school, added a fourth division, "civil philosophy," on the grounds that it demands its own sphere of activity and deals with its own subject matter. Some added a department they call by the Greek name "economics" — the science of managing one's own household. Still others made a separate heading for the various kinds of life. Yet there isn't one of these subdivisions that won't be found already filed under "moral" philosophy.

The Epicureans held that philosophy was twofold, natural and moral; they threw out the rational branch. Then, when the facts themselves forced them to sort out ambiguous ideas and expose the fallacies that hide under the costume of truth, they too smuggled in a heading — they call it "on judgment and rule" — which is simply "rational" under another name, though they treat it as an appendix to the natural department. The Cyrenaic school abolished the natural and the rational departments both, and settled for the moral side alone — and then they too readmitted, under other titles, exactly what they had rejected. For they divide moral philosophy into five parts: first, what to avoid and what to seek; second, the passions; third, actions; fourth, causes; fifth, proofs. But the causes of things really belong to the natural division, and the proofs to the rational. Aristo of Chios declared that the natural and the rational were not only superfluous but actually got in the way. Then he trimmed even the moral part, the only one he had left: he abolished the department that gives advice, insisting that advice was a tutor's business, not a philosopher's. As if the wise man were anything other than the tutor of the human race!

Since philosophy is threefold, then, let's begin by setting the moral side in order. It has been agreed that this, too, divides into three parts. First comes appraisal — the part that assigns each thing its proper function and weighs what each is worth — and in usefulness it ranks highest. For what is so indispensable as putting the right price on everything? The second part deals with impulse, the third with action. The first duty is to determine what things are worth; the second, to form toward them an impulse that is measured and orderly; the third, to make your impulse and your actions agree, so that in all these matters you are consistent with yourself. Let any one of the three go wrong, and the others fall into confusion with it. What good is it to have everything correctly appraised, each in its proper place, if your impulses run to excess? What good is it to have checked your impulses and to hold your desires in your own hands, if when the moment for action arrives you don't know the right times and seasons — don't know when, where, and how each thing should be done? It is one thing to understand the worth and merit of facts, another to know the precise moment for acting, and still another to curb your impulses and walk, rather than rush, toward what has to be done. So life is in harmony with itself only when action has not deserted impulse, and when impulse arises in each case from the worth of its object — languid or eager as the case may be, depending on whether the object is worth pursuing.

The natural side of philosophy is twofold: bodily things and bodiless things. Each divides into its own ranks of importance, so to speak. The topic of bodies deals first with these two ranks: what creates and what is created — and the created things are the elements. Now this very topic of the elements, some writers hold, is a single integral subject; others divide it into matter, the cause that moves all things, and the elements themselves.

It remains for me to divide rational philosophy into its parts. All speech is either continuous or split up between questioner and answerer. It has been agreed that the first should be called rhetoric and the second dialectic. Rhetoric deals with words, meanings, and arrangement. Dialectic divides into two: things said, and the words they are said in. Then comes a subdivision of each — and it is vast. So I shall stop at this point, and

  only treat the climax of the story;

because if I took a fancy to list all the subdivisions, this letter would turn into a debater's handbook. I'm not trying to discourage you from reading on the subject, excellent Lucilius — provided only that whatever you read, you promptly refer to conduct.

It's your conduct you must hold in check. Rouse what has gone slack in you, bind fast what has come loose, conquer what is stubborn, make war on your own appetites — and on mankind's — as hard as you can. And to those who say, "How long will this unending talk go on?" answer: "I ought to be asking you, 'How long will these unending sins of yours go on?'" Do you really want my remedies to quit before your vices do? I'll speak of them all the more — and precisely because you object, I'll keep on talking. Medicine begins to do good at the moment a touch makes the diseased body tingle with pain. I'll say things that help men even against their will. Now and then something other than compliments should reach your ears, and since as individuals you refuse to hear the truth, hear it all together.

How far will you extend the boundaries of your estates? An estate that once held a whole nation is too narrow for a single owner. How far will you push your plowed fields — you who won't confine even a single farm within the borders of a province? Noble rivers flow down through your private grounds; mighty streams that once marked the border between nations now run through your property from source to mouth. Even that is too little for you, unless your estates fence in whole seas, unless your steward holds sway on the far side of the Adriatic, the Ionian, and the Aegean, unless islands that were home to famous chieftains are counted among your most trifling possessions. Spread them as wide as you like; have as a "farm" what was once called a kingdom; make everything yours that you can — provided only that it's more than your neighbor has.

And now a word with you, whose luxury sprawls as wide as those men's greed. To you I say: "Will this go on until there is no lake your rooftop towers don't loom over? No river whose banks aren't lined with your lordly buildings? Wherever hot springs gush from the ground, you'll be raising new resorts of luxury. Wherever a shore bends into a bay, you'll be laying foundations on the spot — and, not content with any land you haven't manufactured yourselves, you'll bring the sea inside your boundaries. Let your rooftops flash in the sun on every side — perched on mountain peaks commanding wide views of sea and land, or hoisted from the plain to the height of mountains; build your endless structures, your enormous piles — you are still only individual men, and puny ones at that. What good are all your bedrooms? You sleep in one. No place is yours where you yourselves are not."

"Next I come to you, whose bottomless, insatiable gullet ransacks the seas on one side and the earth on the other, hunting down its prey with enormous toil — now with hook, now with snare, now with nets of every kind; no animal is left in peace unless you're already stuffed with it. And of those banquets so many hands prepare for you, how small a portion does your pleasure-jaded palate actually taste! Of all that game, so dangerous to take, how small a portion does the master's sick and squeamish stomach relish! Of all those shellfish, imported from so far away, how small a portion slips down that insatiable gullet! Poor wretches — don't you know that your appetites are bigger than your bellies?"

Talk this way to other men — provided that while you talk, you also listen. Write this way — provided that while you write, you read; and remember that everything you hear or read is to be applied to conduct, and to the calming of passion's fury. Study, not to add anything to your knowledge, but to make your knowledge better.

Farewell.
