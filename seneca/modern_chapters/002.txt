Letters 10–13

Letter 10: On Living to Oneself

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

No, I haven't changed my mind: avoid the crowd, avoid the small circle, avoid even the single companion. There is no one I know of that I'd be willing to share you with. And notice what a compliment I'm paying you here — I trust you alone with yourself. Crates, they say, the student of that same Stilbo I mentioned in an earlier letter, once noticed a young man walking by himself and asked him what he was doing out there all alone. "I'm talking with myself," the young man answered. "Then be careful," said Crates. "Pay close attention — you're talking with a bad man."

When someone is grieving, or frightened about something, we make a point of keeping watch over them, so their solitude can't do them harm. No thoughtless person should ever be left alone. Alone, he only plots foolishness and piles up future dangers for himself or for others. Alone, his low desires come out to play; whatever fear or shame used to keep hidden, the mind now puts on display. Solitude sharpens his recklessness, stirs his lusts, and needles his temper. And the one real benefit solitude offers — no one to trust, no witnesses to fear — is completely wasted on a fool. He informs on himself.

So mark what my hopes are for you — no, mark what I am promising myself, since "hope" is just the name we give a blessing that isn't certain yet: I don't know anyone whose company I would rather you keep than your own. I remember the great-hearted way you flung out certain phrases, and how much strength was in them. I congratulated myself on the spot and said: "Those words didn't come from the edge of the lips. There's a foundation under what this man says. He's not one of the many; he has his eye on his real welfare." Speak that way, and live that way. See to it that nothing drags you down. As for your old prayers, you can release the gods from answering them — file new ones instead. Pray for a sound mind and for good health, of the soul first and then of the body. And pray for these things often, of course. Ask God boldly; you won't be asking him for anything that belongs to someone else.

But, as usual, I need to send a little gift along with this letter. Here's a true saying I found in Athenodorus: "Know that you are free of all desires when you have reached the point of asking God for nothing you couldn't ask for out loud." How foolish people are about this now! They whisper the filthiest prayers up to heaven, and the moment anyone is listening, they go silent. What they refuse to let other people hear, they tell God. So consider whether this might be the healthiest advice anyone could give you: Live among men as if God saw you; speak with God as if men were listening.

Farewell.
Letter 11: On the Blush of Modesty

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Your friend and I have had a talk. He's a man of real ability; his very first words showed what spirit and understanding he has, and how far he has already come. He gave me a foretaste of himself, and he won't fail to live up to it — because he wasn't speaking from a script. He was caught off guard, and when he tried to collect himself, he could barely shake off that flush of modesty — a good sign in a young man. The blush that spread over his face seemed to rise from somewhere deep. And I'm confident that habit of blushing will stay with him even after he has toughened his character, stripped off all his faults, and become wise. No amount of wisdom removes the body's natural weaknesses. What's planted in us at birth can be trained down, but never trained out. The steadiest public speaker often breaks into a sweat before a crowd, as if he'd worn himself out or overheated. Some men's knees shake when they stand up to speak. I know men whose teeth chatter, whose tongues stumble, whose lips tremble. Training and experience never fully shake this off; nature exerts her own power, and through a weakness like this she reminds even the strongest men that she exists. The blush, I'm sure, belongs to this family — it spreads suddenly across the faces of the most dignified men. It shows up more in the young, yes, because their blood is warmer and their faces more sensitive; but it touches veterans and old men too. Some men are at their most dangerous when they redden, as if their shame were draining out of them. Sulla was at his fiercest when the blood rushed to his cheeks. No one had a more sensitive face than Pompey; he blushed in front of every gathering, and especially at a public assembly. And I remember Fabianus reddening when he appeared as a witness before the senate — and the embarrassment suited him remarkably well. A habit like this doesn't come from weakness of mind but from the newness of a situation. An inexperienced man isn't necessarily rattled, but he's usually affected, because his body's natural bent slides him into it. Some men are simply full-blooded; others have quick, restless blood that rushes straight to the face.

As I said, wisdom can never cure this. If wisdom could erase all our flaws, she would be mistress of the universe. Whatever the terms of our birth and the mix of our constitution assign to us will stick to us, no matter how hard or how long the soul works at mastering itself. We can no more forbid these feelings than we can summon them. Actors in the theater, who imitate the emotions — who play fear and nervousness, who act out sorrow — imitate bashfulness by hanging their heads, lowering their voices, fixing their eyes on the ground. But a blush is the one thing they cannot produce. It can't be prevented, and it can't be performed. Wisdom offers no promise here and no help; the blush comes and goes on its own orders. It is a law unto itself.

But my letter is calling for its closing line. Hear this one and take it to heart, because it's useful and it's healthy: "Cherish some man of high character, and keep him always before your eyes — live as if he were watching you, and act in everything as if he saw it." That, my dear Lucilius, is Epicurus's advice, and it's exactly right: he has given us a guardian and an attendant. We would drop most of our sins if a witness stood beside us whenever we were about to go wrong. The soul should have someone it can revere — someone whose authority makes even its inner sanctuary more sacred. Happy is the man who improves other people not just when he's with them, but even when he's in their thoughts! And happy too is the man who can revere someone so deeply that merely remembering him calms and straightens him out. A man who can revere another like that will soon be worth revering himself. So choose yourself a Cato — or, if Cato seems too severe a model, choose a Laelius, a gentler spirit. Choose a master whose life, whose conversation, whose very face — the face that shows the soul — has won you over; keep him always in view, as your protector or your pattern. Because we truly do need someone to measure our characters against. You can never straighten what is crooked unless you use a ruler.

Farewell.
Letter 12: On Old Age

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Everywhere I turn, I see evidence of how old I'm getting. I visited my country place recently and complained about the money being poured into that crumbling building. My caretaker insisted the flaws weren't his fault — "he was doing everything possible, but the house was old." And this is the house that went up under my own hands! What does the future hold for me, if stones my own age are already crumbling? Annoyed, I seized the next excuse to vent at him. "It's obvious," I said, "that these plane trees are being neglected. They have no leaves. Look how gnarled and shriveled the branches are, how rough and shabby the trunks! That wouldn't happen if someone loosened the soil around them and gave them water." He swore by my guardian spirit that "he was doing everything possible, he never let up — but those trees were old." Between you and me, I planted those trees myself. I saw their first leaves. Then I turned toward the door and asked, "Who is that broken-down wreck? Good thinking, posting him at the entrance — he's clearly on his way out. Where did you get him? What possessed you to steal another man's corpse for burial?" But the slave said, "Don't you know me, sir? I'm Felicio — you used to bring me little clay figures. My father was Philositus the steward. I'm your old pet." "The man is stark raving mad," I said. "Has my little pet become a little boy again? Well, it's entirely possible — his teeth are falling out."

I owe my country place this much: it showed me my old age wherever I looked. So let's embrace old age and love it. It's full of pleasure, if you know how to use it. Fruit is sweetest just before it spoils; youth is loveliest as it's ending; for the dedicated drinker it's the last drink that delights — the one that tops him off and crowns the drunkenness. Every pleasure saves its best for last. Life is most delightful on the downward slope — before the edge, but on the way there. And I honestly believe that even the years standing at the roof's very edge have pleasures of their own. Or else this itself replaces pleasure: not needing any. How comforting it is to have worn out your appetites and left them behind! "But," you say, "it's grim to have death staring you in the face." First, death should be stared at by young and old alike — we aren't called up in the order of the census list. Second, no one is so old that he can't decently hope for one more day. And one day, mind you, is a stage on life's journey.

Our whole span of life is built of parts — larger circles enclosing smaller ones. One circle wraps around and bounds all the rest: it runs from birth to our last day. Inside it, a second circle closes off the years of our youth. A third holds the whole of childhood in its circumference. Then there's the year, a class of its own, containing every division of time whose multiplication adds up to a life. The month is ringed by a narrower circle. The smallest circle of all is the day — but even a day has its beginning and its end, its sunrise and its sunset. That's why Heraclitus, whose murky style earned him his nickname, said: "One day is equal to every day." People take this different ways. Some say days are equal in hours, which is true enough — if a "day" means twenty-four hours, then all days are equal, since the night gains whatever the day loses. But others say that one day equals all days by resemblance: even the longest stretch of time contains nothing you won't find in a single day — light and darkness — and eternity just multiplies those alternations; it doesn't make them different, shorter here and longer there. So live each day as if it closed the series — as if it rounded off and completed your life.

Pacuvius, who held Syria so long he made it his own, used to stage a funeral sacrifice in his own honor — wine, the full funeral banquet — and then have himself carried from the dining room to his bedroom while eunuchs clapped and sang in Greek to music: "He has lived his life! He has lived his life!" Not a day passed without Pacuvius carrying himself out for burial. Let's do from a good motive what he did from a rotten one. Let's go to our sleep glad and cheerful, saying:

  I have lived; the course that Fortune set me
  is finished.

And if God is pleased to add another day, let's welcome it with glad hearts. The happiest man, the most securely in possession of himself, is the one who can wait for tomorrow without anxiety. Whoever has said "I have lived" collects a bonus every morning he gets up.

But now I should close this letter. "What?" you say. "It's coming to me without any little present?" Don't worry — it carries something. More than something: a great deal. What could be nobler than the saying I'm handing it to deliver: "It is wrong to live under constraint; but no man is constrained to live under constraint." Of course not. On every side, short and simple roads to freedom lie open. Let's thank God that no one can be held prisoner in life. We are free to trample the constraints themselves. "Epicurus said that," you object. "What business do you have with someone else's property?" Whatever is true is my property. And I'm going to keep piling Epicurus on you, so that the people who swear by a name — who price the speaker instead of the words — may learn that the best ideas belong to everyone.

Farewell.
Letter 13: On Groundless Fears

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I know you have plenty of spirit. Even before you began equipping yourself with maxims — the wholesome kind, with real power over hardship — you took pride in your standing bout with Fortune; and that's truer now that you've actually grappled with her and tested your strength. Our powers can never give us solid confidence in ourselves until difficulties have crowded us from every side — until they've occasionally closed to grappling range. That is how the true spirit proves itself: the spirit that refuses to come under the jurisdiction of anything outside itself. That's its touchstone. No prizefighter can walk into the ring with high hopes if he's never been beaten black and blue. The only man who steps up with real confidence is the one who has seen his own blood, felt his teeth rattle under a fist, been tripped and taken the full weight of a charge — the one who has been knocked down in body but never in spirit, and who rises from every fall more defiant than before. So, to keep the metaphor going: Fortune has had you on the ground before, and you didn't surrender — you sprang back up and planted your feet more eagerly than ever. Courage grows when it's challenged. Still, if you'll let me, I'd like to offer a few extra defenses to fortify yourself with.

There are more things likely to frighten us, Lucilius, than there are to crush us. We suffer more often in imagination than in reality. I'm not talking to you in the Stoic strain now, but in my milder style. Our Stoic custom is to dismiss everything that draws cries and groans as trivial, beneath notice — but let's you and I drop the grand language, true as it is, God knows. Here's my actual advice: don't be miserable ahead of the crisis. The dangers that made you go pale, as if they were bearing down on you, may never arrive. They certainly haven't arrived yet. Some things torment us more than they should; some torment us before they should; and some torment us when they shouldn't torment us at all. We exaggerate our suffering, or we invent it, or we borrow it early.

Set the first of those three aside for now — the matter is disputed and the case is still in court, so to speak. What I call trivial, you'll insist is deadly serious; and I'm well aware that some men laugh under the whip while others wince at a box on the ear. We'll consider later whether such evils get their power from their own strength or from our weakness.

Do me this favor: when people crowd around you working to convince you how unhappy you are, weigh not what you hear but what you actually feel. Consult your own feelings; put the question to yourself independently — you know your own affairs better than anyone. Ask: "Why exactly are these people condoling with me? Why are they nervous, why afraid of catching something from me, as if trouble were contagious? Is there any actual evil here, or just an evil reputation?" Ask yourself, voluntarily: "Am I tormenting myself without good reason? Am I gloomy over nothing? Am I converting something that isn't an evil into one?" You'll come back at me: "How am I supposed to know whether my sufferings are real or imaginary?" Here's the rule. We are tormented by the present, by the future, or by both. The present is an easy verdict: if your body is free and healthy and nothing external is injuring you, then today there is nothing wrong. "But something will happen!" First, examine whether your evidence of coming trouble is solid. Far more often, we're troubled by our own suspicions — we get played by that great mocker, rumour, which has been known to end wars and far more often ends individuals. Yes, my dear Lucilius: we sign on too quickly to whatever people say. We never put our fears to the test, never examine them. We go pale and retreat like soldiers abandoning camp over a dust-cloud kicked up by stampeding cattle, or panicked by some story nobody has verified. And somehow it's the empty report that rattles us most. Truth has fixed boundaries; whatever comes out of uncertainty is handed over to guesswork and to the runaway license of a frightened mind. That is why no fear is so ruinous, so impossible to rein in, as panic fear. Other fears are groundless; panic is witless.

So let's look at this carefully. It's likely some trouble will come to us; but it is not a present fact. How often the unexpected has happened! How often the expected has never shown up! And even if suffering is on its way, what do you gain by running out to meet it? You'll suffer soon enough when it arrives. In the meantime, count on better things. What does that buy you? Time. Plenty will happen in the interval to postpone your trials, or cancel them, or pass them along to someone else — even the ones nearby, even the ones on top of you. A fire has opened a road to escape. Disaster has set men down gently. The sword has been stopped at the very throat. Men have outlived their own executioners. Even bad fortune is fickle. Maybe it will come, maybe it won't; in the meantime, it is not. So count on better things.

Sometimes, with no sign anywhere pointing to trouble, the mind manufactures its own false shapes of evil. It wrings the worst possible meaning out of an ambiguous word. It decides some grudge against us is graver than it is — dwelling not on how angry the enemy actually is, but on how far he might go if he were. But life is not worth living, and there is no bottom to sorrow, if we give fear everything it asks for. Here, let prudence be your help: reject fear with a resolute spirit even when it's standing in plain sight. If you can't manage that, then fight one weakness with another — temper your fear with hope. Nothing we're afraid of is so certain as this: that what we dread comes to nothing, and what we hope for makes fools of us.

So weigh your hopes and your fears together, and whenever the whole question is in doubt, rule in your own favor: believe the version you'd prefer. And if fear carries the vote anyway, lean the other way regardless, and stop harassing your soul. Keep reminding yourself that most mortals work themselves into a froth even when no trouble is at hand and none is certainly coming. No one applies the brakes once he's been given a push; no one scales his alarm to the truth. No one says: "The man who started this story is a fool; the man who believes it is a fool; so is the man who made it up." We drift with every breeze. We panic at uncertainties as if they were certainties. We keep no sense of proportion — the slightest weight tips the scale and dumps us straight into panic.

But I'm embarrassed to be lecturing you sternly, or to be coaxing you along with remedies this mild. Let somebody else say, "Maybe the worst won't happen." You are the one who should say: "And what if it does? Let's see who wins. Maybe it will happen for my benefit — maybe a death like that will do my life credit." The hemlock is what made Socrates great. Wrench from Cato's hand that sword of his, liberty's defender, and you rob him of the greater part of his glory. But I've been exhorting you far too long, when what you need is a reminder, not a sermon. The path I'm leading you down is the same one your nature is already taking; you were born for the conduct I'm describing. All the more reason, then, to build up the good in you, and make it beautiful.

Now, to close this letter, I have only to press the usual seal into it — that is, to entrust it with one noble message for you: "The fool, among all his other faults, has this one too: he is always getting ready to live." Think about what that saying means, my excellent Lucilius, and you'll see how revolting it is — this fickleness of men who lay down fresh foundations for their lives every day, who start erecting new hopes at the very brink of the grave. Search your own memory for examples and you'll think of old men busy preparing, at that very hour, for a political career, or for travel, or for business. And what is more shameful than getting ready to live when you are already old? I wouldn't bother concealing the author's name, except that this saying is little known — it isn't one of Epicurus's greatest hits, the ones I've allowed myself to praise and appropriate.

Farewell.
