Letters 46–49

Letter 46: On a New Book by Lucilius

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Your book arrived — the one you promised me. I opened it intending only to sample it; the plan was to skim now and read properly later. But the book itself coaxed me in deeper. It charmed me into going on. You can judge how eloquent it is from this: it felt light in the hand, though at first glance you'd never guess it was your work or mine — it reads like something by Titus Livius, the historian, or even Epicurus. And the charm gripped me and carried me along so completely that I finished it without setting it down once. The sunshine was calling me outside; hunger was nagging; rain clouds were piling up. And still I swallowed the book whole, from the first page to the last.

I wasn't just pleased with it. I was delighted. What wit it has, what spirit! I would have said what force, if the book had had quiet stretches in it — if it had gathered itself and surged only now and then. But there was no surging; instead there was a steady, even flow, a style muscular and clean. Here and there, though, I caught your sweetness breaking through, and in places that gentleness of yours. The style is tall and noble. That's the manner I want you to keep — that direction, that stride. The subject helped too, which is why you should choose rich, productive topics: subjects that grab hold of the mind and wake it up.

I'll say more about the book after a second reading. For now my judgment is still unsettled — it's as if I'd heard the book read aloud rather than read it myself. Let me really examine it. And don't worry: you'll get the truth from me. What a lucky man you are, to give a friend no opening to lie to you at such long range! Though I suppose that even now, with every excuse for lying stripped away, we still have one left: habit — we lie to each other because we always have.

Farewell.
Letter 47: On Master and Slave

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I was glad to hear, from the people who've been visiting you, that you live on friendly terms with your slaves. That's exactly what I'd expect from a man of your good sense and education. "They are slaves," people say. No — they are men. "Slaves!" No — comrades. "Slaves!" No — unpretentious friends. "Slaves!" No — fellow-slaves, if you stop to consider that Fortune holds exactly the same power over them and over us.

That's why I laugh at the people who think it's degrading to dine with your slave. Degrading — why? Only because of the snobbish custom that surrounds a rich man at his dinner with a crowd of slaves who must stand while he eats. And how he eats! More than he can hold, with monstrous greed, loading a belly stretched so far it forgets how to do a belly's work — so that he labors harder to get all that food back out of himself than he did to cram it in. And the whole time, the poor slaves may not so much as move their lips. Not to speak — not for anything. The rod silences every murmur. Even accidents — a cough, a sneeze, a hiccup — are paid for with the lash. Any sound that breaks the silence is punished savagely. All night long they stand there, hungry and mute.

The result? Slaves who may not talk in front of the master talk about the master. But the slaves of the old days — the ones allowed to talk not just in the master's presence but with him, the ones whose mouths were not stitched shut — those slaves were ready to bare their own necks for their master, to pull any danger hanging over his head down onto their own. They talked at dinner and kept silent under torture. It was this same arrogant treatment that gave rise to the proverb: "You have as many enemies as you have slaves." We do not acquire them as enemies. We make them enemies.

I'll pass over the other cruel and inhuman ways we treat them — how we abuse them not as if they were men, but as if they were pack animals. While we sprawl at the dinner table, one slave wipes up the spat-out food, another crouches under the table collecting what the drunken guests let fall. Another carves the expensive birds, guiding his practiced hand through breast and thigh in sure, exact strokes — a miserable man, if he exists only to carve fat poultry correctly. Though the man who teaches this art for the sake of pleasure may be more miserable still than the man who learns it because he has no choice. Another slave, the wine-server, is dressed up like a woman and forced to fight against his own advancing years: he cannot escape his boyhood — he's dragged back into it. His body is a soldier's already, but he's kept smooth-cheeked, his hair rubbed away or plucked out at the roots, and he stays awake all night, dividing it between his master's drunkenness and his master's lust — a man in the bedroom, a boy at the table. Another has the job of appraising the guests: he must stand there, poor man, and watch to see whose flattery, whose shamelessness of appetite or of tongue, will win an invitation for tomorrow. And think of the caterers, who study the master's palate with delicate expertise — which flavors will sharpen his appetite, which dishes will please his eye, what new combination might rouse a stomach sick with excess, what he'll refuse today out of sheer glut, and what he'll be hungry for. These are the slaves the master cannot bear to eat with. He'd think it beneath his dignity to share a table with his own slave. God forbid!

And yet how many masters he is creating among those very men. I have seen Callistus's former master standing in line at Callistus's door — I have seen him shut out while others were shown in: the same master who once pinned the "For Sale" ticket on Callistus and put him up in the market with the worthless lots. That slave has paid him back — the slave who was shuffled into the first batch, the ones the auctioneer warms up his voice on. Now it is the slave's turn to strike a name off his list, his turn to judge a man unfit to enter his house. The master sold Callistus. But how dearly Callistus has made the master pay.

Please remember this: the man you call your slave sprang from the same stock as you, is smiled on by the same skies, and on precisely equal terms breathes, lives, and dies. You could as easily see the free man in him as he could see the slave in you. When the massacres of Marius's day were over, plenty of men of distinguished birth — men doing their army service as the first step toward the Senate — were flattened by fortune: one ended up a shepherd, another the caretaker of a country cottage. Go ahead and despise a man in whose place you may find yourself at any moment — despise him if you dare.

I don't want to launch into the whole enormous question of how we treat slaves — and we Romans are excessively arrogant, cruel, and insulting toward them. But here is the kernel of my advice: live with your inferior as you would have your superior live with you. Every time you remember how much power you hold over your slave, remember that your own master holds exactly that much power over you. "But I have no master," you say. You're still young; you may have one yet. Have you forgotten how old Hecuba was when she was enslaved? Or Croesus? Or the mother of Darius, or Plato, or Diogenes?

Live with your slave on kind terms — friendly terms, even. Let him talk with you, plan with you, share your life. I know the whole club of exquisites will howl at me for this: "Nothing could be lower," they'll say, "nothing more shameful." These are the same men I catch kissing the hands of other people's slaves. And don't you see how our ancestors stripped away everything hateful from the master's position and everything humiliating from the slave's? They called the master "father of the household" and the slaves "members of the household" — a usage that survives in the theater to this day. They established a holiday on which masters and slaves ate together — not the only day it was allowed, but a day when it was required. They let slaves hold offices within the household and pronounce judgments there; they considered the household a miniature commonwealth.

"So you're telling me," comes the retort, "that I have to seat every one of my slaves at my table?" No — no more than you have to invite every free man to it. But you're wrong if you think I'd turn away certain slaves because their work is dirty — that muleteer, say, or that herdsman. I propose to value men by their character, not by their jobs. A man's character is his own doing; his job is assigned by accident. Invite some to your table because they deserve the honor, and others so that they may come to deserve it — for if there's anything servile in them from the low company they've kept, eating with better men will shake it out. You don't have to hunt for friends only in the forum or the Senate-house, my dear Lucilius. If you pay attention, you'll find them in your own house. Good material often stands idle for want of a craftsman — try it and see for yourself. A man who goes to buy a horse and inspects not the animal but its saddle and bridle is a fool; the man who appraises another man by his clothes and his rank is a fool twice over — for rank is only one more robe we wear.

"He is a slave." But perhaps a free man in soul. "He is a slave." And will that count against him? Show me a man who isn't one. One man is a slave to lust, another to greed, another to ambition — and every one of us is a slave to fear. I can show you an ex-consul who is slave to a little old woman, a millionaire who is slave to a serving-girl; I can show you young men of the noblest families in bondage to pantomime dancers. No slavery is more shameful than the slavery we choose ourselves.

So don't let those fastidious people scare you out of showing your slaves a friendly face instead of a superior one. Let them respect you rather than fear you. Someone will say I'm calling for the liberty-cap for slaves everywhere, that I'm toppling masters from their thrones, because I said slaves should respect a master rather than fear him — "That's his meaning exactly: slaves paying their respects like clients, like morning callers!" Whoever says this forgets that what satisfies a god cannot be too little for a master. To be respected is to be loved — and love cannot be mixed with fear. So I think you are entirely right in not wanting your slaves to fear you, and in doing your correcting with words. The whip is for animals that can't understand them.

Not everything that annoys us actually harms us. But soft living drives us into fits of rage, so that whatever fails to answer our whim sets off our anger. We put on the temper of kings — for kings, too, forgetting their own strength and other men's weakness, go white-hot with fury as though they'd been injured, when their high station keeps them perfectly safe from any such injury. And they know it perfectly well. But by complaining they seize their chances to do harm; they insist they have received injuries in order to inflict them.

I won't keep you any longer — you don't need the sermon. Good character has this mark, among others: it makes its own judgments and sticks to them. Badness is fickle; it changes constantly — never into something better, only into something different.

Farewell.
Letter 48: On Quibbling as Unworthy of the Philosopher

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

That letter you wrote me on the road — as long as the road itself — I'll answer later. I need to go off by myself and work out what advice to give you. You, after all, thought long and hard before deciding to consult me; shouldn't I think longer and harder before answering? It takes more deliberation to settle a question than to raise one — especially when one answer suits you and a different one suits me.

Am I talking like an Epicurean again? The truth is that what's good for you is good for me. I'm no friend of yours unless everything that concerns you concerns me too. Friendship makes us partners in everything — there is no such thing as good luck or bad luck for one of us alone; we live in common. And no one can live happily who looks only at himself, who converts everything into a question of his own profit. You must live for others if you want to live for yourself. This fellowship — kept carefully and reverently, the fellowship that binds us to all human beings and holds that the human race has certain rights in common — does great service to that closer fellowship I was speaking of, friendship. A man who shares much with his fellow man will share everything with his friend.

And this, my excellent Lucilius, is what I'd rather have your subtle logicians teach me: how to help a friend, how to help a fellow human being — not how many senses the word "friend" can carry, or how many things "man" can mean. Look: Wisdom and Folly are lining up on opposite sides. Which do I join? Which side do you want me on? For one man, "friend" and "man" mean the same thing; for another, "friend" and "man" are worlds apart — the first wants a friend for his own advantage, the second wants to make himself an advantage to his friend. And what do you offer me? Word-twisting and syllable-splitting. Apparently, unless I can rig up some cunning premises and stitch a fallacy onto them by false deduction — some lie grown from a seed of truth — I'll never manage to tell what to pursue from what to avoid! I'm ashamed of us. Old men, facing a matter this serious, and we're playing games.

"'Mouse' is a syllable. A mouse eats cheese. Therefore, a syllable eats cheese." Suppose I can't untangle that one — what terrible danger does my ignorance put me in? What a fix I'll be in! No doubt I'd better watch out, or one of these days I'll be catching syllables in a mousetrap — or, if I get careless, a book will eat my cheese. Or wait — here's a shrewder syllogism still: "'Mouse' is a syllable. A syllable does not eat cheese. Therefore a mouse does not eat cheese." What childish nonsense. Is this what we knit our brows over? Is this why we let our beards grow long? Is this what we teach with faces sour and pale?

Do you want to know what philosophy actually offers the human race? Advice. Death is calling one man away; poverty is grinding another; a third is tormented by his neighbor's wealth — or by his own. This man dreads bad luck; that one wants to escape his good luck. Some are mistreated by men, some by the gods. And you're setting up parlor games for me? This is no time for jokes. You have been retained as counsel for suffering humanity. You have promised to help the shipwrecked, the captive, the sick, the poor, the man whose neck waits under the lifted axe. Where are you wandering off to? What are you doing?

The man you're playing word games with is afraid. Help him — take the noose from around his neck. From every side, hands are stretched out to you, begging; ruined lives, lives about to be ruined, plead for some kind of aid; men's hopes and men's resources hang on you. They ask you to pull them out of their restlessness; they ask you to show them, scattered and wandering as they are, the clear light of truth. Tell them what nature has made necessary and what superfluous. Tell them how simple her laws are — how pleasant and unobstructed life is for those who follow them, how bitter and tangled for those who trust opinion instead of nature.

I might grant your logic-games some value in easing men's burdens if you could first show me which part of the burden they will ease. Which of these games drives out lust? Which one even restrains it? I only wish they were merely useless. They are positively harmful. Whenever you like, I can show you plainly that a noble spirit gets impaired and weakened when it's tangled up in such subtleties. I'm ashamed to say what weapons these men hand to soldiers marching out to war against Fortune, and how wretchedly they equip them. Is this the road to the greatest good? Does philosophy get there by if-and-provided-that — by quibbles that would disgrace even a lawyer drafting contracts? For when you deliberately trap the man you're questioning, what are you doing but making him lose his case on a technicality? And yet — just as a judge can reinstate a litigant tricked out of his suit, so philosophy has reinstated these victims of quibbling to their former condition. Why do you men walk away from your own mighty promises? You swore to me, in the grandest language, that gold's glitter would dazzle my eyes no more than a sword's gleam; that I would trample, with magnificent steadiness, on everything all men crave and everything all men fear — and now you descend to the schoolteacher's ABC's? What is your answer?

  Is this the path to heaven?

Because that is exactly what philosophy promises me: that I will be made equal to God. That is the invitation I received; that is what I came for. Philosophy — keep your promise!

So, my dear Lucilius, pull back as far as you can from these exceptions and objections of the so-called philosophers. Frankness and simplicity are the right dress for true goodness. Even if you had many years left, you'd have to spend them thriftily to cover life's necessities. As it is, your time is scarce — and what madness it is to spend scarce time learning superfluous things!

Farewell.
Letter 49: On the Shortness of Life

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

A man is truly lazy and careless, my dear Lucilius, if he remembers a friend only when a landscape jogs the memory. And yet there are times when the old familiar places wake a sense of loss that had been stored away in the soul — not restoring dead memories, but rousing sleeping ones, the way the sight of a lost friend's favorite slave, or his cloak, or his house, sharpens the mourner's grief even after time has softened it.

And now — look — Campania, and above all Naples and your beloved Pompeii, have struck me with an astonishingly fresh longing for you. You stand in full view before my eyes. I am at the moment of parting from you all over again. I see you choking down your tears, fighting — and failing — against the feelings that surge up at the very instant you try to stop them. It's as if I lost you only a moment ago.

But what isn't "only a moment ago," once you start using your memory? Only a moment ago I was a boy sitting in the school of the philosopher Sotion; only a moment ago I began pleading cases in the courts; only a moment ago I lost the desire to plead them; only a moment ago I lost the ability. The flight of time is infinitely fast — and no one sees it more clearly than the man looking backward. While we're fixed on the present, we miss it: the rush is so smooth, so headlong, that it feels gentle. Do you want to know why? All past time is in the same place. It wears one face; it lies in one heap. Everything drops into the same abyss. Besides, a thing that is short in its entirety can't contain long stretches. The time we spend living is a point — no, less than a point. And nature has played a trick even on this infinitesimal point, dressing it up to look longer: she made one piece of it infancy, another childhood, another youth, another the long downslope, so to speak, from youth to old age, and old age itself another still. So many steps — for so short a climb!

It was only a moment ago that I saw you off on your journey. And yet that "moment ago" is a healthy share of a lifetime — a lifetime so brief, we should remind ourselves, that it will soon run out altogether. Time never used to seem this fast to me. Now its speed is beyond belief — maybe because I feel the finish-line moving toward me, or maybe because I've finally started paying attention and adding up my losses.

That's why it makes me all the angrier that some men spend the greater part of this time on superfluous things — time which, guard it as carefully as you like, isn't enough even for the necessary ones. Cicero declared that if his days were doubled, he still wouldn't have time to read the lyric poets. Put the logicians in the same bin — only their foolishness is more depressing. The lyric poets are at least openly frivolous; the logicians actually believe they're doing serious work. I don't deny that one should give logic a glance — but only a glance, a nod from the doorway, just enough to make sure you're not being tricked into thinking there's buried treasure in it.

Why torture yourself, why lose weight over some puzzle it's cleverer to scorn than to solve? A soldier at his ease, marching in peacetime, can hunt for trinkets along the road. But when the enemy is closing in from behind and the order comes to quicken the pace, necessity makes him throw away everything he picked up in the easy hours. I have no time to chase disputed inflections of words or to try my cleverness on them.

  Look at the clans assembling, the gates slammed shut,
  the weapons being whetted for the war.

I need a stout heart to hear this din of battle around me without flinching. And everyone would rightly call me mad if — while the old men and the women were hauling rocks for the fortifications, while armed young men inside the gates were waiting for the order to charge out, or demanding it, while enemy spears quivered in our gates and the very ground shuddered with tunnels and mines — if in the middle of all that I sat idle, posing dainty little puzzles like this one: "What you have not lost, you have. You have not lost horns. Therefore, you have horns" — or some other trick built on the same model of pure silliness. And yet you'd be just as right to call me mad if I spent my energy on that stuff now — because I am under siege right now. In the other case the danger would only be outside me, with a wall between me and the enemy. As it is, the deadly threats are in the room with me.

I have no time for such nonsense. There's an enormous piece of work in my hands. What am I to do? Death is on my trail, and life is running away from me. Teach me something that meets that. Bring it about that I stop fleeing from death, and that life stops fleeing from me. Give me courage against hardship; give me calm in the face of the unavoidable. Widen the narrow limits of the time I've been allotted. Teach me that the good in life doesn't hang on life's length but on the use we make of it — and that it is possible, in fact usual, for a man who has lived long to have lived too little. Say to me when I lie down to sleep: "You may not wake again." And when I wake: "You may never sleep again." Say to me when I walk out my door: "You may not come back." And when I come back: "You may never leave again." You are wrong if you think it's only at sea that the gap between life and death is paper-thin. The gap is exactly that narrow everywhere. Death doesn't show himself this close everywhere — but everywhere he is this close.

  The language of truth is simple.

So let's not tangle that language up. Nothing suits a soul aiming at great things less than this sly cleverness.

Farewell.
