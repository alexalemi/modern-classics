Letters 26–29

Letter 26: On Old Age and Death

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Just recently I told you I was within sight of old age. Now I'm afraid I've left old age behind me. Some other word belongs to my years now — or at least to my body — because "old age" means a time of life that is tired, not broken down. Count me among the worn out, the ones nearing the end.

Even so, I give myself thanks, with you as my witness. Age has done no damage to my mind, though I feel it in my body. Only my vices have grown senile, along with the organs that served them. My mind is strong, and delighted to have so little business with the body anymore. It has set down most of its load. It is alert. It even picks an argument with me about old age — insists that old age is its season of bloom. Let's take it at its word and let it make the most of what it has. It tells me to do some thinking: to sort out how much of this peace and moderation I owe to wisdom and how much simply to my age; to examine carefully what I can no longer do and what I no longer want to do. And if a power fails that was bound to end anyway — why complain? Why call that a loss?

"But," you say, "it is the worst loss there is — to wear out, to die off, or to put it bluntly, to melt away. We aren't cut down with a single blow; we're worn down, and every day shaves off a little more of our strength."

But is there any better ending than to glide into your own harbor while nature slips the cable? Not that there's anything painful about a sudden blow and an abrupt exit from existence — only that this other way out is gentle: a gradual withdrawal. As for me, I act as if the test were at hand — as if the day had come that will pronounce its verdict on all my years — and I watch over myself and talk to myself like this: "Everything we've shown so far, in word or deed, counts for nothing. It's all a small, deceptive down payment on our spirit, wrapped in a great deal of showmanship. I'll let death decide what progress I've really made. So I am preparing myself for that day, and not faintheartedly — the day when the stage tricks and the actor's makeup come off and I pass judgment on myself: am I merely reciting brave sentiments, or do I actually feel them? Were all those bold threats I hurled at Fortune a pretense and a performance? Set aside the world's opinion; it wavers forever and always votes both ways. Set aside the studies you've pursued all your life; death will deliver the final verdict in your case. I mean this: your debates and learned discussions, your maxims collected from the sayings of the wise, your cultured conversation — none of it proves the real strength of your soul. The most timid man alive can deliver a bold speech. What you have really done will show only when you draw your last breath. I accept the terms. I do not shrink from the verdict." That's what I say to myself — but consider it said to you as well. You are younger. What of it? Death doesn't count our years. You don't know where death is waiting for you, so be ready for it everywhere.

I was about to stop — my hand was already shaping the closing line — but the rites must still be performed and this letter's traveling money paid out. Assume I'm not telling you where I'll borrow the sum; you know whose account I draw on. Wait just a moment and I'll pay you out of my own purse; in the meantime, Epicurus will oblige me with this: "Rehearse death" — or, if you prefer the phrase, rehearse the "migration to heaven." The meaning is plain: it is a wonderful thing to learn thoroughly how to die. Maybe you think it pointless to learn a lesson you can use only once. But that is exactly why we ought to rehearse it. When we can never test whether we truly know a thing, we have to keep learning it. "Rehearse death." Whoever says that is telling us to rehearse freedom. The man who has learned how to die has unlearned how to be a slave. He stands above every outside power — or at least beyond it. What are prisons and chains and bars to him? His way out is always open. There is only one chain that keeps us shackled to life, and that is the love of life. We don't have to throw that chain off; we can wear it thin, so that when the moment demands it, nothing holds us back or hinders us from doing at once what someday we must do anyway.

Farewell.

Letter 27: On the Good Which Abides

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

"What's this," you say, "you're giving me advice? Have you already advised yourself? Already fixed your own faults? Is that why you have the spare time to reform everyone else?" No — I'm not so shameless as to set up as a doctor while I'm sick myself. What I'm doing is talking through the troubles we share and passing the medicine across, as if we were lying in the same hospital ward. So listen to me as you would if I were talking to myself. I'm letting you into my private thoughts; I'm having it out with myself, and merely using you as my excuse.

I keep crying out to myself: "Count your years, and you'll be ashamed to want and chase the same things you wanted as a boy. Make sure of this one thing before your dying day: let your faults die before you do. Away with those disordered pleasures that must be paid for so dearly — it isn't only the ones still ahead that hurt me, but the ones already come and gone. Crimes, even when they go undetected, don't let the anxiety end when the act does; guilty pleasures are the same — the regret outlasts them. They have no substance, no reliability; even when they do no harm, they vanish. Look around instead for some good that will last. And there is none — except what the soul discovers for itself, within itself. Virtue alone gives a joy that is permanent and secure. Even if something gets in its way, it's only a passing cloud, drifting beneath the sun but never winning against it."

When will it be your luck to reach that joy? You haven't been slow so far, but you need to quicken your pace. A great deal of work remains, and if you want it done, you must spend your own waking hours and your own effort on it. This is one job that can't be delegated. Other kinds of book-work accept outside help. Within our own lifetime there was a rich man named Calvisius Sabinus, who had the bank account of a millionaire and the brains of one. I never saw a man whose good luck was a greater offense against decency. His memory was so bad he would forget the name of Ulysses, or Achilles, or Priam — names we know as well as we know our own servants'. No senile old butler, the kind who can't get anyone's name right and has to invent them, ever botched the roll call of his master's tribesmen the way Sabinus botched the Trojan and Greek heroes. And still he wanted to seem learned. So he devised a shortcut to scholarship: he paid staggering prices for slaves — one to know Homer by heart, another to know Hesiod, and one apiece for the nine lyric poets. That he paid top price shouldn't surprise you; when he couldn't find them ready-made, he had them made to order. Once he'd assembled this retinue, he began making life miserable for his dinner guests. He kept the fellows stationed at the foot of his couch and asked them, again and again, for verses he could repeat — and then broke down in the middle of a word anyway.

Satellius Quadratus — a sponger on rich fools, therefore a flatterer of them, and therefore (the third quality follows the other two) a mocker of them — suggested that Sabinus hire scholars to pick up his dropped lines. When Sabinus mentioned that each slave had cost him a hundred thousand sesterces, Satellius replied: "You could have bought that many bookcases for less." But Sabinus held firmly to the opinion that whatever anyone in his household knew, he himself knew too. This same Satellius then urged him to take up wrestling — Sabinus, who was sickly, pale, and thin. "How can I?" said Sabinus. "I can barely stay alive as it is." "Don't say that, I beg you," said Satellius. "Look how many perfectly healthy slaves you have!"

A sound mind cannot be borrowed or bought. In fact, I suspect that even if sound minds were up for sale, they'd find no buyers. Depraved ones, though, are bought and sold every day.

But let me pay off my debt and say goodbye: "Poverty brought into line with the law of nature is great wealth." Epicurus says this over and over, in one form or another — but a thing that can never be learned too well can never be said too often. For some patients you need only write the prescription; for others you must force the dose down their throats.

Farewell.

Letter 28: On Travel as a Cure for Discontent

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Do you think this has happened to you alone? Are you actually surprised — as if it were news — that all that travel, all those changes of scenery, haven't shaken off the gloom and the weight on your mind? You must change the mind, not the sky. You can cross ocean after ocean; as our Virgil says,

  Lands and cities drop astern —

but your faults will travel with you wherever you go. Socrates said the same thing to a man with the same complaint: "Why are you surprised that globe-trotting doesn't help you, when you take yourself with you? The thing that drove you out the door is right at your heels." What pleasure is there in seeing new countries? What good is touring cities and famous sights? All that rushing around is wasted. You ask why running away doesn't help? Because you run away in your own company. You must set down the burdens of the mind; until you do, no place on earth will please you.

Consider that your present condition is like the prophetess Virgil describes — worked up, goaded into frenzy, filled with a spirit not her own:

  The priestess raves, and strains to shake
  the mighty god out of her breast.

You hurry from place to place to shake off a burden that only chafes more with the motion — the way a ship's cargo causes no trouble while it lies stowed, but the moment it shifts, it heels the vessel toward whichever side it has slid to. Everything you do counts against you; you hurt yourself by the very restlessness. You're jostling a sick man.

But remove that trouble, and every change of scene becomes a pleasure. Drive you to the ends of the earth, set you down in any corner of a savage country you like — and that place, however forbidding, will be a hospitable home to you. Who you are when you arrive matters more than where you arrive; that is why we should never chain the mind to any one place. Live with this conviction: "I was not born for one corner of the world; this whole universe is my country." If you saw that clearly, you would stop being surprised that moving to fresh scenery, after growing weary of the old, does you no good. The first place would have pleased you too, if you had believed every place was yours. As it is, you aren't traveling; you're drifting — driven along, trading one place for the next, when the thing you're looking for — living well — is available everywhere.

Can anywhere be as chaotic as the Forum? Even there you can live in peace, if you must. Though frankly, given a free choice, I would flee far from the very sight and neighborhood of the place. Just as pestilential districts wear down even the strongest constitution, so some places are unhealthy for a mind that is healthy but not yet quite sound — still convalescing. I disagree with the people who wade straight into the breakers, who welcome a stormy life and wrestle daily, gamely, with hardship. The wise man will endure all that, but he won't choose it; he would rather be at peace than at war. It does little good to have thrown out your own faults if you must now brawl with other people's. "But there were thirty tyrants standing over Socrates," someone says, "and they couldn't break his spirit." What does it matter how many masters a man has? Slavery has no plural. Whoever has scorned it is free — no matter how thick the mob of overlords around him.

Time to stop — but not before I've paid the toll. "To know your sin is the beginning of salvation." That strikes me as a noble saying of Epicurus. A man who doesn't know he is doing wrong has no wish to be corrected; you must catch yourself in the act before you can reform. Some people brag about their vices — and do you think anyone who counts his faults as accomplishments gives a single thought to curing them? So, as far as you can, put yourself on trial. Investigate yourself; gather the evidence against yourself. Play the accuser first, then the judge, and only last the advocate pleading for mercy. And now and then, be harsh with yourself.

Farewell.

Letter 29: On the Critical Condition of Marcellinus

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You've been asking about our friend Marcellinus — you want to know how he's getting along. He rarely visits me, for exactly one reason: he's afraid to hear the truth. For the moment he's in no danger of that, because you can't talk to a man unless he's willing to listen. That's why people have long debated whether Diogenes and the other Cynics — who handed out blunt honesty indiscriminately and gave advice to anyone who crossed their path — were right to work that way. What's the sense of scolding the deaf, or people mute from birth or illness?

"Why should I be stingy with words?" you answer. "They cost nothing. I can't know whether I'll help the particular man I advise, but I know I'll help someone if I advise many. I have to scatter this advice by the handful. Try often enough, and sooner or later you succeed."

That, my dear Lucilius, is exactly what I believe a great soul should not do. His influence gets watered down; it loses its force with the very people it might have straightened out, if it hadn't gone stale. An archer shouldn't hit the mark only sometimes; he should miss it only sometimes. Whatever works by chance is not an art. And wisdom is an art. It should have a definite aim: choose the students who will make progress, and withdraw from the ones it has come to see as hopeless — though not giving up on them too soon, and trying the drastic remedies at exactly the point where the case turns desperate.

As for our friend Marcellinus, I haven't lost hope yet. He can still be saved — but the helping hand must be offered soon. There is a real risk he'll pull his rescuer down with him: there's a native vigor in the man, though it's already bending toward vice. Still, I'll brave the danger and dare to show him his faults. He'll do what he always does — reach for his wit, the wit that can get a smile out of mourners. He'll turn the joke first on himself, then on me. He'll say everything I'm about to say before I can say it. He'll ridicule our philosophic systems; he'll accuse philosophers of taking handouts, keeping mistresses, and indulging their appetites. He'll point out one philosopher caught in adultery, another who haunts the taverns, another who hangs around court. He'll bring up Aristo, Marcus Lepidus's philosopher, who used to hold his discussions in his carriage — that being the time slot he'd set aside for producing his researches — so that Scaurus, asked what school Aristo belonged to, said: "Well, one thing's certain — he's not one of the Walking Philosophers." And Julius Graecinus, a distinguished man, asked his opinion on the same question, replied: "I can't tell you — I don't know what he does on foot," as though he'd been asked about a chariot fighter. Charlatans of that sort — men who would have done philosophy more credit by leaving her alone than by peddling her — are what Marcellinus will throw in my face.

But I've decided to put up with the taunts. He may stir my laughter; I may yet stir him to tears. And if he insists on joking, then I'll rejoice, so to speak, in the midst of sorrow, that he's been blessed with such a merry strain of madness. But that kind of merriment doesn't last. Watch such men and you'll see: within a short span they laugh too hard and they rage too hard. My plan is to approach him and show him how much more he was worth back when so many thought him worth less. Even if I don't root out his faults, I'll put a check on them. They won't stop, but they'll pause — and perhaps they'll even stop, if pausing becomes a habit. That is nothing to sneer at: for the gravely ill, a good remission stands in for health.

So while I get myself ready for Marcellinus, you — who can act, who understand where you started and how far you've come, and can therefore guess how far there is still to go — put your character in order. Rouse your courage. Stand firm against the things that have frightened you. Don't count the people who scare you. You'd call a man a fool for fearing a crowd at a spot where only one person can pass at a time, wouldn't you? Just so: not many can actually reach you to kill you, however many threaten it. Nature has arranged it this way — just as only one person gave you life, only one will take it away.

If you had any shame, you'd have let me off this last installment. But no — I won't be stingy at the finish either; I'll clear my debts to the last penny and force on you what I still owe: "I have never wished to please the crowd; for what I know, they do not approve, and what they approve, I do not know." "Who said that?" you ask — as if you didn't know whose payroll I'm on. Epicurus. But every school will shout the same watchword at you — Peripatetic, Academic, Stoic, Cynic. If virtue delights you, how can you delight the crowd? Popular approval has to be won by trickery: you must make yourself just like them, and they will withhold their applause unless they recognize you as one of their own. And what you think of yourself matters far more than what others think of you. The favor of low men can be bought only by low means. So what will that celebrated philosophy do for you — the one we praise, the one we're told outranks every art and every possession? This: it will make you prefer pleasing yourself to pleasing the public. It will make you weigh men's judgments, not merely count them. It will make you live without fear of gods or men. It will make you either conquer your troubles or end them. Otherwise — if I see you carried along on popular applause, if your entrance is met with a roar of cheering and clapping, honors fit for an actor, if the whole city, women and children included, sings your praises — how could I help pitying you? I know what road leads to that kind of popularity.

Farewell.
