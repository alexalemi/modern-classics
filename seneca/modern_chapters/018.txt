Letters 67–69

Letter 67: On Ill-health and Endurance of Suffering

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Let me open with small talk: spring is finally showing itself. But even as it rounds the corner toward summer, when you'd expect real heat, it has stayed cool, and you can't trust it yet — it keeps sliding back into winter. Want proof of how unreliable it still is? I don't dare get into a genuinely cold bath; even now I take the chill off the water first. "That's no way to build up your tolerance for heat or cold," you'll say. True enough, dear Lucilius, but at my age a man's own natural chill is all the cold he needs. I can barely thaw out in midsummer. So I spend most of my time bundled up, and I actually thank old age for pinning me to my bed. Why shouldn't I thank it? Everything I ought not to want to do, I no longer can do. Most of my conversation these days is with books. Whenever your letters arrive, I feel as if I'm with you — as if I'm about to speak my reply instead of writing it. So let's take up this question of yours together, as if we were talking face to face.

You ask me whether every good is desirable. You put it this way: "If it's a good to be brave under torture, to walk to the stake with a steady heart, to endure sickness patiently, then it follows that these things are desirable. But I don't see that any of them is worth praying for. At least I've never heard of a man paying off a vow of thanks because he'd been cut to ribbons by the whip, or twisted out of shape by gout, or stretched taller on the rack." My dear Lucilius, you have to pull these cases apart; do that, and you'll see there is something in them worth wanting. I'd prefer to be spared torture; but if the time comes when it must be endured, then I'll want to bear it with bravery, honor, and courage. Of course I'd prefer that war never come; but if war does come, I'll want to bear the wounds, the hunger, and everything else war's necessity brings — and bear them nobly. I'm not so crazy as to wish for sickness; but if I must be sick, I'll want to do nothing weak, nothing unworthy of a man. The conclusion isn't that hardships are desirable. It's that virtue is desirable — and virtue is what lets us endure hardships patiently.

Some members of our own school think that of all these qualities, brave endurance is not actually desirable — not to be rejected either, but not to be prayed for — because we should ask heaven only for goods that are pure, peaceful, and out of trouble's reach. Personally, I disagree. Why? First, because nothing can be good without also being desirable. Second, because if virtue is desirable, and nothing good is without virtue, then everything good is desirable. Third, because brave endurance even under torture is desirable in itself. Here let me ask you: isn't bravery desirable? And yet bravery scorns danger and dares it. The most beautiful, most admirable thing about bravery is that it doesn't flinch from the stake, that it advances to meet its wounds, that sometimes it doesn't even dodge the spear but takes it chest-first. If bravery is desirable, then patiently enduring torture is desirable too, for that is a part of bravery. Just sort these things out, as I've suggested, and nothing will lead you astray. For it's not mere endurance of torture that is desirable — it's brave endurance. That "brave" is what I desire; and that is virtue.

"But who ever prayed for such a thing?" you say. Some prayers are open and explicit, when we ask for things one by one; others are folded up, when many requests hide under a single heading. For example: I pray for an honorable life. Now an honorable life is made up of many kinds of action. It may include the chest Regulus was locked into, or the wound Cato tore open with his own hands, or the exile of Rutilius, or the cup of poison that moved Socrates from prison to heaven. So in praying for an honorable life, I have also prayed for the things without which, on some days, no life can be honorable.

  Three times and four times blessed were the men
  who died before their parents' eyes, beneath
  the high walls of Troy!

What difference does it make whether you pray for this outcome for someone, or admit that it was desirable when it happened? Decius sacrificed himself for the state: he put spurs to his horse and charged into the middle of the enemy, hunting for death. The second Decius, matching his father's courage, repeated the words that had become sacred, already a household prayer, and crashed into the thickest of the fighting, worried about only one thing — that his sacrifice bring a good omen with it — and counting a noble death as something to be desired. Do you doubt, then, that it is best to die gloriously, in the act of doing something brave? When a man endures torture bravely, he is using every virtue at once. Endurance may be the one out front, the most visible; but bravery is there too — endurance and resignation and patient suffering are its branches. Foresight is there, because without foresight no plan gets made, and it is foresight that advises us to bear as bravely as we can what we cannot avoid. Steadfastness is there, which can't be pushed off its ground, which no force can wrench away from its purpose. The whole inseparable company of the virtues is there. Every honorable act is the work of a single virtue, but it is done with the approval of the entire council. And whatever all the virtues approve — even if one alone seems to have done the work — is desirable.

What — do you think the only desirable things are the ones that arrive amid pleasure and ease, the ones we decorate our doorways to welcome? Some goods have grim faces. Some prayers are offered not by a cheering crowd, but by a crowd that bows down in reverence and awe. Don't you think that is how Regulus prayed when he prayed to reach Carthage? Put on a hero's courage, and step back for a moment from the opinions of ordinary men. Get a true picture of virtue — a thing of surpassing beauty and grandeur, an image we worship not with incense and garlands but with sweat and blood. Look at Marcus Cato, laying his clean hands on that sacred breast and tearing open the wounds that hadn't gone deep enough to kill him. Which will you say to him — "I'm sorry for you" and "I hope it all turns out as you wish"? Or "Good fortune in your undertaking!"?

This brings to mind our friend Demetrius, who calls an easy life untroubled by any attack of Fortune a "Dead Sea." If you have nothing to stir you up, nothing to rouse you to action, nothing to test your resolve with threats and assaults; if you lie back in unshaken comfort — that isn't tranquility. That's just a flat calm. The Stoic Attalus used to say: "I would rather Fortune kept me in her camp than in the lap of luxury. If I'm tortured but bear it bravely, all is well. If I die, but die bravely, that is well too." Listen to Epicurus, and he'll tell you it's actually pleasant. I myself will never hang so soft a word on an act so honorable and austere. If I go to the stake, I'll go unbeaten. Why shouldn't I count that desirable — not because the fire burns me, but because it does not conquer me? Nothing is more excellent or more beautiful than virtue; whatever we do at her command is both good and desirable.

Farewell.

Letter 68: On Wisdom and Retirement

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I agree with your plan: retire, and hide yourself in rest. But hide the retirement itself as well. In doing this you'll be following the Stoics' example, if not their official teaching — though in fact you'll be following the teaching too, so you can satisfy both yourself and any Stoic you like. We Stoics don't push men into public life in every case, at all times, with no exceptions. Besides, once we have assigned our wise man the public sphere that's actually worthy of him — namely, the universe — he is never outside public life, even when he withdraws. If anything, he has quit one small corner of it and crossed over into greater, wider territory; set among the heavens, he understands how low a seat he occupied when he climbed onto the magistrate's chair or the judge's bench. Take this to heart: the wise man is never more active than when things divine as well as things human have come into his view.

Now back to the advice I started to give you: keep your retirement quiet. There's no need to pin a sign on yourself reading "Philosopher and Quietist." Give your project some other name — call it poor health, physical weakness, or plain laziness. Bragging about retirement is just vanity in hiding. Certain animals conceal their dens by scuffing up the tracks around the entrance. You should do the same; otherwise there will always be somebody following your footprints. Many people walk right past what's in plain sight and go poking after what's hidden and locked away. A locked room invites the thief. What lies in the open looks cheap; the housebreaker passes by what's on display. That's the way of the world, and the way of every ignorant man: they itch to break in on secrets. So it's best not to advertise your retirement — but making too much of your concealment, of your withdrawal from human sight, is itself a kind of advertising. So-and-so has gone into seclusion at Tarentum; that fellow has shut himself up at Naples; this one hasn't crossed his own threshold in years. To advertise your retirement is to collect a crowd. When you withdraw from the world, your business is to talk with yourself — not to have men talk about you.

And what should you talk about? Do exactly what people love to do when they gossip about the neighbors: speak ill of yourself when you're by yourself. You'll get used to telling the truth — and to hearing it. Above all, dwell on whatever you come to feel is your greatest weakness. Every man knows his own body's defects best. That's why one man relieves his stomach by vomiting, another props his up with frequent small meals, another drains and purges himself with regular fasting. Men with painful feet give up wine, or the baths. Careless about everything else, men go out of their way to treat the ailment that keeps coming back. It's the same with our souls: they have certain parts on the sick-list, so to speak, and it's to those parts that the cure must be applied.

What am I doing with my own leisure, then? I'm treating my own sores. If I showed you a swollen foot, an inflamed hand, or the shriveled tendons of a withered leg, you'd give me leave to lie quiet in one place and put compresses on the damaged limb. My trouble is bigger than any of those, and I can't show it to you: the abscess, the ulcer, is deep in my chest. Please, please — don't praise me, don't say, "What a great man! He has learned to despise it all; he has condemned the madness of men's lives and made his escape!" I have condemned nothing except myself. There's no reason to come to me hoping to make progress. You're mistaken if you expect any help from this quarter: no physician lives here, only a patient. I'd rather you said, on your way out: "I used to think him a happy man and a learned one, and I came with my ears pricked to listen. I've been cheated. I saw nothing, heard nothing that I wanted and would come back for." If you feel that way, and say so, some progress has been made. I'd rather you pardoned my retirement than envied it.

"So it's retirement you're recommending, Seneca? You'll be falling back on Epicurus's maxims next!" I do recommend retirement — but only so you can use it for greater and more beautiful work than what you're giving up. Knocking at the proud doors of the powerful, keeping alphabetical lists of childless old men with money, wielding the biggest influence in public life — that kind of power earns you hatred, doesn't last, and, if you price it honestly, is cheap stuff. One man will be far ahead of me in political clout, another in an officer's pay and the rank that comes with it, another in the size of his crowd of clients. It's worth being beaten by all of them, provided I myself can beat Fortune. And in a crowd I'm no match for her — she has the bigger following.

If only you had set your mind on this course years ago! If only we weren't discussing the happy life within plain sight of death! But even now, no delays. Experience has now confirmed what reason should have convinced us of long ago: that a great many things are useless, and many are hostile. Let's do what travelers do when they've started late and want to make up time by speed — let's use the spur. Our time of life is actually the best possible for this work: the boiling and foaming stage is over. The faults that raged unchecked in the first hot blast of youth have weakened; it will take only a little more effort to put them out.

"And when," you ask, "will learning do you any good, if you only learn it on your way out the door — and what good will it do?" Just this good: I shall leave a better man. But don't imagine any age is better suited to a sound mind than the age that has conquered itself through long testing — that has tamed its passions through long and repeated regret for its mistakes, and so arrived at health. This is precisely the season for acquiring that good. Whoever reaches wisdom in old age has reached it by means of his years.

Farewell.

Letter 69: On Rest and Restlessness

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I don't like you changing headquarters and scurrying from place to place. My first reason: all this flitting around is the sign of an unsteady spirit. And the spirit can never settle into unity through retirement until it has stopped its wandering and its sightseeing. To hold your spirit in check, you must first stop the body's runaway flight. My second reason: the cures that help most are the ones that aren't interrupted. Don't let anything break in on your quiet, or on the forgetting to which you've consigned your old life. Give your eyes time to unlearn what they've seen, and your ears time to get used to healthier words. Every time you go out, even just in passing from one place to the next, you'll run into things that wake your old cravings. A man trying to get over an old love must avoid every reminder of the person he once adored — nothing regrows as easily as love — and in the same way, a man trying to lay down his desire for all the things he once craved so hotly must turn both eyes and ears away from what he has left behind. The passions are quick to counterattack; wherever they turn, they'll spot something in front of them worth their attention. There is no evil that doesn't offer a signing bonus. Greed promises money; luxury, a whole menu of pleasures; ambition, the purple robe and the applause, and the influence applause brings, and everything influence can do. Vices recruit you with wages; in the life I'm describing, you must work unpaid. A whole lifetime is barely enough to break our vices and bring them under the yoke, swollen as they are from long indulgence — much less if we chop our short span into pieces with interruptions. Even constant, unbroken attention can barely carry a single undertaking through to the finish.

If you'll take my advice, ponder and practice this: how to welcome death — or even, if circumstances call for it, to invite it. It makes no difference whether death comes to us or we go to death. Convince yourself that the ignorant are wrong when they say, "It is a beautiful thing to die one's own death." There is no man who doesn't die his own death. And here is a further thought to chew on: no one dies except on his own day. You are throwing away none of your own time — for what you leave behind was never yours.

Farewell.
