Letters 14–16

Letter 14: On the Reasons for Withdrawing from the World

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I'll admit it: we are all born with an affection for our own body. I'll admit we've been made its guardians, and I'm not saying it should get no consideration at all. What I'm saying is that we must not be its slaves. A man who makes his body his master will have many masters — the man who is over-anxious on its behalf, who runs every decision through the body first. We should act not as if the body were the thing we live for, but as if it were the thing we can't live without. Loving it too much rattles us with fears, loads us with worries, and leaves us open to insults. Virtue comes cheap to the man who prices his body too high. Care for it, by all means, and carefully. But be ready, when reason, self-respect, or duty demands the sacrifice, to hand it over even to the flames.

Still, as far as we can, let's avoid discomfort as well as danger, and pull back onto safe ground by thinking constantly about how to keep the things we fear at a distance. Unless I'm mistaken, they come in three classes: we fear poverty, we fear sickness, and we fear what stronger men can do to us by violence. Of these, nothing shakes us like the threat that hangs over us from another man's power, because it arrives with shouting and uproar. The natural evils I mentioned — poverty and sickness — steal in silently; they bring no terror for the eye or the ear. The other kind of evil comes as a huge parade. It travels with an escort of swords and fire and chains, and a pack of beasts kept ready to be loosed on human flesh. Picture, under this heading, the prison, the cross, the rack, the hook, the stake driven through a man's body until it comes out at his throat. Think of limbs torn apart by chariots driven in opposite directions, of the shirt woven and smeared with pitch to burn on its wearer, and of every other device cruelty has invented besides. No wonder, then, that this fate is our greatest terror: it comes in so many shapes, and its equipment is terrifying. The torturer accomplishes more the more instruments he lays out — the display breaks men who could have withstood the pain itself. In the same way, of all the forces that grip and master our minds, the most effective are the ones with something to show. The other plagues are no less serious — I mean hunger, thirst, ulcers of the stomach, fever burning us up from within — but they are hidden. They have nothing to brandish and nothing to announce them. These others, like great armies, win by sheer display of force.

So let's make sure we give no offense. Sometimes it's the people we need to fear; sometimes, if the state is run so that most business goes through the Senate, its influential men; and sometimes individuals handed power by the people and used against the people. Keeping the friendship of all such men is heavy work; it's enough not to make enemies of them. So the wise man will never provoke the anger of those in power — no, he will steer clear of it, exactly as he'd steer clear of a storm at sea. When you traveled to Sicily, you crossed the Straits. The reckless pilot laughed off the blustering south wind — the wind that roughens the Sicilian sea and churns it into chop — and made not for the shore on the left but for the strand hard by the waters where Charybdis throws the sea into confusion. Your more careful pilot asks the locals about the tides and what the clouds mean, and holds his course far from that stretch infamous for its whirlpools. The wise man does the same. He avoids the powerful man who can hurt him — while making a point of not seeming to avoid him. A large part of staying safe is not visibly seeking safety; for what we avoid, we condemn.

Next, let's look around for ways to protect ourselves from the mob. First of all, want nothing they want; rivalry breeds fights. Second, own nothing that could be snatched from you at great profit to a schemer — carry as little loot on your person as possible. Nobody sheds human blood for its own sake, or almost nobody; more murderers calculate their profits than vent their hatred. If your hands are empty, the highwayman passes you by; even on an infested road, the poor travel in peace. Then remember the old rule and take special care to avoid three things: hatred, envy, and contempt. And only wisdom can show you how. The balance is hard to strike: in dodging envy we can slide into contempt, and while declining to trample others we can make them think we're safe to trample. The power to frighten has left many men frightened. So withdraw on every side — being scorned is as harmful as being admired.

That's why you must take refuge in philosophy. In the eyes of good people — and even of the moderately bad — this pursuit is a kind of sacred emblem. Courtroom eloquence, or anything else that plays to the crowd, makes enemies; philosophy is peaceful and minds her own business. She cannot be scorned; every profession honors her, down to the lowest. Evil will never grow so strong, and good character never be so conspired against, that the name of philosophy stops being holy.

Philosophy itself, though, should be practiced with calm and moderation. "Oh really?" you shoot back. "Do you call the philosophy of Marcus Cato moderate? Cato's voice tried to stop a civil war. Cato stepped between the swords of maddened warlords. When some attacked Pompey and others attacked Caesar, Cato took on both sides at once!" Fair enough — but you can question whether a wise man should have entered public life at all just then. "What are you doing, Marcus Cato? Freedom isn't the question anymore; freedom was wrecked long ago. The question is whether Caesar or Pompey owns the state — and what is that dispute to you? Neither side is yours. A tyrant is being chosen. What does it matter to you which man wins? The better man may win, but the winner is bound to be the worse man." I've cited Cato's final act; but even in earlier years the times didn't allow a wise man to step into that looting of the state. What could Cato do but raise his voice and speak words that went nowhere? One day he was "hustled" — mobbed, spat on, dragged from the forum, marked for exile; another day he was marched from the senate-house straight to prison.

Whether the wise man should give his attention to politics at all, we'll consider later. In the meantime, I ask you to consider those Stoics who, shut out of public life, withdrew into privacy — and there set about improving human existence and framing laws for the human race, without ever offending the men in power. The wise man won't upset the customs of the people, and he won't draw the crowd's eye by some novel way of living.

"So what? Is a man who follows this plan guaranteed to be safe?" I can no more guarantee that than I can guarantee health to a man who lives with moderation — though moderation does, in fact, produce health. Ships sometimes sink in the harbor; what do you think happens on the open sea? And how much more exposed would this man be, busy with many projects, when even his leisure isn't secure! Innocent people sometimes perish — who denies it? — but the guilty perish more often. A soldier's skill isn't disproved when the blow comes through his armor. And finally: the wise man weighs the reason for everything he does, not the results. The beginning is in our power; Fortune decides the outcome — but I do not grant her the right to pass sentence on me. "But she can inflict a measure of suffering and trouble." The highwayman who kills you isn't passing sentence.

Now you're stretching out your hand for the daily gift. Golden indeed is the gift I'll load you with today — and since we've mentioned gold, let me tell you how to get more use and joy out of it. "He who needs riches least, enjoys riches most." "Author's name, please!" you say. Just to show you how generous I am, I've made it my policy to praise the sayings of the other schools: this one belongs to Epicurus, or Metrodorus, or someone else from that workshop. But what difference does it make who said it? It was said for everyone. Whoever craves riches fears for them, and no one enjoys a blessing that keeps him anxious. He's always trying to add a little more; and while he puzzles over growing his wealth, he forgets how to use it. He collects on his accounts, wears out the pavement of the forum, flips through his ledger — he stops being the master and becomes the bookkeeper.

Farewell.
Letter 15: On Brawn and Brains

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

The old Romans had a custom that survived into my own lifetime: they would open a letter with the words, "If you are well, it is well; I also am well." We would do better to write: "If you are studying philosophy, it is well." Because that is what being well actually means. Without philosophy the mind is sick — and the body, however powerful, is strong only the way a madman's is strong. So this is the health to cultivate first; the other kind comes second, and it will cost you little effort — if all you want is to be physically fit. For it is foolish, my dear Lucilius, and thoroughly unbecoming in an educated man, to spend your days building muscle, broadening your shoulders, and expanding your lungs. Even if the heavy feeding pays off and your bulk piles up, you will never match a first-class bull for strength or for weight. Meanwhile the load of food smothers the soul and slows it down. So ration the flesh as much as you can, and give the spirit free play. Devote yourself to that regimen and the inconveniences pile up. First come the workouts, where the effort drains your vital energy and leaves it unfit for the strain of serious study. Then heavy eating dulls the mind's edge. On top of that, you take orders from slaves of the worst stamp — men who divide their day between the oil-flask and the wine-jug, who count the day a success if they've worked up a good sweat and then poured in, to replace what they lost, huge drinks that sink in all the deeper on an empty stomach. Drinking and sweating — it's the life of a heartburn patient!

There are short, simple exercises that tire the body quickly and so save time — and time is something we ought to keep strict account of. Running; swinging weights; and jumping, high or long, or the kind I might politely call "the Priest's dance," or, less politely, "the laundryman's stomp." Pick any one of them and you'll find it plain and easy. But whatever you do, come back quickly from body to mind. The mind is the thing to exercise day and night: it feeds on moderate labor, and this workout is stopped by no cold weather, no hot weather, not even old age. Cultivate the good that improves with the years. I'm not ordering you to hang forever over your books and your writing tablets; the mind needs a break — but the kind of break that loosens it without unstringing it. Riding in a litter jostles the body and doesn't interrupt study: you can read, dictate, talk, or listen. Walking doesn't prevent any of these either.

Nor should you sneer at voice training — though I forbid you the drills, running your voice up and down through scales and set intonations. What next — lessons in walking? Let in the sort of coach that hunger has taught new tricks and you'll have someone regulating your stride, watching every mouthful you eat, and going exactly as far as your patience and your faith in him encourage his nerve to go. "So what, then?" you'll ask. "Is my voice supposed to start out shouting, straining my lungs to the limit?" No. The natural thing is for it to build by easy stages — even people quarreling start in ordinary conversational tones and work up to screaming at the top of their lungs. No speaker opens with "Citizens, help me!" So whenever your spirit prompts you, raise a racket, now louder, now softer, as your voice and your spirit suggest. Then, when you rein it in and call it back down, let it come down gently, not collapse; let it trail off in a middle register, not break off in the raw rustic way, straight down from its ranting. The point isn't for us to give the voice exercise; it's for the voice to give us exercise.

There — I've relieved you of no small nuisance. And I'll throw in one small bonus gift — Greek, this time. Here's the proverb, and it's an excellent one: "The fool's life is empty of gratitude and full of fears: its course lies wholly toward the future." "Who said that?" you ask. The same man as before. And what life do you suppose he means by the fool's life? That of Baba and Isio, the famous idiots? No — he means our own. Blind desire hurls us into ventures that will hurt us and will certainly never satisfy us; if anything could satisfy us, it would have done so long ago. We never stop to think how pleasant it is to ask for nothing, how noble it is to be content and not depend on Fortune. So keep reminding yourself, Lucilius, how many ambitions you have already attained. When you see how many people are ahead of you, count how many are behind. If you want to thank the gods and be grateful for your past life, contemplate how many men you have outrun. But what do the others have to do with it? You have outrun yourself.

Set a limit you wouldn't even want to pass, if you had the power. And then away with all these treacherous goods! They look better to the people hoping for them than to the people who have them. If there were anything solid in them, they would sooner or later fill you up; as it is, they only make the drinker thirstier. Away with the showpieces! As for what the future's uncertain lot has in store — why should I beg Fortune to give, rather than command myself not to crave? And why should I crave? Should I pile up winnings and forget how flimsy a man's lot is? What would I be toiling for? Look: today is the last day — and if not the last, then near it.

Farewell.
Letter 16: On Philosophy, the Guide of Life

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

It's clear to you by now, Lucilius, I'm sure, that no one can live a happy life — or even a bearable one — without the study of wisdom. You know, too, that the happy life arrives when our wisdom is brought to completion, but that life is at least endurable the moment wisdom is begun. Clear as this is, though, it has to be strengthened and driven deeper by daily reflection. Keeping the resolutions you've already made matters more than making noble new ones. You must persevere, adding strength by continuous study, until a good inclination becomes a good settled purpose. So you can spare me the long speeches and protestations: I know you've made great progress. I understand the feelings behind your words; they aren't faked or dressed up. Still, I'll tell you frankly what I think: I have hopes for you, but not yet full confidence. And I want you to take the same attitude toward yourself — there's no reason to trust yourself too quickly and easily. Examine yourself; scrutinize yourself; watch yourself from different angles. And check, before anything else, whether the progress you've made is in philosophy or merely in life. Philosophy is not a trick for the crowd; it isn't built for show. It's a matter of facts, not words. It isn't taken up so the day will pass amusingly, or so leisure won't bore us. It molds and builds the soul; it orders our life, guides our conduct, shows us what to do and what to leave undone; it sits at the helm and holds the course while we waver in the swells. Without it, no one can live fearless, no one can live at peace. Every hour, countless things happen that call for advice — and philosophy is where that advice must be sought.

Someone may say: "What good is philosophy to me if Fate exists? What good, if God rules the universe? What good, if Chance governs everything? What's already determined can't be changed, and what's undetermined can't be planned for: either God has forestalled my plans and decided what I'm to do, or Fortune leaves my plans no room to play." Whether the truth, Lucilius, lies in one of these views or in all of them, we must be philosophers. Whether Fate binds us with an unbreakable law, or God as arbiter of the universe has arranged everything, or Chance kicks human affairs around without method, philosophy must be our defense. She will encourage us to obey God cheerfully and Fortune defiantly; she will teach us to follow God and to endure Chance. But this isn't the moment to be pulled into the debate over what's in our own control — whether foreknowledge rules everything, or a chain of fated events drags us along in its grip, or the sudden and unexpected lord it over us like tyrants. I come back to my warning and my plea: don't let the impulse of your spirit weaken and grow cold. Hold onto it, and plant it firm, so that what is now an impulse becomes a habit of the mind.

If I know you at all, you've been scanning this letter from the first line to find out what little contribution it carries. Sift it and you'll find it. And don't marvel at any genius of mine — so far I'm lavish only with other men's property. Though why do I say "other men's"? Whatever anyone has said well is mine. This, too, is a saying of Epicurus: "If you live according to nature, you will never be poor; if you live according to opinion, you will never be rich." Nature's wants are slight; opinion's demands are bottomless. Suppose the property of many millionaires were heaped in your lap. Suppose fortune carried you far beyond any private income — decked you with gold, dressed you in purple, and brought you to such a pitch of luxury and wealth that you could bury the earth under your marble floors: that you didn't merely own riches, but walked on them. Add statues, paintings, and whatever any art has devised to feed luxury. All these things will teach you is to crave still bigger ones.

Natural desires have a limit; desires born of false opinion have no stopping point — the false has no boundary. When you travel a road, there's an end somewhere; wander off it, and you can stray forever. So call your steps back from empty things, and when you want to know whether what you're chasing rests on natural desire or on a blind one, test whether it can stop anywhere. If you've gone far and there is always a farther goal in view, you can be sure the thing is contrary to nature.

Farewell.
