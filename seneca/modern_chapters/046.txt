Letter 108

Letter 108: On the Approaches to Philosophy

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

The question you ask belongs to that class of subjects where the knowing is the whole point — we want the knowledge for its own sake. Still, because it does concern us, you are in a hurry. You are not willing to wait for the books I am arranging for you at this very moment, the ones that cover the whole field of moral philosophy. I will send them right away. But first let me write to you about how this eagerness to learn — and I can see you are on fire with it — ought to be managed, so it does not trip over its own feet. Knowledge is not to be grabbed at random, and subjects should not be gulped down wholesale in a greedy rush; you arrive at the whole by mastering the parts. The load should be fitted to your strength; do not shoulder more than you can properly carry. Take in not everything you want, but everything you can hold. Just get yourself a sound mind, and then you will be able to hold everything you want. The more a mind receives, the more it expands.

This, I remember, was the advice Attalus gave me back in the days when I practically laid siege to his classroom — first to arrive, last to leave. Even while he paced up and down, I would draw him into one discussion after another; for he did not merely make himself available to his students, he came out to meet them halfway. His rule was: "Teacher and student should share a single purpose — the one aiming to help, the other aiming to improve." Whoever studies under a philosopher should carry home one good thing every day: he should return each evening a sounder man, or at least on the way to becoming one. And he will, because it is one of philosophy's powers to help not only those who study her but even those who just spend time in her company. A man who walks in the sun gets a tan, whether or not that was the plan. A man who lingers in a perfume shop, even briefly, carries the scent out with him. And a man who keeps a philosopher's company is bound to pick up some benefit, even if he is careless about it. Note the word: careless — not defiant.

"What's this?" you say. "Don't we all know men who sat at a philosopher's feet for years without picking up the faintest tint of wisdom?" Of course I know such men — very persistent gentlemen, too, who really stuck at it. I don't call them students of the wise; I call them squatters. Some of them come to hear, not to learn, just as we are drawn to the theater to please our ears with a speech, a song, or a play. This crowd, you will find, makes up a large share of any philosopher's audience — people who treat the lecture hall as a comfortable place to park their leisure. They are not there to drop any of their faults, or to pick up a rule of life to measure their character against; they only want to give their ears a treat. Some even arrive with notebooks — not to take down the substance, but the phrases, so they can repeat them later to others with as little profit as they got themselves. Others are swept up by the ringing sentences and mirror the speaker's feelings with eager faces and eager minds — exactly like those Phrygian priests who work themselves into a frenzy on cue the moment the flute begins to play. But the true listener is seized and stirred by the beauty of the subject itself, not by the jingle of empty words. When some brave word is flung in the face of death, or some defiant jab at Fortune, we take delight and itch to act at once on what we have heard. Men are moved by such words, and they become what the words command — if only the impression stays in the mind, and if the crowd, that great discourager of honorable things, does not immediately ambush the noble impulse. Only a few manage to carry home the state of mind they were inspired with. Yet it is easy to rouse a listener to crave what is right, because Nature has laid the foundations and planted the seeds of virtue in every one of us. We are all born with these advantages; add the spark, and the good in us stirs as if it had been let out of chains. Haven't you noticed how the theater roars whenever some line is spoken whose truth we all recognize and confirm with one voice?

  The poor man lacks much; the greedy man lacks everything.

  The greedy man does good to no one — and most harm
  Of all to himself.

At verses like these your meanest miser claps and cheers, delighted to hear his own sins abused. Now imagine how much stronger the effect when such things come from a philosopher's mouth — when he weaves verses in among his wholesome teachings so they sink deeper into a beginner's mind. Cleanthes used to say: "Just as our breath makes a louder sound when it is driven through the long narrow tube of a trumpet and escapes through the flared opening at the end, so the tight constraints of verse make our meaning ring out clearer." The very same words are received more carelessly and strike us less when spoken in prose; but add meter — compress a noble idea into regular rhythm — and the selfsame thought comes hurtling at you with all its force. We talk endlessly about despising money; we give the longest speeches urging mankind to believe that true riches live in the mind, not in the bank account, and that the man who fits himself to slender means and makes himself wealthy on a little is the man who is truly rich. But our minds are struck harder by one verse like this:

  He needs but little who desires but little.

or:

  He has his wish, whose wish is only for
  What is enough.

When we hear words like these, we are driven toward admitting the truth.

Even men who think nothing is ever enough will marvel and applaud at such lines, and swear eternal hatred against money. When you see them in that mood, strike home. Keep after them, press this duty on them, and drop the double meanings, the syllogisms, the hair-splitting, and all the other sideshows of pointless cleverness. Preach against greed, preach against luxury; and when you see that you are making progress and getting through to your hearers, press harder still. You cannot imagine how much good a speech of that kind can do when you are bent on curing your listeners and wholly devoted to their best interests. A young mind is easily won over to love what is honorable and upright; truth, given a decent advocate, lays strong hands on those who can still be taught — the ones only lightly spoiled.

I can tell you, when I used to hear Attalus denouncing sin, error, and the evils of life, I often felt pity for the whole human race, and I looked on Attalus as a noble and majestic being, standing above our mortal height. He called himself a king; to me he was more than a king, because he was qualified to pass judgment on kings. And when he began to praise poverty, and to show what a useless and dangerous burden everything is that goes beyond our need, I often wanted to walk out of that lecture hall a poor man. Whenever he lashed our pleasure-chasing lives and praised a clean body, a modest table, and a mind free of unnecessary pleasures — never mind unlawful ones — I felt the urge to put limits on my food and drink. And some of those habits have stayed with me, Lucilius. For I had laid out my whole life in a set of grand resolutions; and later, when I returned to a citizen's duties, I did keep a few of them. That is why I have sworn off oysters and mushrooms forever: they are not food at all, but relishes designed to bully a full stomach into eating more — the darlings of gourmands who stuff themselves past what they can digest: down it goes quickly, and up it comes quickly! That is why, all my life, I have avoided perfume: the best scent for the body is no scent at all. That is why my stomach is a stranger to wine. That is why, all my life, I have shunned the baths, convinced that stewing the body and sweating it thin is as useless as it is soft. Other resolutions I have broken — but broken in such a way that where I gave up total abstinence, I have kept to a limit that sits right next door to abstinence. Perhaps that is even harder; for some things it is easier for the will to cut off completely than to enjoy in moderation.

Since I have started telling you how much hotter my rush toward philosophy was in youth than my pace has been in old age, I will not be ashamed to confess the passion Pythagoras once lit in me. Sotion used to explain why Pythagoras refused to eat animals, and why Sextius, in later days, refused as well. Their reasons were different, but each was a noble reason. Sextius held that a man has food enough without resorting to blood, and that cruelty becomes a habit once butchering is practiced for pleasure. He added that we ought to shrink the sources of our luxury, and he argued that a varied diet works against health and is wrong for our bodies. Pythagoras, on the other hand, held that all beings are related, and that there is a system of exchange among souls, which pass from one bodily shape into another. If you believe him, no soul ever perishes or even pauses in its work, except for the tiny interval while it is being poured from one body into the next. We may leave open the question of when, and after how many changes of station, a soul returns to human form after wandering through so many dwellings; in the meantime, the doctrine made men afraid of guilt — afraid of parricide — since without knowing it they might be attacking a parent's soul, wounding it with knife or with teeth, if the kindred spirit happened to be lodging for a while in that piece of flesh. When Sotion had laid out this teaching, and filled it in with proofs of his own, he would say: "You don't believe that souls are dealt out to one body after another, and that what we call death is merely a change of address? You don't believe that in cattle, in wild beasts, in the creatures of the deep, there may linger the soul of what was once a man? You don't believe that nothing on this earth is annihilated, but only changes its neighborhood — that animals too have their cycles, and their souls travel an orbit of their own, no less than the heavenly bodies wheeling in their fixed circuits? Great men have believed this. So keep your own opinion if you like, but hold the whole question open in your mind. If the doctrine is true, abstaining from flesh is purity; if it is false, it is thrift. And what does it cost you to believe it? All I am taking from you is the diet of lions and vultures."

I was soaked in this teaching, and I began to abstain from animal food; and at the end of a year the habit was as pleasant as it was easy. I began to feel that my mind was quicker — though today I would not swear to you whether it really was. You ask how I came to give it up? It happened this way. My young days fell in the early part of the reign of Tiberius Caesar. Certain foreign rites were then being stirred up, and abstaining from particular animal foods was counted as evidence of devotion to the strange cult. So at the request of my father — who did not fear an accusation, but who hated philosophy — I went back to my old habits; and, to be honest, it did not take much persuading to get me to dine more comfortably.

Attalus used to recommend a pillow that does not give way beneath the body; and old as I am, I still use one so hard it takes no imprint from your weight. I have told you all this to show you how fiercely beginners charge toward the highest ideals, when someone does his part by urging them on and fanning the flame. But things do go wrong — partly through the fault of teachers who train us to argue rather than to live, and partly through the fault of students who come to their teachers to sharpen their wits rather than their souls. And that is how the study of wisdom has turned into the study of words.

Now, it makes a great deal of difference what you have in mind when you come to a subject. If a future language scholar is examining Virgil, he does not read that noble line

  Time flies away, and cannot be recalled

in this spirit: "We must wake up. Unless we hurry, we will be left behind. Time rolls swiftly on and rolls us along with it; we are swept forward, ignorant of our destination; we lay all our plans for the future, and lounge at our ease on the edge of a cliff." No — he points out that whenever Virgil speaks of the speed of time, he uses the word "flies":

  The choicest days of our unhappy life
  Fly first; disease and bitter age press on,
  And toil, till death's hard hand sweeps all away.

The man who reads these lines as a philosopher takes the words in their proper sense. "Virgil never says time goes," he comments, "but always time flies, because flying is the fastest of all movements — and our best days are always the first to be snatched away. Why then are we so slow to spur ourselves on, to try to match the pace of the swiftest thing there is?" The good flies past, and the worse takes its place. Just as the purest wine pours from the top of the jar while the heaviest dregs settle at the bottom, so in our human life the best comes first. Shall we let other men drink off the best, and keep the dregs for ourselves? Let this line fasten itself to your soul; let it satisfy you as if an oracle had spoken it:

  Each choicest day of our unhappy life
  Flies first.

Why the "choicest" day? Because what is coming is unsure. Why the "choicest"? Because in youth we can learn — we can bend minds still supple and ready toward nobler purposes; because this is the season for work, for keeping the mind busy with study and the body busy with useful effort. What comes after is more sluggish and short of spirit — closer to the end.

So let us strive with our whole heart, ignoring the attractions along the roadside; let us struggle with a single purpose, or we will discover too late — after we have been left behind — just how fast that flying time moves which we can never stop. Let every day, the moment it arrives, be welcomed as the choicest, and be made our own property. What is fleeing must be caught. But the man who reads those same lines with a scholar's eye never reflects that our first days are the best because disease is on the way, because old age is already pressing down and hanging over our heads while we are still daydreaming about our youth. He notes instead that Virgil always sets disease and age side by side — and, to be fair, rightly so, for old age is a disease without a cure. "And besides," he says to himself, "look at the epithet Virgil attaches to age: he calls it bitter" —

  Disease and bitter age press on.

And elsewhere Virgil says:

  There pale disease has made its home, and bitter age.

There is no reason to marvel that each man harvests from the same field material suited to his own pursuits: in one and the same meadow the cow finds grass, the dog hunts the hare, and the stork hunts the lizard. When Cicero's book On the State is opened by a philologist, a language scholar, and a follower of philosophy, each man runs his investigation his own way. The philosopher is astonished that so much could be said in it against justice. The philologist opens the same book and notes this: that there were two Roman kings, one with no father and one with no mother — for no one can settle who Servius's mother was, and Ancus, Numa's grandson, has no father on record. He also notes that the officer we call dictator, and read about in our histories under that title, was in the old days named the magister populi, "master of the people" — the name survives today in the augurs' records, and the proof is that the man the dictator chose as his second-in-command was called the magister equitum, "master of the horse." He will remark, too, that Romulus met his end during an eclipse; and that even under the kings there was a right of appeal to the people — so the pontiffs' register says, and Fenestella, among others, agrees. When the language scholar unrolls the very same volume, he enters into his notebook the forms of words: he notes that Cicero uses reapse for re ipsa, "in actual fact," and sepse just as often for se ipse, "he himself." Then he turns to shifts in usage. Cicero, for instance, writes: "since his interruption calls us back from the very calx" — for the finish line in the racecourse that we now call the creta, the chalk, was in the old days called the calx, the lime. Next he collects verses of Ennius, especially the ones about Africanus:

  A man whom neither friend nor foe could pay
  The worth of all his labors and his deeds.

From this passage the scholar declares he can infer that the word opem once meant not merely "aid" but "effort" — for Ennius must mean that neither friend nor foe could pay Scipio a reward equal to his efforts. Then he congratulates himself on discovering the source of Virgil's lines:

  Over whose head the mighty gate of heaven
  Thunders,

remarking that Ennius stole the idea from Homer, and Virgil stole it from Ennius; for preserved in this same book of Cicero's, On the State, is a couplet of Ennius:

  If it is right for a mortal to climb to the regions of heaven,
  Then for me alone the sky's great gate swings open.

But I must not slip into playing philologist or language scholar myself while I am busy with another errand. So here is my advice: all study of philosophy, and all reading, should be applied to the goal of living the happy life. We should not go hunting for archaic or far-fetched words, for eccentric metaphors and figures of speech; we should look for teachings that will help us — brave and spirited sayings that can be turned into fact on the spot. We should learn them in such a way that words become deeds. And I hold that no man has done mankind a worse turn than the one who studies philosophy as a trade to be sold — who lives one way and teaches another. Men like that, guilty of every fault they denounce, walk around as advertisements for useless training. A teacher of that sort can help me no more than a seasick pilot can help in a storm. The pilot has to grip the tiller while the waves are tossing him; he has to wrestle with the sea itself; he has to strip the sails when the storm rages — what good to me is a helmsman who is terrified and vomiting? And how much greater, do you think, is the storm of life than any storm that ever tossed a ship! One must steer, not talk.

Every word these men utter and juggle before an admiring crowd belongs to someone else. Plato said it, Zeno said it, Chrysippus said it, or Posidonius, or one of that whole company of Stoics, as numerous as they are excellent. I will show you how men can prove that the words are their own: let them do what they have been saying. And now, since I have delivered the message I wanted you to have, I will satisfy your eagerness — but I will save the complete answer to your question for a fresh letter, so that you do not come tired to a subject that is thorny, and that demands an attentive and painstaking ear.

Farewell.
