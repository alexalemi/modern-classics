Letter 74

Letter 74: On Virtue as a Refuge from Worldly Distractions

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Your letter gave me real pleasure, and it shook me out of a sluggish spell. It also gave my memory a nudge — it has been slack and drowsy for some time now.

You are right, of course, my dear Lucilius: the chief means of reaching the happy life is the conviction that the only good is what is honorable. Anyone who counts other things as good has put himself in Fortune's power and handed the controls to someone else. But the man who has defined the good, in every case, by what is honorable carries his happiness inside him.

One man is crushed when his children die; another is anxious when they fall sick; a third is embittered when they disgrace themselves or take a stain on their reputation. You will see one man tortured by passion for his neighbor's wife, another by passion for his own. You will find men wrecked by losing an election, and others actually tormented by the offices they won. But the biggest crowd of unhappy people in the whole throng of mortals are the ones driven to despair by the expectation of death — death that threatens them from every side. For there is no direction death cannot come from. So, like soldiers scouting in enemy country, they have to keep looking every way and turning their heads at every sound; unless the heart is rid of this fear, a man lives with his pulse pounding. You will easily call to mind the people driven into exile and stripped of their property. You will also call to mind — and this is the most serious kind of destitution — the people who are poor in the middle of their riches. You will call to mind men who have suffered shipwreck, or whose sufferings amount to shipwreck: they were untroubled and at ease when the anger, or perhaps the envy, of the crowd — a missile most deadly to those in high places — dismantled them, like a storm that tends to blow up exactly when you are most confident the calm will last, or like a lightning bolt that sets even the surrounding countryside trembling. For just as anyone standing near the strike is stunned and looks like a man who was hit, so in these sudden, violent disasters, only one person is crushed by the blow itself, but the rest are crushed by fear — the mere possibility of suffering leaves them as downcast as the man who actually suffered.

Every one of us is shaken in spirit by evils that fall suddenly on a neighbor. Like birds, who cower at the whirr of an empty sling, we are rattled by mere sounds as much as by blows. No one can be happy who gives himself up to fancies like these. For nothing brings happiness unless it also brings calm; a life spent in dread is a bad way to exist. Whoever has surrendered himself wholesale to Fortune's power has woven himself a huge web of anxiety, and he cannot get free of it. There is only one road to safety: despise externals and be content with what is honorable. For those who think anything is better than virtue, or that there is any good besides virtue, are spreading their arms to catch what Fortune throws to the crowd, and waiting nervously on her favors. Picture it: Fortune is holding a festival, showering honors, riches, and influence down on this mob of mortals. Some of the gifts have already been ripped to pieces in the hands of the people grabbing for them; some have been carved up by treacherous partnerships; some have been seized — to the great harm of the very people they landed on. A few of these favors fell to men who weren't even paying attention; others were lost to their seekers precisely because they snatched too eagerly, knocked from their hands by their own greed. And in that whole crowd there is not one man — not even the one who got lucky in the scramble — whose joy in his haul lasted until the next morning.

The sensible man, then, runs out of the theater the moment he sees the free handout being carried in, because he knows how high a price is paid for small favors. Nobody will wrestle with him on the way out; nobody will hit him as he leaves. The brawling happens where the prizes are. It is the same with the gifts Fortune tosses down to us. Wretches that we are, we get worked up, we are torn every which way, we wish we had a dozen hands, we glance back now this way, now that. The gifts seem to fall toward us far too slowly; all they do is inflame our cravings, since they can reach only a few and are awaited by all. We are desperate to intercept them as they drop. We celebrate if we grab hold of anything — while some are mocked by the empty hope of grabbing. Either we have paid dearly for worthless loot and damaged ourselves in the bargain, or we have been cheated and left standing there empty-handed. So let us walk out of this game and leave the field to the greedy rabble. Let them stare up at their "goods" dangling overhead — and dangle in suspense themselves.

Whoever makes up his mind to be happy should conclude that the good consists only in what is honorable. For if he counts anything else as good, then to begin with he is passing an unfavorable judgment on Providence — on the grounds that upright men often meet with misfortune, and that the time allotted to us is short and skimpy compared with the eternity allotted to the universe.

It is from complaints like these that we turn ungrateful in our commentary on heaven's gifts: we grumble that they are not granted always, that they are few, uncertain, and fleeting. And so we end up willing neither to live nor to die; we are gripped by hatred of life and fear of death. All our plans drift rudderless, and no amount of prosperity can satisfy us. The reason for all of it is that we have not yet reached the good that is beyond measure and beyond surpassing — the good at which all our wishing has to stop, because there is nowhere beyond the highest. Do you ask why virtue needs nothing? Because it is pleased with what it has and does not lust after what it lacks. In virtue's eyes, whatever is enough is abundant.

Break with this judgment, and duty and loyalty will not survive it. For anyone who wants to show those two qualities must endure much of what the world calls evil, and must sacrifice many of the things we are addicted to under the impression that they are goods. Gone is courage, which ought to be testing itself continually; gone is greatness of soul, which can never stand out clearly until it has learned to scorn as trivial everything the crowd covets as supremely important; gone are kindness and the repaying of kindness, if we fear hard work, if we have admitted that anything is more precious than loyalty, if our eyes are fixed on anything but the best.

But set those questions aside. Either these so-called goods are not goods, or else man is luckier than God — because God gets no enjoyment out of the things that are given to us. Lust has nothing to do with God, nor do elegant banquets, nor wealth, nor any of the things that entice mankind and lead him along by cheap pleasure. So either you must believe — hard as that is — that there are goods God does not possess, or else the very fact that God does not possess them is itself the proof that they are not goods. Besides, many things commonly counted as goods are granted to animals in fuller measure than to men. Animals eat their food with a heartier appetite, are not worn down to the same degree by sex, and have greater, steadier constancy in their strength. It follows that they are far luckier than man. For there is no wickedness in their way of living, no injuring of themselves. They enjoy their pleasures — and take them more often and more easily, without any of the fear that comes from shame or regret.

That being so, ask yourself whether anything deserves to be called good when God is outdone in it by man. Let us confine the Supreme Good to the soul; it loses its meaning if it is taken from the best part of us and handed to the worst — that is, transferred to the senses, for the senses are livelier in dumb beasts than in us. The sum total of our happiness must not be lodged in the flesh. The true goods are the ones reason bestows: substantial and eternal, they cannot fall away, cannot shrink, cannot be whittled down. Other things are goods only by opinion; they share a name with the true goods, but the essence of goodness is not in them. So let us call them "advantages" — or, to use our technical term, "preferred" things. And let us recognize that they are our property, not parts of us. Keep them in your possession, but keep remembering that they stand outside you. Even while we hold them, they should be filed under the subordinate and the second-rate — things no man has a right to preen himself on. For what is more foolish than being smug about something you never accomplished by your own effort? Let all such things be attached to us, not grafted into us, so that if one is taken away it comes off without tearing away any part of us. Use them, don't boast of them — and use them sparingly, as things handed over for safekeeping that will be called back. Anyone who holds them without reason never holds them long; prosperity, left unmanaged by reason, buries itself under its own weight. If a man has put his trust in the most fleeting of goods, he is soon stripped of them — and, to avoid being stripped, he suffers in advance. Few men have been allowed to set prosperity down gently. The rest fall together with everything they rose by, and the very things that once lifted them up come down on top of them. That is why foresight has to step in and impose a limit — a frugality in the use of these things — because license wrecks and squanders its own abundance. What has no limit has never lasted, unless reason, which sets limits, has kept it in check. The fate of many cities will prove the point: their power collapsed at its very peak because they had given themselves to luxury, and excess ruined everything virtue had won. We should fortify ourselves against such calamities. But no wall can be built that Fortune cannot take by storm. Let us strengthen the defenses within. If the inner citadel is safe, a man can be attacked — but never captured.

Do you want to know what this defensive weapon is? It is the ability not to chafe at whatever happens to you — the knowledge that the very forces which seem to do harm are working for the preservation of the world, and are part of the plan that carries the universe to the fulfillment of its order and its functions. Let man be pleased with whatever has pleased God. Let him marvel at himself and his own resources for this very reason: that he cannot be conquered, that he holds the powers of evil themselves under his control, and that he brings chance and pain and wrong into subjection by means of that strongest of all powers — reason. Love reason! The love of reason will arm you against the worst that can happen. Wild beasts throw themselves onto the hunter's spear out of love for their young, and it is their wildness, their unthinking charge, that keeps them from being tamed. A hunger for glory has often driven young minds to despise both sword and stake. The mere vision, the mere semblance of virtue drives certain men to a self-chosen death. And reason is sturdier and steadier than any of these impulses — so with that much more force will she drive her way through the thick of terrors and dangers.

People say to us: "You are mistaken if you claim that nothing is good except what is honorable. A defense like that will not make you safe from Fortune and free from her attacks. You yourselves count dutiful children, a well-governed country, and good parents as goods — but you cannot watch these dear things in danger and stay at ease. Your calm will be shattered by a siege against your country, by the death of your children, by the enslavement of your parents." I will first give the answer we Stoics usually make to these objectors, and then add what I think should be said on top of it.

The situation is entirely different with goods whose loss brings some hardship in as a substitute. When good health is damaged, it changes into illness; when an eye is put out, blindness moves in; when the tendons of our legs are cut, we do not merely lose our speed — lameness takes speed's place. But no such danger attends the goods we were just talking about. Why not? If I have lost a good friend, no false friend is installed whom I must endure in his place; if I have buried a dutiful son, I do not have to face unfilial behavior in exchange. And in the second place, what has happened is not the taking away of a friend or a child — only the taking away of their bodies. A good can be lost in one way only: by changing into something bad. And that is impossible under the law of nature, because every virtue, and every work of virtue, endures uncorrupted. Besides, even if friends have perished, or children of proven goodness who lived up to everything their father prayed for, something exists that can fill their place. Do you ask what it is? The very thing that made them good in the first place: virtue. Virtue leaves no space in us unoccupied; it takes possession of the whole soul and removes all sense of loss. It is sufficient by itself, for the strength and the seed of every good lies in virtue herself. What does it matter if running water is cut off and drains away, so long as the spring it flowed from is unharmed? You will not claim that a man's life is more just if his children are alive than if they have died — nor better ordered, nor more intelligent, nor more honorable. Therefore not better, either. Adding friends does not make a man wiser, and losing them does not make him more foolish — therefore not happier or more wretched, either. As long as your virtue is unharmed, you will not feel the loss of anything that has been taken from you. "Come now," you say, "isn't a man happier when he is surrounded by a great company of friends and children?" Why should he be? The Supreme Good is neither diminished nor increased by them; it stays within its own bounds no matter how Fortune has behaved. Whether a long old age falls to your lot or the end comes well short of old age, the measure of the Supreme Good is the same, whatever the difference in years.

Draw a circle large or draw it small: its size affects its area, not its shape. One circle may sit undisturbed for a long time; the other you may rub out at once, merging it back into the sand it was drawn in — yet each circle had the same shape. What is straight is not judged by its size, or its number, or its duration; it can no more be stretched than it can be shortened. Scale the honorable life down as far as you like from the full hundred years — reduce it to a single day — and it is every bit as honorable. Sometimes virtue operates on a broad front: governing kingdoms, cities, and provinces, making laws, cultivating friendships, regulating the duties that hold between relatives and children. At other times it is hemmed in by the narrow bounds of poverty, exile, or bereavement. But it is no smaller for being brought down from proud heights to a private station, from a royal palace to a humble cottage, or for being gathered from a broad, general jurisdiction into the confines of a private house or a tiny corner. Virtue is just as great even when it has retreated into itself and is shut in on every side. Its spirit is no less great and upright, its judgment no less complete, its justice no less unbending. It is, therefore, equally happy. For happiness has its home in one place only — the mind itself: noble, steadfast, and calm. And that state cannot be reached without knowledge of things divine and human.

The other answer, the one I promised to add, follows from this reasoning. The wise man is not crushed by the loss of children or friends. He endures their death in the same spirit in which he awaits his own — and he fears the one as little as he grieves for the other. For the underlying principle of virtue is harmony: all the works of virtue are in tune and in agreement with virtue itself. And that harmony is lost if the soul, which ought to stand tall, is dragged down by grief and longing. It is always a disgrace for a man to be fretting and agitated, to be numb when the moment calls for action. What is honorable is free of anxiety and unhampered; it is unafraid, and it stands ready to act. "What," you ask, "will the wise man feel no stirring at all — no disturbance of spirit? Won't his face lose its color, his features twitch, his limbs go cold? And there are other reactions we produce not by choice but unconsciously, by a kind of natural reflex." I grant all of that. But the wise man will hold on to the settled conviction that none of these things is an evil, or weighty enough to make a healthy mind break down. Whatever still needs doing, virtue will do it with courage and readiness. For anyone would agree it is the mark of a fool to do what he has to do sluggishly and under protest — to send the body one way and the mind another, and be torn apart between contradictory impulses. Folly is despised for the very things she brags about and admires in herself; she does not even do gladly the things she takes pride in. And if folly fears some evil, she is weighed down by it in the very act of waiting for it, exactly as if it had already arrived — suffering in apprehension everything she is afraid she may suffer. Just as in the body the symptoms of hidden ill-health show up before the disease — a certain limp sluggishness, a tiredness no work has earned, a trembling, a shiver running through the limbs — so the feeble spirit is rattled by its troubles long before it is overwhelmed by them. It lives them in advance, and collapses ahead of schedule.

But what is greater madness than to be tortured by the future — not saving your strength for the actual suffering, but inviting your misery in and hurrying it along? If you cannot get rid of it, you can at least postpone it. Will you not understand that no man should be tormented by what is still to come? A man who is told he will have to face torture fifty years from now is not disturbed by it — unless he vaults over all the intervening years and plants himself in the middle of a trouble scheduled to arrive a generation later. In just the same way, souls that enjoy being sick, that hunt for excuses to be sorrowful, grieve over events long past and wiped from the record. Past and future are both absent; we feel neither one. And there can be no pain except from what you feel.

Farewell.
