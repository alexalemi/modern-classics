Letter 93: On the Quality, as Contrasted with the Length, of Life

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I was reading the letter in which you mourn the death of Metronax — as if he could have lived longer, and somehow should have. And I missed something in it: the fairness you show everywhere else. You have fairness in abundance when you deal with people and events; it deserts you on this one subject, as it deserts every one of us. I have met plenty of people who are fair to their fellow men. I have never met anyone who was fair to the gods. Every day we curse Fate: "Why was this man cut down in the middle of his career? Why not that one instead? Why does he get to drag out an old age that is a burden to himself and everyone around him?"

But tell me honestly — which seems fairer to you: that you obey Nature, or that Nature obey you? And what difference does it make how soon you leave a place you must leave sooner or later? Our aim should be not to live long, but to live rightly. Living long is Fate's department; living rightly is the soul's. A life is long if it is full — and it becomes full only when the soul has secured its own proper good, only when it has taken command of itself. What good are eighty years to a man who spent them doing nothing? A man like that hasn't lived; he has loitered awhile in life. He didn't die late; he just took a long time dying. He lived eighty years, did he? That depends on which date you count as his death. Your other friend, though, died in the bloom of his manhood — but he had fulfilled every duty of a good citizen, a good friend, a good son; he came up short in nothing. His age was incomplete, but his life was complete. The other man existed for eighty years — unless by "he lived" you mean what we mean when we say a tree "lives."

Let's see to it, my dear Lucilius, that our lives are like precious jewels: remarkable not for their size but for their weight. Let's measure them by their performance, not by their duration. Do you want to know the difference between the hardy man who despises Fortune, serves through every campaign of life, and reaches life's Supreme Good — and the man who has merely had many years pass over his head? The first still exists after his death; the second was dead before he died.

So let's praise, and count among the blessed, the man who invested well whatever time he was given, however little. He saw the true light. He was not one of the common herd. He didn't just live; he flourished. Sometimes he enjoyed clear skies; sometimes, as usually happens, the radiance of that mighty star reached him only in flashes through the clouds. Why do you keep asking, "How long did he live?" He is living still! In one bound he has crossed over into posterity and handed himself into the keeping of memory.

Not that I would turn down a few extra years for myself. But if my span is cut short, I will not say I lacked anything essential to a happy life. I never planned to live out the very last day my greedy hopes promised me; I have looked on every day as if it were my last. Why ask my birth date, or whether I'm still on the register of the younger men? What I have is my own. Just as a short man can be a complete man, a short life can be a complete life. Age belongs among the externals. How long I exist is not mine to decide; how long I go on truly living, in my present way, is entirely in my control. Ask only this of me — that I stop measuring out an inglorious old age in the dark, and devote myself to living instead of being carried along past life.

And what, you ask, is the fullest span of life? Living until you possess wisdom. Whoever reaches wisdom has arrived — not at the farthest goal, but at the most important one. That man may boldly rejoice and give thanks to the gods — and to himself as well — and count Nature in his debt for having lived. He has earned the right: he paid her back a better life than the one he received. He set up the pattern of a good man; he showed what a good man is, and how great he can be. One more year would only have been like all the rest.

And really, how long are we supposed to keep on living? We have had the joy of learning the truth about the universe. We know the beginnings from which Nature rises; how she orders the course of the heavens; by what regular changes she calls back the year; how she has set an end to everything that has ever existed, and made herself the only end of her own being. We know that the stars move by their own motion; that nothing stands still except the earth, while every other body races on without pause. We know how the moon overtakes the sun; why the slower body leaves the faster behind; how she receives her light and loses it again; what brings on the night and what brings back the day. Where you are going, you will see all of this from closer up. "And yet," says the wise man, "it is not this hope that makes me depart more bravely — not my judgment that the road lies clear to my own gods. I have earned admission to their company; in fact, I have already been in it. I sent my soul to them, as they had already sent theirs to me. But suppose I am wiped out completely, and nothing of a man remains after death — my courage is not one bit less, even if my road, when I go, leads nowhere at all."

"But," you say, "he could have lived so many more years." There are books of only a few lines that are admirable and useful despite their size — and then there are the Annals of Tanusius. You know how bulky that book is, and what people say about it. Some men's long lives are exactly that: the Annals of Tanusius. Do you think the fighter killed on the last day of the games is luckier than the one who dies in the middle of the festival? Do you believe anyone is so foolishly greedy for life that he would rather have his throat cut in the dressing-room than in the arena? The head start we have on one another is no longer than that. Death makes its rounds to everyone; the slayer soon follows the slain. What people discuss with so much anxiety is, in the end, a trifle. And anyway — what does it matter how long you avoid the one thing you cannot escape?

Farewell.
