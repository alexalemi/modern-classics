Letters 64–65

Letter 64: On the Philosopher's Task

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

  And restless among the harmless flocks he prays
  that a foam-flecked boar will cross his path, or a tawny lion
  come stalking down from the hills.

That is the mood exactly. I want something to overcome — something I can test my endurance against. And this is another remarkable thing about Sextius: he shows you the grandeur of the happy life without ever making you despair of reaching it. You come away understanding that it stands high above you, but that it is within reach of anyone with the will to climb.

Virtue herself has the same double effect: she makes you admire her, and she makes you hope to win her. In my own case, at any rate, just contemplating wisdom takes up much of my time. I stare at her in amazement, the way I sometimes stare at the sky itself, which I often look at as if I were seeing it for the first time. And so I revere the discoveries of wisdom and their discoverers. Stepping into the inheritance of so many predecessors is a delight. It was for me that they stored up this treasure; it was for me that they did all that work. But we should act like a responsible head of the household: we should make what we inherited grow. That inheritance will pass from me to my descendants larger than it came. There is still much work left, and much will always be left; a person born a thousand generations from now will not be shut out from the chance of adding something more.

But suppose the old masters really did discover everything. One thing will always be new: the use of those discoveries — studying them, sorting them, applying them. Say we have inherited prescriptions for healing the eyes. I don't need to hunt for new ones; but the ones we have still must be fitted to the particular disease, and to the particular stage of the disease. Use this one to soothe an inflamed eyelid, that one to bring down the swelling; this one to head off a sudden stab of pain or a rush of tears, that one to sharpen the vision. Then combine them, watch for the right moment, and apply the right treatment to each case.

The cures for the spirit were also discovered by the ancients; but learning how and when to apply them — that is our task. The people who went before us accomplished a great deal, but they did not finish the job. Even so, they deserve reverence; we should honor them the way we honor gods. Why shouldn't I keep statues of great men to fire my enthusiasm, and celebrate their birthdays? Why shouldn't I greet them, every time, with respect and honor? The reverence I owe my own teachers I owe in the same measure to those teachers of the human race, the source from which so much good first flowed. If I meet a consul or a praetor, I will pay him all the honor his office is used to receiving: I will dismount, bare my head, and yield the road. Then what of Marcus Cato, the Elder and the Younger? Laelius the Wise? Socrates and Plato, Zeno and Cleanthes? Shall I let them into my soul with anything less than the highest marks of respect? I revere them in earnest, and I always stand up in honor of such noble names.

Farewell.

Letter 65: On the First Cause

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Yesterday I split the day with illness. It claimed the whole morning for itself; in the afternoon it gave way to me. So I began by testing my spirit with some reading. When that went well, I dared to demand more of it — or maybe I should say, to indulge it more. I wrote a little, and with more concentration than usual, since I am wrestling with a difficult subject and refuse to be pinned. In the middle of this, some friends came by, with the declared intention of using force to stop me — as if I were a sick man sneaking pleasures he'd been forbidden. So conversation took the place of writing, and from that conversation I'll pass along to you the question that is still in dispute. We have appointed you the referee. You have more work on your hands than you think, because the case has three sides.

Our Stoics, as you know, say there are two things in the universe from which everything comes: cause and matter. Matter lies there inert, a stuff ready for any use, but certain to stay idle if no one sets it moving. Cause — by which we mean reason — molds matter and turns it wherever it wants, producing all the concrete things we see. So for anything at all, there must first be something it is made from, and then an agent it is made by. The first is its material; the second is its cause.

All art is imitation of nature, so let me carry these general principles over to the things human beings make. A statue involves matter that had to submit to the artist's hands, and an artist who had to give that matter its shape. In the statue's case, then, the material was bronze and the cause was the sculptor. Every object works the same way: it consists of what it is made from and who made it.

The Stoics hold that there is one cause only — the maker. But Aristotle thinks "cause" can be used in three ways. "The first cause," he says, "is the matter itself, without which nothing can be made. The second is the workman. The third is the form that is stamped on every work" — on a statue, say. (His own word for this is the eidos.) "And there is a fourth as well: the purpose of the work as a whole." Let me show you what each of these means. Bronze is the "first cause" of the statue, since the statue could never have been made without something to cast and mold it from. The "second cause" is the artist: without a workman's skilled hands, that bronze could never have taken on the statue's outlines. The "third cause" is the form, because our statue could never be called The Spear-Bearer or The Boy Tying His Hair if that particular shape had not been stamped into it. The "fourth cause" is the purpose of the work — for if there had been no purpose, the statue would not have been made. And what is a purpose? Whatever drew the artist on, whatever he was pursuing when he made it: money, if he made it to sell; fame, if he worked for a reputation; piety, if he made it as a gift for a temple. Surely that too is a cause contributing to the statue's existence — or do you think we should refuse to count, among the causes of a finished thing, the very element without which it would never have been finished?

To these four Plato adds a fifth: the pattern, which he calls the "idea" — for this is what the artist kept gazing at while he created the work he had set out to make. It makes no difference whether that pattern is outside him, where he can turn his eyes to it, or inside him, conceived and set up there by himself. God has these patterns of all things within himself; his mind holds the harmonies and measures of everything that is to be brought into being. He is filled with those shapes Plato calls the "ideas" — imperishable, unchangeable, immune to decay. And that is why, though men die, humanity itself — the idea of man, the pattern man is molded on — lasts; and while men labor and perish, it suffers no change at all.

So by Plato's count there are five causes: the material, the agent, the form, the model, and the end in view. And last comes the result of all of them. Take the statue again, since that's where we started: the material is the bronze; the agent is the artist; the form is the shape fitted onto the material; the model is the pattern the agent imitated; the end in view is the purpose in the maker's mind; and the result of all these is the statue itself. The universe, in Plato's opinion, has all the same elements. The agent is God. The source is matter. The form is the shape and arrangement of the visible world. The pattern, of course, is the model on which God made this vast and supremely beautiful creation. And the purpose is his reason for making it. You ask what God's purpose is? Goodness. At least, that is what Plato says: "What was God's reason for creating the world? God is good, and no good person begrudges anything good. So he made it the best world he could." Hand down your verdict, then, judge: tell us who seems to you to say what is truest — not who says what is absolutely true. That is as far beyond our reach as truth itself.

Now, this crowd of causes assembled by Aristotle and Plato takes in either too much or too little. If they count as "causes" everything without which a thing cannot be made, they have named too few. Time would have to be included — nothing is made without time. And place — if there is nowhere for a thing to be made, it will not be made. And motion — nothing is made or destroyed without motion; there is no art, no change of any kind, without it. But what I am after is the first cause, the general cause. And that must be simple, since matter, too, is simple. What is cause, then? Surely it is Creative Reason — in other words, God. All those items you listed are not a series of separate, independent causes; they all hang on one, the one that actually creates. You say form is a cause? Form is only what the artist stamps on his work — a part of a cause, not the cause. The pattern is not a cause either, but an indispensable tool of the cause. The pattern is as necessary to the artist as his chisel or his file; without them art gets nowhere; but they are neither parts of the art nor causes of it. "Well then," someone will say, "the artist's purpose — the thing that moves him to create — that is the cause." Perhaps it is a cause; but not the efficient cause, only an accessory one. And accessory causes are countless; what we are after is the general cause. In fact, when Plato and Aristotle call the whole universe — the finished, perfected work — a cause, they fall short of their usual sharpness. There is a great difference between a work and the cause of a work.

Now either give your verdict or do what is easier in cases like this: declare that the matter is not clear and send it back for another hearing. But I hear you object: "What pleasure do you get from wasting time on these puzzles? They rid you of no passion, they drive out no desire." For my part, I raise and discuss these questions as things that greatly calm the spirit; and I examine myself first, and then the world around me. And even now I am not wasting time, whatever you think. All these questions — provided they aren't minced and shredded into useless hair-splitting — lift and lighten the soul. The soul is weighed down by a heavy load; it longs to be freed and to return to the elements it once belonged to. For this body of ours is the soul's burden and its punishment. Under that pressing weight the soul is crushed and held in chains — unless philosophy comes to its rescue, tells it to breathe freely again in the contemplation of the universe, and turns it from things earthly to things divine. There the soul has its liberty; there it can roam; meanwhile it slips its guards and is restored in heaven. Think of craftsmen doing close, delicate work that strains their eyes: if their light is scanty or uncertain, they go outside and, in some park set aside for the public's recreation, treat their eyes to the free light of day. Just so the soul, shut up in this gloomy, darkened house, escapes into the open sky whenever it can, and finds its rest in contemplating the universe.

The wise man — and the seeker after wisdom too — is tied tightly to his body, but in his better self he is elsewhere; his thoughts are fixed on higher things. He is like a man bound by an oath of service: he treats his lifespan as his term of enlistment. He has trained himself neither to love life nor to hate it; he endures a mortal lot, knowing a larger one awaits him.

Do you forbid me to contemplate the universe? Do you drag me away from the whole and confine me to a part? May I not ask what the beginnings of all things are — who shaped the universe, who took the confused, sluggish jumble of matter and sorted it into its parts? May I not ask who the Master Builder of this universe is, how so vast a bulk was brought under law and order, who gathered the scattered atoms, who separated the tangled elements and gave outward form to what lay in one great shapeless heap? Or where all this flood of light comes from — whether it is fire, or something brighter still? Am I not to ask these questions? Must I stay ignorant of the heights I came down from? Whether I will see this world once only, or be born many times? Where I go afterward — what home waits for my soul when it is released from the terms of its human slavery? Do you forbid me a share in heaven — which is to say, do you order me to live with my head bowed? No. I am above that. I was born for something greater than to be my body's property, and I regard this body as nothing but a chain clamped on my freedom. So I hold it out as a kind of buffer against Fortune, and I let no blow pass through it to reach my soul. My body is the only part of me that can be injured; inside this vulnerable house, my soul lives free. This flesh will never drive me to fear, or to any pretense unworthy of a good man. I will never tell a lie for this little body's sake. When it seems right, I will dissolve our partnership. And even now, while we are bound together, we are not equal partners: the soul hears every dispute in its own court. To despise the body is sure freedom.

To come back to our subject: this freedom is helped enormously by the contemplation we were just discussing. All things are made of matter and of God. God governs matter, which surrounds him and follows him as its guide and leader. What creates — that is, God — is more powerful and more precious than matter, which merely receives his action. God's place in the universe is the soul's place in a man; what matter is to the world, the body is to us. So let the lower serve the higher. Let us be brave in the face of every hazard. Let us not fear wrongs, or wounds, or chains, or poverty. And what is death? Either an end or a crossing. I do not fear ending — it is the same as never having begun. And I do not shrink from crossing into another state, because nowhere will I be as cramped as I am here.

Farewell.
