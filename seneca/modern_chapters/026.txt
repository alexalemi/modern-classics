Letters 78–79

Letter 78: On the Healing Power of the Mind

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I'm sorry to hear that you keep being hit by congestion in the head and by the short fevers that follow when the congestion turns chronic — sorry above all because I've had this very illness myself. In its early stages I laughed it off. When I was young I could take hardship and stare a sickness down. But eventually it beat me. I sank to the point where I could do nothing but sniffle, wasted away to skin and bone. More than once I had the impulse to end my life right then. What held me back was the thought of my father — a kind old man. I considered not how bravely I could die, but how little brave he could be at losing me. So I ordered myself to live. Sometimes even staying alive is an act of courage.

Now let me tell you what consoled me in those days, saying up front that these comforts worked on me like medicine. Honest consolation becomes a cure; whatever lifts the soul helps the body too. My studies saved me. I credit philosophy with my recovery, with my getting back on my feet. I owe her my life — and that is the least of what I owe her. My friends also did a great deal for my health. Their encouraging words, the hours they sat by my bed, their conversation — all of it comforted me. Nothing, my excellent Lucilius, restores and strengthens a sick man like the affection of his friends; nothing so quietly steals away the expectation and the fear of death. With them around me, I honestly could not believe I was dying at all. I felt sure I would go on living — not with them, but through them. I imagined myself not surrendering my soul but handing it over to them for safekeeping.

All of this gave me the will to help myself and to endure anything. And besides, it is a miserable state to have lost your appetite for dying without having any appetite for living. These, then, are the remedies you should turn to. The doctor will prescribe how much to walk and how much to exercise. He will warn you not to sink into idleness, the way the inactive invalid tends to do. He will have you read aloud in a stronger voice to work the lungs, whose passages and hollows are the trouble; or take a sail and stir up your insides with a little gentle rocking. He will tell you what to eat, and when wine will build your strength and when to skip it so your cough doesn't get raw and hacking. But my prescription for you is this — and it is a cure not just for this illness but for your whole life: despise death. Once we have escaped the fear of death, there is no sorrow left in the world.

Every illness carries three serious evils: fear of death, bodily pain, and the interruption of our pleasures. About death enough has been said, and I'll add only this: the fear of it is not a fear of illness but a fear of nature. Illness has often postponed death, and looking like a dying man has saved more than one man's life. You will die not because you are sick, but because you are alive. Even cured, the same end waits for you; in recovering, you have escaped ill health, not death.

Now back to the evil that belongs to illness itself: it comes with great suffering. But the suffering is made bearable by its interruptions, because pain at its most extreme has to end. No one can suffer both intensely and for long. Nature, who loves us most tenderly, has built us so that pain is either bearable or brief. The sharpest pains strike in the slenderest parts of the body: nerves, joints, and the other narrow passages hurt most cruelly when trouble builds inside their cramped spaces. But those parts soon go numb, and the very pain robs them of the feeling of pain — whether because the life-force, blocked from its natural course and turned bad, loses the particular power by which it thrives and by which it warns us, or because the diseased fluids of the body, with nowhere left to flow, back up on themselves and deaden the parts they have flooded. That is why gout in the feet and hands, and every pain in the spine and the nerves, has its truces — the moments when it has stunned the parts it was torturing. In all these cases it is the first twinges that do the damage; time blunts the attack, and the pain ends when the numbness sets in. Pain in the teeth, eyes, and ears is so acute precisely because it starts in the body's narrowest spaces — no less acute, frankly, than pain in the head itself. But when it grows more violent than usual, it tips over into delirium and stupor. So here is the consolation for extreme pain: feel it too much and you are guaranteed to stop feeling it. What makes the untrained buckle under bodily suffering is that they have never learned to be content in spirit. They have been too intimate with the body. That is why a great-souled and sensible man separates soul from body and spends his time with the better, divine part, keeping only as much company as he must with this whining, fragile one.

"But it's hard," people say, "to give up the pleasures we're used to — to fast, to go thirsty and hungry." Yes, abstinence is hard at first. Later the craving dies down, because the appetites that drive the craving get tired and quit. Then the stomach turns fussy; then the food we once ached for turns our own stomach. The wants themselves die away. And there is no bitterness in doing without what you have stopped wanting. Besides, every pain stops sometimes, or at least eases up. Besides, you can take precautions against its return and, when it threatens, head it off with remedies. Every kind of pain sends warning signals — at any rate the habitual, recurring kind does. The suffering an illness brings can be endured, if you have learned to scorn what it threatens. But do not make your troubles heavier than they are and weigh yourself down with complaining. Pain is slight if opinion adds nothing to it. If instead you cheer yourself on the other way — "It's nothing, or nearly nothing; keep a stout heart, it will pass" — then by thinking it slight, you will make it slight. Everything hangs on opinion. Ambition, luxury, greed — all look to opinion. It is according to opinion that we suffer. A man is as wretched as he has convinced himself he is. So I say we should throw out all whining over past suffering, all talk like this: "No one was ever worse off. The agonies, the horrors I endured! Nobody thought I'd pull through. How many times my family wept over me, how many times the doctors gave up on me! Men on the rack aren't torn apart like that!" Even if all of it is true, it is over. What is the good of dragging up old sufferings and being miserable now because you were miserable once? Besides, everyone inflates his own ills and lies to himself about them. And what was bitter to bear is sweet to have borne; it is only natural to rejoice when your troubles end.

Two things, then, must be cut out once and for all: fear of future suffering and memory of past suffering. The past no longer concerns me; the future does not concern me yet. And a man caught in the very middle of troubles should say:

  Someday, perhaps, even this pain
  will be sweet to remember.

Let him fight them with everything he has. Give way once, and he is beaten; strain against his sufferings, and he wins. As it is, most men pull down on their own heads the very ruin they ought to be propping up. When something is toppling toward you, tottering, about to crash, step back from under it and it will follow you and lean on you harder; stand your ground and push back, and it will be forced away. Think of the blows athletes take to the face, to the whole body. For the sake of fame they endure every torment — and they take those blows not only while fighting but in order to be fit to fight. Their very training is torture. Let us win our victories in all our own struggles the same way — for our prize is not a garland or a palm branch or a trumpeter calling for silence while our name is announced, but virtue, steadiness of soul, and a peace won for all time, once Fortune has been beaten outright in even a single bout. "I'm in terrible pain," you say. And does the pain let up because you whimper through it? Just as an enemy is deadliest to an army in retreat, so every trouble Fortune sends hits hardest when we give ground and turn our backs. "But it's serious." What — is that what our strength is for, to carry light loads? Would you rather your illness be long, or quick and short? If it is long, it comes with intermissions: it gives you stretches to recover in, it grants you plenty of time; as it builds, it must also subside. A short, sharp illness will do one of two things: it will be snuffed out, or it will snuff you out. And what difference does it make whether the illness ends or I do? Either way, the pain is over.

This helps too: turn the mind to other things and walk away from the pain. Call up the honorable and brave things you have done; go over the good side of your own life; run through your memory of everything you have most admired. Then think of all the brave men who have conquered pain: the man who went on reading his book while he had his varicose veins cut out; the man who never stopped smiling, though that smile so enraged his torturers that they tried every instrument of their cruelty on him. If pain can be beaten by a smile, can it not be beaten by reason? Name whatever you please — head colds, coughing fits so hard they bring up pieces of our insides, fever that cooks us from the core, thirst, limbs wrenched until the joints stick out in different directions. Worse still are the stake, the rack, the red-hot plates, the device that reopens wounds while they are still swollen and drives its imprint deeper. And yet there have been men who never let out a groan under those tortures. "More!" says the torturer — and the victim doesn't beg off. "More!" — no answer. "More!" — and the victim smiles, and means it. After an example like that, can't you bring yourself to laugh at pain?

"But my illness won't let me do anything," you object. "It has pulled me away from all my duties." It is your body that is hampered by ill health, not your soul too. Illness will slow a runner's feet and tie up the hands of a cobbler or a craftsman; but if your soul has kept in training, you will still advise and teach, listen and learn, investigate and reflect. What more is needed? Do you think you are doing nothing when you keep your self-control while sick? You will be demonstrating that an illness can be overcome — or at least endured. There is room for virtue, I promise you, even on a sickbed. It is not only the sword and the battle line that prove a soul alert and unafraid; a man can show bravery wrapped in his blankets. You have work to do: wrestle well with your disease. If it forces you to nothing and talks you into nothing, you are setting a remarkable example. What a stage for glory it would be, if anyone came to watch us be sick! So be your own spectator; seek your own applause.

Again, there are two kinds of pleasures. Illness checks the pleasures of the body, but it does not abolish them. In fact, truth be told, it sharpens them: a drink delights a man more the thirstier he is; food delights him more the hungrier he is. Whatever comes after a fast is received with extra relish. And the other kind — the pleasures of the mind, which are higher and more reliable — no doctor can deny a sick man. Whoever pursues these, and knows what they really are, scorns all the flatteries of the senses. "Poor sick fellow!" people say. Why exactly? Because he doesn't chill his wine with snow? Because he doesn't chip ice into his cup to refresh the cold of a drink already mixed in a good-sized bowl? Because Lucrine oysters aren't shucked fresh at his table? Because there's no racket of cooks around his dining room, hauling in their stoves right along with the dishes? That is luxury's latest invention: the kitchen accompanies the dinner, so that no course goes lukewarm and nothing arrives insufficiently scalding for a palate already grown leathery. "Poor sick fellow!" — he will eat as much as he can digest. There will be no boar lying in state before his eyes, banished from the table as if it were too common a meat; his sideboard will not be heaped with the breast-meat of birds, because the sight of birds served whole turns his stomach. And what harm has been done to you? You will dine like a sick man — which is to say, once in a while, like a healthy one.

All of it is easy to endure — the gruel, the warm water, everything that seems unbearable to a fussy man gone soft with luxury, sick in soul rather than body — if only we stop shuddering at death. And we will stop, once we have learned the limits of good and evil. Then and only then will life not exhaust us or death frighten us. For weariness of self can never seize a life that surveys all the things that are — manifold, great, divine; it is idle leisure that makes men come to hate their lives. To one who roams the universe, the truth never grows stale; it is the falsehoods that cloy. And on the other side, if death comes calling early — even if it cuts a man down in his prime — he has already tasted everything the longest life has to give. Such a man has largely come to understand the universe. He knows that honorable things do not depend on time to grow. Any life must look short to people who measure it by pleasures that are empty and therefore bottomless.

Refresh yourself with thoughts like these — and meanwhile set aside some hours for our letters. A time will come when we are together again, reunited; however short that time is, we will make it long by knowing how to use it. For as Posidonius says: "A single day among the learned lasts longer than the longest life of the ignorant." In the meantime hold on to this, grip it tight: do not yield to adversity; do not trust prosperity; keep the full reach of Fortune's power before your eyes, as if she would surely do everything she has the power to do. What has been long expected arrives more gently.

Farewell.

Letter 79: On the Rewards of Scientific Discovery

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

I have been waiting for a letter from you telling me what your tour around Sicily turned up — above all, a full report on Charybdis itself. Scylla, I know perfectly well, is just a rock, and not even a rock sailors fear; but about Charybdis I want a complete description, to see whether it matches the myths. And if you happen to have investigated it — it certainly deserves investigating — enlighten me on this: is it whipped into a whirlpool only by wind from one quarter, or do storms from every direction churn its depths alike? And is it true that whatever the whirlpool sucks down in that strait is carried underwater for miles and surfaces on the beach near Tauromenium? Write me a full account of all that, and then I'll have the nerve to ask a further favor: climb Etna, too, at my special request. Certain naturalists have concluded that the mountain is wearing away and gradually sinking, because sailors used to be able to see it from farther off. The reason may be not that its height is shrinking but that the fires have dimmed — the eruptions weaker and thinner, and the smoke, for the same reason, lazier by day. Either explanation is believable: that the mountain is growing smaller because it eats itself daily, or that it stays the same size because it is not devouring itself at all — the boiling matter collects in some underground hollow and is fed from elsewhere, using the mountain not as fuel but simply as an exit. There is a well-known spot in Lycia — the locals call it Hephaestion — where the ground is riddled with holes and ringed by a harmless fire that does no damage to the plants growing there. The place is actually fertile and lush, because the flames don't scorch; they merely glow, with a force too mild and feeble to burn.

But let's shelve that question until you have gone and can tell me just how far from the crater the snow lies — I mean the snow that doesn't melt even in summer, so safe is it from the fire next door. And don't bill this errand to my account: you were going to indulge your itch for fine writing with no commission from anybody. In fact, what would I have to pay you not to work Etna into your poem — not to touch the theme every poet treats as a required rite? Virgil had already covered it in full, and that didn't stop Ovid from taking it up; and neither of them scared off Cornelius Severus. Besides, the ground has been lucky for all of them, and to my eye the earlier poets did not use up what can be said — they merely opened the way.

It makes all the difference whether you take up a subject that is exhausted or one where the ground has only been broken. In the second case the topic grows day by day, and what is already discovered is no obstacle to discovering more. Besides, the one who writes last gets the best of the bargain: he finds the words lying ready, and arranged in a new order they show a new face. And he isn't stealing them, as if they belonged to somebody else — words are common property. Now, if Etna doesn't make your mouth water, I've misjudged you. You have been itching for a while to write something big, something on the scale of the old school. Your modesty won't let you hope any higher — indeed it's so pronounced that I think you would rein in your own talent if there were any danger of outdoing your betters. That is how much you revere the old masters. Wisdom has this advantage, among others: no one can be outdone except during the climb. Once you have reached the top, it's a draw — no room left to rise, the game is over. Can the sun grow bigger? Can the moon swell past her usual fullness? The seas do not increase. The universe keeps the same character, the same limits. Whatever has reached its full stature cannot get taller. So all who have attained wisdom will be equal, on level footing. Each will have his own particular gifts — one more genial, another quicker, another readier in speech, a fourth more eloquent — but in the thing that matters here, the thing that makes life happy, they are all equal. Whether this Etna of yours can collapse into ruins, whether that towering summit, visible for miles across the open sea, is being eaten away by the relentless fire, I do not know. But this I do know: virtue will never be brought lower, by flames or by ruins. Hers is the one greatness that knows no lowering; she can neither rise further nor sink. Her height, like the height of the stars, is fixed. Let us strive to raise ourselves to that altitude.

Much of the climb is behind me already — no; if I make myself tell the truth, not much. Goodness does not mean merely being better than the worst. Who would brag about his eyesight because he could catch a glimmer of daylight? A man who sees the sun through fog may be glad for now that he has escaped the dark, but he does not yet enjoy the blessing of light. Our souls will have no cause to celebrate until they have broken out of this darkness they grope in — until they have not merely glimpsed the brightness with weak eyes but absorbed the full light of day and been restored to their place in the sky, the place assigned them at birth. The soul is summoned upward by its very origin. And it can arrive there even before it is released from this prison below — as soon as it has shaken off its faults and leaps, pure and light, into celestial thoughts.

I am glad, dearest Lucilius, that this is the ideal that occupies us, that we pursue it with everything we have, even if few people know it — even if no one does. Fame is virtue's shadow: it follows her whether she wants it or not. But as a shadow sometimes walks ahead and sometimes trails behind, so fame sometimes goes before us in plain sight and sometimes hangs back — and is all the greater for coming late, once envy has retired from the field. How long did men take Democritus for a madman! Glory barely found Socrates in time. And how long did Rome fail to recognize Cato! The state rejected him and did not learn his worth until it had lost him. If Rutilius had not resigned himself to injustice, his innocence and virtue would have gone unnoticed; the hour of his persecution was the hour of his triumph. Didn't he give thanks for his lot and greet his exile with open arms? So far I have named men whom Fortune made famous in the very moment she was crushing them. But how many men's progress toward virtue came to light only after they died! And how many have been ruined by their reputations rather than raised by them! Take Epicurus: see how he is admired now, not just by the cultivated but by the ignorant crowd as well. Yet this man was unknown to Athens itself, though he had tucked himself away practically next door. So when he had outlived his friend Metrodorus by many years, he added these last words to a letter, celebrating their friendship with grateful pride: "So blessed were Metrodorus and I that it has done us no harm to be unknown, and almost unheard of, in this famous land of Greece." Isn't it true, then, that men did not discover him until after he was gone? And hasn't his renown blazed out all the same? Metrodorus admits the same thing in one of his letters — that he and Epicurus were hardly known to the public — but he declares that whoever chose to follow in their footsteps after they were gone would find a great name ready-made and waiting.

Virtue is never truly hidden; and yet to have been hidden is no loss to her. A day will come that reveals her, however buried or suppressed by the spite of her own times. The man who thinks only of his own generation was born for a mere handful. Many thousands of years, many thousands of peoples, are still to come — look to them. Even if envy has silenced every mouth of your own day, men will come who will judge you without malice and without favor. If fame is any reward virtue receives, that reward cannot perish either. True, the talk of posterity will not reach us; but posterity will cherish and celebrate us all the same, unaware though we are. Virtue has never failed to repay a man, in life and after it, provided he followed her honestly — provided he never dressed himself up or painted himself over, but was the same man whether people saw him announced and expected or suddenly and unprepared. Pretending accomplishes nothing. A mask thrown loosely over the face fools very few. Truth is the same through and through. Whatever deceives has no substance. Lies are thin stuff: hold them to the light and you see right through them.

Farewell.
