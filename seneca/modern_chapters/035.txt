Letter 90

Letter 90: On the Part Played by Philosophy in the Progress of Man

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Who can doubt, my dear Lucilius, that life is the gift of the immortal gods, but that living well is the gift of philosophy? It would follow that we owe philosophy more than we owe the gods — by exactly as much as a good life outweighs mere life — if philosophy itself were not something the gods gave us. The knowledge of it they have given to no one; the capacity for it they have given to everyone. If they had made philosophy a blanket gift too — if we were all born understanding — then wisdom would have lost her finest quality: that she is not one of Fortune's handouts. As things stand, this is precisely what is precious and noble about her: she does not come out to meet us. Each man owes her to himself alone. We do not get her secondhand.

What would there be in philosophy worth respecting if she were passed out like charity? Her one job is to discover the truth about things divine and things human. Religion never leaves her side, nor duty, nor justice, nor the whole company of virtues that travel together, arm in arm. Philosophy has taught us to worship what is divine and to love what is human; she has told us that dominion belongs to the gods, and fellowship to men. And that fellowship stayed whole for a long time — until greed tore the community apart and became the cause of poverty even for the people it had made richest. For men stop possessing everything the moment they want everything for themselves.

But the first men, and the generations that sprang from them, were still unspoiled; they followed nature. They took one man as both their leader and their law, and put themselves in the hands of someone better than they were. Nature makes a habit of subjecting the weaker to the stronger. Even among the dumb animals, the biggest or the fiercest hold sway. It is no weakling bull that leads the herd; it is the one that has beaten every other male with muscle and might. Among elephants, the tallest walks first. Among men, the best man ranked highest. So the ruler was chosen for his mind — and that is why the happiest peoples were the ones where a man could not become more powerful without being better. A man can safely be given all the power he wants when he believes he can do nothing except what he ought to do.

So in that age we call the golden age, Posidonius holds that government was in the hands of the wise. They kept their own hands in check and protected the weak from the strong. They advised people what to do and what not to do; they showed what was useful and what was useless. Their foresight kept their people from lacking anything; their bravery kept dangers away; their generosity made their subjects prosperous and their lives handsome. Ruling was a service, not a throne. No ruler tested his power against the very people who had given him his power in the first place; and no one had the urge — or the excuse — to do wrong, because the ruler ruled well and the ruled obeyed well, and the sternest threat a king could make against a disobedient subject was banishment from the kingdom.

But once vice crept in and kingdoms turned into tyrannies, laws became necessary — and at first the wise framed those too. Solon, who set Athens on a foundation of just laws, was one of the seven men famous for their wisdom. If Lycurgus had lived in the same era, an eighth would have joined that hallowed number. The laws of Zaleucus and Charondas are still praised; and those two men learned the principles of justice they would establish in Sicily (flourishing, in those days) and through the Greek cities of Italy not in the forum, not in the offices of legal experts, but in the silent, holy retreat of Pythagoras.

Up to this point, I agree with Posidonius. But that philosophy discovered the crafts our daily life depends on — that I refuse to admit. I will not hand her a workman's diploma. Posidonius says: "When men were scattered across the earth, sheltering in caves, or in hollows dug out of cliffs, or in the trunks of hollow trees, it was philosophy that taught them to build houses." I say philosophy no more devised these clever stacked dwellings of ours — story piled on story, city crowding against city — than she invented the fish-ponds, walled in so that no man's gluttony has to take its chances with the weather, so that however wildly the sea is raging, luxury has its own safe harbors where it can fatten exotic breeds of fish. What! Did philosophy teach men the use of locks and keys? What was that but a hint dropped to greed? Was it philosophy that stacked up all these towering tenements, so dangerous to the people who live in them? Wasn't it enough for a man to roof himself with whatever came to hand, and find himself some natural shelter without engineering and without fuss? Believe me, that was a happy age — before there were architects, before there were builders. All of that was born when luxury was being born: this business of cutting timber square, of sawing a beam with a practiced hand along the marked-out line.

  The earliest men split wood with wedges.

They were not framing a roof for a future banquet-hall; they did not haul pines and firs down trembling streets in long trains of wagons just to hang paneled ceilings, heavy with gold, from the rafters. Two forked poles, one at either end, propped up the house. Packed branches and leaves, heaped and laid on a slope, let even the heaviest rain run off. Beneath such roofs they lived — but they lived in peace. A thatched roof once covered free men; under marble and gold lives slavery.

I part ways with Posidonius on another point too: he holds that the wise invented our mechanical tools. On that logic you might as well say the wise were the men who taught

  The setting of the snare for game, the liming
  Of twigs for birds, the circling of great woods with dogs.

It was man's ingenuity that discovered all these tricks, not his wisdom. And I disagree with him again when he says it was wise men who discovered our mines of iron and copper — "when the earth, scorched by forest fires, melted the shallow veins of ore and the metal gushed out." No: the sort of men who discover such things are the sort of men who work at them. Nor does the question strike me as subtle as it strikes Posidonius — whether the hammer or the tongs came first into use. Both were invented by some man whose mind was nimble and sharp, but not great and elevated. And the same goes for anything else that can only be found by a man whose back is bent and whose eyes are on the ground.

The wise man lived simply. Of course he did — even in our own day he would prefer to travel as light as possible. How, I ask you, can you admire both Diogenes and Daedalus? Which of the two looks like a wise man to you: the one who invented the saw? Or the one who saw a boy drinking water out of his cupped hand, immediately pulled the cup from his knapsack and smashed it, scolding himself — "What a fool I am, hauling around superfluous baggage all this time!" — and then curled up in his tub and went to sleep? And in our own times, which man do you call wiser: the one who invents a system for spraying saffron perfume to a tremendous height from hidden pipes, who fills and empties canals in a single rush of water, who builds a dining room with a ceiling of sliding panels that shows one pattern after another, a new roof for every course of the meal — or the one who proves, to himself and to everyone else, that nature has laid down no harsh or difficult law? That we can live without the marble-cutter and the engineer; that we can clothe ourselves without a silk trade; that we can have everything we actually need if we will only be content with what lies on the earth's surface? If mankind were willing to listen to this sage, they would learn that the cook is as superfluous as the soldier. The men who made caring for the body a simple problem were wise men, or something like them. What is indispensable takes no elaborate effort to get; it is the luxuries that demand the labor. Follow nature and you will need no craftsmen.

Nature did not want us harassed. Whatever she required of us, she equipped us for. "But the naked body can't endure cold." Well? Aren't there the hides of wild beasts and other animals, which can keep off the cold well enough and more than enough? Don't plenty of tribes cover themselves with the bark of trees? Aren't birds' feathers sewn together to serve as clothing? To this very day, doesn't a large part of the Scythian people dress in the skins of foxes and mice — soft to the touch, and windproof? "Even so, men need something thicker than skin to keep off the summer sun." Well? Hasn't time itself hollowed out plenty of retreats — caverns opened up by decay or by some accident or other? Well? Didn't the very first men weave twigs into wicker mats by hand, plaster them with common mud, roof them over with straw and wild grasses, and pass their winters snug and dry, the rain sliding off the sloping gables? Well? Don't the peoples along the edge of the Syrtes live in dug-out houses — along with every other tribe whose sun burns too fiercely for any shelter to keep out the heat except the parched earth itself?

Nature was not so spiteful that, having given every other animal an easy time of it, she made man alone unable to live without all this apparatus. None of it was imposed on us by her; none of it had to be painfully invented to keep us alive. Everything was ready for us at birth. It is we who have made everything difficult for ourselves by turning up our noses at what is easy. Housing, shelter, comfort, food — all the things that have now become a colossal industry of trouble — were ready to hand, free to all, obtainable for trivial effort. In those days the limit of everything matched the need. It is we who have made these things expensive, we who have made them marvelous, we who have made them the objects of elaborate, endlessly multiplying arts of pursuit. Nature is sufficient for everything she asks of us. Luxury has turned her back on nature; every day she swells herself further, gathering strength through all the centuries, her cleverness in the service of the vices. First luxury began craving what was superfluous; then what was contrary to nature; and finally she sold the soul to the body and ordered it to be the utter slave of the body's appetites. All these trades that keep the city bustling — or should I say, in an uproar — are doing the body's business. There was a time when everything was supplied to the body as to a slave; now everything is procured for it as for a master. So we get the workshops of the weavers and the carpenters; so we get the smells of the professional kitchens; so we get the dancing-masters with their suggestive poses, and the suggestive, affected singing. The moderation nature prescribes — desires limited to means, and means limited to needs — has left the field. It has now come to this: wanting only what is enough is taken as proof that you are a bumpkin, or a beggar.

It is hard to believe, my dear Lucilius, how easily the charm of eloquence can lure even great men away from the truth. Look at Posidonius — in my judgment one of the men who have given the most to philosophy — when he sets out to describe the art of weaving. He tells how some threads are twisted and others drawn out from the soft, loose mass of wool; how the upright warp holds its threads taut with hanging weights; how the inserted thread of the woof, which softens the stiff texture of the web gripping it on either side, is driven home by the batten and pressed into a tight union with the warp. And he maintains that even the weaver's art was discovered by wise men — forgetting that the elaborate loom he is describing was invented much later, the art in which

  The web is bound to the frame; the reed divides
  The warp; between the threads the pointed shuttles
  Carry the woof, and the broad comb's notched teeth
  Drive it home.

What if he had lived to see the weaving of our own day, which produces clothing that conceals nothing — clothing that offers no protection, I won't say to the body, but even to modesty!

Posidonius then moves on to the farmer. With just as much eloquence he describes the ground broken up by the plough and crossed again, so that the loosened earth gives the roots freer play; then the seed sown, and the weeds pulled by hand so that no stray growth or wild plant can spoil the crop. This trade too, he declares, is the creation of the wise — as if farmers were not discovering countless new ways to improve the soil's yield at this very moment! And not content with these arts, he actually demotes the wise man to the mill. For he tells us how the sage began making bread by copying nature's own processes. "The grain," he says, "once taken into the mouth, is crushed by the hard teeth meeting in collision, and whatever escapes them the tongue pushes back to those same teeth. Then it is blended into a mass, so it can slide more easily down the throat. When it reaches the stomach, it is cooked by the stomach's even heat, and then — only then — is it taken up into the body. Following this model," he goes on, "someone set two rough stones one on top of the other, in imitation of the teeth: one set fixed, waiting on the motion of the other. Then by rubbing stone against stone the grain is crushed, and brought back again and again, until repeated grinding reduces it to flour. Then this man sprinkled the flour with water, worked it and kneaded it into obedience, and shaped the loaf. At first it was baked in hot ashes, or in a red-hot earthen vessel; later, ovens were gradually discovered, and the other devices whose heat obeys the sage's will." One step more and Posidonius would have declared that the cobbler's trade was the discovery of the wise man.

All these things were devised by reason — but not by right reason. They were invented by man, but not by the wise man. The same goes for ships, in which we cross rivers and seas — fitted with sails to catch the force of the wind, with rudders hung at the stern to swing the vessel's course this way or that. The model was the fish, which steers itself with its tail and by the slightest flick to one side or the other bends its darting course. "But," says Posidonius, "the wise man did discover all these things; they were just too petty for him to handle himself, so he handed them over to his humbler assistants." No. These early inventions were thought up by exactly the class of men who run them today. We know that certain devices have appeared within living memory — windows of transparent tile that let in clear light; vaulted baths with pipes set into the walls to spread the heat and keep the temperature even from floor to ceiling. Need I mention the marble that makes our temples and our private houses gleam? Or the huge rounded and polished blocks of stone we use to raise colonnades and halls roomy enough for nations? Or our signs for whole words, which let us take down a speech however fast it is delivered, the hand keeping pace with the tongue? All of this has been devised by the lowest grade of slaves. Wisdom sits higher up. She does not train the hands; she is the teacher of minds.

Would you like to know what wisdom has actually brought to light — what she has accomplished? Not the body's graceful poses. Not the varied notes of horn and flute, where the breath goes in and comes out transformed into voice. Not weapons, not walls, not the machinery of war — her voice is for peace, and she calls all mankind to live in concord. She is not, I insist, the manufacturer of our everyday tools, however indispensable. Why assign her such small things? What you see in her is the master craftsman of life itself. The other arts do fall under her authority, it's true — for the servant of life is also served by the things that equip life. But wisdom's course is set toward the happy life. That is where she leads us; that is the road she opens for us. She shows us what things are evil and what things only look evil; she strips the mind of empty illusion. She gives us a greatness that is solid, and deflates the greatness that is puffed up, all show and hollowness; and she never lets us stay confused about the difference between what is great and what is merely swollen. She delivers to us the knowledge of the whole of nature, and of her own nature. She reveals what the gods are, and of what sort; what the gods below are, and the household deities, and the guardian spirits; what those souls are that have won lasting life and been admitted into the second rank of divinities; where they dwell, and what they do, and what they can do, and what they will.

These are wisdom's rites of initiation — and what they unlock is not some village shrine but the vast temple of all the gods, the universe itself, whose true images and true faces she offers to the gaze of our minds; for the eyes are too dull for sights that great. Then she goes back to the beginnings of things: to the eternal Reason poured into the whole, and to the force alive in every seed, shaping each thing after its kind. Then wisdom begins asking about the soul — where it comes from, where it lives, how long it lasts, into how many parts it falls. Finally she has turned from the bodily to the bodiless, and examined truth up close, and the marks by which truth is known — asking next how the ambiguous can be told apart from the true, in life and in language alike; for in both, the false is mingled with the true.

My own view is that the wise man did not withdraw from those arts, as Posidonius thinks. He never took them up in the first place. For he would have judged nothing worth inventing that he would not afterward judge worth using always. He would not pick up what he would have to put down.

"But Anacharsis," says Posidonius, "invented the potter's wheel, whose spinning gives shape to vessels." And then, because the potter's wheel turns up in Homer, people would rather believe the verses are spurious than that Posidonius's story is! I maintain Anacharsis was not the inventor of that wheel — and if he was, then a wise man invented it, but not as a wise man; just as there are plenty of things wise men do as men, not as wise men. Suppose a wise man happens to be exceptionally fast on his feet: he will beat every runner in the race because he is fast, not because he is wise. I would love to show Posidonius a glass-blower, whose breath moulds glass into shapes the most skillful hand could scarcely form. These discoveries have been made since we stopped discovering wisdom.

Posidonius again: "Democritus is said to have discovered the arch, in which a curved line of stones, leaning gradually inward, is locked together by the keystone." I am inclined to call this false. There must have been bridges and gateways before Democritus, curved toward the top. And it seems to have quite slipped your memory that this same Democritus discovered how to soften ivory, and how a pebble could be boiled into an emerald — the very process still used today for coloring stones that take the treatment. A wise man may well have discovered such things; but he did not discover them because he was wise. He does plenty of things that we see done just as well — or more deftly and skillfully — by men with no wisdom whatsoever.

You ask, then, what the wise man has actually tracked down, what he has brought into the light? First, truth, and nature — which he has not followed the way the other animals do, with eyes too dull to see the divine in it. Second, the law of life: he has made life conform to universal principles; he has taught us not merely to know the gods but to follow them, and to welcome whatever chance brings exactly as if it were a divine command. He has forbidden us to listen to false opinions, and has weighed the worth of everything on a true scale. He has condemned the pleasures that come mixed with remorse, and praised the goods that will always satisfy; and he has published this truth to the world: the happiest man is the man who needs no happiness, and the most powerful man is the man who has power over himself.

I am not talking about the philosophy that has set the citizen outside his country and the gods outside the universe, and handed virtue over to pleasure — but about the philosophy that counts nothing good except what is honorable; the philosophy that cannot be sweet-talked by the gifts of men or of Fortune; whose price is that she cannot be bought at any price. That this philosophy existed in that rough age, when the arts and crafts were still unknown and useful things could only be learned by using them — that I refuse to believe.

Next came the fortune-favored era when nature's bounty lay open to everyone, for everyone's use alike, before greed and luxury broke the bonds that held mortals together and men abandoned their common life, scattered, and turned to plunder. The men of that second age were not wise men, even if they did what wise men should do. There is no other condition of the human race anyone would rate more highly; and if God commissioned a man to fashion earthly creatures and hand institutions to peoples, that man would approve no other system than the one that held among the men of that age, when

  No ploughman worked the soil; it was not lawful
  To mark off land or fence one's property.
  Men pooled their gains, and the earth gave her riches
  More freely to sons who never asked for them.

Was any race of men ever more blessed than that one? They enjoyed all of nature in partnership. She was sufficient for them all, guardian now as she had been parent before; and her gift was this — the secure possession, by every man, of the common resources. Why shouldn't I call that race the richest in human history, since you could not have found a poor man in it?

But greed broke in on this happy arrangement, and in its eagerness to set something aside for its own private use, it made everything the property of somebody else, and shrank itself from boundless wealth down to cramped need. It was greed that introduced poverty: by craving much, it lost all. So now she tries to recover her losses; she adds estate to estate, buying out one neighbor and cheating out the next; she swells her country places to the size of provinces and calls it "ownership" when a tour of your own property counts as extensive travel — and for all that, no pushing back of boundary lines will ever bring us back to where we started.

When we have done everything that can be done, we shall possess much. We used to possess the whole world. The earth itself was more productive untilled — generous enough for peoples who did not rob one another. Whatever nature produced, a man took as much delight in showing it to someone else as in finding it. No one could have more than anyone else, and no one less; whatever there was, was divided among friends who did not quarrel. The stronger had not yet begun laying hands on the weaker; the miser had not yet begun hiding away what lay in front of him, shutting his neighbor off from the bare necessities. Each man cared as much for his neighbor as for himself. Weapons lay unused; hands unstained by human blood turned all their hostility against the wild beasts. Those men, sheltered from the sun by a thick grove, safe from the bite of winter and rain in their humble hideaways, spent their days under the branches and passed untroubled nights without a sigh. We toss with worry in our purple sheets — it prods us out of bed with the sharpest of goads; but how soft was the sleep the hard earth gave those men! No carved and paneled ceilings hung over them. They lay out under the open sky, and the stars glided overhead, and the great pageant of the night — the firmament — swept silently past, doing its mighty work without a sound. By day and by night alike, the views of that most glorious dwelling-place stood open and free. It was their joy to watch the constellations sinking down from mid-heaven, and others rising up out of their hidden places. What could it be but joy, to wander among marvels scattered so far and wide? But you people of today tremble at every sound your houses make; if anything creaks while you sit among your frescoes, you flinch and run. They had no houses the size of cities. The open air; the breezes blowing free across open ground; the drifting shade of a crag or a tree; springs crystal-clear, and streams not spoiled by man's meddling — no water-pipes, no forced channels, just water running where it liked; and meadows beautiful without a landscaper — this was the setting of their rough homes, trimmed by rustic hands. That was a dwelling in accordance with nature. It was a joy to live in it, afraid neither of the house nor for it. Today our houses make up a large share of our dread.

But however splendid and guileless the life of those men was, they were not wise men; that title is reserved for the highest achievement of all. Still, I would not deny they were men of lofty spirit — fresh from the gods, if I may put it that way. There is no doubt the world turned out better offspring before it was worn out. But though their native powers were sturdier than ours across the board, and better built for hard work, those powers had not been perfected in every case. For nature does not hand out virtue; becoming good is an art. They, at least, did not go rummaging through the lowest dregs of the earth for gold or silver or translucent gemstones; and they were still merciful even to the dumb animals — that age stood so far from the custom of man killing man not in anger, not in fear, but for the pleasure of the spectacle. They had no embroidered clothing yet; cloth was not yet woven with gold; gold was not yet even mined.

What is the upshot, then? Those men were innocent because they were ignorant — and it makes a very great difference whether a man refuses to sin or simply does not know how. They had no justice, no prudence, no self-control, no bravery. Their rough life had certain qualities that resembled all these virtues; but virtue itself comes only to a soul that has been trained and taught and brought to its full growth by unrelenting practice. We are born for virtue, but not with it. And even in the best of men, until you refine them by instruction, there is only the raw material of virtue — not virtue itself.

Farewell.
