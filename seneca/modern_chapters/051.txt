Letters 118–119

Letter 118: On the Vanity of Place-seeking

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You have been demanding letters from me more often. But if we compare the account books, you will not come out on the credit side. Our agreement, in fact, was that your part came first: you were to write the first letters, and I was to answer them. Still, I won't be difficult about it. I know you're good for the money, so I'll pay in advance. And even so I will not do what the eloquent Cicero tells Atticus to do: "Even if you have nothing to say, write whatever comes into your head." I will always have something to write about, even if I skip all the kinds of news Cicero stuffs his letters with — which candidate is in trouble; who is campaigning on borrowed money and who on his own; who is running for consul leaning on Caesar, or on Pompey, or on his own strongbox; what a merciless moneylender Caecilius is, out of whom his own friends can't pry a penny at less than one percent a month.

It is better to deal with your own ills than with someone else's — to sift yourself, to see how many empty offices you are secretly a candidate for, and to cast your vote for none of them. This, my dear Lucilius, is a noble thing; this is what brings peace and freedom — to campaign for nothing, and to walk straight past all of Fortune's elections. When the voting tribes are called together and the candidates are sweating in their favorite temples — one promising cash handouts, another doing his business through an agent, another wearing his hand raw with the kisses of men he won't touch with one finger after he's elected — when everyone is waiting breathless for the herald's announcement, how can you call it anything but delightful to stand idle and watch this whole vanity fair without buying and without selling? And how much greater is the joy of the man who watches, untroubled, not just the election of a praetor or a consul, but the great election underneath it — where some are running for offices that last a year, others for permanent power, others for a triumph and a lucky war, others for riches, or a marriage and children, or the safety of themselves and their families! What a great-souled thing it is to be the one person campaigning for nothing, petitioning no man, and saying: "Fortune, I have no business with you. I am not at your service. I know that you snub men like Cato and promote men like Vatinius. I ask you for nothing." That is how you demote Fortune to the ranks.

So these are the things we can write each other about — the ever-fresh material we can keep digging out as we look over the restless mobs of men, all of them struggling through evil after evil to reach something ruinous, chasing what they will soon have to run from, or find sickening once they have it. Was anyone ever satisfied, once he got it, with the thing that loomed so large while he was praying for it? Happiness is not the greedy thing people imagine. It is a lowly thing — and that is why it never glutted anyone's appetite. The things you're chasing look lofty to you because you are down low and far away from them; to the man who has reached them, they look cheap. And I am much mistaken if he isn't itching to climb higher still. What you take for the summit is only a rung on the ladder. The trouble with everyone is ignorance of the truth: misled by common report, men chase these things as if they were good, and then, after winning them and suffering plenty on the way, they find them evil, or empty, or smaller than advertised. Most people admire whatever fools them at a distance; to the crowd, good things and big things are the same thing.

To keep that from happening to us, let's ask: what is the Good? It has been explained in various ways; different thinkers have described it differently. Some define it like this: "The Good is what attracts and calls the spirit to itself." But the objection is immediate: what if it attracts you straight to ruin? You know how seductive many evils are. The true differs from the plausible; and the Good is bound up with the true — it isn't good unless it is also true. But what merely attracts and entices is only plausible: it steals your attention, demands your interest, pulls you in. So some have defined it instead: "The Good is what stirs desire for itself, or rouses toward itself the impulse of a striving soul." The same objection applies: plenty of things rouse the soul's impulses, and the chase does the chaser harm. This definition is better: "The Good is what rouses the soul's impulse toward itself in accordance with nature, and is only to be sought when it becomes thoroughly worth seeking." By that point it is already an honorable thing — for that is what is completely worth seeking.

The topic prompts me to state the difference between the Good and the honorable. They share one quality that blends into both and can't be pried out of either: nothing can be good without some element of the honorable in it, and the honorable is necessarily good. What, then, is the difference? The honorable is the perfect Good; the happy life is fulfilled by it; through its influence other things become good as well. I mean something like this: there are things that are neither good nor bad — military service, a diplomatic post, the pronouncing of legal judgments. When such things are conducted honorably, they begin to be good; they cross over from the "indifferent" column into the Good. The Good comes from partnership with the honorable; the honorable is good in itself. The Good springs from the honorable; the honorable springs from itself. What is good might have been bad. What is honorable could never have been anything but good.

Some have offered this definition: "The Good is what is according to nature." Now follow my point carefully: what is good is according to nature, but what is according to nature does not thereby become good. Many things harmonize with nature but are too petty to deserve the name of good; they are trivial, fit to be ignored. There is no such thing as a tiny, contemptible good — for as long as it is scanty, it is not good, and when it begins to be good, it stops being scanty. How, then, is the Good recognized? Only when it is completely according to nature.

"You admit," someone says, "that what is good is according to nature — that's its special property. And you admit that other things are according to nature which are not good. How can the one be good and the other not? How can the essential character change, when both share the same distinguishing mark of agreeing with nature?" By sheer magnitude. It is nothing new for things to change as they grow. A person who was a child becomes a youth; his essential character is transformed, for the child could not reason and the youth can. Some things don't merely get bigger as they develop — they grow into something else. "But," comes the reply, "getting bigger doesn't mean becoming different. It makes no difference whether you pour wine into a flask or into a vat; the wine keeps its character in both. Honey tastes the same in small amounts and large." You are citing different cases. Wine and honey have a uniform quality; enlarge the quantity all you like, the quality stays the same. Some things do persist in their kind and character even when they are enlarged.

But there are others which, after many additions, are transformed by the last one: a new character is stamped on them, different from what they were before. One stone makes an archway — the one that wedges the leaning sides and holds the whole arch together by sitting in the middle. Why does the last addition, tiny as it is, make so much difference? Because it does not increase; it completes. Some things, as they develop, shed their old shape and are altered into a new one. When the mind has dwelt on some idea a long time and grown weary trying to grasp its size, the thing comes to be called "infinite" — and then it has become something very different from what it was when it seemed vast but finite. In the same way, we think of something as hard to divide; at the very end, as the task grows harder and harder, it turns out to be "indivisible." So too, from what could barely be budged we advance step by step to the "immovable." By the same reasoning, a certain thing was according to nature; its magnitude has changed it into a different character altogether, and made it a Good.

Farewell.

Letter 119: On Nature as Our Best Provider

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Whenever I make a find, I don't wait for you to shout "Halves!" — I say it to myself on your behalf. If you want to know what I've discovered this time, open your pocket: it is pure profit. What I am going to teach you is how to get rich as fast as possible. You're all ears now, and rightly so: I am going to lead you by a shortcut to the greatest wealth there is. You will need a loan, though. To do business, you must go into debt — but I don't want you borrowing through a middleman, and I don't want the brokers gossiping about your credit rating. I will supply you with a ready lender: Cato's famous one, who says, "Borrow from yourself!" However little it is, it will be enough, if we can only make up the shortfall out of our own resources. For it makes no difference, my dear Lucilius, whether you don't crave a thing or you have it. In either case the essential point is the same: freedom from worry.

I am not advising you to refuse nature anything. Nature is insistent; she cannot be beaten down; she demands what she is owed. But you should understand that anything beyond nature's wants is an "extra" — not a necessity. If I am hungry, I must eat. Nature doesn't care whether the bread is coarse or the finest wheat; she doesn't want the stomach entertained, only filled. If I'm thirsty, nature doesn't care whether I drink water from the nearest tank or chill it artfully in heaps of snow. Nature's one order is that the thirst be quenched. Whether the cup is gold, or crystal, or precious carved stone, or common clay from Tibur, or my own cupped hands — it doesn't matter. Look to the end of a thing, in every case, and you will throw the superfluities away. Hunger calls me: I reach for whatever is nearest; my hunger itself has made whatever I can grab look appetizing. A starving man despises nothing.

Do you want to know, then, what has delighted me? This noble saying I've discovered: "The wise man is the keenest seeker after the riches of nature." "What!" you say. "You hand me an empty plate? What is this? I had my strongboxes arranged. I was already scouting for some stretch of water to send trading ships across, some public revenues to farm, some merchandise to buy up. This is a swindle — promising me riches and showing me poverty." But, my friend, do you call a man poor who lacks nothing? "Yes — thanks to himself and his endurance, not thanks to his fortune." And you hold that such a man isn't rich — precisely because his wealth can never run out? Tell me: would you rather have much, or enough? The man who has much wants more — proof that he doesn't yet have enough. The man who has enough has arrived at something no rich man ever reaches: a stopping point. Or do you refuse to call this riches because no one was ever put on the execution lists for owning it? Because no wife or son ever poured poison down a throat to get it? Because in wartime it goes unplundered? Because in peacetime it means leisure? Because it is neither dangerous to own nor troublesome to invest?

"But a man possesses too little, if all he's free of is cold and hunger and thirst." Jupiter himself possesses no more. Enough is never too little, and not-enough is never too much. Alexander was still poor after conquering Darius and the Indies. Am I wrong? Look at him: hunting for something he can call his own, probing unknown seas, sending fresh fleets out over the Ocean, breaking down — you might say — the very bars of the universe. What is enough for nature is not enough for man. There have been men who craved something more after obtaining everything — so blind are our wits, so easily does each man forget his beginnings once he is under way. The man who only yesterday was the disputed lord of one obscure corner of the world reaches the edge of the globe — and is gloomy, because now he must march home through a world that belongs to him. Money never made anyone rich; all it does is infect its owner with a greater craving for itself. You ask the reason? The man who has more has begun to be capable of more.

To sum it up: haul out for inspection any of the millionaires whose names come up whenever people talk of Crassus and Licinus. Let him bring his credit rating, his current holdings, and his future prospects, and add them all together. By my reckoning, such a man is poor; by yours, he may become poor someday. But the man who has arranged his affairs by the measure of nature's demands is free of the fear of poverty and of the feeling of it too. And just so you know how hard it is to narrow one's wants down to nature's limits — even this man we're describing, the one you call poor, owns something superfluous. Wealth dazzles and draws the mob: a great pile of ready cash carried out of a man's house, walls crusted thick with gold, a retinue of attendants picked for their handsome build or handsome dress. All that kind of prosperity faces the public; but the man we have snatched out of the hands of the people and of Fortune is happy from the inside. As for those in whose lives busy, bustling poverty has stolen the title of riches — they have riches the way we say we "have a fever," when really the fever has us. We're used to putting it the other way around: "A fever grips him." And in the same way we should say: "Riches grip him." There is no advice I would rather give you than this — and of this advice no one can have too much: measure everything by nature's demands, for those can be satisfied either free of charge or very cheaply. Only don't stir any vices into the demands. Why ask how your food should be served, on what sort of table, with what sort of silver, by what well-matched, smooth-cheeked serving boys? Nature demands nothing but food.

  When thirst is burning in your throat, do you demand a golden cup?
  When hunger comes, do you turn up your nose at everything
  but peacock and turbot?

Hunger is not ambitious. It is quite content to stop, and it doesn't much care what stops it. All those other things are the tools of a luxury that has nothing to do with happiness — a luxury that studies how to stretch hunger out past the point of fullness, how to stuff the stomach rather than fill it, how to reawaken a thirst that the first drink already quenched. So Horace's words are excellent: it makes no difference to your thirst, he says, how costly the goblet is or how elegantly the hand that pours the water. If you care how curly-haired the boy is who serves you, or how transparent the cup he offers — you are not thirsty.

Among her other gifts, nature has granted us this special blessing: sheer necessity is never picky. Only the superfluous things allow for choosiness. "That's not suitable"; "this doesn't come well recommended"; "that offends my eye." The Builder of the universe, who laid down the laws of life for us, arranged that we should live well, not that we should live in luxury. Everything that serves our well-being is prepared and lying ready to hand; what luxury demands can never be scraped together without misery and anxiety.

So let us use this blessing of nature and count it among the great ones. Let us reflect that nature has no better claim on our gratitude than this: whatever we want out of sheer necessity, we accept without turning up our nose.

Farewell.
