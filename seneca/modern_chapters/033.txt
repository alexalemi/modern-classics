Letter 88

Letter 88: On Liberal and Vocational Studies

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You have been wanting to know what I think of the liberal studies. Here is my answer: I have no respect for any study, and I count no study good, whose end is making money. Those are trades — useful exactly as far as they train the mind for something greater, and no farther. You should stay with them only as long as your mind can manage nothing better; they are our apprenticeship, not our real work. You can see why they are called "liberal" studies: they are the studies thought worthy of a free man. But there is only one study that is truly liberal — the one that makes a man free. That is the study of wisdom, and it is lofty, brave, and great-souled. All the others are small and childish. You surely don't believe there is any real good in subjects whose teachers are — look at them — men of the cheapest and shabbiest stamp? We shouldn't be learning these things; we should be done learning them.

Some people have decided that the real question about the liberal studies is whether they make men good. But the teachers of these subjects don't even claim to know anything about that. The scholar busies himself with language; if he wants to range farther, he takes up history; if he wants to push out to the farthest limits, poetry. Which of these paves the road to virtue? Sounding out syllables, investigating words, memorizing plays, drawing up rules for the scansion of verse — what is there in any of it that removes fear, roots out desire, or bridles passion? The question is simple: do these men teach virtue, or don't they? If they don't teach it, they can't pass it on. If they do teach it, they are philosophers. Would you like to know how far their lecture chairs are from being chairs of virtue? Look how unlike their subjects are — and they would resemble one another if they were all teaching the same thing.

Perhaps they persuade you that Homer was a philosopher — though they disprove it with the very arguments they use to prove it. Sometimes they make him a Stoic, approving nothing but virtue, shunning pleasure, refusing to trade honor even for immortality. Sometimes they make him an Epicurean, praising the quiet city that passes its days in feasting and song. Sometimes he is a Peripatetic, sorting goods into three classes; sometimes an Academic, holding that everything is uncertain. Clearly none of these doctrines belongs to Homer, precisely because all of them are in him — for they contradict one another. But let us grant these men their point: Homer was a philosopher. Then surely he was wise before he ever knew a line of poetry. So let us learn the things that made Homer wise.

Asking whether Homer or Hesiod was the older poet is no more to the point than knowing why Hecuba, though younger than Helen, wore her years so badly. And what, I ask you, would be the point of settling the ages of Achilles and Patroclus? Do you ask, "Where did Ulysses wander?" instead of working to keep ourselves from wandering all the time? We have no leisure for lectures on whether he was tossed between Italy and Sicily or blown beyond the known world — a wandering that long could hardly have fit inside such narrow bounds. We meet storms of the spirit every day of our lives, and our own badness drives us into every trouble that ever plagued Ulysses. For us there is never any shortage of beauty to tempt the eye, or of enemies to attack us; here are savage monsters that delight in human blood, there the treacherous songs that seduce the ear, and out beyond, shipwreck and the whole varied catalogue of misfortune. Teach me this instead, by the example of Ulysses: how to love my country, my wife, my father — and how, even after shipwreck, to keep sailing toward those honorable harbors. Why try to settle whether Penelope was a model of purity or just fooled her generation? Whether she suspected the man standing in front of her was Ulysses before she knew it? Teach me instead what purity is, and how great a good we have in it — and whether it lives in the body or in the soul.

Now I turn to the musician. You, sir, are teaching me how the treble and the bass harmonize, how strings sounding different notes can still produce concord. Teach my soul harmony instead — bring it into accord with itself, and don't let my purposes fall out of tune. You are showing me which keys are mournful; show me instead how to keep from sounding a mournful note in the middle of adversity. The mathematician teaches me how to survey my estates; I would rather be taught how to measure what is enough for a man. He teaches me to count, and lends my fingers to greed; I would rather he taught me that all that arithmetic is beside the point — that a man is no happier for possessions that wear out his bookkeepers. Or rather, how useless property is to a man for whom it would be the worst of misfortunes to have to total up his own holdings using his own head. What good is knowing how to divide a field into parcels, if I don't know how to share it with my brother? What good is calculating an acre to the inch, and catching the sliver that slipped past my measuring rod, if I turn bitter the moment a bad-tempered neighbor scrapes off a corner of my land? The mathematician teaches me how to lose none of my boundaries; what I want to learn is how to lose them all with a light heart. "But I'm being driven off the farm my father and grandfather owned!" So? Who owned the land before your grandfather? Can you name the people — never mind the person — who held it first? You didn't enter it as a master; you entered as a tenant. And whose tenant are you? If your case goes well, your heir's. The lawyers say public property can't become private by squatting on it; well, what you hold and call your own is public property — it belongs to the human race. What marvelous skill you have! You can measure the circle; you can square any shape set in front of you; you can name the distances between the stars; nothing in the universe escapes your calculations. If you are really a master of your craft, then measure a human soul. Tell me how great it is — or how small. You know a straight line when you see one; what good is that, if you don't know what is straight in this life of ours?

Next I come to the man who boasts of knowing the heavens — who knows

  Where Saturn's chilly star conceals itself,
  And through what orbit Mercury goes straying.

What is the benefit of knowing this? That I should worry when Saturn and Mars stand in opposition, or when Mercury sets in the evening in plain view of Saturn — instead of learning that those stars, wherever they stand, are kindly, and cannot change? They are driven along by an unending round of destiny, on a course they cannot leave. They return on schedule; they either set the whole world's work in motion or mark off its intervals. But if they are responsible for everything that happens, what does it help you to know the secrets of what cannot be altered? And if they merely give signs, what is the use of foreseeing what you cannot escape? Know these things or don't — they will happen anyway.

  But watch the racing sun, and watch the stars
  That follow in his train, and then tomorrow
  Will never take you in, and no clear night
  Will ever lead you wrong.

Provision enough has already been made to keep me safe from anything that could take me in. "What," you say, "tomorrow never plays me false? Whatever happens without my knowledge plays me false." I don't know what will happen; but I do know what can happen. And about that I will file no complaints. I expect the future in its entirety; if any of its severity is remitted, I take it as a gift. Tomorrow deceives me if it treats me kindly — and even then it doesn't really deceive me. For just as I know that anything can happen, I also know that it won't all happen. So I count on the favorable and stand ready for the bad.

In this discussion you must bear with me if I don't follow the standard course. For I refuse to admit painting into the list of liberal arts, and sculpture too, and marble-work, and the other servants of luxury. I also strike off wrestling and the whole science compounded of oil and mud — otherwise I would have to admit the perfumers as well, and the cooks, and everyone else who hires out his wits to the service of our pleasures. For what is "liberal," tell me, about those ravenous gulpers of emetics, whose bodies are fattened while their minds stay thin and dull? Or do we seriously believe that sort of training is "liberal" for the young men of Rome — the young men our ancestors used to teach to stand straight and hurl a spear, to wield a pike, to manage a horse, to handle weapons? Our ancestors taught their children nothing that could be learned lying down. But neither the new system nor the old one teaches or nourishes virtue. What good does it do us to guide a horse and check his speed with the curb, and then discover that our own passions, completely uncurbed, have bolted with us? What good is beating opponent after opponent at wrestling or boxing, and then being beaten by our own temper?

"What then," you say, "do the liberal studies contribute nothing to our welfare?" A great deal in other respects — nothing at all toward virtue. Even the admittedly low-grade arts that work with the hands contribute plenty to the equipment of living, yet they have nothing to do with virtue. "Then why do we educate our children in the liberal studies?" Not because these studies can bestow virtue, but because they prepare the soul to receive it. Just as the old "primary course," as the ancients called it, which taught boys their letters, does not teach the liberal arts but clears the ground for learning them, so the liberal arts do not carry the soul all the way to virtue; they merely set it moving in that direction.

Posidonius divides the arts into four classes. First come the common and low; then the arts of amusement; then the arts for educating boys; and finally the liberal arts. The common ones belong to workmen and are plain handwork; their business is equipping life, and they make no pretense of beauty or honor. The arts of amusement are the ones that aim to please the eye and the ear. Here you may file the stage-machinists, who invent scaffolding that rises on its own, floors that climb silently into the air, and plenty of other surprises — things that fit together and then fall apart, things that stand separate and then join by themselves, things that tower up and then slowly collapse. Such tricks stun the inexperienced eye; people who don't know the causes marvel at everything that happens without warning. The arts for educating boys, which come somewhat nearer the liberal arts, are the ones the Greeks call the "cycle of studies" and we Romans call the "liberal." But the only arts that are really liberal — or, to give them their truer name, really free — are the ones whose business is virtue.

"But," someone will say, "just as philosophy has one part dealing with nature, one with ethics, and one with reasoning, so this family of liberal arts claims its own place in philosophy. When you come to questions about nature, the decision rests on a word from the mathematician; therefore mathematics is a department of the branch it assists." But many things assist us without being parts of us — indeed, if they were parts of us, they wouldn't assist. Food is an aid to the body, but it is not a part of the body. Mathematics performs a service for us; it is as necessary to philosophy as the carpenter is to the mathematician. But carpentry is not a part of mathematics, and mathematics is not a part of philosophy. Besides, each has its own boundaries. The wise man investigates and learns the causes of natural things; the mathematician follows after and computes their numbers and measurements. The wise man knows the laws by which the heavenly bodies hold their courses, what powers they have and what natures; the astronomer merely logs their comings and goings, the rules of their rising and setting, and the occasional stretches when they seem to stand still — though in fact no heavenly body can stand still. The wise man will know what causes the reflection in a mirror; the mathematician can only tell you how far the object must be from the image, and what shape of mirror will produce what reflection. The philosopher will prove that the sun is a large body; the astronomer will compute exactly how large, working forward by trial and observation — but to work forward at all he must borrow certain principles. And no art stands on its own feet if its foundation is held on loan. Philosophy asks no favors of anyone; it raises its whole structure on its own ground. Mathematics is, so to speak, built on the next man's property — it builds on borrowed soil. It accepts its first principles as a favor, and only by that favor reaches its further conclusions. If it could march unassisted to the truth, if it could grasp the nature of the whole universe, I would say it offered real help to our minds — for the mind grows by contact with things heavenly, and draws down something from above. But there is only one thing that brings the soul to perfection: the unshakable knowledge of good and evil. And no other art investigates good and evil.

I would like to run through the virtues one by one. Bravery scorns everything that inspires fear; it looks down on, challenges, and crushes the powers of terror and everything that would drive our freedom under the yoke. Do "liberal studies" strengthen that virtue? Loyalty is the holiest good in the human heart. No compulsion can force it into betrayal; no reward can bribe it. Loyalty cries: "Burn me, cut me, kill me — I will not betray my trust; and the harder torture digs for my secret, the deeper I will bury it!" Can the "liberal arts" produce that spirit in us? Self-control governs our desires: some it hates and routs outright; others it disciplines back to a healthy measure; and it never goes near a desire for the desire's own sake. It knows that the best measure for appetite is not what you want to take but what you ought to take. Kindness forbids you to be overbearing with your companions, and forbids you to be grasping. In words, in deeds, in feelings, it shows itself gentle and courteous to everyone. It counts no trouble as merely someone else's. And the chief reason it loves its own good is that someday that good will belong to someone else. Do "liberal studies" teach a man this kind of character? No — no more than they teach simplicity, moderation and self-restraint, thrift and economy, or the mercy that spares a neighbor's life as if it were its own and knows that no man is a thing for other men to waste.

"But," comes the objection, "since you declare that virtue can't be attained without the liberal studies, how can you deny that they assist virtue?" Because you can't attain virtue without food either, and food has nothing to do with virtue. Wood gives no assistance to a ship, even though a ship can't be built without wood. There is no reason, I tell you, to think a thing is made by the help of whatever it can't be made without. And we could actually go further: it is possible to attain wisdom without the liberal studies at all. Virtue has to be learned, yes — but it isn't learned through them.

And really, why should I assume that a man who doesn't know his letters can never be wise, when wisdom isn't found in letters? Wisdom deals in facts, not words — and perhaps memory is more dependable when it has nothing to lean on outside itself. Wisdom is a large and spacious thing. It needs room to breathe. One must learn about things divine and human, the past and the future, the perishable and the eternal — and about Time. Look how many questions arise about time alone: first, whether it is anything in and of itself; second, whether anything exists before time and apart from time; and again, did time begin along with the universe — or, since something existed even before the universe began, did time exist then too? About the soul alone the questions are countless: where it comes from, what it is, when it begins to exist, how long it lasts; whether it moves from place to place, changing its lodgings, passed on from one animal shape to another, or serves only a single term and then roams the universe once it is set free; whether it is corporeal or not; what it will do when it stops using us as its instrument; how it will spend its freedom once it has escaped this present prison; whether it forgets its whole past, and begins to know itself only at the moment when, released from the body, it has withdrawn to the skies.

So whatever province of things human and divine you take up, you will be worn out by the sheer mass of what must be answered and what must be learned. And to give these vast and weighty subjects free lodging in your soul, you must throw out everything superfluous. Virtue will not squeeze herself into our narrow spaces; a great thing needs wide room to move in. Drive everything else out; empty the whole chest for virtue.

"But it's a pleasure to be acquainted with many arts." Then let us keep only as much of them as we actually need. Do you blame the man who ranks the superfluous alongside the useful and fills his house with a parade of costly objects — and then not blame the man who has buried himself in the useless furniture of learning? Wanting to know more than enough is its own kind of intemperance. Why? Because this unseemly chase after the liberal arts makes men tiresome, wordy, tactless, self-satisfied bores, who fail to learn the essentials precisely because they've learned the non-essentials. Didymus the scholar wrote four thousand books. I would pity him if he had merely read that many useless volumes. In those books he investigates Homer's birthplace, who Aeneas's real mother was, whether Anacreon was more of a lecher or more of a drunkard, whether Sappho was a loose woman — and other questions whose answers, if ever found, ought to be forgotten on the spot. Now don't tell me that life is long! And when you turn to our own countrymen, I can show you plenty of works there too that deserve the axe.

It costs an enormous outlay of time, and enormous wear on other people's ears, to win the compliment: "What a learned man you are!" Let's settle for the plainer, more countrified one: "What a good man you are!" Do I mean it? Should I unroll the annals of the world's history and hunt for the first man who wrote poetry? Should I estimate, without any written records, the number of years between Orpheus and Homer? Should I study Aristarchus's absurd notations, in which he branded the text of other men's verses, and wear my life away on syllables? Shall I go wallow in the geometer's dust? Have I so completely forgotten that useful saying, "Save your time"? Must I know these things? Then what may I choose not to know? Apion the scholar — who drew crowds to his lectures all over Greece in the days of Gaius Caesar, and whom every city hailed as a second Homer — used to maintain that Homer, after finishing his two poems, the Iliad and the Odyssey, added a preliminary poem embracing the whole Trojan war. His proof? That Homer had deliberately planted in his opening line two letters containing a key to the number of his books. A man who wants to know many things must know things like that — and must take no account of all the time stolen from him by illness, public duties, private duties, daily duties, and sleep. Take the measure of your years; they have no room for all of this.

So far I have been speaking of the liberal studies. But think how much superfluous, unusable matter the philosophers carry around! They too have stooped to drawing fine divisions between syllables, to fixing the true meanings of conjunctions and prepositions — they have grown jealous of the grammarians, jealous of the mathematicians. Everything superfluous in those arts they have imported into their own, and the result is that they know more about speaking carefully than about living carefully. Let me tell you what evil this over-fine exactness does, and what an enemy of truth it is! Protagoras declares that you can argue either side of any question with equal success — including this very question, whether every question can be argued from either side. Nausiphanes holds that among the things which seem to exist, there is no difference between existing and not existing. Parmenides maintains that of everything which seems to exist, nothing exists except the universe alone. Zeno of Elea swept away all the difficulties by sweeping away one: he declares that nothing exists. The Pyrrhonean, Megarian, Eretrian, and Academic schools are all working at much the same job; they have introduced a new branch of knowledge — non-knowledge. Sweep the whole lot of them in with the superfluous troop of "liberal" studies. The one group of men gives me knowledge that will be of no use to me; the other takes away all hope of knowledge whatsoever. Better, of course, to know useless things than to know nothing. One set offers no lamp by which I might steer my gaze toward the truth; the other digs out my very eyes and leaves me blind. If I follow Protagoras, nothing in the whole of nature is anything but doubtful. If I follow Nausiphanes, only one thing is sure — that nothing is sure. If Parmenides, there is nothing but the One; if Zeno, not even the One.

Then what are we? What becomes of all these things that surround us, support us, sustain us? The whole universe turns out to be an empty or deceptive shadow. And I honestly cannot say which men annoy me more — the ones who would have it that we know nothing, or the ones who won't leave us even that.

Farewell.
