Letters 96–98

Letter 96: On Facing Hardships

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Still grumbling? Still complaining, after everything? Don't you see that in all the evils you list there is really only one — the fact that you grumble and complain? If you ask me, nothing is miserable for a man unless there is something in the universe he has decided to call miserable. The day I find anything in my life unendurable, I will have become unendurable to myself.

I'm sick — that's part of my lot. My slaves have come down with fever, my income has dropped, my house is falling apart; losses, accidents, hard labor, and alarms have hit me from every side — well, that's a common story. No, that was an understatement: it's an inevitable story. Things like this come by assignment, not by accident. If you'll believe me, I'm opening my inmost feelings to you right now: whenever everything seems to go hard and uphill, I have trained myself not merely to obey God but to agree with his decisions. I follow him because my soul chooses to, not because I have to. Nothing will ever happen to me that I'll receive with a sour temper or a long face. There is no tax I won't pay willingly. And all the things that make us groan and flinch are simply the tax of living — things, my dear Lucilius, you should never hope to escape, and never ask to.

It was disease of the bladder that had you frightened; gloomy letters kept arriving; you kept getting worse. Let me come a little closer to the truth: you were afraid for your life. But come now — when you prayed for a long life, didn't you know that this was what you were praying for? A long life contains all these troubles, just as a long journey contains dust and mud and rain. "But," you cry, "I wanted to live, and to be immune from every ill at the same time." A cry like that does no credit to a man. Here is a prayer of mine instead — consider the spirit you should receive it in, because I offer it in not just a good spirit but a noble one: "May the gods and goddesses never allow Fortune to keep you in luxury!" Ask yourself honestly: if some god gave you the choice, would you rather live your life in a tavern or in an army camp?

And life, Lucilius, is a soldier's service. So the men who are tossed about at sea, who slog uphill and downhill over punishing crags, who draw the campaigns of greatest danger — these are the heroes, the front-rank fighters. The ones who loll in rotten luxury while other men sweat are turtle-doves: safe only because nobody thinks them worth striking.

Farewell.

Letter 97: On the Degeneracy of the Age

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You're mistaken, my dear Lucilius, if you think that luxury, the neglect of decent manners, and the other vices every man charges against his own times belong especially to our era. They are the vices of mankind, not of the times. No age has been free of guilt. In fact, once you start auditing the misconduct of any particular period, you will find — to humanity's shame be it said — that sin never walked around more openly than in Cato's own presence.

Would anyone believe that money changed hands at the trial of Clodius? He stood accused of secret adultery with Caesar's wife, and of profaning the ritual of that sacrifice which is offered on behalf of the people — the rite from which every male is banished so strictly that even pictures of male animals are covered up. And yet the jury was bribed; and — fouler still than the bribe — the seduction of married women and noble youths was demanded and thrown in as a kind of bonus payment. The charge contained less sin than the acquittal did: the defendant in an adultery case parceled out adulteries, and could not feel sure of his own safety until he had made the jurors criminals like himself. All this was done at a trial where Cato gave evidence — though that was his only part in the affair.

I'll quote Cicero's actual words, because the facts are too bad for belief: "He summoned them, made promises, pleaded, gave gifts. And more than that — merciful heavens, what a rotten state of affairs! — on several of the jurors, to round out their pay, he even bestowed nights with certain women and introductions to youths of noble family." The bribe itself isn't worth our shock; the extras were worse. "Would you like the wife of A., that solemn prig? Done. Or of B., the millionaire? I'll guarantee you sleep with her. Fail to commit adultery, and you're free to convict Clodius. That beauty you've been wanting will pay you a visit — I promise you a night in her company without delay, and my promise will be honored inside the legal period of postponement." Parceling out crimes like that means more than committing them; it means blackmailing the dignified married women of Rome. These Clodius jurors had asked the Senate for a bodyguard — a favor only a jury intending to convict could have needed — and the request was granted. Hence the witty remark of Catulus after the defendant got off: "Why did you ask us for the guard? Were you afraid of having your money stolen?" And amid jokes like these a man went unpunished who before the trial was an adulterer, during the trial a pimp — a man who escaped conviction more foully than he had earned it.

Can you believe any morals were ever more corrupt than those — when lust could not keep its hands off either religious worship or the courts of law, when in the very inquiry the Senate convened by special order, more crime was committed than investigated? The question on trial was whether a man could be safe after committing adultery; the answer turned out to be that he could not be safe without committing it. And all this bargaining went on in the presence of Pompey and Caesar, of Cicero and Cato — yes, the very Cato whose presence, they say, made the crowd give up demanding the usual routine of naked actresses at the Floralia, the spring festival. If you can believe it: men behaved more strictly at a festival than in a courtroom! Such things will be done in the future, as they have been done in the past; and the licentiousness of cities will sometimes ebb through discipline and fear — never on its own.

So don't believe that ours is the generation that has yielded most to lust and least to law. Today's young men live far more simply than the young men of an era when a defendant denied an adultery charge before his judges and his judges confessed to it before the defendant — when debauchery was practiced to secure a verdict, and Clodius, in favor thanks to the very vices he was on trial for, played the pimp during the actual hearing of his case. Could anyone believe it? The man whom one adultery would have condemned was acquitted because of many. Every age will produce men like Clodius; not every age will produce men like Cato. We slide into degeneracy easily, because we lack neither guides nor companions in our wickedness — and the wickedness marches on even without guides or companions. The road to vice is not just downhill; it is steep. And what makes so many men incorrigible is this: in every other craft, mistakes shame the good craftsman and gall the one who makes them, but the mistakes of life are a positive pleasure. The pilot does not rejoice when his ship goes over on her beam-ends; the doctor does not rejoice when he buries his patient; the lawyer does not rejoice when his client loses the case through his advocate's bungling. But every man enjoys his own crimes. A. delights in an affair — the difficulty was exactly what attracted him. B. delights in forgery and theft, and is displeased with his sin only when his sin has missed its mark. All of this is what perverted habit produces.

And yet — here is the other side, and it proves that even souls led into the most depraved ways keep a buried sense of right conduct, that men are not ignorant of what evil is but indifferent to it — all men hide their sins. However well things turn out, they enjoy the profits while concealing how they got them. A good conscience wants to step forward and be seen; wickedness is afraid even of the shadows. So I think Epicurus put it very aptly: "The guilty man may have the luck to remain hidden; the confidence that he will remain hidden he can never have." Or, if you think the meaning comes across more clearly this way: "Concealment does wrongdoers no good, because even when they have the luck of it, they never have the assurance of it." What I mean is this: crimes can be well guarded; they cannot be carefree.

This view, I maintain, is not at odds with the principles of our school, if it is explained properly. Why? Because the first and greatest punishment of sinners is the fact of having sinned. Crime may be decked out with Fortune's favors; she may protect it, take it under her wing — it can never go unpunished, since the punishment of the crime lies in the crime itself. But hard on the heels of that first punishment come the second ones: constant fear, constant dread, and distrust of one's own safety.

Why, then, should I let wickedness off with anything less? Why shouldn't I leave it hanging forever in suspense? Let us disagree with Epicurus on the one point — when he declares that nothing is just by nature, and that crime should be avoided only because the fear it breeds cannot be avoided. But let us agree with him on the other: that bad deeds are lashed by the whip of conscience, and that conscience suffers its worst torture because endless anxiety drives and flogs it on, and it can never trust the guarantors of its own peace of mind. For this, Epicurus, is exactly the proof that we shrink from crime by nature: even in perfect safety, there is no guilty man who does not feel afraid. Luck frees many men from punishment; it frees no man from fear. Why should that be, if not that nature has condemned certain things, and a loathing for them is bred into us? That is why even men who hide their sins can never count on staying hidden: their conscience convicts them and shows them to themselves. To be afraid is the very property of guilt. With so many crimes escaping the law and its prescribed punishments, we would have come off badly — except that those grievous offenses against nature must pay their penalty in ready cash, and where the punishment does not fall, the fear falls in its place.

Farewell.

Letter 98: On the Fickleness of Fortune

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Never believe that anyone who depends on happiness is happy. Delight in things that come from outside is a fragile prop; the joy that walked in will someday walk out. But the joy that springs wholly from within is loyal and sound; it grows, and it stays with us to the last. Everything else the crowd admires is a good for a day only. "What do you mean?" you reply. "Can't such things be useful and delightful too?" Of course they can — but only if they depend on us, and not we on them. Everything Fortune looks upon becomes fruitful and pleasant only if its possessor is also in possession of himself, and is not the property of his own property. For men make a mistake, my dear Lucilius, when they hold that Fortune gives us anything, good or evil. She gives us only the raw material of goods and ills — the beginnings of things which, in our keeping, will grow into good or into evil. The soul is stronger than any Fortune; by its own action it steers its affairs in either direction, and by its own power it makes its life a happy one or a wretched one.

A bad man turns everything bad — even things that arrived wearing the look of the best. An upright and honest man corrects the wrongs of Fortune; he softens hardship and bitterness because he knows how to endure them; he accepts prosperity with gratitude and moderation, and stands against trouble with steadiness and courage. A man may be prudent, may weigh every interest with balanced judgment, may attempt nothing beyond his strength — he will still never reach the good that is pure and beyond the reach of threats unless he is sure in his dealings with the unsure. Whether you prefer to watch other men (it is easier to make up your mind when the affairs you're judging are someone else's) or to watch yourself with every prejudice set aside, you will see and admit that none of these desirable, beloved things is any use unless you have armed yourself against the fickleness of chance and everything that follows in its train — unless, at every mishap, you repeat to yourself often and without complaint: "Heaven decreed otherwise." No — let us take a phrase braver and nearer the truth, one that will prop your spirit more safely. Whenever things turn out against your expectation, say to yourself: "Heaven decreed better."

To a man so poised, nothing will come as a blow. And a man will be so poised if he reflects on the possible ups and downs of human affairs before he feels their force — if he regards his children, his wife, his property as things he will not necessarily have forever, and things whose loss will not make him one bit more wretched. It is a tragic soul that dreads the future, that is wretched in advance of wretchedness, eaten up with anxiety that the things it delights in should stay in its hands to the very end. Such a soul will never be at rest; waiting for what is coming, it will lose the blessings in front of it — the ones it could actually enjoy. And there is no difference between grief over a thing lost and the fear of losing it.

Not that I'm advising you to be careless. No — steer away from whatever might cause fear. Whatever planning can foresee, foresee. Whatever is likely to hurt you, watch for it and turn it aside long before it happens. Your best help in this will be a confident spirit and a mind firmly resolved to endure all things. The man who can bear Fortune can also beware of Fortune. At the very least, calm seas throw up no breakers. And nothing is more wretched or more foolish than fearing ahead of schedule. What madness it is to run out to meet your troubles! In short, let me compress my thought and sketch you those busybodies, those self-tormentors: they are as unrestrained in the middle of their troubles as they were before them. He who suffers before it is necessary suffers more than is necessary. The same weakness that keeps such men from being ready for suffering keeps them from ever measuring it; and with the same lack of restraint they fondly imagine that their luck will last forever, and fondly imagine that their gains are bound not merely to continue but to grow. They forget the springboard on which all mortal things are tossed, and guarantee for themselves — of all people — that the gifts of chance will hold steady.

For exactly this reason I think the saying of Metrodorus is excellent — from the letter of consolation he wrote to his sister on the loss of her son, a boy of great promise: "All the good of mortals is mortal." He means the goods men run after in shoals. The real good does not perish; it is certain and lasting, and it consists of wisdom and virtue — the one immortal thing granted to mortals. But men are so wayward, so forgetful of where they are headed and of the point toward which every single day is shoving them, that they are surprised to lose anything — although one day they are bound to lose everything. Whatever you are listed as owner of is in your possession, but it is not yours; nothing weak has strength, nothing frail is lasting and invincible. We must lose our lives as surely as we lose our property — and this, if we understand the truth, is itself a consolation. Lose calmly; you must lose your life as well.

What resource is left to us, then, against these losses? Just this: to keep the things we have lost in memory, and not let the enjoyment we drew from them slip away along with them. To have can be taken from us; to have had, never. A man is thankless in the extreme if, after losing a thing, he feels no debt for ever having received it. Chance robs us of the thing, but leaves us its use and its enjoyment — and it is we who throw that away by the unfairness of our regret. Say to yourself: "Of all these things that look so frightful, not one is unbeatable. Each one has been beaten by many before now: fire by Mucius, crucifixion by Regulus, poison by Socrates, exile by Rutilius, death by the sword by Cato. Let us, too, beat something." And the things that lure the crowd with their look of beauty and happiness — those, too, have been scorned by many men, on many occasions. Fabricius refused riches when he was a general, and condemned them when he was censor. Tubero judged poverty worthy of himself and of the god on the Capitol when he set out earthenware dishes at a public feast — showing that a man should be content with what the gods themselves could still use. The elder Sextius turned down the honors of office: he was born with an obligation to take part in public affairs, and yet he would not accept the broad stripe, the senator's badge, even when the deified Julius offered it to him. For he understood that what can be given can also be taken away.

Let us, then, do something courageous of our own accord — let us take our place among the great examples of history. Why have we gone slack? Why do we lose heart? Whatever could be done once can be done now, if only we cleanse our souls and follow Nature. Stray from Nature, and you are condemned to crave, to fear, and to slave for the things of chance. We may return to the true path; we may be restored to our proper state. Let us be restored — so that we can endure pain in whatever form it attacks the body, and say to Fortune: "You are dealing with a man. Go find someone you can beat."

By these words, and words like them, the anger of the ulcer is being soothed; and I truly hope it can be reduced — either cured, or at least halted, left to grow old along with the patient himself. But I am easy in my mind about him. What we are discussing now is our own loss: the taking-off of a most excellent old man. He himself has lived a full life; anything added to it he craves not for his own sake, but for the sake of those who need his services. In going on living, he is being generous. Another man might have put an end to these sufferings; but our friend considers it no less shameful to flee toward death than to flee from it. "But," comes the objection, "if circumstances call for it, won't he make his exit?" Of course — if he can no longer be useful to anyone, if his one remaining occupation would be pain. This, my dear Lucilius, is what we mean by studying philosophy in practice, by drilling on the real thing: watching how much courage a prudent man has against death, and against pain, when the one draws near and the other bears down. What ought to be done must be learned from a man who does it. Up to now we have dealt in arguments — whether anyone can resist pain, whether the approach of death can bring down even great souls. Why argue further? Here is the fact itself, right in front of us: death does not make our friend braver against pain, and pain does not make him braver against death. Rather, he trusts himself against both. He does not suffer patiently because he hopes for death, and he does not die gladly because he is tired of suffering. Pain he endures; death he awaits.

Farewell.
