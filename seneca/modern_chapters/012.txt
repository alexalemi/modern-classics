Letters 50–53

Letter 50: On Our Blindness and Its Cure

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Your letter reached me many months after you sent it, so I saw no point in asking the courier what you were up to. He'd need a remarkable memory to recall that far back! But I hope by now you're living in such a way that I know what you're up to no matter where you are. And what else would you be up to except improving yourself a little every day, dropping some error, and learning that the faults you blame on your circumstances are actually in you? We love to pin certain vices on the place or the time; but those vices will follow us wherever we move.

You know Harpaste, my wife's clown — she's still in my house, a burden that came with a legacy. Personally I have no taste for these sideshow characters; if I ever want to laugh at a fool, I don't have to look far. I can laugh at myself. Well, this clown suddenly went blind. I know the story sounds unbelievable, but I promise you it's true: she doesn't know she's blind. She keeps asking her attendant to move her to different rooms; she says her apartment is too dark.

You can see at once that what makes us smile at Harpaste happens to every one of us. No one understands that he himself is greedy, or that he is grasping. The blind at least ask for a guide; we wander without one, saying: "I'm not ambitious — but nobody can live at Rome any other way. I'm not extravagant — but city life simply demands a big budget. It isn't my fault that I have a temper, or that I haven't settled on any definite plan of life; blame my youth for that." Why do we lie to ourselves? Our trouble isn't outside us. It's inside us, lodged deep in our vitals — and that is exactly why health is so hard to reach: we don't know we're sick.

Suppose we do begin the cure — when will we shake off all these diseases in full bloom? Right now we won't even call in the doctor, whose job would be far easier if he were brought in while the illness was young. A tender, inexperienced mind would follow his advice if he pointed out the right way. No one finds it hard to return to nature except the person who has deserted her. We blush to be taught good sense; but honestly, if it's beneath us to look for a teacher of this art, we should also give up hoping that so great a good will drop into us by accident.

No — we have to work. And to tell the truth, even the work isn't great, provided we begin to shape and rebuild our souls before the crookedness hardens in. But I don't despair even of the hardened case. There is nothing that won't surrender to persistent treatment, to focused and careful attention. However bent the timber, you can straighten it again; heat unbends curved beams, and wood that grew naturally into one shape gets worked into whatever shape we need. How much more easily is the soul reshaped — pliable as it is, more yielding than any liquid! For what is the soul but air in a certain condition? And air, you notice, is more adaptable than anything else precisely because it's thinner than anything else.

So there is nothing, Lucilius, to keep you from having high hopes for us, just because evil has us in its grip right now, or because it has held us a long while. No one gets a good mind before a bad one; the bad mind takes first possession of us all. Learning virtue means unlearning vice. And we should attack the job of freeing ourselves from our faults with all the more courage because, once the good is handed over to us, it's ours forever. Virtue is not unlearned. Opposites sit uneasily where they don't belong, which is why they can be driven out and hustled away; but what arrives in its rightful place stays faithfully put. Virtue is according to nature; vice is her opponent and her enemy. But though virtues, once admitted, cannot leave and are easy to guard, the first steps toward them are hard going, because a weak and sickly mind is afraid of anything unfamiliar. So the mind has to be forced to make a beginning. After that, the medicine isn't bitter; it starts to please us the moment it starts to cure us. With other treatments the pleasure comes only after the recovery; a draught of philosophy is wholesome and sweet in the same swallow.

Farewell.
Letter 51: On Baiae and Morals

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Every man does the best he can, my dear Lucilius! You over there have Etna, Sicily's towering and most celebrated mountain — though I can't work out why Messala (or was it Valgius? I've been reading both) called it "unique," since plenty of regions belch fire, and not just the high ones, where it happens more often — presumably because fire climbs as high as it can — but low ground too. As for me, I do the best I can: I've had to settle for Baiae. And I left it the day after I arrived. Baiae is a place to avoid, because for all its natural advantages, luxury has claimed it as her own private resort.

"What," you say, "should any place be singled out as an enemy?" Not at all. But just as one style of clothing suits the wise and upright man better than another — not because he hates any particular color, but because he thinks some colors don't fit a person who has chosen the simple life — so there are also places that the wise man, or the man on his way to wisdom, will avoid as bad company for good character. If he's planning his retreat from the world, he won't pick Canopus (though Canopus doesn't forbid anybody to live simply), and he won't pick Baiae either: both have become headquarters of vice. At Canopus luxury pampers itself to the limit; at Baiae it's looser still, as if the place itself licensed a certain amount of misbehavior.

We ought to choose surroundings that are healthy not only for the body but for the character. I wouldn't want to live in a torture chamber; I don't want to live in a nightclub either. Watching drunks stagger along the beach, the rowdy carousing of the boat parties, the lakes ringing with singing troupes, and all the other ways luxury — off the leash of the law, so to speak — not only sins but advertises its sins: why should I have to watch all that? We should put the greatest possible distance between ourselves and whatever provokes vice. We should toughen our minds and drag them far away from the seductions of pleasure. One winter unstrung Hannibal: the soft living of Campania took the fight out of the hero whom Alpine snows could not break. He conquered with his weapons and was conquered by his vices.

We too have a war to fight — a kind of war that allows no rest and no leave. The first enemy to be beaten is pleasure, which, as you see, has carried off even the sternest characters. Anyone who has grasped how big a task he's taken on will know there's no room for daintiness or soft handling. What do I want with those hot baths, or the sweating-rooms where they trap the dry steam that's meant to drain your strength? Sweat should be earned by work.

Suppose we did what Hannibal did — halted mid-campaign, gave up the war, and handed our bodies over to be coddled. Everyone would rightly blame us for quitting early — a dangerous move even for a victor, let alone for a man still fighting his way toward victory. And we have even less right to it than the soldiers under Carthage's flag: we risk more than they did if we slacken, and our labor is greater than theirs even if we press on. Fortune is at war with me, and I will not carry out her orders. I refuse the yoke — no, more than that, I shake off the yoke already on me, which takes even greater courage. The soul must not be pampered. Surrender to pleasure and you have also surrendered to pain, to toil, to poverty. Ambition and anger will demand the same rights over me that pleasure got, and I'll be torn apart — pulled to pieces, rather — among all these competing passions. I have set freedom before my eyes, and that is the prize I'm working for. And what is freedom, you ask? Being a slave to nothing — no circumstance, no compulsion, no accident — and forcing Fortune to meet me on level ground. The day I know I have the upper hand, her power is nothing. Shall I take orders from her when death is in my own hands?

A man occupied with thoughts like these should choose an austere and clean place to live. Surroundings that are too pleasant weaken the spirit, and there's no doubt that where you live can sap your vigor. Animals whose hooves have hardened on rough ground can travel any road; fatten them on soft, marshy meadow and their hooves soon wear through. The bravest soldiers come from stony country; the town-bred and the house-raised are slow off the mark. The hand that goes straight from plough to sword never complains of the work, but your sleek, well-dressed dandy folds at the first cloud of dust. Training in rugged country makes a character strong and fits it for great undertakings. It was more honorable for Scipio to spend his exile at Liternum than at Baiae; a fall like his didn't need so soft a setting. Even the men into whose hands Rome's rising fortunes first poured the wealth of the state — Gaius Marius, Gnaeus Pompey, Caesar — did build villas near Baiae, but they set them on the very tops of the mountains. It seemed more like a soldier's way, to look down from a height on the lands spread wide below. Look at the sites they chose, the positions, the style of building, and you'll see they weren't country houses — they were camps. Do you imagine Cato would ever have lived in a pleasure palace so he could count the loose women sailing past, the barges painted every color there is, the roses drifting across the lake — or so he could listen to serenaders brawling in the night? Wouldn't he rather have stayed behind a trench he'd dug with his own hands to hold for a single night? Wouldn't any real man rather have his sleep broken by a war-trumpet than by a chorus of serenaders?

But I've ranted against Baiae long enough — though against vice, never long enough. Vice, Lucilius, is what I want you to hound, without limit and without end, for it has neither limit nor end. If any vice is tearing at your heart, throw it away from you; and if it can't be pried loose any other way, tear out your heart along with it. Above all, drive pleasures out of your sight. Hate them beyond everything else. They are like those bandits the Egyptians call "lovers" — they embrace us only to strangle us.

Farewell.
Letter 52: On Choosing Our Teachers

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

What is this force, Lucilius, that drags us one way when we're aiming another — that pushes us toward the very place we're longing to leave? What is it that wrestles with our spirit and never lets us settle a desire once and for all? We lurch from plan to plan. Not one of our wishes is free, not one is unqualified, not one lasts. "But that's the fool's problem," you say. "Nothing suits him for long." Yes — but how and when do we tear ourselves loose from that folly? No one has strength enough to climb out on his own. Someone has to reach down a hand; someone has to pull us clear.

Epicurus says that certain men have worked their way to the truth without anyone's help, cutting their own path — and he saves his special praise for these, whose drive came from within, who pushed to the front on their own. Others, he says, need outside help: they won't move unless someone leads the way, but they'll follow faithfully. Metrodorus, he says, was one of these. That kind of man is excellent too, but second class. And we're not first class ourselves; we'll be well treated if we're admitted to the second. Don't sneer at a man who can only be saved with someone else's help, either — the will to be saved counts for a great deal too.

You'll find yet a third class of man, and not one to despise: men who have to be forced and driven into doing right, who need not so much a guide as someone to encourage them — practically to shove them along. If you want a specimen of this pattern too, Epicurus tells us Hermarchus was one. And of the last two classes, he's readier to congratulate the one, but he has more respect for the other: both reached the same goal, and it's a greater credit to have gotten the same result out of tougher material.

Imagine two buildings, equal in height and grandeur but nothing alike underneath. One went up on flawless ground, and construction ran straight ahead. The other swallowed its materials into the foundations, sunk into soft and shifting soil, with enormous labor wasted just reaching solid rock. Looking at the two, you see plainly everything the first has achieved, while the larger and harder part of the second is hidden. It's the same with human dispositions. Some are pliable and easy to manage; others have to be hammered out by hand, so to speak, and spend all their effort laying their own foundations. So I'd call the man who has never had trouble with himself the luckier one — but the other man, I feel, has deserved better of himself: he won a victory over the meanness of his own nature, and he didn't stroll to wisdom. He wrestled his way there.

You can be sure this stubborn nature, the kind that demands heavy labor, is the one planted in us. There are obstacles in the road — so let's fight, and call in some reinforcements. "Whom shall I call?" you say. "This man, or that one?" There's another option open to you: go to the ancients. They have the time to help you. Help can come not only from the living but from the dead. Among the living, though, let's choose — not the men who pour out words with the greatest fluency, grinding out platitudes and putting on their own little private exhibitions — not those, I say, but men who teach us by their lives: men who tell us what to do and then prove it by doing it, who show us what to avoid and are never caught doing what they've ordered us to avoid.

Choose as your guide a man you admire more when you see him act than when you hear him speak. I wouldn't stop you from listening to the philosophers who hold public lectures and debates, provided they come before the crowd for the purpose of improving themselves and their hearers, and not to advance their own careers. For what is uglier than philosophy fishing for applause? Does the patient praise the surgeon in the middle of the operation? Be silent, be reverent, and submit to the cure. Even if you shout your approval, I'll hear it as the groan of a man whose sores are being probed. You want to show that you're paying attention, that the grandeur of the subject stirs you? You may — at the proper time. I'll certainly allow you to judge and cast your vote for the better course. Pythagoras made his pupils keep silent for five years; do you think that entitled them to burst into instant applause?

How mad is the man who walks out of a lecture hall happy — because the ignorant clapped! Why does it please you to be praised by men you couldn't praise yourself? Fabianus used to speak to popular audiences, but his listeners kept their self-control. Now and then a great shout of praise would break out, but it was set off by the greatness of his subject, not by the sound of speech sliding out smooth and soft. Let there be a difference between the applause of the theater and the applause of the school; there is a decent way even to give praise. Watch closely, and every act is significant; you can read character in the smallest signs. The lecher is betrayed by his walk, by a movement of the hand, sometimes by a single answer, by a finger touching his head, by a shifting eye. The scoundrel is exposed by his laugh; the madman by his face and bearing. These things show themselves by their marks — and you can tell the character of any man by watching how he gives and receives praise. The philosopher's audience stretch out admiring hands from every corner, and sometimes the adoring crowd practically hangs over the speaker's head. But if you really understand what you're seeing, that isn't praise; it's just applause. Leave the shouting to the arts whose business is pleasing the crowd; let philosophy be worshipped in silence. Young men, true, must sometimes be given free rein to follow an impulse — but only at moments when it really is impulse, when they can't force themselves to stay silent. Praise like that encourages the listeners themselves and spurs the young mind. But let them be stirred by the matter, not the style; otherwise eloquence does them harm, making them fall in love with itself and not with the subject.

I'll set this topic aside for now; it deserves a long study of its own — how the public should be addressed, what liberties a speaker may take with a crowd, and what a crowd may take with a speaker. There's no doubt philosophy has lost something now that she has put her charms up for sale. But she can still be seen in her sanctuary — if the man presenting her is a priest and not a peddler.

Farewell.
Letter 53: On the Faults of the Spirit

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

You can talk me into almost anything now: I was recently talked into traveling by water. We cast off on a lazily smooth sea. The sky, I admit, was loaded with dirty clouds, the kind that usually break into rain or squalls; but I figured the few miles from Puteoli to your dear Parthenope could be dashed off quickly, doubtful, lowering sky or not. So to get away faster I struck straight out to sea toward Nesis, meaning to cut across all the inlets. When we were far enough out that it hardly mattered whether I turned back or kept going, the smooth water that had lured me out vanished. It wasn't a storm yet, but the ground-swell was running, and the waves kept coming faster. I began asking the pilot to put me ashore somewhere. He said the coast was rough, with nowhere to land, and that in bad weather nothing frightened him like a lee shore. But I was suffering too badly to think about danger: I was in the grip of that sluggish seasickness that brings no relief — the kind that churns the bile without clearing it. So I laid down the law to my pilot and forced him, like it or not, to make for shore. As we drew near, I didn't wait for things to be done by Virgil's book, until

  Prow faced seawards

or

  Anchor plunged from bow;

I remembered my credentials as a veteran devotee of cold water, and, cloak and all, I let myself down into the sea, just as a cold-water bather should. What do you think I went through, scrambling over the rocks, hunting out a path or making one? I understood then that sailors have good reason to fear the land. It's hard to believe what I endured when I could not endure myself. Take my word for it: the reason Ulysses got shipwrecked at every possible opportunity wasn't so much that the sea-god had a grudge against him from birth — he was simply prone to seasickness. And I too, whenever I have to go anywhere by sea, will reach my destination in the twentieth year.

Once I'd finally settled my stomach (you know one doesn't escape seasickness just by escaping the sea) and refreshed my body with a rubdown, I began to reflect on how completely we forget or ignore our failings — even the bodily ones, which keep reminding us that they exist, to say nothing of the failings that hide deeper as they grow more serious. A slight chill fools us; but when it builds and a genuine fever catches fire, it forces a confession even out of a hardy man who can take a great deal of suffering: I am ill. There's a pain in the foot, a prickling in the joints; we still cover the complaint up and announce we've twisted an ankle or overdone the exercise. While the ailment is uncertain and just beginning, it hunts for a name; but once it starts swelling the ankles too, and has turned both our feet into "right" feet, there's no way around it — we have to confess it's gout.

With diseases of the soul, the opposite holds: the worse a man has one, the less he feels it. Don't be surprised at that, my dear Lucilius. A light sleeper follows visions in his sleep, and sometimes even knows, while sleeping, that he's asleep; but heavy slumber wipes out the very dreams and sinks the spirit so deep it has no sense of itself at all. Why will no man confess his faults? Because he's still inside them. Only the man who has woken up can tell his dream; confessing your faults is proof of a sound mind.

So let us rouse ourselves, so that we can catch our own mistakes and correct them. But philosophy is the only power that can wake us, the only power that can shake us out of this deep sleep. Devote yourself to her entirely. You are worthy of her; she is worthy of you — fall into each other's arms. Say goodbye to every other interest, boldly and openly. There is no studying philosophy in your spare time.

If you were ill, you'd drop your personal affairs and forget your business; you wouldn't think enough of any client to go plead his case during a mild afternoon between attacks. You'd try your hardest to be rid of the illness as fast as possible. Well then — won't you do the same thing now? Push every obstacle aside and clear your time for getting a sound mind; no one reaches it while busy with other things. Philosophy wields her own authority; she appoints her own hours and doesn't accept whatever slot you assign her. She is not a hobby for odd moments but a daily practice; she is mistress, and she commands our attendance. Alexander, when some city promised him part of its territory and half its property, answered: "I didn't invade Asia to accept whatever you might give — you'll keep whatever I might leave." Philosophy says the same to every other occupation: "I do not intend to take the time you have left over. You will keep the time I leave."

Turn to her, then, with your whole soul; sit at her feet; cherish her — and a great distance will open between you and other men. You'll be far ahead of all mortals, and the gods will not be far ahead of you. What will be the difference between you and the gods, you ask? They will live longer. But truly, it takes a great artist to fit a full likeness into a miniature. The wise man's life spreads as wide for him as all eternity does for a god. And on one point the sage actually beats the god: the god is free from fear by the gift of nature; the wise man, by his own gift. What a thing that is — to have the weaknesses of a man and the serenity of a god! The power of philosophy to blunt every blow of chance is beyond belief. No missile can lodge in her body; she is well guarded and impenetrable. Some spears she simply robs of their force, shrugging them off with the loose folds of her gown as if they had no power to harm; others she knocks aside and hurls back so hard they rebound on the sender.

Farewell.
