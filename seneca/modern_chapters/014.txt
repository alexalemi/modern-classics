Letter 58

Letter 58: On Being

Greetings from Seneca to his friend Lucilius.

Until today I never fully understood how scant of words our language is — how downright poverty-stricken. We happened to be talking about Plato, and a thousand subjects came up that needed names and had none; and there were others that once had names but have lost them, because we became too fussy about using them. But who can afford to be fussy in the middle of poverty? There is an insect the Greeks call oestrus — a gadfly that drives cattle mad and scatters them all over their pasture. Our old word for it was asilus. Don't take my word for it; take Virgil's:

  Near Silarus' groves and the green oaks
  of Alburnus there swarms an insect that the Romans
  call asilus — in Greek the name is oestrus.
  Fierce, with a harsh and strident buzz, it drives
  the panicked herds stampeding through the woods.

From which I gather the word has died out. And, not to keep you waiting too long: there used to be plain, uncompounded words in circulation. Men once said cernere ferro — "to decide it with the sword" — as Virgil again will prove:

  Great heroes, born in many lands, had come
  to settle matters, man to man, with steel.

This "settling of matters" we now express only with the compound decernere; the plain verb has gone obsolete. And the ancients used to say iusso, instead of iussero, for "I shall have ordered" in a conditional clause. Again, don't take it from me; take it from Virgil:

  The rest of the soldiers shall conduct the fight
  with me, where I shall bid.

My purpose in piling up these examples is not to show off how much time I've wasted on the study of language. I only want you to see how many words that were everyday currency in Ennius and Accius have grown moldy with age — and how even Virgil, whose pages are thumbed daily, has had some of his words filched out from under us.

"And what," I suppose you'll say, "is all this preamble for?" I won't keep you in the dark. I want, if possible, to say the word essentia — "essence" — in your hearing and get a favorable reception. If I can't manage that, I'll risk the word anyway and put you out of humor. I have Cicero as my authority for it, and I count him a powerful authority. If you want a more recent witness, I'll cite Fabianus — careful of speech, cultivated, so polished in style that he'll satisfy even our fussy modern taste. For what else can we do, my dear Lucilius? How else are we to find a word for what the Greeks call ousia — the indispensable thing, the natural underpinning of everything? So I beg you: allow me to use essentia. I promise to exercise the privilege you grant me as sparingly as possible; maybe I'll be content merely to hold the right without using it.

Yet what good will your indulgence do me when — look at this — I still can't express in Latin the very idea that gave me my excuse to rail at our language's poverty? And you'll condemn our narrow Roman limits even more when you learn that the word I can't translate is one syllable long. "What word?" you ask. The Greek on: "being." You think I'm slow; you think the translation is lying right there in the open — just say "that which is." But I notice a great difference: you're forcing me to render a noun with a verb. Still, if it must be done, "that which is" it shall be.

A friend of ours, a man of great learning, was saying today that Plato uses this idea in six different senses. I'll explain all six to you — but first I have to point out that there is such a thing as a genus and such a thing as a species.

For the moment we're hunting the primary genus — the one on which all the different species depend, the source of every classification, the term that gathers up all universal ideas. We'll find it if we start from particulars and reckon backward, for the particulars will lead us up to the first notion. "Man" is a species, as Aristotle says; so is "horse"; so is "dog." So we need some common bond that embraces all three and holds them subordinate to itself. What is it? "Animal." Now we have the beginnings of a genus, "animal," containing man, horse, and dog. But some things have life — anima — and yet are not animals: we all agree that plants and trees are alive, which is why we speak of them living and dying. So "living things" takes a still higher place, since animals and plants both fit inside it. Some things, however, lack life — rocks, for instance. So yet another term outranks "living things," and that term is "substance." I'll classify substance by saying that all substances are either animate or inanimate. But there is still something above substance, because we say some things possess substance and other things lack it. What, then, is the term from which everything else derives? The one we gave that awkward name a moment ago: "that which is." For that term divides into species, so that we can say: that which is either possesses substance or lacks it.

This, then, is what genus means — the primary, the original, the "general," to play on the word. There are other genera, of course, but they are special genera. "Man," for example, is a genus: it contains species by nation — Greek, Roman, Parthian; by color — white, black, yellow; and individuals too — Cato, Cicero, Lucretius. Insofar as "man" includes many kinds, it counts as a genus; insofar as it sits beneath a higher term, it counts as a species. But the genus "that which is" is general absolutely: no term stands above it. It is the first word in the classification of things, and everything falls under it.

The Stoics would set yet another genus above this one, more primary still; I'll come to it in a moment, once I've shown that the genus discussed above deserves its first place, capable as it is of including everything. So: I divide "that which is" into two species — things with substance and things without. There is no third class. How do I divide substance? By saying it is either animate or inanimate. How do I divide the animate? "Some things have mind, while others have only life." Or put it this way: "Some things have the power of movement, of walking, of changing place, while others are rooted in the ground and are fed and grow only through their roots." And into what species do I divide animals? They are either perishable or imperishable.

Now, certain Stoics take the primary genus to be "the something," and here is their reasoning. They say: "In the order of nature, some things exist and some things do not — and even the things that do not exist are part of the order of nature. What these are will occur to you at once: centaurs, giants, and every other figment of faulty reasoning, things that have taken on a definite shape even though they have no bodily reality."

But now I return to what I promised: how Plato sorts everything that is into six different classes.

The first class of "that which is" cannot be caught by sight or touch or any of the senses; it can be grasped only by thought. The generic concept — "man" in general — never comes within range of the eyes; a particular man does: Cicero, Cato. Nobody has ever seen "animal"; we think it, we don't see it. What we see is some particular animal — a horse, a dog.

The second class, according to Plato, is that which is prominent and stands out above everything else; this, he says, exists pre-eminently. The word "poet" gets used loosely of anyone who writes verse; but among the Greeks it has come to be the badge of one man. Say "the poet," and everyone knows you mean Homer. So what is the pre-eminent Being? God, surely — greater and more powerful than anything else.

The third class is made up of the things that exist in the proper sense of the term. They are countless, but they lie beyond our sight. "What things?" you ask. Plato's own private furniture, so to speak: he calls them "ideas," and from them all visible things are created, and on their pattern everything is shaped. They are immortal, unchangeable, inviolable. Listen to what an "idea" is — or rather, what Plato takes it to be: "The idea is the everlasting pattern of the things that nature creates." Let me unpack that definition to set the matter in a clearer light. Suppose I want to paint your portrait. You yourself are the pattern of the picture: from your face my mind receives a certain outline, which it then embodies in its own handiwork. The look that teaches and guides me, the pattern I set out to imitate — that is the "idea." Nature possesses an infinite number of such patterns — of men, of fish, of trees — and everything nature has to make is worked out on their model.

In fourth place we'll put "form." To understand what form means you'll have to pay close attention — and hold Plato, not me, to account for the difficulty. But then, there are no fine distinctions without difficulty. A moment ago I used the painter as an illustration. When that artist wanted to paint Virgil in colors, he gazed at Virgil himself. Virgil's outward appearance was the "idea" — the pattern of the intended work. What the artist draws from that pattern and embodies in his own work is the "form." Where's the difference, you ask? The one is the pattern; the other is the shape taken from the pattern and put into the work. The artist follows the one; the other he creates. A statue has a certain look: that look of the statue is the "form." And the pattern itself has a certain look, which the sculptor studied as he carved: that is the "idea." One more distinction, if you want it: the form is in the artist's work; the idea is outside the work — and not just outside it, but prior to it.

The fifth class is the things that exist in the ordinary sense of the term. These are the first things that actually concern us; here belongs everything around us — men, cattle, objects. And into the sixth class goes whatever has a kind of make-believe existence, like void, or time.

Whatever is solid to the sight or the touch, Plato refuses to count among the things that exist in the strict sense. For such things are in flux, endlessly shrinking or growing. None of us is the same man in old age that he was in youth; none of us is the same tomorrow as he was yesterday. Our bodies are swept along like running water; everything we see races past along with time; nothing we look at stands still. I myself, even as I remark on all this change, have changed. This is just what Heraclitus says: "We go down twice into the same river, and yet into a different river." The stream keeps its name, but the water has already flowed past. This is more obvious in a river than in a man, of course; but we mortals are carried past no less swiftly ourselves — which makes me marvel at our madness: we cling with such affection to the body, the most fleeting thing of all, and we fear that some day we may die, when every instant is the death of our previous condition. Won't you stop fearing that what happens every day may happen once?

So much for man — a substance that flows away and slumps, exposed to every blow. But the universe, too, immortal and enduring as it is, changes and never stays the same. It still has within it everything it ever had, but held differently than before: it keeps rearranging itself.

"Very well," you say, "and what good will I get from all this fine reasoning?" None, if you insist on an answer. But just as an engraver rests his eyes when they've been strained too long, calls them away from the work and "feasts" them, as the saying goes, so we ought at times to unbend the mind and refresh it with a little entertainment. Only, let even your entertainment be work; even from amusements, if you stay alert, you'll pick out something worth keeping. That's my habit, Lucilius: from every field of thought, however far from philosophy, I try to extract something and put it to use. And what could be less likely to reform character than the subjects we've just been sifting? How can Plato's "ideas" make me a better man? What can I draw from them to rein in my appetites? Maybe just this: all the things that pander to our senses, that inflame and excite us, Plato denies a place among the things that really exist. They are imaginary; they wear a certain appearance for the moment, but none of them is lasting or solid — and yet we crave them as if they would exist forever, as if we would possess them forever.

We are weak, watery creatures standing in the middle of unrealities. So let us turn our minds toward the things that last. Let us look up at the ideal shapes of all things, hovering on high, and at God moving among them, planning how to defend from death what he could not make deathless — because its material forbade it — and so to overcome by reason the defects of the body. For all things endure, not because they are everlasting, but because they are protected by the care of their governor; what was imperishable would need no guardian. The Master Builder keeps them safe, overriding the weakness of the material by his own power. Let us despise everything so worthless that we're left wondering whether it exists at all.

And let us reflect on this at the same time: if Providence rescues from its perils the world itself, which is no less mortal than we are, then to some extent our own little bodies can be made to linger longer on earth by our own providence — if we learn to control and check the pleasures that destroy the greater part of mankind. Plato himself, by taking pains, made it to old age. He had the luck, true, of a strong and sound body — his very name came from his broad chest — but sea voyages and desperate adventures had drained much of his strength. Even so, by frugal living, by setting a limit on everything that whets the appetite, and by painstaking attention to himself, he reached old age despite the many obstacles. You know, I'm sure, that thanks to his careful living Plato had the good fortune to die on his own birthday, having completed exactly his eighty-first year. Wise men of the East who happened to be in Athens at the time sacrificed to him after his death, convinced that a lifespan so full was too much for a mortal man, since he had rounded out the perfect number of nine times nine. I have no doubt he'd have gladly given back a few of those days — and the sacrifice with them.

Frugal living can carry you to old age; and old age, to my mind, is no more to be refused than it is to be craved. There is a pleasure in being in your own company as long as possible — once you've made yourself worth enjoying. So here is the question on which we must record a judgment: should one shrink from extreme old age and hasten the end by one's own act, instead of waiting for it to come? The man who sits sluggishly waiting for his fate is next door to a coward — just as a man is immoderately fond of wine if he drains the jar dry and sucks up even the dregs. But we'll ask this question too: is the last stretch of life the dregs, or is it the clearest and purest part of all — provided the mind is unimpaired, the senses are still sound and give the spirit their support, and the body is not worn out and dead before its time? For it makes a great deal of difference whether a man is lengthening his life or lengthening his death.

But if the body can no longer perform its service, why shouldn't one free the struggling soul? Perhaps one ought to do it a little before the debt falls due, so that when it does fall due, he isn't unable to pay. And since the danger of living wretchedly is greater than the danger of dying soon, a man is a fool who won't stake a little time for the chance of a great gain. Few men have lasted through extreme old age to death unimpaired; many have lain inert, making no use of themselves. After that, how much crueler do you think it is to have lost a piece of your life than to have lost the right to end it?

Don't hear me out reluctantly, as if this verdict pointed straight at you; just weigh what I say. And it is this: I will not abandon old age, if old age keeps me whole for myself — whole, that is, in my better part. But if it begins to shatter my mind and pull its faculties down piece by piece, if it leaves me not life but mere breath, I shall rush out of a house that is crumbling and tottering. I will not escape illness by dying, so long as the illness is curable and no hindrance to my soul. I will not lay hands on myself because of pain; to die that way is to be defeated. But if I learn that the pain must be endured forever, I shall depart — not because of the pain itself, but because it will block everything I live for. The man who dies just because he is in pain is a weakling and a coward; the man who lives merely to brave out his pain is a fool.

But I'm running on too long — and besides, there's matter here to fill a whole day. And how will a man be able to end his life if he can't end a letter? So farewell — a word you'll read with more pleasure than all my deadly talk about death.
Farewell.
