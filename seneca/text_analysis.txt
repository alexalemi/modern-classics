TEXT ANALYSIS — Moral Letters to Lucilius (Seneca, c. 63–65 AD)
Source: Richard Mott Gummere's translation (Loeb, 1917–25), the full
124 letters from English Wikisource (the only complete public-domain
source; prep.py strips the wiki apparatus, footnotes, and Gummere's
section numbers). 55 files of consecutive letters.

WHAT THIS IS

The last work of Nero's ex-minister, written in retirement while
waiting for the emperor's death order (which came): letters to his
friend Lucilius, procurator of Sicily, that together form a course in
practical Stoicism. Topics run from saving time, crowds, friendship,
and noise above a bathhouse to slavery, suicide, illness, old age, and
death. This is the book Alex's list calls "Letters from a Stoic" —
here complete, all 124 letters, not the usual selection.

STYLE AND TONE

- LETTERS, not essays: each opens from something concrete (your
  letter arrived; I visited my villa; the ships came in; I was sick)
  and pivots to the day's lesson. Keep every concrete opening — they
  are what make the book alive.
- Seneca's voice: an older friend writing daily — urgent, epigrammatic,
  self-deprecating about his own failures ("I am not a wise man and
  never shall be"), dry about wealth he conspicuously has. The modern
  voice is a brilliant mentor's email you'd actually read: warm,
  pointed, quotable.
- THE EPIGRAMS ARE THE PAYLOAD. Seneca coins one-liners constantly;
  render them AS one-liners, sharpened not padded. The early letters
  each end with a "daily wage" quotation (usually Epicurus) — keep
  that closing ritual and its framing jokes about borrowing from a
  rival school.
- Greetings/closings: each letter opens "Greetings from Seneca to his
  friend Lucilius." — standardize exactly that line; letters end
  "Farewell." — keep it as its own closing word.
- Gummere is accurate but stiff ("Vouchsafe", "aught") — de-archaize
  fully. His Latin tags in footnotes are gone; where Seneca quotes
  Virgil/poets, verse renders as two-space-indented modernized lines.

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY (Stoic core, consistent with the project)

- "the wise man" (sapiens); "Fortune" capitalized as the adversary;
  "the soul"; "virtue"; "philosophy" (never "wisdom-pursuit"
  circumlocutions); "self-control" (temperantia); "the happy life".
- Letter numbering and subtitles: each letter carries its "Letter N:
  Subtitle" heading from the source file EXACTLY.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- Letter 1: "while we are postponing, life speeds by." And the close
  of its opening charge: hold every hour; "Nothing, Lucilius, is ours,
  except time."
- Letter 7: the crowd letter — "I never bring back home the same
  character that I took abroad." And: "associate with those who will
  make a better man of you."
- Letter 12: "Whatever years lie behind us are in death's hands."
- Letter 47 (the slavery letter — the most famous): "'They are
  slaves,' people say. No — they are men." (render Gummere's sequence
  "Slaves! No, comrades... No, they are men" in this compressed
  modern shape; the rest of the sequence follows the source).
- Letter 49: "It is not that we have a short space of time, but that
  we waste much of it." (if the line falls in the Letters' text; it
  is De Brevitate's — do NOT import it if absent. Verify against the
  source; if absent, skip.)
- The "daily wage" ritual: "Ah, but this is borrowed from Epicurus"
  framing — keep the joke alive wherever it recurs.

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Letter 47 on slavery is the point of the book for many readers:
  render with full force, plainly — Seneca's argument is radical for
  its time and the modern text must not blunt OR overclaim it.
- Suicide as Stoic doctrine recurs (letters 70, 77, etc.) — the open
  door; render as the ancient philosophical argument it is, measured
  register, no modern editorializing in either direction.
- The gladiatorial-games letter (7) and Roman brutality: plain
  historical register.
- Illness, old age, and death are the book's constant subjects —
  Seneca's unflinching directness is the feature; keep it.

FORMAT

55 files; heading "Letters N–M" exactly as in the source file; then
each letter: blank line, its "Letter N: Subtitle" line EXACTLY as in
the source, blank line, "Greetings from Seneca to his friend
Lucilius.", the modern text, and "Farewell." on its own line. Word
ratio target 0.85–1.15. No part markers (each file stands alone).
