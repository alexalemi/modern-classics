The Second Day

(Part 2 of 20)

Sagredo
I'm fully persuaded by the necessity with which your argument reaches its conclusion, but a doubt is stirring in me that I can't shake off. It's this: Copernicus assigns the Earth another motion besides the daily one. By the rule you've just laid down, that motion ought to be imperceptible to us here on the Earth, as far as appearances go, but visible in all the rest of the world. So it seems to me I can draw one necessary conclusion or the other: either Copernicus plainly blundered in assigning the Earth a motion whose general correspondence doesn't show up anywhere in the sky, or else, if the correspondence is there, then Ptolemy was just as much at fault for failing to refute this motion the way he refuted the other.

Salviati
A very reasonable doubt; and when we come to deal with that other motion, you'll see by how wide a margin Copernicus outstripped Ptolemy in sharpness and penetration of mind — for he saw what Ptolemy did not see, I mean the marvelous correspondence with which that motion is reflected in all the rest of the celestial bodies. But for now let's set that part aside and come back to our first consideration. Beginning with the most general points, I'll go on proposing the reasons that seem to favor the Earth's mobility, and then we'll hear the contrary ones from Simplicio. First: if we consider nothing but the immense bulk of the starry sphere, compared with the smallness of the terrestrial globe — which it contains many millions of times over — and then think of the speed of a motion that has to complete one entire revolution in a day and a night, I can't persuade myself that anyone could be found who thought it the more reasonable and credible thing that it's the celestial sphere doing the turning while the terrestrial globe stands still.

Sagredo
If, for the whole universe of effects that can depend in nature on motions like these, all the very same consequences followed, to a hair, from the one arrangement as from the other, then — as my first, general impression — I'd judge that anyone who considered it more reasonable to set the entire universe moving in order to keep the Earth still is more unreasonable than a man who climbed to the top of your great dome for no other purpose than to take in a view of the city and the countryside around it, and then demanded that the whole landscape be spun around him so he could be spared the trouble of turning his head. The conveniences to be drawn from that arrangement and not from this one would have to be many and great indeed to balance out, in my mind, and outweigh this absurdity, and so make the first more believable to me than the second. But perhaps Aristotle, Ptolemy, and Simplicio must find some advantages in it — and if there are any, it would be well to have them laid before us too; or else let it be shown to me how and why there are none, and can be none.

Salviati
For all the thought I've given it, I haven't been able to find any difference; and what's more, I think I've found that no difference can exist — so that I judge any further search for one to be in vain. So mark this: motion is motion, and does its work as motion, only in relation to things that lack it; among things that all share it equally, it does nothing — it's as if it weren't there at all. The goods a ship is loaded with move in the sense that, leaving Venice, they pass by Corfu, by Candia, by Cyprus, and go on to Aleppo — Venice, Corfu, Candia, and the rest staying behind, not moving with the ship. But for the bales, the crates, and the other bundles the ship is loaded and stowed with, and with respect to the ship itself, the motion from Venice to Syria is as good as nothing: it doesn't alter the relation among them in the least. And that's because the motion is common to them all, and shared equally by all. If, of all the cargo aboard, one bale should shift a single finger's breadth away from a crate, that alone would be, for the bale, a greater motion in relation to the crate than the two-thousand-mile voyage they made together.

Simplicio
That is good, solid doctrine, and Peripatetic through and through.

Salviati
I take it to be older than that; and I suspect that Aristotle, in taking it over from some good school, didn't penetrate it fully, and that having written it down in altered form, he became a source of confusion — thanks to those who insist on defending his every word. When he wrote that everything that moves, moves upon something immobile, I suspect he was garbling the statement that everything that moves, moves with respect to something immobile. That proposition suffers no difficulty at all; the other suffers many.

Sagredo
Please, let's not break the thread — carry on with the argument you began.

Salviati
It's clear, then, that a motion common to many moving bodies is idle, as good as null, with respect to the relation of those bodies among themselves, since among them nothing changes; it does its work only in the relation those bodies have with others that lack the motion — for with those, their arrangement does change. Now, we've divided the universe into two parts, one of which is necessarily movable and the other motionless. So as far as anything that can depend on such a motion is concerned, moving the Earth alone comes to exactly the same as moving all the rest of the world, since the whole operation of the motion lies in nothing but the relation between the celestial bodies and the Earth — and that relation alone is what changes. Now, if to obtain the very same effect ad unguem — to the last detail — it makes no difference whether the Earth alone moves while all the rest of the universe stays still, or the Earth alone stays still while the whole universe moves with one and the same motion, who is going to believe that nature — which, by common consent, does not employ many things to bring about what can be managed with few — chose to set an immense number of enormous bodies moving, at an inestimable speed, to achieve what could be had by the moderate motion of one single body turning around its own center?

Simplicio
I don't quite see how this vast motion can count as nothing for the Sun, for the Moon, for the other planets, and for the innumerable host of the fixed stars. How can you call it nothing that the Sun passes from one meridian to the next, climbs above this horizon, sinks below that one, brings now the day and now the night — and that the Moon, the other planets, and the fixed stars too go through the same variations?

Salviati
All these variations you list are nothing except in relation to the Earth. To see that this is true, just remove the Earth in your imagination: there is no longer any rising or setting of Sun or Moon left in the world, no horizons, no meridians, no days, no nights — in short, from that motion there never arises any change at all between the Moon and the Sun or any other stars you please, fixed or wandering. All the changes are relative to the Earth; and all of them, when you come down to it, amount to nothing more than the Sun showing itself first to China, then to Persia, afterwards to Egypt, to Greece, to France, to Spain, to America, and so on — with the Moon and the rest of the celestial bodies doing the same. And it all comes out in exactly the same way if, without dragging in so great a part of the universe, you simply let the terrestrial globe turn in place. But let's double the difficulty with another, enormous one. If you attribute this great motion to the heavens, you're forced of necessity to make it contrary to the particular motions of all the planetary orbs — each of which, beyond any dispute, has its own proper motion from west to east, and a gentle, moderate motion at that — and then to have them swept off the opposite way, from east to west, by this furiously rapid daily motion. Whereas if the Earth is made to turn on itself, the contrariety of motions vanishes, and the single motion from west to east accommodates every appearance and satisfies them all completely.

Simplicio
As for the contrariety of motions, that would matter little, because Aristotle demonstrates that circular motions are not contrary to one another, and that theirs cannot be called true contrariety.

Salviati
Does Aristotle demonstrate it, or does he merely say it because it suited a certain design of his? If contraries are, as he himself declares, things that destroy one another, I fail to see how two moving bodies that collide on a circular line should do each other any less harm than two that collide on a straight one.

Sagredo
Hold on a moment, please. Tell me, Simplicio: when two knights run at each other jousting in the open field, or when two whole squadrons, or two fleets at sea, drive into one another and smash and sink each other — would you call encounters like those contrary to one another?

Simplicio
Let's call them contrary.

Sagredo
Then how is it there's no contrariety in circular motions? These encounters, taking place on the surface of the earth or the water — which, as you know, are spherical — turn out to be circular motions. Do you know, Simplicio, which circular motions really aren't contrary to each other? Those of two circles that touch from the outside: turn one of them, and it naturally makes the other move the opposite way. But if one circle is inside the other, it's impossible for their motions, made in different directions, not to fight each other.

Salviati
Contrary or not contrary, these are quarrels over words; and I know that in fact it's a far simpler and more natural thing to be able to save everything with a single motion than to introduce two — and if you don't want to call them contrary, call them opposite. Nor am I putting this forward as something impossible, or claiming to draw a necessary demonstration from it — only a greater probability. And the improbability is compounded a third time by the way this arrangement disrupts, out of all proportion, the order that we see with certainty holding among those celestial bodies whose circling is not in doubt but absolutely settled. The order is this: the bigger the orb, the longer it takes to complete its revolution, and the smaller, the quicker. Thus Saturn, tracing a circle greater than any other planet's, completes it in thirty years; Jupiter goes around its smaller one in twelve years; Mars in two; the Moon runs through its far smaller circle in a single month. And just as plainly we see that of the Medicean Stars — Jupiter's four little moons — the one nearest Jupiter makes its revolution in a very short time, about forty-two hours; the next in three and a half days; the third in seven days; and the most remote in sixteen. And this thoroughly consistent pattern isn't altered in the least so long as the twenty-four-hour motion is assigned to the terrestrial globe turning on itself. But if you insist on keeping the Earth motionless, then after passing from the Moon's very brief period to the successively greater ones — to Mars's two years, from there to the greater sphere of Jupiter with its twelve, and from that to the still greater sphere of Saturn, whose period is thirty years — you must then leap across to another sphere incomparably bigger yet, and make it finish an entire revolution in twenty-four hours. And even that is the least of the disorder you'd be introducing; for if anyone wanted to pass from Saturn's sphere to the starry sphere, and make it as much bigger than Saturn's as its extremely slow motion of many thousands of years would proportionally require, then the leap from that one to a still bigger sphere — while making it swing clear around in twenty-four hours — would be out of proportion by far more.

And there's a fourth difficulty, which you must necessarily accept the moment the starry sphere is made movable: the immense disparity among the motions of the stars themselves. Some of them would be moving at tremendous speed in vast circles, others extremely slowly in the tiniest circles, according as each lies farther from or nearer to the poles. That has its own awkwardness — both because we see all the bodies whose motion is beyond doubt moving in great circles, and because it seems a poorly judged arrangement to station bodies that are meant to move circularly at immense distances from the center, and then have them move in the smallest circles of all.

And not only will the sizes of the circles, and consequently the speeds of motion, of these stars be utterly different from the circles and motions of those others, but — and this will be the fifth inconvenience — the very same stars keep changing their circles and their speeds. For the stars that two thousand years ago sat on the celestial equator, and so traced great circles with their motion, are found in our own day many degrees away from it; so they must have grown slower in motion and been reduced to moving in smaller circles. And it isn't even far from possible that a time will come when one of them — a star that till then had always moved — will reach the pole, join it, and stand still; and then, after resting for some while, will take up its motion again. Whereas the other stars, the ones whose motion is certain, all trace, as I said, the great circle of their orb and keep to it without change.

The improbability grows again — let it be the sixth inconvenience — for anyone who reasons carefully, in that no one can even conceive what the solidity of that vast sphere would have to be, in whose depths so many stars are riveted so firmly that, without shifting position among themselves in the slightest, they are carried around in concert despite so enormous a disparity of motions. Or, if the heavens are fluid — as it's far more reasonable to believe — so that each star goes wandering through them on its own, then what law will govern their motions, and to what end, so that, watched from the Earth, they appear to be moved by one single sphere? To accomplish that, it seems to me as much easier and more convenient to fix them motionless than to send them roaming, as it is easier to keep in their places the many stones set into a piazza's pavement than the swarms of boys who run around on top of them.

And finally, for the seventh objection: if we attribute the daily rotation to the highest heaven, we have to make it of such force and power that it carries along with it the innumerable multitude of the fixed stars — every one of them a vast body, far bigger than the Earth — and all the planetary spheres besides, even though both stars and planets by their own nature move the other way. And beyond that, we're forced to concede that the element of fire and the greater part of the air are swept along too, and that only the little globe of the Earth stands defiant and resistant to so much power. That, to me, seems very hard to swallow; nor could I understand how the Earth — a body hanging suspended and balanced on its center, indifferent to motion and to rest, placed in and surrounded by a fluid medium — should not yield as well and be carried around with everything else. But we meet no such obstacles in making the Earth move — a tiny body, imperceptible in comparison with the universe, and for that very reason incapable of doing it any violence.

Sagredo
I can feel certain notions whirling around in my imagination, stirred up in a jumble by the arguments we've been hearing; and if I'm going to attend properly to what's still to be said, I'd better see whether I can put them in order and draw out whatever substance is in them — if there turns out to be any. Perhaps proceeding by questions will help me explain myself more easily. So I ask Simplicio, first: does he believe that different motions can naturally belong to one and the same simple movable body, or that only one suits it — its own proper and natural motion?

Simplicio
For a simple movable body there can be only one motion that suits it naturally, and no more; all the others belong to it by accident and by participation. Just as, for a man strolling about a ship, his own proper motion is that of his walking, while by participation he has the motion that carries him into port — where he would never have arrived by strolling, if the ship's motion hadn't brought him there.

Sagredo
Tell me, second: that motion which is communicated by participation to some movable body, while the body itself is moving with another motion of its own, different from the participated one — must that motion reside in some subject of its own, or can it exist in nature with no other support?

Simplicio
Aristotle answers all these questions for you. He tells you that just as of one movable body there is one motion, so of one motion there is one movable body; and consequently that without inhering in its subject, no motion can exist or even be imagined.

Sagredo
Third, I'd like you to tell me whether you believe the Moon and the other planets and celestial bodies have motions of their own — and what those motions are.

Simplicio
They have them; and they are the motions by which they run through the zodiac — the Moon in a month, the Sun in a year, Mars in two, the starry sphere in all those thousands of years. These are their own proper and natural motions.

Sagredo
But that motion by which I see the fixed stars, and with them all the planets, travel together from east to west and return to the east in twenty-four hours — in what way does that belong to them?

Simplicio
They have it by participation.

Sagredo
Then it does not reside in them; and since it doesn't reside in them, and cannot exist without some subject in which it does reside, it must be made the proper and natural motion of some other sphere.

Simplicio
On this account the astronomers and philosophers have found another, highest sphere, without any stars, to which the daily rotation naturally belongs; and they have called it the primum mobile — the "first movable" — which then sweeps all the lower spheres along with it, contributing its own motion to them and letting them share in it.

Sagredo
But when — without introducing other unknown and enormous spheres, without other motions or sweepings-along by participation, leaving to every sphere its own single, simple motion, without mixing contrary motions, but having them all run the same way, as they must if they all depend on a single principle — everything proceeds and answers in the most perfect harmony, why refuse that option, and give your assent to such strange and labored conditions?

Simplicio
The whole point lies in finding this way of yours, so simple and so ready.

Sagredo
The way looks to me found already. Make the Earth the primum mobile — that is, make it turn on itself in twenty-four hours, and in the same direction as all the other spheres — and, without this motion being shared with any other planet or star, they will all have their risings, their settings, and in short every other appearance.

Simplicio
The trick is being able to move it without a thousand inconveniences.

Salviati
All the inconveniences will be removed as fast as you propose them. What's been said so far amounts only to the first and most general motives for holding it not altogether improbable that the daily rotation belongs to the Earth rather than to all the rest of the universe. I don't offer them to you as unbreakable laws, but as motives that carry some appearance of reason. And since I understand perfectly well that a single experiment or one conclusive demonstration to the contrary would be enough to knock these and a hundred thousand other probable arguments flat, we mustn't stop here, but press on and hear what Simplicio answers, and what better probabilities or firmer reasons he brings against us.

Simplicio
First I'll say something in general about all these considerations taken together, and then I'll come to some particulars. It seems to me that you build everything on the greater simplicity and ease of producing the same effects: you judge that, as far as causing them goes, moving the Earth alone comes to the same as moving all the rest of the world except the Earth, but that as far as the doing of it goes, you count the first far easier than the second. To that I answer that it seems the same to me too — so long as I measure by my own power, which is not merely finite but utterly feeble. But measured against the power of the Mover, which is infinite, it is no less easy to move the universe than the Earth — or a straw, for that matter. And if the power is infinite, why should not a great part of it be exercised rather than a tiny one? So it seems to me that the argument, in its general form, has no force.
