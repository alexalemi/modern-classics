The Second Day

(Part 13 of 20)

Simplicio
Just so.

Salviati
So then, whenever you actually bring a material sphere up against a material plane, you're bringing an imperfect sphere against an imperfect plane; and these, you say, don't touch at a point. But I tell you that in the abstract too, an immaterial sphere that isn't a perfect sphere can touch an immaterial plane that isn't a perfect plane — not at a point, but along part of its surface. So that so far, what happens in the concrete happens in exactly the same way in the abstract. And it would be a strange new thing indeed if the sums and reckonings done in abstract numbers didn't then match up with the gold and silver coins and the goods in the concrete world. But do you know what really happens, Simplicio? Just as, to make the accounts come out right on sugar, silk, and wool, the accountant has to make his deductions for the weight of crates, wrappings, and other packaging, so too, when the geometer-philosopher wants to recognize in the concrete the effects he's proved in the abstract, he has to subtract the hindrances of matter. And if he knows how to do that, I assure you the results will tally every bit as exactly as arithmetical calculations. So the errors lie neither in the abstract nor in the concrete, neither in geometry nor in physics, but in the reckoner who doesn't know how to do his sums correctly. So if you had a perfect sphere and a perfect plane, material though they were, have no doubt they would touch at a point; and if that was and is impossible to obtain, then it was quite beside the point to say that sphaera aenea non tangit in puncto — a bronze sphere does not touch at a point. But let me add one thing more, Simplicio: granting you that no perfect spherical shape and no perfect plane can be had in matter, do you believe two material bodies can be had whose surfaces are curved in some part and in some way — even as irregularly as you please?

Simplicio
Of those, I don't think we're short.

Salviati
Well, if there are such, these too will touch at a point — for touching at a single point is by no means the special privilege of the perfect sphere and the perfect plane. In fact, anyone who looked into this matter more closely would find it far harder to find two bodies that touch along part of their surfaces than at a single point. Because for two surfaces to fit snugly together, either both must be exactly flat, or, if one bulges out, the other must be hollowed — and hollowed with a curve that answers exactly to the bulge of the other; and these conditions, by their too narrow specification, are far harder to meet than the others, which in their loose randomness are infinite in number.

Simplicio
So you believe that two stones, or two pieces of iron, taken at random and set together, most of the time touch at a single point?

Salviati
In random encounters, I think not — partly because there'll usually be a bit of yielding grime on them, partly because no care is taken to bring them together without some knock, and the slightest one is enough to make one surface give a little to the other, so that they mutually shape themselves, at least in some tiny patch, each to the imprint of the other. But if their surfaces were well polished, and both were laid on a table — so that neither pressed down on the other — and pushed gently toward each other, I have no doubt they could be brought to simple contact at a single point.

Sagredo
With your leave, I really must raise a certain difficulty of mine, which came to me on hearing Simplicio put forward the impossibility of finding a solid material body with a perfectly spherical shape, and on seeing Salviati in a way grant him his assent by not contradicting him. So I'd like to know whether the same difficulty arises in shaping a solid into some other figure — that is, to put it more clearly, whether there's any greater difficulty in reducing a block of marble to the shape of a perfect sphere than to that of a perfect pyramid, or a perfect horse, or a perfect locust.

Salviati
I'll take this first answer myself. And first let me excuse the assent you think I gave Simplicio — it was only for the moment, because I too had it in mind, before moving on to another subject, to say something that may well be the same as your thought, or very close to it. And in answer to your first question, I say that if any shape can be given to a solid, the spherical is the easiest of them all — as it's also the simplest, and holds among solid figures the place the circle holds among plane ones. The drawing of that circle, being easier than any other, has alone been judged by mathematicians worthy of a place among the postulates governing the drawing of every other figure. And forming a sphere is so easy that if you cut a circular hollow in a flat plate of hard metal, and inside it you roll about at random any solid roughly rounded off, it will by itself, with no further contrivance, be reduced to a spherical shape as perfect as can be — provided that solid isn't smaller than the sphere that would pass through that circle. And what's still more worth noting is that within that same hollow, spheres of different sizes will be formed. As for what it takes to form a horse — or, as you say, a locust — I leave that for you to judge, knowing as you do that very few sculptors in the world are up to it. And I believe Simplicio won't disagree with me on this particular point.

Simplicio
I don't know that I disagree with you in the least. My opinion is that none of the shapes named can be perfectly obtained; but as for coming as close as possible to the most perfect degree, I think it's incomparably easier to reduce the solid to a spherical shape than to the form of a horse or a locust.

Sagredo
And this greater difficulty — what do you think it depends on?

Simplicio
Just as the great ease in forming the sphere derives from its absolute simplicity and uniformity, so the utmost irregularity makes it extremely difficult to introduce the other figures.

Sagredo
So then, since irregularity is the cause of difficulty, the shape of a stone broken at random with a hammer will also be among the hard ones to bring about — being itself irregular, perhaps more so than the horse's?

Simplicio
So it must be.

Sagredo
But tell me: that shape, whatever it may be, that the stone has — does it have it most perfectly, or not?

Simplicio
The one it has, it has so perfectly that no other fits it so exactly.

Sagredo
So then, if among irregular shapes — hard, therefore, to achieve — we nonetheless find infinitely many obtained most perfectly, by what reasoning can it be said that the simplest, and therefore the easiest of all, is impossible to find?

Salviati
Gentlemen, by your leave, it seems to me we've wandered into a dispute not much weightier than the old squabble over goat's wool; and whereas our discussions ought to go on being about serious and weighty matters, we're spending our time in frivolous, trifling wrangles. Let's remember, please, that the search for the constitution of the world is one of the greatest and noblest problems there is in nature — and all the greater for being aimed at the solution of that other one, I mean the cause of the ebb and flow of the sea, sought by every great man there's ever been and perhaps found by none. So, when we've nothing left to bring forward for the complete resolution of the objection drawn from the whirling of the Earth — which was the last one raised as an argument for its immobility about its own center — we can move on to examining the things for and against the annual motion.

Sagredo
I wouldn't have you, Salviati, measure the rest of our minds by the measure of your own. You, always used to busying yourself with the loftiest contemplations, judge as frivolous and low some of the very things that seem to us worthy food for our intellects. So now and then, for our satisfaction, don't scorn to stoop and grant something to our curiosity. As for the resolution of that last objection, drawn from the flinging-off of the daily whirl, far less than what's been offered would have satisfied me. Still, the things that have been said over and above the need struck me as so intriguing that, far from tiring my imagination, they've kept it entertained by their novelty, with a delight I couldn't wish greater. So if you have some further speculation to add, do bring it out — for my part I'll very gladly hear it.

Salviati
In the things I've discovered myself I've always felt the greatest delight; and after that, which is the greatest of all, I take great pleasure in sharing them with some friend who understands them and shows a taste for them. Now, since you're one of these, I'll loosen the rein a little on my ambition — which quietly enjoys itself when I show myself sharper than some other reputed to be keen-sighted — and produce, to cap off and round out our past discussion, another fallacy of the followers of Ptolemy and Aristotle, drawn from the very argument already put forward.

Sagredo
There — I'm eagerly bracing myself to hear it.

Salviati
So far we've passed over and granted Ptolemy, as an undoubted fact, that since the flinging-off of the stone proceeds from the speed of the wheel turned about its center, the cause of that flinging grows exactly as the speed of the whirl increases. From which it was inferred that, since the speed of the Earth's whirl is vastly greater than that of any machine we can artificially set spinning, the extrusion of stones and animals and so on would consequently have to be violent in the extreme. Now I note that in this reasoning there's a very great fallacy, in that we compare the speeds with one another indiscriminately and absolutely. It's true that if I compare the speeds of the same wheel, or of two wheels equal to each other, the one turned faster will fling the stones with greater impetus, and as the speed grows, the cause of the projection grows in the same proportion. But when the speed is made greater not by increasing the speed in the same wheel — which would be by making it turn more times in equal stretches of time — but by increasing the diameter and making the wheel bigger, so that the time of one turn stays the same in the small wheel as in the great one, and only in the great one is the speed greater because its circumference is greater: let no one believe that the cause of the flinging in the great wheel grows according to the proportion of the speed of its circumference to the speed of the circumference of the smaller wheel — for this is utterly false, as a very quick experiment can show us roughly for now. A stone that we could fling with a rod one braccio long, we couldn't fling with one six braccia long — even though the motion of the end of the long rod, that is, of the stone set into it, were twice as fast as the motion of the tip of the shorter rod, which would be the case if the speeds were such that in the time of one full turn of the longer rod, the shorter made three.

Sagredo
What you tell me, Salviati, I already grasp must necessarily come out so; but the reason doesn't readily come to me — why equal speeds shouldn't act equally in extruding projectiles, but the smaller wheel's far more than the larger's. So please explain to me how the matter runs.

Simplicio
You, Sagredo, have this time shown yourself unlike your usual self — you who ordinarily see into everything in a moment, and now you've passed over a fallacy lodged in the experiment of the rods, one that I've managed to see into. And it's the different way of operating when you make the throw now with the short rod, now with the long one. Because to make the stone fly out of the notch, you mustn't keep its motion uniform; rather, at the moment it's fastest, you have to hold back your arm and check the rod's speed, whereupon the stone, already in very swift motion, flies off and moves with impetus. But such a check can't be made with the longer rod, which, by its length and flexibility, doesn't wholly obey the arm's braking; instead, going on to accompany the stone for some distance, by gently reining it in it keeps it attached, and doesn't — as if it had struck a hard obstacle — let it fly free of itself. For if both rods were to strike against a stop that halted them, I believe the stone would fly off equally from the one and the other, even though their motions were equally fast.

Sagredo
With Salviati's leave, I'll make some reply to Simplicio, since he's turned to me. And I say that in his account there's good and bad: good, because nearly all of it is true; bad, because it doesn't quite bear on our point. Very true it is that when the thing carrying the stones at speed strikes an immovable stop, they run on with impetus — the same effect one sees happen all the time in a boat that, running swiftly, runs aground or strikes some obstacle: everyone aboard, caught off guard, suddenly pitches and falls toward the way the vessel was going. And if the terrestrial globe were to meet an obstacle such that it wholly resisted its whirl and stopped it, then indeed I believe that not only the beasts, the buildings, and the cities, but the mountains, the lakes, and the seas would be overturned — and it would be a wonder if the globe itself didn't fall to pieces. But none of this bears on our point, for we're speaking of what might follow from the motion of the Earth turning uniformly and placidly upon itself, even if at great speed. Likewise what you say about the rods is in part true, but Salviati didn't bring it forward as something that fits our subject exactly — only as an example that might roughly rouse our minds to consider more carefully whether, when the speed is increased in whatever way, the cause of the projection increases in the same proportion. So that, for example, if a wheel ten braccia in diameter, moving so that a point on its circumference passed over a hundred braccia in a minute of an hour, thereby had the impetus to fling a stone — whether that impetus grows a hundred thousand times over in a wheel a million braccia in diameter. This Salviati denies, and I'm inclined to believe the same; but not knowing the reason, I've asked him for it, and I'm eagerly awaiting it.

Salviati
Here I am to give you what satisfaction lies within my powers; and though in my first words it may seem I'm going after things foreign to our point, I believe that as the reasoning proceeds we'll find they aren't so after all. So let Sagredo tell me in what things he's observed the resistance of a movable body to being moved to consist.

Sagredo
For now I don't see any internal resistance in the body to being moved except its natural inclination and propensity toward the contrary motion — as in heavy bodies, which have a propensity to motion downward, the resistance is to motion upward. And I said internal resistance because I believe that's the one you mean, not the external ones, which are accidental and many.

Salviati
That's what I meant, and your sharpness has outrun my care in asking. But if I've been sparing in my questioning, I suspect that Sagredo hasn't, with his answer, fully matched the question — and that in the body, besides the natural inclination toward the contrary terminus, there's another quality, likewise intrinsic and natural, that makes it resistant to motion. So tell me again: don't you believe that the inclination, say, of heavy bodies to move downward is equal to their resistance to being pushed upward?

Sagredo
I believe it's exactly so; and that's why I see two equal weights in a balance stay still in equilibrium, the heaviness of the one resisting being lifted against the heaviness with which the other, pressing down, would raise it.

Salviati
Very good; so that to have the one lift the other, you'd have to add weight to the one pressing down, or take some from the other. But if the resistance to upward motion consists in heaviness alone, how does it come about that in a balance with unequal arms — that is, in the steelyard — a weight of a hundred pounds, by its pressing down, sometimes isn't enough to lift one of four pounds that opposes it, while this four-pound one, by lowering, can lift the hundred-pound one? For such is the effect of the sliding weight against the heavy load we want to weigh. If the resistance to being moved resides in heaviness alone, how can the sliding weight, with its mere four pounds, resist the weight of a bale of wool or silk that runs to eight hundred or a thousand — indeed, how can it overcome the bale with its moment and lift it? We really must say, Sagredo, that here we're working with a resistance and a force other than that of simple heaviness.

Sagredo
It must be so; but tell me, what is this second power?

Salviati
It's what wasn't there in the equal-armed balance. Consider what's new in the steelyard — and in that novelty necessarily lies the cause of the new effect.

Sagredo
I think your prompting has jogged something in me. In both instruments we work with weight and with motion: in the balance the motions are equal, and so one weight has to exceed the other in heaviness to move it; in the steelyard the smaller weight won't move the larger unless the larger moves little — hanging at the smaller distance — while the smaller moves much, hanging at the greater distance. So we must say that the smaller weight overcomes the larger's resistance by moving much, while the other moves little.

Salviati
Which is as much as to say that the speed of the less heavy body makes up for the heaviness of the heavier, slower one.

Sagredo
But do you believe that speed makes up for heaviness exactly? That is, that the moment and force of a body of, say, four pounds is as great as that of one of a hundred, whenever the first has a hundred degrees of speed and the second only four?

Salviati
Certainly, as I could show you with many experiments; but for now let the confirmation of this one alone, the steelyard, suffice — where you'll see the light sliding weight able to hold up and balance the very heavy bale just when its distance from the center, on which the steelyard rests and turns, is as much greater than the bale's smaller distance as the bale's absolute weight is greater than the sliding weight's. And that the great bale can't with its weight lift the sliding weight, so much lighter — no cause for this can be seen but the disparity of the motions each would have to make, since the bale, by lowering a single finger's width, would raise the sliding weight a hundred fingers' width (granting that the bale weighed as much as a hundred sliding weights, and that the sliding weight's distance from the steelyard's center was a hundred times the distance between that same center and the point where the bale hangs). And the sliding weight's moving a hundred fingers' width in the time the bale moves a single one is the same as saying the speed of the sliding weight's motion is a hundred times greater than the speed of the bale's. Now fix it firmly in your imagination, as a true and well-known principle, that the resistance coming from the speed of motion makes up for what depends on the heaviness of another body. So that, consequently, a body of one pound moving with a hundred degrees of speed resists being checked exactly as much as another body of a hundred pounds whose speed is a single degree. And two equal bodies will resist being moved equally, if they're to be made to move with equal speed; but if one is to be moved faster than the other, it will offer greater resistance, in proportion to the greater speed you want to give it. These things settled, let's come to the working-out of our problem; and for easier understanding let's draw ourselves a little figure. Let there be two unequal wheels about this center A: let the circumference of the smaller be BG, and of the larger CEH, and let the semidiameter ABC stand upright to the horizon; through the points B and C let's mark the straight tangent lines BF and CD, and on the arcs BG, CE let two equal parts BG, CE be taken. And let it be understood that the two wheels are turned about their centers with equal speeds, so that two bodies — which would be, say, two stones — placed at the points B and C are carried along the circumferences BG, CE with equal speeds, such that in the same time the stone B would run through the arc BG, the stone C would pass through the arc CE. I now say that the whirl of the smaller wheel is far more powerful to make the projection of stone B than the whirl of the larger wheel is for stone C. For since, as already explained, the projection must be made along the tangent, when the stones B, C were to separate from their wheels and begin the motion of projection from the points B, C, they'd be flung along the tangents BF, CD by the impetus conceived from the whirl. Along the tangents BF, CD, then, the two stones have equal impetuses to run, and would run along them if they weren't deflected by some other force. Isn't that so, Sagredo?
