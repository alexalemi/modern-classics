The First Day

(Part 10 of 12)

Sagredo
Experience and reason are enough for me. It now remains for Simplicio to answer my other question, and tell me what it is that moves the Peripatetics to insist on this roundness in the celestial bodies being so exact.

Simplicio
The celestial bodies' being ingenerable, incorruptible, inalterable, impervious to change, immortal, and so on, makes them absolutely perfect; and their being absolutely perfect carries the consequence that every kind of perfection is in them — and therefore that their shape, too, is perfect: that is, spherical, and absolutely and perfectly spherical, not rough and irregular.

Salviati
And this incorruptibility — from what do you derive it?

Simplicio
Immediately, from their having no contraries; mediately, from their simple circular motion.

Salviati
So then, as far as I gather from your account, in constituting the essence of the celestial bodies as incorruptible, inalterable, and the rest, roundness doesn't enter into it as a cause or a necessary requirement. For if roundness were what produced inalterability, then we could make wood, wax, and other elemental materials incorruptible whenever we pleased, simply by working them into a spherical shape.

Simplicio
And isn't it obvious that a wooden ball will keep better and longer than a spire, or any other angular shape, made from the same amount of the same wood?

Salviati
That is perfectly true; but it won't thereby pass from corruptible to incorruptible. It stays corruptible — merely longer-lasting. Note this, then: the corruptible admits of more and less. We can say, "This is less corruptible than that" — jasper, for instance, is less corruptible than soft gray sandstone. But the incorruptible admits no more and no less; you cannot say, "This is more incorruptible than that other," if both are incorruptible and eternal. Difference of shape, therefore, can operate only on materials capable of lasting a longer or a shorter time; but in eternal things, which can only be equally eternal, the operation of shape ceases. And so, since celestial matter is incorruptible not through its shape but through something else, there is no need to be so anxious about this perfect sphericity; for once the matter is incorruptible, let it have whatever shape you like — it will always remain so.

Sagredo
But I'm turning over something further, and I say this: grant that the spherical shape had the power to confer incorruptibility, and then all bodies, of whatever shape, would be eternal and incorruptible. For since the round body is incorruptible, corruptibility would have to reside in those parts that spoil the perfect roundness. Take a die, for example: inside it there is a perfectly round ball, and as such incorruptible; so what remains corruptible must be those corners that cover and hide the roundness. The very most that could happen, then, is that those corners — those excrescences, so to speak — should decay. But if we look deeper still, within those parts toward the corners there lie other, smaller balls of the same material, and they too, being round, are incorruptible; and in the remnants surrounding these eight smaller little spheres one can conceive still others; so that at last, resolving the whole die into innumerable balls, you will have to confess it incorruptible. And this same argument, with a similar resolution, can be run on every other shape.

Salviati
The argument runs beautifully. So that if, say, a sphere of crystal owed its incorruptibility to its shape — that is, the power to resist every internal and external alteration — then it's hard to see why adding more crystal to it and reworking it into, say, a cube should alter it within or without, or leave it less able to resist a new surrounding made of its very own material than it was against a former one of different material. Especially if it's true that corruption is worked by contraries, as Aristotle says: for what can you wrap around that ball of crystal that is less contrary to it than crystal itself? But we're not noticing how the hours are slipping away, and we'll come late to the end of our discussions if we make such long speeches over every particular; besides which, my memory gets so tangled in the multitude of things that I can barely recall the propositions Simplicio laid out for us, in order, to be considered.

Simplicio
I remember them very well. And on this particular point of the Moon's mountainousness, the cause I brought forward for that appearance still stands: it can perfectly well be accounted for by saying it is an illusion arising from the parts of the Moon being unequally opaque and transparent.

Sagredo
A little while ago, when Simplicio was attributing the Moon's apparent unevenness — following the opinion of a certain Peripatetic friend of his — to its parts being variously opaque and transparent, on the model of the similar illusions one sees in crystals and gems of many kinds, I thought of a material far better suited to produce such effects, one I feel certain that philosopher would pay any price for: mother-of-pearl. It is worked into various shapes, and even when brought to an extreme polish it looks to the eye so variously hollowed and swelling from place to place that the touch itself can scarcely be trusted to vouch for its smoothness.

Salviati
A truly beautiful thought; and what hasn't been done yet may well be done another time — if other gems and crystals have been brought forward that have nothing to do with the illusions of mother-of-pearl, these will surely be brought forward too. In the meantime, so as not to cut off anyone's opportunity, I'll keep quiet about the answer that would meet it, and for now try only to satisfy the objections Simplicio has raised. I say, then, that this reason of yours is far too general; and unless you apply it, one by one, to all the appearances that are seen in the Moon — the ones that have moved me and others to hold it mountainous — I don't believe you'll find anyone content with such a doctrine. Nor do I believe that you yourself, or the author himself, find any more rest in it than in anything else remote from the point. Of the many, many varied appearances that are seen evening after evening through one lunar month, you could not imitate so much as one by building a ball to your own specification out of parts more and less opaque and transparent, with a polished surface. Whereas, on the other side, from any solid, non-transparent material whatever, one can make balls which — by nothing but ridges and hollows, and by catching the light in different ways — will reproduce exactly, to a hair, the same sights and changes that are seen in the Moon from hour to hour. On them you will see the crests of the ridges facing the Sun's light show quite bright, and behind them the cast shadows lying very dark; you will see those shadows greater and smaller, according as the ridges stand nearer to or farther from the boundary dividing the lit part of the Moon from the dark; you will see that same dividing line drawn not evenly, as it would be if the ball were smooth, but winding and notched like battlements; you will see, beyond that line, in the dark part, many illuminated summits standing detached from the region already luminous; you will see the shadows I've described shrink as the illumination climbs, until they vanish altogether, not one of them left once the whole hemisphere is lit; and then, as the light passes over toward the other lunar hemisphere, you will recognize the same ridges you observed before, and see the projections of their shadows thrown the opposite way and growing. Of all these things — I say it again — you cannot show me even one with your opaque-and-transparent.

Sagredo
One of them, in fact, will be imitated: the full moon, when everything is lit and no shadow is seen any longer, nor anything else that takes any variation from ridges and hollows. But please, Salviati, lose no more time on this particular; for anyone who had the patience to make the observations through one or two lunations and still could not take in this truth that the senses themselves deliver might fairly be sentenced as wholly devoid of judgment — and with such people, why spend time and words for nothing?

Simplicio
I honestly haven't made such observations, because I've never had that curiosity, nor an instrument fit to make them with; but I fully mean to. And meanwhile we can leave this question hanging and pass to the point that follows: your producing the grounds on which you hold that the Earth can reflect the Sun's light no less vigorously than the Moon. For to me it looks so dark and opaque that such an effect strikes me as utterly impossible.

Salviati
The cause that makes you consider the Earth unfit for illumination is not at all what you think, Simplicio. And wouldn't it be a fine thing if I understood your own reasoning better than you do yourself?

Simplicio
Whether I reason well or badly, you may perhaps know better than I do; but that you can see into my reasoning better than I can myself — that I will never believe.

Salviati
Then I'll make you believe it right now. Tell me a little: when the Moon is nearly full, so that it can be seen by day and at midnight too, when does it look more brilliant to you — by day, or at night?

Simplicio
At night, beyond all comparison. It seems to me the Moon is like that pillar of cloud and fire that went before the children of Israel: in the Sun's presence it showed like a little cloud, but at night it was most splendid. So I have sometimes observed the Moon by day among small clouds, looking exactly like one of them, whitish; but then at night it shines magnificently.

Salviati
So that if you had never happened to see the Moon except by day, you would never have judged it brighter than one of those little clouds.

Simplicio
I firmly believe I wouldn't have.

Salviati
Now tell me: do you believe the Moon is really more luminous at night than by day, or does it only show that way through some accident?

Simplicio
I believe it shines in itself just as much by day as by night, but that its light looks greater at night because we see it against the dark field of the sky; whereas by day, the whole surrounding air being quite bright, the Moon exceeds it in light by only a little, and so appears to us much less luminous.

Salviati
Now tell me this: have you ever, at midnight, seen the terrestrial globe illuminated by the Sun?

Simplicio
That strikes me as a question to be asked only as a joke — or else of someone known to be an outright fool.

Salviati
No, no: I hold you a most sensible man, and I ask the question in earnest. So answer anyway; and then, if my talk seems beside the point, I'm content to be the fool myself — for the one who asks foolishly is a bigger fool than the one who's asked.

Simplicio
If, then, you don't take me for a complete simpleton, consider that I've answered you, and said this: it is impossible for anyone standing on the Earth, as we do, to see by night that part of the Earth where it is day — the part the Sun is striking.

Salviati
So it has never fallen to you to see the Earth illuminated except by day; whereas the Moon you see shining in the sky even in the deepest night. And that, Simplicio, is the reason you believe the Earth doesn't shine like the Moon. If you could see the Earth illuminated while you stood in a place as dark as our night is, you would see it more resplendent than the Moon. Now, if the comparison is to be run fairly, we must set the Earth's light against the light of the Moon seen by day — not against the nighttime Moon — since we never get to see the Earth illuminated except by day. Isn't that so?

Simplicio
So it must be.

Salviati
And since you yourself have already confessed to having seen the Moon by day among whitish little clouds, looking in appearance exactly like one of them, you have thereby already granted, to begin with, that those little clouds — which after all are elemental matter — are as fit to receive illumination as the Moon is; more fit, in fact, if you will call back to mind having sometimes seen certain enormous clouds white as snow. And there can be no doubt that if such a cloud could stay that luminous through the deepest night, it would light the surrounding country more than a hundred Moons. If we were certain, then, that the Earth is lit by the Sun as much as one of those clouds, no doubt would remain that it shines no less than the Moon. And on this point every doubt vanishes when we see those same clouds, in the Sun's absence, stay as dark by night as the Earth does; and — what's more — there is not one of us who hasn't many times seen certain low, distant clouds and stood in doubt whether they were clouds or mountains: clear evidence that mountains are no less luminous than those clouds.

Sagredo
But why go on arguing? Look — there's the Moon up there, more than half full; and there's that high wall the Sun is beating on. Step back this way, so the Moon shows beside the wall. Now look: which seems brighter to you? Don't you see that if either has the advantage, it's the wall? The Sun strikes that wall; from there the light bounces onto the walls of the hall; from those it is reflected into that room beyond, so that it arrives there at the third reflection — and even so, I am certain there is more light in that room than if the Moon's light reached it directly.

Simplicio
Oh, that I don't believe. The Moon's light — above all when it's full — is a great illumination.

Sagredo
It seems great because of the darkness of the shadowy places around it, but in absolute terms it isn't much; it's less than the twilight half an hour after sunset. That's plain, because it's only around then that the shadows of bodies lit by the Moon begin to be distinguishable on the ground. As for whether that third reflection lights that room better than the Moon's first — that can be settled by going in there and reading a book, and then trying again tonight, by moonlight, whether it reads more easily or less. Less, I have no doubt at all.

Salviati
Now, Simplicio — if you are satisfied, that is — you can see how you yourself truly knew all along that the Earth shines no less than the Moon, and that merely being reminded of things you knew on your own, and were never taught by me, is what has made you certain of it. For I did not teach you that the Moon shows more resplendent by night than by day — you knew it yourself; just as you knew on your own that a little cloud shows as bright as the Moon. You knew likewise that the Earth's illumination cannot be seen at night; in short, you knew everything, without knowing that you knew it. From here, then, it should not reasonably be hard for you to grant that the Earth's reflection can light the dark part of the Moon with a light no weaker than that with which the Moon lights the darkness of our night — indeed, so much the stronger, inasmuch as the Earth is forty times larger than the Moon.

Simplicio
Truly, I believed that secondary light — that dim glow on the dark part — was the Moon's own.

Salviati
And this too you know on your own, and don't notice that you know it. Tell me: haven't you known, all by yourself, that the Moon shows far more luminous by night than by day, on account of the darkness of the surrounding field? And consequently, don't you know in general that every shining body shows brighter, the darker its surroundings?

Simplicio
That I know perfectly well.

Salviati
When the Moon is a thin crescent and shows you that secondary light quite plainly, isn't it always near the Sun, and consequently in the glow of twilight?

Simplicio
It is; and many times I've wished the air would grow darker so I could see that light more clearly — but the Moon always set before full dark.

Salviati
So you know very well that in deep night that light would show more?

Simplicio
Yes indeed — and more still, if one could take away the great light of the horns the Sun touches, whose presence badly dims the other, lesser light.

Salviati
Well then — doesn't it sometimes happen that one can see the Moon's whole disk on the darkest of nights, without its being lit by the Sun at all?

Simplicio
I don't know that that ever happens, except in total eclipses of the Moon.

Salviati
Then at those moments this light of its own ought to show at its most vivid, standing as it does in a pitch-dark field, undimmed by the brightness of the luminous horns. In that state, how bright have you seen it?

Simplicio
I've seen it sometimes the color of copper, faintly whitish; but at other times it has stayed so dark that I lost sight of it entirely.

Salviati
How, then, can that light be the Moon's own — this light you see so clearly in the pale of twilight, despite the interference of the great splendor of the horns right beside it, and which then, in the darkest night, with every other light removed, doesn't appear at all?

Simplicio
I understand there have been some who believed such light comes to the Moon as a share from the other stars, and in particular from Venus, its neighbor.

Salviati
And that likewise is an empty notion; because at the time of its total darkening it ought then to show more brilliant than ever — for no one can say the Earth's shadow blocks its view of Venus or of the other stars. But in fact the Moon is then wholly deprived of that light, because the terrestrial hemisphere facing the Moon at that time is the one where it is night — that is, a total privation of the Sun's light. And if you go observing carefully, you will see with your own senses that just as the Moon, when it is a thin crescent, gives very little light to the Earth, and as the Sun-lit part of it grows, the splendor reflected down to us grows along with it — so too the Moon, while it is a thin crescent and, standing between the Sun and the Earth, looks out over a very great part of the illuminated terrestrial hemisphere, shows quite bright; and as it draws away from the Sun toward quadrature — the half-moon position — that light is seen to languish; and past quadrature it shows quite feeble, because it keeps losing more and more of its view of the luminous part of the Earth. Yet exactly the contrary ought to happen if that light were its own, or lent to it by the stars — for then we could see it in deep night, against very dark surroundings.

Simplicio
Stop, please — for just now I remember reading, in a recent little book of theses, one full of novelties, "that this secondary light is not caused by the stars, nor is it the Moon's own, and least of all is it lent to her by the Earth; rather it derives from the Sun's own illumination, which — the substance of the lunar globe being somewhat transparent — penetrates through the whole of its body; but it lights most vividly the surface of the hemisphere exposed to the Sun's rays, while the depths beneath, drinking in that light and, so to speak, soaking themselves with it after the manner of a cloud or a crystal, transmit it and become visibly luminous." And this (if I remember rightly) he proves by authority, by experience, and by reason, citing Cleomedes, Witelo, Macrobius, and some other modern author; and he adds that experience shows the Moon very bright in the days near conjunction — that is, when it is a crescent — and shining above all around its rim; and further, he writes that in solar eclipses, when the Moon stands beneath the Sun's disk, it can be seen glowing through, especially around its outermost circle. As for the reasons, I think he says that since this light can come neither from the Earth nor from the stars nor from the Moon itself, it necessarily remains that it comes from the Sun; and besides, once this supposition is granted, tidy explanations can be given for every particular that arises. That the secondary light shows livelier around the outer rim has for its cause the shortness of the space the Sun's rays have to penetrate there — since, of the lines crossing a circle, the greatest is the one through the center, and of the others, those farther from it are always shorter than those nearer. From the same principle, he says, it follows that this light diminishes very little. And finally, on this account one can assign the cause of why that brighter circle around the Moon's outer margin is seen, in a solar eclipse, in the part standing under the Sun's disk, but not in the part outside it; this comes about because the Sun's rays pass straight through the parts of the Moon lying under the disk and on to our eye, while from the parts outside the disk they fall wide of the eye.
