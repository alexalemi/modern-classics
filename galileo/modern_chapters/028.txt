The Second Day

(Part 16 of 20)

Simplicio
The author will prove that it can be neither internal nor external.

Salviati
And I'll answer that the ball up in the concave wasn't moving at all — which frees me from having to explain how, in coming down, it stays forever vertical over the same point, seeing that it won't stay there in the first place.

Simplicio
Fine; but since heavy and light bodies can have no principle, internal or external, for moving in a circle, neither will the terrestrial globe move with circular motion — and so we'll have exactly what we set out to prove.

Salviati
I never said the Earth has no principle of circular motion, internal or external; I said I don't know which of the two it has — and my not knowing has no power to strip it of one. But if this author knows from what principle the other bodies of the world are moved around in their circles — bodies that certainly do move — then I say that whatever makes the Earth move is something like whatever moves Mars and Jupiter, and like whatever he believes moves the starry sphere as well. And if he'll assure me who the mover is of any one of these moving bodies, I bind myself to tell him who makes the Earth move. What's more, I'll do the very same thing if he can teach me who moves the parts of the Earth downward.

Simplicio
The cause of that effect is perfectly well known — everyone knows it's gravity.

Salviati
You're mistaken, Simplicio; you should have said that everyone knows it's called gravity. But I'm not asking its name — I'm asking the essence of the thing; and of that essence you know not one bit more than you know of the essence of whatever moves the stars around. The only difference is the name: to this one we've given a name and made it familiar and homely through the frequent experience of seeing it happen a thousand times a day. But we don't really understand any better what principle or power moves a stone downward than we understand what moves it upward once it's parted from the thrower, or what moves the Moon around — except, as I said, the name: to this one we've assigned the more particular and proper name of gravity, whereas to that one, in a more general term, we assign impressed force; to another we give an intelligence, or an assisting spirit, or an informing one; and to countless other motions we give nature as their cause.

Simplicio
It seems to me this author asks far less than what you're refusing to answer; for he doesn't ask you what, particularly and by name, the principle is that moves heavy and light bodies in a circle — whatever it may be, he only asks whether you judge it intrinsic or extrinsic. So that, for example, even though I don't know what gravity is — the gravity by which the Earth descends — I do know that it's an internal principle, since, unimpeded, it moves spontaneously; and, on the other hand, I know that the principle moving it upward is external, even though I don't know what the force impressed on it by the thrower actually is.

Salviati
Into how many questions we'd have to wander off, if we wanted to settle every difficulty that keeps hooking onto the last one in a chain! You call external — and preternatural and violent too — the principle that moves a heavy projectile upward. But perhaps it's no less internal and natural than the one that moves it down. It might, if you like, be called external and violent while the moving body is still joined to the thrower; but once they're separated, what external thing is left to move the arrow or the ball? So we're forced to admit that the power carrying it upward is no less internal than the one that moves it down; and I hold the upward motion of heavy bodies, from the impetus they've taken on, to be every bit as natural as their downward motion, which depends on gravity.

Simplicio
This I'll never grant; because the one has an internal principle, natural and perpetual, while the other has an external one, violent and finite.

Salviati
If you back away from granting me that the principles of the downward and upward motions of heavy bodies are equally internal and natural, what would you do if I told you they might even be one and the same in number?

Simplicio
I'll leave that for you to judge.

Salviati
No, I want you yourself for judge. So tell me: do you believe that in one and the same natural body there can reside internal principles that are contrary to each other?

Simplicio
Absolutely not, I believe.

Salviati
Of earth, of lead, of gold — in short, of the heaviest kinds of matter — what do you take their natural, intrinsic inclination to be? That is, toward what motion do you believe their internal principle draws them?

Simplicio
Toward motion to the center of heavy things — that is, to the center of the universe and of the Earth, where, unimpeded, they would take themselves.

Salviati
So that if the terrestrial globe were bored through by a well passing through its center, a cannonball dropped down it, moved by a natural and intrinsic principle, would carry itself to the center; and all this motion it would perform spontaneously, by an intrinsic principle — isn't that so?

Simplicio
That I hold for certain.

Salviati
But once it reached the center, do you believe it would pass on beyond, or that right there it would immediately cease from motion?

Simplicio
I believe it would go on moving for a very long stretch.

Salviati
But this motion past the center — wouldn't it be upward, and, by your own account, preternatural and violent? And on what other principle will you make it depend, except that very same one that carried the ball to the center — the one you called intrinsic and natural? Find me an external thrower that comes along again to drive it up. And what we say about the motion through the center can be seen up here among us too: for the internal impetus of a heavy body falling down a sloping surface, if that same surface curves up at the bottom and bends back upward, will carry the body up as well, without interrupting its motion in the least. A lead ball hanging from a string, drawn aside from the vertical, descends spontaneously, pulled by its internal inclination, and — without any pause of rest in between — passes through the lowest point and, with no fresh mover arriving, moves upward. I know you won't deny that the principle moving heavy bodies down is every bit as natural and internal to them as the one moving light bodies up. So I put before you a ball of wood, which, coming down through the air from a great height — and therefore moving by an internal principle — reaches the surface of a deep pool of water and continues its descent, sinking for a long stretch with no other external mover; and yet its downward motion through the water is preternatural to it, and for all that it depends on a principle that is internal, not external, to the ball. There, then, you have it demonstrated: one and the same internal principle can move a moving body in contrary motions.

Simplicio
I believe there are answers to all these objections, though for the moment they don't come to me. But however that may be, the author goes on to ask on what principle this circular motion of heavy and light bodies depends — that is, whether on an internal or an external one — and proceeds to prove that it can be neither, saying: Si ab externo, Deusne illum excitat per continuum miraculum? an vero angelus? an aër? Et hunc quidem multi assignant. Sed contra... — "If from something external, is it God who stirs it up by a continual miracle? Or is it an angel? Or the air? And indeed many assign it to the air. But against this..."

Salviati
Don't wear yourself out reading the objections, because I'm not one of those who attribute such a principle to the surrounding air. As for the miracle or the angel, I'd lean rather that way; for something that begins from a divine miracle or an angelic operation — such as the carrying of a cannonball up to the concave of the Moon — makes it not at all improbable that, by virtue of the same principle, it should do the rest as well. But as for the air, it's enough for me that it doesn't hinder the circular motion of the bodies that are said to move through it; and to do that, it's enough — nothing more is required — that the air move with the same motion and complete its circuits at the same speed as the terrestrial globe.

Simplicio
And he'll rise up against this too, asking who carries the air around — nature, or violence? And he refutes nature, saying that this goes against truth, against experience, against Copernicus himself.

Salviati
Against Copernicus it's nothing of the kind — Copernicus writes no such thing, and this author credits it to him with far too much courtesy. On the contrary, Copernicus says — and to my mind says it well — that the part of the air near the Earth, being rather a terrestrial exhalation, may share the Earth's nature and naturally follow its motion; or else, being right next to it, follow it in the way the Peripatetics say the upper region and the element of fire follow the motion of the Moon's concave. So it's for them to explain whether that motion is natural or violent.

Simplicio
The author will reply that if Copernicus makes only a lower part of the air move, the upper part lacking such motion, he won't be able to account for how that still air could carry the heavy bodies along with it and make them follow the Earth's motion.

Salviati
Copernicus will say that this natural propensity of the elemental bodies to follow the Earth's motion has a limited sphere, beyond which that natural inclination would cease; and besides, as I've said, it isn't the air that carries the moving bodies along — bodies that, once separated from the Earth, still follow its motion. So all the objections this author brings to prove that the air might not cause such effects collapse.

Simplicio
Since, then, that's not how it is, we'll have to say that such effects depend on an internal principle; against which position oboriuntur difficillimae, immo inextricabiles, quaestiones secundae — "there arise most difficult, indeed inextricable, second-order questions" — which are these: Principium illud internum vel est accidens, vel substantia: si primum, quale nam illud? nam qualitas loco motiva circum hactenus nulla videtur esse agnita. — "That internal principle is either an accident or a substance: if the first, what is it, then? For no quality producing motion in a circle has, up to now, been recognized to exist."

Salviati
How is it that none is known? Aren't these here the very ones — the qualities that move all this elemental matter around, together with the Earth? See how this author assumes as true the very thing in question.

Simplicio
He says that this isn't seen, and on this point it seems to me he's right.

Salviati
It isn't seen by us, because we're going around together with them.

Simplicio
Listen to the next objection:

Salviati
Granting for now that water and fire are contraries — and likewise air and earth (though there'd be plenty to say about that) — the most that could follow from it is that motions which are contrary to each other can't be common to them: so that, for example, the upward motion which naturally belongs to fire couldn't belong to water; rather, since water is by nature contrary to fire, the motion that suits it is the one contrary to fire's motion, which will be the motion deorsum — downward. But circular motion, which is contrary neither to sursum nor to deorsum — neither to up nor to down — and can in fact be mixed with both, as Aristotle himself affirms, why shouldn't it belong equally to heavy and to light bodies? Then again, the motions that can't be common to the living and the non-living are those that depend on the soul; but the motions that belong to the body, insofar as it is elemental and consequently shares in the qualities of the elements — why shouldn't those be common to the corpse and to the living thing? And so, if circular motion is proper to the elements, it will have to be common to the compounds as well.

Sagredo
This author must believe that when a dead cat falls from a window, it can't be that a live one could fall too — since it wouldn't be fitting for a corpse to share the qualities that belong to a living thing.

Salviati
So this author's argument doesn't hold against anyone who'd say that the principle of the circular motion of heavy and light bodies is an internal accident. How well he'll manage to prove it can't be a substance, I don't know.

Simplicio
He rises up against this with many objections, the first of which is this: Si secundum (nempe si dicas, tale principium esse substantiam), illud est aut materia, aut forma, aut compositum; sed repugnant iterum tot diversae rerum naturae, quales sunt aves, limaces, saxa, sagittae, nives, fumi, grandines, pisces, etc., quae tamen omnia, specie et genere differentia, moverentur a natura sua circulariter, ipsa naturis diversissima, etc. — "If the second — namely, if you say that such a principle is a substance — then it is either matter, or form, or a composite; but again there stand in the way so many different natures of things, such as birds, snails, stones, arrows, snow, smoke, hail, fish, and so on — all of which, differing in species and in genus, would nonetheless be moved by their own nature in a circle, they themselves being utterly different in nature, and so forth."

Salviati
If these things he lists are of different natures, and things of different natures can't have a common motion, then, if we're to satisfy them all, we'll have to think of something more than just two motions, up and down. And if we must find one motion for arrows, one for snails, another for stones, one for fish, then we'll also have to think about earthworms, and topazes, and agaric — which are no less different in nature from one another than hail and snow.

Simplicio
It seems you're making fun of these arguments.

Salviati
Not at all, Simplicio; but it's already been answered above — namely, that if a downward or an upward motion can belong to the things he lists, a circular one can belong to them no less. And staying within Peripatetic doctrine: wouldn't you put a greater difference between an elemental comet and a celestial star than between a fish and a bird? And yet those two both move in a circle. Now go on to the second argument.

Simplicio
Si Terra staret per voluntatem Dei, rotarentne caetera annon? si hoc, falsum est a natura gyrari; si illud, redeunt priores quaestiones; et sane mirum esset, quod gavia pisciculo, alauda nidulo suo et corvus limaci petraeque, etiam volens, imminere non posset. — "If the Earth stood still by the will of God, would the rest go on turning or not? If yes, then it's false that they spin by nature; if no, the earlier questions come back. And it would surely be a marvel that a gull could not hover — even if it wanted to — over its little fish, nor a lark over its nest, nor a crow over a snail or a stone."

Salviati
For my part, I'd give a general answer: that, granting the Earth were to stop its daily whirl by the will of God, those birds would do whatever that same will of God pleased. But if this author wants a more particular answer, I'd tell him they'd do exactly the opposite of what they would do if, while they were up in the air separated from the Earth, the terrestrial globe were by divine will suddenly thrown into a headlong motion. It's now for this author to assure us what would happen in that case.

Sagredo
Please, Salviati, grant this author my request — that, the Earth having stopped by the will of God, the other things separated from it went on wheeling around in their natural motion — and let's hear what impossibilities or absurdities would follow. For my own part, I can't see any greater disorders than the ones the author himself produces: namely, that the larks, however much they wanted to, couldn't keep themselves over their nests, nor the crows over the snails or the stones; from which it would follow that the crows would have to go without their craving for snails, and the little larks would die of hunger and cold, since they could be neither fed nor brooded by their mothers. That's the whole ruin I can draw out of it, going by what the author says. See for yourself, Simplicio, whether any greater troubles ought to follow.

Simplicio
I can't make out any greater ones myself, but it's quite believable that the author sees, beyond these, other disorders in nature which perhaps, for reasons of his own worthy of respect, he chose not to bring forward. So I'll go on to the third objection: Insuper, quî fit ut istae res tam variae tantum moveantur ab occasu in ortum parallelae ad aequatorem? ut semper moveantur, numquam quiescant? — "Besides, how does it come about that things so various all move only from west to east, parallel to the equator? That they always move and never rest?"

Salviati
They move from west to east, parallel to the equinoctial, without stopping — in exactly the way you believe the fixed stars move from east to west, parallel to the equinoctial, without stopping.

Simplicio
Quare quo sunt altiores celerius, quo humiliores tardius? — "Why do the higher ones move faster, the lower ones slower?"

Salviati
Because in a sphere or a circle turning about its center, the parts farther out trace larger circles, and the nearer ones trace smaller circles in the same time.

Simplicio
Quare quae aequinoctiali propiores in maiori, quae remotiores in minori, circulo feruntur? — "Why are the ones nearer the equinoctial carried in a larger circle, and those farther off in a smaller one?"

Salviati
To imitate the starry sphere, in which the ones nearer the equinoctial move in larger circles than the ones farther away.

Simplicio
Quare pila eadem sub aequinoctiali tota circa centrum Terrae ambitu maximo, celeritate incredibili, sub polo vero circa centrum proprium gyro nullo, tarditate suprema, volveretur? — "Why would one and the same ball, under the equinoctial, revolve wholly around the center of the Earth in the greatest circuit, with incredible speed, but under the pole revolve around its own center with no circuit at all, at the utmost slowness?"

Salviati
To imitate the stars of the firmament, which would do the same if the daily rotation were theirs.

Simplicio
Quare eadem res, pila v. g. plumbea, si semel Terram circuivit descripto circulo maximo, eamdem ubique non circummigret secundum circulum maximum, sed translata extra aequinoctialem in circulis minoribus agetur? — "Why does the same thing — a lead ball, say — if it has once gone around the Earth describing a great circle, not travel everywhere along a great circle, but instead, carried away from the equinoctial, get driven in smaller circles?"

Salviati
Because that's just what they'd do — indeed, on Ptolemy's doctrine, what they have done: certain fixed stars that were once very close to the equinoctial and traced enormous circles, and now, being far from it, trace smaller ones.

Sagredo
Oh, if only I could keep all these fine things in my head, I'd feel I'd made a real fortune! Simplicio, you simply must lend me this little book — there's bound to be a whole sea of rare and exquisite things inside it.

Simplicio
I'll make you a present of it.

Sagredo
Oh no, that I'd never do — I wouldn't deprive you of it for anything. But are the questions finished yet?

Simplicio
No, sir; just listen: Si latio circularis gravibus et levibus est naturalis, qualis est ea quae fit secundum lineam rectam? nam si naturalis, quomodo et is motus qui circum est, naturalis est, cum specie differat a recto? si violentus, quî fit ut missile ignitum, sursum evolans, scintillosum caput sursum a Terra, non autem circum, volvatur, etc.? — "If circular motion is natural to heavy and light bodies, what sort of motion is the one that goes in a straight line? For if it's natural, how is the motion that goes around also natural, since it differs in species from the straight? And if it's violent, how does it come about that a fiery missile, flying upward, turns its sparkling head upward, away from the Earth, and not around it, and so on?"

Salviati
It's been said a thousand times already that circular motion is natural to the whole and to the parts, so long as they're in their best arrangement; the straight is there to bring disordered parts back into order — though it's better to say that they never, whether ordered or disordered, move with straight motion, but with a mixed motion, which might even be pure circular. Only, of this mixed motion just one part stays visible and observable to us, namely the straight part, while the other part, the circular, remains imperceptible to us, because we share in it too. And this answers the case of the rockets, which move upward and around, but we can't make out the circular part, because we're moving along with it. But I don't think this author has ever grasped this mixture, since one can see how flatly he says that rockets go straight up and don't go around at all.

Simplicio
Quare centrum spherae delapsae sub aequatore spiram describit in eius plano, sub aliis parallelis spiram describit in cono? sub polo descendit in axe, lineam gyralem decurrens in superficie cylindrica consignatam? — "Why does the center of a falling sphere, under the equator, describe a spiral in the plane of the equator; under the other parallels, describe a spiral on a cone; while under the pole it descends along the axis, running out a spiral line traced on the surface of a cylinder?"

Salviati
Because of the lines drawn from the center to the circumference of the sphere — the lines along which heavy bodies descend — the one ending at the equinoctial marks out a circle, and those ending at the other parallels trace conical surfaces, while the axis traces nothing at all, but stays as it is. And if I'm to tell you my opinion freely, I'll say that I can't get out of all these questions any conclusion that weighs against the motion of the Earth; because if I asked this author — granting him that the Earth doesn't move — what would happen with all these particulars supposing it did move as Copernicus wants, I'm quite sure he'd say that all these very effects would follow: the ones he now urges as absurdities to rule out the Earth's mobility. So that in this man's mind, necessary consequences are reckoned as absurdities. But please, if there's anything more, let's get ourselves free of this tedious business.

Simplicio
In what comes next, there's an attack on Copernicus and his followers, who hold that the motion of the parts, once separated from their whole, is only for the sake of reuniting with that whole, but that what's absolutely natural is to move in a circle with the daily whirl. Against them he presses, saying that, according to these men's opinion, si tota Terra, una cum aqua, in nihilum redigeretur, nulla grando aut pluvia e nube decideret, sed naturaliter tantum circumferretur; neque ignis ullus aut igneum ascenderet, cum, illorum non improbabili sententia, ignis nullus sit supra. — "if the whole Earth, together with the water, were reduced to nothing, no hail or rain would fall from a cloud, but would only be carried around naturally; nor would any fire or fiery thing rise, since, by their not improbable opinion, there is no fire up above."

Salviati
This philosopher's foresight is marvelous, and worthy of great praise, seeing that he isn't content to think about the things that might happen while nature runs its course, but wants to be provided in advance in case those things should come about that we know absolutely will never come about. So then — just to hear some fine subtlety — I'll grant him that if the Earth and the water went to nothing, neither hail nor rain would fall anymore, nor would fiery matter rise any higher but would keep going around: what then? And what will the philosopher have to raise against me?
