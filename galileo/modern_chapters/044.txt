The Third Day

(Part 12 of 16)

Sagredo
I've listened to your argument with great pleasure — and profit, too; and to make sure I've followed it correctly, I'll sum up the conclusions in a few words. It seems to me you've explained that there are two distinct kinds of appearance we could observe in the fixed stars by means of the Earth's annual motion. One is the change in their apparent magnitudes, as we, carried along by the Earth, draw nearer to them or farther away. The other — which likewise depends on that same approaching and receding — is their showing up in the same meridian sometimes higher and sometimes lower. What's more, you tell me (and I understand it perfectly) that neither of these changes happens equally in all the stars, but more in some, less in others, and not at all in still others. The approach and recession by which a given star should look now larger and now smaller is imperceptible — practically nil — in the stars near the pole of the ecliptic, but greatest in the stars sitting right on the ecliptic, and middling in those in between; while the reverse holds for the other difference, that is, the raising and lowering is nil in the stars on the ecliptic, greatest in those near the pole of the ecliptic, and middling in the ones between. Beyond that, both of these differences are more noticeable in stars that happen to be closer, less noticeable in the farther ones, and finally, in the extremely distant ones, they would vanish altogether. That's my part of it; what remains, as I see it, is to satisfy Simplicio, who I don't think will readily agree to wave off as imperceptible such differences — differences springing from so vast a movement of the Earth, and from a change that carries the Earth to places twice as far apart from each other as we are from the Sun.

Simplicio
To speak freely, I do feel a great reluctance to grant that the distance of the fixed stars has to be so enormous that in them the differences we've been describing must be completely undetectable.

Salviati
Don't give yourself up entirely to despair, Simplicio — there may still be some easing of your difficulties. First of all, that the apparent size of the stars isn't seen to change noticeably shouldn't strike you as at all improbable, once you notice how badly people's estimation deceives them in this sort of thing, especially when they're looking at bright objects. Take yourself, for example, gazing at a lit torch 200 paces off: if it came 3 or 4 arm-lengths closer, would you claim to notice it, because it looked bigger? I certainly wouldn't notice, not even if it came 20 or 30 closer. In fact I've sometimes happened to watch a light like that at such a distance and couldn't make up my mind whether it was coming toward me or moving away, when in reality it was drawing nearer. But what of it? If that same approaching and receding (I mean twice the distance from the Sun to us) is almost totally imperceptible in the star of Saturn, and barely observable in Jupiter, what must it be in the fixed stars, which I don't think you'd be unwilling to place at twice the distance of Saturn? In Mars, which by coming closer to us —

Simplicio
Don't trouble yourself any further on this particular point; I'm already convinced that what's been said about the unchanged apparent size of the fixed stars could very well be the case. But what shall we say about the other difficulty — the one that arises from our not detecting any variation in the change of position?

Salviati
We may say something that could set you at ease on this side too. And to come straight to it: wouldn't you be satisfied if we actually did observe in the stars those changes which you think ought to be seen if the annual motion belonged to the Earth?

Simplicio
I'd be satisfied beyond any doubt, as far as this particular point is concerned.

Salviati
I'd like you to say that if such a difference were observed, nothing would be left that could cast doubt on the Earth's mobility, seeing that no other explanation could be assigned to such an appearance. But even supposing it doesn't show up perceptibly, that still doesn't remove the mobility, nor does it necessarily prove immobility, since it may be (as Copernicus maintains) that the immense distance of the starry sphere makes such tiny appearances impossible to observe. And those appearances, as I've already said, may be that up to now they've never even been looked for — or, if looked for, not in the way they should be, that is, with the exactness that such minute precision would require. That exactness is hard to come by, partly through the shortcomings of astronomical instruments, which are subject to many distortions, and partly through the fault of those who handle them with less care than they ought. A necessarily conclusive proof of how little such observations are to be trusted is the disagreement we find among astronomers in assigning the positions — I won't say of new stars and comets, but of the fixed stars themselves, right down to the polar altitudes, where more often than not they're at odds with one another by many arcminutes. And to tell the truth, who, working with a quadrant or a sextant whose side is at most 3 or 4 arm-lengths long, could be sure — in the seating of the plumb line, or in the cut of the sight-vane — not to be off by two or three arcminutes, which around its circumference will be no wider than a grain of millet? On top of that, it's nearly impossible for the instrument to be built and kept with absolute accuracy. Ptolemy himself shows distrust of an armillary instrument built by Archimedes for catching the Sun's entrance into the equinoctial.

Simplicio
But if the instruments are so suspect and the observations so doubtful, how can we ever put ourselves on secure ground and free ourselves from error? I'd heard great things preached about Tycho's instruments, made at immense expense, and about his singular diligence in his observations.

Salviati
All of that I grant you; but neither the one nor the other is enough to make us secure in a matter of such importance. I want us to make use of instruments far, far larger than Tycho's — supremely exact, and made at hardly any expense at all — whose side is 4, 6, 20, 30, or 50 miles, so that a degree comes out a mile wide, an arcminute 50 arm-lengths, and an arcsecond a little under an arm-length. In short, we can have them, without spending a thing, of any size we please. Staying at a villa of mine near Florence, I plainly observed the Sun's arrival at, and departure from, the summer solstice: one evening, as it set, it slipped behind a crag of the Pietrapana mountains about 60 miles off, leaving a thin sliver of itself uncovered toward the north, a sliver whose width was not the hundredth part of the Sun's diameter; and the next evening, at a similar setting, it again showed a similar uncovered sliver, but noticeably thinner — a sure sign that it had begun to pull away from the tropic. The Sun's backward shift from the first observation to the second certainly didn't amount to so much as one arcsecond on the horizon; and the observation, made with a superb telescope that magnifies the Sun's disc more than a thousand times, comes out both easy and delightful. Now, with instruments like these I want us to make our observations of the fixed stars, using one of those in which the change ought to be most conspicuous — namely, as already stated, the ones most remote from the ecliptic. Among these the Lyre, a very bright star near the pole of the ecliptic, would be well suited for very northern countries, worked in the manner I'll describe shortly; but I'll use a different star. I've already scouted out a spot quite well suited for such an observation. The place is an open plain, above which, toward the north, there rises a very lofty mountain, on whose summit is built a small church, set from west toward east, so that the ridge of its roof can cut the meridian of some dwelling down in the plain at right angles. I mean to fix a little beam parallel to that ridge, or crest of the roof, and about an arm-length away from it. Once that's set, I'll look in the plain for the spot from which one of the stars of the Wain — the Big Dipper — as it crosses the meridian, comes to be hidden behind the beam I've placed there; or else, if the beam isn't thick enough to hide the star, I'll find the spot from which that same beam is seen to cut the star's disc in half — an effect that a superb telescope brings out exquisitely. And if the spot from which this occurs happens to have some dwelling on it, so much the better; if not, I'll have a stake driven firmly into the ground, with a fixed mark to show where the eye should be placed each time one wants to repeat the observation. The first of these observations I'll make around the summer solstice, and then go on from month to month, or whenever I please, until the other solstice; with this observation one could detect the raising and lowering of the star, however slight it might be. And if, in carrying it out, we should manage to make out any change at all, what a great gain there would be for astronomy! For by such means, besides assuring ourselves of the annual motion, we could come to know the size and distance of that very star.

Sagredo
I follow the whole procedure perfectly, and the operation seems to me so easy, and so well suited to what's needed, that one might very reasonably believe Copernicus himself, or some other astronomer, had already carried it out.

Salviati
It seems to me quite the opposite, because it isn't likely that, if anyone had tried it, he wouldn't have made some mention of the outcome — whether it turned out in favor of the one opinion or the other. Besides, neither for this nor for any other purpose does one find that anyone made use of such a way of observing — which, moreover, could scarcely be carried out at all without an exact telescope.
