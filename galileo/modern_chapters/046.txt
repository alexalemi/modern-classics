The Third Day

(Part 14 of 16)

Simplicio
To be honest, I haven't fully grasped it — perhaps because I don't have ready to hand the explanations Ptolemy gives for these same effects: I mean the stations and retrogradations of the planets, their drawing nearer and moving farther off, the lengthening and shortening of the days, the changing of the seasons, and so on. But setting aside the consequences that follow from the first assumptions, I feel real difficulties — and no small ones — in those assumptions themselves; and when the assumptions are knocked down, they bring the whole edifice crashing down with them. Now, since the entire Copernican machine seems to me to rest on shaky foundations — for it leans on the mobility of the Earth — once that is taken away, there's no need to go on to any further disputes. And to take it away, Aristotle's axiom seems to me more than sufficient: that of a simple body only a single simple motion can be natural. Yet here the Earth, a simple body, is assigned three motions — if not four — and very different ones at that. For besides the straight motion downward toward the center, which, being heavy, it can't be denied, they credit it with a circular motion in a great circle around the Sun once a year, and with a spin on its own axis every twenty-four hours; and then — the most outlandish of all, and perhaps that's why you passed over it in silence — yet another turning about its own center, opposite to the first, the twenty-four-hour one, and completed once a year. To this my mind feels the very greatest resistance.

Salviati
As for the downward motion, we've already settled that it belongs in no way to the terrestrial globe as a whole, which has never moved with such a motion and never will; it belongs (if it belongs to anything) to the parts, as they rejoin their whole. As for the annual and the daily motions, since these both run the same way, they're perfectly compatible — just as, if we let a ball roll down a sloping surface, it will, in coming down of its own accord, also turn on itself. And now for the third motion Copernicus assigns to it, the yearly turn on its own axis whose sole purpose is to keep its axis tilted and pointing toward the same region of the firmament — here I'll tell you something well worth the greatest consideration. Namely, that so far from there being any resistance or difficulty in it (even though it runs contrary to the other yearly motion), this turn belongs, most naturally and without any moving cause at all, to any body whatsoever that is suspended and balanced: for if such a body is carried around the circumference of a circle, it immediately, and all by itself, takes on a turning about its own center, contrary to the one that carries it around, and of just such a speed that both complete one revolution in precisely the same time. You can see this remarkable experiment — and how neatly it fits our case — by putting a ball to float in a basin of water and holding the basin in your hands: if you turn yourself around on the soles of your feet, you'll see the ball at once begin to turn on itself with a motion contrary to the basin's, and finish its revolution just when the basin finishes its own. Now, what is the Earth but a globe hung and balanced in thin, yielding air, which, carried around once a year along the circumference of a great circle, must surely take on — with no other mover — a yearly turning about its own center, contrary to that other, likewise yearly, motion? You'll see this effect. But if you then look more carefully, you'll realize it isn't a real thing at all, but a mere appearance; and what looks to you like a turning-on-itself is in fact a not-moving, a keeping of the whole utterly unchanged with respect to everything outside you and the basin that stays still. For if you mark some spot on that ball and note which part of the wall of the room you're in — or of the countryside, or of the sky — it faces, you'll see that spot, as the basin and you turn, keep facing that same part the whole time; but compared with the basin and with yourself, which are the ones moving, it will certainly appear to change direction, and with a motion contrary to yours and the basin's it will seem to seek out every point along the basin's circuit. So that it's truer to say that you and the basin turn around the motionless ball than that the ball turns inside the basin. In just this way the Earth, hung and balanced on the circumference of the great orb, and set so that one of its marks — say, its north pole — faces toward some star or other region of the firmament, keeps itself always pointed toward that same region, even as it's carried by the annual motion around the circumference of that great orb. This alone is enough to make the wonder cease and to remove every difficulty. But what will Simplicio say if, to this needing-no-cooperating-cause, we add a marvelous power built into the terrestrial globe itself — a power of facing, with certain fixed parts of it, toward certain fixed parts of the firmament? I'm speaking of the magnetic power, shared out most constantly by every last piece of lodestone. And if every tiniest particle of that stone has such a power in it, who could doubt that the same power resides, on a far grander scale, in this whole earthly globe, which abounds in such material — and that perhaps the globe itself, in its inner and primary substance, is nothing but one immense mass of lodestone?

Simplicio
So you're one of those who subscribe to the magnetic philosophy of William Gilbert?

Salviati
I certainly am, and I believe I have as my companions everyone who has read his book with attention and checked his experiments for themselves. Nor would I give up hope that what happened to me in this matter might happen to you as well — provided a curiosity like mine, and a recognition that endless things in nature remain unknown to human minds, were to free you from bondage to this or that particular writer on natural things, loosen the reins on your reasoning, and soften the stubbornness and reluctance of your judgment, so that it wouldn't refuse, now and then, to lend an ear to voices it had never heard before. But — if I may use the word — the faint-heartedness of ordinary minds has reached such a point that they not only blindly give away, indeed pay as tribute, their own assent to everything they find written by those authors whom their teachers held up to them in the first infancy of their studies, but they refuse even to listen to — let alone examine — any new proposition or problem whatsoever, though it has not only never been refuted but never so much as examined or considered by their authors. One such question is precisely this: to investigate what is the true, proper, primary, inner, and general matter and substance of this earthly globe of ours. And although it never crossed the mind of Aristotle, or of anyone before Gilbert, to ask whether it might be lodestone — much less did Aristotle or anyone else ever refute such an opinion — nonetheless I've run into plenty of people who, at the very first mention of it, like a horse shying at a shadow, have shrunk back and dodged discussing it, dismissing the idea as an empty fantasy, indeed as sheer madness. And perhaps Gilbert's book would never have come into my hands if a Peripatetic philosopher of great reputation hadn't made me a present of it — to keep his own library safe from the contagion, I imagine.

Simplicio
I, who freely admit I've been one of these ordinary minds — and only in these last few days, since I was granted leave to take part in your discussions, do I recognize that I've stepped a little off the well-worn, popular roads — still don't feel myself so far raised up that the roughness of this new and fantastic opinion doesn't strike me as very hard and difficult to get over.

Salviati
If what Gilbert writes is true, then it isn't an opinion but a subject of science; it isn't something new but as old as the Earth itself; and (being true) it can't be harsh or hard, but smooth and perfectly easy. And I, whenever you please, will let you feel with your own hands how you frighten yourself with your own shadow, recoiling in horror from something that holds nothing dreadful in it at all — like a small child afraid of the witches' sabbath, knowing nothing of it but the name, which, apart from the name, is nothing.

Simplicio
I'll be glad to be enlightened and drawn out of my error.

Salviati
Then answer the questions I'll put to you. And first, tell me whether you believe that this globe of ours, which we live on and call Earth, consists of one single, simple material, or is rather a mixture of materials different from one another.

Simplicio
I see it as made up of very different substances and bodies. First of all, as to its largest components, I see water and earth, utterly unlike each other.

Salviati
Let's set the seas and the other waters aside for now and consider the solid parts. Tell me: do they seem to you all one and the same thing, or rather different things?

Simplicio
As to appearance, I see them as different. There are vast plains of barren sand, and others of rich, fruitful soil; there are countless mountains, sterile and rugged, full of hard rocks and stones of the most varied kinds — porphyry, alabaster, jasper, and a thousand thousand other sorts of marble; there are the enormous mines of metals of so many species; and, in short, so many different materials that a whole day wouldn't be enough merely to count them.

Salviati
Now, of all these different materials, do you believe that equal portions of each go into making up this great mass? Or that among them all there's one part that far surpasses the rest and is, as it were, the chief matter and substance of the vast bulk?

Simplicio
I believe the stones, marbles, metals, gems, and all the other varied materials are really like jewels and outward, surface ornaments of the primary globe, which in bulk I think immeasurably exceeds all these other things.

Salviati
And this chief, vast bulk — of which the things you've named are, so to speak, mere outgrowths and ornaments — what material do you believe it's made of?

Simplicio
I think it's the simple, or less impure, element of earth.

Salviati
But what do you mean by earth? Perhaps this stuff that's spread over the fields, that's broken up with spades and plows, where the grain is sown and the fruit trees planted, where great forests spring up of their own accord, and which, in short, is the dwelling place of all the animals and the womb of all the plants?

Simplicio
That, I'd say, is the primary substance of this globe of ours.

Salviati
Now that doesn't seem to me well said. For this earth that's broken up, sown, and made fruitful is one part — and a very thin one — of the globe's surface, going down only a short way compared with the distance to the center. And experience shows us that you don't dig far down before you come upon materials quite different from this outer rind: firmer ones, no good for growing plants. Besides, the innermost parts, pressed as they are by the enormous weights bearing down on them from above, must in all likelihood be packed together and as hard as the hardest crag. Add to this that fruitfulness would have been given in vain to materials that were never going to bear fruit anyway, but only to lie buried forever in the deep and gloomy abysses of the Earth.

Simplicio
And who assures us that the innermost parts, near the center, are barren? Perhaps they too have their own products — things unknown to us.

Salviati
You, as well as anyone, could be certain of this — since you can readily grasp that if the integral bodies of the universe are produced solely for the benefit of the human race, then this one above all the rest must be destined for the sole conveniences of us who live on it. But what benefit could we ever draw from materials so hidden and remote from us that we'll never be able to make any use of them? So the inner substance of this globe of ours can't be a crumbling, loose, incoherent material like this surface stuff we call earth; it has to be a body extremely dense and solid — in short, a rock of the hardest kind. And if it must be such, what reason should make you any more reluctant to believe it's lodestone than porphyry, or jasper, or some other hard marble? Perhaps if Gilbert had written that this globe is made inside of grey freestone, or of chalcedony, the paradox would have struck you as less outrageous?

Simplicio
That the inner parts of this globe are more compressed, and therefore more packed and solid — and more and more so the deeper down they lie — I grant, and so does Aristotle. But that they degenerate, and are anything other than earth of the same sort as this surface earth, I hear nothing that forces me to grant.

Salviati
I haven't taken up this discussion in order to prove to you demonstratively that the primary and real substance of this globe of ours is lodestone, but only to show you that there's no reason to be any more reluctant to grant that it's lodestone than that it's some other material. And you, if you'll think it over carefully, will find it not at all improbable that a single, bare, arbitrary name has moved people to believe it's made of earth. And that name is this: from the start we've commonly used the word earth to mean both the material that's plowed and sown and to name this whole globe of ours. If its name had instead been taken from stone — as it could just as well have been, no less than from earth — then to say that its primary substance was stone would surely have met with no reluctance or contradiction from anyone. And this is all the more likely because I hold it for certain that, if you could strip the bark off this great globe — peeling away a layer a thousand or two thousand arm-lengths thick — and then separate the stones from the earth, the heap of rock would be far, far greater than the heap of fertile soil. As for the arguments that would prove conclusively, in fact, that this globe of ours is lodestone, I've offered you none, nor is this the time to offer them — all the more since at your leisure you can find them in Gilbert. Only, to fire you up to read him, I'll lay out for you with a little comparison of my own the method he follows in his philosophizing. I know you're perfectly aware how much the knowledge of a thing's properties contributes to the investigation of its substance and essence. So I want you to take pains to inform yourself well of the many properties that are found in lodestone alone, and in no other stone or body: as, for instance, its attracting iron; its conferring on iron, merely by its presence, that same power; and likewise its imparting to iron the property of facing toward the poles, just as it holds that property in itself. And beyond this, see for yourself, by trial, how there resides in it the power to give the magnetic needle not only the settling of itself along a meridian toward the poles with a horizontal motion (a property known long ago), but a newly observed property of dipping — for when it's balanced beneath a meridian marked out on a little sphere of lodestone, it dips, I say, to definite degrees, more or less, according as the needle is held nearer to or farther from the pole; until, right over the pole, it plants itself upright and perpendicular, whereas over the middle parts it lies parallel to the axis. What's more, take pains to test how, since the power of attracting iron is far more vigorous toward the poles than around the middle parts, that force is markedly stronger in one pole than in the other — and this in every piece of lodestone, the stronger pole of which is the one that faces south. Note next that in a small lodestone this southern pole, the more powerful of the two, becomes the weaker whenever it has to hold up iron in the presence of the north pole of another, much larger lodestone. And, not to make a long speech of it, satisfy yourself by experiment of these and many other properties described by Gilbert, all of which so belong to lodestone alone that not one of them attaches to any other material. Now tell me, Simplicio: suppose you were offered a thousand pieces of different materials, each of them covered and wrapped in a cloth that hid it, and you were asked to guess, without uncovering them, the material of each from outward signs; and suppose that, in trying, you came upon one that plainly showed all the properties you already knew reside in lodestone and in no other material — what would you judge the essence of that body to be? Would you say it might be a piece of ebony, or alabaster, or tin?

Simplicio
I'd say, without the slightest hesitation, that it was a piece of lodestone.

Salviati
Then say so outright: under this covering and rind of earth, of stones, of metals, of water, and so on, there hides a great lodestone — since, for anyone who takes the trouble to observe them, all those very same properties are found in it that are seen to belong to a genuine, bare globe of lodestone. Why, if you saw nothing but that business of the dipping needle, which, carried around the Earth, tips more and more as it nears the north pole and dips less toward the equinoctial, under which it finally comes into balance — that alone ought to persuade the most stubborn judgment. I say nothing of that other marvelous effect plainly seen in every piece of lodestone: that for us, dwellers in the northern hemisphere, the southern pole of the lodestone is the stronger of the two, and the difference shows up the greater the farther one moves from the equinoctial; while under the equinoctial both parts are of equal force, but notably weaker; and in the southern regions, far from the equinoctial, nature switches, and the part that was the weaker for us gains the upper hand over the other. And all this squares with what we see a little piece of lodestone do in the presence of a large one, whose power, prevailing over the smaller, makes it obey; and depending on whether it's held on this or that side of the big stone's equinoctial, it undergoes the very same changes I've described every lodestone undergoing when carried this side or that of the Earth's equinoctial.

Sagredo
I was persuaded at my first reading of Gilbert's book; and, having come across a piece of the most excellent lodestone, I spent a long time making many observations, all of them worthy of the utmost wonder. But above all I find astounding the one about increasing its power to hold up iron so enormously by arming it in the way that same author teaches. By arming that piece of mine, I multiplied its force eightfold: whereas unarmed it could barely hold up nine ounces of iron, armed it held more than six pounds. And perhaps you'll have seen this very piece in the Gallery of your Most Serene Grand Duke (to whom I gave it up), holding up two little iron anchors.
