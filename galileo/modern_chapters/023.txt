The Second Day

(Part 11 of 20)

Simplicio
The objection seems to me very well set up and tightly knotted, and it'll take something considerable, I believe, to shift it and untie it.

Salviati
Untying it depends on certain facts that you know and believe no less than I do; but because they don't come to mind, you don't see the solution. So without my teaching them to you — since you already know them — simply by reminding you of them I'll get you to resolve the objection yourself.

Simplicio
I've paid attention more than once to your way of reasoning, and it's stirred up a thought in me — that you lean toward that opinion of Plato's, that nostrum scire sit quoddam reminisci — that our knowing is a kind of recollection. So please, clear up this doubt for me by telling me what you really think.

Salviati
What I think of Plato's opinion I can convey to you both in words and in deeds. More than once already, in the discussions we've had so far, I've shown my hand by deed; I'll keep to the same style in the particular case we have in hand, which can then serve you as an example for grasping more easily my notion about how knowledge is acquired — that is, if we have time left over on another day, and if it won't bore Sagredo to have us make this digression.

Sagredo
On the contrary, it'll be most welcome, because I remember that when I studied logic I could never get my head around that so-vaunted demonstratio potissima — the "most powerful proof" — of Aristotle's.

Salviati
Let's go on, then. Simplicio, tell me: what is the motion of that little stone gripped in the notch of the staff while the boy swings it to throw it far?

Simplicio
The stone's motion, as long as it's in the notch, is circular — that is, it travels along an arc of a circle, whose fixed center is the joint of the shoulder, and whose radius is the staff together with the arm.

Salviati
And when the stone escapes from the staff, what is its motion? Does it go on continuing its earlier circular motion, or does it travel along some other line?

Simplicio
It doesn't go on moving in a circle any longer, because if it did it wouldn't get away from the thrower's shoulder — whereas we see it fly off very far indeed.

Salviati
By what motion, then, does it move?

Simplicio
Let me think about it a little, because I've never turned my mind to it before.

Salviati
Sagredo, a word in your ear: there's the quoddam reminisci out in the open field, plain as day. — You're thinking hard, Simplicio!

Simplicio
As I see it, the motion taken on as the stone leaves the notch can only be along a straight line — indeed it must necessarily be along a straight line, if we mean the pure acquired impetus alone. It bothered me a bit to see it trace an arc; but since that arc always bends downward, and not to any other side, I realize that the bending comes from the stone's own weight, which naturally pulls it down. The impressed impetus, I say without hesitation, is along a straight line.

Salviati
But along which straight line? For infinitely many, running off in every direction, can be drawn from the notch of the staff and from the point where the stone separates from it.

Simplicio
It moves along the one that lies in line with the motion the stone made together with the staff.

Salviati
The stone's motion, while it was in the notch, you've already said was circular; now, "circular" and "in a straight line" are at odds, since there's no straight part anywhere in a circular line.

Simplicio
I don't mean the projected motion is in line with the whole of the circular motion, but with that last point where the circular motion ended. I understand it inside myself, but I can't quite put it into words.

Salviati
And I too can see that you understand the thing, but you don't have the proper terms to express it. Well, those I can certainly teach you — teach you the words, that is, not the truths, which are things. And to let you feel it with your own hands, that you know the thing and only lack the terms to express it, tell me: when you fire a ball from a harquebus, in what direction does it acquire its impetus to travel?

Simplicio
It acquires the impetus to travel along that straight line which follows the line of the barrel — that is, one that veers neither right nor left, neither up nor down.

Salviati
Which in short amounts to saying that it makes no angle at all with the line of the straight motion made along the barrel.

Simplicio
That's what I meant to say.

Salviati
If, then, the line of the projectile's motion is to continue without making an angle upon the circular line it described while it was with the thrower, and if it must pass from this circular motion to straight motion, what will this straight line have to be?

Simplicio
It can only be the one that touches the circle at the point of separation, because all the others, it seems to me, would — if extended — cut the circumference, and so would contain some angle with it.

Salviati
You've reasoned very well, and shown yourself half a geometer. Keep it in memory, then, that your real idea is spelled out in these words: the projectile acquires the impetus to move along the tangent to the arc described by the thrower's motion, at the point where the projectile separates from the thrower.

Simplicio
I understand perfectly, and that's just what I wanted to say.

Salviati
Of a straight line that touches a circle, which of its points is the closest of all to the center of that circle?

Simplicio
The point of contact, without a doubt; because that one is on the circle's circumference, and the others outside it, and the points of the circumference are all equally distant from the center.

Salviati
Therefore a moving body, setting out from the point of contact and moving along the straight tangent, goes continually farther from the point of contact, and also from the center of the circle.

Simplicio
That's certainly so.

Salviati
Now, if you've kept in mind the propositions you've told me, put them back together and tell me what follows from them.

Simplicio
I don't think I'm so forgetful that I can't recall it. From what's been said it follows that the projectile, swung rapidly around by the thrower, on separating from it keeps the impetus to continue its motion along the straight line that touches the circle described by the thrower's motion, at the point of separation; and by this motion the projectile goes ever farther from the center of the circle described by the thrower's motion.

Salviati
So by now you know the reason why heavy bodies clinging to the surface of a rapidly turning wheel are extruded — flung off, I mean, beyond the circumference, ever farther from the center.

Simplicio
Of this I feel I've got a pretty good grasp; but this new understanding rather increases than lessens my disbelief that the Earth could turn with such speed without flinging stones, animals, and the rest up toward the sky.

Salviati
In the same way you've known things up to now, you'll know — indeed you already know — the rest too; and by thinking it over you'd recall it on your own again. But to save time, I'll help you remember it. So far you've known by yourself that the thrower's circular motion impresses on the projectile the impetus to move (whenever they happen to separate) along the straight tangent to the circle of the motion, at the point of separation, and that, continuing its motion along that tangent, it goes ever farther from the thrower. And you've said that the projectile would keep moving along that straight line if its own weight didn't add to it a downward inclination, from which comes the curving of the line of motion. It also seems to me you've known by yourself that this bending always tends toward the center of the Earth, because that's where all heavy bodies tend. Now I go a little further, and ask you whether the moving body, after separation, in continuing its straight motion, goes ever equally farther from the center — or, if you like, from the circumference — of that circle of which the earlier motion was a part; which is the same as asking whether a moving body that sets out from the point of the tangent, and moves along that tangent, gets equally farther from the point of contact and from the circumference of the circle.

Simplicio
No, sir; because near the point of contact the tangent departs very little from the circumference, with which it contains a very tight angle, but as it goes farther and farther, the separation always grows in greater proportion. So that in a circle with, say, a diameter of ten braccia, a point on the tangent two palms away from the contact would be found three or four times farther from the circumference than a point one palm away from the touch; and the point half a palm away, likewise, I believe, would barely be a quarter as far as the second; so that within a finger or two of the contact, one can hardly make out that the tangent is separated from the circumference at all.

Salviati
So that the projectile's departure from the circumference of the earlier circular motion is, at the very start, extremely small?

Simplicio
Almost imperceptible.

Salviati
Now tell me: the projectile that from the thrower's motion receives the impetus to move along the straight tangent — and would still go on along it if its own weight didn't pull it down — how long does it take, after separation, to begin to bend downward?

Simplicio
I believe it begins at once, because having nothing to hold it up, it can't be that its own weight doesn't act.

Salviati
So that if that stone flung from that wheel turning at great speed had a natural tendency to move toward the center of that same wheel, just as it has one to move toward the center of the Earth, it would be an easy thing for it to return to the wheel — or rather, for it never to leave it; because, at the start of separation, the departure being so very minimal, thanks to the infinite sharpness of the angle of contact, every least bit of inclination drawing it back toward the wheel's center would be enough to keep it on the circumference.

Simplicio
I have no doubt at all that, granting what is not and cannot be — namely, that the inclination of those heavy bodies were to go to the center of that wheel — they wouldn't be extruded or flung off.

Salviati
Nor do I suppose, or need to suppose, what is not, because I don't want to deny that stones do get flung off; I'm just saying it by way of supposition, so that you'll tell me the rest. Now picture the Earth as the great wheel that, turning with such speed, is to fling off the stones. You've already been able to tell me very well that the projected motion will have to be along that straight line which touches the Earth at the point of separation. And how far, notably, does this tangent get from the surface of the terrestrial globe?

Simplicio
I believe that in a thousand braccia it doesn't get a finger's width away.

Salviati
And don't you say that the projectile, pulled by its own weight, bends away from the tangent toward the center of the Earth?

Simplicio
I've said it — and I'll say the rest too, and I understand perfectly that the stone won't separate from the Earth, since its departure at the start would be so very minimal that the stone's inclination to move toward the center of the Earth is fully a thousand times greater; and in this case that center is also the center of the wheel. And really one is forced to grant that stones, animals, and other heavy bodies can't be extruded. But now the very light things give me a new difficulty — the ones that have a very feeble inclination to sink toward the center; so that, since they lack the power to draw themselves back to the surface, I don't see why they wouldn't be flung off. And you know that ad destruendum sufficit unum — one instance is enough to destroy the claim.

Salviati
We'll satisfy this point too. But first tell me what you mean by "light things" — whether you mean stuff so truly light that it goes upward, or not absolutely light, but so slightly heavy that it does come down, only slowly; because if you mean the absolutely light ones, I'll let them be flung off even more than you'd like.

Simplicio
I mean the second kind — things like feathers, wool, cotton wadding, and the like, which the least force is enough to lift; yet we see them resting quite peacefully on the ground.

Salviati
As long as this feather has some natural tendency to descend toward the surface of the Earth, however slight it may be, I tell you it's enough to keep it from being lifted; and this isn't unknown to you either. So tell me: if the feather were flung off by the Earth's whirling, along what line would it move?

Simplicio
Along the tangent at the point of separation.

Salviati
And if it had to come back and rejoin, along what line would it move?

Simplicio
Along the one that goes from it to the center of the Earth.

Salviati
So that here two motions come into consideration: one of projection, which begins at the point of contact and follows along the tangent; and the other of the downward inclination, which begins at the projectile and goes along the secant toward the center. And for the projection to take place, the impetus along the tangent must prevail over the inclination along the secant — isn't that so?

Simplicio
So it seems to me.

Salviati
But what do you think must be present in the projecting motion, so that it prevails over that of the inclination, and from that follows the feather's detachment and departure from the Earth?

Simplicio
I don't know.

Salviati
How do you not know? Here the moving body is the same — the same feather; now how can one and the same moving body outdo itself in motion and prevail over itself?

Simplicio
I don't see how it could prevail over or yield to itself in motion, except by moving now faster and now slower.

Salviati
There, you see, you did know it after all. So then, if the feather's projection is to take place, and its motion along the tangent is to prevail over the motion along the secant, what must their speeds be?

Simplicio
The motion along the tangent must be greater than the other along the secant. Oh, poor me! But isn't it even a hundred thousand times greater — and not only than the downward motion of the feather, but even than that of the stone? And I, like a real simpleton, had let myself be persuaded that stones couldn't be flung off by the Earth's whirling! So I take it all back, and say that if the Earth moved, the stones, the elephants, the towers, and the cities would necessarily fly up toward the sky; and since that doesn't happen, I say the Earth does not move.

Salviati
Oh, Simplicio, you fly up so quickly that I'll start fearing more for you than for the feather. Calm down a little, and listen. If, to keep the stone or the feather attached to the surface of the Earth, it were necessary that its descent downward be more than — or as much as — the motion made along the tangent, you'd be right to say it would have to move as fast or faster along the secant downward as along the tangent toward the east. But didn't you tell me just now that a thousand braccia of distance along the tangent from the contact scarcely move it a finger's width from the circumference? It's not enough, then, for the motion along the tangent — which is the motion of the daily whirl — simply to be faster than the motion along the secant, which is the feather's downward motion; it has to be so much faster that the time enough to carry the feather, say, a thousand braccia along the tangent is too little for it to move a single finger's width downward along the secant. And this, I tell you, will never be — make that motion as fast, and this one as slow, as you please.

Simplicio
And why couldn't the one along the tangent be so fast that it gave the feather no time to reach the surface of the Earth?

Salviati
Try putting the case in definite terms, and I'll answer you. So tell me how much faster you think that motion would have to be than this one.

Simplicio
I'll say, for example, that if that one were a million times faster than this one, the feather — and the stone too — would be flung off.

Salviati
You say so, and you say what's false, only for a lack — not of logic, or of physics, or of metaphysics, but of geometry. Because if you understood only the first elements, you'd know that from the center of the circle one can draw a straight line out to the tangent that cuts it in such a way that the part of the tangent between the contact and the secant is one, two, three million times greater than that part of the secant left between the tangent and the circumference; and as the secant comes closer and closer to the contact, this ratio grows greater without limit. So there's no fear that, however fast the whirl and slow the downward motion, the feather — or anything else lighter — could begin to rise; because the downward inclination always overcomes the speed of the projection.

Sagredo
I'm not entirely satisfied about this business.

Salviati
I'll give you a thoroughly universal demonstration of it, and quite an easy one too. Let the given ratio be that of BA to C, and let BA be greater than C by as much as you like; and let there be the circle whose center is D, from which we must draw a secant such that the tangent bears to that secant the ratio of BA to C. Take AI, the third proportional of the two lines BA and C; and as BI is to IA, so make the diameter FE to EG; and from the point G draw the tangent GH. I say that what was required has been done, and that as BA is to C, so HG is to GE. For, since as BI is to IA so FE is to EG, then — componendo, combining the terms — as BA is to AI so FG is to GE; and because C is the mean proportional between BA and AI, and GH is the mean between FG and GE, therefore as BA is to C, so will FG be to GH, that is, HG to GE — which is what needed to be done.

Sagredo
I follow this demonstration; yet it doesn't entirely take away every scruple — rather, I feel a certain confusion turning about in my mind which, like a thick, dark fog, doesn't let me make out, with the clarity that's usually proper to mathematical reasoning, the plainness and necessity of the conclusion. And what confuses me is this. It's true that the spaces between the tangent and the circumference go on diminishing without limit toward the contact; but it's also true, on the other hand, that the moving body's tendency to descend keeps growing ever smaller in it the closer it finds itself to the first terminus of its descent — that is, to the state of rest — as is clear from what you declared to us, showing that a descending heavy body, setting out from rest, must pass through all the intermediate degrees of slowness between that rest and any given degree of speed, degrees that are smaller and smaller without limit. Add to this that this speed and tendency to motion goes on diminishing, for another reason, likewise without limit, and this comes about because the heaviness of the moving body itself can be diminished without limit. So that the causes that diminish the tendency to descend — and consequently favor the projection — are two, namely the lightness of the moving body and its nearness to the terminus of rest, both of them increasable without limit; and against them, on the other hand, stands the opposition of a single cause producing the projection, which, though it too is increasable without limit, I can't grasp how, all by itself, it wouldn't end up overcome by the union and coupling of the other two, likewise increasable without limit.
