The Second Day

(Part 18 of 20)

Salviati
How careful one has to be before affirming or denying any proposition! I'll say it again: hearing it stated so confidently that, because the top of the mast moves through a greater distance than its foot, everyone will be persuaded that using a telescope up high must be far harder than down low. And in the same way I'm even willing to excuse those philosophers who fall into despair and throw up their hands at people who won't grant them that a cannonball — which they plainly see coming straight down along a straight, perpendicular line — really does move that way; those people insist instead that its motion runs along an arc, and a very, very slanting and sideways one at that. But let's leave them in that fix, and listen to the other objections the author we have in hand raises against Copernicus.

Simplicio
The author goes on to show how, on Copernicus's teaching, one has to deny the senses — and the plainest sensations at that. Take this: we, who feel the stirring of the lightest little breeze, would then have to feel nothing of the force of a perpetual wind striking us at a speed of more than 2,529 miles an hour — for that is how much ground the center of the Earth covers in a single hour, with its annual motion, along the circumference of the great orb (the Earth's yearly path), as he carefully calculates. And because — as he says, still following Copernicus's own view — cum Terra movetur circumpositus aër; motus tamen eius, velocior licet ac rapidior celerrimo quocumque vento, a nobis non sentiretur, sed summa tum tranquillitas reputaretur, nisi alius motus accederet. Quid est vero decipi sensum, nisi haec esset deceptio? — that is: when the Earth moves, the air surrounding it moves too; and yet its motion, faster and swifter though it be than any wind however swift, would not be felt by us, but would rather be reckoned the deepest calm, unless some other motion were added to it. And what is it to be deceived in one's senses, if this would not be a deception?

Salviati
This philosopher must believe that the Earth Copernicus sends wheeling around — together with the surrounding air — along the circumference of the great orb is not the one we live on, but some other, separate Earth; because ours carries us along with it too, at the very same speed as itself and the air around it. And what buffeting could we possibly feel, while we flee at exactly the same pace as the thing that means to strike us? This gentleman has forgotten that we, no less than the Earth and the air, are being carried around in the turn — and that in consequence we are always in contact with the same portion of air, which therefore never strikes us at all.

Simplicio
On the contrary — here are the very words that follow immediately: Præterea nos quoque rotamur ex circumductione Terræ, etc. — that is: besides, we too are spun around by the Earth's carrying-round, and so on.

Salviati
Now I can neither help him nor excuse him any longer; you excuse him and help him, Simplicio.

Simplicio
For the moment, caught off guard like this, no defense comes to me that satisfies even myself.

Salviati
Well then, sleep on it tonight and defend him tomorrow; meanwhile, let's hear the other objections.

Simplicio
He keeps pressing the same objection, showing that on Copernicus's road we have to deny our own sensations. For this principle by which we go around with the Earth is either internal to us, or external — that is, a snatching-along by the Earth itself. And if it's the second, then, since we feel no such snatching-along, we'd have to say that the sense of touch does not feel its own proper object when that object is joined to it, nor feel the impression it makes on the sensorium. But if the principle is internal, then we won't feel a local motion arising from ourselves, and we'll never notice an inclination perpetually bound up with us.

Salviati
So this philosopher's objection comes down to this: whether the principle by which we move with the Earth is external or internal, we ought in either case to feel it; and since we don't feel it, it's neither the one nor the other; and therefore we don't move, and consequently neither does the Earth. And I say it can be either way without our feeling it. As for its possibly being external, the experiment with the boat clears away every difficulty and more — and I say "and more" because, since we can set the boat moving whenever we like, and just as well bring it to rest, and watch with great care to see whether, from some difference the sense of touch can pick up, we might learn to tell whether it's moving or not — seeing that no such knowledge has ever yet been gained, why be surprised if the same fact stays hidden from us on the Earth, which may have been carrying us along forever, without our ever being able to test it at rest? Now, Simplicio, I believe you've gone by boat to Padua a thousand times; and if you'll confess the truth, you never once felt in yourself any share in that motion — except when the boat ran aground or struck some obstacle and stopped, and you, with the other passengers, caught off guard, were nearly pitched over headlong and in danger. The terrestrial globe would have to run into some obstacle that brought it up short; and I promise you that then you would indeed become aware of the impetus lodged in you, when it flung you off toward the stars. It's true you can become aware of the boat's motion by another sense — but only with the help of reasoning — namely by sight, when you look at the trees and buildings out in the countryside, which, being separate from the boat, seem to move the opposite way. But if you'd like to rest content about the Earth's motion on the strength of such an experience, I'd tell you to look at the stars, which for that very reason appear to move the opposite way. As for being surprised at not feeling this principle, supposing it were internal to us — that's an even less reasonable thought; because if we don't feel one that comes from outside us and frequently leaves us, why should we feel it when it resides in us unchangingly and without a break? Now, is there anything more in this first argument?

Simplicio
There's this little outburst: Ex hac itaque opinione necesse est diffidere nostris sensibus, ut penitus fallacibus vel stupidis in sensibilibus, etiam coniunctissimis, diiudicandis; quam ergo veritatem sperare possumus, a facultate adeo fallaci ortum trahentem? — that is: from this opinion, then, we are bound to distrust our senses, as utterly deceptive or dull in judging sensible things, even the ones most closely joined to us. What truth, then, can we hope for, when it takes its origin from so deceptive a faculty?

Salviati
Oh, I'd rather draw more useful and more reliable lessons from it — learning to be more circumspect and less trusting about whatever the senses present to us at first glance, since they can easily deceive us. And I wish this author hadn't worn himself out so trying to make us grasp, through the senses, that the motion of falling heavy bodies is simply straight and of no other kind; nor taken offense and cried out because something so clear, plain, and obvious is called into question. For by doing so he betrays a belief that those who say such motion is not straight after all, but rather circular, seem to actually see that stone travel in an arc — since he appeals more to their senses than to their reasoning to settle the matter. And that's not so, Simplicio; because just as I, who am neutral between these opinions and only put on Copernicus's mask, actor-fashion, in these performances of ours, have never seen — nor ever thought I saw — that stone fall any way but plumb straight down, so I believe the very same thing appears to everyone else's eyes. Better, then, that we set aside the appearance, on which we all agree, and bring reasoning to bear — either to confirm that the appearance is real, or to expose it as a fallacy.

Sagredo
If I could just once meet this philosopher — who does seem to me to rise well above many other followers of the same doctrines — I'd like, as a token of affection, to remind him of something he has certainly seen a thousand times, from which, much in keeping with what we're discussing, one can grasp how easily a person can be fooled by the bare appearance — or let's say, the presentation — of the senses. And the thing is this: to people walking along a road at night, the Moon seems to follow them, keeping step with them, as they watch her come skimming the eaves of the rooftops above which she appears — exactly as a cat would do that, really walking along the roof tiles, kept pace behind them. An appearance that, if reasoning didn't step in, would only too plainly deceive the eye.

Simplicio
True enough, there's no shortage of experiences that assure us of the fallacies of the bare senses. But setting such sensations aside for now, let's hear the arguments that follow, which are drawn, as he says, ex rerum natura — from the nature of things. The first is that the Earth cannot by its own nature move with three greatly different motions, unless we're willing to reject many plain axioms — self-evident truths. The first of these is that every effect depends on some cause. The second, that nothing produces itself — from which it follows that it's impossible for the mover and the thing moved to be entirely one and the same. And this is plain not only in things moved by an external mover; from the principles laid down, the same is gathered to hold in natural motion that depends on an internal principle. Otherwise, since the mover, as mover, is cause, and the moved, as moved, is effect, the very same thing would be entirely both cause and effect. So a body does not move the whole of itself — that is, such that the whole moves and the whole is moved; rather, in the thing moved we must somehow distinguish the efficient principle of the motion from that which is moved by it. The third axiom is that, among things subject to the senses, one thing, insofar as it is one, produces a single thing only. Thus the soul in an animal does indeed produce different operations — sight, hearing, smell, generation — but with different instruments; and in short, one sees that in sensible things the different operations derive from some diversity in the cause. Now, if we put these axioms together, it will be perfectly clear that a simple body, such as the Earth, cannot by its own nature move with three greatly different motions at once. For, by the assumptions made, the whole does not move the whole of itself; we must therefore distinguish in it three principles for three motions, otherwise one and the same principle would produce several motions. But since it would then contain within itself three principles of natural motion, besides the part that is moved, it would not be a simple body, but one compounded of three moving principles and of the part moved. If, then, the Earth is a simple body, it will not move with three motions. Indeed, it won't move with any of those Copernicus assigns it, since it must move with one only — it being plain, by Aristotle's reasoning, that it moves toward its own center, as its parts show, which descend at right angles to the spherical surface of the Earth.

Salviati
Many things could be said and weighed about the fabric of this argument; but since we can settle it in a few words, I don't care to spread myself out needlessly right now — all the more since the answer is handed to me by the author himself, when he says that in an animal different operations are produced by a single principle. So for now I answer him that, in the same way, different motions derive in the Earth from a single principle.

Simplicio
The author of the objection won't be quieted in the least by this answer; on the contrary, your answer is completely knocked down by what he adds immediately afterward to shore up the attack he's made — as you're about to hear. He reinforces the argument, I say, with another axiom, which is this: that nature is neither deficient nor excessive in things necessary. This is plain to observers of natural things, and above all of animals, in which, because they had to move with many motions, nature has given them many joints, and there has aptly bound the parts together for movement — as at the knees and the hips, for the walking of animals and for lying down at their pleasure; and further, in man, she has built many bends and articulations at the elbow and the hand, so as to be able to carry out many motions. From these things the argument against the Earth's triple motion is drawn: either a single, continuous body, without being articulated by any joint, can carry out different motions, or it cannot without having joints. If it can without them, then nature made the joints in animals in vain, which is against the axiom; but if it cannot without them, then the Earth, a single continuous body devoid of joints and articulations, cannot by its own nature move with several motions. Now see how shrewdly he goes to meet your answer — almost as if he'd foreseen it.

Salviati
Are you speaking in earnest, or ironically?

Simplicio
I'm speaking with the best sense I've got.

Salviati
Then you must feel you have such a good hand that you can even keep up this philosopher's defense against some further reply that might be made against him. So do me the kindness, please, of answering me on his behalf, since we can't have him here in person. First, you grant it as true that nature made the joints, bends, and articulations in animals so that they could move with many and various motions; and I deny you this proposition. I say the joints were made so that the animal can move one or more of its parts while the rest stays still; and I say that as for the kinds and differences of motion, they are all of one sort only — that is, all circular. And this is why you see that all the heads of the movable bones are either rounded or hollowed: of these, some are spherical, namely those that have to move every which way — as the standard-bearer's arm does at the shoulder joint when he handles the flag, and the falconer's when he calls back the falcon with the lure; and such is the bend of the elbow, on which the hand turns when boring with an auger. Others are circular in one direction only, and nearly cylindrical, serving the limbs that bend in a single way — like the sections of the fingers, one upon another, and so on. But without going through more particular cases, a single general argument can make this truth known: namely, that when a solid body moves while one of its ends stays in place without shifting, the motion can only be circular; and since, when the animal moves one of its limbs, it does not separate it from the neighboring limb, therefore that motion is of necessity circular.

Simplicio
I don't understand it that way; on the contrary, I see the animal move with a hundred non-circular motions, all utterly different from one another — running and jumping and climbing and descending and swimming and many more.

Salviati
Fair enough — but those are secondary motions, depending on the primary ones, which belong to the joints and bends. From the bending of the legs at the knees and the thighs at the hips, which are circular motions of the parts, there follows the jump or the run, which are motions of the whole body, and these can be non-circular. Now, since in the terrestrial globe there's no need to move one part over another that stays still — the motion being of the whole body — there's no need for joints.

Simplicio
This (the other side will say) could hold when the motion is just one; but their being three, and utterly different from one another — it's impossible that they be accommodated in an unjointed body.

Salviati
That, I truly believe, would be the philosopher's reply; and against it I rise from another quarter, and ask you whether you think the terrestrial globe could be fitted, by way of joints and bends, to share in three different circular motions. You don't answer? Since you keep silent, I'll answer for the philosopher, who would absolutely say yes — because otherwise it would have been pointless and beside the case to bring up that nature makes joints so that a movable body can move with different motions, and that therefore, the terrestrial globe having no joints, it cannot have the three motions assigned to it. For if he had judged that not even by way of joints could it be made fit for such motions, he'd have freely declared that the globe cannot move with three motions at all. Now, this being so, I beg you — and through you, if it were possible, the philosopher who authored the argument — to be so kind as to teach me in what manner the joints would have to be arranged so that the three motions could be comfortably carried out; and I'll allow you four, or even six, months for your answer. Meanwhile it seems to me that a single principle can cause several motions in the terrestrial globe, in exactly the way I answered before — that a single principle, by means of various instruments, produces multiple and different motions in an animal. And as for jointing, there's no need of it, since the motions must be of the whole and not of some parts; and because they have to be circular, the simple spherical shape is the finest jointing one could ask for.

Simplicio
The most that could be granted you would be that this might happen with a single motion; but with three different ones, in my opinion and the author's, it's not possible — as he goes on, still reinforcing the objection, writing: "Let us imagine, with Copernicus, that the Earth moves, by its own faculty and from an internal principle, from west to east in the plane of the ecliptic; and beyond that, that it turns, also from an internal principle, about its own center from east to west; and, for the third motion, that by its own inclination it tilts from north to south and back again. Since it is a continuous body, not linked by bends and joints, can our estimation and our judgment ever grasp that one and the same natural, undivided principle — that is, one and the same propensity — should split itself at once into different and almost contrary motions? I cannot believe that anyone would say such a thing, except one who had set himself, rightly or wrongly, to uphold this position."

Salviati
Hold on a moment, and find me this passage in the book; show me. Fingamus modo cum Copernico, Terram aliqua sua vi et ab indito principio impelli ab occasu ad ortum in eclipticæ plano, tum rursus revolvi ab indito etiam principio circa suimet centrum ab ortu in occasum, tertio deflecti rursus suopte nutu a septentrione in austrum et vicissim — that is: let us suppose, with Copernicus, that the Earth is driven by some force of its own, and from an inborn principle, from west to east in the plane of the ecliptic; then again turned, also by an inborn principle, about its own center from east to west; and, third, deflected once more, by its own nod, from north to south and back again. I had my doubts, Simplicio, that you might have made a slip in reporting the author's words to us; but I see that he himself, and all too gravely, is mistaken — and to my regret I realize he has set out to attack a position he hasn't properly understood; for these are not the motions Copernicus assigns to the Earth. And where does he get the idea that Copernicus makes the annual motion along the ecliptic run contrary to the motion about its own center? He can't have read Copernicus's book, which in a hundred places — and even in the opening chapters — writes that both these motions go toward the same quarter, that is, from west to east. But even without hearing it from anyone else, shouldn't he have grasped on his own that, when you assign to the Earth the motions taken away one from the Sun and the other from the primum mobile, they'd necessarily have to be made in the same direction?

Simplicio
Just take care that you don't go wrong yourself — and Copernicus along with you. Isn't the daily motion of the primum mobile from east to west? And isn't the Sun's annual motion along the ecliptic, on the contrary, from west to east? How, then, do you expect these same motions, transferred to the Earth, to turn from contrary into concordant?

Sagredo
Simplicio has certainly uncovered for us the source of this philosopher's error: he too must have reasoned in just this way.

Salviati
Well, while we can, let's at least clear Simplicio of his error. Seeing the stars rise above the eastern horizon at their rising, he'll have no trouble understanding that, if that motion did not belong to the stars, one would necessarily have to say that the horizon sank with a contrary motion — and consequently that the Earth turned upon itself the opposite way from how the stars seem to move to us, that is, from west to east, which is the order of the signs of the zodiac. As for the other motion: since the Sun is fixed at the center of the zodiac and the Earth moves along its circumference, then for the Sun to appear to us to move through that zodiac in the order of the signs, the Earth must travel in that same order — seeing that the Sun always appears to us to occupy the degree of the zodiac opposite the degree in which the Earth is found. And so, while the Earth runs through, say, Aries, the Sun will appear to run through Libra; and as the Earth passes through the sign of Taurus, the Sun will run through Scorpio; the Earth through Gemini, the Sun through Sagittarius. But this is for both to move in the same direction — that is, according to the order of the signs — just as the Earth's revolution about its own center was too.
