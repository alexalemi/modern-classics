The Second Day

(Part 8 of 20)

Salviati
The demonstration is very easy. Imagine the line I E is drawn. Since the radius of the circle C D — that is, the line C A — is double the radius C E of the circle C I, the larger circumference will be double the smaller, and every arc of the larger circle will be double the corresponding arc of the smaller, so that half of any arc of the larger circle equals the matching arc of the smaller. Now the angle C E I, formed at the center E of the smaller circle and standing on the arc C I, is double the angle C A D, formed at the center A of the larger circle and subtended by the arc C D. Therefore the arc C D is half of the arc of the larger circle that corresponds to the arc C I, and so the two arcs C D and C I are equal. The same can be shown for all the parts. But that the business proceeds exactly this way as far as the fall of heavy bodies goes, I won't insist on for now. What I will say is that, if the line traced by a falling body isn't precisely this one, it comes very close to it.

Sagredo
But now, Salviati, I find myself dwelling on another marvelous thing — namely, that given these considerations, straight-line motion goes entirely out the window, and nature never makes use of it at all. Even the one job that was granted to it at the outset — to carry the parts of whole bodies back to their proper place when they'd been separated from the whole and were therefore in a bad arrangement — even that is now taken away from it and assigned instead to circular motion.

Salviati
This would follow necessarily if it had been established that the terrestrial globe moves in a circle — something I don't claim we've done. All we've done so far, and all we'll go on doing, is to weigh the force of the reasons the philosophers offer as proof of the Earth's immobility. Of these, the first, drawn from bodies falling straight down, runs into the difficulties you've heard. How much weight those difficulties carry in Simplicio's eyes I don't know, so before we move on to test the other arguments, it would be good if he brought out anything he has to say in reply.

Simplicio
As for this first argument, I confess I've heard several subtleties I hadn't thought of, and since they're new to me, I don't have my answers ready. But this argument from bodies falling straight down I don't count among the strongest proofs of the Earth's immobility, and I don't know how things will turn out with the artillery shots — especially those fired against the daily rotation.

Sagredo
I only wish the flight of birds troubled me as little as the artillery and all the other experiences brought up so far! But these birds, which fly back and forth at their pleasure and wheel about in a thousand ways, and — what matters more — hang suspended in the air for hours on end: these, I say, throw my imagination into confusion. I can't understand how, amid so many spinning turns, they don't lose track of the Earth's motion, or how they can keep pace with a speed that in the end outstrips their own flight many, many times over.

Salviati
Your doubt really isn't without reason, and perhaps Copernicus himself never found a solution to his full satisfaction — which may be why he passed over it in silence. Even in examining the other arguments against him he was quite terse, I think out of loftiness of mind, being grounded on greater and higher contemplations, the way lions stir little at the yapping of small dogs. So we'll save the objection about the birds for last, and meanwhile try to satisfy Simplicio on the others, showing him in the usual way that he himself holds the solutions in hand, though he doesn't realize it. Let's start with the point-blank shots, fired with the same piece, the same powder and ball, one toward the east and the other toward the west. Tell me: what makes you believe that the westward shot (supposing the daily rotation belonged to the terrestrial globe) ought to come out much longer than the eastward one?

Simplicio
I'm moved to believe it because, in the eastward shot, while the ball is outside the cannon it's followed by the cannon itself, which — carried by the Earth — is also racing swiftly in the same direction, so that the ball falls to earth not far from the piece. In the westward shot, on the contrary, before the ball strikes the ground the piece has drawn back a good way toward the east, so that the space between the ball and the piece — that is, the range of the shot — will look longer than the other by as much ground as the artillery, and hence the Earth, has covered during the time both balls were in the air.

Salviati
I wish we could find some way to make an experiment matching the motion of these projectiles, the way the shipboard experiment matched the motion of bodies falling from a height. I'm thinking about how.

Sagredo
I think a very suitable test would be to take an open carriage and fit it with a heavy crossbow for bolts, set at half elevation so the shot comes out the longest possible; then, while the horses are running, fire once toward the direction of travel and once toward the opposite direction, marking very carefully where the carriage is at the exact moment the bolt sticks in the ground, in each shot. That way you could see exactly how much longer one comes out than the other.

Simplicio
It seems to me a very suitable experiment, and I've no doubt the shot — that is, the space between the arrow and where the carriage is at the moment the arrow sticks in the ground — will be much smaller when you fire toward the carriage's motion than when you fire the opposite way. Say, for example, the shot in itself is three hundred arm-lengths, and the carriage's travel while the bolt is in the air is a hundred. Then, firing toward the direction of travel, out of the three hundred arm-lengths of the shot the carriage covers a hundred, so that when the bolt strikes the ground the space between it and the carriage will be only two hundred arm-lengths. But in the other shot, on the contrary, the carriage running opposite to the bolt, when the bolt has covered its three hundred arm-lengths and the carriage its hundred in the opposite direction, the distance between them will turn out to be four hundred arm-lengths.

Salviati
Would there be any way to make these two shots come out equal?

Simplicio
I know of no way except to hold the carriage still.

Salviati
That's obvious. But I'm asking with the carriage running at full speed.

Simplicio
You'd have to strengthen the bow when firing with the direction of travel, and then weaken it for firing against it.

Salviati
So there's yet another remedy after all. But by how much would you need to strengthen it, and by how much then weaken it?

Simplicio
In our example, where we supposed the bow shot three hundred arm-lengths, you'd need — for the shot with the direction of travel — to strengthen it so it shot four hundred, and for the other to weaken it so it shot no more than two hundred. That way each shot would come out at three hundred arm-lengths relative to the carriage, which with its own travel of a hundred arm-lengths — subtracting them from the four-hundred shot and adding them to the two-hundred — would bring both back to three hundred.

Salviati
But what effect does the greater or lesser strength of the bow have on the arrow?

Simplicio
A strong bow drives it with greater speed, a weaker one with less; and the same arrow flies farther one time than another in proportion to how much greater the speed with which it leaves the nock one time than the other.

Salviati
So, to make the arrow — fired equally one way and the other — move away from the running carriage by the same amount, if in the first shot of the proposed example it leaves with, say, four degrees of speed, in the other shot it must leave with only two. But if you use the same bow, it always gets three degrees from it.

Simplicio
Just so; and that's why, firing with the same bow while the carriage is running, the shots can't come out equal.

Salviati
I forgot to ask with what speed we're supposing, in this particular experiment, that the carriage runs.

Simplicio
We have to suppose the carriage's speed at one degree, compared with the bow's, which is three.

Salviati
Yes, yes, that makes the reckoning come out right. But tell me: when the carriage runs, don't all the things in the carriage move with the same speed too?

Simplicio
Without a doubt.

Salviati
So the bolt too, and the bow, and the string it's drawn on.

Simplicio
Just so.

Salviati
So, in discharging the bolt toward the carriage's direction of travel, the bow impresses its three degrees of speed on a bolt that already has one degree — thanks to the carriage, which carries it that way with just that speed — so that as it leaves the nock it finds itself with four degrees of speed. On the contrary, firing the other way, the same bow confers its same three degrees on a bolt that's moving the opposite way with one degree, so that as it parts from the string it's left with only two degrees of speed. But you yourself have already granted that to make the shots equal the bolt must leave one time with four degrees and the other with two. So, without changing the bow, it's the carriage's own motion that squares the accounts, and experience then seals the matter for those who wouldn't or couldn't grasp the reasoning. Now apply this discussion to the artillery, and you'll find that whether the Earth moves or stands still, shots fired with the same force must always come out equal, aimed in whatever direction you please. The error of Aristotle, of Ptolemy, of Tycho, of yourself, and of everyone else, is rooted in that fixed and inveterate impression that the Earth stands still — an impression you can't or don't know how to strip off, not even when you want to philosophize about what would happen supposing the Earth did move. It's the same in the other argument: not considering that while the stone is on the tower it does, as regards moving or not moving, exactly what the terrestrial globe does — because you have it fixed in your mind that the Earth stands still — you reason about the stone's fall always as though it set off from rest. Whereas you ought to say: if the Earth stands still, the stone sets off from rest and descends straight down; but if the Earth moves, the stone likewise moves with equal speed, and doesn't set off from rest, but from a motion equal to the Earth's, with which it blends the added downward motion and composes a slanting one.

Simplicio
But, good God, if it moves on a slant, how is it I see it move straight down and perpendicular? This is simply to deny plain sense; and if we're not to trust the senses, by what other door are we to enter into philosophizing?

Salviati
With respect to the Earth, the tower, and ourselves — all of which move together, with the daily rotation, along with the stone — the daily rotation is as if it weren't there: it stays imperceptible, undetectable, without any effect at all, and the only motion left observable to us is the one we don't share, which is the stone's coming down grazing the tower. You're not the first to feel great reluctance in grasping this — that motion has no effect among the things that share in it.

Sagredo
Now I recall a certain fancy of mine that passed through my imagination one day while I was sailing on the voyage to Aleppo, where I was going as consul of our nation. It might perhaps be of some help in explaining how motion shared in common has no effect and is as if it weren't there for all who share in it. And if it please Simplicio, I'd like to discuss with him what I was daydreaming then, all by myself.

Simplicio
The novelty of the things I'm hearing makes me not just tolerant but curious to listen. So do go on.

Sagredo
If the point of a writing pen that had been aboard ship throughout my whole voyage from Venice to Alexandretta had had the power to leave a visible trace of its entire journey, what mark, what track, what line would it have left?

Simplicio
It would have left a line stretched from Venice all the way there — not perfectly straight, or rather, not stretched in a perfect arc of a circle, but here more and there less wavering, according as the vessel had pitched now more, now less. But this bending, in some places by an arm-length or two, right or left, up or down, over a length of many hundreds of miles, would have made only a small alteration in the whole stretch of the line — so slight it would scarcely have been noticeable, and without any error worth mentioning you could have called it a part of a perfect arc.

Sagredo
So the true, true, very truest motion of that pen-point would also have been a perfect arc of a circle, provided the vessel's motion, setting aside the tossing of the waves, had been calm and smooth. And if I had held that same pen continually in my hand, and only now and then moved it a finger's-width or two this way or that, what alteration would I have made to that main and very long stretch?

Simplicio
Less than the deviation, here and there, from absolute straightness by the breadth of a flea's eye would make to a straight line a thousand arm-lengths long.

Sagredo
So if a painter, on setting out from port, had begun to draw on a sheet of paper with that pen, and continued the drawing all the way to Alexandretta, he could have derived from the pen's motion a whole story of many figures perfectly outlined and cross-hatched in a thousand thousand directions, with landscapes, buildings, animals, and other things — even though the whole true, real, and essential movement traced by the point of that pen would have been nothing but one very long but utterly simple line. And as for the painter's own proper work, he would have drawn exactly the same thing, to a hair, had the ship stood still. That no trace of the pen's very long motion remains except those strokes marked on the paper — the reason is that the great motion from Venice to Alexandretta was shared in common by the paper, the pen, and everything else aboard the ship. But the little motions back and forth, right and left, imparted by the painter's fingers to the pen and not to the sheet, being proper to the pen alone, could leave a trace of themselves on the paper, which stayed motionless with respect to those movements. In the same way it's true that, the Earth moving, the stone's motion in coming down was really a long stretch of many hundreds and even many thousands of arm-lengths; and if it could have marked in still air, or on some other surface, the track of its course, it would have left a very long slanting line. But that part of this whole motion which is shared by the stone, the tower, and ourselves stays imperceptible to us, as if it weren't there, and the only part that stays observable is the one neither the tower nor we share — which in the end is what the stone, in falling, measures the tower by.

Salviati
A very subtle thought for explaining this point, which many find quite hard to grasp. Now, if Simplicio has nothing further to reply, we can move on to the other experiences, whose solution will draw no small ease from what's been laid out so far.

Simplicio
I have nothing more to say, and I was half lost in that image, and in thinking how those strokes drawn in so many directions — here, there, up, down, back and forth, and interwoven with a hundred thousand curlicues — are, in essence and in strictest reality, nothing but little pieces of a single line drawn all in one same direction, with no other alteration than a slight leaning, now and then, a little to right and left from the perfectly straight track, and the pen-point moving now faster, now slower, but with the least inequality. And I consider that a letter would be written the same way; and that those more elegant penmen who, to show off the freedom of their hand, without lifting the pen from the paper trace in a single stroke, with a thousand thousand windings, a lovely interlacing — if they were in a boat running swiftly, they would convert the whole motion of the pen (which in essence is a single line drawn all toward the same side, and very little bent or leaning from perfect straightness) into a mere flourish. And I'm delighted that Sagredo has awakened this thought in me. So let's press on, for the hope of hearing more of the same will keep me more attentive.

Sagredo
If you're curious to hear subtleties of this kind, which don't occur to just anyone, we've no shortage of them — especially in this matter of navigation. Won't you find it a fine thought, this one that occurred to me on that same voyage, when I noticed that the ship's mast, without breaking or bending, had made a longer journey with its top — that is, with the crow's-nest — than with its foot? Because the top, being farther from the center of the Earth than the foot is, came to describe the arc of a larger circle than the circle the foot passed through.

Simplicio
And so, when a man walks, he makes a longer journey with his head than with his feet?

Sagredo
You've penetrated it beautifully, by yourself and by your own wit. But let's not interrupt Salviati.

Salviati
I'm glad to see Simplicio getting some training — provided, that is, the thought is his own, and he didn't learn it from a certain little book of conclusions, where there are several others no less pretty and clever. Let's go on to speak of the artillery aimed straight up, perpendicular to the horizon — that is, of the shot toward our zenith — and finally of the ball's return down the same line onto the same piece, even though during the long while it stays separated from the piece the Earth has carried it many miles toward the east; so it would seem that over so much ground the ball ought to fall far from the piece, toward the west. This doesn't happen; therefore the artillery, without having moved, has waited for it. The solution is the same as that of the stone falling from the tower, and the whole fallacy and equivocation consists in always supposing as true the very thing in question — because our opponent has it firmly in his head that the ball sets off from rest when it's driven out of the piece by the fire, and to set off from a state of rest can only be assumed on the supposition that the terrestrial globe is at rest, which is then the very conclusion under dispute. So I reply once more that those who make the Earth mobile answer that the artillery and the ball inside it share the same motion the Earth has — indeed, that they have this motion, along with her, by nature — and that the ball therefore doesn't set off from rest at all, but joined to its motion around the center, which the upward projection neither takes away nor hinders. In this way, following the universal motion of the Earth toward the east, it stays continually over the same piece, both in rising and in returning. And you'll see the same thing happen by making the experiment aboard ship with a ball shot straight up with a crossbow, which returns to the same place whether the ship moves or stands still.

Sagredo
This satisfies everything perfectly. But since I've seen that Simplicio takes pleasure in certain clever tricks for catching (as they say) his companion, I want to ask him whether — supposing for now that the Earth stands still, and on it the artillery raised perpendicularly and aimed at our zenith — he has any difficulty in understanding that this is the true perpendicular shot, and that the ball, in setting off and in returning, will travel along the same straight line, always understanding all external and accidental impediments removed.
