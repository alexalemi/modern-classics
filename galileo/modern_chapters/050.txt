The Fourth Day

(Part 2 of 6)

Salviati
For these effects to follow as consequences of the motions that naturally belong to the Earth, it is necessary not only that they meet no resistance or obstacle, but that they follow easily — and not only that they follow easily, but of necessity, so that it would be impossible for things to happen in any other way; for that is the property and condition of things natural and true. So, having established that it is impossible to account for the movements we see in the waters and at the same time keep motionless the vessel that holds them, let us go on to see whether the mobility of the container can produce the effect, under the conditions in which we observe it to occur.

Two kinds of motion can be given to a vessel, by which the water contained in it would gain the ability to run now toward one end and now toward the other, and there to rise and fall. The first would be when now one end and now the other is lowered; for then the water, running toward the tilted side, would in turn rise and fall, now on this side, now on that. But since this rising and falling is nothing other than moving away from and toward the center of the Earth, this kind of motion cannot be attributed to the hollows of the Earth itself — the vessels that hold the waters — because no matter what motion you assign to the terrestrial globe, the parts of those vessels can neither approach nor recede from its center. The other kind of motion is when the vessel moves (without tilting at all) with a forward motion that is not uniform but changes speed, now speeding up and now slowing down. From this unevenness it would follow that the water — held in the vessel, but not fixed to it like its other solid parts, indeed, by its fluidity almost separate and free and not bound to follow every change of its container — when the vessel slows down would retain part of the impetus already built up and run forward toward the leading end, where it would necessarily rise; and conversely, when new speed is added to the vessel, the water, keeping part of its own slowness and lagging somewhat behind before it grows used to the new impetus, would stay toward the trailing end, where it would rise a little. We can lay out these effects more openly, and show them plainly to the senses, with the example of one of those barges that come continually from Lizzafusina, full of fresh water for the use of the city. So let us picture such a barge coming along the Lagoon at a moderate speed, placidly carrying the water it is full of; and then, either through running aground or through some other obstacle set in its way, it is noticeably slowed. The water it holds will not, like the barge, lose the impetus already built up; keeping it, the water will run forward toward the bow, where it will rise noticeably, dropping away from the stern. But if, on the contrary, that same barge in the middle of its placid course is given a notable added burst of new speed, the water it holds, before growing used to it and keeping its own slowness, will lag behind — that is, toward the stern — where it will consequently rise, dropping away from the bow. This effect is beyond doubt and clear, and can be tried at any hour; and in it, for the moment, I want us to note three particulars. The first is that, to make the water rise at one end of the vessel, there is no need of new water, nor for it to run there leaving the other end. The second is that the water in the middle does not rise or fall noticeably — unless the barge's course were extremely fast, and the jolt or other check that stopped it very strong and sudden, in which case all the water might not only run forward but for the most part leap out of the barge; and it would do the same when, while moving slowly, it were suddenly struck by an extremely violent impetus. But when a moderate slowing or quickening overtakes a quiet motion of the barge, the middle parts (as I said) rise and fall imperceptibly, and the other parts rise less the closer they are to the middle, and more the farther off. The third is that, whereas the parts near the middle make little change in rising and falling compared with the waters at the ends, they, on the contrary, run far forward and back compared with the ends. Now, gentlemen, what the barge does with respect to the water it contains, and what the contained water does with respect to the barge that holds it, is exactly, to a hair, what the Mediterranean basin does with respect to the waters it contains, and what those waters do with respect to the Mediterranean basin that holds them. It now follows that we demonstrate how, and in what way, it is true that the Mediterranean and all other gulfs — in short, all parts of the Earth — move with a notably uneven motion, even though no motion that is not regular and uniform is assigned to the globe as a whole.

Simplicio
At first sight, to me — who am neither a mathematician nor an astronomer — this looks like a great paradox; and if it is true that, the motion of the whole being perfectly regular, the motion of the parts, while they stay always joined to their whole, can be irregular, then the paradox will destroy the axiom that states eandem esse rationem totius et partium — that the whole and its parts have one and the same character.

Salviati
I shall demonstrate my paradox, and to you, Simplicio, I'll leave the task of defending the axiom against it, or of reconciling the two; and my demonstration will be short and very easy, depending on things treated at length in our past discussions, without bringing in so much as a single syllable for the sake of the ebb and flow.

We have said there are two motions assigned to the terrestrial globe: the first, the annual, made by its center along the circumference of the great orb under the ecliptic, in the order of the signs — that is, from west to east; the other, made by the globe itself, turning about its own center in twenty-four hours, and this likewise from west to east, though about an axis somewhat tilted and not parallel to that of the annual revolution. From the compounding of these two motions, each of them uniform in itself, there results — I say — an uneven motion in the parts of the Earth. To make this more easily understood, I'll explain it by drawing the figure. First, about the center A I'll draw the circumference of the great orb, BC; on it, taking any point B, and about B as center, we'll draw this smaller circle DEFG, representing the terrestrial globe. We'll understand it to travel, with its center B, all the way around the circumference of the great orb, from west to east — that is, from the side B toward C. And beyond that, we'll understand the terrestrial globe to turn about its own center B, likewise from west to east — that is, in the succession of the points D, E, F, G — in the space of twenty-four hours. But here we must carefully note how, as a circle turns about its own center, any part of it must move at different times in contrary directions. This is clear if we consider that, while the parts of the circumference around the point D are moving to the left — that is, toward E — the opposite parts, those around F, are gaining toward the right — that is, toward G; so that when the parts at D have come to F, their motion will be contrary to what it was before, when they were at D. Moreover, in the same time that the parts at E descend, so to speak, toward F, those at G ascend toward D. Given, then, this contrariety of motions in the parts of the Earth's surface while it turns about its own center, it necessarily follows that, in coupling this daily motion with the other, annual one, there results for the parts of that terrestrial surface an absolute motion now much accelerated and now just as much retarded. This is clear if we first consider the part around D, whose absolute motion will be very fast, being born of two motions made toward the same side — that is, to the left: the first of them a part of the annual motion, common to all parts of the globe; the other belonging to the point D itself, carried likewise to the left by the daily whirl. So in this case the daily motion increases and accelerates the annual. The opposite happens to the opposite part, F, which, while it is carried by the common annual motion — together with the whole globe — toward the left, is carried by the daily turning toward the right; so that the daily motion comes to subtract from the annual, and the absolute movement resulting from the compounding of the two is greatly retarded. Around the points E and G, then, the absolute motion comes to be about equal to the simple annual, since the daily adds to it or subtracts from it nothing, or little, tending neither to the left nor to the right, but down and up. We conclude, therefore, that just as it is true that the motion of the whole globe, and of each of its parts, would be even and uniform if they moved with a single motion — whether the simple annual or the daily alone — so it is necessary that, when these two motions are mixed together, there result for the parts of the globe uneven motions, now accelerated and now retarded, through the additions or subtractions the daily rotation makes to the annual revolution. So that if it is true (as it most certainly is, and experience shows it) that the acceleration and slowing of the vessel's motion make the water it holds run and run back along its length, and rise and fall at its ends, who will make any difficulty about granting that this effect can — indeed, must of necessity — happen to the sea waters, held within their vessels, which are subject to such alterations, and above all in those that stretch lengthwise from west to east, which is the direction in which the motion of those vessels is made? Let this, then, be the principal and primary cause of the ebb and flow, without which nothing of the kind would follow. But because many and varied are the particular incidents observed in different places and times, which must depend on various other concomitant causes — though all of them must have a connection with the primary one — it is necessary to go on proposing and examining the various incidents that can be the causes of these various effects.

The first of these is that, whenever the water — thanks to a notable slowing or speeding of the motion of the vessel that holds it — has gained cause to run toward one end or the other, and has risen at one and dropped at the other, it will not stay in that state even when the primary cause ceases; but by its own weight and natural inclination to level and balance itself, it will of itself return, quickly, back again. And, being heavy and fluid, it will not only move toward equilibrium but, driven by its own impetus, will overshoot it, rising on the side where before it was lower; nor will it stop even here, but, going back once more, with ever more repeated back-and-forth surges it will show us that it will not pass at once from a speed of motion it has taken on to the loss of that motion and to a state of rest, but wants to be brought to it gradually, dying away little by little, slowly. In just the way we see a weight hanging from a cord, once it has been moved from its state of rest — that is, from the vertical — return there of itself and come to rest, but not before it has crossed it many times, this way and that, with its alternating runs and returns.

The second incident to note is that the back-and-forth surges just described are made and repeated with greater or lesser frequency — that is, in shorter or longer times — according to the different lengths of the vessels that hold the waters; so that in the shorter spaces the surges are more frequent, and rarer in the longer ones. Just as, in the same example of hanging bodies, we see the swings of those hung from longer cords to be less frequent than those of the ones hanging from shorter threads.

And here, for the third point worth knowing, it should be understood that not only the greater or lesser length of the vessel causes the water to make its surges in different times, but the greater or lesser depth does the same; and it happens that of waters held in basins of equal length but unequal depth, the one that is deeper makes its levelings in shorter times, and the surges of the shallower waters are less frequent.

Fourth, two effects the water makes in these levelings deserve to be noted and carefully observed. One is the rising and falling alternately toward one end and the other; the other is the moving and running, so to speak horizontally, forward and back. These two different motions reside differently in different parts of the water. For its end parts are the ones that rise and fall the most; those in the middle do not move up or down at all; and of the rest, step by step, those nearer the ends rise and fall proportionately more than the more distant ones. But conversely, in the other, forward-and-back progressive motion, the middle parts move a great deal, going and returning, while the waters at the very ends gain nothing of it — except insofar as, in rising, they overtop their banks and spill out of their original bed and basin; but where there is the check of banks to rein them in, they only rise and fall. Yet the middle waters do not for that reason stop running forward and back, which the other parts do too, proportionately, running more or less according as they are placed more distant from or nearer to the middle.

The fifth particular incident must be considered all the more attentively in that it is impossible for us to represent its effect by experiment and practice; and the incident is this. In the vessels we make by art, and move — like the barges named above — now faster and now slower, the acceleration and slowing are always shared in the same way by the whole vessel and by each of its parts; so that while, for example, the barge is checked in its motion, the leading part is slowed no more than the trailing one, but all share equally in the same slowing; and the same happens with acceleration — that is, when a new cause of greater speed is given to the barge, the bow and the stern accelerate in the same way. But in the immense vessels that are the very long beds of the seas — even though these too are nothing but certain hollows made in the solid body of the terrestrial globe — it marvelously happens that their ends do not increase and diminish their motion together, equally, and at the same moments of time; rather, it happens that when one of its ends finds itself, by virtue of the compounding of the two motions, the daily and the annual, greatly retarded in its speed, the other end finds itself still affected and joined with a very fast motion. This, for easier understanding, we'll explain

by taking up the figure just now drawn. In it, if we take a stretch of sea to be long, say, a quadrant — a quarter of the circle — such as the arc BC: because the parts at B are, as was explained above, in a very fast motion, through the union of the two motions, the daily and the annual, toward the same side, while the part at C is then in a retarded motion, being deprived of the advance that depends on the daily motion — if, I say, we take a gulf of sea as long as the arc BC, we shall already see how its ends move at the same time with great inequality. And the speeds would be utterly different in a stretch of sea half a circle long, placed in the position of the arc BCD, since the end B would be in a very fast motion, the other, D, in a very slow one, and the middle parts, toward C, in a moderate motion. And the shorter these stretches of sea are, the less they will share in this strange incident of having their parts, at certain hours of the day, differently affected by speed and slowness of motion. So that if, as in the first case, we see by experience that the acceleration and slowing — though shared equally by all parts of the containing vessel — are still the cause of the contained water's running forward and back, what must we suppose ought to happen in a vessel so marvelously arranged that slowing and speeding of motion are contributed to its parts very unequally? Certainly we can say nothing else but that a greater and more marvelous cause of commotions in the water — and stranger ones — must be found there. And though it may seem impossible to many that, in artificial machines and vessels, we could experiment the effects of such an incident, nonetheless it is not entirely impossible; and I have the design of a machine in which, in particular, the effect of these marvelous compoundings of motions can be discerned. But as far as concerns the present subject, what you have been able to grasp so far with your imagination is enough. For my part, I understand very well that this marvelous incident must necessarily be found in the gulfs of the seas, and above all in those that stretch over great distances from west to east — that is, along the course of the terrestrial globe's motions; and since it is in a certain way unthinkable and without parallel among the motions we can produce, so I find it not hard to believe that from it there may derive effects not imitable by our artificial experiments.
