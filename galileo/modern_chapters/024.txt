The Second Day

(Part 12 of 20)

Salviati
A doubt worthy of Sagredo; and to clear it up, so that we may grasp it more distinctly — since you say yourself that you hold it only confusedly — let us sort it out by reducing it to a figure, which may perhaps also make it easier to resolve. So let us draw a line perpendicular toward the center, and let this be AC, and at right angles to it the horizontal AB, along which the motion of the projection would take place, and along which the projectile would keep traveling with uniform motion, if gravity did not draw it downward. Now imagine a straight line drawn from the point A, making any angle you please with AB, and let this be AE; and let us mark off on AB some equal spaces AF, FH, HK, and from them draw the perpendiculars FG, HI, KL up to AE. And because, as we've said before, a heavy body falling from rest keeps acquiring an ever greater degree of speed from one moment to the next, as the time itself increases, we can picture the spaces AF, FH, HK as representing equal times, and the perpendiculars FG, HI, KL as the degrees of speed acquired in those times — so that the degree of speed acquired in the whole time AK is as the line KL, compared with the degree HI acquired in the time AH, and the degree FG in the time AF; and these degrees KL, HI, FG have (as is plain) the same proportion as the times KA, HA, FA. And if other perpendiculars are drawn from points marked at will on the line FA, we shall always find smaller and smaller degrees, on to infinity, as we proceed toward the point A, which represents the first instant of time and the first state of rest. And this drawing-back toward A represents to us the first propensity toward downward motion, diminished to infinity by the body's approach to that first state of rest — an approach that can itself be increased to infinity.

Now let us find the other diminution of speed, which can likewise be carried on to infinity through the diminution of the body's heaviness; and this will be represented by drawing other lines from the point A, making angles smaller than the angle BAE — as this line AD would be, which, cutting the parallels KL, HI, FG at the points M, N, O, pictures for us the degrees FO, HN, KM acquired in the times AF, AH, AK, smaller than the other degrees FG, HI, KL acquired in the same times — but these last as if by a heavier body, and those first by a lighter one. And it's plain that by drawing the line EA toward AB, narrowing the angle EAB (which can be done to infinity, just as heaviness can be diminished to infinity), the speed of the falling body is likewise diminished to infinity, and with it the cause that hindered the projection. And so it seems that, from the union of these two grounds against the projection — both diminished to infinity — the projection cannot possibly be hindered.

And reducing the whole argument to a few words, we'll say: by narrowing the angle EAB the degrees of speed LK, IH, GF are diminished; and moreover, by drawing the parallels KL, HI, FG back toward the angle A, the same degrees are likewise diminished; and each of the two diminutions extends to infinity. Therefore the speed of the downward motion can be diminished so far and so far (being diminishable doubly to infinity) that it will not suffice to restore the body onto the circumference of the wheel, and consequently to make the projection be hindered and eliminated. On the other hand, to keep the projection from happening, the spaces through which the projectile must descend to rejoin the wheel would have to be made so short and narrow that, however slow — indeed, however infinitely diminished — the body's descent may be, it still suffices to carry it back; and so there would have to be found a diminution of those spaces made not only to infinity, but of such an infinity as would exceed the double infinity produced in the diminution of the falling body's speed. But how shall one magnitude be diminished more than another that is being diminished doubly to infinity? Now let Simplicio note how well one may philosophize about nature without geometry! The degrees of speed, diminished to infinity — both by the diminution of the body's heaviness and by the approach to the first terminus of motion, that is, to the state of rest — are always determinate, and correspond proportionally to the parallels contained between two straight lines meeting at an angle, whether the angle BAE or BAD, or another infinitely more acute, but always rectilinear. But the diminution of the spaces through which the body has to be carried back onto the circumference of the wheel is proportional to another sort of diminution altogether — one contained within lines that make an angle infinitely narrower and more acute than any acute rectilinear angle whatsoever. And here it is. Take any point C on the perpendicular AC, and making it the center, describe with the radius CA an arc AMP, which will cut the parallels that determine the degrees of speed, however small they may be and however contained within a most narrow rectilinear angle. Of these parallels, the parts that remain between the arc and the tangent AB are the amounts of the spaces and of the returns onto the wheel — always smaller, and smaller in ever greater proportion the closer they come to the point of contact; smaller, I say, than those parallels of which they are parts.

The parallels contained between the straight lines, as they draw back toward the angle, always diminish in the same proportion: as, for example, AH being divided in half at the point F, the parallel HI will be double FG, and subdividing FA in half, the parallel produced from the point of division will be half of FG, and continuing the subdivision to infinity, the successive parallels will always be half of the ones just before. But it does not go this way with the lines intercepted between the tangent and the circumference of the circle; for, making the same subdivision on FA and supposing, say, that the parallel coming from the point H were double the one coming from F, this one will then be more than double the following one; and continually, as we come toward the point of contact A, we shall find the preceding lines containing the next following ones three, four, ten, a hundred, a thousand, a hundred thousand, a hundred million times over, and more, to infinity. The shortness, then, of such lines is reduced to such a degree that it far exceeds what is needed to make the projectile — however light it may be — return, indeed even keep itself, upon the circumference.

Sagredo
I follow the whole argument perfectly well, and the force with which it presses home; still, it seems to me that anyone who wanted to give it more trouble could raise a difficulty for us, by saying that of the two causes which make the body's descent slower and slower to infinity, it's plain that the one depending on nearness to the first terminus of the descent always grows in the same proportion — just as the parallels always keep the same proportion among themselves, and so on. But that the diminution of that same speed depending on the diminution of the body's heaviness (which was the second cause) is also made in the same proportion — that doesn't seem so plain. And who assures us that it isn't made according to the proportion of the lines intercepted between the tangent and the circumference, or even in a greater proportion still?

Salviati
I had taken it as true that the speeds of naturally descending bodies follow the proportion of their heavinesses — as a favor to Simplicio and to Aristotle, who in several places asserts it as an obvious proposition. You, as a favor to our adversary, put this in doubt, and assert that the speed may increase in a greater proportion — even infinitely greater — than that of the heaviness, so that the whole preceding argument falls to the ground. It remains for me, in order to uphold it, to say that the proportion of the speeds is much smaller than that of the heavinesses, and thus not only to sustain but to fortify what has been said. And as proof of this I bring in experience, which will show us that a body even thirty or forty times heavier than another — as, for example, a ball of lead and one of cork — will not move even by a long way twice as fast. Now, if the projection would not occur even when the falling body's speed were diminished according to the proportion of the heaviness, much less will it occur whenever the speed is lessened only a little, however much weight is taken away. But even granting that the speed were diminished in a proportion far greater than that by which the heaviness is lessened — even if it were the very proportion by which those parallels between the tangent and the circumference diminish — I still perceive no necessity whatsoever that would persuade me the projection must occur with materials however light you please; indeed, I affirm that it will not occur — understanding, however, materials that are not properly weightless, that is, devoid of all heaviness and by their nature rising upward, but materials that descend most slowly and have the very least heaviness. And what moves me to believe so is this: the diminution of heaviness, made according to the proportion of the parallels between the tangent and the circumference, has for its last and utmost terminus the nullity of weight, just as those parallels have for the last terminus of their diminution the contact itself, which is an indivisible point. Now heaviness is never diminished all the way to that last terminus, for then the body would not be heavy at all; but the space of the projectile's return to the circumference is indeed reduced to the utmost smallness — which is when the body rests upon the circumference at the very point of contact, so that to return there it needs no space at all. And therefore, however slight the propensity toward downward motion may be, it is always more than enough to bring the body back onto the circumference, from which it is distant by the smallest space — that is, by nothing.

Sagredo
Truly the argument is very subtle, but just as conclusive; and one is forced to confess that to try to treat questions of nature without geometry is to attempt what is impossible to be done.

Salviati
But Simplicio won't say so — though I don't believe he's one of those Peripatetics who dissuade their disciples from the study of mathematics, on the grounds that it corrupts the reasoning and makes it less fit for contemplation.

Simplicio
I wouldn't do Plato that wrong; but I would say, with Aristotle, that he immersed himself too deeply and grew too enamored of that geometry of his. For in the end these mathematical subtleties, Salviati, are true in the abstract, but applied to sensible, physical matter they don't answer. The mathematicians will prove very well, with their principles, that (for example) sphaera tangit planum in puncto — a sphere touches a plane at a point — a proposition much like the present one; but when it comes to matter, things go another way. And I'd say the same of these angles of contact and these proportions: they all come to nothing when one comes to material, sensible things.

Salviati
So you don't believe, then, that the tangent touches the surface of the terrestrial globe at a point?

Simplicio
Not at a single point; I believe that a straight line goes on touching the surface even of water — let alone of the Earth — for many, many dozens, and perhaps hundreds, of braccia before it separates from it.

Salviati
But if I grant you this, don't you see that it's so much the worse for your case? For if, even granting that the tangent were separated from the Earth's surface everywhere but a single point, it has nonetheless been demonstrated that, because of the great narrowness of the angle of contact (if it should even be called an angle), the projectile would not separate — how much less cause will it have to separate if that angle is closed up altogether, and the surface and the tangent proceed together as one? Don't you see that in this way the projection would take place upon the Earth's very surface, which is as much as to say that it wouldn't take place at all? You see, then, the force of truth: while you try to knock it down, your own assaults raise it up and strengthen it. But now that I've drawn you out of this error, I wouldn't want to leave you in this other one — your supposing that a material sphere does not touch a plane at a single point. I'd wish, rather, that this conversation, of just a few hours though it be, held with people who have some knowledge of geometry, might make you appear a little more intelligent among those who know nothing of it. Now, to show you how great is the error of those who say that a sphere — say, of bronze — does not touch a plane — say, of steel — at a point, tell me: what would you make of someone who said, and constantly maintained, that the sphere were not truly a sphere?

Simplicio
I'd judge him utterly devoid of reason.

Salviati
In just this state is the man who says that a material sphere doesn't touch a plane — a material one too — at a point; for to say this is the same as saying that the sphere is not a sphere. And to prove that this is so, tell me: in what do you place the essence of the sphere? That is, what is it that makes the sphere differ from all other solid bodies?

Simplicio
I believe that being a sphere consists in having all the straight lines drawn from its center to the circumference equal.

Salviati
So that when such lines were not equal, that solid would no longer be a sphere.

Simplicio
No, sir.

Salviati
Tell me next whether you believe that, of the many lines that can be drawn between two points, there can be more than a single straight one.

Simplicio
No, sir.

Salviati
But you understand, too, that this single straight line will then of necessity be the shortest of all the others.

Simplicio
I do, and I even have a clear demonstration of it, brought forward by a great Peripatetic philosopher; and it seems to me, if I remember rightly, that he offers it while reproving Archimedes, who assumes it as known when it could be demonstrated.

Salviati
This must have been a great mathematician, to have been able to demonstrate what Archimedes neither knew how nor was able to demonstrate; and if the demonstration comes back to you, I'd gladly hear it, for I remember perfectly well that Archimedes, in his books On the Sphere and the Cylinder, puts this proposition among the postulates, and I hold it for certain that he took it to be indemonstrable.

Simplicio
I think it will come back to me, for it's quite easy and short.

Salviati
So much the greater, then, will be Archimedes' shame, and the glory of this philosopher.

Simplicio
I'll draw his figure. Between the points A and B draw the straight line AB and the curve ACB, of which he wants to prove the straight one to be the shorter; and the proof runs like this. On the curve take a point, which would be C, and draw two other straight lines AC, CB, which two are longer than AB alone — as Euclid demonstrates. But the curve ACB is greater than the two straight lines AC, CB; therefore, a fortiori — all the more so — the curve ACB will be much greater than the straight line AB, which is what was to be demonstrated.

Salviati
I don't believe that if you searched out all the paralogisms in the world you could find one more fitting than this to give an example of the most solemn fallacy among all fallacies — namely, the one that proves ignotum per ignotius, the unknown by the more unknown.

Simplicio
How so?

Salviati
How so? The unknown conclusion you want to prove — isn't it that the curve ACB is longer than the straight line AB? And the middle term, taken as known — isn't it that the curve ACB is greater than the two lines AC, CB, which are known to be greater than AB? And if it's unknown to you that the curve is greater than the straight line AB alone, how will it not be much more unknown that it's greater than the two lines AC, CB, which are known to be greater than AB alone? And yet you take that as known?

Simplicio
I still don't quite understand where the fallacy lies.

Salviati
Since the two straight lines are greater than AB (as is known from Euclid), then whenever the curve is greater than the two lines AC, CB, will it not be much greater than the straight line AB alone?

Simplicio
Yes, sir.

Salviati
That the curve ACB is greater than the straight line AB is the conclusion — better known than the middle term, which is that the same curve is greater than the two lines AC, CB. Now, when the middle is less known than the conclusion, that's called proving ignotum per ignotius. Now let's get back to our subject: it's enough that you grant the straight line to be the shortest of all the lines that can be drawn between two points. And as for the main conclusion — you say the material sphere doesn't touch the plane at a single point: what, then, is its contact?

Simplicio
It will be a part of its surface.

Salviati
And the contact, likewise, of another sphere equal to the first — will that too be a similar little piece of its surface?

Simplicio
There's no reason it shouldn't be so.

Salviati
Then the two spheres too, in touching, will touch with those same two little pieces of surface; because, since each of them fits the same plane, they must necessarily fit each other as well. Now imagine the two spheres, whose centers are A and B, touching, and let their centers be joined by the straight line AB, which will pass through the point of contact. Let it pass through the point C, and taking another point D in the contact, let the two lines AD, BD be joined, so that the triangle ADB is formed, of which the two sides AD, DB will be equal to the single other line ACB — since both those and this contain two radii, which by the definition of the sphere are all equal. And thus the straight line AB, drawn between the two centers A and B, will not be the shortest of all, there being the two AD, DB equal to it — which, by your own concessions, is absurd.

Simplicio
This demonstration holds for spheres in the abstract, not for material ones.

Salviati
Then point out to me where the fallacy of my argument lies, since it doesn't hold for material spheres but does hold for immaterial, abstract ones.

Simplicio
Material spheres are subject to many accidents that immaterial ones aren't. And why couldn't it be that, when a metal sphere rests on a plane, its own weight presses in such a way that the plane gives a little, or else that the sphere itself gets dented at the contact? Besides, that plane can hardly be perfect — if for no other reason, at least because matter is porous; and perhaps it will be no less hard to find a sphere so perfect that it has all the lines from center to surface exactly and precisely equal.

Salviati
Oh, all these things I readily grant you, but they're quite beside the point; for while you want to show me that a material sphere doesn't touch a material plane at a point, you make use of a sphere that isn't a sphere and a plane that isn't a plane — since, by your own account, either these things aren't to be found in the world, or, if they are found, they get spoiled in being applied to produce the effect. It would have been less bad, then, for you to grant the conclusion, but conditionally — that is, that if there were given in matter a sphere and a plane that were, and stayed, perfect, they would touch at a single point — and then to deny that this can ever be had.

Simplicio
I believe the philosophers' proposition should be understood in that sense; for there's no doubt that the imperfection of matter makes it so that things taken in the concrete don't answer to those considered in the abstract.

Salviati
Don't answer? On the contrary — what you yourself have just said proves that they answer exactly.

Simplicio
How so?

Salviati
Don't you say that, because of the imperfection of matter, that body which ought to be a perfect sphere, and that plane which ought to be a perfect plane, don't turn out in the concrete to be such as one pictures them in the abstract?
