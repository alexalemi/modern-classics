The Third Day

(Part 11 of 16)

Sagredo
Here, Simplicio, there's another confusion — one you really do understand, only it doesn't occur to you to understand it; and I'll try to remind you of it. So tell me: suppose that, after adjusting your quadrant on a fixed star and finding, for instance, its elevation to be 40 degrees, you — without shifting from your place — tilted the arm of the quadrant so that the star stayed raised above that line of sight; would you say, on that account, that the star had gained a greater elevation?

Simplicio
Certainly not, because the change was made in the instrument, not in the observer — who would have had to change place by moving toward the star.

Sagredo
But when you're sailing or walking over the surface of the Earth, would you say that no change at all was made in that same quadrant — that it always kept the same elevation with respect to the sky — so long as you yourself didn't tilt it, but left it standing in its first setting?

Simplicio
Let me think about it a moment. I'd say without a doubt that it wouldn't keep it, because the journey I make isn't over a flat plane, but over the curved surface of the terrestrial globe, which step by step changes its inclination with respect to the sky, and consequently makes the instrument — which keeps its setting upon that surface — change too.

Sagredo
Very well put; and you also grasp that the larger and larger that circle was, over which you moved, the more miles you'd have to walk to make that star rise by that one degree for you; and finally, that when the motion toward the star was along a straight line, you'd have to move even farther than along the circumference of any circle, however enormous.

Salviati
Yes, because in the end the circumference of an infinite circle and a straight line are the same thing.

Sagredo
Oh, that I don't understand — nor do I think Simplicio understands it either; and there must be some hidden mystery underneath, because we know that Salviati never speaks at random, nor brings out a paradox that doesn't turn on some far-from-trivial idea. So, in due place and time, I'll remind you to explain this business of the straight line being the same as the circumference of the infinite circle — but for now I don't want us to interrupt the discussion we have in hand. And getting back to the point, I'd have Simplicio consider that the approaching and withdrawing the Earth does to that fixed star which is near the pole is done as if along a straight line — namely the diameter of the great orb; so that to try to gauge the rising and falling of the polar star by the motion along that diameter as though it were the motion over the Earth's own tiny little circle is a great sign of small understanding.

Simplicio
But we're still stuck in the same difficulties, since not even that slight difference which there ought to be is seen to be there; and if this is nil, then we have to confess that the annual motion along the great orb, attributed to the Earth, is nil as well.

Sagredo
Now here I'll let Salviati carry on — for it seems to me he wasn't dismissing as nil the rising or falling of the polar star, or of some other fixed star, even though it's grasped by no one, and even though Copernicus himself set it down not, I'd say, as nil, but as unobservable for its smallness.

Salviati
I've already said above that I don't believe anyone has set himself to observe whether, at different times of the year, any change can be detected in the fixed stars that might depend on the annual motion of the Earth; and I added, moreover, that I doubt whether anyone has even properly understood what the changes are, and among which stars they ought to appear. So it's well that we go through this point carefully. Finding it written only in general terms — that the annual motion of the Earth along the great orb ought not to be admitted, because it's not likely that no apparent change would be seen in the fixed stars on account of it — and then not hearing said what in particular such apparent changes should be, and in which stars, makes me very reasonably reckon that those who rest on that general pronouncement have not understood, nor perhaps even tried to understand, how the business of these changes actually works, nor what the things are that they say ought to be seen. And what moves me to judge so is knowing that the annual motion attributed by Copernicus to the Earth, when it has to become perceptible in the starry sphere, doesn't have to produce an apparent change equally with respect to all the stars; rather, that appearance must be greater in some, smaller in others, smaller still in others, and finally, in others, absolutely nil — however enormous the circle of this annual motion is supposed to be. Now the changes that ought to be seen are of two kinds: one is the stars' changing their apparent magnitude, and the other, their varying their meridian altitudes — from which follows, in consequence, the changing of their risings and settings, and of their distances from the point overhead, and so on.

Sagredo
It seems to me I see a whole skein of these turnings being got ready for me — God grant I'm ever able to disentangle myself from it; for, to confess my failing to you, Salviati, I've thought about this now and then, and could never find the loose end of the thread — and I don't mean so much about this that concerns the fixed stars, as about another, more terrible business that you've brought back to my mind by mentioning these meridian altitudes, rising latitudes, and distances from the point overhead, and so on. And my brain's tangle comes from what I'll tell you now. Copernicus places the starry sphere motionless, and the Sun at its center, likewise motionless; therefore every change that appears to us to occur in the Sun or in the fixed stars must necessarily belong to the Earth — that is, to us. But the Sun rises and falls in our meridian through an enormous arc, of nearly 47 degrees, and through arcs greater and greater still it varies the breadth of its risings and settings on the slanting horizons. Now how can the Earth ever tilt and rise so notably toward the Sun, and not at all toward the fixed stars — or by so little that it's an imperceptible thing? This is the knot that has never once been able to pass through my comb; and if you'll untie it for me, I'll think you more than an Alexander.

Salviati
These are difficulties worthy of Sagredo's wit; and the doubt is such that even Copernicus himself almost despaired of being able to explain it in a way that would make it intelligible — which is plain both from his own confession of its obscurity, and from his falling back on it twice, in two different ways, to explain it. And I frankly confess that I didn't grasp his explanation until, by another and different method, quite plain and clear, I made it intelligible to myself — though not without a long and laborious application of mind.

Simplicio
Aristotle saw this same difficulty and used it to refute certain ancients who wanted the Earth to be a planet: against them he argues that, if that were so, then it too, like the other planets, would have to have more than one motion, from which would follow this variation in the risings and settings of the fixed stars, and in their meridian altitudes as well. And since he raised the difficulty and didn't resolve it, it must be — if not impossible — at least hard to solve.

Salviati
The size and force of the knot make its untying all the finer and more admirable; but I don't promise it to you for today, and I beg you to excuse me until tomorrow. For now, we'll go on considering and explaining those changes and differences that ought to be detected in the fixed stars on account of the annual motion, just as we were saying — and in explaining them there come to be set out certain preparatory points for the untying of the greatest difficulty. Now, taking up again the two motions attributed to the Earth (and I say two, because the third isn't really a motion at all, as I'll explain in its place), that is, the annual and the daily: the first is to be understood as made by the center of the Earth along the circumference of the great orb — that is, of a greatest circle described in the plane of the ecliptic, fixed and unchanging; the other, the daily, is made by the globe of the Earth on itself, about its own center and its own axis — not upright, but tilted to the plane of the ecliptic, with an inclination of about 23 and a half degrees. This inclination is maintained all year long, and — what is most of all to be noted — it always keeps toward the same part of the sky, so that the axis of the daily motion stays perpetually parallel to itself. Thus, if we imagine that axis prolonged out to the fixed stars, then while the center of the Earth circles the whole ecliptic in a year, that same axis describes the surface of an oblique cylinder, which has for one of its bases the said yearly circle, and for the other a like circle imaginarily described by its extremity — or, if you like, its pole — among the fixed stars. And this cylinder is oblique to the plane of the ecliptic according to the inclination of the axis that describes it, which we've said is 23 and a half degrees; and this inclination, keeping perpetually the same (except that over many thousands of years it makes some very slight change, which matters nothing to the present business), causes the terrestrial globe never to tilt any further or to rise, but to keep itself unchanging. From which it follows that, as regards the changes to be observed in the fixed stars depending on the annual motion alone, the same will happen to any point whatever of the Earth's surface as to the very center of the Earth; and so in the present explanations we'll make use of the center, as of any point of the surface. And for easier understanding of the whole, we'll draw the line-figures; and first let's mark, in the plane of the ecliptic, the circle ANBO, and let's take the points A, B to be the extremes toward the north and toward the south — that is, the beginning of Cancer and of Capricorn — and let's prolong the diameter AB indefinitely through D and C toward the starry sphere. I say now, first, that none of the fixed stars set in the ecliptic will ever, by any change made by the Earth through that plane of the ecliptic, vary its elevation, but will always be seen in the same plane; but the Earth will indeed approach it and withdraw from it by as much space as the diameter of the great orb. This is plainly seen in the figure: for whether the Earth is at point A or at B, the star G is always seen along the same line ABC; but the distance BC has become smaller than CA by the whole diameter BA. The most, then, that can be detected in the star C — and in any other set in the ecliptic — is the increased or diminished apparent magnitude, from the Earth's approaching or withdrawing.

Sagredo
Hold on a moment, please, because I feel some scruple or other bothering me, and it's this. That the star C is seen along the same line A B C whether the Earth is at A or at B — this I understand very well; and moreover I grasp that the same would happen from all the points of the line A B, while the Earth passed from A to B along that line. But since it passes, as is supposed, along the arc A N B, it's a plain thing that when it's at point N — and at any other point except the two, A and B — it will be seen no longer along the line A B, but along other and other lines. So that if showing itself along different lines must cause an apparent change, some difference will have to be detected. What's more, I'll say — with that philosophical freedom which among friends who are philosophers ought to be allowed — it seems to me that you, contradicting yourself, are now denying what this very day you declared to us, to our wonder, to be a most true and important thing: I mean what happens with the planets, and in particular the three outer ones, which, being continually in the ecliptic or very near it, not only show themselves now near to us and now most remote, but so irregular in their otherwise regular motions that they sometimes appear to us stationary, and sometimes, by many degrees, retrograde — and all for no other cause than the annual motion of the Earth.

Salviati
Even though a thousand proofs have made me certain of Sagredo's shrewdness, I wanted, with this further trial, to reassure myself all the more of what I can promise myself from his wit — and all for my own benefit, since when my propositions can stand firm to the hammer, or to the assayer's cupel, of his judgment, I can be sure they're of good alloy under any test. I say, then, that I had deliberately disguised that objection — yet not with any intent to deceive you and persuade you of some falsehood, as could have happened if the instance I disguised, and you passed over, had really been such as it shows itself in appearance, that is, truly strong and conclusive. But it isn't such; on the contrary, I now suspect that you, to test me, are pretending not to recognize its nullity. But in this particular I mean to be craftier than you, by dragging out of your mouth by force what you were artfully trying to hide from us: so tell me, what is it by which you recognize the stations and retrogradations of the planets deriving from the annual motion — an effect so great that at least some trace of a like effect ought to be seen in the stars of the ecliptic?

Sagredo
Your question contains two queries, to which I must reply: the first concerns the charge you lay on me, of being a dissembler; the other is about what may appear in the stars, and so on. As to the first, I'll say — with your leave — that it's not true I pretended not to understand the nullity of that instance; and to reassure you of it, I tell you now that I understand that nullity perfectly well.

Salviati
But I don't understand how it can be that you weren't speaking dissemblingly when you said you didn't understand that particular fallacy — which you now confess you understand perfectly well.

Sagredo
The very confession that I understand it can assure you I wasn't dissembling when I said I didn't understand it; for if I'd wanted, and did want, to dissemble, who could stop me from continuing in the same pretense, still denying that I understood the fallacy? I say, then, that I didn't understand it then, but that I grasp it well now — thanks to your having woken up my intellect, first by telling me resolutely that it's nil, and then by beginning to question me so broadly, what that thing was by which I recognized the stations and retrogradations of the planets. And since this is recognized from the comparison one makes of them with the fixed stars — in relation to which their motions are seen to vary, now westward and now eastward, and sometimes to stay as if motionless — and since above the starry sphere there's no other, immensely more remote and still visible to us, with which we could compare our fixed stars, therefore we can detect no trace in the fixed stars answering to what appears to us in the planets. This, I think, is just what you meant to draw out of my mouth.

Salviati
That it is — with the added bonus of your very subtle wit. And if with a little hint I opened your mind, you with another make me recall that it's not altogether impossible that something might, at some time, be found observable among the fixed stars, by which one could make out in which of them the annual revolution resides — so that they too, no less than the planets and the Sun itself, might be willing to appear in court to bear witness to such a motion in the Earth's favor. For I don't believe that the stars are strewn over a spherical surface, all equally distant from a single center; rather I reckon that their distances from us are so various that some of them may be 2 and 3 times more remote than certain others. So that if there were found, with the telescope, some very tiny star quite close to one of the larger ones — and that larger one were therefore very high up — it might happen that some perceptible change would occur between them, answering to that of the outer planets. And so much be said for now about the particular of the stars set in the ecliptic. Let's come now to the fixed stars set outside the ecliptic, and let's imagine a greatest circle erect to the plane of the ecliptic — say, for example, a circle that in the starry sphere answers to the colure of the solstices — and let's mark it CEH, which will at the same time turn out to be a meridian; and in it let's take a star outside the ecliptic, such as E would be. Now this one, at the Earth's motion, will indeed vary its elevation; for from the Earth at A it will be seen along the ray AE, with the elevation of the angle EAC; but from the Earth placed at B it will be seen along the ray BE, with the elevation of the angle EBC, greater than the other EAC, since that one is exterior and this interior and opposite, in the triangle EAB. The distance of the star E from the ecliptic will thus be seen changed; and likewise its altitude in the meridian will have become greater in the position B than in the place A, according as the angle EBC exceeds the angle EAC, which is the size of the angle AEB. For, the side AB of the triangle EAB being prolonged to C, the exterior angle EBC (being equal to the two interior and opposite ones, E and A) exceeds A itself by the size of the angle E. And if we take another star in the same meridian, more remote from the ecliptic — such as, for instance, the star H would be — the difference in its being seen from the two places A and B will be greater still, according as the angle AHB becomes greater than the other, E; which angle will keep growing, the farther the observed star is from the ecliptic, until finally the greatest change will appear in that star which was set in the very pole of the ecliptic — as, for total understanding, we can demonstrate thus. Let the diameter of the great orb be AB, whose center is G, and let it be understood as prolonged out to the starry sphere at the points D, C; and from the center G let the axis of the ecliptic GF be raised up to the same sphere, in which let there be understood described a meridian DFC, which will be erect to the plane of the ecliptic; and taking in the arc FC any points you please, H, E, as places of fixed stars, let the lines FA, FB, AH, HG, HB, AE, GE, BE be joined, so that the angle of the difference — or let's say the parallax — of the star set at the pole F is AFB, that of the star set at H is the angle AHB, and that of the star at E is the angle AEB. I say that the angle of the difference of the polar star F is the greatest, and, of the others, the one nearer the greatest is greater than the more remote — that is, that the angle F is greater than the angle H, and this greater than the angle E. Let there be understood a circle described about the triangle FAB; and because the angle F is acute (its base AB being smaller than the diameter DC of the semicircle DFC), it will be set in the larger portion of the circumscribed circle cut off by the base AB; and because that AB is divided in half and at right angles by FG, the center of the circumscribed circle will be on the line FG: let it be the point I. And because, of the lines drawn from the point G — which is not the center — to the circumference of the circumscribed circle, the greatest is the one that passes through the center, GF will be greater than any other drawn from the point G to the circumference of that same circle; and therefore that circumference will cut the line GH (which is equal to the line GF), and cutting GH it will also cut AH: let it cut it at L, and let the line LB be joined. The two angles AFB, ALB will therefore be equal, being in the same portion of the circumscribed circle; but ALB, being exterior, is greater than the interior H: therefore the angle F is greater than the angle H. And by the same method we'll demonstrate that the angle H is greater than the angle E, because the center of the circle described about the triangle AHB is on the perpendicular GF, to which the line GH is nearer than GE, and therefore its circumference cuts GE, and also AE: whence the point is manifest. Let's conclude, then, that the difference of appearance (which, by a term proper to the art, we may call the parallax of the fixed stars) is greater and smaller according as the observed stars are more or less near the pole of the ecliptic; so that finally, of the stars that are in the ecliptic itself, that difference is reduced to nothing. As for the Earth's approaching or withdrawing from the stars by this motion: from those that are in the ecliptic it approaches and withdraws by as much as the whole diameter of the great orb, as we saw just now; but from the stars around the pole of the ecliptic that approach or withdrawal is almost nil, and for the others this difference grows greater according as they are nearer the ecliptic. We can, in the third place, understand how that difference of apparent position grows greater or smaller according as the observed star is nearer to us or more remote; for, if we mark another meridian less distant from the Earth — such as this DFI would be — a star set at F and seen along the same ray AFE, the Earth being at A, when it was then observed from the Earth at B, would be seen along the ray BF, and would make the angle of the difference, that is BFA, greater than the other first one AEB, being exterior to the triangle BFE.
