The Second Day

(Part 3 of 20)

Salviati
If I had ever said that the universe does not move for lack of power in its Mover, I would have been wrong, and your correction would be well aimed; and I grant you that for an infinite power it is as easy to move a hundred thousand bodies as to move one. But what I said had no reference to the Mover — only to the moving bodies, and in them not just to their resistance, which is without doubt smaller in the Earth than in the whole universe, but to the many other particulars we were considering just now. As for the claim that it is better for an infinite power to exercise a great part of itself rather than a tiny one, I answer that no part of the infinite is greater than another, so long as both are finite; nor can one say that a hundred thousand is a greater part of an infinite number than two is, even though a hundred thousand is fifty thousand times bigger than two. And if moving the universe required a finite power — however enormous compared with what would suffice to move the Earth alone — the part of the infinite employed would be no greater for that, nor would what remained idle be any less than infinite. So applying a little more or a little less power to one particular effect matters not at all; besides which, the operation of such a power does not stop and end at the daily rotation alone — there are plenty of other motions in the world that we know of, and there may be many more unknown to us. Looking, then, at the moving bodies; not doubting that it is a shorter and readier operation to move the Earth than the universe; and keeping an eye, moreover, on the many other savings and simplifications that this one choice secures — a very true axiom of Aristotle, which teaches us that frustra fit per plura quod potest fieri per pauciora — it is pointless to do with more what can be done with less — makes it more probable that the daily rotation belongs to the Earth alone than to the whole universe minus the Earth.

Simplicio
In citing the axiom you have left out a clause that is the whole point, especially in the present case. The phrase omitted is æque bene — "equally well." So we must examine whether the two assumptions can satisfy everything equally well.

Salviati
Whether both positions satisfy equally well will emerge from the particular examination of the appearances they have to satisfy; for so far we have argued, and shall go on arguing, ex hypothesi — as a supposition — assuming that as far as satisfying the appearances goes, the two positions are equally serviceable. As for the phrase you say I left out, I rather suspect you have superfluously added it. "Equally well" is a relation, and a relation necessarily requires at least two terms — a thing cannot stand in relation to itself; one doesn't say, for example, that rest is equally as good as rest. And when we say "it is done in vain by more means what can be done by fewer," we understand that the thing to be done must be the same thing, not two different things; and since the same thing cannot be said to be done equally as well as its very self, the addition of the phrase "equally well" is superfluous — a relation with only one term.

Sagredo
If we don't want the same thing to happen to us as yesterday, please, let's get back to the matter at hand; and let Simplicio begin producing the difficulties that seem to him to tell against this new arrangement of the world.

Simplicio
The arrangement is not new — on the contrary, it is very ancient; and the proof is that Aristotle refutes it, and his refutations are these. "First: if the Earth moved — whether in itself, staying at the center, or in a circle, being outside the center — that motion would necessarily be violent, for it is not her natural motion; if it were hers, every one of her particles would share it, but each of them moves in a straight line toward the center. The motion being therefore violent and preternatural, it could not be everlasting; but the order of the world is everlasting; therefore, and so on. Second: all the other bodies that move circularly appear to lag behind and to move with more than one motion — excepting only the primum mobile, the outermost sphere that carries all the rest. On that account the Earth too would have to move with two motions; and if that were so, of necessity changes would have to show up in the fixed stars — which is not seen; rather, without any variation, the same stars always rise from the same places and set in the same. Third: the motion of the parts and of the whole is naturally toward the center of the universe, and for that reason too the whole rests there." He then raises the question whether the motion of the parts is aimed naturally at the center of the universe or at the center of the Earth, and concludes that its own proper instinct is to go toward the center of the universe, and only per accidens toward the center of the Earth — a doubt we discussed at length yesterday. Finally he confirms the same point with a fourth argument, drawn from the experience of heavy bodies: falling from high to low, they come down plumb onto the surface of the Earth; and likewise projectiles thrown plumb upward return plumb down along the same lines, however immense the height to which they were thrown — arguments that conclude of necessity that their motion is toward the center of the Earth, which, without moving at all, waits for them and receives them. Last of all he notes that the astronomers produce further reasons in confirmation of the same conclusions — I mean, that the Earth is at the center of the universe and immobile — and he cites just one of them: that all the appearances seen in the movements of the stars answer to the Earth's position at the center, a correspondence that would fail if she were not there. The other reasons, produced by Ptolemy and by other astronomers, I can bring forward now, if you like, or after you've said whatever occurs to you in reply to these of Aristotle's.

Salviati
The arguments produced in this matter are of two kinds: some have regard to earthly happenings, without any relation to the stars, and others are drawn from the appearances and observations of things celestial. Aristotle's arguments are mostly drawn from the things around us, and he leaves the others to the astronomers; so it will be well, if you agree, to examine the ones taken from the Earth's own experiences, and then come to the other kind. And since Ptolemy, Tycho, and other astronomers and philosophers — besides taking up, confirming, and fortifying Aristotle's arguments — have produced others of their own, we may as well gather them all together, so as not to have to give the same or similar answers twice. So, Simplicio: would you like to recite them, or shall I relieve you of the trouble? I am happy to oblige either way.

Simplicio
Better that you present them: having studied them more, you'll have them readier to hand, and in greater number too.

Salviati
As the strongest reason of all, everyone produces the argument of heavy bodies: falling from high to low, they come down along a straight line perpendicular to the surface of the Earth — an argument held to be irrefutable proof that the Earth stands still. For if she had the daily rotation, then a tower from whose top a stone was dropped would be carried along by the Earth's spin, and in the time the stone consumed in its fall, the tower would race many hundreds of braccia toward the east — and the stone ought to strike the ground that far from the tower's base. They back this effect up with another experiment: dropping a lead ball from the top of the mast of a ship standing still, and marking where it hits — which is near the foot of the mast; but if the same ball is dropped from the same place while the ship is under way, its impact will land as far from the first mark as the ship has run forward during the time of the lead's fall — and this for no other reason than that the natural motion of the ball, once set free, is in a straight line toward the center of the Earth. They fortify the argument with the experience of a projectile launched upward to a very great height — say, a ball fired from a cannon aimed plumb vertical above the horizon. In its climb and return the ball consumes so much time that, at our latitude, the cannon and we along with it would be carried many miles eastward by the Earth, so that the ball, falling, could never come back near the gun, but would land as far to the west as the Earth had run on ahead. They add, further, a third and very effective experience, which is this: if you fire a culverin — a long cannon — at full range toward the east, and then another, with an equal charge and at the same elevation, toward the west, the westward shot would carry vastly farther than the eastward one. For while the ball travels west and the gun, carried by the Earth, travels east, the ball would strike the ground at a distance from the gun equal to the sum of the two journeys — the one made by the ball itself toward the west, the other by the gun, carried by the Earth, toward the east; and conversely, from the journey of the ball fired eastward you would have to subtract the journey the gun made in following it. Suppose, for example, that the ball's own journey is five miles, and that at that latitude the Earth runs three miles during the ball's flight: in the westward shot, the ball would fall to earth eight miles from the gun — its own five to the west plus the gun's three to the east; but the eastward shot would carry no farther than two miles, which is what remains when you subtract the gun's three miles of travel in the same direction from the shot's five. But experience shows the shots to be equal; therefore the gun stands immobile, and consequently so does the Earth. And no less than these, shots toward the south or the north likewise confirm the Earth's stability: you would never hit the mark you had aimed at, for the target, carried by the Earth toward the east while the ball is in the air, would slide away, and the shots would always land slanting off to the west. Nor is it only shots along the meridian lines: even those fired east or west would fail to fly true — the eastward ones would run high and the westward ones low, whenever you fired point-blank, dead level. For since the ball's journey in both shots is made along the tangent — that is, along a line parallel to the horizon — and since, if the daily rotation belongs to the Earth, the horizon keeps dropping away toward the east and rising in the west (which is why the eastern stars appear to us to climb and the western ones to sink), the eastern target would keep dropping below the line of the shot, so the shot would run high; and the rising of the western target would make the westward shot run low. So you could never fire true in any direction; and since experience says the opposite, one is forced to say the Earth stands still.

Simplicio
Oh, now these are real arguments — arguments to which it is impossible to find any answer worth giving.

Salviati
They come to you as new, perhaps?

Simplicio
Truly they do. And now I see with how many beautiful experiments nature has been kind enough to help us come to knowledge of the truth. Oh, how well one truth chimes with another, and how they all conspire together to make themselves unassailable!

Sagredo
What a pity there was no artillery in Aristotle's day! He'd have stormed ignorance with it, and spoken of the things of this world without the slightest hesitation.

Salviati
I'm very glad these arguments have come to you as new — so that you won't remain of the opinion of most of the Peripatetics, who believe that if anyone departs from Aristotle's doctrine, it must be because he never understood or properly penetrated his demonstrations. But you will certainly hear other novelties yet: you'll hear the followers of the new system produce, against their own case, observations, experiments, and arguments of far greater force than those produced by Aristotle and Ptolemy and the other assailants of the same conclusions; and so you'll come to be certain that it was not out of ignorance or inexperience that they were led to follow this opinion.

Sagredo
This is the moment for me to tell you a few things that have happened to me since I first began hearing this opinion talked about. When I was quite a young man, having only just finished my course of philosophy — which I then set aside, having applied myself to other occupations — it happened that a certain northerner from Rostock, whose name I believe was Christian Wursteisen, a follower of Copernicus's opinion, turned up in these parts and gave two or three lectures on the subject at an academy, with a crowd in attendance — drawn, I think, more by the novelty of the subject than by anything else. I didn't go, having formed the fixed impression that this opinion could be nothing but solemn madness. When I later questioned some who had been there, I heard them all making fun of it — except one, who told me the business was not entirely ridiculous. And since I considered him a thoroughly intelligent and very cautious man, I regretted not having gone; and from that time on, whenever I ran into anyone who held the Copernican opinion, I would ask him whether he had always been of that view. Among all the many I've questioned, I have not found a single one who didn't tell me he had long been of the contrary opinion, and had come over to this one moved by the force of the reasons that argue for it. Then, testing them one by one to see how well they had mastered the reasons of the other side, I found every one of them with those reasons at his fingertips — so I truly cannot say they threw themselves into this opinion out of ignorance, or vanity, or to play, as they say, the clever wit. On the other hand, of all the Peripatetics and Ptolemaics I have asked — and out of curiosity I've asked a good many — what study they had made of Copernicus's book, I found very few who had so much as seen it, and of those I believe actually understood it, none. And among the followers of the Peripatetic doctrine, I've likewise tried to learn whether any of them ever held the other opinion — and likewise found none. Whereupon, considering that everyone who follows Copernicus's opinion first held the contrary one and is perfectly well informed of the reasons of Aristotle and Ptolemy, while on the other hand not one follower of Ptolemy and Aristotle ever formerly held Copernicus's opinion and abandoned it to come over to Aristotle's — considering these things, I say, I began to believe that a man who leaves an opinion drunk in with his mother's milk and followed by countless others, to come over to one held by a bare handful, denied by all the schools, and looking, truly, like an enormous paradox, must of necessity have been moved — not to say forced — by more powerful reasons. And so I have become curious in the extreme to get, as they say, to the bottom of this business; and I count it my great good fortune to have met the two of you, from whom, without any labor of my own, I can hear everything that has been said — and perhaps everything that can be said — on this matter, confident that by the power of your reasonings I shall be pulled out of doubt and set in a state of certainty.

Simplicio
Provided the opinion and the hope don't fail you, and you don't find yourself, at the end, more confused than before.

Sagredo
I feel quite sure that cannot happen in any way whatsoever.

Simplicio
And why not? I am a good witness in my own case: the further we go, the more confused I get.

Sagredo
That is a sign that the reasons which until now seemed conclusive to you, and kept you secure in the truth of your opinion, are beginning to change their look in your mind, and to let you slip little by little — if not all the way over to the contrary opinion, at least into leaning toward it. But I, who am and have been until now indifferent, have great confidence that I shall come to rest and to certainty; and you yourself won't deny it, if you'll hear what persuades me to hope so.

Simplicio
I'll hear it gladly — and I'd be no less pleased if it worked the same effect on me.

Sagredo
Then favor me by answering my questions. First, tell me, Simplicio: isn't the conclusion we are trying to settle precisely this — whether we should hold, with Aristotle and Ptolemy, that the Earth alone stands still at the center of the universe while all the celestial bodies move; or whether, the starry sphere standing still with the Sun at its center, the Earth stands outside it, and the motions that appear to belong to the Sun and the fixed stars are really hers?

Simplicio
These are the conclusions in dispute.

Sagredo
And aren't these two conclusions such that, of necessity, one must be true and the other false?

Simplicio
Just so. We stand in a dilemma, one side of which must of necessity be true and the other false; for between motion and rest, which are contradictories, there is no third possibility — no one can say, "The Earth neither moves nor stands still; the Sun and the stars neither move nor stand still."

Sagredo
The Earth, the Sun, and the stars — what are they in nature? Trifling things, or considerable ones?

Simplicio
They are bodies of the very first rank — most noble, integral parts of the universe, vast, and considerable in the highest degree.

Sagredo
And motion and rest — what sort of conditions are they in nature?

Simplicio
So great and so fundamental that nature herself is defined by them.

Sagredo
So that to move eternally, and to be wholly immobile, are two very considerable conditions in nature, pointing to an enormous difference — above all when attributed to the principal bodies of the universe — from which only the most dissimilar consequences can follow.

Simplicio
Certainly so.

Sagredo
Now answer me on another point. Do you believe that in dialectic, in rhetoric, in physics, in metaphysics, in mathematics — in the whole universe of reasoned argument, in short — there are arguments powerful enough to persuade and demonstrate false conclusions no less than true ones?

Simplicio
No, sir. On the contrary, I hold it firm and certain that for the proof of a true and necessary conclusion there exist in nature not one but many most powerful demonstrations; that one can argue around it and turn it over with thousands upon thousands of cross-checks without ever running into any inconsistency; and that the more some sophist tried to muddy it, the clearer its certainty would always become. And that, on the opposite side, to make a false proposition look true and to persuade people of it, nothing can be produced but fallacies, sophisms, paralogisms, equivocations, and arguments that are empty, incoherent, and riddled with inconsistencies and contradictions.

Sagredo
Now then: if eternal motion and eternal rest are such fundamental conditions in nature, and so different that only the most different consequences can depend on them — above all when applied to the Sun and the Earth, bodies so vast and so eminent in the universe; and if, moreover, it is impossible that one of two contradictory propositions should fail to be true and the other false; and if for proofs of the false one nothing can be produced but fallacies, while the true one can be argued by every kind of conclusive and demonstrative reason — then how can you suppose that whichever of you takes up the defense of the true proposition will fail to persuade me? I would have to be dull of wit, warped in judgment, thick of mind and intellect, and blind in reasoning, not to tell light from darkness, gems from coals, the true from the false.

Simplicio
I tell you, as I have told you before: the greatest master for teaching us to recognize sophisms, paralogisms, and every other fallacy was Aristotle, who in this department can never be deceived.

Sagredo
There you go again with Aristotle, who cannot speak; and I tell you that if Aristotle were here, he would either be persuaded by us, or he would untie our arguments and persuade us with better ones. But come — when you heard the artillery experiments recited, didn't you yourself recognize them, admire them, and confess them more conclusive than Aristotle's? And yet I don't hear Salviati, who produced them and who has surely examined them and sounded them to the bottom with the utmost care, confessing himself persuaded by them — nor even by others of still greater force that he hints he is going to let us hear. And I don't know on what grounds you would scold nature, as though she had grown senile with age and forgotten how to produce speculative minds, and could turn out none anymore except the sort who make themselves Aristotle's slaves, thinking with his brain and sensing with his senses. But let us hear the rest of the arguments favorable to his opinion, so that we can then bring them to the test — refining them in the crucible and weighing them on the assayer's balance.
