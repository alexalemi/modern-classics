The Fourth Day

(Part 4 of 6)

Sagredo
I want, as a final seal on all this, to add one detail I think you don't know, and which also confirms the very same conclusion. You, Salviati, brought up that phenomenon sailors meet with inside the tropics — I mean the perpetual steadiness of the wind that comes to them from the east; and I've had an account of it from someone who has made that voyage many times. What's more (and here's the notable part), I gather the sailors don't call it a wind at all, but by some other name I can't call to mind just now — taken, perhaps, from its being so steady and constant that, once they've fallen in with it, they lash the shrouds and the other sail ropes and, without ever needing to touch them again, can hold their course in safety even while they sleep. Now this perpetual breeze has been recognized for what it is precisely by its blowing on and on without interruption; for if it were broken up by other winds, it would never have been noticed as a single effect distinct from the rest. From which I want to infer that it might well be that our own Mediterranean shares in some such phenomenon, only unobserved, because it is so frequently disturbed by other winds coming on. And I say this not without solid grounds — indeed, with very probable evidence, which came my way from something I had occasion to learn on the voyage I made to Syria, going as consul of our merchant community in Aleppo. It is this: a particular record and register is kept of the days on which ships depart from and arrive in the ports of Alexandria, of Alexandretta, and here at Venice; and in comparing a great many of these against one another — which I did out of my own curiosity — I found that, on average, the returns homeward, that is, the voyages from east toward west across the Mediterranean, are made in less time than the opposite runs, by a good 25 percent. So you can see that, all told, the winds out of the east are stronger than those out of the west.

Salviati
I'm glad to have learned this detail; it brings no small confirmation for the mobility of the Earth. And even though one might say that all the water of the Mediterranean runs perpetually toward the Strait — being water that has to go and discharge into the Ocean the flow of the many rivers that pour into it — I don't believe such a current could be strong enough, by itself alone, to produce so notable a difference. And that's clear enough, too, from the fact that in the Faro — the Strait of Messina — you see the water run back toward the east no less than it runs toward the west.

Sagredo
I, who unlike Simplicio have no spur to satisfy anyone but myself, am content with what has been said on this first part; so, Salviati, whenever it suits you to go on, I'm ready to listen.

Salviati
I'll do as you bid me; but I'd like to hear Simplicio's opinion too, since from his judgment I can gauge how much I might promise myself, regarding these arguments of mine, from the Peripatetic schools — if ever they reached their ears.

Simplicio
I don't want my opinion to carry weight with you, or to serve you as a guess at what others will judge, because — as I've said many times — I'm one of the least in this sort of study, and something will occur to those who have penetrated into the innermost sanctuaries of philosophy that cannot occur to me, who have (as they say) barely greeted it from the doorstep. Still, just to show some sign of life, I'll say that as for the effects you've recounted, and this last one in particular, it seems to me that without the mobility of the Earth one could give a perfectly sufficient account of them by the mobility of the heavens alone, introducing no novelty whatever except the converse of the very thing you yourself put forward. The Peripatetic schools have accepted that the element of fire, and a great part of the air as well, are carried around, following the daily rotation, from east toward west by contact with the concave of the lunar orb, as by the vessel that contains them. Now, without straying from your own footsteps, let's stipulate that the quantity of air taking part in this motion reaches down almost to the tops of the highest mountains, and would reach all the way down to the Earth if the obstacle of those same mountains didn't block it. This matches what you say — for just as you affirm that the air hemmed in by the mountain ridges is carried around by the roughness of the moving Earth, we say the converse: that the whole element of air is carried around by the motion of the heavens, except the part lying below the ridges, which is held back by the roughness of the motionless Earth. And where you said that if that roughness were taken away, the air too would lose being swept along, we can say that with the same roughness removed, all the air would go on with its motion. Hence, because the surfaces of the wide seas are smooth and polished, over them the motion of the breeze carries on, blowing perpetually from the east; and this is felt more in the regions under the equinoctial and within the tropics, where the motion of the heavens is faster. And just as this celestial motion is powerful enough to carry all the free air along with it, so we may very reasonably say that the same motion also acts on the moving water, since water is fluid and not fastened to the immobility of the Earth. And we can affirm this all the more confidently in that, by your own admission, this motion must be very small compared with the cause that produces it — a cause which, circling the whole terrestrial globe in one natural day, covers many hundreds of miles an hour, especially toward the equinoctial, whereas the currents of the open sea run at very few miles an hour. And so the westward voyages come out easy and quick not only thanks to the perpetual easterly breeze, but to the course of the waters as well — a course from which the ebb and flow might perhaps also arise, by way of the different lay of the shores, where the water, going to strike against them, can turn and run back with a contrary motion. Experience shows us as much in the course of rivers: wherever the water, in the unevenness of the banks, meets some part that juts out, or some hollow below, there the water wheels around, and you plainly see it run back the other way. So it seems to me that from the very effects by which you argue for the mobility of the Earth, and which you offer as the cause of them, one can allege a cause that concludes well enough while keeping the Earth at rest and restoring the mobility to the heavens.

Salviati
There's no denying your argument is ingenious and has a good deal of the probable about it — probable, I say, in appearance, but not in fact and reality. It has two parts. In the first, it accounts for the continuous motion of the easterly breeze, and for a like motion in the water; in the second, it tries to draw from the same source the cause of the ebb and flow. The first part has, as I said, some semblance of probability, though far less than what we take from the Earth's motion; the second is not merely improbable but flatly impossible and false. Coming to the first, where it's said that the lunar concave sweeps along the element of fire and all the air up to the tops of the highest mountains, I say, to begin with, that it's doubtful whether there even is an element of fire; but granting there is, there's grave doubt about the sphere of the Moon, as about all the others — whether there really are such solid, enormous bodies, or whether, beyond the air, there extends a continuous expanse of a substance far more tenuous and pure than our air, through which the planets go wandering, as by now a good many of these same philosophers are beginning to hold. But whichever way it is, there's no reason why fire, from the mere contact of a surface you yourself judge to be perfectly polished and smooth, should be carried around throughout its whole depth by a motion foreign to its own natural bent — as has been amply proved, and demonstrated with sound experiments, in The Assayer; setting aside the further improbability of such a motion being transfused from the most subtle fire through the far denser air, and then from the air into the water. But that a body with a rough, mountainous surface, in turning upon itself, carries with it the air right next to it, against which its prominences go striking — that is not merely probable but necessary, and one can actually see it by experiment; though even without seeing it, I don't believe there's any mind that would doubt it. As for the other part: granting that the motion of the heavens did carry the air, and the water too, that motion would still have nothing to do with the ebb and flow. For since from a single, uniform cause nothing can follow but a single, uniform effect, what we ought to observe in the water would be a continuous, uniform course from east toward west — and only in that sea which, closing on itself, encircles the whole globe. But in bounded seas, like the Mediterranean, shut off on the east, there could be no such motion; because if its water could be driven westward by the course of the heavens, it would have been left dry many centuries ago. Besides which, our water doesn't run only westward but comes back the other way, toward the east, and does so on regular schedules. And though you say, with the example of the rivers, that even if the sea's course were originally the single one from east to west, still the different lay of the shores can make part of the water surge back — this I grant you. But you must take note, my dear Simplicio, that wherever the water runs back for that reason, it runs back there perpetually, and wherever it runs straight on, it always runs the same way — that's exactly what the example of the rivers shows you. Whereas in the case of the ebb and flow, you have to find and produce a reason why, in one and the same place, the water should now run one way and now the opposite; and those effects, being contrary and unlike, you'll never be able to deduce from a uniform, constant cause. And the same thing that overthrows this notion — of the sea's motion being contributed by the daily motion of the heavens — also demolishes the notion of anyone who'd want to admit only the daily motion of the Earth and thought that by that alone he could account for the ebb and flow. For since that effect is not uniform, the cause of it absolutely must be non-uniform and changeable.

Simplicio
I've nothing to say in reply — neither of my own, given the weakness of my wit, nor from anyone else, given the novelty of the opinion. But I'd well believe that, once it were put about in the schools, there'd be no shortage of philosophers who'd know how to challenge it.

Sagredo
We'll wait, then, for such an occasion; and meanwhile, if it so please you, Salviati, let's go on ahead.

Salviati
Everything said so far belongs to the daily period of the ebb and flow. First we demonstrated, in general, its primary and universal cause, without which none of this effect would follow at all; then, moving on to the particular incidents — varied, and in a way irregular — that are observed in it, we treated the secondary and attendant causes on which they depend. Now come the other two periods, the monthly and the annual, which bring no new and different incidents beyond those already considered in the daily period, but work upon those same ones by making them greater and smaller at different points of the lunar month and at different seasons of the solar year — almost as if both the Moon and the Sun had a hand in the work and in producing these effects. And that's something my mind refuses utterly; for seeing, as I do, that this business of the seas is a local, tangible motion, worked in an immense mass of water, it can't bring itself to sign on to influences, to mild warmths, to dominances by occult qualities, and to similar empty fancies — which, tantum abest, so far from being, or possibly being, the causes of the tides, that on the contrary the tides are the cause of them: the cause, that is, of their springing up in the sort of brains fitter for chatter and show than for the study and investigation of nature's more hidden works. Such men, rather than bring themselves to utter that wise, honest, modest little word "I don't know," rush to let fall from their mouths — and from their pens too — any great absurdity you please. And the mere fact that this same Moon and this same Sun don't act — not with their light, not with motion, not with heat great or mild — upon smaller bodies of water; that indeed, to make water rise by heat, you'd have to bring it very nearly to a boil; and, in short, that we can't artificially imitate the movements of the tide by any means whatever except by moving the vessel — shouldn't all this convince everyone that the other things put forward as causes of the effect are empty fantasies, wholly alien from the truth? I say, then, that if it's true that one effect has only one primary cause, and that between the cause and the effect there's a firm, constant connection, then it necessarily follows that whenever a firm, constant change is seen in the effect, there's a firm, constant change in the cause. And because the changes that come over the tides at different parts of the year and month have their own firm, constant periods, we're forced to say that a regular change occurs, at those same times, in the primary cause of the tides. Now the change found at those times in the tides consists in nothing but their magnitude — that is, in the waters rising and falling more or less, and running with greater or smaller force. Therefore what is the primary cause of the ebb and flow must, at those determined times, increase or diminish its own strength. But we've already concluded that the primary cause of the tides is the unevenness and non-uniformity in the motion of the vessels holding the water; therefore this non-uniformity must, from time to time, correspondingly grow more uneven — must, that is, become greater and smaller. Now we should recall how this non-uniformity — the varying speed of motion of the vessels, that is, of the parts of the Earth's surface — depends on their moving with the composite motion that results from coupling the two motions, annual and daily, belonging to the whole terrestrial globe. Of these, it's the daily whirl, by now adding to and now subtracting from the annual motion, that produces the non-uniformity in the composite motion; so that in the additions and subtractions the daily whirl makes to the annual motion lies the original cause of the uneven motion of the vessels, and consequently of the ebb and flow. In such a way that, if these additions and subtractions were always made in the same proportion to the annual motion, the cause of the ebb and flow would indeed persist, but so as to act perpetually in the same manner. But we need to find the cause of these same tides being made, at different times, greater and smaller; therefore we must (if we want to keep the cause the same) find some change in these additions and subtractions that makes them more and less powerful in producing the effects that depend on them. And I don't see how such power and weakness could be brought about except by making these same additions and subtractions now greater and now smaller, so that the quickening and slowing of the composite motion happens now in a greater and now in a smaller proportion.

Sagredo
I feel myself being led very gently by the hand; and although I meet no stumbling blocks along the road, still, like a blind man, I don't see where your guidance is taking me, nor can I imagine where this journey is to end.

Salviati
Great as the difference is between my slow philosophizing and your very swift reasoning, still, on this particular point we now have in hand, I won't be surprised that the sharpness of your wit is left clouded by the high, dark fog that hides from us the goal we're walking toward. And my surprise fades when I remember how many hours, how many days — and, more than that, how many nights — I spent on this speculation, and how many times, despairing of ever reaching the end of it, I struggled, to console myself, to believe, like the unhappy Orlando, that what the testimony of so many trustworthy men set before my eyes could not, after all, be true. So don't be surprised if this once, against your custom, you don't foresee the mark; and if you are surprised, I believe the outcome — quite unexpected, as far as I can judge — will put an end to your surprise.

Sagredo
I thank God, then, that He kept such despair from carrying you off to the end the tale gives the wretched Orlando — or to the one, no less fabulously reported, they tell of Aristotle — so that neither I nor anyone else should be left robbed of the discovery of a thing as hidden as it is longed for. I beg you, then, to feed my starving hunger as soon as you can.

Salviati
Here I am, ready to satisfy you. We had got as far as finding out in what way the additions and subtractions of the Earth's whirl upon the annual motion could be made now in a greater and now in a smaller proportion — a variation which, and nothing else, could be assigned as the cause of the monthly and annual changes seen in the magnitude of the tides. Now, I observe that this proportion, between the additions and subtractions of the daily whirl and the annual motion, can be made greater and smaller in three ways. The first is by increasing and diminishing the speed of the annual motion, while keeping the additions and subtractions made by the daily whirl at the same size; for since the annual motion is about three times greater — that is, faster — than the daily motion (taken even at its great circle), if we increase it further, the additions or subtractions of the daily motion will alter it less; whereas, on the contrary, if we make it slower, it will come to be altered in a greater proportion by that same daily motion. It's like adding or taking away four degrees of speed from something moving at twenty degrees: that alters its course less than the same four degrees added or subtracted would alter something moving at only 10 degrees. The second way would be to make the additions and subtractions greater or smaller while keeping the annual motion at the same speed — as easy to grasp as it's plain that a speed of, say, 20 degrees is altered more by adding or subtracting 10 degrees than by adding or subtracting 4. The third way would be when these two are joined together, diminishing the annual motion and increasing the daily additions and subtractions. Up to here, as you see, it hasn't been hard to get; but it was laborious indeed for me to find out in what way this could be brought about in nature. Yet in the end I find that she makes marvelous use of it, and by means almost past imagining — marvelous and unimaginable to us, I mean, but not to her, who works with the utmost ease and simplicity even the things that fill our intellect with boundless wonder; and what is hardest for us to understand is easiest for her to do. Going on further, then, and having shown how the proportion between the additions and subtractions of the whirl and the annual motion can be made greater and smaller in two ways (I say two, because the third is compounded of the first two), I add that nature makes use of both; and, further, I add that if she made use of only one, one of the two periodic changes would have to be taken away. That of the monthly period would cease, if the annual movement were not altered; and if the additions and subtractions of the daily whirl kept themselves continually equal, the changes of the annual period would be lacking.
