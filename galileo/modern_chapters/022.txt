The Second Day

(Part 10 of 20)

Sagredo
For my part, I'm fully satisfied up to this point. I understand perfectly well that anyone who fixes in his imagination this general sharing of the daily rotation among all earthly things — a rotation that naturally belongs to them all, in just the way the old conception supposed that rest around the center belonged to them — will have no trouble at all making out the fallacy and the equivocation that made the arguments brought against us seem conclusive. There's only one scruple I have left, the one I hinted at earlier, about the flight of birds. Birds are living creatures, so they can move themselves at will through a hundred thousand different motions, and can keep themselves aloft, cut off from the Earth, hanging in the air for a long time, and go wandering about with the most disorderly twists and turns. So I don't quite grasp how, in such a great jumble of motions, they don't get confused and lose that first shared motion — and how, once they've been stripped of it, they can make it up and match it again by flying, and keep pace with the towers and the trees that go rushing eastward at such a headlong speed. And I mean truly headlong: on the greatest circle of the globe it comes to a little under a thousand miles an hour, of which I don't believe a swallow's flight makes even fifty.

Salviati
If the birds had to keep pace with the racing trees by the help of their wings, they'd be in a sorry state; and if they were stripped of the universal rotation, they'd be left so far behind, and their course toward the west would look so furious — to anyone who could see them — that it would far outstrip an arrow's. But I don't think we could make them out at all, any more than we can see cannonballs while they streak through the air, driven by the fury of the gunpowder. The truth is that the birds' own motion — I mean their flying — has nothing to do with the universal motion, to which it neither adds help nor gives hindrance. What keeps that motion unaltered in the birds is the very air they go wandering through, which, following the Earth's spin naturally, carries the clouds along with it and likewise carries the birds and everything else hanging in it. So as for keeping up with the Earth, the birds needn't give it a thought — for that service, they could sleep forever.

Sagredo
That the air can carry the clouds along with it — those being materials very easily moved because of their lightness, and stripped of any contrary inclination, or rather materials that themselves share in the conditions and properties of the Earth — that I grasp without any difficulty. But that the birds, which, being living creatures, can move even with a motion contrary to the daily one, should have it restored to them by the air once they've broken it off — that seems to me a bit hard to swallow. Especially since they're solid, heavy bodies; and we, as was said above, see that stones and other heavy bodies stay stubborn against the push of the air, and even when they do let themselves be overcome, they never pick up as much speed as the wind that carries them.

Salviati
Let's not credit moving air with so little force, Sagredo. When it moves quickly it's powerful enough to shift and drive fully loaded ships, to uproot forests and topple towers — and yet in these very violent operations you can't say its motion is anywhere near as fast as that of the daily revolution.

Simplicio
So there it is — moving air can indeed keep up the motion of projectiles, in line with Aristotle's doctrine. And I did think it strange that he should have gone wrong on this particular point.

Salviati
It could, no doubt, if it were able to keep the motion up in itself. But just as, once the wind dies, the ships stop traveling and the trees stop being uprooted, so, since the motion isn't kept up in the air after the stone has left the hand and the arm has stopped, it follows that something other than the air is what makes the projectile move.

Simplicio
And how is it that when the wind stops, the ship's motion stops? On the contrary, you can see that even when the wind has died and the sails have been furled, the vessel keeps running on for whole miles.

Salviati
But that's against you, Simplicio, since once the air is stilled — the air that, striking the sails, was driving the ship — the vessel nevertheless keeps up its course without the help of the medium.

Simplicio
One might say the water was the medium that carried the ship along and kept up its motion.

Salviati
One might indeed say so — to say the exact opposite of the truth. The truth is that the water, with its great resistance to being parted by the body of the vessel, fights it with a great roar, and won't let it take on anything like the speed the wind would give it if the water's obstacle weren't there. You, Simplicio, can never have paid attention to the fury with which the water goes scraping around the boat while it's driven swiftly along by oars or by wind, running through still water. If you'd noticed such an effect, it wouldn't now occur to you to come out with such nonsense. And I'm beginning to suspect that up to now you've been one of the flock of those who, to learn how such matters proceed and to acquire knowledge of nature's effects, don't go out on boats or around crossbows and cannon, but shut themselves up in the study to leaf through the indexes and reference-lists, hunting to see whether Aristotle has said anything about it — and once they've made sure of the true sense of the text, they desire nothing further and reckon there's nothing else worth knowing.

Sagredo
A great happiness, and much to be envied. For if knowledge is naturally desired by all, and if to be is as good as to fancy oneself to be, then they enjoy an enormous good: they can persuade themselves that they understand and know all things — snapping their fingers at those who, knowing that they don't know what they don't know, and consequently seeing that they don't know even the tiniest little particle of what can be known, wear themselves out with late nights and contemplations, and grind themselves down over experiments and observations. But please, let's get back to our birds. On their account you'd said that air moving with tremendous speed could restore to them that part of the daily motion which, amid the play of their flights, they might have lost. To which I object that moving air doesn't seem able to give a solid, heavy body as much speed as it has itself; and since the air's speed is only equal to the Earth's, it didn't seem that the air would be enough to make good the loss in the birds' flight.

Salviati
Your argument has a good deal of the plausible about it in appearance, and to raise a doubt at just the right point is no mark of an ordinary mind. All the same, once you strip away the appearance, I think that in reality it hasn't a hair more force than the others already considered and dissolved.

Sagredo
There's no doubt whatever that when an argument isn't necessarily conclusive, its force can only be absolutely nothing; because when the conclusion holds necessarily in one way only, no reasoning of any weight can be produced for the other side.

Salviati
Your finding more difficulty in this case than in the others seems to me to hinge on the birds' being living creatures, and therefore able to use force at their own pleasure against the primary motion inborn in earthly things — in just the way we see, while they're alive, that they can fly upward too, a motion impossible for them as heavy bodies, whereas when dead they can only fall. And so you reckon that the reasons which hold for all the sorts of projectiles mentioned above can't hold for the birds. That's perfectly true; and because it's true, you don't see those projectiles doing what the birds do, Sagredo. For if from the top of the tower you let a dead bird and a live one drop, the dead one will do just what a stone does — it will first follow the general daily motion, and then the downward motion, as a heavy body. But if the bird let go is alive, what's to stop it — the daily motion always remaining in it — from throwing itself, by beating its wings, toward whatever quarter of the horizon it pleases? And this new motion, being its own particular one and not shared by us, must become perceptible to us. And once it has moved by its flight toward the west, what's to stop it from returning to the tower with an equal beating of feathers? For, in the end, setting off in flight toward the west was nothing but subtracting one single degree from the daily motion, which has, say, ten degrees of speed, leaving it nine while it was flying; and once it settled on the ground, the ten shared degrees came back to it, to which, by flying eastward, it could add one, and with the eleven return to the top of the tower. In sum, if we consider carefully and contemplate more intimately the effects of the birds' flying, they differ in nothing from projectiles moving toward every part of the world, except that these are moved by an external thrower and those by an internal principle.

And here, as a final seal on the nullity of all the experiences brought forward, I think it's the time and place to show the way to test them all with the greatest ease. Shut yourself up with a friend in the main cabin below decks on some large ship, and have with you there some flies, butterflies, and other small flying creatures. Have a big bowl of water with some fish in it; hang up a little bucket that drips water, drop by drop, into another vessel with a narrow neck placed underneath. Then, with the ship standing still, watch carefully how those little flying creatures go with equal speed toward every part of the cabin; the fish will be seen swimming indifferently in all directions; the falling drops will all go into the vessel placed below; and if you throw something to your friend, you won't need to throw it any harder toward one side than the other, when the distances are equal; and if you jump, as they say, with both feet together, you'll cover equal spaces in every direction. Once you've carefully observed all these things — though there's no doubt they must happen this way while the ship stands still — have the ship move with as much speed as you please. So long as the motion is uniform and not rocking this way and that, you won't recognize the slightest change in any of the effects just named, nor from any of them will you be able to tell whether the ship is moving or standing still. In jumping, you'll cover the same distances on the deck as before, and you won't make bigger jumps toward the stern than toward the bow just because the ship is moving very fast — even though, during the time you're in the air, the deck beneath you runs the opposite way to your jump. If you throw something to your companion, you won't need any more force to reach him whether he's toward the bow and you toward the stern or the other way around. The drops will fall as before into the lower vessel, without a single one falling toward the stern, even though, while the drop is in the air, the ship runs on many handbreadths. The fish in their water will swim toward the front of the bowl with no more effort than toward the back, and will come with equal ease to food set on any spot along the bowl's rim. And finally the butterflies and the flies will go on with their flights indifferently toward every part, and it will never happen that they gather toward the wall facing the stern, as though tired out from keeping up with the ship's swift course — from which they've been separated a long while by staying up in the air. And if you burn a little grain of incense so that a bit of smoke rises, you'll see it climb upward and, like a little cloud, hang there, moving no more toward one side than the other. The cause of all this correspondence of effects is that the ship's motion is shared by everything contained in it, and by the air too — which is why I said you should be below decks. If you were up above, in the open air that doesn't follow the ship's course, you'd see more or less noticeable differences in some of the effects named: there's no doubt the smoke would be left behind, along with the air itself; the flies too and the butterflies, hindered by the air, wouldn't be able to follow the ship's motion once they'd separated from it by any considerable distance. But keeping close by, they'd follow the ship without any hindrance or effort, because the ship — being of a rambling, hollowed-out shape — carries along with it part of the air nearest to it; and for a like reason we sometimes see, in riding post, the pestering flies and horseflies following the horses, flying now to this part of their body and now to that. But with the falling drops the difference would be very slight, and in the jumps and in the heavy projectiles altogether imperceptible.

Sagredo
Even though it never occurred to me, while sailing, to make these observations on purpose, still I'm more than sure they'd turn out in the way you've described. In confirmation of which, I remember that a hundred times, being in my cabin, I've wondered whether the ship was moving or standing still; and sometimes, lost in a daydream, I've believed it was going one way when the motion was the opposite. So up to this point I'm satisfied, and entirely convinced of the nullity of all the experiences brought forward to prove the negative rather than the affirmative side of the Earth's rotation. There now remains the objection founded on seeing by experience how a swift whirling has the power to extrude and scatter the materials clinging to the machine that goes spinning around. Because of this it seemed to many — and to Ptolemy too — that if the Earth turned upon itself with such speed, the stones and the animals would have to be flung off toward the stars, and that the buildings couldn't be fastened to their foundations with mortar tough enough to keep them from suffering the same ruin.

Salviati
Before coming to the solution of this objection, I can't keep silent about something I've noticed a thousand times, not without a laugh, coming into the minds of almost all men at the first word they hear of this moving of the Earth. They've believed it so utterly fixed and motionless that not only have they never doubted that rest, but they've firmly believed that all other men, along with themselves, have reckoned it was created immovable and has stayed that way through all the centuries gone by. Settled in this notion, they're then astonished to hear that someone grants it motion — as if, after he had held it motionless, he foolishly supposed that only then, and not before, did it set itself in motion, at the moment when Pythagoras (or whoever it was) first said it moved. Now, that such a foolish thought — I mean the belief that those who admit the Earth's motion first held it fixed from its creation down to Pythagoras's time, and only made it mobile afterward, once Pythagoras reckoned it so — should find a place in the minds of common men of light judgment, doesn't surprise me. But that the Aristotles and the Ptolemies should have fallen into this childishness too seems to me a much stranger and more inexcusable simplicity.

Sagredo
So then, Salviati, you believe that Ptolemy thought he had to maintain, in his disputation, the stability of the Earth against men who, granting that it had been motionless down to Pythagoras's time, only then asserted that it had become mobile, when Pythagoras attributed motion to it?

Salviati
One can't believe otherwise, if we consider carefully the manner he uses in refuting their claim. His refutation consists in the demolition of buildings and the flinging of stones, of animals, and of men themselves toward the sky. And since such a ruin and hurling-off can't be done to buildings and animals that aren't first on the Earth, and men can't be set on the Earth nor buildings raised except while it stands still, it's clear from this that Ptolemy is proceeding against those who, having for some time granted rest to the Earth — that is, while the animals, the stones, and the builders could dwell on it and raise the palaces and the cities — then suddenly make it mobile, to the ruin and destruction of the buildings and the animals, and so on. For if he had undertaken to dispute against someone who attributed such a whirling to the Earth from its first creation, he'd have refuted him by saying that if the Earth had always moved, there could never have been established on it either beasts or men or stones, much less buildings raised or cities founded, and so on.

Simplicio
I don't quite grasp this Aristotelian and Ptolemaic inconsistency.

Salviati
Ptolemy either argues against those who have reckoned the Earth always mobile, or against those who have reckoned that it stood still for some time and then set itself in motion. If against the first, he ought to have said: "The Earth hasn't always moved, because there would never have been men or animals or buildings on the Earth, the terrestrial whirling not allowing them to dwell there." But since, in arguing, he says instead: "The Earth doesn't move, because the beasts, the men, and the buildings already set on the Earth would fall headlong" — he's supposing that the Earth was once found in such a state that it allowed the beasts and the men to dwell there and to build; which carries as a consequence that it was for some time at rest, that is, fit for the dwelling of animals and the raising of buildings. Are you now clear on what I've been trying to say?

Simplicio
I am and I'm not. But this matters little to the merit of the case, nor can a little slip of Ptolemy's, committed through inadvertence, be enough to move the Earth when it's motionless. But leaving the jokes aside, let's come at last to the nerve of the argument, which seems to me insoluble.

Salviati
And I, Simplicio, want to knot it up and tighten it still further, by showing even more palpably how it's true that heavy bodies, whirled with speed around a stable center, acquire an impetus to move away from that center, even when they have a natural propensity to go toward it. Tie a little bucket to the end of a cord, with water in it, and holding the other end firmly in your hand, making the cord and your arm the radius and the joint of your shoulder the center, swing the vessel quickly around so that it describes the circumference of a circle. Whether that circle is parallel to the horizon, or upright to it, or inclined in any way you please, in every case it will follow that the water doesn't spill out of the vessel; in fact, the one swinging it will always feel the cord pull and strain to get farther from the shoulder. And if you make a hole in the bottom of the bucket, you'll see the water spurt out no less toward the sky than sideways and toward the ground. And if instead of water you put in little pebbles, swinging them the same way, you'll feel them make the same force against the cord. And finally, we see children throw stones to a great distance by swinging around a piece of cane with the stone lodged in its tip — all arguments for the truth of the conclusion, namely that whirling confers on the moving body an impetus toward the circumference when the motion is fast; and therefore, if the Earth turned upon itself, the motion of its surface — especially toward the greatest circle, being incomparably faster than the ones just named — would have to extrude everything toward the sky.
