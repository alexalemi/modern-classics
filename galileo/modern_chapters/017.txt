The Second Day

(Part 5 of 20)

Sagredo
For Simplicio's sake I'd like to defend Aristotle, if it can be done — or at least come away with a better grasp of the force of your inference. You say: seeing the stone graze the tower isn't enough to assure us that its motion is vertical — which is the middle term of the syllogism — unless one assumes the Earth stands still, which is the very conclusion to be proved; because if the tower were moving along with the Earth while the stone grazed it, the stone's motion would be slanting, not vertical. But I'll answer this way: if the tower were moving, it would be impossible for the stone to fall grazing it; and therefore from its falling along the tower one infers the stability of the Earth.

Simplicio
Just so. For the stone to come down grazing the tower while the tower was being carried along by the Earth, the stone would have to have two natural motions — the straight one toward the center and the circular one around the center — and that is impossible.

Salviati
So Aristotle's defense comes down to this: that it is impossible — or at least that he judged it impossible — for the stone to move with a motion mixed of straight and circular. For if he had not held it impossible for the stone to move toward the center and around the center at the same time, he would have understood that the falling stone could come down grazing the tower whether the tower was moving or standing still, and consequently he would have realized that from this grazing nothing whatever can be inferred about the motion or rest of the Earth. But that in no way excuses Aristotle — not only because, if he had had such a notion, he ought to have said so, it being so central a point in his argument, but even more because it cannot be said either that the effect is impossible or that Aristotle judged it impossible. The first cannot be said, because in a moment I'll show that it is not merely possible but necessary; nor can the second, because Aristotle himself grants that fire goes upward naturally in a straight line, and also moves in a circle with the daily rotation, which the heavens share with the whole element of fire and with the greater part of the air. If, then, he doesn't hold it impossible to mix the straight-upward motion with the circular one communicated to fire and air by the hollow underside of the Moon's sphere, much less should he count it impossible to mix the stone's straight-downward motion with a circular motion that was natural to the whole terrestrial globe, of which the stone is a part.

Simplicio
That doesn't look right to me. If the element of fire goes around together with the air, it is a very easy — indeed a necessary — thing that a little particle of fire, climbing up from the Earth, should receive that same movement as it passes through the moving air, being a body so thin and light and so very easily moved. But that a very heavy stone, or a cannonball dropping down from on high and already left to its own devices, should let itself be carried along by air or anything else — that is utterly unthinkable. Besides, there is that experiment so exactly to the point: the stone dropped from the top of a ship's mast, which falls at the foot of the mast while the ship stands still, but when the ship is under way falls as far from that same spot as the ship has run ahead during the time of the stone's fall — and that is no few braccia, when the ship's course is swift.

Salviati
There's a great disparity between the case of the ship and that of the Earth, if the terrestrial globe has the daily rotation. For it's perfectly obvious that the ship's motion, just as it is not natural to her, is likewise incidental to everything aboard her; so it's no wonder that the stone which was held at the masthead, once set free, comes down without any obligation to follow the ship's motion. But the daily rotation is attributed to the terrestrial globe as its own natural motion, and consequently to all its parts; and being stamped in them by nature, it is indelible in them. So the stone at the top of the tower has, as its primary instinct, the going around the center of its whole once in twenty-four hours, and this natural tendency it exercises eternally, whatever state you put it in. To be persuaded of this you need only revise one old impression stamped on your mind, and say: "Just as until now — having held it a property of the terrestrial globe to stand motionless about its center — I have never had the least difficulty or reluctance in grasping that each of its particles likewise remains naturally in that same rest, so it stands to reason that if it were the natural instinct of the terrestrial globe to go around in twenty-four hours, then every one of its parts would also have an intrinsic and natural inclination not to stand still, but to follow that same course." And so, without running into any awkwardness at all, one may conclude that since the motion conferred on the ship by the force of the oars — and through her on everything in her — is not natural but foreign, it's only right that the stone, once separated from the ship, should return to its natural state and go back to exercising its pure and simple natural tendency. Add to this that at least the part of the air that lies below the highest mountaintops must be seized and carried around by the roughness of the Earth's surface — or else, mixed as it is with so many earthly vapors and exhalations, must naturally follow the daily rotation; and none of that is true of the air around the ship driven by the oars. So arguing from the ship to the tower carries no inferential force: the stone that falls from the masthead enters a medium that does not share the ship's motion, but the one that leaves the top of the tower finds itself in a medium that has the same motion as the whole terrestrial globe — so that, far from being hindered by the air, it is rather helped by the air's motion, and can follow the universal course of the Earth.

Simplicio
I still can't see how the air could impress upon an enormous stone, or a great ball of iron or lead weighing, say, two hundred pounds, the motion with which it moves itself — a motion it may well communicate to feathers, snow, and other featherweight things. In fact I observe that a weight of that sort, exposed to the most furious wind you please, isn't shifted from its place by so much as a finger's breadth. Now imagine whether the air will carry it along.

Salviati
There's a great disparity between your experience and our case. You have the wind come upon a stone at rest; we are placing into air that is already moving a stone that is moving too, and at the same speed — so the air doesn't have to confer any new motion on it, but only maintain, or rather not impede, the motion it has already received. You want to drive the stone with a motion foreign to it and outside its nature; we want to conserve it in its natural one. If you wanted to produce a better-fitted experiment, you should have said: let someone observe, if not with the eye in his forehead then at least with the mind's eye, what would happen if an eagle, carried on the rush of the wind, let a stone drop from its talons. Since that stone was already flying with the wind when it left the claws, and after leaving them enters a medium moving at the same speed, I strongly suspect it would not be seen to drop straight down, but rather, following the wind's course and adding to it the pull of its own weight, would move on a slant.

Simplicio
One would have to be able actually to perform such an experiment, and then judge by the outcome. Meanwhile the result on the ship has, so far, come out applauding our opinion.

Salviati
"So far" — well said; for perhaps in a moment things will change their look. And so as not to keep you dangling any longer, as they say, tell me, Simplicio: do you honestly feel that the ship experiment fits our purpose so well that we may reasonably believe that whatever is seen to happen on the ship must also happen on the terrestrial globe?

Simplicio
So far, yes; and though you've brought up a few small discrepancies, they don't seem to me weighty enough to shake my opinion.

Salviati
On the contrary, I want you to keep holding it — to hold firm that the effect on the Earth must answer to the one on the ship — provided that, when that turns out to damage your case, you don't take a fancy to change your mind. You say: "Since, when the ship stands still, the stone falls at the foot of the mast, and when she is under way it falls far from the foot, then conversely, from the stone's falling at the foot one infers that the ship stands still, and from its falling far away one argues that the ship is moving; and since what holds for the ship must equally hold for the Earth, from the stone's falling at the foot of the tower one infers of necessity the immobility of the terrestrial globe." Isn't that your argument?

Simplicio
That's it exactly, boiled down to a brevity that makes it very easy to grasp.

Salviati
Now tell me: if the stone dropped from the masthead while the ship was running at great speed were to fall in precisely the same spot on the ship where it falls when she stands still, what service would those falls render you toward determining whether the vessel is standing still or under way?

Simplicio
Absolutely none — just as from the beating of the pulse, for instance, you can't tell whether someone is asleep or awake, since the pulse beats the same way in sleepers as in the waking.

Salviati
Very good. Now, have you ever performed the ship experiment?

Simplicio
I haven't; but I well believe that the authors who cite it have observed it carefully. Besides, the cause of the disparity is so plainly understood that it leaves no room for doubt.

Salviati
That those authors may cite it without ever having performed it, you yourself are a fine witness: without having performed it, you offer it as certain and take it on good faith at their word — just as it is not only possible but necessary that they did the same, I mean relied on their own predecessors, without anyone ever reaching back to someone who had actually done it. For whoever does perform it will find that the experiment shows the exact opposite of what is written: it will show that the stone always falls in the same place on the ship, whether she stands still or moves at any speed you please. And since the same reasoning holds for the Earth as for the ship, from the stone's falling always plumb at the foot of the tower nothing can be inferred about the motion or rest of the Earth.

Simplicio
If you refer me to any means other than experience, I truly believe our disputes won't be finished in a hurry; for this strikes me as a thing so remote from all human reasoning that it leaves not the smallest room for belief or even probability.

Salviati
And yet it has found room in me.

Simplicio
So you haven't made a hundred trials of it — no, not even one — and yet you assert it so freely as certain? I go straight back to my incredulity, and to my conviction that the experiment was performed by the principal authors who make use of it, and that it shows what they affirm.

Salviati
Even without the experiment I am certain the effect will follow as I tell you, because it must follow; and I'll add further that you yourself already know it cannot follow otherwise — however much you feign, or pretend to feign, not to know it. But I'm a good enough breaker-in of minds that I'll wring the confession out of you by sheer force. — But Sagredo sits there very quiet; I thought I saw you stir just now, as if to say something.

Sagredo
I did in fact mean to say something or other; but the curiosity stirred in me by hearing you threaten such violence against Simplicio — forcing him to disclose the knowledge he wants to keep hidden from us — has driven out every other wish. So I beg you to make good the boast.

Salviati
Provided Simplicio is willing to answer my questions, I won't fail to.

Simplicio
I'll answer what I know, confident it will give me little trouble; for about the things I hold to be false, I don't believe I can know anything at all — since knowledge is of truths, not of falsehoods.

Salviati
I don't want you to say or answer anything except what you know for certain. So tell me: suppose you had a flat surface, polished like a mirror and made of a material hard as steel, and not parallel to the horizon but somewhat tilted; and suppose that on it you set a perfectly spherical ball of some heavy, very hard material — bronze, say. Left to itself, what do you think it would do? Don't you believe — as I do — that it would stay still?

Simplicio
If that surface were tilted?

Salviati
Yes — that's what I supposed.

Simplicio
I don't believe it would stay put at all; on the contrary, I'm certain it would move down the slope of its own accord.

Salviati
Careful what you say, Simplicio, because I'm certain it would stay still wherever you set it down.

Simplicio
As long as you help yourself to suppositions of that sort, Salviati, I'll stop being surprised that you arrive at utterly false conclusions.

Salviati
So you're absolutely sure it would move down the slope of its own accord?

Simplicio
What doubt is there?

Salviati
And you hold this firmly not because I taught it to you — I was trying to persuade you of the opposite — but by yourself, by your own natural judgment.

Simplicio
Now I see your game: you said that to test me — to trip me up, as the common phrase has it — not because you really believed it.

Salviati
Just so. And how long would that ball keep moving, and how fast? Bear in mind that I specified a perfectly round ball and an exquisitely polished plane, precisely to remove all external and accidental impediments. In the same way, I want you to set aside the impediment of the air — its resistance to being parted — and any other incidental obstacles there might be.

Simplicio
I've understood the whole thing perfectly well. To your question I answer that it would continue moving indefinitely, as long as the slope of the plane lasted, and with a continually accelerating motion — for such is the nature of heavy moving bodies, that vires acquirant eundo, they gather strength as they go. And the steeper the slope, the greater the speed.

Salviati
But if someone wanted the ball to move upward along that same surface, do you think it would go?

Simplicio
Of its own accord, no; but dragged up, or hurled up by force, yes.

Salviati
And if it were driven by some impetus violently impressed on it, what sort of motion would it have, and how great?

Simplicio
The motion would keep languishing and slowing, being against nature, and it would last a longer or shorter time according to the greater or lesser impulse and the greater or lesser steepness of the climb.

Salviati
So far, then, you've explained to me how a moving body behaves on two different planes: on the downward slope the heavy body descends of its own accord, continually gaining speed, and force is needed to hold it at rest; on the upward slope force is needed to push it, and even to stop it, and the motion impressed on it keeps dwindling until at last it dies away entirely. You say further that in both cases a difference arises from the slope being steeper or gentler, so that a steeper descent brings greater speed, while conversely, on a rising plane, the same body driven by the same force travels a greater distance the smaller the elevation. Now tell me: what would happen to that same body on a surface that sloped neither up nor down?

Simplicio
Here I need to think a moment about my answer. With no downward slope there can be no natural inclination to motion, and with no upward slope there can be no resistance to being moved; so the body would stand indifferent between propensity and resistance to motion. It seems to me, then, that it ought naturally to stay put. But I'm being forgetful — it wasn't long ago that Sagredo gave me to understand that this is what would happen.

Salviati
So I believe, provided someone set it down at rest. But if it were given an impetus toward some direction, what would follow?

Simplicio
It would follow that it moves in that direction.

Salviati
But with what sort of motion? Continually accelerated, as on the downward slope, or progressively retarded, as on the upward one?

Simplicio
I can see no cause for acceleration or for retardation, there being neither descent nor climb.

Salviati
Right. But if there is no cause for retardation, still less should there be any for coming to rest. How long, then, would you have the body keep moving?

Simplicio
As long as the length of that surface — neither rising nor falling — lasted.

Salviati
Then if such a space were unbounded, the motion in it would likewise be without bound — that is, perpetual?

Simplicio
It seems so to me, provided the body were of material durable enough to last.

Salviati
That has already been assumed, since we said that all accidental and external impediments were to be removed — and the brittleness of the moving body is, in this business, one of the accidental impediments. Now tell me: what do you take to be the cause of that ball's moving of its own accord on the downward slope, and only under violence on the rising one?

Simplicio
Because the inclination of heavy bodies is to move toward the center of the Earth, and only by violence upward toward the circumference; and the sloping surface is the one that gains nearness to the center, while the rising one leads away from it.

Salviati
Then a surface that was to be neither downhill nor uphill would have to be, in all its parts, equally distant from the center. Is there any such surface anywhere in the world?

Simplicio
There's no lack of them: there is the surface of our terrestrial globe, if only it were quite smooth, and not, as it actually is, rough and mountainous; and there is the surface of the water, when it lies placid and tranquil.

Salviati
Then a ship moving across a calm sea is one of those bodies running along a surface that is neither downhill nor uphill, and is therefore disposed — if every accidental and external obstacle were removed — to move, with the impulse once received, incessantly and uniformly.

Simplicio
It seems it must be so.

Salviati
And the stone at the top of the mast — doesn't it too move, carried by the ship, along the circumference of a circle about the center, and consequently with a motion that is indelible in it, once the external impediments are removed? And isn't this motion as fast as the ship's?

Simplicio
So far everything runs smoothly. But the rest?

Salviati
Then draw the final consequence yourself, and welcome — if by yourself you've known all the premises.

Simplicio
You mean, as the final conclusion, that the stone, moving with a motion indelibly impressed on it, is not about to abandon the ship but to follow her, and in the end to fall in the very same place where it falls when the ship stands still. And I too say this would follow, if there were no external impediments to disturb the stone's motion once it is set free. But there are two such impediments: one is that the moving body is powerless to cleave the air by its own impetus alone, once it has lost the impetus of the oars' force, which it shared in, as a part of the ship, while it was up on the mast; the other is the new motion of falling downward, which must surely be an impediment to the forward one.

Salviati
As for the impediment of the air, I don't deny it to you; and if the falling body were of light material — a feather, or a tuft of wool — the retardation would be very great; but in a heavy stone it is very small indeed. You yourself said a moment ago that the force of the most furious wind doesn't suffice to budge a big stone from its place; now imagine what the still air will do when met by a stone moving no faster than the whole ship. Nevertheless, as I said, I concede you this small effect that may depend on such an impediment — just as I know you will concede to me that if the air were moving at the same speed as the ship and the stone, the impediment would be absolutely nil. As for the other point, the supervening downward motion: first of all, it is obvious that these two motions — the circular around the center and the straight toward the center — are not contraries, are not destructive of one another, are not incompatible; for on the side of the moving body there is no repugnance at all to such motion. You yourself have already granted that its repugnance is against the motion that carries it away from the center, and its inclination is toward the motion that brings it nearer the center; from which it follows necessarily that toward a motion which neither approaches nor recedes from the center, the body has neither repugnance nor propensity — nor, in consequence, any cause for the faculty impressed on it to be diminished. And since the moving cause is not a single one, which might be weakened by the new operation, but two distinct ones — of which gravity attends solely to drawing the body toward the center, while the impressed force attends to carrying it around the center — there remains no occasion whatever for impediment.
