The First Day

(Part 9 of 12)

Simplicio
I'm afraid there's some sleight of hand at work here. I do see, when I look at that mirror, a great blaze of light coming off it that nearly blinds me; and — what matters more — I see it from every spot I look at it from, and I see it shift position across the mirror's surface as I move to view it from this place or that. Which is a conclusive sign that the light is reflected vividly toward every side, and so strikes that whole wall as powerfully as it strikes my eye.

Salviati
Now you see how careful and reserved we need to be about granting our assent to what reasoning alone sets before us. No doubt what you say has plenty of surface plausibility; and yet you can see how the experience of the senses shows the contrary.

Simplicio
Then how does this business work?

Salviati
I'll tell you what I make of it, though I don't know how far it will satisfy you. First: that brilliant splash of light you see on the mirror, which seems to you to take up a good part of it, isn't anywhere near that large — in fact it's very, very small. But its intensity produces in your eye — by way of the reflection made in the moisture at the rims of your eyelids, which spreads across the pupil — a borrowed radiance, like the fringe of fine hairs we seem to see around the flame of a candle set some distance away; or, if you prefer, compare it to the adventitious sparkle of a star. If you set the tiny little body of, say, the Dog Star, seen by day through the telescope, when it shows without any radiance, beside the same star seen at night by the naked eye, you'll grasp beyond any doubt that with its rays it looks more than a thousand times bigger than the bare, real little body. And the Sun's image that you see in that mirror undergoes a similar enlargement, or a greater one — greater, I say, because it's more vivid than the star, as is obvious from the fact that you can stare at the star with far less injury to your eyes than at this reflection from the mirror. So the reverberation that has to be shared out over this whole wall comes from a small part of that mirror; whereas a moment ago, what came from the whole flat mirror was delivered to — and confined within — a very small part of the same wall. What wonder, then, that the first reflection illuminates very vividly, while this other remains almost imperceptible?

Simplicio
I find myself more tangled up than ever, and now the other difficulty comes over me: how can it be that that wall, made of such dark material, with a surface so badly polished, throws back light more powerful and vivid than a mirror that's been beautifully smoothed and cleaned?

Salviati
More vivid, no — but more universal, yes. As for intensity, you can see for yourself: the reflection from that little flat mirror, where it strikes over there under the loggia, illuminates powerfully; and the rest of the wall, which receives the reflection from the wall the mirror hangs on, is nowhere near as brightly lit as the small patch the mirror's reflection lands on. And if you want to understand the whole of this business, consider that the roughness of that wall's surface amounts to the same thing as its being composed of countless tiny little surfaces, set at countless different inclinations — among which, of necessity, many happen to be placed so as to send the rays they reflect to one particular spot, and many others to another; in short, there is no place that doesn't receive a great many rays, reflected from a great many little surfaces scattered over the entire face of the rough body the luminous rays fall on. From which it necessarily follows that reflected rays — and with them illumination — reach every part of any surface whatever that faces the one receiving the primary incident rays. It follows, too, that the body the illuminating rays fall on shows itself wholly lit and bright when viewed from anywhere at all. And that is why the Moon, having a rough rather than a polished surface, sends the Sun's light back in every direction and shows equally bright to every observer. Whereas if its surface, being spherical, were also as smooth as a mirror, it would be completely invisible — since that very tiny part of it that could reflect the Sun's image to the eye of any one person would, across so great a distance, remain invisible, as we've already said.

Simplicio
I follow your reasoning quite well; still, I think I can dispose of it with very little effort, and maintain perfectly well that the Moon is round and highly polished and reflects the Sun's light to us the way a mirror does — nor does it follow that the Sun's image must be seen in the middle of it. For "it is not by the image of the Sun itself that the Sun's small figure can be made out at so great a distance; rather, what we perceive is the illumination of the whole lunar body, produced by the Sun's light. We may see the like in a gilded and well-burnished plate, which, when struck by a luminous body, shows itself all resplendent to one who views it from afar; only from close up does one make out, at its center, the small image of the luminous body."

Salviati
Frankly confessing my own incapacity, I'll say that of this speech of yours I understand nothing except the part about the gilded plate. And if you'll permit me to speak freely, I strongly suspect you don't understand it either, but memorized those words — written by somebody out of the itch to contradict and to show himself cleverer than his opponent; to show himself, that is, to the sort of people who, wanting to look clever themselves, applaud what they don't understand, and think the more highly of a writer the less they understand him. Provided the writer himself isn't (as many are) one of those who write what they themselves don't understand — which is why what they write is understood by nobody. So, leaving the rest aside, here is my answer about the gilded plate. If it's flat and not very large, it can indeed appear all ablaze from a distance while a strong light strikes it — but only when the eye lies on one determinate line, namely the line of the reflected rays; and it will look more fiery than if it were, say, silver, because it's colored, and because the extreme density of the metal lets it take an absolutely perfect polish. And if its surface, though beautifully polished, were not exactly flat but carried various inclinations, then its blaze would be visible from more positions — from as many, that is, as the different reflections made by the different surfaces reached. That's why diamonds are cut with many facets: so their delightful sparkle can be seen from many places. But if the plate were very large, it would not show all ablaze from a distance even if it were perfectly flat. Let me put it more precisely: imagine a huge flat gilded plate set out in the Sun. To a distant eye, the Sun's image will appear to occupy only one part of that plate — the part the reflection of the incident solar rays comes from. It's true that, because of the light's intensity, the image will appear garlanded with a great many rays, and so will seem to occupy a considerably larger portion of the plate than it really does. And to test that this is true: note the particular spot on the plate the reflection comes from, mark out likewise how large the blazing patch looks to you, then cover the greater part of that space, leaving only the region around its middle exposed. The apparent blaze won't shrink one bit for the distant viewer — on the contrary, you'll see it spread wide over the cloth or whatever else you covered it with. So if anyone, from seeing a small gilded plate all ablaze from a distance, has convinced himself the same must happen with plates as big as the Moon, he has fooled himself no less than if he believed the Moon to be no bigger than the bottom of a wine vat. And if the plate's surface were spherical, the powerful reflection would be seen in only a single small patch of it — though, thanks to its intensity, that patch would indeed show wreathed in many quivering rays; the rest of the ball would appear merely tinted, and even that only if it were not polished to the highest degree — for if it were perfectly burnished, it would appear dark. We have an example of this before our eyes every day in silver vessels: so long as they've only been boiled in the whitening bath, they're all white as snow and return no images at all; but burnish any part of them, and that part immediately turns dark, and from it images come back as from a mirror. That darkening comes from nothing else than the flattening of the very fine grain that made the silver's surface rough — rough in just the way that reflected light toward every side, so that from every position it showed equally illuminated. When burnishing then planes those minute inequalities exquisitely smooth, so that the reflection of the incident rays is all directed to one determinate place, the burnished part shows, from that place, far brighter and shinier than the rest, which is merely whitened; but from every other position it looks quite dark. And it's well known that the change of viewpoint in looking at burnished surfaces produces such differences of appearance that painters, to imitate a burnished breastplate, say, have to set pure blacks and pure whites side by side on parts of the armor where the light falls equally.

Sagredo
So then: if these philosopher gentlemen would content themselves with granting that the Moon, Venus, and the other planets have surfaces not quite so glossy and polished as a mirror — a hair's-breadth less, that is: like a silver plate that has been whitened but not burnished — would that be enough to make it visible, and fit to bounce the Sun's light back to us?

Salviati
It would be enough in part; but it wouldn't give a light as powerful as it does by being mountainous — full, in short, of great heights and hollows. But these philosopher gentlemen will never grant it to be less polished than a mirror; they'll have it far more so, if more can be imagined, because, holding that the most perfect shapes belong to the most perfect bodies, they need the sphericity of those celestial globes to be absolute. Besides, if they granted me any unevenness at all, however tiny, I'd help myself without the least scruple to a much bigger one — for since a perfection of that kind consists in indivisibles, a hair spoils it as thoroughly as a mountain does.

Sagredo
Here two questions occur to me: one is to understand why a greater unevenness of surface should make a more powerful reflection of light; the other, why these Peripatetic gentlemen insist on this exactness of shape.

Salviati
I'll answer the first, and leave to Simplicio the job of answering the second. You should note, then, that the same surfaces are more or less illuminated by the same light according as the illuminating rays fall on them more or less obliquely; the greatest illumination is where the rays are perpendicular. And look — I'll show it to your senses. I bend this sheet of paper so that one part makes an angle with the other; and holding it up to the light reflected from that wall opposite, you see how this face, which receives the rays obliquely, is less bright than this other, where the reflection arrives at right angles; and notice how, as I turn it to catch them more and more obliquely, the illumination grows weaker.

Sagredo
I see the effect, but I don't grasp the cause.

Salviati
If you thought about it for a hundredth of an hour, you'd find it; but so as not to waste time, here's a quick demonstration of it in this figure.

Sagredo
The mere sight of the figure has cleared the whole thing up for me — go on.

Simplicio
Kindly spell out the rest for me; I'm not so quick on the uptake.

Salviati
Take all the parallel lines you see setting out from the points A and B to be the rays that arrive at the line CD at right angles. Now tilt CD so that it leans like DO: don't you see that a good number of the rays that were striking CD now pass by without touching DO? So if DO is lit by fewer rays, it stands to reason that the light it receives is weaker. Now back to the Moon. Being spherical in shape, if its surface were as smooth as this paper, the parts of its Sun-lit hemisphere toward the rim would receive vastly less light than the parts in the middle, since the rays fall on the former at the most extreme slant and on the latter at right angles. So at full Moon, when we see nearly the whole hemisphere illuminated, the parts toward the middle ought to show more brilliant to us than the parts toward the circumference — which is not what we see. Now picture the Moon's face full of good high mountains: don't you see how their slopes and ridges, rising above the curve of the perfect spherical surface, stand exposed to the Sun's view, placed to receive its rays far less obliquely — and so to show as brightly lit as the rest?

Sagredo
All well and good. But if such mountains are there, then it's true the Sun will strike them far more squarely than it would the slant of a smooth surface; but it's also true that all the valleys between those mountains would stay dark, because of the enormous shadows the mountains would cast at that time; whereas the middle regions, though full of valleys and mountains, would be free of shadow, the Sun standing high above them — and so a great deal brighter than the outer parts, which would be sown with as much shadow as light. And yet we see no such difference.

Simplicio
A similar difficulty was winding its way through my own imagination.

Salviati
How much readier Simplicio is at penetrating the difficulties that favor Aristotle's opinions than at penetrating their solutions! And I have a suspicion that at times he deliberately keeps quiet about those, too; in the present case, since he was able to spot the objection on his own — and it's a genuinely ingenious one — I can't believe he hasn't also noticed the answer. So I'll try to draw it out of his mouth, as they say. Tell me, then, Simplicio: do you believe there can be shadow anywhere the Sun's rays strike?

Simplicio
I believe — no, I'm certain — that there cannot; because the Sun, being the greatest of luminaries, driving out the darkness with its rays, it's impossible that any place it reaches should stay dark. Besides, we have the definition: tenebrae sunt privatio luminis — darkness is the absence of light.

Salviati
So the Sun, gazing at the Earth or the Moon or any other opaque body, never sees any of their shadowed parts — since it has no other eyes to see with than its own rays, the bearers of light. And consequently someone standing on the Sun would never see anything in shadow at all, because his visual rays would always travel in company with the Sun's illuminating rays.

Simplicio
Perfectly true — no contradiction possible.

Salviati
Now: when the Moon stands at opposition to the Sun, what difference is there between the path traveled by the rays of your sight and the path traveled by the rays of the Sun?

Simplicio
Now I've got it. You mean that since the rays of sight and the rays of the Sun travel along the same lines, we can't make out any of the Moon's shadowed valleys. And please — rid yourself of this notion that I'm a faker or a dissembler. I swear to you on my honor as a gentleman that I had not penetrated to that answer, and might never have found it without your help, or without long thinking.

Sagredo
The solution the two of you have produced between you for this last difficulty has genuinely satisfied me as well. But at the same time, this point about the rays of sight traveling with the rays of the Sun has stirred up another scruple in me about the earlier part — though I don't know whether I'll manage to explain it, because it's only just been born and I haven't yet got it into order to my own liking. But let's see, among us, if we can bring it into the clear. There's no doubt at all that the parts toward the rim of a smooth but unburnished hemisphere lit by the Sun, receiving the rays obliquely, receive far fewer of them than the middle parts, which receive them head-on. It may well be that a band, say, twenty degrees wide out toward the edge of the hemisphere receives no more rays than another band toward the middle only four degrees wide; so the first will certainly be much darker than the second, and will look so to anyone who views them both face-on — full-face, as we say. But if the observer's eye were positioned so that the twenty-degree width of the dark band presented itself looking no longer than a four-degree band set at the middle of the hemisphere, then I don't hold it impossible that it might show him just as bright and luminous as the other — because, in the end, within two equal angles of four degrees each, the eye receives the reflections of two equal multitudes of rays: those reflected from the middle band four degrees wide, and those reflected from the other band of twenty degrees but seen foreshortened, compressed into the span of four. And the eye will get itself such a position when it stands between that hemisphere and the body illuminating it, because then the sight and the rays travel along the same lines. So it doesn't seem impossible that the Moon should have a perfectly even surface and nonetheless, at full Moon, show itself no less luminous at the rim than in the middle.

Salviati
The doubt is an ingenious one and deserves consideration; and since it came to you just now, unrehearsed, I'll likewise answer with whatever falls into my mind unrehearsed — and it may well be that with more thought a better answer would occur to me. But before I bring anything else into it, we'd do well to make sure by experiment that your objection holds up in fact the way it seems to conclude in appearance. So, taking the same sheet of paper again and bending a small part of it so that it tilts against the rest, let's test whether, when we hold it to the light so that the rays fall directly on the smaller part and obliquely on the other, the part receiving the direct rays shows brighter. And there — the experiment already settles it: it's noticeably more luminous. Now, if your objection is conclusive, then when we lower the eye far enough that, viewing that other, larger, less illuminated part foreshortened, it appears no wider than the brighter part — so that we see it under no greater an angle — then, I say, its light will have to increase enough to look as brilliant to us as the other. There: I'm looking at it, viewing it so obliquely that it appears narrower than the other; and yet its darkness doesn't lighten for me in the least. See now whether the same happens for you.

Sagredo
I've looked; and however far I lower my eye, I don't see that surface pick up any light or brighten in the slightest — if anything, it seems to me to darken.

Salviati
So far, then, we can be sure the objection has no force. As for the solution: I believe that since this paper's surface falls only a little short of smooth, very few rays get reflected back toward the incident ones, compared with the multitude reflected toward the opposite side; and of those few, more and more are lost the closer the visual rays draw to the incident luminous rays. And since it's not the incident rays but the rays reflected to the eye that make an object look luminous, in lowering the eye you lose more than you gain — just as you said yourself, seeing the sheet grow darker.
