The Third Day

(Part 16 of 16)

Sagredo
I follow you perfectly, and it reminds me of an argument very much like this one in its emptiness, put forward by certain writers of textbooks on the sphere — among them, if I remember rightly, Sacrobosco. To prove that the element of water shapes itself, together with the Earth, into a spherical surface, so that the two of them make up this globe of ours, he writes that we have conclusive proof of it in seeing the tiniest particles of water take on a round shape — as we can see every day in droplets of dew, and on the leaves of many plants. And since, by the well-worn axiom "The same reasoning holds for the whole as for the parts," the parts crave that shape, it must be that the same shape belongs to the whole element. And really it seems to me quite an unseemly thing that men like this don't notice a bit of foolishness that's all too plain to see: they fail to consider that, if their reasoning were sound, it wouldn't just be the tiny drops, but any larger quantity of water whatsoever, once separated from the whole element, that would draw itself up into a ball — which is not at all what we see. What we can see with our senses, and grasp with our minds, is this: the element of water, loving to shape itself into a sphere around the common center of gravity toward which all heavy bodies tend (which is the center of the terrestrial globe), is in this followed by all its parts, just as the axiom says; so that all the surfaces of the seas, of the lakes, of the ponds — in short, of all the bodies of water held in their basins — spread themselves out in a spherical figure, but a sphere whose center is the center of the terrestrial globe, and they do not make little spheres of their own.

Salviati
The error really is childish, and if it were nobody's but Sacrobosco's, I'd forgive it him readily; but having to pardon it in his commentators too, and in other great men, and even in Ptolemy himself, I can't do it without some blush for their reputation. But it's time to take our leave, the hour being late by now — so that we can be here tomorrow, as usual, for the final conclusion of everything we've discussed.

Simplicio
Please, gentlemen, allow me to bring my mind back to some quiet — right now I find it much tossed about by a certain point Salviati has just touched on — so that afterward, once the waves have been smoothed, I can take in your speculations more distinctly; for images don't imprint themselves well on a rippling mirror, as the Latin poet gracefully put it:

. . . nuper me in littore vidi cum placidum ventis staret mare — I lately caught sight of myself at the shore, when the sea lay calm, stilled by the winds.

Salviati
You're quite right — so tell us your doubts.

Simplicio
A moment ago you dispatched, as equally dull-witted, both those who deny the Earth the daily rotation — because they don't find themselves carried off to Persia or Japan by it — and those who oppose the annual motion, out of the repugnance they feel at having to admit that the enormous, ponderous bulk of the terrestrial globe could lift itself up on high and then sink down low, as it would have to do while wheeling around the Sun with that motion. And I — not blushing to be counted among these fools — feel that same repugnance in my own head, at least as to this second point raised against the annual motion; all the more when I see how much resistance is put up against being moved even along a level surface, and I don't mean a mountain but a stone that's only a small chip off some alpine crag. So, not despising objections of this sort, I beg you to resolve them — and not only for my sake, but for others, to whom they seem utterly conclusive; because I find it very hard for anyone, however simple, to recognize and confess his own simplicity, moved by nothing more than hearing himself called simple.

Sagredo
On the contrary — the simpler he is, the more impossible it'll be to persuade him of his failing. And this gives me occasion to reflect that it's worth resolving this objection, and others of the same kind, not only to satisfy Simplicio but for another reason no less important: for you can see that there's no shortage of men, thoroughly versed in the common philosophy and in other sciences, who — for want of astronomy, or of mathematics, or of whatever other discipline it is that sharpens the mind for penetrating the truth — remain persuaded by discourses as empty as these. And so the plight of poor Copernicus seems to me to deserve our pity: he can't be sure that the judging of his doctrines won't happen to fall into the hands of people who, unable to grasp his most subtle reasonings (which are therefore hard to understand), but already persuaded by such empty appearances that they're false, go about preaching them as false and mistaken. So, when such people can't be brought to understand the more abstruse arguments, it's worth taking pains that they at least recognize the emptiness of these others — and from that recognition may come a more measured judgment and condemnation of the doctrine they now hold to be in error. Let me put forward, then, two more objections — but against the daily rotation — which not long ago I heard raised by persons of great learning; and afterward we'll come to the annual motion. The first was this: if it were true that the Sun and the other stars don't rise above the eastern horizon, but rather that the eastern part of the Earth drops down beneath them while they stay put, then within a few hours the mountains lying to the east, tilting downward through the turning of the terrestrial globe, would have to reach such a state that, where a little before you had to walk uphill to reach their ridge, you'd afterward have to go downhill to get up there. The other was this: if the daily motion belonged to the Earth, it would have to be so fast that a man standing at the bottom of a well could see a star directly overhead only for a single instant — able to see it only for that very brief time in which it crosses the 2 or 3 braccia of the Earth's circumference that make up the width of the well. Yet experience shows that the apparent passage of such a star, in crossing the well, takes up quite a long time; which is a necessary proof that the mouth of the well is not moving with anything like the fury the daily turning would require — and consequently that the Earth is standing still.

Simplicio
Of these two arguments, the second really does seem quite conclusive to me; but as for the first, I think I could get myself out of it on my own. For I consider that it comes to the same thing whether the terrestrial globe, turning on its own center, carries a mountain toward the east, or whether — the globe staying still — the mountain were torn up by the roots and dragged across the Earth; and carrying the mountain over the surface of the Earth is, so far as I can see, no different an operation from taking a ship across the surface of the sea. So that, whenever the mountain objection held good, it would follow just the same that, as the ship kept up its voyage and had drawn many degrees away from our ports, we'd have to climb its mast no longer by going up, but by moving along the flat and then even going down — which doesn't happen. Nor have I ever heard any sailor, even among those who've sailed clear around the globe, report any difference in that operation, or in any other task done aboard ship, on account of the vessel's being in one part of the world rather than another.

Salviati
You reason very well. And if it had ever crossed the mind of the author of that objection to consider that his neighboring mountain, set to his east, would — when the terrestrial globe turned — be carried in 2 hours by that motion to the place where, say, Mount Olympus or Mount Carmel now stands, he'd have realized that by his own way of arguing he was forced to believe and confess that, to reach the peak of those mountains, one must de facto go downhill. These are the sort of brains fit to deny the antipodes, on the grounds that you can't walk with your head hanging down and your feet stuck to the ceiling; men who, from true ideas — even ones they understand perfectly — still can't manage to draw the easiest solutions to their own doubts. I mean that they understand perfectly well that to gravitate and to descend is to tend toward the center of the terrestrial globe, and that to rise is to move away from it; and then they get lost when it comes to understanding that our antipodes have no trouble at all supporting themselves and walking, because they do exactly as we do — that is, they keep the soles of their feet toward the center of the Earth and their heads toward the sky.

Sagredo
And yet we know that men of lofty wit in other fields have been dazzled by matters like these; which all the more confirms what I was just saying — that it's good to clear away every objection, even the very weakest. So let's have an answer to those men of the well, too.

Salviati
This second argument does have, on the face of it, a certain something more than merely plausible; and yet I'm firmly convinced that if we could question the very man it occurred to — get him to explain himself better by stating exactly what the effect is that ought to follow, and that he thinks doesn't follow, once the daily turning is granted to be the Earth's — I believe, I say, that he'd get himself just as tangled up in laying out his difficulty and its consequences as he would in thinking his way out of it.

Simplicio
If I'm to tell the truth, I'm quite sure that's how it would go — because I too find myself, right now, in that same confusion. The argument seems to grip, at first apprehension; but on the other hand I see, as if through fog, that if the reasoning were carried through properly, that immense speed of travel we'd have to detect in the star if the motion belonged to the Earth would have to be detected in it just as much — indeed far more — if the motion belonged to the star itself, since it would have to be many thousands of times faster in the star than in the Earth. And on the other hand, having to lose sight of the star for the mere crossing of the well's mouth — which would be 2 or 3 braccia across, while the well, along with the Earth, travels no less than 2,000,000 in an hour — seems as though it would have to be something so momentary that it couldn't even be perceived; and yet from the bottom of that same well the star is seen for quite a long stretch of time. So I find myself wanting to be brought clear on this business.

Salviati
Now I'm confirmed all the more in believing the objection's author was confused, seeing that you too, Simplicio, only shadow forth — and don't really possess — what you're trying to say; which I gather chiefly from your leaving out a distinction that is a most crucial point in this whole matter. So tell me: in performing this experiment — this passage of a star over the mouth of the well, I mean — do you make any difference according to whether the well is deeper or shallower, that is, according to whether the observer is farther from the mouth or nearer to it? Because I haven't heard you make anything of that.

Simplicio
To tell the truth, I hadn't turned my thoughts to it; but your question wakes up my mind, and suggests to me that such a distinction must be absolutely necessary. And I'm already beginning to see that, in fixing the time of that passage, the depth of the well may perhaps make no less difference than its width.

Salviati
Indeed, I rather suspect the width has nothing to do with it — or next to nothing.

Simplicio
And yet it does seem to me that having to cross 10 braccia of width would take ten times as long as the passage of a single braccio; and I'm sure that a little boat 10 braccia long will pass across my line of sight sooner than a galley a hundred long.

Salviati
And still we go on clinging to that inveterate notion — that we don't move except as far as our own legs carry us. What you say, my dear Simplicio, is true when the object you see is the one moving while you stand still to watch it; but if you'll be down in the well when the well and you together are carried along by the Earth's turning, don't you see that neither in an hour, nor in a thousand, nor in all eternity will the mouth of the well pass you by? Whether the Earth is moving or not moving, whatever effect that has on you can't be detected in the mouth of the well, but only in some other object, separate from you, that doesn't share your same condition — of motion, I mean, or of rest.

Simplicio
That's all fine; but granting that I, down in the well, am carried along in company with it by the daily motion, and that the star I'm looking at is standing still — the opening of the well, which alone gives my sight a passage, being no more than three braccia out of the many millions of braccia of the rest of the Earth's surface that blocks my view — how can the time I see the star for be any perceptible fraction of the time it's hidden?

Salviati
And there you fall back into the same mix-up; you really do need someone to help you out of it. It isn't the width of the well, Simplicio, that measures how long the star is in view — for in that case you'd see it forever, since the mouth of the well gives your sight a passage forever. That measure has to be taken instead from the amount of motionless sky that stays visible to you through the opening of the well.

Simplicio
But what's uncovered to me of the sky — isn't it the same fraction of the whole celestial sphere as the mouth of the well is of the whole Earth?

Salviati
I'd like you to answer that for yourself. So tell me: is the mouth of the well always the same portion of the Earth's surface?

Simplicio
Beyond any doubt, always the same.

Salviati
And the part of the sky seen by the man down in the well — is that always the same fraction of the whole celestial sphere?

Simplicio
Now I'm beginning to clear the fog from my mind, and to understand what you hinted at a moment ago — that the depth of the well has a great deal to do with the present business. For there's no doubt that the farther the eye draws back from the mouth of the well, the smaller the part of the sky it will uncover; and that part, in consequence, will be crossed and lost from view all the sooner by the man gazing up at it from the depths of the well.

Salviati
But is there any place in the well from which one would uncover exactly that fraction of the celestial sphere which the mouth of the well is of the Earth's surface?

Simplicio
It seems to me that if the well were sunk all the way to the center of the Earth, then perhaps from there one would uncover a part of the sky that would stand to it as the well does to the Earth. But moving away from the center and rising toward the surface, one keeps uncovering an ever greater part of that sky.

Salviati
And finally, with the eye set in the plane of the well's mouth, one uncovers half the sky, or very little less — to cross which (supposing we were under the equinoctial) would take 12 hours of time.
