The First Day

(Part 7 of 12)

Simplicio
Corruptibility, alteration, change, and the rest are not in the terrestrial globe as a whole. As far as its integrity goes, the Earth is no less eternal than the Sun or the Moon; it is generable and corruptible only in its outer parts. But it's quite true that in those parts generation and corruption are perpetual, and as perpetual they require the eternal operations of the heavens; and therefore the celestial bodies must be eternal.

Sagredo
That all runs along nicely. But if the corruptibility of the surface parts does no harm whatever to the eternity of the whole terrestrial globe — if, in fact, being generable, corruptible, alterable, and the rest brings it great ornament and perfection — then why can't you, why shouldn't you, admit alterations, generations, and so on in the outer parts of the celestial globes as well? They would add ornament without diminishing perfection or robbing those globes of their actions; rather they'd increase them, by having them all act not only upon the Earth but mutually upon one another, and the Earth upon them in turn.

Simplicio
That cannot be, because the generations, changes, and so forth that took place, say, on the Moon would be useless and vain, and natura nihil frustra facit — nature does nothing in vain.

Sagredo
And why would they be useless and vain?

Simplicio
Because we plainly see, and touch with our own hands, that all the generations, changes, and so on that occur on Earth are directed — all of them, directly or indirectly — to the use, the convenience, and the benefit of man. Horses are born for men's convenience; for the horses' nourishment the Earth produces hay, and the clouds water it. For men's convenience and nourishment grow the herbs, the grains, the fruits, the beasts, the birds, the fish; in short, if we go through all these things and examine them carefully, we'll find that the end to which they are all directed is the need, the use, the convenience, and the delight of men. Now of what possible use to the human race could generations taking place on the Moon or on some other planet ever be? Unless you meant to say that there are men on the Moon as well, enjoying its fruits — a notion either fabulous or impious.

Sagredo
Whether on the Moon or on any other planet there grow herbs or plants or animals like ours, whether rains and winds and thunderstorms occur there as they do around the Earth — I don't know it and I don't believe it, and still less that the Moon is inhabited by men. But I don't at all see how it follows that, because nothing like our things is generated there, no alteration can take place there at all — that there can be no other things there that change, come to be, and dissolve: things not merely different from ours, but utterly remote from our imagination, and in a word wholly beyond our power to conceive. I am certain that a man born and raised in some immense forest, among beasts and birds, with no knowledge whatever of the element of water, could never so much as imagine that there exists in nature another world quite unlike the land, full of animals that travel swiftly without legs and without wings — and not only along the surface, as beasts do on land, but through the whole depth of it; and that they not only travel, but stop motionless wherever they please, which the birds cannot do in the air; and that men live there too, and build palaces and cities there, and find travel so convenient that without any effort at all they go off to the most distant countries with their whole family, house and all — with entire cities, even. As I say, I'm certain that such a man, however keen his imagination, could never picture to himself the fish, the ocean, the ships, the fleets, the navies. So too, and much more easily, it may be that on the Moon — removed from us by so vast an interval, and made, perhaps, of material very different from the Earth's — there are substances, and operations going on, that are not merely remote from all our imaginings but entirely outside them: things bearing no resemblance whatever to ours, and for that reason wholly inconceivable to us. For whatever we imagine has to be either something already seen, or a composite of things, or of parts of things, seen at some other time — which is exactly what sphinxes, sirens, chimeras, centaurs, and the rest are.

Salviati
I've often let my fancy roam over these matters, and in the end I think I can indeed pick out some of the things that are not and cannot be on the Moon — but none of the things that I believe are and can be there, except in the very broadest generality: things that adorn her, acting and moving and living, and perhaps — in a manner utterly different from ours — beholding and admiring the greatness and beauty of the world and of its Maker and Ruler, and singing His glory with continual praise; doing, in short (and this is my whole meaning), what the sacred writers so often affirm: all creatures perpetually occupied in praising God.

Sagredo
Those are among the things that, speaking in the most general terms, may well be there. But I'd gladly hear you name the ones you believe are not and cannot be there — those, surely, can be named more particularly.

Salviati
Take note, Sagredo: this will be the third time that, step by step and without noticing it, we've drifted away from our main plan; and at this rate of digression we'll be a long while getting to the end of our reasonings. So perhaps it would be well to defer this discussion, along with the others we've agreed to save for a session of their own.

Sagredo
Please — since we're on the Moon, let's finish the business that belongs to her, so that we don't have to make such a long journey a second time.

Salviati
As you like. And to begin with the most general points: I believe the lunar globe is very different from the terrestrial, though in some respects certain resemblances can be seen. I'll give the resemblances first, and then the differences. The Moon certainly conforms to the Earth in shape, which is beyond doubt spherical, as follows necessarily from our seeing her disk as a perfect circle, and from the way she receives the light of the Sun. If her surface were flat, the whole of it would be clothed in light at one and the same instant, and then likewise stripped of light all at once in a single moment — not the parts facing the Sun first and the following parts in succession, so that only when she reaches opposition — the point directly opposite the Sun — and not before, does the whole apparent disk stand illuminated. And the exact contrary would happen if her visible surface were concave: the illumination would begin from the parts turned away from the Sun. Second, she is, like the Earth, dark and opaque in herself, and it is this opacity that fits her to receive and throw back the Sun's light — which she could not do if she were otherwise. Third, I hold her material to be every bit as dense and solid as the Earth's; my clearest evidence for this is that her surface is for the most part uneven, full of the many prominences and hollows that the telescope reveals. Of these prominences many are in every respect like our most rugged and precipitous mountains, and some can be seen stretched out in continuous chains hundreds of miles long; others come in more compact clusters; and there are also many detached, solitary crags, very steep and sheer. But what occurs most frequently of all are certain raised banks (I'll use that word, since no other comes to mind that pictures them better), quite elevated, which enclose and encircle plains of various sizes, forming various figures, though most of them circular; many of these have a considerably raised mountain in the middle, and a few are filled with somewhat dark material — like that of the great spots seen with the naked eye — and these are the largest of the enclosures; of the smaller and smaller ones the number is enormous, and still almost all of them circular. Fourth, just as the surface of our globe is divided into two chief parts, the land and the water, so in the lunar disk we see a grand distinction between certain great fields that shine more brightly and others less. And I believe the Earth would present much the same appearance to anyone who could see her lit by the Sun from the Moon, or from some similar distance: the surface of the sea would appear darker, and that of the land brighter. Fifth, just as from the Earth we see the Moon now all luminous, now half, now more, now less, sometimes a sickle, and sometimes wholly invisible — namely when she lies beneath the Sun's rays, so that the part facing the Earth is left in darkness — just so, from the Moon, one would see the Sun's illumination of the face of the Earth, with precisely the same period, to the letter, and under the same changes of shape. Sixth —

Sagredo
Gently a moment, Salviati. That the illumination of the Earth, as far as the variety of shapes goes, would look to someone on the Moon exactly like what we observe in the Moon — that I understand perfectly well. But I don't yet see how it would show itself with the same period, seeing that what the Sun's illumination does upon the lunar surface in a month, it does upon the terrestrial in twenty-four hours.

Salviati
It's true that the Sun's effect, so far as lighting these two bodies and sweeping their entire surfaces with its splendor, is accomplished on the Earth in one natural day and on the Moon in a month. But the variation of the shapes under which the illuminated parts of the terrestrial surface would be seen from the Moon doesn't depend on that alone; it depends on the changing aspects the Moon keeps taking up with respect to the Sun. So that if, for example, the Moon followed the Sun's motion exactly and happened to stand always directly in line between it and the Earth — in the aspect we call conjunction — then, since she would always be seeing the same hemisphere of the Earth that the Sun sees, she would see it perpetually all bright. If, on the contrary, she stood always at opposition to the Sun, she would never see the Earth at all, since the Earth's dark part would be continuously turned toward the Moon, and therefore invisible. But when the Moon is at quadrature with the Sun — a quarter-circle away from it — then, of the terrestrial hemisphere exposed to the Moon's view, the half toward the Sun is luminous and the other half, turned away from the Sun, is dark; and so the illuminated part of the Earth would present itself to the Moon in the shape of a half circle.

Sagredo
I'm entirely satisfied; and now I understand it perfectly. As the Moon departs from opposition to the Sun, where she saw nothing of the illuminated part of the terrestrial surface, and comes day by day toward the Sun, she begins little by little to uncover some small portion of the Earth's illuminated face, and she sees it in the shape of a thin sickle, the Earth being round; and as her motion brings her day by day closer to the Sun, she keeps uncovering more and more of the illuminated terrestrial hemisphere, so that at quadrature she uncovers exactly half of it, just as we see the same amount of her; then, continuing on toward conjunction, she uncovers successively more of the lit surface, and finally, at conjunction, sees the entire hemisphere all luminous. In short, I understand very well that what happens to the inhabitants of the Earth in watching the Moon's changes would happen to anyone on the Moon watching the Earth, but in the reverse order: when the Moon is full for us, at opposition to the Sun, for them the Earth would be at conjunction with the Sun, wholly dark and invisible; and conversely, the configuration that for us is conjunction of Moon with Sun — the Moon silent, as they say, and unseen — would there be opposition of Earth to Sun: a full Earth, so to speak, all luminous. And finally, however much of the illuminated lunar surface shows itself to us at a given time, just so much of the Earth, at that same time, would be seen from the Moon to be dark; and however much of the Moon is left without light for us, just so much of the Earth is illuminated for the Moon. Only at the quadratures, then, do we see a half circle of the Moon luminous, and they just as much of the Earth. In one thing, though, these reciprocal operations seem to me to differ: granting, for argument's sake and not conceding it, that there were someone on the Moon who could gaze at the Earth from up there, he would see the whole terrestrial surface every single day, thanks to the Moon's motion around the Earth in twenty-four or twenty-five hours; whereas we never see anything but half of the Moon, since she does not revolve upon herself, as she would have to do to be able to show us all of her.

Salviati
Provided it isn't the other way around — that it's precisely her turning upon herself that keeps us from ever seeing the other half; for that is how it would have to be, if she rode on the epicycle — that small secondary circle the astronomers assign her. But you're leaving out another difference, to set against the one you've just pointed out.

Sagredo
And which is that? No other comes to my mind at the moment.

Salviati
It's this: if the Earth (as you've rightly noted) sees no more than half of the Moon, while from the Moon the whole Earth is seen, then, on the other hand, all the Earth sees the Moon, but only half the Moon sees the Earth. For the inhabitants, so to speak, of the upper hemisphere of the Moon — the one invisible to us — are deprived of the sight of the Earth; and these, perhaps, are the antichthones, the "counter-dwellers" the ancients talked of. But here I'm reminded of a particular phenomenon, newly observed in the Moon by our mutual friend, the Academician, from which two necessary consequences follow: one, that we actually see somewhat more than half of the Moon; the other, that the Moon's motion is related exactly to the center of the Earth. And the phenomenon and the observation are these. If the Moon has a correspondence and a natural sympathy with the Earth, toward which she keeps a certain fixed part of herself turned, then the straight line joining their two centers must always pass through the same point of the Moon's surface — so that anyone gazing at her from the center of the Earth would always see the same lunar disk, bounded precisely by one and the same circumference. But for someone stationed on the Earth's surface, the ray running from his eye to the center of the lunar globe would not pass through that same point of her surface through which the line drawn from the Earth's center to the Moon's passes — except when she stood directly overhead. With the Moon placed in the east or in the west, however, the point where the visual ray strikes lies above the point of the line joining the centers; and so some part of the lunar hemisphere toward the upper circumference comes into view, and just as much is hidden on the lower side — comes into view and is hidden, I mean, with respect to the hemisphere that would be seen from the Earth's true center. And since the part of the Moon's circumference that is uppermost at her rising is lowermost at her setting, the difference in the appearance of those upper and lower parts should be quite noticeable, with spots or other marked features in those regions now showing themselves, now hiding. A similar variation should also be seen toward the northern and southern edges of the same disk, according as the Moon stands in one or the other belly of her "dragon" — the old astronomers' name for the two arcs of her path that swing north and south: when she is to the north, some of her northern parts are hidden from us and some southern parts come into view, and the reverse when she is to the south. Now, that these consequences are in fact borne out, the telescope makes us certain. For there are on the Moon two particular spots, one of which, when the Moon is on the meridian, faces toward the northwest, while the other lies almost diametrically opposite; and the first is visible even without the telescope, but not the second. The northwesterly one is a small oval spot, separate from the other very large ones; the opposite one is smaller, likewise detached from the great spots, and set in a quite bright field. In both of these the variations I've described are observed very plainly: they are seen — each moving contrary to the other — now near the rim of the lunar disk, now drawn back from it, and with such a difference that the interval between the northwesterly spot and the circumference of the disk is at one time more than double what it is at another; and as for the other spot, since it lies closer to the circumference, its shift amounts to more than triple from one time to the next. From all this it is plain that the Moon, as if drawn by a magnetic power, constantly faces the terrestrial globe with one and the same face, and never turns away from it.

Sagredo
And when will there be an end to the new observations and discoveries of this admirable instrument?

Salviati
If its progress follows the course of other great inventions, we may hope that with the passage of time we'll come to see things we can't now so much as imagine. But to return to our original discussion: I say, as the sixth point of resemblance between the Moon and the Earth, that just as the Moon, a great part of the time, makes up for the absence of the Sun's light and renders our nights quite bright by the reflection of her own, so the Earth repays her in kind when her need is greatest, reflecting the solar rays back at her with a very vigorous illumination — greater, in my judgment, than the light that comes to us from her, in the same proportion as the Earth's surface is greater than the Moon's.

Sagredo
No more, no more, Salviati! Leave me the pleasure of showing you how, at this first hint, I've penetrated the cause of a phenomenon I've thought about a thousand times without ever getting to the bottom of it. You mean that a certain pale glow we see in the Moon, above all when she's a sickle, comes from the reflection of the Sun's light off the surface of the land and the sea; and that this light shows the more clearly, the thinner the crescent is — because then the luminous part of the Earth seen from the Moon is larger, in keeping with what we concluded a moment ago: that the luminous part of the Earth showing itself to the Moon is always exactly as large as the dark part of the Moon that faces the Earth. So when the Moon is a thin sickle, and her dark portion consequently great, great too is the illuminated part of the Earth as seen from the Moon, and so much the more powerful its reflection of light.

Salviati
That is exactly what I meant to say. Truly, it's a great sweetness to talk with people of judgment and quick understanding — above all when one is strolling and discoursing among truths. I've run into brains so thick that, though I repeated to them a thousand times this very thing you've just penetrated on your own, they never once managed to grasp it.

Simplicio
If you mean that you couldn't persuade them to the point of understanding it, I'm greatly amazed, and I'm confident that if they didn't understand it from your explanation, they'll hardly understand it from anyone else's — your way of putting it seems very clear to me. But if you mean you didn't persuade them to the point of believing it, that doesn't amaze me in the least; for I confess that I myself am one of those who understand your discourses without being set at rest by them. Indeed, on this and on parts of the other six points of resemblance, many difficulties remain with me — and I'll bring them forward once you've finished recounting them all.
