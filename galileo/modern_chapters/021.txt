The Second Day

(Part 9 of 20)

Simplicio
I follow you — that's exactly how the thing must turn out.

Sagredo
But suppose the cannon were set up not straight up, but tilted off to one side — what would the ball's motion be then? Would it perhaps travel, as in the other shot, along the vertical line, and then come back down along that same line?

Simplicio
No, it wouldn't do that. Once it left the gun it would keep moving along the straight line that continues the line of the barrel — except insofar as its own weight would pull it down, away from that line, toward the ground.

Sagredo
So the line of the barrel is what governs the ball's motion; the ball doesn't move — and wouldn't move — outside that line, unless its own weight pulled it downward. And that's why, when the barrel is set straight up and the ball is driven upward, it comes back down along that same straight line: because the motion the ball gets from its heaviness is downward along that very vertical. So the ball's journey outside the gun continues the line of that little stretch of journey it made inside the gun — isn't that so?

Simplicio
So it seems to me.

Sagredo
Now picture the barrel standing straight up, and the Earth turning on itself with the daily rotation, carrying the cannon along with it. Tell me: once the powder is fired, what will the ball's motion be inside the barrel?

Simplicio
It will be a straight, vertical motion, since the barrel is aimed straight up.

Sagredo
Look carefully, because I don't think it will be vertical at all. It would indeed be vertical if the Earth stood still, because then the ball would have no motion except what the firing gives it. But if the Earth turns, the ball inside the gun already shares the daily rotation too; so when the push of the powder comes on top of that, it travels from the breech of the gun to the muzzle with two motions, and out of the compound of the two it turns out that the path traced by the ball's center of gravity is a slanted line. To make it clearer: let the cannon AC stand upright, with the ball B inside it. Plainly, if the gun stays still and is fired, the ball will come out at the muzzle A, and its center, traveling up the barrel, will have traced the vertical line BA, and it will go on following that same line outside the gun, moving up toward the point overhead. But if the Earth were turning, and so carried the cannon along with it, then during the time the ball driven by the powder moves up the barrel, the cannon carried by the Earth would pass to the position DE, and the ball B, as it emerged, would be at the mouth D; and the motion of the ball's center would have followed the line BD — no longer vertical, but slanted toward the east. And since (as we've already concluded) the ball must go on moving through the air along the direction of the motion it made inside the gun, its flight will follow the slant of the line BD: and so it won't be vertical after all, but tilted toward the east — toward where the gun itself is also traveling — so that the ball can keep up with the motion of the Earth and of the gun. And there, Simplicio, I've shown you how the shot that seemed bound to go straight up doesn't, after all.

Simplicio
I'm not quite satisfied about this business. What about you, Salviati?

Salviati
I'm partly satisfied; but I have some scruple or other — God grant I can put it into words. It seems to me that, by what's just been said, when the gun points straight up and the Earth moves, the ball ought not only to fail to fall back far from the gun toward the west, as Aristotle and Tycho want, but not even back onto the gun, as you'd have it — rather, quite far off toward the east. Because, on your explanation, it would have two motions, both driving it in that direction together: the shared motion of the Earth, which carries the cannon and the ball from CA toward ED, and the firing, which drives it along the slanted line BD — both motions eastward, and so greater than the Earth's motion alone.

Sagredo
No, sir. The motion that carries the ball eastward comes entirely from the Earth; the firing has no part in it. And the motion that drives the ball upward comes entirely from the firing, with which the Earth has nothing to do. To see that this is true: don't fire, and the ball will never leave the gun, nor rise so much as a hair; and likewise, stop the Earth and fire — the ball, without leaning aside at all, will go straight up the vertical. So the ball has two motions, one upward and one circular, which together make up the slanting line BD: the upward push is all from the firing, the circular is all from the Earth and equal to the Earth's; and because it's equal, the ball stays always straight above the mouth of the gun, and in the end falls back into it. And since it stays always over the line of the barrel, it also keeps appearing continuously over the head of anyone standing near the gun — and so it looks to us as if it rises exactly straight up toward the point overhead.

Simplicio
I have another difficulty left. Since the ball's motion inside the gun is extremely fast, it doesn't seem possible that in that instant of time the shift of the cannon from CA to ED could give the slanting line CD enough tilt for the ball to be able, afterward, through the air, to keep pace with the Earth's course.

Sagredo
You're mistaken on several counts. First, the tilt of the diagonal CD is, I believe, much greater than you imagine, because I hold it beyond doubt that the speed of the Earth's motion — not only at the equator, but even at our own latitude — is greater than that of the ball while it moves inside the gun; so that the interval CE would be absolutely greater than the whole length of the gun, and the tilt of the diagonal, consequently, greater than half a right angle. But whether the Earth's speed is small or large compared with the firing's, that doesn't matter in the least: for if the Earth's speed is small, and so the tilt of the diagonal is small, then only a small tilt is needed to make the ball keep to its flight over the gun. In short, if you consider it attentively, you'll see that the motion of the Earth, by carrying the gun along with it from CA to ED, gives the diagonal CD exactly as much tilt — less or more — as is required to adjust the shot to its need. But you're mistaken in a second way, when you'd credit the ball's ability to keep up with the Earth's motion to the impetus of the firing; there you fall back into the very error Salviati seemed to slip into a moment ago. For keeping up with the Earth is that most ancient and perpetual motion, shared indelibly and inseparably by the ball itself, as by a thing that is earthly and by its own nature possesses that motion and always will.

Salviati
Let's set our minds at rest, Simplicio, because the business really does work just like this. And now, from this discussion, I've come to understand the reason behind a hunting puzzle of those fowlers who bring down birds on the wing with the harquebus — a shoulder gun. I'd imagined that to bag the bird they aimed the sight some distance ahead of it, leading it by a certain interval — more or less according to the speed of the flight and the bird's distance — so that, firing and sending the ball straight along the line of sight, the ball with its motion and the bird with its flight would arrive at the same point at the same moment, and so meet. But when I asked one of them whether that was their practice, he told me no: the trick was far easier and surer, and they do exactly the same thing as when they shoot at a bird sitting still — that is, they fix the sight on the flying bird and follow it by swinging the gun, keeping the sight always on it until they fire, and so they hit the flying birds just like the still ones. So that motion, slow though it is, which the gun makes as it turns to follow the bird's flight with the sight, must be communicated to the ball as well, and must join in the ball with the other motion from the firing — so that the ball gets from the firing its straight motion upward, and from the barrel its leaning to follow the bird's flight, just as was said just now about the artillery shot; where the ball gets from the firing its going up toward the point overhead, and from the Earth's motion its bending toward the east, and out of both makes a compound that follows the Earth's course and that, to anyone watching, seems only to go straight up, coming back down afterward along the same line. So keeping the sight steadily fixed on the target is what makes the shot strike true; and to keep the sight on the mark: if the target stands still, the barrel must be held still too; and if the target moves, the barrel is kept on the mark by moving with it. And on this depends the proper answer to that other argument about firing artillery at a target set toward the south or toward the north — where they urged that if the Earth moved, the shots would all veer off toward the west, because in the time the ball, once out of the gun, travels through the air to the mark, the mark, carried eastward, leaves the ball behind to the west. So I answer by asking: once the cannon has been adjusted to the mark and left just so, does it go on aiming always at the same mark, whether the Earth moves or stands still? The answer must be that the aim doesn't change at all: for if the mark stands still, the cannon likewise stands still, and if the mark, carried by the Earth, moves, the cannon moves right along with it in the same measure; and since the aim is kept, the shot always comes out true, as is clear from what's been said above.

Sagredo
Hold on a moment, please, Salviati, while I put forward a thought that's stirred in me about these shooters of birds on the wing. The way they work is, I believe, just as you say, and I believe the result — hitting the bird — follows just as you describe; but it doesn't seem to me that this operation is entirely of a piece with that of the artillery shots, which must strike both when the gun and the target are in motion and when both are at common rest. And the differences seem to me these. In the artillery shot, the gun and the target move with equal speed, both being carried by the motion of the terrestrial globe; and even if sometimes the gun's being planted more toward the pole than the target — and so its motion somewhat slower, as made in a smaller circle — such a difference is imperceptible, given how little distance there is from gun to mark. But in the fowler's shot, the motion of the gun, with which he follows the bird, is extremely slow compared with the bird's flight; from which it seems to me to follow that the small motion the turning of the barrel gives to the ball inside it cannot, once the ball is out, multiply itself through the air up to the speed of the bird's flight, so that the ball would stay always aimed at it — rather, it seems the ball must fall behind and be left at the bird's tail. Add to this that in this case the air through which the ball has to pass isn't supposed to share the bird's motion; whereas in the artillery's case the gun and the target and the air between them all share equally in the universal daily motion. So for the fowler's hitting, I'd believe the causes were — besides following the flight with the motion of the barrel — leading it somewhat, by keeping the sight ahead; and besides that, firing (as I believe) not with a single ball but with a good number of little pellets, which, spreading through the air, take up a very large space; and besides this, the extreme speed with which, coming out of the barrel, they're carried to the bird.

Salviati
And there's how far the flight of Sagredo's wit outruns and forestalls the slowness of mine — which would perhaps have noticed these disparities, but not without a long application of thought. Now, coming back to our subject, we still have to consider the point-blank shots toward the east and toward the west. The first of these, if the Earth moved, ought always to come out high above the target, and the second low; seeing that, by the daily rotation, the eastern parts of the Earth are continually sinking below the tangent line parallel to the horizon — which is why the eastern stars appear to us to rise — while, on the contrary, the western parts are rising, so that the western stars appear to sink. And so the shots aimed along that tangent at an eastern target, which sinks while the ball travels along the tangent, ought to come out high; and the western ones low, because the target rises while the ball runs along the tangent. The answer is like the others: just as the eastern target, by the Earth's motion, keeps sinking below a tangent that stayed fixed, so likewise the gun, for the same reason, keeps tilting and goes on aiming always at the same target, so that the shots come out true. But here it seems a fitting occasion to point out a certain generosity — shown, perhaps, with too much liberality — by the followers of Copernicus toward the opposing side: I mean, granting them as sure and certain some experiments the opponents have in truth never made — such as, for example, that of the bodies dropping from the mast of the ship while it's in motion, and many others; among which I hold it for certain that one is this — testing whether the eastern artillery shots come out high and the western low. And because I believe they've never made it, I'd like them to tell me what difference they think ought to be seen between these same shots, whether the Earth is set at rest or set in motion; and let Simplicio answer for them now.

Simplicio
I won't presume to answer as soundly as someone more knowledgeable than me perhaps could, but I'll say what I think, off the top of my head, they would answer — which is, in effect, what's already been brought up: namely, that if the Earth moved, the eastern shots would always come out high, and so on, since the ball, as seems likely, must move along the tangent.

Salviati
But if I said that this is in fact what happens, how would you go about refuting what I said?

Simplicio
One would have to come to experiment to clear it up.

Salviati
But do you believe you'd find a gunner so skilled that he'd undertake to hit the target with every single shot at a distance of, say, five hundred braccia?

Simplicio
No, sir; and I don't think there'd be anyone, however expert, who'd promise not to miss, on average, by more than a braccio.

Salviati
How, then, could we settle what we're in doubt about with shots as unreliable as that?

Simplicio
We could settle it in two ways: one, by firing many shots; the other, because, given the great speed of the Earth's motion, the deviation from the target would, in my opinion, be enormous.

Salviati
Enormous — that is, much more than a braccio; seeing that a variation of that much, and even more, is admitted to happen ordinarily even when the terrestrial globe is at rest.

Simplicio
I firmly believe the variation would be much greater.

Salviati
Now, for our own satisfaction, let's do a bit of rough calculation, if you like — one that will also serve us (if the reckoning comes out as I hope) as a warning not to let ourselves be so easily carried away, as the saying goes, by the shouting, giving our assent to everything that first presents itself to our fancy. And, to give every advantage to the Peripatetics and the Tychonians, let's imagine ourselves at the equator, firing point-blank westward with a culverin at a target five hundred braccia away. First let's find, roughly (as I said), how much time the ball takes, once out of the gun, to reach the mark — which we know to be very brief, and certainly no more than the time in which a man on foot takes two steps; and this is still less than one second of an hour. For, granting the walker goes three miles an hour, which is nine thousand braccia, and since an hour holds three thousand six hundred seconds, that comes to two and a half steps in a second: so a second is more than the time of the ball's motion. And since the daily revolution is twenty-four hours, the western horizon rises fifteen degrees an hour — that is, fifteen first minutes of a degree for one first minute of an hour, that is, fifteen seconds of a degree for one second of an hour; and since one second is the time of the shot, in this time the western horizon rises fifteen seconds of a degree, and so does the target: fifteen seconds, that is, of that circle whose radius is five hundred braccia (for that's what we set the target's distance from the culverin to be). Now let's look in the table of arcs and chords — and here's Copernicus's book, right at hand — for what part the chord of fifteen seconds is of a radius of five hundred braccia. Here we see that the chord of one first minute is less than thirty of the parts of which the radius holds a hundred thousand; so, of those same parts, the chord of one second minute will be less than half — that is, less than one of the parts of which the radius is two hundred thousand — and therefore the chord of fifteen seconds will be less than fifteen of those same two-hundred-thousandth parts. Now fifteen of those two-hundred-thousandth parts, taken of a radius of five hundred braccia, come to less than four hundredths of a braccio; and so the rise of the target during the ball's flight is less than four hundredths — that is, a twenty-fifth — of a braccio, and thus about a finger's width. And a single finger's width, consequently, will be the deviation of each westward shot, if the daily rotation belonged to the Earth. Now, if I tell you that this deviation actually happens in every shot — I mean that the shots strike a finger's width lower than they would if the Earth didn't move — how, Simplicio, would you go about convincing me, showing me by experiment that it doesn't happen? Don't you see it's impossible to shake me off, unless you first find a way of aiming so exact that you never miss by a hair? Because, so long as the shots come out varying by whole braccia, as they in fact do, I'll always say that contained in each of those variations is that one finger's width, caused by the motion of the Earth.

Sagredo
Forgive me, Salviati, you're too generous; for I'd say to the Peripatetics that even if every shot struck the very center of the target, that wouldn't contradict the motion of the Earth in the least: because the gunners have always trained themselves to fit the aim to the target, and have made a practice of setting the gun on the mark in such a way that they hit it, given the motion of the Earth; and I say that if the Earth stopped, the shots would not come out true, but the western ones would come out high and the eastern low. Now let Simplicio convince me.

Salviati
A subtlety worthy of Sagredo. But whether this variation is to be seen in the Earth's motion or in its rest — since it can only be extremely tiny — it can only stay submerged among the very large ones that continually happen through many accidents. And let all this be said and granted, in full measure, to Simplicio, and only as a warning of how cautious one must be in granting as true so many experiments to people who have never made them, but boldly put them forward just as they'd need them to be, to serve their cause. I say this is thrown in as a bonus to Simplicio, because the plain truth is that, as regards the effects of these shots, exactly the same thing must happen whether the terrestrial globe is in motion or at rest — just as it will with all the other experiments brought forward, and that can be brought forward, which have at first sight some semblance of truth only insofar as the old, ingrained notion of the Earth's immobility keeps us among the ambiguities.
