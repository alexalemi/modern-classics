The Second Day

(Part 14 of 20)

Sagredo
That's how the matter seems to stand.

Salviati
But what force do you suppose could be the one that deflects the stones from moving along the tangents, where the impetus of the whirl truly flings them?

Sagredo
It's either their own gravity, or some glue that keeps them settled and stuck onto the wheels.

Salviati
But to deflect a moving body from the path in which it has its impetus, doesn't it take a greater or a smaller force depending on whether the deflection has to be greater or smaller — that is, depending on whether, in the deflection, the body must in the same time cross a greater or a smaller space?

Sagredo
Yes, because we already concluded above that to make a body move, the greater the speed you have to give it, the greater the moving power must be.

Salviati
Now consider this. To deflect the stone on the smaller wheel from the projectile motion it would make along the tangent BF, and to keep it fastened to the wheel, its own gravity has to draw it back by the length of the secant FG — that is, by the perpendicular dropped from point G onto the line BF. On the larger wheel, though, the drawing-back need be no more than the secant DE — the perpendicular dropped from point E onto the tangent DC — which is far shorter than FG, and shorter and shorter still as the wheel is made larger. And since these drawings-back must be accomplished in equal times — that is, in the while that the two equal arcs BG and CE are traversed — the one belonging to stone B, namely the pull-back FG, will have to be faster than the other, DE. So much greater force is needed to keep stone B joined to its little wheel than to keep stone C joined to its big one; which is the same as saying that some trifling thing will block the flinging-off on the big wheel that would not prevent it on the little one. Plainly, then, the larger the wheel grows, the weaker the cause of projection becomes.

Sagredo
From what I now understand, thanks to your patient breaking-down of it, I think I can satisfy my mind with a fairly brief account. Since the equal speed of the two wheels impresses an equal impetus along the tangents into both stones, one sees that the great circumference, parting only slightly from its tangent, goes along humoring the stone, in a way — with a gentle bit softly curbing in it the appetite, so to speak, to leave the circumference — so that any small restraint, whether of the stone's own downward inclination or of some glue, is enough to keep it joined. But that same restraint turns out too weak to do the job on the little wheel, which, humoring the tangent's direction so little, tries with too greedy a hunger to hold the stone to itself; and since the curb and the glue are no stronger than what kept the other stone united with the larger wheel, the halter snaps and off it runs along the tangent. So I not only see that everyone was mistaken who believed the cause of projection grows as the speed of the whirl grows; I go further, and consider that, since the projection diminishes as the wheel grows — provided the same speed is kept in the wheels — it might perhaps be true that, to make the big wheel fling like the little one, you'd have to increase its speed by as much as you increase its diameter. That would be the case when the whole revolutions are completed in equal times; and so one might reckon that the whirl of the Earth is no more able to fling off stones than any other little wheel turning so slowly that in twenty-four hours it makes a single turn.

Salviati
For now I don't want us to press the inquiry that far. It's enough that we've shown — abundantly enough, if I'm not mistaken — the emptiness of an argument that at first glance looked utterly conclusive, and was reckoned so by very great men. And I'll count the time and the words well spent if I've won even a little belief in Simplicio's mind — not, I'll say, in the mobility of the Earth, but at least in this: that the opinion of those who believe it is not so ridiculous and foolish as the ranks of the common philosophers hold it to be.

Simplicio
The solutions offered so far to the objections raised against this daily revolution of the Earth — the ones drawn from heavy bodies falling from the top of a tower, and from projectiles shot straight up, or at any sideways slant, toward east, west, south, or north, and so on — have in some measure lessened the old disbelief I'd conceived against this opinion. But other, larger difficulties are now turning in my imagination, ones I absolutely would not know how to work myself free of — nor perhaps do I think you yourselves could untangle them. It may even be that they haven't reached your ears, since they're quite recent. These are the objections of two authors who write ex professo against Copernicus. The first are read in a little book of natural conclusions; the others are by a great philosopher and mathematician both, set into a treatise he wrote in favor of Aristotle and his opinion about the unalterability of the heavens, where he proves that not only the comets but also the new stars — I mean the one of '72 in Cassiopeia and the one of 1604 in Sagittarius — were not above the spheres of the planets at all, but absolutely below the concave of the Moon, in the elemental sphere. And this he demonstrates against Tycho, Kepler, and many other observing astronomers, beating them with their own weapons, that is, by way of the parallaxes. If it please you, I'll lay out the reasons of the one and the other, for I've read them more than once with attention; and you can weigh their force and give your opinion.

Salviati
Since our chief aim is to bring out and weigh everything that has been urged for and against the two systems, the Ptolemaic and the Copernican, it's not right to pass over anything written on the matter.

Simplicio
Then I'll begin with the objections in the little book of conclusions, and come to the others afterward. First, then, the author very shrewdly goes calculating how many miles per hour a point on the Earth's surface set under the equinoctial line travels, and how many are traveled by other points set on other parallels; and not content to investigate these motions by the hour, he finds them also for a single minute, and not content with the minute, he tracks it down to a single second. And more: he goes on to show, as plainly as could be, how many miles an artillery ball would travel in such spans of time if it were placed in the concave of the lunar sphere — supposing that concave even as large as Copernicus himself imagines it, so as to strip the adversary of every loophole. And having made this most ingenious and exquisite reckoning, he demonstrates that a heavy body falling from up there would take a good deal more than six days to reach the center of the Earth, toward which all heavy things naturally tend. Now, if by God's absolute power, or by some angel, a very large artillery ball were miraculously carried up there and set at the point directly above us, our vertical point, and from there let go free, it is — in his view and mine — a most incredible thing that in coming down it would keep itself always in our vertical line, continuing to turn with the Earth about its center for so many days, tracing under the equinoctial a spiral line in the plane of that greatest circle, and under other parallels spiral lines around cones, and under the poles falling by a simple straight line. He then establishes and confirms this great improbability by putting forward, in the form of questions, many difficulties impossible for the followers of Copernicus to remove; and they are, if I remember rightly —

Salviati
Gently, a moment — please, Simplicio, don't tangle me up with so many novelties at one stroke. I have a poor memory, and so I need to go step by step. And since it comes back to me that I once wanted to calculate how long a similar heavy body, falling from the concave of the Moon, would take to arrive at the center of the Earth — and I seem to recall that the time wouldn't be so long — it'll be well for you to tell us by what rule this author made his computation.

Simplicio
He made it so as to prove his point a fortiori, granting a great advantage to the opposing side: he supposes that the speed of the falling body down the vertical line toward the center of the Earth was equal to the speed of its circular motion made on the greatest circle of the concave of the lunar sphere — at which rate it would come to travel twelve thousand six hundred German miles in an hour, a thing that truly has the impossible about it. Yet, to abound in caution and give every advantage to the other side, he supposes it true, and concludes that the time of the fall must in any case be more than six days.

Salviati
And that's the whole of his procedure? And with this demonstration he proves the time of such a fall must be more than six days?

Sagredo
It seems to me he's conducted himself all too modestly. Since it lay within his own choosing to give the falling body whatever speed he liked — and consequently to make it reach the Earth in six months, or even six years — he contented himself with six days. But please, Salviati, restore my appetite a little by telling me how your own computation went, since you say you once did it. For I'm quite sure that if the problem hadn't called for some clever operation, you'd never have set your mind to it.

Salviati
It isn't enough, Sagredo, that the conclusion be noble and grand; the point is to treat it nobly. Who doesn't know that in cutting up the limbs of an animal one can uncover infinite marvels of provident and most wise nature? Yet for every one that the anatomist cuts, the butcher hacks up a thousand. And I, in trying now to satisfy your request, don't know in which of the two costumes I'm to appear on stage. Still, taking heart from the entrance of Simplicio's author, I won't refuse to recite for you — if it comes back to me — the method I used. But before I set my hand to anything else, I can't help saying that I strongly doubt Simplicio has faithfully reported the way this author of his finds that the artillery ball, in coming from the concave of the Moon to the center of the Earth, would take more than six days. For if he had supposed its speed in descending to be equal to that of the concave — as Simplicio says he supposes — he'd have shown himself utterly stripped even of the first and simplest notions of geometry. Indeed, I marvel that Simplicio himself, in accepting the supposition he describes, doesn't see the immense absurdity it contains.

Simplicio
That I may have blundered in reporting it is possible; but that you should uncover a fallacy inside it — that, surely, is not.

Salviati
Perhaps I haven't rightly grasped what you reported. Don't you say this author makes the speed of the ball's motion in descending equal to the speed it had while going around, standing in the concave of the Moon — and that, dropping with that speed, it would take itself to the center in six days?

Simplicio
So he seems to write.

Salviati
And you don't see so great an absurdity? But surely you're dissembling — for it can't be that you don't know that the semidiameter of a circle is less than a sixth part of the circumference; and that, in consequence, the time in which a moving body will pass over the semidiameter will be less than a sixth part of the time in which, moved with the same speed, it would pass over the circumference; and therefore the ball, descending with the speed at which it was moving in the concave, will arrive at the center in less than four hours — granting that in the concave it completed one revolution in twenty-four hours, as he must suppose in order to keep it always in the same vertical line.

Simplicio
Now I understand the error perfectly; but I wouldn't want to lay it on him undeservedly, and it must be that I erred in reciting his argument. To avoid saddling him with still more, I'd like to have his book; and if there were someone to go and fetch it, I'd be much obliged.

Sagredo
There'll be no shortage of a footman to go flying for it; and it can be done without any loss of time, for meanwhile Salviati will favor us with his computation.

Simplicio
He can go — he'll find it lying open on my desk, together with the other man's, who also argues against Copernicus.

Sagredo
Let's have that one brought as well, for extra safety; and meanwhile Salviati will make his calculation. I've dispatched a servant.

Salviati
Before anything else, we have to consider that the motion of descending heavy bodies is not uniform: starting from rest, they go on continually accelerating — an effect known and observed by everyone except the aforesaid modern author, who, saying nothing of acceleration, makes it steady. But this general knowledge is of no use unless one knows according to what proportion this increase of speed is made — a conclusion that, down to our own times, was unknown to all philosophers, and was first discovered and demonstrated by the Academician, our mutual friend. In certain of his writings, not yet published but shown in confidence to me and to some others of his friends, he demonstrates how the acceleration of the straight-line motion of heavy bodies follows the odd numbers ab unitate — starting from one. That is: mark off however many equal intervals of time you please; if in the first interval the moving body, setting out from rest, has passed over a certain space — say, one canna, a rod's length — then in the second interval it will pass three canne, in the third five, in the fourth seven, and so on in order according to the succeeding odd numbers. Which in sum is the same as saying that the spaces passed by the moving body, starting from rest, stand to one another in the doubled ratio of the times in which those spaces are measured — or, if you like, that the spaces passed are to one another as the squares of the times.

Sagredo
A marvelous thing I'm hearing. And you say there's mathematical demonstration of this?

Salviati
Purest mathematics — and not of this alone, but of many other beautiful properties belonging to natural motions, and to projectiles too, all discovered and demonstrated by our friend. I've seen and studied them all, with the greatest delight and wonder, watching a whole new field of knowledge spring up around a subject on which hundreds of volumes have been written — and not a single one of the infinite admirable conclusions it contains had been observed and understood by anyone before our friend.

Sagredo
You're making me lose my appetite to hear more of the discussions we've begun, and want only to hear some of the demonstrations you're hinting at. So either tell them to me now, or at least give me your firm word to hold a special session on them with me — and with Simplicio present too, if he'd care to hear the properties and incidents of nature's primary effect.

Simplicio
I'll certainly have the will for it, though as far as the natural philosopher is concerned, I don't believe that descending to certain minute particulars is necessary — a general knowledge of the definition of motion, and of the distinction between natural and violent, equable and accelerated, and the like, being enough. For if that had not been enough, I don't believe Aristotle would have failed to teach us all that was lacking.

Salviati
Maybe so. But let's lose no more time on this, for I promise to spend half a day on it separately, for your satisfaction — indeed, it comes back to me now that I once before promised you this very satisfaction. And returning to our begun calculation of the time in which the falling heavy body would come from the concave of the Moon to the center of the Earth: to proceed not arbitrarily and by chance, but by a thoroughly conclusive method, we'll first try to make sure, by experience repeated many times, in how much time a ball — of iron, say — comes to the ground from a height of a hundred braccia.

Sagredo
Taking, though, a ball of some determinate weight — the very one on which we mean to make the computation of the time of the descent from the Moon.

Salviati
That matters not at all, because balls of one, of ten, of a hundred, of a thousand pounds will all measure out the same hundred braccia in the same time.

Simplicio
Oh, that I don't believe — nor does Aristotle believe it, who writes that the speeds of descending heavy bodies stand to one another in the same proportion as their weights.

Salviati
If you, Simplicio, want to admit that as true, then you must also believe that, when two balls of the same material are let fall at the same instant — one of a hundred pounds, the other of one — from a height of a hundred braccia, the big one arrives at the ground before the smaller has descended a single braccio. Now fit your brain, if you can, to imagining that you see the big ball landed on the ground while the little one is still within less than a braccio of the top of the tower.

Sagredo
That this proposition is utterly false, I haven't a doubt in the world; but that yours is entirely true, I'm not quite able to grasp — still, I believe it, since you affirm it so resolutely, which I'm sure you wouldn't do unless you had sure experience or firm demonstration of it.

Salviati
I have both; and when we treat the matter of motions separately, I'll share it with you. Meanwhile, so as not to have occasion to interrupt the thread further, let's set ourselves to make the computation on an iron ball of a hundred pounds, which by repeated experiments descends from a height of a hundred braccia in five seconds. And since, as I told you, the spaces measured by the falling body increase in the doubled proportion — that is, as the squares of the times — then, the time of one minute being twelve times the time of five seconds, if we multiply the hundred braccia by the square of 12, that is, by 144, we'll have 14,400, which will be the number of braccia the same body will pass in one minute. Following the same rule, since an hour is 60 minutes, by multiplying 14,400 — the number of braccia passed in a minute — by the square of 60, that is, by 3,600, we get 51,840,000, the number of braccia to be passed in an hour, which is 17,280 miles. And wanting to know the space that would be passed in 4 hours, we'll multiply 17,280 by 16 (which is the square of 4), and we'll get 276,480 miles — a number far greater than the distance from the concave of the Moon to the center of the Earth, which is 196,000 miles, making the distance of the concave 56 Earth semidiameters, as the modern author does, and the Earth's semidiameter 3,500 miles of 3,000 braccia each, which are our Italian miles. So, Simplicio, that stretch from the concave of the Moon to the center of the Earth, which your reckoner said could not be traversed except in a good deal more than six days — see how, making the computation on experience and not on one's fingers, it would be traversed in a good deal less than 4 hours; and, making the exact computation, it's traversed in 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds.

Sagredo
Please, my dear sir, don't cheat me of this exact calculation, for it must be a most beautiful thing.

Salviati
So it truly is. Having, then (as I said), observed by careful experiment how such a body, falling, passes the height of 100 braccia in 5 seconds, we'll say: if 100 braccia are passed in 5 seconds, in how many seconds will 588,000,000 braccia be passed (for that is what 56 semidiameters of the Earth come to)? The rule for this operation is that the third number be multiplied by the square of the second; that gives 14,700,000,000, which is to be divided by the first, that is, by 100; and the square root of the quotient, which is 12,124, is the number sought — that is, 12,124 seconds, which are 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds.

Sagredo
I've watched the operation, but I understand nothing of the reason for operating so — nor does it seem the moment now to ask it.

Salviati
On the contrary, I want to tell you, even though you don't ask, because it's quite easy. Let's mark these three numbers with the letters A first, B second, C third: A and C are the numbers of the spaces, B is the number of the time; the fourth number is sought, likewise of the time. And since we know that whatever proportion space A has to space C, the same must the square of time B have to the square of the time sought, therefore, by the golden rule — the rule of three — the number C will be multiplied by the square of the number B, and the product divided by the number A, and the quotient will be the square of the number sought, and its square root will be the very number sought. Now see how easy it is to understand.
