The Third Day

(Part 2 of 16)

Sagredo
Not only without boredom — I'll listen to your discussions with real pleasure. And I only wish all the Peripatetic philosophers could sit in on them too, so they might grasp how much they owe to this protector of theirs.

Salviati
Tell me, Simplicio, whether you've thoroughly taken in this: since the new star is located on the meridian circle up toward the north, then for someone traveling from the south toward the north, that same new star would climb above the horizon by exactly as much as the pole does — provided it were truly located among the fixed stars; but that if it were notably lower, that is, nearer the Earth, it would appear to rise more than the pole itself, and ever more so the greater its nearness.

Simplicio
I think I've grasped it perfectly, and to show it I'll try my hand at a mathematical figure. In this large circle I'll mark the pole P, and in these two lower circles I'll mark two stars seen from a point on Earth — call it A — and let the two stars be these, B and C, seen along the one line ABC against a fixed star D. Then, traveling on Earth as far as the point E, the two stars will appear to me separated from the fixed star D and drawn nearer the pole P — the lower one, B, more so, appearing at G, and C less so, appearing at F; but the fixed star D will have kept the same distance from the pole.

Salviati
I see you understand it very well. I believe you also see how, because star B is lower than C, the angle formed by the sightlines that set out from the two places A and E and meet at C — that is, this angle ACE — is narrower, or we might say more acute, than the angle formed at B by the lines AB, EB.

Simplicio
That's plain to the eye.

Salviati
And likewise, since the Earth is exceedingly small and all but imperceptible next to the firmament, and since in consequence the distance AE — as far as one can travel on Earth — is very short compared with the immense length of the lines EG, EF stretching from Earth up to the firmament, you come to understand that star C could be raised and carried so very far from the Earth that the angle formed at it by the rays setting out from those same points A and E would become extremely acute, and virtually null and imperceptible altogether.

Simplicio
This too I understand perfectly.

Salviati
Now know, Simplicio, that astronomers and mathematicians have found infallible rules, by way of geometry and arithmetic, whereby — from the size of these angles B and C and their differences, joined with knowledge of the distance between the two places A and E — they can find the distance of these lofty things to within a hand's breadth, provided always that the distance and the angles are taken correctly.

Simplicio
So then, if the rules that depend on geometry and arithmetic are sound, all the fallacies and errors one might meet in trying to investigate such heights — of new stars, or comets, or anything else — must come down to the distance AE and the angles B, C not being well measured. And so all the discrepancies one sees in these twelve investigations depend not on any defect in the rules of calculation, but on errors committed in investigating those angles and those distances by means of instrumental observations.

Salviati
Just so, and no difficulty arises there. Now you must carefully note how, as the star is carried farther from B toward C — so that the angle grows ever more acute — the ray EBG keeps drawing away from the ray ABD on the lower side of the angle, as the line ECF shows, whose lower part EC is more remote from the part AC than EB is. But it can never happen, however immense the removal, that the lines AD, EF part company altogether, since in the end they must meet at the star; one could only say they separate and become parallel if the removal were infinite, and that case cannot occur. But because — note this well — the firmament's distance, set against the smallness of the Earth, is reckoned (as already said) as infinite, the angle enclosed by the rays that, drawn from points A and E, would end at a fixed star is judged to be null, and those rays are taken as two parallel lines. And so one concludes that only then may it be affirmed that the new star was in the firmament, when a collation of observations made in different places yields, by calculation, that this angle is imperceptible and the lines as good as parallel. But when the angle is of notable size, the new star must necessarily be lower than the fixed stars, and lower even than the Moon — that is, whenever the angle ABE were greater than the one that would be formed at the center of the Moon.

Simplicio
So then the Moon's distance is not so great that a similar angle at it stays imperceptible?

Salviati
No, sir. On the contrary, it is perceptible not only at the Moon, but at the Sun as well.

Simplicio
But if that's so, it could also be that such an angle is observable in the new star without its being lower than the Sun — let alone lower than the Moon.

Salviati
That can be, and it is indeed so in the present cases, as you'll see in due place — that is, once I've smoothed the road enough that you too, though no expert in astronomical calculations, can grasp and touch with your own hand how far this author has aimed at writing to please the Peripatetics, by cloaking and disguising various things, rather than at establishing the truth by bringing them forward with naked honesty. But let's press on. From what's been laid out so far, I believe you're fully able to see how the new star's distance can never be made so immense that the angle we've named so often entirely vanishes, and the two observers' rays from points A and E become parallel lines. And in consequence you come to understand perfectly that, if the calculation drew from the observations that this angle is totally null, or the lines truly parallel, we would be certain the observations were — by at least some tiny amount — in error; but if the calculation gave us those lines separated not merely to equidistance, that is, to being parallel, but past that point, and spread wider at the top than at the bottom, then we must firmly conclude that the observations were made with too little accuracy — in short, that they are erroneous, as leading us to a manifest impossibility. Then you must believe me, and take it for absolutely certain, that two straight lines setting out from two points marked on another straight line are wider at the top than at the bottom whenever the angles they enclose upon that straight line are greater than two right angles; and when those angles equal two right angles, the lines are parallel; but if they are less than two right angles, the lines converge, and extended far enough would beyond all doubt close up into a triangle.

Simplicio
Without taking it on your word, I have the science of it, and I'm not so bare of geometry that I don't know a proposition I've had occasion to read a thousand times in Aristotle — namely, that the three angles of every triangle equal two right angles. So that if I take, in my figure, the triangle ABE — granting the line EA to be straight — I understand very well how its three angles A, E, B equal two right angles, and that in consequence the two alone, E and A, fall short of two right angles by exactly the amount of the angle B; whence, by opening the lines AB, EB (while keeping them fixed at points A and E) until the angle they contain toward the B side vanishes, the two below will come to equal two right angles, and those lines will be reduced to being parallel; and if one went on opening them further, the angles at points E and A would become greater than two right angles.

Salviati
You're an Archimedes, and you've spared me spending more words to explain to you how, whenever the calculations turn up the two angles A and E as greater than two right angles, the observations are without question in error. This is exactly what I wanted you to understand perfectly, and what I feared I might not manage to explain in a way that a pure Peripatetic philosopher could take in with any certainty. Now let's carry on with what's left. And picking up again what you granted me a moment ago — namely, that since the new star cannot be in several places, but in one only, then whenever the calculations made upon these astronomers' observations don't give it back to us in the same place, there must be error in the observations: either in taking the polar altitudes, or in taking the star's elevations, or in both operations at once — now, since among the many investigations, made by combining the observations two by two, very few come out placing the star in the same spot, then those very few alone could be the ones not in error, while all the rest are absolutely erroneous.

Sagredo
Then we'll have to trust these very few alone rather than all the others together. And since you say the ones that agree are very few, and I see among these twelve two that both make the star's distance from the Earth's center 4 semidiameters — four Earth-radii — this fifth and the sixth, then it's more probable that the new star was elemental than celestial.

Salviati
It's not so; for if you look carefully, it isn't written that the distance was exactly 4 semidiameters, but about 4 semidiameters — and yet you'll see that those two distances differed from each other by many hundreds of miles. Here they are: you see that this fifth, which is 13,389 miles, exceeds the sixth, which is 13,100 miles, by nearly 300 miles.

Sagredo
Which, then, are these few that agree in placing the star in the same spot?

Salviati
They are, to this author's misfortune, five investigations, all of which lodge it in the firmament — as you'll see in this other note, where I record many further combinations. But I'll grant the author more than he'd perhaps ask of me: namely, that in each combination of observations there is some error. Which I believe is absolutely unavoidable; for since the observations that serve for a single investigation are 4 in number — that is, two different polar altitudes and two different star elevations, made by different observers, in different places and with different instruments — anyone with any knowledge of the practice will say it can't be that among all 4 no error slipped in, especially when we see that in taking a single polar altitude — with the same instrument, in the same place, and by the same observer, who could have done it a thousand times — one still wavers by some minutes, and often by many, as you can see in various places in this very book. These things granted, I ask you, Simplicio: do you believe this author holds his 13 observers to be shrewd, intelligent, and skilled in handling such instruments, or rather crude and inexpert men?

Simplicio
It can't be that he reckons them anything but very careful and intelligent. For if he judged them unfit for the work, he might as well throw out his own book as proving nothing, being founded on suppositions full of errors; and he'd be writing us off as too simple, if he thought he could use their inexperience to persuade us that a false proposition of his was true.

Salviati
Then, since these observers are such, and yet for all that have erred — so that their errors must be amended in order to draw from their observations what further knowledge is possible — the fitting thing is to apply to them the smallest and closest amendments and corrections we can, so long as these suffice to draw the observations out of impossibility and into possibility. So that, for instance, if a manifest error and a patent impossibility in one of their observations can be eased by adding or subtracting 2 or 3 minutes, and by that amendment brought within the possible, one shouldn't insist on adjusting it by adding or subtracting 15 or 20 or 50.

Simplicio
I don't believe the author would contradict this; for, granted that they're judicious and expert men, one should sooner believe they erred by a little than by a lot.

Salviati
Now note what follows. Of the places where the new star might be lodged, some are manifestly impossible, others possible. Absolutely impossible is that it lay an infinite interval beyond the fixed stars, for no such site exists in the world — and if it did, the star set there would have been invisible to us. It's also impossible that it went creeping over the Earth's surface, and still more that it lay inside the terrestrial globe itself. The possible places are these ones under dispute, since it doesn't offend our intellect that a visible object, with the look of a star, could be above the Moon no less than below it. Now, while we try to draw out — by way of observations and calculations made with all the certainty human diligence can reach — where its place truly was, we find that most of these calculations set it more than an infinite interval above the firmament, others set it close to the Earth's surface, and some even below that surface; and of the rest, which lodge it in not-impossible places, none agree with one another. So that we're forced to say all the observations are necessarily fallacious; and thus, if we still mean to draw some fruit from so much labor, we must resort to corrections, amending all the observations.

Simplicio
But the author will say that of the observations which set the star in impossible places, no account should be taken, being infinitely erroneous and fallacious; and one should accept only those that place it in not-impossible places, and among these alone go searching, by way of the more probable and more numerous agreements, for — if not the particular and exact site, that is, its true distance from the Earth's center — at least the knowledge of whether it was among the elements or among the celestial bodies.

Salviati
The reasoning you now make is the very one the author has made on behalf of his own cause — but with too unreasonable a disadvantage to the other side. And this is the chief point that has made me marvel beyond measure at the excessive confidence he's taken — no less in his own authority than in the blindness and heedlessness of the astronomers. I'll speak for them, and you'll answer for the author. And first I ask you: when the astronomers observe with their instruments, seeking, say, how great a star's elevation above the horizon is, can they stray from the truth as much toward the more as toward the less — that is, err by making it sometimes higher than the truth and sometimes lower — or can the error only ever be of one kind: that in erring they always sin toward excess and never toward the less, or always toward the less and never toward excess?

Simplicio
I've no doubt it's equally easy to err in the one way as in the other.

Salviati
I believe the author would answer the same. Now, of these two kinds of error — which are contrary, and into which the observers of the new star may equally have fallen — when applied to the calculation, one kind will make the star higher than the truth, and the other lower. And since we already agree that all the observations are erroneous, on what grounds does this author want us to accept as more in keeping with the truth those that show the star to have been near, rather than the others that show it excessively far?

Simplicio
From what I seem to have gathered from what's been said so far, I don't see that the author refuses those observations and investigations that would set the star farther than the Moon, or even farther than the Sun — but only those that make it remote (as you yourself have said) by more than an infinite interval. And since you too refuse that distance as impossible, he passes over such observations as infinitely convicted of falsity and impossibility. It seems to me, then, that if you want to convince the author, you ought to produce investigations more exact, or more numerous, or by more diligent observers, which set the star at such-and-such a distance above the Moon or above the Sun — in a place, in short, where it could possibly be — just as he produces these twelve, all of which set the star below the Moon, in places that exist in the world and where it could have been.

Salviati
Now — now, Simplicio, here is where the misunderstanding lies, yours and the author's: yours in one respect, and the author's in another. I gather from your talk that you've formed the notion that the wildly exaggerated figures one arrives at in fixing the star's distance grow in proportion to the errors made on the instrument in taking the observations; and that, conversely, from the size of those wild figures one can argue the size of the errors; and that therefore, on hearing it said that a given observation yields the star's distance as infinite, the error in observing must necessarily have been infinite too, and hence beyond amending and so to be refused. But the business, my dear Simplicio, doesn't run that way. And for not having grasped how this matter stands, I excuse you, as inexperienced in such dealings — but I cannot cloak the author's error under a like mantle. For he, disguising his understanding of it (having persuaded himself that we truly weren't going to understand), has hoped to make use of our ignorance in order to lend his doctrine more credit among the crowd of the little-understanding. So, as a warning to those who are more credulous than discerning, and to draw you out of your error, know this: it may be — and most of the time it will happen — that an observation which gives you the star, for example, at the distance of Saturn, will, by adding or subtracting a single minute from the elevation taken with the instrument, make it come out at an infinite distance, and so turn it from possible to impossible; and conversely, those calculations built on such observations that render the star infinitely far, many a time may, by adding or shaving off a single minute, pull it back to a possible site. And what I say of one minute can happen with a correction of a half, or a sixth, or less. Now fix it well in your mind that at the very highest distances — such as, for example, the altitude of Saturn or that of the fixed stars — the very tiniest errors made by the observer on the instrument turn the site from determinate and possible into infinite and impossible. It doesn't work that way with the sublunar distances near the Earth, where it can happen that an observation from which the star was gathered to be, say, 4 terrestrial semidiameters away, can be increased or diminished not merely by a minute, but by ten, and a hundred, and much more, without the calculation rendering it even higher than the Moon, let alone infinitely remote. Understand from this that the size of the errors — the instrumental errors, so to speak — is not to be judged from the outcome of the calculation, but from the very quantity of the degrees and minutes counted off on the instrument. And those observations are to be called more correct, or less erroneous, which, with the addition or subtraction of fewer minutes, restore the star to a possible place; and among the possible places, the true site must be believed to be the one around which the greater number of the distances concur, as calculated upon the more correct observations.
