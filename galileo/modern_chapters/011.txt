The First Day

(Part 11 of 12)

Salviati
If this philosopher had been the first author of that opinion, I wouldn't be surprised that he was so fond of it he took it for true. But since he received it from others, I can't find any excuse good enough for his failure to see through its fallacies — above all after he had heard the true cause of the effect, and could have assured himself, by a thousand trials and plain confirmations, that it comes from the reflection of the Earth and from nothing else. And as much as that knowledge makes us wish for a little more discernment in this author, and in everyone else who withholds assent, so much does the fact that it was never understood — never even occurred to them — excuse the older writers, who, I'm quite certain, would admit it now without the slightest resistance if they understood it. And if I'm to tell you my honest thought: I can't believe this modern author doesn't privately accept it. I suspect, rather, that not being able to claim it as his own invention goads him a little into trying to suppress it, or at least to discredit it in the eyes of the simple — whose numbers, we know, are enormous. And there are plenty of men who enjoy the loud applause of the crowd far more than the assent of the discerning few.

Sagredo
Hold on a moment, Salviati — I don't think you're going straight to the real point. These men who spread their nets for the public also know how to pass themselves off as the authors of other people's inventions, provided the inventions aren't so old, so broadcast from the lecture halls and the piazzas, that everyone already knows them by heart.

Salviati
Oh, I'm more wicked than you are. Why talk about "public" or "well known"? Isn't it all the same whether the opinions and inventions are new to the men, or the men are new to them? If you'd settle for the admiration of beginners in the sciences — and a fresh crop comes up every season — you could set yourself up as the inventor of the alphabet itself and be their marvel. And if in time your little game were discovered, it would hardly hurt your purpose, since new recruits step in to keep the ranks of your admirers full.

But let's get back to showing Simplicio how feeble his modern author's arguments are — arguments containing falsehoods, non-sequiturs, and sheer implausibilities. First of all, it is false that this secondary light is brighter around the outer rim than in the middle, forming a sort of ring or circle more brilliant than the rest of the field. It's true that when you look at the Moon in the twilight, at its first appearance, such a circle does show — but it's an illusion, born of the different borders that bound the lunar disc bathed in this secondary light. On the side toward the Sun, it borders the dazzling horns of the Moon; on the other side, its neighbor is the dark field of the twilight sky, and by that contrast the pale glow of the lunar disc looks brighter to us, while on the opposite side the same glow is drowned out by the greater splendor of the horns. If our modern author had tried placing an obstacle between his eye and the primary brilliance — the roof of some house, or any other screen — so that only the stretch of the Moon outside the horns stayed visible, he would have seen it all equally luminous.

Simplicio
I do seem to remember that he writes of having used exactly that kind of trick to hide the bright sickle from himself.

Salviati
Oh — then what I was charitably calling an oversight becomes a lie, and a reckless one at that, since anyone can repeat the test as often as he likes. As for seeing the Moon's disc during an eclipse of the Sun in any way except as a dark absence, I doubt it very much — especially when the eclipse isn't total, as the ones this author observed must necessarily have been. But even if the disc did show as luminous then, that wouldn't contradict our opinion; it would favor it, because at that moment the entire terrestrial hemisphere lit by the Sun is facing the Moon, and though the Moon's shadow darkens a part of it, that part is tiny compared with what stays illuminated.

What he adds — that in this case the part of the rim lying under the Sun shows quite bright, but not the part outside it, because the Sun's rays reach our eye directly through the one part and not the other — is truly one of those fables that give away all the teller's other fictions. If the Sun's rays have to come straight to our eye for the lunar disc to be visible by secondary light, doesn't the poor man see that we would then never see that secondary light except during a solar eclipse? And if a part of the Moon standing less than half a degree away from the Sun's disc can deflect the solar rays enough to keep them from our eye, what happens when the Moon stands twenty or thirty degrees away, as it does at its first appearance? How are the Sun's rays, which are supposed to pass clean through the body of the Moon, going to find our eye then?

This man goes along, step by step, imagining things as they would need to be to serve his purpose, instead of adjusting his purpose, step by step, to things as they are. Watch: to let the Sun's splendor penetrate the Moon's substance, he makes that substance partly transparent — transparent, say, the way a cloud or a crystal is. But I don't know what he'd make of that transparency once the Sun's rays had to penetrate a depth of cloud more than two thousand miles thick. Suppose he answers boldly that this can perfectly well happen in celestial bodies, which are quite another affair from these impure and dreggy elemental bodies of ours; then let's convict him of his error by means that admit no reply — or rather, no subterfuge. If he wants to maintain that the Moon's substance is transparent, he'll have to say it is transparent when the Sun's rays must pass through its whole depth — more than two thousand miles of it — and yet, when faced with barely a single mile, it blocks them as stubbornly as one of our mountains would.

Sagredo
You remind me of a man who wanted to sell me a secret: how to speak with someone two or three thousand miles away, by means of a certain sympathy between magnetized needles. I told him I'd gladly buy it, but that I wanted to see a trial first, and that it would satisfy me to make it with me standing in one room of my house and him in another. He answered that at so short a distance the operation couldn't be seen properly. So I showed him the door, telling him I didn't feel like traveling to Cairo or to Muscovy just then to watch the demonstration — but if he wanted to go himself, I would hold up the other end from Venice. But let's hear where the author's own logic lands him, and how he's forced to admit that the Moon's material is supremely permeable to the Sun's rays through a depth of two thousand miles, yet more opaque than one of our mountains across the thickness of a single mile.

Salviati
The Moon's own mountains are the witnesses. Struck by the Sun on one side, they cast pitch-black shadows on the other — sharper-edged and more cleanly cut, by far, than the shadows of ours. If they were transparent, we could never have detected the least roughness in the Moon's surface, nor seen those luminous peaks standing detached beyond the line that divides the lit part from the dark. Still less would we see that line itself so distinctly, if it were true that the Sun's light sank deep into the Moon. On the author's own account, in fact, the passage and border between the part the Sun sees and the part it doesn't would have to look thoroughly blurred, a muddle of light and darkness — because material that lets the Sun's rays through two thousand miles of depth must be so transparent that it would scarcely resist them across a hundredth of that thickness or less. Yet the line separating the illuminated part from the dark one is as clean and distinct as the line between white and black — above all where the cut crosses the naturally brighter and rougher part of the Moon. Where it slices across the ancient spots, which are plains, the edge isn't so sharp — those plains curve away with the sphere, so they catch the Sun's rays very obliquely, and the fainter illumination softens the line.

Finally, what he says about the secondary light neither shrinking nor dimming as the Moon waxes, but keeping the same strength throughout, is utterly false. It's barely visible at quadrature — the half-Moon — precisely when it ought to show at its liveliest, since it can then be seen clear of twilight, in the dead of night. Let us conclude, therefore, that the Earth's reflection is very powerful upon the Moon. And — this is what you should prize most — draw from it one more beautiful correspondence: if it's true that the planets act upon the Earth by their motion and their light, then perhaps the Earth is no less able to act back upon them with the same light, and, for all we know, with motion too. And even if the Earth didn't move, the same action could remain; for, as we've seen, the action of the light is exactly the same — reflected sunlight — and motion does nothing but vary the aspects, which change in exactly the same way whether you make the Earth move and the Sun stand still, or the other way around.

Simplicio
You will find no philosopher who has said that these lower bodies act on the celestial ones — and Aristotle says plainly the contrary.

Salviati
Aristotle and the others who didn't know that the Earth and the Moon light each other in turn deserve to be excused. But they would richly deserve blame if, while demanding that we grant and believe on their word that the Moon acts on the Earth by its light, they then turned around and denied — to us, who have taught them that the Earth illuminates the Moon — any action of the Earth upon the Moon.

Simplicio
The plain truth is, I feel an extreme reluctance in myself to admit this fellowship you want to talk me into between the Earth and the Moon — enrolling the Earth, as the saying goes, in the company of the stars. If nothing else, the sheer separation and distance between it and the celestial bodies seems to me to force the conclusion that their natures are enormously dissimilar.

Salviati
See, Simplicio, what an inveterate attachment and a deep-rooted opinion can do. It is strong enough to make the very things you produce against yourself look like points in your favor. For if separation and distance are valid grounds for persuading you of a great diversity of natures, then nearness and contact must, by the same token, imply similarity — and how much nearer is the Moon to the Earth than to any other celestial globe? Confess, then, by your own concession — and you'll have other philosophers for company — that there is a very great affinity between the Earth and the Moon. Now let's push on: tell us whether anything remains to consider among the difficulties you raised against the correspondences between these two bodies.

Simplicio
There would remain something about the solidity of the Moon — which I argued from its being supremely polished and smooth, and you from its being mountainous. And another difficulty occurred to me, from my belief that the sea's reflection ought to be stronger than the land's, because of the evenness of its surface, while the land's surface is so rough and dull.

Salviati
As to the first doubt: just as among the parts of the Earth — all of which, by their heaviness, conspire to press as close as they can to the center — some nonetheless remain farther out than others, the mountains more than the plains, and this because of their solidity and hardness (if they were made of fluid matter, they would level out); so, in the same way, seeing some parts of the Moon standing raised above the sphere of the lower parts tells us they are hard. For it's credible that the Moon's material gathers itself into a spherical shape by the concordant conspiring of all its parts toward the same center.

As for the other doubt: from what we've already observed happening with mirrors, we can understand perfectly well that the reflection of light coming from the sea is far weaker than the one coming from land — speaking, that is, of the universal reflection. As for the particular reflection that a surface of calm water sends toward one determinate place, there's no doubt that whoever stands in that place will see a very powerful reflection in the water; but from every other spot the water's surface will look darker than the land's. And to show it to your senses, let's step into the hall here and pour a little water on the floor. Now tell me: doesn't this wet brick show much darker than the dry ones? Certainly it does — and it will look that way from any point you view it, with one single exception: the spot where the reflection of the light coming in at that window lands. So back away, slowly.

Simplicio
From here I see the wet part brighter than the rest of the floor, and I see that it happens because the reflection of the light coming in at the window is heading toward me.

Salviati
All the wetting has done is fill in the little hollows in the brick and reduce its surface to an exquisite flatness, so that the reflected rays leave it united, all toward the same place. The rest of the floor, dry, keeps its roughness — an innumerable variety of tilts among its tiniest particles — so the reflections of the light go off in every direction, but weaker than if they all traveled united together. That's why its look changes little or nothing as you view it from different sides; it shows the same from everywhere, but far less bright than the reflection from the wet part. I conclude, therefore, that the surface of the sea, seen from the Moon, would look extremely even — islands and reefs aside — but less bright than the surface of the land, mountainous and uneven as it is.

And if I weren't afraid of seeming, as they say, to want too much, I'd tell you that I have observed that secondary light on the Moon — the light I say comes to it from the reflection of the terrestrial globe — to be noticeably brighter two or three days before conjunction than after it: that is, brighter when we see the Moon in the east before dawn than when it shows in the west in the evening, after sunset. The cause of the difference is that the terrestrial hemisphere facing the eastern Moon has little sea and a very great deal of land — all of Asia — whereas when the Moon is in the west, it looks down on immense seas: the whole Atlantic Ocean, clear to the Americas. A rather probable argument that the surface of water shows itself less splendid than the surface of land.

Simplicio
But do you actually believe, then, that those great spots we see in the face of the Moon are seas, and the brighter remainder land, or something of that sort?

Salviati
What you're asking is the beginning of the disparities that I hold to exist between the Moon and the Earth — and it's time we worked our way through those, for we have lingered on this Moon quite long enough. I say, then, that if nature had only one way of making two sunlit surfaces appear one brighter than the other — namely, by one being land and the other water — then we would necessarily have to say the Moon's surface is part earthy and part watery. But because we know of several ways to produce the same effect, and there may be still others unknown to us, I would not dare affirm that this one rather than that exists on the Moon.

We saw above how a plate of whitened silver, touched with the burnisher, goes from bright white to dark. The wet part of the Earth shows darker than the dry. On mountain ridges, the wooded stretches appear much duskier than the bare and barren ones, because among the trees a great quantity of shadow falls, while the open ground lies fully lit by the Sun. And this mingling of shadows works so strongly that in figured velvet the cut silk shows much darker than the uncut, thanks to the shadows scattered between thread and thread — and plain velvet, likewise, much darker than a smooth taffeta made of the very same silk. So if there were things on the Moon that resembled immense forests, their aspect could well present us with the spots we see; the spots would show a like difference if they were seas; and, finally, nothing rules out those spots really being of a darker color than the rest — after all, that is how snow makes mountains look brighter.

What is plainly seen on the Moon is this: the darker parts are all plains, with few reefs and ridges inside them, though there are some; the brighter remainder is all full of reefs, mountains, little rounded ridges, and other shapes; and in particular, around the spots run enormous chains of mountains. That the spots are flat surfaces we can be sure of, from seeing that the line dividing the lit part from the dark cuts evenly where it crosses the spots, while in the bright parts it shows jagged and toothed everywhere. But whether that evenness of surface is enough, by itself, to account for the darkness — that I don't know; I rather think not.

Beyond all this, I judge the Moon to be profoundly different from the Earth. Though I imagine those regions are not idle and dead, I still would not assert that motion and life exist there — much less that plants, animals, or other things like ours are generated there; and if anything is, it would be utterly different, remote from everything we can imagine. I'm moved to think so, first, because I judge that the material of the lunar globe is not earth and water, and that alone is enough to rule out generations and alterations like ours. But even granting that there were water and earth up there, plants and animals like ours still would not arise, and for two principal reasons.

The first is that the varying aspects of the Sun are so necessary to our generations that without them everything would fail — and the Sun's dispositions toward the Earth are very different from its dispositions toward the Moon. As for daily illumination, we, over most of the Earth, get part day and part night in every twenty-four hours; on the Moon, that effect takes a month. And the yearly dipping and climbing by which the Sun brings us the changing seasons and the inequality of days and nights is likewise finished, on the Moon, in a month. And whereas for us the Sun rises and sinks so far that between its greatest and least altitude there runs a difference of about forty-seven degrees — the distance from one tropic to the other — on the Moon it comes to no more than ten degrees or a little over, which is as far as the greatest latitudes of the dragon, the Moon's tilted path, stray on either side of the ecliptic. Now consider what the Sun's action would be within the torrid zone if it went on striking it with its rays for fifteen days without a break: you will grasp at once that all the plants and grasses and animals would be destroyed; and if generations did occur there, they would be of grasses, plants, and animals utterly different from the present ones.

Second, I hold it for certain that there are no rains on the Moon; because if clouds gathered anywhere on it, as they do around the Earth, they would hide from us some of the things we see on the Moon with the telescope — in short, they would change the view in one particular or another. And that is an effect I have never once seen in long and careful observation: I have always found there a uniform, perfectly pure serenity.
