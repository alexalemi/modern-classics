The First Day

(Part 3 of 12)

Sagredo
That takes care of my biggest scruple: I now see that what looked to me like a contradiction is not only possible but — I'll go further — necessary. What I still don't see, though, is that one of these possible or necessary cases is the one we actually need right now. Is it really true that the time of descent along CA stands to the time of fall along CB in the same proportion as the line CA stands to the line CB, so that we can say, without contradiction, that the speeds along the incline CA and along the vertical CB are equal?

Salviati
Be content, for now, that I've cleared away your disbelief; the science itself you must wait for until another occasion — namely, when you see what our Academician has demonstrated about local motions. There you'll find it proved that in the time the falling body drops through the whole of CB, the other body descends along CA only as far as the point T, where the perpendicular drawn down from B cuts it. And to find where the body falling down the vertical would be at the moment the other one reaches A, draw the perpendicular to CA from A itself, extending it and CB until they meet: that intersection is the point you're looking for. In the meantime, notice how this shows that the motion along CB really is faster than along the incline CA (taking C as the starting point of the two motions we're comparing): the line CB is longer than CT, and the line from C down to where CB's extension meets the perpendicular drawn from A is longer than CA, and so the motion along it is faster than along CA. But when we compare the motion through the whole of CA not with the whole motion made in that same time along the extended vertical, but with the motion made in a part of that time through the part CB alone, then there's nothing absurd in this: the body on CA, continuing its descent past T, can reach A in a time that stands to the other time in exactly the proportion the line CA has to the line CB.

Now let's take up our original point again, which was to show that a heavy body, starting from rest, passes in its descent through every degree of slowness before reaching any degree of speed it acquires. Going back to the same figure, remember what we had agreed: that the body falling down the vertical CB and the one descending along the incline CA arrive at B and A having acquired equal degrees of speed. Moving on from there, I don't think you'll have the least difficulty granting that on another plane tilted less steeply than AC — say, DA — the descending motion would be slower still than on the plane CA. So there's no doubt whatever that planes can be marked out so slightly raised above the horizontal AB that the moving body — our same ball — would take as long as you please to reach the point A; after all, to get there along the plane BA itself, even infinite time isn't enough, and the motion grows ever slower as the slope grows smaller. So you must necessarily admit that we can take a point above B, so close to B, that if we drew a plane from it to A, the ball wouldn't cross it even in a year.

Now here is what you need to know: the impetus — that is, the degree of speed — that the ball has acquired by the time it arrives at point A is such that, if it kept moving at that same degree uniformly, neither speeding up nor slowing down, then in a second interval equal to the time of its descent it would cover a distance twice the length of the inclined plane. For example: if the ball had taken an hour to travel the plane DA, then, continuing uniformly at the speed it had on reaching A, it would in one hour cover a distance twice the length of DA. And since (as we were saying) the degrees of speed acquired at B and at A — by bodies that set out from any point you please on the vertical CB, one descending along the inclined plane and the other straight down the vertical — are always equal, it follows that the falling body can start from a point so close to B that the degree of speed acquired at B would not be enough (kept forever constant) to carry it through a distance twice the length of the inclined plane in a year, or in ten years, or in a hundred.

So we can conclude as follows. Suppose it's true that, in the ordinary course of nature, a body — with all external and accidental hindrances removed — moves down inclined planes more and more slowly as the tilt gets smaller, until finally the slowness becomes infinite, which happens when the tilt runs out altogether and we arrive at the horizontal plane. And suppose it's equally true that the degree of speed acquired at some point on the inclined plane is equal to the degree of speed of the body falling down the vertical, at the point cut off by a horizontal line drawn through that same point of the incline. Then we are forced to admit that a falling body, starting from rest, passes through all the infinite degrees of slowness; and consequently, that to acquire any given degree of speed it must first move in a straight line, descending through a short or a long distance according as the speed to be acquired is smaller or greater, and according as the plane of descent is gently or steeply tilted. So there can be a plane with so little tilt that, to acquire a given degree of speed on it, the body would first have to move through an immense distance, over an immense time. On the horizontal plane, then, no speed at all will ever be acquired naturally, since there the body will never move in the first place. But motion along a horizontal line, which slopes neither down nor up, is circular motion around the center. Therefore circular motion is never acquired naturally without straight motion coming first; but once it has been acquired, it will continue perpetually at uniform speed.

I could explain these same truths to you, and even demonstrate them, by other lines of argument; but I don't want to interrupt our main discussion with such long digressions. I'll come back to them on some other occasion — all the more since we got onto this point not to use it as a necessary demonstration, but to dress up a Platonic conceit. And to that conceit I want to add one further particular observation, again our Academician's, and it has something marvelous about it. Let's imagine that among the decrees of the divine Architect was the thought of creating in the world these globes we see continually moving in circles; of establishing the center of their revolutions, and placing there the Sun, immovable; and then of fashioning all those globes in one and the same place, and giving them from there an inclination to move, descending toward the center, until they had acquired those degrees of speed that seemed right to the divine Mind itself — whereupon, the speeds acquired, they were turned into circulation, each in its own circle, keeping the speed it had already gained. The question is: at what height, at what distance from the Sun, was the place where those globes were first created — and could they all have been created in the same place? To carry out the investigation, you take from the most expert astronomers the sizes of the circles in which the planets revolve, and likewise the times of their revolutions. From those two pieces of knowledge you work out how much faster, say, Jupiter's motion is than Saturn's; and having found (as is in fact the case) that Jupiter moves faster, it must be that, both having set out from the same height, Jupiter descended farther than Saturn — as we know is really true, his orbit lying below Saturn's. But pressing further: from the proportion between the speeds of Jupiter and Saturn, from the distance between their orbits, and from the ratio of acceleration in natural motion, one can work out at what height, at what distance from the center of their revolutions, lay the place they set out from. Once that's found and fixed, we ask whether Mars, descending from there down to his own orbit, checks out — and we find that the size of his orbit and the speed of his motion agree with what the calculation gives us. The same is done for the Earth, for Venus, and for Mercury; and the sizes of their circles and the speeds of their motions come so close to what the computations yield that it is a thing of wonder.

Sagredo
I've listened to this idea with the keenest pleasure; and if I didn't think that carrying out those calculations precisely would be a long and laborious undertaking — and perhaps too hard for me to follow — I'd insist you do it here and now.

Salviati
The operation really is long and difficult, and besides, I wouldn't trust myself to reconstruct it on the spot; so we'll save it for another time. For now let's return to our original subject, picking up right where we digressed. If I remember correctly, we were in the middle of establishing that motion in a straight line can be of no use whatever among the parts of a world in good order. And we were going on to say that it's just the opposite with circular motions: the one a body makes turning on itself keeps it always in the same place, while the one that carries a body around the circumference of a circle, about its own stable and fixed center, puts neither the body nor its neighbors into any disorder. For such motion, in the first place, is finite and bounded — indeed, not just finite and bounded, but there is no point on the circumference that isn't at once the first and the last terminus of the circuit; and continuing around its assigned circumference, the body leaves everything else, inside that circle and out, free for the needs of others, without ever hindering or disturbing them. Now, this being a motion in which the moving body is forever departing from its terminus and forever arriving at it, circular motion alone, in the first place, can be uniform. For acceleration happens in a body when it's moving toward a terminus it feels an inclination for, and slowing happens through its reluctance to leave that terminus and move away; and since in circular motion the body is always departing from its natural terminus and always moving toward that very same terminus, the reluctance and the inclination in it are always of equal strength. From that equality comes a speed neither slowed nor quickened — that is, uniform motion. And from this uniformity, and from the motion's being bounded, perpetual continuation can follow, the circuits repeating forever — which can never be found naturally on an unbounded line, or in a motion that is continually slowing or accelerating. And I say naturally, because straight motion that slows down is violent motion — forced motion — which cannot be perpetual; and accelerated straight motion necessarily arrives at its terminus, if there is one; and if there isn't one, there can't be any motion toward it either, for nature does not set out for a place it is impossible to reach. I conclude, therefore, that circular motion alone can naturally suit the natural bodies that make up the universe and stand in their best arrangement; and that straight motion, at the very most, is assigned by nature to bodies and their parts only when they find themselves outside their proper places, stuck in a bad arrangement, and therefore in need of getting back to their natural state by the shortest road. From this it seems to me we can quite reasonably conclude that, to maintain perfect order among the parts of the world, the movable ones must be movable only in circles; and if there are any that do not move circularly, they must of necessity be immovable — for nothing except rest and circular motion is fit for the preservation of order. And I'm more than a little surprised that Aristotle, who after all held that the terrestrial globe is located at the center of the world and stays there immovably, didn't say that among natural bodies some are movable by nature and others immovable — especially since he had already defined nature as the principle of motion and of rest.

Simplicio
Aristotle, being a man who never promised himself more from his own genius — piercing as it was — than is fitting, held in his philosophizing that the experiences of the senses must be set above any argument fabricated by human ingenuity; and he said that those who would deny the senses deserved to be punished by being deprived of the sense in question. Now, who is so blind that he cannot see the parts of earth and of water moving, as heavy things do, naturally downward — that is, toward the center of the universe, appointed by nature herself as the goal and terminus of straight motion deorsum — downward? And who cannot see, just as plainly, fire and air moving straight up toward the concave arch of the lunar orb, as toward the natural terminus of motion sursum — upward? And this being so manifestly seen, and since we are certain that eadem est ratio totius et partium — the same reasoning holds for the whole as for its parts — why shouldn't we call it a true and manifest proposition that the natural motion of earth is straight motion ad medium — toward the center — and that of fire, straight motion a medio — away from it?

Salviati
On the strength of this argument of yours, the very most you could claim to have won is this: that just as the parts of the earth, when removed from their whole — that is, from the place where they naturally reside, and so, in the end, put into a bad and disordered arrangement — return to their place spontaneously, and therefore naturally, in straight motion, so (granting that eadem sit ratio totius et partium — that whole and parts follow the same rule) one might infer that if the terrestrial globe were forcibly removed from the place nature assigned it, it would go back there in a straight line. That, as I say, is the most that could be conceded to you, and only after granting you every sort of accommodation along the way. But anyone who wanted to audit these accounts rigorously would first deny that the parts of the earth, in returning to their whole, move in a straight line at all, rather than in a circular one, or in some mixture of the two; and you would certainly have your work cut out demonstrating the contrary, as you'll see clearly in the replies to the particular reasons and experiments brought forward by Ptolemy and Aristotle. Secondly, if someone told you that the parts of the earth move not in order to go to the center of the world but to go and reunite with their whole, and that this is why their natural inclination is toward the center of the terrestrial globe — an inclination by which they conspire to form it and preserve it — then what other whole, what other center, would you find in the world for the entire terrestrial globe to seek to return to, if it were removed, so that the reasoning about the whole would still match the reasoning about the parts? Add to this that neither Aristotle nor you will ever prove that the Earth is de facto — in actual fact — at the center of the universe; if any center can be assigned to the universe at all, we shall find it is rather the Sun that sits there, as you'll come to understand as we proceed.

Now, just as from the concordant conspiring of all the parts of the earth to form their whole it follows that they converge on it from every side with equal inclination, and, to unite as closely as possible, fit themselves together into a sphere — why shouldn't we believe that the Moon, the Sun, and the other bodies of the world are likewise round for no other reason than the concordant instinct and natural convergence of all their component parts? And if at some time one of those parts were separated from its whole by some violence, isn't it reasonable to believe it would return there spontaneously, by natural instinct? And in this way to conclude that straight motion belongs equally to all the bodies of the world?

Simplicio
There isn't the least doubt that if you're prepared to deny not only the first principles of the sciences but manifest experience and the senses themselves, you can never be convinced or budged from any opinion you've conceived; and I will hold my peace, rather because contra negantes principia non est disputandum — with those who deny first principles there is no arguing — than because your reasons have persuaded me. But staying with what you've just now pronounced — since you throw doubt on whether even the motion of heavy bodies is straight — how can you ever reasonably deny that the parts of earth, that is, the heaviest of materials, descend toward the center in straight motion? When they're dropped from a very tall tower, with walls dead straight and built plumb, they come down grazing the wall, so to speak, and strike the ground at exactly — to a hair — the point where a plumb bob would land, hung from a cord fixed above at the very spot the stone was released. Isn't that a more than evident argument that this motion is straight, and toward the center? In the second place, you call into doubt whether the parts of the earth move, as Aristotle affirms, toward the center of the world — as though he hadn't demonstrated it conclusively from contrary motions, arguing like this: the motion of heavy things is contrary to that of light things; but the motion of light things is seen to be straight upward, that is, toward the circumference of the world; therefore the motion of heavy things is straight toward the center of the world, and it happens per accidens — merely incidentally — that it is toward the center of the Earth, since the one center happens to coincide with the other. As for asking what a piece of the lunar globe or of the Sun would do if it were separated from its whole — that's an idle question, because it asks what would follow from an impossibility; for, as Aristotle also demonstrates, celestial bodies are impervious to change, impenetrable, and unbreakable, so the case can never arise. And even if it did arise, and the separated piece returned to its whole, it wouldn't return as a heavy body or a light one — for the same Aristotle proves that celestial bodies are neither heavy nor light.

Salviati
How reasonable my doubt is, about whether heavy bodies move in a straight and plumb line, you'll hear when I come to examine that particular argument, as I said just now. As for the second point, I'm amazed you should need Aristotle's paralogism — his flawed reasoning — exposed for you, when it's so obvious on its face, and that you don't notice Aristotle is assuming the very thing in question. So observe...

Simplicio
Please, Salviati — speak of Aristotle with more respect. To whom could you ever sell the idea that a man who was the first, the only, the admirable expounder of the syllogistic form, of demonstration, of refutations, of the ways of detecting sophisms and paralogisms — in short, of the whole of logic — should then blunder so gravely as to assume as known the very thing in question? Gentlemen, first understand him perfectly; then try your hand at refuting him.

Salviati
Simplicio, we're here talking among ourselves, familiarly, to investigate some truth. I'll never take it badly that you point out my errors; and whenever I've failed to grasp Aristotle's meaning, correct me freely — I'll thank you for it. In the meantime, allow me to lay out my difficulties, and to answer a word or two of what you just said. Logic, as you know perfectly well, is the organ with which we philosophize — the instrument of the trade. But just as a craftsman can be excellent at building organs and yet untrained in playing them, so a man can be a great logician and yet little practiced in actually using logic — just as there are many who know the whole art of poetry by heart and then are hopeless at composing four lines of verse, and others who possess every one of Leonardo's precepts and couldn't paint a footstool. Playing the organ isn't learned from those who know how to build organs, but from those who know how to play them; poetry is learned from constant reading of the poets; painting is picked up by constant drawing and painting; and demonstration is learned from reading books full of demonstrations — and those are the books of the mathematicians alone, not of the logicians. Now, back to our subject. What Aristotle sees, concerning the motion of light bodies, is fire departing from any point on the surface of the terrestrial globe and moving straight away from it, rising upward. That truly is moving toward a circumference larger than the Earth's — indeed, Aristotle himself has it move up to the concave arch of the Moon's orb. But that this larger circumference is the circumference of the world, or concentric with it, so that moving toward the one is also moving toward the other — that cannot be affirmed unless you first suppose that the center of the Earth, the point we watch ascending light bodies move away from, is the same as the center of the world. And that is as much as to say that the terrestrial globe sits at the center of the world — which is precisely what we are doubting, and precisely what Aristotle set out to prove. And will you tell me this isn't a manifest paralogism?
