The Third Day

(Part 1 of 16)

Sagredo
The great eagerness with which I've been awaiting your arrival — to hear the fresh thinking about the annual motion, our globe's yearly circuit around the Sun — made the hours of the night seem endless to me, and these morning ones too. Though I didn't waste them: I spent a good part of the night awake, going back over yesterday's discussions in my mind, weighing the arguments each side brought forward for the two opposing positions — that of Aristotle and Ptolemy on the one hand, and that of Aristarchus and Copernicus on the other. And truly it seems to me that whichever of these has gone wrong deserves to be forgiven; so plausible, on the face of it, are the reasons that could have won them over — at least so long as we stick to the ones the great original authors themselves put forward. But since the Peripatetic view, by its sheer antiquity, has had many followers and cultivators, while the other has had very few — first because of its obscurity, then because of its novelty — I seem to notice that among all those many, and the moderns in particular, there are some who, to prop up the opinion they take for true, have brought in other arguments so childish I hardly dare call them anything but ridiculous.

Salviati
The same thing has happened to me — and even more so than to you, since I've heard such things produced that I'd be ashamed to repeat them. Not, I'll admit, to spare the reputations of their authors, whose names can always be kept quiet, but so as not to drag down the honor of the human race itself. In the end, from watching them, I've become convinced that among men there are some who reason backwards: they first plant the conclusion in their heads, and — whether because it's their own or belongs to someone they hold in high esteem — they fix it there so firmly that it becomes utterly impossible to root it out. To any argument that occurs to them, or that they hear others produce in support of their settled notion — however simple and insipid it may be — they instantly give their assent and applause; while to any brought against it, however ingenious and conclusive, they react not merely with distaste but with disdain and the bitterest anger. And some of them, driven by fury, would not even hold back from attempting any contrivance to suppress their opponent and shut him up. I've seen a little of that firsthand.

Sagredo
So these men don't draw their conclusion from their premises, nor establish it by reasons — instead they fit, or rather force and twist, the premises and reasons to fit conclusions they've already settled and nailed down. It's just as well, then, not to test ourselves against such people; all the less so given that their company is not only unpleasant but downright dangerous. So we'll carry on with our own Simplicio, whom I've known for ages as a man of the greatest candor, wholly stripped of any malice. Besides, he's so well versed in Peripatetic doctrine that I can be sure whatever won't occur to him in support of Aristotle's opinion won't easily occur to anyone else. But here he is now, out of breath — the fellow's kept us waiting a good long while today. We were just speaking ill of you.

Simplicio
Don't blame me — blame Neptune for my long delay. This morning's ebb tide pulled the waters back so far that the gondola carrying me, having turned into a certain canal not far from here where there are no foundations along the bank, ran aground on the mud, and I had to sit there more than a solid hour waiting for the sea to come back. And while I was stuck there, unable to step out of the boat, which grounded almost all at once, I found myself watching a detail that struck me as quite marvelous. As the waters fell, you could see them fleeing away very fast through various little channels, the mud already showing in several places; and while I'm busy taking this in, all of a sudden I see the motion stop — and, without a single instant's pause, the very same water begins to run back the other way, the ebb turning straight into flow, without staying still for even a moment. It's an effect I've never happened to see once in all my time in Venice.

Sagredo
You must not have run aground very often among such tiny channels. Since they have so little slope, a fall or rise of even a paper's thickness in the surface of the open sea is enough to send the water racing back and forth through them for quite long stretches — just as, on certain seacoasts, a rise of only four or six braccia — arm-lengths — spreads the water across those flats for many hundreds and thousands of pertiche, the old land-rods.

Simplicio
That much I understand perfectly. But I'd have thought that between the last point of the fall and the first beginning of the rise there had to be some noticeable interval of rest.

Sagredo
That will look true to you when you fix your eyes on the walls or the pilings, where these changes happen straight up and down; but there's no genuine rest in it at all.

Simplicio
It seemed to me that since these are two contrary motions, there had to be some rest in between them — in keeping with Aristotle's doctrine, too, which proves that in puncto regressus mediat quies, that at the turning point a moment of rest steps in.

Sagredo
I remember that passage very well. But I also remember that when I was studying philosophy I wasn't persuaded by Aristotle's demonstration — indeed, I had a good many experiences pointing the other way, which I could lay out for you. But I don't want us wading off into other deep waters, since we've gathered here to discuss our own subject, if it's at all possible, without breaking it off the way we did these past days.

Simplicio
And yet we'll have to — if not break it off, then at least stretch it out considerably. Because when I got home last night I set about rereading the little book of theses, where I find some very conclusive demonstrations against this annual motion attributed to the Earth; and since I didn't trust myself to report them so precisely, I decided to bring the book along with me.

Sagredo
You did well. But if we want to take up our discussion according to yesterday's arrangement, we'll need to hear first what Salviati has to report about the book on the new stars, and then, with no further interruptions, we'll come to the annual motion. Now — what does Salviati have to say about these stars? Were they really carried down from the heavens into these lower regions by the power of the calculations of that author Simplicio brought us?

Salviati
Last night I set about reading through his line of argument, and this morning I gave it another once-over, just to be sure that what I thought I'd read in the evening was actually written there — or whether it had been phantoms and fantastic night-imaginings of mine. And in the end I find, to my great sorrow, that what I'd never have wished to see, for this philosopher's own reputation, is really written and printed there. That he doesn't recognize the emptiness of his whole undertaking seems impossible to me — both because it's far too plain to miss, and because I remember hearing him named with praise by our friend the Academician. It also seems far too unlikely that, to please someone else, he could have brought himself to hold his own reputation so cheap as to publish a work from which he could expect nothing but blame from anyone who knows the subject.

Sagredo
Add that those will amount to fewer than one in a hundred, compared with the crowd who'll celebrate and exalt him above every greatest mind that is or ever was. A man who's known how to defend the Peripatetic unchangeableness of the heavens against a whole army of astronomers — and, to their greater shame, floored them with their own weapons! What can four or six per province, who see through his frivolities, do against the countless multitude who, being unable to detect or grasp them, are swept along by the shouting and applaud him all the louder the less they understand? Add, too, that even those few who do understand will decline to answer writings so shabby and so empty of any real conclusion — and rightly so, for the knowledgeable have no need of it, and for those who don't understand, it's effort thrown away.

Salviati
The punishment best fitted to what they deserve really would be silence — if there weren't other reasons that make speaking up almost necessary. One of them is that we Italians get ourselves passed off, one and all, as ignorant, and give the foreigners north of the Alps something to laugh at — especially those cut off from our religion. I could point you to some quite famous ones who mock our Academician, and every mathematician in Italy, for having let the nonsense of a certain Lorenzini against the astronomers come out into the open and stand there unchallenged. But even that could be let go, compared with a still greater occasion for laughter we might hand them — one that comes precisely from the knowledgeable holding their tongues about the frivolities of opponents like these, who attack doctrines they don't understand.

Sagredo
I want no better example of the sheer insolence of these people — and of the misfortune of a man like Copernicus, exposed to being attacked by someone who doesn't even understand the basic position he's making war on.

Salviati
You'll be no less astonished at the way he sets about refuting the astronomers who maintain that the new stars were higher than the orbs of the planets — and perhaps in the firmament itself.

Sagredo
But how could you, in such a short time, have gone through that whole book? It's a big volume, and the demonstrations must be very many.

Salviati
I stopped at these first refutations of his. In them, with twelve demonstrations founded on the observations of twelve astronomers — all of whom judged that the new star of '72, the one that appeared in Cassiopeia, was in the firmament — he proves the opposite: that it was sublunar, below the Moon. He does it by comparing, two at a time, the meridian altitudes taken by different observers in places of different latitude, proceeding in the manner you'll understand shortly. And since it seems to me, examining this first stretch of his, that I've found this author a very long way from being able to conclude anything against the astronomers in favor of the Peripatetic philosophers — and that, on the contrary, the astronomers' opinion comes out far, far more firmly confirmed — I didn't bother applying the same patience to his other methods, but gave them only a superficial glance, confident that whatever ineffectiveness is in these first attacks runs through the rest as well. And as you'll see, in fact, very few words are enough to demolish this whole work, for all that it's built up with so very many laborious calculations, as you can see. So listen to how I proceed. To transfix his opponents, as I said, with their own weapons, this author takes a great many observations made by the astronomers themselves — there are twelve or thirteen of them in all — and on some part of these he makes his calculations, and concludes that these stars were below the Moon. Now, since I'm quite fond of proceeding by questions, and the author himself isn't here, let Simplicio answer the questions I put with whatever he believes the author would answer. Supposing we're dealing with that star of '72 I mentioned, the one that appeared in Cassiopeia — tell me, Simplicio: do you believe it could have been in several places at once? That is, among the elements, and also among the orbs of the planets, and also above those and among the fixed stars, and also infinitely higher still?

Simplicio
There's no doubt one has to say it was in a single place, at one single, determinate distance from the Earth.

Salviati
Then, if the observations made by the astronomers were correct, and the calculations made by this author weren't in error, it would necessarily follow that from all of the former and all of the latter you'd always gather one and the same distance, exactly. Isn't that so?

Simplicio
My reasoning gets this far — that it would have to be so of necessity; nor do I believe the author would contradict it.

Salviati
But if, out of the many, many computations made, not even two came out agreeing with each other, what would you make of that?

Simplicio
I'd judge that all of them were faulty, either through the fault of the one doing the computing or the failing of the observers. At most I could say that one — one only, and no more — was correct, but I wouldn't be able to pick which one.

Salviati
So would you want to deduce and establish a doubtful conclusion as true, from false foundations? Certainly not. Now, this author's calculations are such that no one of them agrees with another — so you can see how much faith they deserve.

Simplicio
Truly, if the matter stands like that, it's a notable failing.

Sagredo
Let me lend a hand to Simplicio and the author, and say to Salviati that his point would follow of strict necessity only if the author had set out to determine precisely how far the star was from the Earth. But I don't think that was his aim — only to show that from those observations one drew the result that the star was sublunar. So that if, from those observations and from all the computations made on them, the star's altitude always comes out less than the Moon's, that's enough for the author to convict of the crassest ignorance all those astronomers who, through some failing of geometry or arithmetic, hadn't known how to draw true conclusions from their own observations.

Salviati
Then it'll be fitting for me to turn to you, Sagredo, since you're defending this author's doctrine so shrewdly. And to try to make even Simplicio — inexperienced though he is in calculations and demonstrations — at least able to see how inconclusive this author's demonstrations are, let me first point out that both he and all the astronomers he's quarreling with agree on this: the new star had no motion of its own, and only went around with the daily motion of the primum mobile. Where they disagree is over its location. The astronomers put it in the celestial region — that is, above the Moon, and perhaps among the fixed stars — while he judges it near the Earth, that is, below the concave of the lunar orb. Now, since the new star in question stood toward the north and not far from the pole, so that for us northerners it never set, it was an easy matter to take its meridian altitudes with astronomical instruments — both the lowest, below the pole, and the highest, above it. From the comparison of these altitudes, taken from different places on Earth set at various distances from the north — that is, differing from one another in their polar altitudes — one could work out the star's distance. For if the star had been up in the firmament among the other fixed stars, its meridian altitudes, taken at different pole-elevations, would have to differ from one another by the very same differences found among those pole-elevations. For example: if the star's height above the horizon was 30 degrees when taken in the place where the pole's altitude was, say, 45 degrees, then the same star's height would have to have grown by 4 or 5 degrees in those more northerly countries where the pole stood 4 or 5 degrees higher. But if the star's distance from the Earth was quite small compared with that of the firmament, then its meridian altitudes must, as we move north, grow noticeably more than the polar altitudes do. And from that extra growth — that is, from the excess of the increase in the star's height over the increase in the pole's altitude (this is what's called the parallax difference) — one calculates promptly, by a clear and reliable method, the star's distance from the center of the Earth. Now, this author takes the observations made by 13 astronomers at different pole-elevations, and, comparing a small selection of them of his own choosing, he calculates, through twelve pairings, that the new star's altitude was always below the Moon. But he pulls this off only by counting on such crass ignorance in everyone into whose hands his book might fall that it honestly turned my stomach. And I sit here wondering how the other astronomers — and Kepler in particular, against whom this author chiefly rails — can keep so silent, when the tongue doesn't usually die in his mouth. Unless, that is, he judged such an undertaking too low to bother with. Now, to put you in the picture, I've copied out onto this sheet the conclusions he draws from his twelve investigations. Of these, the first is from the two observations —

These, then, are twelve investigations the author made at his own choosing, out of the very many that, as he says, could be made from the combinations of the observations of these thirteen observers. And it's fair to assume these twelve are the ones most favorable to proving his case.

Sagredo
But I'd like to know whether, among all the other investigations the author passed over, there are any that told against him — that is, from which, calculating, one would gather that the new star was above the Moon. It seems to me, at first glance, that I can reasonably suspect as much, when I see the ones he did produce differing so wildly among themselves: some give me the new star's distance from the Earth as 4, 6, 10, 100, even a thousand and fifteen hundred times greater than others. So I can well suspect that among the ones he left uncalculated there was some in favor of the opposing side — and I'm all the more inclined to believe it since I don't imagine those observing astronomers lacked the skill and practice for these computations, which I don't think depend on the most abstruse things in the world. In fact it'll strike me as more than miraculous if, when even these twelve investigations alone include some that put the star just a few miles from the Earth, and others that put it below the Moon by a tiny margin, not a single one turns out that, in favor of the other side, puts it even 20 braccia above the lunar orb — and, what would be stranger still, that all those astronomers were so blind they failed to spot a fallacy so glaring.

Salviati
Start bracing your ears now, to hear with boundless amazement the excesses of confidence — in one's own authority and in everyone else's stupidity — to which a man can be carried by the itch to contradict and to show himself cleverer than others. Among the investigations the author left out, there are some that put the new star not merely above the Moon, but above the fixed stars as well; and these aren't a few — they're the majority, as you'll see on this other sheet, where I've written them down.

Sagredo
But what does the author say about these? Perhaps he hasn't taken them into account?

Salviati
He's taken them into account all too well. But he says that the observations on which the calculations put the star infinitely far off are erroneous, and can't be combined with one another.

Simplicio
Oh, that strikes me as a feeble retreat — because the other side could say, with just as much reason, that the erroneous ones are the very observations he's drawing on to place the star down in the elemental region.

Salviati
Oh, Simplicio — if I manage to make you see through the trick of this author, unremarkable as the trick really is, I mean to stir up wonder in you, and even indignation. For then you'd see how he, cloaking his cunning behind the veil of your good faith and that of the other honest philosophers, tries to worm his way into your favor by tickling your ears and puffing up your ambition — parading how he's supposedly convinced and struck dumb these little astronomers who dared to assault the impregnable unchangeableness of the Peripatetic heaven, and, what's more, silenced and beaten them with their own weapons. I mean to give it my best effort. And in the meantime, let Sagredo forgive Simplicio and me for wearying him perhaps a bit too much, while, with a longer circuit of words than his own very quick grasp really needs, I go about laying plain something it's well he shouldn't be left in the dark about.
