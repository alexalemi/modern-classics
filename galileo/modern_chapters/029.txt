The Second Day

(Part 17 of 20)

Simplicio
The objection is in the words that come right after; here they are: Quibus tamen experientia et ratio adversatur — yet experience and reason stand against these.

Salviati
Now I have to give in, since he has so great an advantage over me — namely experience, which I lack; for up to now I've never once had the luck to see the terrestrial globe, together with the element of water, reduced to nothing, so that I might have observed what the hail and the water got up to in this little end-of-the-world. But does he at least tell us, for our instruction, what they were doing?

Simplicio
He doesn't say.

Salviati
I'd pay anything to be able to have a word with this person, to ask him whether, when this globe vanished, it carried off the common center of gravity as well — as I believe it did; in which case I think the hail and the water would have been left dazed and stupefied up among the clouds, with no idea what to do with themselves. It could also be that, drawn by that great empty space left behind by the departure of the terrestrial globe, all the surroundings would rarefy — the air in particular, being supremely stretchable — and would rush in at top speed to fill it; and perhaps the more solid and material bodies, like the birds, of which by rights there must have been many up in the air, would have drawn in more toward the center of the great empty sphere (it seems quite reasonable that substances which pack a lot of matter into a smaller bulk should be assigned the tighter quarters, leaving the roomier ones to the rarer substances); and there, having finally died of hunger and dissolved into earth, they'd have formed a new little globe, with the bit of water that happened to be among the clouds at the time. It could also be that these same materials, seeing no light, wouldn't have noticed the Earth's departure, and would have gone on falling as usual, blindly, expecting to meet it, and little by little would have made their way to the center — where they'd go even now, if the globe itself weren't in the way. And finally, to give this philosopher a less wishy-washy answer, I tell him that I know as much about what would follow the annihilation of the terrestrial globe as he would have known about what was going to happen to it and around it before it was created; and since I'm sure he'd say that he couldn't even have imagined any of the things that in fact followed — of which experience alone has made him the expert — he'll have to grant me pardon and excuse me if I don't know what he knows about the things that would follow the annihilation of that globe, seeing that I lack this experience of his. Now tell me if there's anything else.

Simplicio
There's this figure, which represents the terrestrial globe with a great cavity around its center, filled with air; and to show that heavy bodies don't move downward to join the terrestrial globe, as Copernicus says, he sets this stone at the center and asks what it would do if set free; and he places another in the hollow of this great cavern and puts the same question, saying, as to the first: Lapis in centro constitutus aut ascendet ad Terram in punctum aliquod, aut non: si secundum, falsum est partes ob solam seiunctionem a toto ad illud moveri; si primum, omnis ratio et experientia renititur, neque gravia in suae gravitatis centro conquiescent. Item, si suspensus lapis liberatus decidat in centrum, separabit se a toto, contra Copernicum; si pendeat, refragatur omnis experientia, cum videamus integros fornices corruere — A stone placed at the center will either rise toward the Earth to some point, or it will not: if it will not, it is false that parts move toward the whole merely because they have been separated from it; if it does rise, all reason and experience fight against it, and heavy bodies will not rest at the center of their own gravity. Likewise, if a suspended stone, once released, drops to the center, it separates itself from the whole, against Copernicus; and if it stays hanging, all experience is contradicted, since we see whole vaults collapse.

Salviati
I'll answer, though at great disadvantage to myself, since I'm up against someone who has seen by experience what these stones do in this great cavern — something I've never seen. And I'll say that I believe the heavy bodies come before the common center of gravity; so that it is not a center — which is nothing but an indivisible point, and therefore of no power — that draws the heavy materials to itself, but rather that these materials, conspiring naturally toward union, form for themselves a common center, the one around which parts of equal moment come to rest. So I reckon that, if the great aggregate of heavy bodies were transported to any place whatever, the particles separated from the whole would follow it, and, unimpeded, would penetrate it until they found parts less heavy than themselves; but once they reached the point where they met heavier matter, they would sink no farther. And so I reckon that in the cavern filled with air, the whole vault would press down, and would hold itself up over that air only by force — that is, only when its hardness could not be overcome and broken by its weight; but detached stones, I believe, would descend to the center and would not float on top of the air. Nor could one say for that reason that they weren't moving toward their whole, since they'd be moving to just the place where all the parts of the whole would move, if they weren't impeded.

Simplicio
What's left is a certain error he points out in a follower of Copernicus who, in making the Earth move with the annual and the daily motion in the way that a cart wheel moves along the ground and on its own axle, ended up making either the terrestrial globe too big or the great orb — the annual orbit — too small; since 365 revolutions of the equinoctial are far less than the circumference of the great orb.

Salviati
Watch out — you're mixing it up, and saying the opposite of what must be written in the pamphlet: for it has to say that this author ended up making the terrestrial globe too small or the great orb too big, not the globe too big and the annual orbit too small.

Simplicio
The mix-up isn't mine at all: here are the pamphlet's own words: Non videt quod vel circulum annuum aequo minorem, vel orbem terreum iusto multo fabricet maiorem — He does not see that he makes either the annual circle too small, or the terrestrial globe much too large.

Salviati
Whether the first author erred, I can't know, since the pamphlet's author doesn't name him; but the pamphlet's own error is plain and inexcusable, whether or not that first follower of Copernicus erred, since the pamphleteer passes right over so gross a mistake without noticing it, and neither flags it nor corrects it. But let that be forgiven him, as an error of carelessness rather than anything else. Besides — were I not by now tired and sick of spending any more time, to very little profit, on these very slight squabbles — I could show how it's not impossible for a circle, no bigger even than a cart wheel, by making not just 365 but even fewer than 20 revolutions, to trace out or measure the circumference not only of the great orb but of one a thousand times bigger. I say this to show that there's no shortage of subtleties far greater than the one this author uses to catch Copernicus in error. But please, let's catch our breath a moment, and then come to this other philosopher, another opponent of the same Copernicus.

Sagredo
I really need it too, even though I've only worn out my ears; and if I thought I wasn't going to hear anything more ingenious from this other author, I don't know but I might just decide to go take the cool air out in a gondola.

Simplicio
I think you'll hear things with more weight to them, because this is a most accomplished philosopher, and a great mathematician besides, and he has refuted Tycho on the matter of the comets and the new stars.

Salviati
Is he perhaps the very author of the Anti-Tycho?

Simplicio
He's the very same one: but the refutation against the new stars isn't in the Anti-Tycho, except insofar as he shows there that they were no threat to the inalterability and ungenerability of the heavens, as I already told you. But after the Anti-Tycho, having found a way — by means of parallax — to demonstrate that they too are elemental things, contained within the sphere of the Moon, he wrote this other book, De tribus novis stellis, and so on, and inserted into it his arguments against Copernicus as well. Last time I brought you what he'd written about these new stars in the Anti-Tycho, where he didn't deny that they were up in the heavens, but showed that their appearance didn't upset the heavens' inalterability — and he did this by pure philosophical reasoning, in the way I described. It slipped my mind to tell you how he later found a way to remove them from the heavens, because, since he proceeds in this refutation by way of calculations and parallaxes — matters I understand little or not at all — I hadn't read it, and had only made a study of these objections against the Earth's motion, which are, after all, matters of natural philosophy.

Salviati
I understand perfectly, and it will be fitting — once we've heard the objections to Copernicus — for us to hear, or at least see, the way in which, by means of parallax, he demonstrates that those new stars were elemental — the very stars that so many astronomers of great renown all placed extremely high up, among the stars of the firmament. And if this author brings so great an enterprise to completion — pulling the new stars down out of the heavens and into the elemental sphere — he'll be well worthy of being exalted on high himself and carried up among the stars, or at least of having his name immortalized in fame among them. But let's dispatch as quickly as we can this part that opposes Copernicus's view, and start bringing forward his objections.

Simplicio
There'll be no need to read these ad verbum — word for word — since they're very long-winded; but, as you see, in reading them carefully several times over, I've marked in the margin the words where the whole nerve of the demonstration lies, and it'll be enough to read those. The first argument begins here: Et primo, si opinio Copernici recipiatur, criterium naturalis philosophiae, ni prorsus tollatur, vehementer saltem labefactari videtur — First, if Copernicus's opinion is accepted, the criterion of natural philosophy, if it isn't abolished altogether, seems at the very least severely shaken. This criterion holds — according to the view of every school of philosophers — that sense and experience are our guides in philosophizing; but on Copernicus's assumption the senses come to be greatly deceived, since they plainly see, close up and in the purest media, the heaviest bodies descend straight down along the perpendicular, never deviating so much as a hair from the straight line; and yet, for Copernicus, sight is deceived in a thing so clear, and that motion is not straight at all, but mixed of straight and circular.

Salviati
This is the first argument that Aristotle and Ptolemy and all their followers put forward — to which a full answer has already been given, the paralogism exposed, and it's been made quite plain how the motion shared by us and the other moving bodies is as if it did not exist. But since true conclusions have a thousand favorable confirmations backing them up, I'd like, for this philosopher's sake, to add something more; and you, Simplicio, playing his part, answer my questions. First, tell me: what effect does that stone have on you — the one which, falling from the top of the tower, is the cause of your noticing its motion at all? For if its falling worked nothing more, nothing new, in you than its resting on top of the tower did, you surely wouldn't notice its descent, nor tell its moving apart from its standing still.

Simplicio
I grasp its descent in relation to the tower, because now I see it beside one mark on the tower, then beside a lower one, and so on in succession, until I make it out reaching the ground.

Salviati
So then, if that stone had fallen from the talons of a flying eagle and were coming down through the plain, invisible air, and you had no other visible, fixed object to set it against, you wouldn't be able to grasp its motion?

Simplicio
On the contrary, I would notice it, since, to see it while it's way up high, I'd have to raise my head, and as it came down I'd have to lower it — in short, keep continually moving either my head or my eyes, following along with its motion.

Salviati
Now you've given the true answer. So you recognize that the stone is at rest while, without moving your eye at all, you keep seeing it there in front of you; and you recognize that it's moving when, to keep from losing sight of it, you have to move the organ of sight — that is, the eye. Therefore, whenever you saw an object continuously in the same aspect without ever moving your eye, you would always judge it to be motionless.

Simplicio
I think that would necessarily have to be so.

Salviati
Now imagine you're aboard a ship, and you've fixed your eye on the tip of the yardarm: do you think that, just because the ship was moving — even very fast — you'd need to move your eye to keep your gaze always on the tip of the yardarm and follow its motion?

Simplicio
I'm sure I wouldn't need to make any change at all — and not just my gaze: even if I'd trained the aim of a harquebus on it, I'd never, for any motion of the ship whatever, need to shift it so much as a hair to keep it lined up on the mark.

Salviati
And this happens because the motion the ship imparts to the yardarm, it imparts to you and to your eye as well, so that you don't need to move it at all to keep looking at the top of the yardarm; and consequently it appears motionless to you. Now carry this reasoning over to the whirling of the Earth and to the stone set on top of the tower, in which you can't make out the motion, because the motion needed to follow it you share in common with the stone, from the Earth, and you don't need to move your eye. But when the downward motion is then added — which is the stone's own, and not yours, and which mixes with the circular — the circular part, shared by the stone and your eye, goes on being imperceptible, and only the straight part becomes perceptible, because to follow it you have to move your eye, lowering it. To cure this philosopher of his error, I'd like to be able to tell him: some time when he's out in a boat, let him set up a fairly deep vessel full of water, and fit into it a ball of wax or some other material that sinks to the bottom very slowly — so slowly that in a minute it barely drops a single braccio, an arm-length — and then, driving the boat as fast as it will go, so that in a minute it covers more than a hundred braccia, let him gently drop the ball into the water and let it sink freely, and carefully observe its motion. First of all, he'd see it heading straight toward the point at the bottom of the vessel that it would tend to if the boat were standing still, and to his eye, in relation to the vessel, this motion would look perfectly perpendicular and perfectly straight; and yet one can't say it wasn't compounded of the straight-down and the circular around the element of water. And if these things happen in motions that aren't natural, and in materials on which we can run the experiment both in their state of rest and, oppositely, in motion — and yet, as far as appearances go, no difference is to be seen, and the senses seem to be fooled — what can we hope to make out concerning the Earth, which has forever been in the same condition as regards motion or rest? And at what time do we hope to test on it whether any difference is to be seen between these effects of local motion in its different states of motion and rest, if it eternally keeps to just one of these two?

Sagredo
These discussions have settled my stomach somewhat — those fish and those snails had rather turned it; and the first of them reminded me of the correction of an error so plausible that I'm not sure one in a thousand wouldn't accept it as beyond doubt. It was this: sailing off Syria, and having with me a very good telescope, given me by our mutual friend, who had invented it not many days before, I proposed to the sailors that it would be a great help in navigation to use it up in the maintop to spot ships from far off and identify them. The benefit was granted, but they raised the difficulty of using it, because of the ship's continual pitching and rolling — especially at the top of the mast, where the tossing is so much greater — and said it would be better to use it down at the foot of the mast, where that motion is less than anywhere else on the vessel. I (I won't hide my own mistake) fell in with the same opinion, and for the moment said nothing more; but afterward — I couldn't tell you what prompted me — I went back to chewing the matter over on my own, and finally saw my own simplicity (excusable, though) in accepting as true something utterly false: false, I say, that the maintop's greater tossing, compared with the small motion at the foot of the mast, should make it harder to use the telescope to catch an object.

Salviati
I'd have sided with the sailors — and with you too — at first.

Simplicio
And I likewise would have been, and still am; nor do I think that pondering it for a hundred years I'd understand it any other way.

Sagredo
So this time I'll get to play the schoolmaster over the pair of you (as they say); and since proceeding by questions seems to me to clear things up a great deal — quite apart from the pleasure of tripping up your companion, drawing out of his mouth what he didn't know he knew — I'll make use of that device. First, I'll assume that the ships, galliots, or other craft we're trying to spot and identify are a good way off — say 4, 6, 10, or 20 miles — since for recognizing the ones nearby there's no need of a glass; and consequently the telescope can, at such a distance of 4 or 6 miles, comfortably pick out the whole vessel, and an even bigger hull besides. Now I ask: of what kinds, and how many in number, are the motions made at the maintop, arising from the ship's tossing?

Salviati
Let's suppose the ship is heading east. First, on a perfectly calm sea, there'd be no motion but this forward one; but once the tossing of the waves is added, there'll be one that, raising and lowering the stern and the bow by turns, makes the maintop lean forward and back; other waves, making the vessel heel over, tip the mast right and left; others can turn the ship somewhat and make it swing — by the mizzen, let's say — off the due-east point, now toward the northeast, now toward the southeast; others, heaving the keel up from below, could make the ship, without swinging off course, simply rise and fall. In sum, it seems to me that in kind these motions are two: one that changes the telescope's direction by an angle, and another that changes it — let's say — along a line, without changing the angle: that is, always keeping the tube of the instrument parallel to itself.

Sagredo
Tell me next: if we, having first trained the telescope over there on that tower of Burano, six miles off from here, were to tilt it by an angle right or left, or up or down, by only a nail's-breadth, what effect would it have on our catching that tower?

Salviati
It would make it vanish from sight at once, because such a deflection, however tiny here, can amount over there to hundreds and thousands of braccia.

Sagredo
But if, without changing the angle, always keeping the tube parallel to itself, we shifted it 10 or 12 braccia farther over — right or left, up or down — what effect would that have as regards the tower?

Salviati
Utterly imperceptible; because, since the spaces here and there are contained between parallel rays, the shifts made here and there must be equal; and since the space the instrument takes in over there could hold many of those towers, we wouldn't lose it from sight at all.

Sagredo
Coming back now to the ship, we can state beyond doubt that moving the telescope right or left, up or down, and even forward or back, by 20 or 25 braccia — while always keeping it parallel to itself — cannot throw the line of sight off the observed point on the object by more than those same 25 braccia; and since, at a distance of 8 or 10 miles, the instrument's field takes in a space much wider than the galliot or other vessel being viewed, such a small shift doesn't make me lose it from sight. The hindrance, then, and the cause of losing the object, can come only from the change made by an angle — since, for the ship's tossing, shifting the telescope up or down, right or left, cannot amount to any great number of braccia. Now suppose you have two telescopes fixed in place, one at the lower part of the ship's mast, the other at the top not just of the mast but of the very tallest yardarm — when it's peaked up steeply — and both are trained on a vessel 10 miles off: tell me whether you think that, for any tossing of the ship and leaning of the mast whatever, a greater change of angle is made in the topmost tube than in the lowest. When a wave lifts the bow, it will indeed drive the tip of the yardarm back 30 or 40 braccia more than the foot of the mast, and so will pull the upper tube back by that much space, and the lower by only a single palm; but the angle changes just as much in the one instrument as in the other. Likewise, a wave coming on the beam carries the high tube right and left a hundred times more than the low one; but the angles either don't change or change equally. And whereas the change right or left, forward or back, up or down, brings no perceptible hindrance to the sight of distant objects, the change of angle brings a very great one. So we must necessarily confess that using the telescope at the top of the mast is no harder than at the foot, seeing that the angular changes are equal in both places.
