The First Day

(Part 1 of 12)

Salviati
Yesterday we ended with an agreement: today we would discuss, as clearly and in as much detail as we can manage, the natural arguments — and their real strength — that have so far been offered on either side, by the champions of the Aristotelian and Ptolemaic position and by the followers of the Copernican system. And since Copernicus, by placing the Earth among the moving bodies of the heavens, makes it a globe like a planet, the right place to begin our considerations is by examining just what force and energy there is in the arguments of the Peripatetics — the Aristotelian school — when they set out to demonstrate that this assumption is flatly impossible; given that, on their view, nature requires two substances utterly different from each other, the celestial and the elemental, the one impervious to change and immortal, the other alterable and perishable. Aristotle handles this argument in his books On the Heavens, first working up to it through reasoning that depends on certain general assumptions, and then confirming it with experience and with particular demonstrations. I'll follow his same order: I'll lay the case out, and then say freely what I think of it — submitting myself to your judgment, and especially to that of Simplicio, so vigorous a champion and defender of the Aristotelian doctrine.

The first step in the Peripatetic case is the one where Aristotle proves the completeness and perfection of the world by pointing out that it is not a mere line, nor a bare surface, but a body furnished with length, breadth, and depth; and since there are no dimensions beyond these three, in having them the world has them all, and having all, it is perfect. Now, that from simple length there is constituted the magnitude we call a line; that adding breadth gives us a surface; that adding height or depth on top of that yields a body; and that after these three dimensions there is no passing on to any other, so that in these three alone completeness — totality, so to speak — is exhausted: all this I could well have wished Aristotle had proved to me by strict necessity, above all since it can be done quite clearly and quickly.

Simplicio
What about the beautiful demonstrations in the second, third, and fourth texts, right after the definition of the continuum? Don't you have it there, first of all, that there is no dimension beyond the three because three is everything — three is in every direction? And isn't this confirmed by the authority and doctrine of the Pythagoreans, who say that all things are determined by three — beginning, middle, and end — which is the number of the whole? And where do you leave the other argument: that this number is used, as if by a law of nature, in the sacrifices to the gods? And that nature herself dictates that we give the title "all" to things that come in threes, and to nothing fewer? Of two things we say "both," not "all" — but of three we say it readily. All this doctrine you have in the second text. Then in the third, ad pleniorem scientiam — for fuller understanding — we read that "everything," "the whole," and "the perfect" are formally one and the same; and that this is why, among magnitudes, only body is perfect, because only body is determined by three, which is the whole, and being divisible in three ways, it is divisible in every direction — whereas of the others, one is divisible in one way and another in two, since each has its division and its continuity according to the number allotted to it: one is continuous in one direction, another in two, but body alone in all of them. What's more, in the fourth text, after some further doctrines, doesn't he prove the same point with yet another demonstration — namely, that a transition is made only where something is lacking (thus we pass from the line to the surface, because the line lacks breadth), but it is impossible for the perfect to lack anything, since it is complete in every direction, and therefore there is no passing from body to any further magnitude? Now from all these places, doesn't it seem to you sufficiently proved that beyond the three dimensions — length, breadth, and depth — there is no passage to any other, and that body, which has all three, is therefore perfect?

Salviati
To tell you the truth, in all these discourses the only thing I've felt compelled to concede is that whatever has a beginning, a middle, and an end may and should be called perfect. But that because beginning, middle, and end make three, the number three is therefore a perfect number, with the power to confer perfection on whatever possesses it — there I feel nothing that moves me to agree. I don't understand, and don't believe, that where legs are concerned, for example, the number three is any more perfect than four or two; nor am I aware that the number four does the elements any disservice, or that they'd be more perfect if there were three of them. Better, then, to have left these pretty conceits to the orators, and to have proved his point by necessary demonstration — for that is how one is to proceed in the demonstrative sciences.

Simplicio
You seem to treat these arguments as a joke. Yet all of this is the doctrine of the Pythagoreans, who attributed so much to numbers; and you, who are a mathematician — and, I believe, a Pythagorean philosopher in many of your opinions — now appear to scorn their mysteries.

Salviati
That the Pythagoreans held the science of numbers in the highest esteem, and that Plato himself admired the human intellect and thought it shared in divinity simply because it could understand the nature of numbers — this I know very well, and I'd be inclined to make the same judgment myself. But that the mysteries which led Pythagoras and his sect to hold the science of numbers in such veneration are the silly stuff that circulates in the mouths and on the pages of the crowd — that I don't believe for a moment. On the contrary: I know that they, so that marvelous things would not be exposed to the sneers and contempt of the mob, condemned as sacrilege the publishing of the most hidden properties of numbers, and of the incommensurable and irrational quantities they had investigated, and preached that whoever revealed them would be tormented in the next world. So I think one of them, to throw the crowd a scrap and buy himself free of its questions, told them that their numerical mysteries were precisely those trifles which afterwards spread among the common people — a piece of cunning and shrewdness much like that of the clever young man who, to rid himself of the pestering of his mother, or his inquisitive wife, I forget which, who besieged him to share the secrets of the senate, made up that famous tale which left her, and many other women with her, thoroughly mocked — to the great laughter of that same senate.

Simplicio
I have no wish to be counted among those too curious about the mysteries of the Pythagoreans. But to keep to our subject: I reply that the arguments Aristotle produces to prove that the dimensions are not, and cannot be, more than three seem conclusive to me; and I believe that if a more rigorous demonstration had existed, Aristotle would not have left it out.

Sagredo
Add at least: if he had known it, or if it had occurred to him. But Salviati, you'd be doing me a great kindness by giving me some clear argument of your own — if you have one plain enough for me to grasp.

Salviati
Not only for you, but for Simplicio too; and not merely one you can grasp, but one you already know — though perhaps without ever having noticed it. And to make it easier to follow, let's take paper and pen, which I see lying ready here for just such occasions, and draw ourselves a little figure. First we'll mark these two points, A and B, and draw from one to the other the curved lines ACB and ADB, and the straight line AB. Now I ask you: which of these, in your mind, is the one that determines the distance between the endpoints A and B — and why?

Sagredo
I'd say the straight line, and not the curves. Partly because the straight one is the shortest; partly because it is one — single and definite — while the others are infinitely many, unequal, and longer; and it seems to me the determination ought to be taken from what is one and certain.

Salviati
So we have the straight line, then, as the determiner of length between two endpoints. Now let's add another straight line, parallel to AB — call it CD — so that a surface lies enclosed between them; and I'd like you to assign me that surface's breadth. So, starting from the endpoint A, tell me where and by what route you'd go to land on the line CD, so as to assign me the breadth contained between the two lines. Will you determine it by the length of the curve AE, or by the straight line AF, or...

Simplicio
By the straight line AF, and not by the curve — curves having already been ruled out for such a use.

Sagredo
But I wouldn't use either of them, seeing that the straight line AF runs at a slant. I'd draw a line square to CD, because that one, it seems to me, would be the shortest — and the single one among the infinitely many longer, mutually unequal lines that can be drawn from the endpoint A to one point after another of the opposite line CD.

Salviati
Your choice, and the reason you give for it, strike me as perfect. So far, then, we have this: the first dimension is determined by a straight line; the second — breadth — by another line, also straight, and not merely straight, but at right angles to the one that determined length. And with that we have defined the two dimensions of a surface: length and breadth. But suppose you had to determine a height — say, how high this ceiling stands above the floor we have beneath our feet. From any point on the ceiling one can draw infinitely many lines, curved and straight, all of different lengths, to infinitely many points on the floor below. Which of all those lines would you use?

Sagredo
I'd fasten a string to the ceiling with a lead plumb hanging from it, and let it pay out freely until it came down almost to the floor. The length of that string — being the straight line, and the shortest of all the lines that could be drawn from that same point to the floor — I'd say is the true height of this room.

Salviati
Excellent. And if from the point marked on the floor by this hanging string (taking the floor to be level, not sloping) you set two more straight lines running, one along the length and the other along the breadth of the floor's surface, what angles would they make with the string?

Sagredo
Right angles, certainly — since the string falls plumb and the floor is good and flat and level.

Salviati
Then if you fix some point as the origin and starting place of your measurements, and from it draw one straight line as the determiner of the first measure, namely the length, then of necessity the line that is to define the breadth must set out at a right angle to the first; and the line that is to mark the height, the third dimension, setting out from that same point, must form with the other two angles that are not oblique but right. And so from the three perpendiculars — as from three lines that are each single, certain, and shortest — you'll have the three dimensions determined: AB length, AC breadth, AD height. And since it is clear that no further line can meet at that same point so as to make right angles with these, and since the dimensions must be determined by straight lines alone that make right angles with one another, it follows that the dimensions are no more than three. And whatever has the three has them all; and whatever has them all is divisible in every direction; and whatever is that, is perfect — and so on.

Simplicio
And who says other lines can't be drawn? Why can't I bring another line up from below to the point A, square to all the others?

Salviati
You most certainly cannot make more than three straight lines meet at one and the same point so as to form right angles with one another.

Sagredo
Yes — because the line Simplicio means would be, it seems to me, simply DA itself extended downward. In that fashion one could draw two more; but they would be the same first three lines, differing only in this: where they now merely touch at the point, they would then cut through it. They'd bring in no new dimensions.

Simplicio
I won't say this argument of yours can't be conclusive. But I will say, with Aristotle, that in the things of nature one need not always demand the necessity of mathematical demonstration.

Sagredo
Perhaps not — where it can't be had. But if here it can, why don't you want it used? Still, we'd best not spend more words on this point, because I believe Salviati would have granted Aristotle — and you — without any further demonstrations, that the world is a body, and perfect, and supremely perfect, as the greatest work of God.

Salviati
So he would, truly. So let's leave the general contemplation of the whole and come to the consideration of the parts, which Aristotle in his first division makes two, very different from each other and in a certain way contrary: the celestial and the elemental. The celestial: ingenerable, incorruptible, inalterable, impervious to change, and so on. The elemental: exposed to continual alteration, mutation, and the rest. This difference he derives, as from its original principle, from the diversity of local motions, and he proceeds along this path.

Leaving the world of the senses, so to speak, and withdrawing into the ideal world, he begins, architect-fashion, by considering that since nature is the principle of motion, it is fitting that natural bodies be movable with local motion. He then declares that local motions come in three kinds: circular, straight, and a mixture of straight and circular. The first two he calls simple, because of all lines only the circular and the straight are simple. Narrowing things down a bit, he then defines afresh: of the simple motions, one is the circular — that made around the center — and the straight is upward and downward; upward being the motion that departs from the center, downward the motion that goes toward the center. From this he infers that of necessity all simple motions are confined to these three kinds: toward the center, away from the center, and around the center. And this, he says, answers with a certain lovely proportion to what was said above about body — that it too is perfected in three things, and so likewise is its motion. With these motions established, he goes on to say that since some natural bodies are simple and others are compounds of them (and he calls simple those bodies that have from nature a principle of motion, like fire and earth), it is fitting that the simple motions belong to the simple bodies, and the mixed motions to the compounds — with this proviso, that the compounds follow the motion of whichever part predominates in their composition.

Sagredo
Please, Salviati, stop there a moment, because in this progression I feel doubts sprouting up on so many sides that I must either speak them now, if I'm to listen attentively to what you'll add next, or else withdraw my attention from what's to be said, if I'm to hold the doubts in memory.

Salviati
I'll stop very gladly, because I'm running the same risk myself, and at every step I'm on the point of losing my way — having to sail among reefs and waters so broken that they make me, as the saying goes, lose my compass. So before the pile grows any higher, set out your difficulties.

Sagredo
You began, together with Aristotle, by drawing me a little way out of the sensible world, to show me the architecture by which it must have been built; and, to my pleasure, you started by telling me that a natural body is by nature movable — nature having been defined elsewhere as the principle of motion. But here a small doubt was born in me: why didn't Aristotle say that some natural bodies are movable by nature and others immobile? For in the definition, nature is said to be the principle of motion and of rest. If natural bodies all have a principle of movement, then either there was no need to put rest into the definition of nature, or no need to bring such a definition in at this point.

Next, as to his explaining what he understands by simple motions, and how he determines them from the spaces they cover — calling simple those made along simple lines, which are only the circular and the straight — I accept that quietly enough; nor do I care to press the fine-grained objection of the helix wound around a cylinder, which, being in every part similar to itself, could seemingly be counted among the simple lines. But I do bristle a little at hearing him narrow this down — while seeming, in other words, merely to repeat the same definitions — to calling the one "motion around the center," and the other sursum et deorsum, that is, up and down: terms that have no use outside the built world, and that suppose it not only built but already inhabited by us. Now, if straight motion is simple by the simplicity of the straight line, and if simple motion is natural, then let it be made in any direction whatever — I mean up, down, forward, backward, to the right, to the left, and any other way you can imagine — so long as it's straight, it ought to belong to some simple natural body; or, if not, Aristotle's supposition is defective. One sees, moreover, that Aristotle hints there is only one circular motion in the world, and consequently only one center, to which alone the straight motions up and down refer — every sign that he means to switch the cards in our hand, and to fit the architecture to the building rather than build the building according to the precepts of architecture. For if I say that in the universe of nature there can be a thousand circular motions, and consequently a thousand centers, there will then also be a thousand motions up and a thousand motions down.

Moreover, he posits, as I said, simple motions and mixed motion — calling the circular and the straight simple, and the compound of the two mixed. Of natural bodies he calls some simple (namely, those that have a natural principle of simple motion) and others compound; and the simple motions he assigns to the simple bodies, and the compound motion to the compounds. But by compound motion he no longer means the mixture of straight and circular — which can exist in the world; he brings in a mixed motion as impossible as it is impossible to mix opposite motions along one and the same straight line, so as to produce from them a motion that is partly upward and partly downward. And to soften so great an awkwardness and impossibility, he falls back on saying that such mixed bodies move according to the simple part that predominates — which in the end forces one to say that even motion made along the same straight line is sometimes simple and sometimes compound; so that the simplicity of a motion no longer depends solely on the simplicity of its line.

Simplicio
Oh, doesn't it seem to you difference enough, if the simple and absolute motion is a great deal swifter than the motion that comes from predominance? How much faster does a piece of pure earth fall than a scrap of wood?

Sagredo
Fine, Simplicio. But if simplicity has to change on that account, then besides there being a hundred thousand mixed motions, you'll never be able to point out the simple one for me. Worse: if greater and lesser speed can alter the simplicity of a motion, then no simple body will ever move with simple motion — since in all natural straight motions the speed keeps forever increasing, and so keeps forever changing the simplicity, which, to be simplicity, must be unchangeable. And, what matters more, you'll be charging Aristotle with a fresh fault: in his definition of compound motion he made no mention of slowness or of speed, which you now lay down as a necessary and essential condition. Besides, you'll harvest no fruit at all from such a rule; for there will be mixed bodies, and not a few, of which some move more slowly and others more swiftly than the simple one — lead and wood, for example, compared with earth. So among these motions, which will you call the simple, and which the compound?
