The Third Day

(Part 13 of 16)

Sagredo
I'm completely satisfied with what you've said. But since we still have plenty of time before nightfall, if you want me to pass the evening in peace, do me the kindness of laying out those problems whose explanation you asked, a little while ago, to put off until tomorrow. Give us back the leave you were granted — cash it in now — and setting all other discussions aside, come and explain to us how, once we grant the Earth the motions Copernicus assigns it, and hold the Sun and the fixed stars still, these same phenomena can follow — the Sun's rising and sinking in the sky, the changing of the seasons, the inequality of days and nights, and so on — in exactly the way they are so easily grasped in the Ptolemaic system.

Salviati
Nothing Signor Sagredo asks can be, or ought to be, refused. The delay I asked for had no other purpose than to give me time to arrange in my mind the premises needed for a full and open account of how the phenomena you name follow in the Copernican arrangement just as they do in the Ptolemaic — indeed, with far greater ease and simplicity in the one than in the other. From that it will be plain that this hypothesis is as easy for nature to carry out as it is hard for the intellect to grasp. Even so, I hope that by using a different way of laying it out than the one Copernicus used, I can also make it a good deal less obscure to follow. To do that I'll put forward a few assumptions that are self-evident and obvious in themselves, and they are these:

First. Suppose the Earth, a spherical body, turns about its own axis and poles: then each point marked on its surface traces out the circumference of a circle, larger or smaller according as the point is farther from or nearer to the poles. Of these circles, the largest is the one traced by a point equally distant from the two poles; and all these circles are parallel to one another — and "parallels" is what we'll call them.

Second. Since the Earth is spherical in shape and opaque in substance, it is continually lit by the Sun over half its surface, while the other half stays dark. And since the line that divides the lit part from the dark is a great circle, we'll call it the boundary circle of the light.

Third. When the boundary circle of the light passes through the Earth's poles, it cuts (being a great circle) all the parallels into equal parts. But when it does not pass through the poles, it cuts them all into unequal parts, except for the middle circle alone, which, being itself a great circle, is still cut into equal parts.

Fourth. As the Earth turns about its own poles, the lengths of the days and the nights are determined by the arcs of the parallels cut off by the boundary circle of the light: the arc that lies in the lit hemisphere sets the length of the day, and the remainder is the length of the night.

With these things laid down, and for a clearer grasp of what remains to be said, let's draw a figure. First we'll mark the circumference of a circle to stand for the great orb — the Earth's annual path — drawn in the plane of the ecliptic, and we'll divide it into four equal parts with two diameters, Capricorn, Cancer, Libra, and Aries, which at the same time will stand for the four cardinal points, that is, the two solstices and the two equinoxes. At the center of this circle we'll mark the Sun, O, fixed and unmoving. Now, about the four points Capricorn, Cancer, Libra, and Aries, taken as centers, let's draw four equal circles to stand for the Earth, set at those places at different times of year; and in the space of one year its center travels the whole circumference Capricorn, Aries, Cancer, and Libra, moving from west to east — that is, in the order of the signs. It's already clear that while the Earth is at Capricorn, the Sun will appear at Cancer; and as the Earth moves along the arc from Capricorn to Aries, the Sun will appear to move along the arc from Cancer to Libra, and in short to run through the zodiac in the order of the signs over the course of a year. And so this first premise alone accounts, without dispute, for the Sun's apparent annual motion along the ecliptic.

Now, coming to the other motion — the Earth's daily turning on itself — we have to fix its poles and its axis. This axis is to be understood as not standing upright at right angles to the plane of the ecliptic — that is, not parallel to the axis of the great orb — but leaning away from the right angle by about 23 and a half degrees, with its north pole tilted toward the axis of the great orb, while the Earth's center sits at the solstice point of Capricorn. Taking the terrestrial globe, then, to have its center at the point Capricorn, we'll mark its poles and its axis AB, tilted 23 and a half degrees from the perpendicular over the diameter Capricorn–Cancer, so that the angle at A over Capricorn–Cancer comes to be the complement of a quadrant, that is, 66 and a half degrees; and this tilt is to be understood as unchanging. The upper pole A we'll take to be the north, and the other, B, the south. Now imagine the Earth turning on itself about the axis AB in twenty-four hours, again from west to east: then every point marked on its surface traces circles parallel to one another. In this first position of the Earth, we'll mark the greatest of them, CD, and the two that lie 23 and a half degrees from it — EF above and GN below — and the two outermost, IK and LM, lying the same distance from the poles A and B. And just as we've marked these five, so we can imagine countless others, parallel to these, traced by the countless points of the Earth's surface. Now imagine the Earth, by the annual motion of its center, carried into the other places already marked, but passing through them under this law: that its own axis AB not only never changes its tilt with respect to the plane of the ecliptic, but also never changes its direction at all, so that, always keeping parallel to itself, it points continually toward the same part of the universe — of the firmament, we might say. If we imagine it extended that far, its uppermost tip would trace a circle parallel and equal to the great orb Libra–Capricorn–Aries–Cancer, like the upper base of a cylinder described by the axis itself in the annual motion over the lower base Libra–Capricorn–Aries–Cancer. So, given this unchanging tilt, we'll draw these other three figures about the centers Aries, Cancer, and Libra, alike in every respect to the first one drawn about the center Capricorn.

Now let's look at the first figure of the Earth. In it, since the axis AB leans 23 and a half degrees from the perpendicular over the diameter Capricorn–Cancer, toward the Sun O, and since the arc AI is likewise 23 and a half degrees, the Sun's light will illuminate the hemisphere of the terrestrial globe turned toward the Sun (of which we see half here), divided from the dark part by the boundary of the light, IM. By this boundary the parallel CD, being a great circle, is divided into equal parts, but all the others into unequal parts, since the boundary of the light IM does not pass through their poles A and B. The parallel IK, together with all those drawn inside it and nearer the pole A, will remain wholly in the lit part; while, on the other side, the opposite ones toward the pole B, contained within the parallel LM, will remain in the dark. Moreover, since arc AI equals arc FD, and arc AF is shared between them, the two arcs IKF and AFD will be equal, each of them a quadrant. And because the whole arc IFM is a semicircle, the arc MF will be a quadrant, equal to the other, FKI. And so the Sun O will, in this state of the Earth, be directly overhead for anyone at the point F. But by the daily rotation about the fixed axis AB, all the points of the parallel EF pass through this same point F; and so on that day the Sun at noon will be directly overhead for everyone living on the parallel EF, and in its apparent motion it will seem to them to trace the circle we call the Tropic of Cancer. But to those living on all the parallels that lie above EF, toward the north pole A, the Sun sinks from their overhead point toward the south; and, on the other hand, to everyone living on the parallels below EF, toward the equinoctial CD and the south pole B, the noon Sun stands higher than their overhead point, toward the north pole A. It can be seen, next, how of all the parallels only the greatest, CD, is cut into equal parts by the boundary of the light IM, while the others, both below and above that greatest circle, are all cut into unequal parts. And of the upper ones, the daytime arcs — those of the part of the Earth's surface lit by the Sun — are larger than the nighttime arcs, which remain in the dark; and the opposite happens with the rest, which lie below the greatest circle CD toward the pole B, whose daytime arcs are smaller than their nighttime arcs. It can also be plainly seen that the differences between these arcs keep growing as the parallels come nearer the poles, until the parallel IK stays entirely in the lit part, and its inhabitants have a day of twenty-four hours with no night; while, on the contrary, the parallel LM, staying entirely in the dark, has a night of twenty-four hours with no day.

Now let's come to the third figure of the Earth, set with its center at the point Cancer, from which the Sun appears to be at the first point of Capricorn. It is at once plain that, because the axis AB has not changed its tilt but has kept parallel to itself, the aspect and situation of the Earth are exactly the same as in the first figure — except that the hemisphere lit by the Sun in the first is now in the dark, and the one that was dark in the first position is now lit. So what happened before about the differences between days and nights — about which were longer or shorter — now happens in reverse. First we see that where, in the first figure, the circle IK was wholly in the light, it is now wholly in the dark, and the opposite circle LM, which before was wholly dark, is now wholly in the light. On the parallels between the greatest circle CD and the pole A, the daytime arcs are now smaller than the nighttime ones, where before it was the reverse; and likewise on the others toward the pole B, the daytime arcs are now larger than the nighttime ones, the opposite of what happened in the other state of the Earth. Now we see the Sun standing directly overhead for the inhabitants of the tropic GN, and sunk toward the south for those on the parallel EF by the whole arc ECG, that is, 47 degrees — in short, it has passed from one tropic to the other, crossing the equinoctial, rising and sinking on the meridian through that span of 47 degrees. And all this change comes not from the Earth's tilting or rising, but on the contrary from its never tilting or rising at all — in short, from its keeping always the same posture with respect to the universe, merely circling the Sun, which sits in the middle of the very plane along which the Earth moves round it in a circle by the annual motion. And here we should note a marvelous thing: that just as the Earth's keeping its axis pointed in the same direction toward the universe — toward the highest sphere of the fixed stars, we might say — makes the Sun appear to us to rise and sink through so great a span, namely 47 degrees, while the fixed stars neither rise nor sink at all, so, on the contrary, if that same axis of the Earth kept continually the same tilt toward the Sun — toward the axis of the zodiac, we might say — no change would appear in the Sun as to its rising and sinking, so that the inhabitants of any given place would always have the same differences of days and nights and the same arrangement of seasons — some always winter, others always summer, others always spring, and so on — but, on the other hand, a very great change would appear in the fixed stars as to their rising and sinking for us, amounting to those same 47 degrees. To understand this, let's go back and consider the Earth's state in the first figure, where we see the axis AB, with its upper pole A, tilting toward the Sun. But in the third figure, that same axis, having kept the same direction toward the highest sphere by staying parallel to itself, no longer tilts toward the Sun with its upper pole A, but on the contrary has swung 47 degrees away from its first state and tilts toward the opposite side. So, to restore the same tilt of that pole A toward the Sun, we'd have to carry the terrestrial globe around the circumference ACBD, shifting it those same 47 degrees toward E; and by that many degrees any fixed star at all, observed on the meridian, would appear to have risen or sunk.

Now let's come to the explanation of what remains, and consider the Earth set in the fourth figure, that is, with its center at the first point of Libra, so that the Sun will appear at the beginning of Aries. In the first figure the Earth's axis is understood to lean over the diameter Capricorn–Cancer, and so to lie in the very plane that, cutting the plane of the great orb along the line Capricorn–Cancer, stands upright and perpendicular to it. Carried into the fourth figure, and kept, as we've always said, parallel to itself, it comes to lie in a plane that is likewise upright to the surface of the great orb and parallel to the plane that cuts that surface at right angles along the diameter Capricorn–Cancer. And so the line running from the Sun's center to the Earth's center — which is the line O–Libra — will be perpendicular to the axis BA. But the same line from the Sun's center to the Earth's center is always perpendicular to the boundary circle of the light as well; therefore this same circle will pass through the poles A and B in the fourth figure, and the axis AB will lie in its plane. But a great circle passing through the poles of the parallels divides them all into equal parts; therefore the arcs IK, EF, CD, GN, and LM will all be semicircles, and the lit hemisphere will be the one facing us and the Sun, and the boundary of the light will be that same circle ACBD. With the Earth in this place, it makes an equinox for all its inhabitants. And the same happens in the second figure, where the Earth, having its lit hemisphere toward the Sun, shows us the other, dark one with its nighttime arcs, which are likewise all semicircles; and so here too there is an equinox. And finally, since the line drawn from the Sun's center to the Earth's center is perpendicular to the axis AB — to which the greatest of the parallel circles, CD, is likewise upright — that same line O–Libra will necessarily pass through the very plane of the parallel CD, cutting its circumference at the midpoint of the daytime arc CD; and so the Sun will be directly overhead for whoever is found at that point of cutting. But carried by the Earth's daily turning, all the inhabitants of that parallel pass through it; therefore all of them, on that day, will have the noon Sun over their overhead point, while meanwhile, to everyone living on the Earth, the Sun will appear to trace the greatest parallel, called the equinoctial. Furthermore, since with the Earth at either solstice point one of the polar circles IK, LM stays wholly in the light and the other wholly in the dark, but when the Earth is at the equinox points half of each of those polar circles is found in the light and the rest in the dark, it shouldn't be hard to understand how, as the Earth passes, say, from Cancer (where the parallel IK is wholly in the dark) into Leo, a part of the parallel IK toward the point I begins to enter the light, and the boundary of the light IM begins to draw back toward the poles A and B, cutting the circle ACBD no longer at I and M, but at two other points falling between the ends I, A and M, B of the arcs IA and MB — so that the inhabitants of the circle IK begin to enjoy daylight, and those of the circle LM begin to feel the night. And there you have it: with two utterly simple motions, taking place in times proportioned to their sizes and not contrary to one another — indeed made, like all the other motions of the moving bodies of the world, from west to east — assigned to the terrestrial globe, we have given adequate reasons for all those same appearances. To save them by keeping the Earth still, one is forced instead — giving up that symmetry we see between the speeds and the sizes of the moving bodies — to grant one vast sphere, above all the others, an incomprehensible speed, while the other, smaller spheres move very slowly; and further to make that motion run contrary to theirs; and, to pile improbability on improbability, to make all the lower spheres be swept along, against their own tendency, by that topmost one. Here I leave it to your own judgment to decide which is the more likely.

Sagredo
For my part, as far as my own understanding goes, I see no small difference between the simplicity and ease of producing these effects by the means assigned in this new arrangement, and the multiplicity, confusion, and difficulty found in the old, commonly received one. For if the universe were ordered according to that multiplicity, we'd have to strike out of philosophy many axioms commonly accepted by all philosophers: that nature does not multiply things without necessity; that she uses the easiest and simplest means in producing her effects; that she does nothing in vain; and others like them. I confess I've never heard anything more admirable than this, nor can I believe that the human intellect has ever penetrated a subtler line of thought. I don't know how it strikes Signor Simplicio.

Simplicio
These, if I'm to speak my mind freely, seem to me some of those geometric subtleties that Aristotle faults Plato for, when he charges him with drifting away from sound philosophizing through too much study of geometry. And I've known and heard the very greatest Peripatetic philosophers advise their pupils against the study of mathematics, on the grounds that it makes the mind quibbling and unfit for good philosophizing — a program diametrically opposed to Plato's, who would admit no one to philosophy who had not first mastered geometry.

Salviati
I applaud these Peripatetics of yours for their advice to steer their pupils away from the study of geometry, since there's no art better suited for exposing their fallacies. But notice how different they are from the mathematical philosophers, who much prefer dealing with people well versed in the common Peripatetic philosophy than with those who lack that knowledge — for lacking it, they can't compare one doctrine with another. But setting that aside, tell me, please: what extravagances, or overstrained subtleties, make this Copernican arrangement less applaudable to you?
