The Third Day

(Part 7 of 16)

Salviati
You, Sagredo, will see them proceed in such a way that this single conjecture ought to be enough — for anyone not downright stubborn or past all teaching — to make him give his assent to everything else in this doctrine. So let me tell you: with nothing altered in Saturn's motion of thirty years, in Jupiter's of twelve, in Mars's of two, in Venus's of nine months, and in Mercury's of about eighty days, the Earth's annual motion alone — set between Mars and Venus — accounts for the apparent irregularities in the motions of all five of the planets I've named; and for an easy, full grasp of the whole thing I want to draw you its figure. So suppose the Sun is placed at the center O; around it we'll mark the orbit traced by the Earth with its annual motion, BGM; and let the circle traced, say, by Jupiter around the Sun in twelve years be this one, bgm; and in the starry sphere let us take the zodiac to be yus. On the Earth's yearly orbit we'll lay off some equal arcs, BC, CD, DE, EF, FG, GH, HI, IK, KL, LM; and on Jupiter's circle we'll mark other arcs, covered in the same stretches of time in which the Earth covers hers — namely bc, cd, de, ef, fg, gh, hi, ik, kl, lm — each of them proportionally smaller than those on the Earth's orbit, since Jupiter's motion under the zodiac is slower than the Earth's yearly one. Now suppose that when the Earth is at B, Jupiter is at b; drawing the straight line B‑b‑p, he'll appear to us in the zodiac at p. Now let the Earth move from B to C, and Jupiter from b to c in the same time: Jupiter will appear to have come to q in the zodiac, moved forward directly in the order of the signs, p, q. Then, as the Earth passes to D and Jupiter to d, he'll be seen in the zodiac at r; and from E, with Jupiter arrived at e, he'll appear in the zodiac at s — still moving forward directly. But once the Earth begins to slip more squarely between Jupiter and the Sun, having come to F while Jupiter is at f, he'll appear to us at t, already having begun apparently to go backward beneath the zodiac; and in the time the Earth has covered the arc EF, Jupiter will have held himself between the points s and t, showing us almost still and stationary. Then, when the Earth has come to G and Jupiter to g, at opposition to the Sun, he'll be seen in the zodiac at u, greatly gone backward through the whole arc of the zodiac t‑u — even though, still keeping his own steady course, he has really gone forward, not just in his own circle but in the zodiac too, with respect to that zodiac's center and to the Sun set there. Then, as both the Earth and Jupiter carry on their motions, once the Earth has come to H and Jupiter to h, he'll be seen greatly gone backward in the zodiac through the whole arc u‑x; and with the Earth at I and Jupiter at i, he'll have moved apparently through the small space x‑y, and there appear stationary. When, next, the Earth has come to K and Jupiter to k, he'll have passed the arc y‑n in the zodiac with direct motion; and continuing his course, the Earth from L will see Jupiter, at l, at the point z; and finally, with Jupiter at m, he'll be seen from the Earth at M passed on to a, again with direct motion. His whole apparent retrogradation in the zodiac will amount to the arc s‑y, made by Jupiter while in his own circle he passes the arc e‑i and the Earth in hers the arc EI. And what has been said of Jupiter, understand of Saturn and Mars as well: in Saturn these backward loops are somewhat more frequent than in Jupiter, since his motion is slower than Jupiter's, so that the Earth overtakes him in a shorter stretch of time; while in Mars they are rarer, because his motion is faster than Jupiter's, so the Earth spends more time catching up with him. As for Venus and Mercury, whose circles are enclosed within the Earth's, their stations and retrogradations likewise appear — caused not by any motions of theirs that are really such, but by the Earth's own annual motion, as Copernicus, following Apollonius of Perga, shrewdly demonstrates in Book Five of his Revolutions, chapter thirty‑five.

You see, gentlemen, with what ease and simplicity the annual motion — supposing it to be the Earth's — fits the case and explains the apparent oddities observed in the movements of the five planets, Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Venus, and Mercury: it sweeps them all away and reduces them to even, regular motions. Nicolaus Copernicus was the first to make this marvelous effect's cause plain to us. But of another marvel — no less astonishing than this one, and one that binds the human mind, by a knot perhaps harder to loosen, to admit this yearly revolution and grant it to our terrestrial globe — the Sun itself brings us a new and unlooked‑for conjecture; the Sun, which shows that it would not, alone among all, dodge bearing witness to so weighty a conclusion, but rather, as a witness beyond every exception, insisted on taking its part. So hear this high, new wonder.

The first to discover and observe the sunspots — as with all the other novelties of the heavens — was our friend the Academician; he discovered them in the year 1610, while still holding the chair of Mathematics at the University of Padua, and he spoke of them there and in Venice to various people, some of whom are still living; and a year later he showed them at Rome to many gentlemen, as he states in the first of his Letters to Signor Mark Welser, Duumvir of Augsburg. He was the first, against the opinions of those too timid and too jealous of the heavens' unchangeableness, to affirm that these spots are matter produced and dissolved over short stretches of time; that, as to place, they are right up against the body of the Sun and turn around it — or rather, carried by the solar globe itself, which revolves upon its own center in the space of about a month, they finish their circuits with it. This motion he judged at first to be made by the Sun about an axis upright to the plane of the ecliptic, since the arcs the spots traced across the Sun's disk appeared to our eye as straight lines parallel to the plane of the ecliptic — lines that were, however, altered in part by certain accidental, wandering, irregular motions to which the spots are subject, and by which they keep shifting their positions among themselves, tumbling and without any order, now gathering many together, now separating, some splitting into several, and greatly changing their shapes, mostly into very outlandish ones. And though these fickle changes altered in part the primary periodic course of the spots, they didn't make our friend change his mind — as if he thought some fixed, essential cause lay behind such deviations; he went on believing that the whole apparent alteration came from those accidental changes, exactly as would happen to someone who, from far‑off regions, watched the motion of our clouds: he'd see them moving with a very swift, large, and constant motion, carried by the daily whirl of the Earth (if that motion were the Earth's) through twenty‑four hours along circles parallel to the equinoctial — yet altered in part by the accidental motions given them by the winds, which shove them here and there at random toward different quarters of the world. It happened at this time that Signor Welser sent him some letters written by a certain false Apelles on the matter of these spots, pressingly asking him to give his opinion freely on those letters, and further to make known what he himself thought about the essence of these spots. He satisfied him with three Letters, showing first how empty Apelles's notions were, and disclosing to him, second, his own opinions — and predicting, moreover, that Apelles, taking better counsel with time, would come round to his view, as afterward happened. And because it seemed to our Academician (as it seemed to others knowledgeable in the things of nature) that in those three Letters he had investigated and demonstrated, if not all that human curiosity could wish and seek, at least all that human reasoning could reach in such a matter, he set aside the continued observations for a time, busy with other studies, and only to oblige some friend would now and then make a scattered observation — until, running into me after some years, and the two of us being at my villa at Le Selve, one of the sunspots, solitary, quite large and dense, together with a most clear and unbroken calm of the sky, invited us; and at my request observations were made of that spot's whole transit, carefully marking on paper its places from day to day, at the hour when the Sun stood on the meridian. Noticing that its journey was not made along a straight line, but one somewhat curved, we took it into our heads to make further observations from time to time; and to that undertaking we were vigorously spurred by a thought that suddenly dropped into my guest's mind, which he shared with me in these words: "Filippo, it seems to me a road opens to a great consequence. For if the axis about which the Sun revolves is not set upright to the plane of the ecliptic, but tilted upon it — as this curved passage we've just observed hints to me — we'll have a conjecture about the standings of the Sun and the Earth as firm and as conclusive as any that has ever yet been supplied to us by any other line of evidence." Roused by so lofty a promise, I pressed him to lay his thought open to me plainly. And he: "If the annual motion belongs to the Earth, along the ecliptic around the Sun, and the Sun is set at the center of that ecliptic and turns upon itself there — not about the ecliptic's axis (which would be the axis of the Earth's annual motion), but about one that is tilted — then strange changes are bound to show themselves to us in the apparent motions of the sunspots, even granting that the Sun's axis stays forever and unchangeably at the same tilt and in the same direction, toward the same point of the universe. For, as the terrestrial globe walks around him in the annual motion, first of all it must happen that to us, carried by that globe, the passages of the spots will sometimes indeed appear made along straight lines — but this only twice a year, and at all other times they'll show as made along noticeably curved arcs. Second, the curvature of these arcs, for one half of the year, will appear tilted opposite to the way it shows in the other half; that is, for six months the convex side of the arcs will face the upper part of the solar disk, and for the other six months the lower. Third, since the spots begin to appear — to be born, so to speak — to our eye on the left side of the solar disk, and go to hide and set on the right, the eastern ends (that is, of the first appearances) will for six months be lower than the opposite ends of the settings, and for the other six the reverse will happen: the spots will be born from higher points and, descending from them in their courses, will come to hide themselves at lower points; and for only two days in the whole year will these ends, of the risings and settings, be level. After these balancings, the tilt of the spots' journeys will begin little by little, growing greater day by day, and in three months reach its greatest slant; then, beginning to lessen from there, in as much time again it will return to the other balance. It will happen, for the fourth wonder, that the day of the greatest slant will be the same as the day of the passage made along a straight line, and on the day of the balancing the arc of the journey will appear more curved than ever; while at other times, as the tilt lessens and heads toward the balance, the curving of the passages' arcs, on the contrary, will keep increasing."

Sagredo
My dear Salviati, I know that to break in on your discourse is bad manners; but I judge it no less bad to let you spread yourself at greater length in words while they are, as they say, tossed to the wind. For, to put it plainly, I can't form a single distinct notion of even one of the conclusions you've pronounced; yet because, taken so generally and confusedly, they strike me as things of admirable consequence, I'd very much like to be made able to grasp them somehow.

Salviati
The very thing that happens to you happened to me too, while they were brought to me by my guest in bare words; he then eased my understanding by picturing the matter for me on a material instrument — nothing but a plain sphere, using some of its circles, but for a purpose other than the one they're commonly meant for. Now, for want of the sphere, I'll make do by drawing figures on paper as needed. And to represent the first fact I put forward — that the passages of the spots could appear made along straight lines only twice a year — let's take this point O to be the center of the great orb, or say of the ecliptic, and likewise of the globe of the Sun itself, of which, thanks to the great distance between it and the Earth, we earth‑dwellers may suppose ourselves to see the half. So we'll draw this circle ABCD about that same center O, to stand for the extreme boundary that divides and separates the Sun's hemisphere that appears to us from the other, hidden one. And because our eye, no less than the center of the Earth, is understood to be in the plane of the ecliptic — where the center of the Sun also is — then, if we picture the solar body as cut by that plane, the section will appear to our eye as a straight line, say BOD; and setting the perpendicular AOC upon it, that will be the axis of the ecliptic and of the terrestrial globe's annual motion. Now understand the solar body (without changing its center) to turn upon itself, not about the axis AOC (the one upright to the plane of the ecliptic), but about one somewhat tilted, say this EOI — which axis, fixed and unchanging, keeps itself forever at the same tilt and direction toward the same points of the firmament and the universe. And because, in the revolutions of the solar globe, each point of its surface (except the poles) describes the circumference of a circle, larger or smaller according to how far it lies from those poles, take the point F equally distant from them and mark the diameter FOG, which will be perpendicular to the axis EI and will be the diameter of the greatest circle described about the poles E, I. Now suppose the Earth, and we with it, to be at such a place in the ecliptic that the Sun's hemisphere apparent to us is bounded by the circle ABCD, which, passing (as it always does) through the poles A, C, passes also through E, I: it's clear that the greatest circle whose diameter is FG will stand upright to the circle ABCD; and to that circle the ray falling from our eye upon the center O is perpendicular, so that this same ray falls in the plane of the circle whose diameter is FG, and therefore its circumference will appear to us as a straight line, the very same as FG. For this reason, whenever a spot was at the point F, then carried on by the solar rotation, it would trace on the Sun's surface the circumference of that circle which appears to us as a straight line. Straight, then, its passage will appear; and straight too will appear the motions of other spots which in the same revolution traced smaller circles, since all are parallel to the greatest and our eye is set at an immense distance from them. Now, if you'll consider how, after the Earth has run in six months through half of the great orb and stands over against the Sun's hemisphere now hidden from us — so that the boundary of the part then seen is the same circle ABCD, which will still pass through the poles E, I — you'll understand that the same will happen with the journeys of the spots: they'll all appear made along straight lines. But because this fact holds only when the boundary passes through the poles E, I, and that boundary keeps shifting from moment to moment through the Earth's annual motion, its passing through the fixed poles E, I is momentary, and consequently momentary is the time during which the spots' motions appear straight. From what has been said so far, one comes to grasp, too, that since the appearance and start of the spots' motion is from the F side, proceeding toward G, their passages run from the left, climbing toward the right; but with the Earth placed at the diametrically opposite part, the spots' appearance around G will indeed be at the observer's left, but the passage will be descending toward the right, F. Now let's picture the Earth situated a quarter away from its present standing, and mark in this other figure the boundary ABCD and the axis, as before, AC, through which the plane of our meridian would pass — a plane in which the axis of the Sun's revolution would also lie, with its poles, one toward us (that is, in the apparent hemisphere), which pole we'll represent with the point E, and the other falling in the hidden hemisphere, which I mark I. Tilting the axis EI, then, with its upper part E toward us, the greatest circle described by the Sun's turning will be this one, BFDG, whose half seen by us — that is, BFD — will no longer appear a straight line, since the poles E, I are not on the circumference ABCD, but will show itself curved, with its convex toward the lower part C; and clearly the same will appear of all the smaller circles parallel to the greatest, BFD. Understand too that when the Earth is diametrically opposite to this standing, so that it sees the Sun's other hemisphere, now hidden, it will see of that same greatest circle the part DGB curved with its convex toward the upper part A; and the courses of the spots in these positions will be first along the arc BFD and then along the other, DGB, and their first appearances and last settings, made around the points B, D, will be level, neither one more nor less raised than the other. But if we place the Earth at such a spot in the ecliptic that neither the bounding circle ABCD nor the meridian AC passes through the poles of the axis EI — as I now show you by drawing this third figure, where the apparent pole E falls between the arc of the boundary AB and the section of the meridian AC — the diameter of the greatest circle will be FOG, the apparent semicircle FNG, and the hidden one GSF: the first curved with its convex N toward the lower part, and the second bent with its crest S toward the upper part of the Sun. The entries and exits of the spots — that is, the ends F, G — won't be level like the earlier B, D, but F lower and G higher, though with less difference than in the first figure; the arc FNG will also be curved, but not as much as the earlier BFD. So in this position the passages of the spots will be ascending from the left part F toward the right G, and will be made along curved lines. And understanding the Earth to be placed in the diametrically opposite site, so that the Sun's now‑hidden hemisphere is the one seen, and bounded by the same circle ABCD, it's plainly made out that the course of the spots will be along the arc GSF, beginning from the high point G — which will likewise be at the observer's left — and going to end, descending toward the right, at the point F. Once what I've laid out so far is understood, I don't think any difficulty remains in grasping how, from the boundary of the solar hemispheres passing through the poles of the Sun's rotation, or near them, or far from them, arise all the differences in the apparent journeys of the spots: so that the farther those poles are from that boundary, the more the said journeys will be curved and the less oblique — whence at the greatest distance, which is when the poles are in the section of the meridian, the curvature is at its highest but the obliquity at its least, that is, at the balance, as the second figure shows; while on the other hand, when the poles are on the boundary, as the first figure shows, the tilt is greatest but the curvature least, reduced to straightness. And as the boundary parts from the poles, the curvature begins to become noticeable, always increasing, while the obliquity and tilt keep growing smaller.

These are the outlandish changes my guest told me would appear from time to time in the progress of the sunspots, provided it were true that the annual motion belonged to the Earth, and that the Sun, set at the center of the ecliptic, turned upon itself about an axis not upright, but tilted, to the plane of that ecliptic.
