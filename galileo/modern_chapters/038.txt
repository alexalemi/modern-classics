The Third Day

(Part 6 of 16)

Sagredo
But how did this stay hidden from Copernicus, and yet be plain to you?

Salviati
These things can't be grasped except through the sense of sight, which nature hasn't granted to human beings perfect enough to make out such differences; indeed, the very instrument of seeing gets in its own way. But after it pleased God, in our own age, to grant human ingenuity so marvelous an invention — the power to perfect our sight by magnifying it 4, 6, 10, 20, 30, and 40 times — countless objects that were invisible to us, whether because of their distance or their extreme smallness, have, by means of the telescope, been made perfectly visible.

Sagredo
But Venus and Mars aren't objects invisible for their distance or smallness — we take them in perfectly well with plain, natural eyesight. So why can't we make out the differences in their sizes and shapes?

Salviati
A big part of this comes from the interference of our own eye, as I just hinted: because of it, bright, distant objects aren't presented to us plain and clean, but handed to us wreathed in stray, foreign rays, so long and thick that the naked little body shows up magnified 10, 20, 100, even a thousand times more than it would look if you stripped away that radiant head of hair that isn't its own.

Sagredo
Now I recall having read something or other on this subject — I'm not sure whether in the Letters on Sunspots or in The Assayer, by our mutual friend. But it would do no harm — both to refresh my own memory and for Simplicio's benefit, since he perhaps hasn't seen those writings — to set out for us more distinctly how this business stands. Understanding it, I think, is quite necessary to keep proper hold of what we're now discussing.

Simplicio
Honestly, everything Salviati is now bringing forward is new to me; for, to tell the truth, I've had no curiosity to read those books, nor have I so far put much faith in the newly introduced spyglass. In fact, following in the footsteps of my fellow Peripatetic philosophers, I've believed that what others have admired as stupendous feats were really just the tricks and deceptions of the lenses. So if I've been in error up to now, I'll be glad to be drawn out of it; and, tempted by the other novelties I've heard from you, I'll listen more attentively to the rest.

Salviati
The confidence such men have in their own discernment is no less unreasonable than the low regard they hold for other people's judgment; and it's a remarkable thing that they should think themselves fit to judge such an instrument better, without ever having tried it, than those who have made — and make every day — thousands upon thousands of trials with it. But let's leave aside, if you please, this breed of stubborn men, who can't even be reproached without honoring them more than they deserve. Returning to our subject, I say that bright objects — whether because their light is refracted in the moisture on the surface of the pupils, or reflected off the edges of the eyelids, scattering its reflected rays across those same pupils, or for some other reason — show themselves to our eye ringed with extra rays, and hence far larger than their bodies would look stripped of that irradiation. And this enlargement grows in ever greater proportion the smaller and smaller those luminous objects are. Just so — suppose the growth of the radiant hair were, say, four fingers: added around a circle four fingers in diameter, it would increase its apparent size ninefold, but—

Simplicio
I suspect you meant to say three times; because adding four fingers on one side and four on the other to the diameter of a circle that's itself four fingers, you triple its size — you don't increase it ninefold.

Salviati
A little geometry, Simplicio. It's true the diameter grows three times, but the surface — which is what we're talking about — grows nine times. Because, Simplicio, the surfaces of circles are to one another as the squares of their diameters, and a circle four fingers in diameter has to one twelve fingers across the same proportion the square of four has to the square of twelve — that is, 16 to 144 — and so it will be nine times greater, not three. Let that be a caution to you, Simplicio. And to go on: if we add that same head of hair, four fingers, to a circle only two fingers in diameter, the diameter of the wreath is now ten fingers, and the area of the whole disk to the area of the naked little body is as 100 to 4, since those are the squares of 10 and 2; so the enlargement would be 25 times over. And finally, the 4 fingers of hair added to a little circle one finger in diameter would enlarge it 81 times. And so, continually, the growths happen in ever greater proportion, the smaller and smaller the real objects being enlarged.

Sagredo
The difficulty that troubled Simplicio hasn't troubled me; but there are indeed some other things I'd like to understand more clearly — in particular, I'd like to know on what grounds you claim that this growth is always the same in all visible objects.

Salviati
I've already partly explained myself, when I said that only luminous objects grow, and not dark ones. Now let me add the rest: among bright objects, those with a livelier light produce a bigger, stronger reflection on our pupil, and so appear to enlarge much more than the less bright ones. And, so as not to dwell on this point any longer, let's turn to what the true schoolmistress — experience — teaches us. This evening, once the air has grown good and dark, let's look at the star of Jupiter: we'll see it very radiant and very big. Then let's pass our gaze through a little tube — or even through a small chink left between the palm and the fingers when we clench a fist and hold it up to the eye, or through a hole made with a fine needle in a sheet of paper. We'll see the disk of that same Jupiter stripped of its rays, but so small that we'll judge it less than a sixtieth part of what its great torch appears when seen with the free eye. Then we can look at the Dog Star, that most beautiful star, biggest of all the fixed stars, which to the naked eye shows up not much smaller than Jupiter; but once its hair is stripped away in the manner described, its disk will be seen so small that it won't be judged even a twentieth part of Jupiter's — indeed, anyone without perfect eyesight will barely make it out at all. From which one may reasonably conclude that this star, having a far livelier light than Jupiter's, makes its irradiation greater than Jupiter makes his. As for the irradiation of the Sun and the Moon, it's as good as nothing, on account of their size, which by itself takes up so much room in our eye that it leaves no space for stray rays; so their disks are seen sheared and sharply bounded.

We can assure ourselves of the same truth by another experiment I've made many times — assure ourselves, I mean, that bodies shining with a livelier light irradiate far more than those of a fainter light. I've often seen Jupiter and Venus together, 25 or 30 degrees from the Sun, and with the air well darkened, Venus looked a good 8 or even 10 times bigger than Jupiter — so long, that is, as they were viewed with the free eye. But looked at then through the telescope, Jupiter's disk was seen to be truly four times and more the size of Venus's; yet the liveliness of Venus's splendor was incomparably greater than Jupiter's very faint light. And this came from nothing but the fact that Jupiter is very far from the Sun and from us, while Venus is near to us and to the Sun.

With these things explained, it won't be hard to understand how it can be that Mars, when it's at opposition to the Sun — and therefore seven times and more nearer to the Earth than when it's toward conjunction — barely shows itself 4 or 5 times bigger in the one state than in the other, when we ought to see it more than 50 times as big. And the sole cause of this is irradiation: for if we strip it of the stray rays, we'll find it enlarged precisely in the proper proportion. And to remove its mane, the telescope is the one and best means: by magnifying its disk 900 or a thousand times, it lets us see it naked and sharply bounded like the Moon's, and differing from itself in the two positions according to the proper proportion, exactly to a hair.

As for Venus: in its evening conjunction, when it's below the Sun, it ought to show itself almost 40 times bigger than in the other, morning conjunction — and yet it isn't even seen doubled. What happens here, beyond the effect of irradiation, is that it's crescent-shaped, and its horns, besides being thin, receive the Sun's light obliquely, and so quite faintly; so that, being little and weak, its irradiation is less broad and lively than when it shows us its whole lit hemisphere. But the telescope plainly shows us its horns as sharply bounded and distinct as the Moon's, and they're seen as belonging to a very large circle — proportionally almost 40 times bigger than its own disk when it's above the Sun in its last morning appearance.

Sagredo
Oh, Nicolaus Copernicus, what delight would have been yours, to see this part of your system confirmed by such clear experiments!

Salviati
Yes — but how much less would be the fame of the sublimity of his genius among those who understand! For, as I said a moment ago, we see that he steadily went on affirming, led by reason, the very thing whose opposite the evidence of the senses displayed. I can't get over my astonishment that he should so steadfastly have insisted on saying that Venus revolves around the Sun and is at one time more than six times farther from us than at another, and yet always shows itself the same size, when it ought to appear forty times bigger.

Sagredo
In Jupiter, Saturn, and Mercury too, I believe the differences in their apparent sizes must be seen to answer punctually to their varying distances.

Salviati
In the two upper ones I've observed them precisely, almost every year, for twenty-two years now. With Mercury no observation of any weight can be made, since it lets itself be seen only at its greatest elongations from the Sun — its widest swings out from it — where its distances from the Earth are imperceptibly unequal, so that such differences are unobservable, as are the changes of shape, which absolutely must occur, just as in Venus. When we do see it, it ought to show itself in the shape of a half-circle, as Venus too does at its greatest elongations; but its disk is so small and its splendor so lively, being so close to the Sun, that the telescope's power isn't enough to shave off its hair and make it appear fully sheared. That leaves us to clear away what seemed a great impropriety in the motion of the Earth: namely, that while all the planets revolve around the Sun, the Earth alone should go around the Sun in a year not solitary like the others, but in the company of the Moon, together with the whole sphere of the elements — and that at the same time the Moon itself should move every month around the Earth. Here we're forced to exclaim once more, and to exalt the admirable sharp-sightedness of Copernicus, and at the same time to grieve his misfortune, since he doesn't live in our time — when, to take away the apparent absurdity of the Earth and Moon traveling in convoy, we see Jupiter, almost another Earth, going around the Sun in 12 years not in convoy with one Moon, but accompanied by four Moons, along with everything that may be contained within the orbits of the four Medicean stars.

Sagredo
For what reason do you call the four Jovian planets Moons?

Salviati
That's how they'd appear to anyone looking at them from Jupiter. For in themselves they're dark, and they receive their light from the Sun — which is plain from their becoming eclipsed when they enter the cone of Jupiter's shadow; and since only the hemisphere facing the Sun is lit, to us — who are outside their orbits and nearer the Sun — they always show up fully bright. But to someone on Jupiter they'd show fully bright when they were in the upper parts of their circles, while in the lower parts — that is, between Jupiter and the Sun — they'd be seen from Jupiter as crescents. In short, they'd go through, for the Jovians, the very same changes of shape that the Moon goes through for us Earthlings. Now you see how marvelously these first three strings harmonize with the Copernican system, that at the start seemed so dissonant. From this, meanwhile, Simplicio can see with what probability one may conclude that not the Earth, but the Sun, is at the center of the planets' revolutions. And since the Earth is placed among the worldly bodies that undoubtedly move around the Sun — that is, above Mercury and Venus, and below Saturn, Jupiter, and Mars — won't it likewise be highly probable, and perhaps necessary, to grant that it too goes around the Sun?

Simplicio
These occurrences are so great and conspicuous that it's not possible Ptolemy and his other followers had no knowledge of them; and having had it, they must surely also have found a way to give a sufficient account of such striking appearances — and one quite fitting and plausible, too, since for so long it's been accepted by so very many.

Salviati
You reason very well; but you must know that the chief aim of the pure astronomers is only to give an account of the appearances in the heavenly bodies, and to fit to them, and to the motions of the stars, such structures and arrangements of circles that the motions calculated from them answer to those same appearances — caring little about admitting some extravagance that, in fact, on other grounds, might be hard to swallow. And Copernicus himself writes that in his early studies he had restored the science of astronomy on those same suppositions of Ptolemy, and had corrected the motions of the planets so well that the computations answered very neatly to the appearances and the appearances to the calculations — so long, that is, as each planet was taken separately. But he adds that when he then tried to put together the whole structure out of the particular constructions, what resulted was a monster and a chimera made of limbs utterly out of proportion to one another and altogether incompatible; so that, however well the part of the pure calculating astronomer was satisfied, there was still no satisfaction and repose for the philosophical astronomer. And because he understood very well that if the celestial appearances could be saved by assumptions false in nature, it could be done far better from true suppositions, he set himself to search diligently whether any of the distinguished men of antiquity had attributed to the world a structure other than the commonly received one of Ptolemy. And finding that certain Pythagoreans had in particular attributed to the Earth the daily rotation, and others the annual motion as well, he began to check against these two new suppositions the appearances and the particulars of the planets' motions — all of which he had readily at hand — and seeing the whole correspond with its parts with marvelous ease, he embraced this new arrangement and found his repose in it.

Simplicio
But what extravagances are there in the Ptolemaic arrangement that aren't even greater in this Copernican one?

Salviati
In Ptolemy are the diseases, and in Copernicus their remedies. And first: won't all the sects of philosophers call it a great impropriety that a body naturally moving in a circle should move irregularly about its own center and regularly about some other point? And yet the fabric of Ptolemy is full of such misshapen motions; whereas in Copernicus every motion is uniform about its own center. In Ptolemy one must assign contrary motions to the heavenly bodies, and make them all move from east to west and, at the very same time, from west toward east; whereas in Copernicus all the celestial revolutions run one single way, from west to east. But what shall we say of the apparent motion of the planets, so irregular that not only do they now go fast and now slower, but sometimes stop altogether, and even afterward, for a good stretch, turn back? To save this appearance Ptolemy introduced enormous epicycles, fitting one after another to each planet, with certain rules of incongruent motions — all of which are done away with by a single, utterly simple motion of the Earth. And wouldn't you call it, Simplicio, a very great absurdity if, in Ptolemy's construction — where each planet is assigned its own orbits, one above another — it were often necessary to say that Mars, set above the sphere of the Sun, dropped so low that, breaking through the solar orb, it descended below it and came nearer the Earth than the body of the Sun, and a little later rose immeasurably above it again? And yet this extravagance, and others, are cured by the single, utterly simple annual motion of the Earth.

Sagredo
These stations, retrogressions, and direct motions, which have always struck me as great improbabilities, I'd like to understand better — how they come about in the Copernican system.
