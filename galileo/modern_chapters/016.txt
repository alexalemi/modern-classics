The Second Day

(Part 4 of 20)

Salviati
Before we go any further, I have to tell Sagredo that in these discussions of ours I'm playing the Copernican — wearing him, almost, as an actor wears a mask. But as for what these arguments I seem to produce in his favor have actually worked in me, inwardly, I don't want you to judge that from my talk while we're in the heat of performing the play, but after I've taken off the costume — for you may find me a different man from the one you see on stage. Now let's move ahead. Ptolemy and his followers produce another experience, similar to the one about projectiles, drawn from the things that hang in the air a long while, detached from the Earth — the clouds, and birds on the wing. Since one can't say the clouds are carried along by the Earth, not being attached to her, it doesn't seem possible that they could keep up with her speed; rather, it ought to look to us as if they were all racing westward at tremendous velocity. And if we, carried by the Earth, run through our whole parallel in twenty-four hours — and that's at least sixteen thousand miles — how could the birds keep pace with such a course? Whereas in fact we see them fly east just as readily as west, or in any direction whatever, without the least perceptible difference. Besides that: if, when we're galloping on horseback, we feel the air strike our faces pretty briskly, what a wind we ought to feel blowing perpetually out of the east, carried as we'd be on so rapid a course against the air! And yet nothing of the kind is felt at all. Then there's another very ingenious argument, taken from a certain experience, and it goes like this. Circular motion has the power to extrude — to fling off, scatter, and drive away from its center the parts of the moving body, whenever the motion is not quite slow or the parts are not fastened together very solidly. So if, for example, we set one of those great wheels spinning at top speed — the kind inside which one or two men walk in order to move enormous weights, like the massive stones of a mangle-press, or loaded boats that get dragged overland from one waterway to another — then unless the parts of that rapidly whirling wheel were bound together more than solidly, they would all fly apart; and however tenaciously stones or other heavy stuff might be fastened to its outer surface, they couldn't withstand the impetus, which would hurl them with great violence in every direction, far from the wheel and, in consequence, from its center. If the Earth, then, were spinning with so much greater a speed — so very much greater — what weight, what tenacity of mortar or cement, would hold the rocks, the buildings, whole cities, and keep so headlong a whirl from launching them into the sky? And men and beasts, who aren't attached to the Earth at all — how would they resist so great an impetus? Whereas, on the contrary, we see them, and far feebler resistances too — little stones, sand, leaves — resting on the Earth in perfect quiet, and settling back down onto her when they fall, even with the slowest of motions. There, Simplicio, are the most powerful arguments taken, so to speak, from terrestrial things. There remain those of the other kind — the ones that concern celestial appearances — arguments which really aim more at proving that the Earth is at the center of the universe, and hence at stripping her of the annual motion around it that Copernicus assigns her. Being of somewhat different material, they can be brought out after we've weighed the strength of the ones proposed so far.

Sagredo
What do you say, Simplicio? Does it seem to you that Salviati possesses the Ptolemaic and Aristotelian arguments, and knows how to lay them out? Do you think any Peripatetic is as much a master of the Copernican demonstrations?

Simplicio
If it weren't for the high opinion I've formed, from our conversations so far, of Salviati's solid learning and Sagredo's sharpness of wit, I would — with their kind leave — take myself off without hearing another word, since it seems impossible to me that such palpable experiences can be contradicted; and I'd want to rest in my old opinion without listening further, because it seems to me that even if that opinion were false, being propped on such plausible reasons would make it excusable. And if these are fallacies, what true demonstrations were ever so beautiful?

Sagredo
All the more reason, then, that we should hear Salviati's replies. If they are true, they must be more beautiful still — infinitely more beautiful — and those arguments must be ugly, ugly in the extreme, if that metaphysical proposition holds which says the true and the beautiful are one and the same thing, as are the false and the ugly. So, Salviati, let's lose no more time.

Salviati
The first argument Simplicio produced, if I remember rightly, was this: the Earth cannot move in a circle, because such motion would be violent for her, and therefore not perpetual; and the reason it would be violent was that if it were natural, her parts would also move naturally in a circle — which is impossible, because the natural motion of the parts is straight downward. To this I answer that I'd have been glad if Aristotle had made himself a little clearer when he said "the parts would also move circularly," because this moving-in-a-circle can be understood in two ways. One is that every particle separated from its whole would move circularly around its own center, tracing out its own little circlets; the other is that as the whole globe turns around its center in twenty-four hours, the parts also circle that same center in twenty-four hours. The first would be an absurdity no smaller than saying that every part of a circle's circumference must itself be a circle, or that because the Earth is spherical, every piece of earth must be a ball — because that's what the axiom eadem est ratio totius et partium — whole and parts follow the same rule — supposedly demands. But if he meant it the other way — that the parts, imitating the whole, would naturally move around the center of the whole globe in twenty-four hours — then I say that is exactly what they do; and it will fall to you, standing in for Aristotle, to prove that they don't.

Simplicio
Aristotle proves it in that same passage, when he says that the natural motion of the parts is the straight motion toward the center of the universe, so that circular motion cannot naturally belong to them.

Salviati
But don't you see that those very words contain the refutation of this answer too?

Simplicio
How? And where?

Salviati
Doesn't he say that circular motion would be violent for the Earth, and therefore not eternal? And that this is absurd, because the order of the world is eternal?

Simplicio
He says so.

Salviati
But if what is violent cannot be eternal, then, turning it around, what cannot be eternal cannot be natural. Now the Earth's downward motion cannot possibly be eternal; therefore still less can it be natural — nor can any motion be natural to her that isn't also eternal. But if we make the Earth mobile with circular motion, that motion can be eternal for her and for her parts, and therefore natural.

Simplicio
Straight motion is supremely natural to the parts of the Earth, and it is eternal; it will never happen that they fail to move with straight motion — always understanding, of course, that impediments are removed.

Salviati
You're equivocating, Simplicio, and I want to see if I can free you from the equivocation. So tell me: do you believe that a ship sailing from the Strait of Gibraltar toward Palestine could sail toward that shore eternally, moving always at the same steady pace?

Simplicio
Certainly not.

Salviati
And why not?

Simplicio
Because that voyage is confined and bounded between the Pillars of Hercules and the coast of Palestine; and the distance being bounded, it's crossed in a finite time — unless someone chose to turn back with a contrary motion and then repeat the same trip over again; but that would be an interrupted motion, not a continuous one.

Salviati
A perfectly true answer. But the voyage from the Strait of Magellan across the Pacific, through the Moluccas, around the Cape of Good Hope, and from there through the same strait again and across the Pacific once more, and so on — do you believe that voyage could be kept up forever?

Simplicio
It could; for this being a circuit that returns into itself, by repeating it infinitely many times it could be perpetuated without any interruption.

Salviati
So on this route a ship could keep on sailing eternally.

Simplicio
It could, provided the ship were incorruptible; but with the ship falling apart, the voyage would of necessity come to an end.

Salviati
But in the Mediterranean, even if the ship were incorruptible, it still couldn't move perpetually toward Palestine, since that voyage is bounded. So two things are required for a moving body to move eternally without pause: one, that the motion be, by its own nature, unbounded and infinite; the other, that the moving body be likewise incorruptible and eternal.

Simplicio
All of that is necessary.

Salviati
Then you've already confessed, out of your own mouth, that it's impossible for any moving body to move eternally in a straight line — since straight motion, whether you take it upward or downward, is by your own account bounded by the circumference and by the center. So even if the moving body — the Earth — is eternal, still, since straight motion is not by its nature eternal but bounded through and through, it cannot naturally belong to the Earth. Indeed, as was said just yesterday, Aristotle himself is forced to make the terrestrial globe eternally stationary. And when you say that the Earth's parts will always move downward, impediments being removed, you equivocate mightily; because, on the contrary, they have to be impeded, opposed, and forced if you want them to move at all. Once they've fallen, they must be violently thrown back up in order to fall a second time. And as for impediments, these only prevent their reaching the center; for if there were a well passing clear beyond the center, even then no clod of earth would move past that point — except insofar as, carried by its impetus, it overshot it, only to come back and finally settle there. So as for maintaining that motion along a straight line belongs, or can belong, naturally either to the Earth or to any other moving body while the universe keeps its perfect order — give that up entirely, and (unless you're willing to grant her circular motion) put all your strength into maintaining and defending her immobility.

Simplicio
As for the immobility, it seems to me that Aristotle's arguments, and even more the others you've produced, establish it necessarily so far; and it will take great things, in my judgment, to refute them.

Salviati
Then let's come to the second argument, which was this: those bodies of which we're certain that they move circularly all have more than one motion — except the primum mobile, the first and outermost sphere. So if the Earth moved circularly, she would have to move with two motions, from which changes in the risings and settings of the fixed stars would have to follow; but no such thing is seen to follow; therefore, and so on. The simplest and most fitting answer to this objection lies in the argument itself; Aristotle himself puts it into our mouths — and it cannot be, Simplicio, that you haven't seen it.

Simplicio
I haven't seen it, and I still don't.

Salviati
That can't be, because it's too plainly there.

Simplicio
With your permission, I'd like to take a look at the text.

Sagredo
We'll have the text brought in this instant.

Simplicio
I always carry it in my pocket. Here it is — and I know the exact place: the second book of On the Heavens, chapter 14. Here we are, text 97: Praeterea, omnia quae feruntur latione circulari, subdeficere videntur — "Moreover, all bodies carried with circular motion are seen to lag behind, and to move with more than one motion, except the first sphere; hence the Earth too, whether she moves around the center or is placed at the center, must move with two motions. But if that happened, there would necessarily be shifts and turnings among the fixed stars — which is not seen to happen; rather, they always rise and set at the same places." Now here I see no fallacy whatever, and the argument strikes me as absolutely conclusive.

Salviati
And to me this fresh reading has confirmed the fallacy in the reasoning — and uncovered another falsehood besides. So pay attention. There are two positions, or let's say two conclusions, that Aristotle wants to attack. One belongs to those who, placing the Earth at the center, make her turn upon herself, around her own center; the other to those who, setting her far from the center, make her travel with circular motion around that center. And he attacks both of these positions jointly, with the same argument. Now I say he errs in each attack: his error against the first position is an equivocation, a paralogism, and against the second it's a false consequence. Let's come to the first position — the one that puts the Earth at the center and makes her turn upon herself around her own center — and confront it with Aristotle's objection, which runs: all movable bodies that move circularly seem to lag behind, and move with more than one motion, except the first sphere (that is, the primum mobile); therefore the Earth, moving around her own center while placed at the center, must move with two motions and must lag behind; but if that were so, the risings and settings of the fixed stars would have to vary, which is not seen to happen; therefore the Earth does not move, and so on. Here is the paralogism; and to expose it, I argue with Aristotle like this. You say, Aristotle, that the Earth placed at the center cannot move upon herself, because one would then have to assign her two motions. So if it were not necessary to assign her more than a single motion, you would not hold it impossible for her to move with that single one — since it would have been beside the point to locate the impossibility in the plurality of motions if she couldn't move even with one alone. Now of all the moving bodies in the world, you make exactly one move with a single motion, and all the rest with more than one; and you declare that this body is the first sphere — the one by which all the stars, fixed and wandering alike, appear to us to move in concert from east to west. Then if the Earth could be that first sphere, moving with a single motion that made the stars appear to move from east to west, you wouldn't deny it to her. But whoever says that the Earth, placed at the center, turns upon herself assigns her no motion other than the very one by which all the stars appear to move from east to west; and so she turns out to be that very first sphere which you yourself concede moves with a single motion. So, Aristotle, if you want to conclude anything, you must demonstrate that the Earth placed at the center cannot move even with a single motion — or else that not even the first sphere can have a single movement. Otherwise you commit the fallacy inside your own syllogism and put it on open display, denying and granting the same thing at once. I come now to the second position, that of those who, placing the Earth far from the center, make her mobile around it — who make her, that is, a planet, a wandering star. Against this position the argument does have force, and as to its form it is conclusive; but it fails in its matter. For grant that the Earth does move in that fashion, and moves with two motions: it still does not follow of necessity that changes must then occur in the risings and settings of the fixed stars, as I'll explain in its proper place. And here I'm quite willing to excuse Aristotle's error — indeed, I want to praise him for having brought forward the subtlest argument against Copernicus's position that can be brought. And if the objection is acute and, in appearance, utterly conclusive, you'll find the solution that much more subtle and ingenious — one that could not have been found by a mind less acute than Copernicus's; and from the difficulty of understanding it you may gauge the far greater difficulty of finding it in the first place. For now let's leave the answer hanging; you'll hear it at its proper place and time, after we've restated this same objection of Aristotle's and, what's more, fortified it considerably in his favor. Now let's pass to the third argument, also Aristotle's, about which there's no need to say anything further, since it was answered well enough between yesterday and today: he repeats that the motion of heavy bodies is naturally along a straight line toward the center, then inquires whether toward the center of the Earth or the center of the universe, and concludes that it is naturally toward the center of the universe, and per accidens toward that of the Earth. So we can pass to the fourth, on which we'll need to dwell a good while, because it's founded on the very experience from which most of the remaining arguments then draw their force. Aristotle says, then, that a most certain proof of the Earth's immobility is that we see projectiles shot straight up return along the same line to the same place they were fired from — and this even when the motion reaches a very great height. This could not happen if the Earth moved: for during the time the projectile is moving up and down, separated from the Earth, the place where its motion began would slide a long stretch eastward, thanks to the Earth's turning, and in falling the projectile would strike the ground that far away from the said place. Here, then, we can fit in the argument of the ball fired straight up from a cannon, and likewise the other one, used by Aristotle and by Ptolemy, of seeing heavy bodies fall from great heights along a straight line, perpendicular to the Earth's surface. Now, to begin untying these knots, I ask Simplicio: if someone denied to Ptolemy and Aristotle that heavy bodies, falling freely from a height, come down by a straight and perpendicular line — that is, one aimed at the center — by what means would they prove it?

Simplicio
By means of the senses: which assure us that that tower is straight and plumb, and show us the stone, as it falls, grazing along it without leaning a hair's breadth to one side or the other, and striking at the tower's foot exactly below the spot it was dropped from.

Salviati
But if it happened that the terrestrial globe were turning — and consequently carrying the tower along with it — and the stone in falling were still seen to graze the tower's edge, what would its motion then have to be?

Simplicio
In that case one would rather have to say "its motions": for there would be one by which it came down from top to bottom, and it would need another in order to follow the tower's course.

Salviati
Its motion, then, would be a compound of two: the one by which it measures the tower, and the other by which it follows it. And from this compound it would result that the stone no longer traces that simple straight and perpendicular line, but a slanting one — and perhaps not straight at all.

Simplicio
Whether it wouldn't be straight, I can't say; but I understand well that of necessity it would be slanting, and different from the other, straight perpendicular line it traced when the Earth stood still.

Salviati
Therefore, from merely seeing the falling stone graze the tower, you cannot affirm with certainty that it traces a straight and perpendicular line — unless you first suppose that the Earth stands still.

Simplicio
Just so; because if the Earth were moving, the stone's motion would be slanting, not perpendicular.

Salviati
There, then, is the paralogism of Aristotle and Ptolemy, plain and evident — and uncovered by you yourself: the very thing to be demonstrated is assumed as already known.

Simplicio
How so? To me it looks like a syllogism in proper form, not a begging of the question.

Salviati
Here's how. Tell me: in a demonstration, isn't the conclusion set down as unknown?

Simplicio
Unknown — otherwise demonstrating it would be superfluous.

Salviati
But the middle term — mustn't that be known?

Simplicio
Necessarily; otherwise it would be an attempt to prove ignotum per aeque ignotum — the unknown by the equally unknown.

Salviati
Our conclusion to be proved, the unknown one — isn't that the stability of the Earth?

Simplicio
It is.

Salviati
And the middle term, which has to be known — isn't that the straight and perpendicular fall of the stone?

Simplicio
That is the middle term.

Salviati
But wasn't it concluded only a moment ago that we can have no knowledge that the fall is straight and perpendicular unless we first know that the Earth stands still? So in your syllogism the certainty of the middle term is drawn from the uncertainty of the conclusion. You see, then, what the paralogism is — and how big.
