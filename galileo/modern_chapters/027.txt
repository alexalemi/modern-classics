The Second Day

(Part 15 of 20)

Sagredo
All true things are like that, once they've been found — the whole difficulty is in knowing how to find them. I'm entirely satisfied, and I thank you. And if any other curiosity remains for you on this subject, please, tell it — because, if I may speak freely, I'll say, with Simplicio's leave, that from your discourses I always learn some fine new thing, but from those of his philosophers I can't say I've yet learned anything of much weight.

Salviati
There's unfortunately a great deal more we could say about these matters of local motion — motion from place to place — but, as we agreed, we'll save that for a session of its own. For now I'll say something bearing on the author Simplicio has put forward, who thinks he's granted a great advantage to our side in conceding that the cannonball, in falling from the hollow of the Moon's sphere, could arrive with a speed equal to the speed at which it would have moved in its circle had it stayed up there and turned with the daily rotation. Now I tell him that that ball, falling from the hollow all the way to the center, will pick up a degree of speed well more than double the speed of the daily motion of the Moon's hollow; and I'll show this with assumptions that are perfectly true, and not arbitrary.

You must know, then, that a heavy body, as it falls and keeps gaining fresh speed by the proportion already stated, has in itself, at whatever point of its line of motion it happens to be, such a degree of speed that, if it went on moving with that speed uniformly, without increasing it any further, then in a time equal to the time of its descent it would pass twice the space it had passed in the line of its earlier downward motion. So, for example, if that ball, in coming from the hollow of the Moon to its center, has used up 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds, I say that on reaching the center it finds itself established in such a degree of speed that, if with that speed — without increasing it any further — it went on moving uniformly, it would pass in another 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds twice the space, that is, as much as the whole diameter of the lunar orb. And since from the hollow of the Moon to the center is 196,000 miles, which the ball passes in 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds, then (given what has been said) if the ball went on moving with the speed it has on arriving at the center, it would pass, in another 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds, twice that space, that is, 392,000 miles: but the same ball, staying in the hollow of the Moon — whose circuit is 1,232,000 miles — and moving with it at the daily motion, would cover in the same time, that is, in 3 hours, 22 minutes, and 4 seconds, 172,880 miles, which is far less than half of the 392,000 miles. There, then, you see how the motion in the hollow is not what the modern author says — of a speed impossible for the falling ball to share, and so on.

Sagredo
The argument would run beautifully and set my mind at rest, if only that one item were settled for me — the moving body's passing twice the space it fell through, in another time equal to the time of its descent, supposing it went on moving uniformly with the greatest degree of speed it had acquired in falling. That's a proposition you've assumed as true once before too, but never proved.

Salviati
That's one of the things our friend has demonstrated, and you'll see it in due time; but meanwhile, with a few conjectures, I want — not to teach you anything new — but to shake you loose from a certain contrary opinion, by showing you that perhaps it can be so. Hang a lead ball by a long, thin thread fastened to the ceiling; if we pull it aside from the vertical and then let it go free, haven't you noticed that as it swings down it passes, of its own accord, beyond the vertical by very nearly as much again?

Sagredo
I've observed it very well, and seen — especially if the ball is quite heavy — that it rises so little short of its drop that I've sometimes believed the upward arc equal to the downward, and so wondered whether its swings might go on forever; and I'd believe they would, if one could remove the hindrance of the air, which, resisting being parted, slows and impedes the pendulum's motion a little. But the hindrance is slight indeed — witness the great number of swings that happen before the moving body comes fully to rest.

Salviati
The motion wouldn't go on forever, Sagredo, even if the air's hindrance were removed entirely, because there's another one, far more hidden.

Sagredo
And what's that? Nothing else comes to my mind.

Salviati
You'll enjoy hearing it, but I'll tell you later; for now let's go on. I put the observation of this pendulum to you so that you'd grasp that the impetus acquired in the downward arc, where the motion is natural, is of itself powerful enough to drive that same ball, by violent motion, through just as much space in the matching upward arc; of itself, I say, once all external hindrances are removed. And I think it's understood without any doubt that, just as in the downward arc the speed keeps growing right down to the lowest point of the vertical, so from that point, up the other arc, it keeps diminishing to the topmost extreme point — diminishing by the very same proportions by which it first went on increasing, so that the degrees of speed at points equally distant from the lowest point are equal to one another. From this it seems to me — reasoning by a certain fittingness — that I may believe that, if the terrestrial globe were bored through the center, a cannonball dropping down such a well would pick up, by the time it reached the center, such an impetus of speed that, having passed the center, it would drive it up on the far side through just as much space as the fall had been, its speed always diminishing beyond the center by decrements matching the increments gained in descending; and the time spent in this second, upward motion would, I think, be equal to the time of the descent.

Now, if the moving body — by successively diminishing, down to total extinction, the highest degree of speed it had at the center — carries itself in a given time through a given space, the same space it had traversed in an equal time while gaining speed from the total lack of it up to that highest degree, then it seems quite reasonable that, if it moved always with the highest degree of speed, it would pass in an equal time both those spaces together. For if we go through in our mind dividing those speeds into growing and shrinking degrees — like, say, these numbers, so that the first up to 10 are the growing ones and the rest down to 1 the shrinking ones, the former belonging to the time of the descent and the latter to the time of the rise — you'll see that, all joined together, they add up to as much as if one of the two parts had been made entirely of the greatest degrees. And therefore the whole space passed with all the degrees of speed, growing and shrinking (which is the whole entire diameter), must equal the space passed by the greatest speeds, which are half as many in number as the aggregate of the growing and the shrinking together. I know I've explained myself rather clumsily, and God grant I make myself understood.

Sagredo
I think I've understood it very well, and can even show in a few words that I've understood. You meant to say that, when the motion begins from rest and the speed goes on growing successively by equal increments — like those of the counting numbers, starting from one, or rather from zero, which represents the state of rest — laying them out like this, and going on for as many as you please, so that the least degree is zero and the greatest is, say, 5: all these degrees of speed, with which the moving body has moved, make the sum of 15. But if the moving body were to move with just as many degrees in number as these, and each of them equal to the greatest, which is 5, the aggregate of all these speeds would be double the others, that is, 30. And so, moving for an equal time, but with a steady speed equal to that of the highest degree, 5, it will have to pass twice the space it passed in the accelerated time, which began from the state of rest.

Salviati
You, true to your very quick and very subtle grasp, have explained the whole thing far more clearly than I did, and have even put me in mind to add something more. For since in accelerated motion the increase is continuous, one cannot parcel out the degrees of speed — which is forever growing — into any fixed number, because, changing from moment to moment, they are always infinite. So we can better illustrate our meaning by picturing a triangle, which would be this one, ABC. Take on the side AC as many equal parts as you like — AD, DE, EF, FG — and through the points D, E, F, G draw straight lines parallel to the base BC. Here I want us to imagine that the parts marked off on the line AC are equal times, and that the parallels drawn through the points D, E, F, G represent for us the degrees of speed, accelerated and increasing equally in equal times; and that the point A is the state of rest, from which the moving body, setting out, has, say, in the time AD acquired the degree of speed DH; in the next stretch of time it has increased the speed above the degree DH up to the degree EI; and so it has made it greater in the times that follow, according to the growth of the lines FK, GL, and so on. But because the acceleration happens continuously, from moment to moment, and not in discrete jumps from one whole chunk of time to another — the terminus A being posited as the least moment of speed, that is, as the state of rest and as the first instant of the ensuing time AD — it's clear that before the degree of speed DH is acquired in the time AD, the body has passed through infinitely many other degrees, smaller and smaller, gained in the infinitely many instants that are in the time DA, corresponding to the infinitely many points in the line DA. So, to represent the infinity of degrees of speed that come before the degree DH, we must imagine infinitely many lines, always smaller and smaller, drawn from the infinitely many points of the line DA, parallel to DH; and this infinity of lines represents for us, in the end, the surface of the triangle AHD. And in this way we'll understand that any space passed by the moving body with a motion that, beginning from rest, goes uniformly accelerating, has used up and made use of infinitely many degrees of growing speed, answering to the infinitely many lines that, beginning from the point A, are imagined drawn parallel to the line HD and to IE, KF, LG, BC, the motion continuing as far as you please. Now let's complete the whole parallelogram AMBC, and prolong up to its side BM not only the parallels marked in the triangle, but the infinity of those imagined produced from all the points of the side AC. And just as BC was the greatest of the triangle's infinite lines, representing for us the greatest degree of speed the moving body acquired in the accelerated motion, and the whole surface of that triangle was the mass and the sum of all the speed with which, in the time AC, it passed such a space, so the parallelogram comes to be a mass and aggregate of just as many degrees of speed, but each one equal to the greatest, BC — and this mass of speed turns out to be double the mass of the growing speeds of the triangle, just as the parallelogram is double the triangle. And therefore, if the moving body that fell made use of the degrees of accelerated speed answering to the triangle ABC, and passed a certain space in a certain time, it's quite reasonable and probable that, making use of the uniform speeds answering to the parallelogram, it will pass, with steady motion in the same time, twice the space passed by the accelerated motion.

Sagredo
I'm entirely satisfied. And if you call this a merely probable argument, what will the necessary demonstrations be like? Would to God that in all of common philosophy even one could be found as conclusive as this!

Simplicio
In natural science one mustn't go looking for exact mathematical proof.

Sagredo
But isn't this matter of motion a natural question? And yet I find that Aristotle doesn't demonstrate for me even the least little property of it. But let's not sidetrack our discussion any further; and you, Salviati, please don't fail to tell me what you hinted was the cause of the pendulum's stopping, over and above the medium's resistance to being parted.

Salviati
Tell me: of two things hanging from unequal lengths, doesn't the one fastened to the longer cord make its swings less frequent?

Sagredo
Yes — provided they moved through equal distances from the vertical.

Salviati
That moving out more or less matters not at all, because the same pendulum always makes its swings in equal times, whether they're very long or very short — that is, whether the pendulum is drawn very far or very little from the vertical; and even if they aren't perfectly equal, they differ imperceptibly, as experience can show you. But even were they very unequal, that wouldn't hurt our case — it would help it. So let's mark the vertical AB, and let a weight C hang from the point A on the cord AC, and another, higher up on the same cord, be E. Draw the cord AC aside from the vertical, and then let it go free: the weights C and E will move through the arcs CBD and EGF. And the weight E, hanging from a shorter distance — and also, by your own account, drawn aside less — wants to come back sooner and make its swings more frequent than the weight C, and so it will keep C from running as far out toward the terminus D as it would if it were free; and thus, bringing it a continual hindrance at every swing, it will finally reduce it to rest. Now the cord itself (take away the weights) is a compound of many heavy pendulums — that is, each of its parts is such a pendulum, attached nearer and nearer to the point A, and so disposed to make its swings ever more and more frequent; and consequently it's able to bring a continual hindrance to the weight C. A sign of this is that, if we observe the cord AC, we'll see it stretched not in a straight line but in an arc; and if, instead of a cord, we take a chain, we'll see this effect far more plainly, especially by drawing the heavy weight C well aside from the vertical AB — for, the chain being composed of many jointed links, each quite heavy, the arcs AEC and AFD will be seen noticeably curved. For this reason, then — that the parts of the chain, the nearer they are to the point A, want to make their swings more frequent — they don't let the lower parts run on as far as they naturally would; and by continually subtracting from the swings of the weight C, they finally stop it, even if the air's hindrance could be taken away.

Sagredo
The books have just arrived, right on time. Take them, Simplicio, and find the passage in question.

Simplicio
Here it is, where he begins to argue against the Earth's daily rotation, having first refuted the annual one: Motus Terrae annuus asserere Copernicanos cogit conversionem eiusdem quotidianam; alias idem Terrae hemispherium continenter ad Solem esset conversum, obumbrato semper averso — and so half the Earth would never see the Sun.

Salviati
It seems to me, from this very first step, that this man hasn't rightly pictured Copernicus's position; for if he'd noticed how Copernicus keeps the axis of the terrestrial globe perpetually parallel to itself, he wouldn't have said that half the Earth would never see the Sun, but that the year would have been a single natural day — that is, that over all parts of the Earth there'd have been six months of day and six months of night, as happens now to those who live under the pole. But let that be forgiven him, and let's come to the rest.

Simplicio
It goes on: Hanc autem gyrationem Terrae impossibilem esse, sic demonstramus — this whirling of the Earth we prove to be impossible, as follows. What comes next is the explanation of the following figure, where you can see drawn a lot of heavy bodies descending, and light ones ascending, and birds that hold themselves up in the air, and so on.

Sagredo
Show me, please. Oh, what lovely pictures — what birds, what balls, and what other pretty things are these?

Simplicio
These are balls coming from the hollow of the Moon.

Sagredo
And this one, what is it?

Simplicio
It's a snail — the kind we call buovoli here in Venice — and it too comes from the hollow of the Moon.

Sagredo
Yes, yes — that's because the Moon has such great power over these shelled creatures, which we call pesci armai.

Simplicio
This next is that calculation I mentioned, of the journey that a point of the Earth placed under the equator — and also on the parallel of 48 degrees — would make in a natural day, in an hour, in a minute, and in a second. And then follows this, where I fear I may have gone wrong in reporting it; so let's read it: His positis, necesse est, Terra circulariter mota, omnia ex aëre eidem etc. Quod si hasce pilas aequales ponemus pondere, magnitudine, gravitate, et in concavo spherae lunaris positas libero descensui permittamus, si motum deorsum aequemus celeritate motui circum (quod tamen secus est, cum pila A etc.), elabentur minimum (ut multum cedamus adversariis) dies sex: quo tempore sexies circa Terram etc.

Salviati
You reported this man's objection all too faithfully. From this you can see, Simplicio, with how much caution people ought to proceed who want to talk others into believing things that perhaps they don't believe themselves: because it seems impossible to me that this author shouldn't have noticed he was imagining a circle whose diameter — which, for mathematicians, is less than a third of the circumference — was more than 12 times greater than the circumference itself; an error that makes something less than one out to be more than 36.

Sagredo
Perhaps these mathematical proportions, which are true in the abstract, don't answer quite so exactly once they're applied concretely to physical, material circles — though it does seem to me that coopers, to find the radius of the head they need to make for a barrel, use the mathematicians' abstract rule, even though such barrel-heads are very material, concrete things. So let Simplicio give this author's excuse, and tell us whether he thinks physics can differ so much from mathematics.

Simplicio
The retreat doesn't seem enough to me, because the discrepancy is too great; and in this case I wouldn't know what else to say, except that quandoque bonus etc. — even the best of them nods now and then. But granting that Salviati's calculation is the sounder one, and that the time of the ball's fall was no more than three hours, it still seems to me that, coming from the hollow of the Moon — so vast an interval away — it would be a marvelous thing for it to have an instinct from nature to keep itself always over the same point of the Earth that it stood above when it set out, rather than falling behind by an enormous interval.

Salviati
The effect may be marvelous, or not marvelous but natural and ordinary, according to what the preceding conditions are. For if the ball (in keeping with the author's own assumptions), while it lingered in the hollow of the Moon, had the circular motion of the twenty-four hours together with the Earth and with the rest of what's contained inside that hollow, then the very same power that made it go around before it descended will go on making it go around during the descent too; and so far is it from failing to follow the Earth's motion — from having to fall behind — that it ought rather to run ahead of it, since, as it draws nearer to the Earth, the circular motion has to be made through continually smaller circles: so that, the ball keeping the same speed it had in the hollow, it ought, as I said, to outrun the Earth's whirl. But if the ball, up in the hollow, had no such circling, then in descending it's under no obligation to keep itself perpendicularly over that point of the Earth which lay beneath it when the fall began; nor will Copernicus, nor any of his followers, say so.

Simplicio
But the author will press the point, as you see, asking from what principle this circular motion of heavy and light bodies derives — that is, whether from an internal principle or an external one.

Salviati
Keeping to the problem we're dealing with, I say that the principle which made the ball go around while it was in the Moon's hollow is the same one that keeps up its circling during the descent too; I'll then leave the author free to make it internal or external as he likes.
