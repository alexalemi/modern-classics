The Second Day

(Part 20 of 20)

Sagredo
I seem to hear Kepler answering him that, after all, there are animals that refresh themselves from weariness by rolling about on the ground, and that we needn't fear the terrestrial globe wearing itself out — on the contrary, one might reasonably say it enjoys a perpetual and utterly tranquil rest, keeping itself in an eternal rolling-about.

Salviati
Sagredo, you're too clever by half, and too fond of satire. But let's set the jokes aside while we're dealing with serious matters.

Sagredo
Forgive me, Salviati, but what I'm saying isn't so far beside the point as you perhaps make it out to be. A motion that serves as rest and removes the weariness from a body worn out by travel can far more easily serve to keep that weariness from ever setting in — since preventive remedies are easier than curative ones. And I hold it for certain that if the motion of animals proceeded like this one attributed to the Earth, they wouldn't tire at all; for a body's growing tired derives, to my mind, from employing a single part to move both itself and all the rest of the body. To walk, for instance, we use only the thighs and legs, which have to carry themselves and everything else besides; whereas you'll notice the motion of the heart is, as it were, tireless, because it moves itself alone. What's more, I don't know how true it is that an animal's motion is natural rather than violent — indeed I think one can truly say that the soul moves the animal's limbs naturally by a preternatural motion, a motion beyond their own nature. For if upward motion is preternatural to heavy bodies, then raising the legs and thighs, which are heavy bodies, in order to walk, can't be done without violence, and therefore not without effort on the mover's part. Climbing a staircase carries the heavy body upward, against its natural inclination, and from that follows weariness, by way of gravity's natural resistance to such motion. But to move a movable body with a motion to which it has no resistance whatever — what tiring, what draining of power and force, is there to fear in the mover? And why should force be spent where none is exercised at all?

Simplicio
It's the contrary motions — the ones the terrestrial globe is imagined to move by — that the author builds his objection on.

Sagredo
We've already said they aren't contrary at all, and that on this point the author badly deceived himself, so that the whole force of the objection turns back against the objector himself — once he wants the primum mobile to sweep all the lower spheres along, against the very motion those spheres are exercising at the same time and continuously. So it's the primum mobile that ought to get tired, since, on top of moving itself, it has to drag along so many other spheres — spheres that, what's more, fight back against it with a contrary motion. And so that final conclusion the author drew — that when you run through the effects of nature you always meet things favorable to the opinion of Aristotle and Ptolemy, and never anything that doesn't tell against Copernicus — needs a great deal of rethinking. Better to say that, since one of these two positions is true and the other necessarily false, it's impossible for any reason, experience, or sound argument ever to be found in favor of the false one, just as none of these things can be repugnant to the true one. So there's bound to be a great difference between the arguments and reasons produced on the one side and the other, for and against these two opinions — and I'll leave you yourself to judge their force, Simplicio.

Salviati
Sagredo, carried away by the quickness of your wit, you cut off my train of thought a moment ago, just as I was about to say something in reply to this last argument of the author's. And even though you've answered him more than sufficiently, I'd still like to add a little something I had in mind at the time. He sets it down as highly improbable that a dissipable, corruptible body like the Earth could move perpetually with a regular motion — especially since we see that animals, in the end, tire and need rest; and he heightens the improbability by insisting that such a motion would have to be of incomparable and immense speed, compared with an animal's. Now, I can't see why the Earth's speed should trouble him at present, when the speed of the starry sphere — so very much greater — gives him no more concern than that of a millstone making a single revolution in 24 hours. If, because the speed of the Earth's turning is on the model of the millstone's, nothing of greater consequence follows than that, then let the author stop fearing that the Earth will tire — for not even the feeblest, laziest animal you please, I mean not even a chameleon, would wear itself out by moving no more than five or six arm-lengths in 24 hours. But if he wants to consider the speed not on the millstone's model, but in absolute terms — inasmuch as in 24 hours the moving body has to pass through an enormous space — then he ought to be far more reluctant to grant that speed to the starry sphere, which, at a speed incomparably greater than the Earth's, has to carry along with it thousands of bodies, each one vastly larger than the terrestrial globe.

It would remain now for us to look at the proofs by which the author concludes that the new stars of 1572 and 1604 were sublunar, and not celestial, as the astronomers of those times generally persuaded themselves — a truly great undertaking. But since this piece of writing is new to me, and long, with all its calculations, I've thought it would be more practical for me to look over as much of it as I can between this evening and tomorrow morning, and then tomorrow, returning to our usual discussions, to report what I've gathered. And if we have time left over, we'll come to discuss the annual motion attributed to the Earth. Meanwhile, if you have anything to say — and Simplicio in particular — about matters bearing on the daily rotation, which I've now examined at such length, we still have a little time left to talk it over.

Simplicio
I've nothing left to say, except that the discussions we've had today have struck me as full of very sharp and ingenious thoughts, brought forward on Copernicus's side to confirm the Earth's motion — but I don't feel persuaded to believe it. Because, in the end, what's been said comes to nothing more than that the reasons for the Earth's stability aren't conclusive; and yet no demonstration whatever has been produced on the other side that necessarily proves and clinches the Earth's mobility.

Salviati
I never undertook, Simplicio, to move you from your opinion, and I'd hardly dare to hand down a final verdict on so great a dispute. It has only been my intention — and it will be in the discussions to come as well — to make plain to you that those who have believed this extremely swift 24-hour motion belongs to the Earth alone, and not to the whole universe with the Earth alone left out, didn't persuade themselves it could and should be so blindly, as the saying goes. Rather, they had thoroughly seen, heard, and weighed the reasons for the contrary opinion, and answered them, too, not lightly. With this same intention — whenever it suits you and Sagredo — we can move on to consider that other motion, attributed to the same terrestrial globe first by Aristarchus of Samos and then by Nicholas Copernicus: a motion which, as I believe you've already heard, is made beneath the zodiac, within the space of a year, around the Sun, set motionless at the center of that zodiac.

Simplicio
The question is so great and so noble that I'll listen to it discussed with the keenest curiosity, expecting to hear everything that can be said on the subject. Afterward I'll go on, at my own leisure, reflecting more deeply on the things I've heard and have yet to hear; and even if I gain nothing else, it'll be no small thing to be able to discuss the matter on firmer ground.

Sagredo
Then, so as not to tire Salviati any further, let's put a full stop to today's discussions, and tomorrow we'll take up the conversation again as usual, in the hope of hearing some great new things.

Simplicio
I'll leave the book on the new stars behind, but I'll carry this one of conclusions back with me, to look over what's written in it against the annual motion — which is to be the subject of tomorrow's discussions.
