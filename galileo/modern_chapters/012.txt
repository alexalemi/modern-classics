The First Day

(Part 12 of 12)

Sagredo
To that one might answer either that there are enormous dews there, or that it rains during their night — that is, when the Sun isn't shining on it.

Salviati
If other evidence gave us signs that things are generated there the way they are here, and rain were the only ingredient missing, we could come up with this or some other arrangement to stand in for it — the way the flooding of the Nile stands in for rain in Egypt. But since we haven't found one single feature that matches ours, out of the many that would be needed to produce similar effects up there, there's no point straining to introduce even one — and that one not because we have any solid observation of it, but merely because nothing rules it out. Besides, if I were asked what my first impression and plain natural reasoning tell me about whether the things produced there are like ours or different, I would always answer: utterly different, and entirely beyond our imagining — for that, it seems to me, is what the richness of nature and the omnipotence of her Creator and Governor demand.

Sagredo
It has always seemed to me the height of rashness when people make human capacity the measure of what nature can do and knows how to do — when in fact the opposite is true: there is not a single effect in nature, however small, that the most speculative minds can ever completely understand. That vain presumption of understanding everything can have no other root than never having understood anything at all; for anyone who had experienced, even once, the perfect understanding of one single thing, and had truly tasted what knowledge is made of, would recognize that of the infinity of other conclusions he understands nothing.

Salviati
Your argument is airtight. And confirming it we have the experience of those who understand something, or once did: the wiser they are, the more clearly they recognize — and the more freely they confess — how little they know. And the wisest man in Greece, pronounced so by the oracles themselves, said openly that he knew he knew nothing.

Simplicio
Then we'd have to say that either the oracle or Socrates himself was lying — the one proclaiming him the wisest of men, the other declaring that he knew himself to be utterly ignorant.

Salviati
Neither follows, because both pronouncements can be true. The oracle judges Socrates wisest above other men, whose wisdom is limited; Socrates knows that he knows nothing when measured against absolute wisdom, which is infinite. And since, against infinity, much is just as small a portion as little, or as nothing — to reach an infinite number, piling up thousands gets you no closer than piling up tens, or zeros — Socrates saw very well that his finite wisdom was nothing next to the infinite wisdom he lacked. But since there is nonetheless some knowledge to be found among men, and it isn't shared out equally to all, Socrates could hold a larger portion of it than anyone else — and so the oracle's answer came true.

Sagredo
I think I understand this point very well. Among men, Simplicio, there is the power to act, but not shared equally by all: no one doubts that an emperor's power is far greater than a private person's, yet both are nothing compared with divine omnipotence. Among men there are some who understand farming better than many others; but what does knowing how to plant a vine cutting in a trench have to do with knowing how to make it take root, draw in nourishment, and sort out from that nourishment the part that's good for making leaves, this other part for the tendrils, that one for the clusters, another for the grapes, still another for the skins — all of which are the works of supremely wise nature? This is one single, particular operation among the countless ones nature performs, and in it alone we recognize an infinite wisdom; so we may conclude that divine knowledge is infinitely infinite.

Salviati
Here's another example. Wouldn't we say that the power to discover a beautiful statue inside a block of marble lifted Michelangelo's genius far, far above the ordinary minds of other men? And yet that work is nothing but the imitation of a single posture — one outward, surface arrangement of the limbs of a motionless man. What is it compared to a man made by nature, composed of so many parts outside and in, so many muscles, tendons, nerves, and bones, serving so many different movements? And what shall we say of the senses, of the powers of the soul, and finally of understanding itself? Can't we say, with good reason, that the making of a statue falls infinitely short of the forming of a living man — indeed, of the forming of the lowliest worm?

Sagredo
And what difference do we suppose there was between Archytas' dove — that ancient mechanical bird — and one of nature's own?

Simplicio
Either I'm not one of these men who understand, or there is an open contradiction in this talk of yours. Among the greatest tributes you pay to man, made by nature — indeed as the greatest of them all — you count this power of understanding; and a moment ago you were saying, with Socrates, that his understanding was nothing. So it would follow that not even nature has understood how to make an intellect that understands.

Salviati
A very sharp objection. To answer it, we have to fall back on a philosophical distinction, and say that understanding can be taken in two ways: intensive — in depth — or extensive — in range. Taken extensive, that is, with respect to the multitude of things there are to be understood, which are infinite, human understanding is as nothing, even if it understood a thousand propositions — because a thousand, next to infinity, is like zero. But taking understanding intensive — insofar as that term means grasping some proposition intensively, that is, perfectly — I say that the human intellect does understand some propositions as perfectly, and holds them with as absolute a certainty, as nature herself has. Such are the pure mathematical sciences, geometry and arithmetic. Of these the divine intellect knows infinitely more propositions, to be sure, since it knows them all; but of those few the human intellect does grasp, I believe its knowledge equals the divine in objective certainty — for it reaches the point of comprehending their necessity, and beyond necessity there does not seem to be any greater sureness.

Simplicio
This strikes me as very bold and daring talk.

Salviati
These are commonplace propositions, far from any shadow of rashness or daring, and they take nothing away from the majesty of divine wisdom — just as saying that God cannot make what has been done be undone diminishes His omnipotence not at all. But I suspect, Simplicio, that you've taken alarm because my words reached you with a certain ambiguity in them. So, to explain myself better: as for the truth that mathematical demonstrations give us knowledge of, it is the same truth that divine wisdom knows. But I will gladly grant you that the way God knows the infinitely many propositions, of which we know a few, is supremely more excellent than ours. Ours proceeds by reasoning, by passages from one conclusion to the next; His is a single, simple intuition. Where we, for example, to win the knowledge of a few of the properties of the circle — which has infinitely many — begin from one of the simplest, take it as the definition, and pass by reasoning to a second, and from that to a third, then a fourth, and so on, the divine intellect, by the simple apprehension of the circle's essence, comprehends the whole infinity of its properties without any reasoning through time. And in fact all those properties are virtually contained in the definitions of all things anyway — and being infinite, perhaps they are, in their essence and in the divine Mind, one single property. Nor is any of this entirely unknown to the human intellect either; it is only veiled by a deep, dense fog, which thins and clears in part whenever we have made ourselves masters of some conclusions firmly demonstrated and possessed so readily that we can run swiftly among them. For, in the end, what is it for the square on the side opposite the right angle of a triangle to be equal to the squares on the other two sides, if not for parallelograms on a common base between parallel lines to be equal to each other? And isn't that, finally, the same as two surfaces being equal that, when fitted together, do not spill over each other but close within the same boundary? Now these passages, which our intellect makes in time, moving step by step, the divine intellect runs through in an instant, like light — which is the same as saying that they are all always present to it. So I conclude that our understanding — both in the manner and in the number of things understood — is surpassed by the divine at an infinite distance. But I do not for all that debase it so far as to count it absolutely nothing. No: when I go considering how many and how marvelous are the things men have understood, investigated, and accomplished, I recognize and understand all too clearly that the human mind is a work of God, and one of the most excellent.

Sagredo
Many times, along the lines of what you're saying now, I have turned over in my own mind how great the sharpness of human ingenuity is. And when I run through the many marvelous inventions men have found, in the arts as in letters, and then reflect on my own knowledge — so far from promising me the discovery of anything new that I can hardly hope even to learn what has already been found — I am confounded with wonder and afflicted with despair, and count myself little short of miserable. If I look at one of the excellent statues, I say to myself: "And when would you know how to strip the excess away from a block of marble, and uncover so beautiful a figure hidden inside it? When would you know how to mix and spread different colors over a canvas or a wall, and with them represent every visible object, like a Michelangelo, a Raphael, a Titian?" If I look at what men have discovered in dividing up the musical intervals, in laying down precepts and rules for handling them to the marvelous delight of the ear — when will I ever be done being amazed? What shall I say of all the many, and so different, instruments? What wonder does the reading of the excellent poets fill us with, for anyone who attends closely to the invention of their ideas and the unfolding of them? What shall we say of architecture? What of the art of navigation? But above all stupendous inventions, what towering mind was his who imagined finding a way to communicate his most hidden thoughts to any other person whatsoever, however distant in place and in time? To speak with those who are in the Indies; to speak to those who are not yet born, and will not be born for a thousand or ten thousand years? And with what ease? — with the various arrangements of twenty little characters on a page. Let this be the seal of all humanity's admirable inventions, and the close of our reasonings for today. And now that the hottest hours are past, I think Salviati will enjoy going out to taste the cool of the evening on our boat; and tomorrow I shall be expecting you both, to carry on with the discussions we have begun.
