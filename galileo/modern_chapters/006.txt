The First Day

(Part 6 of 12)

Salviati
Gently, Simplicio. What does this modern author of yours say about the new stars of 1572 and 1604, and about the sunspots? As for the comets, I myself would make little difficulty about placing their birth below the Moon or above it; I've never put much stock in Tycho's long-windedness, and I feel no reluctance at all about believing their matter is elemental stuff, free to rise as high as it pleases without meeting any obstacle in the impenetrability of the Peripatetic heaven — which I take to be far thinner, more yielding, and more subtle than our own air. As for the parallax calculations, there is first the doubt whether comets are even subject to that effect, and then the inconstancy of the observations the computations are built on; together these make me equally suspicious of the one set of opinions and the other — all the more because it seems to me the Anti-Tycho sometimes bends the observations to fit his own design, or writes off as faulty the ones that stand against it.

Simplicio
As for the new stars, the Anti-Tycho makes short work of them in four words: such modern new stars, he says, are no certain parts of the celestial bodies, and if his adversaries want to prove that there is alteration and generation up there, they must demonstrate changes in the stars that were charted ages ago — the ones nobody doubts are celestial things — and that they can never do by any means. As for the matter some claim is generated and dissolved on the face of the Sun, he makes no mention of it at all; from which I gather he takes it for a fable, or for illusions of the telescope, or at most for little effects produced in the air — in short, for anything in the world except celestial matter.

Salviati
But you, Simplicio — what answer have you worked out to the objection of these troublesome spots that have come along to muddy the heavens, and worse still, the Peripatetic philosophy? As that philosophy's intrepid defender, you must have found some remedy, some solution — and you mustn't cheat us out of it.

Simplicio
I have heard various opinions on this particular point. "Some say they are stars which, like Venus and Mercury, revolve around the Sun in orbits of their own, and show themselves dark to us when they pass beneath it; and since they are very numerous, it often happens that some of them cluster together and afterwards separate again. Others believe them to be impressions formed in the air; others, illusions of the lenses; and others, other things still. But I lean strongly toward believing — indeed I hold it for certain — that they are an aggregate of many and various opaque bodies, running together almost at random: which is why we often see, within a single spot, ten or more of these tiny bodies that can be counted, irregular in shape, looking to us like flakes of snow, or tufts of wool, or hovering flies. They shift position among themselves, now scattering, now clustering, and above all beneath the Sun, around which, as around their center, they keep moving. But it does not therefore follow that they are generated and corrupted; rather, at times they hide behind the body of the Sun, and at other times, though well clear of it, they cannot be seen because of the nearness of its overwhelming light. For within the Sun's eccentric orb there is built up a kind of onion, composed of many layers one inside another, each of them studded with certain small spots, and each in motion; and although their movement at first seemed inconstant and irregular, nevertheless it is said to have been observed lately that within fixed periods the very same spots return exactly." This seems to me the most serviceable remedy found so far for accounting for the appearance while still preserving the incorruptibility and ingenerability of the heavens; and if it should fall short, there will be no lack of loftier minds to find better ones.

Salviati
If what we are disputing were some point of law, or one of the other human studies, where there is neither truth nor falsehood, then one could rely a great deal on subtlety of wit, fluency of speech, and wider reading in the authors, and hope that whoever excelled in those things would make his case appear, and be judged, the stronger. But in the natural sciences, whose conclusions are true and necessary and where human preference counts for nothing, a man had better take care not to plant himself in defense of the false — because a thousand Demostheneses and a thousand Aristotles will be left unhorsed by any average mind that has had the luck to seize on the truth. So, Simplicio, put out of your head this thought and this hope of yours, that there could ever be men so much more learned, well-read, and steeped in books than the rest of us that they can make the false become true in defiance of nature. And since, of all the opinions produced so far about the essence of these sunspots, the one you have just laid out strikes you as the true one, it follows — if that is so — that all the others are false. And to free you from this one as well, which is an utterly false chimera, I will pass over the thousand other improbabilities in it and bring you just two observations against it. The first is that many of these spots are seen to be born in the middle of the solar disk, and many likewise to dissolve and vanish far from the Sun's edge — a necessary argument that they are generated and dissolved; for if they appeared there by mere local motion, without generation and corruption, they would all be seen to enter and exit across the outermost rim. The other observation, for anyone not stuck in the lowest grade of ignorance about perspective: from the change in their apparent shapes, and the apparent change in their speed of motion, it follows necessarily that the spots lie right against the body of the Sun — that they touch its surface and move with it or upon it, and in no way circle around it in orbits at a distance. The motion proves it: toward the rim of the solar disk it appears extremely slow, and toward the middle, faster. The shapes prove it too: toward the rim they appear extremely narrow compared with how they show in the middle regions, and this is because in the middle they are seen full-face, as they really are, while toward the rim, where the globe's surface curves away, they show foreshortened. And both diminutions, of shape and of motion, for anyone who has known how to observe and calculate them carefully, answer precisely to what ought to appear if the spots lie against the Sun — and clash inexcusably with their moving in circles at any distance from the solar body, however small — as our mutual friend has demonstrated at length in his Letters on Sunspots, addressed to Mark Welser. From the same change of shape it also follows that none of them is a star, or any other body of spherical shape; for among all shapes the sphere alone is never seen foreshortened, and can never present itself as anything but perfectly round. So if any single spot were a round body, as all stars are held to be, it would show the same roundness in the middle of the solar disk as near the edge; whereas their foreshortening so much, showing so slender near the rim and broad and spacious toward the middle, makes us certain that they are flakes of little depth or thickness compared with their length and breadth. And as for its having "lately been observed" that the spots come back exactly the same after their fixed periods — don't you believe it, Simplicio; whoever told you that means to deceive you. Notice that he kept quiet about the ones that are generated and dissolved on the face of the Sun far from the rim; nor did he say a word about that foreshortening, which is a necessary argument that they lie against the Sun. What truth there is about the same spots returning is nothing more than what may already be read in the Letters I mentioned: namely, that some of them may last so long that they do not break up within a single revolution around the Sun — a circuit that is completed in less than a month.

Simplicio
To tell the truth, I have not made observations long or careful enough to make me master of the quod est — the plain fact — of this matter. But I certainly mean to make them, and then to try for myself whether I can bring what experience offers us into accord with what Aristotle demonstrates — for it is obvious that two truths cannot contradict each other.

Salviati
Whenever you want to square what your senses show you with the soundest doctrines of Aristotle, you'll have no trouble at all. Doesn't Aristotle say that, because of their great distance, the things of the heavens cannot be treated with much certainty?

Simplicio
He says so plainly.

Salviati
And doesn't he likewise affirm that what experience and the senses show us must be preferred to any argument, however well-founded it may seem? And doesn't he say this flatly, without the least hesitation?

Simplicio
He does.

Salviati
Then of these two propositions, both of them Aristotle's own doctrine, the second — that the senses must be put before argument — is a much firmer and more resolute doctrine than the other, which holds the heavens unalterable. And so you will be philosophizing more Aristotelianly if you say, "The heavens are alterable, because my senses tell me so," than if you say, "The heavens are unalterable, because reasoning persuaded Aristotle it was so." Add to this that we can reason about the things of the heavens far better than Aristotle could; for in confessing that such knowledge was difficult for him because of their distance from the senses, he concedes that anyone to whom the senses could present those things better would be able to philosophize about them with greater assurance. Now we, thanks to the telescope, have brought the heavens thirty and forty times closer than they were to Aristotle, so that we can make out a hundred things in them that he could not see — among others, these spots on the Sun, which were absolutely invisible to him. Therefore we can treat of the heavens and the Sun with more assurance than Aristotle.

Sagredo
I can see straight into Simplicio's heart, and I can tell he is deeply shaken by the force of these all-too-conclusive arguments. But on the other side, when he sees the great authority Aristotle has won with the whole world, considers the number of famous interpreters who have labored to explain his meaning, and sees the other sciences, so useful and so necessary to the public, resting a great part of their esteem and reputation on Aristotle's credit — all this confounds him and frightens him badly. I can almost hear him saying: "Once Aristotle is pulled down from his seat, who is to settle our controversies? What other author are we to follow in the schools, the academies, the universities? What philosopher has written out every part of natural philosophy, and so methodically, without leaving behind even a single particular conclusion? Must we lay waste the great building that shelters so many travelers? Must we destroy that refuge, that Prytaneum — that civic hall — where so many students find such comfortable lodging, where, without ever exposing yourself to the weather, merely by turning a few pages you acquire complete knowledge of nature? Must we flatten the very fortress in which we live secure against every enemy assault?" I pity him — no less than I would pity some lord who, over long years, at immense expense, with the labor of hundreds upon hundreds of craftsmen, built himself a most noble palace, and then sees it threatening to collapse because the foundations were badly laid; and who, rather than watch, heartbroken, while the walls hung with so many beautiful paintings are pulled down, the columns that hold up the splendid galleries topple, the gilded ceilings fall, and the doorframes, the pediments, the marble cornices installed at such cost all come to ruin — tries to stave off the collapse with chains, props, braces, buttresses, and shores.

Salviati
Oh, Simplicio needn't fear any such collapse just yet; I would undertake to insure him against the damage at far smaller expense. There is no danger that so great a multitude of clever, sharp-eyed philosophers will let themselves be overrun by one or two men making a bit of noise; without so much as turning the points of their pens against them, by silence alone they will sink them into contempt and derision with the whole world. It is a perfectly idle notion to think anyone could establish a new philosophy just by refuting this author or that one. One would first have to learn how to remake men's brains — to render them fit to tell the true from the false — and that is something only God can do. But from one line of talk to the next, where have we wandered off to? I couldn't find my way back to the trail without your memory as my guide.

Simplicio
I remember it perfectly well. We were on the Anti-Tycho's answers to the objections against the immutability of the heavens; among those objections you inserted this one about the sunspots, which he never touches, and I believe you meant to consider his answer to the challenge of the new stars.

Salviati
Now the rest of it comes back to me. To carry on with the matter: it seems to me the Anti-Tycho's answer contains several things that deserve to be called out. First of all, if the two new stars — which he cannot avoid placing in the highest parts of the heavens, and which lasted a long while and finally vanished — give him no trouble in maintaining the inalterability of the heavens, simply because they were not certain parts of it, nor changes in the ancient stars, then what is the point of campaigning with so much anxiety and sweat against the comets, to banish them from the celestial regions at any cost? Wasn't it enough to say of them exactly what he said of the new stars — that since they are not certain parts of the heavens, nor alterations in any of its stars, they do no damage either to the heavens or to the doctrine of Aristotle? Second, I cannot quite make out what is really going on in his mind. He grants that alterations in the stars would be destructive of the heavens' prerogatives — their incorruptibility and the rest — and this because the stars are celestial things, as everyone unanimously agrees; and yet it does not disturb him in the least if those same alterations take place elsewhere, outside the stars, in the rest of the celestial expanse. Does he perhaps think the heavens are not a celestial thing? For my part, I always supposed the stars were called celestial things because they are in the heavens, or made of the heavens' own matter — so that the heavens should be more celestial than the stars, just as nothing can be said to be more earthly than earth itself, or more fiery than fire. And then his making no mention at all of the sunspots — which have been conclusively demonstrated to be produced and dissolved, to lie next to the body of the Sun, and to turn with it or around it — gives me a strong hint that this author may be writing more to please others than to satisfy himself. I say this because he shows himself competent in mathematics, and it is impossible that a competent man should not be persuaded by the demonstrations that these materials are necessarily in contact with the body of the Sun, and that they are generations and corruptions so vast that nothing so great ever happens on Earth. And if changes of such size, in such numbers, and so frequent take place on the very globe of the Sun — which may reasonably be reckoned among the noblest parts of the heavens — what argument is left strong enough to persuade us that others cannot take place on the other globes?

Sagredo
I cannot hear it without great astonishment — I will say more, without great resistance from my own intellect — when this quality of being impervious to change, immutable, unalterable, and so on, is held up as a great nobility and perfection in the natural bodies that make up the universe, while conversely being alterable, generable, mutable is counted a great imperfection. For my part, I hold the Earth most noble and admirable precisely because of the many and diverse alterations, mutations, and generations that go on in it without pause. If it were not subject to any change at all — if it were one vast solitude of sand, or a mass of jasper, or if at the time of the Flood the waters covering it had frozen and it had remained one immense globe of crystal, where nothing was ever born or altered or changed — I would count it a great useless carcass in the world, idle through and through, and, in a word, superfluous, as if it did not exist in nature at all; and I would draw the same distinction for it that there is between a living animal and a dead one. And I say the very same of the Moon, of Jupiter, and of all the other globes of the world. The deeper I go into the vanity of popular reasoning on this point, the more shallow and foolish I find it. What greater silliness can be imagined than to call gems, silver, and gold precious, and earth and mud worthless? Doesn't it occur to these people that if earth were as scarce as jewels or the most prized metals, there is not a prince alive who wouldn't gladly pay a load of diamonds and rubies and four cartloads of gold just to have enough earth to plant a jasmine in a little pot, or to sow a China orange and watch it sprout, grow, and put out such lovely leaves, such fragrant flowers, such delicate fruit? So it is scarcity and plenty that raise or sink the price of things with the crowd — the same crowd that will call a diamond magnificent because it resembles pure water, and then would not trade it for ten barrels of the real thing. These people who so exalt incorruptibility, inalterability, and the rest are driven to say such things, I believe, by their great longing to keep on living and by the terror they have of death; and they fail to consider that if men were immortal, their own turn to come into the world would never have arrived. They deserve to run into a Medusa's head and be transmuted into statues of jasper or diamond, so as to become more perfect than they are.

Salviati
And perhaps such a metamorphosis would even work to their advantage; for it is better, I think, not to reason at all than to reason backwards.

Simplicio
There is no doubt whatever that the Earth is far more perfect being, as it is, alterable, mutable, and so on, than it would be as a mass of stone — even if it were one whole diamond, supremely hard and impervious to change. But however much nobility these conditions confer on the Earth, they would make the celestial bodies just that much more imperfect, since in them such qualities would be superfluous: the celestial bodies — that is, the Sun, the Moon, and the other stars, which are ordered to no other use than the service of the Earth — need nothing beyond motion and light to achieve their end.

Sagredo
So nature has produced and set in order all those vast, consummate, most noble celestial bodies — impervious to change, immortal, divine — for no other use than the service of the Earth: a thing changeable, transient, and mortal? For the service of what you yourselves call the dregs of the world, the sink of every filth? To what purpose make the celestial bodies immortal and all the rest, in order to serve something perishable and all the rest? Take away this one use, the service of the Earth, and the innumerable host of celestial bodies is left entirely useless and superfluous, since they have not, and cannot have, any mutual interaction among themselves — all of them being unalterable, immutable, impervious to change. If the Moon, for example, is impervious to change, what do you expect the Sun or any other star to work upon her? The effect would surely be far feebler than that of a man trying to melt down a great mass of gold with a glance or a thought. Besides, it seems to me that if the celestial bodies really do cooperate in the generations and alterations on Earth, then they themselves must be alterable too; otherwise I cannot see how the Moon's or the Sun's being brought to bear upon the Earth to produce generation would be anything more than laying a marble statue beside the bride and expecting children from that union.
