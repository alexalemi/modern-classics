The Third Day

(Part 8 of 16)

Sagredo
I'm quite satisfied that I follow these consequences, and I think I'll fix them in my mind better by checking them against a globe set at that tilt, and then looking at it from different sides. What's left now is for you to tell us what came of it all afterward — how the imagined consequences actually turned out.

Salviati
What came of it was this: as we went on for many, many months making the most careful observations, noting down with the utmost accuracy the crossings of various spots at different times of the year, the outcomes were found to answer the predictions exactly.

Sagredo
Simplicio, if what Salviati says is true — and it won't do to cast doubt on his word — then the Ptolemaics and the Aristotelians are going to need solid arguments, mighty conjectures, and rock-firm experiments to counterbalance a blow of such weight, and to keep their opinion from taking its final tumble.

Simplicio
Easy now, my good sir — perhaps you're not yet where you may fancy you've arrived. For although I haven't fully mastered the matter of Salviati's argument, my logic, when I attend to the form of it, does not teach me that this way of arguing forces me by any necessity to conclude in favor of the Copernican hypothesis — I mean the fixity of the Sun at the center of the zodiac and the mobility of the Earth beneath its circumference. For although it's true that, given such-and-such a rotation of the Sun and such-and-such a circling of the Earth, these and those oddities must necessarily be seen in the sunspots, it does not follow that, arguing the other way around, from seeing such oddities in the spots one must necessarily conclude that the Earth moves along the circumference and the Sun is placed at the center of the zodiac. For who assures me that such oddities couldn't also be seen in a Sun that moves along the ecliptic, by inhabitants of an Earth standing fixed at its center? Unless you first prove to me that no account of this appearance can be given when you make the Sun move and the Earth stand still, I won't budge from my opinion, or from my belief that the Sun moves and the Earth stays motionless.

Sagredo
Simplicio bears himself stoutly, and very shrewdly opposes and upholds the side of Aristotle and Ptolemy; and, if I'm to tell the truth, it seems to me that Salviati's company, brief as it's been, has trained him quite a bit in arguing to the point — an effect I gather it has had on others too. As for inquiring into and judging whether a competent account of the apparent extravagances in the sunspots' motions can be given while leaving the Earth motionless and keeping the Sun in motion, I'll wait for Salviati to lay out his thinking; for it's quite believable that he's reflected on it and drawn out whatever can be produced on the point.

Salviati
I've thought about it many times, and talked it over, too, with my friend and host. As for what the philosophers and astronomers will bring forward to prop up the old system: on the one side we're sure — sure, I say — that the true and pure Peripatetics, laughing at anyone who busies himself with such (to their taste) insipid trifles, will write off all these appearances as empty illusions in the glass, and in this way, with little effort, free themselves from any duty to think further. As for the philosopher-astronomers, after we'd speculated with some care on what might be brought to bear, we found no expedient enough to satisfy, at one and the same time, both the course of the spots and the reasoning of the mind. I'll set out for you what occurred to us, and you'll make of it whatever your judgment dictates.

Grant that the apparent motions of the sunspots are just as we've described above; and set the Earth motionless at the center of the ecliptic, on whose circumference the center of the Sun is placed. Then it necessarily follows that the causes of all the variations we see in those motions must reside in motions belonging to the body of the Sun itself. First, the Sun will have to rotate on its own axis, carrying the spots along with it — spots we've assumed, indeed proved, to be stuck fast to the solar surface. Second, we'll have to say that the axis of the Sun's rotation is not parallel to the axis of the ecliptic — which amounts to saying that it isn't set straight up, at right angles to the plane of the ecliptic. For if it were, the spots would appear to cross by straight lines parallel to the ecliptic; so the axis must be tilted, since the crossings mostly appear made along curved lines. Third, we'll need to say that the tilt of this axis is not fixed, forever pointing at the same point of the universe; rather, that from moment to moment it keeps changing its direction. Because if the lean pointed steadily toward the same point, the spots' crossings would never change their look, but — straight or curved, bent up or down, rising or falling — whatever they appeared once, so they would appear always. So we're forced to say that this axis shifts about: sometimes it lies in the plane of the outermost circle that bounds the visible hemisphere — I mean at those times when the spots' crossings appear made along straight lines, and steeper than ever, which happens twice a year; at other times it lies in the plane of the observer's meridian, in such a way that one of its poles falls in the Sun's visible hemisphere and the other in the hidden one, both of them far from the endpoints — the poles, if you like — of another axis of the Sun, one parallel to the axis of the ecliptic (a second axis we'll necessarily have to assign to the globe of the Sun); far from them, I mean, by exactly as much as the tilt of the axis of the spots' revolution comes to. And further, that the pole falling in the visible hemisphere is at one time in the upper part and at another in the lower; for the crossings give us necessary proof of this happening, at the times when they are balanced and at their greatest curvature — now bulging with their convex side toward the lower part of the solar disk, and now toward the upper. And because these states keep changing continuously — the tilts and the curvings growing now greater and now smaller, the tilts sometimes coming to perfect balance and the curves to perfect straightness — we're necessarily driven to lay it down that this very axis of the spots' monthly revolution has a turning of its own, by which its poles trace out two circles around the poles of yet another axis (the one we must, as I said, assign to the Sun), the radius of those circles matching the amount of that axis's tilt. And the period of this turning must be one year, since that is the time in which all the appearances and variations in the spots' crossings come back around. And that this axis makes its turn about the poles of that other axis — the one parallel to the ecliptic's — and not about any other points, is plainly shown by the greatest tilts and the greatest curvings, which are always of the same size. So that, in the end, to keep the Earth fixed at the center, we'll have to credit the Sun with two motions around its own center, on two different axes: one of them completing its turn in a year, the other in less than a month. And this supposition strikes my mind as very hard, next to impossible. And that comes on top of our having to credit the same solar body with two further motions around the Earth on different axes — with the one tracing out the ecliptic in a year, and with the other making spirals, or circles parallel to the equinoctial, one a day. So that for that third motion, the one we'd have to assign to the globe of the Sun in itself (I don't mean the roughly monthly one that carries the spots, but the other, which has to shift the axis and poles of that monthly one) — there is no reason to be seen why it should finish its period in a year, as though it depended on the annual motion along the ecliptic, rather than in twenty-four hours, as though it depended on the daily motion about the poles of the equinoctial. I know that what I'm saying is, for now, quite obscure; but it will become clear to you when we come to speak of the third motion — an annual one — that Copernicus assigns to the Earth. Now, when these four motions, so incompatible with one another (all of which would have to be credited, of necessity, to the one body of the Sun), can instead be reduced to a single, utterly simple motion, assigned to the Sun on an axis that never alters; and when, without introducing anything new into the motions already assigned to the terrestrial globe on so many other grounds, we can so easily save all these outlandish appearances in the motions of the sunspots — then it really does seem that the option is not to be refused.

This, Simplicio, is as much as has so far occurred to our friend and me that might be offered, in explaining this appearance, by the Copernicans and the Ptolemaics in support of their views. Make of it whatever your own judgment tells you.

Simplicio
I know myself unequal to entering into so weighty a decision; and as for my own opinion, I'll stay neutral — hoping, though, that a time will come when, enlightened by higher contemplations than these human discussions of ours, our minds will be unveiled, and the fog that now keeps them clouded will be lifted away.

Sagredo
Excellent and holy is the counsel Simplicio holds to, and worthy to be received and followed by everyone — for, deriving from the highest wisdom and the supreme authority, it alone can be embraced in safety. But as far as human reasoning is permitted to penetrate — keeping myself within the bounds of conjecture and probable argument — I'll say, a little more decidedly than Simplicio does, that among all the subtle arguments I've ever heard, I've never met anything that struck my mind with greater wonder, or bound my understanding more tightly (setting aside pure geometrical and arithmetical demonstrations), than these two conjectures: the one drawn from the stations and retrogradations of the five planets, the other from these strange behaviors in the motions of the sunspots. And because they seem to me to give, so easily and so lucidly, the true cause of appearances so bizarre — showing how a single simple motion, blended with many others that are likewise simple but different from one another, without raising any difficulty at all, and indeed sweeping away every difficulty that dogs the other position — I keep concluding to myself that those who stay obstinate against this doctrine must either not have heard, or not have understood, these so plainly conclusive arguments.

Salviati
I won't call them either conclusive or inconclusive; for, as I've said before, my aim has not been to settle anything on so lofty a question, but only to lay out the natural and astronomical arguments that I can bring on either side, leaving the decision to others — a decision that, in the end, ought not to be ambiguous. For since one of the two arrangements must necessarily be true and the other necessarily false, it's impossible that (staying, of course, within the bounds of human doctrine) the arguments offered for the true side should not show themselves just as conclusive as those on the other side are empty and ineffective.

Sagredo
Then it must be time we heard the objections in that little book of conclusions, or disquisitions, that Simplicio has brought along.

Simplicio
Here's the book; and here's the passage where the author first briefly describes the system of the world according to Copernicus's position, saying: Terram igitur una cum Luna totoque hoc elementari mundo Copernicus, etc. — "Copernicus, then, has the Earth, together with the Moon and this whole elemental world," and so on.

Salviati
Hold on a moment, Simplicio, for it seems to me that this author, at his very entrance, shows himself to understand very little of the position he's undertaking to refute — since he says Copernicus has the Earth, together with the Moon, trace out the great orb in a year while moving from east to west; a thing which, being false and impossible, was never once asserted by Copernicus. On the contrary, Copernicus has it go the other way — from west to east, I mean, that is, in the order of the signs — which is why the Sun's annual motion then appears as it does, the Sun set motionless at the center of the zodiac. See the overbold self-assurance of the man! — to set himself up to refute another man's doctrine, and be ignorant of its very first foundations, on which the greatest and most important part of the whole structure rests. This is a poor way to win credit with the reader. But let's go on.

Simplicio
Having set out the general system, he begins to raise his objections against this annual motion; and the first are these — which he delivers ironically, in mockery of Copernicus and his followers — writing that in this fantastical arrangement of the world one is bound to utter the most solemn absurdities: namely, that the Sun, Venus, and Mercury are below the Earth; that heavy matter naturally goes upward and light matter downward; that Christ, our Lord and Redeemer, ascended to hell and descended to heaven when he drew near the Sun; that when Joshua commanded the Sun to stand still, it was the Earth that stopped, or else the Sun moved contrary to the Earth; that when the Sun is in Cancer, the Earth runs through Capricorn; that the winter signs make summer and the summer signs make winter; that it is not the stars that rise and set to the Earth, but the Earth to the stars; that the east begins in the west and the west in the east; and, in short, that nearly the whole course of the world is turned upside down.

Salviati
It all pleases me well enough — except for his mixing passages of Holy Scripture, ever to be revered and feared, in among these childish and all-too-scurrilous jibes, and his wanting to wound with sacred things a man who, philosophizing in jest and in play, neither affirms nor denies, but, having laid down certain suppositions or hypotheses, reasons in a familiar way.

Simplicio
It scandalized me too, and not a little — above all with what he adds right after: that although the Copernicans do answer these and other such arguments (though in a very twisted way), still they'll never be able to satisfy and answer the things that follow.

Salviati
That, now, is worst of all — because it implies he has things more forceful and conclusive than the authority of Holy Writ. But please, let us revere Scripture, and pass on to natural and human arguments. Indeed, if among his natural reasons he produces nothing of better sense than what he's brought so far, we may set this whole enterprise aside — for I certainly don't mean to spend a word answering such silly trifles. And what he says, that the Copernicans reply to these objections, is utterly false; nor can anyone believe that a single man would set about wasting his time so uselessly.

Simplicio
I join in the same judgment; but let's hear the other objections, which he brings forward as much more powerful. And here, as you see, with the most exact calculations he concludes that if the great orb of the Earth — the one along which Copernicus has it run once a year around the Sun — were as good as imperceptible next to the immensity of the starry sphere (as Copernicus himself says one must suppose it to be), then one would necessarily have to say and grant that the fixed stars are at an unimaginable distance from us, and that the smallest of them are bigger than the whole great orb itself, and some others far larger than the entire sphere of Saturn — bulks truly all too vast, and incomprehensible, and incredible.

Salviati
I've seen something like this before, urged by Tycho against Copernicus; and it's not just now that I've uncovered the fallacy — or better, the fallacies — of this argument, built on utterly false assumptions and on a statement of Copernicus's own, seized by his opponents with the most pinpoint strictness, in the way those litigants do who, being in the wrong on the main merits of the case, fasten on a single little word the other side let drop in passing, and raise an endless clamor over it. And, for your clearer understanding: when Copernicus had set out those marvelous consequences that follow, in the other planets, from the Earth's annual motion — namely the forward and retrograde motions of the three upper planets in particular — he added that this apparent change (which showed up more in Mars than in Jupiter, Jupiter being farther off, and less still in Saturn, farther off than Jupiter) remained imperceptible in the fixed stars, on account of their immense distance from us compared with the distance of Jupiter or Saturn. Here the opponents of this view rise up; and taking that "imperceptibility" Copernicus spoke of as if he had laid it down for something really and absolutely equal to nothing, and adding that even one of the smaller fixed stars is nonetheless perceptible, since it falls under the sense of sight, they go on calculating — with the help of other false assumptions — and conclude that in Copernicus's doctrine one must admit that a fixed star is far bigger than the whole great orb. Now I, to expose the emptiness of this whole line of argument, will show that from supposing a fixed star of the sixth magnitude to be no bigger than the Sun, it follows by a true demonstration that the distance of those fixed stars from us comes out so great that it is enough to make the Earth's annual motion — which in the planets causes such large and observable variations — show no noticeable effect in them; and at the same time I'll show, point by point, the great fallacies in the assumptions of Copernicus's opponents.

And first, I suppose, with Copernicus himself and in agreement with the opponents, that the radius of the great orb — the half-diameter, that is, which is the distance from the Earth to the Sun — contains 1,208 radii of the Earth itself. Second, I lay down — with their assent, and with the truth — that the apparent diameter of the Sun, at its mean distance, is about half a degree, that is, 30 arcminutes, which are 1,800 seconds, that is, 108,000 thirds. And since the apparent diameter of a fixed star of the first magnitude is no more than 5 seconds, that is 300 thirds, and the diameter of a sixth-magnitude star is 50 thirds (and here lies the greatest error of Copernicus's opponents), it follows that the diameter of the Sun contains the diameter of a sixth-magnitude star 2,160 times. And so, if one supposed a sixth-magnitude star to be really equal to the Sun, and no bigger — which is the same as saying, if the Sun were moved away far enough that its diameter appeared one of the 2,160 parts of what it appears now — its distance would have to be 2,160 times greater than it actually is now; which is as much as to say that the distance of the sixth-magnitude fixed stars is 2,160 radii of the great orb. And because the distance of the Sun from the Earth contains, by common agreement, 1,208 radii of the Earth, while the distance of the fixed stars (as we've said) is 2,160 radii of the great orb, it follows that the radius of the Earth, compared with the great orb, is much larger — nearly double, in fact — than the radius of the great orb is in relation to the distance of the starry sphere. And for that reason, the difference of apparent position in the fixed stars, caused by the diameter of the great orb, can be scarcely more observable than the one we observe in the Sun that arises from the radius of the Earth.
