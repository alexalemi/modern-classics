The Second Day

(Part 1 of 20)

Salviati
Yesterday's digressions, which pulled us off the straight thread of our main discussions, were so many and so varied that I don't know whether I can find the trail again without your help, so as to move forward.

Sagredo
I'm not surprised that you, with your imagination filled and cluttered both with what's been said and with what remains to be said, should find yourself in some confusion. But I, being a simple listener, carry nothing in my head but the things I've heard; so perhaps, by recalling them in summary, I can set the reasoning back on its thread. As far as my memory serves, then, the sum of yesterday's discussions was an examination, from their very foundations, of which of two opinions is the more probable and reasonable: the one which holds that the substance of the celestial bodies is ingenerable, incorruptible, inalterable, impervious to change — in short, exempt from every kind of alteration except change of place, and therefore a fifth essence utterly different from this substance of our elemental bodies, which are generable, corruptible, alterable, and so on; or else the other opinion, which strips this lopsidedness out of the world and holds that the Earth enjoys the very same perfections as the other integral bodies of the universe — that it is, in short, a globe that moves and wanders no less than the Moon, Jupiter, Venus, or any other planet. In the end many particular parallels were drawn between this Earth of ours and the Moon — more with the Moon than with any other planet, perhaps because we have greater and more solid knowledge of her, thanks to her lesser distance. And having finally concluded that this second opinion has more of the likely about it than the first, it seems to me the natural next step was to begin examining whether the Earth should be judged immobile, as most people have believed up to now, or mobile, as certain ancient philosophers believed and as others have come to think in more recent times — and, if mobile, what its motion might be.

Salviati
I already recognize the landmarks of our road. But before we set out any further, I have to say a word about those last remarks of yours — that we concluded the opinion which grants the Earth the same conditions as the celestial bodies to be more likely than its contrary. I concluded no such thing, just as I don't intend to reach a verdict on any other of the disputed propositions. My intention was only to produce, for each side equally, the reasons and replies, the objections and solutions, that others have thought of up to now, along with a few more that have dropped into my mind after long thinking about it — and then to leave the decision to the judgment of others.

Sagredo
I let myself be carried away by my own feeling; and believing that what I felt in myself must be what others felt too, I made universal a conclusion I should have kept particular. I truly was wrong, especially since I didn't know the view of Simplicio, here present.

Simplicio
I'll confess to you that all last night I was ruminating over yesterday's matters, and truly I find many fine, new, and vigorous considerations in them. All the same, I feel myself gripped far more strongly by the authority of so many great writers, and in particular... You're shaking your head, Sagredo, and smirking, as though I'd said something wildly out of line.

Sagredo
I'm only smirking — but believe me, I'm about to burst with the effort of holding back a much bigger laugh. You've reminded me of a wonderful incident I witnessed not many years ago, together with some noble friends of mine whom I could still name for you.

Salviati
Then you'd better tell it, so that Simplicio doesn't go on believing it was he who set you laughing.

Sagredo
Gladly. One day I was at the house of a highly esteemed physician in Venice, where people gathered from time to time — some for their studies, others out of curiosity — to watch a dissection at the hands of an anatomist who was truly as learned as he was careful and skilled. It happened that day that the search was on for the origin and root of the nerves, the subject of a famous controversy between the Galenist physicians and the Peripatetics. The anatomist showed how the great trunk of nerves sets out from the brain, passes through the nape of the neck, then stretches down along the spine and branches out through the whole body, while only a single strand, fine as sewing thread, arrives at the heart. Then he turned to a gentleman he knew to be a Peripatetic philosopher — for whose benefit he had uncovered and demonstrated the whole thing with extraordinary care — and asked whether he was now satisfied and certain that the nerves originate in the brain and not in the heart. And the philosopher, after standing lost in thought for a while, answered: "You have made me see this matter so plainly and palpably that if Aristotle's text did not stand against it — for it says openly that the nerves are born in the heart — I would be forced to confess it true."

Simplicio
Gentlemen, I want you to know that this dispute over the origin of the nerves is by no means as digested and settled as some people may persuade themselves.

Sagredo
Nor will it ever be, safe to say, so long as it has opponents of that sort. But what you say doesn't shave one bit off the extravagance of the Peripatetic's reply: against such palpable evidence of the senses he produced no other experiments or arguments of Aristotle's — only bare authority, the pure Ipse dixit: he himself said it.

Simplicio
Aristotle did not acquire so great an authority except by the force of his demonstrations and the depth of his reasoning. But you have to understand him — and not merely understand him, but have such long practice in his books that you've formed a complete and perfect idea of them, so that every saying of his is always present to your mind. He did not write for the crowd, nor did he bind himself to string out his syllogisms in the ordinary schoolroom order. Instead, using a scattered method, he sometimes placed the proof of a proposition among texts that seem to deal with anything but. So you must hold that whole grand scheme in your head, and know how to combine this passage with that one, to piece this text together with another far removed from it. There's no doubt that whoever has this practice will be able to draw from his books the demonstrations of everything that can be known — because everything is in them.

Sagredo
My dear Simplicio: since having things scattered here and there doesn't bother you, and since you believe that by piecing together and combining various little scraps you can squeeze out their juice — well, what you and the other brave philosophers do with the texts of Aristotle, I will do with the verses of Virgil or Ovid, stitching them into centos — patchwork poems — and using them to explain all the affairs of men and the secrets of nature. But why do I even mention Virgil, or any other poet? I have a little book much shorter than Aristotle or Ovid, which contains all the sciences, and with very little study anyone can form a most perfect idea of it: the alphabet. There's no doubt that whoever knows how to couple and arrange this vowel and that one with those consonants or these others will draw from them the truest answers to every doubt, and the teachings of every science and every art — in exactly the way a painter, from simple colors laid out separately on his palette, goes about combining a little of this with a little of that and a little of the other, and shapes men, plants, buildings, birds, fish; in short, imitates every visible object, though there are no eyes on his palette, no feathers, no scales, no leaves, no stones. In fact it's necessary that none of the things to be imitated, nor any part of them, be actually present among the colors, if you want those colors to be capable of representing everything; if there were feathers among them, say, they'd serve to paint nothing but birds and plumes.

Salviati
There are gentlemen still alive and well who were present when a doctor lecturing in a famous university, hearing the telescope described — he hadn't yet seen it — announced that the invention was lifted from Aristotle. He had a text brought to him, found a certain passage where Aristotle gives the reason why, from the bottom of a very deep well, one can see stars in the sky during the day, and said to the company: "Here you have the well, which stands for the tube; here the thick vapors, from which the invention of the lenses was taken; and here, finally, the strengthening of vision as the rays pass through a transparent medium that is denser and darker."

Sagredo
That's a way of containing everything knowable which is very like the way a block of marble contains within itself one beautiful statue — no, a thousand beautiful statues; the trick is knowing how to uncover them. Or we might say it's like the prophecies of Abbot Joachim, or the answers of the pagan oracles, which are understood only after the prophesied events have happened.

Salviati
And where do you leave the predictions of the horoscope-casters, which after the outcome can be seen so clearly in the birth-chart — or, as they say, the celestial figure?

Sagredo
It's in just this fashion that the alchemists, led on by their melancholy humor, discover that all the loftiest geniuses of the world never really wrote about anything except how to make gold — only, so as to say it without revealing it to the crowd, each went fancifully hiding it under a different cover. And it's the most delightful thing to hear their commentaries on the ancient poets, uncovering the weighty mysteries concealed under the fables: what the loves of the Moon really mean, and her coming down to Earth for Endymion, and her fury at Actaeon; and when Jupiter turns himself into a shower of gold, and when into burning flames; and what great secrets of the art lie in that Mercury the interpreter, in those abductions by Pluto, in those golden boughs.

Simplicio
I believe — indeed in part I know — that the world doesn't lack for thoroughly extravagant brains; but their follies shouldn't redound to the discredit of Aristotle, of whom it seems to me you sometimes speak with too little respect. His antiquity alone, and the great name he has won in the minds of so many distinguished men, should be enough to make him honorable in the eyes of all the learned.

Salviati
That's not how the matter runs, Simplicio. It is certain of his followers, too fainthearted by half, who give occasion — or rather, who would give occasion — for thinking less of him, if we chose to applaud their frivolities. Tell me, honestly: are you really so naive that you don't see that if Aristotle had been there to hear the doctor who wanted to make him the inventor of the telescope, he'd have been far angrier with him than with the people laughing at the doctor and his interpretations? Do you have any doubt that if Aristotle could see the new discoveries in the heavens, he would change his opinion, amend his books, move toward the doctrines that agree with the senses, and drive away from himself those pitifully small brains who too timidly let themselves be drawn into defending his every word — never grasping that if Aristotle were the man they imagine, he'd be an intractable brain, an obstinate mind, a spirit full of barbarity, a tyrant's will, someone who reckoned everyone else to be witless sheep and wanted his own decrees set above the senses, above experience, above nature herself? It is his followers who have given Aristotle his authority; he never usurped it or took it for himself. And since it's easier to hide behind another man's shield than to show one's own face in the open, they're afraid — they don't dare step one single pace away from him; and rather than admit any alteration in Aristotle's heaven, they insolently deny the alterations they can see with their own eyes in nature's heaven.

Sagredo
These people put me in mind of that sculptor who carved a great block of marble into the image of — Hercules, I think it was, or a thundering Jupiter — and gave it, by his marvelous craft, such vividness and ferocity that it frightened everyone who looked at it; and then he began to be afraid of it himself, though all its spirit and menace was the work of his own hands. The terror got such a grip on him that he no longer dared face it with his chisels and mallet.

Salviati
I have often marveled at how these strict upholders of Aristotle's every word can fail to notice what damage they do to his reputation and credit — how much, in trying to swell his authority, they diminish it. When I see them obstinately defending propositions that I can touch with my hand to be manifestly false, and trying to persuade me that this is what a true philosopher does, and that Aristotle himself would do the same, my confidence sinks that he reasoned rightly about the other conclusions that lie beyond my reach. Whereas if I saw them yield and change their minds before manifest truths, I'd be ready to believe that where they held firm, they might have solid demonstrations I hadn't understood or hadn't heard.

Sagredo
Or else — if they felt they'd be staking too much of their own reputation, and Aristotle's, by confessing they hadn't known some conclusion discovered by someone else — wouldn't it be the lesser evil to go find it among his texts, by piecing several of them together, according to the practice Simplicio has described to us? For if everything knowable is in there, then it must follow that everything can be found in there.

Salviati
Sagredo, don't make fun of this stratagem — I can see you propose it as a joke, but it's no laughing matter. Not long ago a philosopher of great name composed a book on the soul, in which, reporting Aristotle's opinion on whether or not it is immortal, he adduced many texts — not the ones cited by Alexander, because in those, he said, Aristotle wasn't even treating that subject, let alone settling anything about it — but others he had dug up himself in various out-of-the-way places, which leaned toward the dangerous reading. Warned that he'd have trouble getting the book licensed, he wrote back to his friend not to let that stop the approval going forward: if no other obstacle turned up, he'd have no difficulty at all in changing Aristotle's doctrine, and with different expositions and different texts he'd defend the contrary opinion — and it would still agree with the mind of Aristotle.

Sagredo
Oh, now this doctor can give me orders any day! He won't let Aristotle bamboozle him — he means to lead Aristotle by the nose and make him talk to order! See how much it matters to know how to seize the right moment. You don't go negotiating with Hercules while he's raging and in a fury, but while he's spinning tales among the Maeonian serving-girls. Ah, the unheard-of cowardice of servile minds! To make yourself a slave of your own free will; to accept another man's decrees as inviolable; to bind yourself to declare yourself persuaded and convinced by arguments so effective, so clearly conclusive, that the defenders themselves cannot decide whether they were even written on that subject, or whether they prove that conclusion at all! And here is the greater madness still: among themselves they are still in doubt whether the author himself held the affirmative side or the negative. What is this but making a wooden statue their oracle — running to it for answers, fearing it, revering it, adoring it?

Simplicio
But if we abandon Aristotle, who is to be our escort in philosophy? Name some author, if you can.

Salviati
You need an escort in unknown and savage country; in open, level places only the blind need a guide. Whoever is blind would do best to stay home; but whoever has eyes in his head and in his mind — those are the escort he should use. Not that I'm saying we shouldn't listen to Aristotle; on the contrary, I applaud reading him and studying him diligently. What I blame is handing yourself over to him as his property, so that you blindly countersign his every word and, without looking for any further reason, are bound to hold it as an inviolable decree. That is an abuse, and it drags another extreme disorder behind it: people stop trying to understand the force of his demonstrations at all. And what is more shameful than to hear, in public disputations about demonstrable conclusions, someone cut in from the sidelines with a text — often written on some entirely different subject — and shut his opponent's mouth with it? If you insist on going on studying this way, then put down the name of philosophers and call yourselves historians, or doctors of memory; for it isn't right that people who never philosophize should usurp the honorable title of philosopher. But we'd better pull back to shore, or we'll drift into an endless sea we couldn't get out of all day. So, Simplicio: come forward, please, with reasons and demonstrations — your own or Aristotle's — and not with texts and bare authorities, because our discussions are to be about the sensible world, and not about a world on paper. Yesterday's discourse hauled the Earth up out of the darkness and set it out under the open sky, showing that the wish to count it among what we call the celestial bodies is not a proposition so beaten and flattened that no breath of life is left in it. It follows, then, that we go on to examine how much probability there is in holding the Earth fixed and wholly immobile — I mean as to its entire globe — and how much likelihood in making it mobile with some motion, and of what kind. And since I'm undecided on this question, while Simplicio, along with Aristotle, stands resolute on the side of immobility, he shall bring out, step by step, the motives for their opinion; I'll bring the replies and the arguments for the contrary side; and Sagredo shall tell us the motions of his own mind, and which way he feels himself pulled.

Sagredo
That suits me very well — provided I also keep the freedom to bring up whatever plain natural common sense may dictate to me now and then.

Salviati
Indeed, that's the very thing I beg of you in particular. For of the easier — the material, so to speak — considerations, I think few have been left out by the writers on the subject, so that only some of the subtler and more remote ones can still be wanting or missing; and for hunting those down, what subtlety could be better suited than the wit of Sagredo, sharp and penetrating as it is?

Sagredo
I am whatever Salviati pleases. But please, let's not slide into yet another kind of digression — the ceremonial kind — because right now I'm a philosopher, and in the schoolroom, not at the Broglio, trading compliments.

Salviati
Then let our contemplation begin with this consideration: whatever motion may be attributed to the Earth, it is necessary that for us — as inhabitants of the Earth and therefore sharers in that motion — it remain completely imperceptible, as if it didn't exist, so long as we look only at terrestrial things; but on the other hand, it is just as necessary that this same motion appear to us as a motion shared in common by all the other bodies and visible objects which, being separated from the Earth, lack it. So the true method for investigating whether any motion can be attributed to the Earth, and if so, what it is, is to consider and observe whether in the bodies separated from the Earth we can detect some appearance of motion that belongs equally to all of them. Because a motion that showed up only in the Moon, say, and had nothing to do with Venus or Jupiter or the other stars, could not possibly belong to the Earth — or to anything but the Moon itself. Now, there is one motion that is the most general and the greatest of all: the one by which the Sun, the Moon, the other planets, and the fixed stars — in short, the whole universe, the Earth alone excepted — appear to us to move together from east to west in the space of twenty-four hours. And this motion, as far as this first appearance goes, might just as well belong to the Earth alone as to all the rest of the world with the Earth left out; for the same appearances would be seen in the one arrangement as in the other. That's why Aristotle and Ptolemy, who had grasped this point thoroughly, argue against no other motion than this daily one when they set out to prove the Earth immobile — except that Aristotle does touch briefly on a certain something against another motion attributed to the Earth by one of the ancients, which we'll speak of in its proper place.
