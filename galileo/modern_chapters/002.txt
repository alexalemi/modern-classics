The First Day

(Part 2 of 12)

Simplicio
The simple motion will be the one performed by a simple body, and the mixed motion the one performed by a compound body.

Sagredo
Very fine indeed. But what's this, Simplicio? A moment ago you wanted simple and mixed motion to teach me which bodies are simple and which are compound; and now you want me to learn, from the simple bodies and the compound ones, which motion is simple and which is mixed. An excellent rule for never coming to know either the motions or the bodies. And besides, you're now declaring that greater speed no longer satisfies you either — you're after a third condition for defining simple motion, where Aristotle contented himself with just one, namely the simplicity of the path. But now, on your account, the simple motion will be the one performed along a simple line, at a certain determinate speed, by a simple movable body. Well, have it however you like, and let's get back to Aristotle — who defined mixed motion for me as the one compounded of the straight and the circular, but then never found me a single body that naturally moves with such a motion.

Salviati
Then I return to Aristotle. He began his discourse very well and methodically; but since his eye was fixed more on reaching and striking a target he had set up in his mind beforehand than on where the argument was straightforwardly leading him, he breaks the thread and comes at us sideways, offering as a known and obvious fact that as for the straight motions up and down, these naturally belong to fire and to earth — and that therefore, besides these bodies here around us, there must be another body in nature to which circular motion belongs, a body all the more excellent as circular motion is more perfect than straight motion. And just how much more perfect the one is than the other, he determines from the perfection of the circular line over the straight one, calling the circle perfect and the straight line imperfect: imperfect because, if it is infinite, it lacks an end and a boundary; and if it is finite, there is something outside it into which it can be extended.

This is the first stone — the base and foundation of the entire fabric of the Aristotelian world — on which all the other properties lean: neither heavy nor light, ingenerable, incorruptible, exempt from every change except change of place, and so on. All these attributes, he asserts, are proper to the simple body that moves with circular motion; and the contrary conditions — heaviness, lightness, corruptibility, and the rest — he assigns to the bodies naturally movable with straight motions. So whenever a flaw turns up in what has been established this far, we may reasonably doubt everything else that gets built on top of it. I don't deny that what Aristotle has introduced so far by a general discourse, resting on universal first principles, is later reconfirmed as he goes along with particular reasons and with experiences — all of which will have to be considered and weighed one by one. But since what has been said so far already presents many difficulties, and not small ones (and first principles and foundations really ought to be secure, firm, and stable, so that one can build on them with full confidence), it may be no bad idea, before we pile the doubts any higher, to see whether by chance — as I suspect — striking out along another road might set us on a straighter and surer path, and whether, with better-considered rules of architecture, we might lay the first foundations properly. So, suspending Aristotle's line of argument for now — we'll take it up again in due course and examine it point by point — I say that of the things he has said so far, I agree with him, and I grant that the world is a body endowed with all the dimensions, and therefore most perfect. And I add that as such it is necessarily supremely ordered — that is, made of parts arranged among themselves in the highest and most perfect order. This assumption I don't believe will be denied by you or by anyone else.

Simplicio
And who would you expect to deny it? First of all, it is Aristotle's own; and besides, the world's very name — cosmos — seems to be drawn from nothing else than the order it so perfectly contains.

Salviati
With that principle established, then, we can immediately conclude that if the integral bodies of the world are to be by nature movable, it is impossible for their motions to be straight, or anything other than circular. The reason is easy and plain enough. Whatever moves with straight motion changes place; and as it keeps moving, it gets farther and farther from the point it started from and from every place it successively passes through. Now if that motion belongs to it naturally, then at the beginning it was not in its natural place — and so the parts of the world were not arranged in perfect order. But we are supposing them to be perfectly ordered; as such, then, it is impossible that they should have from nature a tendency to change place, and consequently impossible that they move with straight motion.

Moreover, since straight motion is by its nature infinite — because the straight line is infinite and indeterminate — it is impossible for any movable body to have from nature a principle of moving along a straight line, that is, toward a place it is impossible to reach, there being no fixed end to arrive at; and nature, as Aristotle himself rightly says, does not undertake to do what cannot be done, nor undertake to move toward a place it is impossible to arrive at. And if someone objects that although the straight line, and consequently motion along it, can be extended to infinity — has no built-in end — nature has nevertheless, so to speak, arbitrarily assigned certain end-points to it, and given her natural bodies natural instincts to move toward them, I will answer that this might perhaps be told as a fable of the first chaos, where indistinct materials wandered about confusedly and in disorder. To set them in order, nature might very suitably have used straight motions — for just as such motions disorder well-arranged bodies when they move them, so they are suited to putting badly arranged ones into order. But after the best possible distribution and placement, it is impossible that any natural inclination to keep moving in a straight line should remain in those bodies; because from straight motion, now, nothing would follow but their removal from their own natural place — that is, their disordering. We can say, then, that straight motion serves to carry the materials for building the work; but once the work is built, it either stays motionless or, if it moves at all, moves only circularly.

Unless we would rather say, with Plato, that the world's bodies too, after they were made and fully established, were moved by their Maker for some time in straight motion, and that afterwards, on arriving at certain determinate places, they were turned one by one into circuits, passing from straight motion to circular — in which they have been maintained ever since, and are preserved still. A lofty thought, and truly worthy of Plato; and I remember hearing our mutual friend, the Academician, discourse on it. If I remember rightly, his discourse went like this. Every body constituted, for whatever cause, in a state of rest, but movable by its nature, will move when set at liberty — provided, that is, that it has by nature an inclination toward some particular place; for if it were indifferent to every place, it would stay at rest, having no more reason to move this way than that. And from its having such an inclination, it necessarily follows that in its motion it will keep continually accelerating. Beginning with the slowest possible motion, it will not acquire any degree of speed without first passing through all the lesser degrees of speed — or, if you prefer, the greater degrees of slowness. For setting out from the state of rest — which is the degree of infinite slowness of motion — there is no reason whatever why it should enter into one particular degree of speed before entering into a smaller one, and into another still smaller before that. On the contrary, it seems entirely reasonable that it should pass first through the degrees nearest to the one it starts from, and from those to the more remote; and the degree from which the body takes up its motion is that of extreme slowness, namely rest. Now, this acceleration of motion happens only as the moving body gains something by its moving; and its gain is nothing other than drawing nearer to the place it desires — the place its natural inclination pulls it toward; and it will make its way there by the shortest route, that is, by the straight line. So we may reasonably say that nature, in order to confer some determinate speed on a movable body first constituted at rest, makes use of having it move, for some time and through some space, with straight motion.

Granting this discourse, let us imagine that God created the body of, say, Jupiter, and had determined to confer on it a certain speed, which it must then keep perpetually uniform. We can say with Plato that He gave it at the beginning a straight and accelerated motion, and then, once it had reached that particular degree of speed, converted its straight motion into circular motion — whose speed is then naturally suited to remain uniform.

Sagredo
I hear this discourse with great pleasure, and I believe the pleasure will be greater still once you've removed one difficulty for me. It is this: I can't quite see how it must necessarily be that a movable body, setting out from rest and entering a motion toward which it has a natural inclination, passes through all the prior degrees of slowness lying between any assigned degree of speed and the state of rest — and those degrees are infinite. Couldn't nature, then, have given the body of Jupiter its circular motion, at just such-and-such a speed, the instant it was created?

Salviati
I did not say, nor would I dare to say, that it was impossible for nature or for God to confer that speed you speak of immediately. But I will say this: de facto — in actual fact — nature does not do it; so that doing it would be an operation outside the natural course of things, and therefore miraculous.

Sagredo
Then you believe that a stone, leaving rest and entering into its natural motion toward the center of the Earth, passes through every degree of slowness below any given degree of speed?

Salviati
I believe it — no, I am sure of it, and sure with such certainty that I can make you sure of it too.

Sagredo
If in the whole of today's reasoning I gained nothing but that one piece of knowledge, I'd count it a handsome return.

Salviati
As far as I can gather from the way you talk, a great part of your difficulty lies in this: that the body has to pass, in some stretch of time — even a very short one — through those infinite degrees of slowness that come before whatever speed it acquires in that time. So before going any further, I'll try to remove that scruple; and it should be an easy job, once I reply that the body does pass through all those degrees, but the passage is made without dwelling in any of them. Since the passage requires no more than a single instant of time, and any little stretch of time whatever contains infinite instants, we will never run short of instants to assign one to each of the infinite degrees of slowness — however brief the time may be.

Sagredo
So far I follow. Still, it seems an astonishing thing to me that a cannonball — for that's how I picture the falling body — which we see come down so headlong that in less than ten pulse-beats it will pass more than two hundred braccia — arm-lengths — of height, should at some point in its motion have been at so small a degree of speed that, had it kept moving at that rate without accelerating any further, it would not have covered that distance in a whole day.

Salviati
Say rather in a whole year — or in ten, or in a thousand; as I will try to persuade you, and perhaps even without your objecting, by a few quite simple questions I'll put to you. So tell me whether you have any difficulty granting that the ball, in coming down, keeps acquiring greater impetus — greater built-up drive — and greater speed.

Sagredo
Of that I am completely sure.

Salviati
And if I say that the impetus acquired at any point whatever of its motion is enough to carry it back up to the height it started from — would you grant me that?

Sagredo
I'd grant it without objection, provided the ball could apply its whole impetus, unimpeded, to that single operation of carrying itself, or another ball equal to it, back to that same height. So, for example, if the Earth were bored through the center, and the ball were let fall from a hundred or a thousand braccia above it, I firmly believe it would pass beyond the center, rising as far as it had fallen. And experience shows me the same thing happening with a weight hanging from a cord: pulled away from the vertical — which is its state of rest — and then set free, it swings down to that vertical and passes beyond it by just as much space, or falls short only by as much as the resistance of the air and the cord and other such accidents impede it. Water shows me the same: descending through a siphon, it climbs back up as far as it came down.

Salviati
You reason perfectly. And since I know you have no doubt about granting that the gaining of impetus comes from moving away from the point where the body starts and drawing nearer to the center toward which its motion tends — will you have any difficulty in granting that two equal movable bodies, even though they descend along different lines, acquire equal impetus, so long as their approach to the center is equal and nothing at all impedes them?

Sagredo
I don't quite understand the question.

Salviati
I'll explain it better by drawing a little figure. So: I'll mark this line AB, parallel to the horizon; on the point B I'll raise the perpendicular BC; and then I'll join the two with the slanting line CA. Now, take the line CA to be an inclined plane, exquisitely polished and hard, down which a perfectly round ball of the hardest material rolls, and let a similar ball fall freely down the perpendicular CB. I ask whether you would grant that the impetus of the one descending along the plane CA, once it has reached the endpoint A, could be equal to the impetus acquired by the other at the point B after its fall down the perpendicular CB.

Sagredo
I resolutely believe it could; because in fact both have approached the center equally, and, by what I granted just now, their impetuses would be equally sufficient to carry them back up to the same height.

Salviati
Now tell me what you believe that same ball would do if it were set on the horizontal plane AB.

Sagredo
It would stay still, that plane having no slope at all.

Salviati
But on the inclined plane CA it would descend — though with a slower motion than down the perpendicular CB.

Sagredo
I was about to answer a flat yes, since it seems necessary to me that the motion along the perpendicular CB must be faster than along the incline CA. And yet, if that's so, how can the body falling along the incline, when it reaches the point A, have as much impetus — that is, as great a degree of speed — as the body falling along the perpendicular has at the point B? These two propositions seem to contradict each other.

Salviati
Then it will seem far more false to you if I say that, taken absolutely, the speeds of the bodies falling along the perpendicular and along the incline are equal. And yet this is a perfectly true proposition — just as it is also true to say that the falling body moves faster along the perpendicular than along the incline.

Sagredo
To my ear those sound like contradictory propositions. And to yours, Simplicio?

Simplicio
They seem the same to me.

Salviati
I think you're teasing me, pretending not to grasp what you understand better than I do. So tell me, Simplicio: when you imagine one movable body being faster than another, what concept do you picture in your mind?

Simplicio
I picture one of them passing a greater space than the other in the same time — or else passing an equal space, but in less time.

Salviati
Very good. And for bodies that are equally fast, what concept do you picture?

Simplicio
I picture them passing equal spaces in equal times.

Salviati
And no other concept than that?

Simplicio
That, it seems to me, is the proper definition of equal motions.

Sagredo
Let's add one more to it, though: let the speeds also be called equal when the spaces passed have the same proportion as the times in which they are passed. That will be a more universal definition.

Salviati
So it is; because it covers equal spaces passed in equal times, and also unequal spaces passed in unequal times — provided those times are proportional to the spaces. Now take up the same figure again, and applying the concept you've formed of the faster motion, tell me why it seems to you that the speed of the body falling along CB is greater than the speed of the one descending along CA.

Simplicio
It seems so to me because, in the time the falling body passes the whole of CB, the descending one will pass, on CA, a part smaller than CB.

Salviati
Just so; and that is how it's verified that the body moves faster along the perpendicular than along the incline. Now consider whether, in this same figure, the other concept might be verified in some way — whether we might find the moving bodies to be equally fast along both the lines CA and CB.

Simplicio
I can see no such thing; in fact it seems to me a contradiction of what's just been said.

Salviati
And what do you say, Sagredo? I certainly wouldn't want to teach you what you already know — the very thing you brought me a moment ago as a definition.

Sagredo
The definition I offered was that movable bodies may be called equally fast when the spaces they pass have the same proportion as the times in which they pass them. So for the definition to apply in the present case, the time of the descent along CA would have to bear the same proportion to the time of the fall along CB as the line CA itself bears to the line CB; but I cannot understand how that can be, given that the motion along CB is faster than along CA.

Salviati
And yet you must understand it. Tell me now: don't these motions keep continually accelerating?

Sagredo
They do — but more in the perpendicular than in the incline.

Salviati
But is this acceleration in the perpendicular such, compared with the acceleration in the incline, that if we took two equal stretches anywhere at all on those two lines, the perpendicular and the incline, the motion in the stretch of the perpendicular would always be faster than in the stretch of the incline?

Sagredo
No indeed. On the contrary, I could take a stretch on the incline in which the speed is far greater than in an equal stretch taken on the perpendicular — and this will happen if the stretch on the perpendicular is taken near the end C, and the one on the incline far away from it.

Salviati
You see, then, that the proposition "motion along the perpendicular is faster than along the incline" does not hold universally, except for motions that begin from the first endpoint — that is, from rest. Without that condition, the proposition would be so defective that even its flat contradictory could be true: namely, that motion on the incline is faster than on the perpendicular; for it is true that on the incline we can take a stretch which the body passes in less time than it takes to pass an equal stretch on the perpendicular. Now, since motion on the incline is in some places faster, and in others slower, than on the perpendicular, it follows that along some places of the incline, the time of the body's motion will bear a greater proportion to the time of the body's motion along certain places of the perpendicular than the space passed bears to the space passed; and in other places, the proportion of time to time will be smaller than the proportion of space to space. For example: let two bodies set out from rest — that is, from the point C — one along the perpendicular CB and the other along the incline CA. In the time in which the body on the perpendicular will have passed the whole of CB, the other will have passed CT, which is smaller. And therefore the time along CT will bear a greater proportion to the time along CB — the two times being equal — than the line TC bears to the line CB, since the same magnitude bears a greater proportion to a smaller one than to a larger. And conversely: if on CA, prolonged as far as need be, we took a stretch equal to CB, but passed in a shorter time, then the time on the incline would bear a smaller proportion to the time on the perpendicular than the space bears to the space. If, then, on the incline and on the perpendicular we can conceive of spaces and speeds such that the proportions between the spaces come out both smaller and greater than the proportions between the times, we may very reasonably grant that there are also spaces along which the times of the motions retain the same proportion as the spaces.
