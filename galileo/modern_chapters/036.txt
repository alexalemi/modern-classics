The Third Day

(Part 4 of 16)

Sagredo
I'm with you on this point; but since this whole business is hemmed in by so much confusion, uncertainty, and error, on what confidence did so many astronomers so positively declare that the new star was very high up?

Salviati
On two kinds of observation, both of them utterly simple, easy, and reliable — and either one alone is more than enough to assure us that the star was set in the firmament, or at least a very long way above the Moon. The first is drawn from the equality, or the very slight inequality, of its distances from the pole, whether it stood in the lowest part of the meridian or in the highest. The second is that it kept, permanently, the very same distances from certain fixed stars near it — in particular from the eleventh star of Cassiopeia, from which it was never more than a degree and a half away. From these two facts one gathers, beyond any doubt, either that there was no parallax at all, or else a parallax so small that a very quick calculation assures us of the star's great distance from the Earth.

Sagredo
But didn't this author take these things into account? And if he did see them, how does he defend himself?

Salviati
We have a saying: that when a man, finding no dodge that will hold up against his own blunders, comes out with the feeblest of excuses, he's trying to catch hold of the ropes of the sky. Well, this author reaches not for the ropes but for the threads of the sky's cobwebs — as you'll plainly see once we go through the two points I just mentioned. First, what the polar distances of each observer show us I've set down in these short calculations. To follow them fully, I must first point out this: whenever the new star, or any other phenomenon, is near the Earth, then as it wheels with the daily rotation around the pole, it will appear farther from the pole when it lies in the lower part of the meridian than when it is in the upper part — as this figure shows. In it, the point T marks the center of the Earth, O the place of the observer, the firmament the arc VPC, and P the pole. The phenomenon, moving along the circle FS, is seen now below the pole, along the ray OFC, and now above it, along the ray OSD, so that the places seen in the firmament are D and C; but the true places, reckoned from the center T, are B and A, equally distant from the pole. Now it is already clear that the apparent place of the phenomenon at S — that is, the point D — is nearer the pole than the other apparent place, C, seen along the ray OFC. That's the first thing to note.

In the second place, you should note that the amount by which the apparent lower distance from the pole exceeds the apparent upper distance from the pole is greater than the lower parallax of the phenomenon. What I mean is that the excess of arc CP (the apparent lower distance) over arc PD (the apparent upper distance) is greater than arc CA (which is the lower parallax). This is easy to gather: for CP exceeds PD by more than it exceeds PB, since PB is greater than PD; but PB equals PA, and the excess of CP over PA is the arc CA; therefore the excess of arc CP over arc PD is greater than arc CA, which is the parallax of the phenomenon placed at F. That's what we needed to know. And to give the author every advantage, let's suppose that the parallax of the star at F is the whole excess of arc CP (the lower distance from the pole) over arc PD (the upper distance).

Now I come to examine what the observations of all the astronomers cited by the author give us — and among them there isn't a single one that doesn't work against him and run contrary to his aim. Let's begin with Busch's. He found the star's distance from the pole, when it was above the pole, to be 28° 10′, and the lower to be 28° 30′, so the excess is 0° 20′, which I'll take (in the author's favor) as if it were all the parallax of the star at F — that is, the angle TFO. The distance from the point overhead, that is arc CV, is 67° 20′. Once these two things are found, extend the line CO, and onto it drop the perpendicular TI, and consider the triangle TOI, in which the angle I is a right angle and the angle IOT is known (being at the tip of the angle VOC, the star's distance from the point overhead). Moreover, in the triangle TIF, also right-angled, the angle F is known, taken as the parallax. So set aside the two angles IOT and IFT, and take their sines, as you see noted. And since in the triangle IOT, of the parts of which the whole sine TO is 100000, the sine TI is 92276, and since in the triangle IFT, of the parts of which the whole sine TF is 100000, the sine TI is 582 — then to find out how many parts TF is, of those of which TO is 100000, we'll say, by the rule of three: when TI is 582, TF is 100000; but when TI is 92276, how much would TF be? Multiply 92276 by 100000; that gives 9227600000; and this must be divided by 582, which yields, as you see, 15854982 — and TF will contain that many parts of those of which TO holds 100000. So, to learn how many lengths TO there are in TF, we'll divide 15854982 by 100000; that gives roughly 158½; and that many semidiameters is the distance of the star F from the center T. And to shorten the operation: seeing that the product of 92276 multiplied by 100000 must first be divided by 582 and then the quotient by 100000, we can reach the same result at once, without multiplying 92276 by 100000, by a single division of the sine 92276 by the sine 582, as you see below — where 92276 divided by 582 gives us that same 158½ or so. So let's keep in mind that simply dividing the sine TI, as the sine of the angle TOI, by the sine TI, as the sine of the angle IFT, gives us the distance we're after, TF, in that many semidiameters TO.

Now see what Peucer's observations give us: his lower distance from the pole is 28° 21′, the upper 28° 2′, the difference 0° 19′, and the distance from the point overhead 66° 27′; from all of which the star's distance from the center comes out at nearly 166 semidiameters.

Here is what Tycho's observation shows us, taking the one most favorable to our opponent: the lower distance from the pole, 28° 13′, and the upper, 28° 2′, leaving the difference, which is 0° 11′, as if it were all parallax; the distance from the point overhead, 62° 15′. Here below is the operation, with the star's distance from the center found to be 276 9/16 semidiameters.

Reinhold's observation, which follows, gives us the star's distance from the center as 793 semidiameters.

From the Landgrave's next observation, the star's distance from the center comes out at 1057 semidiameters.

Taking two of Camerarius's observations most favorable to the author, the star's distance from the center is found to be 3143 semidiameters.

Muñoz's observation gives no parallax, and so puts the new star among the fixed stars, very high up. Hainzel's makes it infinitely far off, but with a correction of half a minute it sets it among the fixed stars; and the same follows from Ursinus, with a correction of 12′. As for the other astronomers, we don't have their distances above and below the pole, so nothing can be drawn from them. Now see how all the observations, of every one of them, agree — against the author — in placing the star in the high celestial regions.

Sagredo
But what defense does he find against such open contradictions?

Salviati
One of those flimsiest of threads. He says the parallaxes are reduced by refraction, which, working in the opposite direction, lifts the phenomenon up, whereas the parallaxes push it down. Now, judge how much this wretched refuge is worth from this: even if the effect of refraction were as powerful as some astronomers have lately claimed, the most it could do to raise a phenomenon higher than its true place above the horizon — when the object is already 23 or 24 degrees up — would be to shave off about 3 minutes of parallax; and that allowance is far too meager to drag the star down below the Moon. In some cases it's even smaller than the advantage we've already granted, in admitting that the excess of the lower distance from the pole over the upper is entirely parallax — an advantage that is far clearer and more palpable than the effect of refraction, whose size I doubt, and not without reason. But there's more. I ask this author whether he believes that the astronomers whose observations he uses knew about these effects of refraction and took them into account, or not. If they knew and reckoned with them, it's reasonable to think they allowed for them in fixing the star's true elevations — making, from the degrees of altitude their instruments showed, the corrections that were fitting on account of the alterations of refraction, so that the distances they announced were then the corrected and true ones, not the apparent and false ones. But if he believes those authors gave no thought to refraction, then he must confess that they likewise erred in determining all the things that can't be perfectly adjusted without correcting for refraction — among which is the precise finding of the polar altitudes, which are commonly taken from the two meridian altitudes of some fixed star that is always visible. Those altitudes will be altered by refraction in exactly the same way as the new star's, so that the polar altitude deduced from them will come out defective, sharing the very same flaw the author ascribes to the altitudes assigned to the new star — that is, both the one and the others are set, with equal error, higher than the truth. But such an error, as far as our present business goes, does no harm at all; for since we need to know nothing beyond the difference between the two distances of the new star from the pole, when it was below and then above the pole, it's clear that those distances will be the same once the alteration of refraction is applied in common to both the star and the pole, being commonly corrected for the one and for the other. The author's argument would carry some weight, however feeble, if he had assured us that the height of the pole had been fixed precisely, corrected for the error due to refraction, while the same astronomers had then failed to guard against that error in fixing the new star's altitudes for us. But of this he has given us no assurance — nor perhaps could he — and perhaps (and this is the likelier case) the observers left out that very precaution.

Sagredo
This objection seems to me more than thoroughly demolished; but tell me now, in what fashion does he then free himself from the star's having always kept the same distance from the fixed stars near it?

Salviati
By clinging, in the same way, to two threads even weaker than the last. One of them is again tied to refraction, but this time even less firmly. He says that refraction, working on the new star and lifting it above its true place, makes the distances we see differ from the true ones when the star is compared with the fixed stars near it. And I can't marvel enough at how he pretends not to notice that the very same refraction will work the same way on the new star as on the old one beside it, lifting them both equally — so that from such an accident the interval between them stays unchanged. The other refuge is even more hapless, and has a good deal of the ridiculous about it. It rests on the error that can arise in the very handling of the instrument, when the observer — unable to set the center of the pupil of his eye at the center of the sextant (the instrument used for observing the intervals between two stars), but holding it raised above that center by the distance of the pupil from some cheekbone or other, against which the head of the instrument is propped — comes to form in his eye an angle sharper than the one made by the sides of the sextant; and this angle of the rays also differs from itself, depending on whether one looks at stars low over the horizon or the same stars set at a great height. Such an angle, he says, comes out different as one raises the instrument while keeping the head still; but if, in lifting the sextant, one bent the neck back and raised the head along with the instrument, the angle would then stay the same. The author's answer therefore supposes that the observers, in using the instrument, did not raise their heads as they needed to — which has no likelihood about it. But even granting it happened so, I'll leave it to you to judge what difference there can be between the two sharp angles of two isosceles triangles, the sides of one being four arm-lengths each, and those of the other four arm-lengths less the diameter of a lens. For the difference between the lengths of the two lines of sight can be no greater than that: when the line is drawn perpendicularly from the center of the pupil onto the plane of the sextant's bar (a line no longer than the thickness of a thumb), and the length of those same rays when, the sextant being raised without lifting the head with it, that line no longer falls perpendicular onto the plane but slants, making the angle toward the circumference somewhat sharp. But to free this author, once and for all, from these most miserable beggaries, let him know (since it's plain he hasn't much practice in the use of astronomical instruments) that on the sides of the sextant or quadrant two sights are fitted, one at the center and the other at the opposite end, raised a finger or more above the plane of the bar; and the ray from the eye is made to pass over the tops of these sights, the eye itself being held a hand's breadth or two, or even more, away from the instrument — so that neither pupil, nor cheekbone, nor any part of the person touches or leans against the instrument. Nor is the instrument even held up or raised in the arms — least of all if it's one of the big ones, as is customary, which, weighing tens and hundreds and even thousands of pounds, are supported on the firmest of bases. So the whole objection vanishes. These are this author's subterfuges, which, even were they made all of steel, couldn't lift him a hundredth of a minute; and with these he flatters himself that he can make us believe he has made up for that difference of more than a hundred minutes — I mean the fact that no notable variation was observed in the distances between a fixed star and the new star throughout their whole circuit, which, had the star been close to the Moon, would have had to grow greatly conspicuous even to the naked eye, without any instrument at all — especially when set against the eleventh star of Cassiopeia, its neighbor at a degree and a half, from which it would have had to vary by more than two diameters of the Moon, as the more intelligent astronomers of those days rightly noted.

Sagredo
I seem to be looking at that unlucky farmer who, after the hail has beaten down and destroyed his whole expected harvest, goes about with a drooping, downcast face, scraping together leavings so scanty they won't be enough to feed even a single chick for a single day.

Salviati
Truly this author has taken the field against the champions of the heavens' unchangeableness with too poor a stock of weapons, and has tried, with chains too fragile, to haul the new star of Cassiopeia down out of the highest regions into these low and elemental ones of ours. And since it seems to me we've shown quite clearly the great difference between the reasoning of those astronomers and that of their opponent here, it will be well to leave this part and return to our main matter. Next in it comes the consideration of the annual motion, commonly attributed to the Sun, but then — first by Aristarchus of Samos, and afterward by Copernicus — taken away from the Sun and transferred to the Earth. Against that position I hear Simplicio come stoutly armed, and in particular with the rapier and the shield of the little book of mathematical conclusions, or disquisitions — whose attacks it will be well to begin laying out.

Simplicio
If it's all the same to you, I'd like to hold those back for the end, since they're the latest to have been thought up.

Salviati
Then it will be necessary that you, following the method we've kept to so far, set out in orderly fashion the arguments to the contrary, both Aristotle's and those of other ancients — which I too intend to do, so that nothing is left behind without being carefully weighed and examined. And likewise Signor Sagredo, with the liveliness of his wit, will offer up his thoughts as they stir in him.

Sagredo
I'll do it with my usual freedom; and since you command it, you'll also be obliged to excuse it.

Salviati
The favor will oblige me to thank you, not to excuse you. But let Simplicio begin now to bring forward the difficulties that hold him back from being able to believe that the Earth, like the other planets, can move in a circle around a fixed center.

Simplicio
The first and greatest difficulty is the repugnance and incompatibility between being at the center and being far from it. For if the terrestrial globe is to move for a year along the circumference of a circle — that is, under the zodiac — it's impossible that it should at the same time be at the center of the zodiac; but that the Earth is at that center is proved in many ways by Aristotle, by Ptolemy, and by others.

Salviati
You reason very well; and there's no doubt that whoever wants to make the Earth move along the circumference of a circle must first prove that it isn't at the center of that circle. So it now follows that we should see whether the Earth is, or is not, at that center around which I say it turns and where you say it's placed. And before that, we must also make clear whether you and I have the same idea of this center or not. So tell me: what and where is this center you have in mind?

Simplicio
By center I mean the center of the universe, of the world, of the starry sphere, of the heavens.

Salviati
Though with very good reason I might set up a dispute with you over whether there is any such center in nature — since neither you nor anyone else has ever proved whether the world is finite and shaped, or else infinite and boundless — nevertheless, granting you for now that it's finite and bounded in a spherical figure, and that it therefore has its center, it will be worth seeing how believable it is that the Earth, rather than some other body, is to be found at that center.

Simplicio
That the world is finite, bounded, and spherical, Aristotle proves with a hundred demonstrations.

Salviati
Which then all reduce to a single one, and that single one to nothing; for if I deny him his premise — namely, that the universe is in motion — all his demonstrations collapse, since he proves nothing to be finite and bounded except the part of the universe that is in motion. But so as not to multiply disputes, let's grant for now that the world is finite, spherical, and has its center; and since that figure and center were argued from its mobility, it will be nothing but reasonable if, from those same circular motions of the world's bodies, we go on to the particular investigation of the proper site of that center. Indeed, Aristotle himself reasoned and settled the matter in just this way, making the center of the universe that very point around which all the celestial spheres turn, and in which he believed the terrestrial globe to be placed. Now tell me, Simplicio: if Aristotle found himself forced by the most obvious experiences to alter this arrangement and order of the universe in part, and to confess he had been mistaken in one of these two propositions — either in putting the Earth at the center, or in saying that the celestial spheres move around that center — which of the two confessions do you think he would choose?
