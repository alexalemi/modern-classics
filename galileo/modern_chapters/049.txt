The Fourth Day

(Part 1 of 6)

Sagredo
I can't tell whether your return to our usual discussions really was later than usual, or whether my eagerness to hear Salviati's thoughts on so intriguing a subject only made it seem so. For a good hour I've been posted at the window, watching moment by moment for the gondola I'd sent to fetch you to come into view.

Salviati
I really do think it was your imagination, more than any lateness on our part, that stretched out the time. So as not to stretch it any further, we'd do well to come straight to the point, without more preamble, and show how nature has allowed — whether the matter in rei veritate, in actual fact, stands this way, or whether she is only playing, amusing herself at the expense of our fancies — has allowed, I say, that the very motions long ago assigned to the Earth for every purpose other than accounting for the ebb and flow of the sea should now turn out to serve that cause so exactly, and how, in return, that same ebb and flow shows up to confirm the Earth's mobility. Up to now the evidence for that mobility has been drawn from what appears in the heavens, since among the things that happen here on Earth not one had the power to settle the question either way — as we've examined at length, showing that all the terrestrial happenings by which people commonly hold the Earth to be at rest and the Sun and the firmament in motion must look exactly the same to us whether we grant the Earth its motion and those bodies their rest, or the reverse. The single element of water — being enormously vast, and not fastened and chained to the terrestrial globe the way all its solid parts are, but rather, by its fluidity, left in part its own master and free — is the one thing, among all things below the Moon, in which we can recognize some trace and sign of what the Earth is doing as to motion or rest. I, after examining over and over to myself the effects and occurrences observed in the movements of the waters — some seen, some learned from others — and after reading and hearing the great foolishness that many have put forward as the causes of such things, have felt myself drawn, and not slightly, to admit these two conclusions (granting, of course, the necessary premises): that if the terrestrial globe is motionless, the ebb and flow of the sea cannot come about naturally; and that when the same globe is granted the motions already assigned to it, the sea must be subject to an ebb and flow that matches everything observed in it.

Sagredo
That's a tremendous claim — both in itself and for everything that follows from it. All the more attentively, then, will I listen to your explanation and your proof of it.

Salviati
In questions about nature — and the one we have in hand is one of them — it's our knowledge of the effects that leads us to investigate and discover the causes. Without that knowledge, ours would be a blind man's walk — worse than blind, in fact, since a blind man at least knows where he wants to end up, while we wouldn't even know that. So before anything else we need a knowledge of the effects whose causes we're after. And of those effects, you, Sagredo, must be more fully and more reliably informed than I am. Besides having been born in Venice, and lived there a long time — where the tides are quite remarkable for their size — you've also sailed to Syria, and, being a lively and curious mind, you must have made many observations. Whereas I, who have only been able to observe for a while — and briefly at that — what happens here at this far end of the Adriatic gulf, and in our sea to the south, along the shores of the Tyrrhenian, must for many things rely on other people's reports. And those reports, being mostly not in good agreement, and so quite uncertain, can bring our speculations confusion rather than confirmation. Still, from the ones we can trust — which are also the main ones — I think I can arrive at the true and primary causes. I don't claim to be able to supply all the proper and adequate reasons for effects that might be new to me, ones I therefore couldn't have thought about beforehand. What I'm about to say I offer only as a key that opens the door to a road nobody has ever walked before, in the firm hope that minds more speculative than mine will push on and penetrate much further than I will in this first discovery of mine. And even though, in other seas remote from us, things may happen that don't happen in our Mediterranean, that won't keep the reason and cause I'll give from being true — provided it checks out and fully satisfies the things that occur in our own sea. Because in the end there has to be a single true and primary cause for effects that are of one and the same kind. So I'll tell the story of the effects I know to be real, and I'll assign the cause I believe to be the true one; and you, gentlemen, will add others known to you, beyond mine, and then we'll test whether the cause I've brought forward can satisfy those as well.

So then: there are three periods to be observed in the ebb and flow of sea water. The first and chief one is the great, well-known daily period, by which, at intervals of some hours, the waters rise and fall — and these intervals in the Mediterranean run mostly about six hours each, that is, six hours rising and another six falling. The second period is monthly, and seems to take its origin from the motion of the Moon — not that she introduces any other motions, but only that she alters the size of the ones just mentioned, with a notable difference according to whether she is full, or waning, or at quadrature with the Sun. The third period is yearly, and appears to depend on the Sun, again only altering the daily motions, making them, at the times of the solstices, different in size from what they are at the equinoxes.

We'll speak first of the daily period, as the chief one, on which the Moon and the Sun seem to exert only a secondary action, with their monthly and yearly alterations. Three differences are to be observed in these hourly changes. In some places the waters rise and fall without any forward movement; in others, without rising or falling, they move now toward the east and then run back toward the west; and in still others they vary both in height and in course, as happens here in Venice, where the waters rise as they come in and fall as they go out. This they do at the far ends of those gulfs that stretch from west to east and end in beaches, over which the water, as it rises, has room to spread; whereas if its course were cut off by mountains or by very high banks, there it would rise and fall without forward movement. They run and run back, without changing height, in the middle stretches — as happens most notably in the Strait of Messina, between Scylla and Charybdis, where the currents, because of the narrowness of the channel, are extremely swift. But in the more open seas, and around the islands out in the middle — such as the Balearics, Corsica, Sardinia, Elba, the part of Sicily toward Africa, Malta, Candia, and so on — the changes in height are very small, though the currents are quite notable, and especially so where the sea narrows between the islands, or between them and the mainland.

Now these effects alone, true and certain as they are, seem to me — even if nothing else were seen — quite likely to persuade anyone willing to stay within natural limits to grant the mobility of the Earth. For to hold the basin of the Mediterranean fixed, and yet have the water it contains do what it does, is beyond my imagination — and perhaps beyond anyone else's who looks past the surface and digs into the question.

Simplicio
These occurrences, Salviati, aren't new. They're extremely ancient, observed by countless people, and many have racked their brains to give a reason for them — one this reason, another that. Not many miles from here there's a great Peripatetic who brings forward a cause freshly fished out of a certain passage in Aristotle that his interpreters hadn't properly noticed. From that passage he gathers that the true cause of these movements comes from nothing but the differing depths of the seas: the waters of the greater depths, being larger in quantity and therefore heavier, drive out the waters of the shallower places, which, once raised up, then want to descend; and from this continual struggle comes the ebb and flow. Then there are the many who refer it to the Moon, saying she has a special dominion over water. And most recently a certain prelate has published a little treatise in which he says the Moon, wandering across the sky, draws and lifts toward herself a heap of water that keeps following after her, so that the sea is always high in whatever part lies beneath the Moon. And since, even when she's below the horizon, the rising still returns, he says the only way to save the effect is to hold that the Moon not only naturally keeps this faculty in herself, but in this case has the power to confer it on the point of the zodiac opposite her. Others — as I think you know — say that the Moon, by her mild warmth, has the power to rarefy the water, which, once rarefied, comes to rise up. Nor have we been without someone who —

Sagredo
Please, Simplicio, don't report any more of them. It doesn't seem worth spending the time to recite them, much less the words to refute them. And if you were to lend your assent to any of these trifles, or others like them, you'd be doing an injustice to your own judgment — which we know to be very well refined.

Salviati
Being a bit more phlegmatic than you, Sagredo, I'll spend fifty words for Simplicio's sake, in case he thinks there might be some probability in the things he's recounted. So here's what I say. Waters, Simplicio, whose outer surface stands higher do drive out the ones below them, the lower ones; but the waters that are greater in depth do not do this. And the higher waters, once they've driven out the lower, quickly settle and come level. This Peripatetic of yours would have to believe that all the lakes in the world that lie still, and all the seas where the ebb and flow is imperceptible, have perfectly level beds. And I was so simple-minded that I'd persuaded myself that, even if there were no other sounding, the islands rising above the waters were a pretty plain indication that the bottoms are uneven. To that prelate you might say that the Moon runs over the whole Mediterranean every day, and yet the waters don't rise except at its eastern extremities and here with us in Venice. To the people with the mild warmth strong enough to make water swell, tell them to put a fire under a cauldron full of water, keep their right hand in it until the heat raises the water by a single finger's width, and then pull it out and write about the swelling of the sea. Or at least ask them to teach you how the Moon manages to rarefy a certain part of the waters and not the rest — say, these here at Venice and not those at Ancona, Naples, or Genoa. One has to conclude that poetic minds come in two kinds: some deft and apt at inventing fables, others disposed and fitted to believing them.

Simplicio
I don't think anyone believes fables while he recognizes them as such. As for the many opinions about the causes of the ebb and flow, since I know that an effect has only a single primary and true cause, I understand perfectly well, and am certain, that at most one of them could be true, while all the rest, I know, are fabulous and false. And perhaps the true one isn't even among those brought forward so far — indeed, that's what I really believe, for it would be a strange thing if the truth could give off so little light that nothing of it showed among the darkness of so many falsehoods. But I'll say, with the freedom allowed among us, that bringing in the motion of the Earth and making it the cause of the ebb and flow seems to me, so far, a notion no less fabulous than any of the others I've heard. And if I were offered no reasons more in keeping with natural things, I'd pass without any resistance to believing this is a supernatural effect, and therefore miraculous and unfathomable to human minds — like the countless others there are that depend immediately on the omnipotent hand of God.

Salviati
You reason very prudently, and in keeping too with the doctrine of Aristotle — who, as you know, at the start of his Mechanical Questions attributes to miracle the things whose causes are hidden. But that the true cause of the ebb and flow is among the impenetrable ones — I don't think you have any greater sign of that than seeing how, among all the causes so far brought forward as the real ones, there isn't a single one by which, with whatever contrivance you employ, we can produce a like effect ourselves. For neither with the light of Moon or Sun, nor with mild heat, nor with differing depths, will anyone ever make the water contained in a motionless vessel run and run back, rise and fall, in one place and not another, by any artificial means. But if, by making the vessel move — with no contrivance at all, in the very simplest way — I can reproduce for you, point by point, all the changes observed in sea water, why would you refuse this cause and take refuge in a miracle?

Simplicio
I'll take refuge in a miracle if you don't dislodge me from it with some natural cause other than the motion of the vessels of the sea waters — because I know those vessels don't move, seeing that the whole entire terrestrial globe is naturally motionless.

Salviati
But don't you believe the terrestrial globe could be made movable supernaturally — that is, by the absolute power of God?

Simplicio
And who doubts it?

Salviati
Then, Simplicio — since to produce the ebb and flow of the sea we need to bring in a miracle — let's miraculously set the Earth in motion, and let the sea then move naturally at its motion. And this operation will be all the simpler, and I'd even say the more natural, among the miraculous ones, in that setting a globe spinning in a circle — when we see so many others moving that way — is less difficult than making an immense mass of water go forward and back, faster here and slower there, rise and fall here more and there less and elsewhere not at all, and produce all these variations in the very same vessel that contains it. Besides, these are many different miracles, and that is only one. And add to this that the miracle of making the water move drags another after it as a consequence: keeping the Earth steady against the water's impulses, which would be strong enough to make it sway now toward one side and now toward the other, unless it were held fast miraculously.

Sagredo
Please, Simplicio, let's suspend our judgment for a little while before pronouncing the new opinion Salviati means to explain to us worthless, and let's not so quickly throw it into the same bag with the old, ridiculous ones. As for the miracle, let's turn to it likewise after we've heard the reasoning that stays within natural limits — though, to speak my own mind, all the works of nature and of God look miraculous to me.

Salviati
And I judge the same. Saying that the natural cause of the ebb and flow is the movement of the Earth doesn't keep that movement from being a miraculous operation. Now, taking up our reasoning again, I repeat and affirm: it remains unknown, so far, how the waters contained within our Mediterranean gulf can make those movements we see them make, so long as that same gulf and containing vessel stays motionless. And what creates the difficulty, and makes this matter so hard to untangle, are the things I'll mention next, which are observed daily. So take note.

Here we are in Venice, where the waters are now low, the sea calm and the air still. The water begins to rise, and within five or six hours it swells ten palms and more. This rise isn't produced by the first water having rarefied; it's produced by new water that has come in — water of the same sort as the first, of the same saltiness, the same density, the same weight. Ships, Simplicio, float in it just as in the first, without sinking so much as a hair deeper; a barrel of this second water weighs not a single grain more or less than the same quantity of the other; it keeps the same coldness, not altered in the least. It is, in short, water newly and visibly come in through the cuts and mouths of the Lido. Now find me how and from where it got here. Are there perhaps chasms or channels round about, in the sea floor, through which the Earth draws in and pours back the water, breathing like some vast and boundless whale? But if that's so, how is it that in the space of six hours the water doesn't rise the same way at Ancona, at Ragusa, at Corfu, where the swelling is very small and perhaps unnoticeable? Who will find a way to pour new water into a motionless vessel, and have it rise only in one particular part of it and nowhere else? Perhaps you'll say this new water is lent to it by the Ocean, handed in through the Strait of Gibraltar? That won't remove the difficulties already mentioned; it'll bring greater ones. First, tell me what the course of that water must be, if, entering through the strait, it travels in six hours all the way to the farthest shores of the Mediterranean, a distance of two or three thousand miles, and then repasses that same distance in as much time on its way back. What would become of the ships scattered across the sea? What of those in the strait, caught in a continual headlong rush of an immense flood of waters that, entering through a channel no more than eight miles wide, has to give passage to enough water that in six hours it floods a space hundreds of miles across and thousands long? What tiger, what falcon, ever ran or flew with such speed — a speed, I mean, that would cover four hundred miles and more in an hour? There are currents, granted, along the length of the Gulf, but so slow that oared vessels outdo them, though not without loss to their own progress. Furthermore, if this water comes in through the strait, there still remains the other difficulty: how it's carried to rise so high here, in parts so remote, without first rising to a like or greater height in the parts nearer by. In short, I don't believe that either stubbornness or subtlety of wit could ever find a remedy for these difficulties, nor, in consequence, uphold against them the stability of the Earth, so long as one stays within natural limits.

Sagredo
Of this I'm now perfectly convinced, and I'm eagerly waiting to hear in what way these marvels can follow, without a hitch, from the motions already assigned to the Earth.
