The First Day

(Part 4 of 12)

Sagredo
This argument of Aristotle's had struck me as deficient and inconclusive in another respect as well, even if we grant him that the circumference toward which fire moves in a straight line is the one that encloses the world. For take, inside a circle, not just the center but any other point you please: every moving body that sets out from it along a straight line, in any direction whatever, will without any doubt be heading toward the circumference, and if it keeps moving it will get there too — so it's perfectly true to say that it moves toward the circumference. But it will not be true that a body moving along those same lines with the contrary motion is heading toward the center — not unless the point we chose was the center itself, or the motion ran along that one single line which, drawn from the chosen point, passes through the center. So the argument "Fire, moving in a straight line, goes toward the circumference of the world; therefore the parts of earth, which move along the same lines with the contrary motion, go toward the center of the world" concludes nothing at all — unless we first assume that fire's lines, extended, pass through the center of the world. And since we know for certain that those lines pass through the center of the terrestrial globe (being perpendicular to its surface, not slanted), then to make the conclusion work we must assume that the center of the Earth is the same as the center of the world — or at least that the parts of fire and earth rise and fall only along a single line that passes through the center of the world. And that is false, and it contradicts experience, which shows us that the parts of fire ascend not along one single line but along the infinitely many lines drawn from the center of the Earth toward every part of the world — always rising perpendicular to the surface of the terrestrial globe.

Salviati
Sagredo, you very ingeniously drive Aristotle into the same difficulty by exposing the obvious equivocation; but add one more awkwardness to it. We see that the Earth is spherical, so we're certain it has a center. We see that all its parts move toward that center — we're forced to say so, since their motions are all perpendicular to the Earth's surface. We understand that in moving toward the center of the Earth, they move toward their whole, toward their universal mother. And then we're so obliging that we let ourselves be talked into believing their natural instinct is not to head for the center of the Earth but for the center of the universe — which we don't know where it is, or whether it exists at all; and which, even if it does exist, is nothing but an imaginary point, a nothing, with no power to do anything.

As for Simplicio's last remark — that debating whether parts of the Sun or the Moon or some other celestial body, if separated from their whole, would naturally return to it is a waste of breath, because the case is impossible, it being evident from Aristotle's demonstrations that celestial bodies are impervious to change, impenetrable, indivisible, and so on — I answer this: not one of the conditions by which Aristotle makes celestial bodies differ from elemental ones has any footing except what he deduces from the difference between their natural motions. So that once we deny that circular motion belongs to celestial bodies alone, and affirm that it suits all naturally movable bodies, one of two conclusions necessarily follows: either the attributes of generable or ingenerable, alterable or inalterable, divisible or indivisible, and so on, belong equally and in common to all the bodies of the universe — celestial and elemental alike — or else Aristotle went badly and mistakenly wrong in deducing from circular motion the attributes he assigned to celestial bodies.

Simplicio
This way of philosophizing tends toward the subversion of all natural philosophy — toward throwing heaven and Earth and the whole universe into disorder and wreckage. But I believe the foundations of the Peripatetics are solid enough that there's no fear of new sciences being built on their ruins.

Salviati
Don't worry yourself about heaven or the Earth, and don't fear their subversion — nor philosophy's either. As for heaven, it's pointless for you to fear for the very thing you yourself hold to be inalterable and impervious to change. As for the Earth, we're trying to ennoble and perfect it: we're working to make it like the celestial bodies and, in a certain way, to set it almost in heaven — the place your philosophers banished it from. And philosophy itself can only profit from our disputes: if our thoughts turn out to be true, new ground will have been gained; if false, then in refuting them the original doctrines will stand all the more firmly confirmed. Worry instead about certain philosophers, and see how you might help them and prop them up — because the science itself cannot help but advance. And now, back to our subject: bring forward freely whatever occurs to you in support of the total difference Aristotle sets up between celestial bodies and the elemental region, making the former ingenerable, incorruptible, inalterable, and so on, and the latter corruptible, alterable, and the rest.

Simplicio
I don't yet see that Aristotle needs any rescuing. He is still on his feet, firm and strong — indeed, he hasn't yet even been attacked by you, let alone knocked down. And what will your shield be against this first assault? Aristotle writes: What is generated comes to be from a contrary, in some subject; and likewise it is corrupted, in some subject, from a contrary into a contrary — so that (mark this well) corruption and generation occur only among contraries. But the motions of contraries are contrary to each other; so if no contrary can be assigned to the celestial body — since no other motion is contrary to circular motion — then nature did very well indeed to exempt from contraries the thing that was meant to be ingenerable and incorruptible. With this first foundation laid, it follows immediately that the celestial body cannot grow, cannot alter, is impervious to change, and finally is eternal — a dwelling fit for the immortal gods, in agreement with the opinion of every person who has any conception of the gods at all. He then confirms the same thing by the senses: in all past time, according to tradition and record, nothing is seen to have changed, either in the whole of the outermost heaven or in any one of its proper parts. And that no other motion is contrary to circular motion, Aristotle proves in many ways; but without rehearsing them all, it stands demonstrated plainly enough by this: the simple motions are only three — toward the center, away from the center, and around the center; of these, the two straight ones, sursum et deorsum — upward and downward — are manifestly contrary; and since one thing has only one contrary, there remains no other motion that could be contrary to the circular. There you have Aristotle's argument, as sharp and as conclusive as could be, by which the incorruptibility of heaven is proved.

Salviati
That is nothing more than the bare course of Aristotle's argument, which I sketched for you already — and the moment I deny that the motion you attribute to celestial bodies is off-limits to the Earth, his inference comes to nothing. So I tell you plainly: that circular motion you assign to celestial bodies suits the Earth as well. From which — granting that the rest of your argument is conclusive — one of three things will follow, as I said a little while ago and now repeat: either the Earth is itself ingenerable and incorruptible, just like the celestial bodies; or the celestial bodies are, like the elemental ones, generable, alterable, and so on; or else this difference of motions has nothing to do with generation and corruption.

Aristotle's argument — and yours — contains many propositions that shouldn't be granted lightly, and to examine it better it will be worth reducing it to as clean and distinct a form as possible. Sagredo will forgive me if he finds it tedious to hear the same things said over more than once — let him imagine he's listening to the arguments being rehearsed again in a public disputation. You say: "Generation and corruption occur only where there are contraries; contraries exist only among simple natural bodies, movable with contrary motions; contrary motions are only those made along straight lines between contrary endpoints; and of these there are only two, namely away from the center and toward the center; and such motions belong to no natural bodies except earth, fire, and the other two elements. Therefore generation and corruption exist only among the elements. And since the third simple motion — the circular motion around the center — has no contrary (for the other two are each other's contraries, and one thing has only one contrary), the natural body to which that motion belongs lacks a contrary; and having no contrary, it remains ingenerable and incorruptible, and so on, because where there is no contrariety there is no generation or corruption. But that motion belongs only to celestial bodies; therefore they alone are ingenerable, incorruptible, and so forth."

Now, first of all: it strikes me as a far easier thing to make sure whether the Earth — a vast body, and, being so near us, by far the easiest to handle — moves with an enormous motion, such as turning on itself once every twenty-four hours, than it is to understand and make sure whether generation and corruption arise from contraries — indeed, whether corruption and generation and contraries exist in nature at all. And if you, Simplicio, could tell me nature's method of operation when she generates, in the briefest time, a hundred thousand gnats out of a little vapor rising off fermenting grape must — showing me what the contraries are in that case, what thing is corrupted, and how — I would think even more highly of you than I already do, because I understand none of it. Furthermore, I would dearly love to know how and why these corrupting contraries are so gentle with crows and so fierce with doves, so tolerant of stags and so impatient with horses, granting the former more years of life — which is to say, of incorruptibility — than they grant the latter weeks. Peach trees and olive trees put their roots into the same soils; they stand exposed to the same cold spells, the same heat, the same rains and winds — in short, to the same contrarieties — and yet the one is destroyed in a short time while the other lives many hundreds of years. What's more, I have never managed to feel at ease with this substantial transmutation (keeping strictly within purely natural terms) by which a piece of matter is supposed to be so transformed that we must say it has been utterly destroyed, with nothing of its first being remaining in it, and that another body, completely different from it, has been produced out of it. When a body presents itself to me under one aspect and, a little later, under another very different one, I don't consider it impossible that this comes about by a simple rearrangement of parts, without anything being corrupted or anything new generated — we see metamorphoses of that kind every day of our lives. So I say it once more: if you mean to persuade me that the Earth cannot move in a circle by way of an argument from corruptibility and generability, you will have far more work on your hands than I will — for I'll prove you the contrary with arguments a good deal harder, but no less conclusive.

Sagredo
Salviati, forgive me for interrupting your reasoning. It delights me greatly — I find myself tangled in these same difficulties — but I suspect there's no getting to the bottom of it without abandoning our principal subject altogether. So if the original discussion can be carried forward, I'd judge it best to set this question of generation and corruption aside for a separate, complete conversation of its own — and the same, if it suits you and Simplicio, for the other particular questions the course of our talks brings up. I'll keep a separate note of them, so we can raise them another day and examine them in detail. As for the present one: since you say that once Aristotle is denied the claim that circular motion belongs to the celestial bodies but not to the Earth, it follows that whatever holds of the Earth — being generable, alterable, and so on — holds of the heavens too, let's leave aside whether generation and corruption exist in nature or not, and get back to investigating what the terrestrial globe actually does.

Simplicio
I cannot bring my ears to listen to anyone doubting whether generation and corruption exist in nature — a thing we have continually before our eyes, and about which Aristotle wrote two entire books. Once you start denying the principles of the sciences and casting doubt on the most manifest things, who doesn't know that you can prove whatever you please and defend any paradox whatsoever? If you don't see grasses, plants, and animals generated and corrupted every day, then what do you see? How can you fail to see the contrarieties perpetually jousting against one another — earth changing into water, water converting into air, air into fire, and air again condensing into clouds, rains, hail, and storms?

Sagredo
Of course we see all these things; and so, as far as this part goes — generation and corruption produced by contraries — we're happy to grant you Aristotle's argument. But if, using those very propositions you've granted to Aristotle, I prove to you that the celestial bodies are themselves every bit as generable and corruptible as the elemental ones, what will you say then?

Simplicio
I'll say you've done the impossible.

Sagredo
Tell me something, Simplicio: aren't these properties contrary to one another?

Simplicio
Which ones?

Sagredo
These: alterable and inalterable, subject to change and impervious to change, generable and ingenerable, corruptible and incorruptible?

Simplicio
As contrary as can be.

Sagredo
Well then — if that's so, and if it's also true that celestial bodies are ingenerable and incorruptible, I'll prove to you that of necessity the celestial bodies must be generable and corruptible.

Simplicio
That can only be a sophism.

Sagredo
Hear the argument first; then give it its name and refute it. The celestial bodies, because they are ingenerable and incorruptible, have contraries in nature — namely, the generable and corruptible bodies. But where there is contrariety, there is generation and corruption. Therefore the celestial bodies are generable and corruptible.

Simplicio
Didn't I tell you it could be nothing but a sophism? This is one of those horned arguments they call sorites — like the one about the Cretan who said that all Cretans were liars. Being a Cretan, he was telling a lie when he said Cretans were liars; therefore Cretans must be truthful, and consequently he, being a Cretan, was truthful; and so, in saying Cretans were liars, he spoke the truth; and since he was included among the Cretans, he must have been a liar after all. With sophisms of this sort you could keep spinning in circles for all eternity without ever concluding anything.

Sagredo
So far you've only given it its name. It remains for you to solve it — to show where the fallacy lies.

Simplicio
As for solving it and exposing its fallacy — don't you see, first of all, the open contradiction? The celestial bodies are ingenerable and incorruptible; therefore the celestial bodies are generable and corruptible? And besides, the contrariety isn't among the celestial bodies at all; it's among the elements, which have the contrariety of the motions sursum et deorsum and of lightness and heaviness. But the heavens, which move circularly — a motion to which no other is contrary — lack all contrariety, and are therefore incorruptible, and so on.

Sagredo
Gently, Simplicio. This contrariety, by which you say certain simple bodies are corruptible — does it reside in the very body that gets corrupted, or does it stand in relation to some other body? I mean: the moisture, say, by which a portion of earth is corrupted — does it reside in that earth itself, or in another body, such as the air or the water? I believe you'll say that just as the motions up and down, and heaviness and lightness — which you make the primary contraries — cannot exist in one and the same subject, so neither can wet and dry, or hot and cold. So you're forced to say that when a body is corrupted, this happens through a quality residing in another body, contrary to its own. Therefore, to make the celestial body corruptible, it's enough that there exist in nature bodies that have a contrariety to the celestial body — and the elements are exactly such bodies, if it's true that corruptibility is contrary to incorruptibility.

Simplicio
That is not enough, my dear sir. The elements alter and corrupt because they touch and mingle with one another, and so can bring their contrarieties to bear; but the celestial bodies are separate from the elements — the elements don't even touch them, though they touch the elements. If you want to prove generation and corruption in the celestial bodies, you must show that the contrarieties reside among those bodies themselves.

Sagredo
Then watch me find them there. The first source from which you draw the contrarieties of the elements is the contrariety of their motions up and down; therefore the principles those motions depend on must be contraries as well. And since one body moves upward by lightness and the other downward by heaviness, lightness and heaviness must be contrary to each other; and no less must we believe that those further principles are contraries which cause one body to be heavy and another light. But by your own account, lightness and heaviness follow from rarity and density; therefore density and rarity are contraries. And these conditions are found so abundantly in the celestial bodies that you yourselves hold the stars to be nothing but denser parts of their own heaven; and if that's so, the density of the stars must exceed the density of the rest of heaven by an almost infinite margin — which is obvious from heaven's being supremely transparent and the stars supremely opaque, and from the fact that up there no other qualities are to be found, beyond the more and less dense or rare, that could serve as principles of greater and lesser transparency. Since such contrarieties exist among the celestial bodies, then, it necessarily follows either that they too are generable and corruptible in the very same way the elemental bodies are — or else that contrariety is not the cause of corruptibility, and so on.

Simplicio
Neither one is necessary — because density and rarity in the celestial bodies are not contraries to each other the way they are in elemental bodies. They don't depend on the primary qualities, hot and cold, which are contraries, but on there being much or little matter in proportion to the bulk. Now "much" and "little" express only a relative opposition — the weakest kind of opposition there is — and that has nothing to do with generation and corruption.

Sagredo
So then: for density and rarity to do their job among the elements — to be the cause of heaviness and lightness, which in turn can be causes of the contrary motions sursum et deorsum, on which in turn the contrarieties for generation and corruption depend — it isn't enough for them to be the kind of dense and rare that contain much or little matter under the same bulk; they must be dense and rare thanks to the primary qualities, cold and hot. Otherwise nothing gets accomplished. But if that's so, Aristotle has cheated us: he should have said so from the start, and left it in writing, that the generable and corruptible bodies are those simple bodies which are movable with the simple motions up and down, depending on lightness and heaviness, caused by rarity and density, made by much or little matter, by virtue of heat and cold — and not have stopped at simple upward and downward motion. Because I assure you that as far as making bodies heavy or light — so that they can then move with contrary motions — any density and rarity whatever will do the job, whether it comes from hot and cold or from anything else you please; hot and cold have nothing to do with the operation. You can see for yourself that a piece of iron glowing red — which surely may be called hot — weighs just the same, and moves in just the same way, as when it's cold. But leaving even that aside: how do you know that celestial density and rarity don't depend on cold and heat?
