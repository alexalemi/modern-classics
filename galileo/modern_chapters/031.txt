The Second Day

(Part 19 of 20)

Simplicio
I understand perfectly, and I couldn't produce a single thing to excuse so great a blunder.

Salviati
But gently, Simplicio — there's another mistake bigger than this one. He has the Earth turn with its daily rotation about its own center from east to west, and he doesn't grasp that if that were so, the twenty-four-hour motion of the universe would appear to us to run from west to east — exactly the opposite of what we actually see.

Simplicio
Oh, I — who've barely glimpsed the first elements of the sphere — I'm certain I wouldn't have gone so badly wrong.

Salviati
Judge for yourself, then, how much study you can credit this critic with having put into Copernicus's books, if he takes backwards the one principal, supreme hypothesis on which rests the whole sum of the matters where Copernicus parts company with Aristotle and Ptolemy. As for this third motion that the author — again claiming to speak Copernicus's own mind — assigns to the terrestrial globe, I don't know which one he thinks he means. It's certainly not the one Copernicus gives it alongside the other two, the annual motion and the daily rotation, and which has nothing to do with any tilting toward north or south, but serves only to keep the axis of the daily revolution perpetually parallel to itself. So we have to say either that the critic didn't understand this, or that he pretended not to. And though this one grave failing would be enough to release us from any duty to keep occupying ourselves with his objections, still I want to hold them in esteem — for they truly deserve to be valued far more than a thousand others from other empty critics. Coming back, then, to his challenge: I say that the two motions, the annual and the daily, are not in the least contrary; on the contrary, they run the same way, and so can spring from one and the same principle. And the third follows so naturally from the annual, of itself and spontaneously, that there's no need to summon any principle, inner or outer (as I'll show in its proper place), to produce it as a cause.

Sagredo
I'd like to add a word of my own to this critic, guided by plain common sense. He wants to condemn Copernicus if I can't resolve, point by point, every doubt and answer every objection he raises — as though the falsity of Copernicus's doctrine followed necessarily from my ignorance. But if this way of condemning writers strikes him as sound legal procedure, then he shouldn't find it unreasonable that I withhold my approval of Aristotle and Ptolemy, so long as he can't resolve, any better than I can, the very same difficulties I raise against their doctrine. He asks me what principles move the terrestrial globe through its annual motion in the zodiac, and its daily rotation on itself along the equinoctial. I tell him they're much like the principles by which Saturn moves through the zodiac in thirty years, and turns on itself in a far shorter time along its own equinoctial — as the appearing and vanishing of its flanking globes shows us. And much like the principle by which he himself would concede, without a qualm, that the Sun runs along the ecliptic in a year and turns on its own axis, parallel to the equinoctial, in less than a month — as its spots plainly show. And much like the principle by which the Medicean Stars — Jupiter's four little moons — run through the zodiac in twelve years, and meanwhile wheel in tiny circles and very short times around Jupiter itself.

Simplicio
This author will deny you all these things — as illusions of eyesight, produced by the crystals of the telescope.

Sagredo
Oh, that would be claiming far too much for himself. He insists the naked eye can't be fooled when it judges the straight downward motion of falling weights, and yet he wants that same eye fooled when it takes in these other motions — even with its power perfected and multiplied thirtyfold. So let's tell him instead that the Earth shares in a plurality of motions in a way much like — perhaps exactly like — the way the lodestone moves downward as a heavy body and also has two circular motions, one horizontal and the other vertical, beneath the meridian. But what more? Tell me, Simplicio: which do you think this author would judge more different from each other — straight motion and circular, or motion and rest?

Simplicio
Motion and rest, surely. And that's obvious, because for Aristotle circular motion is not contrary to straight motion — he even grants that the two can be mixed — whereas that's impossible for motion and rest.

Sagredo
Then it's the less improbable proposition to lodge in one natural body two inner principles, one for straight and the other for circular motion, than to lodge two — also inner — one for motion and the other for rest. Now, as for the natural inclination that resides in the parts of the Earth, to return to their whole when they're forcibly torn away from it, both positions agree; they part company only over what the whole does. The one wants the whole to stand still by an inner principle; the other credits it with circular motion. But by your own admission and this philosopher's, two principles — one for motion, the other for rest — are mutually incompatible, since their effects are incompatible. Whereas that doesn't happen with the two motions, straight and circular, which have no quarrel between them at all.

Salviati
Add to this that, very probably, the motion made by a piece of the Earth as it works its way back to its whole may itself be circular too, as we've already stated. So that on every count, as far as the present case goes, mobility seems more acceptable than rest. Now go on, Simplicio, with whatever's left.

Simplicio
The author reinforces his challenge by pointing out another absurdity — namely, that the very same motions belong to natures utterly different from one another. But observation teaches us that the operations and motions of different natures are themselves different; and reason confirms it, for otherwise we'd have no way in to knowing and distinguishing natures, if they didn't have their own motions and operations to guide us to a knowledge of the substances.

Sagredo
Two or three times now I've noticed, in this author's arguments, that to prove a thing stands in such-and-such a way, he falls back on saying that in that way it fits our intelligence, or that otherwise we'd have no access to the knowledge of this or that particular, or that the whole standard of philosophy would be ruined — as if nature first made the brains in men, and only afterward arranged things to suit the capacity of their intellects. But I'd sooner reckon that nature first made things her own way, and only afterward fashioned human reasoning capable of grasping (though with great effort) some little part of her secrets.

Salviati
I'm of the same opinion. But tell me, Simplicio: which are these different natures to which Copernicus, against observation and reason, assigns the same motions and operations?

Simplicio
Here they are: water and air (which are, after all, natures different from earth), and everything found in those elements, will each have those three motions that Copernicus invents for the terrestrial globe. And he goes on to demonstrate geometrically how, on Copernicus's scheme, a cloud that hangs in the air and stays a long while over our heads without changing place must necessarily have all three of the motions the terrestrial globe has. Here's the demonstration — you can read it for yourself, for I couldn't repeat it from memory.

Salviati
I won't trouble to read it. In fact I think it was pointless to include it, because I'm sure not one of those who hold the Earth to move will deny it to him. So, granting him the demonstration, let's talk about the challenge itself — which doesn't seem to me to have much force to conclude anything against Copernicus's position, since it takes nothing away from those motions and operations by which we come to know natures, and so on. Answer me, if you'd be so kind, Simplicio: those properties in which certain things agree most exactly — can they serve to make us recognize the different natures of those things?

Simplicio
No, sir — quite the opposite, because from an identity of operations and properties you can argue nothing except an identity of natures.

Salviati
So the different natures of water, of earth, of air, and of the other things scattered through these elements — you don't infer them from the operations in which all these elements and their contents agree, but from other operations. Is that right?

Simplicio
That's how it stands, in fact.

Salviati
So that anyone who left the elements all those motions, operations, and other properties by which their natures are distinguished would not deprive us of the power to come to know them — even if he then removed the one operation in which they all agree together, and which therefore does nothing to distinguish such natures.

Simplicio
I believe the argument runs very well.

Salviati
But that earth, water, and air are by nature equally constituted to be motionless around the center — isn't that your opinion, and the author's, and Aristotle's, and Ptolemy's, and all their followers'?

Simplicio
It's accepted as an irrefutable truth.

Salviati
Then from this common natural condition — resting around the center — you can draw no argument about the different natures of these elements and elemental things; such knowledge has to be gathered from other, non-shared qualities. And so anyone who stripped from the elements only this shared rest, and left them all their other operations, would in no way block the road that leads us to knowledge of their essences. But Copernicus strips from them nothing except this shared rest, and transmutes it into a most thoroughly shared motion — leaving them their heaviness, their lightness, their motions up and down, slower and faster, their rarity, their density, the qualities of hot, cold, dry, wet, and in short everything else. So the absurdity this author imagines simply isn't there in the Copernican position; nor does agreeing in an identity of motion count for any more or less than agreeing in an identity of rest, as far as diversifying natures — or not — is concerned. Now tell me if there's any other argument on the other side.

Simplicio
A fourth challenge follows, again drawn from natural observation: that bodies of the same kind have motions that agree in kind, or else agree in rest. But on Copernicus's position, bodies that agree in kind, and are extremely alike, would as to their motion be in supreme disagreement — indeed in flat, diametrical opposition. For stars so alike among themselves would nonetheless be so unlike in motion, since six planets would revolve forever, while the Sun and all the fixed stars would forever stay still.

Salviati
The form of the argument seems conclusive to me; but I strongly suspect the application, or the subject matter, is defective — and provided the author insists on his assumption, the conclusion will come out flatly against him. The argument runs like this. Among the bodies of the world there are six that move perpetually, and these are the six planets. Of the rest — that is, the Earth, the Sun, and the fixed stars — it's in doubt which of them moves and which stands still; for necessarily, if the Earth stands still, the Sun and the fixed stars move, and it could equally be that the Sun and the fixed stars stand motionless while the Earth moves. So the question, with the fact in doubt, is to whom motion can more fittingly be assigned, and to whom rest. Natural reasoning dictates that the motion should be reckoned to belong to whatever agrees most, in kind and in essence, with the bodies that indisputably move, and rest to whatever differs from them most. And since eternal rest and perpetual motion are properties as unlike as can be, it's plain that the nature of the ever-moving body must be utterly unlike the nature of the ever-still one. Let's search, then — while we're still undecided between motion and rest — to see whether, by way of some other telling condition, we might discover which agrees more with the surely moving bodies: the Earth, or the Sun and fixed stars. And behold, nature, kind to our need and desire, furnishes us two striking conditions, no less different than motion and rest: light and darkness — that is, being by nature most brilliant, and being dark and stripped of all light. Utterly unlike in essence, then, are the bodies adorned with an inner and eternal splendor and the bodies deprived of all light. The Earth is deprived of light; the Sun is most brilliant of itself, and no less the fixed stars; the six moving planets wholly lack light, like the Earth. Therefore their essence agrees with the Earth and differs from the Sun and fixed stars. So the Earth is a moving body; the Sun and the starry sphere are motionless.

Simplicio
But the author won't concede that the six planets are dark, and he'll dig in on that denial — or else he'll argue the great likeness of nature between the six planets and the Sun and fixed stars, and their unlikeness to the Earth, from conditions other than darkness and light. In fact, now that it strikes me, in the fifth challenge that comes next he sets out the supreme disparity between the Earth and the heavenly bodies. There he writes that great confusion and disorder would be introduced into the system of the universe and among its parts on Copernicus's hypothesis. For among heavenly bodies immutable and incorruptible — according to Aristotle and Tycho and others — among bodies, I say, of such nobility (as everyone confesses, and Copernicus himself, who declares them ordered and arranged in the finest constitution and clears them of every inconstancy of power) — among bodies, I say, so pure, that is, between Venus and Mars, to plant the sink of all corruptible matter: the Earth, the water, the air, and all the mixed bodies! Whereas how much finer a distribution, and more befitting nature — indeed befitting God himself as architect — to sequester the pure from the impure, the mortal from the immortal, as the other schools teach; schools that show us how these impure and perishable materials are confined within the narrow concave of the lunar orb, above which the heavenly things then rise in an unbroken series!

Salviati
It's true that the Copernican system throws Aristotle's universe into turmoil; but we're dealing with our universe, the true and real one. Now, when this author wants to infer the disparity of essence between the Earth and the heavenly bodies from the incorruptibility of the latter and the corruptibility of the former — in Aristotle's manner — and from that disparity to conclude that motion must belong to the Sun and fixed stars and immobility to the Earth, he goes wandering into the paralogism of assuming the very thing in question. For Aristotle infers the incorruptibility of the heavenly bodies from motion — and it's precisely that motion whose ownership is in dispute, whether it's theirs or the Earth's. As for the emptiness of these rhetorical inferences, enough has been said. And what could be more foolish than to say the Earth and the elements are banished and cut off from the heavenly spheres, penned up inside the lunar orb? But isn't the lunar orb one of the heavenly spheres — and, by their own agreement, set in the very middle of all the rest? A fine new way to separate the pure from the impure and the plague-stricken from the healthy: to lodge the infected in the heart of the city! I'd have thought the pest-house ought to be pushed as far off as possible. Copernicus admires the arrangement of the parts of the universe precisely because God set the great lamp, which was to pour its supreme splendor over all his temple, at the center of it, and not off to one side. As for the terrestrial globe standing between Venus and Mars, we'll deal with that shortly — and you yourself, for this author's sake, will try your hand at moving it out from there. But please, let's not weave these rhetorical posies together with the solidity of demonstrations; let's leave them to the orators, or rather to the poets, who've known how, with their charms, to lift up with praise the vilest things — and sometimes even harmful ones. And if anything else is left, let's dispatch it as quickly as we can.

Simplicio
There's the sixth and last argument, in which he sets it down as most unlikely that a corruptible, dissipable body could move with a perpetual, regular motion; and he backs this with the example of animals, which, though they move with a motion natural to them, still grow tired, and need rest to restore their strength. But what has such movement to do with that of the Earth, which is immense by comparison with theirs? And, worse, to make it move with three motions running and pulling in different directions? Who could ever assert such things, except men who'd sworn themselves its defenders? Nor does it help in this case what Copernicus brings forward — that because this motion is natural to the Earth, and not violent, it produces effects contrary to those of violent motions; and that the things to which force is applied do indeed dissolve, and can't last long, whereas the things made by nature are preserved in their best condition. This answer, I say, is no help, for it's overthrown by our own. Because the animal is, after all, a natural body, not built by art, and its movement is natural, deriving from the soul — that is, from an inner principle; while violent motion is the kind whose principle lies outside, and to which the thing moved contributes nothing. And yet, if the animal keeps up its motion a long time, it grows tired, and even dies, if it stubbornly tries to force itself. See, then, how in nature one meets, on every side, traces that run counter to Copernicus's position, and never a single one in its favor. And so as not to have to take up this critic's part yet again, hear what he brings forward against Kepler (with whom he's arguing), concerning the point Kepler pressed against those who find it awkward — indeed impossible — to enlarge the starry sphere immensely, as Copernicus's position requires. Kepler presses them, then, saying: Difficilius est accidens praeter modulum subiecti intendere, quam subiectum sine accidente augere — it is harder to stretch a property beyond the scale of its subject than to enlarge the subject without the property. Copernicus igitur verisimilius facit, qui auget orbem stellarum fixarum absque motu, quam Ptolemaeus, qui auget motum fixarum immensa velocitate — Copernicus, then, does the more plausible thing, who enlarges the sphere of the fixed stars without motion, than Ptolemy, who enlarges the motion of the fixed stars to an immense velocity. This challenge the author dissolves, marveling at how badly Kepler deceives himself in saying that on Ptolemy's hypothesis the motion is stretched beyond the scale of its subject. For to him it seems the motion is stretched only in keeping with the scale, and that as the scale grows the velocity of the motion grows with it. He proves this by imagining a millstone that makes one revolution in twenty-four hours — a motion we'd call extremely slow. Now suppose its radius stretched out to the distance of the Sun: its far end would match the velocity of the Sun. Stretched to the starry sphere, it would match the velocity of the fixed stars — even though at the rim of the millstone the motion is extremely slow. Now apply this consideration of the millstone to the starry sphere: take a point on its radius, as near the center as the millstone's radius is short. That same motion, which out at the starry sphere is exceedingly swift, will at that point be exceedingly slow. It's the size of the body that turns the exceedingly slow into the exceedingly swift, even while the motion stays one and the same. And so the velocity grows not beyond the scale of the subject, but in keeping with it and with its size — quite differently from what Kepler supposes.

Salviati
I don't believe this author has formed so thin and low an opinion of Kepler that he could persuade himself Kepler failed to understand that the topmost end of a line drawn from the center out to the starry orb moves faster than a point on that same line only a couple of arm-lengths from the center. So he's bound to grasp and understand that Kepler's meaning and intention was to say this: it is the smaller drawback to enlarge a motionless body to an enormous size than to attribute an enormous velocity to a body already vast — bearing in mind the scale, that is, the norm and the example, of the other natural bodies, in which we see that as the distance from the center increases, the velocity decreases; that is, that the periods of their revolutions require longer times. Whereas in rest — which can't be made greater or smaller — the largeness or smallness of the body makes no difference at all. So that, if the author's reply is really to meet Kepler's argument, the author must be reckoning that it's all the same to the moving principle whether it moves, in the same span of time, a tiny body or an immense one — since on his view the increase of velocity follows, without more ado, from the increase of the bulk. But that runs against the architectural rules of nature, which, in the pattern of the smaller spheres — as we see in the planets, and most plainly in the Medicean Stars — makes the smaller orbs revolve in shorter times; so that Saturn's period of revolution is longer than all the periods of the other, smaller spheres, being thirty years. Now to jump from this to a vastly greater sphere and make it turn in twenty-four hours may well, and reasonably, be called going outside the rules of the pattern. So that, if we consider carefully, the author's reply goes not against the concept and sense of the argument, but only against its wording and manner of expression — where, moreover, the author is in the wrong, and can't deny having deliberately feigned not to understand the words, so as to saddle Kepler with a too-gross ignorance. But the fraud has been so crude that, for all so large a discount, it couldn't knock down the opinion of his doctrine that Kepler has stamped on the minds of learned men. Then, as for the challenge against the perpetual motion of the Earth, drawn from its being impossible for the Earth to keep it up without tiring — seeing that the very animals, which do move naturally and from an inner principle, grow tired and need rest to relax their limbs...
