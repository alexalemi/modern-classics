The Third Day

(Part 9 of 16)

Sagredo
That's quite a comedown for a first step.

Salviati
It truly is. A fixed star of the sixth magnitude, which on this author's reckoning had to be — if Copernicus's claim was to hold up — as big as the whole great orb: just by setting it merely equal to the Sun, and the Sun is far less than the ten-millionth part of that great orb, he makes the starry sphere so vast and so high that it's enough to dispose of the objection raised against Copernicus.

Sagredo
Do work that calculation for me, please.

Salviati
The calculation is easy and very quick. The Sun's diameter is eleven of the Earth's semidiameters, and the diameter of the great orb contains 2,416 of those same semidiameters, by the common account of these measures; so the diameter of the orb contains that of the Sun about 220 times over. And since spheres are to one another as the cubes of their diameters, let's take the cube of 220, which is 10,648,000, and we'll have the great orb bigger than the Sun ten million six hundred forty-eight thousand times over — and to this great orb this author said a star of the sixth magnitude would have to be equal.

Sagredo
So their error consists in going badly wrong when they take the apparent diameter of the fixed stars.

Salviati
That's the error, but it isn't the only one. And I'm truly amazed that so many astronomers — men of great name, too, like Alfraganus, Albategnius, and Thebit, and more recently men like Tycho and Clavius, in short all our Academician's predecessors — should have gone so badly wrong in determining the sizes of all the stars, both fixed and moving, apart from the two luminaries, and should have paid no heed to the stray radiance — that fringe of light around them — which deceptively shows them a hundred times and more larger than when they're seen without their rays. And this oversight of theirs can't be excused, because it was in their power to see the stars, whenever they liked, without that fringe; you need only look at them at their first appearance in the evening or their last vanishing at dawn. And if nothing else, Venus — which one often sees at midday so small that you really have to strain your eyes, and which then, the following night, shows up as an enormous torch — ought to have alerted them to their mistake. For I certainly won't believe they supposed the true disk to be the one that shows in deep darkness, and not the one seen in a lit setting; because our own lamps, which seen at night from far off look big but up close show their true flame small and sharply bounded, could well enough have put them on their guard. In fact, if I must speak my mind freely, I absolutely believe that none of them — not even Tycho himself, so careful in handling astronomical instruments, who built such large and exact ones sparing no expense — ever set out to take and measure the apparent diameter of a single star, apart from the Sun and the Moon; instead I think that arbitrarily, and as they say by eye, one of the older among them pronounced the thing to be so, and his followers then, without any further check, simply stuck with that first pronouncement. For had any one of them applied himself to putting it to some test, he would without doubt have caught the error.

Sagredo
But if they lacked the telescope — and you've already said our friend came to know the truth with that instrument — then the others ought to be excused, not charged with negligence.

Salviati
That would follow if the aim couldn't be achieved without the telescope. It's true that the instrument, by showing the star's disk bare and magnified a hundred and a thousand times over, makes the job much easier; but one can also, without the instrument, reach the same result — if not so exactly. And I've done it many times; the method I've used is this. I hung a fine cord toward some star — I used Lyra, which rises between north and northeast — and then, by moving closer to the cord and farther from it, with the cord placed between me and the star, I found the spot from which the thickness of the cord exactly hides the star. This done, I took the distance from my eye to the cord, which turns out to be one of the sides enclosing the angle formed at the eye and standing on the thickness of the cord — an angle similar to, indeed the very same as, the angle that at the starry sphere stands on the diameter of the star. And from the ratio of the cord's thickness to the distance from eye to cord, using the table of arcs and chords, I immediately found the size of the angle — using, of course, the usual precaution one observes in taking such sharp angles: not to place the meeting of the visual rays at the center of the eye, where they only reach after being bent, but beyond the eye, where in reality the size of the pupil sends them to converge.

Sagredo
I understand this precaution, though I have some slight doubt about it; but what troubles me more is that in this operation, when it's done in the dark of night, it seems to me one measures the diameter of the radiant disk, and not the true, bare diameter of the star.

Salviati
No, sir — because the cord, in covering the star's bare little body, strips away the hairs, which are not the star's but our own eye's, and of which it is robbed the moment its true disk is hidden. And when you make the observation, you'll see how unexpectedly that quite large torch — which seemed as though it couldn't be hidden except behind a much bigger obstacle — is covered by a thin little cord. Then, to measure most exactly and find out how many such cord-thicknesses fit into the distance to the eye, I take not a single diameter of the cord, but — laying many pieces of it side by side on a board so that they touch — I take with a compass the whole space occupied by fifteen or twenty of them, and with that measure I gauge the distance (already taken with another, finer thread) from the cord to the convergence of the visual rays. And by this quite exact operation I find that the apparent diameter of a fixed star of the first magnitude — commonly reckoned at 2 arcminutes, and even 3 by Tycho in his Astronomical Letters, page 167 — is no more than 5 arcseconds, which is a twenty-fourth or a thirty-sixth part of what they believed. Now see on what grave errors their doctrines are founded.

Sagredo
I see and understand it very well; but before we go any further, I'd like to raise the doubt that occurs to me about locating the convergence of the visual rays beyond the eye, when one looks at objects subtending very sharp angles. My difficulty comes from its seeming to me that this convergence may be now farther off and now nearer — and this not so much because of the greater or smaller size of the object looked at, as because, in looking at objects of the same size, it seems to me the convergence of the rays must, for some other reason, become more or less remote from the eye.

Salviati
I already see where Sagredo's sharp mind is heading — a most diligent observer of nature's workings. And I'd lay any bet you like that, among a thousand who have watched the pupil of a cat's eye contract and widen enormously, there aren't two — perhaps not one — who has noticed that a like effect happens in men's pupils when they look, according as the medium is much or little illuminated, and that in broad daylight the little circle of the pupil shrinks a great deal; so that in looking at the disk of the Sun it shrinks to something smaller than a grain of millet, whereas in gazing at non-shining objects, and within a dimmer medium, it widens to the size of a lentil or more. In short, this widening and narrowing varies by much more than a tenfold ratio. From which it's clear that when the pupil is much dilated, the angle where the rays converge must be more remote from the eye — which is what happens when we look at faintly lit objects. This is a lesson freshly supplied to me by Sagredo, by which, whenever a most exact observation of great consequence is to be made, we're warned that we must carry out the investigation of that convergence in the very act of the same, or a very similar, operation. But in this case, to expose the astronomers' error, so much accuracy isn't needed — because even if, in their favor, we supposed the convergence to fall right at the pupil itself, it would matter little, their error being so great. I don't know, Sagredo, whether this was the point you were driving at.

Sagredo
That's it exactly, and I'm glad it wasn't unreasonable — as I'm assured by finding I'd hit on the same thing as you. But now, while we're on it, I'd gladly hear in what way one can investigate the distance of the convergence of the visual rays.

Salviati
The method is quite easy, and it goes like this. I take two strips of paper, one black and one white, and I make the black one half as wide as the white. Then I fasten the white one to a wall, and at some distance from it I fix the other on a stick or some other support, fifteen or twenty braccia away. And moving off from this second strip, along the same straight line, by as much again, it's clear that at that distance the straight lines would converge — the lines that, setting out from the ends of the white strip's width, pass just grazing the width of the other strip set in the middle. Whence it follows that if the eye were placed at that meeting point, the black strip in the middle would hide the white one opposite exactly — provided sight took place at a single point. But if we find that the ends of the white strip appear uncovered, that will be a necessary proof that the visual rays don't issue from a single point. And to make the white strip get hidden by the black, we'll have to bring the eye closer: so, having moved it up until the middle strip covers the far one, and having noted how much closer we had to come, that amount of approach will be a sure measure of how far beyond the eye the true convergence of the visual rays falls in this operation. And we'll have besides the diameter of the pupil — or rather of that opening from which the visual rays issue. For the pupil's diameter will be the same fraction of the black paper's width as this ratio: the distance from the convergence of the lines (drawn through the ends of the papers) to the spot where the eye stood when it first saw the far paper hidden by the middle one, compared with the distance between the two papers. And so, if we wanted to measure with precision the apparent diameter of a star, once the observation had been made in the way described above, we'd need to compare the diameter of the cord with the diameter of the pupil. And having found, for instance, that the cord's diameter is four times the pupil's, and the distance from the eye to the cord is, say, 30 braccia, we'll say that the true convergence of the lines drawn from the ends of the star's diameter through the ends of the cord's diameter goes to meet at 40 braccia beyond the cord — for in this way the proportion will be duly preserved between the distance from the cord to the convergence of those lines and the distance from that convergence to the eye's position, which must be the same as the ratio between the cord's diameter and the pupil's diameter.

Sagredo
I've understood it perfectly. So let's hear what Simplicio brings forward in defense of Copernicus's opponents.

Simplicio
Even though that supreme and utterly incredible absurdity urged by these opponents of Copernicus has been much softened by Salviati's argument, it doesn't seem to me removed so completely that enough force doesn't still remain in it to bring down that opinion. For, if I've grasped the sum and final conclusion, even granting that stars of the sixth magnitude are as big as the Sun (which itself seems a lot to believe), it would still be true that the great orb ought to cause in the starry sphere a change and variation like the one the Earth's semidiameter produces in the Sun, which is observable. So, since neither such a variation nor even a smaller one is to be seen in the fixed stars, it seems to me that on this account the Earth's annual motion is left forlorn and demolished.

Salviati
You'd conclude rightly, Simplicio, if there were nothing else to produce on Copernicus's side; but many other things still remain to us. And as for your objection, nothing stands in the way of our supposing the distance of the fixed stars to be even much greater than has been assumed. And you yourself, and anyone else who won't set aside the propositions accepted by Ptolemy's followers, will have to admit it a most fitting thing to place the starry sphere still very much larger than what we've just said it must be reckoned. For, since all astronomers agree that the greater slowness of the planets' revolutions is caused by the greater size of their spheres — and that Saturn is therefore slower than Jupiter, and Jupiter than the Sun, because the one has to trace a larger circle than the other, and this one than that, and so on — considering that Saturn, for instance, the height of whose orb is nine times greater than the Sun's, and whose time for one revolution is therefore 30 times longer than that of one revolution of the Sun; seeing that in Ptolemy's doctrine one revolution of the starry sphere is completed in 36,000 years, whereas Saturn's is finished in 30 and the Sun's in one; arguing by a like proportion, and saying: if Saturn's orb, being 9 times larger than the Sun's, revolves in a time 30 times longer, then by inverse reasoning how large must that orb be which revolves 36,000 times more slowly? — it will be found that the distance of the starry sphere must be 10,800 semidiameters of the great orb, which would be exactly 5 times greater than what we calculated it to be a moment ago, on the supposition that a sixth-magnitude star was as big as the Sun. Now see how much smaller still, on this reckoning, the variation caused in the stars by the Earth's annual motion would have to be. And if by a similar relation we wanted to argue the distance of the starry sphere from Jupiter and from Mars, the one would give it to us as 15,000 and the other as 27,000 semidiameters of the great orb — that is, still larger, the one 7 and the other 12 times over, than the size of a fixed star supposed equal to the Sun gave it to us.

Simplicio
It seems to me one could answer this by saying that the motion of the starry sphere has, since Ptolemy, been observed not to be so slow as he reckoned it; indeed, I think I've heard that Copernicus himself was the observer.

Salviati
You put it very well, but you produce nothing that favors the Ptolemaics' cause in the least — for they never refused the 36,000-year motion of the starry sphere on the grounds that so great a slowness would make it too vast and immense. For if such immensity were not to be granted in nature, they ought — before now — to have denied a revolution so slow that it couldn't, by any reasonable proportion, be fitted to anything but a sphere of intolerable size.

Sagredo
Please, Salviati, let's waste no more time proceeding by such proportions with people who are content to admit the most disproportionate things — so that with them, by this road, it's absolutely impossible to gain anything. And what more disproportionate proportion can be imagined than the one these men swallow and admit? For, while writing that there's no more fitting way to order the celestial spheres than to regulate them by the differences in the lengths of their periods — placing, step by step, the slower above the faster — once they've set the starry sphere highest, as the slowest of all, they set another above it, and therefore larger, and make it move in twenty-four hours, while its lower neighbor moves in 36,000 years. But we spoke enough of these disproportions the other day.

Salviati
I'd like you, Simplicio, to suspend for a moment the affection you bear the followers of your opinion, and tell me sincerely whether you believe they grasp in their minds that magnitude which they then judge cannot, on account of its immensity, be attributed to the universe. Because I, for my part, believe not; and it seems to me that just as, in grasping numbers, once one begins to pass those thousands of millions the imagination gets confused and can no longer form a concept, so it happens too in grasping immense sizes and distances. So that the same effect befalls the reasoning as befalls the senses: when on a clear night I look toward the stars, my senses judge their distance to be a few miles, and the fixed stars no more remote than Jupiter or Saturn — indeed, no farther than the Moon. But, without more ado, consider the past controversies between the astronomers and the Peripatetic philosophers over the distance of the new stars in Cassiopeia and in Sagittarius — the former placing them among the fixed stars, the latter believing them lower than the Moon. So powerless is our sense to distinguish great distances from the very greatest, even though the latter are in fact many thousands of times greater than the former. And finally I ask you, oh foolish man: do you grasp with your imagination that magnitude of the universe which you then judge to be too vast? If you grasp it, will you claim that your apprehension reaches further than divine power — will you say you can imagine things greater than God can bring about? But if you don't grasp it, why do you presume to pass judgment on things you don't understand?

Simplicio
These arguments all run very well, and it's not denied that the heavens may exceed our imagination in size — nor that God could have created them a thousand times larger than they are. But we mustn't grant that anything was created in vain or is idle in the universe. Now, since we see this beautiful order of planets arranged around the Earth at distances proportioned to producing their effects upon it for our benefit — to what end interpose, then, between Saturn's highest orb and the starry sphere, a vast space empty of any star, superfluous and pointless? To what end? For whose comfort and use?

Salviati
We arrogate too much to ourselves, Simplicio, it seems to me, when we insist that the care of us alone is the whole adequate work — the limit beyond which divine wisdom and power does or arranges nothing else. But I wouldn't have us so shorten His hand; rather let us be content to be sure that God and nature are so occupied with the government of human affairs that they could not attend to them more closely even if they had no other care than mankind alone. And this I think I can make clear with a most apt and noble example, drawn from the working of the Sun's light: which, while it draws up those vapors or warms that plant, draws them up and warms it as though it had nothing else to do; indeed, in ripening that cluster of grapes — nay, that single grape — it applies itself so that it could not apply itself more effectively even if the goal of all its business were the ripening of that one grape alone. Now, if this grape receives from the Sun all that can be received, and nothing is filched from it by the Sun's producing, at the same time, a thousand and a thousand other effects, then that grape would be chargeable with envy or with folly if it believed or demanded that the action of the Sun's rays should be spent for its benefit alone. I'm certain that nothing is left undone by divine Providence of what pertains to the government of human affairs; but that there cannot be other things in the universe dependent on His infinite wisdom — that, for my own part, so far as my reason dictates to me, I could not bring myself to believe. Yet, even if the fact stood otherwise, there would be no reluctance in me to believe the reasons that a higher intelligence might bring forward to me. Meanwhile, when I'm told that an immense space set between the planets' orbs and the starry sphere would be useless and pointless, empty of stars and idle — and likewise that so great an immensity, as a receptacle for the fixed stars, surpassing all our comprehension, is superfluous — I say it's rashness to try to make our feeble reasoning the judge of God's works, and to call vain or superfluous everything in the universe that doesn't serve us.
