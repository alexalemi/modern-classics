The Fourth Day

(Part 5 of 6)

Sagredo
So the monthly variation in the tides depends on the change in the Earth's annual motion? And the yearly variation in those same tides derives from the additions and subtractions of the daily whirl? Now I find myself more confused than ever, and more hopeless of ever managing to grasp how this tangle holds together — more knotted, it seems to me, than the Gordian knot. And I envy Simplicio, from whose silence I gather that he's understood the whole of it, and is free of the confusion that so clutters my imagination.

Simplicio
I do believe, Sagredo, that you find yourself confused, and I think I even know the cause of your confusion — which, to my mind, comes from this: of the things Salviati has brought up just now, part you understand and part you don't. It's also true that I'm free of confusion, but not for the reason you suppose — that is, not because I've grasped the whole of it. Quite the opposite: it comes to me from grasping nothing at all. Confusion lies in a multitude of things, not in nothing.

Sagredo
You see, Salviati, how a few little jerks of the bridle given to Simplicio these past days have made him tame, and changed him from a jumper into a gentle palfrey. But please, without any more delay, get us both out of our trouble.

Salviati
I'll strain my clumsy powers of expression as far as they'll go; their dullness will be made up for by the sharpness of your wits. There are two phenomena whose causes we have to track down. The first concerns the differences that occur in the tides over the monthly period; the other belongs to the yearly period. First we'll speak of the monthly, then treat the yearly; and we must resolve all of it according to the foundations and hypotheses already established, without introducing any novelty — not into astronomy, nor into the universe — for the sake of the tides. Instead, let's show that of all the various phenomena we observe in them, the causes reside in things already known, and accepted as true and beyond doubt.

I say, then, that it's a true, natural — indeed necessary — thing that one and the same moving body, set turning in a circle by the same moving power, should take a longer time to complete its course along a larger circle than along a smaller one. This is a truth accepted by everyone, and confirmed by every experiment, of which I'll offer a few.

In wheel-clocks, and the large ones in particular, to regulate the timekeeping the makers fit a certain rod that turns freely in the horizontal, and at its ends they hang two lead weights; and when the clock runs too slow, merely by bringing those leads a little nearer the center of the rod they make its swings more frequent; and on the other hand, to slow it down, it's enough to draw the same weights out toward the ends, because then the swings come more seldom, and consequently the intervals of the hours grow longer. Here the moving power is the same — the counterweight; the moving bodies are the same leads; and their swings are more frequent when they're nearer the center, that is, when they move along smaller circles.

Hang equal weights from unequal cords, and, drawn aside from the vertical, let them go free: we'll see the ones on shorter cords make their swings in shorter times, as being those that move along smaller circles. But more: attach such a weight to a cord that rides over a nail fixed in the ceiling, and hold the other end of the cord in your hand; having set the hanging weight going, then, while it's making its swings, pull the end of the cord you're holding so that the weight rises. You'll see, as it lifts, the frequency of its swings increase, since they're being made continually along smaller circles.

And here I want you to note two particulars, worth knowing. One is that the swings of such a pendulum are made, with such necessity, under certain determined times, that it's utterly impossible to make it swing under any other times except by lengthening or shortening the cord — of which you can even satisfy yourself right now, by experiment: tie a stone to a string, hold the other end in your hand, and try whether, by any device whatever, you can ever manage to make it go back and forth under anything but one determined time, short of lengthening or shortening the string — which you'll see is flatly impossible.

The other particular, truly marvelous, is that the same pendulum makes its swings with the same frequency — or with so slight, so nearly imperceptible a difference — whether they're made along the largest arcs or the smallest of the same circumference. I mean that if we move the pendulum from the vertical by only one, two, or three degrees, or move it 70, 80, or even a full quarter-circle, once let go it will, in the one case and the other, make its swings with the same frequency — the first, where it has to move along an arc of 4 or 6 degrees, just as much as the second, where it has to pass through arcs of 160 degrees or more. This is seen more plainly still by hanging two equal weights from two equally long threads, then drawing one aside from the vertical by a small distance and the other by a very great one; set free, they'll go and return in the same times, the one along quite small arcs, the other along very great ones.

From which follows the conclusion of a most beautiful problem: that, given a quarter-circle (I'll sketch a little figure here on the ground) — say this one AB, set upright to the horizon so that it stands on the plane, touching at the point B — and having made an arc, with a board well planed and smooth on its concave side, bent to the curve of the circumference ADB, so that a ball, well rounded and polished, can run freely inside it (the frame of a sieve suits this experiment) — I say that, put the ball wherever you like, near or far from the lowest terminus B — say, putting it at the point C, or here at D, or at E — and let go, in equal times, or times imperceptibly different, it will arrive at the terminus B, whether it starts from C or from D or from E or from any other place whatever: a phenomenon truly marvelous.

Add another phenomenon, no less beautiful than this: that along all the chords drawn from the point B to the points C, D, E, and to any other — not only taken in the quadrant BA, but in the whole circumference of the entire circle — the same moving body will descend in absolutely equal times; so that in the same time it will descend along the whole diameter erected perpendicular over the point B as it descends along BC, even if BC subtended only a single degree or a smaller arc.

Add the other marvel, which is that the motions of falling bodies made along the arcs of the quadrant AB are made in shorter times than those made along the chords of the same arcs: so that the swiftest motion, made in the shortest time, by a body going from the point A to the terminus B, will be the one made not along the straight line AB (though it's the shortest of all the lines that can be drawn between the points A and B), but along the circumference ADB. And taking, too, any point you like in that same arc — say, for instance, the point D — and drawing the two chords AD, DB, the moving body, starting from the point A, will reach B in less time coming along the two chords AD, DB than along AB alone; but the shortest time of all will be that of the fall along the arc ADB. And understand the same phenomena of all the other smaller arcs, taken from the lowest terminus B upward.

Sagredo
No more, no more! You so overwhelm me with wonder, and pull my mind in so many directions, that I fear little of it will be left free and clear to apply to the main matter we're treating — which is quite obscure and difficult enough on its own. I'll beg you, though, to do me the favor, once we've finished this speculation about the tides, of being here on other days too, to honor this house — mine and yours — and to discourse on so many other problems we've left hanging, which are perhaps no less curious and beautiful than the one we've treated on the past days and must finish today.

Salviati
I'll be at your service; but we'll need more than one or two sessions, if, on top of the other questions reserved for separate treatment, we want to add all the many that belong to local motion — of natural bodies as much as of projectiles — a subject treated at length by our friend the Academician. But coming back to our first purpose, where we were explaining how, for bodies moved in circles by a moving power that keeps itself continually the same, the times of their circuits were fixed and determined, and impossible to make longer or shorter — having given examples, and brought experiments both sensible and doable by us — we can confirm the same truth with the observed motions of the heavenly planets, in which the same rule is seen to hold: that those which move along larger circles use up more time in traversing them. A very ready observation of this we have from the Medicean Stars — Jupiter's four little moons — which in short times make their revolutions around Jupiter. So there's no room for doubt — indeed we may hold it for firm and sure — that when, for example, the Moon, continuing to be moved by the same moving power, were drawn little by little into smaller circles, she would acquire a disposition to shorten the times of her periods, just like that pendulum whose cord we were shortening in the course of its swings — that is, shortening the radius of the circumferences it passed through.

Now know that what I've offered about the Moon as an example actually happens, and holds true, in essence, in fact. Let's recall that it was already concluded by us, together with Copernicus, that it's impossible to separate the Moon from the Earth, around which — beyond dispute — she moves in a month. Let's likewise recall that the terrestrial globe, always accompanied by the Moon, travels along the circumference of the great orb around the Sun in a year, in which time the Moon revolves around the Earth nearly 13 times. From which revolving it follows that the Moon is sometimes near the Sun — that is, when she's between the Sun and the Earth — and sometimes much farther off — that is, when the Earth stands between the Moon and the Sun: near, in short, at the time of her conjunction and new moon; far, at the full moon and opposition. And the greatest distance and the greatest nearness differ by as much as the diameter of the lunar orb.

Now, if it's true that the power which moves the Earth and the Moon around the Sun keeps always the same vigor; and if it's true that the same moving body, moved by the same power but along unequal circles, passes similar arcs of the smaller circles in shorter times — then we must necessarily say that the Moon, when she's at a smaller distance from the Sun (that is, at the time of conjunction), passes greater arcs of the great orb than when she's at a greater distance (that is, at opposition and full moon). And this lunar unevenness must be shared by the Earth as well. For if we conceive a straight line drawn from the center of the Sun through the center of the terrestrial globe, and prolonged as far as the lunar orb, this will be the radius of the great orb, along which the Earth, were she alone, would move uniformly; but if along that same radius we place another body to be carried — putting it now between the Earth and the Sun, and now beyond the Earth at a greater distance from the Sun — it must be that in this second case the shared motion of the two along the circumference of the great orb, by reason of the Moon's remoteness, turns out somewhat slower than in the other case, when the Moon is between the Earth and the Sun, that is, at the smaller distance. So in this business exactly what happens in the clock happens: the Moon representing to us that lead which is fixed now farther from the center, to make the swings of the rod less frequent, and now nearer, to make them more frequent.

From here it can be plain how the Earth's annual motion along the great orb and under the ecliptic is not uniform, and how its irregularity derives from the Moon, and has its own periods and monthly returns. And since it was concluded that the periodic alterations of the tides — monthly and yearly — could derive from no cause other than the altered proportion between the annual motion and the additions and subtractions of the daily whirl; and since such alteration could come about in two ways, namely by altering the annual motion while keeping the amount of the additions fixed, or by changing the size of these while keeping the annual motion uniform — we've now found the first of these two ways, founded on the irregularity of the annual motion, which depends on the Moon and has its monthly periods. It's therefore necessary that, for this cause, the tides have a monthly period, within which they grow greater and smaller. Now you see how the cause of the monthly period resides in the annual motion, and at the same time you see what the Moon has to do with this business, and how she takes a part in it without having anything at all to do with either seas or waters.

Sagredo
If someone who had no acquaintance with any kind of stairs were shown a very tall tower, and asked whether he had the heart to reach its topmost height, I absolutely believe he'd say no, not understanding that it could be reached in any way but by flying; but if a stone no higher than half an arm-length were shown him, and he were asked whether he thought he could climb up onto that, I'm certain he'd answer yes — and wouldn't deny that not once, but 10, 20, and 100 times, he could easily climb it. So that if the stairs were then shown him, by means of which — with the ease he'd granted — one could get up to where a moment before he'd declared it impossible to arrive, I believe that, laughing at himself, he'd confess his little foresight. You, Salviati, have led me by degrees so gently that, not without wonder, I find myself arrived with the least effort at that height where I believed one could not arrive. It's quite true that, the stairway having been dark, I didn't notice I'd approached or reached the top until, coming out into the bright air, I discovered a great sea and a great countryside. And just as in climbing one step there's no effort at all, so, one by one, your propositions seemed to me so clear that, little or nothing new coming to me, the gain seemed to me little or none — whence all the more the wonder grows in me at the unexpected upshot of this discourse, which has led me to the understanding of a thing I reckoned inexplicable. Only one difficulty remains to me, from which I desire to be freed; and this is that if the motion of the Earth, together with that of the Moon under the zodiac, are irregular, that irregularity ought to have been observed and noted by the astronomers — which I don't know to have happened. So you, who are better informed than I in these matters, free me from the doubt, and tell me how the fact stands.

Salviati
Very reasonably you doubt; and, answering the objection, I say that although astronomy, in the course of many centuries, has made great progress in investigating the constitution and the motions of the heavenly bodies, it hasn't yet arrived at such a point that very many things don't remain undecided — and perhaps still many others hidden. It's to be believed that the first observers of the sky knew of nothing but a motion common to all the stars, namely this daily one. I'll readily believe that within a few days they noticed the Moon was inconstant in keeping company with the other stars; but that then many years ran on before all the planets were distinguished. And in particular I think that Saturn, for its slowness, and Mercury, for being seen only rarely, were among the last to be recognized as wanderers and rovers. Many more years, it's to be believed, passed before the stations and retrogradations of the three upper planets were observed, as also their approaches to and recessions from the Earth — occasions necessarily prompting the introduction of eccentrics and epicycles, things unknown even to Aristotle, since he makes no mention of them. Mercury and Venus, with their marvelous appearances, how long they've held the astronomers in suspense over settling — of all things — their very position! So that even the mere order of the world's bodies, and the whole structure of the parts of the universe known to us, remained in doubt until the time of Copernicus, who has at last pointed out to us the true constitution and the true system according to which those parts are ordered — so that we're certain Mercury, Venus, and the other planets revolve around the Sun, and that the Moon revolves around the Earth. But how each planet then governs itself in its particular revolution, and how the structure of its orb precisely stands — which is what's commonly called its theoric — we cannot yet resolve beyond doubt: let Mars, which so troubles the modern astronomers, be our witness; and to the Moon herself varied theorics have been assigned, after Copernicus himself changed hers a good deal from Ptolemy's.

And to come down more to our particular case — that is, to the apparent motion of the Sun and the Moon — of the Sun a certain great unevenness has been observed, by which it passes the two half-circles of the ecliptic, divided by the points of the equinoxes, in quite different times; in passing one of them it uses about nine days more than in passing the other — a difference, as you see, very great and notable. But whether, in passing small arcs — such as, for example, the 12 signs — it keeps a perfectly regular motion, or instead proceeds now somewhat faster and now slower, as must happen when the annual motion is only apparently the Sun's, but in reality the Earth's, accompanied by the Moon — this has not so far been observed, nor perhaps looked into. And of the Moon, whose returns have been investigated chiefly for the sake of eclipses — for which it's enough to have exact knowledge of her motion around the Earth — it has likewise not been investigated with full curiosity what her progress is along the particular arcs of the zodiac. That the Earth and the Moon, then, in running along the zodiac — that is, along the circumference of the great orb — speed up somewhat at the new moons and slow down at the full moons, must not be doubted just because such unevenness hasn't shown itself. This has happened for two reasons: first, because it hasn't been looked for; second, because it may be not very great.

Nor does it need to be very great to produce the effect we see in the alteration of the sizes of the tides, because not only such alterations, but the tides themselves, are a small thing compared with the size of the bodies in which they take place — even though, with respect to us and our littleness, they seem great things. For to add or take away one degree of speed where there are naturally 700 or 1000 can't be called a great alteration, either in what confers it or in what receives it. The water of our sea, carried by the daily whirl, makes about 700 miles an hour (which is the motion common to the Earth and to it, and therefore imperceptible to us); what becomes noticeable to us in the currents is not one mile an hour (I speak of the open sea, not of the straits); and this is what alters the primary, natural, and great motion. And such an alteration is a great deal with respect to us and to ships, because to a vessel that by the force of its oars would make, say, 3 miles an hour in still water, having that current in its favor rather than against it will mean double the voyage — a most notable difference in the boat's motion, but a very tiny one in the sea's motion, which is altered by its seven-hundredth part. The same I say of the rising and falling of one, two, or three feet — and barely four or five at the far end of a gulf two thousand or more miles long, and where there are depths of hundreds of feet: this alteration is far less than if, in one of the boats that carry fresh water, that water, when the boat comes to a stop, were to rise at the prow by the thickness of a sheet of paper. I conclude, then, that very tiny alterations, with respect to the immense size and the utmost speed of the seas, are enough to make in them changes that are great in relation to our littleness and to our own affairs.
