The Fourth Day

(Part 3 of 6)

Salviati
Now that these matters are settled, it's time we turned to examining the particular phenomena — and their variety — that experience shows us in the ebb and flow of the waters. First, we should have no trouble understanding why in lakes, ponds, and even small seas there is no notable ebb and flow. There are two thoroughly conclusive reasons for this. The first is that, because the basin is short, when at different hours of the day it takes on different degrees of speed, those degrees are acquired with little difference by all its parts: the leading and the trailing parts alike — the eastern and the western — speed up and slow down in almost exactly the same way. And since this change happens gradually, and not by suddenly throwing some abrupt check and delay, or some sudden and great acceleration, against the motion of the containing vessel, the vessel and all its parts are slowly and evenly impressed with the same degrees of speed. From this uniformity it follows that the contained water, too, receives the same impressions with little stubbornness or resistance, and so gives only the faintest sign of rising or falling, of running toward one end or the other. You can see this same effect plainly in small artificial vessels, where the water within takes on the very same degrees of speed, provided the speeding up or slowing down is done in a slow and uniform proportion. But in arms of the sea that stretch a great distance from east to west, the acceleration or slowing is far more notable and uneven, since one of its ends will be in a much-retarded motion while the other is still moving very fast. The second cause is the reciprocal swinging of the water, arising from the impetus it, too, would have taken on from the motion of its container — a swinging whose oscillations, as we noted, are very frequent in small vessels. From this it results that, although the cause residing in the Earth's motions contributes motion to the water only once every twelve hours — since only once a day does the motion of the containing vessels reach its greatest slowing and its greatest speeding — nevertheless that other, second cause, depending on the weight of the water as it seeks to return to equilibrium, and having, according to the shortness of the vessel, its swings of one hour, or two, or three, and so on: this second cause, mixing with the first (which by itself, in small vessels, already stays very slight), renders it altogether imperceptible. For before the disturbance produced by the primary cause — the one with periods of twelve hours — has finished being impressed, along comes the other, secondary one, running counter to it, depending on the water's own weight, which, according to the shortness and depth of the vessel, has the time of its swings at one, two, three, or four hours, and so on; and, running counter to the first, it upsets and displaces it, without letting it reach the top, or even the middle, of its movement. And from this opposition the evidence of ebb and flow is wholly annihilated, or greatly obscured. I say nothing of the continual changes in the air, which, by troubling the water, would keep us from ever being sure of a very slight rise or fall of half a finger's breadth, or less, that might really reside in gulfs and basins of water no longer than a degree or two.

I come, in the second place, to resolving the puzzle of how — given that the primary principle contains no cause for stirring the waters except once every twelve hours (once by the greatest speed of motion, and once by the greatest slowness) — the period of ebb and flow nonetheless commonly appears to be one of six hours. To this the answer is that such a determination can in no way come from the primary cause alone; the secondary ones must be brought in — namely, the greater or lesser length of the vessels, and the greater or lesser depth of the waters they contain. These causes, though they have no action at all in the movements of the waters (that action belonging to the primary cause alone, without which nothing of ebb and flow would follow), nonetheless have the chief say in determining the times of the swings — so powerful a say that the primary cause must remain subject to them. So the six-hour period is no more proper or natural than those of other intervals of time; it is simply, perhaps, the most observed, being the one that fits our Mediterranean, which for long ages was the only sea people sailed. And even there this period is not observed in all its parts, since in certain more confined places — such as the Hellespont and the Aegean — the periods are much shorter, and very different from one another besides. This variety and its causes were beyond Aristotle; and some say that, after long observing them from certain rocks of Negroponte, he was driven to despair, threw himself into the sea, and deliberately drowned.

In the third place, we shall have very ready to hand the reason why a certain sea, though very long — such as the Red Sea — is nonetheless almost entirely free of ebb and flow. This happens because its length does not run from east to west, but rather crosswise, from the southeast toward the northwest. But since the Earth's movements are from west to east, the impulses of the waters always strike along the meridians, and do not move from parallel to parallel. Hence in seas that stretch crosswise toward the poles, and are narrow the other way, there remains no cause for ebb and flow except through the sharing of some other sea they communicate with, one that is subject to large movements.

In the fourth place, we shall very easily grasp the reason why the ebb and flow are greatest — in the rising and falling of the waters — at the far ends of gulfs, and least in the middle parts. Daily experience shows this here in Venice, set at the far end of the Adriatic, where the difference commonly amounts to five or six feet; whereas in places of the Mediterranean distant from the ends, the change is very slight — as in the islands of Corsica and Sardinia, and on the shores of Rome and Livorno, where it does not exceed half a foot. We shall also grasp how, conversely, where the risings and fallings are small, the currents' runnings-out and runnings-back are large. It is an easy matter, I say, to understand the cause of these phenomena, since we have plain confirmations of them in every sort of vessel we build by hand, in which these same effects are seen to follow naturally when we move them with an uneven motion — now sped up, now slowed down.

Moreover, considering, in the fifth place, how the same quantity of water, moved even slowly through a spacious channel, must of necessity run with great force when it then has to pass through a narrow place, we shall have no trouble understanding the cause of the great currents in the narrow channel that separates Calabria from Sicily. For all the water that, from the breadth of the island and from the Ionian gulf, is held in the eastern part of the sea, though it descends slowly toward the west through that whole expanse, nevertheless, on being squeezed into the strait between Scylla and Charybdis, drops swiftly and makes an enormous turmoil. A similar turmoil, and a far greater one, is understood to occur between Africa and the great island of St. Lawrence, when the waters of the two vast seas — the Indian and the Ethiopic — that flank it must, in flowing, squeeze into a smaller channel between the island and the coast of Ethiopia. And the currents must be very great indeed in the Strait of Magellan, which links the vast Ethiopic and South oceans.

It follows now, in the sixth place, that in order to account for some more hidden and unexpected phenomena observed in this matter, we go on to make another important consideration regarding the two principal causes of ebb and flow, and then compound and mix them together. The first and simpler of the two is (as has been said many times) the fixed acceleration and slowing of the parts of the Earth, from which the waters would have a fixed period of running toward the east and returning toward the west within a span of twenty-four hours. The other is the one that depends on the water's own weight, which, once stirred by the primary cause, then seeks to return to equilibrium with repeated swings — swings not determined by a single, set time, but having as many different times as there are different lengths and depths of the basins and arms of the seas. As far as this second principle goes, some would run out and back in one hour, others in two, in four, in six, in eight, in ten, and so on. Now, if we begin to join the primary cause — which steadily keeps its period of twelve hours — with some secondary one that had its period of, say, five hours, it will happen that at certain times the primary and the secondary cause agree in making their impulses both toward the same side; and in this conjunction, this so-to-speak unanimous conspiring together, the tides will be large. At other times it will happen that the primary impulse comes in a certain way to oppose what the secondary period would bring; and in this clash, each principle taking away what the other would give, the movements of the waters will be weakened, and the sea will be reduced to a very quiet and almost motionless state. And at still other times, according as the two principles neither wholly oppose nor wholly agree with each other, there will be other variations in the increase and decrease of the tides. It can also happen that two very large seas, communicating through some narrow channel, come — through the mixing of the two principles of motion — to have the one with a cause of rising at the very time the other has a cause of opposite movement. In that case, in the channel where those seas communicate, there arise extraordinary agitations, with opposing movements, whirlpools, and most dangerous boilings — of which there are continual reports and actual firsthand experiences. From such discordant movements, depending not only on the different positions and lengths but also, in great measure, on the different depths of the communicating seas, there will arise at certain times various disturbances in the waters, irregular and beyond observation, whose causes have greatly puzzled — and still puzzle — sailors, who meet with them without seeing that no force of winds, nor any other serious change in the air, can be their cause. Of this disturbance of the air we must, in other phenomena, take great account, and reckon it a third and incidental cause, capable of greatly altering the observation of effects that depend on the primary and more essential causes. And there is no doubt that if violent winds keep blowing — from the east, for example — they will hold up the waters, preventing their ebb; so that, when the second recurrence of the flow arrives at the appointed hours, and then the third, the waters will swell greatly; and thus, held up for several days by the force of the wind, they rise higher than usual, causing extraordinary floods.

We must further (and this will be like a seventh problem) take note of another cause of movement, depending on the great abundance of water from the rivers that empty into seas that are not very vast — where, in the channels or straits that communicate with such seas, the water is seen always to run the same way, as happens in the Thracian Bosphorus below Constantinople, where the water always runs from the Black Sea toward the Propontis. For in that Black Sea, because of its smallness, the principal causes of ebb and flow have little force; but on the other hand, since very great rivers empty into it, when all that flood of water must pass and pour out through the strait, the current there is quite notable, and always toward the south. Here, moreover, we must note that this strait and channel, though quite narrow, is not subject to the disturbances that the strait of Scylla and Charybdis is. For that one has the Black Sea above it toward the north, and the Propontis and the Aegean, along with the Mediterranean beyond, toward the south — though at a long remove. But, as we have already noted, seas that run north to south, however long, are not subject to ebb and flow. Whereas the strait of Sicily lies set between parts of the Mediterranean that stretch over great distances from west to east — that is, along the run of the ebb and flow — and so in it the agitations are very great. And they would be greater at the Pillars of Hercules, if the Strait of Gibraltar were to open less; and they report those of the Strait of Magellan to be very great indeed. This is all that occurs to me for now to tell you about the causes of this first, daily period of ebb and flow, and its various phenomena. If you have anything to propose, do so, and then we can pass on to the other two periods — the monthly and the annual.

Simplicio
I don't think it can be denied that the discourse you've made proceeds very plausibly, arguing, as we put it, ex suppositione — on a supposition — that is, granting that the Earth moves with the two movements Copernicus attributes to it. But if those movements are ruled out, the whole thing remains empty and invalid; and the ruling out of that hypothesis is pointed out to us quite plainly by your own discourse. With the supposition of the two terrestrial movements you account for the ebb and flow, and then, conversely, reasoning in a circle, from the ebb and flow you draw the indication and confirmation of those very movements. And, coming to a more specific point: you say that the water, being a fluid body and not tenaciously attached to the Earth, is not compelled to obey its every motion punctually — from which you then derive its ebb and flow. Now, following in your very footsteps, I argue the opposite, and say: the air is far more tenuous and fluid than water, and less attached to the Earth's surface — to which the water, if for no other reason, clings by its weight, pressing down upon it far more than the featherlight air does. Therefore the air ought much less to follow the Earth's movements; and so, if the Earth moved in that way, we — its inhabitants, carried along by it at the same speed — ought perpetually to feel a wind from the east striking us with intolerable force. And that this must be so, experience makes us aware of daily: for if, riding post at a speed of only eight or ten miles an hour, meeting the still air with our face makes us feel a wind that strikes us no lightly, what ought our rapid course of eight hundred or a thousand miles an hour to do, against air free of that motion? Yet we feel nothing of the sort.

Salviati
To this objection, which has much of the plausible about it, I answer that it is true the air is more tenuous and lighter, and by its lightness less adherent to the Earth, than water, which is so much heavier and more corporeal. But then the conclusion you draw from these conditions is false — namely, that because of this lightness, tenuity, and lesser adherence to the Earth, the air should exempt itself, more than water does, from following the terrestrial movements, so that to us, who share those movements entirely, its disobedience would become sensible and manifest. In fact, just the opposite happens. For, if you remember rightly, the cause of the ebb and flow of the water, as we assigned it, consists in the water's not following the unevenness of the motion of its vessel, but retaining the impetus conceived earlier, without diminishing or increasing it by the precise measure by which it is increased or diminished in its vessel. Since, then, the disobedience to a fresh increase or decrease of motion consists in preserving and maintaining the impetus conceived before, that body which is more apt at such preservation will also be the better suited to display the effect that follows from that preservation. Now, how disposed water is to maintain an agitation once conceived, even after the cause that impressed it has ceased, is shown by the experience of seas mightily stirred by violent winds: their waves, even after the air has calmed and the wind died, remain in motion a long while — as the sacred poet gracefully sang, "Like the high Aegean..." and so on. And this persistence in the agitation depends on the weight of the water; for, as has been said elsewhere, light bodies are indeed far easier to set moving than heavier ones, but they are just so much less able to preserve the motion impressed on them once the moving cause has ceased. Hence the air, being in itself most tenuous and light, is very easily moved by any least force, but is also most unfit to preserve the motion once the mover has ceased. So, as for the air surrounding the terrestrial globe, I would say that by its adherence it is carried around no less than the water is — especially the part contained within basins, those basins being the plains ringed by mountains. And of this portion we can much more reasonably affirm that it is carried around, snatched along by the roughness of the Earth, than the upper air is snatched along by the celestial motion, as you Peripatetics assert.

What I have said so far seems to me a fairly adequate answer to Simplicio's objection; nevertheless, I want, with a new objection and a new reply — founded on a marvelous experience — to satisfy him more than fully, and to confirm for Sagredo the mobility of the terrestrial globe. I said that the air — and in particular that part of it which does not rise above the summits of the highest mountains — is carried around by the roughness of the Earth's surface. From this it seems to follow that if the Earth's surface were not uneven, but smooth and polished, there would be no cause to draw the air along in its company, or at least to carry it along with such uniformity. Now, the surface of this globe of ours is not all rugged and rough: there are very great, quite smooth expanses — namely, the surfaces of the vast seas — which, being also very far from the mountain ridges that ring them, do not seem able to have the power to carry the overlying air along with them. And, not carrying it, one ought in those places to feel just what follows from that.

Simplicio
This is the very difficulty I too was about to raise, and it seems to me of great force.

Salviati
You speak very well: so that, Simplicio, from not feeling in the air what would follow as a consequence if this globe of ours went around, you argue its immobility. But if this thing, which you think one should feel by necessary consequence, were in fact and by experience felt, would you accept it as a strong enough indication and argument for the mobility of that same globe?

Simplicio
In this case one mustn't argue with me alone, because if it happened, and the cause of it were hidden from me, perhaps it might be known to others.

Salviati
So with you one can never win, but always stands to lose — better, then, not to play at all. Still, so as not to leave the third player in the lurch, I'll go on. We were just saying — and I'll repeat it with something added — that the air, being a tenuous and fluid body, not firmly joined to the Earth, seemed to have no need to obey its motion, except insofar as the roughness of the Earth's surface snatches up and carries along with it a portion of air contiguous to it, a portion rising not much above the greatest heights of the mountains. And this portion of air will be all the less resistant to the terrestrial turning insofar as it is full of vapors, fumes, and exhalations — all of them materials partaking of earthly qualities, and consequently born by their nature apt for the same movements. But where the causes of motion were lacking — that is, where the surface of the globe had great flat spaces, and there was less of the admixture of earthly vapors — there the cause by which the surrounding air should wholly obey the snatching of the terrestrial turning would in part cease. So in such places, while the Earth turns toward the east, one ought continually to feel a wind striking us, blowing from the east toward the west; and such a blowing ought to be more perceptible where the whirl of the globe is faster — which would be in the places most remote from the poles and nearest the greatest circle of the daily turning. But in fact experience much applauds this philosophical reasoning: for in the wide seas, in their parts far from land and lying beneath the torrid zone — that is, enclosed between the tropics, where the earthly evaporations are also lacking — one feels a perpetual breeze moving from the east, with so constant a tenor that ships sail prosperously by its grace to the West Indies; and from those same shores, casting off from the Mexican coast, they plow with the same favor across the Pacific toward the Indies that are eastern to us but western to them. Whereas, on the contrary, voyages from there toward the east are difficult and uncertain, and can by no means be made along the same routes; instead one must hug the coast more, to find other winds — accidental and unruly, so to speak — caused by other principles, such as we who dwell among the mainland continually feel by experience. Of these generations of winds the causes are many and various, which there is no occasion to produce at present; and these accidental winds are the ones that blow indifferently from every quarter of the Earth, and that disturb the seas remote from the equinoctial and surrounded by the rough surface of the Earth — which is as much as to say, subject to those disturbances of the air that confound that primary breathing-out, which, if these accidental impediments were lacking, ought perpetually to be felt, and most of all over the sea. Now see how the effects of the water and of the air seem marvelously to agree with the celestial observations in confirming the mobility of our terrestrial globe.
