The First Day

(Part 8 of 12)

Salviati
My desire to get at some truth — a pursuit in which the objections of intelligent men like you two can help me a great deal — will make me very brief in dispatching what's left. Let the seventh point of resemblance, then, be this: that the Earth and the Moon answer each other in injuries no less than in favors. The Moon, quite often at the very peak of her illumination, is robbed of light and eclipsed when the Earth steps between her and the Sun; and she in her turn, by way of payback, places herself between the Earth and the Sun, and with her shadow darkens the Earth. True, the revenge doesn't measure up to the offense: the Moon often remains plunged entirely in the Earth's shadow, and for a good long while, whereas the whole Earth is never darkened by the Moon, nor for any long stretch of time. Still, considering how small her body is compared to the size of the Earth's, you can only say that her fighting spirit, in a certain way, is very great. So much for the resemblances. What would come next is a discussion of the disparities; but since Simplicio means to favor us with his doubts about the resemblances, we'd better hear them and weigh them before moving on.

Sagredo
Yes — since it's a fair bet that Simplicio will have no objections to the disparities and differences between the Earth and the Moon, seeing that he holds their substances to be utterly different.

Simplicio
Of the resemblances you recited in drawing your parallel between the Earth and the Moon, I find I can admit without objection only the first and two others. I admit the first — the spherical shape — though even there there's a catch, since I take the Moon's sphere to be highly polished and smooth as a mirror, whereas this Earth of ours we can touch with our own hands and feel how rough and rugged it is. But that point, concerning the unevenness of the surface, belongs under another of the resemblances you brought forward, so I'll save what I have to say about it for the consideration of that one.

That the Moon is, as you claim in your second resemblance, opaque and dark in herself like the Earth — of that I admit only the first attribute, the opacity, of which solar eclipses assure me: for if the Moon were transparent, the air during a total eclipse of the Sun would not go as dark as it does; a refracted light would come through the transparency of the lunar body, as we see happen through the densest clouds. But as for the darkness, I don't believe the Moon is wholly without light of her own, as the Earth is. On the contrary, that faint brightness we see across the rest of her disc, beyond the slender horns lit by the Sun, I take to be her own proper and natural light, and not a reflection from the Earth — which I judge incapable, by its extreme roughness and darkness, of reflecting the Sun's rays.

In the third parallel I agree with you in one part and dissent in the other. I agree in judging the body of the Moon utterly solid and hard, like the Earth — indeed much more so; for if we take from Aristotle that the heavens are of impenetrable hardness, and the stars are the denser parts of the heavens, then they must necessarily be perfectly firm and perfectly impenetrable.

Sagredo
What beautiful material the stuff of the heavens would be for building palaces, if only one could get hold of it — so hard, and so transparent!

Salviati
Terrible material, rather: being, by its utter transparency, completely invisible, you couldn't walk through the rooms without grave danger of banging into the doorposts and cracking your head.

Sagredo
There'd be no such danger, if it's true, as some Peripatetics say, that the stuff is intangible; and if it can't be touched, still less can it be bumped into.

Salviati
That would be no comfort at all. For even if celestial matter can't be touched, since it lacks the tangible qualities, it can perfectly well touch elemental bodies; and as far as hurting us goes, it comes to the same thing whether it crashes into us — or worse — or we crash into it. But let's leave these palaces, or better, these castles in the air, and not hold up Simplicio.

Simplicio
The question you've raised in passing is one of the hard ones treated in philosophy, and I have some very fine thoughts on it from a great professor who held a chair at Padua; but this isn't the moment to go into them. So, returning to our subject: I say again that I take the Moon to be far more solid than the Earth — but I argue it not, as you do, from the roughness and ruggedness of her surface, but from the opposite: from her being fit to take (as we see among ourselves in the hardest gems) a polish and luster beyond that of the smoothest mirror; for her surface must be like that, to send us so vivid a reflection of the Sun's rays. As for those appearances you speak of — mountains, crags, ridges, valleys, and so on — they're all illusions. I've been present at public disputations where it was stoutly maintained, against these introducers of novelties, that such appearances come from nothing but parts unequally opaque and transparent, of which the Moon is composed inside and out — the kind of thing we often see happen in crystal, in amber, and in many perfectly polished precious stones, where, from the opacity of some parts and the transparency of others, all sorts of hollows and bulges seem to appear in them.

In the fourth resemblance I concede that the surface of the terrestrial globe, seen from far away, would present two different appearances, one brighter and one darker; but I think those differences would fall out the reverse of what you say. That is, I believe the surface of the water would appear bright, because it's smooth and transparent, and the surface of the land would stay dark, because of its opacity and roughness, poorly suited to throwing back the light of the Sun.

On the fifth comparison I admit everything, and I'm satisfied that if the Earth shone as the Moon does, it would show itself, to anyone gazing at it from up there, in shapes matching the phases we see in the Moon. I understand, too, how the period of its illumination and its change of shapes would be a month, even though the Sun sweeps over the whole of it in twenty-four hours; and finally I have no difficulty admitting that only half the Moon sees all the Earth, and all the Earth sees only half the Moon.

The sixth I hold to be utterly false — that the Moon could receive light from the Earth, which is pitch-dark, opaque, and thoroughly unfit to reflect the Sun's light the way the Moon reflects it to us. As I've said, I hold that the light we see on the rest of the Moon's face, beyond the horns blazing with the Sun's illumination, is the Moon's own proper and natural light — and it would take a great deal to make me believe otherwise.

The seventh, on the mutual eclipses, can also be admitted — though what you insist on calling an eclipse of the Earth is properly and customarily called an eclipse of the Sun. And that is all I need say to you for now in contradiction of the seven resemblances. If you'd like to reply to any of these objections, I'll listen gladly.

Salviati
If I've rightly taken in what you've answered, it seems to me that certain conditions I had made common to the Moon and the Earth still remain in dispute between you and us — and they are these. You take the Moon to be smooth and polished like a mirror, and as such fit to reflect the Sun's light to us, and the Earth, on the contrary, too rough to make any such reflection. You grant that the Moon is solid and hard, and you argue this from her being polished and smooth, not from her being mountainous; and for her mountainous appearance you assign as the cause her being made of parts more and less opaque and transparent. And finally, you hold that secondary light to be the Moon's own, and not a reflection from the Earth — though you don't seem to deny the sea some reflection, its surface being smooth. As for undeceiving you on this error — that the Moon's reflection cannot work as from a mirror — I have little hope, when I see that what's written on exactly this subject in The Assayer and in the Letters on Sunspots of our mutual friend has gained no ground at all in your mind — if, that is, you've read attentively what's written there on the matter.

Simplicio
I've skimmed them, only superficially, in what little idle time my more solid studies leave me. So if, by repeating some of those arguments or bringing up others, you think you can resolve my difficulties, I'll listen more attentively.

Salviati
I'll say what comes to mind at the moment, and it may well be a mixture of my own thoughts and what I once read in those books — from which, I remember well, I came away entirely persuaded, though at first sight the conclusions struck me as great paradoxes. What we're asking, Simplicio, is whether, to make a reflection of light like the one that comes to us from the Moon, it's necessary that the surface it comes from be as smooth and polished as a mirror, or whether a surface that is not smooth and not polished — rough and badly finished — is better suited to it. Now, suppose two reflections reached us, one brighter and one less so, from two surfaces facing us. I ask you: which of the two surfaces do you think would appear brighter to our eyes, and which darker?

Simplicio
I believe, without any doubt, that the one reflecting the light to me more vividly would show itself brighter in appearance, and the other darker.

Salviati
Now be so kind as to take that mirror hanging on the wall there, and let's step out into the courtyard. Come, Sagredo. Hang the mirror there, on that wall where the sun strikes; now let's move away and pull back here into the shade. There you have two surfaces struck by the sun — the wall and the mirror. Tell me now: which looks brighter to you, the wall's surface or the mirror's? You don't answer?

Sagredo
I leave the answering to Simplicio; the difficulty is his. As for me, this little beginning of an experiment has already persuaded me that the Moon must, of necessity, have a very badly polished surface.

Salviati
Tell me, Simplicio: if you had to paint a picture of that wall with the mirror hanging on it, where would you use the darker colors — in painting the wall, or in painting the mirror?

Simplicio
Much darker in painting the mirror.

Salviati
Then if the more powerful reflection of light comes from the surface that appears brighter, the wall will reflect the Sun's rays to us more vividly than the mirror.

Simplicio
Very good, my dear sir — is that the best experiment you have? You've stationed us where the mirror's reverberation doesn't strike. But come with me a little this way. No — come along.

Sagredo
Are you looking for the spot where the mirror throws its reflection?

Simplicio
I am.

Sagredo
Well, there it is — on the opposite wall, exactly as big as the mirror, and hardly less bright than if the Sun were striking there directly.

Simplicio
Come over here, then, and look from this spot at the surface of the mirror, and tell me whether it looks darker than the wall's.

Sagredo
Look at it yourself; I'm not ready to blind myself just yet. I know perfectly well, without looking, that it shows as vivid and bright as the Sun itself, or very little less.

Simplicio
So what do you say now — that the reflection of a mirror is less powerful than that of a wall? I see that on this opposite wall, where the reflection from the other sunlit wall arrives together with the one from the mirror, the mirror's is far brighter; and I see likewise that from here the mirror itself looks much brighter than the wall.

Salviati
You've gotten ahead of me with your sharpness, because this is exactly the observation I needed in order to explain the rest. You see, then, the difference between the two reflections made by the two surfaces of the wall and the mirror, struck in precisely the same way by the Sun's rays. And you see how the reflection coming from the wall spreads out toward all the parts facing it, while the mirror's goes toward one single place, not a bit bigger than the mirror itself. You see likewise how the surface of the wall, looked at from any spot whatever, always shows equally bright, and everywhere much brighter than the mirror's — except for that one small place alone where the mirror's reflection strikes, for from there the mirror appears much brighter than the wall. From these experiences, which we can see and all but touch, it seems to me we can very quickly come to know whether the reflection that reaches us from the Moon comes as from a mirror or as from a wall — that is, from a smooth surface or a rough one.

Sagredo
If I were standing on the Moon herself, I don't believe I could touch the roughness of her surface with my hand more plainly than I grasp it right now by following the argument. The Moon, seen in any position whatever with respect to the Sun and to us, shows us the surface the Sun touches as always equally bright — an effect that matches the wall's to a hair, since the wall, viewed from anywhere, appears equally bright; and it clashes with the mirror, which shows luminous from one spot only and dark from all the others. What's more, the light that reaches me from the wall's reflection is bearable and weak compared with the mirror's, which is ferociously strong, and hardly less punishing to the eyes than the primary, direct light of the Sun. And that's why we can gaze comfortably at the face of the Moon: if she were like a mirror — showing herself, because she's so near, as large as the Sun itself — her blaze would be absolutely unbearable, and it would seem to us that we were staring at almost a second Sun.

Salviati
Please, Sagredo, don't credit my demonstration with more than it's worth. Let me raise a counter-objection against you, and I'm not sure it's an easy knot to untie. You count it a great difference between the Moon and the mirror that she sends her reflection toward all parts equally, as the wall does, while the mirror sends it to one single determinate place; and from this you conclude that the Moon is like the wall, and not like the mirror. But I tell you: that mirror sends its reflection to one place alone because its surface is flat, and since reflected rays must leave at angles equal to those of the incoming rays, rays leaving a flat surface must depart together toward the same place. But the Moon's surface is not flat — it's spherical; and rays falling on such a surface find slopes from which to reflect, at angles equal to their angles of incidence, toward every direction, thanks to the infinity of inclinations that make up a spherical surface. So the Moon can send her reflection everywhere, and isn't forced to send it to one place alone, like that mirror, which is flat.

Simplicio
That is precisely one of the objections I wanted to raise against him.

Sagredo
If that's one, you must have others; so out with them — though as for this first one, I think it's going to turn out to count against you rather than in your favor.

Simplicio
You pronounced it as an obvious thing that the reflection from that wall is as bright and illuminating as the one that comes to us from the Moon, and I count it as nothing by comparison. For "in this business of illumination one must pay attention to, and distinguish, the sphere of activity — the reach of a body's power; and who doubts that celestial bodies have a greater sphere of activity than these elemental, frail, and mortal things of ours? And that wall, when all is said — what is it but a bit of earth, dark and unfit to give light?"

Sagredo
Here too, I believe, you're very much deceived. But let me come to the first objection raised by Salviati. I observe that for an object to appear luminous to us, it isn't enough that the rays of the illuminating body fall upon it; the reflected rays have to come to our eye. That's plain in the example of that mirror: no doubt the Sun's luminous rays are landing on it, and yet it doesn't show itself bright and lit up except when we put our eye in that one particular place where the reflection goes. Now let's consider what would happen if the mirror's surface were spherical. We'd find, without question, that of the reflection made by the whole illuminated surface, only the tiniest part reaches the eye of any one particular observer — because the portion of the whole spherical surface whose inclination bounces the ray to the particular spot where the eye is, is a most minute particle. So only the smallest part of the spherical surface can show itself shining to the eye, and all the rest appears dark. If the Moon, then, were polished like a mirror, only the tiniest part of her would show itself to any one person's eyes as lit by the Sun, even though a whole hemisphere lay exposed to the Sun's rays; all the rest would remain, to the observer's eye, as if unilluminated, and therefore invisible — and finally the Moon herself would be entirely invisible, since that little particle sending the reflection would be lost through its smallness and its great distance. And just as she would remain invisible to the eye, so her illumination would amount to nothing; for it's simply impossible that a luminous body should drive away our darkness with its splendor while we fail to see it.

Salviati
Hold on a moment, please, Sagredo, for I see certain movements in Simplicio's face and bearing that tell me he isn't fully convinced or satisfied by what you've said with such complete clarity and absolute truth. And it's just occurred to me that I can remove his every scruple with another experiment. I've seen, in a room upstairs, a large spherical mirror: let's have it brought down here; and while it's on its way, let Simplicio go back and consider how great the brightness is that the flat mirror's reflection throws on the wall here under the loggia.

Simplicio
I see that it's hardly less bright than if the Sun were striking it directly.

Salviati
So it is. Now tell me: if we take away that little flat mirror and set the big spherical one in the same place, what effect do you think its reflection will have on that same wall?

Simplicio
I believe it will throw a much greater and much broader light onto it.

Salviati
But if the illumination turns out to be nothing, or so small you can barely make it out — what will you say then?

Simplicio
When I've seen the effect, I'll think about my answer.

Salviati
Here's the mirror, and I want it set right next to the other one. But first let's go over there, near the reflection thrown by the flat one, and look closely at its brightness. See how bright it is here where it strikes, and how distinctly you can make out all these little details of the wall.

Simplicio
I've looked and observed it perfectly well. Have the other mirror set beside the first.

Salviati
There it is. It was put there the moment you started studying the little details, and you never noticed — that's how great an increase of light it made on the rest of the wall. Now let the flat mirror be taken away. Look: every reflection is gone, even though the big convex mirror is still there. Take that one away too, and put it back as many times as you please — you'll see no change of light at all across the whole wall. So there you have it, shown to your very senses: the reflection of the Sun made in a spherical convex mirror does not perceptibly light up the places around it. Now — what do you answer to this experiment?
