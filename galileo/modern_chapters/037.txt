The Third Day

(Part 5 of 16)

Simplicio
I believe that if the case arose, the Peripatetics—

Salviati
I'm not asking about the Peripatetics; I'm asking about Aristotle himself. As for them, I know perfectly well what they'd say. They, as Aristotle's most reverent and most humble bondsmen, would deny every experiment and every observation in the world—they'd even refuse to look, so as not to have to admit them—and they'd insist the world stands as Aristotle wrote it, not as nature will have it. For once you strip away the prop of that authority, with what would you have them take the field? So tell me, rather, what you think Aristotle himself would have done.

Simplicio
Honestly, I couldn't decide which of the two drawbacks he'd have judged the lesser.

Salviati
Please don't use that word "drawback" for something that might necessarily have to be the case. It was a drawback to want to put the Earth at the center of the heavenly revolutions. But since you don't know which way he'd have leaned—and since I take him for a man of great intelligence—let's examine which of the two choices is the more reasonable, and reckon that one to be the position Aristotle would have adopted. So, picking up our reasoning from the beginning, and granting, for Aristotle's sake, that the world (of whose size we have no sense-based knowledge beyond the fixed stars) is spherical in shape and moves in a circle, and therefore necessarily has a center, both with respect to its shape and with respect to its motion; and being further sure that inside the starry sphere there are many orbs, one within another, each with its stars, that likewise move in circles—we ask what it is more reasonable to believe and to say: that these contained orbs move around the very center of the world, or else around some other point far removed from it. Now tell me, Simplicio, your opinion on this particular point.

Simplicio
If we could rest on this single assumption alone, and were sure we'd meet nothing else to trouble us, I'd say it's far more reasonable to hold that the container and the parts it contains all move around one common center, rather than around different ones.

Salviati
Now, if it's true that the center of the world is the same as the point around which the orbs of the world's bodies—that is, of the planets—move, then it's absolutely certain that not the Earth but rather the Sun is the thing situated at the center of the world; so that, as far as this first simple and general grasp of things goes, the middle place belongs to the Sun, and the Earth is as far from the center as it is from the Sun itself.

Simplicio
But from what do you argue that it's the Sun, not the Earth, that lies at the center of the planets' revolutions?

Salviati
It's concluded from the plainest observations—observations that therefore compel the conclusion. The most palpable of them, for excluding the Earth from that center and putting the Sun there, is that all the planets are found now nearer and now farther from the Earth, and by differences so great that, for example, Venus at her farthest is six times more remote from us than when she's nearest, and Mars rises to almost eight times as far in one state as in another. Judge, meanwhile, whether Aristotle was off by a little in believing they were always equally distant from us.

Simplicio
And what are the signs that their motions are around the Sun?

Salviati
For the three outer planets—Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn—the argument comes from their always being nearest the Earth when they're at opposition to the Sun, and farthest when they're toward conjunction; and this approaching and receding matters so much that Mars, when near, looks fully 60 times bigger than when it's at its farthest. As for Venus and Mercury, we're certain they revolve around the Sun from the fact that they never stray far from it, and that they're seen now above it and now below—as the change of phases in Venus necessarily proves. As for the Moon, it's true that she can't in any way be separated from the Earth, for reasons that will be brought out more distinctly as we go on.

Sagredo
I expect I'm about to hear things even more marvelous, following from this annual motion of the Earth, than the ones that followed from the daily rotation.

Salviati
You're not deceived in the least. As for what the daily rotation does to the heavenly bodies, it was nothing—and could be nothing—but to make the universe appear to go rushing past us the other way; but this annual motion, mingling with the particular motions of all the planets, produces a great many strange effects, which have so far made all the greatest men in the world lose their fencing-guard. But coming back to those first general notions, I repeat that the center of the heavenly revolutions of the five planets—Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Venus, and Mercury—is the Sun; and it will be the center of the Earth's motion too, if we manage to set her in the heavens. As for the Moon, she has a circular motion around the Earth, from which (as I've already said) she can in no way be separated; but that doesn't stop her from going around the Sun along with the Earth, by the annual motion.

Simplicio
I still don't quite have a grip on this arrangement; perhaps making a little drawing of it would help me understand it better, and we could discuss it more easily.

Salviati
So be it—indeed, for your greater satisfaction and wonder both, I want you to draw it yourself, and to see how, while thinking you don't understand it, you understand it perfectly; just by answering my questions you'll lay it out exactly. So take a sheet of paper and a compass: let this blank page be the immense expanse of the universe, in which you're to distribute and arrange its parts as reason dictates to you. And first, since without any teaching from me you hold it firm that the Earth is placed in this universe, mark a point wherever you like, around which you take her to be positioned, and label it with some letter.

Simplicio
Let this point, marked A, be the place of the terrestrial globe.

Salviati
Good. Second, I know you know perfectly well that the Earth is not inside the body of the Sun, nor even next to it, but a certain distance away; so assign the Sun whatever other place you please, as far from the Earth as you like, and label that too.

Simplicio
There, done: let the place of the Sun's body be this one, marked O.

Salviati
With these two settled, I want us to think about fitting in the body of Venus in such a way that her position and motion can satisfy what the observed appearances show us about them; so call to mind what, whether from our earlier discussions or from your own observations, you've gathered happens with that star; and then assign her the position you judge suits her.

Simplicio
Granting that the appearances you've described are true—the ones I've also read in the little book of conclusions—namely that this star never departs from the Sun beyond a certain fixed interval of forty-odd degrees, so that she never reaches opposition to the Sun, nor even quadrature, nor so much as the sextile aspect; and further, that at one time she appears nearly forty times bigger than at another—that is, largest when, moving in retrograde, she goes toward her evening conjunction with the Sun, and smallest when, in direct motion, she goes toward her morning conjunction; and moreover, it being true that when she appears largest she shows herself crescent-shaped, and when she appears smallest she's seen perfectly round—granting, I say, that these appearances are true, I don't see how one can avoid asserting that this star circles around the Sun. For that circle can in no way be said to embrace the Earth and hold it inside itself, nor to lie below the Sun—that is, between it and the Earth—nor above the Sun either. The circle can't embrace the Earth, because then Venus would sometimes come to opposition with the Sun; it can't lie below, because around both of her conjunctions with the Sun Venus would show herself sickle-shaped; and it can't lie above, because she'd always show herself round and never crescent. And so, for her lodging, I'll mark the circle CH around the Sun, without its embracing the Earth.

Salviati
Venus settled, you'd do well to think about Mercury, which, as you know, always keeps around the Sun and strays much less far from it than Venus does; so consider what place it's fitting to assign him.

Simplicio
No doubt that, since it imitates Venus, the most fitting quarters for it will be a smaller circle inside this one of Venus, and likewise around the Sun—the liveliness of its brilliance over that of Venus and the other planets being a quite conclusive argument and sign, above all, of its nearness to the Sun. So on that basis we can mark its circle, labeling it with the letters B G.

Salviati
And where shall we put Mars?

Simplicio
Mars, because it comes to opposition with the Sun, must with its circle embrace the Earth; but I see that it necessarily must embrace the Sun as well. For, coming to conjunction with the Sun, if it didn't pass above it but were below it, it would appear crescent, as Venus and the Moon do; yet it always shows itself round. So it must enclose within its circle the Sun no less than the Earth. And since I recall you saying that when it's at opposition to the Sun it shows itself 60 times bigger than when it's toward conjunction, it seems to me that these appearances will be very well satisfied by a circle around the center of the Sun that embraces the Earth—which I now draw and mark D I: where Mars at point D is nearest the Earth and is opposite the Sun; but when it's at point I, it's in conjunction with the Sun and farthest from the Earth. And since the same appearances are observed in Jupiter and Saturn—though with far less variation in Jupiter than in Mars, and less still in Saturn than in Jupiter—it seems to me we'll very neatly satisfy these two planets too with two circles, likewise around the Sun: this first one for Jupiter, marking it E L, and another outer one for Saturn, labeled F M.

Salviati
You've acquitted yourself splendidly so far. And since (as you see) the approaching and receding of the three outer planets is measured by twice the distance between the Earth and the Sun, this makes a greater variation in Mars than in Jupiter, because Mars's circle D I is smaller than Jupiter's circle E L; and likewise, because this E L is smaller than Saturn's circle F M, the same variation is smaller still in Saturn than in Jupiter—and that answers exactly to the appearances. It remains now for you to think about assigning the Moon her place.

Simplicio
Following the same method, which strikes me as thoroughly conclusive: since we see that the Moon comes to conjunction and opposition with the Sun, we must say her circle embraces the Earth; but it need not embrace the Sun, because if she were toward conjunction she wouldn't show herself sickle-shaped, but always round and full of light—besides which she could never give us, as she often does, an eclipse of the Sun by interposing herself between it and us. So we must assign her a circle around the Earth, which would be this one, N P, such that when she's set at P she appears to us from the Earth, A, joined with the Sun—so that she can sometimes eclipse it—and when placed at N she's seen opposite the Sun, and in that state can fall into the Earth's shadow and be darkened.

Salviati
Now what shall we do, Simplicio, with the fixed stars? Do we want to set them scattered through the immense depths of the universe, at various distances from any given point, or else placed on a surface spread out spherically around a center of its own, so that each of them is equally distant from that same center?

Simplicio
I'd rather take a middle road, and assign them an orb drawn around a fixed center and enclosed between two spherical surfaces—one very high and concave, the other lower and convex—between which I'd set the innumerable multitude of the stars, but at various heights; and this could be called the sphere of the universe, containing within itself the orbs of the planets we've already drawn.

Salviati
So then, Simplicio, we've now arranged the bodies of the world exactly according to Copernicus's distribution—and it's been done by your own hand. What's more, you've assigned proper motions to all of them except the Sun, the Earth, and the starry sphere; you've given Mercury and Venus a circular motion around the Sun without embracing the Earth; around the same Sun you make the three outer planets—Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn—move, taking the Earth inside their circles; and the Moon can't move any other way than around the Earth, without embracing the Sun. And in all these motions you agree, once again, with Copernicus himself. What remains to be decided, among the Sun, the Earth, and the starry sphere, are three things: namely rest, which appears to belong to the Earth; the annual motion under the zodiac, which appears to belong to the Sun; and the daily rotation, which appears to belong to the starry sphere, sharing it out to all the rest of the universe except the Earth. And since it's true that all the orbs of the planets—I mean Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn—move around the Sun as their center, it seems all the more reasonable that rest belongs to the Sun than to the Earth, inasmuch as, for moving spheres, it's more reasonable that the center should stand still than any other place remote from that center. To the Earth, then, which is left set in the midst of moving parts—I mean between Venus and Mars, one of which makes its revolution in nine months and the other in two years—we can very neatly attribute the motion of a year, leaving rest to the Sun. And once that's so, it follows by necessary consequence that the daily rotation belongs to the Earth as well: for if, with the Sun standing still, the Earth did not turn on itself but only had the annual motion around the Sun, our year would be nothing but a single day and a single night—that is, six months of day and six months of night, as was said before. See, then, how neatly the headlong motion of 24 hours is lifted away from the universe, and how the fixed stars, which are so many Suns, enjoy—like our own Sun—a perpetual rest. See, moreover, how much ease there is in this first sketch for giving the reasons behind such great appearances in the heavenly bodies.

Sagredo
I see it very well. But just as you gather from this simplicity a great probability for the truth of such a system, someone else might, on the contrary, draw the opposite conclusions—doubting, not without reason, how it is that a construction so ancient, held by the Pythagoreans and fitted so well to the appearances, should nonetheless in the course of thousands of years have had so few followers, and should have been rejected by Aristotle himself, and even after Copernicus should go on meeting the same fate.

Salviati
If you, Sagredo, had ever happened—as I have, many and many a time—to hear what sort of nonsense is enough to make the common crowd stubborn and unpersuadable, unwilling even to lend an ear, let alone their assent, to these new ideas, I think your wonder at finding so few followers of this opinion would shrink considerably. But little esteem, in my view, should be given to minds for whom, to confirm them and hold them fixed in the immobility of the Earth, the most conclusive demonstration is to observe that they won't be dining in Constantinople this morning nor supping in Japan this evening, and that they're certain the Earth, being so very heavy, can't climb up above the Sun and then come tumbling headlong back down. Of such people, whose number is infinite, there's no need to take account, nor to record their silly notions, nor to try to win over men whose definition includes only the genus and lacks the difference, just to have them as companions in opinions of the utmost subtlety and delicacy. Besides, what gain would you ever expect to make, with all the demonstrations in the world, upon minds so dull that they can't by themselves recognize their own most extreme follies? But my wonder, Sagredo, is very different from yours: you wonder that so few are followers of the Pythagoreans' opinion; I, for my part, am astonished that anyone has ever, up to now, been found who embraced and followed it. Nor can I sufficiently admire the towering genius of those who received it and held it true, and who, by the sheer liveliness of their intellect, did such violence to their own senses that they were able to prefer what reason dictated to them over what sense experience showed them, plainly and to the contrary. That the arguments against the Earth's daily whirling, which you've already examined, have a great show of truth, we've seen already; and that the Ptolemaics, the Aristotelians, and all their followers took them as thoroughly conclusive is itself a very strong sign of their force. But those experiences that openly contradict the annual motion carry so much greater an apparent repugnance that—I say it again—I can find no limit to my admiration at how reason could, in Aristarchus and in Copernicus, do such violence to sense as to make herself, against sense, the mistress of their belief.

Sagredo
So we're going to have other vigorous objections against this annual motion as well?

Salviati
We are; and ones so evident and so sensory that, if a higher and more excellent sense than the common and natural ones hadn't kept company with reason, I greatly suspect that I too would have been far more reluctant to accept the Copernican system than I've been ever since a brighter lamp than the usual one gave me light.

Sagredo
Well then, Salviati, let's come, as they say, to grips—because every word spent on anything else seems to me thrown away.

Salviati
Here I am, at your service. I've already drawn you the form of the Copernican system. Against its truth, the first and fiercest assault is launched by Mars itself. For if it were true that Mars varied its distances from the Earth so much that between its least and greatest remove there were a difference equal to twice the distance from the Earth to the Sun, then it would necessarily follow that when it's nearest to us its disk should show itself more than 60 times bigger than it shows when it's farthest away. Yet no such difference in apparent size is to be seen; on the contrary, at opposition to the Sun, when it's near the Earth, it doesn't show itself even 4 or 5 times bigger than when, toward conjunction, it's hidden away beneath the Sun's rays. Venus gives us another and greater difficulty. For if, circling around the Sun as Copernicus asserts, it were now above the Sun and now below—receding from and approaching us by as much as the diameter of the circle it describes—then when it was below the Sun and nearest to us, its disk ought to show itself a little less than 40 times bigger than when it's above the Sun, near its other conjunction; yet the difference is almost imperceptible. Add another difficulty: since the body of Venus is dark in itself, and shines only, like the Moon, by the Sun's illumination—as seems reasonable—then when she's below the Sun she ought to show herself sickle-shaped, as the Moon does when she too is near the Sun; an occurrence that doesn't appear in her. On account of which, Copernicus declared that either she was luminous by herself, or her matter was such that it could soak up the Sun's light and transmit it through her whole depth, so that she could always show herself shining; and in this way Copernicus excused Venus's not changing shape. But of her little-varied size he said nothing at all, and of Mars far less than the case required—I believe because he couldn't, to his own satisfaction, save an appearance so repugnant to his position; and yet, persuaded by so many other correspondences, he held to it and took it for true. Beyond these things, making all the planets, together with the Earth, move around the Sun as the center of their revolutions, while the Moon alone disturbs that order and has its own proper motion around the Earth, and while it, the Earth, and the whole elemental sphere all together move in a year around the Sun—this seems to alter the order in such a way as to render it unlikely and false. These are the difficulties that make me marvel that Aristarchus and Copernicus—who can't possibly have failed to notice them, and who couldn't then resolve them—nevertheless, on the strength of other marvelous correspondences, trusted so much in what reason dictated to them that they confidently affirmed the structure of the universe could have no other form than the one they'd drawn. There are, besides, other very grave and very beautiful difficulties, not so easily resolved by middling minds, but penetrated and set forth by Copernicus, which we'll put off until later, after we've answered other objections raised by others against this position. Now, coming to the explanations and replies to the three grave objections brought forward, I say that the first two not only don't contradict the Copernican system but strongly and absolutely favor it; for both Mars and Venus do show themselves unequal to themselves, according to the proportions assigned, and Venus below the Sun shows herself sickle-shaped, and goes on changing her phases in exactly the same way the Moon does.
