The Second Day

(Part 6 of 20)

Simplicio
The argument really is quite plausible on the surface, but at its core it's troubled by a snag that's not so easy to get past. Through the whole course of it you've made an assumption that the Peripatetic school will not grant you lightly, since it flatly contradicts Aristotle: namely, taking it as obvious and well established that the projectile, once separated from the thrower, continues its motion by a force impressed on it by that same thrower. Now this impressed force is as odious to Peripatetic philosophy as the passage of any accident from one subject into another; in that philosophy the position is — as I believe you know — that the projectile is carried by the medium, which in our case is the air. So if that stone released from the top of the mast were to follow the ship's motion, the effect would have to be attributed to the air, not to any force impressed on it; but you assume the air does not follow the ship's motion, and is calm. What's more, the man who lets it fall is not supposed to hurl it or give it impetus with his arm — he simply opens his hand and releases it. And so the stone can follow the ship's motion neither by a force impressed on it by the thrower nor by any courtesy of the air; and therefore it will be left behind.

Salviati
What I seem to gather from your speech, then, is that since the stone isn't driven out by the man's arm, it doesn't count as a projection at all.

Simplicio
Properly speaking, it cannot be called projectile motion.

Salviati
Then what Aristotle says about the motion, the moving body, and the mover of projectiles has nothing to do with our question; and if it has nothing to do with it, why do you bring it up?

Simplicio
I bring it up for the sake of that impressed force you named and introduced — which, since it doesn't exist in the world, can accomplish nothing, for non entium nullae sunt operationes — what doesn't exist can't act. And therefore the moving cause not only of projectile motion, but of every other motion that isn't natural, must be attributed to the medium, which hasn't been given its due consideration; and so everything said up to now stands ineffective.

Salviati
Fine — all in good time. But tell me: since your objection rests entirely on the nonexistence of the impressed force, if I demonstrate to you that the medium has nothing to do with continuing the motion of projectiles once they've separated from the thrower, will you let the impressed force stand — or will you mount some fresh assault to destroy it?

Simplicio
Once the action of the medium is removed, I don't see what one could fall back on other than the faculty impressed by the mover.

Salviati
Then it will be well, to cut off as far as possible any endless back-and-forth of objections, for you to explain as distinctly as you can just what the medium's operation is in keeping the projectile in motion.

Simplicio
The thrower has the stone in his hand; he moves his arm with speed and force, and with that motion he moves not just the stone but the surrounding air as well; so the stone, at the moment the hand abandons it, finds itself in air that is already moving with impetus, and it is carried along by that air. If the air weren't doing the work, the stone would drop from the hand to the thrower's feet.

Salviati
And you've been so credulous as to let yourself be talked into these absurdities, when you had your own senses right there to refute them and to learn the truth? Tell me, then: that great stone, and that artillery ball, which merely resting on a table stayed immovable against any wind however fierce, as you affirmed a little while ago — if it had been a ball of cork, or the same bulk of cotton wool, do you think the wind would have shifted it?

Simplicio
On the contrary, I know for certain the wind would have carried it clean away — and the faster, the lighter the material. That's exactly why we see the clouds borne along at a speed equal to that of the very wind that drives them.

Salviati
And what is wind?

Simplicio
Wind is defined as nothing other than moving air.

Salviati
So moving air carries the lightest materials much faster and much farther than the heaviest ones?

Simplicio
Certainly.

Salviati
But if you had to throw with your arm first a stone and then a wisp of cotton, which would move faster and farther?

Simplicio
The stone, by a wide margin — the cotton would just drop at my feet.

Salviati
Well then: if what moves the projectile, after the hand releases it, is nothing but the air set moving by the arm, and moving air pushes light materials more easily than heavy ones, how is it that the cotton projectile doesn't fly farther and faster than the stone one? Something must remain in the stone besides the motion of the air. And furthermore: suppose two strings of equal length hung from that beam, with a lead ball tied to the end of one and a cotton ball to the end of the other; draw both aside equally far from the vertical, and then set them free. No doubt both will swing toward the vertical and, driven by their own impetus, will pass beyond it a certain distance and then return. But which of these two pendulums do you think will keep moving longer before it comes to rest hanging plumb?

Simplicio
The lead ball will swing back and forth a thousand times; the cotton one, two or three at most.

Salviati
So that impetus and that mobility, whatever their cause may be, are conserved longer in heavy materials than in light ones. Now I come to another point, and I ask you: why doesn't the air carry off that citron sitting on the table right now?

Simplicio
Because the air itself isn't moving.

Salviati
Then the thrower must confer the motion on the air, and the air must then use it to move the projectile. But if such a force cannot be impressed — since an accident cannot be made to pass from one subject into another — how does it pass from the arm into the air? Isn't the air a subject distinct from the arm?

Simplicio
The answer given is that the air, being neither heavy nor light in its own region, is disposed to receive every impulse with the greatest ease, and to conserve it as well.

Salviati
But the pendulums have just now shown us that the less heaviness a moving body partakes of, the less fit it is to conserve motion. How then can it be that the air, which in air has no heaviness at all, is the one thing that conserves the motion it has received? I believe — and I know that by now you believe it too — that the arm is no sooner stopped than the air around it stops as well. Let's go into a room and stir the air as hard as we can with a towel; then, the cloth held still, let a small lighted candle be brought into the room, or let a leaf of gold foil be set drifting loose in it: from the quiet wandering of either one you'll see that the air has been brought back to calm immediately. I could bring you a thousand experiments, but if one of these weren't enough, the case had better be given up as altogether hopeless.

Sagredo
When an arrow is shot against the wind, what an incredible thing it is that the little thread of air pushed by the bowstring should travel along with the arrow in defiance of the gale! But there's another particular point I'd like to learn from Aristotle, and I beg Simplicio to favor me with an answer. Suppose two arrows were shot from the same bow, one point-first in the usual way and the other sideways — that is, laid lengthwise along the string and shot in that flat position: I'd like to know which of them would go farther. Do me the kindness of an answer, even though the question may strike you as more ridiculous than not; and forgive me, because I — being, as you can see, rather on the thick side — can't reach any higher with my speculating.

Simplicio
I've never seen arrows shot sideways; still, I believe that shot crosswise one wouldn't go even a twentieth of the distance it goes point-first.

Sagredo
And since I've believed the very same thing, that's what gave me occasion to raise a doubt between Aristotle's pronouncement and experience. Because as far as experience goes, if I set two arrows on that table when a strong wind is blowing — one laid along the line of the wind, the other crosswise to it — the wind will quickly carry off the crosswise one and leave the other lying there. And the same thing, it seems, ought to happen with the two shot from the bow, if Aristotle's doctrine were true: for the crosswise arrow would be driven by a great quantity of air set moving by the string — as much air, that is, as the arrow's whole length — whereas the other arrow receives its push from no more air than the tiny little circle of its thickness. And I can't imagine the cause of such a difference, and I'd dearly like to know it.

Simplicio
The cause seems obvious enough to me: it's because the arrow shot point-first has only a small quantity of air to penetrate, while the other has to cleave as much air as its whole length.

Sagredo
So arrows, when shot, have to penetrate the air? But if the air goes along with them — indeed, if the air is what carries them — what penetration can there be? Don't you see that on this account the arrow would have to be moving faster than the air? And who confers this greater speed on the arrow? Will you say the air gives it a speed greater than its own? Understand, then, Simplicio, that the business works exactly backwards from what Aristotle says: it is as false that the medium confers motion on the projectile as it is true that the medium is the one thing that hinders it. And once you've grasped that, you'll understand without any difficulty that when the air really is moving, it carries the arrow along much better crosswise than straight on, because there's a great deal of air pushing it in that posture and very little in the other; but when the shots come from the bow, since the air is standing still, the crosswise arrow, striking against a great deal of air, is greatly impeded, while the point-first one overcomes with the greatest ease the obstacle of the tiny quantity of air that opposes it.

Salviati
How many propositions I've noted in Aristotle — I mean always in natural philosophy — that are not merely false, but false in such a way that their diametrical opposite is true, as happens with this one! But to keep to our purpose: I believe Simplicio is now persuaded that from seeing the stone always fall in the same place, nothing can be inferred about the ship's motion or its stillness. And if everything said so far weren't enough for him, there is the experiment ready at hand, which can settle the matter for him completely: in that experiment, the most he could ever see would be the falling body left behind if it were made of very light material and the air did not follow the ship's motion; but where the air moves with equal speed, no imaginable difference would be found in this or in any other experiment whatsoever, as I'm shortly going to tell you. Now if, in this case, no difference at all shows itself, what could anyone claim to see in the stone falling from the top of the tower — where the circular motion is not adventitious and accidental to the stone but natural and eternal, and where the air follows the tower's motion exactly, and the tower follows that of the terrestrial globe? Have you anything further to say, Simplicio, on this particular point?

Simplicio
Nothing further — except that so far I don't see the Earth's mobility proved.

Salviati
Nor have I claimed to prove it. My only claim was to show that nothing can be extracted from the experiment our adversaries put forward as an argument for the Earth's being fixed — just as I believe I'll show of the other arguments.

Sagredo
Please, Salviati, before you pass on to anything else, allow me to bring up a certain difficulty that's been turning round in my imagination while you were so patiently grinding this ship experiment down fine for Simplicio.

Salviati
We're here to discuss, and it's good for each of us to raise the difficulties that occur to him — that's the road to knowledge of the truth. So speak.

Sagredo
If it's true that the impetus with which the ship moves remains indelibly impressed in the stone after it has separated from the mast, and if it's furthermore true that this motion brings no hindrance or slowing to the straight downward motion natural to the stone, then a marvelous effect must follow in nature. Let the ship stand still, and let the fall of a stone from the top of the mast take two beats of the pulse. Then let the ship be under way, and let the same stone be dropped from the same place: by what we've said, it will still take the time of two pulse-beats to arrive at the bottom, and in that time the ship will have run, say, twenty braccia — so that the stone's true motion will have been a slanting line, considerably longer than the first straight and vertical one, which is simply the length of the mast; and yet the ball will have traversed it in the same time. Suppose next that the ship's motion is speeded up considerably more, so that the falling stone must pass along a slanting line even longer than the last; and in short, however much you increase the ship's speed, the falling stone will trace out slanting paths longer and longer still, and yet will traverse every one of them in those same two pulse-beats. On the same pattern: if a culverin — a long cannon — were mounted level at the top of a tower and fired point-blank, that is, parallel to the horizon, then whatever charge, small or large, were given the piece, so that the ball fell to earth now a thousand braccia away, now four thousand, now six thousand, now ten thousand, and so on, all these shots would be completed in times equal to one another, and each equal to the time the ball would take to go from the muzzle of the piece to the ground if it were simply allowed to fall straight down, with no other impulse. Now it seems a marvelous thing that in the same short time of a plumb fall to the ground from a height of, say, a hundred braccia, the same ball, driven out by the powder, can pass through now four hundred, now a thousand, now four thousand, now ten thousand braccia — so that in all point-blank shots the ball stays in the air for equal times.

Salviati
The observation is beautiful for its novelty, and if the effect is real, it is marvelous — and of its truth I have no doubt. Setting aside the accidental impediment of the air, I hold it for certain that if, at the instant the ball left the gun, another ball were let fall straight down from the same height, both would reach the ground at the same instant, even though one had traveled ten thousand braccia of distance and the other only a hundred — assuming, that is, that the surface of the Earth were level; and to be safe, the shot could be fired over some lake. The impediment that might come from the air would consist in slowing down the extremely rapid motion of the shot. Now, if you please, let's come to the solutions of the other arguments, since Simplicio is — as far as I can judge — well satisfied of the nullity of this first one, drawn from bodies falling from high to low.

Simplicio
I don't feel all my scruples removed; but perhaps the fault is mine, for not being as quick and nimble of understanding as Sagredo. And it seems to me that if this motion the stone shared in while it was on the ship's mast were, as you say, conserved in it indelibly even after it has separated from the ship, then it should likewise follow that when someone riding a galloping horse lets a ball drop from his hand, that ball, having landed, would continue its motion and follow the horse's course without falling behind. And I don't believe that effect is ever seen — except when the rider throws the ball forcefully in the direction of his run; short of that, I believe it will stay on the ground right where it strikes.

Salviati
I believe you're very much mistaken, and I'm sure experience will show you the contrary: the ball, once it has reached the ground, will run along with the horse, and won't fall behind it except insofar as the roughness and unevenness of the road hinders it. And the reason seems clear enough to me. For if you, standing still, threw that same ball along the ground, wouldn't it continue its motion even after leaving your hand — and for a longer stretch, the smoother the surface, so that on ice, for instance, it would travel a very long way?

Simplicio
No doubt of that, so long as I give it impetus with my arm; but in the other case, we're assuming the man on the horse merely lets it drop.

Salviati
That's exactly what I want to happen. When you throw it with your arm, what remains with the ball, once it has left your hand, except the motion received from your arm — which, conserved in it, keeps carrying it forward? And what does it matter whether that impetus is conferred on the ball by your arm or by the horse? While you're on horseback, doesn't your hand — and consequently the ball — travel as fast as the horse itself? Certainly it does. So at the mere opening of your hand, the ball departs with the motion it has already received — not from your arm by any private motion of your own, but from the motion that depends on the horse itself, which is communicated to you, to your arm, to your hand, and finally to the ball. And I'll tell you more: if the rider, at a run, throws the ball with his arm in the direction opposite to his course, then once it reaches the ground it will sometimes follow the horse's course all the same, despite having been hurled the other way; and sometimes it will lie still on the ground; and it will move against the direction of the run only when the motion received from the arm exceeds the speed of the gallop. And it's nonsense, what some people say — that a horseman can launch a javelin through the air in the direction of his run, then follow it with the horse, catch up to it, and finally take hold of it again. Nonsense, I say, because to have the projectile come back into your hand you must throw it straight up, exactly as if you were standing still; for however fast the run may be, provided it's uniform and the projectile isn't something extremely light, it will always fall back into the thrower's hand, no matter how high it's thrown.

Sagredo
From this doctrine I come to see some quite curious problems in the matter of these projectiles — the first of which is bound to strike Simplicio as very strange. The problem is this: I say it's possible that a ball simply dropped by someone running fast, in whatever manner, once it has reached the ground, will not only follow his course but get well out ahead of him. And that problem is connected to this one: that a body launched by a thrower over the horizontal plane can acquire new speed, considerably greater than what the thrower gave it. I've often watched this effect with astonishment while standing to see people playing at ruzzole — hurling those wooden discs. Once out of the hand, you see the discs going through the air at a certain speed, which then increases considerably when they reach the ground; and if in rolling they strike some obstacle that bounces them up into the air, you see them move through the air quite slowly, and on falling back to earth they move again with greater speed. But what's stranger still, I've also observed not only that they always go faster along the ground than through the air, but that of two stretches both covered along the ground, the motion in the second stretch is sometimes faster than in the first. Now what would Simplicio say to that?

Simplicio
I'd say, first of all, that I've never made any such observation; second, I'd say I don't believe it; and then I'd say, in the third place, that if you convinced me of it and taught it to me demonstratively, you'd be a great demon.

Sagredo
One of Socrates' demons, though — not one of Hell's. But there you go again with this "teaching." I tell you that when a man doesn't know a truth on his own, it's impossible for anyone else to make him know it. I can certainly teach you things that are neither true nor false; but the true ones — that is, the necessary ones, the ones that cannot possibly be otherwise — every mind of ordinary discourse either knows on its own, or can never know at all. And I know Salviati believes the same. And so I tell you that the reasons behind these present problems are known to you — you just may not have noticed them.
