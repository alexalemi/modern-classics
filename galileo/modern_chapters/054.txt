The Fourth Day

(Part 6 of 6)

Sagredo
I'm fully satisfied on that point. What remains is for you to explain how those additions and subtractions arising from the daily whirl come to be now greater and now smaller — the variation on which, as you hinted, the yearly cycle of the increases and decreases of the ebb and flow depends.

Salviati
I'll do everything I can to make myself understood; but the difficulty of the thing itself, and the great mental abstraction it takes to grasp it, daunt me. The inequality of the additions and subtractions that the daily whirl makes upon the annual motion depends on the tilt of the axis of the daily rotation with respect to the plane of the great orb — or, if you like, of the ecliptic. Because of this tilt, the equinoctial cuts the ecliptic, remaining tilted and oblique upon it by the same amount as the axis is tilted. The size of the additions comes to equal the whole diameter of that equinoctial when the center of the Earth is at the solstitial points; but away from those points it amounts to less and less, as that center approaches the equinoctial points, where these additions are smaller than anywhere else. That is the whole of it — but wrapped in the obscurity you can see.

Sagredo
In the obscurity I can't see, rather — because so far I understand nothing of it.

Salviati
I foretold as much. Still, let's try whether by drawing a bit of a figure we can gain some light — though it would be far better to represent it with solid bodies than with mere drawings. We'll help ourselves out with perspective and foreshortening. So let us mark, as before, the circumference of the great orb, in which take the point A to be one of the solstitial points, and the diameter A P to be the common section of the solstitial colure and the plane of the great orb — or, if you like, of the ecliptic — and at that point A let the center of the terrestrial globe be located, whose axis CAB, tilted upon the plane of the great orb, falls in the plane of the said colure, which passes through both axes, that of the equinoctial and that of the ecliptic. And for less confusion we'll mark only the equinoctial circle, labeling it with these letters DGEF, whose common section with the plane of the great orb is the line DE, so that the half of this equinoctial DFE will remain tilted below the plane of the great orb, and the other half DGE raised above it. Now understand the revolution of this equinoctial to run according to the sequence of the points D, G, E, F, and the motion of the center to go from A toward E. And because, with the center of the Earth at A, the axis CB (which stands upright to the diameter of the equinoctial DE) falls, as we said, in the solstitial colure — whose common section with the great orb is the diameter PA — that line PA will be perpendicular to DE, the colure being upright to the great orb, and therefore DE will be the tangent to the great orb at the point A. So that in this position the motion of the center along the arc AE, which is one degree a day, differs very little — indeed, it's as if it were made along the tangent DAE. And because, through the daily whirl, the point D carried through G to E adds to the motion of the center — moved almost along the same line DE — as much as the whole diameter DE, and on the other hand subtracts just as much in moving through the other half-circle EFD, the additions and subtractions in this place, that is at the time of the solstice, will be measured by the whole diameter DE.

Let us now go on to see whether, at the times of the equinoxes, they're of the same size. Transferring the center of the Earth to the point I, a quarter away from the point A, understand the same equinoctial GEFD, its common section with the great orb DE, the axis with the same tilt CB. But the tangent to the great orb at the point I will no longer be DE, but another that will cut it at right angles — let this be marked HIL — along which the motion of the center I will be directed, proceeding along the circumference of the great orb. Now in this position the additions and subtractions are no longer measured on the diameter DE, as before, because, since that diameter doesn't extend along the line of the annual motion HL but rather cuts it at right angles, those termini D, E neither add nor take away anything. Instead the additions and subtractions have to be taken from the diameter that falls in the plane upright to the plane of the great orb and that cuts it along the line HL — which diameter will now be this one, GF. And the additive motion, so to speak, will be the one made from the point G through the half-circle GEF, and the subtractive the remainder, made through the other half-circle FDG. Now this diameter, since it isn't in the same line HL of the annual motion but rather cuts it, as you see, at the point I — the terminus G remaining raised above and F sunk below the plane of the great orb — does not determine the additions and subtractions along its whole length. Rather, their amount must be taken from the part of the line HL intercepted between the perpendiculars dropped upon it from the termini G, F — namely these two, GS, FV — so that the measure of the additions is the line SV, smaller than GF, or than DE, which was the measure of the additions at the solstice A. Then, as the center of the Earth is set at other points of the quadrant AI, by drawing the tangents at those points and the perpendiculars falling upon them from the termini of the equinoctial's diameters — marked out by the planes upright, through those tangents, to the plane of the great orb — the parts of those tangents (which will always be smaller toward the equinoxes and greater toward the solstices) will give us the amounts of the additions and subtractions. As for how much the smallest additions differ from the greatest, that's easy to know, because between them is the same difference as between the whole axis or diameter of the sphere and the part of it that lies between the polar circles — which is smaller than the whole diameter by about a twelfth part — meaning, of course, the additions and subtractions made on the equinoctial; on the other parallels they're smaller, in proportion as their diameters shrink.

This is as much as I can tell you on this subject, and as much, perhaps, as can fall under any knowledge of ours — which, as you well know, can be had only of those conclusions that are firm and constant, such as the three cycles of the ebb and flow considered in general, depending as they do on causes invariable, single, and eternal. But because with these primary and universal causes there then mix in the secondary and particular ones, capable of producing many variations — and these secondary causes are partly unobservable and inconstant, as, for example, the shifting of the winds, and partly, though fixed and determinate, still not observed because of their multitude, such as the lengths of the gulfs, their various slopes toward this side or that, the many and so different depths of the waters — who could, except perhaps after very long observations and thoroughly reliable reports, form such ready histories of them as could serve as secure hypotheses and suppositions to anyone who wished, by combining them, to give adequate reasons for all the appearances — I'll even say the anomalies and particular irregularities — that may be discerned in the movements of the waters? I'll content myself with having pointed out that accidental causes do exist in nature, and are powerful enough to produce many variations; the minute observations I'll leave to be made by those who frequent the various seas. And only, to close this discussion of ours, I'll set down for your consideration how the precise times of the ebb and flow are altered not only by the lengths of the gulfs and the varying depths, but also — I think — how a notable alteration may come from the joining of different stretches of sea, differing in size and in position, or let's say slope. Just such a difference falls out right here in the Adriatic gulf, far smaller than the rest of the Mediterranean and set at so different a slope that, whereas the latter has its boundary closing it off on the eastern side — the shores of Syria — this one is shut in at its most western part. And because at the far ends the ebbs and flows are much greater — indeed, only there are the risings and fallings very great — it may very likely happen that the times of the floods at Venice fall during the ebbs of the other sea, which, being much larger and more directly stretched from west to east, comes in a certain way to have dominion over the Adriatic. And so it wouldn't be surprising if the effects depending on the primary causes did not verify themselves at the proper times, answering to the cycles, in the Adriatic, but rather in the rest of the Mediterranean. But these particulars call for long observations, which I have neither made up to now nor am likely to be able to make in the future.

Sagredo
It seems to me you've done a great deal in opening for us the first entrance to so lofty a speculation. Even if you'd brought us nothing beyond that first general proposition — which to me seems to admit no reply, where it's so conclusively declared that, with the vessels containing the sea-waters standing still, it would be impossible, by the common course of nature, for those movements to take place in them that we see taking place; and that, on the contrary, granting the motions attributed for other reasons by Copernicus to the terrestrial globe, similar variations must necessarily follow in the seas — even if, I say, there were nothing else, this alone seems to me to surpass by so great an interval the empty notions brought forward by so many others, that merely to think back on those turns my stomach. And I marvel greatly that among men of sublime intellect — and there've been not a few — it never crossed anyone's mind, the incompatibility there is between the back-and-forth motion of the contained water and the immobility of the containing vessel, a contradiction that now seems to me so obvious.

Salviati
More to be marveled at is that, though it did occur to some to refer the cause of the ebb and flow to the motion of the Earth — showing in that a sharper insight than the common run — when they came to pin the business down they grasped nothing, for not having noticed that a single uniform motion isn't enough — such as, for instance, the simple daily motion of the terrestrial globe — but that an uneven motion is required, now accelerated and now retarded. Because when the motion of the vessels is uniform, the contained waters get used to it and never make any change at all. To say too (as is reported of a certain ancient mathematician) that the motion of the Earth, meeting the motion of the lunar orb, causes the ebb and flow by that clash, is utterly empty — not only because it isn't explained, nor can one see how it should follow, but the manifest falsity of it is plain, seeing that the turning of the Earth isn't contrary to the motion of the Moon but goes the same way. So that all that's been said and imagined by the others up to now remains, in my opinion, entirely invalid. But of all the great men who have philosophized on this marvelous effect of nature, I marvel at Kepler more than at any of them: a man of free and acute intellect, who had in his hands the motions attributed to the Earth, and yet lent his ear and his assent to a dominion of the Moon over the water, and to occult properties, and suchlike childishness.

Sagredo
I'm of the opinion that what's happening to me at present has happened to these more speculative men too — namely, not being able to grasp the tangle of the three cycles, annual, monthly, and daily, and how their causes appear to depend on the Sun and the Moon without the Sun or the Moon having anything to do with the water. It's a business that, for full understanding, needs of me a steadier and longer application of mind, which for now is quite clouded over by the novelty and the difficulty. But I don't despair — by coming back to it on my own, in solitude and silence, to chew over what remains ill-digested in my imagination, of coming to possess it. So then, from the discussions of these four days we have great testimonies in favor of the Copernican system — among which these three, taken, the first from the stations and retrogradations of the planets and from their approaches to and retreats from the Earth; the second from the revolution of the Sun upon itself and from what is observed in its spots; the third from the ebb and flow of the sea — show themselves quite conclusive.

Salviati
To these one might perhaps shortly add a fourth, and maybe even a fifth: the fourth, I say, taken from the fixed stars, if in them, by the most exact observations, those minimal changes should appear that Copernicus takes to be imperceptible. And there's now arising a fifth novelty, from which mobility in the terrestrial globe might be argued — by means of what the most illustrious Signor Cesare, of the most noble Marsili family of Bologna, likewise a Lincean Academician, is very subtly discovering: in a most learned paper he is setting out how he has observed a continual change, though a very slow one, in the meridian line. This paper, which I lately saw with astonishment, I hope he'll make copies of for all students of the marvels of nature.

Sagredo
This isn't the first time I've heard tell of the exquisite learning of this gentleman, and of how anxious a protector he shows himself of all men of letters. And if this or any other of his works comes to light, we can be sure already that it will be something distinguished.

Salviati
Now, since it's time to put an end to our discussions, it remains for me to beg you that, if in going back over the things I've brought forward more calmly you should meet difficulties or doubts not well resolved, you'll excuse my failing — both for the novelty of the thought, and for the weakness of my wit, and for the greatness of the subject, and finally because I don't claim, and never have claimed from others, that assent which I don't give to this fancy myself — a fancy I could very easily admit to be a most empty chimera and a most solemn paradox. And you, Sagredo, though in the discussions we've had you've many times, with great applause, shown yourself contented with some of my thoughts, that, I reckon, came in part more from their novelty than from their certainty, but much more from your courtesy, which has believed, and wished by its assent to bring me that pleasure we naturally take in the approval and praise of what is our own. And just as your kindness has obliged me, so Simplicio's candor has pleased me; indeed his steadfastness in sustaining, with so much force and so intrepidly, the doctrine of his master has made me most fond of him. And as I give thanks to you, Sagredo, for your most courteous affection, so I ask Simplicio's pardon if now and then I've upset him with my too bold and resolute speech; and let him be sure that I haven't done it moved by any ill feeling, but only to give him greater occasion to bring forward lofty thoughts, so that I might make myself more learned.

Simplicio
There's no need for you to make these excuses, which are superfluous, and above all to me — who, being used to finding myself among gatherings and public disputes, have a hundred times heard the disputants not only heat up and grow angry with one another, but even break out into insulting words, and sometimes run very close to coming to blows. As for the discussions we've had, and in particular this last one concerning the cause of the ebb and flow of the sea, I truly don't remain entirely satisfied of it; yet by whatever slight idea I've formed of it, I confess your thought seems to me far more ingenious than any of the others I've heard, though even so I don't hold it for true and conclusive. Rather, keeping always before the eyes of my mind a most solid doctrine that I once learned from a most learned and eminent person, and in which one must rest, I know that both of you, if asked whether God, with His infinite power and wisdom, could confer upon the element of water the back-and-forth movement we see in it by some other means than by moving the containing vessel — I know, I say, that you'll answer that He could have, and known how to, do this in many ways, and even in ways unimaginable to our intellect. Whereupon I immediately conclude to you that, this being so, it would be excessive boldness for anyone to want to limit and confine the divine power and wisdom to one particular fancy of his own.

Salviati
An admirable and truly angelic doctrine — to which very harmoniously answers that other, likewise divine, which, while it allows us to dispute about the constitution of the world, adds to us (perhaps so that the exercise of human minds should not be cut off or grow slack) that we are not to find out the work fashioned by His hands. Let the exercise permitted and ordained to us by God, then, avail to make us recognize, and so much the more admire, His greatness, the less we find ourselves fit to penetrate the deep abysses of His infinite wisdom.

Sagredo
And let this be the last close of our four days' reasonings. After which, if it should please Salviati to take some interval of rest, our curiosity must grant it him — on condition, though, that when it's less inconvenient to him he'll come back to satisfy the desire, mine in particular, concerning the problems left behind, which I've noted down to put to him in one or two further sessions, as we agreed. And above all I'll be waiting with the utmost eagerness to hear the elements of our Academician's new science of local motions, natural and violent. And meanwhile we can, as usual, go and enjoy an hour of the cool of the evening in the gondola that awaits us.
