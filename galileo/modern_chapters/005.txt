The First Day

(Part 5 of 12)

Simplicio
I do know: it's because such qualities have no place among the celestial bodies, which are neither hot nor cold.

Salviati
I can see we're about to plunge back into a boundless sea with no way out — because this is sailing without a compass, without stars, without oars, without a rudder, so that we'd be forced to grope from reef to reef, or run aground, or sail on forever lost. So if we mean to press ahead toward our main subject, as you advise, we must for now set aside this general question of whether straight-line motion is necessary in nature and belongs to certain bodies, and come down to the particular demonstrations, observations, and experiments. First we'll lay out everything that Aristotle, Ptolemy, and others have offered up to now as proof of the Earth's stability; second, we'll try to answer it; and last, we'll bring forward the arguments by which a person might be persuaded that the Earth, no less than the Moon or any other planet, deserves to be counted among the natural bodies that move in circles.

Sagredo
I'll hold to that plan all the more gladly because your grand architectural argument satisfies me far more than Aristotle's does. Yours puts my mind at rest without a single snag, while the other trips me over some stumbling block at every step. And I don't see how Simplicio wasn't instantly persuaded by the reason you gave to prove that straight-line motion can have no place in nature — provided we assume the parts of the universe are arranged in their best constitution and perfectly ordered.

Salviati
One moment, please, Sagredo — for it has just occurred to me how I might satisfy Simplicio too, so long as he isn't so bound to Aristotle's every word that he counts it sacrilege to depart from any of them. There's no doubt that, to maintain the perfect arrangement and order of the universe's parts as far as position is concerned, nothing will serve but circular motion and rest. As for motion in a straight line, I don't see that it can be good for anything except restoring to its natural constitution some particle of one of the integral world-bodies that had been separated from its whole by some accident, as we said earlier. Now let's consider the whole terrestrial globe, and see what can become of it, given that it and the other world-bodies must keep themselves in their best and natural arrangement. One of three things must be said: either it remains and keeps itself perpetually motionless in its place; or, staying always in the same place, it spins upon itself; or it goes around a center, traveling along the circumference of a circle. Of these possibilities, Aristotle and Ptolemy and all their followers say that it has always observed, and will maintain forever, the first — perpetual rest in the same place. Well then, why in heaven's name shouldn't they say that its natural tendency is to remain motionless, rather than making its nature the motion downward — a motion it has never once moved with, and never will? As for straight-line motion, let nature use it to restore to their wholes those particles of earth, of water, of air, of fire, and of every other integral world-body, whenever one of them happens to find itself separated, and so transported to a place out of order — unless it should turn out that even for making this restitution some circular motion is better suited. This primary position, it seems to me, answers all the other consequences far better — even keeping to Aristotle's own way of thinking — than attributing straight-line motions to the elements as an intrinsic and natural principle. And that's plain to see. For if I ask the Peripatetic whether, holding as he does that the celestial bodies are incorruptible and eternal, he believes the terrestrial globe is not so — believes it corruptible and mortal, so that a time must come when, while the Sun and the Moon and the other stars carry on their existence and their operations, the Earth is no longer to be found in the world but has been destroyed along with the rest of the elements and gone to nothing — I'm certain he will answer no. Therefore generation and corruption belong to the parts, not to the whole — and to the very smallest and most superficial parts at that, which are as good as imperceptible compared with the whole mass. And since Aristotle infers generation and corruption from the contrariety of straight-line motions, let's leave such motions to the parts, which alone alter and decay, and assign to the whole globe and the whole sphere of the elements either circular motion or a perpetual staying in their own place — the only conditions fit for perpetuation and for keeping the perfect order. What we say here of earth can be said with the same reasoning of fire and of the greater part of the air: the Peripatetics have been reduced to assigning those elements, as their intrinsic and natural motion, one with which they have never moved and never will, and to calling foreign to their nature the very motion with which they move now, have always moved, and will move perpetually. I say this because they assign to air and fire the motion upward, with which neither of those elements as a whole has ever moved — only some particle of them, and that for no other purpose than to return to its perfect constitution after finding itself outside its natural place. And on the other hand they call contrary to their nature the circular motion with which they move incessantly — forgetting, it would seem, what Aristotle has said again and again: that nothing violent can last for long.

Simplicio
To all of this we have the most fitting replies, which I'll set aside for now in order to come to the more particular reasons and to the evidence of the senses — which in the end must be given precedence, as Aristotle rightly says, over anything human reasoning can supply.

Sagredo
Then let everything said so far serve to help us weigh which of the two general accounts is the more probable. I mean Aristotle's, which would persuade us that the sublunar bodies — everything below the Moon — are by nature generable and corruptible and so on, and therefore utterly different in essence from the celestial bodies, those being impervious to change, ingenerable, incorruptible, and the rest, a conclusion drawn from the difference of their simple motions; or this account of Salviati's, which, supposing the integral parts of the world to be arranged in their best constitution, necessarily excludes straight-line motions from the simple natural bodies as being of no use in nature, and takes the Earth to be itself one of the celestial bodies, adorned with every privilege that belongs to them. So far his account rings far truer to me than the other. So let Simplicio be good enough to produce all the particular reasons, experiments, and observations, natural and astronomical alike, by which one might be persuaded that the Earth is different from the celestial bodies, motionless, and set at the center of the world — and whatever else there is that would rule out its moving like a planet, like Jupiter or the Moon and so on. And Salviati, out of his courtesy, will kindly answer them point by point.

Simplicio
Here you are, then, to begin with: two very powerful demonstrations proving that the Earth is completely different from the celestial bodies. First: bodies that are generable, corruptible, alterable, and so on are utterly different from bodies that are ingenerable, incorruptible, inalterable, and so on. The Earth is generable, corruptible, alterable, and so on; the celestial bodies are ingenerable, incorruptible, inalterable, and so on. Therefore the Earth is utterly different from the celestial bodies.

Sagredo
For your first argument, you're bringing back to the table the same dish that has been sitting on it all day and was only just now cleared away.

Simplicio
Gently, sir; hear the rest, and you'll see how different it is. Before, the minor premise was proved a priori — from first principles; now I want to prove it to you a posteriori — from experience. Judge for yourself whether that's the same thing. I prove the minor premise, then, since the major is perfectly obvious. The experience of the senses shows us that on Earth continual generations, corruptions, alterations, and so on take place — and not one of these, whether by our own senses or by the traditions and records of our ancestors, has ever been seen in heaven. Therefore heaven is inalterable and so on, and the Earth alterable and so on, and therefore different from heaven. The second argument I take from a principal and essential attribute, and it's this: a body that is by nature dark and devoid of light is different from bodies that are luminous and shining. The Earth is dark and without light; the celestial bodies are splendid and full of light; therefore, and so on. Answer these first, so we don't pile up too great a heap, and then I'll bring out others.

Salviati
As for the first, whose force you draw from experience, I'd like you to set out for me more precisely the alterations you see happening on Earth and not in heaven, on account of which you call the Earth alterable and heaven not.

Simplicio
On Earth I see grasses, plants, and animals continually generated and decaying; winds, rains, tempests, and storms stirred up; in short, this face of the Earth in perpetual metamorphosis. And not one of these changes can be made out in the celestial bodies, whose constitution and configuration agree to the letter with every record we possess — with nothing new ever generated there, and nothing old destroyed.

Salviati
But if you're going to rest your case on these visible — or rather, these actually seen — experiences, then you must count China and America as celestial bodies, because you've certainly never seen in them the alterations you see here in Italy; so as far as your own perception goes, they are inalterable.

Simplicio
Even if I haven't witnessed those alterations in those places with my own senses, there are reliable reports of them; besides which, cum eadem sit ratio totius et partium — the same reasoning holding for whole and parts — those countries, being parts of the Earth just like ours, must be alterable just as ours are.

Salviati
And why haven't you observed and seen these things for yourself, with your own eyes, instead of having to fall back on other people's reports?

Simplicio
Because those countries, besides not being exposed to our eyes, are so remote that our sight could never reach far enough to make out such changes.

Salviati
Now see how you have stumbled, all on your own, onto the flaw in your argument. If you say that the alterations seen on Earth near at hand couldn't be made out in America because of the great distance, much less could you see them on the Moon, hundreds of times farther away. And if you believe in the changes in Mexico on the strength of news arriving from there, what dispatches have reached you from the Moon to inform you that there is no alteration in it? So from your not seeing alterations in heaven — where, even if there were any, you couldn't see them for the distance, and from which no news is to be had — you cannot conclude that there are none, the way you rightly conclude, from seeing and hearing of them, that there are alterations on Earth.

Simplicio
Then I'll find you changes that have taken place on Earth so vast that, if any such had happened on the Moon, they could perfectly well have been observed from down here. We have it from the most ancient records that once, at the strait of Gibraltar, the headlands Abila and Calpe were joined together, along with lesser mountains that held the Ocean back; but when those mountains were parted — whatever the cause — and the opening let in the waters of the sea, the flood ran so far inland that it formed the whole Mediterranean Sea. If we consider its vastness, and the difference in appearance that the surface of water and the surface of land must present when seen from far away, there's no doubt that such a change could easily have been made out by anyone standing on the Moon — just as we inhabitants of the Earth would be able to make out alterations of that size on the Moon. But there is no record that any such thing was ever seen. So there's no foothold left for saying that any of the celestial bodies is alterable, and so forth.

Salviati
That changes so vast have taken place on the Moon, I wouldn't dare to say; but neither am I at all certain that they couldn't have. And since a change of that kind could show itself to us only as some variation between the brighter and the darker regions of the Moon, I don't know that we have had, here on Earth, any curious selenographers — Moon-mappers — who over a long run of years have kept us supplied with lunar maps so exact that we could be sure no such change has ever taken place in the face of the Moon. For a detailed description of its features, I find nothing more precise than one person saying it looks like a human face, another that it's like a lion's muzzle, and another that it's Cain with a bundle of thorns on his back. So to say "Heaven is inalterable, because on the Moon or in some other celestial body we don't see the alterations we make out on Earth" has no force to conclude anything at all.

Sagredo
And for my part there's another scruple still nagging me about this first argument of Simplicio's, which I'd like removed. So I ask him: was the Earth generable and corruptible before the Mediterranean flood, or did it only begin to be so then?

Simplicio
It was without doubt generable and corruptible before as well; but that was a change so vast that it could have been observed even from the Moon.

Sagredo
Well then: if the Earth was generable and corruptible even before that flood, why can't the Moon be so equally, without any such change? Why is something required of the Moon that counted for nothing in the case of the Earth?

Salviati
A very sharp thrust. But I begin to suspect that Simplicio is bending a little the meaning of the texts of Aristotle and the other Peripatetics. They say they hold heaven inalterable because no star has ever been seen generated or destroyed in it — and a star is perhaps a smaller part of heaven than a city is of the Earth; yet countless cities have been destroyed so thoroughly that not even their traces remain.

Sagredo
I truly thought otherwise: I believed Simplicio was keeping quiet about this reading of the text to spare the Master and his disciples a charge even uglier than the first. What foolishness it is to say "the celestial region is inalterable because stars are not generated and destroyed in it"! Is there anyone who has seen one terrestrial globe decay and another regenerated in its place? And isn't it accepted by all philosophers that very few stars in heaven are smaller than the Earth, while a very great many are much, much bigger? So for a star in heaven to decay is no smaller a thing than for the whole terrestrial globe to be destroyed. If, then, in order to introduce generation and corruption into the universe with any truth, it's required that bodies as vast as a star be destroyed and regenerated, you may as well strike the whole idea out — for I assure you that no one will ever see the terrestrial globe, or any other integral body of the world, decay in such a way that, after standing in view for centuries past, it dissolves without leaving a trace of itself behind.

Salviati
But to give Simplicio satisfaction in overflowing measure, and to free him from error if that can be done, I say that in our own age we have new events and observations of such a kind that, if Aristotle were alive today, I have no doubt at all he would change his opinion. That follows plainly from his own way of philosophizing. When he writes that he considers the heavens inalterable and so on because nothing new has been seen generated there and nothing old dissolved, he implicitly lets us understand that if he had seen any such event, he would have judged the contrary — and would have put the experience of the senses ahead of natural reasoning, as is proper. For if he had meant to give no weight to the senses, he would not have argued immutability from the fact that no change was ever sensibly seen.

Simplicio
Aristotle laid his principal foundation on a priori reasoning, showing the necessity of the heavens' inalterability by his natural principles, manifest and clear; and he then established the same conclusion a posteriori, by the senses and by the traditions of the ancients.

Salviati
What you describe is the method by which he wrote out his doctrine, but I don't believe it's the method by which he investigated it. I take it as certain that he first worked to assure himself of the conclusion as far as possible by way of the senses, experiments, and observations, and afterward went hunting for the means to demonstrate it — because that's how things are mostly done in the demonstrative sciences. And the reason is this: when the conclusion is true, then working backward from it — the method of resolution — you readily come upon some proposition already demonstrated, or arrive at some principle known in its own right; but if the conclusion is false, you can go on forever without ever meeting a single known truth — unless you happen to run into some impossibility or manifest absurdity along the way. And have no doubt that Pythagoras, long before he found the demonstration for which he offered the hecatomb — that sacrifice of a hundred oxen — had already made sure that in a right triangle the square on the side opposite the right angle is equal to the squares on the other two sides; and certainty about the conclusion helps not a little in the search for the demonstration — speaking always of the demonstrative sciences. But however Aristotle actually proceeded, whether the a priori reasoning came before the a posteriori evidence of the senses or the other way around, it's enough that this same Aristotle puts sensory experience ahead of all reasonings, as has been said more than once; and besides, as for the a priori arguments, we've already examined how much force they carry. Now, coming back to our subject, I say that what has been discovered in the heavens in our own times is such, and has been such, as can give complete satisfaction to all philosophers. In particular bodies and in the universal expanse of heaven alike, phenomena have been seen — and are being seen still — exactly like those we call generations and corruptions here among ourselves. Excellent astronomers have observed many comets generated and dissolved in regions higher than the Moon's orbit, besides the two new stars of 1572 and 1604, indisputably far above all the planets; and on the face of the Sun itself, thanks to the telescope, dense dark materials are seen being produced and dissolved, looking very like the clouds that surround the Earth — and many of them are so vast that they far exceed not only the Mediterranean basin but all of Africa and Asia besides. Now, if Aristotle saw these things, what do you think he would say and do, Simplicio?

Simplicio
I don't know what Aristotle would do or say, for he was the master of the sciences; but I know in part what his followers do and say — and what they must do and say, if they're not to be left without a guide, without an escort, and without a chief in philosophy. As for the comets: weren't those modern astronomers who wanted to make them celestial defeated by the Anti-Tycho, the book written against Tycho — and defeated with their own weapons, I mean by way of parallaxes — those apparent shifts in position — and calculations turned round and round in a hundred ways, concluding at last, in Aristotle's favor, that the comets are all elemental? And with that torn up — and it was the whole foundation the lovers of novelty had — what do they have left to stand on?
