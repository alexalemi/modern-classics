The Second Day

(Part 7 of 20)

Simplicio
Let's set this dispute aside for now, and just let me admit that I don't understand, and don't know, these matters you're discussing — so please, see if you can make me grasp the problems.

Sagredo
This first problem depends on another — namely, why it is that when you throw the ruzzola with a string, it goes much farther, and so with greater force, than when you throw it with your bare hand.

Simplicio
Aristotle too raises some problems or other about these projectiles.

Salviati
Yes, and very ingenious ones — in particular the problem of why round ruzzole travel better than square ones.

Sagredo
And this one, Simplicio — don't you feel up to working out the reason on your own, without anyone teaching you?

Simplicio
All right, all right — but enough of the teasing.

Sagredo
Then you already know the reason for this other one too. So tell me: you know that a thing in motion, when it's obstructed, stops?

Simplicio
I know it — provided the obstruction is enough to do it.

Sagredo
And you know that a moving body meets greater obstruction having to move along the ground than through the air, the ground being rough and hard, the air soft and yielding?

Simplicio
And because I know this, I know the ruzzola will go faster through the air than along the ground — so what I know is the exact opposite of what you were expecting.

Sagredo
Not so fast, Simplicio. Do you know that in the parts of a body spinning about its center, you find motions in every direction — so that some rise, some fall, some go forward, some backward?

Simplicio
I know it — Aristotle taught me that.

Sagredo
And by what demonstration? Do tell me, please.

Simplicio
By the one from the senses.

Sagredo
So Aristotle made you see what you wouldn't have seen without him? Did he ever lend you his eyes? You meant to say Aristotle had told you, pointed it out, reminded you — not taught you. So then: when a ruzzola, without changing place, spins on itself — not lying flat, but standing upright to the horizon — some of its parts rise and the opposite parts fall; the upper parts go one way, the lower the contrary way. Now picture a ruzzola that, without changing place, spins fast on itself while hanging suspended in the air; and while spinning that way, it's let drop straight down to the ground. Do you think that, once it reaches the ground, it will go on spinning in place, as before?

Simplicio
No, it won't.

Sagredo
But what will it do?

Simplicio
It'll run fast along the ground.

Sagredo
And in which direction?

Simplicio
Toward wherever its spin carries it.

Sagredo
But in its spin there are parts — the upper ones — that move contrary to the lower ones; so you have to say which it will obey. As for the rising and falling parts, neither will yield to the other, and the whole won't go down — the ground blocks it — nor up, since it's heavy.

Simplicio
The ruzzola will go rolling along the ground toward wherever its upper parts are heading.

Sagredo
And why not toward where the contrary parts head — the ones touching the ground?

Simplicio
Because the parts at the ground are held back by the roughness of the contact — by the very unevenness of the ground; but the upper parts, in the thin, yielding air, are held back very little or not at all, so the ruzzola will go their way.

Sagredo
So that gripping, so to speak, of the lower parts against the ground keeps them back, and only the upper parts drive forward.

Salviati
And so, if the ruzzola fell on ice or some other very smooth surface, it wouldn't slide forward so well, but might perhaps go on spinning in place without gaining any forward motion.

Sagredo
That could easily happen; at least it wouldn't go rolling along so briskly as when it falls on a somewhat rough surface. But tell me, Simplicio: when the ruzzola, spinning fast on itself, is let fall, why doesn't it move forward through the air too, the way it does once it's on the ground?

Simplicio
Because, with air above and below, neither those parts nor these have anything to grip; and having no reason to go forward rather than back, it falls straight down.

Sagredo
So the spin by itself, without any other impetus, can drive the ruzzola quite fast once it reaches the ground. Now let's get to the rest. That string the thrower ties to his arm, and with which — wound around the ruzzola — he throws it: what effect does it have on it?

Simplicio
It forces it to spin on itself, as it unwinds from the cord.

Sagredo
So when the ruzzola reaches the ground, it arrives already spinning on itself, thanks to the string. Doesn't it then have within itself a cause to move faster along the ground than it did while in the air?

Simplicio
Certainly yes: because in the air it had no impulse but that of the thrower's arm, and though it also had the spin, that — as we said — drives nothing in the air; but on reaching the ground, to the arm's motion is added the forward push of the spin, so the speed doubles. And now I understand perfectly that when the ruzzola bounces up, its speed will drop, because it loses the help of the spinning; and on falling back to the ground it recovers that help, and so goes back to moving faster than in the air. The only thing left for me to grasp is that in this second stretch along the ground it moves faster than in the first — because at that rate it would move forever, always speeding up.

Sagredo
I didn't say flatly that this second stretch is faster than the first, only that it can sometimes happen to be faster.

Simplicio
That's the very thing I don't understand and would like to.

Sagredo
This too you know for yourself. So tell me: if you let the ruzzola drop from your hand without its spinning at all, what would it do on striking the ground?

Simplicio
Nothing — it would just stay put there.

Sagredo
Couldn't it happen that, in striking the ground, it gained some motion? Think it over more carefully.

Simplicio
Unless we let it fall on some stone that had a slope — the way children do in their games — and by striking slantwise on the sloping stone it gained a spinning motion of its own, with which it then kept moving forward along the ground, I wouldn't know any other way it could do anything but stop where it struck.

Sagredo
There — so in some fashion it can gain fresh spin. So when the ruzzola, having bounced up, falls back down, why couldn't it happen to strike the slant of some stone set in the ground and sloped toward the direction of the motion, and — gaining fresh spin from that blow, on top of the first spin from the string — double its motion and make it faster than it was on its first strike against the ground?

Simplicio
Now I see that this could easily happen. And I'm realizing that if the ruzzola were made to spin the opposite way, then on reaching the ground it would produce the opposite effect — that is, the motion of the spin would slow the thrower's.

Sagredo
And it would slow it, and sometimes stop it entirely, when the spin was fast enough. And this is the source of the trick the more expert tennis players use to their advantage — deceiving their opponent by slicing the ball (that's their word for it), that is, returning it with the racquet held at a slant, so that the ball takes on a spin contrary to its projected motion. From which it follows that, on reaching the ground, the bounce — which, if the ball weren't spinning, would carry toward the opponent and give him his usual time to return it — is left as if dead: the ball squashes against the ground, or bounces far less than usual, and breaks the timing of the return. For the same reason you'll see players of wooden bowls, competing to land closest to a set mark, do this: when they play on a stony road full of bumps that would knock the ball off course a thousand ways and keep it from the mark, then, to dodge all those obstacles, they toss the ball not rolling along the ground but straight through the air, as if throwing a flat quoit. But since, in throwing, the ball leaves the hand with some spin imparted by the fingers — whenever the hand is held under the ball, as it usually is — the ball, on striking the ground near the mark, would run far off, carried by the thrower's motion and the spin together. So to make it stop, they cunningly grip the ball with the hand above and the ball below, so that as it escapes, the fingers give it the opposite spin, by which, on striking the ground near the mark, it stops there or runs only a little farther. But to return to the main problem, which was the cause of raising all these others: I say it's possible for someone moving very fast to let a ball leave his hand which, once on the ground, not only keeps up with his motion but even outruns it, moving faster than he does. And to see such an effect, let the moving thing be a cart, to whose outer side is fixed a sloping board, so that its lower end faces the horses and its upper end the rear wheels. Now, at the cart's fastest, if someone inside lets a ball drop down the slope of that board, in coming down it will roll and gain a spin of its own, which — added to the motion impressed by the cart — will carry the ball along the ground much faster than the cart. And if you fitted another sloping board the opposite way, you could tune the cart's motion so that the ball, after running down the board, on reaching the ground would stay still, or even sometimes run backward, against the cart. But we've strayed too long from our subject; and if Simplicio is satisfied with the solution of the first argument against the Earth's mobility — the one drawn from bodies falling straight down — we can move on to the others.

Salviati
The digressions we've made so far aren't so foreign to the subject we're treating that they can be called wholly separate from it. Besides, our reasonings depend on whatever things get stirred up in the imagination — and not one imagination, but three; and, on top of that, we're talking for our own pleasure, and aren't bound by the strictness of someone who ex professo — as a formal undertaking — sets out to treat a subject methodically, meaning also to publish it. I don't want our poem so tied to that unity that it leaves us no open field for episodes; to bring one in, the slightest connection should be enough — as if we'd gathered to swap tales, where I'm free to tell whatever story hearing yours happens to call to mind.

Sagredo
This pleases me greatly; and since we're allowing ourselves this latitude, let me — before we go any further — ask you, Salviati, whether it's ever crossed your mind what one might take to be the line traced by a heavy body falling naturally from the top of a tower to the bottom. If you've reflected on it, do tell me your thought.

Salviati
I've thought about it now and then; and I've no doubt that if one were sure of the nature of the motion by which a heavy body descends to reach the center of the terrestrial globe, and then combined it with the common circular motion of the daily rotation, one would find exactly what sort of line is traced by the body's center of gravity out of the composition of those two motions.

Sagredo
As for the simple motion toward the center, due to gravity, I think one may believe, quite without error, that it's along a straight line — exactly as it would be if the Earth were motionless.

Salviati
On that point we not only may believe it — experience makes us certain of it.

Sagredo
But how does experience assure us of it, if we never see any motion but the compound of the two, circular and downward?

Salviati
On the contrary, Sagredo — we see nothing but the simple downward motion, since the other, the circular one shared by the Earth, the tower, and us, stays imperceptible and as if it didn't exist; the only motion left noticeable to us is the stone's, which we don't share. And of that one, the senses show it to be along a straight line, since it comes down always parallel to the tower itself, which stands built straight up and perpendicular on the Earth's surface.

Sagredo
You're right — and I've shown myself far too dull, not to have hit on something so easy. But since this is perfectly well known, what else do you say we need in order to understand the nature of this downward motion?

Salviati
It's not enough to know that it's straight; we need to know whether it's uniform or non-uniform — that is, whether it keeps always the same speed, or goes on slowing down or speeding up.

Sagredo
It's already clear that it keeps continually speeding up.

Salviati
Nor is that enough; we'd need to know by what proportion this acceleration takes place — a problem that so far, I don't think, has been solved by any philosopher or mathematician, even though whole and enormous volumes have been written on motion by philosophers, and by the Peripatetics in particular.

Simplicio
Philosophers concern themselves chiefly with universals; they find the definitions and the most common properties, then leave certain subtleties and trifling details — which are really more curiosities — to the mathematicians. Aristotle was content to define excellently what motion is in general, and, for local motion, to show its principal attributes: that one kind is natural, another violent; one simple, another compound; one uniform, another accelerated. And for accelerated motion he was content to give the cause of the acceleration, leaving the investigation of the proportion of that acceleration, and of other more particular details, to the mechanic or some lower craftsman.

Sagredo
All very well, my dear Simplicio. But you, Salviati — stepping down now and then from the throne of Peripatetic majesty — have you ever toyed with the investigation of this proportion of the acceleration of falling heavy bodies?

Salviati
I've had no need to think about it, since the Academician, our mutual friend, once showed me a treatise of his on motion, where this was demonstrated, along with many other details. But it would be too great a digression if, for this, we wanted to interrupt the present discussion — which is itself already a digression — and make, as they say, a play within a play.

Sagredo
I'm content to let you off that account for now — on condition, though, that this be one of the propositions set aside to be examined, among the others, in another special session, since that knowledge is something I most keenly want. And meanwhile, let's return to the line traced by the heavy body falling from the top of the tower down to its base.

Salviati
If the straight motion toward the center of the Earth were uniform — the circular motion toward the east being uniform as well — the two together would compose a motion along a spiral line, of the kind Archimedes defined in his book on spirals: those you get when a point moves uniformly along a straight line while that line, also uniformly, turns about one of its endpoints, fixed as the center of its rotation. But because the straight motion of the falling body is continually accelerated, the line composed of the two motions must keep drawing away, in ever greater proportion, from the circumference of the circle that the stone's center of gravity would have traced had it stayed always atop the tower. And this drawing-away must, at the start, be small — indeed tiny, indeed the tiniest of all — since the falling body, setting out from rest (that is, from the absence of downward motion) and entering into the straight downward motion, must pass through all the degrees of slowness between rest and any given speed; and those degrees are infinite, as we discussed and concluded at length earlier.

Granting, then, that such is the course of the acceleration, and given too that the falling body is bound to end at the center of the Earth, the line of its compound motion must be such that it keeps drawing away in ever greater proportion from the top of the tower — or, better put, from the circumference of the circle traced by the top of the tower through the Earth's rotation — yet with those departures growing smaller and smaller without limit, the less and less the body has moved away from the first terminus where it rested. On top of this, this line of the compound motion must necessarily end at the center of the Earth. Now, having made these two assumptions, I went ahead and drew, about the center A with the radius AB, the circle BI, which represents the terrestrial globe to me. Extending the radius AB out to C, I laid off the height of the tower BC, which, carried by the Earth over the circumference BI, traces with its top the arc CD. Then, dividing the line CA in half at E, with center E and radius EC I draw the semicircle CIA — along which I now say that one may quite probably believe a stone, falling from the top of the tower at C, comes to move with the compound motion of the common circular motion and its own straight one. For if I mark on the circumference CD some equal parts CF, FG, GH, HL, and from the points F, G, H, L draw straight lines toward the center A, the segments of those lines caught between the two circumferences CD and BI will always represent that same tower CB, carried by the terrestrial globe toward DI. And on those lines, the points where they're cut by the arc of the semicircle CI are the places where, from moment to moment, the falling stone is found; and those points keep drawing away in ever greater proportion from the top of the tower — which is what makes the straight motion along the tower show itself more and more accelerated. One sees, too, how — thanks to the infinite sharpness of the angle of contact between the two circles DC and CI — the falling body's departure from the circumference CFD, that is from the top of the tower, is at the start the very smallest, which is as much as to say that the downward motion is the very slowest, and slower and slower without limit the nearer it is to the terminus C, that is to the state of rest. And finally one understands how, at the last, such motion would end at the center of the Earth, A.

Sagredo
I understand the whole thing perfectly, and I can't believe the falling body traces, with its center of gravity, any line but one like this.

Salviati
But gently, Sagredo — I still have three little musings to bring you, which perhaps won't displease you. The first is that, if we consider it rightly, the body really moves with nothing but a simple circular motion, just as, when it rested on the tower, it likewise moved with a simple circular motion. The second is even prettier: for it moves not a whit more or less than if it had stayed continuously on the tower, since the arcs CF, FG, GH, and so on, that it would have passed through by staying always on the tower are precisely equal to the arcs of the circumference CI that answer to them beneath those same CF, FG, GH, and so on. From which follows the third marvel: that the true and real motion of the stone is not accelerated at all, but is always steady and uniform, since all the equal arcs marked on the circumference CD, and their counterparts marked on the circumference CI, are passed through in equal times. So we're freed from hunting for new causes of acceleration, or of any other motions, since the body — both while resting on the tower and while descending from it — always moves in the same way, that is, circularly, with the same speed and the same uniformity. Now tell me what you make of this odd fancy of mine.

Sagredo
I tell you, I couldn't put into words how marvelous it seems to me; and as far as it now presents itself to my mind, I don't believe the business goes any other way. Would to God all the demonstrations of the philosophers had half the plausibility of this one. I'd very much like, for my complete satisfaction, to hear the proof that those arcs are equal.
