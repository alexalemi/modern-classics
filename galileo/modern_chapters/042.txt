The Third Day

(Part 10 of 16)

Sagredo
Say rather — and I think you'll put it better — that we don't know what purpose they serve for us. And I count it one of the greatest arrogances, one of the greatest follies, that anyone could put forward, to say: "Because I don't know what use Jupiter or Saturn is to me, therefore they're superfluous — in fact they don't even exist in nature." Why, you utter fool, I don't even know what use my arteries are to me, or my cartilage, my spleen, my gall bladder; I wouldn't so much as know I had a gall bladder, a spleen, or kidneys, if they hadn't been shown to me cut out of many corpses; and even then I could only learn what my spleen does in me at the moment it was taken out of me. To understand what this or that heavenly body does in me — since you insist that every operation of theirs is aimed at us — I'd have to remove that body for a while, and then whatever effect I felt go missing in me, I could say depended on that star. What's more, who is going to claim that the space these people call too vast and useless, between Saturn and the fixed stars, is empty of any other worldly bodies? Perhaps because we don't see them? So then the four Medicean Stars and the companions of Saturn came into the sky when we first began to see them, and not before? And the other countless fixed stars weren't there before men saw them? And the nebulae were once just little whitish patches, but then with the telescope we made them turn into throngs of many bright and beautiful stars? Presumptuous — no, reckless — ignorance of men!

Salviati
There's no need, Sagredo, to go on any further with these fruitless outbursts. Let's stick to our plan, which is to weigh the force of the reasons brought forward on the one side and the other, without settling anything, and leave the judgment then to those who know more than we do. And coming back to our natural, human discussion, I say that these terms — great, small, immense, tiny, and the rest — are not absolute but relative, so that the very same thing, compared with different things, may now be called immense and now imperceptible, let alone small. That being so, I ask: in relation to what can Copernicus's starry sphere be called too vast? In my view, it can't be compared or called such except in relation to something else of the same kind. So let's take the smallest of the same kind, which will be the lunar orb; and if the starry orb is to be judged too vast compared with the Moon's, then every other magnitude that exceeds another of the same kind by a like or greater proportion will have to be called too vast — and by this reasoning we'll also have to deny that it exists in the world at all. And so elephants and whales will surely be chimeras and poetic fancies; because the former, being too vast compared with ants, which are land animals, and the latter compared with sprats, which are fish — and ants and sprats are certainly there in rerum natura, in the real world — would be too enormous, since, absolutely speaking, the elephant and the whale exceed the ant and the sprat by a far greater proportion than the starry sphere exceeds the Moon's, if we picture that sphere just as large as it needs to be to fit the Copernican system. What's more, how large is the sphere of Jupiter, how large that of Saturn, each assigned as the housing for a single star — and a very small one, compared with a fixed star? Surely, if each fixed star had to be given such a portion of the world's space for its lodging, the orb where that countless multitude of them resides would have to be made many, many thousands of times bigger than what suffices for Copernicus's needs. Besides, don't you call a fixed star very small — I mean even the most conspicuous of them, let alone those that escape our sight? And we call them so in comparison with the space surrounding them. Now, if the whole starry sphere were a single shining body, who doesn't grasp that within infinite space one could assign a distance so great that this shining sphere would look just as small — smaller, even — than a fixed star looks to us now from the Earth? From there, then, we would judge small the very same thing that from here we now call immeasurably great.

Sagredo
Enormous, it seems to me, is the folly of those who would have God make the universe more in proportion to the small capacity of their reasoning than to His immense — indeed, infinite — power.

Simplicio
Everything you say is fine; but the point the other side presses on is having to grant that a fixed star must be not merely equal to, but so much larger than the Sun — and both, after all, are particular bodies set inside the starry orb. And it seems to me very much to the purpose that this author asks, and demands: "To what end, and for whose benefit, are machines so vast? Produced, perhaps, for the Earth — that is, for a tiny little point? And why so remote, so that they appear so slight and can do absolutely nothing on Earth? To what purpose that monstrous, immense chasm between them and Saturn? Empty and pointless are all those things not supported by probable reasons."

Salviati
From the questions this man asks, it seems to me one can gather that if we leave the heavens, the stars, and the distances at the quantities and sizes he has believed so far — even though he's never securely pictured any comprehensible size for them — he grasps perfectly well, and is quite satisfied about, the benefits that come from them upon the Earth, which would then no longer be a tiny little thing, nor they so remote as to appear so small, but big enough to be able to act upon the Earth, and the distance between them and Saturn perfectly well proportioned; and that he has very probable reasons for all these things — some of which I'd gladly have heard. But to see that in these few words he tangles himself up and contradicts himself makes me think he's very poor and short of these probable reasons, and that what he calls reasons are rather fallacies — shadows, even, of empty fancies. For I now ask him whether these heavenly bodies really do act upon the Earth, and whether it's for such an effect that they were produced at such-and-such sizes and set at such-and-such distances — or whether, on the contrary, they have nothing to do with earthly things. If they have nothing to do with the Earth, it's great foolishness for us earthlings to want to be arbiters of their sizes and regulators of their local arrangements, when we're utterly ignorant of all their affairs and concerns. But if he says they do act, and that they're directed to this end, then he comes to affirm what, from another angle, he himself denies — and to praise what he's just now condemned, when he said that heavenly bodies placed at such a distance that they appear tiny from the Earth can't act upon it at all. But, my dear man, in the starry sphere — already fixed at the distance it's at, which you judge to be well proportioned for its influences on these earthly things — a great many stars appear extremely small, and there are a hundred times as many altogether invisible to us (which is to appear smaller still than tiny). So you must either — contradicting yourself — now deny that they act upon the Earth; or else — contradicting yourself again — grant that appearing tiny takes nothing away from their action; or else — and this will be the more honest and modest concession — grant and freely confess that our judgment about their sizes and distances is a vanity, not to say a presumption or a rashness.

Simplicio
Truly, I saw it myself at once, reading this passage — the plain contradiction, in saying that the stars of Copernicus, so to speak, appearing so tiny, couldn't act upon the Earth, and yet not noticing that he'd granted action upon the Earth to those of Ptolemy and his own, which appear not merely tiny, but for the most part are invisible.

Salviati
But I come to another point. On what basis does he say that the stars appear so small? Perhaps because that's how we see them? Doesn't he know that this comes from the instrument we use in looking at them — namely, our eye? And that this is true is shown by the fact that if we change the instrument we'll see them bigger and bigger, as much as we please; and who knows whether to the Earth, which gazes at them without eyes, they don't show themselves enormous, and just as they really are? But it's time we left these trifles and came to weightier matters. So, having already proved these two things — first, how far off the firmament must be placed so that in it the diameter of the great orb makes no greater difference than the terrestrial orb makes in the distance of the Sun; and then having likewise proved how, to make a star of the firmament appear to us the size we see it, there's no need to make it bigger than the Sun — I'd like to know whether Tycho, or any of his followers, has ever tried to investigate in any way whether in the starry sphere any appearance can be detected by which one could more decisively deny, or admit, the annual motion of the Earth.

Sagredo
I'd answer no on their behalf — nor did they even have any need to; since it's Copernicus himself who says there is no such difference, and they, arguing ad hominem — against the man's own admission — grant it to him, and on this assumption they show the improbability that follows: namely, that one would have to make the sphere so immense that a fixed star, in order to appear as large as it appears to us, would in reality have to be a bulk so immense that it exceeded the size of the whole great orb — which is then, as they say, altogether incredible.

Salviati
I'm of the same opinion, and I believe precisely that they argue against the man more to defend another man than out of any longing to come to knowledge of the truth. And not only do I not believe that any of them has applied himself to making such an observation, but I'm not even sure whether any of them knows what difference the annual motion of the Earth would produce in the fixed stars, if the starry sphere were not at so great a distance that such a difference vanished in them for its smallness. For to stop short of such an inquiry and fall back on Copernicus's mere say-so may well be enough to defeat a man in argument, but not to make the fact itself clear — since it may be that the difference is there, but not looked for, or, for its smallness or for lack of exact instruments, not detected by Copernicus. This wouldn't be the first thing that he, for lack of instruments or some other shortcoming, failed to know, and yet, grounding himself on other utterly sound conjectures, affirmed something that the things he didn't grasp seemed to contradict. For, as was said before, without the telescope neither could Mars be seen to grow 60 times, nor Venus 40 times, larger in the one position than in the other — indeed, their differences appear far smaller than the truth; and yet we've since come to be certain that these changes exist exactly as the Copernican system required. So it would be well done to search out, with the greatest precision one could manage, whether such a change — which ought to be discernible in the fixed stars, granting the Earth's annual motion — is actually observed: something I absolutely believe has not so far been done by anyone, and not only not done, but perhaps (as I said) not even well understood by many, what one ought to be looking for. Nor do I say this at random; for I once saw a certain handwritten paper by one of these anti-Copernicans, who said that, if that opinion were true, there would necessarily follow a continual raising and lowering of the pole every six months, as the Earth in that span withdraws, over as much space as the diameter of the great orb, now toward the north, now toward the south; and it seemed to him reasonable — indeed, necessary — that, we following the Earth, when we were toward the north we should have the pole higher than when we're toward the south. Into this same error fell a mathematician who was otherwise quite intelligent, and likewise a follower of Copernicus, according to what Tycho reports in his Progymnasmata, on page 684: he said he'd observed the polar altitude change, and be different in summer from winter. And because Tycho denies the merit of the cause but doesn't condemn the procedure — that is, he denies that any change is seen in the polar altitude, but doesn't condemn the inquiry itself as unsuited to obtaining what's sought — he comes to declare that he too reckons the polar altitude, whether varied or unvaried from six months to six months, to be a good test for excluding or admitting the annual motion of the Earth.

Simplicio
Really, Salviati, it seems to me too that the same thing ought to follow. For I don't believe you'll deny me that if we walk only 60 miles toward the north, the pole will rise a degree for us, and drawing likewise another 60 miles nearer the north, the pole will rise another degree for us, and so on. Now, if drawing 60 miles nearer or farther makes so notable a change in the polar altitudes, what must carrying the Earth — and us with it — not 60 miles, but 60 thousand, do?

Salviati
It will have to make (if we're to follow that proportion) the pole rise a thousand degrees for us. See, Simplicio, how much an inveterate impression can do! You, for having fixed it in your imagination for so many years that it's the heavens that turn round in twenty-four hours, and not the Earth, and that consequently the poles of that revolution are in the sky and not in the terrestrial globe, can't for even an hour strip yourself of this habit and dress up in the opposite — picturing that it's the Earth that moves, just for as long as it takes to conceive what would follow if this fib were true. If the Earth, Simplicio, is what turns on itself in twenty-four hours, then in it are the poles, in it is the axis, in it is the equinoctial — that is, the great circle described by the point equally distant from the poles — in it are the infinitely many parallels, greater and smaller, described by the points of its surface more and less distant from the poles: in it are all these things, and not in the starry sphere, which, being motionless, lacks them all, and only in imagination can they be pictured there — by prolonging the Earth's axis out to where, ending, it will mark two points overhead of our poles, and the plane of the equinoctial, extended, will trace out in the sky a circle answering to it. Now, if the true axis, the true poles, the true terrestrial equinoctial don't change on Earth so long as you too stay in the same place on Earth, then carry the Earth wherever you please — you'll never change your relation, either to the poles, or to the circles, or to anything else terrestrial; and this because such a carrying is common to you and to all earthly things, and motion, where it's common, is as though it weren't there at all. And just as you won't change your relation to the earthly poles — a relation, I mean, such that they rise or fall for you — so likewise you won't change it to the poles figured in the sky, so long as by celestial poles we understand (as was already defined) those two points marked out there by the Earth's axis prolonged that far. It's true that those points in the sky change when the carrying of the Earth is done in such a way that its axis goes to strike other and other points of the motionless celestial sphere; but our relation to them doesn't change, such that the second should rise higher for us than the first. Whoever wants one of the firmament's points answering to the Earth's poles to rise, and the other to fall, for him must walk on Earth toward the one, moving away from the other; for carrying the Earth, and us with it (as I've already said), does nothing.

Sagredo
Do me the kindness, Salviati, of letting me lay this business out quite clearly with an example — a crude one, maybe, but all the more suited to the point. Imagine, Simplicio, that you're aboard a galley, and that, standing at the stern, you've trained a quadrant or some other astronomical instrument on the top of the foremast, as though you wanted to take its elevation, which was, let's say, 40 degrees. There's no doubt that if you walk along the gangway toward the mast some 25 or 30 paces, and then train the same instrument again on the same masthead, you'll find its elevation to be greater — grown, let's say, by 10 degrees. But if, instead of walking those 25 or 30 paces toward the mast, you stayed put at the stern and made the whole galley move that way, do you believe that, by the voyage it had made of those 25 or 30 paces, the elevation of the foremast would show itself increased to you by 10 degrees?

Simplicio
I believe, and understand, that it wouldn't gain so much as a single hair's breadth by a voyage of a thousand or a hundred thousand miles, let alone of 30 paces. But I do believe that if, in sighting along the top of the foremast, a fixed star happened to lie in the same line, then — I mean — keeping the quadrant fixed, after sailing 60 miles toward the star, the sight would strike the tip of the foremast just as before, but no longer the star, which would have risen a degree for me.

Sagredo
But surely you don't believe that the line of sight would fail to strike that point of the starry sphere which answers to the line of the masthead?

Simplicio
No, that it wouldn't; but the point would have changed, and would lie below the star first observed.

Sagredo
That's exactly how it is. But just as, in this example, what answers to the elevation of the masthead is not the star, but the point of the firmament that lies in line with the eye and the top of the mast, so in the case we're illustrating, what in the firmament answers to the pole of the Earth is not a star or any other fixed thing of the firmament, but that point at which the terrestrial axis, prolonged straight out that far, comes to end — a point that isn't fixed, but obeys the changes the earthly pole might make. And therefore Tycho, or the others who brought this objection, ought to have said that, given such a motion of the Earth (if it were true), one should detect and observe some difference in the rising and falling not of the pole, but of some fixed star toward that quarter which answers to our pole.

Simplicio
Now I understand perfectly the confusion these people fell into; but it still doesn't rob me of the force — which seems to me very great — of the argument brought on the other side, once it's referred to the change of the stars, and no longer of the pole. For if the galley's motion, of only 60 miles, makes a fixed star rise for me by a degree, how could a like change — and a far greater one, too — not come to me when the galley is carried toward that same star over as much space as the diameter of the great orb, which you say is twice the distance from the Earth to the Sun?
