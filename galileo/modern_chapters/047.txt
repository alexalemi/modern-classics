The Third Day

(Part 15 of 16)

Salviati
I saw it many times, and with great wonder — until an even greater astonishment was handed to me by a small piece that our friend the Academician has in his keeping. It weighs no more than six ounces, and unarmed it barely holds up two ounces; but armed — fitted with an iron cap — it holds up a hundred and sixty, so that it comes to support eighty times more armed than unarmed, and to bear a weight twenty-six times greater than its own. That is a marvel far greater than any Gilbert had been able to meet with, for he writes that he could never find a lodestone able to hold up four times its own weight.

Sagredo
This stone seems to me to open up a vast field for the human mind to philosophize in. I have turned it over in my own thoughts a thousand times — how can it be that it gives to the iron that arms it a force so much greater than its own? — and in the end I find nothing that sets me at rest; nor do I get much sense out of what Gilbert writes on this particular point. I don't know whether the same happens to you.

Salviati
I praise, admire, and envy this author to the highest degree, for having had so stupendous a conception fall into his mind about a thing that countless sublime intellects had handled, and none had noticed. He seems to me worthy of the greatest praise, too, for the many new and true observations he made — to the shame of so many lying and empty authors who write not only what they know, but everything they hear from the foolish crowd, without troubling to make sure of it by experiment, perhaps so as not to shrink their books. What I would have wished in Gilbert is that he had been a somewhat greater mathematician, and in particular well grounded in geometry; for the practice of it would have made him less ready to accept as conclusive demonstrations the reasons he offers as the true causes of the true conclusions he himself observed. Those reasons (speaking freely) do not knot and bind with the force that must belong, beyond all doubt, to reasons drawn from natural, necessary, and eternal conclusions. And I don't doubt that as time goes on this new science will be perfected, with further new observations, and still more with true and necessary demonstrations. Yet that should take nothing from the glory of the first observer. I esteem no less — indeed I admire far more — the first inventor of the lyre (even though one must suppose the instrument was very crudely built, and more crudely played) than the hundred other artists who in later centuries brought that craft to great refinement. And it seems to me quite reasonable that antiquity numbered the first inventors of the noble arts among the gods, since we see that the common run of human minds has so little curiosity, and cares so little for rare and refined things, that even when they see and hear them exquisitely practiced by masters, they are not moved to want to learn them. Now think whether brains of that sort would ever have set themselves to investigate the making of the lyre, or the invention of music, enticed by the whistling of the dry sinews of a tortoise, or the strokes of four hammers. To fasten upon great inventions, spurred by the tiniest beginnings, and to judge that beneath a first, childish appearance marvelous arts may lie hidden — that is not for ordinary minds; those are the conceptions and thoughts of superhuman spirits. Now, to answer your question: I say that I too have long thought about what the cause might be of this tenacious and powerful joining that we see form between the one iron that arms the lodestone and the other that clings to it. And first I made sure that the virtue and force of the stone is not increased in the least by being armed, since it neither attracts from any greater distance, nor holds up any more firmly an iron between which and the armature there is placed a very thin paper — down to a single leaf of beaten gold. On the contrary, with such a thing between, it holds more iron bare than armed. So there is no change in the virtue, and yet there is a new result in the effect; and since a new effect must have a new cause, I looked for what novelty is introduced in the act of holding with the armature, and could find no other change than in the different touching: for where before iron touched lodestone, now iron touches iron. So one must necessarily conclude that the different touchings are the cause of the difference in the effects. And this difference between the contacts, I can see no way for it to come from anything other than this: that the substance of iron is of parts finer, purer, and more tightly packed than those of the lodestone, which are coarser, less pure, and looser. From which it follows that the surfaces of the two irons that are to touch, so long as they are exquisitely flattened, polished, and burnished, join together so exactly that all the infinite points of the one meet the infinite points of the other, so that the filaments (so to speak) linking the two irons are far more numerous than those linking lodestone to iron — because the substance of the lodestone is more porous and less pure, which means that not all the points and filaments of the iron's surface find, in the surface of the lodestone, counterparts to unite with. And that the substance of iron (especially the well-purified kind, like the finest steel) is of parts far denser, finer, and purer than the matter of the lodestone, is shown by the fact that its edge can be brought to an extreme thinness, like the edge of a razor — something the edge of a piece of lodestone could never come near. The impurity of the lodestone, and its being mixed with other kinds of stone, is first seen plainly in the color of certain little specks, mostly whitish; and then in this: present to it a needle hanging from a thread, and over such little stones the needle cannot settle, but, drawn by the parts surrounding them, seems to flee them and jump onto the lodestone next to them. And since some of these foreign parts are, by their size, quite visible, we may believe that others in great abundance — too small to be seen — are scattered through the whole mass. What I say (namely, that the multitude of touchings made between iron and iron is the cause of so firm a joining) is confirmed by an experiment. It is this: if we present the sharp point of a needle to the armature of the lodestone, it will attach no more strongly than to the same lodestone bare — which can come from nothing but the two touchings being equal, that is, both being of a single point. But there's more. Take a needle and lay it on the lodestone so that one of its ends sticks out a little, and present a nail to that end: the needle will at once attach to it, so that as you draw the nail back, the needle is left hanging, held by its two ends to the lodestone and to the iron; and drawing the nail still further will pull the needle off the lodestone — provided the eye of the needle is the end joined to the nail, and the point is the end on the lodestone. But if the eye is toward the lodestone, then when you take the nail away, the needle stays stuck to the lodestone. And this (in my judgment) for no other reason than that, the needle being thicker toward the eye, it touches at many more points than the very sharp point does.

Sagredo
The whole argument has seemed to me very conclusive, and these experiments with the needle make it little inferior to a mathematical demonstration. And I frankly confess that in all of magnetic philosophy I have neither heard nor read anything else that accounts, with like force, for any other of its many marvelous behaviors — of which, if we had the causes explained with such clarity, I don't know what sweeter food our intellect could desire.

Salviati
In investigating the reasons for conclusions unknown to us, one must have the luck to steer the argument, from the very start, onto the road of truth. When a man sets out along that road, it readily happens that he meets one true proposition after another — known to be true whether by reasoning or by experience — and from their certainty the truth of his own gains force and evidence. That is exactly what happened to me with the present problem. Wishing to make sure, by some further check, whether the reason I had worked out was true — namely, that the substance of the lodestone is really far less continuous than that of iron or steel — I had one face of that very piece of lodestone that was once yours flattened by the craftsmen who work in the Gallery of the Grand Duke, my lord, and then polished and burnished as much as possible. And there, to my satisfaction, I touched with my hand what I was after. For there came to light many specks of a color different from the rest, but as bright and lustrous as any dense hard stone; the rest of the field was smooth, but only to the touch, being not at all shiny — rather, as if clouded by a haze. That dull part was the substance of the lodestone; the bright part, of other stones mixed into it — as one could plainly tell by bringing the flattened face over iron filings, which in great abundance jumped to the lodestone, but not a single grain to those specks. And the specks were many: some as large as a quarter of a fingernail, others somewhat smaller, very many quite small, and the barely visible ones almost past counting. So I was assured that my idea had been perfectly true, when I first judged that the substance of the lodestone must be not solid and closed, but porous — or better, spongy — yet with this difference: where a sponge holds air or water in its cavities and cells, the lodestone has its own filled with very hard, heavy stone, as the exquisite luster they take on shows us. And so, as I said at the start, when you apply the surface of the iron to the surface of the lodestone, the least particles of the iron — though more continuous, perhaps, than those of any other body (as its taking a finer polish than any other material shows) — meet pure lodestone not all of them, but few; and the contacts being few, the attachment is weak. But because the armature of the lodestone, besides touching a great part of the stone's surface, also clothes itself in the virtue of the neighboring parts even where they are not touched, then — that face of it being exactly flattened, and the matching face of the iron to be held being likewise well flattened — the touching is made of countless least particles, if not perhaps of the infinite points of both surfaces, so that the attachment turns out extremely strong. This point, of flattening the surfaces of the irons that are to touch, Gilbert did not notice; on the contrary, he makes his irons rounded, so that their contact is small, and the result is that far weaker is the grip with which those irons hold together.

Sagredo
As I said just now, the reason you have assigned leaves me hardly less satisfied than if it were a pure geometric demonstration; and since this is a physical problem, I judge that Simplicio too will find himself satisfied, so far as natural science allows — for he knows that in it one must not look for geometric proof.

Simplicio
It truly seems to me that Salviati, with a fine roundabout of words, has explained the cause of this effect so clearly that any middling mind, even one that is no scientist, could take it in. But we, keeping within the terms of the discipline, trace the cause of these and similar natural effects to sympathy, which is a certain agreement and mutual appetite arising between things that are alike in quality — just as, on the other hand, that hatred and enmity by which other things naturally flee one another and hold each other in horror, we call antipathy.

Sagredo
And so, with these two names, one manages to account for a great number of the happenings and effects that we see, not without wonder, produced in nature. But this way of philosophizing seems to me to have great sympathy with a certain manner of painting a friend of mine used to practice. On the canvas he would write in chalk: "Here I want the fountain to be, with Diana and her nymphs; here, some greyhounds; in this corner I want a hunter, with the head of a stag; the rest, countryside, wood, and little hills." The rest he then left to the painter to fill in with colors — and so he convinced himself that he had himself painted the tale of Actaeon, having put in nothing of his own but the names. But where have we brought ourselves, with so long a digression, against the rules we had already laid down? I've all but forgotten what the matter was that we were treating when we strayed into this magnetic talk — and yet I did have some point or other in mind to make about it.

Salviati
We were on the verge of demonstrating that the third motion Copernicus assigns to the Earth is not really a motion at all, but a rest — a keeping of itself unchangeably aimed, with its fixed parts, toward the same fixed parts of the universe; that is, a perpetual keeping of the axis of its daily rotation parallel to itself and turned toward certain fixed stars. This most constant state, we were saying, belongs naturally to every body balanced and suspended in a fluid, yielding medium, which, though carried around, does not change its direction with respect to external things, but only seems to turn upon itself with respect to whatever is carrying it, and to the vessel in which it is carried. Then, to this simple and natural behavior, we added the magnetic virtue, by which the terrestrial globe could all the more firmly hold itself unchangeable, and so on.

Sagredo
Now it all comes back to me. What was passing through my mind then, and what I wanted to bring out, was a certain thought about the difficulty and objection Simplicio had raised against the Earth's mobility — the one drawn from the multiplicity of motions, which cannot be attributed to a simple body, since, in Aristotle's teaching, only one single, simple motion can be natural to it. And what I wanted to put forward was precisely the lodestone, to which we plainly see three motions naturally belong. The first, toward the center of the Earth, as a heavy body. The second is the horizontal circular motion by which it restores and keeps its axis toward fixed parts of the universe. The third is this one, newly discovered by Gilbert: the tilting of its axis — lying in the plane of a meridian — toward the surface of the Earth, and this more or less according to how far it is from the equinoctial, beneath which its axis stays parallel to the Earth's axis. Besides these three, it is perhaps not unlikely that it may have a fourth: turning about its own axis, whenever it were balanced and suspended in air or some other fluid, yielding medium, so that all external and accidental hindrances were taken away — and to this thought Gilbert himself seems to give his applause. So you see, Simplicio, how shaky Aristotle's axiom is left.

Simplicio
This not only fails to wound the maxim, it isn't even aimed its way — since he speaks of a simple body and of what may naturally belong to it, while you bring up what happens to a mixed one. Nor are you saying anything new within Aristotle's teaching, since he too grants a compound motion to mixed bodies, and so on.

Sagredo
Hold on a moment, Simplicio, and answer the questions I'll put to you. You say the lodestone is not a simple body, but a mixed one: now I ask you, which are the simple bodies that mix together to compose the lodestone?

Simplicio
I couldn't tell you the ingredients, nor the exact proportions, but it's enough that they are elementary bodies.

Sagredo
That's enough for me as well. And of these simple, elementary bodies, which are their natural motions?

Simplicio
They are the two simple straight ones, sursum et deorsum.

Sagredo
Tell me next: do you believe that the motion which will remain natural to such a mixed body must be one that can result from compounding the two simple natural motions of the simple component bodies — or that it might also be a motion impossible to compound out of those?

Simplicio
I believe it will move with the motion resulting from the compounding of the motions of the simple component bodies, and that it is impossible for it to move with a motion that cannot be compounded from these.

Sagredo
But, Simplicio, with two simple straight motions you will never compound a circular motion — and circular are the two or three different motions the lodestone has. See, then, into what tight corners ill-founded principles lead us — or, better put, badly drawn conclusions from good principles. For now you are forced to say that the lodestone is a mixture composed of elementary and of celestial substances, if you want to maintain that straight motion belongs to the elements alone, and circular motion to the celestial bodies. So, if you want to philosophize more safely, say instead that of the integral bodies of the universe, those that are by nature mobile all move in circles, and that the lodestone, therefore, being part of the true, primary, integral substance of our globe, keeps that same nature. And notice this fallacy of yours: you call the lodestone a mixed body, and the terrestrial globe a simple one — when the globe is plainly a hundred thousand times more composite, since, besides containing a thousand and one materials utterly different from one another, it contains a great quantity of this very thing you call mixed, I mean the lodestone. This seems to me the same as if someone called bread a mixed body, and called simple that olla podrida — that Spanish hodgepodge — into which there also goes no small amount of bread, along with a hundred different sundries. A marvelous thing indeed seems to me, among others, this business of the Peripatetics. They grant (nor can they deny it) that our terrestrial globe is in fact a compound of endless different materials; they grant, next, that the motion of compound bodies must be compound; the motions that can be compounded are the straight and the circular, since the two straight ones, being contrary, are incompatible with each other; they affirm that the pure element of earth is nowhere to be found; they confess that it has never moved with any local motion. And then they want to place in nature that body which is not to be found, and make it move with a motion it has never performed and never will; while to the body that is, and always has been, they deny the very motion they first granted must naturally belong to it.

Salviati
Please, Sagredo, let's not wear ourselves out any further over these particulars — the more so since you know our aim has never been to settle firmly, or to accept as true, this or that opinion, but only to lay out, for our own enjoyment, the reasons and answers that can be brought forward on either side. And Simplicio gives this reply to ransom his Peripatetics. So let's leave the verdict hanging, and the decision in the hands of those who know more than we do. And since it seems to me that we've discoursed at quite enough length, over these three days, about the system of the universe, it will be time now to come to the greatest matter of all, the one that first set our conversations going: I mean the ebb and flow of the sea, whose cause, it seems, may quite probably be traced to the motions of the Earth. But that, if you please, we'll save for the following day. Meanwhile, so as not to let it slip my mind, let me tell you a certain point on which I would not have wished Gilbert to lend an ear — I mean his allowing that if a little sphere of lodestone could be perfectly balanced, it would spin upon itself; for there's no reason why it should. For if the whole terrestrial globe has it by nature to turn about its own center in twenty-four hours, and all its parts must do the same — I mean, turn, together with their whole, about the center of it in twenty-four hours — then they already do so, in effect, while, resting upon the Earth, they go around together with it. To assign them a rotation about their own center would be to give them a second motion, quite different from the first, so that they'd then have two: the turning about their whole's center in twenty-four hours, and the turning about their own. Now this second one is arbitrary, and there is no reason to bring it in. If, when a piece of lodestone is detached from the whole natural mass, it were robbed of following that mass — as it did while joined to it — so that it were left deprived of turning about the universal center of the terrestrial globe, then one might, perhaps, with somewhat more probability, believe that it would take on a new spin about its own particular center. But if it — no less when separated than when joined — still keeps up its first, eternal, natural course, why want to saddle another new one onto it?
