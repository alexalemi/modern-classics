To the Grand Duke

DIALOGUE ON THE TWO CHIEF WORLD SYSTEMS

A Dialogue by Galileo Galilei, member of the Lincean Academy, Extraordinary Mathematician of the University of Pisa, and Chief Philosopher and Mathematician to the Most Serene Grand Duke of Tuscany.

In which, over the course of four days of conversation, the two chief systems of the world — the Ptolemaic and the Copernican — are discussed, and the philosophical and natural arguments for each side are set out without a verdict.

The difference between human beings and the other animals is enormous; and yet anyone who claimed that a difference nearly as great exists among human beings themselves would not be talking nonsense. What is the ratio of one to a thousand? And still the proverb is on everyone's lips: one man may be worth a thousand, where a thousand are not worth one. A difference like that comes down to the varying powers of our minds, and for me it reduces to this — being or not being a philosopher. For philosophy is the proper food of such minds, and whoever can feed on it is set apart, in fact, from the common run of humanity, to a greater or lesser degree of worth depending on how rich that nourishment is. Whoever looks higher stands out more highly; and turning to the great book of nature — which is the true object of philosophy — is the way to lift one's eyes. In that book, everything we read, being the work of an all-powerful Craftsman, is for that reason perfectly proportioned; but the reading is swiftest and worthiest where the work and the artistry appear, to our sight, the greatest. Among all the natural things our minds can grasp, the constitution of the universe deserves, I believe, first place: for as it contains everything and surpasses everything else in size, so, as the rule and sustainer of everything, it must surpass everything else in nobility as well. Therefore, if it ever fell to any men to stand out in intellect above the rest of humanity, it was Ptolemy and Copernicus, who read so loftily in the constitution of the world, fixed their gaze on it, and philosophized about it. Since these Dialogues of mine turn chiefly on the works of those two, it seemed they should be dedicated to no one but Your Highness; for their doctrine rests on those two men, whom I judge the greatest minds ever to have left us their works on such speculations, and so, to spare them any loss of rank, I had to place them under the favor of the one who is, in my eyes, greatest of all, from whom they may receive both glory and protection. And if those two have brought so much light to my understanding that this work of mine may largely be called theirs, it may just as well be called Your Highness's; for by your generous magnificence I was given not only the leisure and peace to write it, but also, through your effective help — never weary of honoring me — it has at last been brought into the light. Accept it, then, Your Highness, with your customary kindness; and if lovers of truth find in it anything that yields them fuller knowledge or real benefit, count it as your very own, accustomed as you are to doing good — so much so that in your happy dominion no one feels any of the universal hardships of this world troubling him. Praying for your prosperity, and that you may grow ever greater in this pious and magnanimous habit, I offer you my most humble reverence.

Your most humble and most devoted servant and vassal,
Galileo Galilei.

To the Discerning Reader

Some years ago a salutary edict was issued in Rome which, to head off the dangerous scandals of the present age, imposed a timely silence on the Pythagorean opinion that the Earth moves. There was no shortage of people who rashly asserted that this decree was the offspring not of judicious examination but of poorly informed passion; and complaints were heard that consultants wholly inexperienced in astronomical observation ought not, with a sudden prohibition, to clip the wings of speculative minds. My zeal could not keep silent when I heard the recklessness of such complaints. Being fully informed about that most prudent determination, I judged it right to appear publicly on the stage of the world as a witness of the honest truth. I happened to be in Rome at the time; I received not only audiences but applause from the most eminent prelates of that court; nor was the decree published without my having had some advance notice of it. Therefore it is my purpose in the present work to show foreign nations that as much is known of this matter in Italy — and particularly in Rome — as ultramontane diligence could ever have imagined; and, by gathering together all my own speculations on the Copernican system, to make it known that awareness of every one of them preceded the Roman censure, and that from this climate come not only dogmas for the salvation of the soul, but ingenious discoveries for the delight of the mind as well.

To this end I have taken the Copernican side in the discussion, proceeding upon a pure mathematical hypothesis, and striving by every artful means to present it as superior — not to the doctrine of the Earth's stillness absolutely, but to that doctrine as it is defended by certain men who profess to be Peripatetics yet keep only the name, content, without ever taking a walk, to worship shadows, philosophizing not with due attention but from the mere memory of four ill-understood principles.

Three main topics will be treated. First, I shall try to show that all experiments that can be performed on the Earth are insufficient means for proving its mobility, since they can be fitted indifferently to a moving Earth or to one at rest; and I hope that in this connection many observations unknown to the ancients will come to light. Second, the celestial phenomena will be examined, reinforcing the Copernican hypothesis as if it must emerge absolutely victorious, and adding new speculations — which, however, serve for convenience in astronomy, not as necessities of nature. In the third place I shall propose an ingenious fancy. I found myself saying, many years ago, that the unsolved problem of the tides might receive some light if the Earth's motion were granted. This remark of mine, flying from mouth to mouth, had found charitable fathers ready to adopt it as the child of their own wit. Now, so that no foreigner may ever appear who, arming himself with our own weapons, reproaches us for inattention to so important a matter, I have judged it best to set out the probable arguments that would make it persuasive, granted that the Earth moves. I hope that from these considerations the world will learn that if other nations have navigated more, we have speculated no less; and that to submit to asserting the Earth's stillness, and to take the contrary as nothing more than a mathematical caprice, comes not from ignorance of what others have thought about it, but — if from nothing else — from those reasons supplied by piety, by religion, by the knowledge of divine omnipotence, and by the consciousness of the weakness of the human mind.

I have thought it fitting, moreover, to unfold these ideas in the form of a dialogue, which, not being bound to the rigorous observance of mathematical laws, leaves room for digressions too — at times no less interesting than the main argument.

Many years ago I often found myself in the marvelous city of Venice in conversation with Signor Giovan Francesco Sagredo, a man of most illustrious birth and most penetrating mind. From Florence came Signor Filippo Salviati, the least of whose splendors were the purity of his blood and the magnificence of his wealth: a sublime intellect that fed on no delicacy more avidly than on exquisite speculations. With these two I often found myself discussing these matters, in the presence of a Peripatetic philosopher — one for whom nothing seemed to stand more in the way of understanding the truth than the fame he had acquired as an interpreter of Aristotle.

Now, since a most bitter death has, in the very prime of their years, deprived Venice and Florence of those two great lights, I have resolved to prolong their fame, as far as my feeble powers allow, upon these pages of mine, by bringing them in as speakers in the present controversy. Nor will the good Peripatetic lack his place: because of his excessive fondness for the commentaries of Simplicius, it seemed fitting, without revealing his name, to leave him the name of that revered author. May those two great souls, forever venerable to my heart, receive this public monument of my undying love; and may the memory of their eloquence help me to set forth to posterity the speculations here promised.

It happened (as it will) that various scattered discussions had arisen among these gentlemen, which had inflamed rather than quenched the thirst for learning in their minds; so they made the wise resolution to spend a few days together in which, with every other business set aside, they would devote themselves to contemplating, in more orderly speculations, the wonders of God in the heavens and on the earth.
