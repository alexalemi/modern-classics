TEXT ANALYSIS — Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems
(Galileo Galilei, 1632)
Source: the ITALIAN original from it.wikisource (the Edizione
Nazionale text). TRANSLATED FROM THE ITALIAN — the project's first
Italian volume; the from-the-Latin pipeline pattern applies (translate
from the original; there is no bundled crib — the public-domain
English, Salusbury 1661, is itself the archaic barrier; agents work
from the Italian directly).

WHY THIS BOOK IS THE PROJECT'S THESIS

Galileo wrote in Italian instead of Latin PRECISELY so that ordinary
educated Italians — not just professors — could read it. It was a
bestseller, then a banned book (the Inquisition trial of 1633 followed
directly), and in English it has been locked behind either the 1661
Salusbury or in-copyright modern translations. Retelling it in plain
modern English restores exactly the audience Galileo wrote for.

WHAT THIS IS

Four days of conversation in a Venetian palazzo among three men:
- SALVIATI — Galileo's stand-in: sharp, patient, devastating.
- SAGREDO — the intelligent amateur host: curious, witty, the
  reader's surrogate; swings the argument's emotional weight.
- SIMPLICIO — the Aristotelian professor: earnest, bookish, forever
  quoting authorities. NOT a buffoon — render him as the smartest
  possible defender of a doomed system (the book's comedy works
  better, and it's truer to the text).
Day One: against the Aristotelian earth/heavens divide. Day Two: the
Earth's daily rotation (the ship's-cabin relativity argument — the
most famous passage). Day Three: the annual orbit. Day Four: the
tides (Galileo's proudest and, ironically, wrongest argument —
translate it with full conviction; no editorial winking).

STYLE AND TONE

- CONVERSATION, real and Italian: interruptions, jokes, courtesies,
  digressions, flashes of temper. The modern voice is three smart
  people arguing brilliantly over long dinners — natural contemporary
  speech, contractions welcome, wit intact.
- Speaker-tag format (established by prep): the speaker's name alone
  on its own line (SALVIATI / SAGREDO / SIMPLICIO), then the speech
  paragraphs — the Plato-dialogues convention; assemble.py renders
  bold speaker tags. NEVER merge turns or drop tags.
- The science must stay RIGHT: thought experiments (the tower drop,
  the ship's cabin, the cannon shots) rendered concretely and
  correctly. Where Galileo uses geometry, keep it followable in
  prose; spell out ratios plainly.
- Long periodic Italian sentences break into modern rhythm, but keep
  the courtly flourishes ("as your excellency well knows...") lightly
  — they are period charm, thinned not purged.

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY

- The three names always SALVIATI, SAGREDO, SIMPLICIO (tags) and
  Salviati, Sagredo, Simplicio (in running speech).
- "the Ptolemaic system" / "the Copernican system"; "the daily
  rotation"; "the annual motion"; "natural motion"/"violent motion"
  (the Aristotelian pair — keep, glossed once); epicycles, deferents
  kept with one-time glosses.
- "Peripatetics" for the Aristotelians (glossed once: "the
  Aristotelian school").
- The Academician = Galileo himself when the characters cite him
  ("our mutual friend, the Academician") — keep the device.

SENSITIVE / EDITORIAL

- The preface "To the Discerning Reader" is Galileo's ironic
  Inquisition-mandated cover story (claiming to treat Copernicanism
  as mere hypothesis). Translate it straight — the irony is in the
  text, not the translator.
- Simplicio's closing recitation of the "divine omnipotence" argument
  (Day Four's end) is the passage that enraged Urban VIII; render it
  exactly as written, no commentary.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain)

- Day Two, the ship's cabin: "Shut yourself up with a friend in the
  main cabin below decks on some large ship" — keep the experiment's
  full inventory (flies, fish in a bowl, dripping bottle, jumping)
  vivid and complete.
- Day One, on the book of the universe vs. authorities: the argument
  that nature "does not care a fig" for our reasoning — keep
  Sagredo's/Salviati's jabs at arguing from texts instead of the
  world.

FORMAT

55 files: 000 = Front Matter (split headings "To the Grand Duke",
"To the Discerning Reader"); Days One–Four as sections stitched from
parts (First Day 001–012, Second 013–032, Third 033–048, Fourth
049–054). The printed margin summaries (postille) are stripped at
prep — the chapter files are continuous prose; if a short third-person
summary fragment ever seems to break a sentence's syntax, flag it to
the orchestrator rather than translating around it. Ratio target: Italian → modern English ≈ 0.95–1.25 (verify
with --min-ratio 0.85 --max-ratio 1.35). Parts 2+ never re-introduce
a Day; non-final parts never conclude it. Turns may continue across a
part boundary — a part may open mid-conversation with a speaker tag.
