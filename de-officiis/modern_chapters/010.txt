Book III: The Conflict Between the Right and the Expedient

(Part 3 of 4)

Chapter 17

But the laws get rid of sharp practice in one way and philosophers in another: the laws, as far as they can lay hands on it; philosophers, as far as reason and understanding can reach it. And what reason demands is this: nothing done by ambush, nothing by pretense, nothing by fraud. Well then — is it an ambush to set out nets, even if you have no plan to flush the game and drive it in? Wild animals fall into nets often enough with no one chasing them. Would you do the same with a house — post it for sale, put up the notice-board like a net, and let some unsuspecting buyer run into it because you are selling the place for its defects?

Custom has slipped so far that this kind of thing is not considered shameful by common opinion, I know, and no statute or civil law forbids it. But the law of nature forbids it. For there is a fellowship — I have said it often, but it needs saying oftener still — that stretches at its widest over everyone, all of us bound to all; a closer one among people of the same nation; a nearer one still among citizens of the same state. That is why our ancestors held the law of nations to be one thing and the civil law another. What belongs to the civil law does not automatically belong to the law of nations; but what belongs to the law of nations ought also to be civil law. The trouble is that we possess no solid, sharply drawn likeness of true law and genuine justice. We make do with a shadow and a few sketches — and I wish we were faithful even to those, for they are drawn from nature's and truth's best models. Think how much weight there is in that old formula: "That I not be trapped and defrauded because of you and my good faith in you!" And how much gold in this one: "As between good men, there must be good faith in dealing, and no fraud!" But who counts as a "good man," and what "good faith in dealing" really means — that is a very large question.

Quintus Scaevola, the chief priest, used to say that the greatest force lay in all those arbitrations to which the words "as good faith requires" were attached. He held that the phrase "good faith" reached extremely wide: it lived in guardianships and partnerships, in trusts and commissions, in buying and selling, in hiring and letting — all the dealings that hold the fellowship of life together. In these cases, he said, it takes a judge of real stature to settle what each party owes the other, especially since most of them allow counter-claims.

So away with sharp practice, and away with the malice that wants so badly to pass for practical wisdom, and is nothing like it, and stands miles away from it. Practical wisdom lies in choosing between good things and bad. Malice — since everything shameful is bad — puts the bad ahead of the good.

And it is not only in land sales that the civil law, drawing on nature, punishes malice and fraud. In the sale of slaves, too, every kind of fraud by the seller is shut out. Under the aediles' ruling, the seller — who is the one in a position to know — is answerable for the slave's health, for whether he is a runaway, for whether he is a thief. Heirs who have just come into ownership are a different case.

Since nature is the wellspring of law, the lesson is this: it is according to nature that no one should make his profit out of another's ignorance. And you will find no greater plague in life than malice wearing the mask of understanding. That is the source of those countless cases where the useful seems to fight against the right. For how many people will you find who could hold back from wrongdoing if they were promised total secrecy and total impunity?

Chapter 18

Let us put this to the test, if you like — and in exactly those cases where ordinary opinion probably sees no wrong at all. There is no need, here, to discuss hired killers, poisoners, will-forgers, thieves, and embezzlers of public money; those men are to be worn down not with philosophers' words and arguments but with chains and a prison cell. Let us look instead at what is done by men who are considered good.

Certain men brought a forged will from Greece to Rome — the will of Lucius Minucius Basilus, a rich man. To push it through more easily, they wrote in as co-heirs Marcus Crassus and Quintus Hortensius, the most powerful men of the day. These two suspected the will was a fake; but since they were conscious of no guilt of their own, they did not refuse a little gift born of another man's crime. What then? Is that enough to clear them? I cannot think so — though I loved one of them while he lived, and do not hate the other now that he is dead. Basilus had wanted his sister's son, Marcus Satrius, to carry his name, and had made him his heir. (I mean the Satrius who is now "patron" of Picenum and the Sabine country — and what a shameful brand upon the times that title is!) It was not fair that two leading citizens should take the property while nothing came to Satrius but the name. For if a man does wrong who fails to ward off and repel an injury when he can — as I argued in the first book — what are we to make of the man who does not merely fail to push the injury back but actually helps it along? For my part, even genuine inheritances do not look right to me if they were hunted down by calculated flattery — by attentions that only impersonated duty instead of meaning it.

And yet in cases like these, one course will sometimes look useful and another look right. The look is a lie: the measure of advantage is the very same as the measure of the right. Whoever fails to see through this will be capable of any fraud, any crime. For if he reasons, "Yes, that is the right thing — but this is the profitable one," he will dare, in his blindness, to tear apart two things nature has joined — and that tearing is the source of every fraud, every misdeed, every crime there is.

Chapter 19

So then: suppose a good man had this power — that by snapping his fingers he could slip his name into rich men's wills. He would not use it, not even if he were absolutely certain that no one would ever suspect a thing. But give that power to a Marcus Crassus — the power to get himself written in as heir when he was no heir at all — and believe me, he would dance in the forum. The just man, though, the one we mean when we say "a good man," will take nothing from anyone to move it into his own pocket. Anyone astonished by that should admit he does not know what a good man is. But let anyone unfold the idea of a good man that lies folded up in his own mind, and he will teach himself the answer at once: a good man is one who helps whom he can and harms no one — unless he has been provoked by a wrong. Well then: is a man doing no harm who works a kind of poison to push the true heirs aside and slide into their place? "So he shouldn't do what is useful, what pays?" someone will say. No — he should understand that nothing pays and nothing is useful if it is unjust. Whoever has not learned that lesson cannot be a good man.

When I was a boy I used to hear from my father about Gaius Fimbria, the ex-consul, who was judge in a case involving Marcus Lutatius Pinthia, a thoroughly honorable Roman knight. Pinthia had staked a legal wager, forfeit "if he were not a good man." Fimbria told him he would never decide that case: he did not want to strip a well-regarded man of his reputation by ruling against him, and he did not want to be seen ruling that anyone positively was a good man — a thing built out of countless duties and countless merits.

To this good man, then — a man even a Fimbria could recognize, not only a Socrates — nothing can possibly look useful that is not right. Such a man will not dare to do, or even to think, anything he would not dare to announce out loud. Isn't it a disgrace that philosophers dither over questions that even farmhands never dither over? For it was farmhands who coined the proverb that is now worn smooth with age: when they praise someone's good faith and decency, they call him "a man you could match fingers with in the dark." What does that mean, if not this — that nothing unfitting pays, even when you could get away with it and no one could ever prove a thing?

Do you see that under this proverb there is no pardon for the famous Gyges, and none for the man I invented a moment ago, who could sweep up everyone's inheritances with a snap of his fingers? What is shameful cannot be made right no matter how well it is hidden; and in just the same way, what is not right cannot be made useful, because nature refuses and fights back.

Chapter 20

"But surely," comes the objection, "when the prize is enormous, there is a reason to do wrong."

Gaius Marius was a long way from any hope of the consulship. Six years and more after his praetorship he was still lying flat, and it looked as though he would never even stand for the office. He was serving as a deputy to Quintus Metellus, a great man and a great citizen, and when Metellus — his own commander — sent him to Rome, Marius stood before the Roman people and accused him of dragging out the war. Make me consul, he said, and in short order I will deliver Jugurtha, alive or dead, into the power of the Roman people. So he was made consul, yes — but he walked away from good faith and justice, since by a false charge he dragged into public hatred an excellent and highly respected citizen, whose deputy he was, and on whose orders he had come.

Even our own kinsman Gratidianus once fell short of a good man's duty. In his praetorship, the tribunes of the people called in the college of praetors to fix a standard for the currency by joint resolution — for in those days the coinage was pitching about so wildly that no one could tell what he was worth. Together they drafted an edict, penalty and procedure included, and agreed that they would all mount the rostra — the speakers' platform — together that afternoon. The others scattered, each his own way; but Gratidianus went straight from the benches to the rostra and published alone what had been composed by all of them together. And that move, if you want to know, brought him enormous honor: statues in every street, incense and candles burning before them. In short, no one was ever dearer to the crowd.

These are the cases that sometimes tangle our deliberations — when the point where fairness is violated looks small, and what the violation buys looks huge. To Gratidianus it did not seem so very shameful to snatch the people's favor away from his colleagues and the tribunes; but to become consul by it — which was exactly what he had in view — seemed extremely useful. But there is one rule for every case, and I want you to know it cold: either the thing that looks useful must not be shameful — or, if it is shameful, it must not look useful. What follows, then? Can we call the great Marius a good man, or this one? Unfold your understanding, shake it out, and see what shape and idea of "a good man" it holds. Does it fit a good man to lie for his own profit, to smear, to snatch ahead of others, to deceive? Nothing could fit less.

Is there anything worth so much, then — any advantage so desirable — that you would give up the shine and the name of a good man for it? What can this so-called advantage bring you that is worth what it takes away, if it robs you of the name of a good man and strips off your good faith and your justice? What difference does it make whether a man turns into a beast, or keeps a man's shape and carries a beast's savagery inside it?

Chapter 21

Then what about those who trample everything right and honorable so long as they end up with power? Aren't they doing exactly what the man did who wanted for a father-in-law the very man whose audacity would make him powerful? It seemed useful to him to be supreme while the hatred fell on the other man; and he could not see how unjust to his country that was, and how shameful. As for the father-in-law himself, he was forever quoting the Greek lines from the Phoenician Women — I will give them as best I can, clumsily perhaps, but clearly enough for the point:

    If law must ever be broken, break it for a throne;
    in everything else, keep your duty to the gods.

Eteocles deserved the executioner for that — or rather Euripides did — for carving out one exception, and making it the wickedest crime of them all! So why are we collecting the petty stuff — inheritances, sharp trades, fraudulent sales? Here, look at this: a man who longed to be king of the Roman people and master of every nation — and who did it. Anyone who calls that ambition right is out of his mind, for he is approving the death of law and liberty and counting their hideous, detestable suppression as glory. And anyone who admits it is not right to reign as king in a state that was free and ought to be free, yet insists it is useful for the man who can pull it off — with what scolding, or rather what shout, could I try to drag him back from a mistake that size? By the immortal gods, can the foulest, ugliest murder of all — the murder of one's own country — be useful to anybody, even if the man who has bound himself to that crime is called "father" by the very citizens he has crushed? No: advantage must be steered by the right, and steered so completely that the two words sound different to the ear but ring as one single thing in fact.

By the crowd's standards, I do not know what greater advantage there could be than to reign. But when I start calling the reckoning back to truth, I find nothing more disadvantageous for the man who got there unjustly. Can anguish be useful to anyone? Can worry, fear by day and by night, a life crammed to the brim with plots and dangers?

"Many are hostile to a throne, many faithless, few devoted," says Accius. But what throne did he mean? A legitimate one, handed down from Tantalus and Pelops. How many more enemies, do you think, had that king who used the Roman people's own army to crush the Roman people themselves — who forced a state that was not merely free but the commander of nations to be his slave? What stains do you think that man carried on his conscience, what wounds? And can a man's life be useful to himself when the terms of it are these: whoever takes that life will earn the greatest gratitude and the greatest glory? If even these things are not useful — the things that look most useful of all — because they are full of disgrace and shame, then we should be thoroughly persuaded by now that nothing is useful that is not right.

Chapter 22

That very judgment, in fact, has been handed down many times, and never more memorably than in the war with Pyrrhus, by the consul Gaius Fabricius — in his second consulship — and by our senate. King Pyrrhus had made war on the Roman people unprovoked; the contest was for supremacy, against a generous and powerful king. A deserter slipped out of his camp and into the camp of Fabricius, and promised that, for a price, he would slip back into Pyrrhus's camp as secretly as he had left it — and poison the king. Fabricius had the man escorted back to Pyrrhus, and the senate praised him for it. And yet, if we go by the mere look of advantage and the common notion of it, one deserter would have ended that grinding war and removed a formidable rival to our supremacy. But it would have been a deep disgrace and scandal to have beaten, by crime instead of by courage, the man with whom the contest was a contest for glory.

Which was more useful, then — for Fabricius, who was to this city what Aristides was to Athens, and for our senate, which never split advantage from honor — to fight the enemy with steel or with poison? If supremacy is to be sought for glory's sake, then keep crime out of it, for there can be no glory in crime. If it is raw power that is wanted, at any cost, that power cannot be useful with infamy hanging on it.

So that famous motion of Lucius Philippus, son of Quintus, was not useful. Certain states, by decree of the senate, had bought their exemption from tribute with a lump sum paid to Lucius Sulla. Philippus moved that they be made tributary again — and that we keep the money they had paid for their freedom. The senate agreed. A disgrace to our empire! Pirates keep better faith than the senate did. "But the revenues went up — so it was useful." How long will people have the nerve to call a thing useful that is not right? Can hatred and infamy be useful to any empire — an empire that ought to stand on glory and the goodwill of its allies?

I often disagreed even with my dear Cato on this. He seemed to me too unbending in guarding the treasury and the revenues: he refused the tax contractors everything, and the allies much, when we ought to have been generous to the allies and dealt with the contractors the way we habitually deal with our own tenants — all the more because harmony between the orders mattered to the safety of the republic. Curio was wrong too, when he kept saying the cause of the peoples beyond the Po was just, but always added: "Let advantage win!" Better to have argued that their cause was not just, because it was not useful to the republic, than to admit it was just while calling it useless.

Chapter 23

The sixth book of Hecato's On Duties is full of questions like this one: "In a famine, when grain is at its dearest, is it a good man's part to stop feeding his slaves?"

He argues it both ways, but in the end he steers duty by advantage, as he reckons advantage, rather than by humanity.

He asks: if cargo must be thrown overboard at sea, should a man jettison an expensive horse or a cheap slave? Here his property pulls him one way, humanity the other.

"If a fool has grabbed a plank from a shipwreck, will a wise man wrench it away from him if he can?"

No, says Hecato — that would be an injustice.

"What about the ship's owner? Won't he seize the plank, since it is his?"

"Not at all — no more than he would be willing to throw a passenger overboard on the open sea because the ship is his. Until she reaches the port she was hired for, the ship belongs not to her owner but to her passengers."

"What if there is one plank and two shipwrecked men — both of them wise? Should each grab it for himself, or should one give way to the other?"

"One should give way — to the one whose life is worth more, for his own sake or for the republic's."

"And if they are equal on both counts?"

"Then there will be no struggle. One will yield to the other as if the point were settled by lot or by a round of matching fingers."

"What if a father robs temples, or digs a tunnel into the treasury — should his son report it to the magistrates?"

"That would be an unholy act. In fact he should defend his father if he is charged."

"Doesn't our country, then, come before every other duty?"

"Certainly — but it serves the country itself to have citizens devoted to their parents."

"And what if the father tries to seize a tyranny, or to betray his country — will the son keep silent?"

"Hardly. He will beg his father not to do it. If that gets him nowhere, he will accuse him, and threaten him too; and at the end, if the matter points toward the country's destruction, he will put the country's survival above his father's."

Hecato also asks: if a wise man unknowingly accepts counterfeit coins as good ones, will he — once he finds out — pass them off as good to settle a debt? Diogenes says yes; Antipater says no, and I side with him.

If a man knowingly sells wine that is going off, must he say so? Diogenes thinks it is not required; Antipater thinks a good man would. These are like the disputed points of law among the Stoics. "In selling a slave, must his faults be declared — not just the ones the civil law makes you declare on pain of taking the slave back, but the rest of it: that he is a liar, a gambler, a thief, a drunk?" One says they must be declared; the other says not.

"If a man selling gold thinks he is selling brass, should a good man tell him it is gold — or buy for one denarius what is worth a thousand?"

By now it is clear enough what I think, and what the dispute between the philosophers I have named comes down to.

Chapter 24

Must pacts and promises always be kept — the ones, as the praetors' formula runs, "made neither by force nor by criminal fraud"?

Suppose a man gives someone a remedy for dropsy, on the condition that, if it cures him, he will never use it again. It cures him; some years later the same disease returns; and the man he made the bargain with will not grant him leave to use it a second time. What should he do? Since the one refusing is being inhuman, and since no injury is done to him by the remedy's use, the sick man should look to his own life and health.

Now suppose a wise man is asked, by someone about to make him his heir with a hundred million sesterces in the will, to dance in broad daylight in the open forum before he enters on the inheritance. He promises to do it, because otherwise the man would not have written him in. Should he keep the promise or not? I wish he had never made it; that, I think, would have been the dignified course. But since he did promise: if he holds that dancing in the forum is shameful, he will act more honorably by breaking the promise and taking nothing from the inheritance than by taking it — unless, perhaps, he puts the money toward some grave crisis of the republic. In that case, since he would be serving his country, it would not even be shameful to dance.

Chapter 25

Nor do those promises bind that are not useful to the very people you made them to. To go back to the old stories: the Sun told his son Phaethon he would do whatever the boy wished. He wished to be taken up into his father's chariot. He was taken up — and before he ever touched down, he was burned to ashes by a bolt of lightning. How much better it would have been if that father's promise had gone unkept! And what of the promise Theseus called in from Neptune? Neptune had granted him three wishes, and he wished for the death of his son Hippolytus, when the boy had fallen under his father's suspicion over his stepmother. The wish was granted — and Theseus was plunged into the blackest grief. And what of Agamemnon, who had vowed to Diana the most beautiful thing born that year in his kingdom? He sacrificed Iphigenia — for nothing born that year was more beautiful than she. Better to have broken the promise than to commit a crime that hideous.

So promises are sometimes not to be made good, and deposits are not always to be returned. A man in his right mind leaves a sword with you; gone mad, he demands it back. To return it would be a sin; not to return it, a duty. And if a man who deposited money with you makes war on your country — do you hand back the deposit? I think not: you would be acting against the republic, which ought to be dearest of all. So it is with many things: right by nature, they become, as circumstances change, not right. Keeping a promise, standing by an agreement, returning a deposit — let the advantage shift, and they stop being right.

And about these things — the seeming advantages that cut against justice under a mask of practical wisdom — I think enough has now been said.

But since in the first book we drew our duties from the four wellsprings of moral goodness, let us stay with the same four as we show how the things that seem useful, and are not, are the enemies of virtue. Practical wisdom, which malice tries to counterfeit, has been dealt with, and so has justice, which is always useful. Two parts of moral goodness remain: one is seen in the greatness and preeminence of a surpassing spirit, the other in the shaping and governing of restraint and self-control.
