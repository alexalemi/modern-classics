Book I: Moral Goodness

(Part 1 of 5)

Chapter 1

My dear son Marcus, you have been studying under Cratippus for a full year now, and in Athens, no less. By this point you ought to be overflowing with the precepts and principles of philosophy, given the towering reputation of both your teacher and your city — the one can build up your knowledge, the other can supply you with living examples. Still, I have always found it useful to combine Latin studies with Greek, and I did this not only in philosophy but in my training as a speaker. I think you should do the same, so that you become equally at home in both languages. In this, if I may say so, I have done our countrymen a real service: not just people with no Greek, but even the well-educated feel they have gained something — both for speaking well and for judging well.

So by all means keep learning from the leading philosopher of our age, and keep learning as long as you like — and you should like it just as long as you are not disappointed with your own progress. But read my books too. They do not differ much from what the Peripatetics teach, since both they and I want to count as followers of Socrates and Plato. On the substance, use your own judgment — I will not stand in your way. But reading my work will certainly make your Latin fuller and richer. And please do not take that as bragging. Plenty of people know more philosophy than I do, and I gladly say so. But the orator's particular gift — speaking aptly, clearly, and gracefully — is a craft I have spent my whole life on, and if I claim some mastery there, I think I claim it with a certain right.

So I urge you, my Cicero, with all my heart: read not only my speeches but also these books of philosophy, which by now nearly rival them in bulk. The speeches have more raw force, yes — but this calm, even, measured style is worth cultivating too. And here is something I notice: no Greek has yet managed to work in both arenas, mastering both the fighting style of the courtroom and this quiet style of philosophical discussion. Unless perhaps Demetrius of Phalerum counts — a subtle reasoner, an orator without much fire, but pleasant enough that you can recognize the student of Theophrastus in him. How far I have succeeded in the two fields, let others judge. I have certainly attempted both.

For my part, I believe that if Plato had wanted to take up courtroom speaking, he could have done it with tremendous weight and richness; and that if Demosthenes had held on to what he learned from Plato and chosen to deliver it, he could have done it with elegance and brilliance. I judge Aristotle and Isocrates the same way: each was so in love with his own pursuit that he looked down on the other's.

Chapter 2

I have decided to write you something now, and much more later, and I wanted to begin with the subject best suited both to your age and to my authority as your father. Philosophy takes up many questions that are weighty and useful, and philosophers have worked through them carefully and at length; but the widest reach belongs to what they have handed down about duties. No part of life can be free of duty — not public life or private life, not the courts or the household, not your dealings with yourself or your bargains with another person. Everything morally good in life lies in honoring duty; everything shameful lies in neglecting it.

And this question belongs to all philosophers in common. Who would dare call himself a philosopher while handing down no teaching about duty at all? Yet there are schools whose accounts of the ultimate good and evil overturn duty completely. Think it through: a man who sets up the highest good with no connection to virtue, measuring everything by his own advantage rather than by what is right — that man, if he is consistent with himself and not occasionally overruled by his own better nature, can cultivate neither friendship nor justice nor generosity. And there is simply no way to be brave while judging pain the worst of all evils, or self-controlled while declaring pleasure the highest good.

All this is so obvious it hardly needs arguing, though I have argued it elsewhere. The point is this: if those schools want to be consistent, they can say nothing about duty. Firm, stable rules of duty, rooted in nature, can come only from people who say that moral goodness deserves to be pursued for its own sake — either solely, or at least chiefly. So teaching about duty properly belongs to the Stoics, the Academics, and the Peripatetics; the opinions of Aristo, Pyrrho, and Erillus were laughed off the stage long ago. Even they would have kept their right to discuss duty if they had left us any basis for choosing between one thing and another, some path toward discovering what duty is. For now, and on this question especially, I will follow the Stoics — not as their translator, but in my usual way: drawing from their springs at my own judgment and discretion, as much as seems right, however it seems best.

Since the whole discussion ahead is about duty, I would like to start by defining what duty is — something Panaetius skipped, to my surprise. Every rational treatment of any subject ought to begin with a definition, so that everyone understands exactly what is being discussed.

Chapter 3

Every question about duty comes in two kinds. One kind concerns the ultimate good; the other consists of practical rules by which everyday life, in all its corners, can be shaped. Examples of the first kind: whether all duties are absolute; whether one duty outranks another; and questions of that sort. The duties for which practical rules are handed down do connect to the ultimate good, but the connection is less visible, because these rules seem aimed at organizing ordinary shared life. And those are the duties I intend to explain in these books.

There is another division of duty as well. There is what is called a "middle" duty, and a "complete" one. A complete duty, I think, we should call "right" — the Greeks call it katorthoma, while the ordinary duty they call kathekon. They define them this way: whatever is right is a complete duty; a middle duty is one for which a reasonable account can be given of why it was done.

So then: as Panaetius sees it, deliberating over a decision runs three ways. First, people are unsure whether the thing they are weighing is right to do or shameful — and in thinking it through, their minds are often torn toward opposite conclusions. Second, they investigate and take counsel about whether the thing will serve life's comfort and enjoyment, their means and resources, their wealth, their power — the tools for helping themselves and the people they love. That whole deliberation turns on advantage. The third kind of hesitation comes when what seems useful appears to fight against what is right. Advantage seems to grab the mind and drag it one way; the right calls it back the other; and the result is a mind pulled apart in its deliberating, carrying an anxious, two-headed worry into its thinking.

But leaving things out is the worst mistake you can make in dividing a subject, and this division leaves out two. We deliberate not only about whether something is right or shameful, but also, when two right courses lie before us, which is more right — and when two advantageous ones do, which is more advantageous. So the scheme Panaetius thought was threefold turns out to need five parts. First, then, we must discuss the right — in two ways; then, on the same plan, the useful; and last, how to weigh one against the other.

Chapter 4

Start at the beginning. Nature has granted every species of living creature the drive to protect itself — its life and its body — to shy away from whatever looks harmful, and to hunt down and secure everything necessary for living: food, shelter, and things of that kind. Common to all animals, too, is the urge to mate and reproduce, and a certain care for what they have produced. But here is the greatest difference between human and beast. The beast, moved only as far as its senses move it, adjusts itself only to what is present, the here and now, with barely any sense of past or future. But a human being shares in reason. Through reason he sees consequences coming; he sees the causes of things and is not blind to what led up to them; he compares one case with a similar one, and ties the future to the present. So he takes in the course of a whole life at a glance, and prepares in advance what he will need to live it.

The same nature, through the power of reason, draws human beings toward one another, into fellowship of speech and of life. Above all she plants in us a singular love for our children, and she drives us to want gatherings and assemblies of people to exist and to take our own part in them — and, for those reasons, to work at providing what supports a decent life, not for ourselves alone but for a spouse, for children, for all the others we hold dear and are bound to protect. And this responsibility stirs the spirit too, and makes it greater for getting things done.

Above all else, the hunt for truth — searching it out, digging into it — belongs distinctively to human beings. So whenever we are free of necessary business and cares, we itch to see something, hear something, learn something new, and we count knowledge of nature's hidden or wonderful things as essential to living happily. From this you can see that what is true, simple, and genuine is the best fit for human nature. And joined to this appetite for seeing truth is a certain hunger to stand first: a mind well formed by nature is unwilling to obey anyone — except someone giving guidance, or teaching, or commanding justly and lawfully for the common benefit. From this comes greatness of spirit, and a way of looking down on merely human concerns.

Nor is it a small thing that nature and reason have done in this: the human being is the only animal that senses what order is, what is fitting, what the right measure is in deeds and in words. No other animal perceives beauty, charm, or the harmony of parts even in the things the eye can see. Nature and reason carry that perception over, by analogy, from the eyes to the mind, and conclude that beauty, consistency, and order deserve to be preserved far more in our decisions and our deeds. They keep watch that we do nothing unbecoming or soft, and that in every opinion and every act we neither do nor think anything driven by mere appetite.

Out of these elements is forged and fashioned the thing we are looking for: moral goodness — the right. Even with no fame attached, it is still right; and we say truly that even if no one ever praised it, it is by its very nature worthy of praise.

Chapter 5

There, Marcus my son, you see the very shape of moral goodness — its face, so to speak — "which, if it could be seen with the eyes," as Plato says, "would awaken an astonishing love of wisdom." Everything that is right springs from one of four sources. It is found in the clear-eyed perception of truth, and skill in reaching it; or in preserving human fellowship — giving each person what is his, and keeping good faith in agreements; or in the greatness and strength of a lofty, unconquerable spirit; or in the order and measure of everything done and said, which is where moderation and self-control live.

These four are tied and woven together, yet each one singly gives rise to its own definite kinds of duty. Take the part listed first, where we place wisdom and practical wisdom: to it belongs the search for truth and its discovery, and that is this virtue's particular work. The more clearly a man perceives what is truest in any matter, and the more sharply and swiftly he can see the reason for it and explain it, the more he is considered wise and sound in judgment — and rightly so. So truth is, you might say, the raw material this virtue handles and works in.

The other three virtues have a different assignment: to provide and protect the things on which the active business of life depends — so that human society and connection are kept safe, and so that a spirit's excellence and greatness can shine out, both in building up resources and advantages for oneself and one's own, and far more in rising above those very things. Order, consistency, moderation, and everything like them belong to the sphere where actual action is required, not just the mind's activity. It is by bringing a certain measure and order to the affairs of daily life that we will preserve what is morally good and honorable.

Chapter 6

Of the four headings into which we have divided the nature and force of the right, the first — knowledge of truth — touches human nature most closely. All of us are pulled and drawn toward the desire to learn and to know; we think it a beautiful thing to excel at it, and we count it bad and shameful to slip, to go wrong, to be ignorant, to be deceived. In this pursuit — natural and right as it is — there are two faults to avoid. The first is treating the unknown as known and signing off on it rashly. Whoever wants to escape that fault (and everyone should want to) will give real time and care to weighing the evidence. The second fault is the opposite: some people pour enormous study and effort into subjects that are obscure, difficult — and unnecessary.

Avoid those two faults, and whatever work and care you spend on subjects that are right and worth knowing will earn well-deserved praise. So it was with Gaius Sulpicius in astronomy, as we have heard, and with Sextus Pompeius in geometry, whom I knew myself; so with many in logic and more still in civil law. All these arts are engaged in the search for truth. But to let that search pull you away from getting things done is contrary to duty. All the praise virtue earns, it earns in action. Still, action pauses often enough, and gives us many openings to return to study; and the mind's motion, which never rests, can keep us occupied with questions of knowledge without any effort on our part. In the end, all our thinking and every movement of the mind will be spent either on deciding about things that are right — things that bear on living well and happily — or on the pursuits of knowledge and understanding.

So much for the first source of duty.

Chapter 7

Of the three sources that remain, the widest reach belongs to the principle that holds human society together — the shared life, as we might call it. It has two parts. The first is justice, in which virtue shines at its brightest, and from which good men get their very name. Joined to it is the second, generosity, which you may also call kindness or open-handedness.

The first task of justice is this: that no one harm anyone else, unless provoked by a wrong. The next: that we treat common property as common, and private property as our own.

Now, nothing is private by nature. Property becomes private through long-standing occupation, as with people who moved into empty land long ago; or through victory, as with those who took possession in war; or by law, by bargain, by agreement, by lot. That is how the territory of Arpinum comes to be called the Arpinates', and the Tusculan land the Tusculans'; and the marking-out of private holdings follows the same pattern. So, since each person's share of what nature made common has become his own, let each keep what has fallen to him. Whoever grabs for more than that violates the law of human society.

But we are not born for ourselves alone. As Plato wrote so memorably, our country claims a share of us, our friends another. And since, as the Stoics hold, everything the earth produces is created for human use, and human beings themselves are born for the sake of human beings — so that they can be of service to one another — here we ought to follow nature as our guide: to put shared benefits into the common pool through the exchange of duties, by giving and by receiving, and to bind person to person in society through our skills, our labor, and our means.

The foundation of justice is good faith — that is, standing truly and steadily by what you have said and what you have agreed. And so — even if this strikes someone as a stretch — let us dare to imitate the Stoics, who love to dig up where words come from, and accept that good faith, fides, got its name because what is said gets done, fiat.

Injustice comes in two kinds. One belongs to the people who inflict a wrong; the other to the people who fail to shield the victims from it when they could. The man who unjustly attacks someone, whipped up by anger or some other passion, is like a man laying hands on his own comrade. But the man who does not defend against a wrong, does not stand in its way when he can, is as much at fault as if he had abandoned his parents, or his friends, or his country. Notice, too, that wrongs inflicted deliberately, for the sake of harming, often begin in fear: the man plotting to hurt another is afraid that unless he strikes first, he will suffer some harm himself. For the most part, though, people set out to do wrong to get their hands on what they crave — and within that vice, greed covers the widest ground.

Chapter 8

Riches are pursued partly for the necessities of life, partly for the enjoyment of pleasures. In men with bigger ambitions, the appetite for money looks toward influence and the power to bestow favors — as Marcus Crassus said not long ago: no fortune is large enough for a man who means to be first in the state, unless he can feed an army on its income. Splendid establishments and a style of living elegant and abundant give pleasure too; and the result of all this is that the appetite for money has become bottomless. Not that growing your estate deserves blame, so long as it hurts no one — but wrongdoing is always to be shunned.

Most people, however, arrive at a real forgetfulness of justice when they fall into craving commands, high office, and glory. Ennius says, "No fellowship is sacred, no faith is kept, where a throne is at stake" — and the truth of it stretches wider than thrones. Wherever a situation allows only one person to come out on top, the competition usually gets so fierce that keeping any "sacred fellowship" becomes nearly impossible. The recklessness of Gaius Caesar proved it just now: he overturned every law of gods and men for the sake of the supremacy that his own deluded imagination had dreamed up for him. And here is the troubling part: it is precisely in the greatest spirits and the most brilliant minds that cravings for honor, command, power, and glory tend to spring up. All the more reason to take care that nothing goes wrong on that ground.

In every injustice, though, it matters enormously whether the wrong is done in some disturbance of the mind — which is usually brief and momentary — or deliberately and with forethought. Wrongs that break out of a sudden impulse are lighter than wrongs planned and prepared in advance.

But enough now about inflicting wrong.

Chapter 9

The reasons people skip the defense of others and desert that duty tend to be many. They do not want to take on enemies, or effort, or expense; or carelessness, laziness, and sluggishness hold them back; or they are so wrapped up in their own studies and occupations that they let the people they ought to protect fall by the wayside. Because of this, I suspect Plato's claim about philosophers falls short. They are just, he says, because they are absorbed in the search for truth, and because they despise, and count as nothing, the things most people chase so hotly and fight each other tooth and nail to win. Well — they achieve one kind of justice, in that they inflict wrong on no one, but they tumble into the other kind: kept back by their devotion to learning, they abandon the very people they ought to protect. Plato even thinks they will not enter public life at all unless forced to. But it would be fairer for it to happen willingly; because even an action that is right in itself is just only if it is voluntary.

There are also people who — out of devotion to guarding their own property, or out of a kind of distaste for their fellow human beings — say they are simply minding their own business, and are convinced they wrong no one. They are free of one kind of injustice and walk straight into the other: they desert the shared life, because they contribute nothing to it — none of their attention, none of their effort, none of their means.

So then: we have laid out the two kinds of injustice and attached the causes of each, and earlier we established what justice itself consists of. From here it will be easy to judge what our duty is at any given moment — unless we are far too much in love with ourselves. Caring about other people's affairs is genuinely hard. Terence's old Chremes may "think nothing human is foreign to him"; still, we perceive and feel what happens to us, good or bad, more sharply than what happens to others — which we see as if from a great distance — and so we judge their cases differently from our own. That is why the advice is sound: never do a thing when you are in doubt whether it is fair or unfair. Fairness shines by its own light; doubt is a sign the mind is already contemplating a wrong.

Chapter 10

But times often come when the very actions that seem most worthy of a just man — a "good man," as we say — turn around and become their opposites: returning what was entrusted to you, keeping a promise. Things that belong to truth and good faith can shift, until the just course is to break them rather than keep them. What matters is to refer back to the foundations of justice I laid down at the start: first, harm no one; second, serve the common benefit. When circumstances change these, duty changes with them; it is not always the same. A promise or agreement can turn out so that keeping it damages either the person it was made to or the person who made it. Take the story from the tragedies: if Neptune had not kept his promise to Theseus, Theseus would not have lost his son Hippolytus. Of the three wishes Neptune had granted him, so the story runs, this was the third — the one he wished, in a fit of rage, for Hippolytus' destruction. It was granted, and it plunged him into the blackest grief. So promises are not to be kept when keeping them harms the very people you made them to. And if a promise would hurt you more than it helps the person you made it to, it is no offense against duty to put the greater good before the lesser. Suppose you have agreed to appear in person as someone's advocate, and meanwhile your son falls seriously ill: it is not against duty to fail to do what you said. It is the other man who strays from duty if he complains of being abandoned. And promises extorted by fear, or won by deceit — who fails to see that those do not bind at all? Most of them are voided by the edict of the praetor, Rome's senior judge; some by the laws themselves.

Injustice also springs up, again and again, from a kind of trickery: an over-clever but malicious reading of the law. This is what gave us the saying, worn smooth by now with use: "Law at its strictest, injustice at its worst." Plenty of wrong gets done under this heading even between states. There was the commander who, after a thirty-day truce had been struck with the enemy, went out ravaging their fields by night — because, he said, the truce had bargained for days, not nights. Nor does our own countryman deserve approval, if the story about Quintus Fabius Labeo is true — or whoever it was, for I have nothing beyond hearsay. Appointed by the senate to arbitrate a boundary dispute between Nola and Naples, he arrived at the site and spoke with each side separately: don't be greedy about this, he told them, don't grab; better to step back a little than push forward. Both sides did as he asked — and a strip of land was left over in the middle. So he set their boundaries exactly where they themselves had said; and the strip left in the middle he awarded to the Roman people. That is swindling, not judging. Cleverness of that kind is to be avoided in every affair, always.
