Book II: Expediency

(Part 1 of 3)

Chapter 1

How duties are drawn from the right, Marcus my son — from moral goodness and from every kind of virtue — I think I explained well enough in the previous book. What comes next is to work through the kinds of duty that concern the equipment of life: the means of getting the things people use, influence, resources. Here, as I said, the questions are: what is useful and what is not, and then, among useful things, which is more useful and which most useful of all. I will take these up — after a few words, first, about my purpose here and my own judgment.

My books have stirred quite a few people to an appetite not only for reading but for writing. Even so, I sometimes worry that certain good men find the very name of philosophy hateful, and wonder why I pour so much work and time into it.

My answer is this. As long as the republic was being run by the men to whom she had entrusted herself, I gave her all my care and all my thought. But when one man's mastery came to hold everything, and there was no longer any room anywhere for counsel or authority; and when, finally, I had lost my partners in guarding the republic — men of the highest rank — I did not surrender to the grief that would have crushed me if I had not fought it, and I did not surrender, on the other side, to pleasures unworthy of an educated man.

How I wish the republic had held the footing it started from, instead of falling into the hands of men eager not so much to change the state as to demolish it! Then, first, I would be spending my effort on action rather than on writing, as I used to do while the republic stood; and second, I would be committing to paper not these essays but my own public acts, as I so often did before. But when the republic — where all my care, thought, and work used to go — ceased to exist entirely, those writings of the forum and the senate naturally fell silent. And since my mind could not simply do nothing, I decided that the most honorable way to lay down my troubles was to return to philosophy, where I had lived from the start of my life. As a young man I gave it a great deal of time as a student; but once I began to chase office and handed myself over entirely to the state, philosophy got only whatever room was left over from friends and public business — and all of that went into reading. There was no leisure to write.

Chapter 2

So out of the greatest evils I seem to have salvaged at least this much good: the chance to put into writing things our countrymen did not know well enough, things richly worth knowing. For what, in the name of the gods, is more desirable than wisdom? What is finer? What is better for a human being, what more worthy of one? The people who go after it are called philosophers, and philosophy, if you translate the word, is nothing other than the pursuit of wisdom. And wisdom, as the old philosophers defined it, is knowledge of things divine and human, and of the causes that hold those things together. If a man sneers at studying that, I honestly cannot see what he thinks does deserve praise. If it is delight for the mind you want, and rest from cares, what can compare with the pursuits of people who are always searching out something that looks toward living well and happily? If it is a plan for steadiness and virtue, either this is the art by which we reach them or there is no art at all. To say that there is no art of the greatest things, when not even the smallest things lack one, is the talk of people who speak without thinking and go wrong about what matters most. And if there is some school of virtue, where will you look for it once you have walked away from this kind of learning?

Now, when I am urging someone toward philosophy, I usually argue all this more carefully — I have done it in another book. Just now I only needed to explain why, stripped of my duties to the state, I turned to this study above all others.

But an objection is raised against me, and by learned and educated men at that: am I being consistent? Our school says nothing can be grasped with certainty — and yet here I am, in the habit of holding forth on all sorts of subjects, and at this very moment laying down rules of duty. I wish these critics understood our position properly. We are not people whose minds drift in confusion with nothing to steer by. What kind of mind would that be — what kind of life, rather — with every standard thrown away, not just for arguing but for living? No: where other schools say some things are certain and others uncertain, we differ and say some things are probable, others not.

What, then, is to stop me from following whatever looks probable to me, rejecting whatever does not, and — while avoiding the arrogance of flat assertion — steering clear of the recklessness that is as far from wisdom as anything can be? And if our school argues against every position, that is because the probable itself cannot shine out unless the case has been fought from both sides.

But I explained all this carefully enough, I think, in my Academics. And though you, my Cicero, are working in the oldest and noblest philosophy, with Cratippus for your guide — a man very like the ones who fathered those famous doctrines — I still did not want our school, which lives next door to yours, to be unknown to you.

But now let us get on with the plan.

Chapter 3

Five headings, then, were laid out for tracking down duty. Two concerned what is fitting and moral goodness; two concerned life's conveniences — resources, wealth, means; and the fifth concerned how to choose, if ever the ones I have named seemed to clash. The part about moral goodness is finished — and that is the part I most want you to know inside and out.

What we take up now is the very thing called "the useful." On this word, usage has slipped off the road. Little by little it has drifted to the point of splitting the right from advantage — of granting that something can be right without being useful, and useful without being right. Nothing more destructive than this could have been brought into human life.

Philosophers of the very highest authority draw these three ideas apart strictly and honorably in thought, though in reality they run together. Whatever is just, they hold, is also useful; and likewise whatever is right is just; from which it follows that whatever is right is also useful. People who see this poorly are the ones who gape admiringly at sly, clever operators and mistake malice for wisdom. That error has to be torn out, and their whole way of thinking brought over to this hope: that it is by honorable plans and just deeds, not by fraud and malice, that they can get what they want.

Now, of the things that keep human life going, some are inanimate — gold and silver, what grows from the earth, other things of that kind — and some are alive, with their own drives and appetites. Of the living, some lack reason and some use it. Without reason: horses, oxen, the rest of the herd animals, and bees, whose work produces something for human use and human life. Of those that use reason there are held to be two kinds: gods and men. Devotion and clean living will keep the gods favorable; and right after the gods, second only to them, it is men who can be most useful to men.

The same division applies to things that harm and hinder. But since people do not believe the gods do harm, the gods drop out — and the conclusion is that men do the most damage to men.

Even the inanimate things we just mentioned are mostly the product of human work. We would not have them at all if hands and skill had not been applied, and we could not use them without human management. Care of the sick, seafaring, farming, the gathering and storing of grain and the rest of the earth's produce — none of it could have existed without human labor. And there would certainly be no exporting of our surpluses or importing of what we lack, if men did not perform those services. By the same reasoning, no stone would be cut from the earth for our needs, and no "iron, copper, gold, and silver, hidden deep away" would be dug out, without men's labor and men's hands.

Chapter 4

And roofs — shelter to drive off the bite of the cold and ease the misery of the heat: where could the human race have gotten it in the beginning, or found help afterward, when storm or earthquake or sheer age brought a building down, if shared life had not taught men to turn to one another for aid in such things? Add the aqueducts, the channeling of rivers, the irrigation of fields, the breakwaters set against the waves, the harbors built by hand — where would we get any of it without human work? From these examples and many more, one thing is plain: whatever profit and advantage we take from inanimate things, we could never have captured any of it without human hands and human labor.

Finally, what profit or convenience could we draw from the animals, if men did not help? It was men, after all, who led the way in discovering what use we could make of each creature; and to this day, without human work, we could not feed them, tame them, protect them, or take what they yield in season. It is men, too, who kill the ones that do harm and capture the ones that can serve.

Why should I even count up the multitude of crafts without which life could not exist at all? How would the sick be helped, what pleasure would the healthy have, what food or comfort would there be, if so many crafts did not wait on us? It is in these things that the cultivated life of human beings stands so far above the feeding and existence of beasts. And cities could never have been built, or filled with people, without human beings banding together. Out of that came laws and customs; then the fair marking-out of rights and a settled discipline of living; those in turn brought gentler spirits and a sense of decency — and the result was a life better defended, in which, by giving and receiving, by lending and exchanging what we have, we go without nothing.

Chapter 5

I am running longer here than I need to. Who does not already see the point Panaetius spells out at such length — that no one, whether a general in war or a leader at home, could ever have done great and salutary things without the devoted support of other men? He cites Themistocles, Pericles, Cyrus, Agesilaus, Alexander, and says none of them could have accomplished so much without human help. He calls unnecessary witnesses to a case nobody disputes.

And yet, just as we win great advantages through men's cooperation and agreement, so there is no plague so detestable that it does not come to man from man. Dicaearchus, a great and prolific Peripatetic, wrote a book on the destruction of human life. He collects all the other causes — floods, pestilence, devastation, even sudden swarms of wild beasts, whose attacks, he shows, have wiped out whole tribes of men — and then he makes the comparison: far more human beings have been destroyed by the assaults of other human beings, that is, by wars and civil strife, than by every other calamity put together.

Since there is no room for doubt on this point — that men are the greatest help to men, and the greatest harm — I set this down as the special work of virtue: to win the hearts of men and bind them to one's own service. The useful things human life draws from inanimate objects and from the handling and use of animals we owe to the productive crafts; but the eager, ready devotion of men to enlarging our fortunes is called out by the wisdom and virtue of outstanding men. Indeed, virtue as a whole turns on roughly three things. The first is seeing clearly what is true and genuine in each situation — what fits with what, what follows from what, and what causes each thing. The second is reining in the mind's disturbed motions, which the Greeks call pathe, and making the appetites, which they call hormai, obedient to reason. The third is dealing with the people around us with moderation and skill, so that with their support we may have what nature requires, supplied in full and heaped high — and through those same people push back any harm that comes at us, take vengeance on those who have tried to hurt us, and punish them as far as fairness and humanity allow.

Chapter 6

By what means we can gain this ability — to win men's devotion and hold on to it — I will explain, and not much later; but a few things must be said first.

Who does not know the great power of fortune in both directions, toward success and toward disaster? When we run before her favoring breeze, we are carried to the harbor we hoped for; when she blows against us, we are wrecked. Still, fortune's own blows are the rarer kind: first, from inanimate nature — squalls, storms, shipwrecks, collapses, fires; then from beasts — kicks, bites, attacks. These, as I say, are the rarer thing. But the destruction of armies — three of them just recently, and often many more; the loss of commanders — lately of a very great and extraordinary man; the hatred of the crowd, and because of it the expulsion, ruin, and exile of citizens who often deserved well of their country; and on the other side the successes — offices, commands, victories: all of these, though chance runs through them, cannot come about in either direction without the resources and devotion of other men.

With that understood, I can now say how we may attract men's devotion and rouse it to our advantage. If the discussion runs long, set its length beside the size of the advantage; it may even come to look too short.

Whatever people give a man, then, to build him up and honor him, they give for one of these reasons. Out of goodwill, when for some reason they are fond of him. Out of respect, if they look up to his virtue and think him worthy of the most splendid fortune there is. Or because they trust him and believe he serves their interests well. Or because they fear his power. Or the reverse — because they expect something from him, as when kings or crowd-courting politicians hold out handouts. Or, last of all, because they are moved by pay and price — the filthiest motive of all and the dirtiest, both for the people gripped by it and for those who stoop to using it. For things are in a bad way when what ought to be won by virtue is attempted with money. But since this crutch is sometimes necessary, I will explain how to use it — after I have spoken of the things that stand closer to virtue.

Men also place themselves under another man's rule and power for several reasons. They are led by goodwill, or by the size of favors received, or by his outstanding rank, or by hope that it will pay them, or by fear of being forced to obey anyway, or they are captured by the hope of handouts and by promises — or, finally, as we have seen so often in our own republic, they are simply hired.

Chapter 7

Of all things, though, nothing is better suited to winning power and keeping it than to be loved — and nothing is worse suited than to be feared. Ennius put it perfectly: "Whom they fear, they hate; and whom a man hates, he wants dead."

That no amount of power can hold out against the hatred of the many — if that was unknown before, it has been learned recently. And it is not only the death of this tyrant, whose grip the city endured under force of arms and obeys even now that he is dead as much as ever, that shows how much men's hatred can do to destroy; it is the similar ends of the other tyrants, hardly one of whom has escaped such a death. Fear is a bad guardian of permanence; goodwill keeps faith, even to the end of time.

Grant that men who hold a conquered people down by force must use harshness — as a master must with his slaves, if they cannot be controlled any other way. But people who arrange things in a free city so that they will be feared — nothing could be more insane. However deep the laws are sunk by some man's power, however thoroughly freedom is cowed, sooner or later these things surface — in silent verdicts, or in secret ballots for office. And freedom bites harder after it has been interrupted than if it had never been touched. So let us embrace the rule that reaches widest and counts most, not only for safety but for wealth and power too: banish fear, hold on to affection. That way we will get what we want most easily, in private life and in public life alike.

Besides, men who want to be feared cannot help fearing the very people who fear them. Think of the elder Dionysius: what torture of dread he lived in, so afraid of the barber's razor that he singed off his own hair with a glowing coal. Think of Alexander of Pherae — what a state of mind he must have lived in. He loved his wife Thebe dearly, we read; and yet, coming from dinner to her bedroom, he had a barbarian — tattooed with Thracian markings, as the record says — walk ahead of him with a drawn sword, and he sent men of his bodyguard ahead of that to rifle through the lady's chests and make sure no weapon was hidden in her clothing. Miserable man, to think a barbarian, a branded slave, more faithful than his own wife! And he was not wrong: she killed him herself, over a suspicion of another woman.

No power of command is great enough to last while fear is pressing on it. Witness Phalaris, whose cruelty stands out beyond everyone's: he did not die by ambush, like the Alexander I just named, and not at the hands of a few, like this tyrant of ours — the whole population of Agrigentum charged him at once.

More: did not the Macedonians abandon Demetrius and carry themselves over to Pyrrhus as one man? And when the Spartans ruled unjustly, did not nearly all their allies desert them and stand by as idle spectators of the disaster at Leuctra?

Chapter 8

In matters like this I would rather recall foreign examples than our own. Still, this much. As long as the empire of the Roman people was held together by services, not by wrongs, our wars were fought either for our allies or for supremacy; the endings of our wars were merciful, or no harsher than necessary; the senate was a harbor and refuge for kings, peoples, and nations; and our magistrates and generals strained after one distinction above all — to have defended the provinces and the allies with fairness and good faith. So Rome could more truly be called the protectorate of the world than its empire.

This habit and discipline we were already letting slip, bit by bit, even before; after Sulla's victory we lost it completely. Once such cruelty had been shown to citizens, nothing done to allies could look unjust anymore. In Sulla's case, an honorable cause was followed by a victory that was not honorable: when he had planted the spear — the sign of a public auction — and was selling off in the forum the goods of good men, of wealthy men, of Roman citizens at the very least, he dared to announce that he was "selling his plunder." After him came a man whose cause was impious and whose victory was fouler still: he did not stop at confiscating the property of individual citizens, but swallowed whole provinces and regions in one common sentence of ruin.

And so, with foreign nations battered and destroyed, we watched a model of Massilia — Marseilles today — carried along in a triumph, an exhibit of our lost empire; we saw a triumph celebrated over the very city without which our generals never once triumphed in a war beyond the Alps. I could list many more outrages against our allies, if the sun had ever looked down on anything more shameful than this one. So we are being punished justly. If we had not let the crimes of so many go unpunished, license on this scale would never have descended to one man. His estate passed by inheritance to a few; his appetites passed to many scoundrels. And the seed and cause of civil wars will never be lacking so long as ruined men remember that bloody auction spear and hope to see another. Publius Sulla brandished it while his kinsman was dictator; and thirty-six years later the same man did not step back from a spear more criminal still. Another Sulla, who had been a clerk in the first dictatorship, was city quaestor — a treasury office — in this last one. From which you should understand: with prizes like these on offer, civil wars will never run out.

So the walls of the city stand and remain — and even they now dread the final extremes of crime — but the republic itself we have utterly lost. And we fell into these disasters (I am coming back to my subject now) because we preferred to be feared rather than to be held dear and loved. If this could happen to the Roman people for ruling unjustly, what should individual men expect for themselves? Since it is plain, then, that goodwill is strong and fear is feeble, the next task is to discuss how we can most easily win the affection we want — affection joined with honor and trust.

But we do not all need it in the same measure. Each man's plan of life decides whether he needs the love of many or whether the love of a few is enough. But let this much be fixed — the first thing, and the most necessary: to have the faithful friendship of people who love us and admire what we do. On this one point the greatest men and ordinary men are not far apart, and both must secure it almost equally.

Honor, glory, and the goodwill of fellow citizens — perhaps not everyone needs those equally. But whoever has them finds that they help a good deal, toward everything else and especially toward gaining friends.

Chapter 9

But friendship I have discussed in another book, the one called Laelius. Now let us talk about glory — though I have two books on that subject as well. Let us just touch on it here, since it helps so much in the management of greater affairs.

The highest and most complete glory rests on these three things: the people love you; they trust you; and, with a certain admiration mixed in, they judge you worthy of honor. These responses, to put it simply and briefly, are won from the many by nearly the same things that win them from individuals. But there is also another kind of approach to the many — a way of flowing, so to speak, into the hearts of everyone at once.

Of the three I named, take goodwill first, and the rules for winning it. Goodwill is captured above all by kind services. Second, it is stirred by the willingness to serve, even when the means happen to fall short. And the love of the crowd is powerfully moved by the mere name and reputation of open-handedness, of doing good, of justice, of good faith, and of all the virtues that belong to gentleness of character and ease of manner. For the very thing we call the right and the fitting pleases us in and of itself, and moves every heart by its own nature and beauty; and since it shines out — almost glows through — from the virtues I have just named, nature herself compels us to love the people we believe possess them. These are the weightiest causes of love; there can be some lighter ones besides.

Trust can be produced in two ways: people must judge that we have acquired practical wisdom joined with justice. For we trust those we think understand more than we do — who we believe can see ahead into the future and, when the moment of action comes and things reach a crisis, can clear a path and make the right call on the spot. That is what people count as useful and genuine practical wisdom. And trust is given to just and faithful men — good men, that is — on the understanding that in them there is no suspicion of fraud or wrongdoing. So it is to them that we think our safety, our fortunes, and our children are most rightly entrusted.

Of these two, justice has the greater power to create trust, since justice even without practical wisdom carries plenty of authority, while practical wisdom without justice is worthless for creating trust. The craftier and cleverer a man is, the more he is hated and suspected once his reputation for honesty is stripped away. So justice joined with intelligence will have all the strength it wants for creating trust. Justice without practical wisdom can do a great deal; practical wisdom without justice can do nothing.
