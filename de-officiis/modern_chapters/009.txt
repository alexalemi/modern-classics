Book III: The Conflict Between the Right and the Expedient

(Part 2 of 4)

Chapter 8

When something flashes an appearance of advantage at us, we can't help being stirred. But if, when you look closely, you see moral wrong attached to the very thing that carried the appearance of advantage, the conclusion is not that advantage must be sacrificed. The conclusion is that where there is wrongdoing, there was never any advantage to begin with. And if nothing is so contrary to nature as shameful conduct — for nature demands what is right, harmonious, and consistent, and rejects their opposites — and nothing so in accord with nature as advantage, then surely advantage and wrongdoing cannot live in the same act.

Consider it from another side. If we are born for moral goodness, and it is either the only thing worth pursuing, as Zeno held, or at least outweighs everything else put together, as Aristotle believes, then what is right must be either the sole good or the highest good. But whatever is good is certainly useful. So whatever is right is useful.

Here is where dishonest men go wrong: the moment they grab hold of something that looks useful, they cut it loose from the question of right. This is where daggers come from, and poisons, and forged wills; this is the root of theft, embezzlement of public money, the fleecing and plundering of allies and citizens; from this grows the craving for excessive wealth, for power no one should hold, and finally — even in free states — for kingship itself, and nothing fouler or uglier than that craving can be imagined. Their judgment is fooled: they see the profits, but they don't see the penalty. I don't mean the penalty of the law, which they often smash through — I mean the penalty of the wrongdoing itself, which is the bitterest of all.

So let us throw this whole breed of deliberators out of the discussion — the lot of them are criminal and godless — the ones who sit weighing whether to follow what they can see is right or to knowingly dirty their hands with crime. The crime is already in the hesitation, even if they never go through with it. Some things should not be deliberated at all, because the deliberating itself is a disgrace.

And banish from every deliberation the hope, the very thought, of covering it up and getting away with it. If we have made any progress in philosophy at all, we should be thoroughly convinced of this: even if we could hide our act from every god and every human being, we must still do nothing greedy, nothing unjust, nothing lustful, nothing out of control.

Chapter 9

This is where Plato brings in the famous Gyges. After heavy rains had split the earth open, Gyges climbed down into the chasm and noticed — so the story goes — a bronze horse with doors set in its sides. He opened them and found the body of a dead man of superhuman size, with a gold ring on its finger. He pulled off the ring and put it on — he was one of the king's shepherds — and then rejoined the shepherds' gathering. There he made a discovery: whenever he turned the ring's bezel in toward his palm, he became invisible to everyone, while he himself saw everything; turn it back, and he was visible again. Using the opening the ring gave him, he seduced the queen, and with her help murdered the king, his own master; he removed everyone he thought stood in his way, and in all these crimes not one person could see him. And so, by the kindness of a ring, he suddenly rose to be king of Lydia.

Now, if a wise man held that very ring, he would not think himself one bit freer to do wrong than if he didn't have it. Good men are after what is right, not what can be hidden.

At this point certain philosophers — not bad men, just not very sharp — object that Plato has served up a made-up, fanciful tale. As if he were claiming it actually happened, or even could happen! The whole force of the ring, the whole point of the example, is this: if no one would ever know, no one would even suspect, when you did something for money, power, mastery, or lust — if the act would stay hidden from gods and men forever — would you do it? They answer that it can't happen. Of course it can't. But my question is: if the thing they call impossible were possible, what would they do? They dig in like clods. It can't happen, they insist, and they stick to that — they can't see what the word "if" is doing. When we ask what they would do if they could hide it, we are not asking whether they can hide it. We are putting them on a kind of rack: if they answer that with impunity guaranteed they would do whatever paid best, they confess they are criminals; if they say they would not, they concede that everything shameful must be avoided for its own sake.

But let's get back to the subject.

Chapter 10

Cases come up all the time that throw the mind into confusion with an appearance of advantage. The question in these cases is not whether to abandon what is right for the sake of some huge advantage — that is simply wicked — but whether the apparently useful thing can be done without doing wrong. When Brutus stripped his colleague Collatinus of his command, it could look like an injustice; Collatinus had been Brutus's partner and right hand in the plot that drove out the kings. But the leading men had decided that every relative of Superbus, the very name of the Tarquins, and every reminder of the monarchy had to go. The useful course — looking out for the country — was so clearly right that even Collatinus himself should have approved it. Advantage won the day because of moral goodness; without that, there would have been no advantage at all.

Not so with the king who founded our city. There, the appearance of advantage struck home. It seemed more useful to him to reign alone than to share the throne with another — so he killed his brother. He threw away family duty and human feeling to grab something that seemed useful and wasn't, and then he offered the wall as his excuse — a show of rightness that was neither believable nor remotely adequate. He did wrong, then — with all due respect to Quirinus, or to Romulus, let me say it.

And yet we are not required to neglect our own advantages and hand them over to others when we need them ourselves. Each of us should serve his own interest, as far as he can without wronging anyone else. Chrysippus put it neatly, as he usually does: "A man running a race ought to strain and drive with everything he has to win; but trip the man he's racing, or shove him off with a hand — never. So in life: it isn't unfair for each of us to seek what serves our needs; we have no right to snatch it from someone else."

It is in friendships that duties get most tangled. To refuse a friend what you rightly could do for him is a breach of duty; so is doing for him what is not fair. But for this whole class of cases the rule is short and easy. The things that look like advantages — offices, riches, pleasures, and the rest — must never be ranked above friendship. But a good man will never act against the state, or against his oath and good faith, for a friend's sake — not even if he sits as judge over that very friend. He takes off the role of friend when he puts on the role of judge. He will grant friendship only this much: he will hope his friend's case is the true one, and he will accommodate the timing of the trial as far as the laws allow. But when the moment comes to give a verdict under oath, let him remember that he is calling God as his witness — which means, as I understand it, his own mind, the most divine thing God has given to man. That is why the custom our ancestors handed down — if only we still kept it — is such a fine one: to ask of the juror only "what he can do without breaking faith." That request covers exactly what I said a moment ago a judge may honorably grant a friend. Because if we were bound to do everything our friends wanted, such things would have to be counted conspiracies, not friendships. And I am speaking of ordinary friendships; among perfectly wise men, nothing of the kind can even arise.

They tell of Damon and Phintias, the Pythagoreans, that the bond between them ran this deep: when the tyrant Dionysius had fixed the day of execution for one of them, and the condemned man asked for a few days to put his family's affairs in order, the other stood surety for his return — on the terms that if his friend did not come back, he himself would die. When the friend returned on the appointed day, the tyrant was so struck by their good faith that he begged them to enroll him as the third member of their friendship.

So then: when what looks like advantage in a friendship is weighed against what is right, let the appearance of advantage fall flat and the right prevail. And when a friend asks for what is not right, let conscience and good faith come before friendship. That gives us what we are looking for — a way of choosing between duties.

Chapter 11

In public affairs, wrong is done through the appearance of advantage more often than anywhere else — as our own people did in the destruction of Corinth. The Athenians were harsher still: they decreed that the Aeginetans, whose strength was their fleet, should have their thumbs cut off. It seemed useful; Aegina sat too close to Piraeus and loomed too large. But nothing cruel is useful. Cruelty is the greatest enemy of human nature, and human nature is what we ought to follow. Those who bar foreigners from the use of their cities and drive them out do wrong too — Pennus did it in our fathers' time, Papius recently. It is right, of course, that a man who is not a citizen not pass himself off as one; that law was carried by Crassus and Scaevola, the wisest of consuls. But shutting foreigners out of the use of the city altogether is plainly inhuman.

Splendid, by contrast, are the cases where the appearance of public advantage is treated with contempt next to moral goodness. Our republic is full of such examples, and never more than in the Second Punic War: after the catastrophe at Cannae, Rome showed greater spirit than she ever showed in success — not one sign of fear, not one mention of peace. Such is the force of the right: it eclipses the appearance of advantage.

When the Athenians could not withstand the Persian onslaught by any means, and decided to abandon their city, place their wives and children at Troezen, board the ships, and defend the freedom of Greece with their fleet, a man named Cyrsilus urged them to stay in the city and let Xerxes in. They stoned him to death. And yet he seemed to be pursuing advantage — but there was no advantage in it, because the right stood against it.

After the victory in the war with Persia, Themistocles announced in the assembly that he had a plan for the safety of the state, but that it must not be publicly known; he asked the people to appoint someone he could share it with. They appointed Aristides. Themistocles told him that the Spartan fleet, hauled ashore at Gytheum, could be secretly set on fire — and with that done, Spartan power would inevitably be broken. Aristides heard him out, then walked into the assembly, where everyone waited on his word, and said that the plan Themistocles brought was extremely useful — and anything but right. And so the Athenians reasoned that what was not right was not even useful, and on Aristides' word they rejected the whole scheme without so much as hearing it. They were better at this than we are — we let pirates sail free of tribute while our allies pay it.

Chapter 12

Let this stand fixed, then: what is shameful is never useful, not even when you gain the thing you thought useful. The mere act of thinking a shameful thing useful — that is the disaster. But as I said above, cases keep arising in which advantage seems to clash with the right, and we have to look carefully: is the clash real, or can the two be reconciled? Questions of this kind: suppose, for example, a good man has shipped a large cargo of grain from Alexandria to Rhodes, at a time when the Rhodians are suffering shortage and famine and grain is fetching extreme prices. Suppose he also knows that several merchants have set sail from Alexandria, and on his voyage he has seen their ships, loaded with grain, making for Rhodes. Does he tell the Rhodians? Or does he keep quiet and sell his own stock for the best price he can get? We are imagining a wise and good man. The question concerns the inner deliberation of someone who would not hide the facts from the Rhodians if he judged that shameful — but who is genuinely unsure whether it is shameful.

In cases like these, Diogenes of Babylon, a great and weighty Stoic, tends to answer one way, and his student Antipater, one of the sharpest minds alive, another. Antipater says everything must be laid open, so that the buyer is ignorant of nothing at all that the seller knows. Diogenes says the seller must declare defects as far as the civil law requires, and beyond that deal without trickery — and, since he is selling, sell for the best price he can.

"I shipped it, I put it on the market, I sell my goods for no more than the others charge — maybe less, now that supply is larger. Who is wronged?"

Up rises Antipater's argument on the other side: "What are you saying? You, who ought to look out for your fellow men and serve human society — you who were born on those terms, and carry those first principles of nature which you are bound to obey and follow, that your advantage should be the common advantage and the common advantage in turn yours — you will hide from people the relief and plenty that is on its way to them?"

Diogenes will perhaps answer: "Hiding is one thing; staying silent is another. I am not hiding anything from you right now just because I don't tell you the nature of the gods or the highest good — things that would profit you far more to know than the fact that wheat is about to get cheap. I am under no obligation to tell you everything it would be useful for you to hear."

"Oh yes you are," the other will say, "if you remember that nature has bound human beings together in one society."

"I remember," he will reply. "But is that society the kind in which nothing belongs to anyone? If so, then nothing should be sold at all — only given away."

Chapter 13

You see that in this whole dispute, nobody says, "This is shameful, but it pays, so I'll do it." One side argues that it pays and is not shameful; the other, that precisely because it is shameful, it must not be done.

Suppose a good man is selling a house because of certain defects that he knows and no one else does. It is unhealthy, but has the reputation of being healthy; nobody knows that snakes turn up in every bedroom; it is badly timbered and near collapse — but no one knows that except the owner. My question: if the seller doesn't tell the buyers, and sells the house for far more than he expected to get, has he acted unjustly or dishonestly?

"He certainly has," says Antipater. "At Athens, refusing to show a lost man the road is banned under public curses. What is this, if not that — letting a buyer rush ahead and run, through his own mistake, into a ruinous loss? In fact it is worse than not showing the road. It is knowingly leading another man astray."

Diogenes takes the other side: "Did he force you to buy? He didn't even encourage you. He listed for sale what he no longer wanted; you bought what you did want. If people who advertise 'a fine, well-built villa' aren't judged to have cheated anyone even when the place is neither fine nor sensibly built, then surely those who simply didn't praise their house are cheating even less. Where the buyer is free to use his own judgment, what fraud can there be on the seller's part? And if not even everything that is said has to be made good, do you really think what was never said must be? What could be more foolish than a seller reciting the defects of the thing he's selling? What could be more absurd than an auctioneer crying out, on the owner's orders, 'Unhealthy house for sale'?"

So this is how it goes in certain doubtful cases: on one side, the right is defended; on the other, advantage is argued in such a way that doing the apparently useful thing is not merely right — failing to do it would be shameful. This is that clash which seems to break out so often between the useful and the right. And these cases have to be decided; I didn't set them out to raise questions, but to settle them. My verdict, then: the grain merchant should not have kept the Rhodians in the dark, and this house-seller should not have kept his buyers there. Not everything you keep quiet about counts as hiding. Hiding is when, for your own profit, you want people to remain ignorant of something you know — people with a real stake in knowing it. And who can't see what sort of hiding that is, and what sort of man practices it? Certainly not an open man, not a straightforward one, not honest, not just, not good. No — a shifty man, murky, cunning, deceitful, malicious, crafty, a sly old hand at fraud. Isn't it obviously bad business to take on so many names for vice — and plenty more besides?

Chapter 14

If those who merely kept quiet deserve blame, what should we think of those who added outright lies? Gaius Canius, a Roman knight, a witty and reasonably well-read man, had taken himself to Syracuse — for loafing, as he liked to put it, not for lawyering. He kept telling everyone he wanted to buy a little estate with gardens where he could invite his friends and enjoy himself without interruptions. Word got around, and a certain Pythius, who ran a banking house at Syracuse, told him he had just such gardens. They weren't for sale, but Canius could treat them as his own, if he liked — and in the same breath he invited the man to dinner in the gardens the next day. Canius accepted. Then Pythius, who as a banker had pull with people of every class, called the local fishermen to him, asked them to do their fishing in front of his gardens the next day, and told them exactly what he wanted them to do. Canius came to dinner on time. Pythius had laid on a magnificent spread. Before their eyes bobbed a whole crowd of little boats; one fisherman after another came up with his catch, and the fish were tossed down at Pythius's feet.

"Tell me, Pythius," said Canius, "what is this? So many fish? So many boats?"

"No surprise," said Pythius. "This is where all the fish in Syracuse are. This is where the fresh water comes in. These men can't do without this place."

Canius caught fire with desire and pressed Pythius to sell. Pythius dragged his feet — at first. To make a long story short, he got his way. The man was rich, and he wanted the place badly: he bought at the price Pythius named, and bought it furnished. Pythius entered the debt in his books and closed the deal. The next day Canius invited his friends over and arrived early himself. Not a single oar-pin in sight. He asked his nearest neighbor whether it was some fishermen's holiday — he couldn't see a soul out there.

"Not that I know of," said the neighbor. "But nobody fishes here. Yesterday I couldn't work out what on earth was happening."

Canius was livid. But what could he do? My colleague and friend Gaius Aquilius had not yet published his legal formulas on malicious fraud. When people asked him what "malicious fraud" in those formulas meant, he would answer: when one thing is pretended and another done. A brilliant definition, as you would expect from a master of the craft. So Pythius — and everyone else who does one thing while pretending another — is faithless, dishonest, and malicious. And nothing such men do can be useful, stained as it is with so many vices.

Chapter 15

Now, if Aquilius's definition is correct, then pretense and cover-up must be banished from the whole of life. A good man will not pretend or conceal anything, whether to buy on better terms or to sell on them. Besides, this "malicious fraud" was already punished by the laws: in guardianship, by the Twelve Tables; in the swindling of the young, by the Plaetorian law; and, even without a statute, in every trial where the words "in good faith" are added to the formula. In other proceedings, these phrases stand out above the rest: in the arbitration over a wife's property, "the better and the fairer"; in cases of trust, "honest dealing, as between honest men." Well then — can any scrap of fraud hide inside "the better and the fairer"? Can anything be done deceitfully or maliciously under "honest dealing, as between honest men"? Malicious fraud, as Aquilius says, lives in pretense. So every lie must be cleared out of our contracts. The seller will not plant a fake bidder to run the price up; the buyer will not plant a man to bid low against him and hold it down; and if either comes to naming a price, he will name it once, and once only. Indeed, when Quintus Scaevola, son of Publius, asked that the price of a farm he was buying be named once and for all, and the seller named it, Scaevola said he valued it higher — and added a hundred thousand sesterces. No one denies that was the act of a good man; people deny it was the act of a wise one — as they would if he had sold for less than he could have gotten. There it is, the poisonous idea: the world counts some men good and other men wise. That is what lets Ennius say, "The wise man's wisdom is wasted if it cannot profit himself." True enough — if Ennius and I could only agree on what "profit" means.

Hecaton of Rhodes, a student of Panaetius, says in the books on duty he wrote for Quintus Tubero that "it is a wise man's part to look after his family fortune while doing nothing against custom, law, or institutions. For we want wealth not for ourselves alone, but for our children, relatives, and friends, and above all for the state; the means and resources of individuals are the riches of the city." A man like Hecaton can hardly approve of Scaevola's act, which I described a moment ago; for he openly declares that the only profit-seeking he will refrain from is the kind the law forbids. No great praise for such a man, and no gratitude either.

Either way the conclusion holds. If pretense and cover-up are both malicious fraud, then very few dealings are free of malicious fraud; and if the good man is the one who helps whom he can and harms no one, then that good man is certainly not easy to find.

So: doing wrong is never useful, because it is always shameful; and because being a good man is always right, it is always useful.

Chapter 16

As for the law of real estate, our civil law made this binding: in a sale, the defects known to the seller must be declared. Under the Twelve Tables it was enough to make good whatever had been formally stated aloud — and whoever denied his own statement paid double damages. But the jurists went further and established a penalty for silence as well: they ruled that any defect in a property, if the seller knew of it, must be made good unless it had been expressly declared. Here is a case. The augurs were preparing to take the auspices from the citadel, and they ordered Tiberius Claudius Centumalus, who owned a house on the Caelian Hill, to demolish the parts whose height blocked their view of the sky. Claudius promptly put the building up for sale, and Publius Calpurnius Lanarius bought it. The augurs then served the same notice on him. So Calpurnius did the demolishing — and when he learned that Claudius had listed the house only after the augurs had ordered him to tear it down, he hauled him before an arbiter on the formula "whatever he ought to give or do for him in good faith." The verdict was delivered by Marcus Cato — the father of our own Cato (other men take their distinguishing name from their fathers; this man, who fathered that shining light, deserves to be named from his son). Sitting as judge, he ruled: "since the seller knew the fact at the time of sale and did not declare it, he must make good the buyer's loss."

So he laid down the principle that good faith requires the buyer to be told of any defect the seller knows. If his ruling was right, then our grain merchant was wrong to keep silent, and so was the seller of the unhealthy house. Not every silence of this kind can be caught by the civil law; but the ones that can be caught are dealt with firmly. Marcus Marius Gratidianus, a relative of ours, had sold to Gaius Sergius Orata a house he himself had bought from that same Orata a few years earlier. The house carried a servitude — a standing legal claim on the property — but Marius had said nothing about it in the formal transfer. The case went to court. Crassus appeared for Orata; Antonius defended Gratidianus. Crassus pressed the letter of the law: "a defect the seller knew and did not declare must be made good." Antonius pressed fairness: "since the defect was hardly unknown to Sergius — he had sold the house himself — there was no need to say anything, and no one was deceived who knew exactly what legal condition came with the thing he bought."

Why am I telling you all this? So that you will understand: our ancestors had no patience with sharp operators.
