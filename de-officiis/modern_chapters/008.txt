Book III: The Conflict Between the Right and the Expedient

(Part 1 of 4)

Chapter 1

Cato — who was nearly his contemporary — records, Marcus my son, that Publius Scipio, the first to be called Africanus, used to say that he was never less idle than when idle, never less alone than when alone. A magnificent saying, and worthy of a great and wise man. It tells us that even at leisure his thoughts were on public business, and that in solitude he talked things over with himself — so that he was never at a standstill, and sometimes had no need of anyone else's conversation. The two things that make other men go slack — leisure and solitude — only sharpened him. I wish I could honestly say the same of myself. But if I cannot match such greatness of mind by imitation, I come as close as I can in wanting to. Shut out from the republic and from work in the courts by the arms of traitors, I am pursuing a life of leisure; that is why I have left the city, and as I wander the countryside I am often alone.

But this leisure of mine should not be set beside the leisure of Africanus, nor this solitude beside his. He was resting from the most splendid services to his country when he took time for himself now and then, and withdrew from the crowds and the press of people into solitude as into a harbor. My leisure comes from having no work to do, not from any desire for rest. With the senate snuffed out and the courts destroyed, what is there worthy of me to do in the senate house or in the forum? So I, who once lived in the fullest publicity, before the eyes of my fellow citizens, now flee the sight of the criminals who swarm on every side; I hide myself as much as I am allowed, and I am often alone. But the learned have taught me that among evils one should not only choose the least, but also squeeze out of those very evils whatever good may be in them. So I am making use of my leisure — though it is not the leisure owed to a man who once won peace and quiet for the whole state — and I refuse to let this solitude sit slack, though necessity imposed it on me, not choice.

And yet, in my judgment, Africanus earned the higher praise. No written monuments of his genius survive; no work of his leisure, no product of his solitude exists. From that we should understand that with his mind always in motion, always chasing down the questions his thinking pursued, he was never idle and never alone. I do not have that kind of strength — silent thought alone cannot pull me out of my solitude — so I have turned all my effort and care to this work of writing. And the result is that I have written more in the short time since the republic fell than I did in all the years it stood.

Chapter 2

Now the whole of philosophy, my Cicero, is fertile and fruitful, with no part of it left untilled or abandoned; but no ground in it is richer or more productive than the subject of duties, from which flow the rules for living a steady and honorable life. So although I trust you are hearing these things constantly from our friend Cratippus, the leading philosopher of the age, I still think it good for such words to ring in your ears from every direction — and, if it were possible, for you to hear nothing else. That goes for everyone who intends to lead an honorable life, but for no one, perhaps, more than for you. People expect you — and not a little — to imitate my industry; they expect much from you in public honors, and something, perhaps, in matching my name. You have also taken on a heavy responsibility in Athens and in Cratippus. You went to them as a man goes to market for the finest learning, and to come home empty-handed would be an utter disgrace, shaming the city's authority and your teacher's alike. So strain your mind as hard as it will go; work as hard as effort allows (if learning is really labor and not pleasure); and see to it that you deliver. Do not let anyone say that when I supplied everything you needed, you were the one who failed yourself.

But enough of that. I have written to you often, and at length, to spur you on. Let us get back to the remaining part of our plan.

Panaetius, then — beyond any dispute the most careful writer on duties, and the one I have chiefly followed, with some corrections of my own — laid out three headings under which people deliberate and weigh questions of duty: first, whether the act in question is right or shameful; second, whether it is useful or not; third, when something with the look of the right clashes with what seems useful, how the two ought to be told apart. The first two headings he worked through in three books; the third he said he would take up in its turn — and he never made good on the promise. That surprises me all the more because his student Posidonius records that Panaetius lived thirty years after publishing those books. And I am surprised that Posidonius touched the subject only briefly in certain notebooks of his — especially since he writes that no topic in all of philosophy is so essential.

I flatly disagree with those who say Panaetius did not overlook this topic but left it out deliberately — that it never should have been written at all, because advantage can never conflict with the right. On one point there is room for doubt: whether this heading, the third in Panaetius's scheme, ought to have been included or dropped entirely. On the other there is no room at all: Panaetius took it on and then left it unwritten. A man who has finished two parts of a three-part plan necessarily has the third still ahead of him; besides, at the end of his third book he promises to deal with this part in its turn. And Posidonius is a weighty witness to the same fact. He writes in one of his letters that Publius Rutilius Rufus, who had studied under Panaetius, used to say that just as no painter could be found to finish the portion of the Venus of Cos that Apelles left incomplete — the beauty of her face killed all hope of matching it in the rest of the body — so no one ever took up what Panaetius had passed over and left undone, because what he had finished was too excellent to follow.

Chapter 3

About Panaetius's intention, then, there can be no doubt. Whether he was right to bolt this third part onto the inquiry about duty is perhaps open to argument. For whether the right is the only good, as the Stoics hold, or whether — as your Peripatetics believe — the right is the highest good in such a way that everything piled on the other side of the scale would barely register a feather's weight, one thing is certain: advantage can never genuinely compete with the right. That is why, we are told, Socrates used to curse the men who first pried apart in theory what nature had joined together. The Stoics agreed with him fully: they held that whatever is right is useful, and that nothing is useful that is not right.

If Panaetius were the sort of man to say that virtue is worth cultivating because it produces advantage — like the philosophers who measure what is desirable by pleasure or by freedom from pain — then he could consistently say that advantage sometimes fights the right. But he is a man who judges that the right is the only good, and that the things which clash with it under some appearance of usefulness make life no better by arriving and no worse by leaving. So he should not, the argument runs, have introduced a deliberation in which what seems useful gets weighed against what is right. After all, when the Stoics name the highest good "living in agreement with nature," what they mean, as I understand it, is this: always be in harmony with virtue, and from everything else that accords with nature, choose only what does not fight against virtue. Since that is so, some people think this whole comparison was wrongly introduced, and that no instruction on the topic should have been given at all.

And yet the right in its true and proper sense exists only in the wise, and can never be torn away from virtue. In those whose wisdom is not perfect, that perfect right cannot exist at all — only likenesses of it. The duties we discuss in these books are the ones the Stoics call "middle" duties. They are common property, and their reach is wide; many people attain them through natural goodness of heart and through progress in learning. The duty they call "right" duty is another matter: complete and absolute, "having all the numbers," as the same school puts it, and possible for no one but the wise man. But when some act is done in which the middle duties show themselves, it looks fully complete, because ordinary people generally cannot see how far it falls short of perfection; as far as their understanding reaches, they think nothing is missing. The same thing happens with poems, paintings, and plenty else: the untrained enjoy and praise work that deserves no praise. The reason, I suppose, is that such work contains some genuine merit that catches the inexpert, who cannot judge the flaws in any given piece. So when the experts set them straight, they give up their opinion readily enough.

Chapter 4

These duties, then, the ones I am discussing in these books, are said by the Stoics to be a kind of second-tier right — not the private property of the wise, but shared with the whole human race. And so they stir everyone in whom there is any natural bent toward virtue. When the two Decii or the two Scipios are remembered as brave men, or Fabricius or Aristides is called just, no one is citing them as models of courage or justice the way one would cite a truly wise man. None of them was wise in the sense we want "wise" to carry. Even the men who were considered wise and carried the name — Marcus Cato, Gaius Laelius — were not; not even the famous Seven Sages. It was their constant practice of the middle duties that gave them a certain likeness to wise men, the outward look of wisdom.

So then: the truly right may never lawfully be weighed against a conflicting advantage — and the right as we commonly use the word, the kind cultivated by people who want to be counted good men, must never be weighed against profit either. We ordinary people are bound to guard and preserve the right that falls within our understanding just as strictly as the wise must guard what is right in the proper and true sense. Otherwise, whatever ground we have gained on the road to virtue cannot be held.

So much for those who count as good men because they keep their duties.

But those who measure everything by profits and gains, and refuse to let the right outweigh them — they are the ones who habitually, when deliberating, weigh the right against what they consider useful. Good men do not. So I believe that when Panaetius said people "tend to" hesitate over this comparison, he meant exactly what he said: that they tend to, not that they ought to. For it is utterly shameful not merely to value what seems useful above what is right, but even to compare the two at all — even to hesitate between them.

What is it, then, that does sometimes raise a doubt and seem to need thinking through? I believe it is this: the occasional uncertainty about what kind of act you are actually looking at. Circumstances often make something that is usually considered shameful turn out not to be shameful. Let me set out one example with a very wide reach. What greater crime can there be than to kill — not just a human being — but a close friend? Well then: has a man bound himself to a crime if he kills a tyrant, however close a friend? The Roman people do not think so; of all glorious deeds, they judge that one the most beautiful. Did advantage defeat the right, then? No — the right led the way, and advantage followed close behind.

So, in order that we may judge without error whenever what we call the useful seems to clash with what we understand to be right, some formula must be laid down; if we follow it when comparing courses of action, we will never desert our duty. And this formula will agree most closely with the reasoning and teaching of the Stoics. I follow them in these books for a simple reason. The older Academics, and your Peripatetics (who were once the same school as the Academics), do rank what is right above what seems useful; but these questions are argued more brilliantly by people who hold that whatever is right is useful and nothing useful that is not right, than by people who allow that something right can fail to be useful and something useful can fail to be right. And our own Academy grants us generous freedom: whatever strikes us as most probable, we are entitled to defend on our own authority. But back to the formula.

Chapter 5

Here it is, then. For one man to take something from another — to grow his own gain out of another man's loss — is more against nature than death, than poverty, than pain, than anything else that can strike our bodies or our outward possessions. Before anything else, it destroys human community and fellowship. For if we reach the point where each of us is ready to rob or wrong his neighbor for his own profit, then the fellowship of the human race — the thing most in accord with nature — necessarily breaks apart. Suppose each limb of the body got the idea that it could be strong by pulling its neighboring limb's health over to itself: the whole body would inevitably weaken and die. In the same way, if each of us grabs other people's goods for himself, taking whatever he can from everyone for his own gain, human society and community are inevitably overturned. That each man should prefer to win the necessities of life for himself rather than for someone else — nature has no quarrel with that. What nature will not allow is this: that we build up our means, our resources, and our wealth out of other people's plunder.

Nor is it only nature — that is, the law of nations — that lays this down. The laws of individual peoples, which hold each state's commonwealth together, decree the very same thing: no one may harm another for his own benefit. This is what laws aim at, this is what they want — that the union of citizens stay unbroken; and whoever tears it apart they punish with death, exile, chains, or fines.

And nature's own reason drives the point home far more forcefully still — reason, which is law both divine and human. Anyone willing to obey it (and all will obey it who want to live according to nature) will never bring himself to covet another man's property or to pocket what he has stripped from someone else. For loftiness and greatness of spirit — and likewise courtesy, justice, generosity — are far more in accord with nature than pleasure, than life itself, than riches; and to look down on those things, to count them as nothing when set against the common advantage, is the mark of a great and lofty spirit. But to strip another man for your own benefit is more against nature than death, than pain, than everything of that kind.

Likewise, it is more in accord with nature to shoulder the greatest labors and troubles to preserve and help all nations — if such a thing could be done — imitating the great Hercules, whom human gratitude, remembering his good deeds, seated in the council of the gods, than to live off by yourself in solitude, not merely free of every burden but wallowing in pleasures, rich in everything, outstanding even in beauty and strength. That is why every man of the finest and most brilliant nature prefers the first life to the second, and by a wide margin. From which it follows that a man who obeys nature cannot harm his fellow man.

Next: a man who wrongs another to gain some benefit for himself either thinks he is doing nothing against nature, or he judges that death, poverty, pain — even the loss of children, relatives, and friends — are more to be avoided than doing anyone an injustice. If he thinks nothing against nature happens when human beings are wronged, what is the point of arguing with him? He has stripped the human out of the human being. But if he grants that injustice should be avoided, yet ranks death, poverty, and pain as far worse, he is making a mistake: he is counting damage to body or fortune as heavier than damage to the soul.

Chapter 6

So there should be one goal set before us all: that the advantage of each individual and the advantage of everyone together be one and the same. If each man snatches it for himself alone, the whole human partnership will dissolve.

Go further. If nature prescribes that one human being should want to look out for another — whoever he may be, for the simple reason that he is human — then by that same nature the advantage of all is necessarily common. And if that is so, we are all held together by one and the same law of nature; and if that too is so, then certainly the law of nature forbids us to wrong one another. Now the first premise is true; therefore the conclusion is true as well. As for the position some people take — that they would take nothing from a parent or a brother for their own gain, but that the rest of their fellow citizens are another matter — it is absurd. Such men declare that they share no bond of right with their fellow citizens, no partnership for the common advantage; and that opinion tears the fellowship of the state to pieces.

Others say that fellow citizens must be respected but foreigners need not be; these men rip up the common fellowship of the human race. And when that is gone, kindness, generosity, goodness, and justice are destroyed to the root — and the men who destroy them must be judged impious even toward the immortal gods. For it was the gods who set up the fellowship among human beings that these men overturn; and the tightest bond of that fellowship is the conviction that it is more against nature for one man to strip another for his own gain than to endure every hardship there is — loss of goods, harm to the body, even wounds to the soul itself — provided justice is not involved; for justice is the one virtue that is mistress and queen of all the rest.

Perhaps someone will say: "Well then — if a wise man were starving to death, wouldn't he take food from another man, some fellow useful for nothing at all?" Not at all. For my life is not more useful to me than a certain disposition of my soul: that I wrong no one for my own gain. "What about this, then: if a good man could strip the clothes off Phalaris — that cruel and monstrous tyrant — to keep from freezing to death, wouldn't he do it?"

These cases are very easy to judge. If you take something from a man who is useful for nothing, purely for your own advantage, you have acted inhumanly and against nature's law. But if you are someone who, by staying alive, could bring great advantage to the state and to human society, and for that reason you take something from another — that is not to be condemned. Where the case is not like that, each man must bear his own hardship rather than strip away what belongs to someone else. Sickness, then, or want, or anything of the kind is not more against nature than seizing and coveting what is another's — but abandoning the common advantage is against nature, because it is unjust. And so nature's own law, which preserves and protects the advantage of mankind, will surely decree that the necessities of life pass from an idle and useless person to a man who is wise, good, and brave — a man whose death would subtract a great deal from the common good. Only let him take care that self-regard and self-love never become his pretext for doing wrong. On those terms, he will always do his duty, watching out for the advantage of mankind and for that human fellowship I return to so often.

As for Phalaris, the judgment could not be simpler. We have no fellowship with tyrants — rather, the widest possible gulf — and there is nothing against nature in stripping a man, if you can, whom it is right to kill; indeed this whole pestilent, godless breed ought to be driven out of human society altogether. Just as certain limbs are amputated when they begin to lose their blood and, as it were, their breath, and start to harm the rest of the body, so this savagery — a wild beast in the shape of a man — must be cut away from the common body of humanity.

Of this sort are all the questions in which duty has to be worked out from the circumstances of the moment.

Chapter 7

These, I believe, are the matters Panaetius would have gone on to pursue, if some accident or press of business had not cut short his plan. For questions of just this kind, his earlier books supply rules in plenty, from which one can see what must be avoided because it is shameful, and what need not be avoided because it is not shameful at all.

But since I am now setting the rooftop, so to speak, on a work begun and very nearly finished, I will do what the geometers do. They make a practice of not proving everything; they ask that certain things be granted them, so that they can explain what they want more easily. In the same way I ask you, my Cicero, to grant me — if you can — that nothing is worth seeking for its own sake except what is right. If Cratippus will not allow you that much, you will at least grant this: that what is right is the thing most worth seeking for its own sake. Either one is enough for me; sometimes the first, sometimes the second seems the more probable — and beyond these two, nothing seems probable at all.

And first, Panaetius deserves a defense on one point. He never said that the truly useful could sometimes clash with the right — his own principles barred him from saying it — only that what seemed useful could. That nothing is useful which is not also right, and nothing right which is not also useful, he attests again and again; and he says that no greater plague has ever invaded human life than the thinking of those who pulled these two apart. So the conflict he introduced was one that seems to exist, not one that does — and his purpose was never that we should sometimes prefer the useful to the right, but that we should be able to judge between them without error if they ever appeared to collide. This part, then, the part he left behind, I will finish out with no reinforcements — fighting, as the saying goes, under my own flag. For nothing worked out on this subject since Panaetius, at least nothing that has come into my hands, satisfies me at all.
