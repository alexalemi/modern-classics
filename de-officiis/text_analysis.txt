TEXT ANALYSIS — On Duties / De Officiis (Cicero, 44 BC)
Source: Gutenberg #47001, the 1913 Loeb edition. THIS TRANSLATION IS
MADE FROM CICERO'S LATIN — the first from-the-Latin volume in the
project (democracy2 did the same from Tocqueville's French). Walter
Miller's 1913 English translation, from the same edition, sits in
reference/NNN.txt as a comprehension crib ONLY.

HOW TO USE THE TWO TEXTS

- chapters/NNN.txt (Latin) is the source. Translate from it.
- reference/NNN.txt (Miller) is a crib: consult it to construe a knotty
  period or check a proper name, then put it down and write YOUR
  modern English from the Latin. Never paraphrase Miller — his 1913
  diction ("moral rectitude", "propriety", "in fine") is precisely the
  register we are replacing, and verify.py's ratio check runs against
  the LATIN (modern English should land 1.2–1.6x the Latin word count;
  the orchestrator runs verify.py --min-ratio 1.0 --max-ratio 1.8).

WHAT THIS IS

Cicero's last philosophical work, finished months after Caesar's
assassination and months before his own murder: a long letter of
practical ethics to his son Marcus, 21 and studying (loafing) in
Athens. Three books: I — what the right (honestum) requires, via the
four virtues; II — what is advantageous (utile); III — the famous
collision, arguing the conflict is always an illusion: nothing wrong
is ever truly advantageous. It was the second book ever printed after
the Gutenberg Bible, and the founders' standard manual of public
ethics — Adams quoted it constantly.

STYLE AND TONE

- A father writing to his son: warm, direct, occasionally needling
  (study harder, read my books too), always confident. Keep the
  second-person address alive — it is a letter, not a lecture.
- Cicero argues in long periodic sentences; break them into modern
  paragraphs of short, clear argument WITHOUT flattening the logical
  connectives (therefore, and yet, but suppose) that carry the
  reasoning.
- Constant concrete examples: Regulus returning to Carthage, the
  merchant at Rhodes, Gyges' ring, the collapsing house sale. These
  stories are the book's pulse — render them vividly, as stories.
- Cicero quotes poets (Ennius, Terence, his own verse) — brief verse
  stays inline in quotation marks; longer quotations as set-off lines.

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY (the argument depends on consistency)

- officium → "duty" (plural "duties").
- honestum / honestas → "the right" / "moral goodness" (never
  "honesty"; "what is right" where rhythm needs it).
- utile / utilitas → "the useful" / "advantage" (Book II-III's pivot;
  "expediency" allowed sparingly where the modern word earns it).
- decorum → "what is fitting" (the fitting; never "propriety").
- summum bonum → "the highest good".
- magnitudo animi → "greatness of spirit".
- beneficentia / liberalitas → "generosity" (the paired treatment in
  I.14+ may distinguish "doing good" and "open-handedness").
- temperantia → "self-control"; prudentia → "practical wisdom";
  iustitia → "justice"; fides → "good faith".
- res publica → "the republic" or "the state" as context wants.

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Written amid civil war: Caesar is condemned as a tyrant, his
  assassination praised; Cicero's own consulship (executing the
  Catilinarians) is defended. Render his positions as HIS, in a
  measured historical register — no both-sidesing, no amplification.
- Slavery appears as an ordinary institution (advice on treating
  slaves justly, I.13; the slave-sale disclosure rules, III.17+).
  Translate plainly and historically, per project policy.
- War ethics (fair dealing with enemies, the fetial law) and Roman
  imperialism are discussed approvingly — keep his frame, his voice.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- I.7: "we are not born for ourselves alone" (…our country claims a
  share of us, our friends another).
- I.22, Cicero quoting his own poem: "Let arms yield to the toga" —
  keep "toga" (the famous line; a brief appositive like "to civilian
  power" may follow it).
- III.1, Scipio via Cato: "never less idle than when idle, never less
  alone than when alone."
- "the highest good" must appear as the standing term for summum bonum.

FORMAT

- 12 files across 3 Books; each file: heading exactly as in the source
  file (e.g. "Book I: Moral Goodness"), then "(Part n of k)", then
  "Chapter N" standalone headings (arabic, as in the source files).
- Chapter numbering restarts each Book. Parts 2+ never re-introduce a
  book; non-final parts never conclude it.
