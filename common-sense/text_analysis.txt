TEXT ANALYSIS AND TRANSLATION STRATEGY
Common Sense by Thomas Paine (1776)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. OVERVIEW

Common Sense is a political pamphlet written by Thomas Paine and published
anonymously in Philadelphia on February 14, 1776. It argued for American
independence from Great Britain at a time when many colonists still hoped for
reconciliation. The pamphlet was a sensation — it sold roughly 500,000 copies
in a population of about 2.5 million, making it the bestselling work in
American history relative to population.

The text consists of an introduction, four main sections, and an appendix
(added in the second edition). The sections build a cumulative argument:
government is a necessary evil, monarchy is illegitimate, the colonies have
no reason to stay connected to Britain, and America has the resources to
succeed on its own.

Structure:
  000 - Title page and table of contents
  001 - Introduction (brief author's preface)
  002 - Of the Origin and Design of Government in General
  003 - Of Monarchy and Hereditary Succession
  004 - Thoughts on the Present State of American Affairs
  005 - Of the Present Ability of America
  006 - Appendix (response to the King's Speech + address to the Quakers)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

2. STYLE AND TONE

Paine's voice is distinctive: he writes like a brilliant pub debater who has
read everything. His style combines:

- DIRECTNESS: Paine was deliberately writing for common people, not elites.
  His sentences start strong and make clear points. Preserve this energy.

- RHETORICAL AGGRESSION: He uses rhetorical questions, challenges to the
  reader, and direct address ("I ask you..."). He names his opponents
  ("interested men," "weak men," "prejudiced men"). Keep this combative edge.

- EMOTIONAL INTENSITY: Paine shifts between cool logic and passionate outrage.
  The famous passages ("The sun never shined on a cause of greater worth,"
  "O ye that love mankind!") are powerful emotional crescendos. Preserve the
  rhythm and impact of these moments.

- WIT AND SARCASM: Paine is frequently funny and cutting. "A French bastard
  landing with an armed banditti" (on William the Conqueror); "Oliver Cromwell
  thanks you" (to the Quakers). Keep the humor sharp.

- ANALOGY AND METAPHOR: He constantly uses concrete analogies — government as
  dress, the colony as a child outgrowing milk, nature never making the
  satellite larger than the planet. These are already accessible; preserve them.

The overall tone is urgent, persuasive, and increasingly impassioned. He begins
with theoretical calm (Section I) and builds to fiery calls to action
(Sections III-IV) and pointed rebuke (Appendix).

3. KEY THEMES TO PRESERVE

- Government as necessary evil (not positive good)
- Natural rights vs. inherited privilege
- The illegitimacy of monarchy and hereditary rule
- America's unique opportunity to start fresh
- The practical case for independence (trade, military, geography)
- Time-sensitivity: the moment for action is NOW
- Religious arguments against monarchy (biblical case)
- The distinction between society and government

4. VOCABULARY MAPPINGS

Archaic/Formal → Modern Equivalent

PRONOUNS AND VERB FORMS:
- "hath" → "has"
- "doth" → "does"
- "shew/shewn" → "show/shown"
- "ye" → "you" (when addressing readers/groups)
- "thee/thou/thy" → "you/your" (in direct address; but KEEP in biblical quotations)
- "thereof/therein/thereon/therefrom" → "of it/in it/on it/from it" or rephrase
- "wherefore" → "therefore" / "which is why" / "so"
- "hither" → "here" / "to this place"
- "whence" → "from where" / "where...from"

CONNECTING WORDS:
- "viz." → "namely" / "that is" / "specifically"
- "i.e." → "that is" / "in other words"
- "&c." → "and so on" / "etc."
- "moreover" → keep or use "besides" / "what's more"
- "notwithstanding" → "despite" / "even though"
- "howbeit" → "however" / "even so"

COMMON ARCHAISMS:
- "connexion" → "connection"
- "expence" → "expense"
- "independance/independancy" → "independence"
- "encreases" → "increases"
- "hereafter" → "in the future" / "from now on"
- "heretofore" → "up to now" / "before this"
- "cotemporaries" → "contemporaries"
- "burthens" → "burdens"
- "reflexion" → "reflection"
- "felo de se" → "self-destruction" (with brief note if needed)
- "disesteem" → "social disapproval" / "public shame"
- "impowered" → "empowered"
- "negociate" → "negotiate"
- "subtility/subtile" → "cunning"/"subtle"
- "stile" → "style"
- "compliment" → "flatter" (when used in the old sense)
- "complimental" → "ceremonial"

POLITICAL/HISTORICAL TERMS:
- "the Crown" → keep as "the Crown" (still understood)
- "the commons" → "the House of Commons" or "the common people" (context-dependent)
- "peers" → "the nobility" / "the House of Lords"
- "the continent" → keep as "the continent" (meaning the American colonies collectively)
- "the provinces" → "the colonies"
- "mother country" / "parent country" → keep (Paine is debunking this metaphor)
- "Tories" → keep, but add brief context on first use
- "Whigs" → keep, but add brief context on first use

5. SENTENCE STRUCTURE APPROACH

Paine's sentences often run long with semicolons, colons, and parenthetical
asides. The strategy:

- BREAK LONG SENTENCES: Split at semicolons where each clause makes an
  independent point. A sentence with 3+ semicolons usually becomes 2-3
  sentences.

- PRESERVE SHORT PUNCHY SENTENCES: Paine already uses them for emphasis.
  "Time makes more converts than reason." Leave these alone.

- SIMPLIFY NESTED PARENTHETICALS: Paine often inserts (parenthetical remarks)
  mid-sentence. Convert to separate sentences or em-dashes where clarity
  improves.

- MAINTAIN PARALLEL STRUCTURES: Paine loves parallel construction
  ("neither...nor," "the one...the other," listed "First...Secondly...Thirdly").
  These are effective rhetoric — keep them.

- PRESERVE RHETORICAL QUESTIONS: These are central to his persuasive style.
  Keep them, just modernize the vocabulary within.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

6. BIBLICAL AND CLASSICAL REFERENCES

Common Sense is saturated with biblical references, especially in Section III
(Monarchy and Hereditary Succession). Strategy:

- KEEP BIBLICAL NARRATIVES: The Gideon story, the Samuel story — these are
  central to Paine's argument. Modernize the framing but preserve the
  substance. When Paine paraphrases scripture, modernize his paraphrase.
  When he quotes directly, keep closer to the original but still clarify.

- ADD BRIEF CONTEXT: For references that modern readers won't know
  (e.g., Massanello, Dragonetti, the Stamp Act, the "fatal nineteenth of
  April"), add a brief parenthetical or contextual phrase.

- LATIN PHRASES: Replace with English equivalents.
  "felo de se" → "an act of self-destruction"
  "viz." → "namely" / "that is"

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

7. CONTENT HANDLING NOTES

- THE TITLE PAGE (000): Modernize into a clean title page. Keep the epigraph
  from Thomson. The Bradford publication info is historical context worth
  preserving in simplified form.

- THE INTRODUCTION (001): Short and powerful. Light touch needed. The P.S.
  about the second edition and author's anonymity should be preserved as
  historical context.

- SECTION I (002): The most philosophical/theoretical section. The thought
  experiment about settlers forming government is brilliant and accessible.
  The critique of the English constitution is more complex — simplify the
  constitutional mechanics while preserving the argument.

- SECTION II (003): Heavy on biblical references. The Gideon and Samuel
  passages are long. Modernize the language but don't shorten the biblical
  arguments — they were central to Paine's persuasion of a religious audience.
  The Wars of the Roses passage is concrete history; keep it vivid.

- SECTION III (004): The emotional heart of the pamphlet. Contains the most
  famous passages. Be especially careful with: "The sun never shined on a
  cause of greater worth"; "O ye that love mankind!"; "We have it in our power
  to begin the world over again" (from section V actually — the sentiment is
  echoed). The passage about Boston is devastating and should stay powerful.

- SECTION IV (005): Contains detailed naval cost tables. Modernize the
  framing but keep the specific numbers — they show Paine doing homework.
  The economic argument is practical and concrete.

- APPENDIX (006): Two distinct parts: (1) response to the King's Speech,
  and (2) address to the Quakers. The Quaker address is the most complex
  section — it requires understanding of Quaker political theology. The
  Barclay footnote should be preserved. The FINIS should be rendered simply.

- DATED LANGUAGE ABOUT RACE/COLONIALISM: Paine mentions "the naked and
  untutored Indian" and refers to Africa being "in a state of barbarism."
  These reflect 18th-century attitudes. Translate faithfully without
  softening, but the modernized phrasing can avoid gratuitously offensive
  formulations while preserving Paine's actual point in each case. A brief
  translator's note in the text analysis section of the output can flag this.

8. VOICE PRESERVATION GUIDELINES

Paine's voice is his greatest asset. Key aspects to maintain:

- CONFIDENCE: Paine never hedges. He states things as obvious truths.
  Don't add qualifiers or softeners.

- DIRECT ADDRESS: He speaks directly to the reader, to opponents, to
  the Quakers. Keep the "you" and "I" voice.

- ESCALATING INTENSITY: Each section builds on the last. The translation
  should reflect this building momentum.

- CONCRETE OVER ABSTRACT: Paine always grounds abstract principles in
  concrete examples. Preserve every analogy and illustration.

- MORAL CLARITY: Paine presents the choice as simple: right vs. wrong,
  courage vs. cowardice, freedom vs. slavery. Don't muddy this clarity.

9. CHAPTER-BY-CHAPTER NOTES

000 (Title Page):
  - Simplify formatting while preserving essential information
  - Keep the Thomson epigraph
  - Note the publication date and place

001 (Introduction):
  - Very light modernization needed
  - "Time makes more converts than reason" — iconic, keep exactly
  - The P.S. reveals context about the second edition; keep it

002 (Of Government):
  - The society vs. government distinction opens the whole work — make it crisp
  - "Government, like dress, is the badge of lost innocence" — famous line, keep
  - The thought experiment about settlers is accessible; preserve its narrative
  - The English constitution critique has technical content about King/Lords/Commons
  - Key metaphor: "shut and lock a door against absolute monarchy... put the
    crown in possession of the key"

003 (Of Monarchy):
  - Longest biblical section — the Gideon and Samuel narratives
  - Must remain engaging while being faithful to Paine's biblical argument
  - "A French bastard landing with an armed banditti" — keep the irreverence
  - The Wars of the Roses summary is vivid history; keep it concrete
  - "Of more worth is one honest man to society... than all the crowned
    ruffians that ever lived" — iconic, preserve

004 (Thoughts on American Affairs):
  - The emotional crescendo of the pamphlet
  - "The sun never shined on a cause of greater worth" — keep exactly or very close
  - The Boston passage is deliberately heart-wrenching; preserve its power
  - "Hath your house been burnt?" — the series of questions is devastating rhetoric
  - The proposed constitutional framework (annual assemblies, Continental Congress)
    is specific and practical — keep the details
  - "But where says some is the King of America? I'll tell you Friend, he reigns
    above" — one of the great moments; preserve the drama
  - "O ye that love mankind!" — the famous peroration. Keep its power.
  - Massanello footnote: keep with brief additional context

005 (Present Ability):
  - Most practical/technical section
  - Naval cost tables: preserve the numbers and structure, modernize framing
  - The argument about timber and natural resources is concrete
  - "We have it in our power to begin the world over again" — the single most
    famous line. Preserve exactly.
  - "The birthday of a new world is at hand" — powerful; keep close
  - Discussion of religious tolerance is ahead of its time; highlight this

006 (Appendix):
  - Response to King's Speech: fiery and contemptuous
  - "the naked and untutored Indian, is less a Savage than the King of Britain"
    — handle the racial language thoughtfully while preserving the anti-monarchical point
  - Address to the Quakers: the most complex argumentative section
  - "Oliver Cromwell thanks you" — devastating sarcasm; keep it
  - The logical trap Paine sets (if God controls kings, then Cromwell was
    God's will too) is brilliant; make sure it comes through clearly
  - Barclay footnote: preserve
  - FINIS: end simply

10. CONSISTENCY RULES

- Always use "independence" (not "independance")
- Always use "connection" (not "connexion")
- Always use "has/have" (not "hath")
- Always use "does" (not "doth")
- Always use "show/shown" (not "shew/shewn")
- Always use "you" for "ye" in direct address (except in preserved quotations)
- Keep "the continent" as Paine's term for the American colonies collectively
- Keep "the Crown" as is
- Keep proper nouns: Tories, Whigs, Quakers, Continental Congress
- Preserve Paine's formatting of numbered arguments (First... Secondly... Thirdly...)
- Preserve footnotes as bracketed notes or parenthetical context
- When Paine uses italics or ALL CAPS for emphasis, use bold or italics in modern text
- Maintain paragraph breaks as in the original where possible

11. FAMOUS QUOTATIONS TO PRESERVE (near-verbatim)

These are widely quoted and should be changed as little as possible:

- "Time makes more converts than reason."
- "Society is produced by our wants, and government by our wickedness."
- "Government, like dress, is the badge of lost innocence."
- "The sun never shined on a cause of greater worth."
- "Of more worth is one honest man to society, and in the sight of God,
   than all the crowned ruffians that ever lived."
- "O ye that love mankind! Ye that dare oppose, not only the tyranny,
   but the tyrant, stand forth!"
- "We have it in our power to begin the world over again."
- "These are the times that try men's souls." (NOTE: this is actually from
   The American Crisis, not Common Sense — do not insert it)
