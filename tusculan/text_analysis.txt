TEXT ANALYSIS — Tusculan Disputations (Cicero, 45 BC)
Source: The Latin Library's Latin text (5 books; canonical § numbering
kept as our "Section N" headings). TRANSLATED FROM THE LATIN — the
second Latin volume after On Duties; same pipeline. C. D. Yonge's
public-domain translation (Gutenberg #14988) sits per book in
reference/book-N.txt as a comprehension crib ONLY.

HOW TO USE THE TWO TEXTS

- chapters/NNN.txt (Latin) is the source. Translate from it.
- reference/book-N.txt is Yonge's ENTIRE book — find your section
  span (his Roman-numeral chapters run in parallel; match by content),
  consult it to construe hard syntax or check a name, then write YOUR
  modern English from the Latin. Never echo Yonge's Victorian diction.
- Ratio: modern English should land 1.2–1.6x the Latin word count
  (verify runs with --min-ratio 1.0 --max-ratio 1.8).

WHAT THIS IS

Written in 45 BC, the terrible year: Cicero's daughter Tullia has just
died, Caesar rules alone, and Cicero, exiled from politics, pours
himself into philosophy at his villa in Tusculum. Five days, five
dialogues, one per book: I — death is not an evil; II — pain can be
endured; III — grief is not natural and can be treated; IV — the
soul's disorders (the passions) and their cure; V — virtue by itself
is sufficient for a happy life. It is consolation literature argued
like a courtroom case, and the founders' favorite Cicero after On
Duties — Adams and Jefferson both quote it.

STYLE AND TONE

- Frame: each book opens with a preface in Cicero's own essay voice
  (dedicated to Brutus), then shifts into a DIALOGUE between Cicero
  and a young companion who states a thesis ("Death seems to me an
  evil") that Cicero spends the day demolishing.
- The Latin text has NO speaker tags (Yonge's M./A. marks are his
  addition). Render exchanges as embedded quoted dialogue with light
  narration attribution — the Memorabilia convention, NOT Plato-style
  speaker lines. The student's interjections are brief; Cicero's
  replies grow into sustained teaching. Use Yonge's M./A. marks to
  identify whose turn it is when the Latin is ambiguous.
- Cicero here is warm, wounded, and combative at once — a man
  consoling himself out loud. Keep the personal undertow (especially
  in III on grief) without ever naming what the text doesn't name.
- Constant quotation of poets (Ennius, Pacuvius, his own Latin
  renderings of Sophocles and Aeschylus) — verse stays verse:
  two-space-indented lines. His famous self-quotations from his own
  poetry stay too.
- Set-piece stories are the book's jewels — render them as vivid
  complete stories: the Sword of Damocles (V), Cicero discovering
  Archimedes' tomb (V, first person — he did this himself as
  quaestor), the athletes and the whipped Spartan boys (II), Alcibiades
  weeping at Socrates (III).

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY (carried from On Duties where shared)

- "the highest good" (summum bonum); "self-control" (temperantia);
  "practical wisdom" (prudentia); "greatness of spirit" (magnitudo
  animi); "the right"/"moral goodness" (honestum/honestas).
- New for this work: perturbationes (the Stoic pathe) → "the
  disorders of the soul" (with "passions" allowed in apposition);
  aegritudo → "grief" (Book III's subject; keep it distinct from
  "pain" (dolor), Book II's subject); constantia → "steadiness";
  sapiens → "the wise man".
- Greek terms romanized, no Greek script; a two-word appositive gloss
  at first use where needed.

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Suicide is discussed philosophically (I and V: Cato's death, Theramenes,
  Socrates' hemlock) — render as the ancient argument it is, measured
  register, neither endorsing nor editorializing.
- Torture, the tyrants' cruelties (Dionysius, Phalaris), Spartan
  flogging rituals, and war deaths appear as examples — plain
  historical register per project policy.
- Roman disdain for certain peoples and for "womanish" grief is
  Cicero's, rendered as his, unamplified.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- I (§74): "the whole life of a philosopher is a preparation for
  death."
- II (§13): "as a field, however fertile, cannot be fruitful without
  cultivation, neither can a mind without teaching — philosophy is the
  cultivation of the mind."  (lock: "philosophy is the cultivation of
  the mind")
- V (§5), the invocation: "O philosophy, guide of life!" (lock:
  "philosophy, guide of life")
- V (§61–62): the Sword of Damocles — "Damocles" by name, and the
  sword hung "by a single horsehair".
- V (§64–66): Cicero finding "Archimedes" tomb — keep the first-person
  pride.

FORMAT

16 files across 5 books; each file: heading exactly as in the source
file (e.g. "Book III: On Grief of Mind"), then "(Part n of k)", then
"Section N" standalone headings (the canonical § numbers). Section
numbering restarts each book. Parts 2+ never re-introduce a book;
non-final parts never conclude it.
