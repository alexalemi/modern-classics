Book V: Whether Virtue Alone Is Sufficient for a Happy Life

(Part 4 of 4)

Section 90

Could Anacharsis the Scythian count money as nothing, and our own homegrown philosophers not manage it? A letter of his survives, in these words: "Anacharsis to Hanno, greetings. My clothing is a Scythian hide; my shoes, the calluses on my soles; my bed, the earth; my sauce, hunger; and I live on milk, cheese, and meat. So you may come to me as to a man at peace. As for those gifts you take such delight in — give them to your own citizens, or to the immortal gods." Nearly all philosophers of every school could have been of this same mind, except those whom a flawed nature had twisted away from right reason.

Section 91

When a great load of gold and silver was being carried past in a procession, Socrates said, "How many things there are I do not need!" When ambassadors from Alexander brought Xenocrates fifty talents — an enormous sum of money in those days, above all at Athens — he took the ambassadors to dinner in the Academy and set before them just enough, with no show whatever. When they asked him the next day to whom he wanted the money paid out, he said, "What? Didn't yesterday's little dinner tell you that I have no need of money?" Then, seeing them rather downcast, he accepted thirty minas, so as not to seem to snub the king's generosity.

Section 92

Diogenes, though, was blunter about it, as a Cynic would be. When Alexander asked him to say if there was anything he needed, he answered, "For the moment — stand a little out of my sun." The king, evidently, was blocking his light while he warmed himself. And indeed this same man used to argue how far he outstripped the king of Persia in life and in fortune: he himself lacked nothing, while for the king nothing would ever be enough; he felt no craving for the king's pleasures, with which the king could never be satisfied, while the king had no possible way of obtaining his.

Section 93

You see, I trust, how Epicurus divides up the kinds of desire — not with any great fineness, perhaps, but usefully all the same: some are natural and necessary; some natural but not necessary; some neither. The necessary ones can be satisfied with next to nothing, for nature's riches lie ready to hand. The second kind of desire, he judges, is neither hard to obtain nor hard to do without. The third kind — since they are entirely empty, touching neither necessity nor even nature — he thought should be thrown out root and branch.

Section 94

On this ground the Epicureans argue at length, and they talk those pleasures down one at a time — the kinds of pleasure they do not despise, though they do go hunting for a good supply. Even the pleasures of sex, about which they have a great deal to say, are easy, they claim, common, and freely available; and if nature demands them, they think the measure is not birth or station or rank, but looks, age, and figure; abstaining from them is not the least bit difficult, if health or duty or reputation requires it; and altogether, this kind of pleasure may be worth choosing if it does no harm — good it never does.

Section 95

His whole teaching about pleasure comes to this: pleasure itself, he thinks, simply because it is pleasure, is always to be wished for and pursued, and by the same reasoning pain, precisely because it is pain, is always to be fled. So the wise man will run a kind of balance sheet: he will flee a pleasure if it is going to produce a greater pain, and will take on a pain that produces a greater pleasure. And everything pleasant, though it is judged by the body's senses, is referred back all the same to the mind.

Section 96

That is why the body rejoices only as long as it feels the pleasure in front of it, while the mind both perceives the present pleasure alongside the body and foresees the one that is coming, and does not let the past one slip away downstream. So the wise man will always have his pleasures continuous and woven together, as the expectation of pleasures hoped for is joined to the memory of pleasures already felt.

Section 97

The same line of thought gets carried over to the table, and the magnificence and expense of banquets get talked down, on the ground that nature is content with modest fare. Really, who fails to see that appetite is the seasoning in all these things? Darius, on the run, drank water that was muddy and fouled with corpses, and said he had never drunk anything more delicious — naturally, for he had never before drunk thirsty. Nor had Ptolemy ever eaten hungry; when he was crossing Egypt and his escort failed to keep up with him, coarse bread was handed him in a hut, and nothing ever seemed to him more delicious than that bread. They tell of Socrates that he once kept up a hard walk right until evening, and when he was asked why he was doing it, he answered that he was shopping for a seasoning to his dinner: walking up a hunger.

Section 98

And what about the Spartans' fare in their common messes? When the tyrant Dionysius dined there, he said he took no pleasure in that black broth which was the centerpiece of the meal. The man who had cooked it said, "No wonder. The seasonings were missing." "What seasonings?" asked Dionysius. "Hard work in the hunt, sweat, a run down to the Eurotas, hunger, thirst — that is how Spartan feasts are seasoned." And this can be understood not only from the ways of men but from the beasts too, who — provided what is tossed in front of them is not against their nature — are content with it and look no further.

Section 99

Whole cities, schooled by custom, take delight in plain living, as I said a moment ago about the Spartans. Xenophon lays out the diet of the Persians, who, he says, take nothing with their bread except cress. And yet, if nature should crave something sweeter after all, how many things spring from the earth and from trees, as easy in their abundance as they are outstanding in their sweetness! Add the leanness that follows this restraint in eating; add unbroken health. Now set beside it the men who sweat and belch, stuffed with feasting like fattened oxen:

Section 100

then you will understand that the men who chase pleasure hardest catch it least, and that the delight of eating lies in appetite, not in being full. They tell of Timotheus, a famous man at Athens and the first citizen of his city, that after dining with Plato and enjoying that dinner enormously, he saw him the next day and said, "Your dinners are delightful not only while they last, but the day after as well." And there is this besides: we cannot even use our minds properly when we are loaded with food and drink. There is a splendid letter of Plato to Dion's relatives, in which he writes, in nearly these words: "When I arrived there, that famous 'happy life' — a life full of Italian and Syracusan tables — pleased me in no way at all: to be stuffed twice a day, never to spend a night alone, and all the rest that goes with that kind of life, in which no man will ever be made wise, and moderate far less.

Section 101

"For what nature could possibly be so miraculously tempered as to stand it?" How, then, can a life be pleasant when practical wisdom is missing from it, and moderation is missing? This lays bare the error of Sardanapalus, the richest king Syria ever had, who ordered these lines cut into his tomb:

  These are mine still: what I ate, and what my glutted lust
  drank down. The rest — so much, and so splendid — lies there, left behind.

"What else," says Aristotle, "would you inscribe on the grave of an ox, not of a king? He says that in death he possesses the very things which even in life he possessed no longer than the moment he was enjoying them."

Section 102

Why, then, should riches be longed for — and where exactly does poverty forbid a man to be happy? You have a taste, I suppose, for statues and paintings. Well, if anyone genuinely delights in these, don't men of slender means enjoy them better than the men who own them in heaps? In our city there is the fullest public supply of all such things; the people who hold them privately own fewer, and see them rarely, only when they visit their country places — and even then something pricks them, when they remember how they came by them. The day would run out on me if I chose to plead poverty's case in full. The thing is obvious, and nature herself reminds us every day how few things she needs, how small, and how cheap.

Section 103

Well then: will obscurity, or low birth, or even the people's hostility keep the wise man from being happy? Watch out that favor with the crowd, and this glory men chase after, doesn't hold more vexation than pleasure. Our Demosthenes was undeniably a bit of a lightweight when he said he was delighted by the whisper of a poor woman carrying water, as the custom is in Greece, whispering to another: "That's him — that's the famous Demosthenes." What could be lighter than that? Yet what an orator! He had learned, evidently, to speak before others; he had not talked much with himself.

Section 104

So we must understand that popular glory is not worth seeking for its own sake, and that obscurity is nothing to dread. "I came to Athens," says Democritus, "and no one there knew me." A steady and serious man, to boast of standing apart from glory! Shall pipers and men who play the lyre set their tunes and rhythms by their own judgment and not the crowd's, and shall the wise man, endowed with a far greater art, go hunting not for what is truest but for what the mob wants? Is anything more foolish than to write men off one by one as hired hands and boors, and then to think they amount to something when they crowd together? No — the truly wise man will look down on our ambitions and our vanities, and will refuse the people's honors even when they are pressed on him unasked; we, for our part, have no idea how to despise them until we have begun to regret them.

Section 105

There is a story in Heraclitus, the natural philosopher, about Hermodorus, the leading man of Ephesus. The Ephesians, he said, deserved to be put to death to the last man for what they said when they drove Hermodorus out of their city: "Let no one of us stand out above the rest; if anyone does, let him be somewhere else, among other people." Doesn't this happen in every populace? Don't they hate every towering excellence? What about Aristides — I would rather take my examples from the Greeks than from ourselves — wasn't he expelled from his country for the precise reason that he was just beyond measure? What troubles they escape, then, who have no dealings with the people at all! And what is sweeter than leisure spent with books? I mean the books through which we come to know the boundlessness of things and of nature — and, in this world of ours, the sky, the lands, the seas.

Section 106

With honor despised, then, and money despised as well, what is left to be terrified of? Exile, I suppose, which is counted among the greatest evils. If exile is an evil because of the people's ill will turned against you, I have just said how contemptible that is. But if being away from your own country is misery, then the provinces are packed with miserable men — of whom precious few ever return home.

Section 107

"But exiles are stripped of their property." What of it? Hasn't enough been said about bearing poverty? As for exile itself — if we look at the nature of the thing and not the disgrace of the name, how far does it really differ from a life of constant travel? And in constant travel the noblest philosophers spent their lives: Xenocrates and Crantor, Arcesilaus and Lacydes, Aristotle and Theophrastus, Zeno and Cleanthes, Chrysippus and Antipater, Carneades and Clitomachus, Philo and Antiochus, Panaetius and Posidonius, and countless others who, once they set out, never came home again. "Ah, but they traveled without disgrace." Can exile bring disgrace on a wise man at all? This whole discussion is about the wise man, and exile cannot justly fall on him — and a man exiled justly is not owed consolation.

Section 108

Finally, the easiest way of meeting every misfortune belongs to those who measure everything they pursue in life by pleasure: wherever pleasure is on hand, there they can live happily. So Teucer's line can be fitted to every case: "Wherever you are well off, there is your country." Socrates, asked what state he claimed as his own, said, "The world" — for he counted himself an inhabitant and citizen of the whole world. And Titus Albucius — didn't he pursue philosophy at Athens, in exile, with a perfectly level mind? Though even that would never have happened to him if he had obeyed the laws of Epicurus and kept quiet in public life.

Section 109

How was Epicurus, living in his own country, any happier than Metrodorus, living at Athens? In what did Plato outdo Xenocrates in happiness, or Polemo outdo Arcesilaus? And what is a city worth, anyway, that drives out its good and wise men? Demaratus, the father of our king Tarquinius, could not endure the tyrant Cypselus: he fled from Corinth to Tarquinii, set up his fortunes there, and raised his children there. Was it stupid of him to prefer the freedom of exile to slavery at home?

Section 110

Then too, the mind's agitations — its worries and its griefs — are soothed by forgetting, once the thoughts are led across to pleasure. So Epicurus was not speaking without reason when he dared to say that the wise man is always amid a majority of goods, because he is always amid pleasures. And from this, he thinks, the very point we are after follows: that the wise man is always happy.

Section 111

"Even if he loses the use of his eyes? Even his ears?" Even then — for he holds those very things cheap. First of all, what pleasures does this blindness we find so dreadful actually go without? Some people even argue that while the other pleasures live in the senses themselves, what is perceived by sight involves no delight residing in the eyes at all: what we taste, smell, touch, or hear is felt in the very organ that senses it, but nothing of the kind happens in the eyes — it is the mind that receives what we see. And the mind can be delighted in many and various ways even when sight is never called in. I am speaking, remember, of a learned and cultivated man, for whom to live is to think; and the wise man's thinking rarely summons the eyes as assistants to its inquiries.

Section 112

After all, if night does not take the happy life away, why should a day that resembles night take it away? The remark of Antipater the Cyrenaic on this point is a shade indecent, but the thought is not absurd. When the women around him were wailing over his blindness, he asked, "What is the matter with you? Do you think the night holds no pleasure?" As for old Appius, who was blind for many years — his magistracies and his record make it plain that in that misfortune of his he failed no duty, private or public. The house of Gaius Drusus, we are told, was regularly packed with people seeking his advice: when the men whose business it was could not see their own affairs, they took a blind man for a guide. When I was a boy, Gnaeus Aufidius, an ex-praetor, was still giving his opinion in the Senate; he never failed a friend who wanted counsel; he was writing a history in Greek — and in literature he could see.

Section 113

Diodotus the Stoic lived blind for many years in my house. He — and this is the part that is scarcely believable — worked at philosophy far more steadily than before; he played the lyre in the manner of the Pythagoreans; he had books read to him night and day, pursuits in which he had no need of eyes; and — the thing that hardly seems possible without eyes — he kept up his post as a teacher of geometry, telling his students in words where each line began and where to draw it. They say that Asclepiades, an Eretrian philosopher of some standing, when someone asked him what blindness had brought him, answered: one boy the more in his escort. Just as even the most extreme poverty is bearable, if a man may live as some Greeks do every day, so blindness can easily be borne, provided the props for its disabilities are not lacking.

Section 114

When Democritus lost his sight, he could no longer tell white from black, true; but good and bad, just and unjust, honorable and shameful, useful and useless, great and small — those he could still tell apart. Living happily without the variety of colors is possible; without a grasp of things, it is not. In fact this man held that the mind's keen vision is actually hampered by the seeing of the eyes; and while other men often failed to see what lay in front of their feet, he went ranging through all infinity, halting at no outermost edge. Tradition says Homer was blind as well. But what we have from him is painting, not mere poetry: what region, what coastline, what spot in Greece, what shape and image of combat, what battle-line, what rowing of ships, what movements of men and of beasts has he not painted so vividly that he made us see what he himself could not? Well then — do we suppose that Homer, or any learned man, ever went without delight of mind and pleasure?

Section 115

If it were otherwise, would Anaxagoras — or this same Democritus — have abandoned their fields and family estates, and given themselves with their whole soul to this divine delight of learning and inquiry? So it is that the poets, who imagine the augur Tiresias as a wise man, never once bring him on lamenting his blindness; while Homer, after drawing Polyphemus as a monster and a savage, has him chat with his ram and praise the animal's luck, because it could walk wherever it wanted and reach whatever it wanted. And rightly, in his case — for the Cyclops had no more sense than the ram.

Section 116

And in deafness, what evil is there, exactly? Marcus Crassus was slightly hard of hearing, but something else galled him more: the abuse of himself that he did hear — undeservedly, as it seemed to me. Our Roman Epicureans mostly know no Greek, and the Greeks no Latin: so each side is deaf to the other's speech, and all of us are truly deaf in the countless languages we do not understand. "But the deaf never hear the voice of the singer at his lyre." No — and they never hear the shriek of a saw being sharpened, either, or the squeal of a pig at its slaughter, or, when they want to rest, the roar of the muttering sea. And if songs happen to delight them, they ought to reflect, first, that many wise men lived happily before any song was invented, and second, that a far greater pleasure can be taken in reading verses than in hearing them.

Section 117

Then, just as a moment ago we directed the blind toward the pleasures of the ears, so we may direct the deaf toward the pleasures of the eyes. Besides, a man who can converse with himself will not miss another man's conversation. Now pile everything onto one person: let him be blind and deaf both, and crushed on top of it by the fiercest bodily pains. Such pains, in the first place, usually finish a man off by themselves; but if they happen to drag on at length, and torture him more violently than there could be any reason for enduring them, why in the world, good gods, should we struggle? A harbor lies close at hand — death, the eternal refuge where nothing is felt. Theodorus said to Lysimachus, who was threatening him with death: "A mighty achievement, truly — you have matched the power of a poison beetle."

Section 118

When Perses begged Paulus not to lead him in his triumph, Paulus said, "That is in your own power." Much was said about death on the first day, when death itself was our question, and not a little on the day after, when the subject was pain; and anyone who remembers it all is in no great danger of failing to think death either something to wish for or, at the very least, nothing to fear. My own view is that in life we should keep the rule that holds at Greek banquets: "Let him drink," it says, "or let him leave." And rightly. Either a man should enjoy the pleasure of the cups together with the others, or he should leave early, so that a sober man does not collide with the violence of the drunk. Just so with the injuries of fortune you cannot bear: you escape them by leaving. Hieronymus says exactly what Epicurus says here, in as many words.

Section 119

Now, if these philosophers — men whose position is that virtue by itself counts for nothing, and that everything we call honorable and praiseworthy is a hollow thing dressed up in the empty sound of a word — if even they consistently hold that the wise man is always happy, then what do you think should be done by the philosophers who trace their descent from Socrates and Plato? Some of them say the goods of the soul stand so high that the goods of the body and the external goods are buried out of sight beneath them; others do not count those as goods at all, and place everything in the soul.

Section 120

Their dispute Carneades used to settle, like an arbiter called in as a courtesy. Since whatever counted as goods to the Peripatetics counted as advantages to the Stoics, and since the Peripatetics granted no more weight to riches, sound health, and the rest of that kind than the Stoics did, he held that once these things were weighed by their reality and not by words, there was no ground for quarreling at all. So let the philosophers of the other schools see for themselves how they can secure this point; for my part, I am glad they profess something worthy of a philosopher's voice about the wise man's unbroken power of living well.

Section 121

But since we must leave in the morning, let us hold in memory these five days of discussion. In fact I believe I will even write them out — for how could I put this leisure of mine, whatever it amounts to, to better use? — and I will send these five further books to our Brutus, who not only urged me toward the writing of philosophy but goaded me into it. How much good I will do other people by it, I cannot easily say; but for my own griefs, the bitterest of griefs, and the troubles that surround me, various and pressing in from every side, no other relief could be found.
