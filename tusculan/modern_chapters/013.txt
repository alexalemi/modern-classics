Book V: Whether Virtue Alone Is Sufficient for a Happy Life

(Part 2 of 4)

Section 29

For my part, I count as happy those who possess good things with no evil mixed in. When we call someone "happy," the word carries no other idea than this: the full and heaped-up possession of good things, with every evil shut out. And virtue cannot reach that state if anything besides virtue itself counts as good. For a whole crowd of evils will come pressing in — if we do count them as evils: poverty, obscurity, low position, loneliness, the loss of loved ones, severe bodily pain, ruined health, weakness, blindness, the destruction of one's country, exile, and finally slavery. The wise man can land in all of these — so many of them, and so great, and still more can happen — because chance brings them on, and chance can strike the wise man. But if these things are evils, who can guarantee that the wise man will always be happy, when he might be caught in every one of them at a single moment?

Section 30

So I do not easily concede — not to my friend Brutus, not to our common teachers, not to the old masters Aristotle, Speusippus, Xenocrates, and Polemon — that they may count the things I have just listed as evils and still say that the wise man is always happy. If that splendid and beautiful title delights them — a title fully worthy of Pythagoras, Socrates, and Plato — then let them bring themselves to despise the things whose glitter has captured them: strength, health, beauty, riches, honors, wealth. Let them count the opposites of these as nothing. Then they will be able to declare at the top of their voice that no assault of fortune, no opinion of the crowd, no pain, no poverty frightens them; that everything they have rests within themselves; that nothing they count as good lies outside their own power.

Section 31

As things stand, there is no way we can allow one and the same man to talk like some great and lofty spirit while counting the very same things good and evil that the crowd does. Stung by the glory of this position, up springs Epicurus, who — heaven help us — also holds that the wise man is always happy. He is captivated by the dignity of the claim, but he would never say it if he listened to himself. For what fits together worse than this: a man who declares pain the greatest evil, or the only evil, and who then supposes that in the middle of torment the wise man will say, "How sweet this is!"? Philosophers are not to be judged by isolated remarks, then, but by the whole sustained run of their thought and its consistency.

Section 32

"You're bringing me around to your view," he said. "But take care your own consistency isn't found wanting."

"How so?" I asked.

"Because I recently read your fourth book On Ends. Arguing there against Cato, you seemed to me to be trying to show — and to my mind you succeeded — that between Zeno and the Peripatetics there is no difference except novelty of words. If that's so, then if it follows from Zeno's reasoning that virtue has power enough in it for living happily, what possible reason is there why the Peripatetics shouldn't be free to say the same? It's the substance we ought to look at, I think, not the words."

Section 33

"Now you're dealing with me under sealed documents," I said, "calling witnesses to whatever I once said or wrote. Save that method for people who argue by fixed rules. We philosophers live from day to day: we say whatever strikes our minds as probable, and that is why we alone are free." And I pressed on. Still, since consistency came up a moment ago, I don't think the question in this place is whether the view of Zeno — and of his student Aristo — is true, that the only good is what is right. The question is: granting that view, was it then consistent to rest the whole of living happily on virtue alone?

Section 34

So by all means let us grant Brutus this much — that the wise man is always happy. How well that squares with his own principles is his business; and who, after all, deserves the glory of that opinion more than he does? Still, let us hold our own ground: the same man is not merely happy but happiest. And if Zeno of Citium — a foreigner, really, and an obscure craftsman of words — seems to have wormed his way into the old philosophy, still the weight of this doctrine should be traced back to the authority of Plato, in whose pages the claim appears again and again that nothing deserves to be called good except virtue.

Section 35

So in the Gorgias, when Socrates was asked whether he didn't consider Archelaus the son of Perdiccas — then held to be the most fortunate of men — a happy man, he answered, "I have no idea. I've never talked with him."

"Really? There's no other way you could know?"

"None at all."

"Then you can't even say of the great king of Persia whether he's happy?"

"How could I, when I don't know how learned he is, or how good a man?"

"What — you think the happy life rests on that?"

"That is exactly what I think: the good are happy, the wicked are miserable."

"Then Archelaus is miserable?"

"Certainly — if he's unjust."

Section 36

Doesn't he seem here to rest the whole of the happy life on virtue alone? And what does the same man say in the Funeral Oration? "The man who has everything that makes for living happily anchored in himself alone — whose hopes do not hang on another's good or bad luck, forced to swing and wander with the turns of someone else's fortunes — that man has secured the best plan for living. He is the temperate man, he is the brave man, he is the wise one. As other advantages come to birth and fall away — children above all — he will submit and obey that old precept: he will never rejoice too much and never grieve too much, because he places every hope for himself in himself." From this sacred and august spring of Plato, then, everything I have to say will flow.

Section 37

And where can we more rightly begin than with nature, the common parent of us all? Whatever she brought forth — not only animals but even the things that spring from the earth and stand on their own stems — she willed to be perfect, each in its own kind. So among trees and vines, and the humbler growths that cannot lift themselves far from the ground, some stay green the year round, while others, stripped bare in winter, warm with the spring and leaf out again; and there is not one of them that is not so quickened by a kind of inner motion, by its own seeds enclosed within it, that it pours out flowers or grain or berries. All of them, in every part, so far as it lies in themselves, are perfect — if no outside force blocks the way.

Section 38

In animals the force of nature is even easier to see, since nature has given them senses. Some she willed to be swimmers, dwellers in the water; some to be birds, enjoying the freedom of the sky; some to crawl, some to walk; and of these, some to live alone and some in herds; some savage, some tame, and a few hidden away under cover of the earth. And each of them, holding to its own office, unable to cross over into the life of a different creature, stays within nature's law. As each animal has been given by nature some special gift of its own, which it keeps and never abandons, so man has been given something far more outstanding — though "outstanding" should really be said only of things that admit comparison, and the human soul, a cutting taken from the divine mind, can be compared with nothing else — if it is lawful to say so — except God himself.

Section 39

So if this soul is cultivated, and if its eye is so cared for that errors never blind it, the result is a perfected mind — that is, reason brought to completion, which is the very same thing as virtue. And if everything is happy that lacks nothing, everything that is filled out and complete in its own kind, and if that is the special mark of virtue, then certainly everyone who possesses virtue is happy. On this point Brutus and I agree — which is to say, I agree with Aristotle, Xenocrates, Speusippus, and Polemon.

Section 40

But to me such men seem not merely happy but happiest. For what does a man lack for living happily if he trusts in his own goods? And how can a man who distrusts them be happy at all? Yet distrust he must, whoever divides goods into three classes. How will he ever be able to count on the body's strength, or on fortune holding steady? And no one can be happy without a good that is stable, fixed, and lasting. What do those people have that is anything like that? I keep thinking that the Spartan's famous retort fits them — the one he gave a merchant who was boasting of the many ships he had dispatched to every coast: "A fortune that hangs on ropes is not much to wish for." Can there be any doubt that nothing which can be lost belongs in the class of things that fill out a happy life? Of all that the happy life consists of, nothing should be able to dry up, to be snuffed out, to fall away. A man who is going to be afraid of losing any of it cannot be happy.

Section 41

For we want the happy man to be safe, unassailable, fenced and fortified — furnished not with a little fear, but with none. Just as we call a man harmless not because he does slight harm but because he does no harm at all, so the man to be counted fearless is not the one who fears little but the one who is altogether free of fear. What else is courage but a disposition of the soul that is patient in facing danger and in labor and pain, and stands far removed from all fear? And this certainly would not hold true unless everything good consisted in moral goodness alone.

Section 42

And that security which everyone most desires and pursues — by security I now mean freedom from grief, the freedom on which the happy life rests — how can anyone possess it who has, or can have, a crowd of evils upon him? And how can a man be lofty and upright, counting everything that can happen to a human being a small thing — which is how we want the wise man to be — unless he judges that everything that matters to him rests in himself? When Philip threatened the Spartans by letter that he would block everything they attempted, they asked whether he was also going to block them from dying. Won't the single man we are looking for be found with that spirit far more easily than an entire city of such men? Now join to this courage we are describing self-control, the governor of every agitation of feeling — and what can be missing for a happy life in a man whom courage rescues from grief and from fear, while self-control calls him back from lust and refuses to let him run riot in exultant high spirits? I would show that virtue accomplishes all this, had it not been laid out on the previous days.

Section 43

The disorders of the soul make life miserable; their calming makes it happy. And the disorders come in two forms: grief and fear rest on evils merely imagined, while exultant delight and lust rest on a mistake about what is good — and all of them war against deliberation and reason. So when you see a man free, released, unburdened by agitations so heavy, so at cross-purposes and pulling so hard against one another, will you hesitate to call him happy? But the wise man is always in that condition; therefore the wise man is always happy. And further: every good is a thing to rejoice in; what we rejoice in deserves to be proclaimed and openly displayed; what is like that is a thing to glory in; what we glory in is certainly praiseworthy; and what is praiseworthy is surely morally right. Therefore what is good is right.

Section 44

But the things those people count as goods, even they do not call right. So the only good is what is right — from which it follows that the happy life is held together by moral goodness alone. Things a man can have in abundance and still be utterly miserable ought not to be called goods, or considered goods, at all.

Section 45

Or do you doubt it? Take a man superb in health, strength, and looks, with the keenest and soundest senses; throw in, if you like, quickness and speed; give him riches, honors, commands, power, glory. If the man who has all this is unjust, without self-control, cowardly, dull-witted or witless outright, will you hesitate to call him miserable? What sort of goods are these, then, which a man can own and still be as miserable as they come? Consider whether the happy life, like a heap built of grains of a single kind, must be built out of parts that resemble it. If so, then happiness must be assembled from goods that are right and nothing else; if the mix is of unlike elements, nothing right can be made from it — and take away the right, and what can you still recognize as happy? For whatever is good is worth seeking; what is worth seeking is certainly worth approving; what you approve you must hold welcome and acceptable; and so it must also be granted dignity. If that is so, it must be praiseworthy. Therefore every good is praiseworthy — from which it follows that what is right is the only good.

Section 46

If we do not hold that line, there will be a great many things we are forced to call goods. I set riches aside — since anybody, however unworthy, can have them, I do not count them among goods; for what is good is not something just anyone can have. I set aside noble birth and popular fame, whipped up by the consensus of fools and scoundrels. But then even the smallest trifles will have to be called goods: white little teeth, pretty eyes, a pleasing complexion, the qualities Anticlea praises as she washes Ulysses' feet — "gentleness of speech, softness of body." If we count those as goods, what will the philosopher's seriousness have to offer that is any weightier or grander than the opinion of the crowd and the mob of fools?

Section 47

"But the Stoics call these same things 'preferred' or 'promoted' — the things your people call goods." So they do; but they deny that such things complete a happy life, while the others think no life is happy without them — or, if it can be happy, they flatly deny it can be happiest. We want it happiest, and that famous Socratic chain of reasoning confirms our claim. For this is how the founding master of philosophy used to argue: as each man's soul is disposed, so is the man; as the man himself is, so is his speech; his actions resemble his speech, and his life resembles his actions. Now the disposition of soul in a good man is praiseworthy; therefore the life of a good man is praiseworthy; and therefore right, since it is praiseworthy. From which the conclusion: the life of good men is happy.

Section 48

And really — in the name of gods and men! — was it not established in our earlier discussions, or were we talking just to amuse ourselves and pass the time, that the wise man is always free of every agitation of soul — what I call the disorders — and that the most tranquil peace always dwells in his mind? A man of self-control, then, steady, without fear, without grief, without any exultant excitement, without lust — is he not happy? But the wise man is always such; therefore he is always happy. And again: how can a good man fail to refer everything he does and everything he feels to the standard of what is praiseworthy? In fact he refers it all to living happily; therefore the happy life is praiseworthy. But nothing is praiseworthy without virtue: therefore the happy life is the work of virtue.

Section 49

The point can be reached this way as well. In a miserable life there is nothing worth proclaiming or glorying in, and none in a life that is neither miserable nor happy. But there is a kind of life in which something is worth proclaiming, glorying in, holding up for all to see — as Epaminondas boasts:

  By my counsels the glory of Sparta was shorn.

And Africanus:

  From where the sun rises beyond Maeotis' marshes,
  no man alive can match my deeds.

Section 50

If that is so, then the happy life is a thing to glory in, to proclaim, to hold up openly — for there is nothing else that deserves proclaiming and displaying. Grant all this, and you see what follows. Indeed, unless the happy life and the honorable life are one and the same, there must be something better than the happy life — for they will certainly admit that whatever is right is better. So there will be something better than the happy life — and what could be said more upside-down than that? Again: when they grant that vice has force enough to make life miserable, must they not grant that virtue has the same force to make life happy? Opposites have opposite consequences.

Section 51

At this point I ask what force there is in the famous scales of Critolaus: he sets the goods of the soul in one pan, the goods of the body and external goods in the other, and judges that the first pan sinks so far that it outweighs the earth and the seas together. Then what stops him — or Xenocrates, that weightiest of philosophers, who exalts virtue so high and thins down and throws out everything else — from placing in virtue not only the happy life, but the happiest?

Section 52

Indeed, if that is not granted, the destruction of the virtues follows. For whoever is open to grief must be open to fear as well — fear being nothing but the anxious expectation of grief to come; and whoever is open to fear is open to dread, timidity, panic, cowardice. So the same man will sometimes be beaten, and will not think that old command of Atreus applies to him:

  Let them so arm themselves for life
  that they know nothing of defeat.

But this man will be defeated, as I said — and not just defeated, but enslaved. We want virtue always free, always unconquered; take that away, and virtue itself is destroyed.

Section 53

And if virtue holds protection enough for living well, it holds enough for living happily too. Virtue is certainly enough for us to live bravely; if bravely, then with greatness of spirit — so that nothing ever frightens us and we are always unconquered. It follows that there is nothing to regret, nothing lacking, nothing in the way; so everything runs smoothly, completely, prosperously — and therefore happily. Virtue is sufficient for living bravely; so it is sufficient for living happily as well.

Section 54

For just as folly never thinks it has gained enough even when it has gotten what it craved, so wisdom is always content with what is present, and never feels a moment's regret over itself. Think of Gaius Laelius and his single consulship — won, at that, after a defeat at the polls (though when a wise and good man like Laelius is passed over, isn't it rather the people who suffer the rejection, losing a good consul, than he, rejected by a good people?). Still, which would you rather be, if the power were given you: consul once, like Laelius, or consul four times, like Cinna?

Section 55

I have no doubt what you will answer — which is exactly why I know whom I am trusting with the question. I would not put it to just anyone. Another man might well answer that he ranks four consulships above one — and more, that he ranks a single day of Cinna's life above whole lifetimes of many famous men. Laelius would have paid a penalty if he had so much as laid a finger on anyone; Cinna ordered the beheading of his own colleague in the consulship, Gnaeus Octavius; the deaths of Publius Crassus and Lucius Caesar, men of the noblest families, whose worth had been proven at home and at war; of Marcus Antonius, the most eloquent man I have ever heard; of Gaius Caesar, who seems to me to have been the very model of humanity, wit, charm, and grace. Happy, then — the man who killed them? To me, on the contrary, he seems miserable, not only for doing these things but for conducting himself in such a way that he was allowed to do them — though strictly speaking no one is allowed to do wrong; that is a slip of language, since we call "allowed" whatever a man is permitted to get away with.

Section 56

Which was the happier Gaius Marius, tell me: when he shared the glory of his victory over the Cimbri with his colleague Catulus — almost a second Laelius; I count the two men very much alike — or when, victorious in civil war and full of rage, he answered the friends of Catulus as they pleaded for his life, and answered not once but again and again: "Let him die"? In that affair the happier man was the one who obeyed the wicked word, not the one who so criminally gave it. For as it is better to receive an injury than to inflict one, so it was better to step forward a little way to meet the death that was already approaching, as Catulus did, than to do as Marius did — by the destruction of such a man, to bury his own six consulships and stain the final stretch of his life.

Section 57

For thirty-eight years Dionysius was tyrant of Syracuse, having seized absolute power at the age of twenty-five. How beautiful the city, how rich the state that he held crushed under slavery! And yet good authorities have left it in writing that this man showed the utmost self-control in his daily habits, and that in the conduct of affairs he was sharp and tireless — and at the same time malicious by nature, and unjust. From which everyone who looks honestly at the truth must conclude that he was utterly miserable. For the very things he had lusted after he failed to obtain even then, at the moment he believed he could do anything.

Section 58

He was born of good parents and respectable position — though the accounts differ on that point — and he had friendships with men of his own age in plenty, close ties with his relatives, and even, in the Greek fashion, young men bound to him in love. He trusted not one of them. Instead he handed the guarding of his body to slaves he had picked out of the households of the rich — he himself struck the name of slavery off them — and to a collection of drifters and savage barbarians. And so, through his unjust lust for power, he had shut himself, in effect, into a prison. He went further: rather than trust his neck to a barber, he taught his own daughters to cut hair — so that royal girls were reduced, like little barber-women, to the mean and servile trade of trimming their father's beard and hair. And even from them, once they were grown, he took the blade away, and set the rule that they should singe his beard and hair with the glowing shells of walnuts.
