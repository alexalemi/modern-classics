Book IV: On the Other Disorders of the Mind

(Part 3 of 3)

Section 58

But since I suspect you are asking not so much about the wise man as about yourself — him you already grant to be free of every disorder; you want to be — let us see how much strength there is in the remedies philosophy applies to the diseases of the soul. For there is a medicine, certainly. Nature has not been so spiteful toward the human race, so much its enemy, as to devise all those healing aids for our bodies and none at all for our souls. In fact she has treated souls even better, because the body's helps have to be brought in from outside, while the soul's health is enclosed within the soul itself. But the greater and more divine the soul's excellence is, the more care it demands. Reason well applied sees what is best; reason neglected tangles itself in error after error.

Section 59

So from here on, everything I say must be aimed at you. You pretend to be asking about the wise man, but perhaps you are really asking about yourself. Now, the disorders I have laid out have various cures. Not every grief is soothed the same way: the mourner needs one medicine, the pitier another, the envier another. And across all four disorders there is a further choice to make — whether the treatment is better aimed at disorder as such, the scorning of reason, an appetite grown too violent, or at the particular kinds, fear, lust, and the rest; and whether you should argue that the thing which set off the grief is not really worth taking hard, or should work to remove grieving altogether. If a man takes it hard that he is poor, do you argue that poverty is no evil, or that a man ought not to take anything hard? Surely the second is better. For if you happen to fail to persuade him about poverty, you have to let him keep his grief; but if you remove grief itself, by the proper arguments we used yesterday, the evil of poverty is, in a way, removed along with it.

Section 60

Any disorder of the soul of this kind can indeed be washed away by the soothing method — by teaching that the thing which gives rise to delight or lust is not a good, and the thing which gives rise to fear or grief is not an evil. But the sure cure, the one proper to the disease, is to teach that the disorders themselves are vicious in their own right — that there is nothing natural about them and nothing necessary. So we see grief itself soften when we reproach mourners with the weakness of an unmanned, effeminate soul, and when we praise the gravity and steadiness of those who bear the human lot without turbulence. And that treatment tends to work even on people who think these things are genuine evils but hold that they should be borne with a calm mind. One man thinks pleasure is the good; another, money; yet the first can still be called back from intemperance, and the second from greed. The other line of argument — the one that removes the false opinion and draws off the grief in the same motion — is certainly the more useful; but it rarely succeeds, and it is not a treatment for the general public.

Section 61

And there are some griefs that this medicine cannot touch at all. Suppose a man is in distress because he has no virtue in him, no spirit, no sense of duty, no moral goodness: what is strangling him really is evil. He needs a different treatment brought to him — and it happens to be a treatment that all the philosophers can supply, however much they quarrel with one another about everything else. For they must all agree on this: motions of the soul that have turned against right reason are vicious. Even if the things that stir fear or grief really are evils, and the things that stir desire or delight really are goods, the motion itself is still vicious. What we mean by a great-spirited and brave man is someone steady, settled, grave, looking down on everything merely human — and nobody can be that man while he is mourning, or fearing, or craving, or exulting. Those states belong to people who have decided that the outcomes of human affairs are bigger than their own souls.

Section 62

That is why all the philosophers, as I said before, have one method of cure: say nothing about what kind of thing it is that has disturbed the soul; speak only about the disturbance itself. Take desire first. When the whole job is to remove it, there is no point asking whether the thing that provokes the lust is good or not — the lust itself is what has to go. So whether the highest good is moral goodness, or pleasure, or the two joined together, or those famous three classes of goods, even if what a man wants too violently is virtue itself, the speech that talks him down should be the same in every case. And human nature, set squarely in view, contains everything needed to calm the soul; to bring the picture into sharper focus, the common condition and the law of life should be spread out in the telling.

Section 63

So it was not for nothing that Socrates, when Euripides was staging his Orestes, is said to have called for the first three lines to be spoken again:

  No tale is so terrible to tell,
  no evil sent by chance or by the anger of heaven,
  that human nature cannot bear it and endure.

And for persuading a man that what has happened can be borne, and ought to be, it is very useful to count off the people who have borne it. Though in fact the calming of grief was laid out both in yesterday's discussion and in my book of Consolation, which I wrote in the middle of mourning and pain — for I was not a wise man. And I did there the very thing Chrysippus forbids: applying the remedy to the soul's swellings, so to speak, while they are still fresh. I did it. I committed violence against nature, so that the greatness of the pain would give way to the greatness of the medicine.

Section 64

Next door to grief — and grief has been discussed enough — stands fear, about which a few things must be said. As grief is of present evil, so fear is of evil to come. That is why some have said fear is a certain part of grief, while others have called it pre-distress — the scout, as it were, that leads in the trouble marching behind it. The same reasonings that make present evils bearable make coming evils contemptible; in facing both, we must see to it that we do nothing low, cringing, soft, effeminate, broken, or abject. But although one should speak of fear's own inconstancy, weakness, and shallowness, it helps enormously to pour contempt on the very things that are feared. So it fell out very neatly — whether by chance or by design — that on the first and second days we discussed the two things men fear most, death and pain. If what was said then stands approved, we are in large part set free from fear.

Section 65

So much for the opinion of evils; now let us look at the opinion of goods — that is, at delight and desire. To my mind, one single fact holds the cause of everything that touches the disorders of the soul: that they are all in our own power, all taken up by judgment, all voluntary. This is the error that must be torn out, the opinion that must be pulled away; and just as with imagined evils the job is to make them more bearable, so with imagined goods it is to take the fever out of whatever is counted great and thrilling. And one rule applies to evils and goods alike: if it proves hard to persuade a man that none of the things which disturb the soul should be counted among goods or among evils, still a different treatment must be fitted to each different motion — the malicious man corrected by one argument, the lover by another, the anxious man by another still, and the timid by yet another.

Section 66

Anyone following the theory of goods and evils that carries the strongest approval could easily deny that a fool can ever feel delight, since a fool never possesses anything good. But for now we are speaking in the common way. Grant, then, that the things men imagine to be goods really are goods — honors, riches, pleasures, the rest: even so, an exulting, over-leaping delight in obtaining them is disgraceful — just as laughing may be allowed while a cackling guffaw still earns blame. The soul spilling out in delight is the same vice as the soul shrinking in pain; desire shows the same shallowness in the reaching that delight shows in the enjoying; and just as men too crushed by trouble are judged shallow, so, rightly, are men too lifted up by delight. And since envying belongs to grief, and taking pleasure in another's misfortune belongs to delight, both are usually chastened by displaying the savagery, the near-brutishness, in them. And as it is becoming to be cautious but not becoming to be afraid, so it is becoming to feel joy but not becoming to feel delight — since, for teaching's sake, we are keeping delight distinct from joy.

Section 67

I said earlier that a shrinking of the soul can never be right, while a lifting of it can. The Hector of Naevius rejoices one way:

  Glad am I to be praised by you, father — praised by a man men praise.

and the character in Trabea quite another:

  The madam, sweetened up with my silver, will be watching for my nod —
  what I want, what I'm angling for. I arrive, I push the door with a fingertip:
  it swings wide. Chrysis catches sight of me, all unexpected;
  she comes flying out to meet me, aching for my embrace.
  She will give herself to me.

How splendid he thinks all this, he will tell you himself: "I will outrun Fortune herself in fortune."

Section 68

Anyone who pays close attention will see straight to the bottom of how disgraceful this delight is. And as those men are disgraceful who lift off in delight while they are enjoying the pleasures of sex, so those are scandalous who crave them with an inflamed mind. In fact the whole of what is commonly called love — and, so help me, I cannot find any other name to call it by — is of such shallowness that I can think of nothing to set beside it. Caecilius reckons that whoever does not hold Love the highest of the gods is "either a fool or a man who knows nothing of life" —

  a god who holds it in his hand
  to make whom he pleases mad, whom sane, whom sick,
  and whom, in turn, be loved, be longed for, be sent for.

Section 69

O poetry, splendid reformer of human life — seating Love, the sponsor of disgrace and shallowness, in the council of the gods! I am speaking of comedy, which could not exist at all if we did not smile on these debaucheries. But what does the captain of the Argonauts say in tragedy?

  You saved me for love's sake, more than honor's.

What then? This love of Medea's — what bonfires of misery it set blazing! And yet in another poet the same woman dares to tell her own father that her husband was

  the man Love gave me — Love, who has more power
  and a stronger claim than any father.

Section 70

But let the poets have their fun; their fables show us Jupiter himself neck-deep in this same disgrace. Let us come to the masters of virtue, the philosophers, who deny that love is a matter of sex — and over this they pick their quarrel with Epicurus, who, in my opinion, is not lying by much. For what is this "love of friendship" of theirs? Why does nobody fall in love with an ugly young man, or with a handsome old one? The custom looks to me to have been born in the gymnasia of the Greeks, where such loves run free and licensed. So Ennius said it well:

  Baring bodies among fellow citizens — that is where disgrace begins.

Even granting that such lovers stay chaste, which I see is possible, they are still anxious and tormented — and the more so precisely because they hold themselves in and rein themselves back.

Section 71

And — to pass over the love of women, to which nature has granted a wider license — who has any doubt what the poets mean by the rape of Ganymede? Who fails to grasp what Laius, in Euripides, is saying and wanting? And what have the most learned men, the greatest poets, published about themselves in their own poems and songs? Alcaeus was known in his own commonwealth as a brave man — and look what he writes about the love of young men. Anacreon's poetry is love poetry from end to end. And Ibycus of Rhegium, his writings make plain, burned with this love beyond them all. Loves of lust, all of them, as we can see. And then we philosophers turned up — with our own Plato at our head, whom Dicaearchus blames, and not unjustly — to lend love our authority.

Section 72

The Stoics go further: they say the wise man will actually love, and they define love itself as "an effort to form a friendship, arising from the appearance of beauty." If there exists anywhere in nature a man who loves without anxiety, without longing, without care, without sighing — very well, let him love; he is free of all lust, and lust is what this discussion is about. But if there is a love — and there certainly is — that stands barely a step from insanity, or not even a step, like the lover's in the Leucadia:

  If only there were any god at all
  who cared what became of me —

Section 73

— as though every god in heaven ought to be busy arranging how this fellow can enjoy his sexual pleasure! "Oh, unlucky me!" Nothing truer. And the other character has it exactly right: "Are you in your senses, wailing away like that?" So even to his own people he looks insane. And then what tragedies he stages!

  Holy Apollo, bring me help! And you I call,
  all-powerful Neptune! And you above all, you Winds!

He thinks the whole universe should drop everything and turn to relieving his love. Venus alone he shuts out, as unfair to him: "Why should I call on you, Venus?" She is too deep in lust, he says, to care about anything — as though it were not lust that has him doing and saying such disgraceful things himself.

Section 74

For a man in this condition, then, the treatment is this. Show him how flimsy the thing he craves is, how contemptible, how utterly worthless — how easily it can be had elsewhere, or had another way, or done without altogether. Sometimes he must also be drawn off to other pursuits — studies, concerns, cares, business; often he must be treated by a change of place, like a sick man who is not yet getting his strength back.

Section 75

Some even think an old love should be driven out by a new one, the way one nail drives out another. But above all he must be warned what a raging madness love is. Of all the disorders of the soul, not one is more violent; so that even if you decline to indict the acts themselves — the seductions, the corruptions, the adulteries, incest itself at the end of the line, the ugliness of every one of them standing ready for indictment — even setting all that aside, the disorder of the mind in love is foul in its own right.

Section 76

For pass over the parts of it that are sheer madness: how much shallowness there is in just the parts that pass for ordinary!

  Affronts, suspicions, feuds, and truces,
  war — then peace again! Try to fix these shifting things
  by fixed reason, and you will get no further
  than if you set yourself to go insane by rule.

This inconstancy, this whipsawing of the mind — who would not be scared off by its sheer crookedness? And here too we must demonstrate what is said of every disorder: that none exists except by opinion, except by judgment, except by choice. If love were natural, everyone would love, and love always, and love the same thing; shame would not scare off one man, reflection another, and plain satiety a third.

Section 77

As for anger — for as long as it has the mind in disorder, there is no doubting it is insanity. Under anger's shove even brothers come to a quarrel like this one:

  "Was there ever a man alive whose impudence outstripped yours?"
  "Was there ever one whose malice outstripped yours?"

You know what follows: insults of the heaviest kind hurled between the brothers in alternating lines, so that you recognize them at once for the sons of Atreus — the Atreus who sits plotting an unheard-of punishment for his own brother:

  Some greater bulk, some greater evil must I brew
  to bludgeon and crush that bitter heart of his.

And where does that bulk come bursting out? Hear Thyestes:

  My own brother urges me — wretch that I am —
  to grind my sons between my jaws.

He is serving him his children's flesh. For where will anger not go? Exactly where madness goes. And so we say, quite properly, that angry men have gone out of their own power — that is, out of the power of counsel, of reason, of mind; for these ought to hold power over the whole soul.

Section 78

With such men, either the targets of their attack must be got out of reach until they collect themselves — and what is collecting yourself but herding the scattered parts of the soul back into their proper places? — or they must be begged and pleaded with, if they hold some power of revenge, to put it off to another time, until the anger simmers down. And "simmering down" plainly admits that a heat had been kindled in the soul against reason's will. Which is why that saying of Archytas is praised: having grown rather angry at his farm steward, "How would I have dealt with you," he said, "if I were not angry!"

Section 79

Where, then, are the people who call hot temper useful — can insanity be useful? — or natural? Can anything be according to nature that happens with reason fighting against it? And if anger were natural, how would one man be angrier than another? How would the lust for revenge ever let go before it had revenged itself? How would any man repent of what he had done in anger — as we see King Alexander did, who, after he had killed his dear friend Clitus, could barely keep his hands off himself, so great was the force of his remorse? Knowing all this, who can doubt that this motion of the soul, too, is wholly a matter of opinion, and voluntary? And who could doubt that the soul's sicknesses — greed, say, or the craving for glory — arise from setting a high price on the very thing that is making the soul sick? From which we ought to understand that every disorder, likewise, lies in opinion.

Section 80

And if confidence — that is, a firm trust in one's own soul — is a kind of knowledge, the weighty opinion of a mind that does not assent rashly, then fear is a mistrust in the face of an expected and impending evil; and if hope is the expectation of good, fear must be the expectation of evil. So fear, and the rest of the disorders with it, consist in evil. Therefore, as steadiness belongs to knowledge, disorder belongs to error. And people who are called angry or pitying or envious by nature are simply constituted with a kind of bad health of the soul — yet curable even so, as is told of Socrates. When Zopyrus, who claimed he could read any man's nature from his looks, had piled a whole heap of vices on Socrates in front of a crowd, the rest laughed him down, since they could find no such vices in Socrates; but Socrates himself came to his rescue, saying that those vices really had been planted in him — and that he had thrown them out by reason.

Section 81

So, just as a man who appears in the finest health may still be naturally more prone to some particular disease, one soul leans toward one set of vices and another toward others. And in those who are called vicious not by nature but by their own fault, the vices stand on false opinions about good and evil things, so that one man is more prone to this motion and disorder, another to that. But entrenchment, as in the body, is harder to drive out than a mere disturbance: a sudden swelling of the eyes is cured quickly; a long-standing inflammation takes far more to shift.

Section 82

But now that the cause of the disorders stands discovered — all of them arise from judgments of opinion, from acts of will — let this discussion have its limit. This much, though, we ought to know: now that the ends of goods and evils have been found, as far as man can find them, nothing greater or more useful can be asked of philosophy than what we have been discussing on these four days. To the contempt of death, and to pain made lighter for the bearing, we have added the calming of grief — and man has no greater evil than grief. For though every disorder of the soul is heavy, and differs little from outright madness, still, when others are in the grip of some disorder — of fear, of delight, of desire — we usually say only that they are stirred up and disturbed; but the ones who have handed themselves over to grief we call miserable, afflicted, wretched, ruined.

Section 83

So it seems to be no accident, but your own reasoned design, that we discussed grief separately from the other disorders; for in grief sits the fountain and head of all miseries. But the cure of grief, and of all the soul's other diseases, is one cure: they are all matters of opinion, all voluntary, all taken up because taking them up seems right. That error — the root, as it were, of every evil — philosophy promises to pull out whole.

Section 84

Let us hand ourselves over to philosophy, then, to be cultivated, and let ourselves be healed. As long as these evils sit lodged in us, we cannot be happy — we cannot even be well. So either let us deny that reason accomplishes anything, when in truth nothing can be done rightly without reason; or else, since philosophy is built of the marshaling of reasons, let us — if we want to be good, and to be happy — seek from her every aid and every help toward living well and living happily.
