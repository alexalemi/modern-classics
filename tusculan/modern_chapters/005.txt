Book II: On Bearing Pain

(Part 2 of 2)

Section 36

That is why the men who gave Greece its forms of government wanted young bodies hardened by labor. The Spartans carried the policy even to their women, who in other cities are raised in the softest luxury, "hidden in the shadow of the walls." The Spartans wanted nothing of that kind for their girls,

  whose passion is the wrestling ground, the Eurotas, sun,
  dust, toil, the drills of war — not breeding like barbarians.

So in these strenuous exercises pain comes running in from time to time: they are shoved, struck, thrown down, they fall — and the labor itself lays a kind of callus over pain.

Section 37

Then there is military service — ours, I mean, not the Spartans', whose battle line steps forward in time to the flute and hardly takes a word of encouragement that isn't set to a marching beat. You can see, first, where our armies get their very name: exercitus, from exercise. And then think what labor a march is. Carrying more than half a month's rations; carrying whatever else they might need; carrying the stakes for the palisade — because as for shield, sword, and helmet, our soldiers no more count those as part of the load than they count their own shoulders, arms, and hands. Weapons, they say, are a soldier's limbs, and they carry them so handily that at need they can drop the packs and fight with their weapons as freely as with their limbs. And the legions' training! The running, the charge, the battle cry — what labor it all costs! That is where the spirit comes from that is ready for wounds in battle. Bring up an untrained soldier with just as much raw courage, and he will look like a woman.

Section 38

Why is there as much difference as we have seen between a raw army and a veteran one? Recruits usually have the better of it in age; but bearing labor and despising wounds are things that only habit teaches. In fact, watch when the wounded are carried off the field. The raw, untrained man, hit ever so lightly, lets out the most shameful wailing; but the trained veteran — braver for that very reason — only looks for a doctor to bandage him, and says:

  Patroclus, I come to you for help, for your hands,
  before an evil death dealt by an enemy's hand takes me down.
  There is no way to stop this flowing blood
  unless your skill can turn my death aside;
  the wounded fill the porches of the sons of Aesculapius —
  there is no getting near them.

That is Eurypylus, no question — a trained man!

Section 39

And while his friend keeps piling on the grief, notice how far from tearful his answer is — he even supplies a reason why he should bear it calmly:

  Whoever plans destruction for another
  should know an equal death is planned for him, to share.

Patroclus will now lead him off, I suppose, to lay him on a bed and dress the wound. If he were an ordinary human being, he would; but nothing of the kind. He asks how the battle went:

  Speak, speak: how does the Argives' cause hold up in the fight?

  No words could ever tell the toil
  as fully as the deeds have shown it.

So rest, then, and have the wound bound up. But even if Eurypylus could stop there, Aesopus — the actor playing him — could not:

  When Hector's fortune pressed our faltering line...

and he unrolls all the rest of it, in pain as he is. That is how unbounded military glory is in a brave man. So a veteran soldier can manage this, and a trained and wise man will not be able to? He will manage it better — and not by a little.

Section 40

But so far I am still talking about habit and training, not yet about reason and wisdom. Little old women often go without food for two or three days. Take an athlete's food away for one single day and he will cry out to Jupiter — Olympian Jupiter, the very god he trains for — and howl that he cannot stand it. Great is the force of habit. Hunters spend whole nights in the snow and let themselves be scorched in the mountains. Boxers, battered by the studded gloves, do not so much as groan.

Section 41

But why mention men who treat a victory at Olympia like one of our old-time consulships? Gladiators — ruined men, or barbarians — what blows they take! See how men who have been properly trained would rather take a hit than dodge it in disgrace! How often it is plain that they want nothing more than to satisfy their owner or the crowd! Even worn down with wounds, they send to their owners to ask what they want: if the owners are satisfied, they are content to lie down and die. What gladiator of even middling quality has ever groaned, ever changed his face? Which of them has disgraced himself standing — or even going down? Which, once down and ordered to take the sword, has pulled in his neck? That is how much training, rehearsal, and habit can do. So this can be done by

  a Samnite — a filthy fellow, worthy of that life and station —

and a man born for glory is going to have some part of his soul so soft that he cannot toughen it with rehearsal and reason? Some people find the gladiator shows cruel and inhuman, and the way they are run now, they may be right. But in the days when it was condemned men fighting it out with steel, there may have been stronger schoolings against pain and death for the ear — for the eye there was none.

Section 42

I have now covered exercise, habit, and rehearsal. Come on, then, let us look at reason — unless you have something to say to all this.

"Interrupt you?" he said. "I wouldn't dream of it. Your argument is carrying me along toward believing it."

So I went on. Whether pain is an evil or not, the Stoics can see to; they try to prove pain is no evil with twisted, fussy little syllogisms that never sink in as far as the senses. My own view is this: whatever pain is, it is not as great as it appears; people are shaken far too hard by its false picture and appearance; and every pain is bearable. Where shall I begin, then? Shall I briefly touch on what I just said, so the argument can travel farther more easily?

Section 43

This much everyone agrees on — the unlearned as well as the learned: that bearing pain with endurance is the mark of brave, great-spirited, patient men who rise above human weakness; and no one ever thought a man who bore it that way was anything but praiseworthy. So here is something demanded of the brave and praised whenever it happens — and is it not shameful to dread it while it approaches, or to buckle when it arrives? And consider this: though all the right conditions of the soul are called virtues, that may not strictly be the proper name for all of them; they may all have been named after the one that towered over the rest. "Virtue" comes from vir, the Latin for "man," and the quality most a man's own is courage, whose two greatest offices are the contempt of death and the contempt of pain. So we must exercise both if we want to possess virtue — or rather, if we want to be men, since virtue borrowed its very name from man. How, you may ask — and rightly, for that is exactly the medicine philosophy claims to offer.

Section 44

Enter Epicurus — by no means a bad man; say rather, the best of men. He gives as much advice as he understands. "Ignore pain," he says. Who is saying this? The same man who calls pain the greatest evil. Not very consistent. Let us hear him out. "If pain is at its greatest," he says, "it is necessarily brief." "Run that past me again!" — because I do not quite grasp what you call "greatest" or what you call "brief." "Greatest is what nothing surpasses; brief is what nothing is briefer than. I despise the size of any pain when the shortness of its stay will rescue me from it almost before it arrives." But what if the pain is as great as Philoctetes' pain? "That looks plenty great to me — but still not the greatest. Nothing hurts him but his foot. The eyes can hurt, the head can, the sides, the lungs — everything can. So he is a long way from the greatest pain. Therefore," he says, "a long-lasting pain has more pleasure in it than trouble."

Section 45

Now, I cannot say that so great a man understands nothing; my suspicion is that he is laughing at us. I say that the greatest pain — and I call it greatest even if some other pain runs ten atoms bigger — is not automatically brief, and I can name plenty of good men who have been racked for years on end by the fiercest pains of gout. Our clever friend never fixes any limit for the size or the duration, so that I could know what he calls "greatest" in pain and "brief" in time. Let us pass over him, then, as a man who says precisely nothing, and make him admit that remedies for pain are not to be had from someone who declared pain the greatest of all evils — however much the same man put up a brave little front under his colic and his kidney torments. No, the medicine has to be fetched from somewhere else — and if we are looking for what hangs together best, from those who hold that moral goodness is the highest good and disgrace the greatest evil. In their company you will certainly not dare to moan and thrash about. Virtue herself will speak to you with their voice:

Section 46

"You? You have watched boys in Sparta, young men at Olympia, barbarians in the arena take the heaviest blows and bear them in silence — and if some pain gives you a pinch, you will scream like a woman instead of bearing it with steadiness and calm?" "It cannot be borne; nature will not allow it." — I hear you. Yet boys bear it, led on by glory; others bear it out of shame, many out of fear — and we still worry that nature cannot endure what is endured by so many people, in so many places? Nature does not merely endure it; she demands it. She has nothing finer, nothing she reaches for more, than moral goodness, than praise, than dignity, than honor. By all these names I mean one single thing; I use several to mark it as strongly as I can. What I want to say is that far the best thing for a human being is whatever is worth choosing for its own sake — springing from virtue or seated in virtue itself, praiseworthy on its own account. I would sooner call it the only good than deny that it is the highest good. And what holds for moral goodness holds in reverse for disgrace: nothing is so foul, so repellent, so unworthy of a human being.

Section 47

If you are convinced of this — and at the beginning you said you saw more evil in disgrace than in pain — then what remains is for you to rule yourself. It is an odd way of talking, as though we were two people, one to command and the other to obey; and yet it is not a foolish way of talking. The soul is divided into two parts, one of which shares in reason while the other has none. So when we are told to command ourselves, this is what is meant: reason must rein in recklessness. In nearly every soul there is by nature something soft, low, cringing — nerveless, so to speak, and limp. If that were all a man had, nothing would be uglier than a human being. But standing ready is reason, the mistress and queen of everything, who by her own striving, advancing further and further, becomes perfected virtue. A man's task is to make sure that reason commands the part of the soul whose duty is to obey.

Section 48

"How?" you will ask. The way a master commands a slave, a general a soldier, a parent a child. If that soft part of the soul I described behaves disgracefully — if it gives itself over to womanish wailing and tears — let it be tied down and put under the guard of friends and family. We often see people broken by shame whom no reasoning could ever defeat. Feelings like those we should lock up like runaway slaves, practically in chains and under watch; but those who are steadier without being fully hardened we should call to order like good soldiers being recalled to the standard: guard your dignity. In the Niptra, the wisest man of Greece does not lament his wound too much — he keeps it measured:

  Step by step — go gently, keep the strain slow and steady,
  or the jolting will let a greater pain
  take hold of me.

Section 49

Pacuvius handles this better than Sophocles, for in Sophocles Ulysses weeps over his wound all too tearfully. Yet even at this light groaning, the men carrying the wounded hero, with an eye on the dignity of the character, do not hesitate to say:

  You too, Ulysses — grave though we see
  your wound to be, you are almost too soft
  in spirit, for a man whose whole life
  was spent in arms...

The shrewd poet understands that long habit in bearing pain is a teacher not to be despised.

Section 50

And Ulysses, though his pain is great, keeps it within bounds:

  Hold me, hold on! The wound is crushing me.
  Uncover it! Oh, wretched me! It is torture!

He begins to slip; then instantly he stops:

  Cover it. Leave me. Let me be —
  your handling and your shaking only swell
  the savage pain.

Do you see how it fell silent? Not the pain of his body — that was not soothed — but the pain of his mind, which was disciplined. And so at the end of the Niptra he scolds others, even as he is dying:

  To complain of hostile fortune is fitting; to wail over it is not.
  That is the duty of a man; weeping was assigned to woman's nature.

The softer part of his soul obeyed reason the way a soldier with a sense of shame obeys a stern general.

Section 51

But the man in whom wisdom will be complete — a man we have never yet seen, though the philosophers' writings spell out what he will be like if he ever appears — that man, or rather the perfect and absolute reason in him, will command the lower part the way a just parent commands upright children: with a nod. It will accomplish what it wants with no labor, no trouble. He will rouse himself, brace himself, equip himself, arm himself, to stand against pain as against an enemy. And what are these arms? Tension, self-encouragement, and inner talk, when a man says to himself: "Nothing shameful now, nothing limp, nothing unmanly."

Section 52

Let the noble examples pass before the mind. Think of Zeno of Elea, who suffered everything rather than name his partners in the plot to bring down the tyranny. Think of Anaxarchus, the follower of Democritus, who fell into the hands of Nicocreon, the king of Cyprus, and neither begged off any form of torture nor refused it. Calanus the Indian — untaught, a barbarian, born at the roots of the Caucasus — was burned alive by his own free choice. And we, if a foot aches, if a tooth does, cannot bear it. For there is a kind of soft, empty-headed opinion at work — in pleasure no less than in pain — and when we melt and go liquid with that softness, we cannot take a bee sting without a scream.

Section 53

But Gaius Marius — a country-bred man, yet every inch a man — when he was operated on, as I said earlier, refused at the start to be strapped down; and no one before Marius is said to have been cut unbound. Why, then, were others able to bear it afterward? The force of his example. So do you see that pain is an evil of opinion, not of nature? And yet the same Marius showed that pain's bite is sharp: he would not offer the other leg. He bore pain like a man; and, like a sensible human being, he declined to bear a greater pain without a necessary reason. The whole point, then, is to be master of yourself. And I have shown what kind of mastery that is: this steady thinking about what is most worthy of patience, of courage, of greatness of spirit not only holds the soul in check but somehow makes the pain itself milder.

Section 54

It is like what happens in battle. The cowardly, frightened soldier throws away his shield the moment he sees the enemy and runs as hard as he can — and sometimes he dies for exactly that reason, without a wound on his body, while the man who held his ground has nothing of the kind happen to him. In the same way, those who cannot bear the mere look of pain throw themselves away and lie there crushed and lifeless, while those who stood up to it very often come away the winners. For the soul has certain resemblances to the body. As loads are carried more easily by a body under tension and crush a body that goes slack, so the soul, by its own tension, pushes off the whole weight pressing on it — and by slackening is so pinned down that it cannot lift itself.

Section 55

If we want the truth, the soul's tension is needed in carrying out every duty; it is duty's only bodyguard, so to speak. But in pain above all we must see to it that we do nothing abjectly, nothing timidly, nothing like a coward, a slave, or a woman — and, first and foremost, that Philoctetes-scream must be refuted and thrown out. Groaning is granted to a man now and then, and rarely at that; howling is not granted even to a woman. That wailing is the "lessus" which the Twelve Tables, our oldest laws, forbade at funerals.

Section 56

In fact, a brave and wise man never groans at all — unless perhaps to tighten himself into firmness, the way runners in the stadium shout as loudly as they can. Athletes do the same in training, and boxers, when they land a blow, actually groan as they swing the studded gloves — not because they are in pain or losing heart, but because in flinging out the voice the whole body tightens, and the blow comes in harder. And men who want to shout at full volume — are they content to strain the sides, the throat, the tongue, the organs we see the voice drawn and poured from? No: the whole body, "every hoof and claw," as the saying goes, is thrown behind the straining of the voice.

Section 57

I swear I saw Marcus Antonius touch his knee to the ground while he strained every nerve speaking in his own defense under the Varian law. Just as catapults and the other engines for hurling stones and darts throw harder the more tightly they are wound and drawn back, so a voice, a sprint, a blow lands heavier the more tension launches it. Since that tension has such power, here is my rule: if groaning in pain will help stiffen the spirit, we will use it. But if the groan is all lament — if it is weak, abject, tearful — then I would hardly call the man who gives in to it a man. Even if groaning did bring some relief, we would still have to ask whether it suited a brave and spirited man. But since it takes away none of the pain, why volunteer for disgrace and get nothing for it? What is more disgraceful in a man than crying like a woman?

Section 58

And this rule given for pain reaches wider. Everything, not only pain, must be resisted with the same tension of soul. Anger flares up, lust is stirred — we run to the same citadel and take up the same weapons. But since pain is our subject, let those go. So, for bearing pain calmly and steadily, it helps enormously to consider with your whole heart, as the saying is, how honorable that is. For by nature — as I said before, and it needs saying often — we are the keenest lovers and seekers of moral goodness, and if we catch so much as a glimmer of its light, there is nothing we are not prepared to bear and to suffer to possess it. From this rush and charge of the soul toward true praise and honor come the dangers men face in battle. Brave men do not feel their wounds in the fighting line — or they feel them, and prefer death to stepping one inch down from the place their dignity holds.

Section 59

The Decii saw the flashing swords of the enemy as they charged into their ranks. The nobility and glory of that death lifted all fear of wounds from them. Do you suppose Epaminondas groaned when he felt his life flowing out together with his blood? No — he was leaving his country ruling over the Spartans, when he had received it as their servant. These are the consolations, these the dressings, for the greatest pains.

Section 60

You will say: what about in peacetime? At home? In bed? You are calling me back to the philosophers, who do not often march into the line. One of them, a decidedly flimsy character, Dionysius of Heraclea, learned courage from Zeno the Stoic — and then pain untaught him. His kidneys were tormenting him, and in the middle of his own howling he kept shouting that everything he had once believed about pain was false. When his fellow student Cleanthes asked what argument had driven him from his old opinion, he answered: "This one. If after giving so much work to philosophy I still could not bear pain, that would be proof enough that pain is an evil. Well, I have spent a great many years on philosophy, and I cannot bear it. Therefore pain is an evil." At that, the story goes, Cleanthes struck his foot on the ground and recited a line from the Epigoni:

  Do you hear this, Amphiaraus, hidden under the earth?

He meant Zeno; he grieved that Dionysius had fallen away from him.

Section 61

Not so our own Posidonius. I saw him often myself, and I will tell you what Pompeius used to relate. When Pompeius reached Rhodes on his way back from Syria, he wanted to hear Posidonius lecture; and on learning that he was gravely ill — his joints wracked with violent pain — he still wished at least to look in on so famous a philosopher. When he had seen him, greeted him, and paid him generous compliments, he said how sorry he was that he could not hear him speak. "But you can," Posidonius said. "I will not let bodily pain make so great a man's visit to me count for nothing." And so, Pompeius used to say, lying on his back he argued, gravely and at rich length, this very thesis: that nothing is good except what is morally good. And every time the pain came at him like torches held to his body, he would say: "You are wasting your effort, pain! Be as troublesome as you like — I will never admit that you are an evil."

Section 62

In general, all hardships that carry fame and distinction become bearable on the spot. Do we not see that among peoples where the contests called gymnastic are held in high honor, the men who step into that arena shirk no pain? That where riding and hunting carry glory, the men who chase it refuse no pain? And what shall I say of our own ambitions, our hunger for office? What fire have candidates not run through to rake in votes one by one? That is why Africanus always had Xenophon, the student of Socrates, in his hands. He especially praised the saying that the same labors do not weigh equally on the general and the common soldier, because the honor itself makes the general's labor lighter.

Section 63

Still, it turns out that even with the unwise crowd the notion of honor carries weight, though they cannot see the thing itself. They are steered by rumor and the verdict of the many, and count as honorable whatever most people praise. But you — even if you stand in the crowd's gaze, I would not have you rest on its judgment or take what it admires to be the finest thing there is. You must use your own judgment. If you can be pleased with yourself when you approve what is right, then you will have conquered not only yourself — which is what I was urging a moment ago — but everyone and everything.

Section 64

So set this before your mind: a largeness of soul, a lifting of the soul as high as it will go, which shows itself most in scorning pains and looking down on them, is the single most beautiful thing there is — and the more beautiful if it does without the public, chases no applause, and finds its delight in itself. To me, in fact, everything seems the more praiseworthy the more it is done without advertisement and without the crowd for witness. Not that the public eye should be avoided — every good deed wants to stand in the light — but still, virtue has no greater theater than conscience.

Section 65

And let us rehearse this above all: the endurance of pain, which I have said again and again must be firmed up by the soul's tension, should show itself the same in every situation. Many men who have bravely taken wounds and stood up under them — out of hunger for victory, or for glory, or to hold on to their rights and their liberty — cannot bear the pain of a disease once that tension is dropped. For they had borne the earlier pain not by reason or wisdom but by eagerness and glory. That is why certain wild barbarians can fight ferociously with the sword but cannot be sick like men, while the Greeks, not particularly brave but sensible as far as human capacity allows, cannot look an enemy in the face and yet bear disease patiently and with composure. And the Cimbri and the Celtiberians exult in battle and wail in sickness. Nothing can be steady that does not start from fixed reason.

Section 66

But when you see that men led by eagerness or mere opinion are not broken by pain in chasing and winning what they are after, you ought to conclude one of two things: either pain is no evil, or — even if we agree to call whatever is harsh and against nature an evil — it is so tiny an evil that virtue buries it until it disappears from view. Think this over, I beg you, day and night. For this argument will spread; it will occupy considerably more ground than the question of pain alone. If we do everything to flee disgrace and to win moral goodness, we will be free to despise not only the needles of pain but even the lightning bolts of fortune — especially since that refuge from yesterday's discussion stands ready.

Section 67

Imagine a sailor with pirates in pursuit, and a god who says to him: "Throw yourself from the ship. Something stands by to catch you — a dolphin, as one caught Arion of Methymna, or those horses of Pelops, Neptune's gift, which are said to have 'swept the chariot along, skimming above the waves.' They will catch you and carry you wherever you wish." That man would drop every fear. Just so, when harsh and hateful pains bear down: if they are so great that they ought not to be borne, you see where the refuge lies. That is about all I thought needed saying today. But perhaps you still hold your old opinion.

"Not in the least," he said. "These two days, I hope, have freed me from the fear of the two things I feared most."

"Tomorrow, then, we meet the water clock — rhetoric, as we agreed. Though I can see the philosophy hour is a debt I will still owe you."

"Exactly — rhetoric before noon, and this at the same hour as today."

"So we will do, and I will gladly go along with this excellent zeal of yours."
