Book V: Whether Virtue Alone Is Sufficient for a Happy Life

(Part 1 of 4)

Section 1

This fifth day, my dear Brutus, will bring our Tusculan Disputations to a close — the day we debated the doctrine you approve above all others. For I have seen clearly, both from the book you wrote for me with such care and from many of our conversations, how firmly you are persuaded that virtue is sufficient by itself for a happy life. Hard as that is to prove, given the many and varied torments fortune can inflict, it is the kind of truth we must labor over, so that the proof comes easier. For of all the subjects philosophy handles, there is none that is stated with greater weight or greater magnificence.

Section 2

Consider what drove the first men who gave themselves to the study of philosophy: they set everything else aside and devoted their whole lives to searching out the best condition of life, and surely it was the hope of living happily that made them invest so much care and effort in the pursuit. If virtue was discovered and perfected by them, and if there is protection enough in virtue for a happy life, then who could fail to think the work of philosophizing gloriously founded by them and well taken up by us? But if virtue is subject to accidents various and uncertain — fortune's handmaid, without strength enough to defend herself — then I fear that our hope of a happy life should rest not so much on confidence in virtue as on prayers.

Section 3

Indeed, when I turn over in my mind the blows with which fortune has so violently exercised me, I begin at times to distrust this doctrine, and to dread the weakness and fragility of the human race. For I fear that nature, having given us feeble bodies and fastened to them incurable diseases and unbearable pains, may have given us souls to match — souls that share the body's pains and are tangled, besides, in anguishes and troubles all their own.

Section 4

But here I correct myself. I am judging the strength of virtue from the softness of others, and perhaps from my own — not from virtue herself. For she — if only there is such a thing as virtue, and your uncle Cato removed that doubt, Brutus — holds everything that can befall a human being beneath her. Looking down from that height, she despises human accidents; and being free of all fault, she judges that nothing but herself is any concern of hers. But we, who enlarge every approaching evil with fear and every present one with grief, prefer to condemn the nature of things rather than our own error.

Section 5

Yet the correction of this fault, and of all our other vices and wrongs, must be sought from philosophy. Into her lap my own will and eagerness drove me from my earliest years; and now, under these heaviest of blows, tossed by a great storm, I have fled for refuge to the same harbor from which I first set out. O philosophy, guide of life! O seeker of virtue and banisher of vice! What would we — what would human life itself — have been without you? You gave birth to cities; you called scattered human beings together into the fellowship of common life; you joined them to one another, first by settled homes, then by marriage, then by the shared bond of letters and speech. You were the inventor of laws; you were the teacher of morals and discipline. To you we fly for refuge, from you we beg help; and to you, as once in great part, so now wholly and completely, I give myself over. One single day lived well, in obedience to your precepts, is worth more than an immortality of doing wrong.

Section 6

Whose resources should I draw on, then, if not yours — you who have granted us tranquility of life and lifted from us the terror of death? Yet philosophy, far from being praised as her services to human life deserve, is neglected by most people and actually reviled by many. To revile the parent of life! Does anyone dare stain himself with what amounts to parricide — dare be so impiously ungrateful as to accuse the very one he ought to revere, even if he lacked the wit to appreciate her? But this error, I suppose — this fog spread over untaught minds — comes of their being unable to look far enough back: they never imagine that the men who first equipped human life were philosophers.

Section 7

The thing itself, we can see, is exceedingly ancient; only the name, we admit, is recent. And who could deny that wisdom itself is ancient in name as well as in fact? Among the ancients it earned that most beautiful name through the knowledge of things divine and human, of the beginnings and causes of everything. So the famous Seven, the men the Greeks called sophoi, were both considered and called wise men by our people; so, many generations earlier, was Lycurgus, in whose time Homer too is said to have lived, before this city of ours was founded; and so, back in the heroic ages, were Ulysses and Nestor — men who both were wise and were reputed to be.

Section 8

Nor would tradition tell us that Atlas holds up the sky, or that Prometheus was nailed to the Caucasus, or that Cepheus was set among the stars with his wife, his son-in-law, and his daughter, had not their more-than-human knowledge of the heavens carried their names over into the error of fable. After them, all who devoted their studies to the contemplation of nature were considered and called wise men, and that name flowed down all the way to the age of Pythagoras. Pythagoras — as Heraclides of Pontus writes, a pupil of Plato and a deeply learned man — is said to have come to Phlius and to have discoursed learnedly and at length on certain subjects with Leon, the leading man of the Phliasians. Leon, admiring his intelligence and his eloquence, asked him what art he most relied on; and Pythagoras answered that he knew no art at all — he was a philosopher. Struck by the newness of the word, Leon asked what philosophers were, and what set them apart from everyone else.

Section 9

Pythagoras answered that human life seemed to him like the great fair held at the games — the festival celebrated with the most magnificent shows before a gathering of all Greece. There, some men sought glory and the distinction of a crown with their trained bodies; others were drawn by the profit to be made in buying and selling; but there was one certain kind — and this by far the most free-spirited — who came neither for applause nor for gain, but simply to see, and who studied closely what was being done and how. In the same way, he said, we have come into this life from another life and nature, as if traveling from some other city into the crowded bustle of a fair: some to be slaves of glory, some of money — and a rare few who, counting everything else as nothing, look eagerly and closely into the nature of things. These call themselves lovers of wisdom — for that is what "philosopher" means. And just as at the fair the most gentlemanly thing is to look on while acquiring nothing for oneself, so in life the contemplation and understanding of nature far surpasses every other pursuit.

Section 10

Nor was Pythagoras merely the inventor of the name; he enlarged the thing itself. When he came to Italy after this conversation at Phlius, he adorned the Greece that was called Great Greece, both in private life and in public, with the most excellent institutions and arts. Of his school there will perhaps be another occasion to speak. But from the old philosophy down to Socrates — who had studied with Archelaus, a pupil of Anaxagoras — the subjects were numbers and motions, the source from which all things arise and into which they fall back; those early thinkers eagerly investigated the sizes of the stars, their distances, their courses, and all things in the heavens. It was Socrates who first called philosophy down from the sky, settled it in cities, brought it even into people's homes, and forced it to ask about life and morals, about good and evil.

Section 11

His many-sided way of arguing, the variety of his subjects, and the greatness of his genius — immortalized in Plato's memory and Plato's writings — produced several schools of philosophers who disagree among themselves. Of these I have followed, above all, the one I believe Socrates himself used: to keep my own opinion concealed, to relieve others of their errors, and in every discussion to search for what is most like the truth. Carneades kept up this practice with the greatest sharpness and fluency, and I have argued by that same custom often elsewhere, and recently at my place in Tusculum. The talk of the first four days I have already sent you, written out in the earlier books. On the fifth day, when we had taken our seats in the same spot, the question we were to debate was proposed like this.

Section 12

"It does not seem to me that virtue can be sufficient for a happy life," he began.

"But by Hercules it seems so to my dear Brutus," I said, "and — with your leave — I rank his judgment far above yours."

"I don't doubt it. But the question now isn't how much you love him; it's the nature of this thing I've just stated my view on. That's what I want you to argue."

"So you deny that virtue can be sufficient for a happy life?"

"I deny it flatly."

"But for living rightly, honorably, admirably — in short, for living well — is there protection enough in virtue?"

"Enough, certainly."

"Then can you call a man who lives badly anything but miserable? Or admit that a man lives well and deny that he lives happily?"

"Why couldn't I? Even under torture a man can live rightly, honorably, admirably — and for that reason well — provided you understand what I mean here by 'well.' I mean with steadiness, with dignity, with wisdom, with courage. Those can be thrown onto the rack too; but the happy life doesn't go anywhere near it."

Section 13

"What then?" I asked. "Is the happy life the only one left standing outside the door and threshold of the prison, while steadiness, dignity, courage, wisdom, and the rest of the virtues are hauled off to the torturer, refusing no punishment and no pain?"

"If you mean to accomplish anything," he said, "you'll have to hunt up something new. That sort of argument doesn't move me in the least — not only because it's shopworn, but much more because, like certain light wines that lose all their strength in water, these Stoic arguments please more in the tasting than in the drinking. Take that chorus of virtues set up on the rack: it puts images of the utmost dignity before your eyes, so that the happy life seems to be hurrying straight toward them, unwilling to let them be abandoned. But once you drag your mind away from that painting, away from those images of the virtues, to the fact and the truth of the matter, this bare question is what remains: can anyone be happy as long as he is being tortured?

Section 14

"So that is what we should be asking now. And don't be afraid that the virtues will protest and complain at being abandoned by the happy life. For if no virtue exists without practical wisdom, then practical wisdom herself sees this much — that not all good men are also happy. She remembers a great deal about Marcus Atilius, Quintus Caepio, Marcus Aquilius; and if you'd rather deal in images than in realities, it is practical wisdom herself who holds the happy life back as it tries to climb onto the rack, and who declares that it has nothing in common with pain and torture."

Section 15

"I can easily put up with your arguing this way," I said, "though it's hardly fair of you to prescribe how you'd like me to conduct the case. But let me ask: do we judge that something was accomplished on the previous days, or nothing?"

"Something, certainly — quite a bit, in fact."

"Well, if that's so, then this question is already battered and brought nearly to its end."

"How so, exactly?"

"Because turbulent movements and agitations of the soul, whipped up and carried along by reckless impulse, beat back all reason and leave no part of a happy life standing. Who can fail to be miserable while he fears death or pain — the one so often at hand, the other always hanging over us? And if the same man also fears poverty, disgrace, infamy, as most men do — or crippling weakness, or blindness, or finally slavery, which has befallen not just single men but powerful nations — can anyone who fears all that be happy?

Section 16

"And what of the man who not only fears these things in the future but bears and endures them in the present? Add to the list exile, mourning, the loss of children. A man broken by such things, crushed under grief — can he possibly be anything but utterly miserable? And again: the man we see inflamed and raging with lusts, snatching at everything with rabid, insatiable desire, thirsting the more fiercely and hotly the more abundantly he drinks in pleasures from every side — wouldn't you be right to call him utterly miserable? And the man carried away by frivolity, exulting in empty delight, strutting for no reason — isn't he the more miserable the happier he thinks himself? So just as these men are miserable, those, on the contrary, are happy: men whom no fears terrify, no griefs gnaw at, no lusts goad, no hollow exultant delights melt into languid pleasures. We recognize the calm of the sea when not even the faintest breeze stirs its waves; in the same way, the soul's state is seen to be quiet and at peace when there is no disorder that could move it.

Section 17

"If, then, there is a man who counts the power of fortune — who counts everything human, everything that can befall anyone — as bearable, so that neither fear nor anguish can touch him; and if the same man craves nothing and is carried away by no empty pleasure of the soul, what reason is there why he should not be happy? And if virtue accomplishes all this, why should virtue by itself not make men happy?"

"The first point can't be denied," he answered. "Those who fear nothing, are anguished by nothing, crave nothing, and are swept away by no unbridled delight are happy — so I grant you that. As for the second, it is no longer an open question: our earlier discussions established that the wise man is free from every disorder of the soul."

Section 18

"Then evidently the case is finished," I said. "The question seems to have reached its end."

"Very nearly so, at least."

"And yet that procedure belongs to the mathematicians, not to the philosophers. When the geometers want to demonstrate something, they take whatever bears on it from their previous demonstrations as conceded and proved, and explain only what hasn't been treated before. Philosophers, whatever question they have in hand, gather into it everything relevant, even if they have argued it all out somewhere else. If it were otherwise, why would the Stoic have so much to say when asked whether virtue is sufficient for a happy life? It would be answer enough for him to say that he had already shown that nothing is good except what is right; with that proved, it follows that the happy life is content with virtue — and just as the one follows from the other, so the other follows from it in turn: if the happy life is content with virtue, then nothing is good except what is right.

Section 19

"Yet that is not how they proceed. They write separate books on the right and on the highest good; and although it follows from the latter that there is force enough in virtue for a happy life, they still treat the point on its own. For every question — above all one this great — deserves to be handled with its own proper arguments and its own encouragements. And make no mistake: philosophy has never uttered a nobler declaration, never made a promise richer or greater. For what does she profess? Good gods! — that she will make the man who obeys her laws armed against fortune forever; that he will have within himself every defense of a good and happy life; in short, that he will always be happy.

Section 20

"What she actually delivers, I will see to later; in the meantime I set great store by the promise itself. Xerxes, stuffed with every prize and gift of fortune, was not content with his cavalry, his infantry, his multitude of ships, his boundless weight of gold: he offered a reward to anyone who could invent a new pleasure. And he wasn't satisfied with that either — desire will never find its limit. I wish we could coax someone out with a reward of our own: someone to bring us an argument that would make us believe this promise more firmly."

Section 21

"I wish that too," he said. "But I have one small thing still to ask. I agree that of the propositions you laid down, each follows from the other: just as, if the right is the only good, it follows that the happy life is achieved by virtue, so, if the happy life lies in virtue, nothing is good except virtue. But your Brutus, on the authority of Aristus and Antiochus, doesn't see it that way. He thinks that even if there were some good besides virtue, virtue would still be sufficient for a happy life."

"What then? Do you imagine I'm going to argue against Brutus?"

"As you see fit. It's not my place to set the rules for you."

Section 22

"What is consistent for each man," I said, "we can settle elsewhere." And I went on.

That very disagreement I had often had with Antiochus, and recently with Aristus, when I stayed at his house in Athens while holding a general's command. To me it seemed that no one could be happy while surrounded by evils; and the wise man could be surrounded by them, if there are any evils of body or of fortune at all. Their side argued as follows — Antiochus has set it down in his books in more than one place: virtue by itself can make a life happy, but not the happiest life possible. Further: most things take their name from their greater part even when some part is missing — strength, health, riches, honor, glory, all judged by their kind, not by their count. So too the happy life, even if it limps in some respect, still earns its name from the far greater part of itself.

Section 23

There is no great need to pick all this apart now, though it does not seem to me argued with much consistency. For one thing, I do not understand what a happy man could still lack that would make him happier — if anything is lacking, he isn't even happy. For another, as to the claim that each thing is named and judged by its greater part, there are cases where that holds. But these same men grant that there are three classes of evils. Picture a man crushed by every evil of two whole classes — everything in his fortune against him, his body worn down and broken with every pain. Shall we say that such a man falls just a little short of the happy life — never mind the happiest?

Section 24

This is the burden Theophrastus could not carry. Having once laid it down that floggings, torments, tortures, the ruin of one's country, exile, and the loss of children have great power to make life bad and miserable, he did not dare to talk in a lofty and grand style, since what he actually thought was low and abject. How right he was is not the question; consistent he certainly was. And so I do not usually approve of attacking conclusions once the premises have been granted. Yet this most elegant and most learned of all the philosophers is not much criticized for saying there are three classes of goods; but everyone savages him for the book he wrote on the happy life, in which he argues at length why a man who is being tortured and racked cannot be happy. He is even thought to say there that the happy life does not climb onto the wheel — a Greek instrument of torture. He never says it anywhere in so many words, but what he does say comes to the same thing.

Section 25

Can I, then, once I have granted a man that bodily pains are evils and that the shipwrecks of fortune are evils, be angry with him for saying that not all good men are happy — when the things he counts as evils can befall any good man? The same Theophrastus is savaged in the books and lecture halls of all the philosophers for having praised, in his Callisthenes, the line:

  Fortune, not wisdom, rules the life of men.

No philosopher, they say, ever said anything so spineless. Right enough — but I cannot see how anything could have been said more consistently. For if there are so many goods in the body, and so many outside it, hanging on chance and fortune, isn't it consistent to conclude that fortune — mistress of everything external and everything bodily — counts for more than deliberation does?

Section 26

Or would we rather imitate Epicurus? He often says many splendid things; how consistently they hang together is no concern of his. He praises plain living. Spoken like a philosopher — but that line belongs to Socrates or Antisthenes, not to a man who says pleasure is the final measure of all goods. He denies that anyone can live pleasantly who does not also live honorably, wisely, and justly. Nothing could be weightier, nothing more worthy of philosophy — had he not measured that very "honorably, wisely, justly" by pleasure. What is better than "fortune scarcely touches the wise man"? But is that said by the man who called pain not only the greatest evil but the only one — a man whose whole body might be crushed by the sharpest pains at the very moment he is boasting loudest against fortune?

Section 27

Metrodorus said the same thing in even finer words: "I have got there ahead of you, Fortune, and caught you. I have blocked every one of your approaches, so that you cannot come near me." Splendid — if Aristo of Chios had said it, or Zeno the Stoic, who counted nothing an evil except what is shameful. But you, Metrodorus, who have buried all good in your guts and your marrow, who define the highest good as a sound condition of the body and the well-grounded hope that it will last — you have blocked fortune's approaches? How? That good of yours can be stripped from you in an instant.

Section 28

Yet these are the pronouncements that captivate the untrained; it is because of sayings like these that such men draw their crowds. But the sharp reasoner looks not at what each man says, but at what each man, by his own principles, is bound to say. Take the very position we have adopted in this discussion: we hold that all good men are always happy. Whom I mean by "good men" is plain — those equipped and adorned with every virtue we call sometimes wise men, sometimes good men. Let us see, then, who ought to be called happy.
