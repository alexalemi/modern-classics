Book I: On the Contempt of Death

(Part 3 of 4)

Section 65

Altogether, a power that accomplishes so many things, and such great ones, seems to me divine. What is memory of facts and words? What, for that matter, is invention? Surely something greater than which nothing can be conceived even in a god. For I do not imagine the gods spend their happiness on ambrosia and nectar, with Juventas, the goddess of youth, pouring their cups; and I don't listen to Homer when he says Ganymede was carried off by the gods for his beauty, to serve Jupiter his wine — hardly a just cause for doing Laomedon so great a wrong. Homer was making all that up, transferring what is human onto the gods. I would rather he had transferred what is divine onto us. And what is divine? To be full of life, to know, to discover, to remember. So the soul, as I keep saying, is divine — or, as Euripides dares to say, a god. And indeed, if god is either breath or fire, then the soul of man is the same. For just as that celestial nature is free of earth and of moisture, so the human soul has no share of either; and if instead there is some fifth nature, first introduced by Aristotle, then it belongs alike to gods and to souls. Following this view, I put it in these very words in my Consolation:

Section 66

"Nowhere on earth can the origin of souls be found. For there is nothing in souls that is mixed or compounded, nothing that seems born or fashioned out of earth — nothing even moist, or airy, or fiery. In natures of that kind there is nothing that carries the power of memory, of mind, of thought — nothing that holds on to the past, foresees the future, and can take in the present. Those powers alone are divine, and no source will ever be found from which they could come to a human being except god. The soul's nature and power, then, is something singular, set apart from these familiar, well-known natures. So whatever it is that feels, that knows, that lives, that thrives, is celestial and divine — and for that very reason it must be eternal. Indeed god himself, as we understand him, can be understood in no other way than as a mind unbound and free, separated from all mortal compounding, aware of everything and moving everything, itself endowed with everlasting motion. Of this kind, and of this same nature, is the human mind."

Section 67

Where, then, is this mind of yours, and what is it like? Well — where is yours, and what is it like? Can you say? If I don't possess everything I might wish for understanding, will you forbid me even to use what I do possess? The soul does not have strength enough to see itself; but like the eye, the soul, without seeing itself, discerns other things. It does not see — the least important point — its own shape (though perhaps it does even that; but let it pass). What it certainly sees is its force, its shrewdness, its memory, its motion, its speed. These are great; these are divine; these are everlasting. What face it has, or where it lives, we need not even ask.

Section 68

Think what we see when we look up: first the beauty and brilliance of the sky; then the speed of its turning, greater than we can even conceive; then the alternation of days and nights, and the fourfold change of the seasons, fitted to ripen the crops and temper our bodies; and the sun, ruler and leader of all of it; and the moon, whose waxing and waning light marks and signals the days like a calendar; and, on that same sphere divided into twelve parts, the five planets holding their courses with perfect constancy though their motions differ among themselves; and the night sky adorned on every side with stars; and the globe of the earth standing up out of the sea, fixed at the center of the whole universe, inhabited and cultivated in two widely separated zones — one of them ours,

  set beneath the pole, toward the seven stars, from which
  the north wind's shuddering shriek drives on the freezing snows,

the other the southern one, unknown to us, which the Greeks call the antichthon, the opposite earth —

Section 69

while the remaining regions lie uncultivated, either stiff with cold or scorched with heat. But here where we live, in its season, without fail,

  the sky grows bright, the trees put on their leaves,
  the gladdening vines grow young with swelling shoots,
  the branches bow beneath their wealth of fruit,
  the grainfields pour out harvest, all things flower,
  the springs well up, the meadows dress in grass —

and then the multitude of animals, some for food, some to work the fields, some for carrying, some to clothe our bodies; and man himself, made as if on purpose to contemplate the sky and worship the gods; and all the fields and seas obedient to man's use —

Section 70

when we take in all this and countless things besides, can we doubt that someone presides over it — a maker, if these things were born, as Plato thinks, or, if they have existed forever, as Aristotle prefers, a governor of so vast a work and so vast a charge? It is the same with the mind of man. You cannot see it, just as you cannot see god; but as you recognize god from his works, so from memory, from invention, from quickness of motion, from all the beauty of virtue, recognize the divine power of the mind. Where is it located, then? In the head, I believe, and I can offer my reasons for believing so. But the soul's address can wait for another time. It is certainly in you. What is its nature? Its own, I think — peculiar to itself. But suppose it is fire; suppose it is breath: nothing to the question before us. Only see this: just as you know god while knowing nothing of his place or his appearance, so your own soul ought to be known to you, even if you know neither its place nor its form.

Section 71

And about the soul we cannot doubt — unless we are absolute lead-heads in natural science — that there is nothing in souls mixed in, nothing compounded, nothing coupled, nothing welded together, nothing double. And if that is so, it certainly cannot be split, or divided, or torn apart, or pulled asunder — and therefore it cannot perish. For perishing is a kind of departure and separation and divorce of parts that, before the perishing, were held together in some sort of union. It was because reasons like these persuaded him that Socrates sought no advocate at his trial for his life and never once groveled before his judges. He carried a free defiance, drawn from greatness of spirit, not from arrogance. On the last day of his life he argued this very question at length; a few days earlier, when he could easily have been slipped out of prison, he refused; and at the end, with that death-bearing cup all but in his hand, he spoke like a man who was not being pushed toward death but climbing up into heaven.

Section 72

For that is what he believed, and that is how he argued it: there are two roads, two different courses for souls as they leave the body. Those who had fouled themselves with the vices common to mankind, who had given themselves over entirely to their lusts and, blinded by them, had stained themselves with private vice and disgrace, or, by violence against their country, had taken on crimes past all atoning — for these there is a byway, walled off from the company of the gods. But those who had kept themselves upright and pure, who had taken the least possible contagion from the body and had always drawn themselves away from it, and who, while in human bodies, had imitated the life of the gods — for these the road back to the beings they came from lies open and easy.

Section 73

And so he recalls the swans, sacred to Apollo — not without reason, but because they seem to hold from him the gift of prophecy: foreseeing what good there is in death, they die with song and with pleasure. All good and learned men, he says, should do the same. And no one could doubt any of this — except that the same thing happens to us, when we think hard about the soul, that often happens to people who stare straight at the setting sun: they lose their sight altogether. So the mind's eye, gazing at itself, sometimes goes dim, and we lose our grip on the contemplation. And so our argument sails on — doubting, peering about, hesitating, wary of many dangers — like a raft on a boundless sea.

Section 74

But all this is old, and Greek. Cato, though, left life in such a way that he rejoiced at having found a cause for dying. For the god who rules within us forbids us to depart from here without his order; but when god himself gives a just cause — as once to Socrates, as lately to Cato, as often to many — then, I swear it, the wise man will go gladly out of this darkness into that light. And yet he will not break the chains of his prison — the laws forbid it — but will go out as one summoned and released by god, as if by a magistrate or some other lawful power. For as Socrates himself says, the whole life of a philosopher is a preparation for death.

Section 75

For what else are we doing when we call the soul away from pleasure — which is to say, from the body; when we call it away from private property, which is the body's servant and handmaid; away from public life; away from business of every kind — what, I ask, are we doing then, except calling the soul into its own company, pressing it to be with itself, and leading it as far away from the body as we can? And to separate the soul from the body — is that anything else but learning how to die? So let us rehearse this, believe me; let us uncouple ourselves from our bodies — that is, let us grow accustomed to dying. While we remain on earth, this will be something like that heavenly life; and when we are carried there, released from these chains, the soul's journey will be less slowed. For souls that have lived forever in the body's shackles move haltingly even once they are freed, like men who have worn irons for many years. When we have arrived there — then, at last, we will live. For this life is really death, and I could raise a lament over it, if I chose.

Section 76

"You have lamented it quite enough in your Consolation," he said. "When I read that book, I want nothing so much as to leave all this behind — and after what I have just heard, far more."

"The time will come," I said, "and quickly, whether you hang back or hurry — for time flies. But death is so far from being the evil it lately seemed to you that I begin to suspect that for a human being nothing else is so certainly not an evil — indeed, that nothing else is so surely a good — if it is true that we are to become gods ourselves, or companions of gods. Still, what does that matter? There are people on hand who won't accept any of it. But I will never release you from this conversation while any argument is left by which death could seem to you an evil."

"How could it, after what I have learned?"

Section 77

"You ask how it could? Whole squadrons of objectors are marching against us — and not only Epicureans, whom I for my part do not despise, though somehow every serious scholar scorns them. My own darling Dicaearchus has argued most fiercely of all against this immortality. He wrote three books, called the Lesbiacs because the conversation is set at Mytilene, in which he sets out to prove that souls are mortal. The Stoics, for their part, grant us a lease on existence, the way you might to crows: souls last a long while, they say — forever, they deny. Wouldn't you like to hear, then, why even if that were true, death still would not be among the evils?"

"As you please — but no one is going to drive me off immortality."

Section 78

"I applaud that — though we shouldn't be too confident about anything. Some sharp little piece of logic often shakes us; we waver and change our minds even on clearer matters than this one, and in this there is real obscurity. So if that should happen, let us be armed for it."

"By all means — but I will see to it that it doesn't happen."

"Then is there any reason we shouldn't dismiss our friends the Stoics? I mean the ones who say souls persist after they leave the body, but not forever."

"Dismiss them indeed — men who take on the hardest point in the whole case, that a soul can exist with no body at all, and then refuse the point that is not only easy to believe but actually follows from what they've granted: that if it can last a long age, it does not perish at all."

Section 79

"A fair rebuke," I said, "and that is exactly how it stands." And I went on. Should we then believe Panaetius where he parts company with his own Plato? Everywhere he calls Plato divine, the wisest, the holiest of men, the Homer of philosophers — and yet this one opinion of his, the immortality of souls, he will not accept. He holds — what no one denies — that whatever is born perishes; and that souls are born, he says, is proved by the resemblance of children to their parents, a likeness that shows in their temperaments and not only in their bodies. He offers a second argument too: nothing feels pain that cannot also fall sick; whatever can fall into disease will also perish; souls feel pain; therefore souls perish.

Section 80

These arguments can be answered. They are the arguments of a man who fails to see that when we speak of the eternity of souls, we are speaking of the mind, which is forever free of any turbulent motion — not of those parts where grief, anger, and lust do their work, parts which the very man he is arguing against holds to be fenced off and separated from the mind. As for resemblance, it shows more clearly in beasts, whose souls have no share of reason; in human beings the likeness lies rather in the build of the body — and it matters a great deal what sort of body a soul is housed in. Many things arise from the body that sharpen the mind, and many that blunt it. Aristotle, indeed, says that all men of genius are melancholics — which lets me feel no great regret at being on the slow side. He lists many cases, and, as though it were settled fact, offers a theory of why it happens. But if things generated in the body have that much power over the cast of the mind — and those are the things, whatever they are, that produce the resemblance — then resemblance proves nothing about souls being born. I say nothing of the cases of unlikeness.

Section 81

I wish Panaetius could be here — he lived in the household of Africanus. I would ask him: which of his family did the grand-nephew of Africanus take after? In face, perhaps his father; in his way of life he was so like every degenerate in Rome that he was easily the worst of them all. And whom did the grandson of Publius Crassus — that wise, eloquent, preeminent man — take after? And the grandsons and sons of so many other famous men, whom there is no point in naming? But what are we doing? Have we forgotten that our plan was, once we had said enough about eternity, to show that even if souls do perish, there is still nothing evil in death?

"I remembered it perfectly well," he said, "but while you were talking about eternity, I was glad to let you wander from the plan."

Section 82

"I see you are looking high," I said, "and want to move to heaven. I have hopes that will be our lot. But suppose, as those objectors insist, that souls do not survive after death."

"If that is so, I see we are robbed of the hope of a happier life."

"But what evil does that opinion actually bring? Suppose the soul perishes just like the body: is there any pain, or any sensation at all, in the body after death? No one claims that — though Epicurus accuses Democritus of it, the Democriteans deny it. Then no sensation remains in the soul either, for the soul is nowhere. Where, then, is the evil, since there is no third thing? Is it that the soul's parting from the body doesn't happen without pain? Even if I believed that, how small a thing it is! But I think it is false: the parting mostly happens without any sensation, sometimes even with pleasure; and the whole affair, whatever it amounts to, is slight — for it takes an instant."

Section 83

"What torments us — no, tortures us — is the departure from all the good things of life." So runs the objection. But look whether it wouldn't be truer to say "from the evils." Why should I now spend time mourning over human life? I could do it truly and justly. But when my whole point is that no one is miserable after death, why make life more miserable still by lamenting it? I did that in the book where I consoled myself as best I could. Death, then, if it is truth we are after, draws us away from evils, not from goods. In fact Hegesias the Cyrenaic argued this so fluently that King Ptolemy, the story goes, barred him from lecturing on it in the schools, because many who heard him went and took their own lives.

Section 84

There is an epigram of Callimachus on Theombrotus of Ambracia, who, he says, though nothing bad had happened to him, threw himself from the city wall into the sea after reading a book of Plato's. And that Hegesias I mentioned wrote a book called the Apokarteron — the man who starves himself — in which a man in the act of leaving life through hunger is called back by his friends, and answers them by cataloguing, one by one, the discomforts of human life. I could do the same — though I would not go as far as he does, since he thinks living profits no one at all. Others aside: does it still profit me? Stripped of the consolations of family and the honors of public life alike — surely, had I died sooner, death would have carried me away from evils, not from goods.

Section 85

So then, take someone who has no evil in his life, who has never taken a wound from fortune. The great Metellus, with his four sons risen to high office; or Priam with his fifty, seventeen of them born of his lawful wife. Fortune held the same power over both men but used it only on one. Metellus was laid on his funeral pyre by a whole crowd of sons and daughters, grandsons and granddaughters; Priam, stripped of all that offspring, fell by an enemy's hand after fleeing to the altar. Had he died while his sons still lived and his kingdom stood whole,

  with all his barbarian wealth about him,
  under carved and paneled ceilings,

would he have been leaving goods behind, or evils? At the time, surely, it would have seemed goods. Yet certainly it would have gone better for him, and we would never have had that tearful song:

  All this I saw go up in flame,
  and Priam's life torn out by force,
  and Jove's own altar fouled with blood.

As if anything better could have happened to him, by that point, than such an end! But had he fallen earlier, he would have escaped the whole catastrophe; as it was, dying when he did, he at least lost all sense of his miseries.

Section 86

Our friend Pompeius had it a little better. When he lay gravely ill at Naples and then recovered, the Neapolitans put garlands on their heads; so, naturally, did the people of Puteoli; town after town sent public congratulations. A silly business, and rather Greek — but still a mark of good fortune. Now suppose he had died then: would he have departed from good things or from evils? Certainly from miseries. He would not have gone to war with his father-in-law; he would not have taken up arms unprepared; he would not have abandoned his house, or fled from Italy; he would not, with his army lost, have fallen defenseless upon the swords and hands of slaves; his children would not have been left to weep, nor his whole fortune passed into the hands of the victors. If he had met death at that moment, he would have died at the very peak of his fortunes; by the extension of his life, how many calamities, how great, how past believing, did he drain to the bottom! These things death lets us escape — for even if they never happen, they can happen; but men never imagine such things can happen to them. Every man hopes for the luck of Metellus — as if the fortunate outnumbered the unfortunate, or anything in human affairs were certain, or hoping were wiser than fearing.

Section 87

But let us grant even this: that death robs men of good things. Does it follow that the dead therefore lack the comforts of life, and that this is misery? That is certainly what they are forced to say. But can someone who does not exist lack anything? "Lack" is a sad word in itself, because underneath it lies this: he had, and now he has not; he misses it, looks for it, feels the want of it. Those, I suppose, are the troubles of a man who lacks: he lacks eyes — blindness is hateful; he lacks children — bereavement. That holds for the living. But among the dead, no one lacks the comforts of life — no one lacks life itself. I am speaking of the dead, who do not exist. We, who do exist — do we lack horns, or wings? Would anyone say so? Certainly not. Why? Because when you do not have something that neither custom nor nature has fitted you for, you do not lack it — even if you are perfectly aware that you don't have it.

Section 88

This point must be pressed again and again, once that first one stands firm which, if souls are mortal, no one can doubt: that the destruction in death is so total that not even the faintest suspicion of sensation remains. With that properly planted and fixed, we must then pin down what "lacking" means, so that no error is left hiding in the word. To lack means this: to be without what you would want to have. Wanting is built into lacking — except when the word is used in quite another sense, as when we say a man "lacks" a fever. For "lack" is also used in a different way, when you do not have something and are aware you do not have it, but bear its absence easily. In death, then, "lacking" has no place — for lacking would be something to grieve over. What people say is "he lacks a good," and that, they say, is an evil. But not even a living man lacks a good unless he feels the need of it. Still, of a living man you can at least understand the phrase "lacking a kingdom" — though it can't be said of you with any precision; it could have been said of Tarquinius, once he had been driven from his throne. Of a dead man it cannot even be understood. To lack belongs to someone who feels; there is no feeling in a dead man; therefore not even lacking is in a dead man.

Section 89

And yet what need is there to philosophize about this at all, when we can see the matter hardly needs philosophy? How many times have not only our generals but whole armies charged straight at certain death! If death were something to fear, Lucius Brutus would not have fallen in battle holding out of the country the tyrant he himself had expelled; the elder Decius would not have thrown himself on the enemy's weapons fighting the Latins, nor his son fighting the Etruscans, nor his grandson fighting Pyrrhus; Spain would not have seen the Scipios fall for their country in a single war, nor Cannae seen Paulus and Geminus, nor Venusia Marcellus, nor Litana Albinus, nor Lucania Gracchus. Is any one of them miserable today? They were not miserable even then, after the last breath — for no one can be miserable once sensation has been destroyed.

Section 90

"But that very thing is hateful — to be without sensation." Hateful, if it meant lacking. But since it is plain there can be nothing at all in one who does not himself exist, what can be hateful in one who neither lacks nor feels? We have said this too often, perhaps — but only because here lies the whole of the soul's cringing before the fear of death. For whoever sees clearly — and it is clearer than daylight — that when soul and body have been destroyed, the whole living creature blotted out, its ruin total, the animal that once was has become nothing, will see just as plainly that there is no difference between a hippocentaur, which never existed, and King Agamemnon — and that Marcus Camillus cares no more about this civil war of ours than I, while he was alive, cared about the capture of Rome. Why then should Camillus have grieved at the thought of these events some three hundred and fifty years after his time, and why should I grieve at the thought that some nation will seize our city ten thousand years from now? Because our love of country is so great that we measure it not by our own sensation but by her own survival.

Section 91

And so death — which hangs over us daily through countless accidents, and which, life being as short as it is, can never be far away — does not deter the wise man from providing for his country and his own people for all time to come. He counts posterity, which he will never sense, as his own concern. And so even a man who judges the soul mortal may build works for eternity — not out of hunger for glory, which he will never feel, but out of virtue, which glory follows of necessity, even when you are not seeking it. Nature, in truth, stands like this: just as our birth brought us the beginning of all things, so death brings the end; and as nothing touched us before birth, so nothing will touch us after death. Where can the evil be in that, since death concerns neither the living nor the dead?

Section 92

The dead do not exist; the living it will not reach. Those who make death out to be a light thing compare it to sleep — as if anyone would choose to live ninety years on the condition that at sixty he sleep through the rest! Not even his own family would want that for him, let alone the man himself. Endymion — if we care to listen to fables — fell asleep once upon a time on Latmus, a mountain in Caria, and I don't believe he has woken up yet. Do you suppose he cares when the Moon is in eclipse — the Moon who is said to have lulled him to sleep so that she might kiss him as he slept? What should he care about, when he cannot even feel? There you have it: sleep, the image of death, and you put it on every day. Can you still doubt that there is no sensation in death, when you see there is none in its very likeness?
