Book III: On Grief of Mind

(Part 1 of 3)

Section 1

What reason should I give, Brutus, for this: we are made of soul and body, and yet an art for the body's care and upkeep has been sought out — its usefulness so prized that its invention was credited to the immortal gods — while medicine for the soul was neither missed before it was discovered, nor much cultivated after it became known, nor welcomed and approved by nearly as many people; in fact, more people eye it with suspicion and dislike. Why? Is it because we use the soul to judge the body's heaviness and pain, but never use the body to feel the soul's disease? So it works out that the soul must sit in judgment on itself at exactly the moment when the judge is the thing that's sick.

Section 2

If nature had made us able to look straight into her and see her clearly, and to finish the course of life with her as our best guide, no one would have any reason to need method and instruction. As it is, she gave us only tiny sparks — and, corrupted early by bad habits and bad opinions, we snuff them out so completely that nature's light never shows anywhere. The seeds of the virtues are born into our characters, and if they were allowed to grow up, nature herself would carry us through to a happy life. Instead, the moment we are delivered into daylight and taken up into a family, we live and move in unbroken crookedness and in the deepest perversity of opinion, so that we seem to have sucked in error almost with our nurse's milk. Then, when we are returned to our parents and afterward handed over to teachers, we are steeped in errors of so many kinds that truth surrenders to emptiness, and nature herself gives way to settled opinion.

Section 3

Then come the poets. Because they put on such a grand show of learning and wisdom, they are listened to, read, memorized — and they sink deep into our minds and stay there. And when the people are added on top — the people, that supreme schoolmaster — and the whole crowd on every side agreeing to be vicious together, then we are dyed through with corrupt opinions and desert nature altogether. So it seems to us that the men who best understood what nature offers are the ones who decided there is nothing better for a human being, nothing more worth wanting, nothing finer, than office, command, and popular glory. Toward that glory every best man is drawn; and while he hunts for the true moral goodness that nature seeks above everything else, he finds himself busy with pure emptiness, chasing not any solid figure of virtue but a sketched outline of glory. For real glory is a solid thing, fully worked, nothing sketchy about it: it is the agreed praise of good men, the uncorrupted voice of those who judge outstanding virtue rightly; it answers back to virtue like an echo. And because it usually walks beside right actions, good men should not wave it away.

Section 4

But the other thing — the one that sets itself up as glory's imitator: rash, unthinking, usually a cheerleader for sins and vices — popular fame — spoils the very shape and beauty of moral goodness by dressing up in its costume. Blinded this way, men who wanted things that really were splendid, but had no idea where those things were to be found or what they looked like, have in some cases overturned their own countries from the foundations, and in others destroyed themselves. Even these men, aiming at the best, went wrong not so much by intention as by mistaking the course. But what about the ones swept along by hunger for money or by lust for pleasures — the ones whose souls get so churned up that they stand not far from madness, which is the condition of everyone who lacks wisdom? Should no treatment at all be applied to them? Is it because the soul's sicknesses do less harm than the body's? Or because bodies can be treated, while for souls there is no medicine at all?

Section 5

But the soul's diseases are both more destructive and more numerous than the body's. They are hateful in themselves, precisely because they touch the soul and keep it in torment; and a sick soul, as Ennius says, "is forever astray: it can neither endure nor hold out, and it never stops desiring." Leave the others aside — what diseases of the body could be graver than these two alone, grief and desire? And how can anyone argue that the soul cannot treat itself, when the soul is what invented medicine for the body in the first place? In bodily cures, constitution and nature themselves carry much of the load, and not everyone who submits to treatment promptly gets well. But souls that want to be healed, and that follow the precepts of the wise, are healed — without any question.

Section 6

There is, most certainly, a medicine for the soul: philosophy. And its help is not to be fetched from outside, the way it is for diseases of the body. We must labor with every resource and all our strength to become able to doctor ourselves. Now, about philosophy as a whole — how much it deserves to be sought and cultivated — I have said enough, I think, in my Hortensius; and since then I have hardly stopped either discussing or writing about the greatest questions. These books lay out the discussions I held with my friends at my villa in Tusculum. The two earlier ones dealt with death and with pain; the third day's discussion will make up this third volume.

Section 7

We came down into our Academy when the day was already leaning into afternoon, and I asked one of those present to give me a subject to argue. Then the business ran like this.

"The wise man seems to me to be subject to grief," he began.

"And to the rest of the soul's disorders too?" I asked. "Fears, lusts, fits of anger? These are, roughly, the things the Greeks call pathe. I could call them 'diseases,' and that would be a word-for-word translation, but it would not fit our way of speaking. Pitying, envying, exulting, rejoicing — the Greeks call every one of these a disease: a movement of a soul that will not obey reason. We call these same movements of an agitated soul disorders, and I think we are right to; 'diseases' would strain ordinary Latin — unless you see it differently."

Section 8

"No, I see it your way," he said.

"And you really think these things befall the wise man?"

"I certainly do."

"Then that much-advertised wisdom is not worth a great deal — if it stands only a step away from madness."

"What?" he said. "Does every stirring of the mind look like madness to you?"

"Not to me only. Our ancestors thought so too — I have often marveled at this — many centuries before Socrates; and from Socrates flows this whole philosophy of life and conduct."

"How so, exactly?"

"Because the very name for madness, insania, declares a sickness and disease of the mind: an un-soundness, that is — a sick and ailing soul is what they labeled mad.

Section 9

"Now the philosophers call all the disorders of the soul diseases, and they deny that any fool is ever free of them. But whoever is in disease is not sound; and the souls of all fools are in disease; therefore all fools are mad. For our ancestors held that soundness of soul rests on a certain tranquility and steadiness; a mind emptied of those they named unsound — insane — because in a disturbed soul, exactly as in a disturbed body, soundness could not exist.

Section 10

"And it was no less sharp of them to give the state of a soul cut off from the mind's light the names amentia and dementia — mindlessness, being out of one's mind. From this you can see that the men who fastened these names onto things had sensed the very truth that Socrates taught and that the Stoics, receiving it from him, have carefully kept: that no one who lacks wisdom is sound. A soul in the grip of some disease — and, as I said a moment ago, the philosophers call these disturbed movements diseases — is no more sound than a body in the grip of one. So wisdom turns out to be the soul's soundness, and folly a kind of un-soundness, which is insania — madness — and dementia besides. And all of this our Latin words mark far better than the Greek ones do —

Section 11

"Something you will find true in many other places as well — but that is for another time. Now for what is in front of us. The thing we are asking about — what it is and what kind of thing — the sheer force of the word declares. We must count as sound those whose minds are disturbed by no movement, as if by disease; and it follows that those in the opposite condition have to be called unsound. So nothing beats the everyday Latin idiom that says a man carried away unbridled by lust or by anger has 'gone out of his own power' — though anger is really just a part of lust; the definition runs: anger is the lust for revenge. Those said to have gone out of their own power are called that because they are no longer in the power of the mind — and nature gave the mind kingship over the whole soul. Where the Greeks got their word mania, I could not easily say; yet we carve up the thing itself better than they do. We keep this madness, insania — which, being twinned with folly, covers a broad field — separate from furor, frenzy. The Greeks want the same distinction, but their word lets them down: what we call furor they call melancholia — as if a mind were unhinged only by black bile, and not, just as often, by some heavier surge of anger, or fear, or pain; it is in that sense we say that Athamas, Alcmaeon, Ajax, and Orestes were in frenzy. A man in that condition the Twelve Tables forbid to remain master of his own property — and notice that the words run not 'if he be mad,' insanus, but 'if he be frenzied,' furiosus. For our ancestors judged that folly, though empty of steadiness — that is, of soundness — could still keep up the middling run of duties and the common, customary business of life; but frenzy they understood to be a blindness of the mind toward everything. And though frenzy looks like the greater affliction, it is of such a kind that the wise man can suffer frenzy — and cannot suffer madness. But that is a separate question; let us get back to our subject."

Section 12

"You said, I believe, that in your view grief can touch the wise man."

"I did, and I still think so."

Only human that he should, I told him — and with that I settled into the day's argument. We are not carved out of flint. There is in our souls, by nature, something tender and soft, which grief shakes the way a storm shakes a ship. And Crantor, one of the most celebrated men our Academy has produced, was not talking nonsense when he wrote: "I do not agree at all with those who make such loud praise of this so-called insensibility — a thing that neither can exist nor should. Let me not be ill; but if I am ill, let me keep my feeling, whether something is being cut out of my body or torn away from it. For this state of feeling nothing is bought at a heavy price: a soul turned savage, a body gone numb."

Section 13

But let us be careful that this is not simply the talk of men flattering our weakness and indulging our softness. Let us have the daring not merely to prune back the branches of our miseries, but to tear out every last fiber of their roots. Something may be left behind even so — that is how deep the roots of folly run — but nothing will be left except what is necessary. Hold on to this much, though: unless the soul is healed, and without philosophy that cannot happen, there will be no end of our miseries. So, since we have begun, let us hand ourselves over to philosophy for treatment. We shall be healed — if we are willing. And I will take the argument further: I am going to deal not with grief alone, though grief comes first, but with every disorder of the soul, as I have named it — every "disease," as the Greeks prefer to say. And first, if you like, we will proceed in the manner of the Stoics, who like to lace their arguments up short and tight; after that we will range more freely, in my usual fashion.

Section 14

Whoever is brave is confident. (I avoid the word confidens, because a bad habit of speech has turned it into a term of blame, though it is drawn from confidere, "to trust fully," which deserves praise.) And whoever is confident is certainly not gripped by dread; confidence and fear do not go together. But whoever grief touches, fear touches too — for the very things whose presence grieves us are the things we fear while they hang over us and come on. That is how grief and courage come to be at war. So it is likely that whoever is open to grief is open to fear as well, and to a broken and sinking spirit besides. And when those fall on a man, they bring him to servitude — to admitting, someday, that he has been beaten. Whoever accepts all that must accept timidity and cowardice with it. But these things do not befall a brave man; therefore neither does grief. And no one is wise who is not brave: therefore grief will not touch the wise man.

Section 15

Further: the brave man is necessarily a man of great spirit; the man of great spirit is unconquerable; and the unconquerable man looks down on human affairs and counts them beneath him. But no one can be grieved by the very things he looks down on; from which it follows that the brave man is never touched by grief. And all wise men are brave: therefore grief does not touch the wise man. Again, just as an eye that is disturbed is in no fit state to do its work, and just as the other parts, and the whole body itself, fail in their work and office when knocked out of their proper condition, so a disturbed soul is not fit to carry out its task. And the soul's task is to use reason well. Now the wise man's soul is always in the condition to use reason at its very best; therefore it is never disturbed. But grief is a disturbance of the soul: therefore the wise man will always be free of it.

Section 16

Here is another likely conclusion, about the man of self-control. The Greeks call him sophron, and the virtue itself sophrosyne — which I render sometimes "self-control," sometimes "moderation," now and then even "modesty." But I rather suspect the virtue could properly be called frugality, frugalitas. The Greek equivalent is narrower: they call frugal men chresimoi, which means merely "useful." Our word reaches wider: all abstinence, all harmlessness — a quality the Greeks have no everyday name for, though ablabeia would serve, since harmlessness is the disposition of a soul that injures no one — frugality embraces the remaining virtues too. If it were not so large a thing — if it were really penned into the narrow limits most people imagine — the surname of Lucius Piso, Frugi, "the Worthy," would never have been held in such honor.

Section 17

But since we never call a man frugi who deserts his post out of fear — that is cowardice — or who secretly keeps back a deposit out of greed — that is injustice — or who bungles an undertaking out of recklessness — that is folly — the word frugality wraps up three virtues inside itself: courage, justice, and practical wisdom. (Though this much is common to all the virtues; every one of them is bound and yoked to the others.) So let frugality itself stand as the remaining, fourth virtue. Its own special property seems to be this: to govern and quiet the soul's grasping impulses, to stand always against lust, and to keep a moderate steadiness in everything it does. The vice set opposite it is called nequitia — worthlessness.

Section 18

Frugalitas, I take it, comes from frux, the fruit of the earth — the best thing earth gives us. Nequitia comes — this may be a stretch, but let us try it; if there is nothing in it, we will be assumed to have been playing — from nequicquam, "to no purpose": everything in such a man being to no purpose, he also gets called nihili, "worth nothing." Well then: whoever is frugal — or, if you like it better, moderate and self-controlled — must be steady; whoever is steady is calm; whoever is calm is free of every disorder, and so free of grief. And those are the marks of the wise man: therefore grief will keep its distance from the wise man. So Dionysius of Heraclea argues rather neatly against the complaint Achilles makes in Homer — it runs, I think, like this:

  and my heart deep inside me swells with bitter anger
  whenever I remember myself stripped of honor and of all my glory.

Section 19

Is a hand in its proper condition, he asks, when it is swollen? Is any limb, once puffed and swollen, in anything but a faulty state? Then a soul that is inflated and swollen is in a faulty state too. But the wise man's soul is always free of fault; it never swells, never puffs up — and the angry man's soul is exactly like that. So the wise man never grows angry. For if he is angry, he also lusts: it is the angry man's defining property to crave to burn the deepest possible pain into whoever seems to have injured him. And whoever craves that must, if he pulls it off, feel great joy — which means he rejoices in another man's misfortune. Since the wise man is not capable of that, he is not capable of anger either. But if grief could touch the wise man, anger could too; and since he is free of anger, he will be free of grief as well.

Section 20

Besides, if the wise man could fall into grief, he could fall into pity, and into enviousness too. I say enviousness, invidentia, not invidia — envy in that strict sense exists only at the moment someone is envied; but from the verb invidere we can properly form invidentia, and so slip past the ambiguous word. The word itself comes from looking too hard — in-videre — into another man's fortune, as in the Melanippus:

  Who has cast an envious eye upon the flower of my children?

Bad Latin, you may say — but Accius put it splendidly: just as with videre, "to see," so "to envy the flower," with a direct object, is more correct than "at the flower." Common usage bars the rest of us; the poet stood on his own rights and said it the bolder way.

Section 21

So pitying and envying fall to one and the same man: whoever aches at somebody's bad fortune also aches at somebody's good fortune. Think of Theophrastus, who, while mourning the death of his comrade Callisthenes, is tormented by the successes of Alexander — which is why he says that Callisthenes fell into the hands of a man of supreme power and supreme fortune who had no idea how good fortune ought to be used. And just as pity is grief over another's adversity, so enviousness is grief over another's prosperity. Whoever is open to pitying, then, is open to envying; but envying does not touch the wise man; so neither does pitying. If the wise man were in the habit of taking things hard, he would be in the habit of pitying too. Therefore grief stands far from the wise man.

Section 22

That is how the Stoics put the case, and their conclusions come out rather tightly twisted. At some point it will all have to be said more broadly and expansively; but we should still lean chiefly on the judgments of the men whose reasoning and verdicts are the bravest and, if I may put it this way, the most manly. For the Peripatetics, my good friends — nothing is richer than they are, nothing more learned, nothing more weighty — do not really win me over with their "moderate amounts" of the soul's disorders or diseases. Every evil, even a moderate one, is an evil; and our aim is that in the wise man there be none at all. A body is not healthy when it is even mildly sick, and in just the same way that "moderate amount" in the soul is an absence of health. So our own countrymen, with their usual fine touch — here as in so much else — took trouble, worry, and anguish and named them all aegritudo, "grief," after their likeness to sick bodies, aegri.

Section 23

The Greeks cover every disorder of the soul with nearly this one word: they call any turbulent motion in the soul pathos — that is, disease. We name it better. Grief truly does resemble a sick body; but lust does not resemble sickness, and neither does unmeasured joy, which is an elated, exultant pleasure of the soul. Even fear is not much like a disease, though it lives next door to grief. But strictly, just as sickness in the body — aegrotatio — so grief in the soul — aegritudo — bears a name that is not divorced from pain. So we must explain where this pain comes from: the cause that produces grief in the soul the way sickness is produced in the body. For just as physicians reckon the cure found once the cause of a disease is found, so we, once we find the cause of grief, will have found the means of treating it.

Section 24

The cause, then, lies wholly in opinion — and not only the cause of grief, but of all the other disorders as well. They are four in kind, with many subdivisions. Every disorder is a movement of the soul that is either empty of reason, or contemptuous of reason, or disobedient to reason; and since that movement is set off in two ways — by an opinion of good or by an opinion of evil — the four disorders divide up evenly. Two come from an opinion of good. One of these is exultant pleasure — joy lifted beyond all measure — springing from the opinion of some great good presently at hand. The other is desire, which can rightly be called lust as well: an unmeasured reaching after an imagined great good, in defiance of reason.

Section 25

So these two kinds, exultant pleasure and lust, are stirred up by an opinion of goods, just as the remaining two, fear and grief, are stirred up by an opinion of evils. For fear is the opinion of a great evil hanging over us, and grief is the opinion of a great evil already upon us — and, more exactly, a fresh opinion of an evil so great that anguish over it seems the right response: that is, the man in distress believes he ought to be in distress. These disorders — the Furies, so to speak, that folly looses on human life and whips forward — must be fought with all our strength and all our resources, if we want to carry the portion of life we have been given across in tranquility and peace. But the rest can wait for other days. For now, let us drive grief out if we can. Let that be our task, since you said grief seems to you to touch the wise man. I say it cannot, in any way; for grief is a hideous thing, wretched, detestable, to be fled with every straining effort — with sails and oars, as the saying goes.

Section 26

Look at this man — how does he strike you? —

  the seed of Tantalus, the son of Pelops, who once
  carried Hippodamea off from her father, King Oenomaus,
  and so became his son-in-law by a stolen wedding —

A great-grandson of Jupiter himself, no less. And is he really so cast down, so utterly broken?

  Come no closer to me, friends — stop there, stand back!
  lest my contagion or my very shadow fall on decent men.
  So monstrous a power of crime is lodged fast in my body.

Will you condemn yourself, Thyestes, and rob yourself of the light of day, because of the force of a crime that was another man's? And what about that son of the Sun — do you not find him unworthy of his own father's light?

  His eyes have shrunk back; his body has withered to a husk;
  tears have eaten channels down his bloodless cheeks;
  his beard, bristling with filth around his ruined face,
  hangs unshorn, darkening a chest scaled over with grime.

These evils, Aeetes, fool above fools, you added to yourself. They were not among the things chance had carted in on you — and your evil was an old one by then; the swelling of the soul had already gone down (for grief, as I shall show, lies in the opinion of an evil that is fresh). No: what you mourn, plainly, is your lost kingdom, not your lost daughter. Her you hated, and maybe with reason; it was the kingdom you could not calmly do without. And it is a shameless grief that eats a man alive because he is no longer permitted to give orders to the free.

Section 27

Dionysius the tyrant, driven out of Syracuse, taught schoolboys at Corinth — that is how impossible he found it to live without command. And what was ever more shameless than Tarquinius, who made war on the very men who had refused to endure his arrogance? When neither the arms of Veii nor those of the Latins could set him back on his throne, he took himself off, the story goes, to Cumae, and in that city old age and grief wore him down to death. Do you really judge, then, that this can happen to a wise man — to be crushed under grief, which is to say, under misery? Every disorder is misery, but grief is the torture chamber itself. Lust carries a fever in it, exultant joy a giddiness, fear an abjectness; but grief carries something worse: wasting, torment, affliction, ugliness. It claws the soul, gnaws it through, and destroys it utterly. Unless we strip grief off and throw it away from us, we cannot be free of misery.
