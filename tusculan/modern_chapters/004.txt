Book II: On Bearing Pain

(Part 1 of 2)

Section 1

In Ennius's play, Neoptolemus says he needs to do philosophy — "but only a little; giving myself to it entirely doesn't appeal to me at all." Well, Brutus, I think I need to do philosophy too — what better use can I make of myself, especially now that I have nothing else to do? — but not "only a little," like him. Philosophy is a field where it's hard to know a few things well unless you know most of it, or all of it. You can only choose a few subjects out of the whole spread; and anyone who has genuinely grasped a few will go after the rest with the same appetite.

Section 2

Still, in a busy life — a soldier's life, as Neoptolemus's was then — even those few things often do a great deal of good and bear real fruit: not as much as the whole of philosophy can yield, but enough to free us, sometimes and in part, from desire, from grief, from fear. Take the discussion I held recently at my villa in Tusculum: out of it seemed to come a genuine contempt for death, and that goes no small way toward freeing the mind from fear. A man who dreads what cannot be avoided has no chance of living with a quiet mind. But the man who does not fear death — not only because dying is inevitable, but because death holds nothing worth shuddering at — has laid in a great store of protection for a happy life.

Section 3

Not that I don't know how many people will be eager to argue the other side. There was no way to avoid that, short of writing nothing at all. My speeches were aimed at the judgment of the crowd — oratory is a popular art, and the proof of eloquence is the audience's applause — and even so, there were critics who praised nothing except what they were confident they could imitate themselves, who set the limit of good speaking exactly where their own hopes gave out, and who, when they were being buried under an abundance of ideas and words, announced that they preferred lean starvation to richness and plenty. That is where the so-called "Attic" style came from — a style unknown to the very men who claimed to be following it, men who have since gone quiet, all but laughed out of the forum itself —

Section 4

so if that is what happened to my speeches, what do I imagine will happen now, when the popular backing I once leaned on is no use to me at all? Philosophy is content with a few judges. She deliberately runs from the crowd, and the crowd in turn finds her suspect and hateful — so that anyone who wanted to run down philosophy as a whole could do it to popular applause, and anyone who attacks the particular school I follow can call in heavy reinforcements from all the other philosophers' camps. But I have already answered the detractors of philosophy in general in my Hortensius, and what needed saying on behalf of the Academy I think I laid out carefully enough in the four books of my Academics. And far from wishing that no one would write against me, I want nothing more. Philosophy would never have been held in such honor in Greece itself if it had not stayed vigorous through the contests and quarrels of its most learned men.

Section 5

So I urge everyone who is able: let us take this glory too away from a Greece that is fading, and carry it into this city, just as our ancestors, by their own effort and industry, carried over every other art worth wanting. The glory of oratory rose from humble beginnings to the very summit — and now, as nature ordains for nearly everything, it is growing old and looks likely to come to nothing before long. Let philosophy, then, be born in Latin letters in our own day; and let us help it grow, and let ourselves be contradicted and refuted without minding it. The people who cannot bear that are the ones bound over and consecrated, so to speak, to fixed and predetermined doctrines — chained so tightly by the demands of consistency that, for consistency's sake, they are forced to defend positions they don't even approve of. We who pursue the probable, and cannot go beyond whatever presents itself as likely, are ready to refute without stubbornness and to be refuted without anger.

Section 6

And if these studies are carried over to our own people, we won't even need the Greek libraries anymore — where the number of books is endless because the number of authors is: so many writers saying the same things that they have stuffed the world with volumes. The same will happen to us, if crowds come pouring into these studies. But let us rouse up, if we can, the men who have a liberal education and who philosophize with method and order — and with some elegance of argument besides.

Section 7

For there is a certain breed who like to be called philosophers, and whose Latin books are said to be numerous indeed. I don't look down on them — I've never read them — but since the authors themselves announce that they write with no distinctions, no organization, no elegance, no polish, I skip a kind of reading that offers no pleasure. What the men of that school say and think, nobody with even a modest education fails to know already. So, since they take no trouble over how they say it, I can't see why they should be read by anyone except each other — the people who already agree.

Section 8

Everyone reads Plato and the other Socratics, and the whole line descended from them — even people who don't accept their views or don't pursue them very eagerly — while hardly anyone besides his own disciples ever picks up Epicurus or Metrodorus. In just the same way, the only people who read these Latin books are the ones who think the doctrines in them are correct. My own view is that anything committed to writing ought to deserve the attention of every educated reader. Even if I can't achieve that myself, I don't think it any less the standard to aim for.

Section 9

That is why I have always liked the custom of the Peripatetics and the Academy: arguing both sides of every question. Not only because there is no other way to find what is probable in each case, but because it is the finest possible training in speaking. Aristotle used it first, and then those who followed him. Within my own memory, Philo — I heard him often — set aside one hour for teaching the rules of the rhetoricians and another for philosophy. My friends drew me into that custom at Tusculum, and we spent the time we had exactly that way. So after we had given the morning to speaking practice, as we had done the day before, in the afternoon we went down into the Academy — the walk I named for Plato's school. What was discussed there I am setting out not as a summary from memory, but almost word for word, just as it was argued.

Section 10

So, as we walked, the conversation got underway, opened by something like this. "I can't tell you how much yesterday's discussion delighted me," he said, "or rather, how much it helped me. I know perfectly well I have never clung too hard to life; but even so, a certain fear and pain used to push into my mind whenever I thought that someday this daylight would end and I would lose everything good that life holds. Believe me, I have been freed from that kind of trouble so completely that nothing now seems to me less worth worrying about."

Section 11

"No wonder at all," I said. "That is what philosophy does: it heals souls, drains off empty anxieties, frees us from cravings, drives away fears. But its power doesn't work the same on everyone; it counts for most when it takes hold of the right nature. 'Fortune favors the brave,' as the old proverb says — but reason favors them far more, using its precepts, you might say, to reinforce the very strength of courage. Nature clearly made you lofty and high-reaching, a man who looks down on merely human concerns, and so a speech against death found easy lodging in a brave spirit. But do you suppose those same arguments carry any weight — outside a very few exceptions — with the very men who invented them, debated them, and wrote them down? How many philosophers can you find whose character, whose settled mind and life, are what reason demands? who treat their doctrine not as a display of learning but as the law of their own life? who obey themselves and follow their own decrees?

Section 12

"You can see some of them so shallow and boastful that they would have been better off never studying at all; some greedy for money, a few for glory, many enslaved to their lusts — so that their talk is at war, spectacularly, with their life. And that seems to me the most shameful thing there is. If a man who calls himself a grammarian speaks like a barbarian, or a would-be musician sings off-key, the failure is all the uglier because it comes in the one field he claims to know. In the same way, a philosopher who fails in the conduct of life is the uglier failure because he is stumbling in the very duty he sets up to teach: he professes the art of living, and botches the living."

"If that's so," he said, "isn't there a danger that you're decking philosophy out in false glory? What stronger proof that it's useless than that some fully finished philosophers live disgracefully?"

Section 13

"That is no proof at all. Not every field that is cultivated bears a crop — and Accius's lines are simply false:

  Though sound seed fall on poor and meager ground,
  its own good nature still will make it shine.

"Not so — and in the same way, not every cultivated mind bears fruit. To stay inside the same comparison: as a field, however fertile, cannot be fruitful without cultivation, neither can a mind without teaching — philosophy is the cultivation of the mind. Each of the two is feeble without the other. Philosophy pulls vices out by the roots, prepares the mind to receive seed, and entrusts to it — sows in it, you might say — what will grow up to bear the richest fruit. So let's proceed the way we began. Tell me, if you would: what would you like discussed?"

Section 14

"Pain seems to me the greatest of all evils," he began.

"Even a greater evil than disgrace?"

"That I don't dare say — and I'm ashamed to be knocked off my position so quickly."

"You would have more to be ashamed of if you had stayed on it. What could be less worthy than for anything to look worse to you than disgrace, outrage, and plain baseness? To escape those, what pain is there that a man shouldn't — I won't just say accept — but go out to meet of his own accord, take on, and welcome?"

"I agree completely. So pain may not be the greatest evil — but it is certainly an evil."

"And do you see how much of pain's terror you have already dropped at one small prompting?"

Section 15

"I see it plainly. But I'd like to drop more."

"I'll try. But it is a large undertaking, and I need a mind on your side that isn't fighting me."

"You have it. As I did yesterday, I will follow reason wherever she leads."

So I began. First, a word about the weakness of many philosophers — and of many different schools. The first of them in authority and in age, Aristippus, Socrates' own pupil, did not hesitate to call pain the greatest evil. Then Epicurus showed himself docile enough to this soft and womanish doctrine. After him, Hieronymus of Rhodes said that the highest good was freedom from pain — that is how much evil he found in pain. The rest, except for Zeno, Aristo, and Pyrrho, said roughly what you said just now: an evil, yes, but with worse evils beyond it.

Section 16

So the thing that nature herself and a certain inborn nobility rejected on the spot — your calling pain the greatest evil, a claim you were driven from the moment disgrace was set against it — that is the position philosophy, the teacher of life, has clung to for all these centuries? What duty, what praise, what honor will be worth so much that a man who has convinced himself pain is the greatest evil will accept bodily pain to win it? And what humiliation, what baseness won't he swallow to escape pain, once he has decided pain is the worst thing there is? And who isn't miserable — not only while the worst pains are actually crushing him, if pain really is the greatest evil, but the moment he knows they could come for him? And who is exempt from that? The upshot is that no one at all can be happy.

Section 17

Metrodorus, it's true, thinks a man perfectly happy if his body is in good condition and he holds a guarantee that it will always stay that way. But who is this man with the guarantee? Epicurus, though, says things that seem to me designed to get a laugh. He declares somewhere that if the wise man is being burned, being tortured — you're waiting, perhaps, for him to say "he will take it, he will bear up, he will not give in"; high praise, by Hercules, and worthy of the very Hercules I just swore by! — but no, that isn't enough for Epicurus, that stern, hardened character. If his wise man is inside the bull of Phalaris — the tyrant's bronze roasting-machine — he will say: "How sweet this is! How little it bothers me!" Sweet, even? Isn't it enough if it's "not bitter"? Even the men who flatly deny that pain is an evil don't usually claim that torture is sweet for anyone. They call it harsh, hard, hateful, contrary to nature — and still not an evil. But this man, who says pain is the only evil and the very worst of all evils, thinks the wise man will call it sweet.

Section 18

I don't ask you to describe pain in the same words Epicurus uses for pleasure — a man, as you know, devoted to pleasure. By all means let him say the same thing in Phalaris's bull that he would say in his own bed. I don't grant wisdom that much power against pain. Let the wise man be brave in enduring it — that satisfies his duty. That he should positively enjoy it, I don't demand. Because pain is, beyond any question, a grim business: harsh, bitter, an enemy to nature, hard to submit to and hard to bear.

Section 19

Look at Philoctetes. Him we may forgive for groaning — he had seen Hercules himself on Mount Oeta, howling under the sheer size of his pains. The arrows Hercules had given him were no comfort at all to Philoctetes when

  from the viper's bite, the veins deep in his body,
  steeped through with venom, woke their hideous torments.

And so he screams, begging for help, longing to die:

  Oh, who will throw me to the salt waves
  from the towering crest of this rock?
  Now, now it devours me — the wound's raw force,
  the ulcer's fire, is eating out my life.

It seems hard to say that a man forced to scream like that is not in the grip of an evil — and a great one.

Section 20

But let us watch Hercules himself, broken by pain at the very moment he was reaching for immortality through death itself. What cries Sophocles gives him in the Trachiniae! When Deianira had dressed him in the tunic dyed with the Centaur's blood, and it had fused itself to his flesh, he says:

  O things heavy to tell, and harsh to suffer,
  which I have drained with my body, borne in my soul!
  Not Juno's terror, never to be appeased,
  not grim Eurystheus loaded me with evil
  to match one mad woman, the daughter of Oeneus.
  She snared me, blind, in this robe of the Furies
  that clings to my sides, and bites, and tears my flesh,
  crushes my lungs and drinks away my breath;
  already it has sucked my blood to nothing.
  My body, eaten by this horrible ruin, is wasted;
  bound fast, I am being murdered by a woven plague.
  No enemy's hand did this; no earth-born
  army of Giants, no two-formed Centaur's charge
  ever struck such a blow against my body;
  no Greek force, no barbarian savagery,
  no cruel tribe exiled at the ends of the earth —
  all the lands I crossed, driving out every wildness:
  I, a man, am killed by a woman's womanly hand.
  My son! Earn that name truly, toward your father;
  don't let a mother's claim outweigh a dying man's.
  Drag her here to me — your loyal hands can bring her.
  Now I will see which of us you hold dearer.

Section 21

  Go on, my son — dare it. Weep for your father's
  agony. Take pity. Nations will weep for our miseries.
  Shame! that I pour from my mouth a girl's wailing —
  I, whom no one ever saw groan at any evil.
  My manhood has been made a woman's; it is dying.
  Come near, son. Stand by me. Look at your father's
  pitiful body, gutted and torn apart.
  See it, all of you! And you, sower of the heavens,
  hurl down on me, I beg, the flash of your lightning!
  Now — now the churning whirlpools of pain are twisting me;
  now the fire crawls. O hands, once all-conquering,

Section 22

  O chest, O back, O knotted muscle of these arms!
  Was it your grip in which the Nemean lion
  once ground his teeth and gasped his final breath?
  Did this right hand slaughter the foul serpent
  and give Lerna peace? Did this hand break
  the two-bodied band of Centaurs? Did this fell
  the ravening beast of Erymanthus? Did this drag
  the three-headed dog, the Hydra's kin, up out
  of the black country of Tartarus? Did this destroy
  the dragon of a thousand coils, whose eyes
  stood guard upon the gold-bearing tree?
  Many another conquest this hand traveled through,
  and no man ever stripped a trophy from my glory.

Can we despise pain, when we watch Hercules himself suffering it with so little power to endure?

Section 23

Now let Aeschylus step forward — not only a poet but a Pythagorean too; that is the tradition we have received. How does his Prometheus bear the pain he takes on for the theft at Lemnos —

  From there came fire, dealt out in secret
  to mortal men; and clever Prometheus
  stole it, they say, by cunning, and paid
  the price to Jupiter by supreme doom.

Paying that price, nailed to the Caucasus, he says this:

  Race of the Titans, blood-kin of my blood,
  children of Heaven — look at me, lashed and bound
  to jagged rock, the way frightened sailors,
  dreading the night, moor their ship down tight
  in a howling strait. So Jupiter, Saturn's son,
  spiked me here, and Jupiter's will enlisted
  the hands of Mulciber, the fire-god. With cruel craft
  he drove these wedges through and split my limbs;
  and pierced by that workmanship, a wretch,
  I garrison this fortress of the Furies.

Section 24

  And now, each third and fatal day,
  Jupiter's winged henchman swoops down grimly
  and rips me open with her hooked talons,
  carving her savage meal. Stuffed fat and glutted
  on my rich liver, she pours out a vast scream
  and, climbing high on the wing, her feathered tail
  strokes at my blood. And when the gnawed liver
  has swelled back whole again, greedy she returns
  to her foul feeding. So I feed this warden
  of my grim torture, who defiles my living body
  with misery that has no end. For look:
  pinned fast in Jupiter's chains, I cannot drive
  the hideous bird away from my breast.

Section 25

  Robbed of my own defense, I take in these torments,
  each one an anguish, in love with death,
  hunting for any end to this evil —
  but Jupiter's will beats me far back from dying.
  This ancient, grief-bringing ruin, massed up
  through hideous centuries, is planted in my body,
  and out of it fall drops, melted in the sun's blaze,
  that drip forever on the rocks of Caucasus.

So it seems we can hardly call a man in that condition anything but miserable; and if he is miserable, then pain is an evil.

Section 26

Here he broke in. "So far, you're pleading my side of the case. But I'll get to that in a moment. Meanwhile — where are those verses from? I don't recognize them."

"I'll tell you, by Hercules — you're right to ask. You see, don't you, that I have time on my hands?"

"What of it?"

"When you were at Athens, I imagine you often sat in at the philosophers' schools."

"Yes — and gladly."

"Then you noticed that even though none of them was exactly a torrent of words, they still mixed verses into their talks."

"Yes — Dionysius the Stoic used a great many."

"Right. But he served them up like dictation — no selection, no elegance. Philo gave true measured lines, well chosen, and set exactly in place. So ever since I fell in love with this old man's declamation of mine, I've been eager to use our own poets — and wherever they have run short, I've translated a good deal from the Greek, so that Latin speech won't lack any ornament in this kind of discussion."

Section 27

Then I pressed on. But do you see how much harm the poets do? They bring on the bravest men weeping over their misfortunes; they soften our minds; and on top of it they are so sweet that we don't just read them — we learn them by heart. So when the poets are added to bad discipline at home and a life lived in the shade among comforts, they cut virtue's sinews clean through. Plato was right to expel them from the city he imagined, when he was searching out the best character and the best condition for a state. But we, schooled by Greece of course, read them and memorize them from childhood, and call that a liberal education.

Section 28

But why be angry with the poets? Philosophers — the professed teachers of virtue — have turned up who said pain is the greatest evil. But you, young man, when you said a moment ago that it seemed so to you, abandoned the opinion at a single word, the moment I asked whether it was greater than disgrace. Put the same question to Epicurus: he will answer that even a moderate pain is a greater evil than the deepest disgrace — since in disgrace itself there is nothing bad, unless pains follow it. Then what pain is dogging Epicurus when he says exactly this, that pain is the greatest evil? A deeper disgrace than that I cannot expect from a philosopher. So you gave me enough when you answered that disgrace seemed to you a greater evil than pain. If you hold on to just that, you will see how far pain deserves to be resisted — and that the real question is not so much whether pain is an evil as how to make the mind strong for bearing it.

Section 29

The Stoics wrap it all up in petty little syllogisms proving pain is not an evil — as if the trouble were about a word and not a thing. Why are you tricking me, Zeno? When you flatly deny that what looks horrifying to me is an evil, I'm caught — I want to know how the thing I count most miserable of all fails even to be an evil. "Nothing is an evil," he says, "except what is base and vicious." You're back to trifling. You haven't removed what was strangling me. I know pain isn't wickedness — stop teaching me that. Teach me this: that being in pain or out of it makes no difference. "It never bears at all," he says, "on living happily — that rests on virtue alone. But pain is still to be rejected." Why? "It is harsh, contrary to nature, hard to endure, grim, hard."

Section 30

There's a wealth of words — being able to say in so many different ways what we call by the one word "evil." You are defining pain, Zeno, not removing it, when you call it harsh, contrary to nature, something scarcely to be endured or borne. And you're not lying. But a man who swaggers in words shouldn't collapse in fact. "Nothing is good but what is honorable; nothing evil but what is base" — that is wishing, not teaching. Better and truer to say this: everything nature recoils from is among the evils, and everything she welcomes is among the goods. Grant that, and clear the quarrel over words out of the way, and still the thing those men rightly hold fast — what we call moral goodness, the right, the fitting, and sometimes wrap up under the single name of virtue — will tower so far above everything else that all the so-called goods of body and fortune look tiny and trivial beside it, and no evil, not even every evil piled together in one heap, comes near comparison with the evil of disgrace.

Section 31

So if, as you conceded at the start, disgrace is worse than pain, then pain is plainly nothing. As long as groaning, shrieking, wailing, being broken and unstrung by pain looks base to you and unworthy of a man — as long as moral goodness, dignity, and honor stand beside you, and you keep your eyes fixed on them and hold yourself in — pain will yield to virtue, count on it, and go slack under the mind's sheer resolve. Either there is no such thing as virtue, or every pain is to be despised. Will you grant practical wisdom, without which no virtue can even be conceived? Then will practical wisdom let you strain at what gains nothing and labor in vain? Will self-control let you do anything without measure? Can justice be honored by a man who, under the force of pain, spills secrets, betrays his accomplices, and abandons one duty after another?

Section 32

And courage, with its companions — greatness of spirit, gravity, patience, the settled contempt for human fortunes — what answer will you give them? Will you lie there flattened, moaning in a pitiful voice, and let someone say, "There's a brave man"? In that state no one would call you a man at all. So courage must be thrown overboard — or pain must be buried. You know how it is with your Corinthian bronzes: lose one piece, and the rest of the collection can still be safe without it. But the virtues are otherwise: lose one — though in truth virtue cannot be lost — but suppose you confess that you lack one, and you will be left with none at all.

Section 33

Well then: can you call him a brave man, a great-spirited man, patient, grave, above human fortune — Philoctetes, say? I'd rather not take you as the example. But he, at least, is no brave man, lying under his dripping roof,

  which, mute itself, resounds to his wails, his moans,
  his groans and roars, and gives him back his weeping.

I do not deny that pain is pain — otherwise why would courage ever be needed? — I say that patience overpowers it, if there is any patience. If there is none, then why do we dress philosophy up so finely, and why do we strut under its name? Pain stabs — fine, let it gouge. If you are naked, offer it your throat. But if you are covered by "the armor of Vulcan" — that is, by courage — then resist. If you don't, that guardian of your dignity will walk off and abandon you.

Section 34

The laws of Crete — laid down by Jupiter, or by Minos at Jupiter's direction, as the poets tell it — and the laws of Lycurgus as well, train the young through hard labor: hunting, running, going hungry, going thirsty, freezing, sweltering. And at Sparta, boys are received at the altar with such whipping "that the blood streams thick out of their flesh" — sometimes, as I used to hear when I was there myself, whipped even to death. Not one of them ever cried out; not one so much as groaned. What then? Boys can do this, and grown men will not be able to? Custom has this much power, and reason will have none?

Section 35

There is a difference between labor and pain. They sit right next to each other, but they differ. Labor is the performance, by mind or body, of some heavier piece of work or duty; pain is a harsh motion in the body, alien to the senses. The Greeks — whose language is richer than ours — call both of these by a single name, ponos. So they call industrious men "devoted to pain," or really "lovers of pain"; we say, more aptly, hardworking. Because laboring is one thing, and hurting is another. O Greece — sometimes so poor in the very words you think you're always rich in! Laboring, I say, is one thing, and hurting is another. When Gaius Marius had his varicose veins cut out, he hurt. When he marched at the head of his column in savage heat, he labored. And yet there is a family resemblance between the two: being accustomed to labor makes the endurance of pain easier.
