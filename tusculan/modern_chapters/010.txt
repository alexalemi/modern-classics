Book IV: On the Other Disorders of the Mind

(Part 2 of 3)

Section 30

For vices are conditions that stay put, while the disorders are conditions in motion — so they cannot be parts of something that stands still. And just as the body's likeness to the soul holds for what is bad in each, so it holds for what is good. The body has its prize qualities: beauty, strength, health, firmness, speed. The soul has its own. When the elements we are made of are in balance with one another, the body is called healthy; and the soul, likewise, is called healthy when its judgments and opinions agree with one another — and that agreement is the virtue of the soul. Some say this virtue simply is self-control; others say it is a quality that obeys the precepts of self-control and follows in its train, with no distinct form of its own. Either way, only the wise man has it. There is also, though, a certain everyday health of soul that even a fool can enjoy — when a doctor's treatment clears the disturbance out of his mind.

Section 31

And as a certain graceful proportion of the limbs, joined with a certain sweetness of coloring, is called beauty in the body, so in the soul an evenness and steadiness of opinions and judgments, joined with a certain firmness and stability — either following virtue or containing the very force of virtue in itself — is called beauty too. Likewise the soul's powers are named, in matching words, after the body's strength, its sinews, its effectiveness. Speed of body is called swiftness, and the same praise goes to the intellect, for the way a mind can run through many things in a short time. Souls and bodies do differ in one respect: a strong soul cannot be attacked by disease at all, while a strong body can. And bodily injuries can happen without any fault of ours; the soul's cannot — every one of its diseases and disorders comes from treating reason with contempt. That is why they exist only in human beings. Animals do things that look similar, but they do not fall into disorders.

Section 32

Between sharp minds and dull ones there is this difference: the gifted, like Corinthian bronze, which is slow to take tarnish, fall into disease more slowly and recover more quickly; not so the dull. Nor does a gifted mind fall into every disease and disorder — never into any that are savage and monstrous. Some disorders even wear, at first sight, a look of humanity: pity, grief, fear. But the sicknesses and diseases of souls are thought harder to tear out than those supreme vices which stand opposite the virtues. Vices can be removed while the diseases remain, because the diseases are not healed as quickly as the vices are removed.

Section 33

There you have what the Stoics work out, kernel by kernel, about the disorders — the part they call "logic," because the arguing is so fine-grained. And now that my talk has sailed clear of those jagged reefs, let me hold course through the rest of the discussion — provided I have been clear enough, given how dark the subject is.

"Perfectly clear," he said. "If anything needs closer study, we can go looking another time. Right now we're waiting for those sails you spoke of, and for open water."

Section 34

Well then. As I have said of virtue elsewhere, and will often have to say again — for most of the questions that touch life and conduct are drawn from virtue's spring — since virtue, I say, is a steady and harmonious disposition of the soul, one that makes the people who have it praiseworthy, and is itself praiseworthy on its own account, apart from any usefulness, out of it flow honorable intentions, opinions, and actions, and all right reason — indeed, the shortest possible definition of virtue is right reason itself. The opposite of this virtue is viciousness. I prefer that word to "malice" for what the Greeks call kakia, because malice is the name of one particular vice, while viciousness covers them all. And out of viciousness the disorders are stirred up — those turbid, agitated motions of the soul I described a little while ago, turned away from reason, the sworn enemies of the mind and of a tranquil life. They bring in anxious, bitter griefs; they crush the soul and cripple it with fear; and they also set it on fire with excessive craving — the thing we call sometimes desire, sometimes lust — a helplessness of the soul as far from self-control and moderation as anything can be.

Section 35

If this craving ever does get what it wanted, it is so carried away with excitement that "nothing it does holds steady," like the man who declared that "excessive pleasure of the soul is the greatest of all errors." The cure for these evils lies in virtue, and in virtue alone. And what is not only more miserable, but uglier and more deformed, than a man crushed by grief, crippled, flat on the ground? Right next door to that misery stands the man who dreads some evil on its way and hangs suspended, breathless. To express the force of this evil, the poets imagine a boulder hanging over Tantalus in the underworld, "for his crimes, his unbridled spirit, and his boastful tongue." That is folly's common sentence: over everyone whose mind revolts from reason there hangs, always, some terror of the kind.

Section 36

And as grief and fear are the wasting disorders of the mind, so the two merrier ones — desire, forever greedily reaching for something, and empty excitement, that is, exultant joy — are not far from outright madness. Which lets you see what sort of person it is we call at one moment moderate, at another modest and temperate, at another steady and self-restrained; sometimes we like to gather all these labels under the single head of "frugality." If that word did not contain the virtues within it, it would never have become so common that it now has the standing of a proverb: "the frugal man does everything right." When the Stoics say the same thing about the wise man, they are thought to be talking too wonderfully, too magnificently.

Section 37

So then: whoever, through moderation and steadiness, is at rest in his soul and at peace with himself — so that he neither wastes away with troubles nor breaks under fear; neither burns with thirsty desire, reaching for something, nor melts into futile, giddy excitement — he is the wise man we are looking for; he is the happy man. To him nothing in human affairs can look unbearable enough to cast his soul down, or delightful enough to send it soaring. For what could look big in human affairs to a man who knows all eternity and the size of the whole universe? What, in human ambitions, in the tiny brevity of this life, can look big to the wise man — whose soul stands sentry at all hours, so that nothing can come at him unforeseen, nothing unexpected, nothing new at all?

Section 38

And this same man directs so keen a gaze in every direction that he always sees a station, a place for living free of trouble and strangling worry, so that whatever blow fortune drives at him, he takes it fittingly and calmly. And whoever does that will be free not only of grief but of all the other disorders too. A soul empty of them makes men perfectly, completely happy; a soul stirred up and dragged away from whole and certain reason loses not just its steadiness but its health. That is why the Peripatetics' theory — and their rhetoric — must be judged soft and nerveless. They say that souls must, of necessity, be disturbed; they only add a certain limit past which the disturbance ought not to go.

Section 39

A limit — you set a limit to vice? Or is it no vice to disobey reason? Does reason not teach plainly enough that nothing is truly good which you should crave with burning heat, or swagger insolently over once you have it; that nothing is truly evil which should flatten you, or drive you half out of your mind with the effort of not being flattened; and that all these things that look too grim or too glorious look so through error? And if that error shrinks in fools with the passage of time, so that though the thing itself stays the same they bear an old trouble very differently from a fresh one — then surely it ought not to touch the wise man at all?

Section 40

Besides, what exactly will this limit of theirs be? Let us hunt for the limit of grief, since that is where most of the labor gets spent. There is a passage in Fannius saying that Publius Rupilius took it hard when his brother was defeated for the consulship; but he clearly went past the limit, because it cost him his life. He ought, then, to have borne it more moderately. Fine — suppose he was bearing it moderately, and then the death of his children was added. "A new grief would arise, but a moderate one." Still, a great addition. Then add severe bodily pain, the loss of his property, blindness, exile. If each new evil brought its own added grief, the sum would be one no man could carry.

Section 41

The man who goes looking for a limit to vice is doing what a man does who believes that someone who has flung himself off the cliff of Leucas can stop his fall whenever he pleases. He cannot — and no more can a disturbed soul, once launched, hold itself back or halt where it chooses. And as a general rule, whatever is destructive as it grows is vicious at its birth;

Section 42

and grief and the rest of the disorders, once they have swollen, are certainly pestilent — so from the moment they are let in, they already stand well inside the plague. They drive themselves forward once reason has been left behind; weakness indulges itself, sails blindly out into deep water, and finds no place to stand. So it makes no difference whether these men approve of moderate disorders or of moderate injustice, moderate cowardice, moderate intemperance. Whoever sets a limit to vices signs on for a share of the vices — which is hateful in itself, and the more troublesome because vices stand on a slope: once set moving, they slide on down, and there is no way to stop them.

Section 43

And what about this: those same Peripatetics say the disorders — which we hold must be torn out root and branch — are not only natural, but were given to us by nature for our good? Their speech runs like this. First they spend a great many words praising hot temper. Anger, they say, is the whetstone of courage; angry men charge an enemy, or a corrupt citizen, with far more vehemence. The little calculations of the other sort weigh nothing — men who reason, "this battle is a just one; it is fitting to fight for our laws, for liberty, for our country." Such thoughts have no force unless courage has caught fire with anger. Nor is their argument confined to fighting men: no command is stern enough, they think, without some bitterness of anger; and as for the orator, they will not accept him prosecuting — will not even accept him defending — without the stings of anger. Even if the anger is not really there, they hold that his words and gestures ought to fake it, so that the orator's delivery may light anger in his listeners. They flatly deny that anyone who does not know how to be angry deserves to be called a man; and what we call gentleness they call, as if it were a defect, mere sluggishness.

Section 44

Nor is that the only lust they praise — for anger, as I defined it a little while ago, is the lust for revenge. No: they declare that this whole class of lust, or desire, was given by nature for the greatest usefulness, since nobody, they say, can do anything superbly except what he has an appetite for. Themistocles used to walk the public streets at night because he could not sleep, and when people asked why, he answered that the trophies of Miltiades kept jolting him awake. Who has not heard of Demosthenes and his vigils? — the man who said it stung him if any workman's before-dawn industry ever beat his own. And the princes of philosophy itself could never have made such progress in their studies without a blazing desire. Pythagoras, Democritus, Plato — we are told they traveled to the ends of the earth: wherever there was something to be learned, there, they judged, they had to go. Do we suppose any of that could have happened without desire at full burn?

Section 45

Even grief itself — which we said must be fled from like some foul, monstrous beast — they say nature established to real advantage: so that men in the wrong would hurt when correction, rebuke, and disgrace landed on them. For sinners seem to have won impunity when they can bear disgrace and infamy without pain; better, on this view, to be bitten by conscience. Hence that scene Afranius drew from life: the dissolute son wails, "Oh, wretched me!" and the hard old father answers, "Let him hurt over something — anything, so long as he hurts."

Section 46

The remaining branches of grief they call useful too: pity, for bringing help and lifting up others who have fallen into undeserved disaster; even rivalry and detraction are not useless, they say — for the man who sees that he has failed to get what another got, or that another has gotten the very thing he has. And fear! — take away fear, they warn, and you take away all the carefulness of life, which runs highest in those who fear the laws, the magistrates, poverty, disgrace, death, and pain. Still, they argue all this while conceding that these feelings should be pruned back; torn out by the roots, they say, they cannot and need not be; and in almost everything, they judge, the middle amount is best. When they lay all that out, do you think they are saying nothing — or something?

"Something, certainly," he said. "So I am waiting to hear what you will answer."

Section 47

Perhaps I shall find an answer, I said — but first, one thing: do you see how much modesty counts for among the Academics? They say plainly only what bears on the matter. The Peripatetics get their answer from the Stoics; the two of them may cross swords for all I care. My one obligation is to hunt for whatever looks most like the truth. So what turns up in this question from which we can touch something probable — the farthest point the human mind can reach? The definition of a disorder — and Zeno's, I think, is the right one. He defines it this way: a disorder is a movement of the soul against nature, turned away from reason. Or, more briefly: a disorder is a craving grown too vehement — where "too vehement" means one that has strayed far from nature's steadiness.

Section 48

What could I say against these definitions? Most of them are the work of men who argue shrewdly and sharply; those other phrases — "the fires of souls," "the whetstones of the virtues" — come straight off the rhetoricians' parade float. Can a brave man really not be brave unless his bile starts to rise? That is gladiator talk. Though even in gladiators, mind you, we often see steadiness: "they talk together, they meet, they ask a favor, they make demands" — more the look of men appeased than of men enraged. But fine — in that trade, let there be some Pacideianus with exactly the spirit Lucilius gives him:

  "I'll kill him, sure, and win — if that's your question," he says,
  "but here's how I think it goes: I'll take one in the face
  before I sink my sword in that thug's gut and lungs.
  I hate the man. I fight angry. And nothing feels longer
  than the wait while the other fits his sword to his hand —
  that's how far my rage runs, my fury and my hatred of him."

Section 49

But without any of this gladiators' anger, look at Ajax in Homer, striding out in high good cheer to fight Hector to the finish. The moment he took up his arms, his very stride poured joy into his comrades and terror into the enemy — Hector himself, just as Homer paints him, trembling through his whole chest, was sorry he had ever issued the challenge. And these two talked together, gently and quietly, before they closed — and even in the fight itself did nothing angry, nothing rabid. Nor do I believe that the first Torquatus, the man who earned that surname, was in a rage when he stripped the torque from the Gaul; nor that Marcellus was brave at Clastidium because he happened to be angry.

Section 50

As for Africanus — whom we know better, since the memory of him is fresher — I could all but swear an oath: he was not on fire with anger when he covered Marcus Allienius the Paelignian with his shield in the battle line and drove his own sword into the enemy's chest. About Lucius Brutus I might allow a doubt — whether his bottomless hatred of the tyrant made him charge Arruns with the reins off; I notice, after all, that each fell to the other's thrust at close quarters. Why, then, do you drag anger into this? Does courage have no drive of its own unless it starts to go insane? What of Hercules, whom that very courage you insist on calling anger lifted into heaven — do you suppose he was angry when he closed with the Erymanthian boar or the Nemean lion? Or Theseus — was he in a fury when he took the Marathonian bull by the horns? Consider instead that courage has nothing rabid in it at all, and that hot temper is shallowness through and through. There is no such thing as courage without reason.

Section 51

"Human affairs are to be looked down on; death is to be disregarded; pains and toils are to be counted bearable." Once these convictions are fixed by judgment and settled decision, then you have that robust, stable courage — unless we are to suspect that everything done with vehemence, keenness, and spirit is done in anger. To my mind, not even the famous Scipio, the pontifex maximus — Rome's chief priest — the man who proved true that Stoic saying that the wise man is never a private citizen, was angry with Tiberius Gracchus that day when he left the consul dithering and, private citizen though he was, gave the order as if he held the consulship himself: "Whoever wants the commonwealth safe, follow me."

Section 52

Whether I myself ever did anything brave in public life, I cannot say; but if I did, I certainly did not do it in anger. Is anything more like insanity than anger? Ennius put it perfectly when he called anger "the beginning of madness." The color of the face, the voice, the eyes, the breathing, the total loss of command over words and actions — what trace of health is in any of that? What is uglier than Homer's Achilles in the quarrel, or his Agamemnon? Ajax, for that matter, anger drove all the way to madness and death. So courage does not need to call in anger as its counsel; it comes sufficiently equipped, prepared, and armed on its own. By that logic we might as well call drunkenness useful to courage — madness too, since the insane and the drunk often do a great many things with more than usual vehemence. Ajax was always brave, but bravest in his frenzy; for

  He did his greatest deed when the Greeks were breaking:
  his one hand hauled the whole cause back.

Section 53

He turned the battle around — out of his mind. Shall we say, then, that madness is useful? Work through the definitions of courage: you will see it has no need of temper. Courage, then, is "a disposition of the soul obedient to the highest law in enduring what must be endured"; or "the stable keeping of one's judgment in facing and repelling the things that look terrifying"; or "the knowledge of what is terrifying, what is its opposite, and what is to be wholly disregarded — a knowledge that keeps one's judgment about all of them stable"; or, more briefly, with Chrysippus (the earlier definitions were Sphaerus's, a man the Stoics rank among the very best framers of definitions; they are all much alike, really, but they bring out our common notions some better than others) — how does Chrysippus put it? "Courage," he says, "is the knowledge of things that must be borne, or a disposition of the soul in suffering and enduring which obeys the highest law, without fear." We may harry these men all we like, the way Carneades used to; I am afraid they may be the only philosophers. For which of those definitions fails to unwrap the notion of courage that each of us carries around, covered and folded away? And once it is unwrapped, who would ask anything more for the fighter, the commander, or the orator — or imagine that none of them can act bravely without going rabid?

Section 54

And the Stoics — the ones who say every unwise man is insane — don't they reach these same conclusions? Take the disorders away, hot temper above all, and their claim will sound like the talk of monsters. But in fact their argument goes like this: all fools are mad, they say, the way all muck stinks — not at every moment. Stir it, and you will smell it. Just so, the hot-tempered man is not angry at every moment; provoke him, and you will watch him rave. And this warrior anger of theirs — when it comes home, what is it like with the wife? With the children? With the household? Is it useful there too? Is there, in short, anything a disturbed mind can do better than a steady one — and can anyone even be angry without disturbance of mind? So our countrymen chose their word well: though every vice is a fault of temperament, they kept the name "temperamental" for angry men alone, because no vice is uglier than hot temper.

Section 55

As for the orator, anger suits him least of all — feigned anger is another matter; that is not unbecoming. Or do you imagine I am angry whenever I say something in court with unusual sharpness and heat? What about afterward, when the business is over and done with and I am writing the speeches out — am I angry as I write? "Is anyone paying attention to this? Bind him!" Do you think Aesopus ever played a scene angry, or that Accius was angry when he wrote one? Such passages are performed splendidly — better by an orator, if he really is an orator, than by any actor — but they are performed evenly, with a tranquil mind. And praising lust — what kind of wantonness is that? You produce Themistocles and Demosthenes for me; you throw in Pythagoras, Democritus, and Plato. What — do you call study a lust? Studies, even of the best things, like the ones you cite, ought to be settled and tranquil. And praising grief, the one supremely detestable thing — what philosophers, please, do that? Afranius, granted, put it neatly: "Let him hurt over something — anything, so long as he hurts." But he said it of a ruined and dissolute young man; our question is about a steady and wise one. Let the centurion keep that anger of yours, or the standard-bearer, or the others it is better not to name — no need to lay open the rhetoricians' trade secrets. For a man who cannot use reason, it is useful to use emotion; but we, as I keep testifying, are asking about the wise man.

Section 56

"But rivalry is useful too, and detraction, and pity." Why pity a man rather than help him, if help is in your power? Or can we not be generous without pitying? We are not obliged to take griefs upon ourselves on other people's account; our obligation is to lift grief off other people, if we can. And detraction — or that vicious rivalry that looks like two suitors circling — what use is there in either, when the rival's lot is to be tormented by another's good because he lacks it himself, and the detractor's to be tormented by another's good because someone besides himself has it too? Who could approve of that — choosing to grieve over a thing rather than exert yourself, if it is a thing you want? And wanting to be its only possessor is the summit of derangement.

Section 57

And "moderate amounts" of evils — who can rightly praise that? Can anyone with lust or desire in him be anything but lustful and desirous? Anyone with anger in him, anything but angry? With anguish in him, anything but anguished? With fear in him, anything but fearful? Is this, then, our verdict on the wise man — lustful, angry, anguished, afraid? His excellence could be described at any length and breadth you like, but the shortest way is this: wisdom is the knowledge of things divine and human, the understanding of the cause of each thing; from which it follows that wisdom imitates what is divine and counts everything human as lower than virtue. And this is the nature into which you told me disturbance seemed to fall, the way storms fall on an open sea? What is there that could shake such gravity, such steadiness? Something unforeseen, something sudden? What of that kind can happen to a man who has thought out in advance everything that can happen to a human being at all? As for their claim that the excess should be pruned away and the natural part left — tell me, what can be natural that is also capable of being excessive? All of these growths spring from the roots of error, and roots of error are not for clipping and trimming. They must be plucked up and pulled out whole.
