Book I: On the Contempt of Death

(Part 1 of 4)

Section 1

Now that I have finally been freed — completely, or at least mostly — from my labors as a courtroom advocate and my duties in the Senate, I have come back, Brutus, with you urging me on more than anyone, to the studies I never let go of in my heart. Circumstances pushed them aside, and a long interval interrupted them, but I have taken them up again. And since the method and training of every art that bears on living rightly is contained in the study of wisdom — the study we call philosophy — I decided that this was the thing I should light up in Latin. Not because philosophy can't be learned from Greek books and Greek teachers, but because it has always been my judgment that our own people have either made their discoveries more wisely than the Greeks did, on their own, or improved whatever they took from them — at least in the fields they judged worth their effort.

Section 2

When it comes to character, the customs of daily life, and the running of home and family, we manage things better, and with more style. As for the state, our ancestors — beyond any dispute — shaped it with better institutions and better laws. And what should I even say about war, where our people have counted for much by courage, and for even more by discipline? Then there are the things they achieved by nature rather than by book-learning: here neither Greece nor any nation on earth stands comparison with us. Where else has there ever been such seriousness, such steadiness, such greatness of spirit, such integrity, such good faith — such outstanding excellence of every kind — that it could be set beside our ancestors?

Section 3

Where Greece did beat us was in learning and in every branch of literature — and it is easy to win when your opponent doesn't take the field. Among the Greeks, the oldest class of learned men is the poets: Homer and Hesiod lived before Rome was even founded, and Archilochus while Romulus was king. We took up poetry much later. It was roughly five hundred and ten years after Rome's founding that Livius Andronicus staged the first play, in the consulship of Gaius Claudius, son of Caecus, and Marcus Tuditanus — one year before Ennius was born; and Ennius was older than Plautus and Naevius. So poets came late to us, late to be known and late to be welcomed. True, Cato's Origins tells us that guests at banquets used to sing about the great deeds of famous men while a flute-player accompanied them. Still, a speech of Cato's makes it clear that this art carried no honor: he throws it in the face of Marcus Nobilior, as a disgrace, that he brought poets along to his province. (The poet that consul brought to Aetolia was, as we know, Ennius.) The less honor poets received, the less anyone pursued the art — and yet the few men of real talent who did take it up have not fallen far short of the glory of the Greeks.

Section 4

Do we really imagine that if painting had brought praise to Fabius, one of our noblest names, we wouldn't have produced plenty of Polyclituses and Parrhasiuses of our own? Honor feeds the arts. Glory is what sets everyone on fire for their work, and whatever a society looks down on always lies flat. The Greeks decided that the height of education lay in singing and in playing strings. So Epaminondas — in my judgment the first man of Greece — is said to have played the lyre brilliantly, while Themistocles, some years earlier, was written off as half-educated because he waved away the lyre at a dinner party. That is why musicians flourished in Greece: everyone studied music, and a man who didn't know it was not considered properly educated.

Section 5

Geometry stood at the very top of their honors, and nothing shone brighter among them than a mathematician. We have trimmed that art down to whatever is useful for surveying land and doing sums. The orator, on the other hand, we embraced quickly — at first not a learned man, but a man quick at speaking; the learning came later. Tradition says that Galba, Africanus, and Laelius were learned men, and that even Cato, who came before them in age, was a serious student; then Lepidus, Carbo, and the Gracchi, and after them orators so great, right down to our own day, that we yield little or nothing to the Greeks. But philosophy has lain flat up to this very moment. It has had no light from Latin literature at all. That is the art I must raise up and set shining — so that if I did my fellow citizens some good while I held office, I can go on doing them good, if I am able, in retirement.

Section 6

And I have to work at this all the harder because there are said to be many Latin books already — written carelessly, by men who were fine men, yes, but not learned enough. It can certainly happen that a person thinks well and still can't put his thoughts into polished words. But to commit your thoughts to writing when you can neither arrange them, nor make them clear, nor give the reader any pleasure to draw him along — that is a man abusing both his leisure and the alphabet, with no self-restraint at all. And so these authors read their own books aloud to their own circle, and nobody else ever touches them except people hoping the same license to scribble will be extended to them. So if my hard work has added anything to the reputation of oratory, I will work far harder still to open the springs of philosophy — the source from which even that eloquence flowed.

Section 7

Aristotle, a man of supreme genius, knowledge, and range, was stung by the fame of the rhetorician Isocrates and began teaching young men to speak, joining practical wisdom to eloquence. In the same way, I have decided not to lay down my old passion for speaking while I work in this greater and richer art. For I have always held that the complete philosophy is the one that can speak on the biggest questions fully and beautifully. I have thrown myself into that practice so seriously that I have even dared to hold sessions in the Greek style. Just recently, after you left, when a number of friends were staying with me at my place in Tusculum, I tried out what I could do in that line. I used to declaim practice speeches for the courtroom, and no one kept at it longer than I did; this is now the declamation of my old age. I would ask someone to name whatever topic he wanted to hear about, and then — sitting or walking — I argued it out.

Section 8

And so I have gathered the "schools," as the Greeks call them, of five days into the same number of books. It worked like this: after the one who wanted to listen had said what he thought, I argued against him. That, as you know, is the old Socratic method — arguing against the other person's position; Socrates believed it was the easiest way to find what comes nearest the truth. But to lay our discussions out more conveniently, I will present them as a scene happening in front of you, not as a report. So let the opening go like this.

Section 9

"Death seems to me an evil," he began.

"For whom?" I asked. "For those who are already dead, or for those who have to die?"

"For both."

"Then it is a misery, since it's an evil?"

"Certainly."

"So the people death has already come for, and the people it is coming for, are both miserable."

"That's how it seems to me."

"Then there is no one who isn't miserable."

"No one at all."

"And if you want to be consistent, everyone who has ever been born or ever will be is not only miserable but miserable forever. If you were calling only those who have to die miserable, you wouldn't be leaving out a single living person — everyone has to die — but at least misery would end at death. Since the dead are miserable too, we are born into a misery that never ends. It follows that the people who died a hundred thousand years ago are miserable — or rather, everyone who was ever born."

"That is exactly what I think."

Section 10

"Then tell me, please: is this the sort of thing that frightens you? Three-headed Cerberus down in the underworld, the roar of Cocytus, the ferry-crossing of Acheron? Tantalus,

  worn out with thirst, the water touching his chin?

"Or that famous line, how

  Sisyphus rolls his stone, sweating and straining,
  and gains not one single inch?

"Perhaps you dread the unbending judges too, Minos and Rhadamanthus. Before that bench no Lucius Crassus will defend you, no Marcus Antonius; and since the case will be heard before Greek judges, you won't even be able to retain Demosthenes. You will have to plead your own cause, in front of an enormous crowd. Maybe that is what you're afraid of, and that's why you count death an everlasting evil."

"Do you really think I'm crazy enough to believe those stories?"

"You don't believe them?"

"Not for a moment."

"That's a shame."

"Why on earth?"

"Because I could have been very eloquent arguing against them."

Section 11

"Who couldn't be, on a subject like that? What work is it to knock down these monsters dreamed up by poets and painters?"

"And yet the philosophers have filled whole books arguing against exactly those things."

"More fool them. Who is so witless that any of that could move him?"

"Well then: if no one is miserable in the underworld, there is no one in the underworld at all."

"Agreed, completely."

"Then where are these miserable people of yours? What address do they occupy? If they exist, they have to be somewhere."

"But I think they are nowhere."

"So they don't exist at all?"

"Just so — and yet they are miserable for that very reason: because they don't exist."

Section 12

"I'd rather have you scared of Cerberus than talking as carelessly as that."

"Meaning what?"

"The same person you say doesn't exist, you say exists. Where is that sharp mind of yours? To call someone miserable is to say that a person who doesn't exist, exists."

"I'm not so dull that I'd say that."

"Then what are you saying?"

"That Marcus Crassus, for example, is miserable because death stripped him of that enormous fortune; that Pompeius is miserable because he was robbed of all that glory; that everyone, in short, is miserable who has lost this light of day."

"You've circled straight back. To be miserable, they have to exist — and a moment ago you denied that the dead exist. If they don't exist, they can't be anything. And in that case they can't even be miserable."

"Maybe I'm not managing to say what I actually feel. The thing itself — not existing, after you have existed — seems to me the depth of misery."

Section 13

"What — worse than never having existed at all? By that logic, the people not yet born are miserable already, because they don't exist; and we ourselves, if we're going to be miserable after death, were miserable before we were born. But I don't remember being miserable before I was born. If your memory is better than mine, I'd love to know what you recall of yourself back then."

"Very funny — as if I'd said the unborn are miserable, and not the dead."

"So you say the dead exist."

"No. I say they are miserable precisely because they don't exist — though they once did."

"Don't you hear the contradiction? What could be more self-contradictory than for something that doesn't exist to be — not just miserable — but anything at all? When you walk out the Capena gate and see the tombs of Calatinus, the Scipios, the Servilii, and the Metelli, do you consider those men miserable?"

"Since you keep pinning me down over a word, from now on I won't say they are miserable. I'll just say 'miserable' — by itself — for the very reason that they don't exist."

"So you're not saying 'Marcus Crassus is miserable,' only 'miserable Marcus Crassus'?"

"Exactly."

Section 14

"As if anything you assert that way didn't have to either be so or not be so! Haven't you had even a first taste of logic? Because this is one of its first lessons: every proposition — that's the word that occurs to me for the moment for what the Greeks call an axioma; I'll use a different one later if I find something better — every proposition is a statement that is either true or false. So when you say 'miserable Marcus Crassus,' either you are saying 'Marcus Crassus is miserable,' so that we can judge whether it's true or false, or you are saying nothing at all."

"All right — I now concede that the dead are not miserable, since you've wrung out of me the admission that what doesn't exist at all can't even manage to be miserable. But what about us, the living, who have death waiting? Aren't we miserable? What sweetness can there be in life when, day and night, you have to keep thinking that at any moment — this one, maybe — you must die?"

Section 15

"So do you realize how much evil you have just lifted off the human condition?"

"How so?"

"Because if death were miserable for the dead too, life would hold an evil that is infinite and everlasting. As it is, I can see the finish line, and once the race is run, there is nothing further to fear. But you seem to me to be following the maxim of Epicharmus — a sharp man, and, for a Sicilian, not without wit."

"What maxim? I don't know it."

"I'll say it in Latin if I can. You know I'm no more in the habit of dropping Greek into a Latin conversation than Latin into a Greek one."

"Quite right, too. But what is this maxim of Epicharmus?"

I gave it to him:

  I do not want to die — but that I will be dead?
  That means nothing at all to me.

"Now I recognize the Greek. But since you've forced me to concede that the dead are not miserable, take the next step, if you can: convince me that having to die isn't miserable either."

Section 16

"That is no work at all. I'm after bigger game."

"How is it no work at all? And what exactly is this bigger game?"

"Like this. Since there is no evil after death, death itself can't be an evil either — because the time that comes right after death is the time you concede holds no evil. So even having to die is no evil; it is only having to arrive at a place we both agree is not evil."

"Spell that out more fully, please. These thorny little arguments force me to admit things before I actually agree with them. But what are these greater things you say you're working toward?"

"To teach you, if I can, that death is not merely not an evil — it is a positive good."

"I won't demand that much, but I am eager to hear it. Even if you fall short of what you want, you will still prove that death is no evil. And I won't interrupt you; I'd rather hear you speak straight through."

Section 17

"But what if I ask you a question — won't you answer?"

"That would be arrogant of me. But unless it's really necessary, I'd rather you didn't ask."

"I'll do as you wish, and explain what you want as well as I can — but not like Pythian Apollo, as if everything I say were fixed and certain. I am one small human being among many, chasing what is probable by best guess. Seeing what looks like the truth is as far as I can go; certainties are for the men who claim such things can be known for sure, and who advertise themselves as wise."

"Do it however you see fit. We are ready to listen."

Section 18

So I began. Death itself, then — the thing that seems the most familiar in the world — is the first thing we have to examine: what is it? Some think death is the departure of the soul from the body. Others hold that there is no departure at all — that soul and body perish together, and the soul is snuffed out inside the body. Of those who think the soul does depart, some say it scatters immediately, some that it lasts a long while, and some that it lasts forever. And as for what the soul itself actually is, or where it lives, or where it comes from — the disagreement is enormous. To some, the soul is the heart itself. That is why in Latin the senseless are excordes, "missing a heart," the deranged vecordes, "wrong in the heart," and the like-minded concordes, "hearts together"; it is why the shrewd Nasica, twice consul, was nicknamed Corculum, "Little Heart," and why the old verse praises "that man of outstanding heart, shrewd Aelius Sextus."

Section 19

Empedocles thinks the soul is the blood that soaks the region of the heart. To others, it seemed that a certain part of the brain holds the soul's command post. Others accept neither the heart itself nor any part of the brain as the soul, but say instead that the heart — or the brain — is the soul's seat and home. Others say the soul is breath, anima, and Roman usage is pretty much on their side, as our own words show: we say a dying man "gasps out his breath" and "breathes his last"; we call the brave "full of spirit" and the steady "right-spirited"; we give an oath "from the bottom of the soul." And animus, our very word for soul, comes straight from anima, breath. To Zeno the Stoic, meanwhile, the soul is fire. Now these opinions I've just named — heart, brain, breath, fire — are the common ones; the rest belong mostly to single thinkers. So it was with a number of the ancients long ago, and most recently with Aristoxenus, a musician as well as a philosopher, who said the soul is a certain tension of the body itself — like what singers and string-players call harmonia: from the nature and shape of the whole body, various motions arise, the way notes come off an instrument.

Section 20

Aristoxenus never strayed far from his own trade, but what he said was something real — and its substance had been stated, and worked out, long before by Plato. Xenocrates denied that the soul has any shape or anything like a body. He said it is number — and the power of number, as Pythagoras had concluded ages earlier, is the greatest power in nature. His teacher Plato imagined a threefold soul. Its ruling part — reason — he placed in the head, as in a citadel; the other two parts, anger and desire, he made reason's subjects and assigned them separate quarters, lodging anger in the chest and desire down below the diaphragm.

Section 21

Dicaearchus, though, in the conversation he sets in Corinth and lays out in three books, fills the first book with a crowd of learned men debating; in the other two he brings on a certain Pherecrates, an old man from Phthia, descended — so he claims — from Deucalion himself. This Pherecrates argues that the soul is nothing at all: the word is an empty name; it is pointless to speak of "animals" and "animate beings"; there is no soul and no life-breath in man or beast. All that power by which we act or perceive is poured evenly through every living body and cannot be separated from the body — because it is nothing on its own. Nothing exists except body, single and simple, shaped in such a way that it lives and feels through the balanced workings of nature.

Section 22

Aristotle — who towers far above everyone in both genius and care (I always set Plato apart) — after taking in the four familiar classes of elements from which all things arise, concluded that there is a certain fifth nature, and that mind comes from it. For thinking, foreseeing, learning, teaching, discovering, remembering so many things — loving, hating, desiring, fearing, being distressed, feeling joy — these and everything like them, he holds, are found in none of those four classes. So he adds a fifth class that has no name of its own, and he gives the soul itself a brand-new label: endelecheia, meaning something like continuous, unending motion.

Unless a few have slipped my mind, these are pretty much the opinions about the soul. Democritus we can leave aside — a great man, certainly, but he builds the soul out of smooth, round little particles colliding by pure chance; with that school, there is nothing a mob of atoms can't accomplish.

Section 23

Which of these opinions is true, let some god decide. Which is most like the truth is a big question. So — would we rather judge between them, or come back to our subject?

"I'd want both, if it were possible," he said, "but it's hard to blend them. So if we can be freed from the fear of death without threshing all of that out, let's do it; but if it can't be done without first settling this question about souls, let's have that now and the other another time."

The course I could see he preferred was, I told him, also the more practical one. For whichever of the opinions I have listed is true, reason will bring us out at the same place: death is either no evil — or a positive good.

Section 24

For if the soul is heart or blood or brain, then being body, it will perish with the rest of the body. If it is breath, it will perhaps disperse; if fire, it will go out; if it is Aristoxenus's tuning, it will fall out of tune. And what should I say of Dicaearchus, who says the soul is nothing at all? On every one of these views, nothing after death can matter to anyone, because feeling is lost together with life — and to a being that feels nothing, nothing makes the slightest difference either way. The remaining opinions, though, bring hope — if this is the sort of thing that cheers you — that souls, once they have left the body, may make their way to heaven as to their own true home.

"It does cheer me," he said. "First of all, I want it to be true; and even if it isn't, I would still like to be convinced of it."

"Then what do you need my help for? Can I outdo Plato in eloquence? Unroll his book about the soul, and read it carefully. There will be nothing left for you to want."

"I have done exactly that, believe me — many times. But somehow, while I'm reading, I agree with every word; the moment I put the book down and start thinking on my own about the immortality of souls, all that agreement drains away."

Section 25

"Well then, do you grant this much: souls either survive after death, or death itself destroys them?"

"I grant it."

"And if they survive?"

"Then I concede they are happy."

"And if they perish?"

"Then they are not miserable, since they don't even exist. You forced that concession out of me just now."

"Then how — and why — can you say that death seems to you an evil, when it will either make us happy, if our souls survive, or spare us all misery, if our feeling is gone?"

Section 26

"Then lay it out, if it's not too much trouble. First — if you can — show that souls survive after death. Then, if that proves too steep a climb (and it is steep), show that death holds no evil at all. Because here is what I'm afraid of: that the evil is exactly this — I don't mean being without sensation, but the fact that we will have to be without it."

For the opinion you want upheld, I answered, we can call on the best authorities there are — which in every kind of case ought to carry the most weight, and usually does. And first of all, we can call antiquity itself: the closer those early generations stood to the beginning of things and to our divine ancestry, the better, perhaps, they saw what was actually true.

Section 27

And so those men of old — the "ancients," as Ennius calls them — held one conviction planted deep: that there is sensation in death, and that when a person leaves this life, he is not so wiped out as to perish root and branch. You can see this in many things, and above all in the law of the pontiffs and the rites of burial. Men of the greatest minds would never have tended those rites with such care, or fenced their violation with such unforgivable penalties, unless it had been fixed in their hearts that death is not an annihilation that removes and erases everything, but a kind of migration — a change of life — which for famous men and women was usually a road into heaven, while for everyone else the soul was held back here on earth, yet lasted all the same.

Section 28

That is why, by the belief of our own people,

  Romulus in heaven lives out his days with the gods,

as Ennius said, following the common report. That is why, among the Greeks — and from them all the way to us, and clear out to the Ocean — Hercules is held to be a god so great and so ready to help; why Liber, Semele's son, is a god; and why the twin sons of Tyndareus enjoy the same bright fame — the brothers who are said not only to have fought beside the Roman people to victory in battle, but to have carried home the news of it. And Ino, Cadmus's daughter — don't the Greeks call her Leucothea, and don't we call her Matuta? More than that: isn't nearly the whole of heaven — I'll stop the list there — populated from the human race?

Section 29

And if I tried to dig into the old records and pull out what the Greek writers have handed down, we would find that even the gods reckoned as heaven's oldest families set out for the sky from here among us. Ask which gods have tombs pointed out in Greece. Remember — since you are an initiate — the things handed down in the mysteries. Then you will finally understand how far all this reaches. Those early people had not yet learned the natural science that men began to work at only many years later; they were persuaded of exactly as much as nature's own promptings had taught them. They did not grasp the reasons and causes of things, and they were shaken, again and again, by visions — mostly at night — until it seemed to them that those who had left this life were living still.

Section 30

Now, the strongest argument anyone offers for believing that gods exist is this: no tribe is so savage, no human being anywhere so wild, that the idea of gods has not taken root in his mind. Many people have crooked notions about the gods — bad habits usually see to that — yet everyone believes in a divine power and a divine nature. And no human conference produced that agreement; the belief was not fixed by custom or enforced by law. But in every matter, the consensus of all nations must be counted as the law of nature. Very well — by the same test — who mourns his own dead without mourning, first and foremost, because he thinks they have been stripped of the good things of life? Take away that belief, and you take away the mourning. No one weeps over his own inconvenience. We may hurt, we may ache; but the wailing of grief, the mourner's tears — those come from believing that the person we loved has lost the good things of life, and feels the loss. And we believe this with nature as our guide, with no argument and no schooling behind it.

Section 31

But the greatest proof is this: nature herself passes silent judgment in favor of the immortality of souls, in the fact that everyone cares — and cares intensely — about what will happen after death.

  He plants trees to serve another age,

as Caecilius Statius says in his Young Comrades. And what is the planter looking to, if not that the ages to come are his business too? So a careful farmer will plant trees whose fruit he will never lay eyes on — and a great man won't plant laws, institutions, a commonwealth? What does having children mean, or carrying a family name forward, or adopting sons, or the care we take over wills, or the grave-monuments themselves and the words we carve on them — what do they all mean, if not that our thoughts stand planted in the future too?
