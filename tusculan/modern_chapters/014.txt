Book V: Whether Virtue Alone Is Sufficient for a Happy Life

(Part 3 of 4)

Section 59

He had two wives — Aristomache, a woman of his own city, and Doris of Locri — and when he visited them at night, he would first have everything searched and probed to the last corner. He had ringed the place where his bed stood with a broad moat, crossed by a little wooden footbridge, and once he had shut the bedroom door he swung the bridge away himself. And since he did not dare stand on the public platforms where speakers address the people, he used to harangue them from the top of a high tower.

Section 60

The story goes that when he wanted to play ball — he was devoted to the game — and took off his tunic, he would hand his sword to a young man he loved. One of his intimates said, joking, "Well, him at least you trust with your life" — and the young man smiled. Dionysius ordered them both killed: the one for pointing out a way he might be murdered, the other for approving the remark with a laugh. And the deed tore at him afterward as nothing else in his whole life did, for he had killed someone he passionately loved. That is how the desires of men without self-command pull against each other. Satisfy this one, and you must fight that one.

Section 61

And yet this tyrant delivered his own verdict on how happy he really was. One of his flatterers, a man named Damocles, was going on in conversation about his troops, his wealth, the majesty of his rule, the abundance of everything he owned, the magnificence of the royal palace — surely no one had ever been happier. "Then would you like, Damocles," said Dionysius, "since this life delights you so, to taste it yourself and try my fortune firsthand?" Damocles said he would love nothing better. So Dionysius had the man installed on a golden couch spread with a gorgeous woven coverlet, embroidered with magnificent scenes, and loaded the sideboards around him with chased silver and gold. Then he ordered serving boys, chosen for their exceptional beauty, to stand at the table and watch for his nod, attending to his every want.

Section 62

There were perfumed oils and garlands; incense burned; the tables were piled high with the choicest delicacies. Damocles thought himself a fortunate man. And in the middle of all this splendor, Dionysius ordered a gleaming sword to be let down from the ceiling, hung by a single horsehair, so that it dangled over the happy man's neck. After that, Damocles had no eyes for the beautiful servers or the silver heavy with art; he could not bring himself to reach for the table; the garlands slid, unnoticed, from his head. Finally he begged the tyrant to let him go — "I no longer wish to be happy." Hadn't Dionysius made his declaration plainly enough: that nothing can be happy for the man over whom some terror always hangs? And for him there was no going back — no returning to justice, no restoring liberty and rights to his fellow citizens. As a young man, at that improvident age, he had tangled himself in wrongs and committed crimes of such a kind that he could not be safe the moment he began to be sane.

Section 63

How much he longed for friendship — while dreading the treachery of friends — he showed in the case of those two Pythagoreans. He had accepted one of them as a hostage for the other's death sentence; the other, to free the friend who had pledged himself, appeared at the very hour appointed for the execution. "How I wish," said Dionysius, "that you would enroll me as the third in your friendship." What misery, to be cut off from the company of friends, from a shared table, from easy familiar talk altogether — and this was a man educated from boyhood, trained in the liberal arts, devoted to musicians, even a tragic poet himself. How good a poet is beside the point; in that art, somehow more than in any other, every man finds his own work beautiful. To this day I have never known a poet — and Aquinius was a friend of mine — who did not think himself the best. That is how it goes: your work delights you, mine delights me. But back to Dionysius. He lived stripped of every human refinement and fellowship — among runaways, criminals, and barbarians — convinced that no one who deserved liberty, or who wanted to be free at all, could be a friend to him.

Section 64

I will not set against this man's life — I can imagine nothing fouler, more miserable, more detestable — the life of Plato or of Archytas, learned men and truly wise. From that same city I will call up instead a humble little man, raising him from his sand-table and measuring rod, who lived many years later: Archimedes. When I was quaestor in Sicily, I tracked down his grave. The Syracusans knew nothing of it — they denied it even existed — but I found it, hedged in on every side, dressed over with brambles and thickets. I had by heart some little verses which, I had been told, were inscribed on his monument, and they declared that on top of the tomb there stood a sphere together with a cylinder.

Section 65

So I ran my eyes over everything — at the Agrigentine Gate there is a great crowd of tombs — and I noticed a small column standing just above the brush, with the figure of a sphere and a cylinder on it. I said at once to the Syracusans — their leading men were with me — that I believed this was the very thing I was looking for. Workmen were sent in with sickles, and they cleared the ground and opened up the spot.

Section 66

When a way in had been made, we walked up to the front of the pedestal. There was the epigram — the second half of each little verse eaten away, worn almost to nothing. And so the noblest city of Greece, once the most learned as well, would not have known the monument of the sharpest mind among all its citizens, if a man from Arpinum had not come and pointed it out. But let my talk return to where it wandered off. Who is there with any dealings at all with the Muses — that is, with humane learning and culture — who would not rather be this mathematician than that tyrant? Look at how each of them lived and what each of them did. The one fed his mind on working out and hunting down proofs, with all the delight of his own ingenuity — the single sweetest nourishment a soul can have. The other fed his on slaughter and injustice, with fear beside him day and night. Come now, set Democritus beside him, and Pythagoras, and Anaxagoras: what kingdom, what riches, would you rank above their studies and their delights?

Section 67

For that best thing you are seeking must be located in the best part of a man — and what is better in a man than a keen and good mind? Its good, then, is what we must enjoy if we want to be happy. But the good of the mind is virtue; therefore the happy life must be contained in virtue. From this source flows everything beautiful, honorable, splendid — as I said above, though the point deserves a somewhat fuller telling — and all of it is full of joys. And since it is clear that the happy life is made out of continuous and brimming joys, it follows that it is made out of moral goodness.

Section 68

But so that we do not merely gesture with words at what we want to show, we should set out something like moving pictures, to turn us more toward real knowledge and understanding. Let us take a man of surpassing excellence in the finest arts, and imagine him, for a moment, in thought. First, he must have an extraordinary mind — virtue does not readily keep company with slow wits. Second, a passionate drive to investigate the truth. Out of that drive comes the mind's threefold harvest: one part lies in the knowledge of the world and the explanation of nature; the second in marking off what should be sought and what avoided, and in the art of living well; the third in judging what follows from a thing and what contradicts it — and in that lies all the fineness of argument, and all the truth of judgment.

Section 69

With what joy, then, must the wise man's soul be filled, living with these concerns, keeping night-watches with them! He surveys the motions and revolutions of the whole world; he sees the countless stars fastened to the sky, fixed in their appointed seats, turning in concert with heaven's own turning; he sees seven others, each holding its own course, standing far apart from one another in height and depth, their wandering motions still tracing out fixed and certain distances along their paths. It was the sight of all this, surely, that stirred those ancient thinkers and prompted them to seek further. From there was born the search for the beginnings of things — the seeds, as it were, from which everything arose, was generated, took form; what the origin is of each kind of thing, lifeless or living, mute or speaking; what its life is, and its death; what change and passage carries one thing into another; where the earth came from, and by what weights it hangs balanced; by what hollows the seas are held up; by what gravity all things, borne downward, forever seek the middle place of the world — which, in a sphere, is also the lowest.

Section 70

To a soul that handles these questions, turning them over night and day, there comes that knowledge the god at Delphi prescribed: the mind comes to know itself, and to feel itself joined to the divine mind — and from that it is filled with a joy that cannot be exhausted. For the very act of thinking about the power and nature of the gods kindles a longing to imitate their eternity; and the mind no longer believes itself penned into the shortness of this life, once it sees the causes of things linked one to another and bound together by necessity — flowing from eternity into eternity, yet governed all the while by reason and intelligence.

Section 71

Gazing on all this, looking upward — or rather looking around at every region and outermost shore of things — with what tranquility of soul does he then turn back to consider human affairs, the things nearer at hand! From this comes the knowledge of virtue; the kinds and branches of the virtues flower out; we discover what nature holds to be the final thing among goods and the last extreme among evils, what our duties should be referred to, and what plan of life we ought to choose. And when these questions and others like them have been worked through, the chief result is the very point this discussion is arguing: that virtue is sufficient in itself for living happily.

Section 72

There follows the third capacity, which seeps and pours itself through every part of wisdom: the one that defines each thing, sorts things into their kinds, joins consequences together, draws complete conclusions, and tells true from false — the art and science of reasoning. It yields the greatest usefulness for weighing any question, and along with that a delight of the most refined kind, entirely worthy of wisdom. But all this belongs to leisure. Now let this same wise man cross over to the protection of the commonwealth. What could be more excellent? His practical wisdom sees where the citizens' true interest lies; his justice diverts not one coin of it into his own house; and he has all the other virtues, so many and so varied, at his command. Add to this the fruit of friendship: in it the learned find counsel for the whole of life, agreeing with their own and almost breathing in time with it, and also the deepest daily pleasure of shared company and shared living. What does such a life lack that could make it happier? To a life packed with joys so many and so great, Fortune herself must give way. And if rejoicing in such goods of the soul — that is, in the virtues — is happiness, and all wise men enjoy those joys to the full, then we are forced to admit that all wise men are happy.

Section 73

"Even under torture?" he broke in. "Even on the rack?"

And did you suppose, I asked him, that I meant a wise man lying on beds of violets and roses? Is Epicurus to be allowed to say it — a man who merely put on the philosopher's mask and awarded himself the title, though as things stand he says it with my applause — that there is no moment when the wise man cannot cry out, even while he is being burned, tortured, cut open, "How little I care!"? This from the man who defines all evil as pain and all good as pleasure, who laughs at everything we call honorable or base and says we are busy with words, pouring out empty noise — that nothing concerns us except what the body feels as smooth or rough. Shall he, as I said — a man whose standard of judgment barely differs from a beast's — be free to forget himself: to despise fortune, when every good and evil he recognizes lies in fortune's power, and to call himself happy under the extremity of torture and torment, when he has laid it down that pain is not only the greatest evil but the only one?

Section 74

And he never equipped himself with the remedies that make pain bearable — firmness of mind, shame at anything base, the practice and habit of endurance, the precepts of courage, a manly hardness. No: he says he settles himself with one thing only, the memory of past pleasures. As if a man sweltering in heat he could hardly stand should console himself by remembering that he once bathed in the cold streams of my own Arpinum. I do not see how past pleasures can quiet present evils.

Section 75

But since he says the wise man is always happy — he who would have no right to say it if he wanted to be consistent with himself — what should they do who hold that nothing is desirable, nothing to be counted a good, that lacks moral goodness? Let me give the advice myself: it is time for the Peripatetics and the old Academics to stop their stammering at last and dare to say, openly and in a clear voice, that the happy life will walk down into the bull of Phalaris.

Section 76

Grant, then, that there are three kinds of goods — let us get clear, for now, of the Stoics' snares; I know I have used them more than I usually do. Grant those kinds of goods, provided the goods of the body and the external ones lie flat on the ground and are called goods only in the sense that they are "to be taken up," while those other, divine goods of the soul spread themselves far and wide and touch the sky. Then why should I call the man who has won them merely happy, and not the happiest of all? But will the wise man be afraid of pain? Pain is the great objector to our position. Against our own death and the deaths of those we love, against grief and the other disorders of the soul, the discussions of the previous days have, I think, armed and prepared us well enough. But pain looks like virtue's fiercest adversary. Pain is what brandishes its burning torches; pain is what threatens to break down courage, greatness of spirit, endurance.

Section 77

Shall virtue go down before this? Shall the happy life of the wise and steady man surrender to it? Good gods, what a disgrace that would be! Spartan boys, flesh torn by the lash, do not so much as groan. I have seen with my own eyes, at Lacedaemon, troops of young men contending with unbelievable intensity — fists, feet, nails, finally teeth — ready to drop dead before they would admit defeat. What barbarian country is wilder or more uncultivated than India? Yet in that nation, to begin with, the men held to be wise pass their whole lives naked, and bear the snows of the Caucasus and the violence of winter without pain; and when they give themselves to the flame, they burn without a groan.

Section 78

And the women of India, when one of them loses her husband, come before a judge to contest which of them he loved the most — for a man there usually has several wives. The winner, exulting, escorted by her family, mounts the pyre beside her husband; the loser walks away in grief. Custom could never defeat nature — nature is always unconquered. It is we who have infected our souls with shade and pampering, leisure, languor, idleness, and softened them, spoiled by opinion and bad habit. Who does not know the custom of the Egyptians? Their minds steeped in the errors of superstition, they would sooner submit to any torture than harm an ibis or an asp, a cat, a dog, or a crocodile — and if they have hurt one even by accident, they refuse no punishment for it.

Section 79

I have been speaking of human beings. But what of the beasts? Do they not endure cold and hunger, ranging and roaming over mountains and through forests? Do they not fight for their young so hard that they take wounds — fearing no attack, flinching from no blow? I say nothing of what the ambitious will suffer for office, or the praise-hungry for glory, or lovers on fire with desire. Life is crowded with examples.

Section 80

But let my talk keep its measure and come back to where it turned aside. The happy life, I say it again, will give itself up to torture. Following behind justice, self-control, and above all courage, greatness of spirit, and endurance, it will not stop short at the sight of the torturer's face; when all the virtues walk out to the rack without one tremor of soul, it will not hang back outside the doors, as I said before, at the prison's threshold. For what could be uglier, what more disfigured, than the happy life left standing alone, cut off from that most beautiful escort? Yet that can never happen at all: the virtues cannot hold together without the happy life, nor the happy life without the virtues.

Section 81

And so they will not let her hang back. They will sweep her along with them to whatever pain and torture they themselves are led. For it is the mark of the wise man to do nothing he could regret, nothing against his will; to do everything splendidly, steadily, gravely, honorably; to count on nothing as certain to happen; to be astonished by nothing when it does happen, as though it were strange and unforeseen; to refer everything to his own judgment; to stand on his own decisions. What could be happier than that, I honestly cannot conceive.

Section 82

For the Stoics, indeed, the conclusion comes easily. Since they hold that the highest good is to agree with nature and to live in harmony with her, and since that lies not only within the wise man's duty but within his power, it necessarily follows that the man who holds the highest good in his power holds the happy life in his power too. And so the wise man's life is always happy. There you have what I believe can be most boldly said about the happy life — and, as things now stand, unless you can bring something better, most truly said as well.

"Something better I certainly cannot bring," he said. "But there is a favor I would gladly win from you, if it is no trouble. No chains of any fixed school bind you; you sip from all of them whatever strikes you most with the look of truth. A moment ago you seemed to urge the Peripatetics and the old Academy to have the courage to say freely, without hedging, that wise men are always the happiest of all. I would like to hear how you think it consistent for them to say so — since you have argued so much against their position, arguing to Stoic conclusions."

Section 83

Then let me use the liberty, I said, which we alone in philosophy have the right to use: our discourse decides nothing on its own authority; it is turned toward every side, so that others may judge it on its merits, with no one's name attached to weight the scales. And since what you seem to want is this — that whatever view the disagreeing philosophers take of the ends of goods, virtue should still hold protection enough for a happy life — well, that is a case we are told Carneades used to argue; but he argued it against the Stoics, whom he refuted with all his zeal, the school against whose teaching his whole talent had caught fire. I shall handle it more peaceably. For if the Stoics have set the ends of goods rightly, the business is finished: the wise man must always be happy.

Section 84

But let us examine, if we can, each of the remaining opinions in turn, so that this splendid ruling, so to call it, in favor of the happy life may be squared with everyone's views and schools. These, I believe, are the opinions on the ends of goods that are still held and defended. First, four simple ones: nothing is good but moral goodness, as the Stoics say; nothing is good but pleasure, as Epicurus holds; nothing is good but freedom from pain, as Hieronymus of Rhodes maintains; nothing is good but the enjoyment of nature's primary goods — all of them, or the greatest — as Carneades used to argue against the Stoics.

Section 85

Those are the simple opinions; now the mixed. Three kinds of goods — the greatest belonging to the soul, the second to the body, the external ones third — as the Peripatetics hold, and the old Academics say much the same. Dinomachus and Callipho coupled pleasure with moral goodness, while Diodorus the Peripatetic joined freedom from pain to moral goodness. These are the opinions with some standing left; those of Aristo, Pyrrho, Erillus, and a few others have long since evaporated. Let us see what these schools can make good — leaving aside the Stoics, whose position I think I have defended enough. The Peripatetics' case has been laid out too, except for Theophrastus and any of his followers, who dread and shrink from pain too feebly. The rest are free to do what they mostly do anyway: pile up the weight and dignity of virtue. And once they have exalted her to the skies — eloquent men can do that at any length — it is easy, by comparison, to grind everything else down and treat it with contempt. For men who say that praise is worth pursuing even at the price of pain are in no position to deny that the men who have won it are happy. Though such men stand amid certain evils, still this word "happy" reaches far and wide.

Section 86

For just as trade is called profitable and farming fruitful — not because the one never takes a loss or the other never suffers a ruined season, but because by far the greater part turns out well — so a life can rightly be called happy not only when it is stuffed with goods on every side, but when the goods preponderate, outweighing everything else by far the greater and heavier measure.

Section 87

By their reasoning, then, the happy life will follow virtue even to the scaffold; it will climb down with her into the bull, on the authority of Aristotle, Xenocrates, Speusippus, and Polemo, and no threat and no enticement will corrupt her into desertion. Callipho and Diodorus will hold the same, since each of them embraces moral goodness so tightly that he judges everything without it fit only to be set far behind. The rest look more cramped for room, but they swim clear all the same — Epicurus, Hieronymus, and whoever cares to defend that abandoned end of Carneades. For there is not one of them who does not make the mind the judge of goods, and school it thoroughly in despising whatever merely seems good or bad.

Section 88

For what you take to be Epicurus's case will be Hieronymus's case too, and Carneades's — and, by Hercules, everyone else's. Who among them is poorly prepared against death or pain? Let us begin, if you like, with the man we call soft and pleasure-loving. Well? Does he look to you like a man afraid of death or pain — a man who calls the day he dies a happy day, and who, gripped by the greatest pains, silences them with the memory and recollection of his own discoveries? And he does not do this in a way that suggests he is babbling on the spur of the moment. On death his settled view is that when the living creature dissolves, sensation is snuffed out, and what is without sensation, he judges, is nothing to us at all. On pain, likewise, he has fixed rules to follow: if it is great, he consoles himself with its shortness; if it is long, with its mildness.

Section 89

Then how exactly are those grand talkers better armed than Epicurus against these two things, which press on us hardest? And against the other things counted as evils — do not Epicurus and the rest of the philosophers seem prepared enough? Who does not dread poverty? Yet not one philosopher does. And this man himself — how little he is content with! No one has said more about plain living. The things that breed the hunger for money — funds for love affairs, for ambition, for daily extravagance — stand so far from him that why should he have any great desire for money, or rather, why should he care about it at all?
