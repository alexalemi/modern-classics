Book I: On the Contempt of Death

(Part 2 of 4)

Section 32

And here is another thing: do you doubt that our sample of nature ought to be taken from nature at its best? And what better nature is there in the human race than the men who consider themselves born to help other people, to protect them, to keep them safe? Hercules has gone to the gods. He never would have gone if he had not paved that road for himself while he was still among men. All this is ancient now, and consecrated by the religion of every people. And in our own republic — what do we suppose all those men were thinking, so many and so great, who laid down their lives for the state? That their names would stop at the same boundary line as their lives? No one ever offered himself to death for his country without a great hope of immortality.

Section 33

Themistocles could have lived at his ease. So could Epaminondas. So — not to go hunting for examples that are old or foreign — could I. But somehow there clings to our minds a kind of prophecy of the ages to come, and it is in the greatest talents and the loftiest souls that it lives most strongly and shows most plainly. Take that away, and who would be insane enough to spend his whole life in toil and danger?

Section 34

I have been speaking of statesmen. But what about poets — don't they want to be famous after death? Where else does this come from:

  Look, citizens, upon the carved face of old Ennius:
  this is the man who set your fathers' greatest deeds in song.

He is demanding the wage of glory from the sons of the very men his poetry had covered with glory. And the same poet writes:

  Let no one honor me with tears, or make
  my funeral a weeping. Why? Because
  I fly, alive, from mouth to mouth among mankind.

But why stop at poets? Craftsmen want fame after death too. When Phidias was not allowed to sign his name to the shield of Minerva, why did he work a likeness of his own face into it? And what about our philosophers? Don't they print their names on the very books they write about despising glory?

Section 35

Now, if the agreement of all people is the voice of nature, and everyone everywhere agrees that there is something that still concerns those who have departed this life, then we must think so too. And if we judge that the men whose souls stand out in talent or in virtue see the power of nature best — because their own nature is best — then, since it is precisely the best men who most devote themselves to posterity, it is likely that there is something they will still have a feeling for after death.

Section 36

But just as we believe that the gods exist because nature tells us so, while what they are like we must learn by reason, in the same way we hold that souls survive on the agreement of all nations, while where they dwell and what they are like has to be learned by reason. It was ignorance on exactly that point that invented the underworld and all those terrors which you seemed, not without cause, to despise. Bodies fall to the earth and are covered with soil — humus, which is why we say the dead are "inhumed" — and so people came to believe that the dead spend the rest of their existence underground. Out of that opinion grew enormous errors, and the poets made them bigger.

Section 37

A packed theater, full of women and boys, shudders when it hears a verse as grand as this:

  Here I am — come up from Acheron at last,
  by a road deep and steep, through caverns built
  of rough rock hanging huge and jagged overhead,
  where the thick, stiff gloom of the dead stands still.

And the error had such force — though by now, I think, it has been cleared away — that even people who knew the bodies had been cremated still imagined things happening in the underworld that could neither happen nor be conceived without a body. They simply could not wrap their minds around souls living on their own, so they went looking for some shape and figure for them. That is where Homer's whole Nekyia comes from, his roll call of the summoned dead; that is where my friend Appius got the seances he used to conduct; that is why, in my own neighborhood, we have Lake Avernus, where

  souls are called up, wrapped in dark shadow,
  images of the dead, out of Acheron's deep gate,
  drawn by blood that deceives them.

Yet they want these images to speak — which is impossible without a tongue, without a palate, without the strength and structure of throat and ribs and lungs. They could see nothing with the mind; they referred everything to the eyes.

Section 38

It takes a great mind to call the soul away from the senses and pull thought free of habit. So I believe, for my part, that there were others across all those centuries; but as far as written record goes, Pherecydes of Syros was the first to say that the souls of men are everlasting — an ancient authority, certainly: he lived while a king of my own family name, Tullius, sat on the throne. His pupil Pythagoras did the most to strengthen this opinion. He came to Italy while Tarquinius Superbus was king, and he held all of Great Greece — the Greek south of Italy — with the prestige of his teaching and with his personal authority; and for many centuries afterward the name of the Pythagoreans stayed so alive that no one else even seemed educated. But back to the ancients. They rarely gave any reason for their position, except where something could be worked out with numbers and diagrams.

Section 39

Plato, they say, came to Italy to get to know the Pythagoreans and learned everything they taught, and on the eternity of souls he was the first who not only agreed with Pythagoras but actually supplied a reason for it. Which — unless you have something to say — let us skip, and let us drop this whole hope of immortality.

"What?" he broke in. "You lead me up to the very peak of expectation and then abandon me there? By Hercules, I would rather be wrong with Plato — I know how highly you rate him, and I admire him from your own mouth — than be right with those others."

Section 40

Well said, I answered — I could happily be wrong in that same man's company myself. Do we really doubt, then, the way we doubt most things? Though here, surely, there is hardly any room for doubt, for the mathematicians convince us of it: the earth sits at the middle of the universe, and compared with the sweep of the whole heaven it amounts to a mere point — the kentron, they call it, the center-spot. And the nature of the four bodies from which all things are generated is this: they have divided their tendencies among themselves, as if by lot. Earthy and moist bodies are carried by their own lean and their own weight at right angles down into the earth and the sea; the remaining two parts, one of fire and one of air, behave the other way around — just as the first two are carried by heaviness and weight into the middle place of the world, these two fly straight up into the region of heaven, whether because their nature reaches for the heights or because lighter things are naturally pushed away by heavier ones. Since all that is established, it should be obvious that souls, once they have left the body, are carried upward — whether they are made of air, which is to say of breath, or of fire.

Section 41

But if the soul is instead some kind of number — a claim more subtle than it is clear — or that famous fifth nature, which has gone without a name rather than without being understood, then it is purer and more untouched still, and carries itself farthest of all from the earth. The soul, then, is one of these things — otherwise so lively a mind would be lying sunk in the heart or the brain, or in Empedocles' blood. As for Dicaearchus, and with him Aristoxenus, his contemporary and fellow student — learned men, no question — let us pass them by. One of them seems never to have felt so much as an ache of grief, since he cannot perceive that he has a soul at all; the other is so delighted with his own music that he keeps trying to carry it over into these questions. Harmonia we can understand from the intervals between sounds, whose varied combinations produce many harmonies; but what harmony the arrangement of limbs and the figure of a body with no soul in it could produce, I do not see. He would do better, learned as he truly is, to leave all this to his teacher Aristotle and stick to teaching music. There is good advice in that Greek proverb: let each man practice the craft he knows.

Section 42

And that random collision of smooth, round little bodies — let us throw it out root and branch, even though Democritus insists his atoms are warmed and breathing, which is to say, alive. Now this soul of ours, if it belongs to one of the four kinds out of which everything is said to be made, consists of fiery air — that seems to be the view Panaetius likes best — and it must make for the heights. For these two elements, fire and air, have no downward pull in them; they always seek what is above. So if souls are scattered, that happens far from the earth; and if they last and keep their form, then all the more certainly they must be carried to heaven, breaking apart and dividing this thick, congealed air that lies nearest the earth. For the soul is warmer — say rather hotter — than the air I just called thick and congealed. You can tell from this: our bodies, built out of the earthy class of elements, are kept warm by the heat of the soul.

Section 43

Add this: the soul escapes and breaks through that air I keep mentioning all the more easily because nothing is faster than the soul. No speed can compete with the speed of the soul. So if it stays uncorrupted and true to itself, it must fly in such a way as to pierce and divide this whole lower sky, where the clouds and rains and winds are massed, damp and murky with the earth's exhalations. When the soul has climbed past that region and met and recognized a nature like its own, it settles among fires blended of thin air and the sun's tempered warmth, and stops rising. For once it has found a lightness and heat like its own, it hangs as if balanced between equal weights and moves in no direction at all; and that, at last, is its natural home — when it has broken through to what is like itself. There, lacking nothing, it will be fed and sustained by the same things that sustain and feed the stars.

Section 44

And since the torches of the body inflame us toward nearly every desire — and burn all the hotter because we envy the people who already have what we are longing for — we will truly be happy when we have left our bodies behind and with them all desire and all rivalry. What we do now, in our moments free of care — wanting to gaze at something, to go and see it — we will do then with far more freedom, giving our whole selves over to contemplating and examining things. For nature has planted in our minds an insatiable desire to see the truth; and the very borders of those regions we will have reached, by making the heavens easier to know, will only feed the desire to know them.

Section 45

It was this beauty, seen even from the earth, that kindled that "ancestral and inherited" philosophy, as Theophrastus calls it, set alight by the desire for knowledge. And those will enjoy it most who longed to see clearly with the mind's eye even while they lived on this earth, wrapped in fog. If people now feel they have achieved something because they have seen the mouth of the Pontus, and those narrows threaded by the ship that was named Argo

  because in her the chosen men of Argos
  sailed out to fetch the ram's golden fleece,

or because they have seen the straits of the Ocean,

  where the greedy wave divides
  Europe from Libya,

then what kind of spectacle do we think it will be when we can look out over the whole earth — its position, its shape, its outline; the regions where people can live, and the regions that lie empty of all cultivation, locked in extremes of cold or heat?

Section 46

Even now, after all, it is not with our eyes that we see what we see. There is no sensation in the body itself; as not only the natural scientists teach but the doctors too, who have seen the body opened and laid bare, there are passages — channels, as it were — bored from the seat of the soul to the eyes, the ears, the nose. That is why we often neither see nor hear, though our eyes and ears are open and perfectly sound, when we are blocked by deep thought or by the force of some illness. Which makes it easy to grasp that it is the soul that sees and hears, not those organs, which are only, so to speak, the soul's windows — and through them the mind can perceive nothing unless it is present and paying attention. And what about this: with one and the same mind we take in the most unlike things — color, taste, heat, smell, sound. The soul could never recognize all five from its five messengers unless everything were referred to it, and it alone were judge of everything. And these things will surely be seen far more purely and clearly when the soul has arrived, free at last, where nature is carrying it.

Section 47

For as things stand, although nature has built those openings that lead from the body to the soul with the most cunning craftsmanship, still they are somehow fenced about with earthy, clotted matter. But when there is nothing left but soul, nothing standing in the way will keep it from perceiving each thing exactly as it is. I could go on as long as you like, if the subject demanded it, about how many, how varied, and how vast the spectacles are that the soul will enjoy in the regions of heaven.

Section 48

When I think about those spectacles, I often find myself marveling at the arrogance of certain philosophers who are so awestruck by the knowledge of nature that they pour out exultant thanks to its discoverer and founder, and worship him as a god. He has freed them, they say, from the cruelest of masters: everlasting terror, fear by night and by day. What terror? What fear? Is there one doddering old grandmother left who is afraid of the things that you, apparently, would tremble at if you had never studied physics —

  the high halls of Acheron, the realm of Orcus,
  death's pale country, places clouded in darkness?

Isn't a philosopher ashamed to boast that he does not fear these things and has discovered they are false? From which you can gather how sharp these men are by nature: left untaught, they would have believed it all.

Section 49

And what a splendid prize their learning has won them — the lesson that when the day of death arrives, they will perish altogether. Suppose that is true; I am not contesting anything. What is there in it to celebrate, or to boast about? Yet for my part, nothing occurs to me to show why the view of Pythagoras and Plato should not be the true one. Even if Plato offered no reason at all — see how much weight I give the man — his sheer authority would break my resistance. But in fact he has brought so many reasons that he plainly wanted to convince everyone else, and most certainly convinced himself.

Section 50

But a great many pull the other way, and sentence souls to death as if they were criminals convicted on a capital charge. And the eternity of souls seems unbelievable to them for exactly one reason: they cannot picture, cannot grasp in thought, what a soul is like without the body. As if they understood what it is like inside the body — its shape, its size, its address! If everything that is now hidden in a living man lay open to view, do they think the soul would come into sight? Or is its texture so fine that it would slip past the eye?

Section 51

Let the people who say they cannot conceive of a soul without a body chew on that — and then let them see how well they conceive of it inside the body. For my part, when I contemplate the nature of the soul, it is a far harder and darker question what the soul is like inside the body — a house not its own, so to speak — than what it will be like when it has left, and come out into the free heaven as into its true home. For unless the rule is that we can never understand anything we have never seen, we certainly can embrace in thought both God himself and the divine soul set free from the body. Dicaearchus and Aristoxenus, because it was hard to understand what the soul is and what it is like, declared there was no soul at all.

Section 52

Of all feats, this is surely the greatest: for the soul to see the soul itself. And that, no doubt, is the whole force of Apollo's precept telling each of us to know himself. He is not, I think, ordering us to get acquainted with our limbs, our height, our figure. We are not bodies; and when I say these things to you, I am not talking to your body. So when the god says "Know yourself," he is saying this: "Know your soul." The body is only a kind of vessel, a container for the soul; whatever your soul does is done by you. If knowing the soul were not something divine, this precept — the work of some keener mind — would never have been credited to a god.

Section 53

But if the soul is not to know what the soul is like, then tell me, please: will it not know even that it exists? Not even that it moves? Out of that question grew Plato's famous argument, which Socrates lays out in the Phaedrus and which I set into the sixth book of my Republic:

"What always moves is eternal. But whatever brings motion to something else, while it is itself set moving from outside, must come to the end of its life when its motion comes to an end. Only what moves itself, then — because it never abandons itself — never stops moving. More than that: for everything else that moves, this is the fountain, this is the first principle of motion.

Section 54

"And a first principle has no origin. Everything arises out of the first principle, but the principle itself can be born from nothing else — for it would be no first principle if it were begotten from somewhere else. And since it never arises, it never perishes either. A first principle once extinguished will never be reborn from anything else and will never create anything else out of itself, since everything must arise from a first principle. So it follows that motion begins from what moves itself; and that can neither be born nor die — or else the whole heaven must fall in and all nature grind to a halt, never again to find any force to strike it into its first motion. Since it is plain, then, that what moves itself is eternal, who is there who would deny that this is the nature souls were given? Everything that is driven by an outside push is soulless; but what has a soul is stirred by a motion that is inward and its own — for this is the soul's peculiar nature and power. And if the soul is the one thing among all things that moves itself, then certainly it was never born, and it is eternal."

Section 55

Let all the plebeian philosophers band together — that seems to me the right name for everyone who breaks away from Plato and Socrates and that family — and not only will they never explain anything so elegantly, they will not even understand how subtly this conclusion has been drawn. The soul, then, senses that it moves; and in the moment it senses that, it senses this too: that it moves by its own power, not another's, and that it can never be abandoned by itself. From which its eternity follows — unless you have something to say against it.

"For my part," he said, "I would be glad if not even a stray thought against it crossed my mind. That is how much I favor the position."

Section 56

Well then, I went on: do you rate the next arguments any lighter — the ones declaring that there is something divine in the souls of men? If I could see how such things could be born, I could also see how they might perish. Blood, bile, phlegm, bones, sinews, veins — the whole shape of the limbs and of the body entire — I think I can say what they were compounded from and how they were made. Even the soul itself: if there were nothing in it beyond the fact that we live by it, I would suppose a man's life was sustained by nature exactly as a vine's is, or a tree's — we say those things "live" too. And if the human soul had nothing in it but pursuing and avoiding, that much it would share with the beasts.

Section 57

But first of all it has memory — and a memory without limit, of things beyond counting. Plato holds that memory is actually recollection of an earlier life. In the book called the Meno, Socrates puts to a small boy some questions in geometry, about measuring a square. The boy answers them the way a boy would; and yet the questions are so easy that, answering one step at a time, he arrives exactly where he would have arrived if he had studied geometry. Socrates' conclusion: learning is nothing other than remembering. And he works this theme out far more carefully in the conversation he held on the very day he left this life. There he teaches that anyone at all, however untrained he appears, shows by answering a good questioner well that he is not learning those things in that moment but recognizing them again by memory. And it could not possibly happen, he argues, that from childhood we carry notions of so many great matters planted and as it were sealed into our souls — the Greeks call them ennoiai, inborn concepts — unless the soul, before it ever entered the body, had been alive and flourishing in the knowledge of things.

Section 58

And since — as Plato argues everywhere — nothing truly is if it arises and perishes, and only that truly is which stays forever such as it is (idea is his name for it; ours is "form"), the soul, shut up in the body, could not have discovered these things while it was there. It knew them already, and brought the knowledge in with it. And with that, our amazement at the sheer range of its knowledge disappears. The soul does not see its ideas clearly right away, when it has suddenly moved into so strange and so disordered a dwelling; but once it has collected and refreshed itself, then it recognizes them by remembering. So learning is nothing but recollection.

Section 59

But I find myself marveling at memory in an even deeper way. What exactly is the thing by which we remember? What power does it have? Where does its nature come from? I am not asking about the memory Simonides is said to have possessed, or Theodectes, or Cineas, the ambassador Pyrrhus sent to our senate, or in recent times Charmadas, or Metrodorus of Scepsis, who died only lately, or our own Hortensius. I am talking about ordinary human memory — and above all the memory of people immersed in some higher study or art. How large their minds must be is hard even to estimate: they remember so much.

Section 60

Where is this speech heading? Toward understanding, I think, what that power is and where it comes from. It certainly does not belong to the heart, or to the blood, or the brain, or the atoms. Whether it is breath or fire, I do not know — and unlike those philosophers, I am not ashamed to admit ignorance where I am ignorant. But this much, if I could swear to anything else so obscure, I would swear: whether the soul is breath or fire, it is divine. Think about it, I beg you. Does it seem possible to you that so vast a power as memory was sown from this earth, or curdled out of this foggy, gloomy atmosphere? If you cannot see what it is, you can still see what kind of thing it is; and if not even that, then you can certainly see how great it is. What follows, then?

Section 61

Shall we suppose there is some holding-space in the soul — a kind of vessel into which everything we remember gets poured? That is absurd. What bottom could such a soul have? What shape? What capacity could possibly be that large? Or shall we suppose the soul takes impressions like wax, and that memory is the tracks of things stamped into the mind? What kind of tracks could words leave — what tracks could things themselves leave — and what surface could be immense enough to take the print of so much?

Section 62

And again: what in the world is that power that hunts down what is hidden — the one we call invention and devising? Does it strike you as something curdled together out of this earthly, mortal, perishable nature? Or take the man who first put names to all things — the summit of wisdom, Pythagoras thought. Or the one who gathered scattered human beings together and called them into the fellowship of a shared life; or the one who penned the sounds of the voice, which had seemed infinite, inside a few written letters; or the one who first charted the courses of the wandering stars — their advances, their appointed laws. All great men. And greater still the ones before them, who discovered crops, clothing, houses, an ordered way of living, defenses against wild beasts — the men who tamed and civilized us, so that we flowed on from the necessary crafts to the finer ones. Great delight was won for the ears when the variety and nature of sounds was discovered and tuned; we lifted our eyes to the stars, both the ones fixed in their places and the ones called wanderers — wandering in name, not in fact. And the man whose soul traced their revolutions and all their motions taught us this: that his soul was like the soul of the one who had built those stars in the sky.

Section 63

For when Archimedes bound the motions of the moon, the sun, and the five wandering stars into a sphere, he accomplished the very thing that Plato's god accomplishes in the Timaeus when he builds the world: one turning governed motions utterly unlike in slowness and in speed. And if that cannot happen in this universe of ours without a god, then not even in his sphere could Archimedes have imitated those same motions without divine genius.

Section 64

For my part, I do not think even the more familiar, more brightly lit arts are empty of divine power. I cannot believe a poet pours out grave, full song without some heavenly quickening of the mind, or that eloquence flows rich in ringing words and fertile thoughts without some force greater than ourselves. And philosophy, the mother of all the arts — what is she but, as Plato says, a gift of the gods, or, as I put it, their invention? She was the one who first taught us the worship of the gods, then the justice that binds mankind, grounded in the fellowship of the human race, and then modesty and greatness of spirit. She was the one who drove the fog from the soul, as from the eyes, so that we could see everything above and below — the first things, the last things, and everything between.
