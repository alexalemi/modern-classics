Book IV: On the Other Disorders of the Mind

(Part 1 of 3)

Section 1

There are many places, my dear Brutus, where I find myself marveling at the talent and virtue of our countrymen, but nowhere more than in those studies which they took up very late, and carried into this city from Greece. From the city's first beginnings, the institutions of the kings — and in part the laws — had established the auspices, the religious rites, the assemblies, the right of appeal, the council of the fathers, the ranking of cavalry and infantry, the whole business of war: all of it set up as if by a divine hand. And then, once the republic was freed from the rule of kings, the advance our people made toward every kind of excellence was astonishing, its pace beyond belief. This is not the place to talk about the customs and institutions of our ancestors, or the discipline and fine balance of the state; I have treated all that carefully enough elsewhere, above all in the six books I wrote On the Republic.

Section 2

But here, thinking about the pursuit of learning, I keep running into reasons to believe that these studies too were not merely fetched from abroad and taken up — they were preserved and cultivated. For our ancestors had, almost within eyeshot, Pythagoras, a man of towering wisdom and nobility, who was in Italy in the very years when Lucius Brutus — the illustrious founder of your own noble line — set his country free. And as the teaching of Pythagoras flowed far and wide, it seems to me it seeped into this city as well. Conjecture makes that likely, and certain surviving traces point the same way. For who could seriously believe that while Greek Italy — the region called Great Greece — was flourishing with its greatest and most powerful cities, and while in those cities the name first of Pythagoras himself and then of the Pythagoreans stood so tall, our people kept their ears closed to those most learned voices?

Section 3

In fact, I suspect it was sheer admiration for the Pythagoreans that led later generations to decide that King Numa must have been a Pythagorean himself. They knew the teaching and the way of life of Pythagoras; they had received from their ancestors the memory of that king's fairness and wisdom; but the ages and dates had blurred with the passing of time. So they concluded that a man who stood out for wisdom must have been a student of Pythagoras. So much for conjecture. As for actual traces of the Pythagoreans — I could collect plenty, but I will use only a few, since that is not today's business. The Pythagoreans, it is said, used to hand down certain teachings in verse, somewhat under a veil of secrecy, and used to bring their minds down from hard thinking to calm with singing and the lyre. And Cato, the weightiest of authorities, says in his Origins that our ancestors kept this custom at their banquets: the guests at table would take turns singing, to the flute, the praises and great deeds of famous men. From which it is clear that there were already songs set for the voice, and poems.

Section 4

The Twelve Tables say as much, showing that songs were being composed even then — for the law made it an offense to compose one to another man's harm. And here is another proof that those were not uneducated times: the lyre played at the sacred couches of the gods and at the banquets of the magistrates, a practice belonging to the very school I am speaking of. To my ear, even the poem of Appius Caecus, which Panaetius praises so warmly in a letter of his to Quintus Tubero, has a Pythagorean sound. Many other things in our institutions are drawn from the Pythagoreans too; I pass over them, so that we won't seem to have learned from others the very things we are credited with inventing.

Section 5

But to bring the discussion back to its point: in how short a time, and what a crowd of poets — and what poets! — and what orators, sprang up among us! Plainly our people could master anything, the moment they decided they wanted to. Of the other studies I will speak elsewhere if there is need, as I often have already. The pursuit of wisdom is genuinely old among us; even so, before the age of Laelius and Scipio I can find no one I am able to name by name. When those two were young, I find Diogenes the Stoic and Carneades the Academic being sent by the Athenians as ambassadors to our senate — men who had never touched public affairs, one of them from Cyrene, the other from Babylon; they would surely never have been pulled out of their lecture halls and chosen for that mission unless some of our leading men, in those days, had cared about learning. Those men put other things down in writing — some the civil law, some their own speeches, some the records of our ancestors — but this grandest art of all, the discipline of living well, they pursued more in how they lived than in what they wrote.

Section 6

And so of that true and elegant philosophy — the one that started from Socrates and lives on still among the Peripatetics, and among the Stoics too, who say the same things in a different way, while the Academics referee their disputes — there are almost no Latin records, or very few. Maybe the subject was too vast; maybe men were too busy; maybe they judged that the untrained simply could not be won over by it. And while they kept their silence, up stood Gaius Amafinius and spoke. When his books came out, the crowd was stirred and flocked to that school above all others — perhaps because it was so easy to grasp, perhaps because pleasure's soft enticements beckoned, or perhaps simply because nothing better had been offered, so people held on to what there was.

Section 7

After Amafinius, many champions of the same system wrote a great deal, and between them they took over all of Italy. And the strongest evidence that their doctrine has no subtlety — that it is so easily memorized and wins the approval of the unlearned — is exactly what they take to be the pillar of their school. But let every man defend what he thinks; judgment is free. I will hold to my own practice: bound by the rules of no single school that I must obey in philosophy, I will always search out what is most probable in each question as it comes. I have done this often elsewhere, and recently I did it in earnest at my place in Tusculum. So, with the discussions of three days already laid out, this book wraps up the fourth day. When we had walked down to the lower promenade, as we had done on the days before, this is how it went.

Section 8

"Let anyone who wants," I said, "name the question he'd like argued."

"It doesn't seem to me that the wise man can be free of every disorder of the soul," he began.

"Free of grief, at least, he seemed to be after yesterday's discussion — unless you were only agreeing with us to save time."

"Not in the least. Your argument won me over completely."

"So you don't think grief can touch the wise man?"

"No — I really don't."

"Well then: if grief can't throw the wise man's soul into disorder, nothing can. What else would do it? Fear? But fear is about the very things, still at a distance, that cause grief when they are present; take away grief, and you have taken away fear. That leaves two disorders — exultant delight, and desire. If those can't reach the wise man, his mind will be at peace forever."

"That is exactly how I see it."

Section 9

"Which would you rather, then? Shall we make sail at once, or row for a while first, like a ship just pulling out of the harbor?"

"What do you mean by that? I don't follow."

"I mean this: when Chrysippus and the Stoics discuss the disorders of the soul, they spend the greater part of their effort carving them up and defining them; the part where they actually treat souls, and keep them from churning, is tiny. The Peripatetics, by contrast, bring plenty for calming the soul, but they skip the thorny work of division and definition. So I was asking whether I should spread the sails of eloquence right away, or first row us out a little with the oars of logic."

"The second — then the whole of what I'm asking will come out more complete, built from both."

Section 10

"That is the sounder way; and if anything turns out too obscure, you can ask me afterward."

"I will. Though even the obscure parts — you'll lay them out, as usual, more clearly than the Greeks do."

"I'll certainly try. But this needs a taut attention: let one piece slip away, and the whole thing may scatter."

So I began. What the Greeks call pathe — the passions — we prefer to name disorders of the soul rather than diseases. In explaining them I will follow first that old division of Pythagoras', and then Plato's. They split the soul into two parts, one sharing in reason, the other without it. In the part that shares in reason they place tranquility — that is, a calm and quiet steadiness. To the other part they assign the churning motions of anger and of desire, the opposites and enemies of reason.

Section 11

Let that stand as our spring. Still, in mapping these disorders let us use the definitions and divisions of the Stoics, for on this question they seem to me to work with the sharpest edge. Zeno's definition runs like this: a disorder — what he calls a pathos — is a movement of the soul turned away from right reason, against nature. Some put it more briefly: a disorder is an appetite grown too violent — "too violent" meaning one that has strayed too far from nature's steadiness. The kinds of disorder, they hold, spring from two imagined goods and two imagined evils, making four in all. From the goods come desire and delight — delight over good things present, desire toward good things to come. From the evils come fear and grief — fear of evils on their way, grief over evils here; for the same things whose approach we fear weigh us down with grief when they arrive.

Section 12

Delight and desire both turn on the opinion that something is good. Desire is lured and set alight and swept off toward whatever looks good; delight is carried away and exults when it has already seized something it craved. For by nature all of us chase what looks good and shy away from its opposite; the instant anything appears that seems good, nature herself pushes us to go and get it. When that reaching happens steadily and sensibly, the Stoics call it boulesis; let us call it will. Will, they say, is found only in the wise man, and they define it so: will is what wants a thing in accordance with reason. But the reaching that is whipped up too violently, in defiance of reason — that is desire, craving with the bridle off, and it is found in every fool.

Section 13

Likewise, when we are moved so as to come into some good, it happens in one of two ways. When the soul is moved calmly and steadily, in step with reason, that is called joy. But when it leaps up with an empty, spilled-over excitement, that is wild or excessive delight, which they define as an elation of the soul without reason. And since nature makes us reach for good things, nature likewise makes us swerve from evils. When that swerve is made with reason, let it be called caution — and let it be understood to exist only in the wise man. When it is made without reason, with a low, broken cowering of spirit, let it be named fear. Fear, then, is caution that has turned its back on reason.

Section 14

Present evil does not touch the wise man at all; grief over it belongs to fools, who are afflicted over evils they only imagine, and who let their souls sink and shrink in disobedience to reason. So the first definition is this: grief is a shrinking of the soul in defiance of reason. Four disorders, then, and three steadinesses — since grief alone has no steadiness set against it. But all the disorders, they insist, come about by judgment and by opinion. And so they define them more tightly still, to show not only how faulty the disorders are, but how completely they lie within our power. Grief is a fresh opinion of a present evil, in which it seems right for the soul to sink and shrink. Delight is a fresh opinion of a present good, in which being carried away seems right. Fear is an opinion of an impending evil that seems unbearable. Desire is an opinion of a good on its way — one it would pay to have here, present, right now.

Section 15

But the judgments and opinions I have named are not the whole of it. The disorders, they say, do not consist in those alone, but also in what the disorders produce: grief inflicts a kind of bite of pain; fear, a certain retreat and flight of the soul; delight, a spilling-over merriment; desire, an unbridled grasping. And the "opining" that I built into all the definitions above, they hold to be a weak assent.

Section 16

Under each single disorder, several species of the same family are ranged. Under grief: enviousness — I use the rarer word here for teaching's sake, since "envy" applies not only to the one who envies but also to the one who is envied — rivalry, jealousy, pity, anguish, mourning, sorrow, tribulation, pain, lamentation, worry, vexation, torment, despair, and whatever else belongs to the same brood. Under fear are ranged sluggishness, shame, terror, dread, panic, fainting, confusion, alarm. Under pleasure: malice, which delights in another's misfortune; enchantment; boastfulness; and the like. Under desire: anger, flaring temper, hatred, feuding, discord, neediness, longing, and everything else of that stamp.

Section 17

And they define these as follows. Enviousness, they say, is grief taken on at another man's good fortune — good fortune that does the envier no harm at all. (For if a man grieves at the success of someone who is hurting him, he is not rightly said to envy — Agamemnon does not envy Hector. It is the man whom another's advantages injure not one bit, and who aches anyway to see the other enjoy them, who truly envies.) "Rivalry" is used in two senses, one for praise and one for blame: the imitation of virtue is called rivalry — though we have no use for that sense here, for it belongs to praise — and rivalry is also the grief felt when someone else has gotten the thing you craved, and you go without. Jealousy — the thing I want understood by the Greek zelotypia — is grief that someone else, too, has gotten the very thing you craved for yourself.

Section 18

Pity is grief at the misery of another who suffers wrongfully (for no one is moved to pity by the punishment of a man who killed his father or betrayed his country). Anguish is grief that presses down. Mourning is grief at the bitter death of someone who was dear. Sorrow is grief with tears; tribulation, grief with hardship; pain, grief that tortures; lamentation, grief with wailing; worry, grief with brooding thought; vexation, grief that lingers; torment, grief that batters the body along with it; despair, grief without any expectation of better things.

Section 19

What is ranged under fear, they define this way. Sluggishness is fear of work to come. Terror is fear that strikes the body a blow — hence blushing follows shame, while pallor and trembling and chattering teeth follow terror. Dread is fear of an evil drawing near. Panic is fear that knocks the mind from its seat, as in that line of Ennius:

  Then panic drove all wisdom from my stricken breast.

Fainting is the fear that follows on panic like its companion; confusion, the fear that scatters one's thoughts; alarm, the fear that lasts.

Section 20

The species of pleasure they describe like this. Malice is pleasure in another's misfortune that brings no gain to yourself. Enchantment is pleasure that seduces the soul through sweetness of hearing; and as this pleasure of the ears works, so do the pleasures of the eyes, of touch, of smell, of taste — all of one family, pleasures melted down, as it were, to be poured over the soul. Boastfulness is pleasure that struts and gloats and carries itself too insolently.

Section 21

What is ranged under desire, they define this way. Anger is the desire to punish a man who seems to have done you an unjust injury. Flaring temper is anger at its birth, just now springing into being — what the Greeks call thymosis. Hatred is anger grown old. Feuding is anger watching for its moment of revenge. Discord is a bitterer anger, conceived in the innermost heart and core. Neediness is desire that cannot be filled. Longing is the desire to lay eyes on someone who is not yet here. And they draw one more distinction: desire attaches to the things said of a person or persons — what the logicians call kategoremata, "predicates" — such as having riches or winning office; neediness is for the things themselves, for the office, for the money.

Section 22

The fountain of all the disorders, they say, is the loss of self-control: a defection of the whole mind from right reason, so far turned from reason's prescription that the appetites of the soul can no longer be governed or reined in at all. Just as self-control calms the appetites, makes them obey right reason, and keeps safe the mind's considered judgments, so its enemy — self-control's collapse — inflames, churns, and whips up the whole state of the soul. From it are born griefs and fears and every other disorder.

Section 23

Just as diseases and sicknesses arise in the body when the blood is corrupted or phlegm or bile runs over, so a tangle of depraved opinions, warring among themselves, strips the soul of its health and racks it with disease. Out of the disorders come, first, the diseases — what the Stoics call nosemata — along with their opposites, which involve a faulty recoil, an aversion and disgust toward particular things; then the sicknesses, arrostemata in the Stoic vocabulary, with their contrary aversions likewise set against them. At this point the Stoics — Chrysippus above all — spend far too much labor matching the diseases of the soul, point for point, to the diseases of the body. Let us skip all that talk, which is hardly necessary, and handle what actually carries the argument.

Section 24

Understand this much, then: a disorder is opinion tossing itself about — restless, turbulent, always in motion. But when that fever and boiling of the soul has grown old, when it has settled, so to speak, into the veins and the marrow, then disease appears, and sickness, and the aversions that stand opposed to those diseases and sicknesses. What I am describing can be separated in thought, though in reality the things are tangled together; and they spring from desire and from delight. Suppose money is craved, and reason — a kind of Socratic medicine to heal the craving — is not brought in at once. The evil seeps into the veins and lodges deep in the gut; there stand disease and sickness, and once they have aged, they cannot be torn out. And that disease's name is greed.

Section 25

The other diseases arise the same way: the hunger for glory; woman-chasing, if I may coin a name for what the Greeks call philogynia; and all the rest of the diseases and sicknesses on the same pattern. Their opposites are thought to be born of fear — hatred of women, for instance, such as fills Atilius' play The Woman-Hater; or hatred of the whole human race, which we hear of in Timon, the one they call the Misanthrope — the man-hater; or the horror of guests. All these sicknesses of the soul are born from a certain fear of the things people flee and hate.

Section 26

Sickness of the soul they define as a vehement opining about a thing not worth pursuing, as though it were richly worth pursuing — an opining stuck fast and planted deep. What springs from aversion they define like this: a vehement opinion, stuck fast and planted deep, about a thing not worth fleeing, as though it had to be fled. And this opining is a judging that you know what you do not know. Under sickness fall such things as greed, ambition, woman-chasing, pig-headedness, gluttony, drunkenness, a weakness for delicacies, and whatever resembles them. Greed is a vehement opinion about money — that it is richly worth pursuing — stuck fast and planted deep; and the definitions of the rest are built on the same frame.

Section 27

The aversions are defined in this style: the horror of guests is a vehement opinion, stuck fast and planted deep, that a stranger is a thing to be fled; and in the same way are defined the hatred of women, like Hippolytus', and the hatred of humankind, like Timon's. Now, to come to the analogy with bodily health — I will use the comparison from time to time, though more sparingly than the Stoics do. Some men lean toward certain diseases more than others; that is why we call some people cold-catchers and others colicky, not because they are sick now but because they often are. In the same way, some lean toward fear, others toward some other disorder. So in some people we speak of anxiety — hence "the anxious" — and in others of an angry streak. An angry streak differs from anger: it is one thing to be quick-tempered, another to be angry — just as anxiety differs from anguish, for not everyone who sometimes feels anguish is anxious, nor are the anxious always in anguish; and just as being drunk differs from being a drunkard, and being a ladies' man differs from being in love. This leaning of different people toward different diseases stretches wide, for it touches every one of the disorders.

Section 28

It shows in many vices too, though the thing has no name of its own. So people are called envious, malicious, timid, or pitying because they lean toward those disorders, not because they are always swept away by them. This leaning, each toward its own kind, may be called "sickness" on the analogy of the body — so long as we understand by it a leaning toward getting sick. In good things — since some people are more apt for one good than another — let it be called aptness; in bad things, proneness, to signal a slide toward falling; where a thing is neither good nor bad, let it keep the former name. Now, just as the body has its disease, its sickness, and its defect, so has the soul. Disease they call a corruption of the whole body; sickness, disease joined with weakness; defect, when the parts of the body are at odds with one another — from which come misshapen limbs, twisting, deformity.

Section 29

So the first two, disease and sickness, arise when the health of the whole body has been shaken and churned up; a defect shows by itself even while health is intact. But in the soul we can pry disease apart from sickness only in thought. Viciousness, on the other hand — vice as a settled state — is a condition or disposition inconsistent through a whole life, at odds with its own self. So it happens that one corruption of opinions produces disease and sickness, while the other produces inconstancy and self-contradiction. For not every vice involves parts at war: the condition of those who stand not far from wisdom is indeed at odds with itself, so long as it remains unwise, but it is not twisted, not deformed. Diseases and sicknesses are parts of viciousness; but whether the disorders are parts of it too — that is a question.
