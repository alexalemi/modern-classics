Book III: On Grief of Mind

(Part 3 of 3)

Section 58

In the same way, by rehearsing examples, the consolers bring forward the losses of children too, and the mourning of those who take it hardest is softened by the examples of others. What the rest of mankind has endured makes what has happened to us seem far smaller than it had been reckoned; and so, little by little, as people think it over, it comes into view how much opinion had lied to them. That is what the famous Telamon declares — "When I fathered him, I knew..." — and Theseus — "I used to rehearse my future miseries in advance" — and Anaxagoras — "I always knew my child was mortal." All these men, from long reflection on human affairs, had come to understand that such things are not remotely as dreadful as the crowd's opinion makes them. And it seems to me that much the same thing happens to those who think troubles through in advance as happens to those whom time heals — except that a kind of reasoning cures the first, while nature herself cures the second, once they have grasped the point that holds the whole matter together: that the evil which opinion had declared the greatest of all is by no means great enough to overturn a happy life.

Section 59

What follows, then, is that the blow lands harder for being unforeseen — not, as those others suppose, that when the same misfortune befalls two men, only the one it caught by surprise feels grief. In fact, some mourners are said to have taken their sorrow even harder for hearing about the common condition of mankind — that we are born under this law, that no one can stay exempt from evil forever. That is why Carneades, as I see our own Antiochus writes, used to criticize Chrysippus for praising these lines of Euripides:

  No mortal lives whom pain and sickness never touch.
  Many must bury their children and beget
  others in their place; death is the end marked out for all.
  These things bring anguish to the human race in vain:
  earth must be given back to earth, and then, for all,
  life must be harvested like grain. So Necessity commands.

Section 60

Carneades denied that talk of this kind did anything at all to lighten grief. The very fact, he said, that we have fallen under so cruel a necessity is itself a thing to grieve over; and a speech built on recounting other people's evils is suited to consoling the spiteful, not the sorrowing. To me it seems far otherwise. The necessity of bearing the human condition forbids us, so to speak, to pick a fight with god, and reminds us that we are human — a thought that goes a long way toward lightening sorrow. And the parade of examples is brought in not to entertain the malicious, but so that the man in mourning may conclude that he can bear what he sees so many others have borne with moderation and calm.

Section 61

For men who are collapsing, who cannot hold themselves together under the sheer weight of grief, must be propped up by every means there is. That is why Chrysippus thinks grief itself got its Greek name, lype, as if it were a dissolving — a coming apart — of the whole man. And the whole of it can be torn out by the roots once the cause of grief has been laid open, as I said at the start; for the cause is nothing but opinion — the judgment that a great evil is present and pressing down. So it is that bodily pain, whose bite is the sharpest of all, is endured when there is hope of good ahead; and a life lived honorably and brilliantly brings so much consolation that men who have lived that way are either never touched by grief at all, or feel only the lightest prick of the soul's pain. But when to this opinion of great evil there is added the further opinion that grieving is proper — that it is right, that it is part of one's duty, to take what has happened hard — then, and only then, you get grief's full, crushing disorder.

Section 62

From that opinion come all the varied and detestable forms of mourning: the unwashed squalor, the womanish clawing of cheeks, the blows to breast and thighs and head. That is where you get Homer's Agamemnon — and Accius's too — "tearing, in his grief, again and again at his unshorn hair." Which drew that joke from Bion the philosopher: the foolish king tore out his hair in mourning as if baldness would relieve sorrow.

Section 63

But men do all these things believing they are supposed to be done. So Aeschines attacks Demosthenes for offering sacrifices on the seventh day after his daughter's death. And with what rhetoric, what abundance! What maxims he piles up, what words he hurls! — you learn from it that a rhetorician may get away with anything. No one would approve a word of it if we did not have this planted in our minds: that every good man ought to mourn the death of his own people as bitterly as he can. This is why some men, in the soul's pains, hunt out solitude — as Homer says of Bellerophon:

  who wandered, wretched and grieving, over the Aleian plains,
  eating his own heart, shunning the tracks of men —

and why Niobe is imagined as turned to stone, I suppose for her everlasting silence in grief, while Hecuba, they think, was pictured as changed into a dog because of a certain bitterness and fury of soul. And there are others who, in their grief, often love to talk to solitude itself, like the nurse in Ennius:

  A longing has seized me now, poor wretch, to cry out
  to heaven and to earth the miseries of Medea.

Section 64

All this men do in their pain because they believe it right, true, and owed. And that it is done from a kind of verdict about duty shows most clearly in this: if mourners who mean to stay in mourning happen to do something a little more human, or say something a little more cheerful, they haul themselves straight back to misery and accuse themselves of a fault for letting the grieving lapse. Mothers and teachers actually punish children — not with words only, but with beatings — if, while the household is in mourning, they do or say anything too lighthearted; they force them to cry. And what of this: when the mourning finally slackens, and a man realizes that his grieving accomplished nothing — doesn't that fact alone prove the whole thing was voluntary?

Section 65

What about the man in Terentius who "punishes himself" — the Self-Tormentor, the Heauton Timorumenos of the play's title?

  I have resolved, Chremes, that so long as I stay miserable
  I do my son less wrong.

This man resolves to be miserable. And does anyone resolve on anything against his will? "Indeed, I would count myself deserving of any evil —": he counts himself deserving of evil unless he is miserable. You see, then, that the evil lies in opinion, not in nature. And what of the cases where the facts themselves forbid mourning? In Homer, the daily slaughter and the deaths of so many bring a kind of settling of sorrow. There it is said:

  For far too many fall, and every single day,
  before our eyes; no one could ever be free of mourning.
  The fairer course is to commit the slain to their graves
  with a firm mind, and close our grief with one day's tears.

Section 66

So it is in our power to throw off pain when we choose, to suit the occasion. And is there any occasion — given that the thing really is in our power — that we should not seize for laying down the burden of grief? It was well known that the men who watched Gnaeus Pompeius fall under his wounds — fearing for their own lives at that most bitter and pitiable sight, because they saw themselves ringed by the enemy's fleet — did nothing at that moment except urge on the rowers and make their escape; only after they reached Tyre did they begin to grieve and lament. Fear, then, was able to push grief away from them — and true reason and wisdom will not be able to? What could do more to make a man lay down his sorrow than realizing that it gains nothing and was taken up in vain? But if it can be laid down, it need never have been taken up. It must be admitted, then, that grief is taken up by choice and by judgment.

Section 67

The point is confirmed by the patience of those who have already been through a great deal: they bear whatever happens more easily, and consider themselves hardened against fortune — like the man in Euripides:

  If this dark day had been the first to dawn on me,
  if I had not sailed so long on such a sea of troubles,
  there would be reason for anguish — as a colt panics
  at the bridle's sudden, unfamiliar touch;
  but misery has broken me in, and I have gone numb.

Since, then, sheer weariness of miseries makes griefs gentler, we are forced to conclude that the thing itself is not the cause and source of the sorrow.

Section 68

Consider the greatest philosophers — the ones who have not yet attained wisdom. Don't they understand that they stand in the greatest evil? For they are unwise, and there is no evil greater than unwisdom. Yet they do not go into mourning. Why not? Because to that class of evils no one attaches the opinion that it is right and fair, and a matter of duty, to be distressed that you are not wise — whereas we do attach that opinion to the grief that involves mourning, which is the greatest grief of all.

Section 69

So Aristotle, taking the older philosophers to task for supposing that philosophy had been brought to perfection by their own talents, says they must have been either very foolish or very vain; but that he himself can see that, since so great an advance had been made in a few years, philosophy would be entirely finished in a little while longer. And Theophrastus, dying, is said to have accused nature of giving stags and crows a long life, which meant nothing to them, and human beings, to whom it mattered most, so short a one. If men's span could have been longer, he said, every art would have been perfected and human life furnished with every kind of learning; he complained, in other words, that he was being snuffed out just as he had begun to see these things. And of the rest of the philosophers — doesn't every one of the best and most serious admit that he is ignorant of many things, and that many things remain for him to learn, and learn again?

Section 70

And yet, though they understand that they are stuck fast in the very middle of folly — and nothing is worse than that — they are not crushed by grief, because no opinion of obligatory pain gets mixed in. And what of those who hold that mourning is simply not for men? Such as Quintus Maximus, who buried a son who had been consul; Lucius Paulus, who lost two sons within a few days; Marcus Cato, whose son died as praetor-elect; and the others I gathered in my Consolation.

Section 71

What calmed these men, except their conviction that mourning and sorrow have no place in a man? So the very thing that others surrender to, thinking it right, these men drove off, thinking it disgraceful — from which we see that grief lies not in nature but in opinion. Against all this the other side argues: Who is so insane as to grieve of his own free will? Pain comes from nature, they say — and your own Crantor, they remind us, holds that we must yield to it; it presses, it bears down, it cannot be resisted. So Oileus in Sophocles, who had once consoled Telamon over the death of Ajax, was shattered when he heard of the death of his own son. Of his changed mind the poet says:

  No man was ever furnished with wisdom so great
  that he can ease another's hardship with his words
  and not — when fortune shifts and swings her attack
  against him — break under his own sudden blow,
  while all the fine precepts he spoke to others fall away.

When they argue this way, what they are trying to establish is that nature cannot be resisted at all. And yet the same men admit that people take on heavier griefs than nature compels. What madness is that, then? — the very question we put right back to them. But there are, in fact, several reasons for taking up pain.

Section 72

First comes that opinion of evil: once the evil is seen and believed in, grief follows of necessity. Second, people imagine that they gratify the dead by mourning them hard. And on top of that comes a certain womanish superstition: they suppose they will square accounts with the immortal gods more easily if, struck by the gods' blow, they confess themselves beaten down and laid flat. But most people never notice how these beliefs contradict one another. They praise a man who dies with an even mind — and then think a man who bears another's death with an even mind deserves blame. As if it could possibly happen, whatever lovers like to say to each other, that anyone loves another person more than himself.

Section 73

It is a noble thing — and, if you ask, right and true as well — to love those who ought to be dearest to us as much as we love ourselves. But more than ourselves? That simply cannot be done. Nor is it even to be wished for in friendship that my friend love me more than he loves himself, or that I love him more than I love myself; if that were the rule, life and every duty in it would be thrown into confusion. But of that another time. For now it is enough not to blame our misery on the loss of those we love — not to love them more than they themselves would wish, if they could feel anything, and certainly not more than we love ourselves. As for the claim that most people get no relief at all from consolations — with the addendum that the consolers themselves admit to being miserable the moment fortune wheels her attack onto them — both points come apart easily. These are faults not of nature but of the people themselves; and folly can be accused at any length you like. Those who refuse relief are inviting their own misery, and those who bear their own misfortunes otherwise than they prescribed for everyone else are no worse than most of mankind — no worse than the greedy who blame the greedy, or the glory-chasers who find fault with the vainglorious. For it is the special property of folly to see other people's vices and forget its own.

Section 74

But surely the point to press hardest is this: since it is agreed that grief is removed by the passage of time, the power at work is not the passing day itself but prolonged reflection. If the thing is the same and the man is the same, how can anything about the pain change, when nothing has changed either in what he grieves over or in the one who grieves? What cures the pain, then, is thinking long and steadily that there is no evil in the thing — not the mere length of time. At this point they offer me their "moderate griefs." But if such moderation is natural, what need is there of consolation at all? Nature herself will set the limit. And if it rests on opinion, then let the whole opinion be removed. I think enough has now been said to show that grief is an opinion that an evil is present — an opinion which includes the belief that taking up grief is one's duty.

Section 75

To this definition Zeno the Stoic rightly adds that the opinion of present evil must be fresh. But his school interprets that word like this: "fresh" does not mean only what happened a short while ago; as long as a certain force remains in that imagined evil — as long as it stays vigorous and keeps a kind of greenness — so long it counts as fresh. Take Artemisia, the wife of Mausolus, king of Caria, who built that famous tomb at Halicarnassus. As long as she lived, she lived in mourning, and in the end she wasted away, consumed by it. For her, that opinion was fresh every single day; and an opinion stops being called fresh only when it has dried out with age. These, then, are the duties of a consoler: to remove grief down to the roots; or to quiet it; or to draw off as much of it as possible; or to hold it down and keep it from spreading further; or to divert it onto other things.

Section 76

Some think the consoler has one duty only: to show that the thing is no evil at all — that is Cleanthes' position. Some argue that it is no great evil — the Peripatetics. Some lead the attention away from evils and toward goods — Epicurus. Some think it enough to show that nothing unexpected has happened — the Cyrenaics. And Chrysippus holds that the chief thing in consoling is to remove from the mourner the opinion that by grieving he is discharging a just and owed duty. There are also those who gather all these methods into one, since different people are moved in different ways — as I threw more or less all of them into a single consolation in my Consolation; for my soul was swollen, and I was trying every treatment on it there. But in diseases of the soul, no less than in those of the body, the right moment must be chosen — like Prometheus in Aeschylus, who, when told,

  And yet, Prometheus, I believe you hold to this:
  that reason has the power to heal an angry heart,

replied:

  Yes — if it applies its medicine at the ripe moment,
  and does not press a hand against the wound and make it worse.

Section 77

So in consolations the first medicine will be to show that the evil either does not exist or is very small; the second, to speak of the common condition of life and, more particularly, of whatever concerns the mourner's own case; the third, to show that it is the height of folly to wear yourself out with grief that accomplishes nothing, once you understand that nothing can be gained. As for Cleanthes — he is consoling the wise man, who needs no consoling. If you convince a mourner that nothing is evil except what is base, you have removed not his grief but his folly — and it is the wrong hour for a lesson. Besides, Cleanthes seems to me not to have seen this clearly enough: that grief can sometimes arise from the very thing that Cleanthes himself admits is the greatest evil. What shall we say of the time when Socrates, as the story has come down to us, convinced Alcibiades that he amounted to nothing as a man — that between Alcibiades, born to the highest rank, and any porter there was no difference at all? Alcibiades went to pieces; in tears, he begged Socrates to give him virtue and drive the baseness out of him. What shall we say to that, Cleanthes? That there was no evil in the thing that was afflicting Alcibiades with such grief?

Section 78

And what about those remarks of Lycon's? Trying to thin grief down, he says it is stirred up by small things — by setbacks of fortune and of the body, not by evils of the soul. What then? Wasn't the thing Alcibiades grieved over made precisely of the soul's evils and vices? As for Epicurus's brand of consolation, enough was said earlier.

Section 79

Nor is that other consolation the most solid — though it is in common use and often does some good: "You are not the only one." It does good, as I say, but not always and not for everyone; there are people who spit it back. What matters is how it is applied: you must dwell on how men who bore such things wisely bore them, not on what blow each sufferer happens to have taken. Chrysippus's method is the soundest as regards truth, but hard to apply in the hour of grief; it is a large undertaking to prove to a mourner that he mourns by his own judgment, and because he thinks he ought to. So then — just as in court we do not plead every case on the same issue (that is our name for the classes of dispute), but fit the pleading to the occasion, to the nature of the controversy, and to the person — so in easing grief you must consider what treatment each patient can accept.

Section 80

But somehow or other the talk has wandered from the question you set. You had asked about the wise man — a man to whom nothing can look like an evil if it is free of baseness, or at most like an evil so small that wisdom buries it until it is scarcely visible; a man who adds nothing to grief by way of opinion, and does not judge it right to torture himself to the limit and let mourning grind him down — than which nothing could be more depraved. Still, our reasoning has taught us, I think — even though the question whether anything is evil except what can also be called base was not, strictly, the question of this hour — that whatever evil there is in grief comes not from nature, but is contracted by a voluntary judgment and an error of opinion.

Section 81

And the kind of grief we have treated is the greatest kind of all, so that once it is removed, we should hardly think it necessary to hunt down remedies for the rest. There are stock things that are said about poverty, and stock things about a life without office and without glory; there are set lectures on exile, on the ruin of one's country, on slavery, on infirmity, on blindness — on every stroke that carries the name of calamity. The Greeks split these up into separate lectures and separate books; they are looking for work to do — though the discussions, I admit, are full of delight.

Section 82

And yet, just as doctors, in curing the whole body, treat even the smallest part that hurts, so philosophy, once it has removed grief as a whole, removes as well whatever particular trouble may show itself from any quarter — if poverty bites, if disgrace stings, if exile spreads its darkness, or if any of the things I just mentioned appears. Each of them has its own proper consolation, and you shall hear them whenever you wish. But we must come back to the same fountain: grief stands far from the wise man because it is empty, because it is taken up for nothing, because it springs not from nature but from judgment — from opinion, from a kind of standing invitation to grieve, once we have decreed that grieving is the thing to do.

Section 83

Subtract that — the part that is entirely voluntary — and grief, the mourning kind, will be gone; yet a sting will remain, a certain small tightening of the soul. Let them call that natural, if they like, as long as they keep off it the name of grief — that grave, ugly, deadly thing which cannot possibly exist alongside wisdom or, so to speak, live under the same roof with it. But the roots of grief — how many they are, and how bitter! Even with the trunk brought down, every one of them must be picked out, by a separate discussion for each if it comes to that. Leisure for the work, whatever kind of leisure it is, is one thing I still have. But one principle underlies all griefs, though the names are many. Enviousness is a grief; so are rivalry and disparagement, pity and anguish, mourning, sorrow, tribulation, lamentation, worry, pain, vexation, torment, despair.

Section 84

The Stoics define all of these; and the words I have listed each belong to a different thing — they do not, as they seem to, mean the same, but differ from one another in some degree, as perhaps I will take up elsewhere. These are those fibers of the roots I spoke of at the beginning, all of which must be traced out and pulled, so that not one can ever spring up again. A great and difficult work — who denies it? But what splendid thing is not also steep? Yet philosophy promises to accomplish it, if only we accept her treatment. So much, then, for this. The rest stands ready for you, whenever you wish, both here and anywhere else.
