Book I: On the Contempt of Death

(Part 4 of 4)

Section 93

So let us sweep away this nonsense, fit for old wives, that it is miserable to die before your time. Before what time, exactly? Nature's? But nature only lent you life, the way one lends money — with no repayment date fixed in advance. What complaint do you have, then, if she calls in the loan when she pleases? Those were the terms on which you took it. The same people who talk this way agree that if a small child dies, the loss must be borne calmly, and if an infant dies in the cradle, there should be no complaint at all. Yet in that case nature demanded back what she gave more harshly than in any other. "The infant hadn't yet tasted the sweetness of life," they say, "while the grown man was already hoping for great things and had begun to enjoy them." But in everything else we think it better to get some part than none at all — why should life be different? Though Callimachus put it well enough when he said that Priam wept far more often than Troilus, who died young. Meanwhile people praise the good fortune of those who die in old age.

Section 94

Why? Because, I suppose, no one's life could have been any sweeter had it run longer — since nothing, surely, is sweeter to a man than practical wisdom, and old age certainly brings him that, whatever else it strips away. But what age is really long? What, for a human being, is long at all? Isn't it as the poet says —

  Chasing hard behind them, now boys, now young men in mid-stride,
  old age has caught them when they least expected it.

But because we have nothing beyond old age, we call it long. All these things get called long or short according to the share each of us happens to be given. Aristotle says that near the river Hypanis, which flows into the Black Sea from the European side, tiny creatures are born that live a single day. One of them that dies at the eighth hour has died at a ripe old age; one that dies at sunset is positively decrepit — all the more so if it happens on midsummer's day, when the daylight runs longest. Set our longest human life against eternity, and we will be found living in very nearly the same brevity as those little creatures.

Section 95

So let us hold all these follies in contempt — what softer name could I give to such shallowness? — and let us place the whole force of living well in strength and greatness of spirit, in contempt and disdain for everything merely human, and in every virtue. As things stand, we make ourselves soft with the tenderest imaginings, so that if death arrives before we have collected everything the Chaldean fortune-tellers promised us, we feel robbed of some tremendous goods — cheated, and left stranded.

Section 96

But if hoping and craving keep our souls hanging in suspense, tormented and strangled with worry — immortal gods! how pleasant the journey must be that ends with no care remaining and no anxiety ahead! How Theramenes delights me! What a lofty spirit! We weep when we read his story, yes, but there is nothing pitiful in the way that famous man dies. Thrown into prison by order of the Thirty Tyrants, he drank down the poison like a thirsty man and then flung the last of it from the cup so that it rang as it splashed — and at the sound he smiled and said, "Here's to the lovely Critias," the man who had been the most vicious of his enemies. The Greeks, at their banquets, customarily name the person to whom they are about to pass the cup. That remarkable man was joking with his last breath, the death already gathered in his chest — and for the man to whom he drank that poisoned toast he prophesied, truly, a death that followed soon after.

Section 97

Who would praise such perfect calm of a very great soul in the very act of dying, if he judged death an evil? A few years later Socrates enters that same prison and that same cup, condemned by a crime of his judges as great as the tyrants' crime against Theramenes. And what is the speech Plato gives him before his judges, after the sentence of death had already been passed? "I have great hope, gentlemen of the jury," he says, "that being sent to death is the best thing that could happen to me. For one of two things must be true: either death takes away all sensation entirely, or it is a migration from this place to some other. So if sensation is snuffed out, and death is like the kind of sleep that sometimes brings the most perfect rest, without even the visions of dreams — good gods, what a gain it is to die! How many days can you find that are better than a night like that? And if the whole endless stretch of time to come is like it, who is happier than I am?

Section 98

"But if what people say is true — that death is a migration to the shores where those who have departed this life now dwell — then that is happier by far. To have escaped the men who insist on being counted as judges, and to come before the ones who genuinely deserve the name — Minos, Rhadamanthus, Aeacus, Triptolemus — and to meet those who lived justly and kept faith: does that strike you as a shabby sort of journey? And to be allowed to talk with Orpheus and Musaeus, with Homer and Hesiod — what price would you put on that? For my part, I would gladly die many times over, if such a thing could be done, to see what I am describing. What delight I would take in meeting Palamedes, and Ajax, and the others betrayed by unjust verdicts! I would test the wisdom of the great king who led his mighty armies to Troy, and the shrewdness of Ulysses and of Sisyphus — and I would not be condemned to death for asking my questions there, the way I was condemned for asking them here. And you too, gentlemen of the jury — you who voted to acquit me — do not be afraid of death.

Section 99

"For nothing bad can happen to a good man, living or dead, and the gods never lose sight of his affairs. What has happened to me did not happen by chance. And I hold no grudge against the men who accused me or the men who condemned me — except for this: that they believed they were doing me harm." And so he went on, in that vein. But nothing is finer than the ending. "Now it is time," he says, "to go from here — I, to die; you, to go on living. Which of the two is better, the immortal gods know. Whether any human being knows it, I doubt." I would rather have that man's spirit than the fortunes of every one of the men who sat in judgment on him. And yet the very thing he says no one but the gods knows — which is better — he does know himself, for he had stated it earlier. But he holds to that rule of his — affirm nothing — right to the very end.

Section 100

As for us, let us hold to this: nothing that nature has given to everyone can be an evil; and let us understand that if death is an evil, it is an everlasting evil. For death is supposed to be the end of a miserable life — but if death itself is misery, then there can be no end at all. But why do I keep citing Socrates and Theramenes, men towering in the glory of virtue and wisdom, when a certain Spartan, whose name has not even come down to us, held death in such contempt that when he was led off to execution, condemned by the ephors, Sparta's magistrates, he wore a bright and cheerful face — and when one of his enemies asked him, "Do you despise the laws of Lycurgus?" he answered, "On the contrary, I am deeply grateful to the man. He set my fine at a price I can pay without a loan — without borrowing from one man to pay another." There was a man worthy of Sparta! Indeed, a spirit that great almost persuades me he was condemned an innocent man. Our own state has produced countless men like him.

Section 101

But why should I name commanders and leading men, when Cato writes that whole legions marched out, eagerly, again and again, to ground they never expected to return from? With that same spirit the Spartans fell at Thermopylae, for whom Simonides wrote:

  Tell them in Sparta, stranger: you saw us lying here,
  still obedient to our country's sacred laws.

And what does their commander Leonidas say? "Forward with brave hearts, Spartans. Tonight, perhaps, we dine among the dead." It was a hardy nation, as long as the laws of Lycurgus held their force. One of them, when a Persian enemy boasted in a parley, "You will not see the sun for the cloud of our javelins and arrows," replied, "Then we will fight in the shade."

Section 102

I have been speaking of the men — but what of the Spartan woman? She had sent her son into battle, and when she heard he had been killed, she said, "That is why I bore him: so there would be a man who would not hesitate to die for his country." Very well — the Spartans were tough and hardened; the discipline of a state carries great force. But what about Theodorus of Cyrene, a philosopher of real standing — doesn't he earn our admiration? When King Lysimachus threatened him with crucifixion, he said, "Save those horrors, please, for your courtiers in purple. To Theodorus it makes no difference whether he rots above ground or under it." That saying of his reminds me that something ought to be said about burial and funeral customs — not a difficult subject, especially in light of what was said a little while ago about the dead feeling nothing. What Socrates thought about it appears in the book in which he dies — the book we have already discussed at such length.

Section 103

He had been arguing about the immortality of souls, and the hour of his death was already pressing, when Crito asked him how he wished to be buried. "My friends," he said, "I have wasted a great deal of effort. I have failed to persuade our Crito that I shall fly away from here and leave nothing of myself behind. Still, Crito — if you can catch me, if you get hold of me anywhere, bury me however you think best. But believe me: not one of you will catch me once I have left this place." Beautifully done — he humored his friend, and at the same time showed that about the whole business he cared nothing at all.

Section 104

Diogenes was harsher. He held the same view, but as a Cynic he put it more roughly: he ordered his body to be thrown out unburied. His friends asked, "What — for the birds and the wild beasts?" "Not at all," he said. "Set a stick beside me so I can drive them off." "How will you manage that? You won't feel anything." "Then what harm does it do me to be torn by wild animals, if I feel nothing?" Anaxagoras put it splendidly too. He lay dying at Lampsacus, and his friends asked whether, if anything should happen, he wanted to be carried home to Clazomenae. "No need," he said. "From anywhere at all, the road to the underworld is the same length." About the whole question of burial, one thing must be held firm: it concerns the body, whether the soul has perished or lives on. And in the body, once the soul has been extinguished or has slipped free, it is perfectly plain that no sensation remains.

Section 105

But the world is full of errors on this point. Achilles drags Hector tied behind his chariot; he thinks, I suppose, that Hector is being torn and feels it. So he is taking his revenge — as he imagines. And Hecuba mourns it as the bitterest of wrongs:

  I saw — what agony it cost me to see —
  Hector dragged behind the four-horse car.

What Hector? And how long will he be Hector? Accius does better, and his Achilles is, for once, wise:

  No — it was a body I returned to Priam;
  Hector I took away.

So it was not Hector you dragged, but a body that had once been Hector's.

Section 106

Now another figure rises up out of the earth, one who will not let his mother sleep:

  Mother, I call on you — you who soothe your cares in the hush of sleep
  and take no pity on me. Rise, and bury your son!

When lines like these are sung to slow and tearful measures that fill whole theaters with sorrow, it is hard not to judge the unburied miserable. "Before the wild beasts and the birds —" He is afraid he will have less use of his limbs if they are mangled; if they are burned, he has no such worry.

  Do not leave my half-eaten remains, the bones stripped bare,
  smeared with gore, to be dragged in shame across the ground —

I fail to see what he is afraid of, when he can pour out such well-turned lines to the pipe. So we must hold to this: nothing is to be cared about after death — though plenty of men take revenge even on enemies already dead. In Ennius, Thyestes calls down curses in truly splendid verses, praying first that Atreus may die in a shipwreck. Harsh, certainly — that kind of death is not free of terrible sensation. But then comes the empty part:

  Himself pinned high on the jagged rocks, ripped open,
  hanging by his side, splattering the stones with filth, with gore and black blood —

Section 107

The rocks themselves will be no freer of all feeling than that man "hanging by his side," for whom Thyestes thinks he is wishing torture. Torments that would be hard if he could feel them are nothing at all without sensation. And then this, which is utterly empty:

  And let him find no tomb to take him in, no harbor for the body,
  where, human life laid down at last, his corpse might rest from its miseries.

You see how deep in error all this runs: he thinks the body has its harbor, and that the dead man rests in his grave. The fault is largely Pelops's, for never educating his son — never teaching him how far anything actually deserves our care.

Section 108

But why linger over the opinions of individuals, when we can survey the varied errors of whole nations? The Egyptians embalm their dead and keep them at home. The Persians even coat them with wax before burial, so that the bodies will last as long as possible. It is the custom of the Magi, the Persian priestly clan, not to bury their dead unless the body has first been torn by wild animals. In Hyrcania the common people keep dogs at public expense, and the nobles keep their own — and it is a distinguished breed of dog, as we know — but the point is that each man, according to his means, provides the animals that will tear his body, and there they consider that the finest form of burial. Chrysippus, curious about every corner of history, collects a great many other customs of this kind, but some of them are so hideous that language shrinks back from them in disgust. So the whole subject is one to despise where we ourselves are concerned, though not to neglect where our own people are concerned — so long as we, the living, understand that the bodies of the dead feel nothing.

Section 109

How much should be granted to custom and to public opinion — let the living see to that, but let them understand that none of it touches the dead. Death is met with the calmest spirit of all when a life at its setting can console itself with its own praises. No one has lived too short a life who has fully discharged the work of perfect virtue. For me, death would have been timely many times over. How I wish I could have met it! There was nothing left for me to gain; the duties of life stood complete; only the battles with fortune remained. So if reason alone cannot bring us to hold death of no account, then let the life we have already lived bring us there — let it show that we have lived enough, and more than enough. For though sensation is gone, the dead are still not without the goods that are properly and truly theirs — the praise and the glory they earned — even if they cannot feel them. Glory may contain nothing in itself that should make us chase it; but it follows virtue like a shadow.

Section 110

As for the crowd's judgment about good men — on the rare occasions when it is sound, it deserves praise more than it makes those men happy. Still, however this may be taken, I cannot say that Lycurgus and Solon lack glory for their laws and their civic order, or Themistocles and Epaminondas for their valor in war. Neptune will drown Salamis itself before he drowns the memory of the trophy raised at Salamis, and Leuctra will vanish from Boeotia before the glory of the battle fought there. Slower still will fame be to desert Curius, Fabricius, Calatinus, the two Scipios, the two Africani, Maximus, Marcellus, Paulus, Cato, Laelius, and countless others. The man who has caught some likeness of them — measuring it not by popular fame but by the genuine praise of good men — will, if it comes to that, walk toward death with a confident step; and in death, as we have established, lies either the highest good or no evil at all. Such a man will even want to die while his fortunes are at their height; for no further piling up of goods can be as sweet as their draining away is bitter.

Section 111

That seems to be the meaning behind the famous Spartan's remark. Diagoras of Rhodes, a celebrated Olympic champion, had just watched two of his own sons win at Olympia on a single day, when the Spartan came up to the old man, congratulated him, and said: "Die now, Diagoras — you are not going to climb up into heaven." The Greeks count such things great — too great, perhaps; or rather, they did count them so then. And the man who said this to Diagoras, reckoning it an enormous thing for one house to produce three Olympic champions, judged that lingering any longer in life, exposed to fortune's throws, could do the old man no good. Now, as for you: I had already answered you well enough, it seemed to me, in a few words — you had conceded that the dead are in no evil. But I pressed on and said more, for this reason: in loss and mourning, this is the greatest consolation there is. Grief that is our own, borne on our own account, we ought to bear with moderation, so that we do not seem to love ourselves too much. What tortures us with unbearable pain is the suspicion that those we have lost still have some feeling, amid the miseries people commonly imagine for them. I wanted to tear that opinion out of myself by the roots — and that is perhaps why I went on so long.

Section 112

"Too long — you?" he said. "Not for me. The first part of your speech made me want to die; the second left me sometimes not unwilling, sometimes simply untroubled. And the whole of it has certainly accomplished this much: I no longer count death among evils."

"Then do we need the orators' epilogue too — the grand closing appeal?" I asked. "Or are we abandoning that art for good?"

"Don't you abandon it — you have always dressed it in honor, and rightly so; though to tell the truth, it was the art that dressed you. But what is this epilogue? I'm eager to hear it, whatever it is."

Section 113

In the schools, I told him, they regularly bring forward the judgments of the immortal gods concerning death — not inventions of their own, but resting on the authority of Herodotus and many others. First they celebrate Cleobis and Biton, the sons of the priestess of Argos. The story is well known. Sacred law required her to be drawn in a chariot to a fixed annual sacrifice at a temple a good distance from the town, and the animals that were to pull her were delayed. So the two young men I have just named stripped off their clothes, rubbed their bodies with oil, and stepped into the yoke themselves. And so the priestess arrived at the temple, her chariot drawn by her own sons; and there, it is said, she prayed to the goddess to give them, as a reward for their devotion, the greatest gift a god can give a human being. The young men feasted with their mother, lay down to sleep — and in the morning were found dead.

Section 114

Trophonius and Agamedes are said to have made a similar prayer. When they had finished building Apollo's temple at Delphi, they worshipped the god and asked for no small wage for their work and toil — nothing specific, only whatever was best for a man. Apollo indicated that he would grant it on the third day from then; and when that day dawned, they were found dead. The god had given his verdict, people say — and that god above all others, since to him the rest of the gods have conceded the power of prophecy beyond them all. There is also a little story told about Silenus. Captured by King Midas, he is recorded to have paid this as the price of his release: he taught the king that by far the best thing for a human being is never to be born at all — and the next best, to die as soon as possible.

Section 115

Euripides uses the same thought in his Cresphontes:

  It would be fitting for us to gather the household in mourning
  whenever someone is born into the light,
  counting over the many evils of human life;
  but the man who has ended life's hard labors in death —
  him his friends should escort out with praise and gladness.

There is something similar in Crantor's Consolation. He tells of a certain Elysius of Terina who, grieving bitterly over the death of his son, came to an oracle of the dead to ask why so great a calamity had struck him — and was handed a tablet with three verses like these:

  Ignorant minds — so mortals wander through their lives.
  Euthynous lies in death by the appointed will of fate.
  This ending was the better — for him, and for you.

Section 116

With these authorities and others like them, they argue that the case has been decided by the immortal gods. Alcidamas, an early rhetorician of the very first rank, even wrote a Praise of Death, which consists of a catalogue of humanity's ills. The philosophers' more finely worked arguments escaped him; richness of language did not. As for glorious deaths met for one's country, the rhetoricians regularly hold them up as not only splendid but happy. They reach back to Erechtheus, whose very daughters went eagerly to death for the lives of their fellow citizens. They cite Codrus, who threw himself into the thick of the enemy dressed as a servant, so that his royal robes would not give him away — because an oracle had declared that if the king were killed, Athens would be victorious. Menoeceus is not passed over, who likewise, when an oracle had been published, poured out his blood for his country. Iphigenia, at Aulis, orders herself led to the sacrifice, so that her blood may draw out the blood of her enemies. From there they come down to more recent cases: Harmodius and Aristogiton are on everyone's lips; the memory of Leonidas the Spartan and Epaminondas the Theban is still green. Our own Romans they do not know — and it would be a long task to list them, so many are the men who, as we see, found death with glory a thing worth desiring.

Section 117

And yet, true as all this is, we must still bring great eloquence to bear — must speak as if from a platform above the crowd — to make men begin to wish for death, or at least stop fearing it. For if that final day brings not extinction but a change of place, what could be more desirable? And if instead it destroys us and blots us out altogether, what is better than to fall asleep in the middle of life's labors and, closing our eyes, to be lulled into a sleep that lasts forever? If that is how it is, then Ennius speaks better than Solon. Our own poet says:

  Let no one honor me with tears, nor hold
  my funeral rites with weeping.

But the wise Solon:

  Let my death not lack for tears: let me leave to my friends
  grief, so they mourn aloud at my funeral.

Section 118

As for us — if it should happen that a god seems to give us notice to depart from this life, let us obey gladly and with thanksgiving, and count ourselves released from prison and loosed from our chains, either to journey home again to a dwelling that is eternal and plainly our own, or to be free of all sensation and all trouble. And if no notice comes, let us still keep such a spirit that the day which others find dreadful we regard as a lucky day for us, and let us count nothing an evil that has been appointed by the immortal gods or by nature, the mother of us all. For it was not at random or by accident that we were sown and created. There is beyond doubt some power that watches over the human race — one that would never have brought forth and nourished a being who, after draining every labor to the dregs, would then fall at death into everlasting evil. Let us think of death instead as a harbor made ready for us, a refuge.

Section 119

If only we could ride into it under full sail! But if headwinds drive us back, still we must be carried, a little later, to the same place. And can what is necessary for everyone be misery for any one? There you have your epilogue — so you won't think anything was skipped or left behind.

"I have it," he said, "and in fact that epilogue has made me steadier still."

"Good," I said. "But for now let us give something to our health. Tomorrow, and for all the days we are here at my place in Tusculum, let us go on with these discussions — above all the ones that bring relief from grief, from fears, and from cravings. That is the richest fruit of the whole of philosophy."
