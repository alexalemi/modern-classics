CHAPTER 020 (Book II, Chapter 9) — Translation Notes

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE:

1. Opening rhetorical question: Locke opens with a powerful "if... why?" structure.
   Preserved faithfully but modernized "absolute lord of his own person" to "absolute
   masters of their own persons" and "subject to nobody" to "answerable to nobody."

2. "Property" definition: Locke's critical definition — property = life, liberty, and
   possessions — appears in the first paragraph. Kept the explicit enumeration "lives,
   liberties, and possessions -- which I call by the general name 'property'" with
   quotation marks around "property" to emphasize this is a technical term.

3. Three defects of the state of nature: Locke's "First... Secondly... Thirdly..."
   structure listing what's missing in the state of nature is preserved exactly. These
   are: (1) no established law, (2) no impartial judge, (3) no enforcement power.

4. "Wanting" (= lacking): Consistently rendered as "missing" or restructured to show
   absence ("there is no..." instead of "there wants...").

5. "Indifferent judge": Rendered as "impartial judge" — "indifferent" in Locke's usage
   means unbiased/neutral, not uncaring.

6. "The legislative" as noun: Rendered as "the legislature" per the established convention,
   except in the final paragraph where "the legislative or supreme power" is preserved as
   Locke uses it as a descriptor.

7. Gender-neutral language: "Man" and "men" in generic senses rendered as "people,"
   "a person," "someone," "they" throughout, per project guidelines.

8. "Politic society": Rendered as "political society" consistently.

9. "Viz.": Rendered as an em-dash with the quoted phrase following, rather than inserting
   "namely."

10. Final paragraph — the capstone argument: This is one of the most important passages
    in the chapter. Locke argues that because people enter society only to be better off,
    government power can never legitimately extend beyond the common good. He then lists
    the requirements: standing laws, impartial judges, force used only to enforce law
    domestically and defend against foreign threats. Preserved the full logical chain and
    the sweeping final sentence about "peace, safety, and public good."

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:

- "Property" continues to mean life, liberty, and possessions (Locke's broad definition).
- "Commonwealth" kept as-is throughout.
- "The legislature" for Locke's "the legislative" when used as a noun.
- "State of nature" kept as a technical term.
- "Law of nature" kept as a technical term.
- The three defects (no established law, no impartial judge, no enforcement power) are
  referenced later in Book II — the next chapter (Ch. 10, "Of the Forms of a
  Commonwealth") is very brief and classifies types of government. Keep the same
  measured, constructive tone.
- Locke's argument that government power cannot extend beyond the common good is a
  thesis that will be elaborated extensively in Ch. 11 ("Of the Extent of the
  Legislative Power"). The translation there should echo the language used here.
