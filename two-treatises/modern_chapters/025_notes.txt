CHAPTER 025 (Book II, Chapter 14: "Of Prerogative") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This chapter defines and explores executive prerogative -- the power of the executive to act at discretion for the public good, even without or against the letter of the law. Locke walks through the origin of prerogative in early small governments, defends the people's right to limit it when abused, addresses the famous "who shall be judge?" question, and concludes with the people's ultimate right to "appeal to heaven" when there is no earthly remedy. The chapter contains 10 sections (159-168) and is moderate in length (~12K characters).

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE:

1. Sentence splitting: Section 159 is one of Locke's signature enormous sentences -- a single unbroken paragraph in the original that covers the basic argument for prerogative, the fire-next-door example, and the case for executive clemency. I broke it into four paragraphs while preserving all the logical connections.

2. "Viz." rendered as "namely" in section 159 (the fundamental law of nature) and as a colon or dash in section 168 (the right to judge). Consistent with project conventions.

3. "Fain" (section 162): Locke says the people "were fain by express laws to get prerogative determined" -- meaning they were forced or compelled. Rendered as "were forced to define prerogative through explicit laws."

4. "The legislators not being able to foresee": Modernized the complex participial opening of section 159 into a direct "Here's why:" explanation followed by clear causal sentences.

5. "Municipal law" (section 159): Kept the concept but folded it into "existing law" since Locke means domestic/statutory law rather than the modern sense of city ordinances.

6. "Nice in the point" (section 161): Locke says the people are "very seldom or never scrupulous or nice in the point" -- meaning picky or exacting. Rendered as "rarely fussy or picky about this."

7. The godlike-princes passage (section 166): This is one of Locke's most subtle arguments -- that good rulers set dangerous precedents for bad successors. Preserved the full logical chain and the famous quotation: "Prerogative is nothing but the power of doing public good without a rule."

8. "Appeal to heaven" (section 168): This is Locke's signature phrase for the right of revolution -- the idea that when no earthly judge can resolve a dispute between rulers and people, the people may resort to force (an "appeal to heaven"). Preserved verbatim as it's a famous and recurring formulation.

9. The final paragraph of section 168: Locke's reassurance that this doctrine doesn't lead to perpetual disorder. The argument is that revolution only happens when the majority feels the harm and sees the necessity -- it's self-limiting. I kept "this right doesn't kick in" as a slightly more conversational rendering of Locke's "this operates not, till..."

10. Gender-neutral language: "men" in generic senses rendered as "people," "anyone," "a person," etc. "A man may come sometimes within the reach of the law" becomes "someone who technically falls within the reach of the law." "A man's power so to submit himself" becomes "anyone's power to submit so completely."

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- All 10 section numbers (159-168) as standard scholarly references
- The complete argument structure: definition of prerogative, historical origin, defense of limiting it, the "who shall judge?" question, appeal to heaven
- The house-fire example (section 159)
- The "herd of inferior creatures" passage (section 163) -- Locke's reductio ad absurdum of the idea that rulers have interests separate from the people
- The England-specific references to parliamentary prerogative (section 167)
- The famous quotation: "Prerogative is nothing but the power of doing public good without a rule" (section 166)
- The "appeal to heaven" doctrine (section 168) -- preserved verbatim
- The final reassurance that this doesn't create perpetual disorder
- Locke's measured, constructive tone throughout

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (026 -- Book II, Ch. 15: "Of Paternal, Political, and Despotic Power, Considered Together"):
- "Prerogative" has been defined as the executive's power to act at discretion for the public good, without or even against the letter of the law. This definition should be treated as established.
- "The legislature" continues as the standard rendering of Locke's "the legislative" used as a noun.
- "Commonwealth" used without further definition.
- "Appeal to heaven" has been introduced as Locke's term for the people's ultimate recourse when no earthly judge exists. It will recur prominently in later chapters (especially Ch. 19).
- The principle that no one in government can have a right to harm the people has been restated emphatically -- this is a recurring foundation for Locke's arguments about tyranny, usurpation, and dissolution of government in the remaining chapters.
- Chapter 026 is a brief synthesis chapter that distinguishes paternal, political, and despotic power. Tone should remain measured and precise, as Locke is drawing together distinctions he's been building across multiple chapters.
