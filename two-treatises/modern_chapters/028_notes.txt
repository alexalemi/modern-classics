CHAPTER 028 (Book II, Chapter 17: "Of Usurpation") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is one of the shortest chapters in the entire work -- just two sections (197-198). Locke draws a parallel between conquest and usurpation: conquest is foreign seizure of power, usurpation is domestic seizure. The key point is that a usurper never has legitimate authority because they hold power that rightfully belongs to someone else. The only way a usurper can gain legitimacy is if the people freely consent to their rule after the fact.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Sentence splitting: The original section 197 is essentially one long sentence with many semicolons and embedded conditions. I broke it into three sentences across two paragraphs: first the definition and key distinction (usurper can never have right), then the clarification that usurpation alone is merely a personnel change, but usurpation plus overreach equals tyranny.

2. Section 198 in the original is a single enormous sentence that weaves together three separate arguments (the necessity of succession rules, the analogy to anarchy, and the conclusion about legitimacy). I broke this into four paragraphs, each handling one logical step.

3. "Hath no right to be obeyed" -- modernized to "has no right to be obeyed" per standard convention.

4. The original contains a near-repetition: Locke states the anarchy analogy twice in section 198 with slightly different wording ("the anarchy being much alike..." and later "for the anarchy is much alike..."). This appears to be intentional emphasis rather than an editing error. I preserved both instances but gave each slightly different phrasing to make the repetition feel purposeful and reinforcing rather than redundant.

5. "Design the person" (meaning "designate" or "identify") -- modernized to "select" and "identify" respectively.

6. "Any deriving from him" -- expanded to "anyone who derives their authority from the usurper" for clarity.

7. "Till the people are both at liberty to consent, and have actually consented" -- Locke is making a crucial two-part point here: consent must be both (a) genuinely free and (b) actually given. I preserved both conditions clearly in the modernized version.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- The conquest/usurpation parallel that opens the chapter
- The critical distinction between usurpation (change of persons) and tyranny (change of persons plus overreach of power)
- The argument that legitimate government requires both a form of government AND a process for selecting rulers
- The anarchy analogy (no government vs. government with no succession rules)
- The bottom line: no right to obedience without lawful appointment
- The two conditions for legitimizing a usurper's power (freedom to consent + actual consent)
- Section numbers 197 and 198

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (029 -- Book II, Ch. 18: "Of Tyranny"):
- The distinction between usurpation and tyranny has been established: usurpation is seizing power that belongs to someone else; tyranny is exercising power beyond what anyone has a right to. Chapter 029 will develop the concept of tyranny in full.
- The principle that legitimacy requires the people's free consent has been restated and should carry forward.
- "Commonwealth" continues to be used consistently in the established sense.
- "The legislature" remains the standard rendering of Locke's "the legislative" as noun.
- Chapter 029 contains one of Locke's most famous lines: "Where-ever law ends, tyranny begins." The tone should build in intensity -- this is Locke at his most passionate and quotable. The chapter also references contemporary English politics, so historical context may occasionally need light glossing.
