Chapter 011 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 11: "Who Heir?"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is the longest chapter in the entire work (~96K characters) and serves as the climactic demolition of Filmer's theory in Book I. Locke's central argument: even if we grant everything Filmer claims about Adam's sovereignty and its descent by inheritance, the theory is useless because Filmer never tells us who the heir is. Without being able to identify the heir, no one can know whom to obey, which makes the entire doctrine of divinely-instituted hereditary monarchy self-defeating. Locke methodically shows that: (1) Filmer's term "eldest parents" is unintelligible; (2) birthright in scripture meant only a double portion of property, not dominion; (3) the examples of Cain/Abel and Jacob/Esau don't prove heirs are lords over their brothers; (4) Filmer's biblical "history" of patriarchal power (Judah, Abraham, Esau, Babel, Noah's sons, Nimrod, the judges, the kings of Israel) proves nothing about Adamic descent; (5) the succession of Israelite kings (Saul, David, Solomon, Jeroboam, Athaliah) contradicts rather than confirms lineal succession; (6) for 1,750 years of Jewish history, hereditary kingly government existed for less than a third of the time.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, per established convention
- "O. 244" / "O. 254" / "O. 253" etc. -- page references to Filmer's Observations kept intact
- "commonwealth" kept as-is per text analysis
- "birthright" and "primogeniture" kept as technical terms
- "fain" (line 51) rendered as "forced to fall back on"
- "Risum teneatis?" (Latin, "Can you keep a straight face?") rendered directly as "Can you keep a straight face?" per guidelines on Latin phrases
- "philosopher's stone in politics" -- kept this vivid metaphor, it's clear in context
- "Jack Cade" and "Oliver Cromwell" -- kept as named, recognizable enough historical references
- "Patriarcha non Monarcha" -- kept as a book title, context makes its role clear
- "politic societies" rendered as "organized political societies" or "political societies"
- "West Indies" kept as period-specific geographical reference
- Judge Jefferies -- kept as-is, Locke's own contemporary analogy
- "Hernando de Soto" modernized from "Ferdinando Soto"

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This chapter required special attention to pacing and reader engagement given its enormous length. Broke Locke's longest sentences (some exceeding 200 words) into shorter, punchier units while preserving his logical chain.
- Preserved the chapter's argumentative structure: the opening statement of the problem (who should have power?), the examination of Filmer's vagueness, the biblical case studies (Cain/Abel, Jacob/Esau, Judah/Tamar, Abraham, Babel, Noah's sons, Nimrod, the judges, the kings), and the devastating statistical conclusion about Jewish history.
- Maintained Locke's deadpan sarcasm throughout -- e.g., "What a remarkable limitation! A limitation to all of humanity!" and "Can you keep a straight face?" and the horse-bought-at-a-fair analogy.
- Preserved Locke's signature conditional reasoning ("even if we grant...") throughout, especially in the passages about birthright and the Babel dispersion.
- The rapid-fire succession questions (grandson by daughter vs. nephew by brother, infant heir vs. capable younger son, etc.) were kept as a flowing sequence to preserve their cumulative rhetorical force.
- Locke's numbered arguments (1. and 2. under the Jacob/Esau analysis) preserved as structured.
- The analogy comparing obedience to Adam's heir with confessing sins to or taking medicine from an unknown "heir" to Adam's priestly/medical power is one of Locke's best -- kept close to original.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 011 is the final chapter of Book I. The argument ends with a devastating statistical summary: out of 1,750 years of Jewish history, hereditary kingly government existed less than a third of the time.
- Chapter 012 begins Book II -- Locke's own positive theory of government. The tone shifts from prosecutorial demolition to constructive theory-building. It's a brief transitional chapter summarizing Book I's conclusions and setting up Book II's project of finding "another rise of government."
- The transition from Book I to Book II should feel like a breath of fresh air -- moving from the exhaustive refutation of Filmer to Locke's own famous and influential ideas.
- Key shift: Book II uses "commonwealth" frequently and introduces technical terms like "political power," "state of nature," "law of nature" that need consistent handling.
- Locke's tone in Book II is more measured and constructive but retains his characteristic precision and occasional passion. Watch for the shift from exasperation to conviction.
