CHAPTER 029 (Book II, Chapter 18: "Of Tyranny") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is one of the most powerful and quotable chapters in the Two Treatises. Locke defines tyranny as the exercise of power beyond right -- power used not for the public good but for the ruler's private advantage. He argues that tyranny is not limited to monarchies but can afflict any form of government. The chapter then systematically addresses the objection that allowing resistance to tyranny would lead to anarchy, offering four carefully ordered responses. The chapter closes with the vivid ship-to-Algiers analogy, showing that when a pattern of abuse is clear, the people have every right to see it for what it is.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Famous quotation preserved: "Wherever law ends, tyranny begins" -- kept near-verbatim per the translation strategy. Modernized only "Where-ever" to "Wherever" and removed the archaic hyphenation.

2. King James I speeches: Both extended quotations from James I's 1603 and 1609 speeches to Parliament were modernized for readability while keeping them clearly as quotations. Archaic phrasing like "the weal of the public" became "the welfare of the public," "leaves to be a king" became "ceases to be a king," and "paction" became "agreement." The substance and rhetorical force are preserved.

3. Section numbers: Locke's original text does not explicitly mark section numbers in the chapter text as given in the source file, but this chapter covers sections 199-210 in standard scholarly editions. I added section numbers (199-208) at the start of each major paragraph to maintain the convention used in previous chapters and allow scholarly cross-referencing.

4. Sentence splitting: Locke's enormous compound sentences (some running 150+ words) were broken into shorter, punchier sentences throughout. The most dramatic example is the final paragraph (section 208), which is a single sentence in the original -- a breathless chain of "if...if...if..." clauses building to the Algiers analogy. I preserved the building, cascading structure but broke it into multiple sentences with "when" clauses, maintaining the rhetorical escalation while making it readable.

5. Currency modernization: Changed "twelve-pence" to "a dollar" and "100l." to "a hundred pounds" -- the specific amounts don't matter to the argument, and the modern equivalents are more immediately graspable. Kept "hundred pounds" for the larger amount since "pounds" is still widely understood.

6. The highway robber example (section 205): This is one of Locke's most famous thought experiments. Translated it fully, preserving the logical contrast between the two scenarios and the key conclusion: what justifies deadly force is not the amount of harm but whether legal remedy is available.

7. "Properties" (Lockean sense): Locke uses "properties" in his broad sense meaning life, liberty, and possessions. Translated as "rights and property" or "property" depending on context, keeping the term accessible while preserving its weight.

8. "A neighbour kingdom has showed the world an odd example": This is Locke's veiled reference to the recent events in France or possibly the Dutch Republic. Kept the reference vague as Locke intended, translating as "a neighboring kingdom has shown the world a rather striking example."

9. The Thirty Tyrants, Syracuse, and the Decemvirs: Kept these classical references without extensive glossing -- they serve Locke's point that tyranny exists in all forms of government, and the meaning is clear from context.

10. "Algiers" reference: In the 17th century, Algiers was the base of Barbary pirates who enslaved Europeans. The analogy is: if the captain keeps steering toward a known slave port, you'd eventually figure out his intentions. Kept as-is since the analogy works even without knowing the specific historical context.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- The complete chapter, all arguments fully translated without truncation
- The four-part structure of the anti-anarchy argument (First through Fourth)
- The famous "Wherever law ends, tyranny begins" quotation
- Both King James I speeches in full
- The highway robber thought experiment
- The ship-to-Algiers analogy
- The distinction between tyranny as one person vs. many
- Locke's emphasis that greater power means greater responsibility
- The escalating rhetorical force of the final paragraphs

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (030 -- Book II, Ch. 19: "Of the Dissolution of Government"):
- The definition of tyranny as "power beyond right" is now established. Chapter 19 builds directly on this, asking what happens when government is dissolved.
- The four-part argument against the "anarchy" objection anticipates the similar (and more extended) argument Locke will make in Ch. 19 about the right of revolution. Maintain consistency in how resistance and its justification are framed.
- "Prerogative" has been defined parenthetically in section 208 as "a discretionary power left in the ruler's hands to do good, not harm." This matches the full treatment in Chapter 14 (025).
- The ship-to-Algiers metaphor at the end of this chapter creates momentum into Ch. 19. The tone should carry forward -- building urgency and passion.
- Ch. 19 is the longest chapter in Book II and the climax of the entire work. It contains extended Latin passages from Barclay -- use the English translations as primary text.
- The famous lines "The end of government is the good of mankind" and "The people shall be judge" appear in Ch. 19. These should be preserved near-verbatim, matching the care given to "Wherever law ends, tyranny begins" in this chapter.
- "The legislature" remains the standard rendering for Locke's "the legislative" used as a noun.
- "Commonwealth" continues to be used without special glossing, per the definition established in Ch. 10 (021).
