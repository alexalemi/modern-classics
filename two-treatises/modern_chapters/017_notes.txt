Chapter 017 Translation Notes
Book II, Chapter 6: "Of Paternal Power"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is a pivotal chapter in Book II where Locke draws the sharp distinction between parental authority and political authority -- a distinction that undermines the entire Filmerian project of deriving royal power from fatherhood. Locke's main arguments: (1) the very term "paternal power" is misleading, since mothers have equal claim -- it should be "parental power"; (2) all people are born equal in rights, but children are born to freedom, not in it -- they must grow into it through the development of reason; (3) law is not a restriction on freedom but a guide that preserves and enlarges it ("where there is no law, there is no freedom"); (4) parental authority is temporary and tied to guardianship during minority, while the honor owed to parents is permanent but carries no governing power; (5) the father's influence over grown children through inheritance is a matter of property incentives, not natural right; (6) in early societies, fathers naturally became rulers by consent, not by paternal right -- and the proof is that they could punish strangers too, which no paternal authority could explain.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "paternal power" kept in title and throughout, as Locke uses it while arguing it should really be "parental power"
- "parental power" used where Locke proposes the correction
- "nonage" rendered variously as "minority," "youth," "early years," "childhood" depending on context
- "estate" (condition) rendered as "condition" or "state"
- "want" (lack) rendered as "lack" or "need"
- "commonwealth" kept as-is per text analysis
- "lists" (= pleases, wishes) rendered as "sees fit" or "wants"
- "pietas" (Latin) rendered as "pietas" with context "what the ancient Romans called pietas" -- this is a well-known concept and Locke is making a specific cultural reference
- "infranchisement" rendered as "age of discretion" matching Locke's own surrounding language
- "lunatics and idiots" rendered as "people with severe intellectual disabilities or mental illness" -- modernized the outdated terminology while preserving Locke's argument about guardianship for those who cannot exercise reason
- "de facto" rendered as "in practice" per text analysis guidelines
- Hooker citations kept with full identification ("the theologian Richard Hooker") on first reference, shortened thereafter
- "pro tempore" concepts rendered as "temporary" throughout
- "magistrate" rendered as "government official" for clarity

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This chapter has a measured, constructive tone characteristic of Book II. Locke is building his own theory rather than demolishing Filmer's, though Filmer is still occasionally referenced.
- The famous passage about law and freedom ("where there is no law, there is no freedom") is one of Locke's most quoted lines -- kept very close to original while modernizing surrounding syntax.
- Locke's long central argument about the relationship between reason, law, and freedom (Section 63 in standard numbering) was carefully broken into shorter sentences while preserving the logical chain: reason -> knowledge of law -> freedom within law -> no reason yet = no freedom yet.
- The parallel structure comparing natural law and English law (Section 65) was preserved as a rhetorical device -- Locke uses the same questions and answers for both, driving home the universality of his principle.
- The passage about mothers having equal authority is one of Locke's most progressive arguments -- kept its force while modernizing.
- The concluding sections about how fathers naturally became rulers in early societies are translated with care to show this was by consent, not natural right -- this is Locke's key theoretical move.
- The final zinger about fathers being priests as well as rulers is classic Locke deadpan -- kept its punch.

KEY PHILOSOPHICAL POINTS TO MAINTAIN CONSISTENCY:
- Law as the preservation and enlargement of freedom (not its restriction)
- The distinction between temporary guardianship power and permanent honor obligation
- Parental authority as duty-based, not right-based
- Political power distinguished from parental power by nature, foundation, and purpose
- Consent (express or tacit) as the basis for early political authority within families

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 018 is Book II, Chapter 7: "Of Political or Civil Society." It builds directly on the distinction established here between parental and political power.
- Key concepts to carry forward: the clear separation of parental power (temporary, duty-based, tied to children's minority) from political power; the idea that every subject who is a parent retains full parental authority even under a government; the principle that freedom requires reason and knowledge of law.
- The tone continues to be constructive and measured. Locke is now moving from what political power is NOT (parental) to what it IS.
- Watch for continued use of "commonwealth" as Locke's standard term for an organized political community.
- The consent principle established at the end of this chapter (fathers became rulers by consent of grown children) will be developed more fully in later chapters, especially Chapter 8 (019).
