Chapter 003 (Book I, Chapter 3) Translation Notes
"Of Adam's Title to Sovereignty by Creation"

OVERVIEW:
This is a relatively short chapter where Locke dismantles Filmer's claim that Adam
received sovereignty over the world simply by being created. Locke shows that "creation"
alone doesn't grant dominion (the lion was created too), that Filmer conflates multiple
meanings of "God's appointment" and "monarch," and that Filmer's reasoning is ultimately
circular. The chapter culminates in one of Locke's best jokes — comparing Filmer's
"monarch in habit but not in act" to saying Filmer was "an author before he writ
his book."

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" / "our A." — kept "our author" throughout to preserve Locke's sarcastic
  detachment. Used "Filmer" occasionally for clarity where Locke also uses "Sir Robert."
- "vouchsafe" — rendered as "bother to do us that favor"
- "de facto" — rendered as "in fact" for accessibility
- "viz." — rendered as "namely"
- "i.e." — rendered as "that is"
- "generatione jus acquiritur parentibus in liberos" — translated the Latin directly
  ("Parents acquire a right over their children by the act of generation") and kept
  the Grotius citation in modernized form
- "omnipotency" — rendered as "almighty power" (clearer for target audience)
- "positive grant/donation" — rendered as "direct grant" or "specific, revealed grant"
  to make the concept clear (Locke means an explicit scriptural command, as opposed
  to natural law)
- "proprietor" — rendered as "owner" for clarity
- "in habit" vs "in act" — kept these terms since Locke's entire joke depends on them,
  but made the meaning clear through context

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Locke's long opening sentence (~200 words) was broken into multiple sentences while
  preserving the logical flow
- The devastating wit in the "author before he writ his book" passage was preserved
  carefully — this is one of Locke's most memorable lines in Book I
- The final long paragraph where Locke apologizes for dwelling on the passage but
  explains why he must — this meta-commentary on Filmer's obscure writing style is
  important for understanding Locke's method throughout Book I. Kept it complete.
- Locke's circular reasoning exposure ("he was governor by right of nature because
  he was governor by right of nature") was made explicit with "A perfect circle."
  added — this felt faithful to Locke's intent without over-explaining

BIBLICAL REFERENCES:
- Gen. 1:28 — preserved as-is
- Gen. 3:16 — preserved as-is
- The Grotius citation (De Jure Belli ac Pacis, Book 2, Ch. 5, Sec. 1) was
  spelled out from the abbreviated form

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- This chapter establishes the pattern of Locke methodically testing every possible
  interpretation of Filmer's words and showing they all fail. The next chapter
  translator should expect this same method to continue.
- "Our author" is the standard way to refer to Filmer — maintain this throughout.
- "Positive grant" vs. "natural right" is an important distinction Locke keeps
  returning to. I've been rendering "positive grant" as "direct grant" or
  "specific grant" — should remain consistent.
- Locke's meta-commentary about Filmer's confusing writing style (the long final
  paragraph) sets up a promise: he says he won't do this close parsing again unless
  forced to. Future chapters may be somewhat less granular in their analysis of
  Filmer's exact phrasing.
- The "habit vs. act" distinction is now established and may recur — Locke has
  thoroughly mocked it here.
- Keep the tone prosecutorial but not shrill — Locke is amused by Filmer's
  incoherence, not enraged by it.
