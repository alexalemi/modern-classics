Chapter 005 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 5: "Of Adam's Title to Sovereignty, by the Subjection of Eve"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
Locke demolishes Filmer's argument that Genesis 3:16 ("he shall rule over thee") grants Adam monarchical authority. His key moves: (1) God was pronouncing a curse, not granting privileges; (2) Adam was also being punished at the same time; (3) the words weren't even spoken to Adam but to Eve; (4) even taken as a law, it only establishes a wife's domestic obedience to her husband, not political subjection; (5) the passage is a prediction, not a grant of authority -- just as Genesis 25:23 about Jacob and Esau was a prediction.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, per text analysis guidelines
- "O. 244" / "O. 254" references to Filmer's Observations kept intact
- "conjugal power" kept as-is (clear enough in context, contrasted with "political power")
- "commonwealth" did not appear in this chapter
- "de facto" rendered as "in fact" in the Jacob/Esau passage
- "vouchsafes to speak" rendered as "chooses to speak" (simpler, maintains register)
- "forwardest in the disobedience" rendered as "first and most eager participant in the act of disobedience"
- "fain" / "bating" did not appear

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Broke Locke's extremely long sentences into shorter ones while maintaining logical flow. The original chapter has several sentences exceeding 150 words; these were split at natural logical joints.
- Preserved the deadpan sarcasm throughout -- e.g., "presto -- absolute monarchy is established by divine right" captures Locke's exasperation without amplifying it beyond his tone.
- The "spade vs. scepter" image is vivid and was kept with minimal change ("shovel" for "spade" to modernize slightly).
- Preserved Locke's signature "even if we grant your premise" structure in the passage about Eve's subjection and the childbirth analogy.
- Maintained direct biblical quotations in their archaic form since Locke quotes them as evidence.
- The closing argument about "assertions without proof" deserving "denial without reasons" is a powerful rhetorical flourish -- kept close to the original.

BIBLICAL REFERENCES PRESERVED:
- Gen. 3:16 (the central text under dispute)
- Gen. 1:26, 1:28 (referenced for Locke's argument about singular vs. plural inconsistency)
- Gen. 9 (Noah reference)
- Gen. 25:23 (Jacob and Esau parallel)
- Verse references within Genesis 3 (verses 19, 23)

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- This chapter wraps up the Genesis 3:16 argument and declares both scriptural bases for Adam's sovereignty now examined (Gen. 1:28 from Chapter 4, Gen. 3:16 from this chapter).
- Chapter 6 is "Of Adam's Title to Sovereignty by Fatherhood" -- a new angle of Filmer's argument. The text analysis warns it's very long and heavy on argument.
- Maintain the established pattern of referring to Filmer as "our author" and keeping the prosecutorial-but-amused tone.
- The Queens Mary and Elizabeth reference in this chapter is a nice concrete historical touch; look for similar opportunities to highlight Locke's real-world examples in future chapters.
- Locke's method of granting premises provisionally then showing they don't support the conclusion continues to be central -- keep this rhetorical structure clear.
