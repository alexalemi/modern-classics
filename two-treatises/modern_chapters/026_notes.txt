CHAPTER 026 (Book II, Chapter 15: "Of Paternal, Political, and Despotical Power, Considered Together") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is a synthesis chapter where Locke brings together three types of power he has discussed separately throughout the work -- parental, political, and despotical -- and lays them side by side for comparison. The chapter is relatively short (five substantial paragraphs) but does important structural work, showing that these three kinds of power differ in their origins (nature, consent, forfeiture), their purposes (benefit of child, benefit of subjects, benefit of masters), and their scope. The key payoff is the final paragraph's argument that absolute dominion is incompatible with civil society, since it resembles despotical power rather than political power.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Sentence splitting: Each of Locke's three main numbered paragraphs is a single massive sentence with multiple semicolons and embedded clauses. These were broken into multiple sentences while preserving the logical flow. The paragraph on despotical power was the most complex, with a long chain of participial clauses ("having quitted reason... having renounced the way of peace... and so revolting from his own kind") that was restructured into clearer sequential statements.

2. "Hath/doth" forms: Converted throughout to "has/does" per established convention.

3. "Paternal or parental power": Locke uses both terms. The chapter title uses "paternal" and Locke writes "Paternal or parental power" in the opening definition. I kept both terms on first use and then shifted to "parental" as the default in subsequent references, since it's the more natural modern term and Locke himself signals the equivalence.

4. "Municipal law": Kept this in a paraphrase -- "the specific laws of their country" -- since the original term "municipal law" in Locke's sense (meaning the positive law of a particular jurisdiction, not city ordinances) would confuse modern readers.

5. "Pretence": Converted to "basis for claiming" per vocabulary mapping (pretence = claim/argument).

6. "Neither nature gives... nor compact can convey": Restructured into two clearer clauses: "neither nature gives... nor can any agreement convey."

7. "Revolting from his own kind to that of beasts": Preserved this vivid image -- the aggressor defects from humanity and joins the animals -- while making the syntax clearer.

8. The footnote marker "11" in the original: This is a note reference in the source text. It was omitted from the modernized text as the notes apparatus is not being carried over.

9. Property definition parenthetical: Locke inserts "(By property I must be understood here, as in other places, to mean that property which men have in their persons as well as goods.)" I preserved this important clarification in a parenthetical, keeping it recognizable as a cross-reference to his consistent usage throughout the work.

10. The final paragraph's tripartite structure: "Paternal power is only where... political, where... and despotical, over such as..." -- this elegant parallel construction was preserved closely, as it's the rhetorical capstone of the chapter.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- Locke's "First... Second... Third..." numbering structure
- The complete argument for each type of power, including origin, purpose, and scope
- The vivid metaphor of aggressors becoming like beasts
- The key distinction: parental power from nature, political power from consent, despotical power from forfeiture
- The important parenthetical definition of "property" as including persons as well as goods
- The concluding argument that absolute dominion is incompatible with civil society
- The final parallel structure comparing the three powers by reference to property

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (027 -- Book II, Ch. 16: "Of Conquest"):
- The threefold distinction between parental, political, and despotical power is now fully established. Chapter 16 will test these categories against the case of conquest.
- "Despotical power" has been clearly defined as arising solely from forfeiture through aggression -- this definition is critical for the conquest chapter, where Locke will argue that even a just conqueror has only limited rights over the conquered.
- The principle that political power originates solely from consent and agreement is firmly restated here. The conquest chapter will reinforce this by showing that conquest alone cannot create legitimate political authority.
- "Property" has again been glossed as including life, liberty, and possessions. This broad definition will be important as Locke discusses what a conqueror can and cannot claim over conquered peoples.
- The tone should remain measured and precise. Chapter 16 is a substantial chapter that works through the limits of conquest carefully and methodically.
