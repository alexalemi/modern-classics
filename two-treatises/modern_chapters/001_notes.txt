Chapter 001 (Book I, Chapter 1) Translation Notes

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "estate of man" in the famous opening line preserved verbatim per text analysis guidance on famous passages. The rest of the opening sentence is lightly modernized.
- "our author" used when Locke uses it (section 2), with Filmer named explicitly elsewhere.
- "thraldom" -> "servitude" (more accessible but retains the gravity).
- "jure divino" -> "by divine right" per vocabulary mappings.
- "viz." -> "namely" per vocabulary mappings.
- "engagements" -> "oaths and promises" / "conditions" depending on context.
- "compact or consent" -> "agreements or give consent" -- "compact" mapped to "agreements" per text analysis.
- "Men are not born free" -> "People are not born free" per guidance on gendered language, except in the direct quotation blocks where Locke is paraphrasing Filmer's position (kept closer to original there for fidelity to what Filmer actually argued).

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Preserved Locke's section numbers (1-5) as they appear in the original.
- Broke Locke's massive sentences into shorter ones while preserving logical flow. The first paragraph of the original is a single enormous sentence; it became three sentences.
- Maintained Locke's dry wit and sarcasm, especially the "rope of sand" metaphor, the "chains are a bad look" modernization of "chains are but an ill wearing," and the "neat little system" / "gold standard of political theory" renderings.
- The set-apart quotation blocks ("All government is absolute monarchy" and "No one is born free") preserved as Locke formatted them -- these are Filmer's core claims that the entire Book I will refute.
- "as fashionable as French was at court" -- kept this comparison as it conveys the social-climbing mockery Locke intends.
- References to Sibthorp and Manwaring left without gloss -- Locke himself doesn't explain them, treating them as common knowledge for his audience. Future assembly could add a footnote if desired.

TONE:
- This chapter sets Locke's prosecutorial, slightly contemptuous tone for all of Book I. He's about to spend eleven chapters demolishing Filmer, and here he's telling the reader why it's worth doing while making clear he finds the whole enterprise somewhat beneath him.
- The swagger is important -- Locke is confident, even cocky. He's genuinely amazed that anyone takes Filmer seriously.

REFERENCES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Filmer's Patriarcha and its core claims have been introduced. The next chapter (002, "Of Paternal and Regal Power") will begin the systematic refutation.
- "Our author" has been established as referring to Filmer.
- The section numbering system is in use (sections 1-5 in this chapter).
- The divine-right doctrine has been framed as a recent novelty, not ancient tradition -- this is a key rhetorical move Locke will build on.
- Heyward, Blackwood, and Barclay have been mentioned as defenders of royal power who nonetheless accepted natural liberty -- Locke will return to these figures.
- The "rope of sand" metaphor sets up the expectation that Locke will now show exactly how flimsy Filmer's arguments are.
