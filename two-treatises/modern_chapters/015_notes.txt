Chapter 015 (Book II, Chapter 4: "Of Slavery") -- Translation Notes

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE:

1. Opening paragraph: Locke's single massive paragraph opening was broken into two
   clear movements -- first defining natural liberty, then defining liberty under
   government. The Filmer quotation is preserved exactly. The contrast between
   Filmer's false definition of freedom and Locke's true definition is made more
   explicit with "That's not freedom. Real freedom for people living under
   government means..."

2. "Man" / "his" language: Replaced with gender-neutral forms ("a person,"
   "no one," "they/them") throughout, since Locke is describing universal
   principles that apply to all people. This follows the text analysis guidelines.

3. The self-enslavement argument: Locke's long conditional chain ("for a man,
   not having the power of his own life, cannot, by compact...") was broken into
   sequential logical steps. The core logic -- you can't give away what you don't
   have, you don't own your own life, therefore you can't sell yourself into
   slavery -- is preserved precisely.

4. The "forfeiture" exception: Locke's argument that a person who commits a
   capital crime forfeits their life, and the conqueror/captor may delay taking it
   and use the captive's labor instead, is translated faithfully. The grim logic
   that the captive can always choose death over servitude is preserved without
   editorial commentary, per the text analysis guidelines.

5. "Compact" consistently rendered as "agreement" or "contract" depending on
   context.

6. The Jewish servitude passage: Locke's distinction between "drudgery" (forced
   labor with legal protections and a fixed term) and "slavery" (absolute, arbitrary
   power over life) is made very clear. The Exodus 21 citation is preserved.

7. "hath" rendered as "has" per consistency rules.

VOCABULARY NOTES:
- "compact" -> "agreement" / "contract"
- "lists" (= wishes) -> "whatever they want"
- "despotical" -> "despotic"
- "drudgery" -> kept as "drudgery" (still understood in modern English)

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- "State of war" has been established as a key concept (from Ch. 3) and appears
  here as the basis of slavery -- "the state of war continued." The next chapter
  (Ch. 5, "Of Property") is the famous labor theory chapter and will need careful
  handling of Locke's technical vocabulary.
- "Commonwealth" is kept as is.
- "Legislature" / "legislative power" used consistently.
- Gender-neutral language used for Locke's universal claims.
- The pattern of breaking Locke's long sentences at semicolons and conditional
  chains is working well and should continue.
- Filmer quotation preserved exactly in quotes.
- Biblical citations kept in standard form (Exodus 21).
- Locke's "I confess" / "I'll grant" voice preserved -- shows his willingness
  to acknowledge complications honestly.
