Chapter 012 Translation Notes
Book II, Chapter 1 (Opening of Book II)

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is a brief but structurally important transitional chapter. It serves two purposes: (1) summarizing the four devastating conclusions of Book I's demolition of Filmer, and (2) pivoting to Book II's constructive project by defining political power. The chapter ends with Locke's precise definition of political power -- the right to make laws (up to and including the death penalty) for the regulation and protection of property, to enforce those laws with communal force, to defend the commonwealth from foreign threats, and to do all of this only for the public good. This definition is foundational -- everything in Book II builds on it.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "pretended" (= claimed) rendered as "That whole claim falls apart" to modernize while preserving the dismissive tone
- "pretence" (= claim) rendered as "claim"
- "the least shadow of authority" kept close to original -- vivid and clear
- "fountain of all power" rendered as "wellspring of all power" (slightly more modern while preserving the metaphor)
- "mischief, tumult, sedition, and rebellion" condensed to "chaos, conflict, rebellion, and disorder" -- preserving the rhetorical weight without the archaic feel of four equally weighty terms
- "another rise of government, another original of political power" -- rendered as "a different origin for government, a different source of political power" for clarity
- "hath" rendered as standard modern English per text analysis
- "magistrate over a subject" rendered as "government official over a citizen" for accessibility
- "captain of a galley" kept -- the comparison is vivid and the meaning of "galley ship" is clear enough with the added word "ship"
- "commonwealth" kept per text analysis conventions

STYLE DECISIONS:
- The opening is one enormous sentence in the original -- a cascading series of "That..." clauses building to a climax. I broke this into separate paragraphs for each premise, preserving the logical stacking but making each step digestible. The final "All these premises having been clearly made out..." paragraph then delivers the accumulated punch.
- Preserved Locke's "even if" conditional chain -- this is his signature rhetorical move, and it's especially effective here as a summary of all of Book I. Each "even if" concedes more ground only to show it doesn't matter.
- The tone shifts noticeably from Book I's prosecutorial demolition. This chapter has a "let's start fresh" energy -- Locke is done with Filmer and ready to build his own theory. The translation reflects this by being cleaner and more direct.
- The final paragraph defining political power is rendered as clearly as possible since it is the foundational definition for all of Book II. Kept the structure but made the components explicit.
- "Things that the followers of that hypothesis so loudly cry out against" -- this is a pointed bit of irony (Filmer's supporters decry rebellion, but their theory is what causes it). Rendered as "the very things that the followers of this theory so loudly complain about" to preserve the ironic sting.

STRUCTURAL NOTES:
- This chapter has no numbered sections (section numbers begin in Chapter 2/013 with section 4).
- "BOOK II" header and "CHAPTER I" preserved to mark the transition clearly.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 013 is "Of the State of Nature" -- one of the most important chapters in the entire history of political philosophy. The opening definition of the state of nature as a condition of perfect freedom and equality is foundational. It directly influenced Jefferson's Declaration of Independence.
- Key terms introduced: "state of nature," "law of nature," "reason" (as natural law). These must be handled consistently from here forward.
- Locke includes a lengthy quotation from Richard Hooker (Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity) -- this should be clearly marked as a quotation and Hooker briefly identified.
- The argument that everyone has the right to punish transgressors of natural law is Locke's distinctive contribution -- it needs to be rendered with precision.
- Tone continues the constructive, measured quality established in this transitional chapter, but with increasing philosophical depth and occasional passion.
