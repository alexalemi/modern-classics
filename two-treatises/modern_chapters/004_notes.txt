Chapter 004 Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is Book I, Chapter 4, "Of Adam's Title to Sovereignty, by Donation, Gen. 1:28."
It is one of the longest and most exegetically dense chapters in Book I. Locke performs
a close textual analysis of Genesis 1:28, examining the Hebrew words and comparing them
across multiple biblical passages to prove that God's grant to Adam was over animals,
not over other people, and was given to humanity collectively, not to Adam privately.

HEBREW AND GREEK TERMS:
- chayyah haromeshet (Hebrew) -- glossed with the Latin "bestiam reptantem" (creeping beast)
  and explained contextually. Kept transliterated throughout.
- chayyah -- used multiple times. Consistently transliterated and identified as "wild beasts."
- haromeshet / romesh -- transliterated and identified as "creeping things" / "moving things."
- theria (Greek, from Septuagint) -- glossed as "wild beasts."
- herpeta (Greek, from Septuagint) -- glossed as "reptiles."
- The Septuagint is glossed on first mention as "the ancient Greek translation of the
  Hebrew scriptures."

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" -- preserved when Locke uses it, per text analysis guidelines. Filmer's name
  used where Locke uses "Sir Robert" or where clarity requires it.
- "private dominion" -- kept as Locke's key term throughout; it means exclusive personal
  ownership as opposed to shared/common rights.
- "commons" / "community" -- kept as Locke uses them (shared use vs. exclusive ownership).
- "compact" -- used once near the end; translated as "compact" since Locke is drawing a
  technical distinction about the source of authority (consent vs. property).
- "propriety" / "property" -- Locke uses both interchangeably for ownership. Standardized
  to "property" throughout except where "ownership" reads more naturally.
- "charter" -- preserved; Locke uses it with deliberate irony (Gen. 1:28 as Adam's
  supposed "royal charter").
- "enlargement of commons" -- kept as Locke's phrase, since it's a term he's quoting from
  Filmer and then dismantling.

STYLE DECISIONS:
- The chapter has a clear two-part structure (marked 1 and 2 by Locke), which I preserved.
  Part 1: the Hebrew word analysis proving "living things" means animals, not people.
  Part 2: the grant was plural ("them"), so it was to humanity collectively.
- After the two numbered proofs, Locke addresses Filmer's attempts to deny community in
  the Noah passage (Gen. 9:1-3), then handles the "even if Adam owned everything" objection.
- Broke Locke's very long sentences (some 150+ words) into multiple sentences while
  preserving logical connectives (therefore, so, but, since, etc.).
- Preserved Locke's dry humor, especially: the "monarchs in habit" joke about discovery,
  the remark about Noah's sons needing permission to sleep with their wives, the observation
  that Filmer cares about monarchs but not about people, the knife-at-the-throat analogy.
- Preserved Locke's signature "even if we grant your point" structure, especially in the
  final section about property and sovereignty.
- The Turkish government comparison is kept as-is; it's Locke's contemporary evidence for
  the failure of absolute monarchy.

BIBLICAL CITATIONS:
- All citations preserved in standard form (Gen. 1:28, Gen. 9:2, Psalm 8, 1 Tim. 6:17, etc.).
- Direct biblical quotations lightly modernized (e.g., "moveth" -> "moves") but kept close
  to the original phrasing.

THINGS FOR THE NEXT CHAPTER TRANSLATOR TO KNOW:
- Locke has now completed his two-part argument against Gen. 1:28 as a source of Adam's
  sovereignty: (1) the words refer to animals not people, (2) the grant was to humanity
  collectively. These conclusions are referred back to later.
- The distinction between "dominion over creatures" and "dominion over people" is now
  firmly established and recurs throughout Book I.
- The argument about property vs. sovereignty (owning things doesn't give you power over
  people) is introduced here and becomes important in Book II's property chapter.
- The charity/surplus argument near the end (the needy have a right to others' surplus)
  anticipates Book II's discussion of property limits.
- "Our author" for Filmer is well established and should continue.
- The Noah passage (Gen. 9:1-3) has been fully analyzed here; later chapters may
  reference it.
- Locke's references to "Observations" (O.) with page numbers are Filmer's other work;
  keep citing them as the original does.
