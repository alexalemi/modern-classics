Chapter 014 Translation Notes
Book II, Chapter 3: "Of the State of War"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is a pivotal chapter in Locke's theory, establishing the critical distinction between the state of nature and the state of war -- a distinction aimed squarely at Hobbes, who had equated them. Locke argues that the state of war arises whenever someone uses or declares force against another without right. Key moves: (1) anyone who threatens your life puts themselves into a state of war with you and may be destroyed like a predator; (2) anyone who attempts to get you under their absolute power is effectively declaring war, because absolute power implies the intent to enslave; (3) this justifies killing even a thief who hasn't hurt you, because the use of illegitimate force implies a willingness to take everything; (4) the state of nature is peace and reason without a common judge, while the state of war is force without right; (5) the state of war can exist even within organized society when justice is perverted; (6) avoiding the state of war is a primary reason people form governments; (7) when no earthly judge exists, the appeal lies to heaven -- and only the individual can judge in conscience whether that threshold has been reached.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "enmity and destruction" -- kept "hostility and destruction" for natural modern phrasing
- "sedate settled design" -- rendered as "calm, deliberate plan"
- "espouses his quarrel" -- rendered as "takes up their cause"
- "noxious creatures" -- rendered as "harmful creatures"
- "i.e. make me a slave" -- rendered as "in other words, to make me a slave"
- "the fence to it" (= the protection of it) -- rendered as "the freedom that protects it"
- "pretence" (= excuse/claim) -- rendered as "stated excuse"
- "want of such an appeal" -- rendered as "lack of such an appeal"
- "want of positive laws" -- rendered as "which lacks established laws"
- "barefaced wresting of the laws" -- rendered as "brazen twisting of the laws"
- "indemnify" -- rendered as "shield"
- "coloured with the name, pretences, or forms of law" -- rendered as "dressed up with the name, claims, or procedures of law"
- "bona fide" -- rendered as "in good faith" (still widely understood either way)
- "every the least difference" -- rendered as "even the smallest disagreement"
- "commonwealth" -- kept as "commonwealth" per text analysis guidelines
- "the great day" -- kept as is, referring to the Day of Judgment

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This chapter features some of Locke's longest and most architecturally complex sentences. The opening sentence of section 16 is a single sentence of over 170 words. Broke it into multiple sentences while preserving the logical flow and the building intensity of the argument.
- Preserved Locke's signature move of using predator analogies (wolf, lion, beasts of prey) for those who abandon the law of reason. This imagery is powerful and recurring.
- The thief example (section 18 / paragraph 4) is one of Locke's most vivid illustrations -- a person may kill a thief who attacks them on the road even though they could only sue a thief who stole their entire fortune. Kept the logical punch of this paradox clear and immediate.
- The distinction between state of nature and state of war (paragraph 4) is one of the most important passages in the chapter. Presented it with clear parallel structure to highlight the contrast Locke is drawing.
- The passage about perverted justice (paragraph 5) -- where courts and judges exist but justice is deliberately twisted -- is remarkably relevant and was translated to preserve its force. Locke's point that violence dressed up in legal forms is still violence needed to land clearly.
- The Jephthah passage (final paragraph) introduces the "appeal to heaven" concept that recurs throughout Book II. Kept the biblical reference and quotation intact.
- Preserved Locke's practice of gendering language as "person/they" for generic references per text analysis guidelines, except where preserving specific rhetorical examples (the thief scenarios).

BIBLICAL REFERENCES PRESERVED:
- Judg. 11:27 (Jephthah and the Ammonites -- "The Lord the Judge be judge this day")

CONTRA HOBBES NOTE:
- The line "which however some men have confounded" is a direct shot at Hobbes, who in Leviathan argued that the state of nature IS a state of war ("war of every man against every man"). Locke's entire point in this chapter is that they are fundamentally different things. This is translated clearly without adding editorial commentary, but the force of the disagreement comes through in the "some people have mixed these two up" rendering.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 015 is Book II, Chapter 4: "Of Slavery." It's brief but important.
- Locke argues that no one can consent to slavery or give someone absolute, arbitrary power over their life. True political freedom means being under no legislative power except what is established by consent.
- The chapter connects back to the state of war concept from this chapter: Locke argues that slavery is really just a continuation of the state of war between a conqueror and a captive.
- Key terms to maintain consistently: "state of war," "state of nature," "absolute power," "commonwealth," "law of nature," "appeal to heaven."
- The "appeal to heaven" concept introduced at the end of this chapter will recur significantly in later chapters, especially in the final chapter on the dissolution of government.
- Locke's tone continues to be constructive and measured in these foundational chapters of Book II, building the theoretical framework before the more passionate later chapters on tyranny and revolution.
