CHAPTER 022 (Book II, Chapter 11: "Of the Extent of the Legislative Power") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is one of the most historically significant chapters in the entire work. It sets out four fundamental limits on what the legislature -- the supreme power in any commonwealth -- can do. These four limits directly influenced the American Founders and the architecture of constitutional government worldwide: (1) No arbitrary power over life and property; (2) Government must rule by established, standing laws, not ad hoc decrees; (3) No taxation without consent; (4) The legislature cannot delegate its lawmaking power. The chapter begins with a powerful statement about why the legislature is supreme, then systematically constrains that supremacy with these four principles.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. The enormous opening section: The original is a single paragraph of staggering length, with multiple semicolons chaining clause after clause. I broke it into three paragraphs: one establishing the legislature as supreme, one explaining why (consent), and one on the implications for obedience and oaths.

2. "The legislative" as noun: Consistently rendered as "the legislature" per established convention, except where Locke is clearly referring to the legislative power abstractly rather than to the body itself.

3. "It is not, nor can possibly be absolutely arbitrary over the lives and fortunes of the people": Preserved this strong phrasing closely, as it's one of the chapter's key declarations. Broke the subsequent long chain of reasoning into shorter sentences while preserving every logical step.

4. The "no man can transfer more power than he has" argument: This is the philosophical core of the first limit. I made the chain of reasoning explicit: nobody has arbitrary power over themselves -> therefore nobody can give arbitrary power to someone else -> therefore the legislature can't have arbitrary power.

5. "Extemporary, arbitrary decrees" vs. "promulgated, standing laws": Rendered as "sudden, arbitrary decrees" vs. "publicly announced, standing laws." The word "promulgated" is still in legal use but "publicly announced" is more immediately accessible.

6. The long argument about why people wouldn't put themselves in a worse position than the state of nature: This is a single enormous paragraph in the original. I broke it into multiple paragraphs, each advancing one step of the argument, while preserving the rhetorical force -- especially the vivid comparison about the person commanding 100,000 soldiers.

7. The soldier example (section on property and taxation): This is one of Locke's most brilliant concrete illustrations. The sergeant who can order you to die but can't take a penny of your money -- I kept this vivid and punchy, preserving the specific details (marching into cannon-fire, standing in a breach) and the logical punch of the contrast.

8. "No penny" / "no farthing": Modernized "one penny" to "a single penny" and "one farthing" to "one cent" for immediate accessibility. "One jot of his goods" became "the smallest bit of his goods."

9. The taxation passage: "If anyone shall claim a power to lay and levy taxes on the people, by his own authority, and without such consent of the people, he thereby invades the fundamental law of property." This is the "no taxation without consent" principle that would echo through the American Revolution. I kept it close to Locke's language while clarifying the syntax.

10. The fourth limit (non-delegation): This is a tightly argued passage about why the legislature can't transfer its lawmaking power. The key distinction -- "only to make laws, and not to make legislators" -- is preserved closely as it's a brilliant formulation.

11. The closing summary: Locke restates all four limits in compressed, declarative form. I preserved the parallel structure and the memorable phrasing -- especially "one rule for rich and poor, for the favourite at court, and the countryman at plough," which I modernized to "for the court favorite and the farmer at the plow."

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- All four fundamental limits on legislative power, with full argumentation
- The logical chain: people in state of nature -> limited natural power -> can't transfer more than they have -> legislature inherits those limits
- The distinction between supreme and absolute power
- The soldier/sergeant/general illustration in full detail
- The principle of no taxation without consent
- The non-delegation doctrine
- The closing four-point summary
- Locke's numbered structure (First, Second, Third, Fourth)
- The measured, authoritative tone appropriate to this enormously influential chapter
