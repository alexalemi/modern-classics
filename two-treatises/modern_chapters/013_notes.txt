Chapter 013 Translation Notes
Book II, Chapter 2: "Of the State of Nature"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is one of the most important chapters in the history of political philosophy. Locke defines the "state of nature" as the condition all people are naturally in before they form political societies: a state of perfect freedom and equality, governed not by chaos but by the law of nature (which is reason). Key arguments: (1) all people are naturally free and equal; (2) the law of nature (reason) prohibits harming others in their life, health, liberty, or possessions; (3) in the state of nature, every person has the right -- and the duty -- to enforce the law of nature, including punishing transgressors; (4) punishment must be proportional and serve only two purposes: reparation and deterrence; (5) the right to punish divides into a universal right (belonging to everyone) and a particular right to seek compensation (belonging to the injured party); (6) civil government is the proper remedy for the inconveniences of the state of nature, but absolute monarchy is actually worse than the state of nature; (7) the state of nature is not hypothetical -- all independent rulers are in it with respect to one another. Locke cites Hooker twice to ground his position in established authority, and uses the example of punishing foreign nationals to prove that natural law enforcement must exist.

SECTION NUMBERING:
Locke's original section numbers 4-15 preserved. These pick up from chapter 012 (Book II, Ch. 1), which contained sections 1-3. This numbering system is used by scholars for citation and is essential to maintain.

FAMOUS PASSAGES:
- Section 4: The opening definition of the state of nature as "a state of perfect freedom" and "a state of equality" -- kept very close to original, only lightly modernized. This passage directly influenced Jefferson's "all men are created equal."
- Section 6: "The state of nature has a law of nature to govern it, which obliges everyone. And reason, which is that law..." -- identified in text_analysis.txt as a passage to preserve near-verbatim. Kept extremely close, only splitting the surrounding sentence structure.
- Section 8: "every person has a right to punish the offender and act as an enforcer of the law of nature" -- Locke's distinctive argument that enforcement of natural law belongs to everyone.
- Section 11: "Whoever sheds man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed" (Gen. 9:6) and the Cain example -- preserved intact.

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This chapter marks the true beginning of Locke's positive political philosophy (Book II, Ch. 1 was transitional). The tone shifts to constructive theory-building -- confident, measured, but with real conviction. Translated accordingly, with a voice that conveys both intellectual authority and accessibility.
- The Hooker quotation in section 5 is long and complex. Modernized the language while preserving the chain of reasoning and keeping it as a block quotation, since Locke presents it as authoritative support.
- The Hooker quotation in section 15 similarly modernized but kept as a block quotation.
- Section 6 contains the chapter's theological grounding (we are God's workmanship, his property, sent into the world on his business). Preserved this reasoning faithfully -- it is essential to Locke's argument, not mere decoration.
- Section 8's metaphor of the offender as "a lion or a tiger" is vivid and effective -- preserved.
- Section 13 contains one of Locke's most brilliant rhetorical moves: granting that the state of nature has problems (people judging their own cases), then turning the objection against absolute monarchy by pointing out that an absolute monarch is just one person judging their own case with no accountability whatsoever. This argumentative structure (concede, then reverse) is Locke's signature and was preserved with care.
- Broke Locke's longest sentences into shorter units while maintaining logical flow through connective words ("here is why," "so here's the point," "and so").
- Used em-dashes for emphasis and parenthetical asides, following the established style from earlier chapters.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 014 is Book II, Chapter 3: "Of the State of War." This is a critical companion to the present chapter. Locke distinguishes the state of nature from the state of war -- a key move against Hobbes, who equated them. The state of nature is peaceful (governed by reason); the state of war begins when someone uses force without right.
- Continue the constructive, measured tone of Book II.
- Maintain consistent use of "state of nature," "law of nature," "state of war" as technical terms.
- Watch for Locke's careful definitions -- he is building a precise conceptual vocabulary in these early Book II chapters that will carry through the rest of the work.
- The section numbering continues from this chapter (sections 16-21 in chapter 014).
- "Commonwealth" and "body politic" should continue to be preserved as-is.
