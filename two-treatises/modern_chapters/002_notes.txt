Chapter 002 (Book I, Chapter 2: "Of Paternal and Regal Power") - Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This chapter is Locke's opening salvo against Filmer's method. Rather than attacking the substance of Filmer's arguments (that comes later), Locke attacks the fact that Filmer never actually defines or proves his central concept -- "fatherly authority." Locke compiles a damning collection of Filmer's own quotes to show how extreme the doctrine is, then points out that Filmer offers essentially zero proof for it.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" / "our A." -- Kept "our author" throughout as per text analysis. This preserves Locke's signature sarcastic detachment from Filmer.
- "en passant" -- Translated to "in passing" in context, rendered as "a passing bow."
- "arcana imperii" -- Translated as "mysteries of government" for readability.
- Greek phrase "Anthropine ktisis" -- Dropped the Greek and rendered the meaning: "the consent of the people, using their reason to join together in society."
- "caput inter nubila" (Latin: "its head among the clouds") -- Omitted the Latin, but preserved the sense with "dizzying heights" and "so far above every earthly thing."
- "fain" -- Replaced with "forced" ("I have been forced to burden my reader...").
- "bating" -- Replaced with "apart from."
- "pretence" -- Translated as "claim" or "argument" depending on context.
- "estate" (= condition/possessions) -- Used "possessions" where Locke means property, "condition" where he means state.
- "viz." -- Replaced with "namely."
- Jean Bodin -- Added brief identification ("the political theorist") on first mention, since Locke introduces Bodin's quotes without identifying him.

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Broke Locke's extremely long sentences into shorter ones while preserving the logical chain. The opening paragraph of the original is one enormous sentence; I split it into three.
- The long passage of Filmer quotations (section starting "Not only Adam...") is preserved in full. These are crucial -- Locke is building his case by letting Filmer hang himself with his own words. I lightly modernized the language within the quotes while keeping them clearly as quotations.
- Preserved Locke's dry wit throughout: the "careful doctor" metaphor for Filmer disguising his extreme claims, the sarcastic listing of Filmer's rhetorical moves in the Patriarcha (bowing, complimenting, then destroying), the devastating "Honor your mother" rebuttal.
- The "Honor your father/mother" argument is one of Locke's sharpest comic moments in Book I. Kept it punchy.
- Preserved the rhetorical structure where Locke says "I expected... but instead" -- this patient demolition is his signature move.

REFERENCES:
- Bellarmine = Robert Bellarmine, Jesuit cardinal and political theorist, whose concession Filmer claims as victory.
- Mr. Hunton = Philip Hunton, author of A Treatise of Monarchy (1643).
- "Observations" / "O." = Filmer's Observations Concerning the Original of Government (upon Hobbes, Aristotle, etc.).
- Bodin = Jean Bodin, French political philosopher.
- "Anarchy of a Mixed Monarchy" = another of Filmer's works.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Locke has now established his method: he will take Filmer's arguments one by one. The three arguments he identifies at the very end of this chapter (God's creation of Adam, the dominion over Eve, the dominion as father) will structure the next several chapters of Book I.
- The sarcastic-but-methodical tone is set. Locke is confident and dismissive of Filmer's logical rigor, but always shows his work.
- "Our author" is the standard way to refer to Filmer -- keep using it.
- Page references to Filmer's works should continue to be preserved as Locke gives them.
- The "Honor your father and mother" argument is flagged as something Locke will return to later -- watch for it in subsequent chapters.
