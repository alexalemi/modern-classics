Chapter 008 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 8: "Of the Conveyance of Adam's Sovereign Monarchical Power"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is a short, sharp chapter where Locke catches Filmer in a devastating self-contradiction. Locke catalogs the different methods Filmer gives (across his various writings) for how Adam's sovereign power gets transferred to later rulers: sometimes it's inheritance alone, sometimes grant or inheritance, sometimes inheritance or usurpation, sometimes all three, and finally "any means at all." Locke gleefully points out that this last position -- that anyone governing by supreme power is "properly a king" regardless of how they got power -- demolishes Filmer's own theory. If it doesn't matter how you get power, then the whole edifice about Adam's authority, heirs, and inheritance was pointless. Locke then mocks this conclusion with vivid examples (Cromwell, Masaniello the fisherman-rebel, Sancho Panza) before setting up the agenda for the remaining chapters of Book I.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, per established convention
- "O. 155" / "O. 158" / "O. 190" / "O. 253" references to Filmer's Observations kept intact
- "p. 19" and "p. 100" page references preserved
- "It skills not" -- kept in the direct quotation from Filmer (archaic for "it doesn't matter"), as Locke is quoting Filmer's exact words
- "commonwealth" did not appear in this chapter
- "alienable by patent" kept in the direct quotation from Filmer
- "Masaniello" -- added brief gloss ("the Neapolitan fisherman who briefly seized power") since modern readers won't know this reference
- "Sancho Panza" -- spelled in the modern conventional way rather than Locke's "Pancha"

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Preserved the escalating structure of Locke's trap: he lists Filmer's contradictory positions one after another, building to the devastating final quotation where Filmer effectively says it doesn't matter at all.
- Broke the very long opening paragraph into shorter paragraphs at natural logical breaks (each new position Filmer takes gets its own paragraph grouping) for readability, while maintaining the sense of accumulation.
- The Cromwell/Masaniello/Sancho Panza passage is peak Locke wit -- translated with care to keep the humor landing naturally without amplification. The fisherman-to-king joke is genuinely funny and was kept vivid.
- "Which in plain English means" -- Locke's own phrase, preserved as it shows him cutting through pretense.
- The closing paragraph's "even if it had been perfectly proved" preserves Locke's signature conditional-concession structure.
- All direct quotations from Filmer preserved in their original wording, since Locke is quoting them as evidence of contradiction.

HISTORICAL REFERENCES:
- Oliver Cromwell: Lord Protector of England (1653-1658), ruled without royal title
- Masaniello (Tommaso Aniello d'Amalfi): Neapolitan fisherman who led a revolt in 1647 and briefly ruled Naples before being assassinated
- Don Quixote / Sancho Panza: Cervantes' novel (1605/1615), in which Sancho Panza is made governor of an "island" as a joke
