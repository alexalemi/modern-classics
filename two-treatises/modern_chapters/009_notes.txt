Chapter 009 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 9: "Of Monarchy, by Inheritance from Adam"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This chapter is Locke's systematic argument that even if Adam had sovereignty (which Locke denies), it could not have been inherited. Locke's key moves: (1) Government requires knowing who specifically has the right to rule, not just that government should exist; (2) Adam's property right (from God's donation) gave equal rights to all his children, not just one heir; (3) the natural basis of inheritance is children's right to sustenance, not political succession; (4) property and government are fundamentally different -- property exists for the owner's benefit, government for the governed's benefit; (5) fatherhood is a personal power arising from the act of begetting and cannot be transferred or inherited; (6) therefore sovereignty built on either property or fatherhood dies with the holder.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, maintaining Locke's characteristic detachment
- "private dominion" kept as Locke's specific term (exclusive personal ownership vs. common rights)
- "paternal power" / "conjugal power" kept as-is; both are clear enough in context and Locke deliberately contrasts them
- "commonwealth" did not appear in this chapter
- "body politic" kept in the passage about government benefiting all members
- "state of nature" used once near the end of the inheritance discussion, kept as Locke's technical term
- "compact" rendered as "agreement" or "contract" depending on context
- "behoof" rendered as "benefit" or "good" throughout
- "fain" / "bating" did not appear
- "viz." rendered as "namely" throughout

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Broke Locke's extremely long opening sentence (the entire first paragraph of the original is essentially one vast sentence) into multiple clear sentences while preserving the logical chain.
- Preserved the key structural move of this chapter: Locke's two-pronged test (power must be inheritable AND traceable to current rulers) that frames the entire argument.
- Maintained Locke's careful distinction between three sources of authority (paternal right, consent, divine appointment) and his argument that each has different rules of succession.
- Preserved the vivid examples: the pirates-vs-princes analogy, the changing-doctors comparison, the foster-father scenario, the Adam-dying-before-Eve thought experiment, the Saul/David/Solomon/Benjamin succession examples.
- Kept the dry humor, especially in the passage about Adam's "hollow, meaningless, useless property" and the reductio arguments about inheriting conjugal power.
- The long middle section on natural inheritance (children's right to parents' goods) is Locke at his most careful and philosophical. Broke the very long paragraphs into more digestible pieces while preserving the logical flow.
- Preserved the parallel structure of Locke's closing: property couldn't convey sovereignty to an heir, fatherhood couldn't convey sovereignty to an heir, therefore Adam's imaginary monarchy dies with him.

BIBLICAL REFERENCES PRESERVED:
- Gen. 1:28 (the donation to Adam, central to the property argument)
- Gen. 25:23 (Jacob and Esau -- implied in the Saul/David/Benjamin discussion)
- "Honor your parents" (the commandment, referenced in the foster-father example)
- Saul, David, Jonathan, Solomon, Benjamin (biblical succession examples)
- "Terror to evildoers" (biblical phrase about the magistrate's sword)

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- This chapter concludes that Adam's sovereignty, whether based on property or fatherhood, could not be inherited. Chapter 10 will examine whether Adam even had an heir of the type Filmer describes.
- The distinction between property (for owner's benefit) and government (for the governed's benefit) is now firmly established and will recur.
- The three sources of authority (paternal right, consent, divine appointment) with their different succession rules are set up here and will be relevant going forward.
- The natural-law basis of inheritance (children's right to sustenance) is thoroughly argued here; this connects to Book II's property chapter.
- The foster-father example is notable -- Locke argues that performing the care of a parent, not biological connection, creates parental authority. This anticipates Book II's discussion of paternal power.
- Locke's closing line about examining "whether Adam had any such heir" sets up Chapter 10 directly.
- Continue using "our author" for Filmer and maintaining the prosecutorial but methodical tone.
