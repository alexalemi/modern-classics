CHAPTER 024 (Book II, Chapter 13: "Of the Subordination of the Powers of the Commonwealth") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This chapter covers sections 149-158 and establishes the hierarchy of powers within a commonwealth. The legislature is supreme over all other powers, but is itself held in trust by the people, who retain the ultimate authority to replace it if it betrays that trust. Locke also addresses the relationship between the executive and the legislature (especially when the executive shares in legislative power, as in the English monarchy), the power to convene and dismiss the legislature, and the problem of unequal representation -- making a case for redistricting as a legitimate use of executive prerogative.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Section 149 -- the massive opening paragraph: The original is a single paragraph that runs the entire length of the section, piling up clause after clause across multiple logical steps. I broke it into four paragraphs, each covering one step: (a) the legislature is supreme, (b) but it's held in trust and the people can revoke it, (c) the people always retain the right of self-preservation, (d) the community is supreme but this power only activates when government dissolves. The logical flow is preserved through explicit connective phrases.

2. "Fiduciary power" -- Locke uses this legal term in section 149 and again in section 156. I kept it but immediately glossed it as "a power held in trust" on first use. By section 156, the concept is established enough to use "fiduciary trust" without a gloss.

3. Section 151 -- the status of the king: This is Locke's careful analysis of how a monarch who shares in legislative power can be called "supreme" in one sense (executive authority) but is not supreme in the full sense (lawmaking authority). The original is a single dense paragraph. I broke it into three paragraphs to separate: (a) in what sense the monarch is "supreme," (b) the nature of allegiance as obedience to law, and (c) what happens when the monarch abandons the public role. The final image -- the monarch "degrading" himself into a private person without authority -- is one of Locke's most powerful passages in this chapter, so I kept it punchy.

4. "Image, phantom, or representative of the commonwealth" -- Locke's striking phrase describing the monarch as merely a vessel for the law. I kept all three terms as Locke uses them; "phantom" is especially vivid and worth preserving.

5. Section 153 -- the legislature's session schedule: Converted "the legislative" (noun) to "the legislature" per established convention. Broke the long conditional chain about when the legislature may assemble into clearer sequential statements. The parenthetical about a single-person legislature was kept as a parenthetical since it's genuinely a side note.

6. Section 155 -- force against the legislature: This is a pivotal passage where Locke states that using force to prevent the legislature from meeting is an act of war against the people. I structured this as a question-and-answer format to match Locke's own rhetorical move ("It may be demanded here... I say..."). The famous principle "the true remedy of force without authority is to oppose force to it" is kept close to verbatim.

7. Section 157 -- the rotten boroughs passage: This is Locke's discussion of unequal representation, which transparently refers to the English system of parliamentary boroughs. The vivid image of a town that's nothing but a name -- "scarce so much housing as a sheepcote, or more inhabitants than a shepherd" -- sending as many representatives as a thriving county is one of Locke's most concrete illustrations. I modernized the language while keeping the rhetorical power of the contrast.

8. Section 158 -- "Salus populi suprema lex": Translated the Latin maxim directly as "The welfare of the people is the supreme law" and integrated it into the text rather than leaving it in Latin. This is one of the most famous principles of English constitutional law.

9. "The legislative" as noun: Consistently rendered as "the legislature" throughout, per established convention.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- The complete argument structure across all 10 sections (149-158)
- The hierarchy: people > legislature > executive > all other powers
- The fiduciary/trust theory of legislative power
- The analysis of the monarch's dual role (executive + legislative participant)
- The principle that allegiance is obedience to law, not to a person
- The powerful image of the monarch who abandons the law becoming "a single private person without power"
- The right to resist force used without authority
- The distinction between fixed and discretionary legislative sessions
- The rotten boroughs critique with its vivid shepherd-and-sheepcote illustration
- The salus populi maxim and its application to redistricting
- The argument that fair redistricting is restoration, not innovation
- All section numbers (149-158)

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (025 -- Book II, Ch. 14: "Of Prerogative"):
- "Prerogative" has been introduced in sections 156 and 158 as the executive's power to act for the public good in situations where rigid laws are inadequate. Chapter 025 will develop this concept extensively, so the groundwork is laid.
- The fiduciary/trust framework is well established: government power is held in trust from the people. This framework will apply to prerogative as well.
- The executive has been characterized as subordinate to the legislature in principle, but with significant practical autonomy when the executive also shares in legislative power. The prerogative chapter will explore the boundaries of that autonomy.
- The principle that "whatever is done clearly for the good of the people... is and always will be legitimate prerogative" sets up the test that Chapter 025 will elaborate: prerogative is legitimate when exercised for the public good, illegitimate when used against it.
- Maintain the distinction between prerogative as a concept (executive discretion for the public good) and prerogative as a danger (executive overreach). Locke sees both sides.
