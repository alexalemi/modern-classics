CHAPTER 021 (Book II, Chapter 10: "Of the Forms of a Commonwealth") — Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is one of the shortest chapters in the entire work -- just two sections (132-133). Section 132 is a concise taxonomy of government forms (democracy, oligarchy, monarchy and its variants, and mixed systems), with the key principle that the form of government is determined by where the lawmaking power is placed. Section 133 is a definitional note clarifying that Locke uses "commonwealth" to mean any independent political community, not "democracy" specifically.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Sentence splitting: The original section 132 is a single enormous sentence that runs through the entire taxonomy of government forms, piling clause upon clause with semicolons and colons. I broke this into seven separate paragraphs, each covering one government form or one logical step, to make the argument easy to follow.

2. "The legislative" as noun: Converted to "the legislature" per the established convention, except in the parenthetical where Locke uses "the legislative" as an abstract concept referring to lawmaking power -- there I used "the legislature" for consistency while preserving the logical point.

3. "The majority having... the whole power of the community naturally in them": Modernized this complex introductory construction into a clearer statement with a cross-reference to the earlier argument ("As I've already shown").

4. "The Latines signified by the word civitas": Simplified to "what the Romans expressed with the word civitas" -- "Latines" is just Locke's term for Latin speakers/Romans.

5. "I crave leave to use": Modernized to "I've chosen to use" -- the original is a polite 17th-century request formula.

6. The final sentence of section 133 -- "which if anybody dislike, I consent with him to change it for a better" -- shows a flash of Locke's dry humor and flexibility. I preserved the wit while making the syntax more natural.

7. Added section numbers (132, 133) explicitly at the start of each section, as these are the standard scholarly reference numbers.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- The complete taxonomy: democracy, oligarchy, hereditary monarchy, elective monarchy, mixed forms
- The key theoretical principle: form of government = placement of legislative power
- The parenthetical about inferior vs. superior powers
- The definitional note about "commonwealth" and its Latin equivalent civitas
- The reference to King James I's usage
- Locke's genial tone in the definitional aside

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (022 — Book II, Ch. 11: "Of the Extent of the Legislative Power"):
- "Commonwealth" has been explicitly defined as meaning any independent community, not democracy specifically. This definition should be treated as established.
- "The legislature" is the consistent rendering of Locke's "the legislative" (used as a noun).
- The principle that the legislature holds supreme power has been stated clearly -- the next chapter will set limits on that power, so maintaining the distinction between "supreme" and "absolute" will be important.
- Chapter 22 is one of the most important chapters for constitutionalism -- it lays out four fundamental limits on legislative power. The tone should be measured and precise, with the weight appropriate to its enormous historical influence.
- Locke's numbered argument structure (First, Secondly, Thirdly, Fourthly) should be preserved clearly.
