CHAPTER 023 (Book II, Chapter 12: "Of the Legislative, Executive, and Federative Power of the Commonwealth") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is a relatively short but theoretically important chapter with six sections (143-148). Locke introduces his theory of the separation of powers -- one of the most influential ideas in the history of constitutional government. He argues that the legislative and executive powers should be separated to prevent abuse, introduces the distinctive "federative" power (foreign affairs), and explains why the executive and federative, though conceptually distinct, must in practice be held by the same people.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Section 143 -- massive sentence splitting: The original section 143 is a single enormous sentence (roughly 200 words) that runs through three distinct arguments: (a) the legislature doesn't need to sit permanently, (b) it's dangerous for lawmakers to also be law enforcers, and (c) therefore well-organized commonwealths separate the two and make legislators subject to their own laws. I broke this into three paragraphs, each covering one step of the argument.

2. "The legislative" as noun: Converted to "the legislature" per established convention, maintaining consistency with previous chapters.

3. "Divers persons" (section 143): Modernized to "multiple people" -- "divers" in 17th-century English means "several" or "various," not "diverse" in the modern sense.

4. "Being separated again, they are themselves subject to the laws" (section 143): Expanded slightly to "they break up and go home. And now they themselves are subject to the laws they just made" -- making the practical sequence clearer.

5. "Which one may call natural" (section 145): Preserved the logical explanation of why the federative power is "natural" -- it corresponds to the power individuals had in the state of nature, now exercised collectively.

6. "May be called federative, if anyone pleases. So the thing be understood, I am indifferent as to the name" (section 146): This is a characteristic flash of Locke's personality -- he cares about the concept, not the terminology. Modernized to capture the casual confidence: "I don't care much about the name, as long as the concept is understood."

7. "Antecedent, standing, positive laws" (section 147): Simplified to "pre-established, fixed laws" -- Locke means laws that can be written ahead of time and that remain permanently on the books, as opposed to ad hoc judgments.

8. "Municipal laws" (section 147): Rendered as "domestic laws" -- Locke uses "municipal" in the older sense meaning "pertaining to the internal affairs of the state," not the modern sense of "pertaining to a city."

9. Section 148's long concluding sentence: Broke the final complex sentence about the impossibility of separating executive and federative powers into clearer sequential statements while preserving the warning about "disorder and ruin."

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- All six sections (143-148) with their section numbers
- The three-power framework: legislative, executive, federative
- The argument for separating legislative and executive powers (to prevent self-exemption from laws)
- The key insight that the federative power is less amenable to standing laws than the executive
- The argument for keeping executive and federative in the same hands (unified command of force)
- The concept that the whole community is "one body in the state of nature" relative to other nations
- Locke's characteristic indifference to terminology ("I don't care about the name")
- The practical/realistic tone -- Locke acknowledges human frailty and the temptation of power

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (024 -- Book II, Ch. 13: "Of the Subordination of Powers of the Commonwealth"):
- The three powers (legislative, executive, federative) are now established. The legislature has been described as needing to be intermittent, not permanent; the executive as a permanently active power; and the federative as dealing with foreign affairs.
- "The legislature" is the consistent rendering of "the legislative" used as a noun.
- "Commonwealth" continues to mean any independent political community (as defined in chapter 021/section 133).
- The key principle that the legislature should not be always in session -- and that legislators should be subject to their own laws -- will be important context for the next chapter's discussion of how these powers relate to each other.
- Chapter 024 discusses the subordination of powers: the legislature is supreme but answerable to the people. This builds directly on the framework established here. The tone should emphasize that even the supreme legislature operates under constraints and can be replaced by the people.
- The distinction between permanent (executive) and intermittent (legislative) bodies will matter for the discussion of who acts when the legislature is not in session.
