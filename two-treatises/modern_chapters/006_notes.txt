Chapter 006 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 6: "Of Adam's Title to Sovereignty by Fatherhood"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
Locke dismantles Filmer's argument that fatherhood gives Adam (and by extension all kings) absolute sovereign power. The chapter has several major threads: (1) Fathers don't truly "give" life -- God does, and no human understands the mystery of life well enough to claim credit for creating it; (2) Even if parents did create children, the mother's role is at least equal to the father's, so fatherhood alone can't ground sovereignty; (3) Historical examples of parents killing or eating their children prove nothing about rights -- they only show how depraved humans can become when they abandon reason; (4) The Fifth Commandment ("Honour thy father and mother") consistently includes the mother, so Filmer's selective quotation of "Honour thy father" alone is dishonest and self-defeating; (5) Filmer's own logic creates an irreconcilable contradiction: if fatherhood grants absolute power, then every father is an absolute king, making sovereignty simultaneously universal and meaningless; (6) Locke's devastating catalogue of all the different people Filmer assigns this power to (Adam, parents, children, heirs, kings, usurpers, etc.) shows the theory is incoherent. The chapter ends with the conclusion that natural human freedom is established, and Filmer's "omnipotent fatherhood" would destroy legitimate government rather than support it.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, per text analysis guidelines
- Filmer's page references (p. 12, p. 23, etc.) and Observations references (O. 155, O. 156, etc.) kept intact
- "jus acquiritur parentibus in liberos" / "jus in liberos" -- the Latin from Grotius rendered in English in-line ("by the act of generation, parents acquire rights over their children" / "this power of parents over their children")
- "pro ratione voluntas" rendered as "my will is my reason"
- "jure divino" rendered as "by divine right"
- "Procrustes" reference kept -- the myth is well enough known and Locke explains the analogy clearly
- "Deucalion and his wife" myth reference kept with brief contextual gloss ("in the old myth -- by tossing pebbles over their shoulders")
- "Garcilaso de la Vega" citation kept in full
- "commonwealth" appears near the end and is kept per guidelines
- "the Decalogue" kept as-is (the context of the Ten Commandments is clear)
- "apocryphal" kept -- Locke's sarcasm about Filmer treating "and mother" as apocryphal text is too good to lose

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This is a very long and argument-heavy chapter. Broke Locke's longest sentences into manageable units while keeping the logical connectives visible.
- Preserved the devastating dry wit throughout -- e.g., the passage about the "common farmer or the even more clueless pleasure-seeker" supposedly designing a human being; the Procrustes comparison; the "much joy of their omnipotent fatherhood" closer.
- The Peru/cannibalism passage is shocking and Locke intends it to be. Translated it faithfully without softening it, as Locke uses the horror strategically to show where "practice equals right" logic leads.
- The long list of biblical passages about father AND mother was kept in full, as Locke deploys this catalogue as overwhelming evidence.
- The catalogue near the end (sometimes Adam, sometimes parents, sometimes children, sometimes usurpers...) is one of Locke's most effective rhetorical devices in the chapter -- preserved the list structure with the "sometimes... sometimes..." rhythm.
- The closing paragraph's sarcasm ("much good do our author and all his followers with their omnipotent fatherhood") is a killer ending -- kept it sharp.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 7 is "Of Fatherhood and Property Considered Together as Fountains of Sovereignty." Locke will examine Filmer's attempt to combine the fatherhood argument with a property argument.
- The contradictions identified in this chapter (especially the "every father is an absolute king" problem and the catalogue of who supposedly holds fatherly power) are likely to be referenced again. Maintain continuity with how these were framed.
- The prosecutorial-but-amused tone has intensified through chapters 4-6. By this point Locke clearly considers Filmer's case demolished, and the tone should reflect confident summation rather than fresh engagement.
- The "mother's equal right" argument is now firmly established and may recur -- keep the framing consistent.
- Continue preserving Filmer's page/section references exactly as given.
- Locke's conclusion that "natural freedom" is established and Filmer's principles are self-defeating should carry forward as the baseline assumption for subsequent chapters.
