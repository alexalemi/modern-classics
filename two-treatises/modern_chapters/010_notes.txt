Chapter 010 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 10: "Of the Heir to Adam's Monarchical Power"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is a short but devastating chapter where Locke springs the logical trap of Filmer's heir-of-Adam doctrine. Filmer claims there is always exactly one person who is the natural rightful heir to Adam's sovereignty. Locke shows this leads to a fatal dilemma: either (1) only one unknown person is the legitimate king and all current monarchs are frauds, or (2) everyone descends from Adam and therefore everyone is an heir, meaning no one owes obedience to anyone. Either horn of the dilemma destroys the political order Filmer claims to be defending.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, consistent with previous chapters
- "O. 253" and "O. 244" references to Filmer's Observations kept intact
- "whatsoever" rendered as standard English or dropped where redundant
- "hath a right" rendered as "has a natural right" or "has a right"
- "multitude of men" rendered as "group of people" to modernize gendered language
- "sceptres" kept as "scepters" (American spelling, still evocative)
- "in conscience" rendered as "honestly" for naturalness
- "made out" (= proven, verified) rendered as "verified"

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This chapter is essentially one extended logical argument with a devastating conclusion. Preserved the building intensity as Locke works through the branching possibilities.
- Broke Locke's extremely long second paragraph into multiple paragraphs at natural logical joints (the "on the other hand" turn, the single-heir scenario, the multiple-heir scenario, and the final either/or conclusion). This makes the logical structure clearer for modern readers.
- The first paragraph's sarcasm ("What an excellent way to secure the thrones of princes") captures Locke's devastating irony without amplifying it.
- Preserved the dilemma structure clearly: Locke's "either...or" chains are the backbone of the argument and were kept explicit.
- The final sentences are tight and powerful -- kept them close to the original for rhetorical impact.
- Preserved Filmer's direct quotations as block quotes where Locke cites them.

BIBLICAL/TEXTUAL REFERENCES:
- No biblical citations in this chapter; the argument is purely logical.
- References are to Filmer's works: O. 253, O. 244, and page 20.
