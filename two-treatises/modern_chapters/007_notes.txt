Chapter 007 Translation Notes
Book I, Chapter 7: "Of Fatherhood and Property Considered Together as Fountains of Sovereignty"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
Locke shows that Filmer's two claimed foundations for monarchical power -- fatherhood (paternal authority) and property (ownership of the earth) -- are logically incompatible. They can't both descend to the same person upon a ruler's death. Property goes to the eldest heir, but paternal power belongs only to the person who actually fathered the children. Using the examples of Cain/Abel and Noah's three sons (Shem, Ham, Japheth), Locke demonstrates that Filmer's own account of biblical history proves the two titles immediately separated upon Adam's death, making it impossible to determine where sovereignty lies. He then turns Filmer's own argument against him, showing that Filmer's objection to "two sorts of power without subordination" applies perfectly to his own theory.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "our author" preserved throughout for Filmer, per established pattern
- "private dominion" kept as Locke's technical term for exclusive personal ownership
- "natural dominion" kept as Locke's term for paternal/fatherly authority
- "O." references to Filmer's Observations kept intact (O. 108, O. 158, O. 210, O. 222, O. 244)
- "viz." rendered as natural phrasing rather than "namely" -- absorbed into sentence flow
- "i.e." rendered as "that is" in the one instance (property in the creatures)
- "presently" (meaning "immediately") rendered as "immediately"
- "issue" (meaning descendants/offspring) rendered as "descendants"
- "Grotius" kept without gloss -- he was introduced in chapter 004
- "Cham" (Filmer's spelling of Ham, son of Noah) standardized to "Ham"
- "Japhet" standardized to "Japheth" (modern standard spelling)

STYLE DECISIONS:
- The chapter is essentially one sustained logical argument with two main examples (Adam's sons, Noah's sons). Maintained the building momentum throughout.
- Broke Locke's extremely long sentences into shorter ones. The opening paragraph is one massive sentence in the original; split into three sentences while preserving the logical chain.
- The passage where Locke quotes Filmer's own words back at him (swapping "property" for "people") is a devastating rhetorical move -- preserved the setup and payoff clearly.
- Preserved the "let us suppose" / "even if we grant" conditional structure that is Locke's signature argumentative method.
- The Cain/Abel/Seth analysis required careful handling to keep the logical chain clear across multiple steps. Used shorter sentences and explicit logical connectives.
- Maintained Locke's deadpan delivery -- e.g., "Sir Robert's whole theory of monarchy is immediately dead in the water" captures the original's "there is presently an end of all Sir Robert's monarchy" with appropriate modern flavor.
- "Filmer's imaginary title of fatherhood has been shown the door" for "his imaginary title of fatherhood is out of doors" -- modernized the idiom while keeping the dismissive tone.

BIBLICAL AND HISTORICAL REFERENCES:
- Gen. 3:16 (referenced from prior chapters as one of the competing foundations)
- The Cain/Abel narrative (Gen. 4) -- property division and the land of Nod
- Noah's three sons: Shem, Ham, Japheth -- the division of the world
- Grotius (Hugo Grotius, Dutch jurist) referenced as Filmer's source for the Cain/Abel property division

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- This chapter ends by promising that "the next chapter" will examine the actual mechanisms of succession -- how sovereignty was supposedly passed from Adam to later rulers. Chapter 8 is "Of the Conveyance of Adam's Sovereign Monarchical Power."
- The core contradiction exposed here (fatherhood vs. property as incompatible titles) should be treated as established in subsequent chapters.
- Continue using "our author" for Filmer and maintaining the prosecutorial-but-amused tone.
- The logical method of granting premises then showing they lead to absurdity remains central.
- Note that no section numbers appear in this chapter (unlike some later chapters, especially in Book II). The chapter is a continuous argument without Locke's numbered paragraph system.
