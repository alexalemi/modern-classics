Chapter 019 Translation Notes
Book II, Chapter 8: "Of the Beginning of Political Societies"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is one of the most important chapters in Book II, laying out the consent theory of government -- the social contract. Locke argues: (1) People are naturally free and can only be placed under political authority by their own consent. (2) When people consent to form a community, majority rule necessarily follows, because unanimity is impossible and without majority rule the society would instantly collapse. (3) Historical evidence supports this: Rome, Venice, and many Indigenous American societies all began from voluntary unions of free people. (4) Early governments tended to be monarchies not because of any natural right of fathers, but because (a) people were accustomed to single-person rule from family life, (b) monarchy was the simplest form, and (c) early societies mainly needed a war leader. (5) Biblical Israel's judges and first kings were primarily military commanders, not absolute rulers. (6) The objection that "everyone is born under a government" fails because it would make all monarchies illegitimate too. (7) No parent can bind their children to a government -- each person must consent for themselves upon reaching adulthood. (8) Tacit consent (using property, living in a territory) creates temporary obligations, but only express consent creates permanent membership.

VOCABULARY DECISIONS:
- "commonwealth" kept as-is per text analysis
- "compact" rendered as "agreement" throughout
- "body politic" kept as-is per text analysis
- "state of nature" kept as technical term
- "leviathan" kept -- Locke's allusion to Hobbes's work, capitalized as a proper noun reference
- "Cato's coming into the theatre" -- kept but added "the old story of" for context; this refers to Cato the Younger who was said to leave theatrical performances he found objectionable
- "amor sceleratus habendi" (Latin, "wicked lust for possession") rendered directly in English per guidelines on Latin phrases
- "ceteris paribus" (Latin, "all other things being equal") rendered in English
- "in vacuis locis" (Latin, "in empty places") rendered as "in any unoccupied part of the world"
- "de facto" rendered as "in practice"
- "jure divino" rendered as "divinely ordained"
- "Josephus Acosta" modernized to "Jose de Acosta" (the standard modern name for the Spanish Jesuit)
- "Salmanasser" modernized to "Shalmaneser" (standard modern spelling of the Assyrian king)
- "denison" kept as "citizen" (modern equivalent)
- "avocations" (= distractions, things calling people away) rendered as "business conflicts"
- "contrariety of interest" rendered as "clash of interests"
- "politic society" / "polities" rendered as "political society" / "governments"
- "the Indians in America" rendered as "the Indigenous peoples in the Americas" or "the peoples of the Americas"
- "pupilage" rendered as "childhood"

STYLE DECISIONS:
- This is a long and argumentatively dense chapter. Broke Locke's longest sentences into shorter units while carefully preserving his logical chains, especially the majority-rule argument in the early sections.
- Preserved Locke's numbered objections structure ("First... Secondly...") and his systematic responses to each.
- Maintained the extended biblical passage about Israel's judges and kings (Jephthah, Gideon, Abimelech, Saul, David) with all scripture citations intact -- this is a key piece of evidence for Locke's argument that early kingship was essentially military leadership.
- Preserved Locke's signature "even if" reasoning, especially in the argument against the "born under government" objection.
- The distinction between express and tacit consent at the chapter's end is philosophically crucial and still debated today. Translated with care to preserve Locke's precise formulations.
- Locke's sarcasm about divine-right monarchy being "revealed to us by the divinity of this last age" rendered as "revealed to us by the theological geniuses of this latest age" to preserve the bite.
- The "golden age" passage preserved its elegiac quality -- Locke's description of early simple societies is genuinely moving.
