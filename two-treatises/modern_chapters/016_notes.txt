Chapter 016 Translation Notes
Book II, Chapter 5: "Of Property"

SUMMARY OF CHAPTER:
This is one of the most famous and influential chapters in the history of political philosophy and economics. Locke develops the labor theory of property -- the argument that people acquire ownership of things by mixing their labor with them. The chapter proceeds through several major stages: (1) God gave the earth to humanity in common, but for it to be useful, individuals must be able to claim parts of it; (2) every person owns their own body and labor, and by "mixing" that labor with natural resources, they make those resources their own; (3) the famous acorns passage -- gathering acorns makes them yours, and requiring universal consent would mean starvation; (4) property in land works the same way as property in gathered goods; (5) natural limits on property -- you can only claim what you can use before it spoils; (6) labor creates almost all of the value of things (the labor theory of value); (7) the invention of money allows people to accumulate beyond the spoilage limit, because money doesn't decay; (8) "In the beginning all the world was America" -- without money, there's no incentive to accumulate; (9) by tacit consent in the use of money, people have agreed to unequal property holdings.

LANDMARK PASSAGES PRESERVED:
- "Every person has a property in their own person. This nobody has any right to but themselves. The labor of their body and the work of their hands, we may say, are properly theirs." -- The famous formulation of self-ownership and the labor theory of property. Kept very close to the original, updating only pronouns to gender-neutral forms per the text analysis guidelines (Locke's "man" -> "person," "his" -> "their"). The text analysis specifically flags this as a passage to preserve near-verbatim.
- "In the beginning, all the world was America" -- Preserved exactly. One of the most quoted lines in political philosophy. Only added a comma after "beginning" for modern punctuation.
- The acorns passage (section 4 in the original) -- "when did they become his? When he digested them? When he ate them? When he cooked them?" -- preserved the rhetorical cascade of questions with minimal changes.
- "at least where there is enough, and as good, left in common for others" -- the famous "Lockean proviso" preserved in recognizable form.

STYLE DECISIONS:
- Broke Locke's enormously long sentences (several exceeding 150 words) into shorter, clearer units while preserving his logical chain. The opening sentence of the chapter, for instance, is one long conditional that runs over 150 words; broken into three sentences.
- Preserved Locke's building argument structure: common ownership -> self-ownership -> labor mixing -> acorns example -> land -> natural limits -> labor theory of value -> money -> inequality. This is one of the great sequential arguments in philosophy and needs to unfold in order.
- Maintained Locke's direct, confident tone throughout. This is Locke at his most constructive -- building rather than demolishing -- and the voice should feel measured and sure.
- Preserved the Devonshire comparison (ten cultivated acres vs. a thousand wild ones) as a concrete, vivid example.
- Kept the island thought experiment about money intact -- it's one of Locke's most effective illustrations.
- The "loaf of bread" supply chain passage (tracing all the labor inputs from iron to ships) preserved in full -- it's a remarkable proto-economic analysis.
- Preserved the spoilage principle clearly: you can own what you can use before it goes bad, no more.
- The transition from barter to money (trading plums for nuts, nuts for metal, sheep for shells) preserved as a narrative sequence -- Locke tells this almost as a story and it works beautifully.

KEY PHILOSOPHICAL CONCEPTS TO TRACK:
- The "Lockean proviso" -- "at least where there is enough, and as good, left in common for others" -- this qualifier on property rights has been enormously influential and debated. Preserved clearly.
- The spoilage limitation -- you can't own more than you can use before it spoils. This is Locke's first natural limit on accumulation.
- Money as the mechanism that dissolves the spoilage limit -- since gold and silver don't spoil, you can accumulate without waste, and by tacit consent people have agreed to this system. This is Locke's explanation of how inequality arises without injustice.
- The labor theory of value -- labor creates 90-99% of the value of goods. This passage directly influenced Adam Smith and Karl Marx.
- "In the beginning all the world was America" -- Locke's use of America as a model of the pre-monetary state of nature.

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- Chapter 017 is Book II, Chapter 6: "Of Paternal Power." Locke distinguishes parental authority from political authority -- parents have temporary, limited authority over children based on the child's need for care and education, not any natural right to dominate.
- Key transition: from property (the economic foundation) to family structure (the social foundation). Locke needs to show that paternal power is fundamentally different from political power, dismantling the patriarchal argument once more but now from his own constructive framework rather than as a refutation of Filmer.
- Vocabulary: "paternal power" is Locke's term but he quickly argues it should really be called "parental power" since mothers have equal claim. This is one of Locke's more progressive moments.
- The tone continues to be constructive and measured, building the positive theory of government brick by brick.
- Watch for: Locke's discussion of the age of majority and when parental authority ends; his distinction between the honor owed to parents (permanent) and obedience (temporary); his argument that children are born to equality even if not born in a state of equality.
