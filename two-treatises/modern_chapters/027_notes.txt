CHAPTER 027 (Book II, Chapter 16: "Of Conquest") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is a substantial and politically charged chapter (sections 175-197) in which Locke argues that conquest -- even in a just war -- provides extremely limited rights to the conqueror, and never produces legitimate government without consent. It's a systematic demolition of the idea that "might makes right" in politics, building from easy cases (unjust war gives no rights at all) to harder ones (just war still doesn't give absolute power over the conquered or their descendants). The chapter has major implications for colonial theory and resistance to tyranny.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Sentence restructuring throughout: Locke's sentences in this chapter are exceptionally long and complex, often running 100+ words with multiple nested clauses. Nearly every section required significant breaking apart. For example, section 176 is essentially two enormous sentences in the original; I broke it into roughly a dozen shorter ones while preserving the logical chain.

2. "Drawcansirs" (section 178): This is a reference to a character from George Villiers' play "The Rehearsal" (1671), a swaggering bully who kills everyone on both sides in battle. Rather than add a footnote, I replaced it with "complete tyrants" which captures the meaning Locke intends -- leaders who treat even their own allies as subjects to dominate.

3. "Hingar, or Hubba" (section 197): These are Ivar the Boneless and Hubba, leaders of the Great Heathen Army that invaded England in the 9th century. I modernized "Hingar" to "Ivar" (the more widely recognized name) and glossed them as "Danish invaders" for clarity.

4. "Wampompeke" (section 185): Locke's term for wampum, the shell bead currency used by Indigenous peoples of northeastern North America. I modernized to "wampum" and adjusted the framing to "Indigenous Americans" rather than Locke's "Americans" to clarify he's referring to Native peoples.

5. "Disseized" (section 185): A legal term meaning dispossessed of property. Replaced with "dispossessed" which carries the same meaning in modern usage.

6. The economic argument in section 185: Locke's argument about the relative value of money, crops, and land is intricate and somewhat counterintuitive. I broke it into smaller pieces and added connecting phrases to make the logic easier to follow, while preserving the full argument including the parenthetical about land-rich/people-poor territories.

7. "Jephthah" reference (section 176): Kept as-is. This is a recurring reference throughout Book II -- Jephthah appealed to God to judge his dispute with the Ammonites (Judges 11). Locke uses it as shorthand for appealing to heaven when earthly justice fails.

8. The Greek Christians / Turkish yoke example (section 193): Locke is referring to the Greek population under Ottoman rule, which had been in place since 1453. This was a current political issue in his time. Preserved as-is since it's a concrete and vivid illustration.

9. "The legislative" as noun: Converted to "the legislature" per established convention in section 175.

10. Gender-neutral language: Used "people," "person," "everyone" in place of "men" where Locke clearly means all humanity. Preserved "wife and children" in the specific inheritance/family examples as these reflect the legal framework Locke is discussing.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- The complete argument structure: unjust conquest (176) -> just conquest over allies (178) -> just conquest over enemies: lives vs. property distinction (179-183) -> rights of wives and children (183-184) -> economic limits on conquest claims (185) -> consent cannot be coerced (187) -> children are always free (189-190) -> the two natural rights (191-193) -> property grants bind the grantor (194-195) -> divine law binds all rulers (196) -> final summary (197)
- All section numbers (175-197)
- The robber/dagger analogy (176) and the highway thief analogy (183)
- The Norman Conquest argument (178)
- The concrete property examples -- thousand acres in perpetuity vs. thousand acres leased (195)
- The Jephthah reference and the Ahaz/Hezekiah biblical narrative (197)
- The 2 Kings 18:7 quotation
- Locke's dry wit, especially the mock-grant speech: "I give you and yours this forever... but I reserve the right to take it back tomorrow"
- The Spartacus reference as a parallel case (197)
- Locke's passionate crescendo in sections 193 and 197 about the right to throw off unjust rule

NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER (028 -- Book II, Ch. 17: "Of Usurpation"):
- The distinction between conquest (external force) and usurpation (internal seizure of power) should be clear. Conquest has been thoroughly analyzed; usurpation is the domestic parallel.
- "Despotic power" has been used consistently to describe the conqueror's power over those who fought unjustly -- maintain this term.
- The principle that consent cannot be coerced has been firmly established. Any forced promise or agreement is void. This principle carries forward.
- The two natural rights (freedom of person, right of inheritance) stated in section 191 are foundational for the remaining chapters -- keep them clearly distinguished.
- "Commonwealth" continues to mean any independent political community per the earlier definition.
- The tone is building toward the tyranny and dissolution chapters. Locke's argument is becoming increasingly urgent and direct -- the rhetoric about throwing off unjust power is growing more forceful.
