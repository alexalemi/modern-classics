CHAPTER 018 (Book II, Chapter 7: "Of Political or Civil Society") — Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This chapter defines what political or civil society actually is by distinguishing it from other forms of human association: marriage, the parent-child relationship, and the master-servant relationship. Locke examines each in turn, showing that none of them -- even combined into a household -- constitute political society. He then delivers his core definition: political society exists wherever people have given up their individual power to judge and punish, handing it to the community, which operates through established laws and impartial judges. The chapter culminates in a powerful argument that absolute monarchy is fundamentally incompatible with civil society.

MAJOR CHANGES AND RATIONALE:

1. Sentence structure: Locke's sentences in this chapter are extremely long and architecturally complex, some running 150+ words with multiple semicolons and nested conditions. These were broken into shorter, clearer sentences while preserving the logical flow and connective tissue ("therefore," "but," "and so").

2. "Man/men" as generic: Consistently replaced generic "man" and "men" with "person," "people," "everyone," "human beings," etc. Retained "man and woman," "male and female," "husband and wife" in the marriage discussion where gender-specific reference is clearly intended.

3. Key terms maintained consistently:
   - "Property" kept as-is (Locke's broad sense: life, liberty, possessions)
   - "Commonwealth" kept as-is
   - "State of nature" kept as-is
   - "Body politic" kept as-is
   - "The legislative" rendered as "the legislature" or "the legislative power" depending on context
   - "Compact" rendered as "agreement" or "contract"
   - "Civil society" / "political society" kept as Locke uses them interchangeably

4. Marriage discussion: Locke's extended comparison of human pair-bonding to animal behavior (grazing mammals, predators, birds) is a surprisingly modern-feeling passage. Kept the naturalistic observations lively and clear. "Viviparous animals which feed on grass" simplified to "grass-eating mammals." "The teat of the dam" simplified to "the mother's milk." "Hymen at his usual anniversary season" (classical reference to the god of marriage, meaning mating season) rendered as "the usual mating season."

5. The "Hymen" reference: Locke uses "Hymen at his usual anniversary season" to mean the return of mating season in animals. This classical allusion would be obscure to modern readers. Replaced with the straightforward meaning: "the usual mating season calls them to choose new partners."

6. Husband's authority passage: Locke's description of the husband having "the last determination" is rendered faithfully -- he limits it to shared property and interest, explicitly denies it extends to power over the wife's life, and notes the wife's right to separation. The translation preserves Locke's own careful qualifications without editorializing.

7. "Paterfamilias": Kept in context but the meaning is made clear from surrounding text (head of household, father of the family).

8. The polecats/foxes/lions metaphor: This vivid analogy -- that it would be foolish to guard against small predators while consenting to be devoured by a lion -- is one of Locke's best rhetorical moments. Preserved closely with minimal modernization.

9. Ceylon reference: Locke refers to "the late relation of Ceylon" -- this is Robert Knox's 1681 account of his captivity in Ceylon (modern Sri Lanka), which described the tyrannical rule of the Kandyan king. Kept as "recent accounts from Ceylon" without further gloss, as Locke's point is clear in context.

10. Footnote markers: The original text contains footnote reference numbers (3, 4, 5, 6). These have been omitted from the modernized text as they reference scholarly apparatus not included in this translation.

11. Final quotation: "No man in civil society can be exempted from the laws of it" is rendered as "No person in civil society can be exempted from the laws of it" -- preserving the quotation-like emphasis Locke gives it while updating the generic "man."

CONSISTENCY NOTES FOR NEXT CHAPTER:
- "Political society" and "civil society" are used interchangeably by Locke; maintain this pattern.
- The definition of political society established here (giving up individual judgment/punishment power to the community, which operates through laws and judges) is the foundation for the next chapter on how political societies begin. The next chapter (Ch. 8, "Of the Beginning of Political Societies") will build directly on this definition to discuss consent and majority rule.
- "Property" continues to mean life, liberty, and possessions (the broad Lockean sense).
- Absolute monarchy has been declared incompatible with civil society -- this will be referenced again in later chapters on tyranny and dissolution of government.
- The idea that government's sole purpose is "the preservation of property" has been stated emphatically here and should be echoed in subsequent chapters.
- Locke's promise to discuss the historical development of monarchy later ("as shall be showed more at large hereafter") refers to content in the next chapter and Chapter 13 (our 024).
- The legislature placed in "collective bodies" (senates, parliaments) is established as the solution to the problems of absolute monarchy -- this connects to Chapters 11-13 of Book II on legislative power.
