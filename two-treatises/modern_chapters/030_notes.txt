CHAPTER 030 (Book II, Chapter 19: "Of the Dissolution of Government") -- Translation Notes

OVERVIEW:
This is the climactic chapter of the entire Two Treatises -- the right of revolution. At approximately 45,000 characters, it is one of the longest chapters in Book II and arguably the most historically consequential. It directly influenced the American Declaration of Independence (Jefferson's "long train of abuses" echoes Locke's section 225 almost verbatim) and provided the philosophical foundation for the right to resist tyranny that shaped the American and French Revolutions.

The chapter builds a careful, escalating argument: (1) governments can be dissolved by foreign conquest; (2) governments can be dissolved from within when the legislature is altered; (3) governments are dissolved when rulers or legislators betray their trust; (4) the people are the ultimate judge of when trust has been broken; (5) this doctrine doesn't cause rebellion -- it prevents it.

MAJOR CHANGES:

1. Sentence restructuring throughout: Locke's sentences in this chapter are among his longest and most architecturally complex, often running 100+ words with multiple nested conditionals and parenthetical qualifications. Nearly every paragraph required splitting sentences and clarifying the logical flow while preserving Locke's argumentation structure.

2. Latin passages from Barclay: The chapter contains two very long Latin passages from William Barclay's Contra Monarchomachos (1600). Following the text analysis instructions and the user's direction, I used Locke's own English translations as the primary text and omitted the Latin entirely. The English translations are presented as block quotations. The short Juvenal quotation in section 234 was also replaced with an English rendering, with just a brief reference to Juvenal as "the Roman satirist."

3. "The legislative" as noun: Consistently rendered as "the legislature" per established convention throughout the series. The distinction between "the legislature" (the body) and "the legislature is altered/changed" (Locke's key argument about dissolution) is maintained clearly.

4. The hypothetical government form (sections 213-218): Locke describes a thinly-veiled version of the English constitution (king, House of Lords, House of Commons) without naming it directly. I preserved this structure clearly, breaking his single-paragraph description into three numbered components for readability.

5. Section 222 -- the breach of trust passage: This is one of the most important passages in the history of political thought. I maintained close fidelity to Locke's language and argument structure while modernizing syntax. The key phrases -- "put themselves into a state of war with the people," "the common refuge which God has provided for all people against force and violence," and the forfeiture-and-reversion-to-the-people argument -- are all preserved with minimal alteration.

6. Section 225 -- "a long train of abuses": This passage famously influenced Jefferson's Declaration of Independence. I kept the essential language very close to Locke's original ("a long pattern of abuses, lies, and manipulations -- all pointing in the same direction -- makes the design visible to the people") while modernizing the supporting syntax.

7. The Polyphemus/Ulysses analogy (section 228): Preserved this vivid analogy in full. Locke uses the Cyclops myth from Homer's Odyssey to devastating rhetorical effect -- the idea that "peace" under tyranny is like Ulysses preaching passive obedience while his men are being eaten alive. The dry sarcasm ("no doubt Ulysses, being a practical man, preached passive obedience") is pure Locke and was preserved with minimal change.

8. "The people shall be judge" (section 239): This is the most famous line from the chapter and one of the most quoted passages in all of Locke. I preserved it nearly verbatim, keeping both the question and the answer in their original rhetorical force: "'Who shall be judge?' ... 'The people shall be judge.'"

9. The concluding section (242): Locke's theoretical capstone, distinguishing between permanent and temporary legislatures and establishing when power reverts to the people. I preserved the logical precision while breaking the single massive sentence into manageable units.

10. "Rebellare" etymology (section 226): Locke makes the etymological argument that "rebellion" literally means "bringing back war" (from Latin re- + bellare), so the real rebels are those who reintroduce force into a society that had replaced it with law. This is a brilliant rhetorical move and I preserved it with a brief gloss on the Latin.

11. Gendered language: Modernized "men" to "people," "everyone," "a person," etc. where clearly generic, per the established convention. Preserved specific historical references (e.g., to Nero, Caligula, specific kings).

12. Section 238 -- the broadside against servile flatterers: Locke's penultimate substantive passage is a fierce denunciation of those who preach absolute obedience. The "Egyptian taskmasters" reference is biblical (Exodus) -- taskmasters who enslaved the Israelites. I preserved the reference as it adds rhetorical force and is recognizable.

WHAT WAS PRESERVED:
- All 32 sections (211-242) translated in full, nothing summarized or truncated
- The complete argumentative architecture: external dissolution, internal dissolution (legislature changed), internal dissolution (trust betrayed), objections and replies, "who shall be judge?"
- Locke's numbered argument structure (First, Second, Third, Fourth) in sections 214-217 and 224-226
- The hypothetical government form and its transparent reference to English governance
- Both Barclay passages in full (using Locke's English translations)
- The Polyphemus/Ulysses analogy with its devastating sarcasm
- The Juvenal reference with English rendering
- "The people shall be judge" in its full rhetorical context
- The rebellare etymology argument
- All historical and scholarly references (Barclay, Buchanan, Winzerus, Bilson, Bracton, Fortescue, Hooker, the Mirror of Justices)
- The concluding theoretical framework on permanent vs. temporary legislatures
- Locke's building intensity -- the chapter starts methodically and ends with passionate conviction
- The key famous passage: "The purpose of government is the good of humanity"

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY:
- "The legislature" for Locke's "the legislative" (as noun)
- "Commonwealth" preserved throughout
- "State of war" / "state of nature" -- technical terms preserved
- "Property" in Locke's broad sense (life, liberty, possessions)
- "Trust" / "breach of trust" -- central to this chapter's argument, preserved consistently
- "Prerogative" preserved
- "Body politic" preserved

HISTORICAL REFERENCES IDENTIFIED:
- William Barclay (1546-1608): Scottish jurist, author of Contra Monarchomachos, defender of absolute monarchy whom Locke uses as a foil
- George Buchanan (1506-1582): Scottish historian who argued for the right of resistance, the one exception Barclay's opponents acknowledge
- Winzerus: Scholar referenced by Barclay on the two cases of royal forfeiture
- Nero and Caligula: Roman emperors cited as examples of rulers who forfeited their authority by planning to destroy their own states
- Thomas Bilson (1547-1616): Bishop of Winchester, author of The True Difference between Christian Subjection and Unchristian Rebellion
- Henry de Bracton (c. 1210-1268): Medieval English jurist
- Sir John Fortescue (c. 1394-1480): English jurist and constitutional theorist
- The Mirror of Justices (Miroir des Justices): 13th-century English legal treatise
- Richard Hooker (1554-1600): Anglican theologian whose Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity Locke frequently cites
- Jephthah: Biblical judge (Judges 11) who appealed to God to judge between himself and the Ammonites -- Locke's standard example of the "appeal to heaven"
- Juvenal (c. 55-130 AD): Roman satirical poet, Satire III referenced
- Polyphemus and Ulysses: From Homer's Odyssey, Book IX -- the Cyclops who trapped and devoured Odysseus's men
