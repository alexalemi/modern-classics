KEY TERMS AND CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- ECONOMIC RENT: Formally defined as the share of produced wealth that flows to landowners purely because they own land/natural resources. Key distinctions established: (1) Narrower than everyday "rent" -- excludes payments for buildings/improvements, which are interest on capital. (2) Wider than everyday "rent" -- includes imputed rent when owner and user are the same person, and includes land value appreciation (buying cheap and selling dear = increase in rent, not wages or interest). (3) "Wherever land has exchange value, there is rent."

- RENT AS MONOPOLY PRICE: "Rent, in short, is the price of monopoly, arising from the reduction to individual ownership of natural resources that human effort can neither create nor expand." This is a core thesis -- rent doesn't reward production, it rewards ownership.

- THE LAW OF RENT (RICARDO'S LAW): "The rent of land is determined by how much more it produces than the same amount of labor and capital could produce on the least productive land still in use." Applies to all land uses, not just agriculture -- manufacturing and commercial land yields the highest rents.

- THE RENT LINE: The metaphor of a "rent line" dividing total output. Everything above it goes to landowners. Wages and interest come from what's below it. The line is set by the margin of cultivation -- the return available on rent-free land.

- THE COROLLARY (Produce - Rent = Wages + Interest): This algebraic relationship is the chapter's biggest payoff. Wages and interest don't depend on total production; they depend on what's LEFT after rent. Therefore, if rent rises as fast as productivity, wages and interest cannot rise. If rent rises faster, they fall.

- MARGIN OF CULTIVATION: Continued use as "the least productive land still in use" or "the margin." This term was introduced in previous chapters and is now formally linked to the law of rent.

ARGUMENT STRUCTURE: George has now laid the groundwork for his central explanation. Book III, Chapter 1 (011) set up the framework (land, labor, capital; rent, wages, interest) and showed that the standard economics got distribution wrong. This chapter (012) establishes the law of rent and its corollary -- that rent determines what's left for wages and interest. The next chapter should develop the laws of wages and interest in light of this framework.

TONE: Careful, definitional, building. George is constructing his positive theory brick by brick. The chapter ends on a note of illumination -- "a flood of light" -- as the explanation clicks into place. The next chapters should maintain this tone of steady construction.

Endnote numbers 32, 33, 34, 35 preserved. Gender-neutral language throughout. "Economics" for "political economy." "Mainstream economics" for "the current political economy." These should remain consistent.
