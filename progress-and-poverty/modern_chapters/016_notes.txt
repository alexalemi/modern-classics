KEY TERMS AND CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED:

- THE LAW OF WAGES: Wages depend on the margin of production -- on the product that labor can obtain at the highest point of natural productivity open to it without the payment of rent. This is the corollary of the law of rent.
- WAGES AS PROPORTION VS. QUANTITY: Critical distinction. When George says "wages fall as rent rises," he means the proportion, not necessarily the quantity. The quantity may stay the same or even increase if technology improves. This allows George to explain how workers can be relatively worse off even as absolute living standards rise.
- THE FOUR CASES: (1) Free land, no capital -> wages = whole product. (2) Free land + capital -> wages = product minus interest. (3) Land owned, rent arises -> wages = margin of cultivation. (4) All land monopolized -> wages can fall to subsistence minimum.
- SUPPLY AND DEMAND CRITIQUE: George argues that "supply and demand" is meaningless as an explanation for general wages because in the economy as a whole, supply = demand. It can explain relative wages between occupations but not the general level.
- ADAM SMITH CONNECTION: Smith saw that open land meant high wages but failed to develop the insight because he was blinded by his theory of capital's role and (George implies) a proto-Malthusian assumption.
- OCCUPATIONAL WAGE DIFFERENCES: All explained by equalizing factors (risk, training, pleasantness, etc.) for apparent differences, and by scarcity of talent for real differences. All ultimately depend on the lowest, widest stratum.

STYLE NOTES: Bold section headers. "Economics" for "political economy." Gender-neutral language. Endnotes preserved (37, 38). Adam Smith quotes preserved verbatim. The dam metaphor, the dog proverb, and the "sword" biblical allusion all retained.

UPCOMING: Chapter 017 (The Correlation and Coordination of These Laws) is very short -- it's George's triumphant summary showing how his three laws (rent, wages, interest) all spring from one principle and all depend on the margin of cultivation, in contrast to the incoherent laws of mainstream economics. Should be translated with the confident, almost celebratory tone of someone who has just completed a major intellectual construction.
