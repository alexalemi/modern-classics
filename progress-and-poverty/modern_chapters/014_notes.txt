"Viz." -> rendered as dashes or colons.

SECTION HEADERS: Added bold headers throughout: "Land Values Are Not Capital," "Stocks and Bonds: Real and Fictitious," "Government Bonds: A Case Study," "When Bonds Do Represent Capital," "The Real Culprit: Monopoly Power," "How Monopoly Power Works in Practice," "The Bigger Picture," "Separating Real Interest from False," "The Great Fortunes," and "Why These Distinctions Matter."

4. GENDER-NEUTRAL LANGUAGE: "everyone is styled a capitalist who..." -> retained with natural flow. "his labor" -> "their labor" where appropriate. The historical examples (Buckingham, James I, Warren Hastings) retain original gendered language since they're specific historical figures.

5. HISTORICAL REFERENCES: Several obscure references are glossed for modern readers:
   - "Buckingham" identified in context through the narrative about James I.
   - "Rohillas" glossed as "the Indian people" with brief context about Warren Hastings as "the colonial governor."
   - "Surajah Dowlah" kept but the context makes the reference followable.
   - "Forty lacs" kept (a lac is 100,000 rupees -- the archaic financial term adds historical flavor and the meaning is clear from context).
   - The "great telegraph company" (Western Union) is left unnamed as George left it, but the reference is clear enough.
   - "Stewart" and other names in the fortunes list are left as-is since they were glossed in the previous chapter and are listed alongside more recognizable names.

6. MONOPOLY VS. INTEREST: This is the chapter's central distinction and is carefully preserved. George argues that much of what people call "interest" or "the earnings of capital" is really one of three other things: (a) rent (returns to land ownership, already dealt with), (b) monopoly profits (returns to concentrated market power), or (c) speculative winnings (zero-sum gambling). Only genuine interest -- the return arising from capital's natural reproductive power -- is legitimate. This distinction is crucial for George's larger argument that labor and capital are NOT natural enemies.

7. GOVERNMENT BONDS PASSAGE: George's argument that government war bonds represent "spurious capital" is strikingly modern. The translation preserves his vivid language about capital "blown out of the mouths of cannons" while making the economic logic clearer. His point -- that interest on war debt is a tax on current production, not a return on productive investment -- is carefully maintained.

8. RAILROAD MONOPOLY: George's comparison of railroad companies to highwaymen is one of his most vivid passages and is preserved with full force. The "Stand and deliver!" comparison is dramatic and effective. The passage about rival boats and rate-slashing describes predatory pricing -- a concept still very much alive in antitrust law.

9. TONE: This chapter shifts between careful economic analysis and passionate denunciation. The early sections on spurious capital are measured and technical. The monopoly section rises to genuine anger -- George is describing abuses he witnessed firsthand in 1870s California. The final paragraph is deliberately ambiguous -- George presents both sides of the labor-vs-capital debate without yet resolving it, setting up the chapters to come.

10. THE FINAL PARAGRAPH: George ends with a deliberately unresolved tension. Both sides of the debate use the same evidence but draw opposite conclusions. George will resolve this in subsequent chapters by showing that the real conflict isn't between labor and capital, but between both of them and the landowner class. The translation preserves this deliberate open-endedness.

KEY TERMS AND CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- SPURIOUS CAPITAL: Things commonly called "capital" that aren't really capital -- particularly government bonds issued for unproductive purposes (war) and inflated corporate stock certificates issued far in excess of actual capital invested. These yield returns that look like interest but are really taxes or monopoly extractions.

- MONOPOLY PROFITS vs. INTEREST: A crucial distinction now established. Much of what's called "interest" or "the earnings of capital" is really monopoly profit -- the power of concentrated capital to extract payments through market control rather than through productive contribution. Examples: railroad monopolies, telegraph monopolies, patent monopolies, tariff-protected industries.

- WAGES OF MANAGEMENT: The modernized term for "wages of superintendence" -- income from personal qualities like skill, enterprise, and organizing ability. Distinct from both interest (return to capital) and monopoly profits (return to market power).

- SPECULATION AND RISK: Returns from speculation are zero-sum (whatever one gains, another loses) and should not be confused with interest, which arises from genuine increase.

- THE GREAT FORTUNES: George has established that the largest fortunes of his era were built primarily through monopoly, land speculation, and market manipulation -- not through the legitimate productive returns of capital. This undermines the argument that great wealth proves the productive power of capital.

- THE UNRESOLVED QUESTION: The chapter ends with the labor-vs-capital debate deliberately unresolved. On one side: deep poverty alongside vast wealth proves capital aggresses against labor. On the other: capital aids labor, so the gulf between rich and poor is just the reward of virtue and the punishment of vice. George will resolve this by showing the REAL culprit is neither labor nor capital but the private appropriation of land rent.

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY: "wages of management" for "wages of superintendence" (new term introduced this chapter). "economics" for "political economy" (though the latter doesn't appear in this chapter). Section headers in bold. Endnote numbers preserved (none in this chapter). Gender-neutral language in George's general statements.

The reader should now be primed to receive a clear, crisp statement of the law.

TONE GOING FORWARD: George is transitioning from careful analytical distinction-making to positive law-stating. The next chapter should feel like the payoff -- "Now that we've cleared away the confusion, here's the actual law."
