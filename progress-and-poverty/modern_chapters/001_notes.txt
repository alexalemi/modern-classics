Major changes and rationale:

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: The most significant change throughout. George's sentences often run 100-200+ words with multiple embedded clauses. The famous "Could a man of the last century" sentence is a single sentence spanning nearly 300 words. I broke it into a series of "Imagine" sentences that preserve the cumulative rhetorical build while being individually digestible. The effect of escalating wonder is maintained through repetition and parallel structure.

2. VOCABULARY UPDATES:
   - "political economy" -> "economics" throughout
   - "pecuniary" -> "financial"
   - "laborsaving" -> "labor-saving"
   - "in nowise" -> "in no way"
   - "viz." -> removed (sentence restructured for clarity)
   - "concomitants" -> "companions" / "everything that comes with it"
   - "laborer" -> "worker" / "working people"
   - "tramp" -> "vagrant"
   - "almshouses" -> "poorhouses"
   - "coal oil sparing the whale" -> "petroleum lamps sparing the whale"
   - "apples of Sodom" -> "ashes" (preserved the biblical fruit-crumbling image but clarified)

3. GENDER-NEUTRAL LANGUAGE:
   - "men" -> "people" / "humanity" / "human beings" where appropriate
   - "man of the last century" -> "someone from the previous century"
   - "he would have seen" -> "they would have seen"
   - "under the eye of a girl" -> "under the watch of a young worker"
   - "business men" -> "business owners"
   - "the man with the muckrake" -> "the person bent over petty concerns"
   - Preserved "women faint and little children moan" in the finale as it is a specific, powerful image (changed "moan" to "cry out in suffering" for modern impact)

4. CULTURAL REFERENCES:
   - "Dives and Lazarus" -> "rich and poor" (the biblical reference to the rich man and beggar from Luke 16 is obscure to most modern readers)
   - "Huns and fiercer Vandals of whom Macaulay prophesied" -> "modern barbarians -- the more dangerous invaders that the historian Macaulay warned about"
   - "slaves of the lamp of knowledge" -> "servants of knowledge" (Aladdin's lamp reference preserved in simpler form)
   - "apples of Sodom" -> "ashes that crumble at the touch" (preserved the tactile image, clarified the reference)
   - "Sphinx of Fate" -> kept as-is (vivid and widely understood)
   - "House of Have and House of Want" -> kept as-is (powerful coinage)

5. TONE PRESERVATION: This is George's most dramatic chapter. I preserved the rhetorical escalation, the emotional peaks, the prophetic urgency. The "immense wedge" metaphor is kept intact. The final paragraph's moral gravity is maintained. The passage about the imagined 18th-century observer retains its sense of wonder-turning-to-dismay.

6. ENDNOTES: The single endnote reference "1" is preserved in place.

7. "Anglo-Saxon vigor" -> "vigorous settlers" -- the original phrase carries racial connotations that George didn't intend (he was describing frontier energy, not racial superiority). The modernization preserves the meaning without the problematic framing.
