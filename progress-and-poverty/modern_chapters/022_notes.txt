KEY TERMS AND REFERENCES ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- SPECULATION IN LAND: The third force driving rent upward, beyond population growth and technological improvement. Speculation means holding land out of use (or out of full use) in expectation of future price increases. This is not a marginal phenomenon -- it operates powerfully in all progressive societies.
- THE FEEDBACK LOOP: Material progress raises rent -> rising rent creates expectation of further increases -> expectation causes land speculation -> speculation pushes the margin of cultivation lower than production needs require -> this raises rent even further, potentially reducing wages in absolute terms.
- ACTUAL vs. NECESSARY MARGIN: The actual margin of cultivation in progressive countries is always lower (worse) than the necessary margin, because speculation forces it down. This gap between actual and necessary is a key concept.
- "LAND POOR": People who hold more land than they can use, remaining poor while preventing others from using it productively. A vivid American-frontier concept.
- CONFEDERATE CURRENCY ANALOGY: Speculative land price increases feed on themselves, just as hyperinflation feeds on itself.
- THE NATURAL LIMIT: Wages can't go below subsistence; this sets a floor on how far rent can rise. But speculation tends to push toward and past this limit, causing economic crises.
- ECONOMIC PARALYSIS / DEPRESSIONS: Previewed as the subject of Book V. George will argue that the speculative overshooting of rent causes recurring economic crises.
