KEY TERMS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER -- must maintain throughout:

- WAGES: All returns for human effort/labor, not just hired manual labor pay. Includes self-employment income, a farmer's crop, a hunter's game, a fisher's catch.
- CAPITAL: Wealth devoted to producing more wealth. Specifically: wealth in the course of exchange. NOT land, NOT labor, NOT personal qualities, NOT debt instruments, NOT land values.
- WEALTH: Natural products modified by human labor to satisfy human desires. "Labor stored in matter." Must have actual (not merely relative) value. Excludes: bonds, mortgages, notes, land values, slaves -- these are "relative wealth" only.
- LAND: All natural materials, forces, and opportunities. The entire material universe outside of human beings. Explicitly NOT capital.
- LABOR: All human exertion. Human abilities (natural or acquired) are NOT capital.
- THREE FACTORS OF PRODUCTION: Land, labor, and capital -- these are mutually exclusive categories.

TONE ESTABLISHED: Conversational, accessible, methodical. George as a careful thinker frustrated by his predecessors' sloppiness. Patient with the reader, impatient with confused economists.

STYLE NOTES: Used bold section headers for navigation. Direct address to reader maintained ("I'd ask the reader..."). First person maintained ("It seems to me..."). Bullet-style examples used for the capital/not-capital illustrations.

GEORGE'S KEY ARGUMENT TO CARRY FORWARD: The standard economists define capital one way in their premises but use it differently in their conclusions. The next chapter (Book I, Ch. 3) will likely begin using these definitions to challenge the claim that wages are drawn from capital.
