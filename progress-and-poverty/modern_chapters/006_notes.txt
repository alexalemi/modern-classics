ESTABLISHED DEFINITIONS (must remain consistent):

BOOK I's CORE ARGUMENT (now complete):
1. The wages-fund theory (wages drawn from capital) is wrong.
2. Wages come directly from the product of labor.
3. Capital doesn't pay wages, sustain workers, or limit industry.
4. Capital's real functions: better tools, harnessing nature, enabling division of labor.
5. Therefore: more workers should mean HIGHER wages, not lower -- "other things being equal."

TRANSITION TO BOOK II:
The final paragraph of this chapter explicitly sets up Book II by asking: "Do the productive powers of nature tend to diminish with increasing population?" This is the Malthusian question. Book II will examine and reject Malthus's theory that population growth outstrips food production.

The phrase "other things being equal" is the pivot -- George acknowledges that his conclusion (more workers = higher wages) depends on nature's productive powers not diminishing. Book II must demolish the Malthusian claim that they do.
