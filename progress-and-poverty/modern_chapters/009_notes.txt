KEY TERMS AND REFERENCES ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- THE ANALOGY ARGUMENT: George has now demolished the second pillar supporting the Malthusian theory -- the argument from analogy with plant and animal life. His key counter-argument: the reproductive power of plants and animals that provide human food actually proves subsistence can grow FASTER than population, because humans uniquely increase their food supply as they increase in number.

- DIMINISHING RETURNS: George addressed and rejected the "law of diminishing returns" from land, arguing it is only relatively true (for a limited area) and becomes false when applied to the whole globe. His reasoning: matter is indestructible and force persists, so humans cannot exhaust nature's powers. This argument will likely recur in later books.

- THE THIRD CHECK: George introduced his alternative to Malthus's positive and preventive checks -- a "third check" on population that comes into play as living standards rise and intellectual life develops. This is his embryonic statement of what he believes the real law of population to be: birth rates naturally decline as people move beyond subsistence-level existence. He promises to develop this more fully later.

- THE REAL LAW OF POPULATION: George has sketched his counter-theory -- that the tendency to increase is strong where more population would bring comfort and where the race's survival is threatened, but weakens as individual development becomes possible. He explicitly states this will be "conclusively demonstrated" later when he traces "the true laws of social growth."

- THE "INFINITE LADDER" OF DESIRE: George's argument that humans differ from all other animals because our desires expand infinitely -- from physical needs to aesthetic pleasure to intellectual curiosity to moral aspiration. This is a key philosophical claim that may recur in Book X (the theory of human progress).

GEORGE'S ARGUMENT STRUCTURE: Chapter 007 set up the Malthusian theory. Chapter 008 (not yet translated) attacked it on factual grounds -- showing that India, China, and Ireland's poverty is caused by bad governance and exploitation, not overpopulation. This chapter (009) attacks it on analogical grounds -- showing that the analogies from plant and animal life don't actually support it. The next chapter (010) will take the affirmative and show the theory is actively disproved by facts, as George explicitly promises at the end.
