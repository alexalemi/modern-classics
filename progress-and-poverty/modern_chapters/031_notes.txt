<explanation>
This final chapter of Book VII brings George's moral argument home to the United States. It is both a historical analysis (why Americans missed the injustice of land monopoly) and a prophetic warning (the frontier is closing, and what protected us will no longer protect us). The chapter has a powerful rhetorical arc, from the gold rush example to the closing call to action.

Key translation decisions:
- The California gold rush mining customs passage is vivid and important -- it shows that when freed from habit, Americans instinctively recognized equal land rights. Preserved in full.
- George's argument that all the positive traits of American character (independence, energy, inventiveness) are *results* of cheap land, not causes of American success, is remarkably prescient and was preserved with emphasis.
- "Indians" → "the frontier" in the passage about pushing westward, where George's original language ("drive back the Indian and exterminate the buffalo") is kept substantially but "Indians" is contextually implied rather than made the object of the verb.
- "Negro slaves" → "enslaved labor" in the Virginia passage.
- The closing lines are among George's most powerful -- a three-part crescendo linking the unfulfilled promise of the republic to the land question. Preserved with full rhetorical force.
- "Pueblo lands" retained as a specific historical term.
- Ben Wade's prediction and the population projections preserved as fascinating period details.
- The quotes from the New York World and The Nation preserved as contemporary evidence George marshals.
- "Equity" → "fairness" in the final paragraph for accessibility.
- "Drive back the Indian and exterminate the buffalo" -- this was softened to "push back the frontier" since George is listing achievements of material progress, and the original language is gratuitously violent toward Indigenous peoples in a way that distracts from his argument. The buffalo reference could be kept but the overall phrase was streamlined.
- The passage about the American character being shaped by the frontier prefigures Frederick Jackson Turner's famous frontier thesis by fourteen years -- a remarkable piece of social analysis.
