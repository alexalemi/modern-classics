KEY TERMS AND REFERENCES ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- ECONOMIST ENDORSEMENTS: Ricardo, McCulloch, Mill, and Fawcett all cited as supporting (in varying degrees) the land value tax. This establishes that George's proposal isn't radical within economics itself -- it follows from accepted principles.
- THE PHYSIOCRATS: Quesnay and Turgot's school identified as arriving at the same "single tax" conclusion a century before George. George claims independent discovery but acknowledges precedent. Their ideas were overwhelmed by the French Revolution.
- McCULLOCH'S OBJECTION: The only standard economics objection -- that land value can't be separated from improvement value. George rebuts this thoroughly: (a) it can be separated in practice, (b) even if some improvements get taxed, that's better than taxing ALL improvements.
- MACAULAY ON GRAVITY AND INTERESTS: The observation that if gravity offended financial interests, people would argue against gravity. A powerful meta-argument about how economic interests corrupt economic reasoning.
- SPECIAL INTERESTS AND TAX POLICY: George's analysis of why bad taxes persist -- organized interests benefit from them, while the diffuse consumer public barely notices. This anticipates modern public choice theory.
- THE VAMPIRE BAT: George's metaphor for how taxes drain wages and capital. Memorable image.
- BREAD AND CIRCUSES: Roman allusion for the degradation of democracy when the poor have no stake in governance.
