Major changes and rationale:

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: This chapter contains some of George's most extraordinarily long sentences. The second paragraph of the original is a single sentence of roughly 150 words about Malthus and Ricardo. Broken into multiple sentences while preserving the logical chain. The "settler parable" paragraphs, which in the original are massive blocks, are given more paragraph breaks for readability without disrupting the narrative flow.

2. THE SETTLER PARABLE: This is one of George's most famous and beloved passages. He tells the story of a first settler on empty prairie land, showing how the arrival of neighbors -- and eventually the growth of a city -- gives the land enormous value that has nothing to do with the land's natural qualities. The translation preserves every element of this vivid narrative: the embarrassment of riches, the logrolling, the quilting bees, the circus passage (which is a tour de force of rhetorical accumulation). The circus passage is kept nearly intact because it's genuinely beautiful and its Biblical/literary references (Daniel, Joseph, Hannibal, the Maccabees, Kubla Khan) are part of George's distinctive voice.

3. KEY TERMS: "margin of cultivation" -- used directly throughout since this chapter depends on the technical concept. "rent" kept as economic rent throughout. "productive points" preserved. "political economy" does not appear but "political economists" -> "economists" at the end. "subsistence" -> "food" or "food supply." "aggregation" doesn't appear.

4. GENDER-NEUTRAL LANGUAGE: The settler is rendered with "they/them" throughout, which works naturally since the passage is a parable about a generic first settler. "He settles" -> "They settle." "His own blacksmith" -> "Their own blacksmith." "Men" -> "people" throughout. "The man who sets out" -> left as-is in one instance since it's describing a specific hypothetical individual, but adjusted where possible. "Every additional pair of hands" -> preserved (gender-neutral already). The only tricky spot is "a man with a span of horses" -> "one person with a team of horses."

5. SECTION HEADERS: Added bold headers to guide readers through this long chapter's argument: "The Malthusian Connection," "The Key Misunderstanding," "Diminishing Returns Don't Tell the Whole Story," "Summary of Population's Effect on the Margin," "The Greater Force: Population Creates Value in Land," "The Parable of the First Settler," "The Land Gains Value Without Changing," "From Farm to City," "The Real Driver of Rising Rent," "The Ship Metaphor," "Recap."

6. THE SHIP METAPHOR: George's brief, powerful metaphor near the end -- comparing Earth to a well-provisioned ship -- is preserved almost verbatim. It's one of his most quotable passages and needs no modernization beyond minor smoothing.

7. HISTORICAL REFERENCES: Preserved all specific references: St. Louis, Chicago, San Francisco, Philadelphia, New Orleans, St. Petersburg, Pennsylvania, Wyoming, Montana. These ground the argument in real geography. "The Comstock" doesn't appear in this chapter.

8. McCULLOCH: Identified as "the economist McCulloch" on his mention.

9. CUBLA KHAN / KUBLA KHAN: George writes "Kubla Khan" (Coleridge's poem). Preserved as-is.

10. TONE: This chapter moves from technical economic argument to one of George's most lyrical passages (the settler parable) and then to a triumphant conclusion. The translation follows these tonal shifts faithfully. The circus passage is deliberately exuberant -- George is showing the richness of human community that population makes possible.

11. ENDNOTES: No endnotes in this chapter.

KEY TERMS AND REFERENCES ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- TWO WAYS POPULATION INCREASES RENT: (1) By lowering the margin of cultivation (the standard Ricardo explanation), and (2) by creating special value in particular locations through the efficiencies of population density. George argues the second is actually more important.
- THE SETTLER PARABLE: The central narrative device of this chapter. A first settler on empty but fertile prairie land is shown progressing from poverty (in solitude) to great wealth (as a city grows around them) -- without the land's natural qualities changing at all. The land's value comes entirely from population growth.
- LOCALIZED PRODUCTIVITY: George's key insight in this chapter -- population growth doesn't just increase labor power generally, it creates productivity that is localized on specific land (the center of population). This localized productivity IS rent.
- THE SHIP METAPHOR: "It is a well-provisioned ship, this vessel on which we sail through space." A memorable image for Earth's resources, with the pointed observation that great power comes to those who can say "This is mine!" as new resources become accessible.
- MALTHUSIAN DISPROOF CONTINUED: George shows that population growth increases total productive power even when it forces cultivation onto inferior land. The gains from cooperation and specialization more than compensate.

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY: "margin of cultivation" used directly throughout. "rent" = economic rent. "wages" and "interest" in George's technical senses. "economists" for "political economists."

TONE: Moving from technical to lyrical to triumphant. The next chapter (021) will examine technological improvement's effect on distribution -- a more purely analytical chapter.

STRUCTURAL NOTE: The chapter established that population growth always increases rent. The next chapter will show that technological improvement ALSO always increases rent. Together these two chapters prove that all material progress enriches landowners at the expense of workers and capital.
