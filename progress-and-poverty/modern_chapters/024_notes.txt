Major changes and rationale:

1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: This chapter contains some of George's most powerful and most complex prose. The long analytical sentences are broken into clearer units while preserving the rhetorical momentum. The great catalogue paragraph ("It explains why interest and wages are higher... It explains why improvements... It explains what is commonly called...") is preserved as a single building paragraph because its cumulative, anaphoric structure is deliberately powerful -- George is showing the explanatory power of his theory by stacking up everything it can account for. Breaking it apart would destroy the rhetorical effect.

2. KEY TERMS: "political economy" does not appear in this chapter. "margin of cultivation" -> "margin" (already established). "speculative rent line" and "normal rent line" preserved. "subsistence" -> "bare living" or "basic wants" depending on context. "aggregation" -> "concentration." "pecuniary interest" -> "financial interest." "effectual demand" does not appear. "amelioration of manners" -> "refinement of social conduct."

3. GENDER-NEUTRAL LANGUAGE: Extensive work in this chapter. "Men" -> "people," "workers," "humanity" throughout. "Man" -> "humanity" or restructured. "He" generics -> "they." This chapter has a powerful arc from diagnosis to evidence to vision, and the headers help readers follow it.

5. SLAVERY REFERENCES: George uses slavery as a deliberate analogy -- comparing wage workers under land monopoly to enslaved people. The translation modernizes the language ("Cuban slave" -> "enslaved person," "the slave point" preserved as George's technical term but contextualized) while preserving the force of George's argument. The passage about English land values being worth more than the nation's population "at average 1860 slave prices" is preserved as a deliberate and devastating comparison.

They're preserved in full, with slight modernization ("He will tell you" -> "They'll tell you"). The punch line ("Go get yourself a piece of ground, and hold on to it") and the devastating coda ("In the new city you may have a luxurious mansion. 8. THE PERORATION: The final passage beginning "In all our long investigation..." is George at his most passionate and visionary. The metaphors are preserved: the advance through enemy territory, the hidden-picture analogy, and the soaring conclusion about land as the foundation of all civilization. "Children of the soil as truly as the blade of grass or the flower of the field" is one of George's most beautiful phrases and is preserved exactly. The Brahmin epigraph is kept as the chapter's closing note.

10. TONE: This is the emotional and intellectual climax of the book's argument. George has spent 24 chapters building his case, and here he delivers the verdict. The translation preserves both the analytical precision (the explanation of how rent intercepts progress) and the passionate conviction (the comparison of civilization's poor to tribal peoples, the devastating thought experiments). The chapter should feel like a door opening -- the mystery is solved, and everything suddenly makes sense.

George's actual argument is egalitarian -- he's saying that the poor of London are *worse off* than indigenous peoples, which is an argument *for* the dignity of those peoples. The modernized language should make his progressive intent clearer, not obscure it.

KEY TERMS AND CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED:

- THE CENTRAL THESIS IS NOW FULLY STATED: Land monopoly is the cause of poverty amid progress. Every increase in productive power is captured by landowners through rising rent, leaving wages stuck at the bare minimum. This is the culmination of Books I-V.

- "THE SLAVE POINT": George's term for the minimum wage level at which workers can barely survive -- equivalent to what enslaved people receive. The speculative advance of rent tends to push wages to this point.

- THE SPECIALIZATION TRAP: An important secondary argument. As civilization advances, division of labor increases total productivity but destroys individual independence. Workers become interchangeable parts in a vast machine, losing the ability to support themselves outside the system. This makes them more vulnerable to exploitation through land monopoly.

- THE THOUGHT EXPERIMENTS: Two powerful hypothetical scenarios. (1) A new island rising in the English Channel with free access -- wages would rise, rents would fall, with minimal actual relocation. (2) A hardheaded business person predicting the future of a growing village -- rent goes up, wages don't, buy land. These may be referenced later.

- HISTORICAL EXAMPLES ESTABLISHED: The Black Death (population drop -> less competition for land -> wages rise). Henry VIII's enclosures (land monopolized -> wages crash, vagrants appear). California gold rush (free access to land -> high wages). Comstock silver lode in Nevada (monopolized -> wealth goes to rent, not wages). These are George's proof cases.

- THE HIDDEN-PICTURE METAPHOR: Once you see that land monopoly is the cause, you can never unsee it. All social facts rearrange themselves. This is George's way of saying his theory provides a unified explanation.

- THE BRAHMIN EPIGRAPH: "To whomsoever the soil at any time belongs, to him belong the fruits of it." This ancient saying caps the chapter.

WHAT COMES NEXT: Book V is now complete. George has identified the problem (land monopoly causes poverty) and explained its mechanism (rent captures all gains from progress). Book VI will propose the remedy: making land common property through a single tax on land values. The tone should shift from diagnosis to prescription -- from "here's what's wrong" to "here's what we do about it."

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY: All established mappings maintained. "Economics" for "political economy." "The margin" for "margin of cultivation." "Purchasing power" for "effectual demand." "Normal rent line" and "speculative rent line." "The slave point" is new in this chapter and should be preserved going forward.

TONE: George's rhetoric has reached its peak. The next chapters (Book VI) will be more practical and programmatic, though still passionate. The remedy chapters should feel like the natural next step after the diagnosis -- not a letdown, but a focused turn toward action.
