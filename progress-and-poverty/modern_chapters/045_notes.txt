This is the most personal, emotionally vulnerable, and philosophically daring chapter of the entire book. It is George's meditation on death, meaning, faith, and hope. Translation decisions:

1. "MY TASK IS DONE": Preserved exactly as written -- the most famous opening line of the book's conclusion.

2. THE NIHILISTS PASSAGE: Preserved in full, including the newspaper account of the execution of Brandtner, Antonoff, and Ossinsky at Kiev. The quoted semi-official report is kept verbatim. George's defiant response -- "I do not believe it. No; not to oblivion!" -- is preserved exactly. This is one of the most moving moments in the book.

3. SPIRITUAL/RELIGIOUS CONTENT: George's sincere reflections on immortality, God, the meaning of life, and the relationship between moral and natural law are translated faithfully. His argument that economics, properly understood, restores grounds for religious hope is presented without amplification or irony. The references to Ormuzd and Ahriman (Zoroastrian good and evil), Christian and Faithful from Pilgrim's Progress, the Valley of the Shadow of Death, and the vision at Patmos are all preserved.

4. THE PLUTARCH QUOTE: Kept verbatim as a direct quotation from an ancient source. This is the book's final words and should not be modernized.

5. "POLITICAL ECONOMY": Became "economics" consistently. The personification of Political Economy as "she" was handled by shifting to "the discipline" and "it" -- George's point about economics being unjustly degraded is fully preserved without the gendered personification.

6. GENDER-NEUTRAL LANGUAGE: "Men" → "people" or "human beings" throughout. "Man's egotism" → "human self-importance." "Red Indians" → "Indigenous peoples." "Black savages" → "peoples around the world" -- George's point here is universalist (all spiritual traditions share a core truth), so the modernization serves his actual argument. "Race development" → "racial development" or "development of the species" as context required.

7. SENSITIVE LANGUAGE: "Red Indians" and "black savages" are the most problematic phrases. George is citing these traditions respectfully -- his point is that all human spiritual traditions, from the most ancient to the most formal, share a common core. The modernized language ("Indigenous peoples," "peoples around the world") preserves his inclusive, universalist intent while removing language that would distract modern readers from his actual meaning.

8. SENTENCE STRUCTURE: Many of George's long, philosophical sentences were broken into shorter ones, but I preserved the rhythm and cadence in the more lyrical passages (especially the closing sections about myth, allegory, and the muster roll).

9. ENDNOTE: Preserved endnote marker 68 in its original position.

10. THE ASTERISK DIVIDERS: George uses "* * *" as section breaks throughout the conclusion, marking shifts in mood and subject. These are preserved as they create the chapter's distinctive meditative rhythm.

11. TONE: This chapter required the most delicate touch. It moves between the voice of the economist, the voice of the reformer, the voice of the doubter, and the voice of the believer. Each register is preserved. The overall effect -- a deeply personal testament from a writer who has spent an entire book arguing with logic and evidence, and who now speaks from the heart about what it all means -- is maintained.
