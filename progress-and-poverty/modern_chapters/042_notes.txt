The passage about the Church nurturing the spirit that eventually broke her own chains is one of George's most striking historical observations and is preserved in full.

10. FINAL PARAGRAPH: George's connection of the law of progress to the moral law, and then to the teachings of Jesus, is preserved in full. This is a key philosophical moment -- George is arguing that economics, properly understood, teaches the same lessons as the great moral and religious traditions. The translation preserves both the intellectual claim and the emotional resonance.

11. TONE: This chapter moves from analytical (stating what the law must explain) through philosophical (articulating the law) to historical (illustrating it) to passionately moral (connecting it to the moral law). The translation follows these shifts, maintaining George's characteristic blend of rigorous argument and moral urgency.

Key terms and conventions from this chapter carrying forward:

THE LAW OF PROGRESS (now fully stated):
- "Association in equality is the law of progress"
- Association = people coming together in cooperation, which frees mental power for improvement
- Equality = justice = freedom = recognition of the moral law, which prevents the waste of mental power in conflict
- The boat analogy: society is like a boat; progress depends on effort devoted to moving forward, reduced by bailing (maintenance) and fighting among the crew (conflict)

INTERNAL RESISTANCES TO PROGRESS:
- As society grows, inequality tends to establish itself
- Power of habit + possibility of moral deterioration = social arrangements that once served progress become obstacles
- Collective power arising from association gets captured by a portion of the community
- Land monopoly identified as the "great cause of inequality"
- The garment metaphor: laws and institutions become "too tight" as society grows
- The labyrinth metaphor: humanity must navigate by reason and justice or lose its way

HISTORICAL FRAMEWORK:
- Asian civilizations: homogeneous, internal resistances develop thoroughly, leading to petrifaction (rigid caste systems)
- Greek civilization: small communities, association never strong enough, inequality eventually won
- Roman civilization: rotted from within due to inequality (great estates, slavery)
- European/modern civilization: superiority due to growth of equality WITH association; northern peoples + Christianity as the two great causes

SLAVERY ARGUMENT: George argues unequivocally that slavery has never aided progress, only hindered it. "No slave-holding people was ever an inventive people." "To freedom alone is given the spell of power..."

RELIGIOUS LANGUAGE: George connects economics to the moral law and to the teachings of Jesus. This is sincere and should be maintained.

FOR CHAPTER 043 (if continuing): George will likely continue applying his law of progress to specific phenomena. Watch for continued use of the association/equality framework and the internal-resistance analysis. The tone may become more specific and applied after the sweeping philosophical and historical arguments of this chapter.
