<explanation>
This chapter extends the argument from Chapter 1 (private property in land is unjust) to its most provocative claim: that it leads, inevitably, to slavery. George's argument is that land monopoly and chattel slavery differ only in form, not in substance -- and that the "free" form may actually be crueler because it lacks the personal accountability of direct slaveholding.

Key translation decisions:
- Preserved George's dramatic island thought experiment in full -- it's one of his most effective rhetorical devices.
- The extensive historical survey (Egypt, Greece, Rome, feudal Europe, India, Polynesia, Yucatan) was kept intact with minor contextual glosses. These examples are central to his argument that land monopoly, not capture in war, is the primary historical source of slavery.
- "Negroes" → "enslaved people" or "freed people" in passages about American slavery. "Slaves" retained when George is using it as a general category.
- "Helotism, villeinage, serfdom" -- kept these historical terms as George uses them to show the variety of land-based slavery across cultures.
- "Métayers" → "sharecroppers" for clarity.
- "Chattel slavery" retained as a technical term George uses specifically.
- The passage about Southern slaveholders comparing conditions is sensitive. George is NOT defending slavery -- he's making the provocative point that wage slavery under land monopoly can be worse than chattel slavery. Translated faithfully.
- Preserved the powerful closing metaphor of the two millstones.
- "Cato the Censor" → "Cato the Elder" (more recognizable modern name).
- Endnote 48 preserved in position.
</explanation>
