<explanation>
This chapter is the opening of Book VII, where George pivots from his proposed remedy (Book VI) to the moral argument for it. The core claim: property in things you make (just) vs. property in land (unjust) is a fundamental distinction most people miss because they lump everything together as "property."

Key translation decisions:
- Preserved George's rhetorical structure, which builds from first principles (what makes property rightful?) to logical deductions (therefore land ownership is unjust) to vivid illustrations (the banquet, the theater, the railroad car).
- "Political economy" → "economics" and "terms of political economy" → "language of economics" throughout.
- Gender-neutral language: "a man" → "a person," "men" → "people," etc., while keeping the structure natural.
- Added section headers (bold) to help modern readers follow the argument's structure, which is long and builds incrementally.
- Kept George's famous analogies (the pen, the banquet, the railroad car, the theater) intact -- they're among his most effective rhetorical tools.
- Preserved endnote markers (46, 47) in their original positions.
- "Indians" → "Indigenous peoples" in the passage about land titles.
- Kept the passionate tone, especially in the crescendo passages about the fundamental wrong and the British Islands.
- "Niggardliness of nature" → "stinginess of nature" as in earlier chapters.
- Preserved the Duke of Westminster and Astor references as vivid historical examples.
