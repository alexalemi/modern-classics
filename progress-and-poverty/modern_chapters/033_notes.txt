KEY TERMS AND REFERENCES ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- THE SINGLE TAX PROPOSAL: George's central remedy is now explicitly stated: "Abolish all taxation except that on land values." The mechanism: don't confiscate land, just tax away the full rental value. Landowners keep their titles (the "shell") but lose the unearned income (the "kernel"). This must be referenced consistently going forward.
- "CONFISCATE RENT": George's memorable formulation. He distinguishes between confiscating land (unnecessary, disruptive) and confiscating rent (sufficient, simple). The distinction should be maintained.
- HERBERT SPENCER: Cited as endorsing a version of land nationalization. George criticizes Spencer's concession of compensation to current landowners.
- "THE SHELL AND THE KERNEL": George's metaphor for leaving nominal ownership in place while taking the economic substance (rent).
- EXISTING TAX MACHINERY: George emphasizes that no new government apparatus is needed -- just redirect existing property tax mechanisms. This practical simplicity is a key selling point.
- COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS: George identifies three: (1) that capital and labor are fundamentally opposed, (2) that taxing land values would burden small farmers/homeowners, (3) that exempting capital from taxation would enrich the rich. He calls all three "confused thinking."

ARGUMENT STRUCTURE: The next chapter (034) tests the single tax proposal against the "canons of taxation" -- the standard criteria economists use to evaluate taxes. George will argue that a land value tax meets all four criteria better than any alternative: (1) effect on production, (2) ease of collection, (3) certainty, (4) equality/fairness.

TONE: Confident, practical, increasingly urgent. George has pivoted from theoretical argument to concrete policy. The next chapter should maintain this practical, testing-the-idea energy.

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY: "economics" for "political economy." "Economic depression" for "industrial depression." "Land values" as the standard term for what's being taxed. "Rent" in its economic sense (returns to landowners from mere ownership). "Capital" in George's sense (excludes land). Gender-neutral language throughout.
