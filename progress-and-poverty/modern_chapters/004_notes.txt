KEY ARGUMENTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER -- must carry forward:

1. WAGES COME FROM PRODUCTION, NOT CAPITAL: George's central thesis for Book I. "Production is always the mother of wages." Workers create value through labor before they receive wages. The employer exchanges one form of capital (money) for another (the product of labor) -- no advance occurs.

2. THE TWO-SENSES FALLACY: George's key logical weapon against the wages-fund doctrine. "Capital" in the premise (all food, clothing, shelter needed by workers) is NOT the same as "capital" in the conclusion (wealth in employers' hands devoted to production). This critical distinction must be referenced consistently going forward.

3. THE ADVANCE RUNS THE OPPOSITE DIRECTION: Where labor is performed before wages are paid, it is LABOR that advances capital to the EMPLOYER, not the other way around. This inversion of the orthodox view is central to George's argument.

4. CAPITAL IS NEEDED FOR ACCUMULATION, NOT FOR WAGES: When employers need capital, it's because they're accumulating stock (goods, tunnel, etc.), not because wages require an advance. The role of capital is as a buffer for the exchange process, not as a fund from which wages are drawn. Employers need capital as merchants/accumulators, not as employers per se.

5. VALUE IS CREATED CONTINUOUSLY: Value doesn't spring into existence only when a product is finished. It is created at every stage, from plowing a field to laying a keel to spinning yarn. A half-built ship has value; plowed land has more value than unplowed land.

6. THE MONEY ILLUSION: Confusion between wealth and money leads people to think capital is being reduced when wages are paid in cash, when really capital is just changing form (from cash to goods/products).
