<explanation>
This is the most historically dense chapter in Book VII, tracing the history of land ownership from primitive communal systems through Greece, Rome, feudalism, and into modern times. George's thesis: private property in land is an aberration, not the norm; common ownership is the original and natural form.

Key translation decisions:
- The extensive historical survey (village communities in India, Russia, Switzerland, Africa, Java, New Zealand; Greek helotism; Roman latifundia; Germanic marks; feudal tenures; English commons) was preserved in full with clarifying glosses.
- "Predial slavery" → "land-based slavery" / "slavery tied to the land."
- "Sclavonic" → "Slavic" (modern spelling).
- "Laplanders" → "Sami" (modern and respectful term for the people George calls Laplanders).
- "Flatheads" retained (a specific Native American people, used by George as a rhetorical comparison).
- "Kaffirs" → "peoples of South Africa" -- the original term is now considered a severe slur, particularly in South Africa.
- "Men of Rome" → "Citizens of Rome" in the Tiberius Gracchus quotation, since the Latin "viri Romani" was a formal address meaning citizens.
- The feudal system passages were carefully preserved because George makes the sophisticated point that feudalism, despite its abuses, at least theoretically recognized that land belonged to the community. This is a counterintuitive argument that needs to come through clearly.
- The passage about English commons and their enclosure is statistically detailed -- preserved the numbers as George provides them.
- "M. Guizot" → "the historian Guizot" for accessibility.
- "M. de Laveleye" → "de Laveleye" after first full identification.
- "Pecuniary" → "monetary."
- Endnotes 51, 52, 53 preserved in position.
- The comparison between what England could have been (no taxes, free trade) if feudal dues had been maintained is one of George's most striking what-if arguments -- preserved in full.
</explanation>
