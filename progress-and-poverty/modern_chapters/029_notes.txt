<explanation>
This chapter addresses the strongest practical objection to George's proposal: what about the people who bought land in good faith? Don't they deserve compensation? George's answer is a forceful no, and he makes the case from multiple angles.

Key translation decisions:
- Preserved George's famous exclamation "In the name of the Prophet -- figs!" -- a colorful period expression (from the Arabian Nights) that conveys his exasperation at Mill's inconsistency. It's too distinctive to paraphrase.
- The long passage about ongoing robbery (the litany of "It takes... it robs... it fills...") is one of George's most powerful rhetorical performances. Preserved its full rhythmic force with only minor modernization.
- "Quirk, Gammon & Snap" → "some sharp lawyers" -- the reference is to a satirical novel (Ten Thousand a Year by Samuel Warren) that modern readers won't know, and the meaning is clear from context.
- "Political economy" → "economics" throughout.
- "Expediency" → "practical benefit" in most cases, since "expediency" in modern English has a slightly negative connotation that George doesn't intend.
- "Nigger to the South" in the fugitive slave case reference → "slave to the South" -- the original offensive term is not necessary to convey the meaning and was already considered coarse in George's time; he was quoting a common saying.
- "Subsistence" → "survival" / "means of survival."
- Mill and Spencer are identified with brief contextual tags on first mention.
- The common law argument (innocent purchaser gets nothing) is a clever rhetorical move -- turning landowners' own legal principles against them. Preserved in full.
- Endnotes 49 and 50 preserved in position.
</explanation>
