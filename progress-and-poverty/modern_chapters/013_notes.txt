KEY TERMS AND CONCEPTS ESTABLISHED IN THIS CHAPTER:

- INTEREST DEFINED: Interest is the return to capital arising from the INCREASE of capital -- specifically from the reproductive forces of nature and the gains from exchange. It is NOT a payment for "abstinence" and NOT a reward for the power of tools to increase labor's productivity. Interest is declared "natural" and "just."

- THREE MODES OF PRODUCTION: George establishes a classification that will be referenced going forward:
  1. ADAPTING -- changing matter in form or location (carpentry, mining)
  2. GROWING -- harnessing vital forces of nature (agriculture, animal husbandry)
  3. EXCHANGING -- harnessing variations in natural/human powers through trade
  In mode 1, labor is the sole active cause. In modes 2 and 3, time is an element, and capital earns its increase through the reproductive forces involved.

- USE VS. INCREASE: Benefits from "use" go primarily to labor; benefits from "increase" go primarily to capital. These average out across all modes of production because of the interchangeability of wealth.

- BASTIAT'S PLANE: George has thoroughly dismantled this famous illustration, showing that the "plank" (interest payment) cannot be justified by the tool's power to increase productivity. The real justification comes from the element of TIME and the reproductive forces of nature.

- INTEREST vs. MONOPOLY: George is careful to distinguish legitimate interest (arising from natural reproductive forces) from returns arising from monopoly power. This distinction becomes central in the next chapter (014), which deals with "spurious capital" and profits mistaken for interest.

VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY: "economics" for "political economy." "mainstream economics" for "the current political economy." Section headers in bold. Endnote numbers preserved. Gender-neutral language in George's general statements. Named characters in examples retain original pronouns.

TONE: Careful, philosophical, step-by-step reasoning. George is building a positive theory rather than tearing one down. He's confident but measured -- this is constructive economics, not polemics.

ARGUMENT FLOW: Chapter 013 establishes that interest is natural and just. Chapter 014 will immediately qualify this by showing that much of what passes for interest in the real world is actually something else entirely -- monopoly profits, risk compensation, or returns on "spurious capital" (like government bonds that don't represent real wealth). The transition should feel like: "Interest is legitimate... but a lot of what people call 'interest' isn't interest at all."
