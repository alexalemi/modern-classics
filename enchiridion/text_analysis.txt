TEXT ANALYSIS — The Enchiridion (Epictetus, c. 125 AD)
Source: Thomas Wentworth Higginson's translation (1865, rev. 1890s),
Project Gutenberg #45109. The 1948 Liberal Arts Press introduction was
stripped in prep; we translate only Higginson's text.

WHAT THIS IS

A pocket field-manual of Stoic practice, compiled by Arrian from the
lectures of Epictetus — a Greek philosopher who spent his youth enslaved
in Rome and taught that the only true freedom is command of your own
mind. It is not an argument; it is a drill book. 51 short sections in
this edition (Higginson merges the sections conventionally numbered
50–53 into his 50 and 51; numbering matches standard editions through
§43 and only diverges at the very end). Median section: ~150 words.

This was one of the most-read books of the American founding generation
(the "founders' library" thread of this project): Franklin, Jefferson,
and Washington all knew it, mostly in Elizabeth Carter's translation.

STYLE AND TONE OF THE ORIGINAL

- Direct address throughout: a teacher talking AT you, second person,
  imperative mood. Keep it. Never drift into third-person essay voice.
- Aphoristic and blunt, with sudden homely analogies: the broken cup,
  the inn, the banquet dish, the voyage and the ship's captain, the
  actor handed a role, the two handles. These images ARE the book —
  keep every one, vivid and concrete.
- A drill-sergeant warmth: severe demands delivered with the confidence
  that you can meet them. The modern voice should sound like a tough,
  plain-spoken coach — bracing, not preachy, never self-help-smooth.
- Rhetorical questions and imagined objections ("But it was a bad man
  who took it." — answer them in the same punchy rhythm).

TRANSLATION CHALLENGES

- The text is already terse. The job is de-archaizing, not expansion.
  Word ratio should land near 1.0 (verify bounds 0.6–1.6; aim 0.9–1.3).
  Resist the urge to explain a point Epictetus deliberately left sharp.
- Higginson's Victorian abstractions need concrete modern equivalents:
  "semblances" → impressions; "conformable to nature" → in line with
  nature; "aversion" → what you avoid / avoidance (keep "desire and
  aversion" as the paired terms "desire and avoidance" consistently).
- Stoic technical terms, locked (see also running_notes.txt):
  - "things within our power / beyond our power" → "up to us / not up
    to us" — the modern standard rendering; lock it in Section 1 and
    reuse the phrase everywhere the distinction recurs.
  - phantasiai ("semblance", "appearance") → "impression". The drill in
    §1/§10/§20 of testing an impression before assenting must use the
    same word each time.
  - "views/notions/principles concerning things" → "judgments" (or
    "views" where rhythm wants it; §5 has a locked rendering below).
  - prohairesis ("will", "choice") → "your will" / "what you choose".
- God / the gods / Zeus / "the Author" (§17): keep exactly as the
  source has them — this is a pagan Stoic text; do not Christianize
  ("God" singular where Higginson has it is fine) and do not scrub.
- Verse quotations (§51): keep as indented verse blocks (two-space
  indent, hard line breaks — assemble.py renders these as verse).
  Attribute inline, briefly, since footnotes are gone: the "Lead me,
  Zeus" prayer is Cleanthes; "Who yields properly to Fate…" is
  Euripides; the last two lines are Socrates in Plato's Crito and
  Apology. A light touch: "as Cleanthes wrote:" etc.

SENSITIVE CONTENT — scholarly modernization of a canonical text

- Slavery: Epictetus was enslaved, and examples mention slaves and
  servants matter-of-factly (§12, §14, §26). Render honestly in a
  measured, historical register — "slave" where that is the meaning,
  without either sensationalizing or softening; this is his world and
  his argument.
- Death of loved ones: §3, §11, §14 use the death of a child or wife as
  the hard test case. Do NOT pad with modern consolation — the shock is
  the pedagogy. Translate plainly and let it land.
- §33/§40 on conduct and on women reflect ancient norms (purity before
  marriage, women valued for appearance). Render the argument as HIS
  argument, faithful and unembellished, in a neutral historical voice.

FAMOUS PASSAGES — locked modern renderings (checked by must_contain.txt)

- §1 opening: "Some things are up to us, and some things are not."
- §5: "People are disturbed not by things, but by the views they take
  of them."
- §8: "Don't demand that things happen as you wish; wish them to happen
  as they do happen, and your life will go smoothly."
- §17: "Remember that you are an actor in a play…" (keep the phrase
  "an actor in a play"; "the Author" may become "the playwright" after
  the first mention, but keep the capital-A Author on first use).
- §43: "Everything has two handles: one that will bear the weight, and
  one that won't."
- §51 verse: first line exactly "Lead me, Zeus, and you too, Destiny,"
- §51 close: "…though you are not yet Socrates, you should live as
  someone seeking to be Socrates."
