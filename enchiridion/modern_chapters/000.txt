Sections 1–15

Section 1

Some things are up to us, and some things are not. Up to us are our opinions, our aims, our desires, our avoidances — in a word, everything that is our own doing. Not up to us are our bodies, our property, our reputations, our positions — in a word, everything that is not our own doing.

The things that are up to us are by nature free, unblocked, unhindered. The things that are not up to us are weak, dependent, easily blocked, and belong to someone else. So remember: if you treat what is naturally dependent as if it were free, and claim what belongs to others as your own, you will be blocked at every turn. You will grieve, you will be shaken, you will find fault with gods and men alike. But if you claim as yours only what is actually yours, and see what belongs to others for exactly what it is, then no one will ever force you and no one will stop you. You will blame no one, accuse no one, do nothing against your will. No one will hurt you. You will have no enemy, and nothing will harm you.

Since you are aiming at something this big, remember that you cannot be halfhearted about it. Some things you must give up entirely; others you must set aside for now. If you want all this and power and wealth besides, you may miss the power and wealth because you were reaching for the higher goal — and you will certainly miss the one thing that actually brings happiness and freedom.

So practice, starting now, saying to every harsh impression: "You are just an impression, and not at all the thing you appear to be." Then examine it and test it by the rules you have — first and foremost by this one: does it concern the things that are up to us, or the things that are not? And if it concerns anything not up to us, be ready to say: this is nothing to me.

Section 2

Remember: desire promises you the getting of what you desire, and avoidance promises you the escaping of what you avoid. The person who fails to get what he desires is disappointed; the person who lands in what he was avoiding is miserable. So if you avoid only the undesirable things within your control, you will never land in anything you are avoiding. But if you try to avoid sickness, or death, or poverty, you are signing up for misery. Pull your avoidance off everything that is not up to us, then, and aim it at the undesirable things that are up to us. As for desire — for now, switch it off completely. If you desire anything not up to us, disappointment is guaranteed; and you do not yet have a firm grip even on the things that are up to us, the ones actually worth desiring. Where you genuinely must pursue or avoid something, do even that with a light touch — gently, with moderation.

Section 3

With everything that delights your mind, or is useful to you, or that you tenderly love, remind yourself what kind of thing it is. Start with the smallest cases. If you have a favorite cup, say, "It is a cup that I am fond of." Then if it breaks, you can bear it. When you embrace your child or your wife, say, "I am embracing a mortal." Then if either of them dies, you can bear it.

Section 4

When you are about to do anything, remind yourself what kind of thing it is. Going to the baths? Picture what usually happens there — people splashing, people shoving, people cursing, people stealing. You will go about it more safely if you say to yourself, "I want to bathe — and to keep my will in line with nature." Do the same with every action. Then if something gets in your way at the baths, you will be ready to say: "I did not want only to bathe. I also wanted to keep my will in line with nature — and I will not keep it there if I lose my temper at what happens."

Section 5

People are disturbed not by things, but by the views they take of them. Death, for instance, is nothing terrible — or it would have seemed terrible to Socrates. The terror is in our view of death, the view that it is terrible. So when we are blocked, or disturbed, or grieving, let us never blame anyone else. Let us blame ourselves — that is, our own views. Blaming others for your own misfortunes is the mark of someone with no training. Blaming yourself means your training has begun. Blaming neither others nor yourself — that is someone fully trained.

Section 6

Don't get puffed up over an excellence that isn't yours. If a horse got puffed up and said, "I am handsome," that would be bearable. But when you get puffed up and say, "I have a handsome horse," understand that you are bragging about a good quality that belongs to the horse. What, then, is actually yours? The use you make of your impressions. Handle your impressions in line with nature, and then you may be proud — for then you will be proud of a good that is your own.

Section 7

Think of a voyage. The ship drops anchor, and you go ashore for water. Along the way you may amuse yourself picking up a shellfish or a truffle — fine. But keep your thoughts fixed on the ship, and keep listening for the captain's call. When he calls, you must drop all of it and run, or you will be carried aboard tied up like a sheep. Life is the same. If, instead of a shellfish or a truffle, you are given a wife or a child, take them — nothing wrong with that. But if the captain calls, run to the ship. Leave everything. Don't look back. And if you are old, never wander far from the ship, or you may be missing when he calls.

Section 8

Don't demand that things happen as you wish; wish them to happen as they do happen, and your life will go smoothly.

Section 9

Sickness blocks the body, but not the will — unless the will chooses to be blocked. Lameness blocks the leg, but not the will. Say this to yourself about everything that happens, and you will find that it blocks something else — but never you.

Section 10

Whenever anything hits you, remember to turn inward and ask what capacity you have for dealing with it. If you meet a beautiful person, you will find self-control is the capacity you need. If pain comes, you will find endurance. If insults come, you will find patience. Make this a habit, and your impressions will not sweep you away.

Section 11

Never say about anything, "I have lost it." Say instead, "I have given it back." Has your child died? Given back. Has your wife died? Given back. Has your estate been taken from you? That too is given back. "But it was a bad man who took it." What is it to you through whose hands the one who gave it asks for it again? While he lets you keep it, hold it as something not your own — the way travelers hold a room at an inn.

Section 12

If you want to make progress, drop this kind of thinking: "If I neglect my affairs, I will have nothing to live on." "If I don't punish my slave, he will be good for nothing." Better to die of hunger, free of grief and fear, than to live rich and shaken. Better your slave be bad than you be unhappy.

So begin with the little things. A little oil gets spilled, a little wine gets stolen. Say to yourself: "This is the price of peace and tranquility — and nothing comes free." When you call your slave, keep in mind that he may not answer, and if he does, he may not do what you want. But things are not so good for him, and not so bad for you, that your calm should ever be in his power.

Section 13

If you want to make progress, be content to look foolish and dull about externals. Don't try to seem like someone who knows things. And if others treat you as somebody special, distrust yourself. Be sure of this: it is not easy to keep your will in line with nature and to hold on to externals at the same time. While you give yourself to the one, you must neglect the other.

Section 14

If you wish your children and your wife and your friends to live forever, you are a fool — you are wishing for things not up to you to be up to you, for what belongs to others to be yours. Likewise, if you wish your slave to be without fault, you are a fool — you are wishing for vice to be not vice but something else. But if you wish not to be disappointed in your desires, that is in your power. So practice what is in your power. Your master is whoever can give you, or take from you, the things you seek or shun. If you want to be free, then, want nothing and refuse nothing that depends on others. Otherwise you must be a slave.

Section 15

Remember to behave in life as you would at a banquet. A dish comes around to you? Reach out your hand and take a modest share. It passes you by? Don't grab at it. It hasn't reached you yet? Don't lean out in longing — wait until it comes to you. Act this way toward children, toward a wife, toward position, toward wealth, and one day you will be worthy to feast with the gods. And if you can go further — if you can pass up even the things set in front of you — then you will be worthy not only to feast with the gods but to rule with them. That is how Diogenes and Heraclitus and others like them became divine, and were rightly called so.
