TEXT ANALYSIS — The Way to Wealth (Benjamin Franklin, 1758)
Source: Gutenberg #43855, an 1810 Darton chapbook printing; prep.py
extracts Franklin's essay only (publisher ads, illustration captions,
and Darton's added Roman-numeral markers are stripped).

WHAT THIS IS

Franklin's most reprinted work: the preface to the 1758 Poor Richard's
Almanack. The frame is a joke Franklin plays on himself — "Richard
Saunders" overhears an old man, Father Abraham, answer a crowd's
complaint about taxes by stitching twenty-five years of Poor Richard's
proverbs into one sermon on industry, frugality, and not buying junk at
auctions. The punchline: the crowd approves every word and immediately
does the opposite — and only Richard himself walks away resolved to keep
wearing his old coat. Keep the irony; it's what saves the sermon from
being a sermon.

STYLE AND TONE

- Two voices: Richard Saunders' wry first-person frame (self-deprecating,
  vain-and-knows-it) and Father Abraham's rolling proverb-sermon. Keep
  them distinct: the frame conversational and amused; the speech warm,
  rhythmic, preacher-like, stacking proverb on proverb with "as Poor
  Richard says" as the drumbeat.
- KEEP "as Poor Richard says" as a verbatim refrain everywhere it occurs
  (~12 times) — the repetition is the running gag.
- The audience-context details (heavy taxes, the auction/"vendue",
  merchants' goods) stay 1750s-colonial — do not modernize the setting,
  only the language.

PROVERB HANDLING — the crux of this text

The proverbs are the payload; readers come for them. Rule: proverbs
stay as close to their famous wording as possible — de-archaize only
pronouns/verb forms (dost→do, thou→you, wilt→will) and only when
needed. Never paraphrase a proverb into plain prose. Where a proverb's
key word is archaic but famous ("dear school"), keep it and let context
carry the meaning. Quotation marks around proverbs as in the source.

Locked renderings (checked by must_contain.txt):
- "Early to bed, and early to rise, makes a man healthy, wealthy, and
  wise" — verbatim.
- "God helps those who help themselves" (source: "them that").
- "There are no gains without pains."
- "The sleeping fox catches no poultry."
- "Do you love life? Then do not squander time, for that is the stuff
  life is made of." (source: "Dost thou love life, then do not
  squander time…")
- "Experience keeps a dear school, but fools will learn in no other."
- "Three moves are as bad as a fire." (source: "Three removes is…")
- "a word to the wise is enough"

ARCHAIC VOCABULARY MAP

- "vendue" → "auction"; "goods at a vendue" etc.
- "wants/wanting" (= lacks) → "needs/lacking" as sense requires
- "perhaps" spelled out from "mayst"/"shalt" constructions; Quaker-ish
  thees/thous in the closing ("thine to serve thee") → modern warm
  sign-off that keeps the joke ("I am, as ever, yours to serve you,
  RICHARD SAUNDERS") — keep the signature line.
- "Philosopher's stone" stays (capital P), it's an image, add no gloss.

SENSITIVE CONTENT

Almost none. Brief references to debtors' prison and to "Ethiopians"
do not appear in this edition; poverty and debt are moralized in
period-typical ways — render plainly, no editorializing. Job's
suffering reference stays.

FORMAT

Single chapter file (000.txt / modern 000.txt), first line
"The Way to Wealth". Keep the "COURTEOUS READER," salutation line as a
short opening line ("Courteous reader," modernized capitalization),
keep the asterisk scene-break line before the closing frame paragraph
exactly as in the source ("       *       *       *       *       *"),
and end with the RICHARD SAUNDERS signature. Word ratio target 0.9–1.3.
