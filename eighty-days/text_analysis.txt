TEXT ANALYSIS — Around the World in Eighty Days
(Jules Verne, 1872; Le Tour du monde en quatre-vingts jours)
Source: the ORIGINAL FRENCH from Project Gutenberg (#800). TRANSLATED
FROM THE FRENCH. Second volume in the Verne recovery project (after
Twenty Thousand Leagues); the from-the-French novel pipeline is proven —
see twenty-thousand-leagues/ for the pattern.

WHY THIS BOOK

Verne's most purely delightful book: a comic race around the globe on a
wager, built out of steamers, railways, and timetables. The Victorian
English translations flattened its wit and stumbled on its geography and
money. Retold complete in brisk, funny modern English, it should read
like the page-turner it is — because it is one.

WHAT THIS IS

London, 1872. The imperturbable Phileas Fogg wagers £20,000 — half his
fortune — that he can circle the world in eighty days, and sets off that
very night with his brand-new French valet. A Scotland Yard detective,
convinced Fogg is a bank robber, chases him across three continents; a
rescued young widow joins the party; and every missed connection,
storm, and sabotage tightens a countdown that turns on a final, famous
trick of the calendar.

THE FOUR VOICES — the book is a character comedy; get these right

- PHILEAS FOGG — the machine-precise English gentleman: exact,
  unhurried, unflappable, laconic, mathematically punctual (whist at the
  Reform Club, life run by the clock). Beneath the cold surface, secretly
  generous, gallant, and fearless. The comedy AND the feeling both come
  from his imperturbability — never let him fluster; let his rare warmth
  land the harder for it. Keep his speech clipped and precise.
- JEAN PASSEPARTOUT — his French valet: warm, agile, loyal, excitable,
  big-hearted, a former gymnast/strongman/fireman who wanted a quiet life
  and got the opposite. The emotional center and the comic engine. His
  name (keep it: "Passepartout" = a skeleton key, "goes anywhere") is a
  running joke. Give him lively, colloquial, feeling speech — the foil to
  Fogg's calm.
- DETECTIVE FIX — the dogged, obsessive Scotland Yard man who trails Fogg
  around the world certain he is the bank thief: comic, single-minded,
  by turns Fogg's shadow and (once he's stuck) his reluctant ally. Not a
  fool — a professional whose fixed idea makes him funny.
- AOUDA — the young Parsee widow Fogg rescues from a forced funeral pyre:
  dignified, brave, gentle, educated; her quiet regard for Fogg becomes
  the book's heart. Render her with full dignity, never as an ornament.

STYLE AND TONE

- COMIC ADVENTURE, brisk and propulsive. The modern voice is witty,
  clean, cinematic — short sentences for the action, dry humor for Fogg,
  warmth for Passepartout. Contractions welcome. This is the lightest,
  fastest book in the project so far; keep it FUN.
- Verne's SIGNATURE CHAPTER TITLES ("In Which Passepartout's Nose Grows
  Enormously Long") are a charm — they are translated in full and kept as
  the chapter headings; match their playful, mock-formal tone.
- The COUNTDOWN is the engine: dates, times, timetables, distances,
  connections made and missed. Keep every figure exact — the plot is a
  clockwork and the reader is doing the math with Fogg.
- Money: pounds sterling (£20,000 wager; £55,000 bank theft; the bag of
  banknotes). Keep sterling and Verne's figures exactly.

LOCKED TERMINOLOGY

- Names kept as Verne has them: Phileas Fogg; Passepartout (French name,
  never anglicized); Detective/Inspector Fix; Aouda; Sir Francis
  Cromarty; the Reform Club; the whist partners (Stuart, Flanagan,
  Fallentin, Sullivan, Ralph). Fogg is "Mr. Fogg" / "Phileas Fogg" /
  "his master" (narration). Passepartout addresses Fogg as "sir" (for
  "monsieur") and "Mr. Fogg"; he refers to him as "my master".
- Conveyances kept as named (italic sense), Verne's forms: the steamers
  Mongolia, Rangoon, Carnatic, General Grant, Henrietta; the sloop
  Tankadère; the Great Indian Peninsular Railway; the Pacific Railroad.
- "eighty days"; the wager "twenty thousand pounds"; "the Reform Club".
- The elephant Kiouni; the suttee (sati) — the widow-burning Fogg
  interrupts; render with the measured historical register (below).
- The DATE-LINE TWIST is the whole ending: traveling continuously
  EASTWARD, the party unknowingly gains a day, so Fogg is a day early and
  wins. Render the reveal crisply and correctly — the reader must feel
  the click of it.

SENSITIVE / EDITORIAL

- Period-dated 1872 colonial and ethnographic content: the suttee rescue
  in India, the "Indians"/Sioux attack on the Union Pacific, the Mormons
  of Utah, various "natives". Translate Verne's meaning faithfully in a
  measured register — neither sensationalized nor bowdlerized — as
  scholarly modernization of a canonical historical text. Aouda in
  particular is drawn with dignity; keep it.

FAMOUS PASSAGES

- The opening portrait of Phileas Fogg and the £20,000 wager at the
  Reform Club.
- The rescue of Aouda from the pyre.
- The final chapters: Fogg believing he has lost by five minutes, then
  the discovery that he gained a day by circling eastward — and the
  closing line that the tour won him nothing but happiness.

FORMAT

37 files, one per chapter (I–XXXVII), single part, no dividers; chapters
are short (~1,800 French words average), none needed splitting. Ratio:
French -> modern English narrative runs ≈ 0.95–1.30 (near 1:1; do NOT
pad). Verify with --min-ratio 0.9 --max-ratio 1.5.
