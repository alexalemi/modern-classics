In Which Phileas Fogg Proves Equal to the Occasion

An hour later, the steamer Henrietta passed the light-ship that marks the mouth of the Hudson, rounded the point of Sandy Hook, and stood out to sea. Through the day she ran along Long Island, well off the Fire Island light, and drove eastward at a fine clip.

The next day, the 13th of December, at noon, a man climbed to the bridge to take the ship's bearings. You would naturally suppose this man to be Captain Speedy. Not in the least. It was Phileas Fogg, Esq.

As for Captain Speedy, he was quite simply locked in his cabin, roaring in a way that betrayed a rage — a thoroughly pardonable rage — worked up to its absolute peak.

What had happened was very simple. Phileas Fogg wanted to reach Liverpool; the captain would not carry him there. So Phileas Fogg had agreed to take passage for Bordeaux, and in the thirty hours he had been aboard he had maneuvered so cleverly, with fistfuls of banknotes, that the crew — sailors and stokers alike, a somewhat shady lot who were on poor terms with the captain — now belonged to him body and soul. And that was why Phileas Fogg was giving the orders in Captain Speedy's place; why the captain was locked in his cabin; and why the Henrietta was steaming toward Liverpool. One thing was very clear from the way Mr. Fogg handled the ship: Mr. Fogg had once been a sailor.

How the adventure would end, no one could yet say. Even so, Aouda could not shake a certain uneasiness, though she said nothing of it. Fix, for his part, had been utterly dumbfounded at first. As for Passepartout, he found the whole thing simply delightful.

"Between eleven and twelve knots," Captain Speedy had said, and sure enough, the Henrietta held to that average speed.

So if — how many "ifs" there still were! — so if the sea did not turn too foul, if the wind did not veer round to the east, if no damage came to the hull and no accident to the engine, the Henrietta might, in the nine days counted from the 12th of December to the 21st, cover the three thousand miles that separate New York from Liverpool. True enough, once she arrived, the business of the Henrietta laid on top of the business of the Bank might carry the gentleman a good deal further than he cared to go.

For the first few days the voyage went beautifully. The sea was not too rough; the wind seemed settled in the northeast; the sails were set, and under her fore-and-aft canvas the Henrietta ran like a genuine transatlantic liner.

Passepartout was overjoyed. His master's latest exploit — whose consequences he refused to let himself consider — thrilled him to the core. The crew had never seen a more cheerful, more nimble young fellow. He showered the sailors with friendship and amazed them with his acrobatic stunts. He lavished the best of nicknames on them and the most tempting of drinks. In his eyes they worked the ship like gentlemen, and the stokers stoked like heroes. His good humor, wildly contagious, spread to every man aboard. He had forgotten the past, the troubles, the dangers. He thought only of the goal, now so near, and now and then he fairly boiled with impatience, as though he too were being fired up by the Henrietta's furnaces. Often, as well, the worthy fellow would drift in circles around Fix; he would fix him with a look that "said a great deal" — but he did not speak to him, for there was no shred of friendship left between the two former friends.

And Fix, it must be said, no longer understood a thing! The conquest of the Henrietta, the buying-up of her crew, this Fogg maneuvering like a seasoned sailor — the whole business left his head spinning. He no longer knew what to think! But then again, a gentleman who began by stealing fifty-five thousand pounds might very well end by stealing a ship. And so Fix was led, naturally enough, to conclude that the Henrietta, under Fogg's command, was not bound for Liverpool at all, but for some corner of the globe where the thief, turned pirate, meant to settle himself comfortably out of reach! It was a hypothesis, one has to admit, that could not have been more plausible, and the detective was beginning to regret, very seriously, that he had ever gotten himself mixed up in this affair.

As for Captain Speedy, he went on roaring in his cabin, and Passepartout, whose job it was to see him fed, did so only with the greatest precautions, strong as he was. Mr. Fogg, meanwhile, no longer seemed even to suspect that there was a captain aboard at all.

On the 13th they passed over the tail of the Newfoundland Bank. These are dangerous waters. In winter especially, fogs are frequent there and the squalls fearsome. Since the day before, the barometer had dropped sharply, hinting at a coming change in the weather. And sure enough, during the night the temperature shifted, the cold grew keener, and at the same time the wind swung round to the southeast.

This was a setback. To keep from wandering off his course, Mr. Fogg had to take in sail and force the engine. Even so, the ship's progress slowed, given the state of the sea, whose long rollers broke against her stem. She pitched violently, and her speed suffered for it. Little by little the breeze was building toward a hurricane, and men were already foreseeing the moment when the Henrietta might no longer be able to hold her head to the waves. And if she had to run before them, that meant the unknown, with all its evil chances.

Passepartout's face darkened along with the sky, and for two days the honest fellow went through mortal agonies. But Phileas Fogg was a bold sailor who knew how to stand up to the sea, and he held his course, without even easing off to half steam. When the Henrietta could not ride over a wave, she went straight through it, and her deck was swept clean fore and aft — but through it she went. Sometimes, too, the screw would lift clear of the water, its blades thrashing wildly at the air as a mountain of sea heaved her stern above the surface — but the ship kept driving forward all the same.

Still, the wind did not freshen as badly as one might have feared. This was none of those hurricanes that come tearing past at ninety miles an hour. It held to a strong gale, but unfortunately it blew with stubborn persistence out of the southeast and made setting canvas impossible. And yet — as we shall see — a little help for the steam would have come in very handy!

The 16th of December was the seventy-fifth day since the departure from London. All told, the Henrietta was not yet running any alarming amount behind. Roughly half the crossing was done, and the worst stretches of water were behind her. In summer, success would have been a safe bet. In winter, they were at the mercy of the bad season. Passepartout offered no opinion. Deep down he was hopeful, and if the wind failed him, at least he was counting on the steam.

Now, that very day, the chief engineer came up on deck, ran into Mr. Fogg, and fell into a rather heated conversation with him.

Without knowing why — by some presentiment, no doubt — Passepartout felt a vague unease come over him. He would have given one of his ears to catch what was being said. As it was, he managed to snatch a few words, these among them, spoken by his master:

"You're certain of what you're telling me?"

"Certain, sir," the engineer answered. "Don't forget that since we set out, we've been burning with every furnace lit, and while we had coal enough to run at half steam from New York to Bordeaux, we haven't got enough to run at full steam from New York to Liverpool!"

"I'll see to it," replied Mr. Fogg.

Passepartout had understood. He was seized with mortal dread.

The coal was going to run out!

"Ah, if my master gets us out of this one," he told himself, "he'll be a mighty fine man indeed!"

And, running into Fix, he could not help bringing him up to date on the situation.

"So," the agent answered through clenched teeth, "you believe we're going to Liverpool!"

"Of course!"

"Idiot!" replied the inspector, who walked off with a shrug.

Passepartout was on the point of paying him back sharply for the epithet — though he could not, in any case, grasp its true meaning — but he told himself that the unlucky Fix must be badly disappointed, badly wounded in his self-esteem, after so clumsily chasing a false trail all the way around the world, and he let the matter drop.

And now, what course would Phileas Fogg take? It was hard to imagine. And yet it seems the phlegmatic gentleman settled on one, for that very evening he sent for the engineer and said to him:

"Pile on the fires and hold our course until the fuel is completely used up."

A few moments later, the Henrietta's funnel was belching torrents of smoke.

The ship kept driving on at full steam; but, just as he had warned, two days later, on the 18th, the engineer reported that the coal would run out before the day was through.

"Don't let the fires go down," replied Mr. Fogg. "On the contrary. Load the safety valves."

That day, around noon, after taking his altitude and calculating the ship's position, Phileas Fogg sent for Passepartout and gave him the order to go fetch Captain Speedy. It was rather like ordering the good fellow to go turn loose a tiger, and he went down to the poop, telling himself:

"He'll be positively rabid!"

Sure enough, a few minutes later, amid shouts and curses, a bomb came flying up onto the poop deck. This bomb was Captain Speedy. He was plainly about to explode.

"Where are we?" These were the first words he got out through his fits of choking fury — and truly, had the good man been the least bit apoplectic, he would never have recovered from it.

"Where are we?" he repeated, his face purple.

"Seven hundred and seventy miles from Liverpool," replied Mr. Fogg with imperturbable calm.

"Pirate!" cried Andrew Speedy.

"I sent for you, sir —"

"Sea rover!"

"— sir," Phileas Fogg went on, "to ask you to sell me your ship."

"No! By all the devils, no!"

"The trouble is, I'm going to be forced to burn her."

"Burn my ship!"

"At least the upper works, since we're out of fuel."

"Burn my ship!" cried Captain Speedy, who could barely get the syllables out anymore. "A ship worth fifty thousand dollars!"

"Here are sixty thousand!" replied Phileas Fogg, holding out a bundle of banknotes to the captain.

This produced a prodigious effect on Andrew Speedy. You cannot be an American without the sight of sixty thousand dollars stirring a certain emotion in you. In an instant the captain forgot his rage, his imprisonment, all his grievances against his passenger. His ship was twenty years old. This might turn into a golden bargain!... The bomb could no longer go off. Mr. Fogg had pulled its fuse.

"And I keep the iron hull," said Speedy, in a curiously softened tone.

"The iron hull and the engine, sir. Is it a deal?"

"It's a deal."

And Andrew Speedy, snatching up the bundle of banknotes, counted them and made them vanish into his pocket.

Through this whole scene, Passepartout had gone white. As for Fix, he nearly had a stroke. Close to twenty thousand pounds spent — and on top of that, this Fogg handing the seller back the hull and the engine, that is to say nearly the entire value of the ship! True, the sum stolen from the bank came to fifty-five thousand pounds!

When Andrew Speedy had pocketed the money, Mr. Fogg said to him:

"Sir, don't let any of this astonish you. Understand that I lose twenty thousand pounds if I am not back in London on the 21st of December, at eight forty-five in the evening. Now, I had missed the New York steamer, and since you refused to carry me to Liverpool —"

"And I did well to refuse, by the fifty thousand devils of hell," cried Andrew Speedy, "since I clear at least forty thousand dollars by it."

Then, more calmly:

"Do you know something, captain?" he added.

"Fogg."

"Captain Fogg — well, there's a bit of the Yankee in you."

And having paid his passenger what he took to be a compliment, he was going off when Phileas Fogg said to him:

"Then this ship now belongs to me?"

"Certainly — from the keel to the masthead, for everything that's timber, of course!"

"Good. Have the interior fittings torn out and burn the wreckage."

One can imagine how much of that dry wood it took to keep the steam at sufficient pressure. That day the poop, the deckhouses, the cabins, the quarters, the false deck — all of it went into the fire.

The next day, the 19th of December, they burned the masting, the spare spars, the booms. They cut down the masts and chopped them up with axes. The crew went at it with unbelievable zeal. Passepartout — hewing, hacking, sawing — did the work of ten men. It was a frenzy of demolition.

The next day, the 20th, the bulwarks, the rails, the upperworks, the greater part of the deck, were all devoured. The Henrietta was nothing now but a hulk cut down flush like a pontoon.

But that day they had raised the coast of Ireland and the Fastnet light.

Even so, by ten in the evening the ship was still only abreast of Queenstown. Phileas Fogg had no more than twenty-four hours left to reach London! And that was exactly the time the Henrietta needed to make Liverpool — even running at full steam. And now the steam itself was about to fail the audacious gentleman!

"Sir," said Captain Speedy to him then, for he had ended up taking an interest in the man's schemes, "I truly pity you. Everything is against you! We're still only off Queenstown."

"Ah," said Mr. Fogg, "so that's Queenstown, that town whose lights we can see?"

"Yes."

"Can we enter the harbor?"

"Not for three hours. Only at high tide."

"Then we'll wait!" Phileas Fogg answered quietly, without letting his face betray that, by one last stroke of inspiration, he was about to try once again to beat the odds stacked against him!

For Queenstown is a port on the Irish coast where the transatlantic liners coming from the United States drop their mailbags in passing. Those letters are carried to Dublin by expresses always standing ready to leave. From Dublin they reach Liverpool aboard high-speed steamers — beating, by a full twelve hours, the fastest ships of the great shipping lines.

Those twelve hours that the American mail gained in this way, Phileas Fogg meant to gain as well. Instead of arriving on the Henrietta at Liverpool the following evening, he would be there by noon, and would consequently have the time to reach London before eight forty-five in the evening.

Toward one in the morning, the Henrietta entered the port of Queenstown on the high tide, and Phileas Fogg, after receiving a hearty handshake from Captain Speedy, left him standing on the razed carcass of his ship — which was still worth half of what he had sold it for!

The passengers went ashore at once. At that moment Fix felt a savage urge to arrest the man Fogg. But he did not do it! Why? What struggle was going on inside him? Had he come round about Mr. Fogg? Did he at last understand that he had been mistaken? Whatever the reason, Fix did not give Mr. Fogg up. Along with him, along with Aouda, along with Passepartout — who no longer even took the time to breathe — he climbed aboard the Queenstown train at half past one in the morning, reached Dublin at daybreak, and immediately boarded one of those steamers — true spindles of steel, all engine — that scorn to ride over the waves and drive invariably straight through them.

At twenty minutes to noon, on the 21st of December, Phileas Fogg at last stepped ashore on the quay at Liverpool. He was now only six hours from London.

But at that moment Fix stepped up, laid a hand on his shoulder, and, producing his warrant, said:

"You are the man Phileas Fogg?"

"I am, sir."

"In the name of the Queen, I arrest you!"
