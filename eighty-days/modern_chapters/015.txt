In Which Fix Appears to Know Nothing at All of the Matters Put to Him

The Rangoon, one of the steamers the Peninsular and Oriental Company runs on its China and Japan routes, was an iron ship, screw-driven, of seventeen hundred and seventy tons gross and four hundred nominal horsepower. She matched the Mongolia for speed but not for comfort, and so Aouda was not lodged as well as Phileas Fogg would have liked. Still, it was only a crossing of three thousand five hundred miles — eleven or twelve days — and the young woman proved no difficult passenger.

During the first days of the crossing, Aouda came to know Phileas Fogg better. At every turn she showed him her deepest gratitude. The phlegmatic gentleman heard her out — in appearance, at least — with the utmost coolness, without a single note or gesture betraying the slightest emotion in him. He saw to it that she wanted for nothing. At certain hours he came to her regularly, if not to talk, then at least to listen. He performed the duties of the strictest courtesy toward her, but with the grace and spontaneity of an automaton whose movements had been calibrated for the purpose. Aouda hardly knew what to make of him, but Passepartout had explained his master's eccentric character to her a little. He had told her what a wager was carrying this gentleman around the world. Aouda had smiled; but after all, she owed him her life, and her rescuer could only gain by her seeing him through the lens of her gratitude.

Aouda confirmed the touching story the Hindu guide had told of her. She belonged, in fact, to that race which holds first rank among the native peoples of India. Several Parsee merchants have made great fortunes in India in the cotton trade. One of them, Sir James Jejeebhoy, had been raised to the nobility by the English government, and Aouda was related to this wealthy man, who lived in Bombay. It was in fact a cousin of Sir Jejeebhoy, the honorable Jejeeh, that she meant to join in Hong Kong. Would she find refuge and help with him? She could not say for certain. To which Mr. Fogg would reply that she needn't worry — that it would all work out mathematically. That was his word for it.

Did the young woman understand that dreadful adverb? No one knows. All the same, her great eyes fixed on Mr. Fogg's — those great eyes "limpid as the sacred lakes of the Himalaya." But the unyielding Fogg, as buttoned-up as ever, seemed hardly the man to go plunging into such a lake.

This first stretch of the Rangoon's crossing went off in excellent conditions. The weather was manageable. That whole portion of the vast bay the sailors call "the fathoms of Bengal" favored the steamer's progress. The Rangoon soon raised the Great Andaman, the chief island of the group, which its picturesque Saddle Peak — two thousand four hundred feet high — signals to navigators from far off.

The coast was skirted fairly close. The wild Papuans of the island did not show themselves. They are creatures placed on the lowest rung of the human ladder, but they are wrongly made out to be cannibals.

The panorama unrolling before them was superb. Immense forests of fan palms, areca, bamboo, nutmeg, and teak, of giant mimosas and tree ferns, covered the country in the foreground, and behind them stood the elegant outline of the mountains. Along the coast swarmed, by the thousand, those prized swiftlets whose edible nests make a sought-after delicacy in the Celestial Empire. But all this varied spectacle offered by the Andaman group passed quickly, and the Rangoon steamed swiftly on toward the Strait of Malacca, which would give her access to the seas of China.

And what was Inspector Fix doing during this crossing, so awkwardly dragged into a voyage around the world? On leaving Calcutta, having left instructions that the warrant, if it ever did arrive, be forwarded to him at Hong Kong, he had managed to get aboard the Rangoon without being spotted by Passepartout, and he fully hoped to keep his presence hidden until the steamer arrived. It would, in fact, have been hard to explain why he was aboard without rousing the suspicions of Passepartout, who must believe him still in Bombay. But he was brought to renew his acquaintance with the honest fellow by the very logic of circumstances. How? You shall see.

All the inspector's hopes and desires were now fixed on a single spot on the globe — Hong Kong — for the steamer stopped too briefly at Singapore for him to act there. It was at Hong Kong, then, that the arrest of the thief must be made, or the thief slipped through his fingers, so to speak, for good.

For Hong Kong was still English soil — but the last that lay along the route. Beyond it, China, Japan, and America offered the man Fogg a refuge all but assured. At Hong Kong, if Fix found at last the arrest warrant that was plainly running after him, he would collar Fogg and hand him over to the local police. No trouble at all. But after Hong Kong, a simple warrant would no longer do. It would take an extradition order. Hence delays, hold-ups, obstacles of every kind, which the scoundrel would exploit to escape once and for all. If the operation failed at Hong Kong, it would be, if not impossible, at least very hard to take up again with any chance of success.

"So," Fix kept telling himself through the long hours he spent in his cabin, "either the warrant is at Hong Kong, and I arrest my man, or it isn't there, and this time I've got to delay his departure at any cost! I failed at Bombay, I failed at Calcutta! If I miss my shot at Hong Kong, my reputation is finished! Whatever it takes, I've got to pull it off. But what means can I use to hold up the departure of that cursed Fogg, if it comes to that?"

As a last resort, Fix was quite prepared to confess everything to Passepartout — to reveal to him just what sort of master he served, and whose accomplice he certainly was not. Passepartout, enlightened by the disclosure and fearing to be implicated himself, would surely come over to his side. But it was a risky expedient all the same, to be used only for want of any other. A single word from Passepartout to his master would be enough to wreck the whole affair beyond repair.

The police inspector was thus in a real quandary when the presence of Aouda aboard the Rangoon, in the company of Phileas Fogg, opened new possibilities to him.

Who was this woman? What combination of circumstances had made her Fogg's companion? The meeting had plainly taken place between Bombay and Calcutta. But at what point in the peninsula? Was it chance that had thrown Phileas Fogg and the young traveler together? Or had this journey across India, on the contrary, been undertaken by the gentleman for the very purpose of joining this charming person? For charming she was — Fix had seen that plainly in the courtroom at Calcutta.

One can understand how intrigued the agent was. He asked himself whether there might not be some criminal abduction in the business. Yes! That had to be it! The idea lodged itself in Fix's brain, and he saw all the use he could make of the circumstance. Whether this young woman was married or not, there had been an abduction, and it might be possible, at Hong Kong, to stir up such trouble for the abductor that he couldn't buy his way clear of it.

But he mustn't wait for the Rangoon to reach Hong Kong. This Fogg had the detestable habit of hopping from one boat to another, and before the affair could even be opened, he might already be far away.

The important thing, then, was to alert the English authorities and flag the Rangoon's passage before her passengers disembarked. Nothing could be easier, since the steamer put in at Singapore, and Singapore was linked to the Chinese coast by a telegraph line.

Still, before acting — and to work more surely — Fix resolved to question Passepartout. He knew it wasn't very hard to get the fellow talking, and he made up his mind to break the incognito he had kept until then. There was no time to lose. It was the 30th of October, and the very next day the Rangoon was due to put in at Singapore.

So that day, Fix left his cabin and came up on deck, meaning to accost Passepartout "first," with every mark of the most extreme surprise. Passepartout was strolling forward when the inspector rushed toward him, exclaiming:

"You, aboard the Rangoon!"

"Mr. Fix, on this ship!" replied Passepartout, utterly astonished, recognizing his fellow passenger from the Mongolia. "What! I leave you at Bombay, and I find you again on the road to Hong Kong! So you're going around the world too?"

"No, no," Fix replied. "I mean to stop at Hong Kong — for a few days, at least."

"Ah!" said Passepartout, who seemed puzzled for a moment. "But how is it I haven't caught sight of you aboard since we left Calcutta?"

"Well — a touch of illness... a bit of seasickness... I kept to my cabin, lying down. The Gulf of Bengal doesn't agree with me the way the Indian Ocean does. And your master, Mr. Phileas Fogg?"

"In perfect health, and as punctual as his timetable! Not a single day behind! Ah, Mr. Fix, you don't know this, but we've got a young lady with us now."

"A young lady?" replied the agent, looking for all the world as if he had no idea what his companion meant.

But Passepartout soon brought him up to speed. He recounted the incident at the pagoda in Bombay, the purchase of the elephant for two thousand pounds, the whole business of the suttee, the abduction of Aouda, the sentence handed down by the Calcutta court, and their release on bail. Fix, who knew the last part of these events, seemed ignorant of all of them, and Passepartout gave himself up to the pleasure of telling his adventures before a listener who showed him such keen interest.

"But in the end," Fix asked, "does your master mean to take this young woman to Europe?"

"Not at all, Mr. Fix, not at all! We're simply going to place her in the care of one of her relatives, a rich merchant in Hong Kong."

"Nothing doing," the detective said to himself, hiding his disappointment. "A glass of gin, Mr. Passepartout?"

"With pleasure, Mr. Fix. It's the least we can do — drink to our meeting aboard the Rangoon!"
