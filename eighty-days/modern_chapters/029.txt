In Which Phileas Fogg Simply Does His Duty

Three passengers, Passepartout among them, had vanished. Had they been killed in the fighting? Were they prisoners of the Sioux? There was no way yet to know.

The wounded were fairly numerous, but it turned out that none had been hit fatally. One of the most seriously injured was Colonel Proctor, who had fought bravely and been dropped by a bullet in the groin. He was carried to the station along with other passengers whose condition demanded immediate care.

Aouda was safe. Phileas Fogg, who had spared himself nothing, hadn't a scratch. Fix had been wounded in the arm — a slight thing, nothing to speak of. But Passepartout was missing, and tears ran from the young woman's eyes.

Meanwhile every passenger had left the train. The wheels of the cars were stained with blood. From the hubs and the spokes hung shapeless scraps of flesh. Across the white plain, as far as the eye could reach, ran long red streaks. The last of the Sioux were disappearing then to the south, off toward the Republican River.

Mr. Fogg stood motionless, his arms folded. He had a grave decision to make. Aouda, beside him, looked at him without a word. He understood that look. If his servant was a prisoner, was he not bound to risk everything to tear him from the hands of the Sioux?

"I will find him, dead or alive," he said simply to Aouda.

"Oh! Mr. Fogg — Mr. Fogg!" cried the young woman, seizing her companion's hands and covering them with tears.

"Alive," added Mr. Fogg, "if we don't lose a minute!"

With that decision Phileas Fogg sacrificed himself utterly. He had just pronounced his own ruin. A single day's delay would make him miss the steamer at New York. His wager was irrevocably lost. But faced with the thought — It is my duty! — he had not hesitated.

The captain commanding Fort Kearney was there. His soldiers — a hundred men or so — had drawn themselves up on the defensive, in case the Sioux should turn and mount a direct attack on the station.

"Sir," said Mr. Fogg to the captain, "three passengers have disappeared."

"Dead?" asked the captain.

"Dead or captured," answered Phileas Fogg. "That is an uncertainty that has to be settled. Do you intend to pursue the Sioux?"

"That's a serious matter, sir," said the captain. "These men could flee clear beyond the Arkansas. I can't abandon the fort placed in my charge."

"Sir," Phileas Fogg went on, "three men's lives are at stake."

"No doubt — but can I risk fifty lives to save three?"

"I don't know whether you can, sir, but you must."

"Sir," replied the captain, "no one here has anything to teach me about my duty."

"Very well," said Phileas Fogg coldly. "I'll go alone."

"You, sir!" cried Fix, who had come up. "Go alone, after those men?"

"Would you have me let this poor fellow die — a man who owes his life to no one, and to whom everyone alive here owes theirs? I'm going."

"No — no, you shall not go alone!" cried the captain, moved in spite of himself. "No! You're a brave heart. Thirty men — volunteers!" he added, turning to his soldiers.

The whole company stepped forward as one. The captain had only to choose among these good men. Thirty soldiers were picked out, and an old sergeant put at their head.

"Thank you, captain," said Mr. Fogg.

"Will you permit me to go with you?" Fix asked the gentleman.

"You will do as you please, sir," Phileas Fogg answered him. "But if you want to do me a service, you'll stay here with Aouda. In case anything should happen to me —"

A sudden pallor spread over the inspector's face. Separate from the man he had followed step by step, and with such persistence? Let him venture off like this into that wilderness? Fix studied the gentleman closely, and — whatever he might think, in spite of his suspicions, and despite the war going on inside him — he lowered his eyes before that calm, frank gaze.

"I'll stay," he said.

A few moments later Mr. Fogg pressed the young woman's hand; then, having handed her his precious traveling bag, he set off with the sergeant and his little troop.

But before he left, he had said to the soldiers:

"There's a thousand pounds for you, my friends, if we save the prisoners!"

It was then a few minutes past noon.

Aouda had withdrawn to a room in the station, and there, alone, she waited, thinking of Phileas Fogg, of that plain and immense generosity, of that quiet courage. Mr. Fogg had sacrificed his fortune, and now he was gambling his life, all of it without hesitation, out of duty, without a fine word about it. Phileas Fogg was a hero in her eyes.

Inspector Fix, for his part, saw it otherwise, and he couldn't master his agitation. He paced the station platform in a fever. For a moment he had been won over; then he became himself again. With Fogg gone, he understood what a fool he'd been to let him go. What! This man he had just followed around the world, and he had agreed to part with him! His nature reasserted itself; he blamed himself, he accused himself, he dressed himself down as if he'd been the head of the Metropolitan Police lecturing an officer caught red-handed in an act of gullibility.

"I've been an idiot!" he thought. "The other one will have told him who I am! He's gone, he won't come back! Where do I lay hold of him now? How could I have let myself be taken in like that — me, Fix, with his arrest warrant right here in my pocket! I'm nothing but a fool!"

So the police inspector reasoned, while the hours dragged by at their own slow pace, to his mind. He didn't know what to do. Now and then he had a mind to tell Aouda everything. But he knew perfectly well how the young woman would take it. What course to steer? He was tempted to strike off across the long white plains himself, in pursuit of this Fogg! It didn't seem impossible to find him again. The detachment's footprints were still printed on the snow — but soon a fresh layer covered them over, and every trace was gone.

Then discouragement took hold of Fix. He felt an overwhelming urge to throw in the game. And just then, precisely, the chance to leave the Kearney station and push on with this journey — so rich in setbacks — was handed to him.

Toward two in the afternoon, in fact, while the snow was falling in thick flakes, long whistles were heard coming from the east. An enormous shadow, led by a tawny glow, was advancing slowly, hugely swollen by the mists, which lent it a fantastic look.

Yet no train from the east was expected. The help called for by telegraph couldn't arrive so soon, and the Omaha-to-San-Francisco train wasn't due until the next day. The mystery was soon solved.

This locomotive coming on at low steam, throwing off great blasts of its whistle, was the very one that — after being cut loose from the train — had run on with such terrifying speed, carrying off the fireman and the engineer, both unconscious. It had raced along the rails for several miles; then the fire had died down for want of fuel; the steam had slackened, and an hour later, gradually slowing, the engine had at last come to a stop twenty miles beyond the Kearney station.

Neither the engineer nor the fireman had died, and, after a fairly long faint, they had come to.

By then the engine had stopped. When he saw himself out in the wilderness, the locomotive alone with no cars behind it, the engineer understood what had happened. How the locomotive had come to be uncoupled from the train, he couldn't guess; but he had no doubt that the train, left behind, was in trouble.

The engineer didn't hesitate over what to do. To keep on toward Omaha was prudent; to go back toward the train, which the Sioux might still be plundering, was dangerous. No matter! Shovelfuls of coal and wood were heaved into the firebox, the fire flared up again, the pressure climbed, and toward two in the afternoon the engine was steaming back toward the Kearney station. It was this that had been whistling in the fog.

It was a great relief to the passengers when they saw the locomotive move up to the head of the train. They would be able to carry on with the journey so unhappily interrupted.

At the engine's arrival, Aouda had left the station, and going up to the conductor, she asked:

"You're leaving?"

"This instant, madam."

"But those prisoners — our poor companions —"

"I can't interrupt the service," the conductor replied. "We're already three hours behind."

"And when will the other train come through, the one from San Francisco?"

"Tomorrow evening, madam."

"Tomorrow evening! But that will be too late. We have to wait —"

"Impossible," answered the conductor. "If you want to leave, get aboard."

"I will not leave," the young woman replied.

Fix had overheard this exchange. A few moments earlier, when he had no means of transport at all, he'd been set on quitting Kearney; and now that the train was here, ready to spring away, with nothing left for him to do but take his seat in the car again, an irresistible force pinned him to the ground. The station platform burned the soles of his feet, and he couldn't tear himself from it. The struggle began again inside him. The rage of failure was choking him. He wanted to fight it out to the end.

Meanwhile the passengers and a few of the wounded — Colonel Proctor among them, whose condition was serious — had taken their places in the cars. You could hear the humming of the overheated boiler, and the steam escaping through the valves. The engineer whistled, the train started, and soon it was gone, mixing its white smoke into the swirl of the snow.

Inspector Fix had stayed behind.

Some hours passed. The weather was very bad, the cold biting. Fix, seated on a bench in the station, sat motionless. You might have thought he was asleep. Aouda, in spite of the gale, kept leaving the room that had been put at her disposal. She would come to the far end of the platform, trying to see through the snowstorm, straining to pierce the mist that closed the horizon in around her, listening for any sound. But nothing. Then she would go back inside, chilled through, only to come out again a few moments later, and always in vain.

Evening came. The little detachment had not returned. Where was it now? Had it managed to catch up with the Sioux? Had there been a fight, or were the soldiers, lost in the fog, wandering at random? The captain of Fort Kearney was very uneasy, though he wanted to let none of his unease show.

Night fell, the snow came down less heavily, but the cold sharpened. The most fearless eye couldn't have looked on that dark immensity without dread. An absolute silence lay over the plain. Not the flight of a bird, not the passing of a wild animal disturbed its endless calm.

All through that night Aouda — her mind full of sinister forebodings, her heart wrung with dread — wandered along the edge of the prairie. Her imagination carried her far off and showed her a thousand dangers. What she suffered through those long hours can't be put into words.

Fix was still fixed in the same place, but he wasn't sleeping either. At one point a man came up and even spoke to him, but the agent sent him off, answering his words with a shake of the head.

So the night wore away. At dawn the sun's half-quenched disc rose over a misty horizon. Still, the eye could now reach a distance of two miles. It was toward the south that Phileas Fogg and the detachment had gone — and the south was utterly deserted. It was then seven in the morning.

The captain, extremely worried, didn't know what to decide. Should he send a second detachment to rescue the first? Should he sacrifice fresh men, with so little chance of saving the ones already sacrificed? But his hesitation didn't last, and with a gesture, calling one of his lieutenants over, he was giving the order to push a reconnaissance to the south — when shots rang out. Was it a signal? The soldiers threw themselves out of the fort, and half a mile off they saw a small troop coming back in good order.

Mr. Fogg marched at their head, and near him Passepartout and the two other passengers, snatched from the hands of the Sioux.

There had been a fight ten miles south of Kearney. A few moments before the detachment arrived, Passepartout and his two companions were already grappling with their captors, and the Frenchman had knocked three of them flat with his fists when his master and the soldiers rushed in to their rescue.

All of them — rescuers and rescued alike — were greeted with shouts of joy, and Phileas Fogg handed out to the soldiers the reward he'd promised them, while Passepartout said to himself, not without some reason:

"There's no denying it — I do cost my master a fortune!"

Fix, without a word, watched Mr. Fogg, and it would have been hard to sort out the impressions warring inside him just then. As for Aouda, she had taken the gentleman's hand and was pressing it in both of hers, unable to say a word.

Passepartout, meanwhile, had gone looking for the train the moment he arrived. He expected to find it there, ready to run for Omaha, and he hoped they might still make up the lost time.

"The train! The train!" he cried.

"Gone," answered Fix.

"And the next train — when does it come through?" asked Phileas Fogg.

"Not till this evening."

"Ah," was all the imperturbable gentleman replied.
