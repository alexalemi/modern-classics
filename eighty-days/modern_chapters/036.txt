In Which It Is Proved That Phileas Fogg Gained Nothing by His Tour of the World, Unless It Were Happiness

Yes! Phileas Fogg himself.

You will remember that at five past eight that evening — some twenty-five hours after the travelers had reached London — Passepartout had been sent by his master to notify the Reverend Samuel Wilson about a certain wedding, one that was to take place the very next day.

So Passepartout had set off, delighted. He walked briskly to the home of the Reverend Samuel Wilson, who had not yet come in. Naturally, Passepartout waited — and he waited a good twenty minutes at least.

In short, it was thirty-five minutes past eight when he came out of the reverend's house. But in what a state! Hair wild, hat gone, running, running as no man has ever been seen to run in all of human memory, knocking pedestrians flat, tearing along the sidewalks like a whirlwind!

In three minutes he was back at the house on Savile Row, and he collapsed, gasping, into Mr. Fogg's room.

He could not speak.

"What is it?" asked Mr. Fogg.

"My master —" Passepartout stammered. "The wedding — impossible."

"Impossible?"

"Impossible — for tomorrow."

"Why?"

"Because tomorrow — is Sunday!"

"Monday," replied Mr. Fogg.

"No — today — is Saturday."

"Saturday? Impossible!"

"Yes, yes, yes, yes!" cried Passepartout. "You've made a mistake of one day! We arrived twenty-four hours early — but there are only ten minutes left!"

Passepartout had seized his master by the collar, and he was dragging him along with irresistible force.

Phileas Fogg, hauled off like that, without so much as time to think, left his room, left his house, sprang into a cab, promised the driver a hundred pounds, and — after flattening two dogs and clipping five carriages — arrived at the Reform Club.

The clock read eight forty-five when he appeared in the great drawing room.

Phileas Fogg had completed his tour of the world in eighty days!

Phileas Fogg had won his wager of twenty thousand pounds!

And now — how could a man so exact, so meticulous, have committed such a blunder of a day? How could he believe it was Saturday evening, the 21st of December, when he stepped ashore in London, when in fact it was only Friday the 20th, a mere seventy-nine days after his departure?

Here is the reason for the mistake. It is quite simple.

Phileas Fogg had, "without suspecting it," gained a day on his itinerary — and that solely because he had made the tour of the world traveling eastward. Had he gone the opposite way, toward the west, he would have lost that same day.

For, traveling eastward, Phileas Fogg was going to meet the sun, and so the days grew shorter for him by four minutes for every degree he crossed in that direction. Now, there are three hundred and sixty degrees around the circumference of the earth, and those three hundred and sixty degrees, multiplied by four minutes, come to exactly twenty-four hours — that is, the day he had unknowingly gained. In other words, while Phileas Fogg, traveling eastward, saw the sun cross the meridian eighty times, his colleagues who had stayed behind in London saw it cross only seventy-nine. Which is why, on that very day — which was the Saturday, and not the Sunday as Mr. Fogg believed — they sat waiting for him in the drawing room of the Reform Club.

And this is exactly what Passepartout's famous watch — which had never stopped keeping London time — would have shown, if only, along with the minutes and the hours, it had also marked the days!

So Phileas Fogg had won the twenty thousand pounds. But since he had spent about nineteen thousand of them along the way, the financial result was slim. Still, as we have said, the eccentric gentleman had sought in this wager only the contest, not the fortune. And indeed, the thousand pounds that remained, he divided between the honest Passepartout and the luckless Fix, against whom he found it impossible to hold a grudge. Only — for the sake of good order — he docked from his servant's share the cost of the one thousand nine hundred and twenty hours of gas that had burned by his fault.

That very evening, Mr. Fogg, as impassive, as unflappable as ever, said to Aouda:

"Does this marriage still suit you, madam?"

"Mr. Fogg," Aouda replied, "it is for me to ask you that question. You were ruined; now you are rich —"

"Forgive me, madam, but this fortune belongs to you. If you had not had the idea of this marriage, my servant would not have gone to the Reverend Samuel Wilson, I would never have learned of my error, and —"

"Dear Mr. Fogg —" said the young woman.

"Dear Aouda —" replied Phileas Fogg.

It goes without saying that the wedding took place forty-eight hours later, and Passepartout — magnificent, resplendent, positively dazzling — stood there as the bride's witness. Had he not saved her, and was the honor not owed to him?

Only, the next day, at the crack of dawn, Passepartout came pounding thunderously at his master's door.

The door opened, and the impassive gentleman appeared.

"What is it, Passepartout?"

"What is it, sir! It's that I've only just this instant found out —"

"Found out what?"

"That we could have made the tour of the world in only seventy-eight days."

"No doubt," replied Mr. Fogg, "by not crossing India. But if I had not crossed India, I would not have saved Aouda, she would not be my wife, and —"

And Mr. Fogg quietly closed the door.

So, then, Phileas Fogg had won his wager. He had accomplished, in eighty days, this journey around the world! To do it he had used every means of transport — steamers, railways, carriages, yachts, trading vessels, sledges, an elephant. Through it all the eccentric gentleman had displayed his marvelous gifts of coolness and precision. But what then? What had he gained by all that going and coming? What had he brought back from the journey?

Nothing, you will say? Nothing, granted — nothing but a charming wife who, however unlikely it may seem, made him the happiest of men!

And in truth — would a man not go around the world for less?
