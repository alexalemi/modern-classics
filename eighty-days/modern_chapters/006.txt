Which Once More Proves the Uselessness of Passports in Police Matters

The inspector went back down onto the quay and made straight for the consul's offices. At his urgent request, he was shown in to that official at once.

"Sir," he said without any preamble, "I have strong reason to believe that our man has taken passage aboard the Mongolia."

And Fix recounted what had passed between himself and that servant on the subject of the passport.

"Well, Mr. Fix," the consul replied, "I wouldn't mind getting a look at this rascal's face. But if he's what you suppose, he may not present himself at my office at all. A thief doesn't like to leave a trail behind him, and besides, the passport formality is no longer required."

"Sir," the agent answered, "if he's a shrewd man, as we have every reason to think, he'll come!"

"To have his passport stamped?"

"Yes. Passports never do anything but inconvenience honest people and help scoundrels slip away. I promise you this one's papers will be in perfect order, but I do hope you won't stamp them."

"And why not? If the passport is in order," the consul replied, "I've no right to refuse my visa."

"All the same, sir, I have to hold this man here until I've received a warrant for his arrest from London."

"Ah, that, Mr. Fix, is your affair," the consul answered, "but I, for my part, cannot—"

The consul never finished his sentence. At that moment there came a knock at the door of his office, and the clerk showed in two strangers, one of whom was precisely that servant who had been chatting with the detective.

It was indeed the master and the man. The master presented his passport, laconically requesting that the consul be so good as to affix his visa.

The consul took the passport and read it attentively, while Fix, in a corner of the office, watched the stranger — or rather, devoured him with his eyes.

When the consul had finished reading:

"You are Phileas Fogg, Esquire?" he asked.

"I am, sir," the gentleman replied.

"And this man is your servant?"

"Yes. A Frenchman named Passepartout."

"You come from London?"

"I do."

"And you are bound for—?"

"Bombay."

"Very good, sir. You're aware that this visa formality is unnecessary, and that we no longer require the presentation of a passport?"

"I'm aware of it, sir," Phileas Fogg replied, "but I wish your visa to record my passage through Suez."

"As you please, sir."

And the consul, having signed and dated the passport, affixed his stamp to it. Mr. Fogg paid the visa fee and, after a cold nod, went out, followed by his servant.

"Well?" asked the inspector.

"Well," the consul replied, "he has the look of a perfectly honest man!"

"That may be," said Fix, "but that's not the question. Do you find, sir, that this cool-blooded gentleman is the exact likeness, feature for feature, of the thief whose description I've received?"

"I grant you he is, but you know how it is with descriptions—"

"I'll get to the bottom of it," Fix replied. "The servant strikes me as less impossible to read than the master. What's more, he's a Frenchman — he won't be able to keep from talking. Until soon, sir."

That said, the agent went out and set off in search of Passepartout.

Meanwhile Mr. Fogg, on leaving the consulate, had made his way toward the quay. There he gave a few orders to his servant; then he stepped into a boat, returned aboard the Mongolia, and went back to his cabin. He took out his notebook, which held the following entries:

"Left London, Wednesday, October 2, 8:45 p.m.

"Reached Paris, Thursday, October 3, 7:20 a.m.

"Left Paris, Thursday, 8:40 a.m.

"Reached Turin by way of Mont Cenis, Friday, October 4, 6:35 a.m.

"Left Turin, Friday, 7:20 a.m.

"Reached Brindisi, Saturday, October 5, 4:00 p.m.

"Boarded the Mongolia, Saturday, 5:00 p.m.

"Reached Suez, Wednesday, October 9, 11:00 a.m.

"Total hours spent: 158½ — that is, in days: 6½ days."

Mr. Fogg noted these dates on an itinerary laid out in columns, which listed — from October 2 to December 21 — the month, the date, the day of the week, the scheduled arrivals and the actual arrivals at each principal point: Paris, Brindisi, Suez, Bombay, Calcutta, Singapore, Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, New York, Liverpool, London — and which let him calculate the time gained or lost at each stage of the journey.

This methodical schedule thus accounted for everything, and Mr. Fogg always knew whether he was ahead or behind.

So on this day, Wednesday, October 9, he entered his arrival at Suez, which, matching the scheduled arrival, put him neither ahead nor behind.

Then he had lunch served to him in his cabin. As for seeing the town, the thought never so much as crossed his mind — for he was of that breed of Englishman who have their servant do the sightseeing in the countries they pass through.
