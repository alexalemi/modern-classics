In Which a Conversation Takes Place That May Cost Phileas Fogg Dearly

Phileas Fogg had left his house on Savile Row at half past eleven, and, after setting his right foot before his left five hundred and seventy-five times and his left foot before his right five hundred and seventy-six times, he arrived at the Reform Club — that vast pile in Pall Mall which cost no less than three million pounds to build.

Phileas Fogg went straight to the dining room, whose nine windows looked out on a handsome garden already touched with the gold of autumn. There he took his usual place at the table where his cover awaited him. His lunch was a hors d'oeuvre, a boiled fish dressed in a first-rate "Reading sauce," a scarlet cut of roast beef seasoned with mushroom relish, a tart stuffed with rhubarb stalks and green gooseberries, and a wedge of Cheshire cheese — all of it washed down with several cups of that excellent tea grown especially for the pantry of the Reform Club.

At forty-seven minutes past noon the gentleman rose and made his way to the great drawing room, a sumptuous chamber hung with richly framed paintings. There a servant handed him an uncut copy of the Times, and Phileas Fogg performed the laborious business of unfolding it with a sureness of hand that betrayed long practice in that difficult operation. Reading the paper occupied him until a quarter to four; the Standard, which followed, saw him through to dinner. That meal ran along the same lines as his lunch, with the addition of a "royal British sauce."

At twenty minutes to six the gentleman reappeared in the great drawing room and buried himself in the Morning Chronicle.

Half an hour later, various members of the Reform Club began arriving and drawing up to the fireplace, where a coal fire burned. These were Mr. Phileas Fogg's regular partners, whist players as rabid as himself: the engineer Andrew Stuart; the bankers John Sullivan and Samuel Fallentin; the brewer Thomas Flanagan; and Gauthier Ralph, one of the directors of the Bank of England — men of wealth and standing, even in a club that counts among its members the leading lights of industry and finance.

"Well, Ralph," asked Thomas Flanagan, "how does this robbery business stand?"

"Well," answered Andrew Stuart, "the Bank can kiss its money goodbye."

"On the contrary, I hope we'll lay our hands on the man," said Gauthier Ralph. "Police inspectors — very shrewd fellows — have been sent to America and Europe, to all the principal ports of embarkation and arrival, and it'll be hard for this gentleman to slip through them."

"So they've got a description of the thief?" asked Andrew Stuart.

"To begin with, he isn't a thief," Gauthier Ralph replied gravely.

"Not a thief — the man who made off with fifty-five thousand pounds in banknotes?"

"No," said Gauthier Ralph.

"A businessman, then?" said John Sullivan.

"The Morning Chronicle assures us he's a gentleman."

The man who offered this last remark was none other than Phileas Fogg, whose head just then emerged from the tide of paper heaped around him. He nodded to his colleagues, who returned his greeting.

The affair under discussion — argued over with great heat by every paper in the Kingdom — had taken place three days earlier, on the 29th of September. A bundle of banknotes, amounting to the enormous sum of fifty-five thousand pounds, had been lifted from the counter of the head cashier at the Bank of England.

To anyone amazed that such a theft could have been pulled off so easily, the deputy governor, Gauthier Ralph, would only reply that at that very moment the cashier had been busy recording a receipt of three shillings and sixpence, and a man can't keep his eye on everything.

But it should be pointed out here — and this makes the thing more understandable — that this admirable establishment, the Bank of England, seems to care enormously about the dignity of the public. No guards, no old pensioners on watch, no iron grilles. Gold, silver, and banknotes lie freely on display, practically at the mercy of the first comer. No one would dream of casting suspicion on the honor of any passerby. One of the shrewdest observers of English manners even tells this story: In one of the Bank's halls one day, curious to get a closer look at a gold ingot weighing seven or eight pounds that lay out on the cashier's counter, he picked the ingot up, examined it, passed it to his neighbor, who passed it to another, and so on, until the ingot traveled from hand to hand all the way down some dim corridor and did not come back to its place until half an hour later — and the cashier never once lifted his head.

But on the 29th of September, things did not go quite so smoothly. The bundle of banknotes did not come back, and when the magnificent clock above the drawing office struck five and closing time, the Bank of England had nothing left to do but charge fifty-five thousand pounds to its profit-and-loss account.

Once the theft was well and duly established, agents — detectives, chosen from among the most able — were dispatched to the principal ports: Liverpool, Glasgow, Le Havre, Suez, Brindisi, New York, and so on, with the promise, should they succeed, of a reward of two thousand pounds plus five percent of the sum recovered. While they waited for the findings of the investigation, just then getting under way, these inspectors were charged with keeping a scrupulous watch on every traveler coming or going.

Now, precisely as the Morning Chronicle claimed, there was reason to suppose that the man behind the theft belonged to none of England's gangs of thieves. During that day of the 29th of September, a well-dressed gentleman of good manners and distinguished bearing had been noticed coming and going about the payments hall, the scene of the crime. The investigation had made it possible to piece together a fairly exact description of this gentleman, a description at once circulated to every detective in the Kingdom and on the Continent. And so certain clever minds — Gauthier Ralph among them — felt justified in hoping the thief would not escape.

As one can imagine, the affair was the talk of London and all of England. People debated it, took sides for and against the odds of the metropolitan police. So it is no surprise to find the members of the Reform Club worrying at the same question — all the more so since one of the deputy governors of the Bank was among them.

The honorable Gauthier Ralph had no wish to doubt the outcome of the search, reckoning that the reward on offer should sharpen the zeal and wits of the agents wonderfully. But his colleague Andrew Stuart was far from sharing that confidence. So the argument went on among the gentlemen as they sat down at a whist table — Stuart facing Flanagan, Fallentin facing Phileas Fogg. During the play the men said nothing, but between rubbers the interrupted conversation started up again livelier than ever.

"I maintain," said Andrew Stuart, "that the odds favor the thief, who's bound to be a clever man."

"Come now," Ralph shot back. "There isn't a single country left where he can take refuge."

"Oh, come!"

"Where do you suppose he'll go?"

"I've no idea," answered Andrew Stuart, "but after all, the world's a big place."

"It used to be," said Phileas Fogg, half under his breath. Then: "Your cut, sir," he added, offering the cards to Thomas Flanagan.

The argument hung fire while the rubber was played. But before long Andrew Stuart took it up again:

"Used to be! Has the world shrunk, by any chance?"

"No doubt of it," answered Gauthier Ralph. "I'm of Mr. Fogg's mind. The world has shrunk, since a man can now cross it ten times faster than a hundred years ago. And that, in the case we're discussing, is exactly what will make the search quicker."

"And make the thief's escape easier too!"

"Your play, Mr. Stuart," said Phileas Fogg.

But the skeptical Stuart was not convinced, and once the hand was finished:

"You must admit, Mr. Ralph," he went on, "that you've found an amusing way of saying the world has shrunk! So because a man can now go around it in three months —"

"In eighty days, only," said Phileas Fogg.

"Quite so, gentlemen," John Sullivan put in. "Eighty days, now that the section between Rothal and Allahabad has opened on the Great Indian Peninsular Railway. Here's the reckoning the Morning Chronicle has drawn up:

From London to Suez, by Mont Cenis and Brindisi, railways and steamers:  7 days.
From Suez to Bombay, steamer:  13 days.
From Bombay to Calcutta, railway:  3 days.
From Calcutta to Hong Kong (China), steamer:  13 days.
From Hong Kong to Yokohama (Japan), steamer:  6 days.
From Yokohama to San Francisco, steamer:  22 days.
From San Francisco to New York, railroad:  7 days.
From New York to London, steamer and railway:  9 days.
Total:  80 days."

"Yes, eighty days!" cried Andrew Stuart, who in his distraction trumped a winning card. "But that leaves out bad weather, headwinds, shipwrecks, derailments, and the rest of it."

"All included," answered Phileas Fogg, playing on — for by now the argument had stopped respecting the whist.

"Even if the Hindus or the Indians tear up the rails?" cried Andrew Stuart. "Even if they stop the trains, loot the baggage cars, scalp the passengers?"

"All included," answered Phileas Fogg, who laid down his cards and added, "Two winning trumps."

Andrew Stuart, whose turn it was to deal, gathered up the cards, saying:

"In theory you're right, Mr. Fogg, but in practice —"

"In practice too, Mr. Stuart."

"I'd like to see you try it."

"Nothing is stopping you. Let's go together."

"Heaven forbid!" cried Stuart. "But I'd gladly bet four thousand pounds that such a journey, made under those conditions, is impossible."

"Perfectly possible, on the contrary," replied Mr. Fogg.

"Well then, do it!"

"Go around the world in eighty days?"

"Yes."

"Gladly."

"When?"

"At once."

"This is madness!" cried Andrew Stuart, who was beginning to be nettled by his partner's insistence. "Look here — let's just play instead."

"Deal again, then," answered Phileas Fogg, "for there's a misdeal."

Andrew Stuart took up the cards with a feverish hand; then, all at once, throwing them down on the table:

"Very well, yes, Mr. Fogg — yes, I bet four thousand pounds!"

"My dear Stuart," said Fallentin, "calm yourself. This isn't serious."

"When I say I bet," answered Andrew Stuart, "I always mean it."

"So be it," said Mr. Fogg. Then, turning to his colleagues:

"I have twenty thousand pounds on deposit with Baring Brothers. I'll gladly stake it —"

"Twenty thousand pounds!" cried John Sullivan. "Twenty thousand pounds that a single unforeseen delay could cost you!"

"There is no such thing as the unforeseen," Phileas Fogg replied simply.

"But Mr. Fogg, this span of eighty days is figured only as a minimum!"

"A minimum well used is enough for anything."

"But to keep from overshooting it, you'd have to leap mathematically out of railways into steamers, and out of steamers into railways!"

"I will leap mathematically."

"You're joking!"

"A good Englishman never jokes about anything so serious as a wager," answered Phileas Fogg. "I bet twenty thousand pounds against anyone who likes that I will go around the world in eighty days or less — that is, in nineteen hundred and twenty hours, or one hundred and fifteen thousand two hundred minutes. Do you accept?"

"We accept," answered Messrs. Stuart, Fallentin, Sullivan, Flanagan, and Ralph, once they had conferred.

"Good," said Mr. Fogg. "The Dover train leaves at eight forty-five. I shall take it."

"This very evening?" asked Stuart.

"This very evening," answered Phileas Fogg. "So then," he added, consulting a pocket calendar, "since today is Wednesday, the 2nd of October, I must be back in London, in this very drawing room of the Reform Club, on Saturday, the 21st of December, at eight forty-five in the evening — failing which the twenty thousand pounds now standing to my credit with Baring Brothers will belong to you, gentlemen, in fact and in law. Here is a check for the amount."

A record of the wager was drawn up and signed on the spot by the six parties. Phileas Fogg had remained perfectly cool. He certainly had not bet in order to win, and had risked these twenty thousand pounds — half his fortune — only because he foresaw that he might have to spend the other half to carry through this difficult, not to say unworkable, undertaking. As for his opponents, they seemed rattled — not by the size of the stake, but by a kind of scruple at contending on such terms.

Seven o'clock was striking. The others offered to break off the whist so that Mr. Fogg could see to his preparations for departure.

"I am always ready," answered this imperturbable gentleman, and dealing the cards:

"I turn up diamonds," he said. "Your play, Mr. Stuart."
