In Which Phileas Fogg Buys a Mount at a Fabulous Price

The train had pulled out exactly on schedule. It carried the usual assortment of passengers — a few officers, some civil servants, and a handful of dealers in opium and indigo whose business called them to the eastern part of the peninsula.

Passepartout shared a compartment with his master. A third traveler occupied the far corner.

This was Brigadier General Sir Francis Cromarty, one of Mr. Fogg's whist partners on the crossing from Suez to Bombay, now on his way to rejoin his troops garrisoned near Benares.

Sir Francis Cromarty was a tall, fair man of about fifty who had distinguished himself in the recent Sepoy Rebellion, and he could very nearly have passed for a native. He had lived in India since he was young, making only rare appearances in the country of his birth. He was a well-read man, and would gladly have shared everything he knew about the customs, the history, and the workings of the country — had Phileas Fogg been the sort of man to ask. But this particular gentleman asked nothing. He was not traveling; he was tracing a circumference. He was a solid body describing an orbit around the globe, in accordance with the laws of rational mechanics. At this very moment he was running back over the hours spent since leaving London, and he would have rubbed his hands together in satisfaction, had it been in his nature to make a useless gesture.

Sir Francis Cromarty had not failed to notice the originality of his fellow traveler, though he had studied him only across a hand of cards, between two rubbers of whist. He had good reason to wonder whether a human heart beat beneath that cold exterior — whether Phileas Fogg had a soul alive to the beauties of nature or to any higher aspiration. It was an open question, as far as he was concerned. Of all the eccentrics the brigadier general had met in his life, not one compared to this product of the exact sciences.

Phileas Fogg had made no secret to Sir Francis Cromarty of his plan to travel around the world, nor of the terms under which he was doing it. The brigadier general saw in the wager nothing but a pointless eccentricity, one that necessarily lacked the transire benefaciendo — the doing of good in passing — that ought to guide any reasonable man. At the rate this odd gentleman was going, he would clearly pass through the world without "doing anything," either for himself or for anyone else.

An hour out of Bombay the train crossed the viaducts, ran over the island of Salsette, and struck out onto the mainland. At Callyan station it left on the right the branch line that runs down through Khandala and Poona toward southeastern India, and reached the station of Pauwell. From there it entered the deeply folded ranges of the Western Ghats, chains of trap rock and basalt whose highest peaks are cloaked in thick forest.

From time to time Sir Francis Cromarty and Phileas Fogg exchanged a few words, and just now the brigadier general, picking up a conversation that kept lapsing, said:

"A few years ago, Mr. Fogg, you'd have run into a delay right about here that would very likely have wrecked your schedule."

"How so, Sir Francis?"

"Because the railway stopped at the foot of these mountains, and you had to cross them by palanquin or on pony-back to reach the station at Khandala, on the far side."

"That delay wouldn't have disturbed my program in the least," replied Mr. Fogg. "I've allowed for the possibility of certain obstacles."

"All the same, Mr. Fogg," the brigadier general went on, "you came very near having a nasty piece of business on your hands, thanks to that young fellow's adventure."

Passepartout, his feet wrapped up in his traveling blanket, was fast asleep, and had no idea he was the subject of conversation.

"The English government is extremely severe, and rightly so, about that sort of offense," Sir Francis Cromarty continued. "Above all it insists that the religious customs of the Hindus be respected, and if your servant had been caught—"

"Well, if he had been caught, Sir Francis," replied Mr. Fogg, "he'd have been sentenced, he'd have served his time, and then he'd have come quietly home to Europe. I don't see how that business could possibly have delayed his master."

And on that note the conversation lapsed again. During the night the train crossed the Ghats, passed through Nassik, and the next day, October 21, it shot out across relatively flat country — the territory of Khandeish. The well-tilled fields were dotted with villages, and above each rose the minaret of a pagoda where a European village would have had a church steeple. Countless small streams, most of them tributaries or sub-tributaries of the Godavery, watered this fertile stretch of land.

Passepartout, awake now, watched it all and could scarcely believe he was crossing Hindu country in a train of the "Great Peninsular Railway." It struck him as wildly improbable. And yet nothing could be more real. The locomotive, guided by the arm of an English engineer and fed on English coal, threw its smoke over plantations of coffee, nutmeg, clove, and red pepper. The steam curled in spirals around clumps of palm trees, among which appeared picturesque bungalows, a few viharas — a kind of abandoned monastery — and marvelous temples lavished with the inexhaustible ornament of Indian architecture. Then vast tracts of land stretched away as far as the eye could see, jungles well stocked with snakes and tigers that the train's whistling sent bolting, and at last forests split by the line of the track, still haunted by elephants that watched the disheveled convoy rush past with a thoughtful eye.

That morning, beyond the station of Malligaum, the travelers crossed a grim stretch of country that had so often been drenched in blood by the worshippers of the goddess Kali. Not far off rose Ellora with its admirable pagodas; not far off, the famous Aurungabad, capital of the fierce Aureng-Zeb, now no more than the chief town of one of the provinces broken off from the kingdom of the Nizam. This was the region where Feringhea, chief of the Thugs, king of the Stranglers, had once held sway. These murderers, bound together in an association impossible to root out, strangled victims of every age in honor of the goddess of Death, never spilling a drop of blood — and there was a time when you could not dig anywhere in this soil without turning up a corpse. The English government has managed to check these killings to a considerable degree, but the dreadful society still exists and is still at work.

At half past twelve the train stopped at the station of Burhampour, where Passepartout managed to buy, at a price fit for gold, a pair of slippers trimmed with imitation pearls, which he put on with an air of unmistakable vanity.

The travelers lunched in a hurry and set off again for the station of Assurghur, after skirting for a moment the bank of the Tapty, a small river that empties into the Gulf of Cambay near Surat.

This is a good moment to describe what was going through Passepartout's mind just then. Right up until his arrival in Bombay, he had believed — and been able to believe — that the whole affair would go no further than that. But now, since he had been racing across India at full steam, a change had come over him. His true nature came galloping back. He recovered all the wild fancies of his youth; he began to take his master's plans seriously; he came to believe in the reality of the wager, and therefore in this trip around the world and this maximum stretch of time that mustn't be exceeded. He had even begun to fret over the delays that might crop up, the accidents that might happen on the road. He felt as if he had a personal stake in the bet, and trembled to think that the day before he had nearly wrecked it with his own unforgivable gawking. And so, far less phlegmatic than Mr. Fogg, he was far more anxious. He counted and recounted the days gone by, cursed every stop the train made, accused it of crawling, and privately blamed Mr. Fogg for not having promised the engineer a bonus. The good fellow had no idea that what was possible aboard a steamer was out of the question on a railway, whose speed is fixed by regulation.

Toward evening they entered the passes of the Sutpour mountains, which separate the territory of Khandeish from that of Bundelkund.

The next day, October 22, when Sir Francis Cromarty asked him the time, Passepartout consulted his watch and answered that it was three in the morning. And in fact this famous watch, still set to the meridian of Greenwich — now nearly seventy-seven degrees to the west — was bound to be running slow, and was indeed four hours behind.

So Sir Francis corrected the time Passepartout had given him, and made the same point that Fix had already made once before. He tried to get across to him that he ought to reset his watch at each new meridian, and that, since he was traveling steadily eastward — that is, toward the sun — the days grew shorter by four minutes for every degree covered. It was no use. Whether the stubborn fellow had understood the brigadier general's point or not, he obstinately refused to move his watch ahead, and kept it fixed forever on London time. A harmless little quirk, in any case, and one that could hurt nobody.

At eight in the morning, fifteen miles short of the station of Rothal, the train stopped in the middle of a wide clearing bordered by a few bungalows and workmen's huts. The conductor walked down the line of cars, calling out:

"Passengers get off here."

Phileas Fogg looked at Sir Francis Cromarty, who seemed to make nothing of this halt in the middle of a forest of tamarinds and date palms.

Passepartout, no less surprised, sprang out onto the track and came back almost at once, crying:

"Sir, the railway's finished!"

"What do you mean?" asked Sir Francis Cromarty.

"I mean the train doesn't go any further!"

The brigadier general climbed down from the car at once. Phileas Fogg followed, in no hurry. The two of them turned to the conductor:

"Where are we?" asked Sir Francis Cromarty.

"At the hamlet of Kholby," the conductor answered.

"And we stop here?"

"We must. The railway isn't finished—"

"What! It isn't finished?"

"No! There's still a stretch of about fifty miles to lay between this point and Allahabad, where the line picks up again."

"But the papers announced the line was open all the way through!"

"What can I tell you, officer — the papers got it wrong."

"And yet you sell tickets from Bombay to Calcutta!" said Sir Francis Cromarty, who was starting to heat up.

"No doubt," the conductor replied, "but the passengers know perfectly well that they have to arrange their own transport from Kholby to Allahabad."

Sir Francis Cromarty was furious. Passepartout would gladly have knocked the conductor flat, though the man was hardly to blame. He didn't dare look at his master.

"Sir Francis," said Mr. Fogg simply, "we'll see about finding some way to reach Allahabad, if you're willing."

"Mr. Fogg, this is exactly the kind of delay that damages your interests."

"No, Sir Francis, it was foreseen."

"What! You knew the line—"

"Not at all, but I knew that some obstacle or other would turn up on my route sooner or later. And nothing is lost. I have two days in hand to spare. There's a steamer leaving Calcutta for Hong Kong on the 25th at noon. This is only the 22nd, and we'll reach Calcutta in time."

There was nothing to say to an answer delivered with such complete assurance.

It was all too true that the railway works stopped at this point. Newspapers are like certain watches that have a mania for running fast, and they had announced the completion of the line prematurely. Most of the passengers knew about this break in the track, and as they got off the train they had grabbed up every kind of vehicle the little town possessed: four-wheeled palkigharis, carts drawn by zebus — a sort of humped ox — traveling wagons that looked like rolling pagodas, palanquins, ponies, and the rest. So Mr. Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty, after searching the whole town, came back having found nothing.

"I'll go on foot," said Phileas Fogg.

Passepartout, rejoining his master just then, made a meaningful grimace as he considered his splendid but wholly inadequate slippers. Luckily he too had been off scouting, and after a bit of hesitation he said:

"Sir, I believe I've found us a means of transport."

"What is it?"

"An elephant! An elephant that belongs to an Indian living a hundred paces from here."

"Let's go and see the elephant," replied Mr. Fogg.

Five minutes later, Phileas Fogg, Sir Francis Cromarty, and Passepartout arrived at a hut adjoining a paddock fenced with tall palisades. In the hut was an Indian, and in the paddock, an elephant. At their request, the Indian led Mr. Fogg and his two companions into the enclosure.

There they found themselves face to face with a half-tamed animal that its owner was raising not as a beast of burden but as a beast of war. To that end he had begun to alter the animal's naturally gentle temperament, working it up by degrees to that frenzy of rage the Hindus call "mutsh," and doing it by feeding the creature sugar and butter for three months. This treatment may seem an unlikely way to get such a result, but breeders use it successfully all the same. Very fortunately for Mr. Fogg, the elephant in question had only just been put on the diet, and the "mutsh" had not yet come on.

Kiouni — that was the beast's name — could, like all his kind, keep up a brisk pace for a long stretch, and, for want of any other mount, Phileas Fogg resolved to use him.

But elephants are expensive in India, where they are beginning to grow scarce. The males, the only ones fit for the fights of the arena, are in great demand. These animals seldom breed once they've been reduced to captivity, so that the only way to get one is by hunting it down. That is why they are the object of such extreme care, and when Mr. Fogg asked the Indian whether he would rent him his elephant, the Indian flatly refused.

Fogg persisted, and offered an outrageous price for the beast — ten pounds an hour. Refused. Twenty pounds? Refused again. Forty pounds? Refused still. Passepartout jumped at every raise in the bid. But the Indian would not be tempted.

And yet it was a handsome sum. Reckoning that the elephant might take fifteen hours to reach Allahabad, that came to six hundred pounds it would earn its owner.

Phileas Fogg, without warming up in the slightest, then offered to buy the beast outright, and started off at a thousand pounds.

The Indian would not sell! Perhaps the rascal scented a magnificent piece of business.

Sir Francis Cromarty took Mr. Fogg aside and urged him to think it over before going any further. Phileas Fogg replied that he was not in the habit of acting without thought, that it came down in the end to a wager of twenty thousand pounds, that this elephant was necessary to him, and that even if he had to pay twenty times its worth, he would have this elephant.

Mr. Fogg went back to the Indian, whose small eyes, lit up with greed, made it plain enough that for him it was only a question of price. Phileas Fogg offered, one after another, twelve hundred pounds, then fifteen hundred, then eighteen hundred, and finally two thousand. Passepartout, usually so red in the face, had gone pale with emotion.

At two thousand pounds, the Indian gave in.

"By my slippers," cried Passepartout, "that puts a fine price on elephant meat!"

The deal done, all that remained was to find a guide. That proved easier. A young Parsee with an intelligent face offered his services. Mr. Fogg accepted, and promised him a handsome fee — one that could only sharpen his wits.

The elephant was brought out and fitted up without delay. The Parsee knew the trade of "mahout," or elephant driver, perfectly. He threw a kind of housing over the elephant's back and rigged up, one on each of its flanks, a pair of rather uncomfortable seats.

Phileas Fogg paid the Indian in banknotes drawn from the famous bag. It really looked as though they were being torn out of Passepartout's own insides. Then Mr. Fogg offered to carry Sir Francis Cromarty as far as the station at Allahabad. The brigadier general accepted. One more traveler was hardly going to tire the gigantic animal.

Provisions were bought at Kholby. Sir Francis Cromarty took his place in one of the seats, Phileas Fogg in the other. Passepartout settled astride the housing between his master and the brigadier general. The Parsee perched himself on the elephant's neck, and at nine o'clock the animal left the little town and plunged by the shortest route into the thick forest of fan palms.
