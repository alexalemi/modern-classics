In Which Passepartout Perhaps Talks a Little More Than He Should

In a few minutes Fix had caught up with Passepartout on the quay, where the Frenchman was strolling and taking in the sights — feeling no obligation, himself, not to look.

"Well, my friend," Fix said, coming up to him, "is your passport stamped?"

"Ah! It's you, sir," the Frenchman answered. "Much obliged. Yes, we're perfectly in order."

"And you're seeing the country?"

"Yes, but we're going so fast it feels like I'm traveling in a dream. So this is Suez, is it?"

"Suez."

"In Egypt?"

"In Egypt, exactly."

"In Africa?"

"In Africa."

"In Africa!" Passepartout repeated. "I can't believe it. Imagine, sir — I never dreamed I'd get farther than Paris, and that famous capital I saw for all of one hour and twenty minutes, from twenty past seven to twenty to nine in the morning, between the Gare du Nord and the Gare de Lyon, through the windows of a cab and in a driving rain! What a shame. I'd have loved to see Père-Lachaise and the circus on the Champs-Élysées again!"

"So you're in a great hurry?" the police inspector asked.

"Me? No, but my master is. That reminds me — I need to buy some socks and shirts! We left without any luggage, just an overnight bag."

"I'll take you to a bazaar where you'll find everything you need."

"Sir," Passepartout replied, "you really are too kind!"

And the two of them set off together. Passepartout kept right on talking.

"Above all," he said, "I must be careful not to miss the boat!"

"You've got time," Fix answered. "It's only noon!"

Passepartout pulled out his big watch.

"Noon," he said. "Come now! It's nine fifty-two!"

"Your watch is slow," Fix said.

"My watch! A family watch, handed down from my great-grandfather! It doesn't gain or lose five minutes in a year. A genuine chronometer!"

"I see what's happened," Fix said. "You're still keeping London time, which runs about two hours behind Suez. You want to reset your watch to local noon in each country."

"Me! Touch my watch!" cried Passepartout. "Never!"

"Well then, it won't agree with the sun."

"So much the worse for the sun, sir! The sun will be the one in the wrong!"

And the good fellow slipped his watch back into his pocket with a magnificent flourish.

A few moments later, Fix said to him:

"So you left London in a great rush?"

"I should say so! Last Wednesday, at eight in the evening — against all his habits — Mr. Fogg came home from his club, and three-quarters of an hour later we were on our way."

"But where's he going, this master of yours?"

"Straight ahead! He's going around the world!"

"Around the world?" Fix exclaimed.

"Yes, in eighty days! A wager, he says — but between you and me, I don't believe a word of it. It makes no sense. There's something else behind it."

"Ah! So he's a character, this Mr. Fogg?"

"I'd say so."

"And he's rich?"

"Obviously — and he's carrying a tidy sum with him, in brand-new banknotes! And he doesn't spare the money on the road, either! Why, he's promised the engineer of the Mongolia a splendid bonus if we reach Bombay well ahead of time!"

"And you've known him a long time, this master of yours?"

"Me?" said Passepartout. "I entered his service the very day we left."

One can easily imagine the effect these answers must have had on the already overheated mind of the police inspector.

This hurried departure from London, so soon after the robbery; the large sum being carried off; the rush to reach distant countries; the pretext of an eccentric wager — it all confirmed Fix in his ideas, as it was bound to. He kept the Frenchman talking and became certain that the fellow knew nothing whatever about his master; that this Fogg lived a solitary life in London, was said to be rich without anyone knowing the source of his fortune, and was a thoroughly impenetrable man — and so on. But at the same time, Fix could now hold it for certain that Phileas Fogg was not landing at Suez, and that he really was bound for Bombay.

"Is it far, Bombay?" Passepartout asked.

"Fairly far," the agent replied. "You've still got a good ten days at sea."

"And where do you place Bombay, exactly?"

"In India."

"In Asia?"

"Naturally."

"The devil! Here's the thing I'll tell you... there's one thing that's been nagging at me... it's my burner!"

"What burner?"

"My gas burner, that I forgot to turn off and that's burning away on my account. Now, I've worked it out that it comes to two shillings every twenty-four hours — exactly sixpence more than I earn — and you can see that if the trip drags on at all..."

Did Fix follow the business about the gas? Not likely. He was no longer listening; he had made up his mind. He and the Frenchman had reached the bazaar. Fix left his companion to do his shopping there, warned him not to miss the departure of the Mongolia, and hurried back to the offices of the consular agent.

Now that his conviction was settled, Fix had fully recovered his composure.

"Sir," he said to the consul, "I no longer have any doubt. I've got my man. He's passing himself off as an eccentric who wants to go around the world in eighty days."

"Then he's a clever one," the consul replied, "and he means to come back to London after throwing the police of two continents off his trail!"

"We'll see about that," Fix answered.

"But aren't you mistaken?" the consul asked once more.

"I'm not mistaken."

"Then why did this thief insist on having his passage through Suez confirmed with a stamp?"

"Why?... I've no idea, Mr. Consul," the detective replied, "but hear me out."

And in a few words he laid out the salient points of his conversation with the servant of the said Fogg.

"Indeed," said the consul, "every presumption is against this man. And what do you mean to do?"

"Fire off a telegram to London with an urgent request to send me a warrant of arrest at Bombay, board the Mongolia, shadow my thief as far as India, and there, on that English soil, walk up to him politely, my warrant in one hand and the other hand on his shoulder."

Having spoken these words coldly, the agent took his leave of the consul and made his way to the telegraph office. From there he fired off to the director of the metropolitan police that dispatch we already know about.

A quarter of an hour later, Fix — his light luggage in hand, and well supplied with money besides — was going aboard the Mongolia, and soon the swift steamer was racing at full steam over the waters of the Red Sea.
